Abstract 
Antibiotic resistance is a growing global issue, annually affecting millions of people worldwide. 
Several multi-resistant pathogenic bacteria have emerged, which can potentially make common 
infections untreatable if novel antibiotics are not discovered. Most antibiotics used today are 
derivatives of natural compounds. Therefore, it is of great importance to bring novel bioactive 
microbes into culture to discover novel natural compounds with antibacterial properties. The marine 
environment is the largest ecosystem on the planet and marine bacteria are known to produce 
unique compounds such as a variety of different enzymes but also anticancer, antifungal and 
antibacterial compounds. Because the marine environment is fairly unexplored, it is considered to 
have great potential for bioprospecting of bioactive compounds. 
Traditional microbiological methods recover approximately 1% of the total microbial diversity, a 
discrepancy referred to as “the great plate-count anomaly”. However, cultivation is still regarded as 
a powerful tool for discovering bioactive compounds as it provides the best conditions for 
understanding the biology, ecology and bioactivity of bacteria. New approaches to cultivating the so 
far uncultured bacteria have successfully relied on changing media substrates, prolonging 
incubation, and using different gelling agents. 
The present study addressed isolation and identification of hitherto uncultured bioactive bacteria 
from the marine environment. In particular, it was of interest to investigate whether substrates and 
gelling agents affected the number of bioactive isolates recovered. In addition to this, development 
of a high-throughput amplicon based method to assess the biosynthetic potential environments and 
bacteria grown in specific substrates was attempted. In this project, using gellan gum instead of agar 
in solid media, resulted in more bioactive isolates recovered. This combined with the use of gellan 
gum result in higher cultivability than agar indicate that it is well suited for bioprospecting for novel 
antibiotics. No hitherto uncultured bacteria had been isolated but a variety of different genera, 
where some are not well described, were recovered and this indicates that potential for biodiscovery 
is present. The substrates and environments included in this project were proven to have 
biosynthetic potential. Nonribosomal peptide synthetase (NRPS) and polyketide synthase (PKS) 
were used to assess biosynthetic potential, and diversity of NRPS genes was detected in all tested 
substrates. However, a high-throughput amplicon sequencing method was not successfully 
developed. Hence, more research is needed before the biosynthetic potential of substrates can be 
assessed in greater detail

Resumé 
Antibiotika resistens er et voksende globalt problem, der årligt påvirker millioner af mennesker på 
verdensplan. Adskillige multi-resistente patogene bakterier er dukket op, hvilket potentielt kan gøre 
almindelige infektioner uhelbredelige hvis nyt antibiotika ikke findes. Det meste antibiotika der 
bruges i dag er afledt af naturlige produkter. Derfor er det vigtigt at bringe nye bioaktive mikrober i 
kultur for at opdage nye naturlige stoffer med antibakterielle egenskaber. Det marine miljø er det 
største økosystem på planeten og det er kendt at marine bakterier producerer unikke stoffer såsom 
forskellige enzymer men også anticancer, antisvampe og antibakterielle stoffer. Fordi det marine 
miljø er relativt uudforsket, antages det at have stort potentiale indenfor bioprospektering af 
bioaktive stoffer. 
Traditionelle mikrobiologiske metoder gendanner cirka 1% af den totale mikrobielle diversitet, en 
uoverensstemmelse der refereres til som ”the great plate-count anomaly”. Imidlertid regnes 
kultivering stadig for et vigtigt værktøj for at kunne opdage bioaktive stoffer da det giver de bedste 
forudsætninger for at forstå biologien, økologien og bioaktiviteten hos bakterier. Nye tilgange til 
kultivering af de indtil videre u-kultiverbare bakterier har med succes baseret sig på at ændre 
substrater i mediet, forlænge inkubationen og brugt forskellige gelerings-midler. 
Dette studie adresserer isolering og identifikation af hidtil ukultiverede bioaktive bakterier fra det 
marine miljø. Særligt var der interesse for at undersøge om substrater og gelerings-midler påvirkede 
antallet af bioaktive bakterier der blev fundet. Oven i dette blev der forsøgt at udvikle en amplikon 
baseret metode med højt gennemløb til at vurdere det biosyntetiske potentiale for miljøer og 
bakterier kultiveret på specifikke substrater. I dette projekt resulterede brugen af gellan gum i stedet 
for agar i flere bioaktive isolater. Kombineret med det at brugen af gellan gum resulterer i højere 
kultivering end agar indikerer at det er egnet for bioprospektering for nyt antibiotika. Ingen hidtil 
ukultiverede bakterier blev isoleret, men flere forskellige slægter, hvor nogle ikke er velbeskrevet, 
blev fundet, hvilket indikerer at der er potentiale for at finde nye bioaktive stoffer. Substraterne og 
miljøerne der blev undersøgt i dette projekt viste sig alle at have biosyntetisk potentiale. 
Nonribosomal peptidsyntetase (NRPS) og polyketidsyntase (PKS) blev brugt til at vurdere 
biosyntetisk potentiale, og diversitet i NRPS gener blev fundet i alle undersøgte substrater. 
Imidlertid lykkedes det ikke at udvikle en amplikon baseret metode med højt gennemløb. Dette vil 
sige at mere forskning er nødvendigt før det biosyntetiske potentiale af substrater kan vurderes 
detaljeret.

Background and aim 
For many years, it has been recognized among microbiologists that the amount of bacteria known to 
be present in situ, is not replicated in the laboratory when environmental samples are cultured using 
standard cultivation techniques. This discrepancy has been termed “the great plate count anomaly”. 
More concrete, traditional microbiological cultivation methods are known to recover approximately 
1% of bacterial communities compared to numbers obtained by various staining techniques (Joint et 
al. 2010). In recent years, culture independent technologies such as metagenomics, proteomics, 
metabolomics and transcriptomics have been on the rise, giving information about uncultured 
microbes (Heidelberg et al. 2010). However, cultivation is still regarded as an essential tool for 
understanding the biology and autecology of bacteria (Joint et al. 2010). 
Most antibiotics used in the clinic today are derivatives of natural products, originally discovered by 
screening of soil-derived actinomycetes (Lewis 2013). When a novel antibiotic is discovered, 
resistance against it is emerging within years. On top of that, the pace of antibiotic discovery has 
slowed down in recent years, leading to untreatable infections due to (multi)resistant pathogens 
(Lewis 2013). Antibiotic resistance is a major health hazard and it is estimated that 2 million people 
are infected annually with antibiotic resistant pathogens claiming as much as 23,000 deaths in the 
United States alone (Blair et al. 2014). 
As a consequence of the wide-spread antibiotic resistance among well-known pathogens, it is 
essential to bring novel microbes into culture to exploit their bioactive capabilities. Chemical 
diversity is said to follow biological diversity. This should make the oceans, which cover 70% of 
the earth and harbor a massive population of microbes, a natural source of bioactive natural 
products (Whitman et al. 1998). However, most research has been made on terrestrial microbes 
making the marine environment a potential untapped source of natural compounds. 
Most approaches for obtaining so-far uncultured bacteria have been based on changing the growth 
media or prolonging the incubation times (Joint et al. 2010). Recently it was shown that substituting 
agar with gellan gum as gelling agent in media targeting marine bacteria increased the number of 
culturable bacteria significantly (Rygaard et al. 2017). This could contribute to the identification of 
novel bioactive bacteria. 
The overall aim of this project is to isolate and identify hitherto uncultured bioactive bacteria from 
marine environments. In continuation of this, partial aims will be to 
==PAGE:6==
5 
 
1) assess whether some culture conditions yield more bioactive isolates than other  
2) assess whether any diversity of the biosynthetic potential between different environments or 
culture conditions can be proven. 
Specifically, this project addresses whether uncultured bioactive bacteria can be isolated from 
estuarine surface water and sediment using three different growth media solidified gellan gum and 
agar, thereby determining which media are most promising for discovery of bioactive bacteria. In 
addition to this, constructing an amplicon sequencing-based approach to estimation of the potential 
for nonribosomal peptide synthetase and polyketide synthase biosynthesis will be attempted. This 
will establish a method for assessing the biosynthetic potential of marine environments and bacteria 
grown on specific substrates.

Introduction 
Microbes have existed in approximately 3.8-4.3 billion years (Dodd et al. 2017) and in that time, 
natural selection has optimized bioactive compounds. As a result, the most important anticancer and 
anti-infective agents are natural products (Joint et al. 2010). Approximately 70 % of the planet’s 
surface is covered by seawater, and this makes the oceans the largest ecosystems on Earth. In light 
of that, it is estimated that marine microbes contribute to 50-90% of global productivity (Glöckner 
& Joint 2010) and microbes inhabiting the marine environment are furthermore estimated to 
comprise approximately 1030 cells (Whitman et al. 1998). Marine microbes have demonstrated to 
produce an array of enzymes. A study from 2012 illustrated that marine bacteria were very relevant 
when discovering new biotechnological relevant enzymes (Mühling et al. 2012). Because of the 
large biodiversity in the oceans, a large number of secondary metabolites are produced by marine 
microbes. Additionally, to adapt and survive in the marine ecosystem, which is characterized  by 
very special conditions that differ from those found in other habitats in respect to pressure, 
temperature, pH, osmolarity and uncommon functional groups, marine microbes sometimes 
accumulate structurally unique bioactive secondary metabolites not found in terrestrial organisms 
(Debbab et al. 2010; Rocha-martin et al. 2014). The bioactive compounds produced by marine 
bacteria can be antimicrobial compounds (Cude et al. 2012) cytotoxic compounds (Hughes et al. 
2008), antitumor agents (Rocha-martin et al. 2014), or various enzymes (Mühling et al. 2012). 
There has been an increase in reported natural products from marine microorganisms in recent 
years. However, the marine environment can still be considered a relatively untapped reservoir of 
bioactivity (Rocha-martin et al. 2014). Hence, bacteria producing novel antibiotics are expected to 
be present in marine environments. It is up to researchers to discover new methods for cultivating 
bacteria from environmental samples, which could facilitate the discovery of novel microbes and 
compounds. Antibiotic resistance is a major reason for such research being of paramount 
importance, and this will be explained in the following section. 
 
Antibiotic resistance 
Antibiotics have become synonymous with antibacterial drugs, which is an essential tool in modern 
medicine. The use of antibiotics has reduced childhood mortality, increased life expectancy and is 
of paramount importance related to invasive surgery and treatments such as chemotherapy. In recent  
years, infections caused by multidrug-resistant bacteria are increasing globally and this can make 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
untreatable bacterial infections a harsh reality (Blair et al. 2014). This is supported by Global risk 
reports from the World Economic Forum, where antibiotic resistance has been stated as one of the 
greatest threats to human health (Global Risks 2013). In Europe, it is estimated that 25000 people 
die each year because of infections caused by multi-drug resistant bacteria and this costs the 
European Union 1.5 billion Euro annually (Annual Report of the Chief Medical Officer 2011). In 
the United states, antibiotic resistance is also a serious problem, where 2 million people are infected 
with antibiotic resistant bacteria annually. A direct result of this are 23000 deaths each year (Blair et 
al. 2014). 
Antibiotic resistance can be the result of different mechanisms. Resistance is divided in two groups; 
intrinsically and acquired resistance. Intrinsic antibiotic resistance of a bacteria to an antibiotic is 
the ability to resist the action of that antibiotic because of inherent structural or functional 
characteristic (Blair et al. 2014). This can be due to the absence of a susceptible target of a specific 
antibiotic in a bacterial species. An example of intrinsic resistance is the lipopeptide daptomycin, 
which is ineffective against Gram-negative bacteria, but is active against Gram-positive bacteria. 
This can be explained by an intrinsic difference in the composition of the cytoplasmic membrane in 
these two groups. Gram-negative bacteria have a lower proportion of anionic phospholipids in the 
cytoplasmic membrane than Gram-positive bacteria have. Because of this, the efficiency of the 
Ca2+-mediated insertion of daptomycin into the cytoplasmic membrane is reduced. This is required 
to exert antibacterial activity, and is the reason for its ineffectiveness against Gram-negative 
bacteria (Randall et al. 2013). The intrinsic resistance of some Gram-negative bacteria to many 
compounds is due to an inability of these compounds to cross the outer membrane (Blair et al. 
2014). Several genes that are responsible for intrinsic resistance to different antibiotic classes, such 
as aminoglycosides, beta-lactams and fluorquinolones, have been identified in recent years. This 
identification has been achieved by high-throughput screens of high-density genome mutant 
libraries in bacteria such as Staphylococcus aureus, Escherichia coli and Pseudomonas aeruginosa 
(Blake & O’Neill 2012; Fajardo et al. 2008; Liu et al. 2010). 
Acquired resistance is more relevant related to this project. The acquired resistance can be mediated 
by several mechanisms which are divided into three main groups; First, there are mechanisms that 
minimize the intracellular concentration of the antibiotic, which can be a result of poor penetration 
into the bacterium, or of increased antibiotic efflux. Second, there are mechanisms that modify the 
antibiotic target by genetic mutation or post-translational modification of the target. The third group 
involves the mechanisms that inactivate the antibiotic by hydrolysis or modification. Acquired 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
resistance against antibiotics can occur via mutations in the chromosomal genes and by horizontal 
gene transfer. (Blair et al. 2014).  
Hydrophilic antibiotics cross the outer membrane by diffusing through the outer-membrane by 
porin proteins. Hence, the permeability of the outer membrane can be achieved by down-regulation 
of porins or by the replacement of porins with more selective channels which will limit antibiotic 
entrance into the cell (Blair et al. 2014). There are examples of clinically relevant resistance to 
carbapenems in Enterobacteriaceae that occur because of mutations that reduce porin production or 
occur if mutant porin alleles are present. This resistance against carbapenems occur in the absence 
of carbapenemase, hence enzymatic degradation is not the cause of resistance (Lavigne et al. 2013; 
Poulou et al. 2013). Another mechanism to prevent antibiotics to reach their target is increased 
efflux pumps. In bacteria, the efflux pumps can have narrow substrate specificity, but many can 
transport a wide range of structurally dissimilar substrates, which are known as multidrug resistance 
efflux pumps. Bacterial efflux pumps actively transport many antibiotics out of the cell and are a 
contributor, along with reduced permeability, to intrinsic resistance of Gram-negative bacteria to 
many of the antibiotics that can be used to treat Gram-positive bacterial infections. However, over-
expressed efflux pumps can yield high levels of resistance to previously clinically useful antibiotics. 
All bacteria carry multiple genes that encode multidrug resistance efflux pumps on their 
chromosomes, but some genes have been mobilized onto plasmids that can transfer between 
bacteria (Blair et al. 2014). For example, in 2012 Dolejeska and colleagues found genes encoding 
for a novel tripartite resistance nodulation division pump carried on an IncH1 plasmid isolated from 
a Citrobacter freundii strain that furthermore carried the gene encoding the antibiotic-targeting 
enzyme “New Dehli metallo-beta-lactamase1” on the plasmid (Dolejska et al. 2012). This indicates 
that efflux, as a resistance mechanism is transmissible and could find way to other clinically 
relevant pathogens. High-level expression of efflux genes seen in multidrug-resistant bacteria is 
often due to mutation in the regulatory network controlling efflux-pump expression, and can be 
within a local repressor or a global transcription factor. Interestingly, increased expression of efflux 
pumps can occur as a result of responses to environmental signals (Blair et al. 2014). 
The mechanism of action for most antibiotics is to bind specifically to their target with high affinity, 
which prevents the normal activity of the target. As previously mentioned, antibiotic resistance can 
also occur based on changes on the antibiotic target, which can be because of genetic modification 
or post-translational modification of the target. Changes in antibiotic targets by mutation can 
prevent efficient antibiotic binding, while the target can still carry out its normal function. Point 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
mutations in a gene encoding an antibiotic target can cause these changes. Furthermore, 
transformation can also cause these mutations (Blair et al. 2014). As an example of this, methicillin-
resistant S. aureus (MRSA) can be mentioned, where the methicillin resistance is conferred by 
acquisition of the staphylococcal cassette chromosome mec (SCCmec) element. This element 
carries the mecA gene that encodes the beta-lactam-insensitive protein PBP2a. When this protein is 
present, cell wall biosynthesis occur, regardless of the native PBP being inhibited in the presence of 
an antibiotic (Katayama et al. 2000). Modification of antibiotic target sites can also happen without 
mutational changes in the genes encoding the target molecules (Blair et al. 2014). This protection of 
targets can be an effective means of antibiotic resistance, such as the erythromycin ribose methylase 
family of genes that methylate 16S rRNA and alter the drug-binding site of the bacteria. This 
prevents the binding  of several antibiotic classes such as macrolides, lincosamines and 
streptogramins (Seppala et al. 1998) 
Lastly, bacteria can destroy or modify antibiotic molecules and by that resist their action. 
Antibiotics can be inactivated by hydrolysis and thousands of enzymes that can degrade and modify 
antibiotic molecules  have been identified including beta-lactams, aminoglycosides, phenicols and 
macrolides (Blair et al. 2014). Furthermore, antibiotics can be inactivated by the addition of 
chemical groups to vulnerable sites on the antibiotic molecule. This can happen via bacterial 
enzymes and causes antibiotic resistance because it prevents the antibiotic from binding to its target 
protein as a result of steric hindrance (Blair et al. 2014). An array of different chemical groups can 
be transferred including acyl, nucleotidyl, phosphate and ribotoyl groups. The enzymes responsible 
for this form a large and diverse family of antibiotic-resistance enzymes (Wright 2005). 
Nearly all antibiotics in use today are compounds, or derivatives of these, that were discovered from 
the 1940s to 1960s. Most of these compounds were discovered by screening soil-derived 
actinomycetes such as Streptomyces spp. (Lewis 2013). However, resistance have unfortunately 
been seen to nearly all antibiotics that have been developed (Figure 1) and several infections can 
now be difficult to treat because of antibiotic resistant pathogens such as: MRSA, vancomycin-
resistant Enterococci, multi-drug resistant Pseudomonas aeruginosa and drug-resistant 
Mycobacterium tuberculosis  (Ventola 2015). Due to the gap in discovery of new antibiotics and the 
high cost of lives caused by this around the globe, it is of great importance to develop new 
antibiotics in the fight against antibiotic resistant pathogens.

Secondary metabolites of marine microorganisms 
As mentioned previously, marine microorganisms are of considerable importance as new promising 
sources of biologically active compounds. This may be due to the fact that stressful habitats often 
give rise to species of high diversity which can produce remarkable and structurally complex 
natural products (Debbab et al. 2010). In this project, focus is mainly on discovery of antibiotics to 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
use in the fight against antibiotic resistant pathogens. As mentioned previously, most antibiotics 
used in the clinic are derived from terrestrial microorganisms (Lewis 2013). However, there are 
different characteristics between these and secondary metabolites produced by marine microbes. 
One of these different traits is that the secondary metabolites produced from marine microbes have 
a high degree of halogenation. Bromide and chlorine are abundant in the marine environment, and 
they are often incorporated into the organic compounds produced in this environment. Examples of 
halogenated secondary metabolites are Marinopyrrole B and streptochlorin, which are both 
produced by marine filamentous bacteria from the Streptomyces genus (Hughes et al. 2008; Shin et 
al. 2007). Especially Marinopyrrole B is interesting in this context, as it was shown to exhibit 
significant inhibitory activity against Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) (Hughes 
et al. 2008). Extensive screening programs have been developed worldwide to explore the oceans 
for new bioactive compounds and great efforts have been devoted aiming at the isolation of new 
metabolites from marine microorganisms. In spite of this, it is believed that only a small fraction of 
the total amount of marine microbes have been investigated for bioactive metabolites (Debbab et al. 
2010).  
Several bioactive secondary metabolites isolated from marine microbes have been described. A new 
lipopeptide named tauramamide have been obtained from cultures of the marine bacterial isolate 
Brevibacillus laterosporus PNG276. This compound showed potent minimum inhibitory 
concentration (MIC) values against the important Gram-positive human pathogen Enterococcus sp. 
In addition to this, tauramamids ethyl ester showed weaker activity against MRSA (Desjardine et al. 
2007). Chemical investigation of a member of the relatively new bacterial genus Marinispora, 
isolated from a sediment sample, identified three novel 4-oxazolidinones named lipoxazolidinone 
A, B and C. These three compounds showed broad spectrum antibiotic activities similar to the 
commercial antibiotic linezolid. Lipoxazolidinones A, B and C were screened against a panel of 
different Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria. This screening displayed broad spectrum 
antibiotic activity against Gram-positive bacteria with lipoxazolidinone A as the most potent 
compound of the three (Macherla et al. 2007). In 2008, McAthur and colleagues isolated a novel 
strain of the new genus Marinispora from marine sediment. A series of new chlorinated bisindole 
pyrroles named lynamicins A-E were identified by chemical investigations. These compounds 
represent the first halogenated bisindole derivatives and their antibacterial activity against a panel of 
Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria were studied. These compounds demonstrated to possess 
broad-spectrum activity against both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria. Last but not least, 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
the compounds showed to be active against drug-resistant pathogens such as MRSA and 
vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus faecium (McArthur et al. 2008). Zafrin is a novel bacterial 
bioactive secondary metabolite found in Pseudomonas stutzeri that was isolated from intestinal tract 
of fish. Screening of zafrin indicated activity against a variety of different important clinical and 
environmental pathogens which belonged to both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria 
including MRSA and Salmonella typhi. Furthermore, zafrin showed to exhibit a faster killing rate of 
Bacillus subtilis than ampicillin, vancomycin or tetracycline (Uzair et al. 2008). In addition to the 
described bioactive compounds derived from marine bacteria, there are also several examples of 
antibacterial compounds derived from marine fungi (Kjer et al. 2010; Nguyen et al. 2007; Trisuwan 
et al. 2008; Trisuwan et al. 2009; Zhang et al. 2008).  
Even though focus in this project is on discovery of antimicrobial compounds, it should be 
mentioned, that marine microorganisms produce several relevant enzymes, and that marine 
enzymes may carry more novel chemical and stereochemical properties than those found in 
terrestrial environments (Rocha-martin et al. 2014). Enzymes acting on carbohydrates, proteolytic 
and lipolytic activities and different alcohol dehydrogenases are known examples identified from 
marine extremophiles. These extremophiles are interesting in that context, as their biocatalysts have 
allowed them to adapt in order to survive in ecological niches such as high or cold temperatures, 
extremes of pH, high salt concentrations and high pressure (Trincone 2011). 
There seem to be great possibilities in bioprospecting for secondary metabolites and enzymes in the 
marine environment. A major drawback is, however, that the vast majority of approximately 90-
99% of marine microorganisms cannot be cultured under standard laboratory conditions (Glöckner 
& Joint 2010). These barriers to isolating marine microbes will be presented in the following 
section. 
 
Cultivation barriers in isolating bacteria 
The great plate-count anomaly, refers to the low cultivability of marine microbes (Staley & 
Konopka 1985). This describes the great difference in the number of colonies developed on 
laboratory medium and the number of colonies that could be counted by epifluorescence 
microscopy of DAPI-stained samples. 
There can be many possible reasons for the challenges in culturing of marine bacteria. First of all, 
marine bacteria may be unable to grow on a given substrate or combination of substrates provided 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
in laboratory cultures. So far, little knowledge is available of organic substrates and of the 
concentration needed for growth of marine microbes and this can make cultivation difficult. 
Furthermore, the laboratory culture can be responsible for destroying interactions that occur 
between organisms in natural environments, i.e. the fastest growing species may overwhelm those 
that divide very slow. This can lead to an imbalance of cell-to-cell communications or inhibitory 
compounds may be produced that inactivate cells of other microbes in the vicinity. In addition to 
this, the concentrations of substrates required for detectable growth in the laboratory can very well 
be toxic to some marine bacteria that have evolved under oligotrophic conditions. This was to name 
some speculations of why marine bacteria are difficult to culture, more reasons have been proposed 
(Joint et al. 2010). 
Several approaches are applied to solve the challenges of cultivation of marine microbes such as 
experimentations with medium and duration of incubation (Joint et al. 2010). In particular, 
mimicking of the natural marine environment for in vitro cultivation combined with long-term 
incubation in low-nutrient media containing marine sediments have resulted in cultivation of  
former “unculturable” marine bacteria (Jensen et al. 2005; Choi et al. 2013; Choi et al. 2015). 
Perhaps the most noteworthy previously unculturable marine bacteria that has been brought to 
culture is Candidatus ‘Pelagibacter ubique’ or SAR11. This was obtained by use of a medium with 
a low concentration of nutrients and seawater from the sampling site (Rappé et al. 2002). SAR11 is 
interesting because it is considered as the most abundant organism on the planet accounting for 25% 
of the bacterial 16S rRNA genes recovered from marine surface waters, and it has been found in 
almost every seawater sample surveyed (Joint et al. 2010). More specifically, Morris and colleagues 
found that 35% of the cells in surface waters belong to SAR11, and that they reached densities of 
450.000 cells/ml in some samples (Morris et al. 2002).  
The communication between bacteria, so called quorum sensing, have also been shown to be 
involved in an increasing number of microbial processes in Gram-positive as well as Gram-negative 
bacteria that have been found in several different environments, including the marine environment. 
Quorum sensing involves the production of signaling molecules that diffuse between cells, and 
thereby affecting the whole population (Joint et al. 2010). Quorum sensing can be involved in a 
variety of different microbial activities such as motility (Daniels et al. 2004), symbiosis 
(Wisniewski-dy & Downie 2002), secondary metabolite production (Griffin et al. 2004), biofilm 
maturation (Parsek & Greenberg 2005), and virulence (Williams et al. 2007) have been described in 
different bacterial genera. In addition to this, it has been demonstrated that some bacterial species 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
can selectively destroy signal molecules produced by other species (Tait et al. 2009), hence the right 
conditions for quorum sensing to influence the isolation of novel clades may not be easy to identify. 
In 2017, Rygaard and colleagues did show that low concentrations of acylated homoserine lactones 
supplemented to the growth medium did increase the relative abundance of Sphingobacteria 
(Rygaard et al. 2017).  Quorum sensing could therefore potentially play a role in the growth of 
individual bacterial clades, and by this influence their ability to grow in the laboratory (Joint et al. 
2010). 
Metabolic consortia are another mechanism for interaction that may be important for successful 
isolation of marine bacteria. In a microbial consortium, compounds produced by one species can be 
metabolized by other species (Joint et al. 2010). This commensalism has been demonstrated, where 
some marine microbes only grew when paired with a specific combination of other species. The 
reason for this was identified as an exchange of siderophores that was the critical growth factor 
(Onofrio et al. 2010).  
The diffusion chamber is designed to utilize microbial consortia for cultivation. The principle 
behind it is that the cells to be cultivated are embedded in agar, which is separated from the natural 
habitat by a membrane filter that only allows exchange of critical growth factors and simultaneously 
removes low-molecular weight inhibitory end products (Epstein et al. 2010). Experimenting with 
the diffusion chamber has been tried at different conditions resembling natural conditions 
(Kaeberlein et al. 2002; Bollmann et al. 2007; Gavrish et al. 2008; Aoi et al. 2009; Sizova et al. 
2012). Cultivation in diffusion chambers has allowed growth of up to 40% of bacterial cells from a 
marine environment. This is a clear improvement compared to the number that grew on traditional 
petri dishes (Kaeberlein et al. 2002). 
Impact of gelling agent in cultivation 
Traditional cultivation methods such as plating have continued to result in isolation of new 
microbial species. These results have been achieved by development of new media, often 
mimicking the natural environment. However, optimization of well-known and often used media by 
changing the media formulation or extend the incubation time have also proven successful (Alain & 
Querellou 2009). This is because different organisms require a different set of nutrients in varying 
concentrations, which does not make the design of growth media an easy task (Alain & Querellou 
2009; Joint et al. 2010). Furthermore, substitution of gelling agents have also proved to affect the 
microorganisms cultivated on several occasions (Shungu et al. 1983; Lin & Casida JR. 1984; Harris 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
1985; Rule & Alexander 1986; Janssen et al. 2002; Tamaki et al. 2005; Tamaki et al. 2009; Joint et 
al. 2010; Tanaka et al. 2014; Rygaard et al. 2017). This effect of changing gelling agent was present 
in various environments and did result in the isolation of previously unculturable bacteria. 
Despite having used alternative gelling agents with success, agar is by far the one most widely used, 
and has been so since the 1890s where it revolutionized microbiology. This may be due to the 
thermal stability of agar, but in addition to this, agar has excellent gelling strength and is easily 
handled (Tanaka et al. 2014). Agar is composed of a mixture of two polysaccharides named agarose 
and agaropectin (Araki 1956). Agarose is composed of repetitive units of β-D-glucose and 3,6-
anhydro-α-L-galactose and agaropectin has the same basic structure, however some hydroxyl 
groups of 3,6.anhydro-α-L-galactose residues are replaced by sulfoxy, methoxy and pyryvate 
residues (Figure 2). Agar is extracted from the cell wall of a group of red algae called 
Rhodophyceae, hence it is a natural part of the marine environment (Fu & Kim 2010). Bacteria that 
are able to degrade agar have also been isolated from the marine environment on several occasions 
(Potin et al. 1993; Sugano et al. 1993; Ohta et al. 2005; Wang et al. 2006; Hu et al. 2009). While 
agar continues to be the preferred gelling agent, several other gelling agents have been discovered 
such as xanthan gum (Paul et al. 1986) carrageenan (Lines 1977), isubgol (Jain et al. 1997), guar 
gum (Jain et al. 2005) and finally gellan gum (Shungu et al. 1983).

Gellan gum (Gelrite ®), formerly known as PS-60 and S-60, is a highly purified heterosaccharide 
biopolymer which is a naturally derived fermentation product of Sphingomonas elodea (formerly 
Pseudomonas elodea and also referred to as Sphingomonas paucimobilis). Gellan gum was 
==PAGE:19==

discovered by Kelco Div. (Merck & Co. inc., San Diego, California) (Shungu et al. 1983) in 1978 
and consists of a backbone of a repeating unit of β-D-glucose, D-glucoronic acid, β-D-glucose and 
α-L-rhamanose respectively (Figure 3) (Giavasis et al. 2000; Morris et al. 2012).

Gellan gum form a firm non-elastic clear gel at room temperature. It does so in the presence of 
multivalent cations such as magnesium or calcium, and the temperature where the gel solidifies are 
dependent on the concentration of the cations (Kang et al. 1982; Morris et al. 2012). Gellan gum 
exhibit excellent thermal stability with a similar percentage decrease of gel-strength as agar, 
however the initial gel strength is higher for gellan gum  (Kang et al. 1982). This makes gellan gum 
ideal for isolation and growth of thermophile microorganisms which have been practiced on several 
occasions (Shungu et al. 1983; Lin & Casida JR. 1984; Harris 1985). The gelling strength of gellan 
gum is, like agar, influenced by pH, where higher pH of the medium results in harder gels. In 
addition to this, macrosalts, sucrose concentration, basal nutrient formulation and concentration of 
Gelrite influence the gelling strength of the gellan gum medium (Huang et al. 1995). 
A substantial amount of enzymes was initially tested on gellan gum for enzymatic degradation, 
without having any effect on the gel strength (Kang et al. 1982). However, some gellan gum 
degrading bacteria have been discovered. In 1994, Kennedy & Sutherland reported that a number of 
bacterial strains being able to degrade gellan gum had been isolated by standard enrichment 
procedures. The strains included both Gram-positive  and -negative bacteria, but the gellan gum 
degradation was similar, namely eliminase-type enzymes (lyases) was cleaving the β-D-glycosyl-
(14)-β-D-glucuronosyl sequence of gellan gum (Kennedy & Sutherland 1994). In addition to this, 
several other gellan gum lyases have been isolated from mesophilic Gram-positive and Gram-
negative bacteria (Hashimoto et al. 1996; Hashimoto et al. 1997; Jung et al. 2006).

The amount of gellan gum used in media preparation is approximately half of that of agar (in gram 
per liter). Yet, the price of gellan gum (Gelzan™) is approximately half the price of agar 
(Comparing Sigmaaldrich’s price of 1 kg Gelzan™ and 1 kg agar on 24/11-2017). This leads to 
gellan gum as a cost-efficient alternative to agar at approximately 25% of the total cost. 
Studies investigating cultivation of bacterial diversity of lake sediment on gellan gum and agar have 
concluded that using gellan gum as gelling agent gives higher diversity of bacteria than using agar 
(Tamaki et al. 2005). Increased bacterial diversity has also been recovered from soil by using gellan 
gum instead of agar as gelling agent (Gavrish et al. 2008). Last and most recently, gellan gum 
proved to recover increased bacterial diversity from the marine environment as well (Rygaard et al. 
2017). The three studies mentioned show that cultivation with gellan gum has obvious advantages 
compared to agar. More, and more diverse, bacteria can be cultivated by using gellan gum.  
Culture-independent techniques 
In addition to culture dependent techniques, increased focus has been on culture-independent 
molecular techniques during the last decade. The aim of these approaches is to sequence entire 
communities of in vivo-isolated bacteria from natural environments via high-throughput sequencing 
(HTS). Hence, HTS has revolutionized the opportunities to explore genetic information from a 
wider phylogenetic range than what was possible with cultivated isolates (Heidelberg et al. 2010) 
The first generation of molecular tools, for example PCR, fingerprinting and Sanger sequencing, 
have during the last decades provided us with insights in central topics of microbiology such as 
ecological interactions, diversity distribution and magnitude, evolutionary histories and community 
metabolism. These insights were provided with first generation of molecular tools by extracting 
DNA and RNA directly from the environment, and these data made it possible to characterize taxa, 
and determine ecological and functional relations (Logares et al. 2012). Using first generation 
sequencing techniques, previous studies have showed large diversity at taxonomic- (Pace 1997) and 
metabolic (Venter et al. 2004) levels. However, these studies did leave a large majority of rare taxa 
unaccounted for, because they only captured the most abundant microbes (Pedro-Alio 2006). First 
generation sequencing, and especially Sanger sequencing dominated the sequencing needs for 
decades (Metzker 2010). A major accomplishment of these techniques was a study (The Human 
Genome Project), that in 2004 resulted in the first finished grade human genome. The study was a 
long-time running process spanning over a decade (Collins et al. 2004). This also shows the most 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
important difference between first generation sequencing and HTS, namely the throughput (Logares 
et al. 2012). 
In one run, traditional Sanger sequencing can potentially generate 102 sequences of 600-900 bp 
length. In comparison, HTS can potentially generate 106-109 sequences of 100-700 bp per run 
(Glenn 2011; Scholz et al. 2012). This much higher throughput results in large amounts of data, 
which offers much higher precision, but also demands powerful computers to process. (Logares et 
al. 2012). 
Several different HTS platforms exist such as Illumina, SOLiD, and 454/Roche all of which have 
pros and cons (Metzker 2010; Glenn 2011; Logares et al. 2012). All platforms have in common that  
library preparation is the starting point for the protocol (Logares et al. 2012). In this project, the 
Illumina platform is used, so this will be described in more detail. Illumina is a popular HTS 
platform based on bridge-amplification to amplify the adaptor-containing template bound to the 
surface of a glass flow cell (Logares et al. 2012). In this project, the idea is to sequence specific 
PCR products. The preparation of PCR products for Illumina is done by PCR with specific primers 
to target the desired sequences, and platform specific adaptors are part of these primers. The final 
amplicons with adaptors can be assembled by two rounds of PCR, which makes them ready for 
sequencing. Multiplexing of the samples can be done using short DNA barcode sequences called 
Multiplex-identifiers (MID) that are part of the PCR primers (Logares et al. 2012). The MID makes 
it possible to identify which sample a specific amplicon belong to. Solid-phase amplification can 
subsequently be performed by Illumina and is composed of two basic steps. First step is initial 
priming and extending of the single stranded, single-molecule template and second step is bridge-
amplification of the immobilized template with immediately adjacent primers to form clusters. The 
imaging of the sequences is done by incorporation of dye-labeled nucleotides one-by-one. The dye-
labeled nucleotides contain reversible terminators, so after imaging, a cleavage step removes the 
fluorescent dye and regenerates the 3’-OH group. This makes the sequence ready for another cycle 
of nucleotide incorporation (Metzker 2010). This process goes on in several million clusters, 
resulting in up to 3×109 100 bp reads or 300 Gb can be obtained with the Illumina platform in a 11-
day run using single end sequencing (6×109 100 bp reads, 600 Gb for paired end sequencing). For 
the Illumina platform, there is also a benchtop sequencer “MiSeq” that yields 150-300 bp paired end 
reads and can produce up to 4.6 Gb data / run in approximately a day. The large amount of 
information generated from this sequencing technique can be analyzed using different open source 
or custom-made software (Logares et al. 2012).

In-depth analyses, where community composition can be compared, can be performed with HTS 
platforms (Rygaard et al. 2017). In addition to this, the biosynthetic capacity of a certain 
environment can also be assessed by these methods (Charlop-powers et al. 2016). Despite the 
possibilities in culture-independent molecular techniques, isolation and culturing of bacteria using 
innovative approaches are still important for future biodiscovery efforts. This is because cultures 
provide ways to discover the physiology of microbes that HTS does not (Joint et al. 2010). Using 
the method of Charlop-powers and colleagues to generate a pipeline for investigating the 
biosynthetic potential of marine environments and media substrates. This method could be an

Nonribosomal peptide synthetases and polyketide synthases 
Currently it is unknown if the increase of bacterial cultivability and diversity from using gellan gum 
as gelling agent affects the potential for discovery of novel bioactive natural products. 
Nonribosomal peptides and polyketides are two large groups of natural products synthesized by 
nonribosomal synthetases (NRPSs) and polyketide synthases (PKSs) respectively. Common for 
these two groups is that the applications of the synthesized products are broad and includes many 
clinically important drugs. These compounds are produced by the secondary metabolism of bacteria 
and fungi, however not in a similar manner (Shen 2003; Strieker et al. 2010). NRPSs and PKSs are 
so central in producing bioactive natural products, that the presence of them, can be used to screen 
for bioactivity, or at least the biosynthetic potential (Martens et al. 2007). 
Nonribosomal peptide synthetases 
Nonribosomal peptide synthetases are large multimodular biocatalysts that can utilize complex 
regiospecific and stereospecific reactions to assemble structurally and functionally diverse peptides. 
This ribosome-independent peptide synthesis work by catalytic domains of NRPSs select, activate 
or modify the covalently tethered intermediates that controls the iterative chain elongation and 
product release (Strieker et al. 2010). As indicated, nonribosomal peptides are very functionally 
diverse, which can be explained by the way they are synthesized by NRPSs (Finking & Marahiel 
2004; Walsh 2008). NRPSs condensates amino acids monomers, which are not limited to the 20 
proteinogenic amino acids. Around 500 different monomers are recognized as building blocks for 
nonribosomal petides and these include nonproteinogenic amino acids, fatty acids and α-hydroxy 
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
acids, thus leading to great diversity in the monomer composition (Caboche et al. 2008; Strieker et 
al. 2010). 
NRPSs are composed of an array of distinct modular sections, where each is responsible for the 
incorporation of one defined monomer into the final peptide product. A certain order of the modules 
are required in the assembly line of nonribosomal peptides: First, the sequence of monomer units 
are activated and incorporated; second, the chemistry that occurs at each point in the assembly line; 
and third, the length and functionality of the peptide product determined and released from the 
distal end of the assembly line (Figure 4). (Fischbach & Walsh 2006). These modules can be 
divided into catalytic domains, where three modules are essential for peptide elongation and always 
present in NRPSs. These catalytic domains have different responsibilities related to assembling the 
nonribosomal peptide: The adenylation domain (A) is responsible for the activation of the amino 
acid; the peptide carrier protein (PCP) is responsible for the propagation of the growing peptide 
chain; and the condensation domain is responsible for the condensation of the amino acids. 
Additionally, the thioesterase (TE) domain is a fourth essential NRPS catalytic unit associated with 
product release. The TE domain is located in the termination module where it catalyzes peptide 
release by hydrolysis or macrocyclization (Strieker et al. 2010). The structure of each domain has 
been determined by either crystal- or NMR structure elucidation, however different conformations 
and structural states of PCP- and adenylation domains have been observed. During the last decade, 
progress have been made in understanding of the mechanisms of nonribosomal peptide synthesis, 
even though we do not understand everything yet (Strieker et al. 2010).

adenylation domain. (2) The amino acid is transferred onto the PCP domain. (3) Condensation of the PCP-
bound amino acids. (4) Amino acid modifications. (5) Transesterification of the peptide chain from the 
terminal PCP onto the TE domain. (6) product release. It should be noted that the number of modification 
domains can be very variable. Modified from (Strieker et al. 2010). 
The applications of nonribosomal peptides are broad and include antibacterial-, antifungal- and 
antitumor drugs in addition of immunosuppressants (Strieker et al. 2010). Daptomycin and 
ramoplanin are two examples of approved antibiotic drugs that are synthesized by NRPSs (Walsh 
2004). The best-understood mode of action of daptomycin is permeabilization of the cytoplasmic 
membrane (Taylor & Palmer 2016) and ramoplanin inhibit the cell wall synthesis by binding 
intermediates along the pathway to mature peptidoglycan (Fang et al. 2005). This shows that the 
mode of action of antibiotic compounds synthesized by NRPSs can be different.  
Polyketide synthases 
Polyketides are biosynthesized from acyl-CoA precursors by PKSs. Three different types of 
bacterial PKSs are known, namely type I PKS, type II PKS, and type III PKS. Type I PKS are 
multifunctional enzymes which are organized into modules where each module harbor  a set of 
==PAGE:25==
24 
 
distinct non-iteratively acting activities that mediate the catalysis of one cycle of polyketide chain 
elongation (Staunton & Weissman 2001). The type II PKSs are multienzyme complexes that carry a 
single set of iteratively acting activities (Shen 2003). Type III PKSs, which is also known as 
chalcone synthase like PKSs are homodimeric enzymes that are iteratively acting condensing 
enzymes  (Moore & Hopke 2001). The three different types of PKSs have structural and mechanic 
differences, however they all do biosynthesize polyketides by sequential decarboxylative 
condensation of the acyl-CoA precursors and the ketoacyl synthase (KS) domain (type I PKS), or 
subunit (Type II and III PKSs) catalyzes the C-C bond-forming step (Figure 5). In addition to this, 
type I and II PKSs use acyl carrier protein (ACP) to activate the acyl CoA substrates. Type III PKSs 
act directly on the acyl CoA substrates, and is hence  independent of an ACP (Shen 2003).

enzymes organized in modules of non-iteratively acting domains. B) type II PKS: multienzyme complexes 
consisting of a single set of iteratively acting subunits. C) type III PKS: a single subunit that act iteratively. 
ACP and KS domains are shown in green and orange. Adapted from (Shen 2003).

Polyketide synthases have remarkably versatility and amenability, which allows generation of 
compounds which would be difficult to produce by other means. Furthermore, the structure, 
mechanism and catalytic reactivity of PKSs provide extraordinary opportunities to investigate the 
molecular mechanisms of enzyme catalysis. Polyketide natural products make up an extraordinary 
class of compounds that exhibit a large range of functional and structural diversity, which gives 
them broad biological activities. Hence, PKSs have promising commercial values leading research 
being conducted on them (Shen 2003). Several compounds synthesized by PKSs have been 
discovered to have a wide variety of different medicinally important activities, including antibiotic, 
antifungal, antiparasitic, anticancer and immunosuppressive properties (Staunton & Weissman 
2001).  
Several relevant antibacterial compounds synthesized by PKSs can be mentioned, for example: 
tetracycline, erythromycin, rifamycin S, and actinorhodin (Staunton & Weissman 2001; Shen 
2003). The antibacterial activity of these compounds furthermore vary, for example, erythromycin 
targets the protein synthesis machinery in bacteria, where it binds competitively to a site near the 
peptidyltranferase center and inhibits protein elongation (Lovmar et al. 2004). Actinorhodin is on 
the other hand a redox-active antibiotic with a complex mechanism of action that involves multiple 
molecular targets and it is  not yet fully  understood (Mak & Nodwell 2017) 
Finally, biosynthesis of complex natural products have also been demonstrated to be produced by 
mixed or hybrid multienzyme complexes containing both NRPSs and PKSs (Martens et al. 2007). 
This formation of hybrid clusters containing elements from each class is allowed due to the 
structural and catalytic similarities between PKSs and NRPSs. The hybrid NRPS-PKS clusters can 
allow even greater variety for potential secondary metabolites produced by microorganisms. Hybrid 
NRPS-PKS compounds have been isolated from several marine bacteria, where most are produced 
by gram-positive actinomycetes (Mizuno et al. 2013). However, andrimide is an example of an 
antibiotic produced by Vibrio spp. by a NRPS-PKS system (Mansson et al. 2011).

Strains and media 
Different media were used during this project. Difco™ 2216 Marine broth (MB; full-strength and 
½-strength) solidified with 15 g/L Applichem agar bacteriology grade or 6.5 g/L Gelrite® Sigma 
life sciences, gellan gum (MBA/½MBA and MBG/½MBG) and sterile-filtered (0.2 µm) seawater

to cultivate bacteria from marine samples. The media solidified with agar and SWG, were prepared 
by mixing the ingredients and sterilized by autoclaving. MBG and ½MBG were prepared in two 
solutions, one with 4 times the desired concentration of MB (14.96 g for MBG and 7.48 g for 
½MBG in 100 ml distilled water) and one with 2.6 g gellan gum and 0.39 g MgSO4 · 7H2O in 300 
ml distilled water. Both solutions were sterilized by autoclaving, cooled to approximately 80˚ C, 
mixed, and poured into plates immediately. For dilutions, 20 g/L Aquarium systems Instant ocean 
in distilled water (2% IO) was used. In addition to these media, 30 g/L Aquarium systems Instant 
ocean, 3.34 Bacto ™ Casamino acids, 10 g/L Applichem agar bacteriology grade (3% IO) were 
used in V. anguillarum assays. Pre-cultures of Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-287 (Skov et al. 1995) 
were made by inoculating colony mass in 20 ml ½YTSS broth (2 g Bacto Yeast extract, 1.25g 
tryptone, and 20 g Sigma Sea Salts) and afterwards incubated overnight at room temperature on a 
Heidolph unimax 2010 shaking table at 200 rpm. 
Sampling of seawater and sediment

18.39 PSU). Three samples were collected within meters of each other to give three biological

DNA extractions from environmental samples 
The DNA extraction was done using an adapted phenol/chloroform protocol (Boström et al. 2004). 
On the day of the sampling, approximately 300 ml of seawater was filtered onto a polycarbonate 
filter (G&E Water & Process Technologies, 0.2 µm, 47 mm) and the filter was submersed in one ml 
lysisbuffer (400 mM NaCl, 750 mM sucrose, 20 mM EDTA, and 50 mM Tris-HCL dissolved in 
MilliQ H2O) containing one mg/ml lysozyme. The sediment samples were prepared by mixing one 
gram of sediment with one ml lysisbuffer with lysozyme. For the other types of samples included in 
this project (Bryozoans and oyster), biomass was mixed with one ml lysisbuffer with lysozyme. All 
sample types were stored at -20˚ C until extraction and purification.

with 500 µl TE buffer, followed by mixing of the TE and lysate. For the sediment, bryozoan, and

16S phylogeny 
Heat treatment was used to extract DNA from the strains that displayed bioactivity in the spot-
assays. Twenty isolates isolated from the same site in 2016 were analyzed in addition to those 
isolated in this study. The supernatant was discarded (from cultures centrifuged at 10000 × g for 10 
minutes) and the pellet was resuspended in 500 µl sterile H2O. The samples were heated to 100˚ C 
for 10 minutes and put on ice for two minutes after. The samples were sonicated with Aerospec 
industrie water sonicator in two minutes. Subsequently, the samples were incubated on ice for two 
minutes and centrifuged at 1000 rpm for 5 minutes. The supernatant was used as template for 16S 
rRNA gene PCR. 
Five µl heat lysate was used for PCR amplification of the 16S rRNA gene, using 10 µM of the 27F 
and 1492 R primers. In addition to this, mastermix (Ampliqon, TEMPase hotstart 2xMastermix C 
BLUE) and distilled water was added to a total volume of 25 µl. The PCR program used were one 
cycle of 15 minutes 95˚ C followed by 35 cycles of 95˚ C (30 seconds), 51˚ C (30 seconds) 72˚ C (2 
minutes) followed by one cycle of 72˚ C for 5 minutes. For visualization, four µl of the PCR 
products were loaded on a 1% agarose gel, with a 1kb GeneRuler (Thermo Scientific) ladder. 
To purify the amplified DNA for Sanger sequencing, four µl distilled water was added to the PCR 
products along with Thermo scientific’s exonuclease I and fastAP (0.5 µl of each). This solution 
was heated to 37˚ C for 15 minutes followed by 15 minutes at 85˚ C. Five µl of each of the purified 
PCR products were mixed with 5 µM 27F and 1492R primer in separate tubes. These tubes were 
sent for Sanger sequencing at GATC biotech (European Custom Sequencing Centre, Gottfried-
Hagen-strasse 20, 51105 Cologne Germany), or at Macrogen-Europe (Meibergdreef 31 1105 AZ, 
Amsterdam the Netherlands).  
In CLC Genomics Workbench, 16S sequences were trimmed according to the quality scores. 
Reverse complement sequences were made from the reverse reads, and the forward and reverse read 
were assembled to a reference 16S sequence (Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-287). The assembled 16S 
sequences were nucleotide blasted against the 16S ribosomal RNA sequences database in Genbank 
(https://blast.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/Blast.cgi) to identify the most closely related cultivated species. 
Close hits were added to an alignment of all the obtained 16S rRNA gene sequences and a neighbor 
joining tree was constructed in CLC (bootstrap 1000) using Bacillus aquaemaris as outgroup.

Diversity of NRPS sequences from different growth substrates 
As it was not possible to obtain suited PCR products for high-throughput amplicon sequencing, a 
cloning based Sanger sequencing approach was adapted. The TOPO® TA Cloning® Kit for 
Sequencing (Invitrogen™ by Life technologies) was used to assess the diversity of NRPSs from the 
bacteria grown on MB and SW substrates solidified with either agar or gellan gum. First, a PCR 
reaction was performed using Ampliqon TEMPase hotstart 2 × Mastermix C BLUE, one µl of 
template DNA (for the extracted DNA from MBA, MBG, SWA and SWG plates), 10 µM a2f and 
a3r primers and distilled water to 25 µl. The PCR program used was 95˚C for 15 minutes followed 
by 40 cycles of 95˚ C for one minute, 70˚ C for one minute and 72˚ C for two minutes, followed by 
a final elongation step at 72˚ C for 7 minutes. For visualization of the PCR products, 5 µl were 
loaded in a 1% agarose gel along with a generuler 1 kb ladder. 
Next, the TOPO® cloning reaction was made. Two out of the three PCR products that produced the 
clearest bands were pooled together. Four µl of the pooled fresh PCR product were mixed with one 
µl salt solution (1.2 M NaCl; 0.06 M MgCl2 that was supplied with the kit) and one µl TOPO® 
==PAGE:37==
36 
 
vector to a final volume of 6 µl. The reaction was mixed gently and incubated at room temperature 
for 10 minutes and afterwards placed on ice. 
Now, the chemical transformation of One Shot® TOP10 competent cells was performed. Two µl of 
the TOPO® cloning reaction was added to a vial of One Shot® chemically competent E. coli 
TOP10 and mixed very gently. The mixture was incubated for 20 minutes and transferred to a 42˚ C 
water bath for exactly 30 second to heat-shock the cells. Immediately after, the tubes were put on 
ice, 250 µl of S.O.C. medium, which was pre-heated to room temperature, was added to each vial. 
The tubes were incubated horizontally for one hour at 37˚ C at 200 rpm. The culture was plated 
onto plates containing Luria Bertani agar with 100 µl ampicillin per ml medium and incubated 
overnight at 37˚ C. 24 colonies with inserts of the PCR product from each medium (MBA, MBG, 
SWA and SWG) were transferred to a 96 well stab ampicillin plate and sent for SUPREMERUN™ 
sequencing at GATC Biotech. 
The area outside of the two EcoRI restriction sites were removed along with the rest of the vector 
(according to information of the nucleotides from the protocol). Sequences that had been inserted in 
reverse were reverse complemented. The nucleotide sequences were translated to proteins while 
making sure that they were in the same frames. Clustering (0.95 sequence identity cut-off) of the 
protein sequences was performed in CD-HIT (http://weizhongli-lab.org/cd-hit/) and one sequence 
from each cluster was protein blasted against non-redundant protein sequences in NCBI. One 
sequence from each cluster was used as reference for that cluster. However, if only sequences with 
low identity (<80%) were found with the protein BLAST a reference sequence from that cluster was 
not included. The sequences were aligned (gap open cost: 10 and gap extension cost 1). And 
neighbor joining un-rooted phylogenetic tree was made with a bootstrap of 1000.

Growth on different media solidified with agar or gellan gum 
The seawater and sediment samples were inoculated on three different media i.e. MB, ½MB and 
SW solidified with agar or gellan gum. Each sample type (water and sediment) represent three 
biological replicates, with a technical duplicates of each biological triplicate. Hence, each CFU 
value is based on CFU counts from 6 plates. In addition to this, SYBR gold staining was performed 
to determine the total amount of cells in situ. The CFU was furthermore determined 2 and 5 weeks 
after inoculation. In Excel, a t-test with was used to determine the significance of the differences 
between means in the CFU counts. 
In general, the CFUs obtained from the sediment samples were approximately 2 log units higher 
than for the water samples. The sediment samples had CFU-values of approximately 106 CFU/ml 
where the water samples had approximately 104 CFU/ml. For the water samples after 2 weeks, there 
was a small non-significant difference (MB t-test, P=0.0959, n=6; ½MB t-test, P=0.0589, n=6) 
between the CFU with MB and ½MB solidified with agar or gellan gum. For the seawater medium 
(SW) the CFU difference after 2 weeks of incubation between agar and gellan gum was statistically 
significant (t-test, P=0.0247, n=6) (Appendix 1A). After 5 weeks of incubation, statistically 
significant differences were calculated between gellan gum and agar for all substrates (Figure 9A). 
For MB (t-test, P= 0.0487, n=6), ½MB (t-test, P=0.0124, n=6) and SW (t-test, P=0.00287, n=6) 
media solidified with agar and gellan gum, gellan gum produced higher CFU/ml than agar. After 2 
and 5 weeks of incubation, the largest differences in CFU between gellan gum and agar was seen 
with SW media. 
Comparing the CFU/ml of the water samples after 2 and 5 weeks of incubation show significant 
differences for some substrates (Figure 9B & Appendix 1B). For all substrates except for MBG and 
SWA there is a significant difference between 2 and 5 weeks of incubation (MBA t-test, P=0.0250, 
n=6; ½MBA t-test, P=0.00480, n=5; ½MBG t-test, P=0.00281, n=6; SWG t-test, P=0.00494, n=6),

were identified as having high identities to some clusters. Protein blasting for representative 
sequences of each cluster identified NRPS sequences from several bacteria (Figure 18). All growth 
substrates clustered with NRPS. Growth substrates were seen to cluster together in several places. 
Additionally, clusters of gellan gum and agar was also seen. However, large clusters with sequences 
==PAGE:49==
48 
 
representing all growth substrates and gelling agents were also present. Some clusters have no 
representative sequences included in the tree, because the identity between them was too low. For 
them, protein blast recovered sequences for marR family transcriptional regulator and acyl-CoA but 
with an identity less than 80%, so they were excluded from the tree.

Discussion 
The cultivation of hitherto uncultured marine bacteria is important for the discovery of novel 
bioactive compounds such as antibiotics, enzymes and anticancer agents. This is due to the fact that 
marine microbes are known to produce a vast variety of these compounds, and the surface of the 
potential of marine microbes have barely been scratched (Joint et al. 2010). 
Especially, the discovery of novel antibiotics is of great importance today, because  antibiotic 
resistance is an increasing problem and untreatable infections might become very real if novel 
antibiotics are not developed (Lewis 2013; Blair et al. 2014). Based on this, it is of paramount 
importance to gain more knowledge of marine bacteria and their metabolism. This is best done 
without further delay for two reasons. Firstly, even though an increasing amount of bioactive 
compounds have been reported lately, few of them make it to the clinical pipeline (Waters et al. 
2010). Secondly, it will take several years from  a bioactive compound is discovered until it enters  
the clinics (Dimasi et al. 2003). So, addressing the need for novel antibiotics should be done before 
we are in dire need of it. Increased knowledge of microbes can be gained through two scientific 
methods, one being culture-dependent and the other culture-independent. Both offer various 
advantages. The application of those two techniques can be used to supplement each other, which 
was attempted in this project. 
The aim of this study was to isolate and identify hitherto uncultured bacteria from the marine 
environment. In extension of this it was addressed whether some culture conditions yield more 
bioactive isolates than others. Also, it was attempted to use high-throughput amplicon sequencing to 
address the biosynthetic potential of environments and bacteria grown on solid substrates, in order 
to create a method for assessing the potential for bioactive compounds based on environments and 
culture conditions i.e. substrates and gelling agents.  
The environmental samples were inoculated onto three different media using marine broth or 
seawater as nutrition source. Three media were made with this, consisting of full-strength MB, ½-
strength MB or seawater with agar or gellan gum as gelling agent respectively. The rationale behind 
this was to include media that was nutrient rich (MB), nutrient poor (SW) and something in 
between (½MB) in the study, while simultaneously assessing the effect of the gelling agent. Marine 
broth was included due to its widespread use in cultivation of marine bacteria (Jensen et al. 1996; 
Choi et al. 2013; Rygaard et al. 2017). Lower nutrient concentrations are known to promote growth 
of slow growing oligotrophic bacteria which would be incapable of growth with higher substrate 
==PAGE:51==
50 
 
concentrations (Ishida & Kadota 1981). This means  that using low nutrient media will lower the 
selection of fast growing organisms, compared to using a  high nutrient media (Watve et al. 2000). 
Specifically, using media with lower amounts of nutrients, like ½MB and especially SW, should 
increase the likelihood of isolating slow growing bacteria, which might have been outcompeted in 
studies solely using marine broth. 
Experimenting with the incubation time of samples is a factor known to affect the isolation of 
marine bacteria, where extended incubation times have led to obtaining isolates of previously 
uncultured bacteria (Connon & Giovannoni 2002). In this study, the samples were incubated for 5 
weeks at 25˚ C. This approach was based on experience gained by Rygaard and colleagues, where 
this proved to be suitable conditions when isolating bacteria from Danish marine environments 
(Rygaard et al. 2017).  
In this project, statistically significant different bacterial counts were seen between media with agar 
and gellan gum as gelling agent. In all cases, gellan gum recovered more CFUs than agar. For 
planktonic bacteria, gellan gum provided significantly more colonies on all media after 5 weeks of 
incubation than agar. However, this was only the case for the seawater medium after 2 weeks of 
incubation. Using gellan gum as gelling agent instead of agar has previously been shown to give 
increased growth of bacteria (Lin & Casida JR. 1984; Tamaki et al. 2005; Tamaki et al. 2009; 
Tanaka et al. 2014), and recently this was shown also to be valid for culturing marine bacteria 
(Rygaard et al. 2017). The finding that the difference between MBA and MBG is not necessarily 
significant after two weeks, but is after 5 weeks correlate with previous findings in the lab. So does 
the fact that there is a significant difference between MBA and MBG after 5 weeks and SWG and 
SWA after 2 and 5 weeks for planktonic bacteria (Rygaard 2016). These results support the 
assumption that when cultivating bacterioplankton from estuarine surface waters, the gelling agent 
does make an impact on the number of CFUs recovered. 
For the cultivated sediment samples, a significant difference in CFU was only seen between SWG 
and SWA, but this difference was seen both after 2 weeks and 5 weeks of incubation. To the 
authors’ knowledge, no other studies have compared the growth between agar and gellan gum in 
marine sediments. However, recently Tamaki and colleagues did study the effect of gelling agent in 
lake sediment. They concluded that gellan gum performed better than agar, and that this especially 
was the case for slow growing bacteria (Tamaki et al. 2009). That MB and ½MB media did not 
have significant CFU differences between gelling agents, could be because the environment in 
==PAGE:52==
51 
 
sediment favors fast growing colonizers more than the environment in the water column and that 
gellan gum especially influences slow growing bacteria. This could also explain why a significant 
difference is seen in the nutrient poor SW medium, as the fast-growing bacteria from the sediment 
did not have sufficient nutrients available to grow on that medium. To the authors knowledge, no 
study has compared the bacterial diversity in estuarine seawater and sediment at the same location. 
This could be addressed by high-throughput amplicon sequencing of 16S genes and facilitate the 
investigation of the community composition and diversity of bacteria in estuarine water versus 
sediment. For the planktonic bacteria, 4 out of 6 substrates had significantly different CFU counts 
between 2 and 5 weeks of incubation, while for the sediment this was only the case for 1 out of 6 
samples. This also indicates that sediment could be colonized by faster growing bacteria, which do 
not need the same incubation times as bacteria from the estuarine water.  Cultivating bacteria from 
sediment has resulted in high cultivation degrees over more than 20% of total bacteria (Köpke et al. 
2005). This is a remarkably higher cultivation degree compared to cultivation of  estuarine seawater 
which is a couple of percent (Rygaard et al. 2017). Even though this is data from various studies 
and therefore not directly comparable, it does indicate that bacteria from sediment generally are 
more cultivable, i.e. more suited for fast growth on high nutrient substrate. 
Another difference between the bacterioplankton and sediment bacteria was the percentage of CFU 
compared to the total abundance of cells. The percent of bacteria that was cultivated for the 
sediment samples compared to SYBR gold was as high as 9 % in one medium (MBA), and not less 
than 1 % in any media. In contrast, none of the CFUs determined for bacterioplankton cultivated on 
any media exceeded 1 % of the total abundance. SYBR gold was used to stain the cells and by that 
determine the total abundance of cells in the samples. The total abundance of bacteria in the 
estuarine water was determined to be 7.11 × 106 cells/ml. This number correlates well with reports 
that have suggested numbers at 105-106 bacteria/ml water over wide ranges of  geographic 
distributions and nutrient regimes at a high consistency (Schmidt et al. 1998).  
The total abundance of bacteria in sediment was determined to be 3.09 × 107 cells/ml. Other studies 
have reported a wide variety of bacterial abundances in sediment as low as 5.01 × 102 cells/ml 
(D’Hondt et al. 2009), but values seem to center around 109 cells/ml (Schmidt et al. 1998). The 
abundance around 107 cells/ml does fit into this window; however, it does seem to be a low estimate 
of the total abundance of sediment bacteria. When the total amount of cells was determined, green 
fluorescent spots were counted on 30 different pictures of the microscopes field of view, which 
should be sufficient to generate reliable results. However, for the sediment samples, it was 
==PAGE:53==
52 
 
impossible to find areas without smudges that made reliable counting difficult. These smudges were 
probably organic material from the sediment, however they did probably overshadow some bacteria 
that were not visible even though they were there. This means that the total amount of bacteria 
determined probably is lower than the real total abundance. That is one possible explanation why 
the cultivation of sediment samples had much higher percentages of total cells than for the water 
samples. In 2002, cultivation of soil samples yielded similar high percentages (5.2 and 7.5) of CFU 
compared to the total count (Janssen et al. 2002). In that regard, soil and sediment could behave 
similar when bacteria were counted in the microscope, due to larger particles with growth attached. 
This could indicate that they experienced similar issues with determining the total bacterial 
abundance in their samples. Flow cytometry adjusted to count all cells passing the laser, could be an 
alternative to SYBR gold staining (Porter et al. 1997). However, SYBR gold was evaluated to be 
more suited for this project. 
After 5 weeks of incubation, 47 random isolates were isolated from each environment and from 
each substrate type. This was done with the highest throughput possible in mind and resulted in a 
total of 564 random isolates, where half originated from sediment and half from water. It was 
investigated whether these 564 isolates were exclusively capable of growing on the substrates they 
were isolated from. Because this was a high-throughput, with many isolates on limited space, it 
proved to be difficult to determine how many isolates that grew exclusively on some gelling agents 
because some isolates produced very large colonies. However, from the counting of the colonies, 
very few, if any isolates grew exclusively on the gelling agent which they were isolated from. This 
result did not correlate with the expected outcome. In 2009 Tamaki and co-workers found that out 
of 108 colonies isolated from gellan gum, 22 did not form visible colonies on media with agar as 
gellan agent (Tamaki et al. 2009). That study investigated bacteria isolated from freshwater 
sediment. Nevertheless, similar observations were expected for the marine isolates in this study. As 
previously stated, gellan gum has been mentioned to stimulate slow growing bacteria in particular 
(Tamaki et al. 2009). In relation to this project this could be true. This study proves that the gelling 
agent is important when isolating the specific isolates from the plates with the environmental 
samples, since we see significantly more colonies when using gellan gum than agar. However, 
when the bacterium was isolated, it does not seem to have any influence which gelling agent is used 
in the media. Only the one study mentioned that addressed this specific dynamic of exclusive 
growth on gelling agents were found. Most studies considering different gelling agents focus solely 
on difference in CFU on environmental samples. The dynamics on solid growth substrates, relating 
==PAGE:54==
53 
 
to what point some colonies inhibit other could therefore also be relevant to investigate further. 
However, experience from this project indicates that isolates from gellan gum exhibited slower 
growth rates on agar than on gellan gum.  
If the use of gellan gum truly is obsolete when isolates have been obtained is also interesting 
considering that agar is easier to work with than gellan gum. Using agar in the media offers lower 
solidifying temperature and easier preparation, which is an advantage when preparing bioactivity 
assays, where bacteria need to be cast into the gel. Furthermore, gellan gum media seemed to be 
more brittle than agar making it more difficult to plate out samples on it. This was especially an 
issue with the MBG media which had the highest salt concentration. These practical challenges with 
gellan gum, do not impact the results from this study and others; gellan gum seems to be 
significantly better at isolating bacteria from marine environments. However, it might not be 
necessary to use gellan gum media after isolates have been obtained. It is also a possibility that a 
repetition of the experiment regarding exclusive growth gelling agents would show different 
outcomes under other circumstances. For example, if fewer spots were applied on each plate, maybe 
some isolates would exhibit exclusive growth on a specific gelling agent, which would then 
correlate with known literature.  
The bioactivity of the randomly selected isolates was assessed by testing if they were able to inhibit 
Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-287 which have been used as first screening for bioactivity for Galathea 3 
isolates (Gram et al. 2010). Furthermore V. anguillarum 90-11-287 is an important fish pathogen 
(Skov et al. 1995). The purpose of this was to screen if any isolates showed antimicrobial potential. 
Based on this screening, media with gellan gum selected the most bioactive isolates, because all 
three media with gellan gum yielded more bioactive isolates compared to agar media. As previously 
mentioned, antibiotics will have a certain spectrum of bacteria that they are effective against. This is 
due to intrinsic mechanisms of some bacteria that make them less susceptible or resistant against 
certain antibiotics. It is therefore relevant to test the isolates against additional pathogens to 
investigate the full spectrum of the isolates.  
Only activity against the one pathogen was investigated in this project, since time did not allow 
further testing. The pathogen used in the screenings was a strain of V. anguillarum that is routinely 
used in this laboratory for inhibition assays. Antimicrobial susceptibility testing against V. 
anguillarum have previously shown that it is susceptible to most antibiotics (Guerin-Faublee et al. 
1996). Even though this was not the specific strain tested, that the species is susceptible to 
==PAGE:55==
54 
 
antibiotics does indicate that it was a suitable pathogen used for the screening in this project. 
Especially a group of human pathogens called ESKAPE (Enterococcus faecium, Staphylococcus 
aureus, Klebsiella pneumoniae, Acinetobacter baumannii, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and 
Enterobacter species) would be appropriate to do additional screenings against. This is due to their 
role as leading causes of life-threatening nosocomial infections. In addition to this, most of them 
have multidrug resistant isolates (Santajit & Indrawattana 2016). Other pathogenic genera could 
furthermore be relevant to screen against, since resistance also have been observed in Streptococcus 
pneumoniae and Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Spellberg et al. 2007). Whether or not screening 
against different pathogens would identify additional bioactive isolates among the randomly 
selected isolates is unknown. Nonetheless, the results from this project indicate that cultivation with 
gellan gum gives more bioactive isolates. 
Remarkable agarolytic activity was discovered in some of the environmental samples, where the 
agar in those plates was completely liquified. Agarolytic bacteria have successfully been isolated 
from marine environments before (Suzuki et al. 2003; Ohta et al. 2005; Wang et al. 2006; Hu et al. 
2009). Bacteria that produce agarases are usually identified by formation of pits in the agar and 
clearing zones around the colony (Hu et al. 2009). This was seen for multiple colonies during the 
project, indicating that agar degradation is a common characteristic of marine bacteria. In contrast, 
colonies degrading gellan gum were not seen during this study, indicating that this feature is less 
distributed among marine bacteria. This is in spite of gellan gum degrading bacteria have been 
identified (Kennedy & Sutherland 1994; Hashimoto et al. 1996; Hashimoto et al. 1997; Jung et al. 
2006). Recently it was discovered that substrates solidified gellan gum and agar cultivated different 
parts of the microbial community from marine environments (Rygaard et al. 2017). The 
observations from this study indicate that degradation of agar is more common than degradation of 
gellan gum. This could favor the agar degrading bacteria on substrates with agar as gelling agent 
and be an issue by limiting the microbial diversity on agar. In order to investigate the bacteria 
responsible for the agar degradation, the potent agarolytic bacteria was isolated from the liquified 
agar. The agarolytic isolate discovered in this study formed deep holes in the agar with clearing 
zones around, and liquified the agar after additional incubation time. Degradation of agar to such 
extent had not been observed before by the author. However, a study from 2006 did observe similar 
characteristics from a bacteria producing agarase (Lakshmikanth et al. 2006).  
Agarases have biotechnological applications where they are widely used for recovery of DNA from 
agarose gel, which makes them indispensable tools used in biological research. Agarases are 
==PAGE:56==
55 
 
furthermore used in the production of agar derived oligosaccharides where they with several 
advantages have contributed to replace acid degradation methods (Fu & Kim 2010). 
Oligosaccharides are for example used in improving gut-microecology and in the last years the 
industrial applications of oligosaccharides have increased (Mussatto & Mancilha 2007). These 
different applications of agarases mean that an isolate with potent agarase activity could have the 
potential for biotechnological applications. The agarolytic isolate which proved to have highest 
identity to Cellvibrio. Cellvibrio is a Gram-negative bacterium isolated from soil known to be 
cellulolytic (Mergaert et al. 2017). That Cellvibrio which is known to degrade cellulose makes it 
possible that those strains could also degrade agar since both are polysaccharides. The agarolytic 
isolate from this study was isolated from the sediment samples. Since the sediment was collected 
just off the coast, it could be originating from soil which had been transferred to the water. 
However, the agarolytic activity exhibited by the isolate does indicate adaption to the marine 
environment, since agar is produced by algae (Fu & Kim 2010). 
The 16S phylogeny of all isolates that inhibited V. anguillarum was investigated. A variety of 
genera were among the bioactive isolates. One genus did stand out by having an extraordinary 
number of isolates: Vibrio. Vibrio belong to the Vibrionaceae family that are widespread in the 
marine environment. Several species of Vibrio are infamous pathogens of both humans and fish 
(Thompson et al. 2004; Mansson et al. 2011). Additionally, Vibrio species are known to produce 
antimicrobial compounds, and this production is believed to be of ecological importance (Wietz et 
al. 2010; Wietz et al. 2011). The 16S rRNA genes are highly conserved and present in several 
alleles among Vibrionaceae leading to identification by 16S phylogeny not being suitable 
(Thompson et al. 2005). This means that the phylogeny in this study can be used to assess that 
many isolates belong to Vibrio, but is not suitable to determine which species of Vibrio a specific 
isolate belong to. So, a more detailed phylogenetic analysis of Vibrio should be based on additional 
household genes for example the fur gene would be well suited for this (Machado & Gram 2015). 
Although vibrios are known as producers of antibacterial secondary metabolites, it is remarkable 
that over 50 % of the bioactive strains isolated here belong to that genus. However, the fact that so 
many isolates were vibrios, could also indicate that they are easily culturable in the laboratory. 
Vibrio are known to grow well in nutrient rich media supplemented with NaCl and high salt 
concentration such as MBA (Thompson et al. 2004). All the isolated Vibrio from this study, except 
one were cultivated on MB or ½MB media that have higher nutrient concentration than SW. This 
indicates that Vibrio does indeed grow well in those standard conditions and maybe even suppress 
==PAGE:57==
56 
 
other bacteria on those media. Vibrios are greatly unexplored for their ability to produce bioactive 
secondary metabolites (Mansson et al. 2011) and this project does indeed indicate that Vibrio could 
have interesting potential in relation to bioprospecting of antibacterial compounds. 
Other genera were also represented among the isolates being able to inhibit V. anguillarum. Four 
isolates had high identity to Pseudoalteromonas which is well known to produce antimicrobial 
compounds. Additionally, Pseudoalteromonas are known to produce a wide range of other 
secondary metabolites as well, which highlights their great potential  for the scientific community 
(Offret et al. 2016; Borchert et al. 2017). Of the four Pseudoalteromonas isolates, three were 
isolated from SWG. This indicates that a low nutrition media with gellan gum could be promising 
for isolation of bioactive Pseudoalteromonas, but this is based on too few isolates to be conclusive. 
Four isolates, where three were from MBA, proved to have high identity to Pseudomonas 
anguilliseptica. This bacterium is known as a fish pathogen that is able to affect a variety of fish 
(Magi et al. 2009), but to the authors’ knowledge it is not known to produce any antimicrobial 
compounds. However, the plant-growth-promoting Pseudomonas fluorescens are known to produce 
several antibiotic compounds (James & Gutterson 1986; Boruah & Kumar 2002), meaning that 
novel antimicrobial compounds could potentially be identified by screening of Pseudomonas 
species. 
Isolates with high identity to Lentibacter and Litoreibacter were also isolated. Both belong to 
Rhodobacteraceae and are therefore related to Roseobacter (Romanenko et al. 2011; Li et al. 2012). 
The fact that they are related to the Roseobacter clade makes them interesting, as roseobacters are 
well known for secondary metabolite production (Wagner-Döbler et al. 2004; Bruhn et al. 2005; 
Cude et al. 2012). In 2010, Newton and colleagues investigated genomes of selected roseobacters to 
learn that NRPS and PKS biochemistry were widely distributed through the clade (Newton et al. 
2010). Even though Lentibacter and Litoreibacter were not a part of that study, it indicates that 
those isolates have potential for discovery of novel bioactive compounds. One isolate related to 
Marivivens donghaensis was isolated, which is a distinct evolutionary lineage independent of other 
taxa of the family Rhodobacteraceae (Park et al. 2016). This correlates with the observed position 
in the phylogenetic tree in figure 16. It is described as a novel species of a new genus in that family, 
hence it is not well known. From this project, it is known that the isolate did produce something that 
inhibited V. anguillarum, which indicates that it has potential. 
==PAGE:58==
57 
 
Sediminicola luteus was the closest related to one of the isolates that was an outlier to the rest. 
Sediminicola luteus is a Gram-negative, isolated from marine sediment that are distant related to 
other genera (Khan et al. 2006). The related isolate was also isolated from sediment, and to the 
authors’ knowledge no bioactive compounds have been identified from the species. Hence, the 
isolate could produce novel antimicrobial secondary metabolites  
So far, all the isolates mentioned have been Gram-negative bacteria. Two genera of Gram positive 
actinobacteria were also among the isolates i.e. Arthrobacter and Streptomyces. Several isolates 
related to Arthrobacter were isolated. Arthrobacter are known to produce antimicrobial secondary 
metabolites and are commonly found in soil. However, it has also been isolated from the marine 
environment (Orlandini et al. 2014). One isolate related to Streptomyces was furthermore identified. 
Streptomyces are a great source of secondary metabolites and are so far, the most important 
producer of secondary metabolites. They are known to be distributed widely in the marine 
environment (Manivasagan et al. 2014). Due to the great importance of actinobacteria related to 
antimicrobial secondary metabolites, isolating an increased amount of them would be of great 
interest. One Streptomyces and 6 Arthrobacter isolates were isolated, but not on one specific type of 
media. Optimizing isolation procedures for actinomycetes are therefore a priority. This could 
happen by for example selective media supplemented with macromolecules such as casein, chitin 
and humic acid (Subramani & Aalbersberg 2012). Furthermore, the use of gellan gum has also 
proved to promote growth of actinomycetes (Tamaki et al. 2005; Rygaard et al. 2017), but this was 
not confirmed in this study. 
To summarize, several isolates being able to inhibit V. anguillarum were identified. Even though no 
isolates could be classified as novel on the species level (16 rRNA gene sequence similarity >97 
%), several isolates were closely related to bacteria that are not well described. This does indicate 
that there is a decent possibility for novel bioactive compounds to be produced by the isolates 
obtained in this project. In addition to this, even though Vibrio are well known, not much is known 
about antimicrobial compounds of Vibrio. This study indicates that Vibrio could be an extremely 
promising source of novel antimicrobial compounds. 
PCR with degenerated primers targeting conserved regions of NRPS and PKS gene clusters were 
used to assess if environments and bacteria from solid growth substrates had biosynthetic potential. 
This method proved that NRPSs was indeed present in all types of environments and solid 
substrates included in this study. However, PKSs were present in all DNA extractions except 
==PAGE:59==
58 
 
bryozoans. All three samples of extracted DNA from bryozoan samples were negative for PKSs but 
positive for NRPSs. This suggests that the observation probably is valid and not a false negative. 
Bacteria are believed to have symbiotic relationships with eukaryotic hosts, which provide the hosts 
with benefits such as nutrients, bioluminescence and antibiotic production and thereby increasing 
the fitness of the host (Sachs et al. 2011). For example, such mechanisms have been speculated to 
happen between the alga Emiliania huxleyi and Phaeobacter inhibens (Seyedsayamdost et al. 2012). 
Because of this, it was expected that all environments and growth substrates would be positive for 
NRPS and PKS and especially in samples that included eukaryotic biomass such as bryozoans and 
oyster. NRPSs was present in the bryozoan samples meaning that they do have biosynthetic 
potential, but this potential could be smaller than for the other samples. All the samples from solid 
substrates have potential for both NRPS and PKS production. However, this approach does not take 
into account if any substrate has more diversity in the sequences i.e. more different bacteria with 
potential and therefore increased potential.  
To determine if different substrates selected for different fractions of the biosynthesis potential in 
seawater and sediment, it was attempted to do targeted sequencing of A and KS domains of NRPS 
and PKS gene clusters. This would have been followed by Miseq high-throughput amplicon 
sequencing if suited PCR products had been obtained. Preparing samples for high-throughput 
sequencing required two rounds of PCR to generate the complete adaptors (Charlop-powers et al. 
2016). However, it was not possible to obtain suited PCR products in the first round. It has been 
described that several approaches were made to obtain suitable PCR products such as; using 
different PCR amplification kits, adjusting the primer and template concentrations were tried to 
optimize the PCR reaction and obtain the PCR products. Charlop-powers and colleagues succeeded 
by using taq polymerase for their amplification with 100 µM of each primer, 2 µl purified eDNA 
and the FailSafe PCR kit (Charlop-powers et al. 2016). This did not reveal the DNA concentration 
for successful amplification. So, it was attempted to get as close to those parameters as possible by 
changing the DNA concentration and the primer concentration. Additional optimization of the PCR 
reaction could have been attempted. Magnesium is present in PCR reactions to form complexes 
with single nucleotides which are substrates for the reaction. This means that Mg and dNTPs are 
interdependent and changing the Mg or dNTP concentration in the reactions could also have been 
attempted (Roux 2009). The PCR programs applied were identical to those used by Charlop-powers 
and colleagues since the primers were the same. The initialization was dependent on the kit used. 
However, gradient PCR could have been used to investigate if different annealing temperature was 
==PAGE:60==
59 
 
needed. Furthermore, the primers were designed for soil actinomycetes (Ayuso-Sacido & Genilloud 
2004; Charlop-powers et al. 2016), and were therefore not designed to amplify DNA from marine 
bacteria. This means that designing new primers based on in silico analyses of marine bacteria with 
NRPS and PKS domains could be a solution. 
Because PCR products for High-throughput amplicon sequencing was not obtained, a cloning based 
sanger sequencing approach was used for assessing the biosynthetic diversity of growth substrates. 
Some sequences were lost as it was not possible to determine the end of the insert. This was 
because the second restriction site (ecoRI) could not be identified which again could be because of 
unspecific inserts. Even though the number of samples was not as large as expected, the sequences 
generated clusters with known NRPS sequences and diversity among them was seen. Some of the 
generated clusters had the highest identity to sequences that were not NRPSs. However, this identity 
was low (<80%). Additionally, the query coverage was low, meaning that a large part of those 
sequences was not similar to anything in the protein database. This could indicate that these 
sequences were NRPSs that had not been sequenced before. 
This method did show that diversity of NRPS exists in the samples. However, high-throughput 
amplicon sequencing would have provided more detail in the diversity of NRPS and PKS. 
Operational taxonomic unit diversity estimates can be calculated from each sample and by that 
determining the diversity. Charlop-powers and colleagues suggested thousands of unique AD and 
KS domain sequences from each of their soil samples with this analysis. It is likely that a similar 
observation could be made for aquatic environmental samples, and more importantly samples from 
growth substrates. Platforms such as eSNaPD can be used to profile the biosynthetic diversity 
hidden within the generated metagenome (Reddy et al. 2014). This would be a great tool for 
analyzing biosynthetic potential in environments but even more importantly for optimizing growth 
media for the isolation of bioactive compounds synthesized by NRPS and PKS. Therefore, it is 
important that an amplicon sequencing method is developed for analyzing biosynthetic potential for 
marine bacteria.

Conclusion 
In conclusion, this project indicates that media with gellan gum as gelling agent select for more 
bioactive isolates than agar. Combined with the establishment of the fact that gellan gum is 
significantly better for cultivating marine bacteria, it does indicate that gellan gum has potential for 
isolation of more novel bioactive bacteria. Screening of bioactive bacteria showed a promising 
variety of different genera including both Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria. Bacteria of 
the Vibrio genus stood out as promising candidates for production of antibacterial compounds. 
Marine environments and solid growth substrates were demonstrated to contain biosynthetic 
potential by amplification of NRPS and PKS genes. Furthermore, diversity in NRPS genes was 
demonstrated with cloning-based sanger sequencing. However, development of a high-throughput 
amplicon sequencing method for NRPS and PKS genes from marine bacteria will be an important 
next step towards increasing the knowledge of how media substrates affect the selection of bacteria 
with biosynthetic potential and should therefore be a priority.
