Abstract
Neutrophil granulocytes are an important and abundant type of leukocytes in the im-
mune system. Besides their beneficial impact during inflammation, an inappropriate
activation or dysregulation may result in severe diseases such as sepsis.
Recently, it has been established that miRNAs play an important role in the pathol-
ogy of inflammation by functioning as fine-tuning regulators of gene expression. It
is proposed that some miRNAs may be involved in neutrophil granulocyte function
and regulation. Therefore, the aim of this study was to characterize changes in lev-
els of intracellular and extracellular miRNA during stimulation of human neutrophil
granulocytes in cell culture.
Healty human neutrophil granulocytes and PBMCs were purified and analysed through
flow cytometry. fMLF, TNF-α, LPS and WKYMVM were applied to activate the
neutrophil granulocytes and PBMCs. It was shown that an increase in CD11b and L-
selectin shedding was a sign of neutrophil granulocyte activation. MiRNA expression
profiles were assessed by high throughput qPCR. Since selected stimulants did not
illustrate any miRNA regulation, grouping of the sample types by principle compo-
nent analysis showed that miR-223 was upregulated in neutrophil granulocyte whereas
miR-155 was upregulated in PBMCs.
Previous studies have shown the role of miR-223 during sepsis, which further elu-
cidated the potential miRNA as biomarkers for sepsis. The results obtained through
this study may contribute to the research investigating the role of miRNA in neu-
trophil granulocytes and their response during inflammation.

Resumé
Neutrofile granulocytter er en type af leukocytter i immunsystemet. Foruden at have
en gavnlig effekt under inflammation, kan en uhensigtsmæssig aktivering resultere i
alvorlige sygdomme såsom sepsis. For nylig er det blevet fastslået, at miRNA spiller
en vigtig rolle i patologien af inflammation ved at fungere som en finjusterende regu-
lator af genekspressionen.
Følgeligt er formålet med dette studie at karakterisere ændringer i niveauet for in-
tracellulære og ekstracellulære miRNA under stimulering af humane neutrofile gran-
ulocytter i en cellekultur.
Neutrofile granulocytter og PBMC’er blev oprenset og
analyseret ved flowcytometri.
fMLF,TNF-α, LPS og WKYMVM anvendt til at aktivere de neutrofile granulocytter
og PBMC’er. Resultatet påpegede at en stigning af CD11b og et fald af L-selectin
var et tegn på neutrofil granulocyt aktivering.
MiRNA ekspression profiler blev vurderede ved hjælp af ”high throughput qPCR”.

uleret i neutrofil granulocyt mens miR-155 blev opreguleret i PBMC’er.

yser deres potentiale som biomarkører i sepsis. De opnåede resultater gennem dette
studie kan bidrage til forskning af miRNA’ers indflydelse i neutrofile granulocytter
samt deres respons under inflammation.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction & Aim
The innate immune system is an important and essential part of the host defense
found in plant and animal life. Specifically, neutrophil granulocytes are the most
abundant type of leukocytes [1]. They are formed by the bone marrow followed by
their release into the circulating blood [2]. They have proven to function as first-
responders during inflammation and rely on their capacity to produce and release
a number of pro-inflammatory cytokines, generate chemotactic signals that attract
other immune cells, and function as antimicrobial effectors [3].
Neutrophil granulocytes have an important role in elimination of foreign agents by
phagocytosis, degranulation, or through neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs). Be-
sides their beneficial impact during inflammation, an inappropriate activation or dys-
regulation may result in severe tissue destruction and can be life threatening to the
host [4]. It has been recognized that neutrophil granulocytes are involved in the patho-
genesis of a number of human diseases such as sepsis, considered the most frequent
cause of death in hospitalized patients under the intensive care unit (ICU) already
admitted with other disease [5].
An emerging evidence suggests that microRNA (miRNAs) play an important role
in the pathology of inflammation and could function as biomarkers for disease diag-
nosis and therapy [6]. MiRNAs are viewed as fundamental contributors in several
events involved in the control and regulation of inflammatory and immune responses
in the innate and adaptive immune systems and function as fine-tuning molecules [7].
The increased interest in miRNAs as regulators of gene expression has evolved into
a large research area. However, the role of miRNAs in neutrophil granulocytes and
their response during inflammation in these immune cells seem to be understudied.
It is proposed that some miRNAs may be involved in neutrophil granulocyte func-
tion and regulation and several regulatory mechanisms based on miRNAs needs to
be understood.
To further investigate, a simple study model related to the investigation of miRNA
regulation in vitro is proposed. This study involves isolated neutrophil granulocytes
from healthy human, stimulated in culture in presence of different stimulants. The
hypothesis of this study is that miRNAs are involved in fine-tuning of neutrophil gran-
ulocytes activation during stimulation with pro-inflammatory neutrophil granulocyte

intracellular and extracellular miRNA during stimulation of human neutrophil gran-

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
The innate immune system
The survival of all organisms require elimination of substances threatening the nor-
mal homeostasis. Therefore, the body’s defence applies a complex array of protective
mechanisms to control and eliminate these substances such as microorganisms and
toxins. There are two types of defence mechanisms in the body, the innate immunity
and the adaptive immunity. The former is mediated by cells and proteins ready to
take action and fight invading microorganisms. The latter defence mechanism pro-
duces long-lived cells with specificity for any individual pathogen, toxin, or allergen
contributing to a more effective host response [8].
Broadly defined, the innate immune system includes physical barriers such as ep-
ithelial cell layers, gastrointestinal tract, respiratory tract (preventing foreign entry),
phagocytic leukocytes (neutrophils and macrophages), natural killer (NK) cells, and
several circulating plasma proteins - proteins of the complement system [8]. The in-
nate immune system involve an immediate and nonspecific response towards foreign
invading agents. These include cellular functions such as phagocytosis and endocyto-
sis by macrophages and neutrophils. These functions are dependent on recognition
of infection through pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) by means of
its pathogen-recognition receptors (PRRs) [9].
In addition, complement proteins,
mannose-binding protein and acute phase reactants are also an important part of the
innate immunity as they are able to opsonize foreign agents [9], see Figure 2.1.
In contrast to the innate immune system, the adaptive immune system comprises
highly specialized cells that are able to adapt to threat of new and divergent pathogenic
microorganisms. These cells are the thymus derived T cells and bone marrow-derived
B cells. Moreover, these cells have also the capacity to produce immunological mem-
ory against a specific foreign agent [9], see Figure 2.1 .
Engagement of PRRs activate secretion of pro-inflammatory molecules including cy-
tokines and chemokines that convey important signals to other immune cells. This
process is commonly known as acute inflammation. As a part of an effective host
immune response, the inflammation response plays a key role in the mobilization of
the host’s defence and repair responses [10].

2.1.1
Inflammation
Inflammation is the body’s initial response to harmful stimuli, which is characterized
as the first line of defence. The inflammatory response involves a coordinated local
and systemic mobilization of immune, endocrine and neurological mediators [11]. One
of the first wave of cells responding to inflammation are phagocytic immune cells
such as macrophages and neutrophils, which are activated by detecting bacterial cell
components [10].
The physical signs of inflammation are characterized by heat, redness, swelling,
pain, and loss of function [13]. These signs are due to extravasation of plasma and
infiltration of leukocytes into the site of inflammation [13]. These vascular changes
describe alteration in vessel diameter leading to increased blood flow i.e. vasodilation.
Modification of the vessel wall (increased vascular permeability) allows the leukocytes
to exit the circulation and activation of endothelial cells results in increased adhesion
and migration of the leukocytes [14].

inflammatory molecules that mediate acute inflammation [15]. The chemokines are
involved in migration of leukocytes along a chemotactic gradient, which consists of
chemokine molecules bound to the extracellular matrix and the surfaces of endothelial
cells and are ready to be accumulated at the site of infection [16].
Although inflammation is a beneficial process, it has also proven to be the basis
for many human diseases. For example, the main function of the phagocytic cells is
to eliminate the invading microorganisms by engulfment and secretion of toxic chem-

2.2
Neutrophil granulocytes
Neutrophils, also known as granulocytes or polymorphonuclear cells (PMN) [1], are
important cellular components of the immune response and play an essential role
during the successful fight and control of invading microorganisms [17]. Following
release from bone marrow, a quick recruitment of neutrophils to the site of infection
is essential for the innate immune system, which leads to the migration from the vas-
culature to extravascular location. This migration is reliant on the receptor-mediated
contact with endothelia and the following received signals through soluble mediators
[18].
Neutrophils are the most abundant leukocytes in human blood and are found within
the bone marrow, liver, spleen and lung. Thus, these organs function as reservoirs of
mature neutrophils, ready to be migrated to the site of inflammation. Neutrophils
are characterized by presence of several granules in their cytoplasm [1] which are im-
portant proteins with the ability to kill invading microorganisms [19]. It is assumed
that neutrophil granules are synthesized from the early promyelocyte stage until the
segmented stage and packed with proteins [19]. Due to difference in expression of
each granule protein, they carry different cargo of matrix and membrane proteins
synthesized at different stages during maturation. The organization of these compo-
nents allow the neutrophils to change from a passively circulating cell to a potent
effector cell of innate immunity. At the same time, release of granules and secretory
vesicles enables the neutrophils to present cytotoxic granules to the target site [20].
2.2.1
Activation and migration
Following maturation, neutrophils leave the bone marrow to enter the circulation
where the inflammatory mediators recruit the neutrophils to the site of infection [21].
This migration requires a recruitment phase including tethering, rolling, adhesion,
crawling and finally transmigration [4], which can be seen in Figure 2.3.
The initiation of neutrophil recruitment takes place as a result of stimulation
by inflammatory mediators, released from tissue resident leukocytes upon contact
with invading microorganism. The chemokines involved in neutrophil recruitment

2.2.2
Action of neutrophil granulocytes
Once an infection has been established, neutrophils recruit and activate other immune
cells, as well as directly attacking invading microorganisms through process of phago-
cytosis, degranulation [20], and formation of neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs)
[21].
Through phagocytosis, the target microorganism is internalized into a phagosome
and destroyed by the hostile phagosomal environment, involving the release of an-
timicrobial and proteolytic peptides [27]. Moreover, neutrophils do also eradicate in-
vading microorganisms through release of granules by a process called degranulation.
The different sorts of granules contained within the neutrophil comprise antimicro-
bial proteins, proteases, and components of the respiratory burst oxidase, which has
the purpose to destroy invading microorganisms [20]. The newly described antimi-
crobial mechanism for neutrophils, NETs, are extracellular structures comprised of
chromatin, with specific proteins from the neutrophilic granules attached to them.
Their main function is to trap and kill extracellular microorganisms through binding
and disarming them [21].
Circulating neutrophils reach the site of infection in high amounts, followed by ful-
filment of their antimicrobial task and finally cell death.
Neutrophils are usually
short-lived cells and during inflammation have a limited life time, as they can cause
severe effect for the host tissue if stored for too long. Therefore, neutrophils are killed
by apoptosis and NETosis. While NETosis leads to release of the intracellular content
of neutrophils, during apoptosis the cell stays surrounded by an intact membrane and
is eventually removed by efferocytosis [28]. Most neutrophils go through apoptosis
in a specific process requiring CD11b/CD18 (Mac1), the production of ROS and ac-
tivation of caspases. These dead neutrophils are removed by macrophages and the
release of damaging proteins by neutrophils is prevented [21].
Although a broad understanding of the immune system has been established, an
unrecognized layer of control impacting the development and physiology of the im-
mune system has been discovered. This control is exerted by small RNAs known as
microRNAs (miRNAs).

2.3.2
Discovery and identification of miRNAs
Prior to miRNA discovery, a large part of the genome was considered to be evolution’s
debris and thereby lacking any functional features. However, it has been suggested
that a part of genome is important in the regulation of gene expression [30] and thus
the emergence of miRNAs as a class of gene expression regulator involved in a wide
range of biological processes have gained much attention. Recently these small RNAs
have proved to be involved in immunity, more specifically in myeloid and lymphoid
differentiation.
In 1993, Victor Ambros and co-workers discovered that a gene involved in control-
ling the timing of Caenorhabditis elegans larval development (lin-4), did not code
for a protein but produced a pair of small RNAs of approximately 22 nucleotides
[31]. Therefore, they were initially denoted as small temporal RNAs (stRNA) due to
their expression in temporal phases of developmental timing [32]. Later on, it was
discovered that lin-4 was part of a large class of small RNAs present in C.elegans,
Drosophila and human cells and were therefore designated as microRNAs [32].
MiRNAs are well-conserved [33] and can be found in plants, animals, and some viruses
and are suggested to be important for their control of development and physiology
[31]. MiRNAs are 20-22 nucleotides long and thousands of miRNAs have been identi-
fied (currently 28645 miRNAs) [34]. It is stated that miRNAs regulate the expression
of up to 60 % of all human protein-coding genes by targeting the 3’untranslated region
==PAGE:26==

(UTR) of target mRNAs, thereby inhibiting protein translation [33]. MiRNAs are
responsible for a modest regulation of genes rather than acting as on-off regulators.
They can appear as fine-tuners of a cell’s responses to external influences [7].
2.3.3
Biogenesis of microRNAs
The biogenesis of miRNAs is initiated through synthesis of a primary miRNA tran-
script (pri-miRNAs) in the nucleus, by RNA polymerase II, see Figure 2.4. These
primary transcripts contain mRNA features such as 5’ cap structure and 3’ poly (A)
tail, which are the unique properties of class II gene transcripts [35]. In addition, miR-
NAs harbor a local hairpin structure of approximately 70 nucleotides that is cropped
by the class 2 ribonuclease III enzyme Drosha and its interacting partner DGCR8
(DiGeorge syndrome chromosomal region 8) [36]. This complex subunit cleaves pri-
miRNA to a characteristic stem-loop structure of premature miRNA (pre-miRNA),
which is recognized by the nuclear export factor called Exportin 5, transporting the
pre-miRNA to the cytoplasm for subsequent processing [33].
In the cytoplasm, a second endonuclolytic cleavage reaction, generally referred to
as dicing, is catalysed by RNase III endonuclease Dicer protein, whereby the terminal
loop of the pre-miRNA is removed. The product of the second cleavage reaction is
called the miR:miR* duplex and the two strands of this duplex RNA are called the
“guide strand” or the “passenger strand”, respectively [36, 37]. Once the miR:miR*
duplex is generated, it is loaded into the Argonaute (Ago2) protein. The miR-guide
strand is retained while the miR* passenger strand is removed. The guide strand will
ultimately be involved in determining which target mRNA will be regulated [38].
The interaction of the miR-guide strand with Ago2 and some additional proteins
leads to formation of the RNA induced silencing complex (RISC), ready for bind-
ing its target. The most important pairing region for the miRNA at the target is
referred to as the seed sequence [37].
Once bound, there are two ways in which
RISC can inactivate the mRNA. Generally, a perfect complementarity can lead to
mRNA degradation, whereas an imperfect complementarity can lead to translational
repression of the target [39], see Figure 2.4.

2.3.3.1
MiRNAs and neutrophils
MiRNAs exert their function by targeting mRNAs. Each of these miRNAs are able
to target several mRNAs within a given cell type and each mRNA can be targeted
by multiple miRNAs. This indicates that a large part of the human transcriptome
is regulated by miRNAs, leading to a post-transcriptional control of every biological
process [41]. In regards to this, it has been suggested that both the innate and adap-
tive immune responses are regulated by miRNAs. This includes one of the important
players of the innate immune response, neutrophils granulocytes [42].
Several studies have investigated the role of miRNA in the modulation of neutrophil
biology. It has been observed that miRNAs play a major regulatory role at the early
stages of granulopoiesis (neutrophil maturation in the bone marrow) and it is clear
that posttranscriptional fine-tuning of protein expression by miRNAs also plays a
very important role in this process [2]. Among several miRNA identified, miR-223
is shown to be one of the most abundant miRNAs expressed by human granulocytes

2.4
Sepsis
Even though the antimicrobial potential of neutrophils is essential for the innate im-
munity, an inappropriate activation or dysregulation of neutrophils may result in se-
vere tissue destruction and can be life threatening to the host [4]. For example, sepsis
which is the host’s deleterious, non-resolving inflammatory response to infection that
leads to organ dysfunction. This is caused by inadequate neutrophil transendothelial
migration of neutrophil to the site of infection [43].
Thus, in order to reduce the risk of inappropriate activation of neutrophils whilst
securing a fast and effective awareness for activation when required, the neutrophil
function must be studied. In line with the increased interest in miRNAs they hold
promise as diagnostic markers or therapeutic targets for tissue- or biological stage-
specific diseases [44]. These miRNA can clinically be used to monitor the activity of
diseases since abnormal expression of miRNA during sepsis might reveal presence of
sepsis in an earlier stage [45].
It is apparent that the role of miRNAs as fine-tuners of gene expression is well
established in most aspects of neutrophil biology, and it has been shown that an
inappropriate activation or dysregulation of neutrophils may lead to development of
severe diseases such as sepsis. In this context, a special aspect of miRNA control is
of particular interest, as miRNAs may play a prominent role in the control and/or
regulation of neutrophil granulocytes. This may thus propose opportunities to study
mechanisms of miRNA control in the innate immune system.
2.5
Theory behind the methods
2.5.1
Flow cytometry
Flow cytometry is a technique used for cell analysis and purification. This technol-
ogy enables simultaneously measurement and analysis of the physical characteristics
of cells as they flow in a fluid stream through a beam of light.
The parameters
measured include size, granularity or internal complexity, and relative fluorescence
intensity of a single cell [46]. During the flow of cells through the beam, the particle
scatters laser light which is dependent on the physical properties of the particle such
as its size and internal complexity [47].
To identify and quantify distinct populations of cells, different fluorescent dyes (flu-
orochrome) are applied and conjugated to a specific antibody.
Some of the most
common fluorochromes for surface or intracellular epitope detection include allophy-
==PAGE:29==
2.5 Theory behind the methods
13
cocyanin (APC), phycoerythrin (PE), and Fluorescein (FITC). APC is excited by
laser lines at 594 and 633 nm, with an absorbance maximum at 650 nm and a fluo-
rescence emission peak at 660 nm, exhibiting far-red fluorescence [48]. PE is excited
by laser lines from 488 to 561 nm, with absorbance maxima at 496, 546, and 565 nm
and a fluorescence emission peak at 578 nm, exhibiting extremely bright red-orange
fluorescence [49]. FITC is excited by laser lines around 488 nm, with absorbance
maxima at 490 and a fluorescence emission peak at 525 nm, exhibiting extremely
bright green fluorescence [50].
The data analysis is based on the measurement of the size of cells by forward-scattered
light (FSC), which is proportional to cell-surface area or size. In addition, the rela-
tive internal complexity/granulation of cells can be measured by side-scattered light
(SSC), which is proportional to cell granularity or internal complexity [47]. Corre-
lated measurements of FSC and SSC leads to differentiation of different cell types in
a heterogeneous cell population [47].
Another way to display the cell populations is by single parameter histogram, which
allows observation of a single parameter against the number of events. The x-axis
of the histogram represents the increasing signal intensity of the parameter, and the
y-axis represents the number of events (count) per channel number [47].
2.5.2
Real-time quantitative PCR (qPCR)
QPCR technique is an important technique in many fields within diagnostics, life sci-
ence, agriculture and medicine. The high sequence-specificity, large dynamic range,
and sensitivity has made it one of the most applied methods for quantification of gene
expression [51]. QPCR enables collection of data simultaneously throughout the PCR
process while it occurs, and thereby combining the amplification and detection into
a single step [52]. This is processed by using different fluorescent chemistries which
are able to correlate the PCR product concentration to the fluorescence intensity [53].
The qPCR reactions are described by PCR cycles where the target amplification is de-
tected for the first time. Each value is denoted as quantification cycle (Cq) according
to the Minimum Information for Publication of Quantitative Real-Time PCR experi-
ments (The MIQE guidelines) [54], which is the time where the fluorescence intensity
is greater than background fluorescence [52]. Accordingly, the larger the quantity of
target DNA in the starting material, the faster a significant increase in fluorescent
signal is observed, resulting in a lower Cq value [54].
The advantages of using qPCR to quantify gene expression includes no requirement
of post-amplification manipulation, greater sensitivity, and they are able to detect
a single copy of a specific transcript [55].
Furthermore, the qPCR technique can
also differentiate between almost identical mRNAs sequences and requires much less
RNA template for the gene expression analysis [52]. On the other hand, there are

and equipment. In addition, due to the high sensitivity, the experimental design and

3.3
Choice of stimulants for activation of granulocytes
by flow cytometry
The choice of stimulants included N-formyl-methionyl-leucyl-phenylalanine (fMLF),
which is of bacterial origin and an important mediator of the inflammatory response in
neutrophil granulocytes by producing reactive oxygen derivatives such as hydrogen
peroxide [58].
fMLF is a prototypic formylated peptide produced by Escherichia
coli (E.coli). It is a known chemotactic bacterial peptide as well as an important
leukocyte chemoattractant, which is able to interact with human N-formyl peptide
receptor 1 (FPR1) and formyl peptide receptor 2 (FPR2) [59]. Likewise, the Gram-
negative bacterial constituent lipopolysaccharide (LPS) was included as it has been
documented to have an essential role in the activation of neutrophils [57].
TNF-
α is a pro-inflammatory cytokine which contribute to recruitment of neutrophils by
activating endothelial cells. It is produced by monocytes as a response of PAMPs such
as LPS. Lastly, the hexapeptide WKYMVM was used in this study due to its role
in activating neutrophils through FPRL1 and that FPRL2 is a chemotactic receptor
transducing signals in myeloid cells [60].

3.4
Purification of granulocytes and peripheral blood
mononuclear cells from buffy coats
Granulocytes were purified according to the protocol described in ”Neutrophil meth-
ods and protocols” [61] with few modifications. Briefly, the content of one-day old
buffy coat was divide into tubes (approximately 10 mL in each) and equal amount
of physiological NaCl (at room temperature) and 2% Dextran (Dextran T500, Pack
no. 6277, Batch no. DB4249, Pharmacosmos A/S, Denmark) was used to remove the
red blood cells. The tubes were shaken to mix the three solutions and left for 20-35
minutes for the erythrocytes to fall to the bottom. The upper phase was discarded
and 15 mL of Ficoll-paque (+NaCl) (Ficoll-paqueTM PLUS, endotoxin tested, Lot
no. 304363, Amersham Bioscience, Sweden) was carefully added to the bottom with
a syringe without disrupting the phases. The tubes were centrifuged at 400 g (SL40R
Centrifuge, Thermo Scientific, Holm & Halby) for 20 minutes at 4 ℃with low accel-
eration and low brake to avoid mixing the separated phases. Ficoll-paque (+NaCl)
enabled the separation of cells providing phases containing the granulocytes at the
bottom, PBMCs as an intermediate layer and a top layer of liquid containing saline
and plasma.
Subsequently, the granulocytes and some erythrocytes were then located in the pellet
at the bottom of the tubes. The upper phase was removed, and the middle phase con-
taining PBMCs (monocytes and lymphocytes) was transferred to a new tube. To lysis
the erythrocytes in the granulocyte fraction, 6 mL dH2O was added and shaken until
the pellet disappeared. After 30 seconds 2 mL phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) with
2.4% NaCl were added for neutralization. Then, 40 mL HBSS w/o calcium (Hank’s
Balances Salt Solution (10X), Lot no. 1491086, Gibco, by Life TechnologiesTM) was
added and the sample was centrifuged at 400g for 10 minutes at 4 ℃with low break.
This step was repeated until no erythrocytes were left and the pellet appeared to be
white. Lastly, the samples were washed with HBSS w/o calcium. This procedure
was performed for both granulocytes and PBMCs. Following this step, the samples
were re-suspended in 5 mL HBSS with calcium (Hank’s Balances Salt Solution – Phe-
nol red, Cat no. SH30268.01, Lot no. AYE160160, Thermo Scientific) and the cells
were counted (NUCLEOCOUNTER® NC-200™, Chemometec) to obtain a concen-
tration of 5¨106 cells/mL. The samples were kept on ice to prevent activation of the
granulocytes and to prolong the durability of the cells.

3.12 Data analysis
23
The data was analysed based on specific pre-processing parameters, such as efficiency
correction, global normalization, average technical repeats and transforming the Cq
values to relative quantities by GenEx 6 software.
The first step of the data analysis included determination of efficiency, which is
defined by the rate of PCR amplicon generated. Efficiency is related to the slope
of the plot generated from Cq as a function of the dilution concentration and was
determined based on the following equation: E “ 10´1{slope ´ 1 [54].
The graph generated a plot fitted into a straight line where the slope and correla-
tion coefficient values were obtained. This was followed by the efficiency correction
by the GenEx 6 software. According to [54], the preferred correlation coefficient is
above R2 > 0.098 and a slope ranging from 3.0-3.8 result in a 100 % efficiency. As
per definition a desired efficiency is 1 (100 %), which corresponds to the doubling of
amplicon at each cycle. For the analysis of data in this study, efficiencies between
80-120 % were accepted and remaining data outside of the range was given a default
value of 1 (100 %).
Subsequently, the difference between the cDNA replicates was calculated (average
technical repeats). To account for technical variation, the miRNA data was normal-
ized by ”Global normalization” parameter, as all miRNAs samples were used as the
normalization factor. The normalization step lead to removal of technical variation
and a variation of more than 8 % was discarded. Lastly, the Cq values were trans-
formed to relative quantities (Rq) to analyse changes in gene expression levels in the
specific sample types relative to the control sample whose mean was scaled to 1. Log 2
transformation was performed for statistical analysis. Changes in miRNA expression
levels were considered statistically significant if p-values < 0.05 and the relative gene
expression difference was > 1.4-fold ´ up or down.

PCA is a multivariate analysis that investigates how several outcome variables vary
together. This method is use to find combinations of variables, called components
that adequately explain the overall observed variation and thus reduce the complex-
ity of the data. The results are expressed as a set of new orthogonal variables called
principal components (PC) [63]. The first principle component (PC1) is the axis that
spans the most variation, whereas the second principle component (PC2) is the axis
that spans the second most variation, the third principle component (PC3) is the
axis that spans the third most variation and so on. These PCs are the structure in
the data set which indicates the direction where there is the most variance and thus
where the data is most spread out (more spread out of data equals a big variation)
[64].

5.1
Neutrophil granulocytes and PBMCs analysis
5.1.1
Purification of neutrophil granulocytes and PBMCs
Neutrophil granulocytes and PBMCs were purified from blood samples from healthy
humans. This was followed by stimulation with different stimuli and identification
of populations of interest from a heterogeneous population by using flow cytometry.
The sample types were analysed through evaluation of the forward scatter and site
scatter plot and specific labels for cells surface markers, which described the activa-
tion of neutrophil granulocytes.
Careful thoughts were given to the purification step as previous studies have pointed
out the influence that contamination of lymphocytes and monocytes in stimulated
granulocytes might have on gene expression analysis. The protocol used in this study
was based on the guidelines of Neutrophil Methods and Protocols [68]. According
to the protocol, there are two steps involved; sedimentation in dextran at 1g and
differential sedimentation in a discontinuous density gradient of Ficoll-Hypaque. De-
pending on which cells are targets to be purified, they can be used in different order.
When processing large volumes of blood (e.g. >200mL) for neutrophil recovering, the
dextran sedimentation is performed first followed by Ficoll-Hypaque [68]. By follow-
ing this protocol, it was possible to achieve a pure population of 94.3% granulocytes
with a contamination of 4.1% lymphocytes and 0.5% of monocytes, as well as a small
number of dead granulocyte cells (3.1%). Since there was a small proportion of lym-
phocytes and monocytes in the granulocyte population, it may have an influence on
the miRNA expression analysis.
During this study, quantitative analysis of miRNA gene expression in human neu-
trophils was carried out through qPCR. This method was used to assess miRNA
expression in response to different stimuli in a population of neutrophil granulocytes
from experiments investigating changes in L-selectin and CD11b. While the reliable
purification methods have managed to obtain >95% pure neutrophils, the residual
monocytes have been discussed to play an important role for the further analysis [69].

Nevertheless, it is commonly presumed that neutrophil contamination is due to mono-

ulocytes (mostly eosinophil) which is non-neutrophil cells in most neutrophil prepara-
tions may also have an influence. These granulocytes (eosinophils, basophils) feature
similar size and thus they cannot be isolated based on density gradient methods [70].
Since no regulation was observed for the miRNAs investigated during this study,
the contribution of lymphocyte and monocyte or eosinophil contamination cannot be
determined and evaluated. However, based on the previous studies mentioned above,
ensuring a pure neutrophil population may be beneficial. Different methods have
been developed to obtain pure neutrophil population. For example, Pelletier et al.
have developed a novel neutrophil procedure based on magnetic negative selection
that allows achieving high levels of purity. This procedure provides evidence that
mRNA expression can directly be credited to neutrophils and thereby excluding the
potential contribution of PBMCs or even eosinophils [72]. Furthermore, the absence
of detectable IFN-β, IL-10 or IL-6 mRNA provides a good control of neutrophil purity.
Although the contamination might be due to use of different techniques, it is sug-
gested that leukocyte contamination is usually donor dependent, reliant on the techni-
cal skills of the performer and not least the equipment applied during the purification
step [70]. This might be the case during this study as it was observed that exchanging
from an electronic pipette to a syringe resulted in smaller amount of contamination,
since the mixing of phases was avoided. Moreover, it was observed that purity of
granulocyte population obtained varied for every donor.
5.1.2
Response after stimulation
An important role of the host immune system is the ability of neutrophils to reach
the site of infection in response to inflammatory stimuli. Activated neutrophil granu-
locytes are able to change phenotype due to stimulation and thereby transmigrate to
the site of infection and eliminate any foreign substances. These changes are associ-
ated with expression of surface receptors for chemoattractants and adhesion molecules
[73]. Giving shedding of L-selectin and upregulation of CD11b on neutrophil gran-
==PAGE:61==
5.1 Neutrophil granulocytes and PBMCs analysis
45
ulocytes as activation indicators, these cell surface markers were used to evaluate
the degree of response after stimulation. These results obtained through this study
demonstrated that response to a specific inflammatory stimulants resulted in downreg-
ulation of L-selectin for example through shedding of this particular molecule, while
CD11b expression was increased. These results are in accordance with other studies
demonstrating modulation of adhesion molecule expression. The study by Sabroe et
al. showed that neutrophil granulocytes responded to stimulation with either LPS or
fMLF by shedding of L-selectin and upregulation of CD11b [74]. Although the same
was observed in this study during stimulation with fMLF, it was not the case dur-
ing stimulation with LPS, despite using different concentrations (1eu/mL, 10eu/mL,
100eu/mL). In addition, the response of TNF-α, which is a specific cytokine involved
in the inflammtion process and produced by activated monocytes [15], also lead to
down regulation of L-selectin and upregulation of CD11b. Thus, indicating presence
of activated neutrophils.
Bacterial LPS is the most common stimulus of the immune system and, depend-
ing on the dose, LPS can result in or be related to sepsis [74]. Neutrophils involved
in protection of the host from bacterial invasion have been suggested to be senstive
to LPS, which leads to modulation of adhesion molecule expression [74]. It could
be suggested that the anadequate of response during LPS stimulation may be due
to absence of serum factors during stimulation. It is known that bacterial LPS is a
surface membrane component present in almost all Gram-negative bacteria and acts
as a strong stimulator of the innate immune system [75]. The LPS-binding protein
(LBP), which is a serum glycoprotein, interacts with LPS aggregates to stimulate
an immune response by presenting the LPS to CD14 [76, 77]. This is followed by
transfer of monomeric LPS to the extracellular adapter protein MD2 (myeloid differ-
entiation factor-2) and Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) and thereby form a TLR4–MD-2
complex [77]. Thus, a response to LPS is induced only in the presence of serum factor
(such as LBP), which enhances the recognition of endotoxin and bacterial surfaces
by the immune system. In this study, 1% serum was applied during characterization
of granulocytes by flow cytometry but not during stimulation, which could be the
reason why only small differences were observed when comparing samples before and
after stimulation with LPS. Furthermore, this could also lead to the possibility that
PBMC sample stimulated with LPS were not activated.
5.1.3
MiRNA gene expression analysis
MiRNA expression analysis did demonstrate highly expressed miRNAs in the three
sample types -neutrophil granulocyte supernatant, neutrophil granulocyte and PBMCs.
However, no regulation could be determined for any of the sample types. This indi-
cates that although the miRNAs are expressed in those sample types and may play
a role, they did not seem to be regulated. During this study, PBMCs were included
in order to ensure that those miRNAs identified were specific for neutrophil granulo-

cytes. The activation of PBMCs was not assessed through flow cytometry, although
they were stimulated. PBMCs were only assessed during miRNA expression analysis.
The PCA analysis showed that neutrophil granulocytes and their respective super-
natants displayed similar miRNA expression profiles, whereas PBMCs were grouped
apart. However, upon stimulation, no significant differences between the stimulants
and their control were observed.
This could be due to the incubation time used.
During this study, the granulocytes and PBMCs were incubated for 30 minutes upon
stimulation. In most studies, granulocytes are isolated from fresh blood or buffy coats,
suspended in culture medium containing up to 10% serum, and then cultured at 37 °C
in a 5% CO2 humidified atmosphere. In contrast, other studies suspend granulocytes
in serum-free medium or in buffered solutions containing various concentrations of
serum or bovine serum albumin. While this is suitable for short-term incubations,
it is not suggested for experiments, such as the determination of neutrophil gene ex-
pression or protein release. Therefore, it has been suggested that purified neutrophil
populations are routinely suspended at no more than 107 cells/mL in medium con-
taining 10% low-endotoxin fetal calf serum. Under these conditions, granulocytes can
be cultured for up to 44 h if appropriate neutrophil survival factor(s) are added to
the cultures [71].
Evaluating the donors separately, it seemed that within the sample cell type (PBMCs),
every donor was grouped together based on the stimulations. Although the same pro-
cedure should be carried out for neutrophil granulocytes, this elucidates donor to
donor variation. This further emphasize the importance of including larger sample
size. In addition, standard procedures should be applied in all samples.
Analysis of miRNA expression in the three sample types by histograms illustrated
up and down regulation of miRNAs specific for each cell type. For example, it was
evident that miR-223 was significantly upregulated in neutrophil granulocytes and
downregulated in PBMCs. A previous study by Johnnidis et al. 2008 have identified
and characterized miR-223 in the haematopoietic system, where it was specially ex-
pressed in the myeloid compartment. It is well known that miR-223 has a key role
in myeloid development and function. The importance of miR-223 during neutrophil
differentiation has been based on studies of miR-223-dificient mice. The experiments
demonstrated that lungs from these miR-223 deficient mice had an increase in in-
flammation and neutrophil influx.
This further indicates that miR-223 regulates
proliferation and granulocyte differentiation and activation [78]. In addition, it has
been suggested that miR-223 could be used as a biomarker for distinguishing sepsis
from SIRS (systematic inflammatory response syndrome).
The potential of miRNAs as biomarkers for several diseases has been promising. How-
ever, their association with sepsis is still under investigation. An identification of an
early biomarker for sepsis has been investigated by many studies. Some biomark-
ers included acute phase proteins, cytokines, and chemokines [79, 80, 81]. However,

they are not specific due to overlap with other inflammatory diseases [82]. Thus, the
challenge of a sepsis biomarker relies in its ability to differentiate between sepsis and
SIRS. Wang et al. revealed that miR-146a and miR-223 could be used as biomarkers
for sepsis. This was due to a significant reduction of these miRNAs in septic patients
compared to SIRS patients and healthy control. Thus, these two miRNAs may be
potential biomarkers for sepsis [83].
Similarly, a variety of miRNAs have been identified to be specific for PBMCs, includ-
ing miR-155 and miR-99a. A study by Long et al. 2013 investigated the expression of
miR-155 and illustrated a 1.9-fold increase in the average relative expression of miR-
155 in rheumatoid arthritis PBMC compared to normal controls during stimulation
with TNF-α [84]. This indicates the increased miR-155 expression in RA inflamma-
tory cells (PBMC), which has also been observed in this study.
The supernatants were included for the purpose of measuring extracellular miRNA
expression profile after stimulation. The results obtained from the chip demonstrated
presence of miRNA in the supernatant sample type. However, the cause of this secre-
tion cannot be determined based on the results obtained in this study. Mechanisms
by which neutrophil granulocytes release a particular set of miRNAs to the super-
natant while other miRNAs are more likely to be kept inside cells has been suggested.
The study by Ortega et al. 2015 evaluated the main effects of inflammation on the
miRNA profile of differentiated human adipocytes, the macrophage-like cell line THP-
1 (M1), and in their supernatants. Based on this study, it could be observed that
inflammation lead to expression of specific miRNAs in cells and their supernatants
[85]. These observations highlight the existence of miRNAs inside and in the super-
natants of isolated neutrophil granulocytes. It is believed that secreted miRNAs may
target neighboring cells and thereby modulate gene expression in recipient cells [86].
Based on the flow cytometry data, it has been demonstrated that neutrophil granu-
locytes were activated upon stimulation. In addition, illustration of highly expressed
miRNAs does indicate that the cells are living under optimal and good conditions and
there is a small number of dead cells present following stimulation. However, it does
not indicate that it could lead to a regulation of a particular miRNA in neutrophil
granulocytes.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The aim of this study was to characterize changes in levels of intracellular and extra-
cellular miRNA during stimulation of human neutrophil granulocytes in cell culture.
A simple study model related to the investigation of miRNA regulation in vitro was
proposed.
Based on the flow cytometry studies, it was shown that an increase in CD11b and
L-selectin shedding was a sign of neutrophil granulocyte activation. Regarding the
selected stimulants, fMLF induced a higher response after stimulation compared to
LPS. The fact that LPS did not illustrate a big response might be due to absence of
LBP.
Expression analysis of selected miRNAs showed no signs of regulation in any of the
miRNAs after stimulation. On the other hand, based on PCA analysis, it was possi-
ble to group the different samples types and thereby separate the populations. This
further illustrated specific miRNAs present in each sample type. More specifically,
miR-223 was upregulated in neutrophil granulocyte whereas miR-155 was regulated
in PBMCs.
In closing, the results obtained through this study did not confirm the hypothe-
sis, however it may contribute to the research investigating the role of miRNA in
neutrophil granulocytes and their response during inflammation.

CHAPTER 7
Future Perspectives
The flow cytometry section was regarded as a quality assessment, where well-known
methods were applied to purify the granulocytes and PBMCs as well as measurement
of activation after stimulation. Although these procedures lead indeed activation of
granulocytes, other measurements regarding the neutrophil granulocyte activation
could be evaluated. This includes identification of different kind of granules secreted
in response to different stimuli. Neutrophils have an impressive number of cytoplas-
mic granules that harbor proteins essential for innate the innate immune system.
Analysis of isolated granules may provide important information regarding miRNAs
present in neutrophil granules [4].
Rørvig et al.
has developed and performed a
method for disruption of cells based on subcellular fractionation of freshly isolated
human neutrophils by nitrogen cavitation and density centrifugation on a four-layer
Percoll gradient. They were able to identify and group unique proteins according
to the neutrophil fraction, in which they displayed maximal expression [41]. Similar
method could be used to identify presence of miRNAs in different granules and iden-
tification of different granules during different stages of neutrophil development.
Since absence of regulation was observed during this study based on the selected
stimulants, other stimulants such as complement C5a and C3a could be used to test
whether they are more potent stimulants. These anaphylatoxins are generated during
complement activation, and are known for their proinflammatory effects [87]. Specif-
ically, C5a has an attractant and activating effects on neutrophil, eosinophil and
basophil leukocytes [88].
As the PBMCs were not evaluated regarding their activation upon stimulation, fur-
ther studies should consider it in order to confirm the activation of PBMCs during
miRNA expression analysis. For example, PBMC activation could be assessed by
analysis of cytokine production in PBMC supernatants. Monocytes are able to se-
crete cytokines upon stimulation. Thus they will be suitable markers of activation,
which can be accomplished by the use of various enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay
(ELISA) kits [89].
Due to absence of miRNA regulation, an alternative could be to perform gene ex-
pression analysis at mRNA level. This could lead to better understanding of the level
of regulation in neutrophil granulocytes.
