Abstract 
ADHD is one of the major disorders in children and is characterised by the inattention, hyperactivity and 
impulsivity. The serious side effects from medicine and expensive therapy sessions have necessitated the 
need for inexpensive and drug-free treatments. This has led to the developments within the neurofeedback 
field in order to respond to these challenges and the requirements.  
A novel neurofeedback BCI system has been designed and developed to train attention in ADHD patients. 
The BCI system is a 3D playground shaped as a story tale where a 3D classroom with 2D games run on the 
blackboard. The control in the 2D games utilises SSVEP while 3D distractions are active in the classroom. 11 
test subjects participated in the experiment to test the developed BCI system. Each of the subjects executed 
10 different tests in the experiment. The experimental setup includes running the BCI system on one 
computer with a single measurement scalp electrode on the occipital lobe (Oz). 
The results from the BCI experiment showed that the subjects were affected by change of environment and 
distractions in the 3D classroom rather than the game elements in the 2D game. Their accuracy fell, number 
of selections became higher and increased selection time. Adding the 2D game elements made the subjects 
hesitate before choosing which leads to a longer selection time. The results also showed that the frequency 
of 9Hz had longer selection time compared to 7Hz. The time also increased when attending the same 
frequency as before. The average selection time for the BCI system was about 3.07 seconds with shortest 
time of 1.33 seconds and the longest time of 4.98 seconds. The average accuracy for the experiment was 
92.26% with the lowest accuracy of 77.41% and the highest of 99.26%. The average ITR for the BCI system 
was 11.85bits/min.

Resumé 
ADHD er en af de største lidelser i den vestlige verden og er karakteriseret af uopmærksomhed, 
hyperaktivitet og impulsivitet. De alvorlige bivirkninger fra medicin og de dyre terapi sessioner har 
nødvendiggjort behovet for en billig og stoffri behandling. Dette har ført til udvikling i neurofeedback 
området til at reagere på disse udfordringer og krav.  
For at træne opmærksomhed i ADHD patienter er et nyt neurofeedback BCI system er blevet designet og 
udviklet. BCI systemet er en 3D legeplads i skikkelse af en fortælling, hvor et 3D klasselokale kører 2D spil på 
tavlen. Styringen i 2D spillet udnytter SSVEP, mens 3D distraktionerne er aktive i klasselokalet. 11 
forsøgspersoner har taget del i eksperimentet for at teste den udviklede BCI system. Hver forsøgsperson har 
udført 10 test i eksperimentet. Eksperimentet’s opsætning inkluderer at BCI systemet kører på en computer 
med en enkelt målings elektrode placeret på occipital lappen (Oz). 
Resultaterne fra BCI eksperimentet viser at forsøgspersonerne blev påvirket mere af ændringerne i miljø og 
distraktioner i 3D klasselokalet end spil elementerne i 2D spillet. Deres præcision faldt, antallet af 
udvælgelser blev forøget og udvælgelsestiden steg. Tilføjelse af 2D spil elementerne fik forsøgspersonerne 
til at tøve og førte til en højere udvælgelsestid. Resultaterne viser også at frekvensen af 9Hz har længere 
udvælgelsestid sammenlignet med 7Hz. Tiden steg også hvis forsøgspersonen skulle rette opmærksomheden 
på den samme frekvensen som før. Den gennemsnitlige udvælgelsestid for BCI system lå omkring 3.07 
sekunder, hvor den korteste tid var på 1.33 sekunder og den længste tid på 4.98 sekunder. Den 
gennemsnitlige præcision for eksperimentet lå på omkring 92.26%, hvor den laveste præcision var på 77.41% 
og den højeste var på 99.26%. ITR for BCI systemet var omkring 11.85bits/min.

Preface 
The thesis is prepared, written and performed at the Department of Electrical Engineering in the Biomedical 
Engineering section at the Technical University of Denmark. The thesis is in fulfilment of the requirements 
for acquiring a Master of Science in Information Technology – Digital Media Engineering.  
The content of this thesis is written with the goal to target a broad audience. However, some general 
knowledge in the fields of biomedical and software may be required to understand some of the chapters. 
This thesis deals with different aspects of ADHD treatments and focuses on neurofeedback BCI systems. It 
touches subjects such as brain physiology, ADHD, attention, EEG and existing BCI systems for ADHD 
treatment. Hereafter the thesis designs and presents a novel neurofeedback BCI system developed to train 
attention for ADHD patients.  
The thesis began as a supplement for a PhD project on neurorehabilitation for ADHD children. A 3D 
playground environment in the shape of 2D games running in 3D classroom controlled by the SSVEP paradigm 
had to be developed for the main project and later incorporated as an interface for his constructed BCI 
system. The BCI system was not made when reaching the testing phase for the playground. After discussing 
the issue with the main supervisor for the project, the author created his own complete BCI system targeting 
attention training for ADHD children. 
The voyage in creating a novel BCI system was hard but also interesting. This was a chance to connect the 
medicine knowledge with the present software knowledge. It delighted the former medicine student in the 
author to delve into the medicine field again. The author was presented by the possibilities of how effective 
health IT can become as an alternative to medicine. While this project ends with this thesis, it does not mean 
that this is the end. On the contrary, the author’s voyage into health IT and BCI continues.

Chapter 1 – Introduction 
Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is one of the major disorders in children and can continue to 
adulthood. As the name indicates, the symptoms include difficulty in staying focused, paying attention, 
difficulty in controlling behaviour and hyperactivity. In Denmark, about 3-7% of the children are estimated 
to be affected by ADHD and the number increases every year [1]. These numbers are prevalent throughout 
the western world [2]. Untreated ADHD costs the Danish society about 2.8 billion kroners a year according 
to a research from Rockwool Research Fund [3]. 
The common treatments of ADHD are counselling or/and medications. Both are expensive and on top of that 
medication comes with addiction and serious side effects necessitating the need and demand for an 
alternative treatment. One of the approaches for the treatment for ADHD seems to be through 
neurofeedback (NF). It tries to fulfil the demand of a treatment with fewer side effects and promises a 
healthier future for the ADHD but does not deliver. In reality, the treatments are mostly uninteresting 
software exercises for memory and attention training, and measure the differences of brain waves before 
and after the exercises to see changes. Brain-computer-interface (BCI) systems goes a step further by letting 
the patients receive positive or negative feedbacks depending on their brain waves. Based on the patient’s 
brain waves, things change in the played application. Only small homogeneous 2D games for concentration 
training exist as BCI systems so far. The target group is mainly children who become bored very fast with both 
these types of treatments. This in consequence lowers the training performance and the patients are not 
able to bring the results into their life. There is some improvement from the treatments but it is very far from 
its potential.  
These issues motivate the discovery of a new approach to utilize NF in BCI and create a better system to train 
attention in ADHD patients. This thesis describes, designs and presents such a system with appurtenant 
experiment results verifying its competences as an attention training BCI system for ADHD patients.  
1.1 Objectives 
The main purpose of this thesis is to develop a novel BCI system that utilizes NF and helps train attention in 
ADHD patients through brain signals. The overall objectives of this thesis are: 
 
Understanding the relationship between the brain, neurons, brain signals, ADHD and attention. 
 
Understanding the common EEG features used in BCI systems. 
 
Investigating the current literature and state of the art on BCI systems for ADHD training and study 
their approaches, techniques and performances.  
 
Designing a real-time BCI system for attention training for ADHD patients. 
 
Designing, developing and testing the application for the BCI system. 
 
Testing the BCI system with subjects to validate its efficiency.  
1.2 Requirements for the BCI system 
The purpose of the BCI system here is to act as a 3D playground for ADHD patients to train attention. To 
achieve this purpose, it needs to fulfil the following essential requirements:  
==PAGE:14==
P a g e  | 2 
 
 
 
 
Different versions of the same solution should exist to train attention so moving from one version to 
another will make the training harder for the ADHD patients. This way the ADHD patients will 
accustom to the changes from one version to another and thereby attain a better attention.  
 
 
The solution needs to show consideration to ADHD patients’ situation regarding elements, such as 
colours and time performance. Otherwise, the patients will become unresponsive to the solution 
after a small period. This will ruin the purpose of the system as a training playground.  
 
 
The exercises to train ADHD attention should not feel as exercises as this will be a hindrance to the 
patient’s motivation to use the training playground. 
 
 
The control of the solution should be achieved by attending predefined targets and receiving a 
feedback from the BCI system. Electroencephalography (EEG) features should be studied wherefrom 
the best will be picked as control mechanism for the system. 
 
 
The solution should fit the targeted age group. It should not be tiresome or lacklustre for the targeted 
age group, as it would remove the purpose of the system being a playground.  
 
 
The solution needs to be as realistic as possible so the patient can carry the results from the training 
environment into real-life environment without feeling abnormal.  
 
 
Interconnected components should enclose the solution so it can easily separate and switch with 
other parts without breaking down the structure of the solution.  
1.3 The complete BCI system trains and improves ADHD attention  
The completed BCI solution is able to train attention by covering the ADHD disabilities through: 
 
Helping with the difficulty in sustaining attention. It will have consequences on the movement of the 
character or the point score if the ADHD children stops looking at the SSVEP flickering squares and 
the objects falling.  
 
Having details the ADHD children cannot miss without being punished e.g. losing points or lives.  
 
Reading the complete text and instructions to excel in the game.  
 
Organising the received information to act on which falling objects to take and leave to win the game.  
 
Trying to avoid distractions and not forget the main task. 
 
Ignore the distractions in the classroom and focus on the game on the blackboard.

Chapter 2 – Literature 
Before designing and developing a novel BCI system, it is important to comprehend how the fundamentals 
work. This chapter looks into different topics to gain the relevant knowledge. First section describes the 
physiology of the brain and its neurons. The next section defines EEG, ADHD and its association with 
attention. The section ends with an explanation on how gaming may help treat ADHD. The second section 
looks into the most common EEG features useable in BCI solutions. It describes their functions and possible 
usability in ADHD BCI systems. The third section is a literature review containing the state of the art and 
relevant articles on ADHD and gaming. The chapter finishes with a short summary and a specification on the 
details of the requirements for the BCI system.  
2.1 Physiology 
As one of the largest organs in the body with a mass about 1300g, makes the brain together with the spinal 
cord a part of the central nervous system (CNS) [4] [5]. CNS is the site of cognitive functions where memories 
are stored and processed. It is also the place of learning and thinking.

2.1.2 Neurons, action potentials and synapses 
The nervous system consists of two principal types of cells: neurons (nerve cells) and neuroglia. About 1013 
neuroglia and 1012 neurons exist in the CNS [6]. While neuroglia do not generate or conduct nerve impulses 
(action potentials), they do have many other functions where they assist neurons in carrying out their 
functionality of sending action potentials. Neurons come in different types but they usually have three parts: 
the soma, dendrites and an axon. Figure 2.2 shows such an example. The soma has a nucleus and the function 
of proteins to replace cellular components, for growth of neurons and regeneration of damaged axons. The 
dendrites are short tree looking structures coming out from the soma and are the input portion of the neuron 
together with the soma. The axon on the other hand conducts action potentials to another neuron, a gland 
==PAGE:18==
P a g e  | 6 
 
 
 
cell or a muscle fibre. It is a long thin cylindrical structure, which joints with the soma at the axon hillock. 
From the axon hillock, the action potentials arise and travel along the axon to the axon terminals that have 
synapses. These are ready to communicate the action potential to another neuron, a gland cell or a muscle 
fibre [4].

An action potential is a sequence of rapidly occurring events that decrease and reverse the membrane 
potential in the cell, in this case the neuron, and then restore it to the resting state at the end. A neuron has 
a voltage of about -70mV as its resting membrane potential caused by its Na+ and K+ balance inside and 
outside the cell. When a stimulus happens from the cells environment, it changes the resting membrane 
potential to reach the threshold (about -55mV) by depolarizing the membrane, and an action potential arises. 
The action potential has two phases. Starting first with the depolarizing phase where the membrane is 
depolarized so it reaches a positive value by the exchange of Na+ inside and outside the cell membrane. Then 
begins a repolarizing phase where the Na+ stops its exchange and instead K+ starts exchanging through the 
cell membrane causing the positive value to drop to a value under the resting membrane potential. When 
the K+ stops its exchange, the membrane potential returns back to the resting potential on -70mV. This 
excitability ability of the cell, which converts stimuli to action potentials, follows the all-or-none principle. In 
neurons, these two phases in the action potential only last about 1ms [4].  
When the action potential reaches from the axon hillock to the synapses, it needs to be transported from 
one cell to another. This is executed by gap junctions in electrical synapses and by using the synaptic cleft in 
chemical synapses. Gap junctions contains many tubules in the plasma between two cells and let the ions 
flow from one cell to another, making a path for the flow of the electrical current. Chemical synapses, which 
are slower by 0.5ms, do not have their plasma touching other cells. Figure 2.3 shows the transmission of a 
typical chemical synapse.  
==PAGE:19==
P a g e  | 7 
 
 
 
 
Figure 2.3: The transport of the action potential in the chemical synaptic cleft from presynaptic neuron to postsynaptic neuron. 
The action potential reaches the synaptic end bulb in the presynaptic neuron and Ca2+ voltage-gated channels opens (left). This 
causes the synaptic vesicles to release neurotransmitters in the synaptic cleft. These stick to the neurotransmitter receptors on 
ion channels on the postsynaptic neuron (middle). The ion channels open and Na+ flows in causing a depolarization leading to an 
action potential in the postsynaptic neuron (right). This figure is adopted from [4], page 227. 
The action potential reaching the synaptic end bulb of the axon causes an intake of Ca2+ from the outside by 
its depolarizing phase. The increase of Ca2+ starts exocytosis of some synaptic vesicles releasing several 
thousand neurotransmitters molecules each into the synaptic cleft, such as dopamine and norepinephrine. 
Both neurotransmitters will be discussed in a later section because of their relation to ADHD. ADHD patients 
lack neurotransmitters and are treated with medicine to gain the same level of neurotransmitters as a healthy 
person. The neurotransmitters bind themselves to the ion channels on the other neuron’s plasma. Allowing 
the intake of Na+ causes a depolarization of the neuron’s membrane while Cl- inflow causes 
hyperpolarization. If the depolarization reaches the threshold in the receiving neuron, then one or more 
action potentials are generated [4].  
2.1.3 Brain waves and electroencephalography   
Every neuron is generating millions of action potentials. Putting together their electrical signals makes up the 
brain waves. Brain waves are generated continuously but their frequency and intensity differ based on the 
activity of the brain. A way to record such waves is through electroencephalography (EEG).  
Richard Caton was the first scientist to use EEG on humans while the actual studies in human EEG began in 
1924. It was not until 1929 that Hans Berger released his first report on EEG and the alpha and beta rhythms. 
Hans Berger, a known discoverer in human EEG and neuropsychiatrist, was able to prove different states on 
the EEG e.g. focused and relaxed state. He continued his studies on EEG recordings of sleep spindles, brain 
tumours and more. He also managed to find some correlation between mental activities and changes in EEG 
signals [7]. Since this discovery, many decades of EEG research have added more rhythms to the list. 
Consequently, the majority of today’s researchers concur on a common agreement on the brainwaves 
structure in EEG recordings. Table 2.2 shows the typical normal brain rhythms with their type, amplitude

The EEG signal itself is a measurement of the electrical current flow during the synaptic excitations of the 
dendrites of many pyramidal neurons in the cerebral cortex. The pyramidal neurons have roughly pyramidal 
shapes with many spinous processes lining the surfaces of the dendrites. As explained before, the synaptic

on the scalp, which is measurable by EEG. What makes the current is the pumping of the positive ions Na+, 
Ca2+, K+ and the negative ion Cl- through the membranes. The electrical potential is caused by the differences 
by summing the synaptic potentials from the pyramidal cells that create the electrical dipole between the 
soma and the apical dendrites. EEG can be measured in three different layers on the head: The brain, the 
skull and the scalp. Most researchers use the scalp instead of the brain because both the skull and the brain

require invasive procedures while the scalp does not. Furthermore, the skull is not used in EEG recordings 
since the signals attenuate about 100 times more in the skull than on soft tissue. On the other hand, most of 
the noise is produced within the brain or over the scalp as internal or external noise. It requires large 
populations of active neurons, such as pyramidal cells, to generate enough potential to be recordable by the 
electrodes on the scalp. These signals are greatly amplified by an amplifier to show the results [10].

2.1.5 Attention and gaming 
Attention is a behavioural and cognitive process where it refers to a person’s capacity to focus on one activity 
or task at a certain time. It is divided into overt attention and covert attention. Overt attention is fixed gaze 
attention aka foveal vision while covert attention is unfixed gaze attention aka peripheral vision.  
People with lacking attention or attention deficit can train their attention. ADHD patients fall nicely into this 
group based on the inattention symptoms in Table 2.3. Those with attention deficit are lacking 
norepinephrine in the frontal and parietal area and need medication to reach the same levels of attention as 
a normal human brain [26]. 
There are studies proving that games help people train their attention. Stuart Johnstone sums up some 
studies about how gaming affects behaviour and how EEG is used to reduce ADHD symptoms [27]. He also 
presents two studies where the second one is of relevance. The study observes 60 children with ADHD and 
68 without ADHD for a period of 4-5 weeks. The children received training on how to use the professionally 
designed portable EEG devices at home. The gaming software had implemented hidden exercises that trained 
inhibitory control and working memory. The results show task and behavioural improvements with the 
cognitive training as attention monitoring. The effects larger for the children with ADHD than children 
without ADHD [28]. A Danish school has implemented gaming as part of the classes for an ADHD study. This 
has resulted in better behaviour, concentration and relaxed ADHD children according to the parents and 
teachers. There is no publication of any academic yet since the experiment is still ongoing [29].  
2.2 EEG features for brain-computer-interface 
An evoked potential (EP) is an EEG change. A change that is useful as a diagnostic tool in psychiatry and 
neurology. EP is also widely used in BCI. This section shortly describes the most important potentials 
concerning the scope of this thesis. 
==PAGE:25==
P a g e  | 13 
 
 
 
2.2.1 Event-related desynchronization/synchronization  
Sensorimotor rhythms (SMRs), also called µ-rhythms, correspond to movement volition and are examined 
mainly in motor/movement imaginary studies. The amplitude of µ decreases when the corresponding motor 
areas activates, such as sensing something or moving a muscle. An event defined as the event-related 
desynchronization (ERD). On the other hand, event-related synchronization (ERS) refers to a µ increase to 
reach the standby state when there are no sensory or motor movements [30]. ERD and ERS are recorded 
over the primary sensorimotor cortex by the frequency shown in Table 2.2 and enable the chance to improve 
hyperactivity symptoms in Table 2.3.  
2.2.2 Event-related potentials 
 
Figure 2.4: Exogenous and endogenous ERPs to visual stimuli. As illustrated, the voltage deflections can be positive and negative 
for both types of potentials. This figure is adopted from [7], page 599. 
Event-related potentials (ERPs) are distinctive patterns of voltage deflections that are time-locked to a 
specific sensory stimulus or event. The deflections can be positive (P) and negative (N), and happens after 
the stimulus or event. As illustrated in Figure 2.4, the numbers after the letters indicate the time in 
milliseconds after the stimuli [10]. ERP splits into two types [7]:  
 
Exogenous ERPs: These are with amplitude and latency occurring under 100ms after stimuli and 
influenced by physical attributes of stimuli such as intensity, presentation and modality rate.   
 
 
Endogenous ERPs: These are non-obligatory responses to stimuli. They vary in amplitude, latency and 
scalp distribution with strategies, expectancies and mental activities triggered by the even eliciting 
ERPs. In contrast to endogenous ERPs, exogenous ERPs are not influenced by physical attributes of 
stimuli.  
P300 is such an endogenous ERP. As the name states, it has a positive voltage deflection peak at about 300ms 
after the stimulus over the central-parietal scalp. P300 elicits using the oddball paradigm where an 
arrangement of sequences of repetitive audio or visual stimuli is irregularly interrupted by an aberrant 
stimulus. This aberrant stimulus will elicit the P300 on the EEG [31]. P300 has its uses in examination of the 
attention in ADHD subjects. Although no such BCI system exists, P300 could be used to examine if the subject 
is detecting the aberrant stimulus.  
==PAGE:26==
P a g e  | 14 
 
 
 
While event-related potentials are often the same as evoked potentials, there are exceptions e.g. the 
readiness potential [32]. 
2.2.3 Slow cortical potentials 
Like SMR, slow cortical potentials (SCPs) are associated with motor imaginary and actual movement. These 
act with relative slow changes in the voltages recorded over the sensorimotor cortex. Categorized under ERP, 
SCPs are time-locked and phase-locked. This means they can arise before, under and after specific 
sensorimotor events at foreseeable times. Typically, they consist of negative potential shifts that precede 
actual or imagined movement. SCPs are thought to represent cortical activation in preparation of action [33]. 
Readiness potential and contingent negative variation are such SCPs [32] [34]. They emerge before the 
stimulus and are considered to be motor preparation or expectation of a stimulus. Since the negative shift is 
believed to reflect behavioural and cognitive preparation e.g. to cognitive task such as mental arithmetic, 
SCP can be used to improve the inattention symptoms in Table 2.3.  
2.2.4 Visual evoked potentials 
As the name states, visual evoked potentials (VEPs) are evoked potentials generated by sudden external 
visual stimuli. The stimuli can be objects such as light flashes, appearance of images, flickering areas or 
patterns and abrupt change in colour. VEPs are produced in or near the primary visual area and therefore 
most prominent over the occipital scalp. The field of medicine use VEP diagnostically to detect possible 
abnormalities in the optical nerves. Different stimuli sizes and patterns help determine the functionality of 
the different parts of the optical nerve. In BCI, VEP has many uses from controlling robots and wheelchairs 
to write sentences and communicate with the world through the screen. BCI uses VEP by having the subject 
fixate their attention on the screen. The screen is showing different stimuli from which the subject chooses 
one. After choosing the stimuli, the brain elicits the specific VEP, which consecutively is a selection in the BCI 
system for the move direction for the robot or the letter on the screen [35]. 
There are different groupings of VEP wherefrom the three well known are shown on Figure 2.5 following a 
short description of each. Each of them applies flickering objects to generate visual stimuli.

P a g e  | 15 
 
 
 
 
Figure 2.5: Three different VEP paradigms. All three have their own visual stimulation pattern (left) attended by the subject

2.2.4.1 Frequency-modulated visual evoked potential 
The procedure for frequency-modulated VEP (f-VEP) entails flickering objects at a certain frequency. f-VEP is 
normally synonymous with steady-state VEP (SSVEP) but there exist another type called transient VEP. 
Transient VEP includes N75, P100 and N145 but has rarely any use in BCI since it is harder to detect than 
SSVEP and only elicits at frequencies less than 3.5Hz [36]. 
On the other hand, SSVEP is widely used in BCI and easily detectable in the EEG. SSVEP uses flickering objects 
at different frequencies as stimuli. Each stimulus has a unique fixed frequency compared to the other stimuli 
so each stimulus represents a certain output on the BCI. As mentioned before, it is through these outputs 
that the subject can write letters or control robots. SSVEP has confirmed to have 48 frequencies successfully 
distinguished in one BCI system [37]. One of the biggest benefits of f-VEP is that there is no constraint of 
stimulus time-locked or phase-locked recording of the EEG. It is therefore well suited to user-paced, 
asynchronous procedure, which is a very common way of interacting with computer systems. There is no 
training requirement to use f-VEP (or SSVEP) [35]. 
2.2.4.2 Code-modulated visual evoked potentials 
The procedure for code-modulated VEP (c-VEP) entails flickering objects at same frequency but with different 
patterns. Each stimulus occurs in a pseudorandom pattern that is orthogonal to the patterns of all the other 
stimuli. The correlation between a template and EEG is calculated for each stimulus. The highest correlation 
is normally achieved by the specific stimulus that is being fixated on. This chosen stimulus produces the 
output on the BCI. Training is a requirement to use c-VEP as to generate the templates for correlation 
calculation [35]. 
==PAGE:28==
P a g e  | 16 
 
 
 
2.2.4.3 Time-modulated visual evoked potentials 
The procedure for time-modulated VEP (t-VEP) entails flickering objects at same frequency. The different 
frequencies are mutually independent and do not overlap in their “on” and “off” states. The average VEP for 
each stimulus is calculated where after the output representing the stimulus that elicits the largest VEP is 
produced. There is no training requirement to use t-VEP [35].  
When it comes to performance, c-VEP is the best. Albeit f-VEP’s performance is not equal to c-VEP, it is still 
very good. On the other hand, t-VEP is much worse when compared to the first two VEPs [38]. 
BCI uses VEP for many of its applications but none for ADHD assessment or treatment. This comes as a 
surprise since VEP has to do with optical nerves and occipital cortex. It should be clear to see that VEP can 
easily be used to train many of the symptoms under inattention in Table 2.3.

in this system's game: Run only, run and jump to pick as many fruits as possible, and run and pick the fruit 
shown on the screen. The levels use a countdown timer and a button for the jumping and turning. The study 
ran for 24 weeks, 8 weeks of training 3 times a week and then check-ups at week 20 and 24.

2.3.1.1 Closing remarks on state of the art 
By observing Table 2.6, it is clear that three studies should be removed from the list of possible BCI treatments 
for ADHD.  Jue Wang et al. and Gabrial Pires et al. should be removed because they both verify their systems 
as ADHD treatment based on a very small sample size of two subjects. Having this small sample size is biased 
and do not present any statistical power, significance or distribution of the specific population. Lijun Jiang et 
al. is added to this group even though they have a sample size of 10 subjects. Their results do not express 
anything except an abstract usability opinion of the simulator while no experiment results exist to verify their 
system as treatment for ADHD. Furthermore, both Jue Wang et al. and Lijun Jiang et al. have very simple 
simulators, which ADHD patients will find tedious very fast.  
The author accepts the rest of the studies as possible BCI treatments for ADHD with slight hesitance. The 
reason is Kavitha P. Thomas et al. and Tih-shih Lee et al. use of EEG sensors/amplifiers. They are not meant 
for scientific experiments. A possible solution could be to confirm the conclusions on gUSBamp before 
moving to cheap consumer EEG sensors/amplifiers. While Darius A. Rohani et al. uses gUSBamp, his system 
has a big disadvantage. He manages to prove that P300 can be found with error rate under 30% in his offline 
experiments but presents no results of the BCI system. The exercises work fine separately without the 
classroom, which is just a part of the whole BCI system.  
From the rest of the studies, Tih-shih Lee et al. is the best example of a fine study. They use 20 ADHD children 
to conduct an experiment for 8 weeks and prove the results to remain after 24 weeks. This happens only in 
rare cases but such an experiment only demonstrates that BCI exercises in shape of games targeted for ADHD 
patients may be the future ADHD treatment. 
2.3.2 Other relevant articles 
A study by Fabio E. G. Santos et al. (2011) tests if it is possible for their game to classify if the subject playing 
the game has ADHD, and which type [49]. The 2D game, Supermarket, is about an avatar walking around in 
the supermarket getting the items on the shopping list without stepping on any old tiles. The collected data, 
time and score, were processed. Although the researchers give up on finding ADHD subtypes because of low 
sample size, they conclude that the Naïve Bayes algorithm with categorical attributes, two classes and 
attribute selection (fast forward) can be used to find ADHD in subjects.

speed and accuracy compared to normal children. The researchers also conclude that there is a significant 
worsening of correct responses and reaction time found in ADHD children compared to the normal children.

executive test and visual memory test [53]. This is done to learn if this new system can be used to assess 
children with ADHD or not. The outcome of the study is that the distractions did lower the subjects’ scores 
in the tests in contrast to without the distractions. Unfortunately, it was a pilot study with only two subjects 
but a large trial with an estimate of 100 children is supposed to be carried out in the future. 
These studies and three previous studies show that the topic for testing is attention disorders mainly ADHD 
[54] [55] [56]. With these latest studies, Albert A. Rizzo et al. manages to take the VR classroom further ahead 
in defining how assessment of ADHD will be carried out in the future.

the idea is good, there are no results or tests on the game so the system cannot be validated.

the game, an avatar walks tightrope with two flickering rectangles on both sides. When the avatar starts to

frequencies for the rectangles are at 6Hz and 25Hz. All subjects manages to complete 41/48 levels with 
reliable control achieving an average of 89.5% correct selections showing that SSVEP is a good control 
mechanism in games for attention training.

2.4 Decisions related to the novel BCI system 
The brain is powerful. It controls nearly everything in the human body. The brain consists of neurons that 
produce action potentials. They can be measured on the cerebral cortex as EEG, which can be used to 
diagnose abnormalities/disorders. ADHD is such a disorder with many symptoms where inattention plays a 
major role. At present, ADHD patients are treated with dangerous medicine and expensive therapies while 
trying to find a better solution. Some games help keeping ADHD down but no game directed towards ADHD 
patients exist yet. Considering the literature BCI may have the answer but at this point, there is no such 
treatment system.  
After understanding these issues, describing the different EEG features in BCI and going through the existing 
literature, including the state of the art, it is now possible to specify some of the requirements of the BCI 
system.  
The BCI system will not be a simple simulator or assessment tool as shown in the previous articles in literature 
review but a tool to train attention for ADHD patients. Since the disorder starts in childhood, it is important 
and evident to choose this age for the target group.   
Games will be perfect for training their attention since children loves them. Using games targeted at ADHD 
children would also cover many of the attention symptoms and the essential requirements mentioned 
previously. The exercises will be concealed as games to motivate the children, in training their attention 
without noticing or feeling that they are doing exercises. By adding levels to the game, which gets tougher 
from one level to another, the game is fun and fulfils the requirement of different version of the same 
solution. Moving from one level to another requires getting a specific score on a level or surviving the level 
meaning the need to get accustomed to the level before moving on to the harder level.  
To avoid any habitually demotivation from the ADHD patients, a few countermeasures regarding their 
impairment and behaviour are taken. The decline in terms of speed and accuracy in long time performance 
will be countered by small game levels where the patient can rest between the levels [52]. The impairment 
of yellow-blue colours will be avoided by using green and red as main colours in the game and use yellow 
and blue colours at its minimum [59] [60] [61] [62] [63]. 
==PAGE:37==
P a g e  | 25 
 
 
 
An environment to run the games is needed. It could be a book with different stories aka games, a virtual 
world with mini quests, a maze or building with different rooms, or a classroom where the games runs on 
the blackboard. Since the target group is children, it would be best to have the games run inside an 
environment that they know very well: the classroom. This has shown to be quite problematic for ADHD 
patients, which means they will also be able to harvest bigger results compared to normal children [51] [52] 
[53]. This also fulfils the naturalistic and realistic environment requirement while helping the children behave 
and settle into an environment they are familiar and troubled with. These harvested results will therefore 
also be carried out in the real-life classroom.  
SSVEP will be the EEG type in the BCI system. As mentioned a few times previously, it is surprising to see that 
no one have tried using SSVEP in any BCI treatment for ADHD as attention training. SSVEP is good 
performance wise and associated with attention. It does not require any training periods or templates like 
the other EEG types.

This chapter focuses on two important aspects regarding the BCI system: SSVEP and BCI system setup. The 
first section looks into two experiments on SSVEP. The first experiment is about overt and covert attention 
using SSVEP and the second experiment is about implementing the SSVEP in Unity game engine. The second

3.1 Pre-experiment 
Two experiments have been carried out with significance to this thesis thus they will both be described here 
in this section. The first experiment is executed by a PhD student and the second experiment is executed by 
the author. Their results decide which elements to implement in the BCI system.  
3.1.1 Selecting frequencies for SSVEP and confirmation of SSVEP with covert/overt attention 
The first experiment is by the PhD student Mehdi Ordikhani-Seyedlar on covert and overt attention. Only the 
results relevant for this thesis will be described. [73] 
The experiment investigates covert and overt attention using SSVEP. A 128-channel Biosemi EEG setup has 
been used with ActiView Labview acquisition tool for the data collection. The sample rate is set to 512Hz for 
the recording. All channels are used to find the best electrode placement for future experimenting while 
referencing on both ear and both mastoids to compare with other studies. Both horizontal and vertical 
movements are recorded by placing electrodes around left eye. The impedance for the electrodes is kept 
under 5kΩ.  
The visual stimulation with SSVEP is implemented in MATLAB 2013b. A LCD screen presents the stimulation 
from a distance of about 60-65cm. The SSVEP frequencies are set to 6Hz, 7Hz, 8Hz and 9Hz. 
 
Figure 3.1: The SSVEP layout for the covert/overt attention experiment. The middle square is a fixation point where the arrow 
shows which square to attend and the number shows which number to react to in that square. This layout is used in all 3 phases 
in the experiment. Right and left squares may flicker or not depending on the specific phase.  
Figure 3.1 illustrates the visual paradigm from the experiment. A small square is placed in the middle of the 
screen with a square on its right and left sides. While the left and right squares flicker at specified frequencies 
(e.g. 6Hz, 7Hz, 8Hz or 9Hz), the middle non-flicker square is the fixation point. A number appears on the small 
square at random time intervals showing which number to attend. Pseudo-random numbers with 
pseudorandom intervals flash in both flickering squares. 
 
==PAGE:39==
P a g e  | 27 
 
 
 
The experiment consists of 14 sessions where each session runs one of 3 phases in a pseudorandom order: 
1. Measure covert attention: The subject attends to either left or right flickering squares while fixating 
their gaze on the small central square. They are to press on a button whenever the cued number 
appears in the right or left squares in their peripheral vision. 
2. Measure overt attention: The subject attends to the squares while fixating the eyes on the right and 
left squares. Again, they press on a button whenever the cued number appears in the right or left 
squares in their foveal vision.  
3. Measure EEG without SSVEP: The procedure is the same as phase 1. However, the squares will not 
blink this time.  
From the study, only one important conclusion is applicable: 
 
6Hz, 7Hz, 8Hz and 9Hz work excellent as frequencies for SSVEP.  
This conclusion presents four possible candidates for the flickering squares. 7Hz and 9Hz are selected for the 
author’s BCI system because their 2nd or 3rd harmonics will not collide with each other. This is the case with 
picking the pair 6Hz and 9Hz or the pair 6Hz and 8Hz. Another reason for picking 7Hz and 9Hz is that they are 
not as close as the pair 7Hz and 8Hz or the pair 8Hz and 9Hz. This could give wrong results in case of a lag 
affecting the flickering frequency.  
3.1.2 Confirming SSVEP to work correctly in Unity 
The second experiment involves the previously chosen SSVEP frequencies, 7Hz and 9Hz, and investigates if 
the SSVEP paradigm can be implemented in the game engine Unity using C#. The reason for choosing Unity 
as a game engine and C# as the programming language is discussed in a later section, see section 4.1.4 about 
decision on tools for creating the application. 
 
Figure 3.2: The layout for the experiment to test if the SSVEP paradigm can work in Unity. The left and right squares flicker in their 
intended frequencies. The middle gray 3D cube moves up and down depending on which flickering square is attended. The red 
cross in the middle is a fixation point.  
The layout for the SSVEP paradigm is simple in this experiment as shown in Figure 3.2. Two flickering squares 
on the right and left side of the fixation point (a red cross). The squares keep switching between white and 
black. On the cross’ vertical line a grey box will be moving up and down depending on which flickering square 
==PAGE:40==
P a g e  | 28 
 
 
 
the subject attends. The cross, the white and black squares are 2D images, while the cube is a 3D object. The 
goal is to implement frequencies, which works correctly and move the square.  
3.1.2.1 First attempt  
The first attempt uses the Timer class from the Microsoft .Net framework to switch between the black and 
white squares. The Timer class, the tick rate, comes from Microsoft Windows platform’s own timer and runs 
in milliseconds. The formula for switching from black to white and back is reached by making an interval for 
the switch time and linking it with a Boolean statement for showing the black or the white square. The 
interval is calculated by dividing 1000 milliseconds with the frequency multiplied with 2. The reason for 
multiplying the frequency with 2 is the switch between black and white before getting one hertz. 
This code has not been tested with an amplifier because the Timer class itself makes the solution inapplicable. 
While it works perfectly when the game runs, the problem starts when it has to stop. When the game stops, 
the timers are still running. The root of problem lies in that they are not timers for the specific game but for 
the system’s timers. The squares will continue flickering even when changing levels and freeze the game 
causing it to crash down. This bad result leads onto finding another solution to make the SSVEP paradigm 
work correctly in Unity.  
3.1.2.2 Second attempt 
The second attempt handles the problem from a different approach. Instead of using the Microsoft Windows 
timers, it makes use of the time.deltatime function in Unity API. time.deltatime stands for the time in seconds 
it takes to complete the last frame and is read-only. This function is used to make the frame rate independent. 
Adding time.deltatime value to a variable specified as time results in a timer independent from the system. 
This means it starts and stops when the level starts and stops in the game.  
The sine equation for simple harmonic motion is implemented into the code to make the flickering squares 
flicker at the right frequencies. The sine equation for simple harmonic is usually used in signal processing but 
by applying it in the code, it can give precise and clear output without any cause of worry on its functionality. 
The sine equation from the normal book version has been derived and has then been implemented into the 
code. It is done in the following manner:  
y(t) = A ⋅ sin(ω⋅t +ϕ),                  
 
                  (3.1) 
where A is the amplitude, ω is angular velocity, t is time and ϕ is phase-shift. 
Since the ω is not known but the frequency is, it is better to derive this part. It is acknowledged that  
ω = π⋅ 2/ T and f = 1/T so ω = π⋅ 2⋅f,          
 
 
(3.2) 
where T is period for the whole wave and f is the frequency.  
This result of the equation can then be used since the data is available:  
y(t) = A ⋅ sin(π⋅ 2⋅f ⋅t+ϕ)                  
 
                  (3.3)    
A equals 1 in this case since it is of no importance. There is no need for any phase shifting either so ϕ is set 
to 0. This means:   
==PAGE:41==
P a g e  | 29 
 
 
 
y(t) = sin(π⋅ 2⋅f ⋅t)                   
                   
             (3.4) 
In the code, it is implemented as: 
float wave = Mathf.Sin( (dtime * 2.0f * Mathf.PI) * cycleHz); 
The cycleHz is set to the desired hertz for the square, here 7Hz or 9Hz depending on which square it is. dtime 
is the variable which time.deltatime is added and contains the time.  
Since the wave follows the sine wave, it will reach positive and negative values, which will be the switch to 
change between black and white squares. To prevent dtime from reaching infinity and lagging the game, 
dtime resets when the wave equals zero.

The results of testing this code are shown on Figure 3.3. It shows that both 7Hz and 9Hz flickering squares

The amplifier in the BCI solution is g.tec’s g.USBamp – usb biosignal amplifier – with 16 channels. g.USBamp 
MATLAB API requires MATLAB 2012a 32-bit version and the Data Acquisition Toolbox thus this version is used 
for analysing the signals from the amplifier and sending commands to the application.  
The electrodes in the experiment are gold plated while the EEG gel is of the brand Ten20 – Conductive. Skin 
cleansing swabs are with 70% alcohol with the name Cutisoft wipes.  
3.2.2 Electrode placement 
From the g.USBamp only the first channel will be used to acquire data. Attention can be measured in the 
occipital lobe. The electrode placement for this area are the positions O1, Oz and/or O2. The system in this 
thesis will follow the 10-20 international standards for electrode placement and only use Oz for 
measurement. There is no need to acquire more information because acquiring data from one or three 
channels will give the same result in this BCI system [65]. Using only one channel is the best option when 
considering computational processing power and time, and comfort for the subject.  
Figure 3.5 marks Oz, Fpz, Fz and A1 where only three placements will be in use at one time. The electrode 
from red channel 1 is placed on Oz, which is about 10% above inion. The channel acquires attention data aka 
SSVEP from this placement.  
On the other hand, the electrode from the yellow channel, representing the ground electrode, is placed on 
Fpz. This is 10% above nasion. Its mission is to reduce the internal and external electromagnetic interference 
on the acquired signal from Oz, such as from power lines of the amplifier or the electrical currents from the 
subject’s body.  
The electrode from the blue channel is the reference electrode. It can be placed on the ear as ear-clip in A1 
or on the head on Fz about 30% from nasion or 20% from Fpz. The signal from the reference node is used to 
remove environmental noise from the actual EEG signal obtained by Oz as to get a cleaner signal.

for a few subjects. 
3.2.3 EEG signal processing 
As mentioned before the activity of neurons within the brain generate small action potentials. These are best 
measured at the cerebral cortex over the scalp. The electrodes on the head measure these and send them 
to the amplifier to amplify the signal. The acquired EEG signals are processed with various signal processing 
techniques to detect the SSVEPs. In this work, the signal processing approach by the PhD student Adnan Vilic 
is used [74]. It has been modified to fit the needs of this BCI system.  
The sampling rate is set on 512Hz and only channel 1 is used throughout all the tests in this thesis.  
EEG signals carry noise with them that interferes with the pure signal. These can be from electrical devices 
or power supplies surrounding the experiment room. It can also be from within the brain or over the scalp 
as internal or external noise. To lower or remove the noise from the signal, band pass filters are used. As the 
flickering frequencies are on 7Hz and 9Hz, the lowest band-pass (5-30Hz) Butterworth filter is used in this 
study. It will attenuate internal noise from the body (e.g. eye and heart activity) and external noise (e.g. 
power supplies from electrical devices in the environment).

The SSVEP detection algorithm consists of three parts:  
 
Autocorrelation 
 
Fast Fourier Transformation 
 
Classifier 
The autocorrelation, also called serial correlation, is a variant of cross correlation where the signal is matched 
(cross-correlated) to itself to detect repeating patterns. This means that it finds the presence of repeated 
patterns of the periodic signal that are obscured by the noise and “boosts” them. This entails in more noise 
reduction where only the dominating signals remain. The clear repeating patterns will when using fast Fourier 
Transformation show a clearer frequency. 
The next step is extracting the present frequencies from the signal. This is normally done by using fast Fourier 
Transformation (FFT) algorithm when it comes to SSVEP. FFT transforms the signal from the time domain to 
the frequency domain. This makes it easier to discover and measure the frequency of the SSVEP.   
 
Figure 3.6: The graphs show an example where 8Hz is used to demonstrate the differences of the same EEG signal in the time 
domain (left) and the frequency domain (right).  
The algorithm uses a special classifier that has two sets of data. These are examined in each iteration. Each 
iteration takes originally 2 seconds but this has been brought down to 1 second in this modified version. This 
leads to lower record time to optimize the signal processing and has shown to work fine without affecting 
the results.  
The data sets are: 
 
SData: Most recent second of EEG 
 
CData: A concatenation of up to three most recent sets of SData. 
After discarding the first 2 seconds because the SSVEP establishes few hundred milliseconds after stimulus 
onset, a sample is recorded for 1 second [75]. Then autocorrelation and FFT is applied on both sets. 
Afterwards classes are produced for each set, representing a target frequency. The value of each class is the 
sum of power amplitudes around the relevant frequencies, also written as:

4.1 Type of game engines 
Many tools exist to make a game whether it is programmed from the scratch or inserting premade elements 
into a scene. A game needs an important software framework called game engine, which is designed to create 
and develop games. All game engines can be grouped into following three categories: 
4.1.1 Make a game engine 
The programmer starts from scratch. He makes the game engine from the beginning before making the game.  
Advantage: The programmer controls everything in the game and can shape it based on his wishes.  
Disadvantage: It takes too many resources and time to create a game engine. This kind of game engines are 
only for major game companies or people with years of time.  
4.1.2 Use a game engine 
Premade game engines are developed by different companies. The programmer uses the game engines API 
and functions to make his game. It is important to find the game engine which best fulfils most or all of the 
programmer’s wishes and needs. 
Advantage: The programmer saves time and resources, and can concentrate on making the game.  
Disadvantage: The programmer is forced to use the game engine API and manoeuvre through conflicts 
between the programmer’s wishes and game engine’s capabilities. Finding the right game engine and 
learning its API can take time. 
4.1.3 Use a game maker 
Game makers are software designed to make games fast and dirty. The programmer cannot program in game 
makers but has to use the elements and options in the program to make his games.  
Advantage: The game is made fast and easy without any programming problems. All settings and options 
related to game making exist already in the program. 
Disadvantage: The programmer is forced to use default elements and options in the game. The programmer 
can only choose between fixed elements with very little freedom.  Usually, game makers allow no 
connections from the games to other applications or games. The game made in game makers also do not run 
without the game maker program.  
==PAGE:48==
P a g e  | 36 
 
 
 
4.1.4 Decision on tools for making the application 
This project does not allow the option of creating the game engine since it is too time consuming. On the 
other hand, using a game maker is not an option either. While this saves much time, it restricts the project 
in many ways. The game maker makes it impossible to add flickering squares into the application, which is 
vital for the BCI system. It does not allow the application to receive inputs so changes can appear in the 
application according to the subject’s brain activity.  
The only choice left is to use a game engine. One that best fulfils the requirements of the BCI system. Only 
Unreal game engine and Unity come out at the top of a list of 10 most popular game engines/makers. 
Appendix B gives detailed information about the found game engines/makers. While both are very good 
choices for game making, Unity is chosen as the game engine for this application. The reason is simple: the 
author has more experience with Unity than with Unreal game engine. Another reason is that Unity has a 
free version while Unreal game engine cost 19$.  
Unity uses three kinds of programming languages and there is the option of combining them while 
programming. The decision falls on only using C# since the author has some knowledge compared to Boo or 
UnityScript. Another reason is that both Boo and UnityScript are languages Unity’s own variations of Python 
and JavaScript, while C# is not. This means that to communicate with other applications Boo and Unityscript 
needs a framework whereas C# does not. C# is already Microsoft Windows platform’s main language and 
capable of communicating with other applications through its client and server protocols.  
The next step is to choose at 2D and 3D graphic tools. Since a game maker is not an option anymore, graphics 
tools are needed to draw objects for the application. For the 2D graphics, the decision falls on using Adobe 
Photoshop because of past experience. For the 3D graphics, the decision falls on using Autodesk 3DS Max 
out of the three best and popular tools for 3D graphic and animations. The other two are Blender and 
Autodesk Maya. The reason is that Blender keeps crashing down after 30 minutes of use. This will only hinder 
the development of 3D graphic elements. Maya is too costly and most books on Maya are for advanced artists

4.2.3.3.1 The Ninja 
 
Figure 4.10: Four states of the ninja. From left, normal ninja, moving ninja, dead ninja and frozen ninja. 
The ninja is the main character controlled through SSVEPs via the flickering squares. There would be no point 
to the game or controlling via SSVEPs without him. He can move either right or left. He can also freeze and 
die. His state is locked when he freezes, dies or moves from one direction to another. The reason for this is 
that the flickering squares keeps flickering whether he moves, freezes or dies. Therefore, by locking the 
ninja’s movement, the flickering squares will have no effect on the ninja or the game. That is until the ninja 
is allowed to move again.  
The ninja moves by changing the 2D image, the sprite, into a blurry one indicating that he is moving fast until 
he reaches his destination as to go back to the normal sprite. Teleportation was first implemented as means 
of moving from point A to point B but it looked horrid and did not have the intended effect. The blurry ninja 
replaces the teleportation to get a better gaming effect. 
When the ninja dies, he goes black and cannot be controlled for 5 seconds where after the game ends. This 
is to show the user that he lost all his life and did not survive until the end.  
The freezing is not used in this version of the game but it is implemented. This is only used when overt and 
covert attention is included in the experiment. The freeze state is used to punish the users if they move their 
eyes and it is recorded in the electrooculography. The freeze state only suspends for 2 seconds but can be 
adjusted. 
The ninja cannot go beyond the borders on the right and left side of the tree. Although it is implemented in 
the game, it is not used. The reason for not using this option is that it will confuse the ADHD patient if the 
ninja moves through the borders from one side and appears from the other side, and vice versa. The purpose 
of implementing and leaving the code piece as comment in the code is to let future programmers or 
researchers use this handy option. They just need to uncomment that piece of code for it to work with the 
rest of the game.  
4.2.3.3.2 Spawners 
In gaming terminology a spawner, also called a spawn point, is a position object from where items, player or 
enemies are created or recreated from. In this game, there are 5 spawners placed at the top of the tree, 
outside the user’s game view; one for each of the 5 positions the ninja can move to. From these spawn apples, 
pears, stones or worms as the falling items. The ninja has now to collect or avoid these. When the items hit 
the ninja or falls down outside from the user’s game view, they are destroyed by the game. This is done to 
save memory and stop any cause of lags. The spawners are controlled by a script to choose randomly which 
==PAGE:59==
P a g e  | 47 
 
 
 
of the 5 spawners should spawn and which of the 4 falling items. The settings for the chance of spawning one 
of the 4 falling items is 12.5% for a worm, 12.5% for a stone, 12.5% for a pear and 62.5% for an apple. The 
reason why the apples appear more frequently is that they give low points compared to the pears. The stones 
and worms have no need to appear more since it would affect the user’s motivation to play the game. These 
settings can easily be changed though. The speed of the falling items are different for each of them and are 
set according to empiric testing.  
4.2.3.3.3 Points, souls and paper planes animation 
There are two kinds of animation that runs automatically when an element is triggered. They both uses the 
same animation editor but with different stats. The first type is the points’ animation that appears when the 
ninja catches fruits or worms whereas the second type is the soul that appears when the ninja is hit by a 
stone.

These animations and the paper planes animations are implemented in the Animation Editor in Unity. The

While only one kind of points is attached to the specific fruits and worm, there are 3 different souls attached

Although showing information to the user, the scoreboard also serves as a distraction.

4.2.4 Render the texture 
2D graphic does normally not run in 3D graphic. The same can be said about 2D games running in 3D 
environments. It is harder to control a 2D game in a 3D environment without using a few tricks to bypass the 
2D/3D barrier.  
One option is to run the 2D game images as flat 3D objects on the 3D blackboard in the classroom. This would 
result in bad graphic and bad code. The user would be able to see the spawning of the fruits, worms and 
stones. It would ruin the enjoyment. This would also go against the requirement for interconnected 
components, which separates easily and replaces with other parts without breaking down the structure of 
the training playground. 
Another option is to run the 2D game away from the 3D classroom but show it as a moving image on the 
blackboard in the 3D classroom. This technique is called render texture. The main camera focuses on the 
classroom while another camera focus on the 2D game. The camera on the 2D game shows its results on a 
texture placed above the blackboard, and it will look like the game is played on the blackboard while in reality 
it is played at the 2D game’s position. Figure 4.12 illustrates how render texture works.  
 
Figure 4.12: A representation of how RenderTexture works in Unity. The game is in reality played somewhere else in the 
playground but recorded and shown live on a screen by the game camera. The screen is placed above the blackboard but the user 
does not know.  From the user’s perspective (main camera) the game is running on the blackboard. The user can only see the 
classroom from the main camera’s perspective.  
On the Internet, there is a code for simulating a render texture in Unity. The code copies the displayed 
elements on screen into a texture. Unfortunately, the solution cannot work for this application. The code 
executes the function Texture.Apply() which makes the application extremely slow. This results in a delay of 
seconds for output on the screen. The movement to the right appears on the texture many seconds after it 
==PAGE:61==
P a g e  | 49 
 
 
 
actually happens. Not being able to get the points or avoiding the dangers because of a delayed render 
texture ruins the enjoyment of the game in the training playground. 
Fortunately, Unity Pro has a render texture function that works much faster. The author has bought the 
license for Unity Pro version to implement the render texture solution into the project as to run 2D games in 
the 3D environment. The final result of the whole application is shown in Figure 4.13.  
 
Figure 4.13: Final result of the 2D game in the 3D classroom environment with SSVEP layout implemented into the classroom.

Chapter 5 – Material and methods 3 
This is the last of three chapters on Material and Methods. This chapter covers at three different topics, each 
within its own section. The first section analyses two different approaches on communicating between the 
MATLAB script and the application. This is important because this is what makes the BCI system function. It 
also matters in the sense of speed. Any time wasted in the communication between MATLAB and the 
application means a slower response in controlling the character in the 3D playground. The next section 
shortly analyses the application architecture from a software design perspective and discusses the 
application’s classes and dependencies. The chapter ends with a section about the experiment procedure. It 
explains the sequences of experiment tests and the routes applied to the tests.  
5.1 Communication in the BCI system 
The commands from MATLAB, which process the EEG signal from the electrodes, needs to be send to the 
application with the 3D classroom and the 2D game. This will result in a change in the application and lets 
MATLAB continue with processing new EEG signals from the user’s brain.  
To handle the matter of communication, the communication protocol User Datagram Protocol (UDP) 
communicates between MATLAB and the application. Compared to its counterpart Transmission Control 
Protocol (TCP), UDP is connectionless protocol. This means when the data is send through UDP, there is no 
guarantee that it reaches its destination. On the other hand, UDP is much faster than TCP since it sends many 
of the same data packages at once and forgets them while TCP sends one data package and waits for an 
acknowledgement message from the destination before forgetting it. Figure 5.1 demonstrates how TCP and 
UDP send data packages for communication between sender and receiver.  
 
Figure 5.1: TCP vs UDP. When Sender sends data via TCP, it waits until it receives an acknowledgement from the Receiver before 
sending more data. If the Sender does not receive an acknowledgement as shown with data 2 then it resends the data until it 
receives an acknowledgement from the Receiver. This means the Receiver cannot continue because the new data received is 
==PAGE:66==
P a g e  | 54 
 
 
 
actually just old data. This results in a lot of wasteful time on waiting for acknowledgement and resending data when there is no 
need. This wastefulness does not occur with UDP. The Sender keeps sending data to the Receiver via UDP. The Sender does not 
wait for any acknowledgement but keeps sending until there is no data to send to the Receiver. The only issue related to UDP is 
that if the data is lost then it does not resend the data again.  
The issues of UDP are of no concern to this BCI system. The reason is that only one-way communication is 
needed where the server, MATLAB, sends data to the client, the application made in Unity. When MATLAB 
sends the command to move right or left, a lot of data packages are sent on the same time. The application 
only need to receive one of these to move the ninja. When the ninja receives the command to move right or 
left, no other command will be able to affect his movement until he completes the given command. This is 
explained in section 4.2.3 regarding the game design. All data packages from MATLAB sent through UDP are 
gone when the command is accomplished by the ninja and he is ready for a new command from MATLAB. 
MATLAB sends the commands right, left or freeze in the form of “1”, “2”, or “3”. These are sent out of 
MATLAB as strings converted to bytes. The ninja has a script attached as a component that receives these 
bytes and converts them back to string again to uncover the commands from MATLAB. These are then 
forwarded to the component containing the ninja controller script that moves the ninja to right, left or freeze. 
When the ninja stops moving, the user decides which flickering square to attend. MATLAB process the new 
signal and sends out a new command through UDP for the ninja’s next movement. Appendix D contains a 
manual of the BCI system and explains how to start and use the system.  
5.2 Application structure 
The architecture of the application has been special from the beginning. Normally, it is recommended to 
follow the Model-View-Control (MVC) architecture, which splits the program into three units: Model, View 
and Control.  
The Model unit deals with storing, manipulating, maintaining the data or the behaviour of the whole 
program. The View unit handles the data that displays the data from the Model unit to the view, typically 
consisting of user interface elements e.g. as graphical user interface (GUI). The last unit, the Control, connects 
the Model and the View unit. Control unit accepts the response through the user interface and instructs the 
Model unit to work on a command for the behaviour of the application. Control receives the results from 
Model unit and sends the results to the View unit for display to the user.  
Since the application is programmed in a game engine, it has not been possible to follow the MVC 
architecture. Game engines have already their own class structure which the new application or game builds 
upon. This is also the case in this application. While the application cannot be divided, it is possible to more 
or less split the BCI system up into those 3 units. The View unit would be the graphic shown through Unity. 
The Model unit would consist of both the classes from Unity but also the MATLAB part, which calculates the 
frequency. The Control unit would exist of classes made only with Unity.  
The issue with MVC is shown when a class dependency diagram is drawn of the implemented classes for the 
application. Figure 5.2 illustrates such a diagram where the classes’ names are shown and the number of 
methods contained appears on the left top corner. The thicker the arrows are from one class to another, the 
more traffic between the classes. The diagram reveals that about half of the classes are communicating with 
each other. The game engine uses all classes and communicates with them all. There is communication 
between the classes through Unity, even if it looks like this is not the case from the diagram.  
==PAGE:67==
P a g e  | 55 
 
 
 
 
Figure 5.2: The class dependency diagram of all the classes implemented for the application. Notice how half of the classes are not 
communicating with each other on the diagram. The communication happens via the game engine, Unity, and is hidden in this 
diagram. The dependencies (arrows) are of different thickness. The size of thickness indicates how much traffic will go through 
from one class to the other when the application is running. 
Unity allows different scripts to communicate through separate components for the same element e.g. the 
ninja has the scripts Ninja Controller, Score, DestroyOnContact, UDPReceive. While every script should be 
connected with the ninja, only the Score script is not. The same happens with the scripts for the 3D movable 
characters. Normally, a program would not function with classes not communicating between each other but 
this is where Unity comes in and communicates between the classes. This is the reason how the whole 
application can function. It allows scripts placed as components for one element to communicate without 
importing the classes into the script directly.  
Looking at the Figure 5.2, it is noticeable that the classes Spawner, NinjaController, GameControl, 
ContinueButton are the ones which receives or sends most information to other classes. The number of 
arrows and the thickness of the arrows illustrate this. Generally, this is seen as a bad sign because it means 
the appearance of hubs, which can become bottlenecks and halt or lag the program. In this case, there is no 
need for concern. Instead, those arrows prove the importance of those classes and the making of the skeleton 
structure of the application. Without these classes, there would be no application whereas removing any of 
the other would not cause any harm to the fundamental functionality of the application. 
Appendix E has the Unified modeling language (UML) dependency diagram in a bigger view and the detailed 
UML class diagrams showing the attributes and methods, also called operations, for all implemented classes.  
The concept of designing this application has always been on making it simple for the ADHD patient without 
smart functions to confuse the patient. It is important that the ADHD patient can use the application without 
reading a big manual as criterion. In simple terms: The focus has been on usability and user experience.

5.3 Experiment procedure 
There are in total 10 different tests and 3 routes in the experiment. The routes are combined with the first 9 
tests to examine the number of selections, the selection time and the selection accuracy. 
A subject has to go through the tests in this sequence:  
1. Game without any game elements + route 1 
2. Empty classroom where the game has no elements + route 2  
3. Classroom with 3D items where the game has no elements + route 3 
4. Game with only fruits + route 1 
5. Empty classroom where the game has only fruit elements + route 2  
6. Classroom with 3D items where the game has only fruit elements + route 3 
7. Game with both fruit and danger elements + route 1 
8. Empty classroom where the game has both fruit and danger elements + route 2  
9. Classroom with 3D items where the game has both fruit and danger elements + route 3 
10. The full application in story mode with 3 levels 
5.3.1 Sequence of test 
The purpose of having this sequence of tests is to investigate how the selection time and accuracy are 
affected when a change happens in the environment around the game and classroom. These changes appear 
from one test to the next as shown above in the sequence order.  
Test 1, 2 and 3 are tests with no elements in the game. They run in 3 settings: without the classroom, within 
the empty classroom and within the full classroom with 3D items. These tests explore the different 
environment while the subject does not have to attend any elements in the game.  
Test 4, 5 and 6 are tests with only the fruit elements appearing in the game. They run in the same 3 settings. 
These tests explore how the subjects attention is affected in different environments while having to attend 
the falling red and green fruits and the point animations in the game.  
==PAGE:69==
P a g e  | 57 
 
 
 
Test 7, 8 and 9 are tests with both the fruits and the danger elements appearing in the game. They run in the 
same 3 settings. These tests explore how subjects attention is affected in different environments while having 
to attend both the falling red-green fruits with their golden point animation and the danger elements that 
cause negative black point animation. Compared to the previous 3 tests, these tests explore the negative 
points animations coupled with the positive on attention, such as a hit by a stone causing a blue soul 
animation from the ninja indicating the subject lost a life.  
The last test (10) is used as a bait to let the subject play the whole application without any testing if they 
finish the previous 9 tests. The application saves the subject’s level scores and total scores on the computer. 
These scores can be used to investigate if there is an effect on the subject’s attention when moving from one 
level to the next.  
5.3.2 Routes 
As mentioned earlier, there are 3 routes used in the experiment. These are combined with the first 9 tests in 
the experiment. These routes are used to find the number of selections, selection time and accuracy for each 
subject.  
Each of the routes has 30 different steps that the subject has to follow on the screen. The routes are executed 
by showing the subject a red arrow above the red cross. This arrow points for 0.5 seconds at the square the 
subject has to attend. It can be either right or left flickering square. When the subject attends the directed 
flickering square and MATLAB recognizes the subject’s EEG signal frequency as attending the correct square, 
the next arrow is shown on the screen above the red cross. This process continues until all 30 steps in a route 
are completed. The arrows receive their input from MATLAB the same way the ninja does, through UDP. 
Figure 5.3 shows 3 different tests with a red arrow above the red cross.  
==PAGE:70==
P a g e  | 58 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 5.3: Three tests illustrating the difference of the arrow tests. The first image (top) shows only the 2D game where only fruits 
are falling. The second image (left bottom) shows the empty 3D classroom where fruits and danger elements are falling. The last 
image (right bottom) shows the full 3D classroom with distractions but no fruits or danger elements falling in the 2D game. Notice 
that all 3 images have a red arrow pointing to the square which should be attended by the subject. As usual the squares flicker 
between white and black at their specified frequencies.  
Selection time is the time it takes from showing the arrow to the subject and having MATLAB recognises that 
the subject attends the correct flickering square. Furthermore, selection accuracy is MATLAB’s recognition 
of which frequency the attended square has compared with the square the subject is supposed to attend. 
The 3 routes can be found in Appendix F. These follow the simple structure of 5 boxes which represents the 
5 positions of the ninja. The black cross in one of the boxes shows the start position of the arrows. The arrows 
are just under the boxes and show the movement to follow on the screen. The route follows the rows of the 
arrow before going to the next row in the same column of the reached arrow. For instance in Route 1, the 
first arrow is a right arrow, continued by another right arrow. Since there is no next arrow connecting to it, 
the next arrow is the one under it, a left arrow. Moving down the column means following the arrows in this 
new column. When the end of the arrows is reached in a row, it is again time to go down the column to the 
next row and arrow. This procedure continues to the end of the route.  
Notice how each of the routes have an arrangement where the arrows path goes from one side of the screen 
to the other, and then the whole way back again. This is to investigate if the selection time is faster when the 
subject has to attend the same frequency compared to the normal selection time. With the exception of this 
arrangement, the steps have a pseudorandom order so that the subject is not able to recognise any patterns 
in the routes.  
==PAGE:71==
P a g e  | 59 
 
 
 
The order of the testing sequence in the experiment and the steps sequence in the routes are intentionally 
generated like that. The aim of these is to avoid the subject being adjusted to the pattern in the routes or the 
environment settings in the tests. This is avoided by letting the subject go through all 3 environment settings 
and routes before adding changes to the 2D game. Although the routes are attached to the same 
environment setting, it cannot be recognisable by the subject who will believe it to be random.

6.2 Expectations for the BCI experiment 
Some expectations have been made before executing the experiment. These are assumptions about the 
outcome of the tests in the experiment. They are established on the concept of the application and the 
written literature about ADHD and BCI systems.  
==PAGE:78==
P a g e  | 66 
 
 
 
6.2.1 The selection time 
The total time for the routes or the average selection time will most certainly result in one of following three 
patterns: 
 
The time will be low for test 1 but will increase the closer it reaches test 9. 
 
The time will increase according to environment. 
 
The time will increase according to game elements. 
The more distractions there are in front of the ADHD patients, the harder it is for them to keep attending the 
intended object. This is the objective of the application. All three patterns lead up to this conclusion. The 
difference lies in which way it happens. The patterns assess if there is an impact on performance if there is 
no game elements in the application or if the environment around the game changes. 
Another assumption is that there is a difference choosing the left flickering square or the right flickering 
square. This assumption is not about left and right vision but about the frequencies 7Hz and 9Hz. The findings 
should explain which frequency has the lower and higher selection time. 
Last assumption about selection time is to check if the average selection of looking four times at the same 
frequency is faster than the average selection time. As mentioned previously, a special structure has been 
implemented into the three routes to execute this part in the tests. 
6.2.2 The selection accuracy  
The selection accuracy may suffer based on the subject’s actions. If the subject’s attention is diverted by a 
distraction while the arrow is shown in the tests, the subject may end up guessing on the wrong flickering 
square. This will result in wrong selections until the subject looks at the correct flickering square. Of course, 
when it happens the anomalies will be quite noticeable.  
Beside this issue, the assumption is that the accuracy will be above the 90.81% for 2 flickering squares since 
the original algorithm has shown as a premise of 90.81% for 8 flickering squares. [65]  
6.2.3 The total selections 
These will follow the opposite pattern of the accuracy. The total number of selections will become higher the 
worse the accuracy gets. This means that if the accuracy is good then the number of total selection will be 
small and vice versa.  
6.2.4 The game points 
The scores gained in the levels may differ from one person to another because the game items fall at random. 
The quick thinkers may also know which ones to catch and leave. Still, there is a hope that there may be a 
pattern where a difference can be seen where the points gained in level 3 are lower than level 2, which is 
lower than level 1. These scores will come from test 10 which is the full game to let the subjects enjoy playing 
while their score for every level is collected.

Chapter 7 – Discussion  
A BCI system has been built after gaining an understanding of the brain physiology, EEG, ADHD, attention 
and literature review of existing BCI systems related to ADHD and training environments. The BCI system has 
been tested as a software application and as a treatment system. The results have been posted in the 
previous chapter. This chapter will not only analyse and discuss these results but will also look at the BCI as 
a complete product. There are three sections in this chapter. The first section analyses and discusses the 
testing executed on the application. The second section analyses and discusses the results achieved from the 
BCI experiment. The last section in this chapter will study the BCI system as a whole and comment on it.  
7.1 Discussion on results from the application testing 
Semantic, syntax and logical errors appear when an application is being implemented. Although most errors 
are fixed under the designing and coding phase, many unknown errors are still left. These appear later when 
the program has been implemented and are called bugs. Bugs mainly consist of semantic and logical mistakes. 
The compiler finds the syntax errors under the application development. 
To ensure that the application works as intended, different kinds of tests need to be run on the application. 
It is impossible to remove all bugs from a program. These tests will remove most of the bugs in the application 
and minimize the occurrences of bugs after the application release.  
This section discusses the four tests performed on the application. These four tests are all unique so the 
errors left by one test is covered by the other tests:  
 
Structural Test  
 
Functional Test  
 
Usability Test 
 
Profiling/Performance test 
7.1.1 Structural test 
White Box testing or structural testing are tests run on the application to check if the code works as designed. 
The testing runs on the methods to confirm their functions. Since these tests run on the inner structure of 
the application, the tests have to be changed if the application’s methods are changed.  
For C# application development, there exist many, such as NUnit, Visual Studio Unit Testing, xUnit, Moq, 
MbUnit,  Testdriven.Net and MSTest. There is just one issue with these; they are not intended to run in Unity. 
Instantiation of GameObjects and operating on them is not possible outside the Unity game engine. 
Fortunately, Unity released the testing package Unity Testing Tools in the summer 2014 [88]. The test tools 
run the framework, NUnit, as an integrated part in the Unity Editor providing unit testing execution for the 
developer inside Unity. 
As shown in Figure 6.1, all unit tests originate from the functions in each of the classes in the application. 
Earlier runs of these unit tests did fail but these errors and bugs have been fixed in the code. This process 
keeps iterating until every test succeeds and no errors are found, exactly as it is envisioned with unit testing.  
A total of 141 unit tests have run on 26 classes. The UDPReceive class is not included because it is not possible 
to make a unit test where the test sends and receives inputs that goes outside Unity. 
==PAGE:89==
P a g e  | 77 
 
 
 
Although many important errors are fixed, only the most relevant errors for this thesis will be commented: 
 
Collision of fruits and danger elements. 
 
Calculation of the total scores based on the three previous levels.  
 
Destroying the fruits and danger elements going out of the 2D game view. 
Fruits, worms and stones hit each other and instead of bypassing, they push the element under them to move 
faster while falling outside the line. This causes disorder in the 2D game view and the ninja is not able to 
catch or avoid many of the falling items. This error is corrected by numbering the elements as layers and 
using the function Physics2D.IgnoreLayerCollision. By adding numbers to the elements as layers, it is possible 
to refrain the collision by telling the code that if two layers collide then they should just ignore each other. 
The problem was first approached with LayerMatrix but it caused lagging thus leading to this solution. 
Total scores are based on calculation of the scores from each level but when the error happened, the output 
was either wrong percentage or the last level scores was shown as total scores. The former thoughtless error 
is corrected by dividing the percentages with overall levels after adding them together instead of dividing 
after each level. The latter thoughtless error is corrected by adding the scores instead of overwriting. 
Fruits, worms and stones that do not touch the player keep falling down ending outside the game view. 
Normally, these are all destroyed but an error caused the pears to keep falling. In the next level, they kept 
falling making a lag after a number of pears appeared in the game. The error is fixed by adding pears into the 
group of elements that should be destroyed by the out-of-bounds destroyer class and by having all the 
elements destroyed when the level ends. This means that even if an element by a mistake gets through the 
out of bounds destroyer, it is still destroyed when the level ends. No fruits, worms or stones will be carried 
to the next level.  
7.1.2 Functional test 
Black Box testing or functional testing is the opposite of White Box testing. While White Box runs on the inner 
structure, Black Box tests the outer structure of the application. The developer of functional tests knows 
nothing about the structure of the application and designs tests based on the functionality of the application. 
Functional testing does not test all the branches in an application construction but helps showing if the 
application is missing any vital functions or if any of the implemented functions are malfunctioning. 
NUnit cannot be used for functional testing because of the interactions that goes between the user and the 
graphical user interface. Only an actor, e.g. a user, can execute some of the tests when the application has 
reached a specific point in the application span. This is a point that the NUnit has no chance of reaching by 
itself.  
Generally, functional tests are executed by use-cases. A use-case is a scenario that is based on a function 
defined from the application’s requirements. The use-cases follow from the user’s perspective and test if a 
given function works as intended.

7.1.3 Usability test 
“The extent to which a product can be used by specific users to achieve specified goals with 
effectiveness, efficiency and satisfaction in a specified context of use.” – [ISO 9241. Del 11. 1998] 
Usability according to International Organization for Standardization (ISO). 
Usability means the product have a fine interaction between effectiveness, efficiency and satisfaction for the 
user. Programmers usually look down on usability because they think it as a waste of time or already 
implemented into their programs. They do not see any need to run usability tests on their programs using 
those reasons. This often results in programs that are complex to use, bad colouring, or missing functions 
and ends up being badly received by the target group. In the worst case, this can lead to the downfall of the 
program and the firm who wasted much time and many resources on the software. Usability can be looked 
as making the product the correct way in which the users will gladly accept and come back for more.  
Usability is important for this application since the application is to be used with ADHD patients. If the 
intended target group does not like the product, it will not be used. The usability testing has been executed 
in 2 phases in this application’s development period: 
 
When deciding on the game design using the paper prototypes in the design phase.  
 
When the application has been constructed at the end of the development phase. 
Executing usability tests in the design phase will save development time and resources needed for other 
processes. The results will state if the design is intended for the target group or not. This kind of usability 
tests has been performed and the results have already been described in section 4.2.3.1 regarding ideas and 
prototyping of the 2D game. 
Executing usability tests after the development phase will express the target group’s acceptance level of the 
complete product. This kind of usability normally results in inputs from the target group about what to 
change, remove, add and extend. It also tells what works well in the product and should not be changed.  
Two groups have carried out the usability test: 15 children near the target group’s age range and the 11 
subjects who participated in the experiment. The children used left and right arrow keys for control in the 2D 
game whereas the subjects used the flickering squares. Appendix I contains the question sheet handed to

7.1.4 Performance test 
Performance testing is to examine the performance of an application in responsiveness and stability under 
particular workload. It can also be used to investigate, measure, validate and verify attributes of the 
application e.g. scalability, reliability and resource usage.  
A performance test can be executed in many ways; one of them is through a profiler. A profiler investigates 
and measures the application’s resource usage and reliability. It can help optimising the application by 
reporting how much time and resources are spent on various areas of the application while it is running. 
Unity has a built-in profiler editor that investigates in detail and can help in optimising the application.  
It is a good idea to run the profiler on the application and investigate which areas uses most resources as to 
optimise them. This is highly needed in the last level in the full classroom, since 3D graphics sometimes 
occupy too many resources, which could lead to application lags. Despite the fact that this would ruin the 
experience for the user, it would also make the flickering squares’ frequencies unrecognisable by the MATLAB 
script.  
Before running the profiler, Unity’s editor allows to check up statistics on all screens and levels in the 
application. From these it is easier to analyse which screens and levels use most resources and could become 
==PAGE:94==
P a g e  | 82 
 
 
 
a bottleneck. These bottlenecks would cause lags on older computers, which would affect the frequencies of 
the flickering squares. These affected frequencies would not be read well and thereby ruin the enjoyment of 
the application.  
The statistics for all of the screens and levels can be found in Appendix S. From these, it can be seen that 
there is no need to worry about memory being too high. It goes from 4.5MB on the screens to 44.4MB as 
maximum in the classroom with distractions, which is not much compared to the typical games coming out 
these days. The main thread runs in a nice pace too with a stable frame per second (FPS) for all screens and 
levels in the application. The lower main thread runs, the less chance there is for a lag to appear in the running 
application. Still, changing these in this application will not have a significant effect on the application’s 
speed.  
A pattern appears when comparing statistics for every screen and level. The draw calls for the screens keep 
themselves stable at 2-16 calls as shown in Figure 6.2. The draw calls are higher in the levels than the screens. 
The draw calls number is also getting higher the more elements exist in a level. The lowest being level 1 (only 
the 2D game) while the highest being level 3 (the classroom with distractions) as shown in Figure 6.3. In 
simple terms, draw calls are directly proportional to the number of materials in the scene. The number of 
draw calls represents the number of times the camera has to draw a frame. It is always good to have this 
number as low as possible because it means less work for the renderer. So having less draw calls means 
better performance of the application. Since first level have only 14 draw calls just like the screens, it is only 
logical that the profiler runs on level 2 and 3 since their draw calls are 13 and 21 times higher than the level 
1.  
After analysing the issue with the profiler, it is found that the problem did not have much to do with the 3D 
elements and 3D characters but with the lights used in the classroom. The camera is forced to draw all light 
points repeatedly for each frame instead of just once. So the more lights in the classroom, the more draw 
calls are needed.  
A point-light covering the whole area above the classroom and a directional-light in the hallway have been 
used as lighting in the 3D environment. By use of empirical testing with the other lights, it is discovered that 
by switching both lights with 2 directional lights, the same effect can be reached. This lowers the draw calls 
too since the area to light have become smaller. These results are good but since an optimising is being done, 
it would be best to make the draw calls go down as much as possible. After consulting the Unity employees, 
Mike Geig and Adam Buckner, about the lighting, the idea of changing ambient lighting for the whole level in 
render settings is discovered. By setting the ambient light to white instead of previous settings, only one 
directional light is needed to light up the classroom. The bonus from this setting is a better lighting and a 
heavily reduced number of draw calls. Figure 6.4 illustrates on a graph the resources being used in level 2 in 
the application while Figure 6.5 illustrates the same for level 3. 
Notice how each drop in the graph demonstrates the drops in draw calls. Remember that level 2 is the 
classroom without any distractions and level 3 is the classroom with distractions. Level 2 starts with 269 draw 
calls while level 3 starts with 432 draw calls to run the level. The first change results in dropping to 191 draw 
calls for level 2 and 300 draw calls for level 3. The drop in draw calls and resources can really be felt when 
the lights are changed with ambient lights where level 2 uses only 77 draw calls and level 3 uses only 146. 
This optimisation is tremendous and the game will now not lag on old computers.

7.2.3 Information transfer rate 
Supposedly, ITR measures the performance of a BCI system. Figure 6.22 presents the ITR of the BCI system 
for each of the subjects where the last column displays the average ITR for the whole system. Depending on 
the subjects, the ITR swings from 3.26 bits/min to 42.24 bits/min. The average ITR for the whole system 
attained is 11.85 bits/min. As shown in the Figure 6.22 only 4 of the 11 subjects come below the average ITR 
with subject 7 being very close. The rest of the subjects place themselves above the average ITR. These results 
show what the previous results have shown already: Subject 9 and 10 have the best performance and subject 
5 and 8 have the worst performance. 
Criticism should follow the results obtained from the formula for ITR. Just by raising the targets and lowering 
the accuracy, another BCI system can easily place itself higher than this BCI system in performance. This BCI 
system has 2 targets and the average accuracy is 92.26% with a selection time on 3.07 seconds. As previously 
mentioned, the ITR for this system is 11.85 bits/min. Now change the targets to 5 and lower the average 
accuracy to 75% with the same selection time. The ITR for this new system calculates to 19.74 bits/min. 
Another setting with 9 targets, accuracy on 60% and same selection time will result in an ITR on 
19.51bits/min.  
The ITR formula puts too much weight on targets while it should put more weight on the accuracy succeeded 
by the BCI system. Although it is standard to use this formula for BCI systems, maybe it is time for a new 
formula used for BCI specific. Nevertheless, the argument is not to take ITR for BCI systems too seriously 
because it does not really put enough weight on accuracy as it does on the targets.

7.2.4 Source of error 
It is important to remember that the measurement error always exist, even when the results are good. This 
is also the case with the results for this BCI system. The most important and possible sources of error will be 
outlined here.  
7.2.4.1 Impedance 
The first one to mention is the impedance. The impedance can affect the selection time since a low 
impedance is less filled with noise than a high impedance. Both subject 4 and 8 had higher impedance than 
the rest. This could be the reason for their times being so high.  
7.2.4.2 The red arrows 
While it is fully intended, the arrows only appear for 0.5 seconds. If the subjects miss the arrow due to a 
distraction, they will have to guess which flickering square to pay attention to. Choosing the wrong flickering 
square results in increasing the number of total selections, the course times and the accuracy. Selection time 
will not be affected since a flickering square is chosen albeit the wrong one. This special design of the red 
arrow is on purpose. Nevertheless to overcome this planned error, a longer appearance time for the arrow 
could be implemented. The arrow could also be made static until a flickering square is selected, where the 
next red arrow will show which flickering square to attend to.  
7.2.4.3 Sample size 
The results are obtained from only 11 subjects. A bigger subject group is needed to clarify how stable the 
results are. This is rarely seen in BCI experiments and requires many resources to be achieved. Still, this may 
be required to prove the legitimacy and stability of the results.  
7.2.4.4 Target group 
The group testing the application and taking part in the experiment are not ADHD patients but healthy 
subjects. They are not children or teenagers but adults from age 23 to 32 years old. 
The usability is directed towards teenagers in the same age range as the target group. Their age range from 
12 to 17 years old. The results do not only come from them but also from the subjects participating in the 
experiment. The teenagers’ inputs have mainly been about the application itself without the BCI part.  
7.2.4.5 Subjects 
The last possible source of error to mention are the subjects participating in the experiment. The two worst 
times are acquired by subjects who are both unemployed graduates, wherefrom one claimed to have ADHD. 
This makes one believe that the attention is low due to the fact that they are both unemployed. They have 
no need to be as attentive compared to the others in the group who are all students. The students need to 
keep their attention when studying whereas the unemployed do not have this need. 
The three people with the best times from the results are from the same University and they all have worked 
or are working with SSVEP. It is possible that the more experience one has with SSVEP, the better 
accomplishments. 
7.3 Discussion on the BCI system 
Being a novel BCI system for ADHD treatment with SSVEP control, the system is a success. The training 
incorporated as a game is unnoticed and instead happily received by the subjects. The distractions are found 
==PAGE:100==
P a g e  | 88 
 
 
 
to be a challenge to overcome by the subjects instead of finding them to be boring exercises as it normally is 
with ADHD training exercises on a computer.  
The BCI system is also faster and more accurate than other BCI systems at present. It has an average selection 
time of 3.07 seconds where most BCI systems have much longer selection times, starting from 6.62 seconds 
and up. The average accuracy of 92.26% is probably one of the best accuracy currently for BCI systems.  
7.3.1 Difference in healthy subjects and ADHD patients 
As mentioned previously, the experiment uses healthy subjects without ADHD. This means that the results 
cannot directly be applied on the ADHD patients. However, since the healthy subjects are affected by the 
changes in the training playground, it is safe to conclude based on past literature that ADHD patients will be 
affected much more than the healthy subjects. This also means that they will harvest bigger gains than the 
healthy subjects when they train attention using the BCI system.  
7.3.2 Averages of 3 different settings 
The results are from the average selection time and accuracy from the first nine tests in the experiment. The 
results will change if the environment is set static. Running the tests only in the 2D game environment or the 
full 3D classroom environment will have a big impact on the results. The 2D game environment will result in 
better selection time, lower total selections and higher accuracy while the 3D classroom environment will 
results in worse selection time, higher total selections and lower accuracy. These conclusions can be made 
from the results by simple looking at environment groupings figures in Chapter 6. This only confirms the 
results being much better than other BCI systems with only one setting.  
7.3.3 Prototype limitations 
Since this system is a prototype BCI system, there is only one 2D game running on the blackboard inside the 
3D classroom. The 3D playground allows different 2D games to be selected and run on the blackboard. The 
only condition is to move the render texture camera to the new game. This can easily be programmed so 
options to choose different ADHD games are available for the subject.   
Another point to mention about this prototype is that when played, the user goes through all 3 levels whether 
they lose all their lives or get a bad score. In an actual product, this would be changed with the option of 
unlocking the next level only when the user does not lose all lives in the level and reach a specified score. A 
setting usually found in normal games.  
7.3.4 Compatibility of the BCI system 
At the beginning of the design phase of the BCI system an idea arose. The idea was to connect BCI to a virtual 
reality head-mounted display and let the ADHD patients train their attention in the virtual reality classroom. 
This idea was dropped on the advice from one of the supervisors since it would result in a paradigm shift, 
which is outside the scope of this project. Still, the playground is compatible with virtual reality since the 
classroom is in 3D and fulfils the requirements to work in the virtual reality world. Oculus Rift, a virtual reality 
head-mounted display device, has already been tested and shown that VR can easily be implemented with 
this BCI system. This option is available in the application if a virtual reality classroom is needed in future 
experiments.

Chapter 8 – Conclusion 
The aim of this thesis was to develop a BCI system for attention training for ADHD patients. The system should 
be shaped as a 3D playground with games, to disguise the training exercises from the patient. The control 
should be achieved by attending targets resulting in feedback from the system.  
This is exactly what has been constructed by using the game engine Unity. The BCI system consists of a 3D 
training playground with 2D games running on the blackboard in a 3D classroom. While the game runs, 
distractions in the 3D classroom try to affect the attention of the patient. The distractions vary from the 
swaying and moving teachers, standing and running children, to flying paper planes. The goal of these 
distractions is to ruin the patient’s attention. On the other hand, the patient has to concentrate on the game 
and complete the level with a good score so the patient can continue to the next level. Colours used in the 
playground have emphasis on red-green and not on blue-yellow. The latter affects ADHD patients so they 
ignore elements with these colours. Since the target group are children with ADHD the setting is on purpose 
a realistic 3D classroom. Training in this environment will enable the children to carry the gained results into 
their real life and into the classroom effortlessly, a place where most ADHD children have problems with 
keeping attention.  
The game on the blackboard has its character controlled by the patient. This is done by attending flickering 
squares based on the SSVEP paradigm. Each square has its own action in the game. The reason for using 
SSVEP in the NF BCI system is that no literature has reported such a solution yet, even if SSVEP seems to be 
an obvious choice towards attention training for ADHD patients. Compared to the rest of the EEG features, 
the SSVEP classifier does not require training so everyone can use it without any concern.   
After the structural and functional tests, the errors have been eliminated from the software. The usability 
testing has exposed that the children in the same age group as the intended target accepts the 3D playground 
amiably. 
The results from the experiment show that the subjects are affected by change of environment and 
distractions in the 3D classroom rather than the game elements in the 2D game. The changes in environment 
and distractions make their accuracy fall, number of selections become higher and increase the time for 
finishing the test. The 2D game elements play a major role on the subjects’ selection time. Together with the 
environment change and distractions, they make the subjects hesitate before their selection. This results in 
longer selection time because the subjects attend to the environment, distractions, and game elements 
instead of the actual selective target.  
The results also show that the frequency of 9Hz has longer selection time compared to the frequency on 7Hz. 
This opens up for possible studies in selection times in SSVEP between higher vs lower frequencies. This thesis 
has also observed that the selection time do not decrease if the next target has the same frequency. This 
means that there is no after image in the head to help to select the same frequency faster. Instead the time 
increases when attending the same frequency. 
The average selection time for the BCI system is about 3.07 seconds with shortest time of 1.33 seconds and 
the longest time of 4.98 seconds. The average accuracy for the experiment lies about 92.26% with the lowest 
accuracy of 77.41% and the highest of 99.26%. These numbers show a good potential for the BCI system as 
a 3D training playground using SSVEP. The ITR for the BCI system is about 11.85bits/min.

8.1 Future development and improvements 
The BCI is fully functional and the experimental results are good but there are always room to expand and 
improve. This last section of the thesis covers the future development and experiment interests: 
 
A female ninja and more items in the game 
This was one of the wishes from the usability testers but because of lack of time these could not be 
implemented. The female character and items are created and already exist in the project folder for 
the application. The female can easily be implemented in the game by having a screen after the name 
input screen where the user decides on an avatar. The solution is flexible and more avatars can be 
included. The items and power ability need to be implemented in the code for the falling items from 
the spawners. 
 
 
More distractions in the 3D classroom 
By adding more distractions there will be more options to choose from the distraction list when a 
distraction has to appear in the 3D classroom. At this moment, the same 3D distractions appear at 
specific intervals but by adding more, the user will not be certain about the order of its appearance. 
This will make it harder for the user to become accustomed to the distractions because the user does 
not know which element is appearing. In real life, the distractions appear randomly in the classroom 
without any order. Adding more distractions and randomising the order will result in larger gains 
because of the realism of the 3D training playground. 
 
 
Sound effects to the distractions. 
There are no sound effects to the distractions because the focus is on visual attention training. Still 
the BCI system can easily expand and include auditory attention by adding sounds to the distractions. 
It will be harder for the user to attend the SSVEP controllers to control the ninja in the game when 
they are being distracted not only visually but also auditory. This could possibly be a harder level in 
the application.  
 
 
One or two more games for the 3D classroom 
Although the 2D game elements seem not to have a big effect on distracting the attention for the 
user, it should be interesting to test if there is any effect and how big it is when a different game runs 
inside the 3D classroom.  
 
 
Consumer EEG headset testing 
Experimenting by using amplifiers made for experiment is one thing but to make the BCI system a 
home-product for ADHD patients, it is important to validate if the results are consistent with a cheap 
consumer EEG headset. This will make the BCI system attractive for the families with ADHD children 
who are in great need of an inexpensive ADHD treatment without dangerous side effects.  
 
 
 
Wireless EEG headset testing 
Electrodes attached on the head have their cables connected to the amplifier. This causes concern 
to anyone using the BCI system about dropping the amplifier by pulling the cables. So far wireless 
==PAGE:103==
P a g e  | 91 
 
 
 
EEG headset from Neurosky and Emotiv exist but because only three electrodes are attached to the 
head, a cheaper wireless EEG headset with only three electrodes should be possible to produce and 
sell with the BCI system. The user would have fun with their attention training without any worry. 
  
 
Virtual Reality effects on subjects 
Since Oculus Rift headset is already compatible with the 3D training playground it is of interest to see 
how virtual reality affects the subjects. Virtual reality adds more realism into the 3D playground so it 
is possible that the performance will go down because the distractions are more realistic than before. 
The subjects can move their view and discover the whole classroom compared to the view that is 
presented to them on the monitor at present.  
 
 
Optimising the EEG signal processing procedure 
The foremost thing the subjects find bad is the reaction time of the ninja. This is also included under 
the suggestions for a better application. This factor is heavily affected by their attention on the SSVEP 
controllers for the ninja. Beside this matter, one adjustment could result in faster response. Because 
only CData and SData are tweaked in the algorithm, the same can be tried with the score. The 
selection of the class could be changed to the same class dominating in three consecutive iterations 
instead of the original four. Of course, this means tweaking CData and SData to a best fit again. If this 
is not enough then a new better algorithm should be designed and implemented. 
 
 
Testing on ADHD patients 
Although the BCI system is verified, it has not been on the target group: ADHD patients. As mentioned 
in the previous chapter under Source of Error, the testing is executed on healthy subjects. The author 
is very curious to know how well the BCI system fares with real 10-15 year old ADHD children, so 
much that the experiment procedure is already prepared for the experiment:   
 
Two groups with at least 25 ADHD children each: One is a control group while the other carry out the 
training. The experiment runs for a period of 5 weeks where the training group train attention via 
the BCI system 2-3 times a week. Both groups take an attention test before and after 5 weeks. Both 
groups takes a new attention test after 2 weeks and 7 weeks after the training. The attention tests 
are to analyse the change in attention from before the experiment. The last two attention tests are 
to assess the sustainability of the gained results.

Ideas for the game scenarios 
Introduction 
We had a meeting last week where we (Sadasivan, Helge, Mehdi and Abdulla) discussed some of the game 
ideas, I had come up with. Those ideas were found after looking through many games and their settings. 
The focus was on games where moves like right and left played a big role, and where other actions could be 
removed by changing the design and still be a good game. What I came up with and how we categorized 
are shown and explained below.  
 
I’m very interested in getting in your input or suggested changes to these ideas. If you have any ideas to 
other kinds of games, which would be good for ADHD children, you are welcome to suggest them. It would 
be nice to hear about them and discussing them with the rest. 
 
THE BAD IDEAS  
 
The Maze – Perspective 1 
 
 
I tried to draw it down on paper, and it is just as you said. A simple game where the user becomes bored 
very fast since there’s no real input except left and right. There are no enemies, no speeding up or down, or 
getting lost. The only penalty the user gets is when they hit the walls because they didn’t manage to avoid 
it or took the wrong turn. The user will get bored after some time and will not replay the game many times. 
The user will also see this as an exercise instead of a fun game, which is the opposite of what we want from 
them.  
 
==PAGE:122==
P a g e  | 110 
 
 
 
The Maze – Perspective 2 
 
This one is still a maze but with bonus and bombs on the road. The user is playing the game from first 
perspective instead from bird perspective (top view), and will turn left/right to take presents or avoid the 
bombs. The user will also turn around in the maze when there’s an entrance (the blue rectable on the right 
side over the TNT-bomb).  
 
Just like in the previous maze, there’s a minimap on the right top corner. This gamesetting is much alike 
Hugo, which you should know. Otherwise, here’s how it looks: 
http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u_r6XJi9EOE  
 
The problem is the same as before: The user gets lost and ends up confused about how to get out of the 
hole/dead-end. While the bonus and bombs will help a bit on their motivation to carry on, the user will 
hate the game in the long run because they can’t find around the maze. The game will become tiresome 
and end up as an exercise. How much the minimap will help on finding back on the correct path is 
unknown.

THE GOOD IDEAS  
 
Avoid/catch the objects 
 
This game is quite simple. Objects fall from the sky and the user either avoids the stones or catches the 
fruit. The user gets points for avoiding/catching the objects and the idea is to see how many they can avoid 
or catch by moving left and right. It is a game style which makes good use of moving only to left and right. It 
is fun and kills time with a simple but fun game and bypass the thinking of playing a whole scenario or 
seeing it as an exercise, since “it’s just a small game”.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Note:  
All of the games will have stuff like points, highscore and time ranking to make it more fun and competitive 
with yourself and others. This will help motivate the user to do better the next time they try it and not wait 
too long to try the game again. 
==PAGE:124==
P a g e  | 112 
 
 
 
Jumping Game 
 
This game is a bit special as you have the user jumping from one platform to another. The user has to avoid 
the enemies (the bird, yes it’s a bird!) and gain points by getting presents and getting fast to the goal. At 
first I had decided to let the user’s avatar jump forward automatically based on a time-interval, where the 
user had to decide if it was supposed to be to the right or to the left. If the user didn’t manage to decide on 
left or right then the avatar would move forward.  
 
Mehdi suggested a change to setting since it posed a problem with the users. What if the user needed less 
or more time for concentration on their left/right move? The avatar would either take too much or little 
time before moving. This would result in boredom and annoyance for the user. If they did chose their 
left/right move fast then there would be long waiting time before the next move, if they didn’t get enough 
time to decide, their avatar would die too fast and too many times.  
 
Mehdi suggested that instead of having a time interval it would be better to insert a threshold so when the 
user reached the threshold for left/right the avatar would move in that direction. If they use didn’t  
concentrate enough so the signal became mixed the avatar would move forward.  
 
This game setting is based on Super Oswald, which you should also know as with Hugo. Otherwise, here’s a 
nice video of the whole gameplay: http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QXWubVgbtg0

High Concept 
One day our ninja couple finds out they are out of fruits at home. Male Ninja is the one at fault because he 
has been slicing fruits for the came, Fruit Ninja. Female Ninja is hungry for some fruits so she orders Male 
Ninja to go out and catch some. Male Ninja, fearing for his life if he says no, decides it is time to leave the 
sword at home and instead wear their fruit catching straw-hat. Before leaving, he receives a warning from 
the lovely yet angry Female Ninja: “From the grand tree, the fruit falls but so does the fruit eating worm. 
Beware of the stones out to kill you, Male Ninja.” Fortunately, Male Ninja is quick on his feet while moving 
right and left so he knows he can escape the dangers and catch sweet fruits for his beloved Female Ninja.  
Gameplay 
Overview 
Male ninja has to move to right and left to catch Happy Apples and Happy Pears until the time limit is up.  
Enemies include Confused Worms and Angry Stones. Male Ninja can defeat none of the enemies but can 
avoid or deflect them.  
Player objective 
Primary Objective: Survive to the end of the time limit and catch as many fruits as possible. 




 
Avoid worms and stones

Camera 
Fruit Catcher uses an orthographic view camera which details the elevation of the environment and profile 
the characters. The camera stands still but shows the whole scene where the characters appears. Male Ninja 
does not leave the camera view.

Happy Apples and Happy Pears are both fruits that loves to fall from the trees in Ninja Fruit Village when 
they know someone is below them. Although they know they could hit the ground and rot, they hope that 
someone will catch them and eat them. This is the only way to reach Fruit Paradise that does not exist in 
Ninja Fruit Village where they suffer on the trees. When Male Ninja catches a Happy Apple, he receives 150 
points but when the infrequent Happy Pear falls, Male Ninja can receive 350 points if he can catch it. 
 
 
 
 
Happy Apples nad Happy Pears looks like apples and pears with cute faces on them. They need to be caught 
but can be deflected if Male Ninja is too slow in catching them. They are the smallest characters/items in the 
game.

BCI system manual 
 
 
Before beginning, make sure that the BCI system has the layout as the figure above. The extra screen can be 
included if there is a need to track the progress in MATLAB.  
 
The BCI system uses brain signals to control the main character in the application’s 2D game. To communicate 
between brain and computer, 3 electrodes are placed on the head. The 3 electrodes are placed according to 
the figure above. 
The red circle marks the recording channel, the yellow circle marks the ground electrode and the blue circles 
marks the reference electrodes. Use only one of the blue circles.   
Before placing the electrodes, clean the areas with scrubbing soap and skin cleansing swabs. Place the 
electrodes on the head with conductive gel.  
Afterwards load “ADHDapplication.m” into MATLAB and run it. Keep the impedance under 10kΩ to get a 
clear signal, preferably under 5kΩ. If the impedance is high, clean the electrode placement areas again.  
Make sure the monitor resolution is on maximum for brightness and contrast, and that the refresh rate is set 
to 120Hz. Centre the test subject or yourself in the middle of the monitor with a distance of about 60cm. 
==PAGE:134==
P a g e  | 122 
 
 
 
Double-click on “SuperheroAcademy.exe” to start the application. This window below will pop up. 
 
Chose the preferred settings and click on “Play!”. In the application, click on “Start” to begin. 
Use the keyboard and the mouse to navigate around in the application. The keyboard is used to type in 
username while the mouse is used to click on the blinking “Click to Continue“ button. The button only works 
when it is visible. If no username is typed then a random username is chosen.   
Read, type and click until the first level is reached. It will look like the image below. 
 
The character, Male Ninja, is controlled with the same procedure in all levels. The black and white squares in 
the figure, flicker between white and black. By attending the left or right square, Male Ninja can be moved 
to left or right. This can be used to avoid stones and worms, and catch apples as pears in the level. The 
concept is to attend the flickering squares to attain a high score and avoid getting disturbed by the enemies 
in the level and the distractions in the 3D classroom levels.
