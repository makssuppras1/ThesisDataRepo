Abstract
Research of green facades in the recent decades is mainly focused on the benefits it has on the
environment, specifically in dense cities where sustainability problems are more pronounced.
Implementing green facades in urban areas is shown to have several beneficial impacts on sur-
rounding areas and the environment. However, fire safety of green facades is not legislated by
the government in most countries as there is limited research on this specific topic. In vernacular,
it is said that green facades are not prone to fire hazards if they are kept properly irrigated and
always maintained, although these statements lack proper research to support the allegations.
This thesis aims to investigate the potential fire hazards of green facades and the tendency of
greater ignition and flame spread properties of dry vegetation on facades. To answer the main
problem, two laboratory experiments were conducted in the DTU Firelab with simultaneous liter-
ature research. 21 individual climbing plants, two different plant species, three different moisture
levels and heat fluxes were prepared for the experiments. The first experiment tests the ignition
properties of the plants by using a Heat-Transfer Rate Inducing System (H-TRIS). The second
experiment investigates flame spread properties by exploring the time it takes for the flame to
spread over a given distance.
The findings from the experiments indicate that there can be a tendency of greater risk of fire
hazard when the plants on a facade are too dry. This applies to both ignition and flame spread
properties. The results from the ignition experiments revealed that in some of the cases if a dry
plant is exposed to medium or high heat flux, which in this case were 32 kW/m2 and 45 kW/m2,
the plant can auto-ignite in a matter of few seconds. However, results showed that a fresh plant
with low heat flux would not likely ignite during a period of one hour. The main findings from the
experiment of ignition are that there is a shorter time to ignition when the moisture level is lower
and heat flux is higher. The main finding from the experiment with flame spread is that the flame
could spread fast after igniting if the plant is noticeably dry. There is, however, a possibility that
the flame would not spread fast enough for it to be a hazard when the plant is fresh.
Ultimately, the findings from this study should shed light on whether legislation of green facades
should be carried out with a view to fire safety. It should further be investigated in order not only
to investigate tendencies but also to give clearer answers as to whether green facades are a fire
hazard, for example for tall buildings. For us to move towards a more sustainable future, green
facades are a great addition to implement in urban areas. It is therefore important to conduct
further studies on the fire safety of green facades to ensure safety, protect life and the well being
of humans.

Introduction
Today, people and corporations take more and more responsibility and action for challenges re-
lated to global warming and climate change. Given that the building industry is responsible for
almost 40% of the global emissions, architects have a responsibility of meeting the sustainable
needs of the future. Sustainable architecture includes responding to environmental problems
and reduce environmental impacts such as exhaustion of natural resources, CO2-emissions, and
emission of other greenhouse gases [1].
Green facades on buildings have several properties contributing to a better environment and
might be the future of enlarged cities and urban spaces. Today, there are many places around
the world where problems associated with providing green space in urban spaces and cities are
occurring. The reason for wanting green areas is, among other reasons, to convert carbon diox-
ide that occurs due to traffic and general overheating to fresher air in the form of carbon hydrates
and oxygen [1]. However, green facades might have less advantageous properties in terms of fire
safety. An example of this is enhanced vertical and horizontal spread of fire due to poor main-
tenance of the vegetation or other factors such as distances to windows and other buildings.
The advantages of green facades might be ignored in the design phase of a building, due to lack-
ing knowledge of fire-safe solutions of green facades among other reasons. This master’s thesis
aims to investigate fire properties associated with green facades on buildings. This includes both
theoretical research on the topic and an associated laboratory experiment.
Firstly, the theoretical research will include an investigation of vegetation on facades in general.
Secondly, it will investigate the research of fire testing of green facades. Lastly, the experimental
part will be carried out by performing fire tests. The purpose of the fire tests is to obtain data that
can help to investigate the problem of the thesis. The fire tests will be performed as a medium-
scaled test of a green facade. As a result, it should be possible to retrieve relevant data such as
time to ignition and flame spread velocity. The purpose of the thesis is to investigate how green
facades affect the fire safety of a building. Based on these results, an insight of how to implement
green facades in a ”good” fire safety way will be presented, in order to tackle fire safety hazards
related to green facades.

1.1
Green facades in architecture
Green facades and living walls are both terms used in architecture to describe all types of vegeta-
tion used on facades of buildings and structures. Vegetation on facades has been used around
the world for several centuries. New technologies and systems have been utilized in the latest
years to adapt green facades to modern urban life [2]. Green facades are used both on existing
buildings and new buildings and have endless opportunities in terms of form, placement and plant
types. Despite the opportunities and benefits it holds, there is still some skepticism associated
with implementing green facades on buildings. These are being connected to the disadvantages
with having plants, like extra costs and maintenance, increased amount of insects in the building,
leaf falls, fire safety and damage on the facade [1]. There may be some skepticism about green
facades, but in many cases the positive will outweigh the negative. Not all cases of green facades
are the same, and it can also be pointed out that the negative sides do not apply to all cases. For
example, damage on facades because of vegetation has earlier been investigated, showing that
this is not exactly the case. Except when the wall already is in bad condition before implementing
a green facade [3].
1.1.1
History of green facades
Vegetation in architecture has been used around the world for centuries to either meet practical
needs, but also to give an aesthetic expression. Vertical gardens, more specifically vines, had
a sensible function on buildings already 2000 years ago in southern Europe. Back then it was
practical because of the cooling characteristics and for shading the sun. History further indicates
that it was used in Central Europe about 500 years ago mostly for the same reasons, but also for
an aesthetic expression [2].
This may show that vertical planting is still aesthetically rewarding for the people around. Vertical
planting also continues to contribute with direct benefits to the building itself, such as sun protec-
tion and protection of the facade underneath. What differs most from the historical perspective
and which is relevant today is how vertical planting contributes to ecological diversity in the cities,
purifies the air and can give people a closer experience with nature, even in dense urban areas
[2]. The technology around vertical planting has thus been developed and a market has emerged
of suppliers who produce vertical planting systems.
The two main types of vertical planting are green facades and living walls. While green facades
are free-standing vegetation on the outside of the facade, living walls are finished components of
vegetation that grow in the wall [4]. Figure 1.1 shows some examples of vertical planting. The two
images on the top present examples of green facades and the two on the bottom show examples
of living walls. This is explained more clearly in the next chapter. Both types of vertical planting
are frequently used, but there is one thing in particular that distinguishes these two types that
is relevant for this thesis. It is that living walls are often categorized as a construction element
in a construction perspective, and can thus be classified and tested in a laboratory by suitable

professionals. Then it will be classified in a fire class using Eurocode, and can thus be used on
buildings with an understanding that the component has a low risk of fire hazard. With green

1.1.2
Sustainability
According to the United Nations Sustainable Development goal number 11 which deals with con-
cerns regarding sustainable cities and communities, 9 out of 10 people around the world living in
urban surroundings breathe air that is not met by the air quality guidelines of the World Health Or-
ganization. Meanwhile, it is stated that some of the most pressing challenges in cities around the
world are the levels of urban energy consumption and pollution of the air [6]. Even though green
facades are not a new concept, rather the opposite, research in the recent decades has been car-
ried out on the benefits of green facades on the environment, and not least in dense cities where
sustainability problems are more pronounced. Implementing green facades in urban areas has

==PAGE:14==
demonstrated several beneficial impacts on surrounding areas and the environment.
According to a study by S.M.Sheweka [7], it is stated that facade greening can contribute to bet-
ter air in urban surroundings. It is also mentioned that besides green facades providing visual
comfort in their surroundings and lowering the urban heat island effect, green facades can con-
tribute to lower the temperature on a facade. Lowering the temperature can result in a reduced
power load to for example air-conditioning and therefore less electricity is consumed. As a gen-
eral conclusion to the study, it is stated that facade greening can be used as a desirable tool for
passive thermal control to buildings. This is mainly in form of thermal insulation, solar radiation,
evaporate cooling and wind on the building [7].
Bosco Verticale, a two-tower residential building in the city of Milan, Italy, represents a pioneering
representation of facade vegetation along its respective 85m and 117m tall towers. The buildings
contain around 20,000 vegetation specimens around both towers with advanced maintenance,
safety and irrigation systems. The architecture of Bosco Verticale works as an example to repre-
sent the sustainable benefits of green facades. A study of the building has shown that the green
facade contributes to reduced energy consumption because of the insulating properties of the
vegetation against the outside humid temperatures and shading from the sun. During summer
the vegetation is supposedly contributing to a decrease of 3◦C inside the residential apartments
and conversely keeping the heat inside during cold winters. The study suggests that the yearly
energy consumption of the building is reduced by 7.5% as a result of the green facade [8]. In
addition to this, the building acts as a contribution to decreasing pollution in Milan by absorbing
pollution particles, noise and carbon dioxide. The vegetation also promotes biodiversity in the
city by creating a habitat for animals and insects. The urban heat island effect describes the phe-
nomenon that cities in general can become hotter than areas outside the city. This is because
masses absorb the radiation of the sun, like asphalt roads and concrete constructions. Bosco

1.2
Fire safety of green facades
All parts, systems and materials in a construction have standards to carefully categorize ele-
ments to ensure their characteristics during a fire to ensure stability and endurance. Standards
and categorization of green facades are not as widespread, although they are technically part
of the facade solution. As later elaborated in this thesis, there are generally two types of green
facades, where one can be climbing plants that grow on the outside of an already laid facade,
or a green facade that is integrated into a wall component. In the latter solution, it is common
for the manufacturer to perform a specific analysis of the component with respect to fire safety,
performed by professionals in a laboratory. However, this is not the case with all manufacturers
around the world. For the first-mentioned solution, there is little information on how it is assessed
by fire engineers in a project whether fire safety is taken care of when implementing this solution.
The United Kingdom is one of few countries which has issued guidelines for fire safety of green
facades. The United Kingdoms’ Department for Communities and Local Government (DCLG) has
issued the document: ”Fire Performance of Green Roofs and Walls” and points out as following
[11]: ”It has been suggested that green roofs and walls may constitute a fire hazard. In relation
to fire performance, the general consensus is that as long as the green roof or wall is kept moist
(which is the normal case in order to keep the roof alive) it is likely to be very resistant to igni-
tion. However, concern has been raised that if the green roof or wall dries out (such as might
happen in a drought if no irrigation is provided) then they might present more of a fire risk. There
has historically been a limited amount of fire testing of green roofs (carried out in Germany) but

significant fire testing of green wall systems”. This excerpt underlines the perception most peo-
ple have about fire safety in green facades. And thus promotes the importance of investigating
tendencies of the fire hazard in dry vegetation.
1.3
Legislation and regulations
Fire legislation has been developed as a foundation for fire safety. It works on the basis that a
building must be functional during a fire for a given period. A building should be functional in the
amount of time it takes for people inside the building to fully evacuate. The requirements in the
fire legislation are thus reflected in the fact that the load-bearing capacity of the building must be
able to last over a given period and that for example escape routes, smoke spread and fire spread
are taken into account when designing the building. The fire legislation was previously based on
direct rules in a building that had to be met, such as specific distances in corridors. This has
recently been changed, to be able to develop architecture with more freedom in design. Therefore,
performance codes are used today, which are to ensure that the building or construction element
fulfills the desired function in a fire context. With performance codes, it is possible to assess the
fire safety of each individual building or construction element [12].
In regards to green facades, it is relevant to look at the legislation and regulations for building
components. Even though green facades is not characterized as a construction element in the
building perspective, it is, however, practically a part of the facade and construction of a building.
1.3.1
Danish and Norwegian building legislation
The Danish building legislation, bygningsreglementet (BR18), indicates in §87 about materials,
structures and building components that [13]: ”Materials, structures and building components
that contribute to fire safety must be used and executed taking into account their fire properties
such as heat generation, flame spread, smoke production, production of burning droplets and
particles, the fallout of parts as well as fire resistance and load-bearing capacity.”. According to
the pre-accepted solutions for buildings in Denmark, the requirements that are linked to fire safety
on facades are variable dependent on, among other things, distance to other buildings, and the
height of the building. But as a general rule, all external facades must be made with cladding with
at least class K110/B-s1, d0 [13].

cue shall be taken into account.”, and ”(2) Materials and products shall have properties that do not
make unacceptable contributions to the development of fire. Emphasis will be placed on the pos-
sibility of ignition, the speed of heat dissipation, smoke production, the development of burning

1.5
Project objective
A literature study with a focus on the fire safety of green facades shows a lack of research into
this specific topic. It is unclear if the lack of knowledge about the fire safety of green facades is
preventing the usage of them. However, adding new research on the topic will be advantageous
for all the stakeholders. Relevant studies underline the importance of keeping the plants irrigated
at all times and not letting them dry out to keep them fire-safe. The project objective of this thesis
is therefore to investigate potential fire hazards of green facades and the tendency of greater
ignition and flame spread properties of drier vegetation on facades.
To answer the main problem, two laboratory experiments were conducted with simultaneous
literature research. 21 individual plants, two different plant types and three different moisture
levels of those were prepared for the experiments. All the plants would be tested against ignition
properties and flame spread properties. The plants would be tested in various calibrations and
moisture levels, to cover as many combinations and scenarios as possible. the moisture levels of
the plants were conducted by drying them out over a period of 14 days. The goal of drying them
out was to have three obviously different states of moisture levels. The first experiment was
executed by using a Heat-Transfer Rate Inducing System (H-TRIS), which is a system developed
by Cristian Maluk [21]. Calibrations for those tests were based on heat fluxes found by Jesper
Antonius Wolters [22], of respectively 22kW/m2, 32kW/m2 and 45kW/m2. 17 plants were tested
with the H-TRIS. The second experiment was a flame spread test, where 4 different plants were
tested due to flame spread properties, representing three different moisture levels. 6 tests were
pursued in total for the flame spread experiment. The literature research focuses on investigating
the fire safety of green facades in general, opting to investigate how a green facade can have
minimal hazards without contributing to increased flammable material and unnecessary spread
of fire.

Ignition
Ignition and flame spread are both important factors to investigate in terms of fire behavior in
materials. However, for plants, the theoretical investigation is not necessarily entirely correct to a
real-life scenario. This is because the plants are living objects with different properties, moisture
levels, sizes, forms and masses. However, theories for ignition and flame spread are investi-
gated with as accurate an approach as possible, with regard to the fact that there may be certain
differences in reality.
The theoretical Ignition of a solid material can originate from either a separate ignition, called
piloted ignition, or from autoignition. The autoignition arises from the material creating a chem-
ical process within the fuel which leads to enough energy to heat the fuel. The occurred fuel
must reach the desired energy level until it gasifies. For the ignition to happen the gasified fuel
needs a high enough temperature. The temperature of the surface of the material is an important
specification to map out the ignition [12]. As stated by Jaskolowski, the process of autoignition
have greater prospects of developing into a fire than with piloted ignition. Accordingly, during
Jaskolowski’s investigation, the time to ignition difference for autoignition and piloted ignition
were insignificant around a heat flux of 50 kW/m2 [26]. When finding the theoretical ignition for a
material, one must divide it into either a thin or thick material. For this project, the ignition focuses
on the autoignition of thin materials.

2.2.1
Heat-Transfer Rate Inducing System
H-TRIS is a fire testing method that monitors a given time-history of incident radiant heat flux at
a test specimen’s exposed surface to directly regulate the thermal boundary conditions imposed
on it [21]. This type of fire test is typically used on structural materials. However, in this thesis,
green facades are identified as a structural element as it is a part of a facade solution. The H-
TRIS was developed to find solutions to concerns about issues associated with fire testing of
temperature instead of heat flux. From a historical perspective, testing of fire safety on struc-
tures and construction materials has mainly focused on fire test standards. What is tested to the
greatest extent is whether a construction material can survive and endure during compartment
fire. Whether a material can survive a fire is based on a critical value given for the specific test.
In many cases, more traditional methods of testing the fire safety of construction materials are
sufficient. In the world of architecture, it should be possible to combine artistic freedom with the
security of materials. This has created room for quantitative test methods alongside standards.
In such quantitative test methods, it must be measured whether the behavior of a material or a
structure is acceptable according to given performance criteria. In general, what the H-TRIS does
is allow the test specimen to be controlled by direct and independent thermal boundary condi-
tions. The exposed surface of the specimen is exposed to a controlled and specified time-history
of incident radiant heat flux [21].

2.3
Flame spread
In general terms, flame spread is a process where the perimeter of the fire grows [12]. In most
cases, a natural fire occurs when it involves diffusion flames that spread over a surface of a
combustible material. The complex interaction between transport and chemical processes that
occurs in the vicinity of the boundary separating both the regions that are burning and not burning
results in a fire that is spreading. In other words, the gasified region has to expand from the
ignition point before a flame spread can occur. For a flame to spread, there is specifically one
primary condition to be met. This is that the surface of the combustible material must have
sufficient heat that can transfer from the burning region of the fuel to the unburnt region of the
material to cause the fuel to pyrolyze [28]. The fuel is based on the gas and air mixture above the
surface of the material.
In order to be able to map out the flame spread in a material, it is important to take various factors
into account. Two factors in particular are important. Both gravity and wind help to influence how
and how quickly a flame can spread. Flame spread can be divided into four different categories,
which correspond to different ways in which a flame spreads. The most natural spread of flames
is caused by flow from the fire’s own buoyancy. Flow can also be caused by natural wind con-
ditions. Both the natural flow and wind can enhance the flame spread, which causes the flame
spread to be wind-aided. It can also have the opposite effect and slow down the spread, which
gives it an opposed flow flame spread [12]. Figure 2.9 show the four different scenarios of flame
spread with various factors.

2.3.1
Downward flame spread
Downward flame spread can only occur under the right conditions. Downward flame spread, as
the name implies, means that the flames spread downwards. Downward flame spread occurs
only if the temperature of the material is above a specific critical value for the material being
tested. Downward and lateral flame spread is examined equally. Various standards have ready-
made data on different materials that show flammability and flame spread properties. The Amer-
ican standard ASTM E 1321 has data on this for various materials. To use an example that to a
certain extent can be compared to climbing plants is plywood. However, plywood is examined
as a thick material. The graph in Figure 2.10 below shows the lateral flame spread for plywood in
still air. The graph shows that the lowest possible temperature for flame spread is approximately
120◦C. This means that if the temperature is lower than this, it will not be able to cause a flame
spread. This temperature is called Ts. If the temperature is higher than Ts,min it will lead to flame
spread. This spread means that the higher the temperature on the surface of the material, the
higher the velocity. In reality during a fire, the surface temperature will increase by means of ra-
diation and convection. Next situation that happens is that the flame will heat the surface more
until it reaches the ignition temperature. In the example below, this is approximately 390◦C.
When looking into this example, one can see that when the plywood has a surface temperature,
Ts, of 120◦C, the flame spread happens at a spread velocity of 0.33 mm/s. Further, the graph
shows that the spread velocity is approximately 2.5 mm/s at 300◦C. Plywood is used as a lead
to know what Ts,min for the specimen used in the experiment in this thesis are.

2.5
Charring
Charring is a part of the fire behavior of organic materials. During exposure to higher tempera-
tures, most organic materials undergo charring. When an organic material is exposed to temper-
atures over 300◦C, it endures a thermal decomposition into a structure closely recognizable as
a graphite structure. However, it can also happen at lower temperatures [33]. Exposure of lower
temperatures between 95◦C and 120◦C over a longer period of time can convert organic material
into char and therefore cellulose goes non-reacted to fire [34].
When examining the phenomenon of charring of construction materials, it can in several cases
be sub-categorized as pyrolysis of the given material. In the construction context, charring is
seen as one of the fire’s damages to the load-bearing capacity of the material. However, in this
context, because vegetation on facades has no structural purpose, charring can be seen as an
advantage over the spread of flame. If the charring occurs before a flame occurs, the outermost
layer of the material can protect against new flames, and thus further flame spread.

Experiments with H-TRIS
The following section presents how the H-TRIS is set up for the experiment, and how the experi-

3.2.1
Experimental setup for H-TRIS
As elaborated in the theory chapter, an experiment with H-TRIS can determine ignition properties
for the specimen. The experiment is pursued to find data on the time until ignition at different heat
flux rates is for the respective specimen. Also, the temperature of ignition is found. In general,
the experiment is pursued by setting up the H-TRIS and specimen at a given distance, and then
watched and investigated until ignition occurs. Ignition is occurring when part of the specimen
develops a glow or flame. This can be either in the leaf or the stem of the plant. In some of the
experiments, the specimen might not ignite. In these cases, the experiment is run for one hour,
which resembles materials durability for one hour during a fire. All the experiments are recorded
to be able to rewatch the experiment.
The H-TRIS consists of two particular parts, the mechanical part, and thermal part, respectively
as shown in Figure 3.5. The thermal part is the movable gas burner frame, whilst the mechani-
cal part is the loading frame with the specimen. Firstly, the thermal part is placed under a large
ventilation hood in the laboratory and secured by locking its wheels. Then the mechanical part
is put in a sturdy position in front of the thermal part. The mechanical part is then loaded with

ready to be opened. To make sure that the system is connected to the gas outlet, an included
manometer is pushed. The next step is to turn on the machine with a gas flow of 50 percent. The
machine should be running for 5 minutes prior to test start, to make sure it is reaching the same
start temperature for every test.

Thermometer
The temperature near the plants is a significant factor during the experiment. A LT-lutron ther-

Test procedure
The experiment is pursued in a manner of methodical procedure and order. The tests are not
performed in the same order as they are categorized in the table before starting. This is because
the experiment has a limited amount of specimen that can be used in the tests. Instead, tests are

and lowest heat flux. Based on the result from that test, the highest moisture level and highest
heat flux were tested next, to see if the middle one were worth testing, or there is a high chance
it will get the same result. This procedure is done through all tests and choices regarding tests
are taken along the way.

3.3.1
Experimental setup for flame spread test
As earlier elaborated, the flame spread test is done to map out to which extent a flame spreads on

purely with observation. The experiment is recorded to grant a thorough observation. The flame
spread experiment is pursued with the specimen, a support rack, a lighter, timer, thermometer,
ruler and an oven. Two types of tests are done on the specimen. The first is an upward flame
spread and the second is a downward flame spread. A schematic illustration of the flame spread
experiment is presented in Figure 3.6.
Prior to the experiment, some preparations are done. Firstly, the specimen is cut from the stem
and soil with a scissor and held together with a metal plier. Then, the specimen is heated up in
an oven to reach Ts.min. However for a plant, the Ts.min is unknown and the specimen is heated
in an oven for 5 minutes at 120◦C to resemble a Ts.min for plywood, which is a reference material
in this case and elaborated in section section 2.3.1. The specimen is then placed directly in its
place on a support rack with a ruler behind. The next step is to find the temperature of the plant
right before the experiment starts. The specimen is then ignited with a lighter from the bottom
for the upward flame spread test, and from the top in the downward flame spread test. The flame
from the lighter is held until the specimen ignites. The time is started the second it ignites. Flame
spreads are observed visually, and data is extracted from the videotapes after the experiments.

4.4
Errors
Possible errors that occurred during the actual experiments have already been explained as a
comment on the individual experiment. Apart from this, there are several error factors that can
contribute to giving an incorrect result and must be taken into account when examining results
as a whole. Followed is a list of errors:
• When setting up the experiment with H-TRIS, the distance from the specimen to the heating
panel should be exact and precise. However, the distance from the specimen is based on
the average ”thickness” of the respective specimen, which can cause small variations in
distances for the tests. It is estimated that the variations are around 2 cm.
• None of the moisture levels in the plant is guaranteed. Apart from them being weighed and
categorized, there will always be a difference between the same categorized specimen.
This can cause a slight difference in results.
• Measuring the temperature is not constant. There might have been factors in the laboratory
giving variations in the measured temperature. This can be an open door, window etc. It is
estimated that the variations are approximately around 10-15◦C.
Otherwise, there are in general not executed enough tests on ignition and flame spread to quan-
titatively conclude exact results and indicative graphs. Because of this, the tests are only able to
give an insight into the tendencies of fire properties based on the results of the tests.

temperature of the specimen were raised beforehand.

shown in table 2.2, results from test 1 and test 2 suggests that in that case it would have been

not conducted enough tests to cover the classes of tests 3-6, and it is therefore not possible to

Both experiments of ignition and flame spread are closely related to each other. When comparing
the results from both experiments together, one is able to get an overview of the importance of
the state of the green facade in relation to their moisture level. The data contributes to a clearer
understanding of how the plants can react to fire depending on their state.

5
Conclusion
The purpose of the study in this thesis is to investigate how green facades can affect the fire
safety of a building. This is done by approaching the problem with two separate experiments,
respectively an investigation of time to ignition and flame spread properties of two different veg-
etation types. The two types of vegetation are Efeu and Boston Ivy, both popular specimens
used on facades in Scandinavia. The literature research indicated that green facades can be a
fire hazard if they become too dry. Therefore, the experimental investigation focused on the ten-
dencies of fire properties for plants at different moisture levels. Firstly, ignition properties were
investigated in the laboratory by using H-TRIS. A total of 17 tests, 3 moisture levels and 3 heat
flux calibrations for two different plant types were used in this exact experiment. Secondly, for
the flame spread experiment, 6 tests and two moisture levels for two different plant types were
part of the investigation.
In the investigation of time to ignition, a selection of the results showed that when the Efeu were
subjected to the lower heat flux of 22 kW/m2, and the plants had the highest moisture level M1,
equal to fresh, none of the plants ignited during a duration of one hour under the experiment.
However, the fresh Efeu ignited for the higher heat flux of 45 kW/m2 after respectively 13 and 24
seconds. The fresh Boston Ivy with moisture level M1, on the other hand, ignited after a longer
period of time. The shortest amount of time to ignition for Boston Ivy with the highest heat flux
was 49 seconds in comparison. Efeu and Boston Ivy are different kinds of species, with different
amounts of moisture and tissue. It is therefore not considerate to compare them, but they can be
a pointer to how diverse vegetation can act in a fire scenario. The Efeu with the medium moisture
level, considered partly dried, proved to give ambiguous results in terms of time to ignition for the
medium heat flux of 32 kW/m2. In one of the tests, the Efeu did not ignite, and in the other test,
the time to ignition was 26 seconds. In the tests with the highest heat flux, the time to ignition
was 13 and 5 seconds, which is the same and lower as the moisture level above. Lastly, the
driest moisture level of Efeu, also gave ambiguous results in the test with the medium heat flux.
However, this might have been because of an error as explained in Section 4.2.1. The results
showed that in this test, the time to ignition for Efeu was 9 seconds for the results without the
error. For the highest heat flux, the time to ignition was 5 seconds in both tests. The main findings
from the experiment with ignition, is that there was a shorter time to ignition when the moisture
level is lower and heat flux is higher.
From the investigation of flame spread properties of the plants, there were also ambiguous re-
sults. Only the plants with the lowest moisture level were able to ignite during these tests. There
was conducted both an upward and downward facing flame spread test. In the test with upward

==PAGE:72==
flame spread, the flame spread 225 mm in 2.18 seconds after ignition in the bottom of the plant.
However, in the downward flame spread test, it was noted that the flame did not move down-
wards, but only sideways and upwards. This corresponds with the findings in the report made
by Kalani C. Dahanayake et al [20]. The flame spread 35 mm upwards in 3.08 seconds before
extinct. On the other hand, either of the plants with medium or high moisture levels ignited, and
therefore it was not possible to map out flame spread properties in those cases. The main finding
from this experiment is that, it can be a rapid flame spread scenario if the plant is dry enough.
As an overall conclusion, the results from the investigation show that there can be a tendency of
greater risk of fire hazard when the plants on a facade are dry. This goes for both time to ignition
and flame spread properties of the dry plants. The results from ignition experiments show that in
some cases, if a dry plant is exposed to medium or high heat flux it can auto-ignite in a matter of
few seconds. It further shows, that the flame can spread relatively fast after ignited if the plant is
notably dry. There is a possibility that the flame would not spread noticeably enough for it to be a
hazard when the plant is fresh. However, it has not been gathered enough data to quantitatively
conclude exact results and indicative graphs on the fire hazards of green facades in this thesis.
The results are therefore only able to give an insight into the tendencies of fire properties based
on the results from the tests.

5.1
Perspective discussion
In an unlikely real-life scenario, green facades could be a hazard in terms of spreading the fire
to other places inside or outside the building. The energy stored in the plants on a facade is not
necessarily very high, but the flames can spread fast vertically. An imaginative scenario could be,
on a hot summer day, there is a residential building with 8 floors and a green facade. It has been
warm for a longer period of time and the plants have not been properly irrigated or maintained.
Then, a car battery caught fire in a parking lot right outside the building. This kind of fire can
produce a high heat flux, and can approximately be compared to the highest heat flux used in this
thesis of around 45 kW/m2. The vegetation ignites and spreads rapidly upwards of the facade.
Some of the windows are wide open because of the hot weather and suddenly, the flames can
spread into objects near the windows like for example curtains.
In the earlier studies, it was found that legislation of green facades is not widespread around
the world, though some countries have implemented fire regulations for green facades, like for
example the United Kingdom. The findings from this thesis could give a point to whether or not
a discussion around implementing legislation and regulations of green facades in Scandinavia
should happen. Even though the findings in this thesis would not give a good enough basis to
argue for creating legislation, it can give an insight into the importance of proper irrigation and
maintenance. As earlier elaborated in the thesis, almost everything that is part of the architec-
ture of a building already has some kind of material classification or is part of a regulation. The
hypothetical legislation of green facades should, however, not go at the expense of architectural
freedom but should be based on performance codes to ensure that they will not be a fire hazard
in addition to the materials and solutions already in the building.

5.2
Future studies
This thesis has focused on a relatively basic investigation of tendencies regarding fire proper-
ties of green facades. The findings give an insight into a field that is not previously thoroughly
investigated, which opens up for several opportunities regarding future studies.
Firstly, as this thesis only looked at fire properties like ignition and flame spread a more thorough
investigation of fire properties could be done. This could be done both to take a greater look
at what this thesis has already investigated, but also to be able to categorize green facades in
the materials classification systems. Further tests could be conducted in accordance with DS
EN 13501-1 classification, and focus on a more detailed reaction to fire test of combustibility,
smoke emission during combustion, and droplet rating. This thesis was originally supposed to
test the plant types in a bomb calorimeter to map out further fire properties of the plants, however,
some important parts for that experiment were missing in the laboratory. Testing with a bomb
calorimeter could give valuable results in an investigation of further looking into how vegetation
acts during a fire on a green facade.
Secondly, an investigation of how vegetation acts together with the facade material behind can
be pursued as a future study. Most green facades that are seen in Copenhagen have brick walls
behind as this is part of the architectural style in this city. An investigation on how much of a fire
hazard a green facade has in regards to the facade material behind can give valuable research
to this topic. For example, vegetation on a wooden facade and aluminum facade can have other
risks than vegetation on a brick facade.
Lastly, the theoretical calculation of ignition and flame spread pursued in this thesis did not give
harmonious results, as expected. In fact, they both deviated a great amount from the results in
the experiments. For vegetation, it is obvious that equations need to be divided into thin and thick
objects, as is done for traditional building materials. This can shed light on, whether or not the
theoretical formulas for ignition and flame spread are designed to be used for organic or living
materials where for example moisture levels and shapes deviate. Therefore, an investigation of
the compatibility of these formulas with organic or living materials could give expanded knowl-
edge and research for the future.
