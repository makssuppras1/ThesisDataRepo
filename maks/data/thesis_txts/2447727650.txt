by Ameya JOSHI
As a renewable source of energy and sometimes carbon neutral form of energy, bio-
gas is the future of bio based renewable fuels. About 40% of Biogas by volume is
Methane. Hence, it is economical and energy efﬁcient to enrich biogas and turn it into
biomethane. This process of conversion of biogas to biomethane requires the removal
of an important impurity in biogas called CO2. Various amine based solvents are in use
for this absorption and enrichment. In this work the most common amine employed in
this industry has been taken as a base case. To make a continuous absorption process,
it is vital to know the equilibrium thermodynamics of the process. This is achieved
by experimental determination of equilibrium solubility of CO2 in solvents. In the
initial part of this project, the experimental set-up and method was validated against
the published literature. Further, the same equilibrium thermodynamics are tested for
same solvent solution containing an additive.

Project Description
Project title: Experimental analysis of CO2 vapour liquid equilibrium on solvent for
biogas upgradation
Period: 19 November 2018 - 23 April 2019
Background
In the recent years of the current century, demand for renewable or alternative fuel has
increased partly because of rising fossil fuel prices and perceived depletion of fossil
fuel reserves. In addition, the management of organic waste has led to an attractive
form of fuel called as Biogas. Biogas is nothing but a byproduct of microbial decay
of organic waste which predominantly contains methane, carbon dioxide, and small
quantities of hydrogen sulphide. To increase caloriﬁc value of this product, carbon
dioxide needs to be removed. This puriﬁcation process is called biogass upgradation.
Removal of carbon dioxide is typically carried out by absorbing it in a suitable solvents
which are mostly amines and various blends of amines. To use this method, an accurate
understanding of the carbon dioxide - amine system behaviour is very important.
Objectives
This project aims to design an experiment and validate an existing vapor liquid equi-
librium (VLE) setup between carbon dioxide and amine. This is done by establishing
it against the historically reported data in literature. Further, effect of certain solvent
additives called as vapor reducing agents (VRA) on the VLE of carbon dioxide over the
amine will be assessed. VRAs reduce the solvent vapour pressure which reduces the
energy required for recovery during solvent regeneration in a continuous operating
plant.
Contents
• A literature review focused on VLE of carbon dioxide - amine system
• Testing and validation of experimental set-up and procedure against literature
data
• Perform VLE experiments with additives in the solvent and compare the data
without additives
• Reﬂect on the similarities or differences between the VLE data of with and without
additives
Timeline
Literature review – 20 December 2018
Testing and validation – 30 January 2019
VRA additive experiment results – 5 March 2019
Modeling and discussion – 31 March 2019
Reporting deadline – 15 April 2019

Chapter 1
Introduction
Overview
Biogas is a renewable form of fuel produced from microbial process of anaerobic diges-
tion of organic waste turning it into methane, carbon dioxide, and other byproducts.
Because of its use in degradation of waste and turning it into useful heat source, biogas
production plants have sprung up across the globe and are gaining popularity by the
day. Ecological purpose of making biogas is that it uses the waste organic matter to
make fuel with a good heating value. Use of farm refuse and dry gas in the biogas
plant is an effective carbon neutral choice in the long run as it avoids the combustion of
petroleum products.
The combustible component of biogas is mainly Methane with carbon dioxide being
one of the major impurity of the product outlet from such a digestor. The volumetric
caloriﬁc value (i.e. energy per unit volume) is less and thus treatment or enriching is
required to strip the carbon dioxide and other gases such as hydrogen sulphide from
the product stream. For stripping carbon dioxide various methods are available. The
simplest idea is to wash the product gas stream with water in an absorber column so
that it dissolves in water and methane content of the biogas is increased.
Organic solvents such as amines are popular for their higher carbon dioxide sol-
ubility. The industrial choice ultimately rests on the economics and optimization.
Higher solubility entails higher energy requirements of solvent regeneration for re-
cycle and lower solubility increases the absorber design costs and capital. There are
other methods for carbon dioxide stripping such as pressure swing adsorption, gas
chromatography, membrane separation technology, cryogenic separation, and chem-
ical hydrogenation of carbon dioxide. Some of these processes will be discussed in
Chapter 2.
Present work
Carbon dioxide scrubbing from the product stream of biogas plant essentially involves
absorption columns. Engineering design of such a column requires vapor-liquid
equilibrium (VLE) of carbon dioxide in the scrubbing solvents. In this work, amines
will be studied as scrubbing solvents. The previously reported VLE data in the literature
is used initially for validating the experimental method used in this project. Thereafter,

Chapter 2

2.1
Biogas
2.1.1
What is it?
Biogas is produced in anaerobic digestion units as a result of biological degradation of
farm waste, household waste and refuse, and industrial organic waste by microbes in
several stages [1]. Main components of biogas are CH4 at around 60 to 70% and CO2 at
around 30 to 40% depending upon the type of organic substrate used and the rest are
H2O, O2 and traces of hydrogen sulﬁde H2S [2].
After the puriﬁcation of this gas by removing the non combustible components, the
almost pure product CH4 is biomethane. Although it has been reported [3] that biogas
of at least 45% of CH4 is ﬂammable, the caloriﬁc value is very low for a direct application
as a fuel supplement. The treatment on biogas to turn it into biomethane is also called
as upgradation of biogas. Pure biomethane can be directly injected into the natural gas
grid or can be used in heat and electricity cogeneration units. It has been predicted [2]
that by the year 2020, biomethane produced from organic feedstock can sufﬁcient to
supply the present demand of natural gas as a fuel. Moreover, the leftover digestate
biomass slurry can be used as an excellent organic fertilizer in agriculture. This also
contributes to economy and ecology by reducing use of artiﬁcial chemical fertilizers [4–
7]. For these reasons, biogas holds a bright prospect in pursuit of generating sustainable
and renewable energy source which can simultaneously handle organic waste and
generate fuel. From the point of view of economics, the most important part of this
process is the biogas upgradation and the present project seeks to handle issues related
to it.
2.1.2
Anaerobic digestion
Transformation of feedstock organic waste to biogas takes place in major four steps.
All these steps require strictly anaerobic conditions i.e. non-presence of any oxygen.
These are called Hydrolysis, Acidogenesis, Acetogenesis, and Methanogenesis. The
Hydrolysis step consists of the breakdown of the outer layers of the feedstock. Outer
layers are the cell walls of the dead organic matter which are broken down mainly by
acid mediated hydrolysis reaction. After this step is completed, the organic susbstrate
is completely exposed to the fermentative bacteria which can then take over the next
==PAGE:26==

processes. The products of these fermentation bacteria are fatty acid equivalents of the
biomass and other acids. Copious amounts of CO2 is liberated from the fermenting
biomass at this stage. This second step is called as Acidogenesis because acids are
produced in this step. These acids get further oxidized to acetates in the next step in the
chain called as Acetogenesis and liberate H2 as a result. After sufﬁcient H2 and CO2
is built up in the chamber the methanogenic bacteria take over and begin ﬁxing both
the compounds thereby liberating CH4 and CO2. This step of generation of methane is
termed as Methanogenesis [8].
2.1.3
Production scenario in Europe
Europe is currently the world leader in biogas production largely due to the joint
support and subsidy systems and schemes in place under the European Union with
almost 62% of global biogas production [1]. In the recent past, biogas with the energy
equivalent of 92 PJ, 167 PJ, 357 PJ, and 654 PJ for the years 2000, 2005, 2010, and 2015
respectively was produced in Europe [9]. Danish biogas sector too has grown rapidly
alongside the rest of Europe due to various public subsidies from the Government.
Most of the biogas units in Denmark utilize the manure and waste at farms while some
are located near cities which utilize waste water or landﬁll sites. According to the
Danish Ministry of Energy, the year 2018 saw biogas production of about 13 PJ which is
expected to grow to 15 PJ by 2020 [10].
2.2
CO2 capture
As mentioned earlier, raw biogas contains contains CO2 and H2S as main impurities
besides the useful CH4. Removal of these impurities is called as biogas upgradation.
The present study is focused on the capture of CO2 from the raw biogas stream and
produce enriched biomethane as a ﬁnished product. Two broad categories of the pro-
cesses are the non-solvent based processes and the solvent based absorption processes.
The non-solvent based processes consist of either direct physical or chemical reaction
separation of CO2. While the solvent based process use a solvent to separate CO2 from
the gas stream.
2.2.1
Non-solvent based processes
Adsorption Processes
Adsorption is the process in which gases adhere or stick to the solid surfaces due to
inter-molecular forces of attraction. For a given solid, different gases get adhered to
varying degree due to the speciﬁc molecular structures. In this way a mixture of gases
can be separated from mixture with the help of varying conditions such as temperature,
partial pressure of the gas components [11]. CO2 separation from a gas stream can be
achieved by two ways. First is the Pressure swing adsorption in which the gas stream
is fed into an adsorption unit at a high pressure which forces the CO2 to adhere to solid.
After a steady state at the outlet is achieved, the inlet to the unit is closed and CO2 is

ﬂushed out with an inert gas. The counterpart of this is Temperature swing adsorption
in which a similar process is involved but with reduced temperature for adsorption
phase and an elevated temperature for ﬂushing the adsorbed CO2 [12].
Cryogenic Processes
As the name of the process suggests, this involves cryogenic refrigeration based sepa-
ration of CO2 from gas stream. Generally, separation of CO2 from low concentration
ﬂue gas streams are feasible for this process as refrigeration is very expensive. Overall,
this process involves several stages of compression and refrigeration cycles until CO2
is obtained either in a liquid or solid state. The drawbacks of this process are that it is
costly for use in precombustion fuel upgradation and the other is water-ice clogging
in the system. Therefore, extensive drying of the gas stream needs to be carried out
beforehand [11].
Calcium looping capture
Calcium looping process basically uses the reversible nature of the carbonation reaction
of CaO to CaCO3. The carbonation takes place at a high temperature in the ﬁrst reaction
vessel followed by calcination of the carbonate to regenerate lime. The high energy
requirement is provided by primary fuel combustion and secondary fuel combustion
such as coke respectively in the two stages [11, 13].

2.2.2
Solvent based absorption processes
The solvent based absorption of CO2 can take place either by physical absorption,
reactive absorption or by both. Physical absorption occurs when CO2 gets dissolved in
the solvent without any reaction, while reactive absorption means that CO2 reacts with
the solvent and gets stripped from the gas stream. Over the years many of these have
been used in a major or a minor way in pilot and industrial scales. The classiﬁcation
is shown in Figure 2.1. Physical absorbents such as Rectisol, Selexol and Purisol have
been developed by industries and are sold as proprietary solvents in CO2 capture
processes. Various criteria go into the choice of solvents to use in a speciﬁc industrial
application. Major criterion is the economics for the speciﬁc application subject to
practical constraints such as high reaction or absorption rates, thermal stability, low
vapor pressure and heat of regeneration [14].
==PAGE:28==

Figure 2.1: Classiﬁcation of CO2 absorbents [14]
A generic process ﬂow for solvent based CO2 capture is shown in Figure 2.2. At
the heart of this process is the absorber and desorber column assembly which has
been widely studied in the literature [14]. The ﬂue gas enters the absorber tower at
the bottom so that while rising up the tower it comes in continuous contact with the
lean solvent. The absorber internals are some kind of a packing which enhances the
gas-liquid contacting area and time. By the time the gas stream leaves the top of the
column it is almost completely stripped of its CO2 content. From the bottom of the
column the CO2 rich solvent is then fed to the top of the desorber column. A reboiler
is installed at the bottom of this column which heats the solvent and liberates the
dissolved CO2 and provides the heat of reaction for releasing the chemically bound
CO2. Additionally the reboiler provides the vapor contact stripping of CO2 rising up
the desorber. The condenser and separator at the top of the desorber column takes out
almost pure CO2 gas and returns the reﬂux.

Current industrial technology
The basic engineering of the equipment of gas absorption has been around since a
long time ago. Absorption stripping of CO2 and H2S have been in use since last 75
years as the most popular choice for treating the the industrial ﬂue gas streams [16].
The ﬁrst patent for solvent based stripping process was awarded to Robert Bottoms in
the USA in the year 1930 [17]. Therefore, it has been argued that there cannot be any
further drastic development in the design engineering of the equipment of absorber

Thermodynamics
Consider a system with some number of species present as some number of phases.
Phase, here is deﬁned as any physically and fundamentally distinct state of matter
which can be gas, liquid, solid, and in some cases plasma. In such a system, at a given
temperature T and pressure P, there will be some distribution of species in various
phases. In addition to that, the species my as well react with each other in various ways
and extents. Intuitively, we can observe that the condition of such a system and the
distribution of species will be dictated by the energy of the system. This distribution of
species in phases at constancy of conditions is called as equilibrium of the system.
The subject of equilibrium thermodynamics deals with the estimation and predic-
tion of the state of such a system and its species and extracting as much predictive
information of the system as possible. The end goal is calculation of the equilibrium
distribution of each species in each phases of the system. Let us move on to deriving
and deﬁning some basic property relations and establish the premise for equilibrium.
The theory in the foregoing discussion has been inspired from [27, 30].

Literature on Experiments
Various experiments and methods used in literature are discussed in the chapter. The
subject of gas absorption is a rather well known and researched in the past century.
Hence, various methods have been tried to determine gas solubility at different condi-
tions of temperature and pressure. A brief summary of the techniques are discussed
below.
4.1
Modes of experiments
Generally, the methods for multi-component vapor liquid equilibrium (VLE) have
been found to be classiﬁed as static, circulation and continuous ﬂow [39]. According
to their nomenclature, it is clear that the gas phase in these methods are either kept
static or in a re-circulation loop or in a continuous unidirectional ﬂow. In all these
methods, the operation is continued until all the physical properties of the system,
namely Temperature, Pressure, and phase compositions of each component is constant
with respect to time. These three modes of operation are discussed below. It has
been suggested that static method is useful and accurate in the high pressure regimes
but is not accurate in the very low pressure regime. While the circulation method is
reportedly accurate at low to moderately high pressures [39].
4.1.1
Static
Most of the experiments since the inception of VLE experimentation, have been carried
out with this static method. The principle of this method is very straightforward. A
cell is kept in a thermostatic water bath at the desired temperature. A known amount
of solvent is added to the cell and then it is purged with N2 to remove all the air and
traces of CO2. After that, an empirically determined amount of CO2 is injected into
the cell. As the solvent absorbs the CO2, the pressure in the cell will gradually drop
and then stabilize at a given value. At this point of time the molar compositions of
components in liquid and gas phase are determined by sampling and the pressure
is measured [39]. The liquid phase analysis were done by NaOH and BaCl2 induced
precipitation followed by titrimetric determination of amount of CO2. The gas phase in
this is determined by gas chromatography or by IR analyzers.

ﬁxed quantities at the start of the experiment are the temperature which is set in the

5.3.2
Estimation of pressure drop from VLE cell to gas outlet
The current experimental set-up does not have the facility to directly measure the total
pressure inside the VLE cell. This necessitates calculation of the pressure within the cell
considering that the gas outlet in ﬁg. 5.5 is open to atmosphere. Therefore, calculating
the pressure-drop in the pipe section from the VLE cell to the outlet to atmosphere will
give the total pressure inside the VLE cell.
In very simplistic terms, pressure is a form of potential energy. Hence, when any
ﬂuid ﬂows from one end of a pipe to the other there is a loss of pressure. Which,
intuitively is the reason we need to push the ﬂuid if we want to make it ﬂow through a
pipe. Now, the pipe section of our interest is from the VLE cell to the gas outlet. This
section has some length and it also has some ’obstructions’ like T-section or bends.
All of these, combined, give certain resistance to ﬂow which means they contribute to
pressure drop. So, if we add the amount of pressure drop to the atmospheric pressure
we get the total pressure in the VLE cell. Some ﬂuid ﬂow dynamics principles explained
in [47] and [48] have been used to discuss the method as follows.

around the Reynolds number of 2100. The laminar ﬂow is characterized by uniformity
in terms of sliding ﬂuid layers without irregular mixing.

Chapter 7
Conclusion and Future Work
The experiments carried out for the validation of method and set-up have been largely
successful in setting the gravimetric method of determination of CO2 loading as reliable
in this work. The results were corroborated with analytical determination of CO2
loading by BaCl2 precipitation method. Although, there is a small negative error
in the gravimetric analysis as has been pointed out in the chapter of Results and
discussion. The experiments with the additives in the solvents did not yield any
grossly different loadings for the range of partial pressures which were investigated
in the current work. This is an expected result, in that the additive Urea has neither
apparent active participation nor inhibition to the absorptive reaction between CO2
and Monoethanolamine (MEA). As a matter of fact as seen in the last chapter chapter 6,
presence Urea slightly enhances the solubility of CO2. Perhaps this may be a peculiarity
of this particular experiment and more runs need to be carried out to establish this as
a matter of fact. But nevertheless, this is a good prospect for the optimization for the
stripping duty in the desorption phase. With this the stripper column can be run at
an average less power per kilogram of CO2 desorbed. This is because reboiler at the
bottom of the stripper can be run at a higher temperature without actually boiling the
solvents like water and MEA. That means the latent heat of vaporization is saved but
at the same time higher temperature would strip CO2 effectively. Less liquid hold-up
in the column also means lower design costs and lower condenser duties. Therefore,
overall, good prospects ahead for the vapor reducing agents to be used in CO2 capture
processes.
Future work
The future work in this project may include the upgrade of the equipment so as to
handle wider range of CO2 partial pressures, both at the lower as well as the elevated
pressures. This is necessary to accurately ascertain the effect of vapor reducing agents
on the VLE. It may be interesting to know the behaviour at extremely low partial
pressures if additives like Urea can inhibit the chemical absorption of CO2 or not. A
wider range may also help in modeling the VLE with thermodynamic models such as
extended UNIQUAC as a wider range provides better data for ﬁtting parameters.
