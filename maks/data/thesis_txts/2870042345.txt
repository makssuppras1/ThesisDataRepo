Abstract 
Candida albicans is present as a commensal microorganism in the mucosal tissues of large parts of 
the population. It is also one of the most common fungal causative agents of sepsis, can give rise to 
chronic colonization in immunocompromised individuals, and is implicated as a potential inducing 
or exacerbating factor in multiple neurodegenerative diseases. The importance of IL-17 for clearing 
fungal infections is well established, the participation of cells expressing the T cell receptor (TCR) 
γδ for production of IL-17 (γδT17 cells) during fungal infection of the brain remains to be fully 
elucidated. We established a model for studying γδ T cell responses to C. albicans s101 infection of 
the brain and found intravenous (IV) administration to be the most effective for producing viable 
colonies in the brain and stimulating an acute inflammatory response. Using this model, we tested 
the effect of impaired retinoic acid dependent signaling using RORγtCre-RARdnF/F transgenic mice 
and found no significant impairment of ability to clear the infection. Additionally, we used 
Fingolimod to block the egression of lymphocytes from the lymph nodes to determine if γδT17 
cells expand locally in the brain. Our results indicate the migration of IL-17 producing γδT17 to the 
brain during infection, as proportions of γδ T cells were reduced in mice treated with Fingolimod. 
Lastly, during a single experiment, we observed a population of γδT17 cells co-expressing 
transcription factors Tbet and RORγt in the brain during C. albicans infection. This population has 
previously only been described in the intestinal lamina propria. Further investigations should be 
conducted into the potential existence of this population and any implications in neuropathology.

Introduction 
Gamma delta T cells 
Gamma delta T cells (γδ T cells) constitute a distinct lineage of unconventional T cells characterized 
by their expression of a gamma (γ) and delta (δ) chain as part of their T cell receptor (TCR). The 
structure of the fully assembled TCRγδ allows for the extracellular domain to engage with a higher 
variety of ligands than the TCRαβ due to increased flexibility (Gully et al., 2024; Wo et al., 2020). In 
vertebrates, they arise from thymic progenitors as the earliest emerging lineage of T cells during 
development, with dendritic epidermal T cells (DETCs) being detected in mice at embryonic day 13 
(E13) in the embryonic thymus of mice (Havran & Allison, 1988; Parker & Ciofani, 2020).  
They are primarily tissue resident, constituting only 1-5% of total circulating lymphocytes, and are 
found in the highest abundance in epithelial tissues such as the gastrointestinal tract (GIT), skin, 
and lungs. There, subsets carry out a variety of functions such as maintaining homeostasis, tissue 
repair, surveillance, and pathogen clearance (Hu et al., 2023; Parker & Ciofani, 2020; Wo et al., 
2020). Unlike conventional αβ T cells, they can initiate innate-like responses upon antigen 
detection or cytokine stimulation without the need for further priming. Furthermore, antigen 
recognition by γδ T cells does not involve MHC bound peptides on antigen presenting cells (APCs) 
but includes non-peptide molecules such as stress-induced antigens (Hu et al., 2023). 
The γδ T cell subsets are classified based on their variable gamma chain (Vγ) in mice and their 
variable delta chain (Vδ) in humans (Qu et al., 2022). From this point forward, mentions of γδ T cell 
function or development will be based on studies performed in mice and the mouse-specific 
nomenclature proposed by Heilig and Tonegawa unless otherwise specified.

As seen in Table 1, γδ T cells can be broadly split into two distinct groups based on cytokine 
expression: those primarily producing interferon γ (IFNγ), γδT1 cells, and those producing 
interleukin 17 (IL-17), γδT17 cells. This functional division corresponds to the one observed in 
adaptive T helper (Th) cells, with IFNγ production mediating a type 1 immune response effective 
against intracellular pathogens, and IL-17 mediating immunity towards extracellular bacteria and 
fungi (Annunziato et al., 2015). 
The IFNγ producing subsets, collectively referred to as γδT1 cells, can be distinguished by surface 
expression of CD27, NK1.1, and CD122 (Parker & Ciofani, 2020). Production of IFNγ and tumor 
necrosis factor (TNF) by these cells support Th1 recruitment, neutrophil mobilization, and 
clearance of intracellular pathogens (Ribot et al., 2021). IL-17 producing γδ T cells promote CD4+ 
Th17 recruitment and expansion, while repressing IFNγ producing cells, driving the inflammation 
towards a type 3 immune response (McKenzie et al., 2018). Transcription factors TCF-1 and c-Maf 
(expressed by γδT1 cells and γδT17 cells respectively) have been implicated in controlling the 
balance between the two effector subsets (Sun et al., 2023).

Both subsets contribute to immune surveillance of barrier tissues and the maintenance of 
homeostasis, with dysregulated expansions of particularly γδT17 cells being implicated in several 
autoimmune pathologies such as chronic skin conditions and neuroinflammation (Kadekar, 
Agerholm, Viñals, et al., 2020; Pikor et al., 2015; Xue et al., 2021) 
 
γδ T cell development 
Initial development of γδ T cells takes place in the embryonic thymus where they, as their αβ 
counterparts, arise from common lymphoid progenitors (CLPs). Development occurs as layered 
ontogeny throughout life.  
During embryonic development, DETCs are first to appear and populate the epidermis where they 
produce IFNγ. The second wave, and subsequently the one of greatest interest to this thesis, is the 
appearance of cells expressing either Vγ4 or Vγ6 before relocating from the thymus to barrier 
tissues and the brain. The last developmental wave occurs postnatally with diverse variant TCR 
chains, producing cells residing primarily in lymphoid organs and already colonized epithelial 
surfaces (Hu et al., 2023; Parker & Ciofani, 2020; Qu et al., 2022). 
While the order and timing of various subsets arising is well explored, the ontogeny of γδ T cell 
infiltration of various tissues has yet to be understood (Qu et al., 2022). 
 
Precursors follow the same developmental path as αβ T cells until reaching the CD4-CD8- stage. 
While the expressed pre-TCR after rearrangement influences lineage commitment, there is also 
evidence suggesting that the strength of the TCR signal influences later effector functions (Born et 
al., 2010; Hu et al., 2023; Kreslavsky et al., 2008). The acquisition of effector functions varies both 
spatially and temporally between γδ subsets, with some becoming functionally competent within 
the thymus whereas others remain naïve until reaching their designated peripheral tissue (Born et 
al., 2010). Depending on the time and location of TCR signaling during maturation, the γδ T cells 
either develop into adaptive types if the signal is received in the peripheral tissue, or as innate 
types if they receive pre-programming during thymic development (Qu et al., 2022). Unlike 
conventional αβ T cells, the innate-like cells do not require priming at a lymph node, enabling them

to carry effector functions directly following antigen recognition or upon receiving inflammatory 
signals from the extracellular space. 
In mice, progenitor cells are thought to commit to the γδ lineage if it receives signaling through its 
TCR and weak to no Notch signaling, whereas development of human cells require Notch signaling 
(Hu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023). 
 
γδT17 cells 
Mice deficient in interleukin-17 (IL-17) have previously been shown to be more susceptible to both 
bacterial and fungal infections, as IL-17 plays a key role in immune activation and direction of host 
defense during type 3 immune responses (Akitsu & Iwakura, 2018). An overview of the functional 
diversity of γδT17 cells can be seen in Figure 1. 
Upon exiting the fetal thymus, γδ T cell subsets are ready to produce IL-17 without further host-
dependent stimulation. However, they undergo reprogramming upon reaching their designated 
tissues (Rizk et al., 2021). They are defined phenotypically as CD27-CD44+ cells in the tissues 
relevant to this thesis (Kadekar, Agerholm, Rizk, et al., 2020; Miranda et al., 2009). The majority of 
IL-17 producing γδ T cells (γδT17) express either the Vγ4 or Vγ6 chains while a minority express 
Vγ1 (McKenzie et al., 2018).

Within the subset of γδT17 cells a population co-expressing the transcription factors Tbx21 (Tbet) 
and retinoic-acid-receptor-related orphan receptor γt (RORγt) has previously been identified in the 
gastrointestinal tract (Kadekar, Agerholm, Rizk, et al., 2020).  
RORγt is a key transcription factor exclusive to a selection of immune cells such as γδT17 cells and 
Th17 cells and governs the expression of IL-17A, IL-17F, and IL-22 among others.  An 
overexpression of these genes have been observed in several autoimmune inflammatory 
conditions such as psoriasis, inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), and experimental autoimmune 
inflammation (EAE) (Kadekar, Agerholm, Viñals, et al., 2020; Xue et al., 2021). Studies done on mice 
have shown a reduced susceptibility to developing EAE, psoriasis, and inflammatory arthritis in 
RORγt deficient mice compared to non-transgenic mice (Ivanov et al., 2006; Kadekar, Agerholm, 
Rizk, et al., 2020; Xue et al., 2016).

Similarly, Tbet also drives the transcription of pro-inflammatory genes, but whereas RORγt is 
essential for coordinating type 3 immune responses, Tbet regulates commitment to the Th1 cell 
lineage and promotes type 1 immune responses (Annunziato et al., 2015; McKenzie et al., 2018). 
During infection, γδT17 infection can receive signals through the TCR that induces co-expression of 
Tbet and RORγt (Lazarevic et al., 2013; Sheridan et al., 2013). The expression of Tbet leads to an 
==PAGE:12==

induction of INFγ production by γδT17, representing the same plasticity as observed in Th17 cells 
(Sheridan et al., 2013). Furthermore, Tbet expression by dendritic cells (DCs) and innate lymphoid 
cells (ILCs) in the intestinal lamina propria has been shown to repress IL-17 production, skewing 
the inflammatory response towards a type 1 response (Lazarevic et al., 2013). 
In the GIT, IL-23 stimulation can induce the shift of γδT17 cells towards type 1 functionality. This 
induced expression is partially dependent on retinoic acid (RA), as established by studies on RA 
receptor-dominant-negative (RARdn) transgenic mice. Mice with reduced capacity for RA signaling 
in RORγt expressing cells had reduced frequencies of γδT17 cells co-producing IL-17 and IFNγ 
(Kadekar, Agerholm, Rizk, et al., 2020). 
During the early stages of an immune response, γδT17 cells respond to stimulation by cytokines 
like IL-7, IL-23, and IL-1β to promote inflammation. They act on nearby epithelial, endothelial, and 
stromal cells to promote the production of various pro-inflammatory chemokines to recruit 
immune cells such as monocytes and neutrophils to the site of infection (Agerholm & Bekiaris, 
2021; Akitsu & Iwakura, 2018; Williams et al., 2022). Furthermore, IL-17 stimulation promotes the 
induction of granulopoiesis through production of granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating 
factor (GM-CSF) albeit at lower levels than production of IL-17 and 22 (Ivanov et al., 2006). 
 
γδ T cell response to yeast infections  
Due to their type 3 functionality, γδT17 cells have been shown to play an important role in the 
innate immune response against fungal infections in both mice and humans (Agerholm & Bekiaris, 
2021). 
After a fungal pathogen enters the host, innate immune cells expressing surface bound pattern 
recognition receptors (PRRs) recognize pathogen associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) and 
damage associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) (Burgess et al., 2022).

Dectin-1, a C-type lectin receptor (CLR), expressed on the myeloid lineage of immune cells has 
proven important for the detection of invasive C. albicans and proliferation and activation of Th17 
cells (Cheng et al., 2011). Activation of Dectin-1 expressing cells by C. albicans has been observed 
both as direct PAMP binding to Dectin-1 and through inflammasome activation and subsequent 
activation of the Dectin-1 signaling pathway. An example of a fungi-specific PAMP would be the 
carbohydrate β-1,3-glucan found in the cell wall of fungal cells. (Brown, 2006; Burgess et al., 2022; 
Cheng et al., 2011). However, mucosal macrophages and dendritic cells are reported able to 
distinguish between commensal C. albicans and pathogenic growth based on whether the fungi is 
growing as yeast or hyphae, with only the latter causing inflammasome activation and activation of 
IL-17 producing cells (Cheng et al., 2011).

Kirchner et al., 2019; Schönherr et al., 2017). Although the specific downstream signaling cascades 
vary between the different types of PRRs, a common event is the activation of the canonical NF-κB 
pathway. Once activated, NF-κB aids in the initiation of transcription of a wide selection of genes 
encoding pro-inflammatory cytokine and chemokine production, cell cycle regulators, and 
adhesion molecules (T. Liu et al., 2017). 
The importance of the IL-17 family of cytokines in controlling fungal infections is made apparent by 
the prevalence of chronic mucocutaneous candidiasis (CMC) in human patients with loss-of-
function (LOF) mutations that either impair IL-17 production, recognition, or the maturation of IL-
17 producing cells. An example of one such gene could be an RORC LOF (Agerholm & Bekiaris, 
2021; Kirchner & LeibundGut-Landmann, 2021). 
Depending on the site of infection, the importance of IL-17 producing γδ T cells may vary. In both 
epidermal and intradermal inoculation of C. albicans γδT17 cells are the largest contributors to 
production of IL-17 and subsequent clearance of the pathogen (Kagami et al., 2010; Kashem et al., 
2017). Likewise, mice with impaired γδT17 cell functionality have an impaired ability to recruit 
neutrophils during fungal infections, leading to prolonged disease (Akitsu & Iwakura, 2018). 
 
Clinical importance of pathogenic C. albicans 
Various species of fungi colonize healthy individuals where they live as commensal organisms on 
epithelial surfaces, constantly kept in check by resident immune cells that prevent pathogenicity 
under homeostatic conditions. As bacteria, certain species of fungi are recognized as part of the 
healthy human mycobiome, with the majority being peaceful commensals (Pérez, 2021). 
C. albicans is a small pleomorphic yeast, with the ability to grow as either budding yeast, 
pseudohyphae, or hyphae, switching between these depending on environmental 
conditions. ’Pseudohyphae’ refers to various growth patterns that all appear as filamentous but 
lack the parallel cell walls of true hyphae (Gavito-Higuera et al., 2016; Sudbery et al., 2004). 
Shifting between growth forms is believed to be an important virulence factor, with strains lacking 
the ability to form hyphae having a reduced virulence (Mayer et al., 2013; Sudbery et al., 2004).

The most common sites of C. albicans colonization include the GIT, oral cavity, and genitourinary 
tract where it resides as an opportunistic pathogen. Estimations of prevalence of C. albicans in 
humans go as high as suggesting the fungi is present in the mycobiome of 70% of the population 
(Kabir et al., 2012; Richardson & Moyes, 2015). Although typically harmless, changes in conditions 
can cause C. albicans to activate virulence-related genes and become pathogenic. In otherwise 
healthy individuals, this typically results in benign superficial infections of mucosal barriers with 
the potential to develop into CMC (Y. Liu et al., 2011). However, C. albicans can under opportune 
conditions enter the bloodstream and cause severe systemic candidiasis and hematogenously 
disseminated candidiasis to internal organs (Lin et al., 2015). Both severe acute infections and 
development of CMC are more prevalent in immunocompromised individuals (Kabir et al., 2012; Y. 
Liu et al., 2011; Wu et al., 2023).  
A study conducted by Wisplinghoff et al. examined patients with nosocomial bloodstream 
infections across 49 American hospitals between 1995 to 2002 and found Candida spp. to be the 
causative agent in 9% of cases (Wisplinghoff et al., 2004). While sepsis itself provides a risk factor, 
another potentially severe complication following C. albicans entering the bloodstream, is the 
dissemination of cells across the blood-brain barrier (BBB). Upon gaining access to the central 
nervous system (CNS), colonization can lead to the onset of meningitis, fungal cerebritis, and 
abscess formation (Gavito-Higuera et al., 2016; Wu et al., 2023). 
 
During recent years C. albicans alongside other fungal pathogens have also been implicated as able 
to either induce or exacerbate chronic neuroinflammation and neurodegenerative diseases (Phuna 
& Madhavan, 2022; Wu et al., 2023). C. albicans has been detected in the brains of patients with 
various neurodegenerative diseases, all without exhibiting symptoms normally seen during acute 
infections (Wu et al., 2023). One study examined corpora amylacea (CA) from the CNS of patients 
with Alzheimer's disease, amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, and Parkinson’s disease and were able to 
detect the presence of proteins typically found in the cell wall of fungi (Pisa et al., 2016). Another 
study found high levels of polysaccharides in peripheral blood from patients with Alzheimer's 
disease (Alonso et al., 2014).

As fungal resistance to current treatments rises and our awareness of their potential role in 
triggering or advancing neurodegenerative diseases, understanding existing host defense 
mechanisms could provide new targets for developing effective treatments (Bohner et al., 2022; 
Ribeiro et al., 2021). The aim of this thesis was to investigate murine γδ T cell responses to C. 
albicans of the brain, with a particular interest in γδT17 cells. 
 
Results 
Fungal burden in brain following C. albicans s101 infection 
A low virulence strain of C. albicans, namely s101, was chosen for the purpose of these 
experiments. Often, models of C. albicans infections are constructed around the highly virulent 
strain SC5314 which preferentially causes an acute inflammatory response and a large influx of 
inflammatory cells and the induction of IL-17 production at the site of infection (Kirchner et al., 
2019; Schönherr et al., 2017). s101 has a lower virulence and has been observed in a model of 
oropharyngeal candidiasis to hardly invoke any acute inflammatory response despite a 
quantification of fungal load between s101 and SC5314 being comparable. Additionally, s101 has 
shown a greater propensity for establishing persistent colonization (Kirchner et al., 2019; Lemberg 
et al., 2022). A more virulent strain had previously been tested by our group at DTU which did not 
yield viable colonies from any organ on day three after infection. The desired outcome of using a 
lower virulence strain was to rely on delayed immune activation to allow the yeast enough time to 
disseminate into the brain. Developing a method of establishing and validating infiltration of C. 
albicans in the brain was a crucial first step in allowing the study of γδ T cell responses to fungal 
infections in the brain.

Due to these changes in the protocol, results were not very consistent, however a reliable and

As an alternative to intravenous administration of the fungi, oral gavage (OG) was also attempted

γδ T cell response to C. albicans in the brain  
After confirming the presence of viable C. albicans in the brain post-infection, the next point of 
interest was to study any changes in the numbers- and composition of γδ T cells present in the 
brain after infection. The working hypothesis was that there would be an observable expansion of 
innate- and innate-like immune cells in response to the infection, with γδT17 cells potentially 
making up a large part of the expanded T cells due to their importance in type 3 immune 
responses (Agerholm & Bekiaris, 2021).  
 
Results 
Colonization by C. albicans of the brain in WT mice resulted in an increased number of TCRγδ+ cells 
and leukocytes (Figure 7). Figure 5 presents flow cytometric plots from one replicate of both 
control and infected groups that are representative of the mean.

The effect of lymphocyte egress from secondary lymphoid tissues on C. 
albicans brain infection. 
Having confirmed a γδT17 expansion in the brain post C. albicans s101 infection, we were curious 
to investigate if the γδT17 cells were expanded locally or if they were recruited from elsewhere. To 
achieve this, mice were given injections of Fingolimod (FTY720) one day prior to infection and then 
every day up until takedown. FTY720 is an agonist for sphingosine 1-phosphate (S1P) G protein-
coupled receptors (GPCRs) which is required for the egress of lymphocytes from the lymph nodes 
and has been used for managing multiple sclerosis (MS) (Brinkmann et al., 2010). The 
administration of FTY720 has been shown to also hinder the egress of TCRγδ+ cells from the lymph 
nodes and lower the number of Vγ4+ γδ T cells in the CNS, the presence of which has been linked 
to contribute to EAE (Maeda et al., 2015). We were interested in investigating if the effect of 
FTY720 would influence the number of γδT17 cells in the brain during an antifungal response. Mice 
were divided into three groups, one receiving both C. albicans and FTY720, one only receiving C. 
albicans, and a control group receiving neither.  
By administering FTY720, we attempted to investigate the point of origin for the γδ T cells that 
appear during inflammation. By preventing the egress of lymphocytes from the lymph nodes, an 
unhindered γδ T cell response would indicate that most of these cells expand locally in the tissue, 
as opposed to migrating either from the thymus or peripheral tissues.  
 
Results 
We observed a reduction in the γδ T cell population detected (Figure 8) in infected mice treated 
with FTY720 compared to those not treated. However, there appeared to still be some γδ T cell 
expansion when compared to the uninfected control, which could indicate that a majority, but not 
all, of γδ T cells migrate to the brain rather than being locally expanded.  
Individuals that received FTY720 had a lower observable number of events registered by the flow 
cytometer present in the brain for both conventional T cells and γδ T cells (Figure 8) and a slight 
increase of the same populations in lymph nodes (Figure 9). When comparing the infected group 
of mice not administered FTY720 to the ‘C. albicans + FTY720’ group, the number of registered

Effect of reduced γδT17 cells on the severity of infection and immune cell 
composition in the brain 
To further investigate the impact of γδT17 cells on fungal infections, fungal burden and flow 
cytometric analysis was done using RORγtCre-RARdnF/F transgenic mice. By utilizing the Cre/Lox 
system, individuals that are RORγtCre+ (referred to simply as ‘Cre+’ for ease) will be unable to 
actively signal through RARα in cells expressing RORγt. Inability to signal using RA has been 
associated with a reduction in IFNγ producing γδ T cells and an increase in IL-17 and IL-22 
producing γδ T cells (Kadekar, Agerholm, Rizk, et al., 2020).  
Regrettably, the low number of γδ T cells present in the brain prevented us from dividing the brains 
to do both fungal burden and flow cytometric analysis. Additionally, constraints in the number of 
available animals meant we were only able to perform the experiment once. The spleen was 
chosen for flow cytometric analysis to see if there was any observable effect of the infection on 
Vγ4+ cells.

observed no significant difference in the mean fungal burden in the brains of Cre+ and Cre- mice 
(Figure 11). Mean CFU/g for both were comparable to what was observed in WT mice (Figure 4).

Discussion 
IV injection of C. albicans s101 leads to colonization of the brain and 
subsequent inflammatory immune responses in WT mice. 
The first objective was to establish a protocol for inducing an inflammatory response in the brain 
by infecting the mouse with C. albicans s101 to allow for the study of γδ T cells in this context. As 
no effect of OG administration of C. albicans in the brain was observed (Figure 6), it would make a 
poor model for studying the immune response to fungal infection. Based on our observations, 
yeast cells most likely never made it across the BBB but were contained to the GIT.  
However, C. albicans is known to have the capacity for translocating from the GIT and into both the 
peritoneal cavity and the blood stream, and is considered a common cause of sepsis (Alonso-
Monge et al., 2021; Delaloye & Calandra, 2014). Several technical parameters and strain-specific 
characteristics could have influenced our inability to isolate viable yeast cells from the brain post 
oral administration of C. albicans. Others have successfully recovered live yeast from the brains of 
mice two days post oral gavage administered infection, and it could be attributed to the low 
virulence of the strain used by our group, preventing translocation from the GIT to the brain before 
the immune system clears the infection (Bimler, 2023). 
Another member of the lab, Maria Virginia Baglioni, ran experiments concurrently with the ones 
presented here and worked on establishing a model for investigating γδ T cell response to fungal 
infection in the gut. After IV administration of C. albicans, she obtained results mirroring the ones 
presented here, observing no colonization or immune activation in the gut.  
As seen in Figure 4A, the IV administration of C. albicans enabled the isolation of viable yeast cells 
using our protocol for tissue processing and yeast cultivation. The model of infection also allowed 
us to detect an expansion of innate immune cells in the brain after infection (Figure 5) at an earlier 
timepoint than previously reported for the same strain in other tissues (Kirchner et al., 2019).  
Apart from seeing only an expansion of TCRγδ+ cells during fungal infection, an increase in the 
number of CD4-CD8- TCRγδ- cells was also apparent (Figure 5C). A subset of T cells matching this 
phenotype and expressing the TCRαβ receptor has been implicated in autoimmune pathologies, 
the ontogeny and function of which is poorly understood (Kashem et al., 2017). 
==PAGE:30==
03/03/2025                                                                                                                                      Isaksen, A.K 
27 
 
Using the strain SC5314 and IV administration of a comparable number of viable fungal cells, C. 
albicans has previously been reported to persist in the brain from day 2 to 10 post injection (Wu et 
al., 2019). While results indicate that IV administration of C. albicans s101 is a reliable model for 
establishing acute brain infection, further investigation should be done to determine the ability of 
this model to establish persistent colonization.  
 
γδ T cell numbers increase during acute C. albicans infection 
In accordance with our hypothesis, fungal colonization of the brain following IV injection of C. 
albicans caused an increase in the number of TCRγδ+ cells in the brain (Figure 7A). Although the 
mean expansion of γδT17 cells was statistically significant compared to that of the WT control, the 
increase in overall γδ T cells did not appear to be driven by preferential expansion of γδT17 cells, as 
the proportion of these out of the total TCRγδ+ population remained statistically insignificant 
(Figure 7B). 
The low numbers of TCRγδ+ present in the brain of especially control animals should also be taken 
into consideration when calculating fractions due to the potential for even few false positives or 
poor gating to skewer the result. Creating gates for the flow cytometric analysis that perfectly 
isolate every cell of the population of interest without including any contaminants and that does so 
for every sample of an experiment is virtually impossible. Although measures were taken to lower 
the impact of technical variables such as compensation settings and light exposure, it is still 
possible for false positives and false negatives to be present.  
Although the proportion of γδT17 cells in relation to TCRγδ+ was not significant, the proportion of 
γδT17 cells out of total T cells nearly doubled in the brain after C. albicans infection (Figure 7B).  
Further subdivision of the γδT17 cells based on TF expression showed a preference towards RORγt 
and thus the expression of IL-17 in accordance with expectations for a type 3 immune response 
(Figure 5D). The flow cytometric plot chosen to illustrate the obtained results originated from the 
only experiment that had a population of γδT17 cells staining positive for both RORγt and Tbet. Co-
expression of these TFs has previously been observed in a γδT17 cells residing in the intestinal 
lamina propria and is thought to contribute to innate immune plasticity and provide both a 
==PAGE:31==
03/03/2025                                                                                                                                      Isaksen, A.K 
28 
 
regulatory and inflammatory advantage in a tissue colonized by large amounts of commensal 
microorganisms (Kadekar, Agerholm, Rizk, et al., 2020).  
Save for the appearance of a RORγt+Tbet+ population of γδT17 in one of the experiments, our 
findings are in accordance with existing literature reporting an increased presence of γδT17 cells 
during fungal infection (Agerholm & Bekiaris, 2021; Sun et al., 2023). Further work needs to be 
done to elucidate whether the appearance of this population was due to a technical issue, and if 
not, what induced the phenotypic shift into co-expression. RNA sequencing of γδ T cells performed 
by another member of the lab, Marcelo Gregorio Filho Fares da Silva, has thus far not shown any 
evidence for the existence of a RORγt+Tbet+ γδT17 population in the brain. 
 
γδ T cell expansion during C. albicans mediated inflammation of the brain 
is impeded by reduced lymphocyte egression from the lymph nodes. 
Upon having observed a population of seemingly RORγt+Tbet+ γδT17 cells in the brain, it was 
decided to investigate whether the increase in γδ T cell numbers during inflammation was caused 
by local expansion of tissue resident cells or migration from other tissues. In the case of local 
expansion, a population co-expressing RORγt and Tbet would be caused by an induced switch in 
gene expression during fungal infection as the population was not detected in control animals 
(Figure 5D). 
As seen in Figure 8 and Figure 10A the injection of FTY720 during the course of the infection 
resulted in a reduction of both conventional αβ T cells and γδ T cells in the brain. This could 
indicate that a majority of γδ T cells are recruited to the site of infection rather than being 
expanded locally in the brain. Others have previously reported a reduction of circulating γδT17 
cells as well as Vγ4+ γδ T cells in the CNS of EAE mice, noting that the sequestering IL-17 producing 
Vγ4+ cells to the draining lymph nodes has a protective effect on autoimmune neuroinflammatory 
diseases (Maeda et al., 2015).

The effect of fungal infection on RORγtCre-RARdnF/F transgenic mice  
As established in the introduction, IL-17 production comprises a key component of type 3 immune 
responses and an essential part of innate responses during the early stages of C. albicans infection. 
RARdn mice (Cre+) were therefore expected to have higher fungal loads than those with unaltered 
γδT17 (Akitsu & Iwakura, 2018; Mielke et al., 2013). However, as seen in Figure 11 there was no 
significant difference between the CFU/g of Cre+ and Cre- mice. One apparent consequence of the 
Cre+ mice’ decreased γδT17 response was the reduced presence of neutrophils in Cre+ mice Figure 
12E, which is in accordance with existing literature (Agerholm & Bekiaris, 2021; Akitsu & Iwakura, 
2018; Williams et al., 2022). 
When considering the comparable fungal loads between strains s101 and SC5314 observed in 
oropharyngeal candidiasis (Kirchner et al., 2019; Schönherr et al., 2017), it appears plausible that 
C. albicans self-regulates the density of cells during colonization. Investigations into quorum 
sensing (QS) capabilities of C. albicans revealed evidence of QS molecules controlling the transition 
of C. albicans between growth as budding yeast cells and filamentous hyphae. It was reported that 
lower cell densities favored transition from the easily dispersed yeast into hyphae, a transition that 
is speculated to be important for the pathogenicity of C. albicans (Hornby et al., 2001; Lin et al., 
2015; Padder et al., 2018). Based on these findings, it could be reasonable to assume that C. 
albicans is the limiting factor on CFU/g. 
While it has yet to be demonstrated in the brain, the specific recognition of C. albicans through 
detection of candidalysin by the GP1ba receptor expressed on platelets has been shown to initiate 
Th2 and Th17 responses in airway mycosis (Moyes et al., 2016). While the Th17 response would be 
negatively affected in the Cre+ mice, it leaves the possibility for mounting a Th2 response.  
However, the CNS could have other, equally important, mechanisms of fungal clearance. One such 
mechanism that appears independent of IL-17 is the formation of fungal-induced glial granulomas 
(FIGGs) observed during acute yet modest C. albicans infection of the CNS (Wu et al., 2019). The 
exact mechanism behind the clearance of fungi by FIGGs remains mostly unknown apart from the 
involvement of CD11b signaling in response to detection of candidalysin. The activation of 
microglial cells for FIGG formation under mild C. albicans happened without detectable 
recruitment of macrophages, neutrophils (Wu et al., 2023).  
==PAGE:33==
03/03/2025                                                                                                                                      Isaksen, A.K 
30 
 
As glial cells are unique to the CNS, one could speculate that the evolution of a CNS-specific 
defense against low levels of fungal invasion might have evolved to prevent the promotion of IL-17 
mediated inflammation to lower tissue damage and potentially lowering the risk of autoimmune 
reactions implicating Vy4+ cells. Following this line of thinking, it might be interesting to investigate 
not only the mechanism of action of FIGG mediated fungal clearance, but also the timing and 
fungal burden when a potential switch between these two takes place. 
Few significant differences in proportions of TCRγδ+ cells and more specifically γδT17 cells were 
observed between the Cre+ and Cre- groups (Figure 12). However, of γδT17 cells, a larger fraction 
was Vγ4+ for both the Cre- control and infected group. Interestingly, the mean fraction of Vγ4+ 
cells was higher in the control group. The viability of the C. albicans solution used for the injection 
was initially questioned, however, based on the neutrophil expansion it does appear as though the 
infection has been detected. Another important note is the transience of C. albicans in both the 
bloodstream and spleen, as concentration declines more rapidly than in the brain  (Lionakis et al., 
2011). 
As inducible Vγ4+ are the primary subset linked to neuroinflammatory diseases such as 
Alzheimer's disease and MS, it would have been interesting to investigate what cytokines were 
primarily being produced by Vγ4+ both in the spleen and brain (Maeda et al., 2015; Wu et al., 
2023). Available literature has shown that a majority of the γδT17 cells that co-express RORγt and 
Tbet are Vγ6+ in the gut (Kadekar, Agerholm, Rizk, et al., 2020). This further increases interest in 
the role of a possible Vγ4+ subset of cells co-producing IFNγ and IL-17 appearing during fungal 
infections of the brain (Figure 5) and the possibility of the same subset being implicated in 
neuroinflammatory diseases. 
Had time allowed, it would have been of great interest to immunize using C. albicans at a non-
lethal intravenous dose before inducing EAE to investigate if mice that had been infected were 
more prone to develop more severe symptoms and at a higher rate. If that is the case, a relevant 
follow-up would be to test if this pathogenic response is in part dependent on γδT17 cells by 
comparing RORγt- Cre x RARdnf/f  responses to WT.  
Overall, the repression of γδT17 cell differentiation and survival did not seem to have any notable 
effects on the innate immune system’s ability to combat C. albicans s101 colonization of the brain.

Conclusion 
A robust and reproducible model for acute fungal cerebritis using the low virulence strain C. 
albicans s101 and IV administration was established. Infection by oral gavage was also attempted 
as a less invasive alternative, but the model requires more work with the strain of choice to invoke 
an inflammatory response in the brain. While γδ T cells and particularly γδT17 cells were expanded 
in the brain following infection, no increase in fungal burden was observed in RORγt- Cre x RARdnf/f 
mice. It is speculated that this could either be due to QS by C. albicans promoting lower growth 
densities or adequate plasticity of the immune system to counteract IL-17 deficiency. In one 
experiment, a subset of γδT17 cells thought contained to the intestinal lamina propria co-
expressing TFs RORγt and Tbet was observed in the brain after infection. Further research should 
be done to conclude whether this co-expression can be induced in the brain, if they can migrate 
there from the GIT upon infection, or if our observations were caused by a technical error. If this 
population does exist, further investigation surrounding these cells would be of great interest as 
they could be implicated in the onset and exacerbation of neuroinflammatory diseases.

Buffers and media 
Yeast extract peptone dextrose (YPD) culture media was prepared by adding 0.5% YPD powder to 
sterile, distilled water. Agar plates were prepared by adding 20g bacteriological agar per L broth to 
the YPD broth before autoclaving. 2mL/L Penicillin-Streptomycin (P/S) was added post autoclaving. 
‘Culture media’ refers to R10 complete medium prepared with the following ingredients. 500mL 
RPMI, 10mL Hepes (stock 1 M), 50mL Gibco FBS, 5mL glutamine, 5mL P/S, and 500uL BME (stock 
50 mM). 
‘Wash media’ refers to a solution of 500mL RPMI, 50mL FBS, and 5mL P/S.’ 
Percoll for separation was prepared by diluting isotonic Percoll (9 parts Percoll, 1 part 1X HBSS) 
with HBSS/Hepes (1x) buffer to the desired concentrations of 37% and 70%.

Isolation of lymphocytes 
Brain 
To isolate lymphocytes from the brain, the animal must first be euthanized using a CO2 chamber to 
keep circulation intact. Next, the animal is placed on its back and the abdominal cavity is opened. 
Being careful, the heart is exposed by cutting through diaphragm and ribcage. A large needle 
(preferably 18G) is inserted into the left ventricle and around 40mL of PBS is used for perfusion. 
The head is removed from the body and the skull cut open. After removal, the brain was rinsed in 
PBS before transferring into culture medium. Brains are stored in separate wells on ice until all 
animals have been dissected. 
Lymphocyte harvesting from the dissected brain begins with transferring the brain to a 12-well 
plate and cutting it into smaller pieces before adding 1mL culture medium and 1mL of brain 
digestion cocktail. Incubate at 37oC for 30 minutes without shaking. Using a cut 1mL pipette tip, 
pipette up and down to mix every 10 minutes. 
Tissue is homogenized using a transfer pipette to pipette up and down and then washed through a 
70µm strainer using 5mL of wash medium. Any undigested pieces are crushed through the strainer 
by using the flat end of a syringe plunger. The strainer was rinsed using an additional 10mL of wash 
media. 
Cells were spun down at 400g for 5 minutes at 4oC. Supernatant was aspirated and the pellet 
resuspended in 5mL of 37% Percoll, transferred to a 15mL tube and 3.5mL of 70% Percoll was 
underlaid using a 3.5mL transfer pipette. Centrifuge at 900g for 30 minutes at RT with the brakes 
off. After centrifugation, the interphase was carefully aspirated and put through a 70µm strainer 
using 15mL of culture medium. Lastly, the cells were spun down at 400g, 4oC for 7 minutes and the 
supernatant aspirated.  
From here, the cells are ready for staining.

Flow Cytometry and Data Analysis

more groups, Kruskal-Wallis test followed by Dunn’s multiple comparisons was chosen.
