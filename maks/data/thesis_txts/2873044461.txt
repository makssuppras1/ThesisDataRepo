Abstract
Green hydrogen is increasingly recognized as a cornerstone in the decarbonization of
energy-intensive sectors, particularly within the framework of the European Union’s cli-
mate neutrality targets. This study investigates the techno-economic challenges of re-
placing fossil-derived hydrogen in ammonia production, one of the most carbon-intensive
processes in the basic chemical industry, using domestically produced renewable hydro-
gen in Northern Germany. A mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) model is devel-
oped to optimize system design and minimize both the net present cost (NPC) and the
Levelized Cost of Hydrogen (LCOH) for two supply chain configurations: decentralized
off-site production and centralized on-site production.
The model simulates hourly operations over a full year and includes onshore wind, off-
shore wind, solar PV, alkaline electrolyzers, battery energy storage systems (BESS), and
hydrogen energy storage systems (HESS), while complying with EU regulatory criteria
for renewable hydrogen certification. Scenario 1 (decentralized) achieves a lower LCOH
of 4.77 €/kg, while Scenario 2 (centralized) results in a higher LCOH of 7.91 €/kg, pri-
marily due to higher renewable electricity costs, greater investment in BESS, and more
expensive on-site HESS infrastructure. Additionally, the centralized configuration shows
a lower renewable energy utilization rate and increased curtailment.
Sensitivity analysis focuses on offshore wind capital expenditure (CapEx), a major cost
driver in renewable energy-based systems, revealing potential LCOH reductions of up
to 18% under favorable cost conditions. While both configurations currently remain eco-
nomically challenged, the findings demonstrate significant decarbonization potential. The
study concludes that integrated system design, combined with continued technological
cost reductions, particularly in offshore wind and electrolyzer technologies, can enable
green ammonia production to become a viable near-term pathway for industrial decar-
bonization.

Introduction
The global energy system remains deeply entrenched in fossil fuels, with over 80% of the
world’s primary energy demand still met by coal, oil, and natural gas, exacerbating climate
change and threatening long-term economic and environmental stability. This continued
reliance on carbon-intensive sources has made the decarbonization of the energy and
industrial sectors one of the most urgent challenges of the 21st century. The industrial
sector alone is responsible for approximately 24% of global greenhouse gas emissions,
largely due to its high dependence on fossil fuels for heat, power, and feedstock [1, 2].
In Germany, for instance, the industrial sector is responsible for around 21% of total na-
tional emissions and consumes close to 30% of the country’s final energy [3]. This seg-
ment of the economy continues to rely heavily on coal, oil, and gas as primary energy
sources. As Germany’s production industry (excluding construction) accounts for approxi-
mately 23.1% of Gross Domenstic product GDP [4], its reliance on energy-intensive indus-
trial activity makes decarbonization not only an environmental necessity but an economic
imperative. Decarbonization efforts must therefore be both ambitious and economically
viable to maintain industrial competitiveness. Additionally, sectors such as steel, cement,
and chemical manufacturing face particular challenges in defossilizing their operations
due to both the scale of emissions involved and the limited availability of viable alterna-
tives [3].
One alternative could be to increase the share of renewable electricity in the public grid
and to electrify processes if possible. Electricity from renewable sources can be used
not only directly but also indirectly by enabling the production of alternative fuels such
as hydrogen or synthetic gases. These options are promising in part because they can
be integrated into existing production systems without requiring full-scale technological
overhauls. They also provide a means of aligning industrial operations with national and
international climate goals, including the targets set for 2030. Among the various alterna-
tives under consideration, hydrogen has emerged as a particularly promising candidate.
It serves multiple roles across the energy and industrial sectors: as a zero-carbon fuel,
a building block for chemicals, and a medium for storing and redistributing renewable
energy. Its ability to decouple energy supply from demand, along with its wide applicabil-
ity, makes it an important tool in addressing the technical and operational challenges of
the low-carbon transition [3]. Green hydrogen, produced through electrolysis powered by
renewable electricity, is widely viewed as a cornerstone of future climate-neutral energy
systems. It is particularly well-suited for sectors that cannot easily switch to electric power,
including high-temperature manufacturing and long-distance freight. In addition, hydro-
gen could potentially play a key role in stabilizing the power grid. Excess electricity could
be converted to hydrogen and redistributed in the form of electricity when demand peaks
[5]. While fossil-based hydrogen is being used across all sectors, green hydrogen is still
in early stages. As Schob et al. emphasize, “Hydrogen usage is crucial for defossilization
of chemical and steel industry [6].” Current efforts are focused on the applications where
hydrogen is both technically viable and economically promising [7].
Recognizing these opportunities, the German government introduced its National Hydro-
gen Strategy in 2020 to accelerate the deployment of hydrogen technologies. This com-
mitment was further reinforced in 2021 when Germany updated its climate legislation,
setting more ambitious emissions reduction targets: a 65% cut by 2030 and climate neu-
trality by 2045 [8]. Green hydrogen is seen as a central pillar in meeting these goals,

particularly in industry, aviation, and shipping. Moreover, the energy crisis following the
2022 invasion of Ukraine highlighted Germany’s vulnerability to fossil fuel imports, adding
a strategic energy security dimension to its hydrogen policy. As the largest economy in

Despite its promising potential, the large-scale adoption of renewable hydrogen in industry
still faces significant challenges. High production costs, limited technological maturity,
and gaps in strategic coordination continue to hinder its economic viability, especially in
comparison to fossil-based alternatives [10, 11]. Addressing these challenges requires
targeted strategies tailored to specific industrial applications and regional contexts.
1.1
Motivation and Background
In response to these challenges, Germany has positioned green hydrogen as the cor-
nerstone of its climate and industrial transformation strategy. The updated National Hy-
drogen Strategy [8] outlines ambitious targets for scaling up domestic green hydrogen
production and building the necessary infrastructure by 2030. To enable market integra-
tion and broader adoption, hydrogen systems must be optimized across the entire value
chain, from renewable electricity generation to final delivery. Contributing to this change
towards green hydrogen is the main motivator to this study.
The chemical industry is a central pillar of industrial value chains, providing the foundation
for the production of a wide variety of goods. Within this sector, the synthesis of basic
chemicals is particularly relevant for decarbonization due to its high energy demand and
associated CO2 emissions. The production of ammonia, a basic chemical product, alone
is responsible for roughly one quarter of the chemical industry’s direct emissions in Ger-
many, concentrated in a small number of large-scale facilities. Replacing fossil-based
hydrogen with renewable hydrogen in this process constitutes a significant step toward
climate neutrality and illustrates the broader potential of hydrogen in hard-to-abate indus-
trial sectors [12].
Among various applications of hydrogen in the chemical industry, the conversion from
fossil to green ammonia during the ramp-up of green hydrogen adoption stands out due
to its multiple strategic advantages. At 10 bar and room temperature, hydrogen gas has a
very low volumetric density of approximately 0.8 kg/m3, whereas ammonia gas can store
up to 123 kg/m3, making it more than 150 times denser in terms of hydrogen content
and suited as a hydrogen carrier. Moreover, the technology for its synthesis, handling,
and global transportation is well established. One key advantage of transitioning from
natural gas-based to electrolysis-based ammonia production is that the synthesis loop
does not require significant modification, since the feedstocks, hydrogen and nitrogen,
remain the same. This allows existing ammonia plants to be retrofitted for green hydrogen
integration with relative ease [13]. These advantages are supported by findings from
Hebling et al. [14], the study notes that in the early stages of the green hydrogen market
development, industrial green hydrogen use is expected to focus primarily on ammonia
production, starting around 2030. Over the longer term, applications are likely to expand
to include processes such as Methanol-to-Olefins, thereby supporting the broader ramp-
up of a renewable hydrogen-based economy.

1.2
Scope of the Study
This study focuses on the upstream provision of green hydrogen intended for use as
feedstock in the basic chemicals industry, specifically for the demand of ammonia pro-
duction. The emphasis lies on the design, operation, and optimization of the hydrogen
supply chain, rather than on the chemical conversion process of ammonia and hydrogen
itself. Downstream applications, such as urea synthesis or other derived products, as well
as end-use sectors like transportation or heating, are not included within the scope of this
work.
Hydrogen production in this study is based solely on electricity as the primary energy
input. The model includes the use of renewable energy sources such as solar photo-
voltaics, onshore wind, and offshore wind. This electricity can be used directly for hy-
drogen production, stored temporarily in battery systems, or curtailed without incurring
financial penalties when supply exceeds demand. The model does not allow electricity
to be fed back into the public grid. However, in the evaluation of results, the potential
financial benefit of selling curtailed electricity to the market is considered as an additional
scenario. Since the aim is to assess how green hydrogen can be supplied to industrial
sites in a reliable and environmentally sustainable manner, the analysis excludes blue
hydrogen production pathways and moreover does not include the assessment of envi-
ronmental impact. It also does not account for electricity purchased from the public grid
that is not generated by the modeled renewable energy systems. Only electricity from the
defined on-site renewable sources is used to produce hydrogen. This approach ensures
consistency with the European Union’s definition of renewable hydrogen, as outlined in
the Delegated Acts for Renewable Hydrogen [16].
The model assumes all hydrogen is produced domestically. No hydrogen imports are
considered, and the industrial hydrogen demand must be fully met by the system defined
within the model boundaries. There is no assumed revenue from the sale of hydrogen,
as no established market price currently exists. Similarly, by-products such as oxygen or
excess heat are not monetized in this study, in alignment with the assumptions used in
Agora Industry and Umlaut [17].

tions. To ensure transparency and methodological consistency, the definition and se-
lection of all cost-related parameters follow the guidelines established by Agora Industry
and Umlaut [17]. These guidelines specify the necessary cost categories and input as-
sumptions for calculating the LCOH. Although the original methodology defines LCOH as
the cost of hydrogen production only (electricity and electrolyzer cost), this study adopts

Given the clear delineation of this study’s focus on upstream hydrogen supply systems

1.3
Identified Research Gaps
While this study focuses on the upstream design and optimization of green hydrogen sup-
ply systems for ammonia production, it builds on and addresses several critical limitations
identified in the existing body of literature.
First, previous studies have not implemented genuine optimization of Power-to-Hydrogen
system components. Instead, sizing decisions were frequently based on static assump-
tions or fixed project parameters rather than derived through rigorous, data-driven opti-
mization methods. This approach limits both the realism and transferability of their find-
ings [18].
Second, hydrogen demand profiles are often represented in simplified or averaged for-
mats, typically expressed as daily consumption levels. Although these approximations
balance total demand over a 24-hour period, they overlook temporal mismatches between
hydrogen production and consumption. Such simplifications can significantly affect both
system reliability and the design of operational strategies [18].
Third, literature frequently treats hydrogen production in isolation, without integrating it
into the broader energy supply chain. Moreover, there is limited investigation into how
the varying combinations of renewable energy sources, particularly solar and wind, and
their integration with hydrogen and battery storage affect economic feasibility. This omis-
sion neglects the complex interaction between generation, storage, and demand, which
is fundamental to actual system performance. Furthermore, few studies examine trade-
offs between cost efficiency and supply reliability under fluctuating renewable output and
industrial hydrogen demand [19, 20].
Fourth, as Dautel et al. [21] point out, many existing models do not capture all costs of the
supply chain including both capital and operational expenditures, as well as the proper
treatment of discounted cash flows over time. Park et al. [20] reinforce this concern,
emphasizing that the exclusion of infrastructure components beyond hydrogen production
substantially weakens the accuracy of economic assessments. This reflects a lack of
financial comprehensiveness that undermines the real-world applicability of such models.
Lastly, there is a prevalent tendency in the literature to simplify critical cost factors, es-
pecially those related to electrolyzer investment, while applying inconsistent definitions of
LCOH. This inconsistency complicates cross-study comparisons and weakens the utility
of findings for policy development and investment planning [21, 19, 22, 17].

1.4
Objectives
The primary objective of this study is to conduct a comprehensive techno-economic anal-
ysis of domestic green hydrogen supply chains for industrial use in Germany. The de-
veloped methodological framework is designed to be uniform and transferable, enabling
application to other geographical regions beyond the case study presented in this work.
The analysis specifically focuses on the following core objectives:
• Minimizing the NPC of the hydrogen supply chain system under economic conditions
and operational constraints.
• Identifying key cost drivers across the hydrogen supply chain, under modeled sce-
nario constraints.
• Exploring the operational flexibility of electrolyzers and storage systems, both (BESS)
and (HESS), as a means to efficiently integrate variable renewable energy sources
into the hydrogen production process.
• Determining the optimal mix of renewable energy sources (solar PV, onshore wind,
offshore wind) for green hydrogen production.
All objectives are pursued in alignment with the current and near-future regulatory frame-
works governing green hydrogen production in the European Union, as outlined in the
Delegated Acts for Renewable Hydrogen [16]. These rules, particularly regarding tem-
poral correlation, are incorporated into the optimization model, as described in detail in
chapter 3.
The research aims to answer the following research questions:
1. Which configuration, centralized or decentralized, achieves a lower Levelized Cost
of Hydrogen (LCOH) for meeting industrial green hydrogen demand under realistic
economic and operational constraints?
2. What is the optimal component sizing strategy for green hydrogen production, stor-
age, and delivery in centralized and decentralized configurations to minimize system
cost and ensure reliable industrial supply?
To address the objectives and research questions, this study introduces a comprehensive
modeling framework, the structure and components of which are detailed in the following
section.

1.5
Methodological Approach
To achieve the outlined objectives, a Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) model is
developed to simulate and economically optimize different green hydrogen supply chain
configurations. The model is designed to represent the technical and economic interac-
tions between all major components of the hydrogen value chain. The simulation covers
one full year of operation with an hourly time resolution, capturing the variability of renew-
able energy availability and industrial hydrogen demand. The outcomes from this repre-
sentative year are then extrapolated and discounted over the project lifetime to calculate
long-term system costs.
The modeled system includes the following components:
• Renewable electricity generation (onshore wind, offshore wind, solar PV),
• Electrolysis technologies (alkaline),
• Compression and hydrogen storage (e.g., salt caverns, steel tanks),
• Transportation infrastructure (e.g., pipeline distribution),
• Industrial hydrogen demand profile.
The model aims to minimize the total NPC of the hydrogen supply system over the project
lifetime. It incorporates a comprehensive set of techno-economic parameters and opera-
tional constraints, which are derived from current literature, industrial data, and regulatory
requirements available in English or German. This approach enables a realistic and com-
parative assessment of centralized and decentralized supply configurations under varying
boundary conditions.

2.1.1
Grey Hydrogen
Grey hydrogen derives its name from the environmental consequences associated with its
production, particularly the release of carbon dioxide. During the process, CO2 is emitted
directly into the atmosphere, significantly contributing to greenhouse gas accumulation
and climate change. Despite its environmental drawbacks, the main advantage of grey
hydrogen lies in its economic competitiveness. The process benefits from existing nat-
ural gas infrastructure and technologies, making it both efficient and widely accessible
[25]. Therefore, grey hydrogen currently represents the most widely produced form of
hydrogen. It is generated primarily through steam methane reforming or coal gasification,
without incorporating Carbon Capture Utilisation and Storage (CCUS) technologies [26].
Coal Gasification
Hydrogen derived from coal is also referred to as black or brown hydrogen in some studies
and is typically produced through the process of coal gasification (see table 2.1). In this

method, coal is exposed to high temperatures in the presence of steam and a limited sup-
ply of oxygen or air, resulting in a gas mixture that includes hydrogen, carbon monoxide,
and carbon dioxide [25].
Coal gasification commonly employs four main types of coal as feedstock: lignite and sub-
bituminous coal (low-rank), bituminous coal (medium-rank), and anthracite (high-rank).
Several gasification technologies are used, including fixed-bed, moving-bed, fluidized-
bed, entrained-flow, and plasma gasification. These processes typically operate above
900 °C [24].
Among them, plasma gasification is considered an advanced emerging
method that employs high-energy plasma torches to generate cleaner and potentially re-
newable syngas.
Different gasification methods operate under distinct thermal conditions. Entrained-flow
and plasma gasification processes are conducted at significantly higher temperatures,
approximately 1200 °C and 1700 °C, respectively. In contrast, fixed-bed, moving-bed, and
fluidized-bed systems generally operate below 1200 °C. These temperature differences
influence both efficiency and syngas composition [27].
The primary hydrogen-forming reaction in coal gasification is the so-called water-gas re-
action:
C(s) + H2O(g) −−→CO(g) + H2(g)
(2.1)
In reaction 2.1, steam reacts with solid carbon to form carbon monoxide and hydrogen
gas. This reaction is a central step in syngas formation, from which hydrogen can be
separated and purified for industrial use [25].
Coal gasification is considered more efficient and cleaner than conventional coal combus-
tion, particularly for processing coal with high moisture and ash content. The resulting gas
has a high calorific value and can be used for power generation or chemical synthesis.
Because it contains substantial amounts of hydrogen, it enables more efficient energy
utilization and offers a pathway to reduce the carbon intensity of coal use [27].
However, despite its technical advantages, hydrogen from coal gasification raises serious
environmental concerns due to its high carbon emissions. On average, around 22–25 kg
of CO2 are released per kilogram of hydrogen produced, making it one of the most carbon-
intensive production methods (see table 2.1). This pathway remains prevalent in coal-rich
regions such as China, where resource availability drives its continued deployment [25].
Steam Methane Reforming (SMR)
Steam Methane Reforming (SMR) is a process in which hydrogen is produced by reacting
hydrocarbons, such as natural gas or methane, with steam. The process begins with
the separate preparation of the two main inputs: methane is compressed, and water is
pumped to reach the required pressure. Before entering the reformer, both streams are
preheated using heat exchangers that recover energy from various parts of the system.
Inside the reformer, methane and steam are mixed and exposed to elevated temperatures.
This causes them to react and form a gas mixture known as syngas, which primarily
consists of hydrogen, carbon monoxide, and some carbon dioxide. The reforming reaction
is highly endothermic and requires continuous heat input. This heat is typically supplied
by burning part of the unconverted gas from the system, along with additional methane
and air, in a combustion chamber. The heat from this combustion is transferred to the
reformer to maintain the necessary operating temperature.

The gas leaving the reformer then passes through a two-stage shift reactor. In the first
stage, operating at a higher temperature, and in the second stage at a lower temperature,
the carbon monoxide reacts further with steam to produce additional hydrogen and carbon
dioxide. Overall, the process involves the steam reforming reaction and the water-gas
shift reaction:
Steam Reforming:
CH4 + H2O −−→CO + 3 H2
(2.2)
Water-Gas Shift:
CO + H2O −−→CO2 + H2
(2.3)
In reaction 2.2, methane (CH4) reacts with steam (H2O) at high temperatures in the pres-
ence of a catalyst to produce carbon monoxide (CO) and hydrogen gas (H2). This is the
primary hydrogen-generating reaction in conventional SMR processes.
Reaction 2.3 represents the Water-Gas Shift (WGS) reaction, in which the carbon monox-
ide produced during steam reforming reacts with additional steam to form carbon dioxide
(CO2) and more hydrogen gas. These two reactions work in tandem to maximize hydro-
gen yield, with the WGS reaction typically carried out in a two-stage setup for enhanced
efficiency.
After this, the gas mixture enters a hydrogen separation unit, where a metallic membrane
selectively allows only hydrogen to pass through, resulting in a nearly pure hydrogen
stream [28]. In the SMR process, temperatures typically range between 700–1000 de-
grees Celsius, with operating pressures from 5 to 20 bar. Given the endothermic nature of
the reaction and its substantial thermal energy requirements, the provision of heat repre-
sents a critical factor. In industrial-scale applications, this thermal input is conventionally
supplied through the combustion of fossil fuels [27].
Despite its widespread use and economic viability, hydrogen produced via SMR is there-
fore a major contributor to carbon emissions. On average, approximately 10–12 kilograms
of carbon dioxide are released per kilogram of hydrogen produced, placing SMR among
the most carbon-intensive hydrogen production methods, second only to coal gasification
(see table 2.1).
Partial Oxidation (POX)
In addition to coal gasification and steam methane reforming, Partial Oxidation (POX)
constitutes a third major pathway for grey hydrogen production. It is particularly applicable
to hydrocarbon feedstocks that are unsuitable for reforming and is used in both large- and
small-scale industrial applications.
POX is a commercially established method in which hydrocarbons react with a limited
supply of oxygen or air. Unlike complete combustion, the restricted oxygen supply results
in incomplete oxidation, producing a gas mixture of carbon monoxide, hydrogen, and,
when air is used, nitrogen [27, 29]. The process may be conducted with or without a
catalyst, depending on feedstock composition and efficiency goals.
Conventional (non-catalytic) POX is an exothermic, high-temperature process, typically
operated at 1300–1500 °C and pressures between 3 and 12 MPa. It is particularly suitable
for processing heavy oils and sulfur- or olefin-rich hydrocarbons that are less compati-
ble with steam reforming [29]. The use of brick-lined reactors and internally generated
heat allows for compact designs and rapid startup. Additionally, the absence of a cat-
alyst avoids sensitivity to feed impurities and removes the need for pre-treatment such

steam reforming, soot formation, and reliance on pure oxygen or high-quality air [27].
Catalytic Partial Oxidation (CPOX) improves efficiency by operating at significantly lower

partial oxidation with steam reforming and the water-gas shift reaction [29]. However, the
exothermic nature of the process presents engineering challenges. Uneven heat distribu-

the high cost and safety risks of pure oxygen handling, the potential for explosive mix-

these challenges, CPOX remains a promising option for small-scale hydrogen production,
particularly where system flexibility and compactness are valued.
In summary, partial oxidation expands the technological scope of grey hydrogen produc-
tion, offering a viable alternative to reforming processes under specific feedstock or in-
frastructure constraints.
Biomass
Hydrogen production from biomass has reached a sufficient level of technological maturity
to support industrial-scale deployment. While the process emits CO2, these emissions are
generally considered carbon-neutral, as the carbon originates from organic material that
previously absorbed it during photosynthesis. This characteristic has sparked debate re-
garding whether biomass-derived hydrogen should be classified as green hydrogen [27].
Biomass encompasses a wide range of organic matter derived from living or recently living
organisms, including plant sources such as algae, agricultural crops, and forest residues,
as well as animal waste such as manure. Functioning as a natural store of solar energy,
biomass captures energy via photosynthesis and retains it in chemical bonds. Despite its
renewable nature and potential to mitigate carbon emissions, biomass presents certain
compositional limitations for hydrogen production. Its hydrogen content is relatively low,
approximately 6% compared to 25% in methane, while its high oxygen content (around
40%) reduces both its energy density and hydrogen yield [30].
Biomass-to-hydrogen conversion technologies are broadly divided into two main cate-
gories: thermochemical and biochemical processes.
Thermochemical conversion includes two main hydrogen production pathways: gasifica-
tion and pyrolysis.
Pyrolysis involves the thermal decomposition of biomass in the absence of oxygen, pro-
ducing a mix of gases, liquids (bio-oil), and solid residue (biochar). To generate hydrogen,
the gaseous phase, primarily composed of CO, CO2, CH4, and H2, undergoes further pro-
cessing through the water-gas shift reaction to maximize hydrogen output [27]. Although
promising, hydrogen yield from pyrolysis is generally lower than from gasification, and the
presence of tars and other contaminants necessitates extensive gas cleaning.
Gasification is the most prominent thermochemical route for hydrogen production from
biomass. It involves partial oxidation of biomass at elevated temperatures (typically 800–
900 °C) using gasifying agents such as air, oxygen, or steam. The resulting syngas is
composed primarily of carbon monoxide and hydrogen, along with CO2, steam, and light
hydrocarbons [30]. Subsequent water-gas shift and gas purification steps are employed
to isolate and enhance hydrogen content. A notable variant of this method is Supercritical
Water Gasification (SCWG), which operates under supercritical conditions (above 374 °C

Biochemical conversion uses hydrogen-producing microorganisms to convert biomass

conversion efficiency, slow reaction kinetics, and limited process stability under industrial
conditions [27]. As a result, their practical implementation remains restricted to laboratory
or pilot-scale settings.
2.1.2
Blue Hydrogen
Blue hydrogen is defined as hydrogen produced via SMR with integrated Carbon Capture,
Utilization, and Storage (CCUS), using natural gas or biomass as feedstocks. Importantly,
to be classified as blue hydrogen a production facility must incorporate a CCUS system,
although no universally accepted threshold has yet been defined for the minimum amount
of carbon that must be captured. Hydrogen produced with integrated carbon capture
is commonly termed blue hydrogen, reflecting its comparatively reduced environmental
impact relative to conventional fossil-based methods [25].
In this process, the carbon dioxide generated, particularly through the water-gas shift reac-
tion (see eq. (2.3)), is not released into the atmosphere but instead captured and stored,
often in deep geological formations. This prevents its contribution to greenhouse gas
emissions. While blue hydrogen offers a substantial reduction in CO2 output compared to
grey hydrogen, it does not achieve full climate neutrality and still results in residual emis-
sions. The addition of CCUS technologies also significantly increases the overall cost of
hydrogen production relative to grey hydrogen [25].
Carbon capture technologies applicable at various stages of hydrogen production include
adsorption, membrane separation, cryogenic separation, and chemical or calcium loop-
ing. These technologies can be implemented through different strategies: pre-combustion
(removing CO2 before fuel combustion), post-combustion (capturing emissions after com-
bustion using biological, physical, or chemical methods), or oxyfuel combustion (using
pure oxygen to enhance CO2 concentration for easier recovery). In steam methane re-
forming, approximately 10-12 kilograms of CO2 are emitted per kilogram of hydrogen
produced (see table 2.1), with the majority of emissions originating from the water-gas
shift stage. Capture efficiencies can reach up to 99%, depending on the technology and
the composition of the source stream. Blue hydrogen is currently regarded as a transi-
tional solution,and serves as an intermediate step toward the full-scale adoption of green
hydrogen. It functions as a bridge technology, combining existing fossil-based produc-
tion methods with carbon capture systems to reduce emissions during the shift to entirely
renewable hydrogen sources [27].
2.1.3
Green Hydrogen
Electrolysis remains the only commercially established method for hydrogen production
from water, whereas alternative approaches, such as plasma-assisted splitting, nuclear-
based water splitting, and biological pathways, are still in the research and development
phase [23]. Therefore, the following section focuses specifically on electrolysis technolo-
gies.

Hydrogen generated via the electrolysis of water using electricity derived from renewable
energy sources is commonly referred to as green hydrogen and is also known as clean
or low-carbon hydrogen. In this process, water molecules (H2O) are split into hydrogen
(H2) and oxygen (O2) through the application of an electric current. The principle reaction
for this electrochemical conversion is represented in Equation (2.4) [25].
2 H2O −−→2 H2 + O2
(2.4)
Water splitting offers a sustainable method for generating zero-emission hydrogen us-
ing abundant water resources, making it a vital technology for industrial decarbonization.
However, several technical barriers remain, including the high cost of electrolyzers, sub-
stantial energy requirements, and infrastructure limitations. Progress in catalyst devel-
opment, renewable energy integration, and water management is essential to enable its
large-scale adoption [23].
At the molecular level, water’s thermodynamic stability prevents it from decomposing
spontaneously. The strong covalent bonds between hydrogen and oxygen atoms, formed
by shared electrons between electronegative oxygen and hydrogen, must be broken for
hydrogen production. This requires a minimum theoretical energy input of 1.23 eV (2241.8
kJ/mol), though in practice, more than 1.8 V is typically needed due to kinetic limita-
tions in both the Hydrogen Evolution Reaction (HER) and the Oxygen Evolution Reaction
(OER)[23].
The difficulty of the process lies in overcoming these energy barriers and breaking multiple
O–H bonds. Additionally, the formation of H2 and O2 as diatomic gases introduces further
complications, including gas bubble formation on electrode surfaces, which hinders mass
transport and reduces efficiency. The strong H–H bond in molecular hydrogen, with a bond
energy of 436 kJ/mol, further contributes to the total energy requirement. Overcoming
these kinetic and structural challenges is crucial for improving the overall performance of
water-splitting systems [23].
The process is based on two simultaneous half-cell reactions: HER at the cathode and
OER at the anode. A key distinction of this technology lies in the diversity of electrolysis
approaches it encompasses, including Alkaline Water Electrolysis (AE), Proton Exchange
Membrane Electrolysis (PEM) and Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cells (SOEC)[25]. Further-
more, each electrolysis technology offers advantages and limitations based on its mate-
rials, operating conditions, and intended applications. Understanding these differences
is crucial for selecting the most suitable system for a given industrial context. The table
below provides a comparative overview of key electrolyzer technologies, AE, PEM Elec-
trolysis, and SOEC, based on their core technical characteristics, efficiency, and typical
use cases [23].

Alkaline Electrolysis (AE)
The AE system for hydrogen production typically consists of three main components:
the hydrogen generation unit, a control cabinet, and a rectifier cabinet. The core sys-
tem includes an alkaline electrolyzer, gas separators, coolers, pumps, tanks, and control
valves. During operation, the electrolyte is circulated and pressurized in the electrolyzer,
where the electrochemical reactions produce a gas–liquid mixture of hydrogen and oxy-
gen. The gases are subsequently separated, cooled, and discharged, while the remaining
liquid electrolyte is recirculated. The electrolyte flows into both the anode and cathode
chambers, which are separated by a permeable membrane. Water molecules can diffuse
through the membrane, enabling interaction between the two sides. Upon application of
an electric current, water at the cathode reacts with electrons to produce hydrogen gas
and hydroxide ions. Simultaneously, at the anode, the hydroxide ions release electrons
and form oxygen gas and water. The membrane inhibits gas crossover, ensuring that the
generated hydrogen and oxygen remain separated (see fig. 2.1) [31].
Figure 2.1: Operating principle of an Alkaline Electrolyzer showing ion flow and gas evo-
lution (modified from [32]).
Electrode materials are critical for efficient current transfer and catalytic performance.
Nickel and stainless steel are commonly used due to their good conductivity and corro-
sion resistance. Enhancing the surface area through increased porosity and roughness
improves reaction rates, while advanced composite materials and surface coatings con-
tribute to improved durability and catalytic efficiency. Nickel-based catalysts, such as
Raney nickel and nickel alloys (e.g., Ni–Fe, Ni–Co), are widely employed for the HER, of-
fering high activity and long-term operational stability. For OER, cobalt-based and mixed
metal oxide catalysts demonstrate strong performance, helping reduce reliance on pre-
cious metals [23].

Proton Exchange Membrane Electrolysis (PEM)
The PEM electrolysis system comprises three primary components: the electrolyzer unit,
a control system, and a DC power supply. Compared to alkaline electrolysis systems,
the PEM setup is more compact and structurally simplified. It eliminates the need for
a separate alkaline tank, incorporates a smaller gas treatment system, and allows the
water tank to function as both a storage reservoir and an oxygen separator [31]. The
overall principle of a PEM can be observed in fig. 2.2.

A key operational advantage of PEM systems is their ability to operate under differential
pressure. Unlike alkaline systems, which require gradual pressurization during start-up,
PEM electrolyzers can reach their target pressure directly via a regulating valve. This
enables stabilization of the system within approximately 15 minutes [31].
Structurally, PEM electrolyzers share similarities with alkaline systems, but they differ sig-
nificantly in the core electrochemical assembly [31]. PEM systems utilize only deionized
water, which is supplied to the anode. At the anode, the water undergoes the OER,
producing protons, electrons, and oxygen gas. The generated protons migrate through a
solid polymer electrolyte membrane, commonly made from Perfluorosulfonic Acid (PFSA)
materials such as Nafion or Fumapem, to the cathode, where the HER occurs, resulting
in the formation of molecular hydrogen [33].

While this section has examined hydrogen production pathways and their associated en-

significant emissions. Among these, the basic chemical industry stands out as both a
major consumer of hydrogen and a critical focus area for decarbonization. The following
section explores key emission sources within the basic chemical sector and evaluates cur-
rent technological practices alongside emerging low-carbon alternatives, such as green
hydrogen integration and electrification of process heat.
2.2
Emission Hotspots and Decarbonization Challenges in
the Basic Chemical Industry
The basic chemical industry represents a cornerstone of Germany’s industrial landscape
and accounts for a substantial portion of the nation’s process-related greenhouse gas
emissions [3]. The production of foundational chemicals such as ammonia, methanol, and
ethylene involves energy-intensive processes and a heavy reliance on fossil feedstocks.
This section reviews the emission profiles of these key chemical products and explores
pathways for their decarbonization, particularly through green hydrogen and electrification
technologies.
2.2.1
Ammonia: A Key Emission Source and Decarbonization
Opportunity
Ammonia is synthesized primarily via the Haber-Bosch process, which combines hydro-
gen and atmospheric nitrogen under high temperature and pressure. As a critical pre-
cursor in nitrogen-based fertilizer production and polyamide synthesis, ammonia is indis-
pensable in agriculture and various manufacturing applications. Due to its high hydrogen
density and favorable storage and transport properties, ammonia is also gaining attention
as a potential energy carrier, particularly in maritime shipping and power generation [14].
The decarbonization of ammonia production hinges on replacing fossil-based hydrogen
(grey hydrogen) with green hydrogen derived from electrolysis. Importantly, the transi-

==PAGE:28==
tion to green hydrogen does not necessitate significant changes to the ammonia synthe-
sis loop, since the core reactants, H2 and N2, remain the same. This enables existing
ammonia plants to be retrofitted relatively easily for green hydrogen integration, thereby
minimizing capital-intensive modifications [13]. Moreover, the global infrastructure for am-
monia synthesis, handling, and transportation is already well established, supporting its
role in a future hydrogen economy.
Ammonia is also central to the production of urea (CH4N2O), formed by reacting ammonia
with carbon dioxide (CO2). As a widely used fertilizer, urea contributes to greenhouse gas
emissions both through the release of CO2 during production and the emission of nitrous
oxide (N2O), a potent greenhouse gas, during its application in agriculture. A sustainable
transformation of the fertilizer industry therefore requires two key strategies: sourcing
CO2 from non-fossil origins (e.g., biomass or direct air capture), and decarbonizing the
hydrogen input through renewable electrolysis [12].
2.2.2
Methanol: Hydrogen Demand and Process Emissions
Methanol serves as a crucial intermediate in the production of chemicals such as formalde-
hyde, acetic acid, and plastics. It also holds potential as a clean-burning fuel or fuel
additive. In 2017, Germany produced approximately 1.05 million tonnes of methanol.
Given that methanol production requires roughly 0.189 tonnes of hydrogen per tonne of
methanol, this translates to a hydrogen demand of about 200 kilotonnes annually, equiv-
alent to approximately 9 TWh of energy [3].
Conventional methanol production typically involves three key stages: synthesis gas gen-
eration, crude methanol synthesis, and purification. The synthesis gas, a mixture of H2,
CO, and CO2, is commonly produced through steam methane reforming or partial oxida-
tion, both carbon-intensive methods. Consequently, decarbonizing methanol production
will require either the use of green hydrogen and captured CO2, or alternative pathways
based on biomass or renewable electricity [3].
2.2.3
Ethylene: Emissions from Thermal Cracking
Ethylene is one of the most important basic chemicals, widely used in the production
of plastics, solvents, and other high-value chemicals. It is typically produced through
the steam cracking of hydrocarbon feedstocks, such as naphtha. This high-temperature
process also yields co-products like propylene and butene. In 2017, ethylene production
in Germany reached approximately 5.2 million metric tonnes [3].
Steam cracking is extremely energy-intensive and relies almost entirely on fossil-derived
thermal energy. The composition and yield of the output products depend heavily on the
type of feedstock used. Given its dependence on high-temperature heat, ethylene pro-
duction poses significant challenges for decarbonization unless alternative heat sources,
such as electrification, can be adopted [3].
2.2.4
Decarbonization Strategies: Electrification and Hydrogen
Integration
To address the emissions intensity of the chemical industry, a number of technological
pathways are being actively explored. Among these, electrification of industrial process
heat offers significant potential. Power-to-Heat technologies, including electrically heated
steam crackers and calciners, are being developed to provide high-temperature heat from
renewable electricity. These systems could replace fossil-based energy in sectors like
ammonia and ethylene production [34].
Power-to-Heat systems exhibit high energy efficiency, particularly when combined with
high-temperature heat pumps capable of recovering waste heat. Compared to hydrogen-

hydrogen use must align with legal definitions of what qualifies as “green.” The next

and classification of renewable hydrogen.
2.3
Regulatory Definitions of Green Hydrogen in the
European Union
While electrolysis technologies such as Alkaline Water Electrolysis (AE), Proton Exchange
Membrane Electrolysis (PEM), and Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cells (SOEC) are technically
capable of producing zero-carbon hydrogen when powered by renewable electricity, the
classification of hydrogen as “green” depends not only on the production method, but also
on specific regulatory criteria.
In the context of the European Union, hydrogen is only considered “green” or renewable if
it meets certain legal and environmental requirements. These are primarily outlined in the
Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2023/1184, which defines under what conditions
hydrogen can be classified as a renewable fuel of non-biological origin (RFNBO) [16]. The
regulation is directly applicable and legally binding in all EU Member States, ensuring a
harmonised approach across the single market.
Hydrogen qualifies as renewable when the electricity used for its production meets specific
criteria. This is straightforward in cases where electricity is generated on-site or directly
connected to the electrolyser without using the public grid. In such cases, the renewable
electricity facility must not be older than 36 months at the time the electrolyser begins
operation [16]. However, if the electricity is drawn from the grid, it can only be consid-
ered renewable if the conditions of additionality, geographical correlation, and temporal
correlation are met [16]. These three principles are described in more detail below.
2.3.1
Additionality
The additionality principle requires that the electricity used for hydrogen production origi-
nates from newly installed renewable sources. This can be achieved either by co-locating
renewable generation with the electrolyser or by signing a Power Purchase Agreement
(PPA) with a renewable electricity supplier. To qualify, the renewable energy installa-
tion must have been commissioned no more than 36 months before the electrolyser and
must not have received public investment or operational subsidies. A transitional period
is granted for early projects: installations that begin operating before 1 January 2028 are
temporarily exempt from this rule. However, they will need to comply with the additionality
requirement starting from 1 January 2038 [16].

2.3.2
Geographical Correlation
Geographical correlation ensures that renewable electricity used for electrolysis is physi-
cally linked to the location of hydrogen production. According to the regulation, one of the
following spatial conditions must be satisfied:
• The renewable electricity generation and hydrogen production occur within the same
bidding zone.
• The electricity is supplied from a neighbouring bidding zone, where day-ahead elec-
tricity market prices are equal to or higher than in the electrolyser’s zone.
• The electricity originates from offshore renewable generation that is directly con-
nected to the same bidding zone as the electrolyser.
These criteria are intended to support transparency and ensure that claims of renewable
hydrogen production are physically credible. Additionally, EU Member States may in-
troduce more specific geographical requirements to align the certification process with
national energy planning and infrastructure strategies [16].
2.3.3
Temporal Correlation
Temporal correlation refers to the alignment between the time of renewable electricity
generation and the time of hydrogen production. This ensures that green hydrogen is
actually produced during periods of renewable energy availability.
Until 31 December 2029, this condition is considered fulfilled if hydrogen is produced
within the same calendar month as the associated renewable electricity. This rule also
applies if the electricity is taken from a newly installed storage system that is either directly
connected to the renewable generator or the electrolyser, and was charged within the
same calendar month.
Starting from 1 January 2030, the requirement becomes stricter. From that point onward,
the electricity used for hydrogen production must be generated within the same one-hour
interval. Alternatively, electricity can be drawn from a newly installed, co-located storage
system that was charged during the same hourly interval through a PPA with a renewable
energy provider.
However, an exception is made during periods of very low electricity prices. If the clearing
price on the electricity market falls below €20 per megawatt-hour, or below 0.36 times
the cost of emitting one tonne of carbon dioxide, temporal correlation is automatically
considered to be fulfilled. This provision acknowledges that such price levels typically
indicate high renewable energy availability [16].

In Germany, this effort is anchored by the Federal Climate Change Act, which sets bind-
ing greenhouse gas reduction targets through 2045. Complementing this overarching
climate policy, Germany’s National Hydrogen Strategy outlines the country’s approach to
scaling hydrogen production, infrastructure, and industrial integration. The following sec-

economy.

Germany’s Path to Climate Neutrality: Policy and

2.4.1
Federal Climate Change Act (KSG)
The Federal Climate Change Act (KSG) [36] establishes the statutory framework for Ger-
many’s national climate policy, defining binding Greenhouse Gas (GHG) reduction targets
aimed at achieving climate neutrality by 2045. This overarching goal is supported by in-
terim targets requiring a reduction of GHG emissions by at least 65% by 2030 and at
least 88% by 2040, both relative to 1990 levels. Beyond 2050, the act also mandates the
pursuit of negative emissions to offset unavoidable residual emissions [36].
The KSG sets sector-specific annual emission budgets covering key economic domains
such as energy, industry, buildings, transport, agriculture, and waste management, as
well as Land Use, Land-Use Change, and Forestry (LULUCF). These annual budgets ex-
tend through 2030 and aim to ensure a transparent and predictable emissions reduction
trajectory. The German Federal Government is obligated to revise and extend this reduc-
tion pathway in line with updated scientific insights and evolving European Union (EU)
climate targets.
Compliance monitoring is entrusted to the Federal Environment Agency. If any sector
exceeds its allocated emission budget, the responsible ministry must, within three months,
submit an immediate action program to bring emissions back within statutory limits. These
mechanisms are designed to institutionalise accountability and enable adaptive policy
responses in the face of changing economic and environmental conditions [36].
By establishing legally binding targets and defining governance mechanisms, KSG serves
as a central legislative instrument in Germany’s decarbonization pathway. It aligns with
the goals of the Paris Agreement [37] and provides a national foundation for EU-wide
climate policies.
To operationalise these emission targets, particularly in hard-to-abate sectors, Germany
has identified hydrogen as a key technology for decarbonization. The following section
presents an in-depth analysis of Germany’s National Hydrogen Strategy (NWS), which
outlines a structured approach to fostering a hydrogen-based economy.
2.4.2
Germany’s National Hydrogen Strategy (NWS)
NWS, published in July 2023, establishes a coordinated framework to scale hydrogen
production, infrastructure, and sectoral deployment in pursuit of the 2045 climate neutrality
target [8]. The strategy outlines six main action areas, supported by quantitative objectives
and targeted policy instruments.
A core element is the expansion of domestic electrolysis capacity. Germany aims to in-
crease installed capacity from 5 GW to at least 10 GW by 2030. Of this, 2.5 GW is to
be realised through the IPCEI Wasserstoff initiative, 1.3 GW via indirect national funding
mechanisms, and 1 GW through a new directive supporting offshore electrolysis. An addi-
tional 2 GW will be supported under the revised Renewable Energy Directive (RED II) for
transport applications, while 0.2 GW is expected from the “Reallabore der Energiewende”
(Living Labs of the Energy Transition). The remaining 3 GW will be achieved through
broader innovation and market-based instruments, including the ”H2Giga” program for
industrial-scale electrolyser manufacturing [8].
Given the projected hydrogen demand of 95 to 130 TWh by 2030, Germany anticipates
that 45 to 90 TWh (50–70%) will need to be imported. To this end, the federal government
is developing a diversified import strategy. This includes the creation of hydrogen-ready

EU and international standards, along with targeted education and training programmes,
form additional pillars of the implementation strategy [8].
In summary, Germany’s updated hydrogen strategy presents a multidimensional approach
to fostering a hydrogen economy. It leverages domestic capacity, international supply
chains, and sectoral integration to support the transition to a low-carbon economy, con-
sistent with the goals of the KSG.

Current Use of Hydrogen in the European Union
By the end of 2023, hydrogen production in the European Union comprised a total of 512
operational facilities, reflecting steady growth within the sector. These facilities repre-

actual hydrogen output reaching 7.94 Mt, corresponding to an average capacity utilization
rate of 71% [38]. Despite this progress, the vast majority of hydrogen, 99.1%, was still

from industrial by-products.

the margins, jointly contributing less than 1% of Europe’s total production capacity [38].
Nevertheless, interest in electrolysis is accelerating: 141 projects were in operation by
2023, 88 of which exceeded 0.5 megawatts (MW) in scale. Collectively, these installa-
tions provided 258 MW of capacity, an increase of nearly 48% compared to the previous
year. Looking ahead, an additional 1,857 MW in electrolysis capacity is currently under
development and anticipated to become operational between 2024 and 2026.

Methodology
To address the objectives and research questions specified in chapter 1, the following
chapter presents a computational framework was created to minimizing the Net Present
Cost (NPC) of different hydrogen supply chains in Germany. The Mixed-Integer Linear
Programming (MILP) model evaluates the optimal sizing of components along the sup-
ply chain, including renewable energy generation, hydrogen production via electrolysis,
compression, storage, and delivery infrastructure. The following chapter details the con-
ceptual structure of the model, outlining the key components and parameter integrated
into the simulation and the methodological approaches. The methodology integrates both
technological performance parameters and economic data, enabling a holistic evaluation
of competing design scenarios.

The overall structure of the optimization framework developed in this study is summarized

Technology Justification
This study selects Alkaline Water Electrolysis (AE) as the reference technology for hy-
drogen production. Among available electrolyzer types, alkaline systems are the most
technologically mature and commercially established, with the largest installed capacity
globally for industrial-scale applications [53]. Despite their lower efficiency and operational

flexibility compared to PEM electrolyzers, alkaline systems are significantly more cost-

hydrogen deployment [54]. The advantages of alkaline electrolysis include widespread
market availability, lower reliance on scarce or precious materials, longer operational life-

Technological Parameter Description
Building on the above, this section outlines the key technical assumptions used to repre-
sent the electrolyzer system in the model. The assumed stack lifetime varies across the
literature and depends on the electrolyzer technology and operational conditions. For in-
stance, Krishnan et al. [55] use 4,000 full-load hours (FLH) over an 8-year period, whereas
Ajeeb et al. [56] report 10 years at 8,300 operating hours per year. Gerloff et al. [57] es-
timate 60,000 to 75,000 hours of operation, and Koj et al. [58] forecast an increase from
60,000 to 80,000 hours by 2045. Superchi et al. [53] use 10 years as a default assump-
tion. Based on this literature, a stack lifetime of 10 years is applied in this study.
For the overall plant lifetime, Park et al. [20] assume 25 years, while Ajeeb et al. [56]
model 20 years. To align with the project simulation horizon and to reflect standard as-
sumptions in techno-economic optimization studies, a plant lifetime of 20 years is adopted
in this work.
Reported efficiency values for alkaline electrolysis systems also vary significantly. IRENA
[59] reports a wide range from 50 to 78 kWh/kg H2, reflecting differing assumptions and
system boundaries. Koj et al. [58] assume 55 kWh/kg H2 (60.06% efficiency), while
Ajeeb et al. [56] report a stack-level efficiency of 80.5%; including balance-of-plant (BoP)
electricity use, this decreases to 72.4%, or 54.3 kWh/kg H2 total.
To reflect current technology trends, this study applies efficiency data from Green Hydro-
gen Systems’ commercial high-pressure AE system for the year 2025 [60]. The electricity
demand is set to 51 kWh/kg H2 at the stack level, and 54.7 kWh/kg H2 for the complete
system including BoP consumption. BoP includes hydrogen purification (99.97% vol.),
internal compression, cooling, and electrolyte circulation.
Stack degradation is modeled based on the findings of Jang et al. [61], who estimate a
1.3% efficiency loss per 10,000 hours of operation. For a total 60,000-hour stack lifetime,
this corresponds to approximately 0.78% degradation per year. In the optimization model,
this is simplified to a total degradation of 3.9% over the lifetime. The resulting average
electrolyzer system efficiency is therefore 57.03%, or 58.44 kWh/kg H2.
Operational Constraints of the Electrolyzer
The dynamic behavior and operational feasibility of the electrolyzer are represented through

The electrolyzer’s modular structure and on/off behavior are modeled using linearized

Case Study
The developed methodology is applied in a case study of Germany’s chemical industry,
specifically focusing on the decarbonization of fossil-based ammonia production.
The case study is based on the total annual demand of the ammonia production of the
company INEOS in West Germany. INEOS Köln is a major producer of base chemicals,
including ammonia, which is essential for fertilizers, cleaning agents, and industrial pro-
cesses. The site integrates ammonia production within its large-scale petrochemical oper-
ations, leveraging naphtha cracking and hydrogen synthesis. As part of the INEOS Group,
the Cologne facility supports regional agricultural and chemical supply chains through its
ammonia output [77].
4.1
Scenario Description
To assess cost-optimal decarbonization pathways for such a large-scale, hard-to-abate
industrial site, the model is applied to simulate and compare two distinct hydrogen supply
scenarios. The first scenario focuses on decentral production and storage in a salt cavern
in northern Germany in proximity to Wilhelmshaven, while the second scenario incorpo-
rates onsite hydrogen production and storage in H2 steel tanks. These are designed to
reflect plausible future pathways for green ammonia production in Germany’s industrial
heartland, particularly in North Rhine-Westphalia a region with dense industrial activity
and a concentration of chemical manufacturers. By comparing these two scenarios under
consistent techno-economic assumptions and hourly energy system modeling, the study
aims to identify the most cost-efficient and technically viable green hydrogen supply chain
configuration for decarbonizing ammonia production at utility scale. In the following sce-
narios, both HESS and BESS are included and analyzed, in line with the findings of Li et
al., who recommend incorporating both storage types for large-scale renewable energy
integration to support future decarbonization targets [52].
4.1.1
Scenario 1: Decentralized Production and Storage
In the first scenario, green hydrogen is produced remotely in northern Germany, where
favorable conditions for large-scale renewable energy integration and geological hydro-
gen storage exist. The system layout includes offshore wind, onshore wind, and solar
PV generation, supported by BESS. These supply an electrolyzer located near Sande,
Lower Saxony. The produced hydrogen is compressed, stored in a nearby underground
salt cavern, and later transported via pipeline to meet industrial demand at the INEOS
ammonia production facility in North Rhine-Westphalia. Hydrogen pipeline transport is
selected in this study as the preferred delivery method due to its superior economic per-
formance over alternative options such as compressed or liquefied trailer transport and
chemical hydrogen carriers (e.g., methanol, ammonia), particularly across medium to long
distances ranging from 500 to 2000 km [52].
The selection of this site is driven by multiple locational advantages.
First, the northern coastal region around Wilhelmshaven and Emden is among Germany’s
key landing zones for offshore wind transmission infrastructure. Specifically, the DolWin6
project, an HVDC connection with a 900 MW capacity, lands near the designated elec-
trolyzer location and connects to a converter station in Emden [78]. This provides access
to substantial renewable electricity flows.

4.1.2
Scenario 2: Centralized Production and Storage
The second scenario analyzes a fully integrated hydrogen supply chain situated directly at
the ammonia production site. This setup represents a localized configuration in which hy-

thereby minimizing dependence on external supply and transport infrastructure.
The system layout, illustrated in fig. 4.2, comprises co-located components including do-

Hydrogen Storage Technology
Several hydrogen storage technologies were evaluated for this scenario. Liquefied hydro-
gen, although featuring high volumetric energy density, was excluded due to the extremely
high energy consumption and technical complexity associated with its liquefaction pro-
cess. Hydrogen must be cooled to below −253◦C, which requires intensive pre-cooling
steps and typically involves liquid nitrogen evaporation. Hourly operation for discharge
and charge as well as part load operation are infeasible. Additionally, the complexity of
operation and cooling management makes the approach economically and energetically
inefficient for onsite applications [80]. Storage in pipelines was also deemed unsuitable.
While analogous natural gas systems can store around 12 tonnes of hydrogen per kilo-
meter, such storage requires underground infrastructure, and to date, no operational hy-
drogen pipeline storage systems exist at commercial scale [13]. Similarly, spherical pres-
sure vessels were ruled out due to the absence of technical and economic data specific
to hydrogen use in such configurations [81]. Instead, this scenario implements modu-
lar high-pressure tank systems, specifically, Multiple Element Gas Containers (MEGCs).
These containers operate at 500 bar and are already in commercial use for hydrogen

4.4
Curtailment
Given the high penetration of Variable Renewable Energy (VRE) sources, namely offshore
wind, onshore wind, and solar PV, in both modeled scenarios, temporary oversupply of
electricity is an anticipated and inherent outcome. Since the system is dimensioned to
meet the full annual hydrogen demand for ammonia production, there are periods during
which total renewable generation exceeds the instantaneous electricity demand from the
electrolyzer and battery charging.
To account for this, curtailment is calculated exogenously, outside of the optimization
framework, by applying the system’s hourly energy balance as defined in eq. (3.6). If the
total renewable output in a given hour surpasses the combined electricity consumption
for electrolysis and battery charging, the surplus is recorded as curtailed energy.
Two cases are distinguished:
• Non-compensated curtailment, in which surplus electricity is discarded without
compensation, and the full capital expenditure for the oversized renewable genera-
tion remains with the system. This case represents a worst-case economic scenario
with no recovery of investment through surplus utilization.
• Grid export of surplus electricity, where excess generation is sold to the elec-
tricity market at hourly day-ahead spot prices obtained from the German wholesale
electricity market [96], as illustrated in figs. 4.9a and 4.9b. This option allows partial
recovery of curtailment-related losses and illustrates the economic value of flexible
grid integration.

4.5
Operational Assumptions
To ensure a fair and methodologically consistent comparison of the two hydrogen supply
chain scenarios, several modeling principles are applied uniformly. Most notably, both
scenarios utilize identical hourly renewable electricity generation profiles, derived from
the same resource availability and technology specifications. This approach guarantees
comparability in system configuration assessments and enables a direct evaluation of
optimal component sizing and operational strategies.
It is further assumed that hydrogen production and consumption must be balanced on an
hourly basis. Consequently, neither linepack nor pipeline storage effects are considered.
This assumption reflects a strictly synchronized supply–demand relationship and models
the constraints of a tightly integrated energy infrastructure, where production must meet
consumption in every time step. Under these conditions, hydrogen storage systems play
a critical role in bridging intra-day imbalances and buffering the variability of renewable
energy supply.
In line with the broader decarbonization strategy of the hydrogen supply chain, this study
assumes that all compressor units are fully electrified and, analogous to the electrolyzer,
are powered through long-term PPAs for renewable electricity eq. (3.7) and eq. (3.6).
This operational assumption ensures that compression does not contribute significantly
to upstream Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions. As emphasized by Riemer et al. [99],
the electrification of compressors using renewable energy sources is a key measure to
minimize life-cycle emissions in green hydrogen systems.

Electrolyzer Operation and Hydrogen Production
The simulation results reveal two fundamentally distinct system behaviors, shaped by the
respective storage configurations and the integration of renewable electricity sources.
Scenario 1 is characterized by the deployment of a large-scale salt cavern for hydrogen
storage with a total capacity of 197 GWh, while omitting any battery storage (see ta-

and industrial demand by allowing surplus renewable electricity to be converted and stored
as hydrogen over extended periods. With an assumed usable depth-of-discharge of 80%
(see table 3.19), the storage provides approximately 814 hours of autonomy, allowing it to
cover up to 10% of the annual hydrogen demand independently. This flexibility reduces
the need for continuous electrolyzer operation and permits the system to operate strate-

increased electrolyzer capacity factor of 60.15%, compared to 53.34% in Scenario 1 (see
fig. 5.14).

Curtailment
Although offshore wind offers high full-load hours (FLH), it is excluded from both scenar-
ios due to its elevated capital expenditure of €3,000,000 per MW, resulting in a LCOE
of 84.65 €/MWh. This LCOE is nearly double that of onshore wind and solar PV, ren-
dering offshore wind economically non-competitive under the modeled techno-economic
framework (see table 5.1).
The composition of the renewable energy mix further influences system behavior. Sce-
nario 1 employs a relatively balanced deployment of onshore wind and solar PV (855 MW
vs. 732 MW; see table 5.2), whereas Scenario 2 is more heavily skewed towards on-
shore wind (1,645 MW vs. 1,079 MW; see table 5.5). This results in a more continuous
and nighttime-oriented generation profile in Scenario 2, where onshore wind contributes
significantly to baseload supply. As a consequence, hydrogen production in Scenario 2
is characterized by increased stability and extended operation during night hours (see
fig. 5.9a), which compensates for the reduced flexibility of the storage system.
However, the increased renewable capacity in Scenario 2, implemented to offset limited
storage availability, leads to substantial curtailment. The system discards approximately
2,757 GWh annually, equivalent to 48% of total renewable generation (see table 5.7). In
comparison, Scenario 1 achieves a much lower curtailment rate of 161.31 GWh per year,
or roughly 5% of total generation (see table 5.4). Curtailment in Scenario 1 is concentrated
in March, coinciding with peak onshore wind output (see fig. 5.1b) and a saturated salt
cavern. Daytime PV peaks also contribute to surplus generation (see fig. 5.4a, fig. 5.2a).
In Scenario 2, curtailment peaks in January and October (see fig. 5.10b), aligning with
periods of elevated wind generation and scheduled downtime at the ammonia plant. On
a sub-daily timescale, Scenario 2 exhibits more consistent curtailment levels throughout
the day (see fig. 5.10a), due to the high and stable contribution from onshore wind across
diurnal hours. This yields a flatter and more uniform generation profile compared to Sce-
nario 1.
Storage
The operation of energy storage systems varies significantly between scenarios, reflect-
ing distinct strategies for temporal balancing. In Scenario 1, the salt cavern exhibits low
cycling frequency, completing approximately 2.9 full equivalent charge–discharge cycles
annually. This confirms its role as a seasonal buffer, with charging concentrated in wind-
rich periods from late winter through early spring and discharging occurring primarily in
summer, when wind yields are reduced (see fig. 5.5).
The low utilization rate may suggest an oversizing of HESS in Scenario 1, potentially re-
sulting in underused capacity. However, the system’s ability to provide extended-duration
discharge supports strategic seasonal balancing and operational stability. In contrast, the
HESS in Scenario 2, comprised of pressurized steel tanks, completes 20.42 full equiva-
lent cycles per year, indicating a short-term balancing role. Frequent occurrences of the

Economics
From an economic standpoint, Scenario 1 achieves a lower LCOH, amounting to 4.77 €/kg.
This cost structure is primarily dominated by renewable electricity expenditures, which ac-

include the electrolyzer at 0.992 €/kg and the salt cavern hydrogen storage system at
0.154 €/kg (see fig. 5.6). No battery-related expenditures are incurred, as battery stor-
age is not utilized in this scenario. The economic benefit of curtailed electricity sales in
Scenario 1 remains marginal due to the limited volume of excess electricity and the rela-
tively small differential between the system’s renewable LCOE and prevailing spot market
prices (see table 5.4).
In contrast, Scenario 2 results in a substantially higher base LCOH of 7.91 €/kg. This

inclusion of battery storage. Renewable electricity costs alone amount to 6.408 €/kg, con-

at approximately €2 billion over the project lifetime, also lowers the renewable electric-
ity share in the LCOH from 81.01% to 63.90%. This illustrates the potential economic
value of market-oriented curtailment management in systems with significant renewable
overcapacity.

6
Discussion
The motivation behind this study is the pressing need to transform the current heavily
fossil-based chemical industry in Germany. This motivation guided the development of the
analytical framework detailed in chapter 3, characterized by techno-economic constraints
and parameter inputs extensively sourced from relevant literature. The primary goal was
to identify the optimal techno-economic pathway to decarbonize the chemical industry,
with a particular focus on an ammonia production facility, utilizing certified green hydrogen,
consistent with the regulatory framework of the European Union. This chapter provides
a critical evaluation of the results and the methodological approach used to answer the
research objective defined in chapter 1. The findings are systematically compared with
those of recent European studies to provide contextual insight and validate the modeling
assumptions and outcomes.
6.1
Evaluation of Research Questions
6.1.1
Comparative Cost Analysis of Centralized vs. Decentralized
Hydrogen Supply
This section presents a comparative evaluation of centralized and decentralized hydrogen
supply chain configurations, focusing on their ability to achieve the lowest LCOH under
realistic techno-economic and operational constraints relevant to industrial-scale applica-
tions.
Due to methodological inconsistencies in how LCOH is defined across the literature, this
study adopts a differentiated approach by evaluating three distinct LCOH configurations:
(1) Full supply chain LCOH including battery and hydrogen storage as well as revenues
from electricity sales, (2) Total supply chain LCOH excluding post-production grid rev-
enues, and (3) LCOH based solely on renewable generation and electrolyzer capital costs,
in line with minimal system boundary assessments.
This tiered methodology enables comparison across a wide range of published values and
techno-economic assumptions. Importantly, the centralized configuration (Scenario 2)
achieves a lower LCOH than the decentralized system (Scenario 1) only in the first case,
when revenues from curtailed electricity sales are included. Under all other boundary
conditions, the decentralized approach remains economically superior.
LCOH with Full System Boundaries and Grid Revenues
When considering the entire hydrogen supply chain and incorporating revenues from cur-
tailed electricity sales, LCOH is calculated at 4.621 €/kg for the decentralized configuration
(Scenario 1) and 4.160 €/kg for the centralized system (Scenario 2). These values align
with the lower end of the LCOH spectrum reported by Stolte et al. [19], who identified a
range of 3.5–8.9 €/kg for various European locations under full supply chain modeling that
also includes revenue from surplus electricity fed into the grid.
A fundamental difference between the two studies lies in the system boundaries and cost
assumptions. While Stolte et al. [19] allow for grid electricity purchases with a price of
150€/MWh, the present thesis exclusively models renewable-based hydrogen production,
without grid imports, thus adhering to stricter decarbonization criteria. The high prices
of electricity further increase the LCOH. Moreover, Stolte et al. [19] assume significantly
higher capital expenditures, 1,099,000 €/MW for PEM electrolyzers and 1,048,000 €/MWp
72

==PAGE:84==
for PV systems, approximately twice the investment costs applied in this study. These
higher costs help explain the comparatively elevated LCOH outcomes in their analysis.
Despite these methodological differences, the results of this study confirm the economic
viability of both supply chain configurations under favorable techno-economic conditions.
The relatively low LCOH values are primarily attributed to the use of alkaline electrolysis
technology, which offers lower specific CapEx, and more optimistic assumptions for solar
PV costs.
It is important to note, that Scenario 2 only achieves cost parity, and ultimately a lower
LCOH, when surplus electricity sales are permitted. The centralized system benefits from
significant overdimensioning of renewable generation, which is not penalized within the
model. Since curtailment incurs no financial penalty in the optimization model and only
electricity revenues are accounted for, the high volumes of curtailed electricity in Sce-
nario 2 can be monetized through grid injection, thereby substantially reducing its effec-
tive hydrogen production cost. This condition is pivotal to the economic performance of
Scenario 2.
LCOH Excluding Revenue from Surplus Electricity
Excluding revenues from surplus electricity significantly alters the LCOH outcome and
highlights structural differences between the two modeled supply chain configurations.
Under these conditions, Scenario 1 (decentralized with salt cavern storage) results in an
LCOH of 4.77 €/kg, whereas Scenario 2 (centralized with tank storage) reaches 7.91 €/kg.
This substantial difference of over 3 €/kg underscores the economic advantage of decen-
tralized hydrogen systems.
When benchmarked against the findings of Dautel et al. [21], the LCOH values observed
in this study are notably lower. Dautel et al. [21] report LCOH values of 11.3 €/kg for a
salt cavern-based system and 21.1 €/kg for a tank-based configuration, both significantly
higher than those derived in this study. The divergence primarily arises from differing
methodological assumptions, most notably the use of Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM)
electrolysis in Dautel et al. [21], which carries substantially higher capital costs than the
alkaline electrolysis technology employed in this study. Moreover, Dautel et al. [21] as-
sume higher electricity prices and rely on a regional setting (Sweden) with limited solar
potential, which restricts access to low-cost PV electricity. In contrast, the present study
assumes favorable solar irradiation and cost conditions, which enable a larger share of
solar PV in the generation mix and contribute to overall LCOH reductions.
Additionally, Dautel et al. [21] incorporate a cost structure that includes significant CapEx
for the development of a new hydrogen pipeline network. In contrast, this study assumes
the existence of an operational hydrogen pipeline infrastructure, applying only transport
tariffs rather than full infrastructure investment costs. While this assumption reflects an op-
timistic future scenario aligned with planned hydrogen backbones in Europe, it represents
a crucial simplification. From a critical perspective, assuming a fully built and accessible
pipeline network, despite its current absence, reduces the cost burden on decentralized
systems and may lead to an underestimation of total system costs in real-world applica-
tions.
Importantly, both studies converge on a core finding: centralized hydrogen supply chains
with short-duration tank storage are significantly more expensive than decentralized sys-
tems supported by seasonal-scale salt cavern storage. The results of this study reinforce
this conclusion, emphasizing that storage scalability and integration with low-cost renew-
able energy sources are key drivers of cost efficiency in green hydrogen production.

Minimal Boundary LCOH: RES and Electrolyzer Cost Only
To ensure methodological comparability with existing studies that isolate the cost contri-
butions of renewable electricity and electrolysis, this section reports LCOH values limited
to these two components. Under this restricted definition, Scenario 1 achieves an LCOH
of 4.51 €/kg, comprising 3.52 €/kg from renewable electricity and 0.99 €/kg from the elec-
trolyzer (see fig. 5.6). Scenario 2, by contrast, results in an LCOH of 7.28 €/kg, with
renewable electricity contributing 6.40 €/kg and electrolysis 0.88 €/kg (see fig. 5.13).
These values fall within the broader European cost range reported by [38], which spans
4.13–9.30 €/kg, with a mean of 6.61 €/kg.
The relatively low LCOH in Scenario 1 under this configuration can also be attributed to
several favorable assumptions. Notably, the model assumes perfect foresight of renew-
able generation and demand, enabling optimal system scheduling without operational un-
certainty. Scenario 1 also benefits from access to favorable coastal renewable resources
and employs theoretical system sizing optimized for the base year only. These assump-
tions may contribute to an underestimation of real-world LCOH when spatial, temporal,
and technical variability are considered.
Scenario 2, by contrast, suffers from its structural limitations under these same assump-
tions. The hydrogen tank storage is capped at 9.17 GWh due to feasibility constraints, and
the system is forced to match hydrogen production and consumption on an hourly basis.
Without the possibility of long-term storage or flexible dispatch, the system requires sub-
stantial renewable oversizing to meet continuous demand, which inflates the attributed
LCOH for renewable electricity.
Importantly, while Scenario 1’s low LCOH value of 4.51 €/kg under this restricted metric
highlights the potential of decentralized, long-duration storage-based hydrogen systems,
it also reflects a set of optimistic and simplified modeling assumptions. Conversely, the
higher LCOH of 7.28 €/kg in Scenario 2 underscores the limited storage flexibility and
redundant renewable capacity under constrained hourly balancing. These findings high-
light that while simplified LCOH comparisons are useful for benchmarking, they require
cautious interpretation in the context of real-world implementation and system complexity.
Summary: Economic Competitiveness of Supply Configurations
LCOH comparison across three boundary definitions confirms the superior economic per-
formance of the decentralized hydrogen supply chain (Scenario 1) in nearly all cases.
Only when surplus electricity sales are allowed does the centralized system (Scenario 2)
achieve a lower LCOH, driven by renewable oversizing and the absence of curtailment
penalties.
Scenario 1 benefits from flexible long-duration storage and optimized renewable integra-
tion, resulting in consistently lower costs. By contrast, Scenario 2 is constrained by limited
storage capacity and rigid hourly balancing, which inflate costs under realistic assump-
tions.
These findings highlight that cost competitiveness in green hydrogen systems depends
strongly on storage scalability, spatial flexibility, and system integration. They also under-
line the importance of consistent LCOH definitions to ensure comparability across studies,
as emphasized by [17].

Supply
This section investigates the optimal sizing strategies for key system components, renew-
able generation, electrolyzers, and storage technologies, in both centralized and decen-

tralized configurations, with the aim of minimizing system costs while ensuring a secure
and uninterrupted industrial hydrogen supply.
The modeling outcomes of this study present a comprehensive techno-economic assess-
ment of optimal system configurations for centralized and decentralized green hydrogen
supply chains. The findings highlight that system design is strongly influenced by the avail-
ability of spatial resources and geological storage potential. Following the approach of
Marocco et al. [42], key sizing parameters, such as renewable-to-electrolyzer and storage-

scales and boundary conditions. This methodological choice enables alignment with ex-

under diverse techno-economic constraints.
Sourcing Strategy: RES-to-Electrolyzer Ratios
In the decentralized scenario (Scenario 1), the system achieves cost-optimality through a
combination of moderate renewable generation capacity, a large-scale salt cavern hydro-
gen storage facility, and the exclusion of battery storage. The total installed renewable
capacity is 1,587 MW, while the electrolyzer is dimensioned at 636 MW, resulting in a
generation-to-electrolyzer ratio of approximately 2.5. The inclusion of 197 GWh of salt
cavern storage allows for extensive temporal decoupling between hydrogen production
and demand, enabling the electrolyzer to operate flexibly over the year.
In contrast, the centralized configuration (Scenario 2) installs 2,724 MW of renewable ca-
pacity with a smaller 564 MW electrolyzer, corresponding to a significantly higher capacity
ratio of 4.82. Limited onsite storage capacity (9.17 GWh in pressure tanks) and spatial
constraints necessitate a substantially larger renewable supply and the integration of a
261 MWh battery to ensure hourly balancing. The different strategies reflect trade-offs
between spatial availability, storage autonomy, and operational flexibility.
The renewable-to-electrolyzer ratio observed in Scenario 1 lies well within the range iden-
tified by Marocco et al. [42], who report optimal values of 2.2 for PV-based systems and
2.8 for wind-only configurations. Their analysis of hybrid PV-wind systems yields an ap-
proximate ratio of 3.2, depending on location-specific resource quality. However, it must
be noted that the study by Marocco et al. [42] does not consider industrial hydrogen de-
mand or the full supply chain, nor does it integrate storage dynamics. Moreover, their
approach relies on purely economic optimization and excludes spatial or technical con-
straints explicitly considered in this study.
Further support for the result in Scenario 1 is provided by Dautel et al. [21], who report a
renewable-to-electrolyzer ratio of 2.1 for cavern-based systems, closely aligned with the
2.5 observed here. This convergence underscores the value of low-cost, scalable geo-
logical storage in minimizing RES overdimensioning while enabling efficient and flexible
electrolyzer operation.
By contrast, Scenario 2’s higher ratio of 4.82 significantly exceeds typical values reported
in the literature. This oversizing is primarily driven by strict space limitations on hydrogen
storage, which prevent longer-duration buffering. To maintain uninterrupted supply and
comply with hourly balancing constraints, the system compensates by expanding RES
capacity and using short-term BESS. Although technically feasible, this design sacrifices
economic efficiency.
It should also be noted that both modeled scenarios use identical renewable genera-
tion profiles and capacity factors. While this ensures comparability, it overlooks regional
resource variability, especially relevant when contrasting a decentralized offshore con-

figuration with a space-constrained industrial site. Although Dautel et al. [21] also rely
on shared RES assumptions for comparative consistency, omitting spatial heterogeneity
represents a notable simplification in this study. Additionally, potential grid contingency
constraints, such as infrastructure bottlenecks or connection limits, are not considered but
could further affect optimal RES sizing in real-world deployments.
In this context, Dautel et al. [21] report a RES-to-electrolyzer ratio of 1.9 for systems
using tank storage, yet their model does not impose a physical cap on tank volume. As a
result, their configurations do not require the same degree of RES oversizing to maintain

The comparison reveals that Scenario 1 achieves a balanced and literature-aligned renewable-

requires significant RES oversizing to compensate for limited storage capacity and spatial
constraints, ultimately reducing economic efficiency.
Storage Sizing: Hydrogen Buffering Capacity
The hydrogen storage strategies in the two scenarios differ markedly and are reflected
in their respective storage-to-electrolyzer sizing ratios. Scenario 1 achieves a ratio of
309.73, supported by the deployment of 197 GWh of geological storage.
This value
closely aligns with the benchmark of 277 reported by Dautel et al. [21] for cavern-based
systems, reinforcing the suitability of large-scale, low-cost storage for enabling temporal
flexibility and stable electrolyzer operation.
Scenario 2, in contrast, is constrained to 9.17 GWh of high-pressure tank storage, yielding
a markedly lower ratio of 16.25. This value falls short of the 35.29 reference identified
for gaseous tank systems by Dautel et al. [21], primarily due to spatial limitations at the
industrial site. While technically feasible, this limited storage capacity reduces system
autonomy and necessitates compensatory measures elsewhere in the system design.
Battery Storage Sizing
Battery energy storage is excluded in Scenario 1, consistent with the broader literature [22,
21], which indicates that current battery costs and short-duration capabilities typically ren-
der them suboptimal in hydrogen supply chains and emphasizing seasonal flexibility.
In Scenario 2, however, a 261 MWh battery is deployed to manage short-term fluctuations
and ensure hourly balancing, compensating for the lack of scalable hydrogen storage.
While not economically favorable in isolation, its integration reflects a technical necessity
arising from the spatial and operational constraints of the centralized configuration.
Summary: Component Sizing Implications for System Design
In summary, the optimal component sizing strategy for green hydrogen supply chains is
highly context-dependent, shaped by spatial availability, storage infrastructure, and op-
erational flexibility. The decentralized configuration (Scenario 1) demonstrates superior
techno-economic performance through moderate renewable oversizing, extensive geo-
logical hydrogen storage, and the exclusion of battery systems. This balanced design
aligns closely with literature benchmarks and highlights the value of seasonal storage in
enabling cost-efficient, flexible electrolyzer operation.
Conversely, the centralized system (Scenario 2) requires significant renewable capacity
oversizing and additional short-term battery storage to compensate for constrained hy-
drogen storage space. While technically viable, this configuration leads to reduced cost-
effectiveness. These findings reinforce the importance of integrated system design and
underscore the critical role of large-scale, low-cost storage in shaping efficient hydrogen
infrastructure.

6.2.1
Economic Performance: Minimization of Net Present Cost (NPC)
Minimizing the NPC of the hydrogen supply chain system under economic conditions
and operational constraints, is the overarching objective in techno-economic hydrogen
system modeling, serving as a consolidated indicator of long-term economic viability. In
the present study, Scenario 1, which employs decentralized production with geological
hydrogen storage, demonstrates superior economic performance, with a total NPC of
2,532.7 million €. In contrast, Scenario 2, characterized by centralized production and
spatially limited onsite infrastructure, incurs a significantly higher NPC of 4,262.2 million
€.
6.2.2
Key Cost Drivers in Hydrogen Supply Chains
This section identifies and analyzes the primary cost drivers across the modeled hydro-
gen supply chain configurations, with a focus on how component-specific expenditures
influence overall system economics under the defined scenario constraints.
The disaggregated LCOH breakdowns shown in fig. 5.6 and fig. 5.13 reveal that electricity
procurement and generation, as well as the cost allocated to the electrolyzer, constitute
the dominant cost drivers in both modeled scenarios. This finding is consistent with exist-
ing research which identifies energy input and electrolyzer investment costs as the prin-
cipal determinants of hydrogen production costs under renewable-based configurations
[19, 21, 11].
Electricity as the Dominant Cost Factor
In Scenario 1, the cost share associated with renewable electricity input amounts to 3.525
€/kg, representing approximately 73.84% of the total LCOH without considering revenue
from grid selling. In Scenario 2, the corresponding value increases sharply to 6.408 €/kg,
primarily due to the extensive oversizing of renewable assets necessitated by the absence
of seasonal hydrogen storage. The higher curtailment losses in Scenario 2 further re-
duce the effective utilization of installed generation capacity, compounding the electricity-
specific cost burden.
This aligns with findings by Oliva H. et al. [11], who highlight that electricity input consti-
tutes 60–80% of total hydrogen production costs under renewable-based supply models.
Similarly, Dautel et al. [21] identify electricity as the primary cost driver in green hydrogen
systems. However, despite structural similarities in system boundaries, their cost alloca-
tions differ notably across storage configurations: in the salt cavern scenario (Scenario 4),
electricity accounts for 58% and electrolyzers for 33%, while in the pressurized tank con-
figuration these shares shift to 40% and 42%, respectively. In contrast, this study finds
electricity to contribute 73.84% in Scenario 1 and 81.01% in Scenario 2, with electrolyzer
costs at 20.78% and 11.13%, respectively.
This divergence is primarily attributable to differences in electrolyzer technology. Dautel
et al. [21] apply cost-intensive PEM electrolyzers at 1,491,000 €/MW, whereas this study
assumes alkaline electrolysis at a significantly lower specific CapEx of 536,692 €/MW. The
results underscore the substantial impact of electrolyzer selection on system-wide costs
and emphasize the importance of carefully evaluating technology choices for cost-optimal
hydrogen supply chains.
Nonetheless, it must be critically noted that the choice of alkaline electrolysis in this study
is based on capital cost advantages rather than dynamic performance considerations.
Due to the model’s hourly temporal resolution, operational characteristics such as start-up
times, part-load behavior, and cycling flexibility are not captured. These dynamic effects
Techno-Economic Analysis of Green Hydrogen Adoption

==PAGE:89==
become particularly relevant at sub-hourly resolutions and may significantly influence the
comparative performance of PEM versus alkaline systems. Consequently, future model-
ing efforts with finer time granularity should evaluate both technologies on operational as
well as economic grounds, rather than relying solely on cost minimization criteria.
To ensure the validity of electricity-related cost assumptions, identified as the primary
cost driver in both scenarios, the calculated LCOE was benchmarked against current
reference values for Germany. Based on 2019 resource data, the resulting capacity fac-
tors and LCOE values align well with literature ranges. Offshore wind yields an LCOE
of 84.64 €/MWh, consistent with the 2024 reference range of 55–100 €/MWh [100]. On-
shore wind and solar PV reach 56.80 €/MWh and 56.34 €/MWh, respectively, both within
the reported intervals of 45–90 and 40–70 €/MWh. These comparisons confirm that the
electricity cost assumptions used in the techno-economic framework of this study are
grounded in up-to-date and regionally specific market data.
Cost Implications of Hydrogen Storage Technologies
Hydrogen storage impacts the LCOH differently in each scenario. Scenario 1, with 197 GWh
of salt cavern storage, incurs a cost of 0.154 €/kg, higher than IRENA’s benchmark of
0.088 €/kg [59], yet still minor in the overall cost structure due to the low specific cost
and high flexibility of geological storage. In contrast, Scenario 2, using pressurized steel
tanks, reaches 0.455 €/kg, aligning with IRENA’s range for daily cycled vessels (0.176–
0.748 €/kg). These results underscore the economic advantage of scalable cavern stor-
age, while highlighting tank storage as a major cost driver in spatially constrained systems.
Ancillary Costs: Hydrogen Transport and Compression
Transport costs arise only in Scenario 1 due to pipeline delivery from the production site,
contributing 0.103 €/kg, consistent with literature estimates [67]. Compression costs are
negligible in both cases, adding just 0.030 €/kg in Scenario 1 and 0.017 €/kg in Scenario 2,
each accounting for less than 1% of total LCOH. This supports previous findings [21] that
compression has minimal cost impact in hydrogen supply chains.
Summary: Cost Driver Assessment
In summary, the analysis confirms that electricity is the dominant cost driver in both cen-
tralized and decentralized hydrogen supply chains, particularly amplified in configurations
lacking seasonal storage. Scenario 1 demonstrates that coupling moderate renewable
capacity with low-cost salt cavern storage enables a more balanced and cost-efficient
system. In contrast, Scenario 2 suffers from high electricity-specific costs due to required
RES oversizing and curtailed flexibility. Furthermore, while the use of alkaline electrolyz-
ers contributes to cost reduction, this choice is grounded in static cost assumptions and
omits dynamic operational behavior, which limits the generalizability of the findings. Stor-
age and transport components also show significant variation in cost impact between
scenarios, reinforcing the importance of geographic, spatial, and infrastructure-related
constraints in optimal system design.
6.2.3
Operational Flexibility in Hydrogen Supply Chains
This section investigates the operational flexibility of electrolyzers and energy storage
systems, namely battery energy storage systems (BESS) and hydrogen energy storage
systems (HESS), as a critical means to enable the efficient integration of variable renew-
able energy sources into green hydrogen production systems.
Efficient integration of Variable Renewable Energy (VRE) into hydrogen systems depends
on the flexibility of electrolyzers and associated storage assets. This study demonstrates
that system flexibility is primarily enabled by hydrogen-based storage, with BESS playing
a limited role. Electrolyzer operation is strongly shaped by the scale and responsiveness
of these storage systems.

Hydrogen Storage Systems (HESS) Flexibility
Scenario 1 leverages extensive salt cavern storage (197 GWh) to decouple hydrogen pro-
duction from VRE availability, supporting seasonal-scale flexibility. The resulting storage-
to-electrolyzer ratio of 310 exceeds benchmarks from Dautel et al. [21] and reflects the
economic viability of geological storage, whose low specific CapEx justifies oversizing.
Scenario 2, in contrast, is limited to 9.17 GWh of pressurized tank storage, constrained by
spatial feasibility rather than economic optimization. The resulting storage-to-electrolyzer
ratio falls to 16.25, far below the 35.29 benchmark reported by [21] for steel tank stor-
age. As a consequence, the system is unable to achieve long-term decoupling between
generation and demand, increasing exposure to intermittency and curtailment.
Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) Flexibility
In Scenario 1, no battery storage is deployed, consistent with findings from Marocco et
al. [22], who argue that BESS is rarely part of cost-optimal hydrogen systems due to high
capital costs and limited duration. Even in solar-heavy configurations, hydrogen storage
remains more economically attractive for shifting energy. Marocco et al. [22] states, that
battery storage only becomes viable at cost levels below 100 €/kWh, an assumption still
considered optimistic in mid-term projections.
In Scenario 2, however, a 261 MWh battery is introduced not as a cost-minimizing as-
set but as a technical necessity. Its primary role is to ensure the availability of sufficient
power for the electrolyzer during hours when renewable generation alone cannot meet
minimum operational thresholds. Rather than providing full operational autonomy, the
battery supports short-term load leveling by topping up residual electricity. It does not
serve as a dispatchable resource to shift energy across days or seasons. This distinction
is critical: the battery is not dimensioned to sustain electrolyzer operation independently,
but to complement insufficient renewable availability over short intervals.
Summary: Operational Flexibility of Supply Chains
Electrolyzer operation is indirectly governed by storage flexibility. In Scenario 1, high-
capacity HESS enables stable, decoupled operation. Scenario 2’s limited storage, by con-
trast, constrains electrolyzer scheduling and increases dependence on immediate VRE
output and BESS support.
However, it is important to acknowledge a key modeling limitation: the current framework
operates at hourly resolution. While this suffices for energy balancing over daily and
seasonal scales, it cannot capture dynamic operational behavior such as ramp rates,
cycling constraints, part-load efficiencies, or start-up delays, especially relevant for small-
scale HESS and BESS. Sub-hourly modeling would be necessary to assess short-term
discharge patterns, transient electrolyzer responses, and the true value of fast-response
storage systems. Without such temporal granularity, the flexibility contribution of BESS
and short-duration HESS remains only partially understood in this study.
6.2.4
Optimal Mix of Renewable Energy Sources
A central objective of this study is to determine the techno-economically optimal mix of
renewable energy sources, specifically solar PV, onshore wind, and offshore wind, for
green hydrogen production, under varying spatial, economic, and operational constraints.
The selection and configuration of renewable energy sources are central to the technical
and economic performance of hydrogen supply chains. This study evaluates two distinct
system layouts, both powered exclusively by solar PV and onshore wind, omitting offshore
wind based on techno-economic optimization.
Scenario 1 features a near-balanced hybrid system of 855 MW onshore wind and 732 MW
solar PV, totaling 1,587 MW of generation against 636 MW of electrolyzer capacity. This

shown earlier to lie well within the national range reported for Germany, lending further
credibility to the renewable generation assumptions.

This sensitivity analysis confirms that offshore wind becomes economically viable in hy-

6.4
Summary of Limitations
This section outlines the primary limitations associated with the modeling framework, data
assumptions, and techno-economic structure used in this study. These are organized into
methodological, technical, and systemic constraints.
6.4.1
Methodological and Economic Assumptions
Cost Assumptions and Temporal Mismatch
Cost parameters used in this study span projections from 2020 to 2030. This includes
CapEx and OpEx for all system components. Furthermore, renewable energy generation
profiles and the ammonia demand is derived from data of the representative year 2019.
Techno-Economic Analysis of Green Hydrogen Adoption
83
==PAGE:95==
This temporal mismatch of input data could lead to divergency of techno-economic results
and therefore, these only allow for theoretical implications.
Simplified Financial Modeling
The model applies a simplified annual cash flow structure and uniform discount rate across
all technologies. In reality, technology-specific discount rates reflect different risk profiles
and investment conditions, substantially impacting the LCOH. A more precise represen-
tation would require technology-specific financing conditions.
Limited Market Participation Modeling
The model does not include the sale of surplus hydrogen or electricity on external mar-
kets. While curtailed electricity is tracked, no revenue is attributed to it and considered in
the main results. In practice, excess electricity could be sold via PPAs or to ancillary ser-
vices markets, providing additional income streams. Furthermore, there is no economic
consequences applied to the curtailment of surplus electricity, which biases the sizing of
the RES, mostly leading to oversizing and effecting the NPC. Additionally, no hydrogen
market (no hydrogen price) is assumed as market readiness is insufficient.
Non-linear Modeling
No selling price for hydrogen has been applied to the model, which leads to a missing
economic incentive for the optimization model to exploit the full production capacity of the
electrolyzer. The results of electrolyzer utilization currently do not reflect the full potential
of hydrogen production based on the same RES capacity. Furthermore, the curtailment
rates are exaggerated, due to the named under utilization of the electrolyzer in both sce-
narios. Furthermore, the linear nature of the optimization does not allow for non-linear
sizing factors, which would take the economy of scale for the component into account,
therefore the prices of the components which are mainly estimates per MW or MWh or
overestimated.
EU Regulatory Framework
The model presented in this study does not incorporate detailed simulation of participa-
tion in electricity markets or integration through PPAs, which would be necessary to more
accurately assess curtailment revenues, grid balancing strategies, and the broader com-
mercial feasibility of green hydrogen systems. Furthermore, while the model applies the
requirement for hourly temporal correlation between renewable electricity generation and
hydrogen production, reflecting a core criterion of the EU Delegated Acts for green hydro-
gen certification, it does not fully comply with the broader set of EU regulatory provisions.
Notably, the model assumes direct capital investment into renewable energy assets, a
practice that deviates from actual market behavior. In practice, hydrogen producers typ-
ically secure green electricity through PPAs rather than owning generation capacity out-
right. The omission of PPA structures in the model is primarily due to the non, transparent
nature of Over-The-Counter (OTC) PPA pricing. These agreements are not publicly dis-
closed and exhibit substantial variability depending on contract terms, project risks, and
market conditions, which complicates their integration into techno-economic modeling.
Absence of Multi-Year Simulation
The model simulates only a single operational year. Therefore the studies results do not
account for the sensitivity of system performance and economics to interannual variabil-
ity in RES availability. Multi-year simulations would allow for more robust system sizing
and performance validation, making sure components could align with there constrains
even under long term volatility of renewable energy supply. Furthermore, enhancing the
operational time horizon would enable a more accurate storage comparison as currently,
the SoC for battery and hydrogen storage is forced to end each year at the same level it
began. This imposes artificial constraints, particularly in Scenario 2, and limits the ability
to model year-to-year operational flexibility.

Fixed Demand Profile and No Penalty Logic
The model assumes a fixed industrial ammonia demand with no flexibility or penalty for
partial supply. Furthermore, the ammonia demand profile is based on standard industrial
values. More accurate, plant-specific demand data for 2025 or beyond would significantly
refine system sizing and hydrogen production scheduling. A more dynamic demand could
better reflect real-world conditions and provide more degrees of freedom for system opti-
mization.
Onsite space constrain
The hydrogen storage volume for the onsite tank in Scenario 2 is limited to 9.17 GWh
based on feasibility considerations. This size has been chosen as smaller capacity would
lead to infeasibility of the optimization model. However, empirical data on maximum allow-
able hydrogen storage volumes at industrial sites are scarce, making it difficult to validate
this assumption. The capped size constraints might therefore be overestimated and com-
pliant with real world safety regulations and available space.
Simplified Component Reliability
Downtime and failure risks of system components (electrolyzers, compressors, BESS,
etc.) are not modeled. This limits the optimization model to be close to real world opera-
tion, mainly as the data is missing and the implementation of uncertainty is complex.
Same RES Profile for Both Scenarios
Both scenarios use the same renewable generation profiles, despite being situated in
different geographic and operational contexts. This limits a more detailed scenario com-
parison, as different local wind and solar resources, land availability, and infrastructure
access would need to be applied. Furthermore, the influence of contingency on the power
grid is not represented in this study, which could be a distinct difference in decentralized
and centralized production.
Perfect foresight
The over all operational and sizing results are heavily influences by the fact that the model
has perfect foresight, as all input data such as renewable energy profiles as well as hourly
demand are available for the whole operations horizon. Input data for renewable energy
generation should be based on multi-year statistical averages to reduce this bias.

Technical Limitations
Time Step Resolution
The model uses hourly time steps to match European green hydrogen regulations requir-
ing hourly correlation between renewable generation and hydrogen production. However,
this resolution averages dynamic behaviors and neglects short-term fluctuations (e.g.,
wind gusts, solar intermittency). Higher temporal granularity would enhance precision of
the operational behavior of the electrolyzer, which is influenced by start up time and short
term (sub-hour) interactions with the BESS.

6.5
Future Work
While this study offers a comprehensive techno-economic assessment of hydrogen supply
chain configurations powered by renewable energy, several avenues remain open for
further exploration to enhance the robustness, realism, and applicability of the presented
results.
A key methodological improvement would involve reformulating the optimization objective.
While the current approach minimizes the NPC, future research could instead adopt a non-
linear LCOH minimization framework. The LCOH more accurately reflects the economic
viability of hydrogen production, especially when benchmarked against fossil-derived al-
ternatives. Additionally, using NPC as the primary objective limits comparability with ex-
isting literature, since it is a non-normalized metric that varies significantly with supply
chain scale, making cross-study evaluations more challenging and potentially misleading
Alternatively, maximizing the Net Present Value (NPV) under realistic sizing and financial
constraints may offer a more investment-oriented perspective. Incorporating non-linear
optimization frameworks would also permit the representation of more complex system
behaviors. This transition would require advanced modeling techniques, but it would bet-
ter capture the economic trade-offs inherent in large-scale hydrogen infrastructure plan-
ning.
In parallel, the integration of market dynamics would significantly strengthen the model’s
real-world relevance. Future studies should consider applying simulated hydrogen market
participation, where hydrogen is sold at dynamic prices rather than merely produced to
meet a fixed demand. The same applies to co-products of electrolysis such as oxygen
and low-grade heat, which can be monetized or used on-site to improve overall system
efficiency. Incorporating these by-product markets could shift optimal design decisions
and further maximize the economic precision.
As this study has identified the limited capacity of onsite high-pressure hydrogen storage
as a principal bottleneck in the economic competitiveness of centralized hydrogen sup-
ply chains, particularly when compared to decentralized configurations utilizing geological
storage, future research should investigate this constraint in significantly greater technical
detail. In particular, further work is needed to assess the regulatory, safety, and spatial
limitations associated with large-scale hydrogen storage at industrial sites in Germany.
Empirical data and national safety regulations governing vessel spacing, explosion pro-
tection zones, and operational risk management should be systematically incorporated to
determine realistic upper bounds for deployable storage capacity. Such an investigation
would help prevent overestimation of the technical feasibility of dense hydrogen storage
systems and improve the validity of techno-economic models, especially in scenarios con-
strained by limited space and strict industrial safety requirements.
Additionally, a more dynamic temporal framework should be introduced. The present

study relies on a single representative meteorological year (2019), which may not cap-
ture interannual variability in renewable generation. Expanding the temporal scope to
include multiple years of historical data or probabilistic weather scenarios would improve

component degradation, stack replacements, and technology cost learning curves, would

Lastly, another important extension concerns the demand modeling of ammonia plants.
In reality, ammonia production is not solely based on hydrogen consumption but also
includes the use of captured CO2 from natural gas reforming to produce urea. In the
context of green hydrogen systems, this CO2 must either be purchased from biogenic
or industrial sources or captured through carbon capture technologies, each introducing
new economic and operational implications. Including ammonia and urea co-production in
the modeling framework would enable more accurate cost comparisons with conventional
fossil-based ammonia plants and allow for informed policy recommendations on subsidies
or carbon pricing mechanisms needed to close the economic gap.
In summary, future research should pursue model extensions in three interrelated do-
mains: optimization methodology, system realism through market and policy integration,
and technical fidelity. These improvements would not only allow for more precise design
of hydrogen supply chains but also support the broader deployment of economically viable
and environmentally sustainable green hydrogen systems across Europe.
6.6
Implications and Applications
This study provides a techno-economic modeling framework to support industrial stake-
holders, planners, and policymakers in identifying cost-efficient configurations for green
hydrogen production under real-world constraints. By systematically comparing central-
ized and decentralized system layouts, the analysis highlights how spatial limitations,
storage technology, and electrolyzer sizing directly influence the LCOH. The findings em-
phasize that hybrid renewable energy integration and appropriate technology selection,
particularly in relation to electrolyzer and storage systems, are essential for achieving
economically viable hydrogen supply chains.
Moreover, the study underlines the critical importance of accurately considering regulatory
feasibility, storage capacity constraints, and renewable generation profiles in the design
phase. As European and national policy frameworks increasingly promote hydrogen in-
frastructure through funding instruments and certification schemes, this model can serve
as a practical tool for assessing cost-optimal system layouts across varying site condi-
tions. The results also inform policy discussions on the need to align hydrogen project
incentives with technical and economic realities, particularly in dense industrial zones
where land use and safety regulations may impose binding limitations.

Conclusion
This study presents a comprehensive techno-economic optimization of two renewable hy-
drogen supply chain configurations, decentralized off-site and centralized on-site, for am-
monia production in Northern Germany, using a mixed-integer linear programming (MILP)
framework. In doing so, it addresses an existing research gap related to the detailed anal-
ysis of hydrogen supply chains for hard-to-abate industrial sectors. By simulating hourly
operations over a one-year period, the model integrates onshore and offshore wind, solar
PV, alkaline electrolyzers, battery energy storage systems (BESS), and hydrogen energy
storage systems (HESS), in accordance with regulatory criteria for renewable hydrogen
certification.
Key findings indicate that the decentralized off-site configuration is the more economically
favorable option, achieving a Levelized Cost of Hydrogen (LCOH) of 4.77 €/kg, compared
to 7.91 €/kg for the centralized system. The cost advantage of the decentralized approach
is primarily attributed to reduced reliance on high-cost on-site storage, lower capital in-
vestment in BESS, and more favorable renewable electricity integration. In contrast, the
centralized configuration incurs higher curtailment and storage-related costs, contributing
to its less competitive LCOH. These insights, along with the generalized methodological
approach, are particularly relevant for both private-sector stakeholders and policy makers
aiming to assess investment and regulatory frameworks for industrial decarbonization.
A sensitivity analysis on offshore wind capital expenditure (CapEx), identified as a ma-
jor cost component, revealed that LCOH reductions of up to 18% are achievable under
favorable cost trajectories. Battery and hydrogen storage capacities also emerged as
critical design levers, offering system flexibility but significantly impacting overall capital
expenditure.
A key limitation of the analysis lies in the limited availability of public data on spatial,
regulatory, and safety constraints for on-site compressed hydrogen storage. As a result,
one of the main comparative criteria relies on informed assumptions.
Looking forward, advancing this research will require more realistic modeling of market
interactions, storage limitations, and integrated process pathways to better capture the
multi-dimensional trade-offs shaping the future of green hydrogen deployment in industrial
applications.
