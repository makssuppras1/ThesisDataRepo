Abstract 
Denmark is currently amongst the most sustainable countries in the world and has in several years 
appeared as a pioneer country on that account. However, the inequality internally in Denmark is 
continuously growing which may challenge their position in the lead. The Danish medias is portraying 
the growing inequality to be caused by the increasing inequality between the capital area and the 
peripherical and rural municipalities. This have been done by highlighting the peripherical areas to 
be dilapidated with both social and economic challenges. Several previous assessments substantiate 
this portrait but none of them analyses sustainability in a holistic manner.  
The aim for this thesis is therefore to create a holistic sustainability analysis of the municipalities on 
Zealand to explore possible reasons for the increasing inequality. Furthermore, it seeks to investigate 
whether there is a linear mathematical relationship between how sustainable the municipalities om 
Zealand are and how far they are located from Copenhagen. This is with the medias portraying of the 
peripherical municipalities being the least sustainable.  
To do so, a comparative sustainability analysis have been conducted from a developed indicator set 
consisting of 12 sustainability goals and 18 indicators. This have been accompanied by the conduction 
of a quantitative survey and several qualitative interviews with selected municipalities on Zealand.  
The analyses have shown that there is no linear mathematical relationship between holistic 
sustainability and distance to Copenhagen. Though, the results imply that the closer the municipalities 
are located to Copenhagen the more socially sustainable they become. Opposite, the closer the 
municipalities are located the less economically sustainable they become. Furthermore, the 
sustainability analysis has shown that Copenhagen is the 33rd most sustainable municipality on 
Zealand, which have been identified partly to be a result of the high housing prices and high crime 
rate. This may have been caused by the urbanisation and big cultural differences in Copenhagen.  
Furthermore, both the majority of the most sustainable municipalities and the least sustainable 
municipalities are located in the capital area implying that the inequality is caused by other aspects 
than distance. The analysis shows that the differences in sustainability between neighbouring 
municipalities may be caused by political difference and different education level. 
Finally, the investigation showed that the municipalities have no uniform definition of sustainability 
which has been identified as being one of the most important aspects in ensuring a sustainable 
development, together with political support, sustainability legislations and necessary economical 
and human resources.

1 Introduction  
 
1.1 Background 
“Inequality is the planet's main 'environmental' problem; it is also its main 'development' problem” 
(Brundtland, 1987).  
This is one of the main statements from the Brundtland report, the first report to increase the focus 
on a holistic view on sustainable development. (Brundtland, 1987).  
In 2015 the United Nations created 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDG), covering both the 
social, environmental and economic aspect of sustainability as seen on Figure 1.1. These goals are 
the ones that the world now try to pursue.   
Denmark is currently amongst the most sustainable countries in the world according to a global 
assessment (RobecoSAM, 2017). Furthermore, Copenhagen has been elected both to be the green 
capital of 2014 (European Commission, 2014) and to be one of the happiest cites in Europe (European 
Commission, 2016) which imply that they have a sustainable mindset. In addition, Denmark possess 
one of the most equal societies in the world (OECD, 2018).  
Despite that, the inequality in Denmark is growing rapidly which may challenge its status amongst 
the most sustainable countries in the world (Danish Statistics, 2016). Additionally, the Danish media, 
have in the last few years, focused on emphasising the increased inequality between the capital area 
and the peripherical and rural municipalities on Zealand. This with the peripherical areas being 
portrayed as dilapidated areas with several social and economic challenges (Samson, 2015). This is

partly because of the mega trend urbanisation going on in Denmark where the big cities continuously 
become more popular and attractive as seen in Figure 1.2.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
In addition, the development has been enhanced by the political prioritising to ensure jobs, education 
options, housing etc. in the bigger cites, instead of trying to make the rural areas more attractive as a

This development, together with the several honourable awards given to Copenhagen, makes it easy 
to hypothesise that Copenhagen is the most sustainable municipality on Zealand, and that the 
municipalities farther away is the least sustainable. This is substantiated by several different 
assessments, presented later, showing that the peripheral municipalities are more challenged than the 
municipalities located closer to Copenhagen. However, all these assessments only cover one or two 
domains of sustainability making it an incomplete assessment regarding exploration of sustainability. 
The motivation for this thesis is therefore based on creating a holistic sustainability analysis of the 
municipalities on Zealand to investigate and identify reasons for the increasing inequality. A holistic 
analysis will, by proper sorting, be able to confirm or deny the statements presented by the media 
concerning inequality between the capital area and the peripheral municipalities on Zealand. 
Furthermore, working with a holistic approach makes it possible to map the aspects of sustainability 
that are causing the increased inequality, and ensure that these will be adjusted to, accordingly, in the 
future. Adjustments that in the end will ensure that Denmark retains their position as one of the most 
sustainable countries in the world.

1.2 Problem Statement 
Based on the preliminary literature study highlighted in the introduction, the thesis is conducted based 
upon the following hypothesis: 
The further away you get from Copenhagen the less sustainable the municipalities become. 
This has emerged because of the positive view on Copenhagen regarding the environment and quality 
of life and more negative portraying of the development in the peripheral municipalities caused by 
the urbanisation. 
From the hypothesis, one main research question (Q1) has been formed together with several sub 
questions to explore the hypothesis and reasons for inequalities in sustainability in the municipalities:

Is there a mathematical linear relationship between how sustainable municipalities on 
Zealand are and in what distance they are located from Copenhagen? 
 
Is Copenhagen really the most sustainable municipality on Zealand? 
Is there a linear relationship between sustainability and demographic, capital and political 
parameters?

10 years?    
What factors have most influence on ensuring a sustainable development in the municipalities? 
What can be the reason for sustainable inequality between neighbouring municipalities?  
What should be done to ensure a progress in sustainability within all the Danish municipalities?
These questions will be answered by: 
• Examining previously created analyses of inequality between the Danish municipalities to explore 
a similar or different pattern as the one stated in the hypothesis. 
• Making a sustainability analysis of the 45 municipalities on Zealand, for three assessment years 
(2007, 2012, 2017), using self-developed sustainable indicators to explore the current and present 
sustainability in all the municipalities. 
• Identify possible patterns in sustainability by sorting the analysis results by different parameters. 
• Identifying possible reasons for the sustainable difference among the municipalities by making a 
survey for the municipalities to answer. 
• Conducting interviews with selected municipalities to get a more qualitative idea of what 
differentiates the municipalities in regard to sustainability and what the biggest challenges are.

2    Methodology 
This chapter will elaborate on the different applied methods used to address the hypothesis and the 
research questions. The methodology mainly consists of a literature study, a sustainability analysis, 
an empirical and a comparative method. The following sections will describe all the applied methods 
individually and highlight their individual contributions to the thesis. 
2.1 Literature Study 
A literature study has been performed as the foundation of the entire thesis. The study has been 
executed partly with the purpose of understanding and exploring the background theory for 
sustainability both in general and in Denmark, and partly to learn how to create a solid indicator set 
as a foundation for a sustainability analysis. Additionally, the purpose has been to examine previous 
studies on municipality level to identify possible patterns between sustainability and location similar 
to the one highlighted in the hypothesis. Finally, the study has contributed with knowledge about 
what other research papers have found as the main challenge toward an equal sustainable 
development across the different municipalities on Zealand.  
The applied literature has partly been found through a standard Google search but also trough more 
scientific sources like Google Scholar and DTU Findit. Initially, a lot of different literature was found 
but only the most relevant has been used directly in the thesis.     
2.2 Sustainability Analysis and Indicator Creation  
To explore the hypothesis and answer several of the research questions, a sustainability analysis has 
been conducted for the 45 municipalities on Zealand. The analysis has been conducted with the 
purpose of examining the current and present sustainability in the municipalities, and from that being 
able to identify possible pattern relevant for the hypothesis and identify reasons for inequality. To 
perform the analysis, an indicator development process has been conducted to find the optimal set of 
goals and indicators to explore the overall sustainability in each municipality. This process has been 
inspired by several different studies on how to create the optimal indicator set. The used course of 
actions will be presented in Section 4.3 where the development process will be presented together 
with the final indicator set.

2.3.1 Mixed-Method Research 
When using an empirical method for data collection, several possible sub-methods are available as 
seen in Figure 2.1.   
To collect the empirical data for exploring the research questions, a mixed-method research has been 
used as it is found to be the most holistic approach. This method consists of a combination of both 
qualitative (non-statistical) and quantitative (statistical) methodology as a contrast to the multi-
method where only one of the sub-methods are used (Saunders, et al., 2009, pp. 152-153). The 
uniqueness of the mixed-method research is, that it does not combine the quantitative and qualitative 
data collection techniques and analysis procedures, but instead use them sequentially or in parallel. 
Tashakkori and Teddlie argue that the use of several methods is useful as it provides a better 
opportunity to answer the research questions and to ensure that the research findings can be trusted 
(Tashakkori & Teddlie, 2010).  
In this thesis, the main quantitative methods used are data collection methods from a survey and 
extractions from statistical databases, whereas the qualitative method covers a conduction of 
interviews with selected municipalities.

The quantitative data collection has been used as the main data source for the sustainability analysis.  
Quantitative is a synonym for any data collection technique or data analysis procedure that generates 
or uses numerical data (Saunders, et al., 2009, p. 151). The results can be presented as graphs, tables, 
statistics etc.

2.3.2.1 Archival Research 
The main part of the data used for the sustainability analysis have been extracted from the two main 
statistical web pages in Denmark; Statestikbanken.dk and Noegletal.dk. This type of data collection 
is called archival research and is based on using data collected by others (Saunders, et al., 2009, p. 
150). The main advantage of using archival research, is that the data originally have been collected 
as a part of a reality study and not with a research purpose. This makes the data reliable and archival 
research a good data collection technique when large amounts of data are needed. However, one of 
the main challenges when using archival research is that the wanted data may not always be available 
or can have some missing information. This have, for this thesis, resulted in some challenges that will 
be mentioned in Section 4.3.5.  
For all the collected data, selection criterions have been applied to ensure that the analysis results can 
be trusted. The selection criterions are; relevance, availability and reliability. The use of these 
criterions will also be elaborated upon in Section 4.3.5.  
2.3.2.2 Survey 
As a contribution to the sustainability analysis, a survey has been conducted with all 45 municipalities 
on Zealand. The survey has the purpose of collecting lacking data for the analysis and collect 
information about what the municipalities find most important for ensuring a sustainable 
development. These results will help clarify why there is a difference in how sustainable the 
municipalities are and which “drivers” that ensure the development process. This information will 
substantiate the hypothesis to affect that some conclusions can be made even if the sustainability 
analysis show no clear connection between sustainability and distance to Copenhagen.   
The survey has been created using SurveyMonkey which is an online application to create surveys. 
This service was chosen to make it as easy as possible to answer and thereby achieve the highest 
possible response rate.  
All of the questions have been phrased as quantitative questions to ease the participation process. 
Included in the survey are questions regarding the wish of anonymity to ensure that no municipalities 
refuse to answer the survey due to the risk of feeling exposed. Additionally, questions about their 
willingness to contribute further to the thesis have been asked, so that the municipalities themselves 
can volunteer for an interview or further email contribution. 
E-mail addresses have been found manually for all the municipalities to identify the most appropriate 
receiver for answering the survey. When possible, personal e-mails have been used to try to reduce 
the time for administrative distribution. To accompany the survey, an introduction e-mail has been 
created to describe the purpose of the survey. The e-mail also states that the survey should be

2.4 Comparative Method  
To explore the main research question and identify the differences in sustainability in the Danish 
municipalities, a comparative method has been used to analyse the results from the sustainability 
analysis. The comparative method is relevant for investigating the differences or similarities between 
different cases especially om a system level. To use this method, it is necessary that the compared 
data have the same format and explores the same thing (Saunders, et al., 2009, p. 269). For the 
conducted sustainability analysis, the main comparison has been of the overall sustainability in the 
different municipalities on Zealand.  
2.4.1 Comparison of Data from the Sustainability Analysis 
The following section well explain how the data, collected for each indicator have been transformed 
into one number that can represent the total sustainability in each municipality, to make the 
municipalities’ sustainability performance comparable.  
To compare the data and evaluate the municipalities, two different methods have been used to get one 
comparable output for each municipality. The first method is a simple ranking of the municipalities 
and the second method is based on a reparameterization using percentage calculations to investigate 
the sustainable development in the municipalities over the last 10 years.   
2.4.1.1 Ranking Score 
The first method is a ranking system weighting the indicators equally. This method has been used to 
give a simplified overview of how sustainable the municipalities are and have been the foundation 
for the assessment of the results for 2017 period. As shown in Table 2.1, the ranking has been 
executed by giving each municipality a number from 1 to 45 (1-5 in the example), based on 
performance. The ranking is divided so the best municipality receives the ranking 1, the second best 
receives the ranking 2 and so on. All the rankings for each indicator have then been summed up to a 
final score. This have resulted in all the municipalities being assigned with a score with a possible 
minimum of 1 point * X indicators and a possible maximum of 45 points * X indicators. 
 
In the case where some municipalities perform identical on an indicator, the same ranking has been 
assigned, and the following municipality have continued with a ranking based on the number of better 
performing municipalities. This means, if two municipalities get the ranking 1, the third best 
municipality will get the ranking 3.

on the final sustainability ranking.  This means that some indicators may not have visible influence 
on the results even though their small percentage differences may be very significant in the real

this method is found acceptable.

3  Theory and Literature Study 
The following chapter contains relevant theory found in the literature review exploring different 
aspects of inequality and sustainability in Denmark. 
The chapter will first clarify the definition of sustainable development and sustainability, used for the 
thesis and highlight some of the main areas of action. Furthermore, the chapter will give a short 
introduction to how the focus on sustainability have increased in the world and in Denmark from the 
introduction of the Brundtland report (Brundtland, 1987) until today. This will be followed by a 
description of the current sustainability in Denmark and how the decision and execution process can 
affect the sustainable progress. Lastly previous analyses of different sustainability aspects will be 
presented to find possible location-based relationships relevant for the hypothesis.  
3.1 Sustainable Development 
To develop the optimal indicator set for a sustainability analysis, the definition of sustainability and 
sustainable development is essential. The definition is crucial to create indicators that have the 
necessary relevance and that covers the entire aspect of sustainability. The definition of sustainable 
development used in this thesis is from the original Brundtland report:  
“Sustainable development is a development that meets the needs of the present without compromising 
the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.” (Brundtland, 1987) 
This is found to be a poetic and incisive definition that implicitly explains the destiny of the future if 
no actions are taken. However, no clear solutions are incorporated in the definition.  
The following sections will explain the different aspects of sustainability and how they have been 
interpreted for this thesis. In that context, different examples on how to address the aspects, will be 
presented, using the United Nations SDGs as inspiration.  
Furthermore, two different ways of weighting the sustainability domains will be presented and the 
weighting used for this thesis highlighted.

3.1.1 Importance of Sustainability Domains 
There are several different ways to explain what sustainability is. However, they all involve the three 
pillars of sustainability; environment, social and economy – Figure 3.1.  
All three domains are clearly embodied in the definition of sustainable development adopted by the 
United Nations in its “Agenda for development”: 
“For sustainable development to be achieved, it is crucial to harmonise three core elements: 
economic growth, social inclusion and environmental protection. These elements are interconnected, 
and all are crucial for the well-being of individuals and societies.” (United Nations, n.d.,b) 
From this it is emphasised that the interfaces between the three domains are faded and that they are 
all central elements in the sustainable development process.   
3.1.2 Environmental 
The environmental aspect of sustainability relates to preserving the planet and its resources. The main 
purpose of environmental sustainability can be described as the unimpaired maintenance of the human 
life-support system – environmental sink and source capacities (Goodland, 1995). This becomes 
relevant in the second that the finite of the Earth is accepted, and when it is acknowledged that 
colonising of the moon is not a durable solution to secure the future of humanity.  
Regarding the protection of resources, the most common focus within environmental sustainability is 
to work with recycling and renewables. This can be done by the sorting of materials and waste both 
in a local and global scale, which can help to ensure a cleaner disposal and promote the possibility of 
reuse and recycle. This have already been partly executed in Denmark where the Ministry of 
Environment and Food in collaboration with the Danish Government in 2013 published a waste 
management strategy that express a goal for Denmark to reuse at least 50 % of all waste in 2022 (The 
Danish Goverment, 2013).  This have resulted in an increased number of waste fractions for the

public transportation is a sustainable move as the majority of cars have less than 4 passengers and

3.1.3 Social 
The second pillar of sustainability address the social aspect. This covers every aspect related to 
achieving a high quality of life for humans. Examples on what that encompass are poverty, education, 
health, safety, social exclusion and general inequality (United Nations, n.d.,c). Because these aspects 
vary greatly around the world, the goals stated by the United Nations may be more relevant to some 
countries than others when looking at the socially sustainability worldwide. However, this does not 
mean that a country like Denmark is social sustainable in a local scale just because the gender 
inequality and poverty is less severe than in other countries. As stated in the definition of sustainable 
development, the covering of the human needs is the key aspect of sustainability. This means that if 
the need in Denmark is different than in other countries, the way to achieve social sustainability 
differs correspondingly. Even though only 0.8 % (Juul, 2017) of the Danish population live below 
the poverty line, contrary to 40 % in Rwanda in Africa (Vilby, 2016), the inequality within the Danish 
border is still a big issue regarding social sustainability (Danish Statistics, 2016). Where the main 
step in Rwanda, toward social sustainability could be to reduce the number of people below the 
poverty line, the next step in Denmark could be to reduce the economic inequality by insuring a lower 
difference between rich and poor.  
Another example could be education, where the goal in the sub-Saharan Africa could be to get more 
than the current 60 % of people into school (UNESCO, 2018), where it in Denmark could be to ensure 
that more than the current 80 % of the population gets a higher education than primary school (Danish 
Statistics, 2017). A higher education level supports productivity and prosperity and contributes to the 
degradation of the social inheritance (The Danish Goverment, 2014a). Furthermore, education is 
closely related to ensuring a good health and a proper income (Danish Health and Medicines 
Authority , 2014).  
Another aspect of social sustainability is accessibility. Besides the basic access to clean drinking 
water and sanitation as stated in the United Nations SDGs’ (United Nations, 2015d), are access to 
things like recreational green and blue areas, transportation, hospitals, grocery shops and daycare 
institutions important aspects in meeting the human needs.  
Other aspects of social sustainability could be availability of affordable housing as stated by the 
previous Ministry of Cities, Housing and Rural affairs (Ministeriet for By, Bolig og Landdistrikter, 
2015) and mobility to ensure the opportunity to get around. This is a big part of achieving quality of 
life for especially elderly people (Henchoz, et al., 2015).

3.1.4 Economy 
The third and last pillar of sustainability puts focus on economy. The widely accepted definition of 
economic sustainability is “Maintenance of capital” (Goodland, 1995). This both relates to economic 
growth for the individuals and economic growth on a more global scale. As stated by the previous 
Danish Government is; “a strong and healthy economy with a high prosperity is the foundation for 
investment in welfare, education and health” (The Danish Goverment, 2014a).  
The maintenance can partly be achieved by reducing the poverty and economic inequality for the 
individual inhabitant as also mentioned as a social aspect. This will, in general, lead to a larger capital 
as well as an increased consumer spending (Ministry of Finance, 2016). To reduce the poverty, a 
sustainable move is to ensure that all inhabitants have access to efficient and attractive jobs no matter 
their location of abode. This will also help reduce the inequality and the unemployment, avoiding an 
extra expense for the public sector.  
Another aspect of economic sustainability is to ensure economic growth in the business industry itself. 
This can for example be done by investing in the future like R&D covering green technology, 
innovation and the environmental initiatives. Based on an analysis created by Begum Erdil Sahin, for 
the International Congress on Economic II in 2015, it is found that “an increase in R&D expenditures 
by 1 % increases GDP by approximately 0.61 %” (Sahin, 2015). This is important for a country to 
ensure that the business industry keeps up with the demand and development. If a country gets behind 
the development, they can become less attractive for the rest of the world to make economic 
agreements with which challenges the economic growth.      
Finally, digitalisation is an important aspect of the economic sustainability today. As everything gets 
more and more digitalised, this should not be neglected as it is a need both for the individuals and for 
the business industry. To highlight this, the Danish government published in 2014 a report containing 
a growth plan for digitalisation in Denmark. The report highlights the need of both a good broadband 
and mobile connection to boost e-trading and communication together with the need of IT and data 
in the business industry to secure growth (Erhvervs- og Vækstministeriet, 2014).

3.1.5 Sustainability Models  
The three presented domains are all important for sustainability, but the clarification of which one is 
most important differs depending on which model that is addressed. The nested and the intersected 
models, presented in this thesis, are just two of the many models created to weight the importance of 
the domains within sustainability. Both models have their own advantages and disadvantages that 
will be explained more in depth in the following.  
3.1.5.1 The Nested Model 
The essential part of the nested model is that it consists of three nested spheres where the economic 
circle is nested in the social circle and those two in turn nested within the environmental circle a seen 
on Figure 3.2 (Pryn, et al., 2015). This illustrates that you cannot achieve economic nor social 
sustainability without the presence of the environmental capital. From this, the model highlights the 
environmental capital as the foundation for all sustainability aspects and that the different domains 
limit each other.  
If this model is used for decision making by policy-makers, their prioritising can be made based on 
the importance of the domains in the model, prioritising environmental actions first. This would for 
this thesis mean that the indicators used for the sustainability analysis should be weighted according 
to the domain they belong in. The main advantage of the model is therefore its ability to help 
prioritising.  
Another aspect of the nested model is the time aspect and its attempt to prioritise a long-term horizon, 
again with the argument that the environment must be kept in balance to secure the future. This is 
clearly in accordance with the definition of sustainable development stated in the Brundtland report 
(Brundtland, 1987). However, not all environmental impacts are long-term processes in concern of 
the future generations. As stated in a report conducted at the Technical University of Denmark this 
can lead to wrong decision making (Pryn, et al., 2015).  Noise is an example, as noise does not

3.1.5.2 The Intersected Model  
The other model presented in this thesis is, the intersected model. The intersected sustainability model 
is probably the most famous one and is based on a so-called Venn-diagram (Moir & Carter, 2012). 
Here all three pillars of sustainability are drawn as three circles that intersects like a prism, Figure 
3.3.  
In this representation of sustainability, the three pillars are seen as individuals and as equally 
important topics within sustainability. The overlaps though illustrate the faded interfaces between the 
three domains which is in accordance with the interconnection mentioned in United Nations Agenda 
for development as stated earlier (United Nations, n.d.,b). Furthermore, the model can change focus 
by changing the size of the domains, but still with the centre point as the optimal place to be. However, 
an equal distribution must be expected as the most correct one when using this model. 
The biggest contrast to the nested model is that the intersecting model clearly does not prioritise the 
environmental aspect of sustainability above the others. This creates a challenge as the model alone 
cannot help in decision making.  
“The three dimensions do not provide much guidance to planners and policy-makers as to how to 
prioritise between the conflicting and interacting factors that can often emerge.” (Pryn, et al., 2015) 
Yet, it can be argued that a decision mainly based on a model and not knowledge is a bad decision.

Another difference that can be a limitation in the intersecting model is that it does not directly cover 
the time aspect of sustainability. Additionally, the model cannot alone help making decisions towards 
securing the future needs by the equal weighting. However, if a future oriented decision only 
prioritises the environment, like in the nested model, this may not be better. Still, the intersecting 
model puts more responsibility on the individual policy-maker to have a future oriented mindset.   
In the thesis, these challenges are not seen as an issue in regard to addressing the overall sustainability 
as the main purpose is to identify a possible linear relationship between location and sustainability, 
and not to identify on which specific indicators to act upon to improve a given sustainability 
performance.  
Despite the limitations, the intersecting model has been the primary choice of model for the 
sustainability analysis in this thesis. A collaboration between the domains are expected to be a more 
beneficial solution, than domains that limits each other as the nested model express.

3.2 Historical introduction of sustainability in Denmark  
The following section will describe the main event contributing to how sustainable development 
transformed from being a term in the Brundtland report to becoming a part of the everyday life in the 
world and in Denmark, to clarify the growth in political focus on sustainability. The timeline is in 
reality very complex and detailed but is in this case reduced to the most important actions. The 
highlighted events cover both a general European progress (left wing) but also a more local progress 
(right wing) within the Nordic countries in the period from 1987 until today as seen in Figure 3.4.

The changes in the environment have been known by scientist since the end of the 19th century. 
However, it was not until 1980’s that an increased doubt of the assumption that future development 
and the environment was incompatible. The idea that a future development based on a sustainable use 
of resources, was an option, got more and more recognised. From that, the United Nation, in 1983, 
decided to create a world commission for environment and development to investigate this new doubt. 
This resulted in the creation of the Brundtland report published in 1987 (Bæredygtig Udvikling, n.d.) 
created by 21 internationals acknowledge personalities with the previously Norwegian Prime 
Minister Gro Harlem Brundtland in the lead.      
The Brundtland report was the first report to mention sustainable development as being about a 
comprehensive view on the social, environmental and economic dimension. This made a clear impact 
on both the international and more local agendas. The main part of the report highlighted the extreme 
poverty that had happened for both the human and animal life and emphasised that the previous 
development had been both unacceptable and unsustainable.   
The strength in the report’s suggestion to, and definition of sustainable development, was that it made 
it possible to unite economic growth and the environment as they were both presented as important 
parts in achieving a sustainable development. Hereby, the definition was accepted both in pour and 
rich countries. The only weakness was the broad definition of sustainability with room for several 
interpretations, which lead to the Rio-conference in 1992. (Bæredygtig Udvikling, n.d.)   
In 1992 it was decided to hold a world summit meeting about the environment and development in 
Rio de Janeiro. At this meeting, where several thousand-attendants participated representing 172 
nations including Denmark, 27 principals for international cooperation on environment and 
development were presented and agreed on.  
One other output from the Rio conference was the Agenda 21 action program that are still in use 
today. The program contained, in a non-legal binding form, a range of very specific guidelines and 
goals for the future work towards a sustainable development. Such goals could be recommendations 
to use non-governmental organisations and other human forces in action and the encouragement for 
every nation to create their own sustainability strategy (Bæredygtig Udvikling, n.d.).     
From this, several more local initiatives blossomed, like in 1998 where the northern Prime Ministers 
agreed on a common declaration of a sustainable North. Here the northern Prime Ministers arranged 
the creation of a cross sectorial strategy for a sustainable development in the Nordic countries. This 
dealt with a common work effort in areas like the climate, biodiversity, the ocean, and food safety. 
This strategy became effective in 2001 and highlighted the high prioritisation that sustainable 
development now had in the Nordic countries.

sustainable development (The Danish Goverment, 2002). The strategy mentioned different sub-goals 
within the main goals as well as short term actions to pursue the goals. Likewise, goals and actions 
towards international sustainability were included in the report. This strategy was the first publicly

Denmark a pioneer country within the area.

to markets and increased global development assistance for developing countries should be combined

As previously mentioned, Denmark has been interested in a positive sustainable development for 
many years and is still amongst the top countries. However, one of the challenges that may cause the 
increasing inequality in sustainability across the municipalities is the different levels that the 
sustainability goals are decided and executed on both nationally and locally.

The Danish system is currently divided into four levels as shown in Figure 3.6 and works primarily 
by a top-down approach (Petersen, 2018). 
This means that the majority of the sustainability agreements and goals are created on a national level 
by the government and must be distributed downwards to the municipalities and communities where 
the necessary resources can be allocated for the execution. This distribution is a major challenge as it 
moves through several levels and people before catching the attention of the municipalities. A new 
survey shows that only one third of the Danish municipalities knows about the government’s newly 
published plan of actions toward reaching the 17 SDGs and only one tenth of the municipalities have 
started using the goals (Jørgensen & Kokholm, 2017). This may be a sign of some communication 
issues that can challenge the further sustainable development in Denmark.  
Today most of the sustainable goals are not followed by legislations which means that the 
municipalities are not forced to act towards a more sustainable development. This may lead to a lower 
priority inside the municipality council if the resources are limited. If a legislation is created and 
thereby obligates the municipalities to deliver some kind of action, examples have shown that the 
municipalities face several challenges. These challenges may result in an enhanced inequality 
between the municipalities.   
In an article, created by Jens-Philip Petersen at the Technical University of Denmark, the challenges, 
that 17 municipalities and communities are facing when being governed to create and implement a 
mandatory Strategic Energy Plan (SEP), have been examined. The first challenge highlighted by Jens-

energy strategy documents created were both too simplistic and inapt to promote the implementation 
of energy targets, or rather rigid technocratic energy scenarios without processual characters 
(Petersen, 2018). Additionally, several documents were found too specific and requires a yearly 
update to cope with the development. Such an update might not be done due to limited resources. 
This leads to the conclusion that the lack of guiding framework has caused that the energy strategy 
development has been based on a local normative or cultural-cognitive process and thereby creating 
procedural deficits. It may be an issue for all sustainable initiatives that no guidance is delivered by

4  Sustainable Development Indicators 
To investigate the theory of whether there is a linear relationship between the municipalities distance 
to Copenhagen and conduct a comparative analysis, a set of 18 indicators covering 12 goals have 
been created. The indicator development process is inspired by the literature found in the literature 
study as mentioned in Section 2.1. The following chapter will first elaborate on what an indicator is 
and continue by explaining the different steps conducted in the indicator development process. 
Additionally, the decisive factors for choosing the specific goals and indicators will be presented 
together with the assumptions. This will be accompanied by the data collection methods found in

4.1  Introduction to Indicators 
An indicator is a parameter that can clarify or simplify a given goal or initiative, and thereby make 
aggregated information’s available to policy makers.  They work as a tool to measure and calibrate 
the progress toward sustainable goals and can give an early warning to prevent environmental, social 
and economic setbacks if used correctly (Nations, 2007). Their main functions are; simplification, 
quantification and communication. Additionally, a good indicator can be described as: 
“Good sustainability indicators are those that are; scientifically valid, representative of a broad 
range of conditions, responsive to change, relevant to the needs of potential users, based on accurate 
and accessible data, based on data that are available over time, understandable by potential users, 
comparable with indicators developed in other jurisdictions, cost-effective to collect and use, 
attractive to the media, and unambiguous.” (Maclaren, 1996, p. 196) 
 
Indicators of sustainability differs from other types of traditional indicators as they are not used as 
independent indicators but as indicators that reflect upon the reality of having three different domains 
being closely connected. As mentioned by Maclaren, urban sustainable indicators can be 
distinguished from simple environmental, economic and social indicators by being: 
 Integrating 
 Forward-looking 
 Distributional  
 Developed with input from multiple stakeholders 
When working with indicators for sustainable development, two main indicator types are relevant. 
The first type is quantitative indicators that are based on numbers and statistics and the second type 
is qualitative indicators. Qualitative indicators are often used in regard to social sustainability as they 
can be used to examine for example quality of life that can be difficult to measure using numbers

solely. However, qualitative indicators are rarely used in the optimal extend, as it requires a large 
amount of data to ensure reliability. For this thesis, quantitative indicators have been used solely due 
to the large amount of data needed.    
4.2 The Good Indicator Set 
Indicator sets are commonly used in sustainability analysis. In spite of that, the definition of a good 
indicator set is just as difficult to define as a homogeneous definition of sustainability. This is because 
the indicator sets are meant to cover the definition of sustainability. When this varies, the indicator 
set varies correspondingly. However, most definitions can agree on the importance of covering all 
three domains of sustainability. As mentioned, by Poul Bardos, studying environmental technology, 
each element of sustainability must (environmental, social and economic) be assessed using 
indicators, with sustainability being a function of all three elements (Bardos, et al., 2009). 
Additionally, a key element in a good indicator set is that the chosen indicators live up to the selection 
criterions; relevance, availability and reliability. These criterions can also change from indicator set 
to indicator set but the three mentioned are the ones most often addressed in an indicator development 
process (Tanguay, et al., 2009).  
Regarding the quantity of indicators, no optimal number have been defined yet. Though it is necessary 
to have a reasonable number of indicators to cover all three domains. Too few indicators give an 
incomplete analysis of sustainability whereas to many indicators can make the data collection process 
to comprehensive and the results difficult to use in a constructive way.  
The most commonly known indicator set is the United Nations earlier presented indicator set with 17 
goals and 232 related indicators. This large number makes sense on a global scale where the purpose 
is to create objectives relevant for all countries worldwide and where each individual country can 
address the indicators and goals relevant for them. For a smaller and more local analysis, addressing 
only a part of a country, a more simplified and limited indicator set may be more appropriate. A 
research study performed at the University of Québec, exploring 17 different sustainability analyses 
of cities and municipalities, shows a variety in number of indicators from 10 to 86. The study states 
that this difference is a big issue when wanting to compare the cities or municipalities to each other 
(Tanguay, et al., 2009). This is very relevant regarding the analysis performed in this thesis as it seeks 
to develop one indicator set to address all municipalities.  
From this, a template for a proper indicator set is not identified in the literature study. The goal for 
this thesis has therefore been to create a holistic indicator set that covers the created definition of 
sustainability and are based on relevant, available and reliable data. The following sections will go 
through the indicator development process and explain the steps used in the evolvement of the final 
indicator set.

4.3.4 Indicator Brainstorm 
The creation of indicators has been conducted by making a brainstorm considering each sustainability 
goal. Neither in this step, limitations were set for the number of indicators as the selection criterions 
were not added yet. When listing indicators, the focus was to ensure that the indicators were relevant 
on a municipality level for all three assessment years. Thus, some initial research of data was made 
to avoid choosing too many indicators without available data. For the brainstorm, an initial maximum 
of 3 hours was set to limit the number of indicators. However, some indicators were added later when 
looking further into data availability. 
From the conduction of the brainstorm, several inspiration sources were used to identify possible 
indicators. Some indicators were created from basic common knowledge like making crimes/1000 
inhabitants an indicator for safety or making DKK/inhabitants used for investment in innovation and 
technology an indicator for investment in innovation and technology. Others were identified by 
exploring other inequality analyses made in Denmark like for example the indicators for the goals 
attractive workplaces and education, inspired by Cevea’s socioeconomic analysis of Denmark 
(Baltzarsen, 2015). 
Additionally, indicators were found by searching for the goals on the internet which lead to indicators 
like Gini-coefficient for economic inequality and square meter price per disposable income as an 
indicator for affordable housing (Sustainable Measures, 2006).  
Lastly, inspiration was found in indicators used in a previous DTU course “42274 Sustainable 
Development Indicators and Sustainable Urban Development”. 
As a result of the brainstorm process, 37 initial indicators were created and divided onto the 17 goals. 
These can be seen in Appendix D.

4.5.2 Arguments for Choosing Indicators with Partly Reliable or Available Data 
The following sections will shortly explain why the indicators with partly reliable or available data 
from Table 4.2 have been included in the final indicator set.  
4.5.2.1 Indicator 2a and 2b – Number of Waste Fractions 
The issue with indicator 2a and 2b are the lack of data for the assessment years 2007 and 2012. 
Because the number of indicators covering the environmental sustainability are very limited it has 
been decided to include the indicators in the final indicator set as reliable data are available for 2017. 
The missing data have been attempted to be collected by including a request for the data in the survey, 
but this have not given sufficient answers to use. 
This means that the indicators will not be included when comparing the development over the three 
assessment years with the percentage share but will only be included in the comparison for 2017. 
However, the assessment of 2017 is found to be most important for the hypothesis making the 
inclusion of the indicator a reasonable choice.  
4.5.2.2 Indicator 3 – DKK used on Nature and Environmental Protection 
The main issue regarding indicator 3 is that the municipalities can make up expenses on their account 
differently. This means that some expenses to nature and environmental protection can be accounted 
under other items than the ones most logical. This can affect the reliability of the data as some 
==PAGE:66==
Chapter 4 | Sustainable Development Indicators 
Page | 52 
  Sustainability Analysis of Danish Municipalities  
 
expenses may be neglected. To accommodate this, all items in the accountment related to the items 
“nature and environmental protection” have been included in the expense. The specific items can be 
seen in Appendix E. By doing this it has been found reasonable to include the indicator as the 
adjustment is expected to reduce the possible errors and thereby not affect the overall result. 
4.5.2.3 Indicator 6a – Access to Hospital 
The main challenge regarding indicator 6a, is that the distance to the hospitals are made using the 
current road network. This means that the driving distance in 2007 and 2012 are based on the same 
road net as for 2017 even though the road network may have changed. However, the changes are 
expected to be so small that it does not affect the results significantly. 
Additionally, the driving distances are measured from the municipality centres which means that it is 
an estimate of an average distance. In the optimal solution the distances would be made by taking the 
distances from every single inhabitants’ abode and then take an average from that, but because the 
used measurement method is done in the same way for all the municipalities, the possible variations 
are expected to be the same for all – justifying inclusion of the indicator.  
4.5.2.4 Indicator 10a and 10b - Internet Access  
The next indicators are indicator 10a and 10b describing the access to internet. For these indicators 
the great issue has been the data from 2012 as the data is only available on a bad quality GIS map 
assessed by postal codes and not municipalities. In addition, the percentages of the areas with access 
to the given internet has been divided into intervals of 10 percent. This gives some uncertainties and 
challenges with the reliability when using the 2012 data.  
However, the uncertainties have been accepted as the development between the three assessment 
years are so large that the unprecise readings will still show a big development, and because the data 
from 2017 is the most relevant and both available and reliable. Additionally, the data from 2012 is 
found in the same way for all the municipalities minimising the uncertainty internally between the 
municipalities. The data collection method can be found in Appendix E.    
4.5.2.5 Indicator 11 – Gini-coefficient  
The last indicator that needs an explanation is the Gini-coefficient. Here, the main challenge is 
missing data from 2017 that is not available yet. An attempt of calculating a 2017 value base on the 
development from the previous years have been performed, but because the inconsistency in the 
development, this have not been used. To keep the data as reliable as possible, it has been decided to 
use the 2016 value instead of 2017 to ensure a proper internally relationship between the 
municipalities. This has additionally been found acceptable because of the small changes from year 
to year which are not expected to affect the results greatly.

5.1.1 Test of Hypothesis 
Based on the presented analysis results, the hypothesis and thereby the main research question has 
been tested.  
To explore the hypothesis, the final sustainability score has been plotted into a graph with the distance 
to Copenhagen out of the x-axis and the ranking score out of the y-axis as seen on Figure 5.2.  
From the coefficient of determination, it is not possible to see a linear relationship between 
sustainability and the distance to Copenhagen. Though the tendency line has a positive gradient, 
which leads towards the hypothesis, the dispersion in the ranking scores are too extreme to show even 
a weak relation as the R2 = 0.0516.  
Despite that, it can be seen from the figure that the dispersion gets smaller between the municipalities 
when moving further away from Copenhagen. This shows that peripherical municipalities (>45 km 
away) are more equal internally than the municipalities located closer to Copenhagen.  
The relation has been tested through a sensitivity analysis where the driving distance to Copenhagen 
have been used instead of the distance in direct line. The analysis shows no clear changes neither in 
the slope of the tendency line nor in the coefficient of determination that only changes to R2 = 0.0593. 
The results of the sensitivity analysis can be seen in Appendix I.    
5.1.1.1 Test of Null Hypothesis and Significance Level 
Due to the low coefficient of determination, that though is above 0, a null hypothesis has been tested 
to see if the very vague linear relationship between sustainability and distance to Copenhagen is 
significant. This is with the purpose of exploring if the possible relation can be coincidental.  
The null hypothesis(H0) is: There is NO linear mathematical relationship between how sustainable 
municipalities on Zealand are and in what distance they are located from Copenhagen.

To explore the significance, the p-value (significant coefficient) has been calculated. The p-value 
describes the smallest level of significance that will lead to a rejection of the null hypothesis. For the 
null hypothesis to be rejected and for the relation to be significant, the p-value must be lower than the 
significance level $ of 0.05 (Brockhoff, et al., 2017).  
The p-value has been calculated automatically in Excel by using the function “Regression” from the 
Excel plugin “Analysis ToolPak” where the only input given is the variables distance and 
sustainability score. The calculation can be seen in Appendix J. 
The calculation gives a p-value of 0.13 > 0.05 which means that the null hypothesis cannot be 
rejected, and that there is no significant relation. This means that it cannot be proven that the very 
small relation is not caused by a coincidence. From that, the missing linear relationship is 
substantiated.  
In the following search for relations, the coefficient of determination will be explored solely with the 
purpose of identifying visible relations. As a coefficient of determination of approximately 0.2 is set 
as a minimum for a conclusion to be made, as explained in Section 2.4.2.1, this is assumed to give 
significant results.

6 Analysis and Discussion   
The following Chapter will encompass a discussion and analysis of the thesis and its results. The 
discussion will be divided into three main parts. The first part will discuss the results, presented in 
Chapter 5, in regard to answering the research questions. The second part will include a critical 
reflection upon the applied methods. Finally, the third section will mention the future work that can 
be made as an extension to this thesis.   
6.1 Analysis and Discussion of Results  
This section will combine the different results to address the research questions listed in Section 1.2. 
The questions will be discussed chronological after the same order as in the problem statement. The 
results will be analysed to some extent to answer the questions. 
6.1.1 Q1 - Linear Relationship Between Sustainability and Distance to 
Copenhagen  
From the results in Section 5.1.1 no linear relationship was found from a holistic view on 
sustainability with each goal being equally important, as the coefficient of determination is 0.0516 
and the p-value is 0.13. This may be a result of the different influence that the domains have on the 
overall sustainability. However, a clear relation was found for the social and economic domains with 
opposite influence on the results. This may be the reason for the unclear tendency and will be 
discussed in the following sections. This implies that the linear relationship between distance to 
Copenhagen and sustainability could be clearer if some domain is weighted higher than the others.  
Furthermore, the results showed that the dispersion between the municipalities becomes lower, the 
further away from Copenhagen they are located. This implies that the inequality is smaller for all the 
domains when addressing the peripherical municipalities alone compared to the municipalities in the 
Greater Copenhagen area. This is in accordance with both the least and most sustainable 
municipalities being located close to Copenhagen.    
6.1.1.1 Social Sustainability 
As Figure 5.7 and Figure 5.9 showed, the municipalities become more socially sustainable the closer 
they are located to Copenhagen. This was an expected result after seeing a relation in health alone. 
As found during the literature study, bad health is often closely related to unemployment and low 
education which clearly emphasises that the different social sustainable aspects are closely related as 
they affect each other (Global Partnership for Education, 2018) (Danish Health and Medicines 
Authority , 2014).  As most of the education institutions and public jobs have been moved towards 
Copenhagen, the last years this make sense. Some of these movements have been dictated by the 
==PAGE:92==
Chapter 6 | Analysis and Discussion 
 
Page | 78 
  Sustainability Analysis of Danish Municipalities  
 
government which means that they have contributed to a greater inequality in social sustainability on 
Zealand.   
The relation with social sustainability is also related to the longer distance to hospitals as the new 
hospital strategy has removed a lot of hospitals in the peripherical areas in favour of fewer but new 
and larger hospitals. This is also a political move.  
In addition, the difference in culture and diet can affect the health. The new healthy and modern food 
alternative are often integrated initially in the bigger cities close to Copenhagen as the bigger cities 
adopt new tendencies quicker than the peripherical areas. At the same time, the culture is more diverse 
closer to Copenhagen which promotes a new way of thinking about food.  
On the contrary, the great mix of cultures can at the same time be the one challenge working against 
social sustainability in the capital area. This is because cultural differences may result in conflicts and 
crime which may be the reason for Copenhagen’s bad score on the indicator concerning crime.  
6.1.1.2 Economic Sustainability 
When exploring the economic sustainability, the results show that the closer the municipalities are 
located to Copenhagen the less economic sustainable they become. This is a different picture than 
implied in the socioeconomic analysis presented in Section 3.4.1. This may be a result of the chosen 
indicators, where the socioeconomic analysis has an indicator focusing on income, promoting a higher 
sustainability level closer to Copenhagen. This thesis uses other indicators like affordable housing 
and economic inequality that affect the results in the opposite direction. This implies that with a low 
number of indicators the results are more sensitive to change.  
When exploring the linear relationship between distance to Copenhagen and the economic indicators, 
affordable housing showed the clearest relation as seen in Table 5.3. The results clearly showed that 
the housing prices are more affordable the further away from Copenhagen you get. This may be a 
result of the financial crises in 2008 resulting in the banks denying the Danes to loan money for 
buying a house in the peripherical areas as they are less attractive (Halskov & Thiemann, 2014). This 
have created a bad tendency making it difficult to buy a house further away from Copenhagen unless 
the price is cheaper. This indicates that the more affordable housing in the peripherical areas can be 
seen as a result of a bad decision made 10 years ago and that may have contributed to enhancing the 
urbanisation responsible for the increased inequality.             
Another indicator highlighting the peripherical municipality as more economic sustainable is the 
investment in innovation as presented on Figure 5.5. This indicator was chosen with the purpose of 
figuring out how each municipality should prioritise innovation and entrepreneurship. The found 
relation was not as expected, as it was expected that the municipalities with the biggest capital would 
==PAGE:93==

 Page | 79  
 
use more money, correspondingly. The reason why the municipalities located closer to Copenhagen 
may invest less is that the majority of the innovative work are a result of partnerships between 
university environments and large companies, which are often located close to Copenhagen and are 
either founded privately or by the government. This may make these municipalities find investment 
less relevant and important for them to invest in. Additionally, the income is higher closer to 
Copenhagen, making it easier to see a progress in entrepreneurship because more inhabitants have 
money themselves to implement a good idea.  
However, these reasons cannot explain all the results. Cities with universities and big companies like 
Roskilde and Hillerød are amongst the best municipalities when it comes to investment. This implies 
that the municipalities that invest most finds innovation most important and feels the biggest 
responsibility for ensuring a development in innovation. However, a better indicator for innovation 
may have been preferred if the data was available.  
6.1.1.3 Environmental Sustainability 
Finally, the environmental sustainability showed no linear relationship between sustainability and 
distance to Copenhagen.  
This may be explained by the environmental progress being more dependent of the political 
prioritisation and different nature challenges than the social and economic aspects. Where the social 
and economic aspects have been an integrated part of the development for many years and cannot be 
neglected, the environmental aspect is all new. This is also what Jakob Jespersen highlighted when 
he was asked why the economic indicators were found less important than the others.  
As pointed out by Karen Marie, Table 5.11, the development in environmental sustainability is 
challenged depending on the political support.  
“The main challenges, in becoming more sustainable, are that the climate related work is not legally 
bound, which means that it is “nice to have” and thereby depends on whether there is political 
support or not.” 
At the same time, Municipality #1, Table 5.9, emphasise that they feel no demand from the 
government for them to work with environmental sustainability. This highlights that the lacking linear 
relationship for environmental sustainability may be a matter of prioritisation from the politicians. 
This is clearly supported by results from the survey as seen in Figure 5.24 which highlight that 87 % 
of the municipalities finds political prioritisation the most important driver in ensuring a sustainable 
development.  
Additionally, the necessary resources and non-governmental initiatives are highlighted as important 
for the development both by the survey and Karen Marie.

Sub Conclusion  
• From the presented and discussed results, it can be concluded that there is no linear relationship 
between the distance to Copenhagen and sustainability holistically. This is shown to be caused 
by the opposite relation found for the social and economic sustainability and the lacking 
relationship between distance and the environmental sustainability.  
• Furthermore, it can be concluded that there is a linear relationship between the social domain 
and distance to Copenhagen, with a coefficient of determination of 0.4371. This shows that 
municipalities located closer to Copenhagen are more socially sustainable as they have a higher 
education level, higher employment, better health etc. 
• Additionally, a linear relationship between the economic domain and distance to Copenhagen 
is found, with a coefficient of determination of 0.6881. This shows that municipalities located 
closer to Copenhagen are less economically sustainable. This may be a result of high housing 
prices and high economic inequality caused by the urbanisation in the greater Copenhagen area. 
• Finally, it has been found that the environmental sustainability shows no clear linear 
relationship with distance to Copenhagen as it can be more depending on political prioritisation 
and differences in nature.      
 
6.1.2 Q2 - Sustainable Performance of Copenhagen 
From the results presented in Section 5.1 Copenhagen is not the most sustainable municipality on 
Zealand but only the 33rd most sustainable municipality. This placement is not good as Copenhagen 
was the 23rd most sustainable municipality in 2007 and the 25th most sustainable in 2012 – Appendix 
G. This shows a setback of 10 places in 10 years.

The reason for Copenhagen’s placement between the bottom half in 2017, is their bad performance 
on several indicators. Copenhagen municipality is amongst the 2 best municipalities in green mobility 
and education which highlight their profit by having a good transportation system and several 
universities within the municipality borders. However, this is not sufficient as Copenhagen are 
amongst the bottom 5 municipalities on indicator 3, 7, 9 and 11 as previously presented in Table 5.2.  
As mentioned, Copenhagen is challenged when it comes to indicator 7, safety from criminal offences. 
The big cultural differences, both ethnic and economic, can create clashes that may be the reason for 
the high crime rate. This may be a sign that Copenhagen has become a too big and diverse city and 
thereby experiences difficulties in accommodating the many cultural differences. This is emphasised 
by comparing Copenhagen with Frederiksberg municipality which is located within the Copenhagen 
borders, but has a visibly lower crime rate and a less diverse demography.  
The other indicators 3 and 11 both relates to investment in nature protection and innovation, 
respectively. Where the low investment can be a result of an automatic progress, it can on the contrary 
also indicate that the municipalities are forced to use money elsewhere. Often, the size of the 
municipality budget is related to the demography which means that the municipalities with more 
elders or more criminal offences gets a bigger budget that the ones without those challenges. This 
will clearly affect the amount of money that the municipalities can use for investments in nature or 
innovation. Appendix L show that the two indicators concerning investment are the ones where 
Copenhagen have experienced the largest setback in placement in the last 10 years. This may imply 
some challenges affecting the use of money.  
In a contrast to Copenhagen, Solrød has, as the best performing municipality, improved its placement 
from 11th best to 1st in the same 10-year period. The difference between the two developments is that 
Solrød have improved its placement evenly on most of the indicators. This implies that an even 
development is more effective than a big progress or setback on few indicators. This is in accordance 
with the intersected model weighing all domains and indicators equally.

probably a result of the urbanisation and big cultural difference.   
• Furthermore, Solrød is the most sustainable municipality on Zealand with a sustainability score 
of 204.

6.1.3 Q3 - Relationships Between Sustainability and Other Parameters  
As a supplement to the exploration of the linear relationship between distance and sustainability, 
several other relationships were tested and presented in Section 5.1.2. The results showed no relation 
when assessing population and municipality size which is in accordance with the very mixed opinions 
of the allegations listed in question 8 in the survey asking about this. A relation was neither found 
when assessing after satisfaction of abode municipality. This indicates that satisfaction is very 
subjective as already seen in Section 3.4.2 where all municipalities had a high degree of satisfaction 
amongst its inhabitants in spite of their location.  
However, some relations were found when comparing sustainability with economy, education, 
politics and direction. These will be discussed shortly in the following. 
6.1.3.1 Economy 
When looking at the linear relationship between sustainability and operation and maintenance cost 
per inhabitant, the results showed a weak relation that the higher the budget the less sustainable 
municipality. This is expected to be related to the previously described connection with budget and 
social challenges. However, from this it is very difficult to say that the size of a budget justifies the 
sustainable development.  
Despite that, a comparison of Figure 5.9 and Figure 5.19  indicates that the municipalities being most 
economically sustainable are the municipalities that have achieved the largest percentage progress in 
sustainable development the last 10 years. This may imply that the economy holistically is an 
important aspect of ensuring a positive development. This is interesting as the municipalities, neglects 
the economic goals to be among the most important ones as shown from survey question 5.  
Additionally, the results show a weak indication that municipalities with higher income is more 
sustainable. This may be caused by the assumption that income, education, health and economic 
growth are connected.

6.1.3.2 Education level 
When exploring the linear relationship between sustainability and education, the results indicated that 
education is one of the main parameters in ensuring a sustainable development. The results showed 
that a higher education level responds to a more sustainable municipality. This is in agreement with 
the statement made by the Global Partnership for Education presented in Section 4.5.1. 
“Education is a vital human right and plays a key role in human, social, and economic development” 
(Global Partnership for Education, 2018) 
This means that despite all the resource and government related challenges, the results imply that 
equality in education level will ensure more equality in sustainability.  
6.1.3.3 Political Party of Mayor 
As emphasised both by the survey question 7, Municipality #2, Karen Marie, Jakob and in the study 
made by Jens-Philip presented in Section 3.3.1, the political support has a big influence on the 
sustainable development. The results in Section 5.1.2.4 showed that the municipalities with a mayor 
from Det Konsernative Folkeparti is in average more sustainable than the municipalities with a mayor 
from Venstre or Socialdemokratiet. This may be because their political program clearly emphasises 
all three sustainability domains equally. From their public political program both the social, economic 
and environmental domains are highlighted with almost equal importance. In addition, they are 
traditional bound and cherish a stable and solid society (Det Konservative Folkeparti, n.d.). This may 
be in contract to Socialdemokratiet’s political program. Their program also mentions all three aspects 
of sustainability, but they have a dominant focus on the social aspects as the most important 
(Socialdemokratiet, 2017). This may be the reason that the municipalities with a mayor from 
Socialdemokratiet is less sustainable compared to the average. Similarly, the political program from 
Venstre focus primarily on economy and social sustainability rather than the environment (Venstre, 
2006). However, it should be noted that these evaluations of the political programmes are subjective 
and thereby may not be the complete truth.  
Although, this theory is challenged when looking at the connection between party of mayor and the 
change in sustainability score over the last 5 years. This is because Figure 5.17 and 5.18 showed that 
the municipalities with the biggest progress have a mayor from Venstre in the period before 2017. 
However, when assessing the political constellations and history in the municipalities with the largest 
progress and largest decline another explanation arises.  
Both Copenhagen and Næstved municipality with the biggest decline in sustainability score have had 
a very one-dimensional political history with Socialdemokratiet and the additional left-oriented 
parties in power. Additionally, both municipalities have had the same political orientation in many 
==PAGE:98==

years without any visible threats from the right-oriented parties. This is in contrast with Ringsted, 
Solrød and Kalundborg which show the biggest progress in the last 5 years. These municipalities all 
have had a broad political constellation in many years and have had their mayor party change after 
almost every election. This implies that a broad cooperation and competition between parties are more 
important for the sustainable development than the actual mayor party.                
6.1.3.4 Direction  
The last linear relation explored, is the relationship between sustainability and direction from 
Copenhagen. As the results showed in Section 5.1.2.3, the municipalities located north of 
Copenhagen significantly are more sustainable than the municipalities located west or south of 
Copenhagen. Additionally, the inhabitants in the northern municipalities are more satisfied with their 
municipality than others as presented in Section 3.4.2. 
When exploring why, it is found that the municipalities in the northern part of Zealand have a general 
higher education level than the western and southern municipalities. This emphasises the results from 
the education assessment in Section 5.1.2.2. The following Figure 6.1 show indicator 5a plotted into 
a map to highlight the education level on Zealand. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
This educational difference may be a coincidence, but it could also be historically conditioned.  Since 
the 1500s the northern part of Zealand has been called the kings north as it has always been very 
attractive. This is due to the lush hunting fields found in the forest and diverse nature. Because of 
that, several cities have evolved early with a great capital related, such as Kongens-Lyngby, Hillerød 
and Fredensborg. This have created a great popularity of the northern part of Zealand, still existing

today amongst the wealthy families. Since these families often are those with the highest education, 
the more sustainable north makes sense in regard to the discussion in Section 6.1.3.2. From that it 
may be argued that the northern part of Zealand is helped by the previous history and that the baseline 
for each municipality to become more sustainable differs. 
 Another argument may be businesses as seen for example in Gentofte. In 2004 Gentofte sold their 
share of the company NESA which resulted in a massive income to the municipality (Tornbjerg, 
2002). This have made it possible for them to develop without having economic issues ever since. 
This has of course also attracted the wealthy and most well-educated population as well. 
From that, the direction relation enhance that a high education level is essential for a sustainable 
municipality and that the previous history may have created inequality in the municipalities starting 
point in becoming more sustainable.   
Sub Conclusion  
• From the results and discussion, it can be concluded that there is a weak but visible linear 
relationship between education and sustainability with a coefficient of determination of 0.2345. 
The results show that a higher education level, relates to a more sustainable municipality. This 
is probably because education is the foundation for several of the other indicators covering 
social and economic aspects.  
• Furthermore, it has been found that municipalities with a mayor from Det Konservative 
Folkeparti are more sustainable than municipalities with a mayor from Venstre or 
Socialdemokratiet. This is probably due to the broad political focus in Det Konservative 
folkeparti’s political program and the narrower focus by Socialdemokratiet and Venstre.  
• Additionally, the analysis of mayor parties indicates that a broad political constellation and 
competition creates a larger sustainable development compared to a narrower constellation with 
only one party dominating.  
• Finally, it has been found that municipalities in the northern part of Zealand in average are more 
sustainable than municipalities located west or south of Copenhagen. This is suspected to be a 
result of the northern municipalities having been more attractive for the wealthiest in the 
country for centuries and thereby have had a higher education, health, income and employment 
level. This implies that the municipalities have a different foundation toward a sustainable 
development.

6.1.4 Q4 – Sustainable Development in the last 10 Years 
Finally, the sustainable development was explored for the last 10 years as presented in Section 5.1.3. 
When exploring the development in sustainability over time, it was found that all municipalities have 
experienced a positive development when looking on the percentage development across the 
indicators from 2007 to 2017. However, from survey question 8, 18 % answered that they perceive 
themselves as less sustainable in 2017 that in 2007. This might either be a result of different 
definitions of sustainability or that they have not experienced the expected development. This 
emphasise the importance of a unified national definition and measurement method. 
The municipalities with the greatest progress in sustainability was found to be the peripherical 
municipalities located towards the west and south. This implies that the peripherical municipalities 
do not have the biggest challenges in becoming more sustainable as expected. This may be a result 
of hard work from the municipalities but may also be a result of not having to deal with the new 
demands that the urbanisations and increased cultural differences have created closer to Copenhagen.  
The average Zealand have had the biggest positive development in indicator 3, 10 and 12 which 
indicates that the inequality has become smaller in these areas over the last 10 years. The development 
in indicator 3 and 12 concerning investment in nature protection and innovation may be a result of 
the increased focus on sustainability. As explained in Section 3.3, one of the primary challenges for 
Denmark to reach the SDGs is to invest more. Therefore, the development may be a result of increased 
political attention. Additionally, the development can be a signal of necessity in the municipalities, 
as the increased investment in nature protection can be an action against the climate changes and the 
increased investment in innovation can be an attempt to create economic growth.  
The big development in indicator 10 concerning digitalisation and better internet access is expected 
to be a result of the published digitalisation strategy in 2014. This indicates that a specific 
governmental goal has created a progress within this area, which emphasises that governmental 
initiatives can help evolve the development.    
On the contrary, Zealand has in average declined on the indicators 1, 4 and 11 in the same period.  
The decline in indicator 1 measuring cars per inhabitants is expected to be closely related to the 
cheaper car prices and a longer commuting distance to work in the last 10 years. This is a development 
that also may have been a result of political decisions in the last decade. This is a bad development 
as pollution additionally is highlighted as one of the biggest challenges in Denmark in achieving the 
SDGs presented in Section 3.3.    
This is closely related to the development in attractive workplaces covered by indicator 4. For a long 
period of time starting in 2010 one fifth of all municipal and governmental jobs was moved from the

6.1.5 Q5 – Factors Influencing the Sustainable Development 
It is interesting to reflect upon which elements that are important to secure a sustainable development 
in the future. This is mainly highlighted in Section 5.2.2 and 5.3 presenting the results from the survey 
and interviews.  
6.1.5.1 Political Support and Initiators 
As emphasised in the survey and the interviews, 87 % of the asked municipalities found political 
support and prioritisation one of the most important aspects to ensure a sustainable development. This 
was also one of the conclusions made in the study conducted by Jens-Philip as presented in Section 
3.3.1. Furthermore, this is substantiated with the analysis of the political relation discussed in Section 
6.1.3.3 showing that a broad political support creates the biggest development. This is very interesting 
as the main sustainable initiatives are made in a sub-department in Helsingør whereas the initiatives 
are made in the more political executive borders in and Lyngby-Taarbæk. This could be the 
explanation for their different performances as the politicians have a massive influence. 
Though, it may not always have to be the politicians that take the initiatives. 43 % of the 
municipalities find initiators internally in the municipality important as well. From the interview with 
Helsingør, Karen Marie does a massive amount of work to create environmentally sustainability 
initiatives in the municipality and have from that convinced the politicians about the importance of 
the work. This is done by continuously communicating what is done and the progress. In Helsingør 
municipality Karen Marie has showed clear results and thereby have influenced the politicians in 
finding the environmental sustainability important. This means that, without initiators influencing the 
politicians, prioritisation by the politicians themselves is a necessity to ensure a positive development.   
6.1.5.2 Human and Economic Resources 
Additionally, 70 % of the municipalities agrees on the necessity having of sufficient human and 
economic resources to ensure sustainable development. This is again in accordance with Jens-Philips 
study presented in Section 3.3.1. The need of economic resources may be obvious, but as Jakob 
emphasised in the interview, the sustainable work is not to be perceived as an extra task but as a 
change in mindset. He elaborates by mentioning that; 
“To achieve this (focus on sustainability), it is important that the administration identifies what they 
can gain by working with sustainability. Because I think that it (sustainability) is a benefit for all, but 
of course we need to find a way to get everyone along.” (Appendix R) 
This indicates that it is not only a matter of extra resources but probably also a matter of using the 
resources in the most profitable way.

Furthermore, municipality #1 points out proper collaboration internally in the municipalities as an 
important aspect to ensure sustainable development. If all departments help each other to understand

6.1.5.3 Legislation or Guidance  
Finally, legislations are highlighted as one of the most important aspects in ensuring a sustainable 
development. This is supported by 61 % of the municipalities and by Karen Marie as mentioned

published report created by Realdania and Sustainia meets this wish by collecting sustainable 
initiatives conducted all over Denmark in one report as an inspiration source (Sustainia, 2018). From

to the municipalities to find their optimal way of reaching these goals, making room for collaboration 
between the municipalities, companies and inhabitants.

as highlighted by 70 % of the municipalities. This is since extra work needs extra personal and 
money.  
• Finally, 61 % of the municipalities find legislations necessary to ensure a sustainable 
development, as lacking legislations can make some municipalities deprioritise the 
sustainability actions. Though legislations may also make the development process too 
controlled.

6.1.6 Q6 - Possible Reasons for Inequality Between Neighbouring Municipalities 
From the overall sustainability analysis in Section 5.1, it was found that the most sustainable 
municipalities in some cases are located next to or close to the least sustainable municipalities. The 
following section will reflect upon why neighbouring municipalities can experience such an 
inequality in sustainability with almost identical locations. This is based on the result discussed 
earlier. The municipalities used for the comparisons are Solrød and Brøndby, Hillerød and Helsingør.  
6.1.6.1 Sustainability Performance 
As shortly mentioned in Section 5.1, Solrød generally perform well across all the indicators whereas 
Brøndby on the contrary performs well in a few indicators but bad in the rest. Especially in the social 
indicators, a clear difference is seen with Brøndby performing the worst. The same thing goes for 
Helsingør that performs worse than Hillerød on the social indicators. From the previous discussion 
this picture can be a result of the demography as also highlighted as a problem by Municipality #1 in 
Table 5.9.     
6.1.6.1 Demography 
Both Solrød and Hillerød have a higher level of education compared to Brøndby and Helsingør as 
presented in Figure 6.1. This is identified to affect the sustainability and thereby create an inequality. 
The same thing goes for attractive workspaces where Solrød and Hillerød clearly performs better than 
Brøndby and Helsingør. However, when exploring the number of workspaces, Brøndby have more 
than twice as many workplaces than Solrød per 100 inhabitants (VIVE, 2017) which may imply that 
it is the attractiveness rather than the number of jobs that affects the sustainability.     
Furthermore, Brøndby is one of the municipalities in Denmark with the biggest percentage of 
inhabitants with a different ethnical origin than Danish at 31.2 % (Olsen & Christensen, 2017). This 
is a big contrast to the 8 % in Solrød. These inhabitants in Brøndby are primarily located in the sub 
area, Brøndby Strand, that are categorised as an exposed area by the Danish government. This implies 
that they have some challenges on the social aspect like the analysis also shows. The ethnicity alone 
may not be the entire reason for the bad performance, but the many different cultures can be a result 
of the bad performance on the indicator covering safety (7). This is the same results as seen earlier 
when comparing Copenhagen and Frederiksberg.  
On the contrary, the ethnicity in Hillerød and Helsingør is not as different, as for Solrød and Brøndby, 
as Hillerød have 11.3 % inhabitant with another ethnical origin than Danish compared to 12.3 % in 
Helsingør. This emphasise that the origin alone may not cause the inequality in sustainability. Yet it 
is still on the social indicators that the two municipalities differ. This again points towards education 
as being a factor influencing the inequality.

6.1.7 Q7 – Future Actions to Ensure a Progress in Sustainability  
To wrap-up all the previous discussions, this section will try to highlight suggested actions needed to 
ensure a sustainable development in the future, both on a national level and within each municipality.   
6.1.7.1 National Level 
The first important action on the governmental level can be to ensure that the municipalities know 
what are expected of them. This can either be through legislation or just better communication. The

results from the e-mail contributions and interviews showed a different opinion in how clear the 
expectations currently are for the municipalities, which can be a big issue if Zealand, and Denmark 
as a whole, want to stay amongst the most sustainable countries in the world. Ensuring a common 
understanding between the municipalities and government is therefore of high priority.  
Furthermore, legislations within the sustainability field may be reasonable to consider as substantiated 
earlier. This is to ensure that the political prioritising alone cannot prevent a sustainable development. 
The legislations should probably not be made in a very detailed scale stating exactly what to do, but 
instead be more loosely described leaving room for the municipalities to find the best way for them 
to address the legislations as promoted by Jakob. A superior legislation could be in the same scale as 
the ones used in the certification arrangement saying that for example all municipalities must reduce 
X % of the electricity used each year. Additionally, it could be legislated that all municipalities must 
create a sustainability strategy as earlier done with the energy strategy. However, this would create a 
need of the government to make a guideline of what such a strategy should encompass as emphasised 
in the study by Jens-Philip explained in Section 3.3.1. This is again to ensure a proper effort by all.   
The only challenge with these legislations is highlighted by Jakob in Appendix R to be that the 
municipalities probably will make a demand of economic support by the government. Though, if this 
can be solved, legislations may contribute a lot to ensuring a wanted development.  
Another governmental initiative working as an alternative to actual legislations could be to let 
specialists create several extra certification arrangements for the Danish municipalities to follow. This 
would according to the asked municipalities, make it more attractive for the municipalities to 
implement sustainability in their strategies.   
Finally, it could boost the sustainable development if inspiration catalogues are created for all three 
pillars of sustainability. Realdania have recently publish an inspiration catalogue, called KLIMA100, 
that have collected 100 of the best climate solutions implemented in the Danish municipalities. This 
could probably work as a big inspiration catalogue for the municipalities that either want to do 
something extra or help inspire the municipalities with no specialists to find the ideas themselves as 
stated missing by Municipality #1.       
6.1.7.2 Municipal Level 
Moving on to the municipal level, the first action, to ensure a sustainable development, can be to 
create a common definition within the municipality border. This is an essential action to ensure a 
common understanding on how to create a sustainable development. This definition must be inspired 
by the national strategy for sustainable development to ensure that weighting of each domain is in 
accordance with the national goals. This is important, as the results from question 5 in the survey,

This creation of a definition can advantageously be substantiated by the creation of a detailed strategy

• Furthermore, it may be importance to ensure that the municipalities know what is expected of 
them as highlighted by Municipality #1. If not the wanted development can stay absent. 
• In Addition, legislations, guidance or more certification arrangement, to achieve a certain goal 
can be needed to ensure an even prioritisation within all municipalities.  
• Finally, it could promote sustainability for all municipalities to create a sustainability strategy 
and to create a measurement method to follow the development across the three domains.

6.2 Discussion of Methodology 
The following part will shortly go through the applied methods used in this thesis. This aim to reflect 
upon the things that could have been done differently or better within each method. This would help 
to avoid the same challenges in potential future projects. In general, the combination of the used 
methods has worked very well together to address the research questions, which means that the 
combination of quantitative and qualitative methods would be recommended for similar projects in 
the future.    
6.2.1 Indicator Development Process 
When developing the indicator set, a few things could have been done differently and thereby may 
have improved the outcome.  
Firstly, it would have been preferred only to look at one assessment year since the three years have 
created a massive challenge of finding appropriate and relevant data. This have been a big limitation 
in the choice of possible indicators excluding some of the preferred indicators.  
Additionally, it would have been optimal with a bit larger indicator set to avoid getting conflicting 
results as seen in the two different economic studies. This would make the results of the analysis more 
representative of the real situation.    
6.2.2 Comparative Method  
When analysing and comparing the data, another ranking system would probably have been preferred 
for the yearly comparison. This is to find a ranking system that weights the difference within each 
indicator equally to ensure that the indicator with the greatest relative difference does not overpower 
the others. This could for example have been done by dividing the performance within each indicator 
into a permanent number of performance categories and then make the ranking based on that. This 
would reflect the actual difference between the municipalities better instead of only being reliable 
when assessing one municipality at the time.  
6.2.3 Survey  
The survey has contributed with a lot of interesting results and have worked as a big inspiration to 
the interviews. In future projects it would be prioritised to have more test-municipalities to evaluate 
the asked questions to ensure that the municipalities do not find the questions too political for them 
to answer. This would probably result in a higher response rate.  
Furthermore, it would have been preferred to get answers from more diverse departments instead of 
the majority being within the environmental field.

6.3 Future Work  
Based on this thesis, several additional questions and possible further investigations have raised. From

development. This could help explain the inequality and help the government to prioritise further

Finally, the exploration of the results shows some interesting patterns between sustainability and 
political constellations. These could be very interesting to explore further for all of Denmark. This 
could possibly identify which political constellation that is the most efficient in ensuring a sustainable 
development in Denmark and at the same time challenge the parties that see themselves as sustainable 
initiators.

7 Conclusion 
Through the sustainability analysis of the 45 municipalities on Zealand and the conduction of survey 
and interviews, this thesis has reached interesting insight in the current and present sustainability level 
and development in parts of the Danish municipalities. From the performed assessments, several 
conclusions can be drawn relevant for answering each research question. These will be presented 
sequentially in the following. 
From the analysis, it can be concluded that there is no linear mathematical relationship between 
sustainability holistically in the municipalities and the distance to Copenhagen. This is argued by a 
coefficient of determination, of 0.0516 and a p-value of 0.13 showing no significance of the relation. 
Though the results showed a linear relation between the social sustainability indicators and distance 
to Copenhagen with a moderate coefficient of determination of 0.4371. This result implied that the 
closer the municipalities are located to Copenhagen, the more social sustainable they become. This 
can be a result of the higher education level in the capital area, positively influencing other indicators 
like health, employment and income as found in the literature review. Furthermore, the results showed 
a linear relationship between economic sustainability and distance to Copenhagen with a coefficient 
of determination above moderate of 0.6881. This is implying that the further away the municipalities 
are from Copenhagen, the more economic sustainable they become. This have been identified mainly 
to be a result of the high housing prices and high economic inequality in Copenhagen that may be a 
result of the urbanisation in the greater Copenhagen area. 
Secondly, the analysis results have showed that Copenhagen is not the most sustainable municipality 
on Zealand with a placement as the 33rd most sustainable municipality with a sustainability score of 
298. This is a decline in placement of 10 places since 2007. This may be a result of the urbanisation 
and increased cultural differences making Copenhagen perform bad on the indicators regarding 
affordable housing and safety from criminal offences. However, Copenhagen is amongst the best 
performing municipalities regarding green mobility, waste management and education. Contrary, 
Solrød is the most sustainable municipality with a general good performance across all indicators and 
a sustainability score of 204. This may imply that a general good performance on all three 
sustainability domains is more effective than a very good performance on few indicators and bad on 
the rest. Finally, the results showed that both the most and least sustainable municipalities are located 
close to Copenhagen. The most sustainable are Solrød (score 204), Hillerød (score 210) and Furesø 
(score 215) and the least sustainable municipalities are Brøndby (score 348), Helsingør (score 340) 
and Høje-Taarstrup (score 333).

same possibility of achieving a sustainable development as 64 % of the asked municipalities also 
believes. Furthermore, the comparison indicated that a broad political constellation can create the 
inequality in sustainability as previously stated.       
Lastly, several national and local actions have been identified as being a possible help in the 
continuation of the sustainable development in the municipalities and in Denmark. From the 
interviews and survey, it has been found a profitable move for the government to make some 
legislations or clear guidelines for the sustainable development. This should be done to ensure that 
the municipalities are aware of what is expected of them and contributes equally to the Danish 
development. Additionally, the interviews and e-mail contribution showed that none of the 
municipalities have a uniform definition of sustainability within the municipality organisation which 
may be a good idea to ensure a common development within all departments. Finally, none of the 
asked municipalities had an actual measurement method to measure their sustainable development 
which also may be a good idea to create, to keep track of the development and what to prioritise.
