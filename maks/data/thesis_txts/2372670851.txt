Abstract
Aalborg Portland A/S is the sole producer of cement in Denmark.
The cement is
produced from local materials, including chalk, which is quarried at the site. Many
years of quarrying has created an azure blue lake at the bottom of the chalk pit, which
will become a recreational area when quarrying at the site has ended. In order to
make the area more accessible, microﬁller - a byproduct from puriﬁcation of ﬂue gas
in the kilns at the factory, is used to smooth the steep slopes surrounding the lake.
Microﬁller contains salts and heavy metals, and is a very alkaline product. Leaching
of heavy metals to the chalk lake is of concern, making the consideration of remedial
techniques necessary. This study assesses the potential for using phytoremediation, a
technique where plants are used to take up contaminants, to prevent leaching from the
microﬁller.
In order to test the toxicity of microﬁller towards plants, the willow tree acute toxicity
test was used. Furthermore microﬁller, soil, plant and water samples were collected at
the site, which were analyzed for heavy metals, along with solutions from the willow
tree toxicity test. In addition, simulations were made using a plant uptake model in
combination with a tipping buckets model, to predict the fate and transport of the
individual heavy metals at the site with grass and tree covers.
The results of the willow tree acute toxicity test showed that the high salinity (4800
µS/cm) and alkalinity (pH=12.7) of the microﬁller was toxic to the willow tree cuttings.
In the preliminary test inhibition of >70% was observed already after 24 hours. A
dilution series was prepared with the ratios 1:1, 1:4 and 1:9. The results showed that
only the 1:1 dilution had a toxic eﬀect on the trees, where transpiration was inhibited
by >70% after 48 hours.
The results of the heavy metal analysis showed highest
uptake of chromium (Cr) (55-208 mg/kg fw), nickel (Ni) (42-185 mg/kg fw) and copper
(Cu) (84-447 mg/kg fw).
The results of the heavy metal analysis of the samples
collected at the site, showed that microﬁller contains high concentrations of heavy
metals, especially zinc (Zn) (603 mg/kg dw). Furthermore, the results showed that
the soil at the site contained heavy metal concentrations resembling Danish background
levels. The heavy metal concentrations in the plants were higher than those found at
urban gardening sites, and some of the measured values of lead (Pb) and cadmium (Cd)
exceed tolerated concentrations in fruits and vegetables. Results of the plant uptake
model showed that planting trees instead of grass decreases leaching of heavy metals
from the microﬁller. Predictions also showed that leaching is the most dominating
process for Pb, selenium (Se) and partly Zn.
The dominating process for Cd was
plant uptake, but in general simulations showed that neither process is signiﬁcant in
comparison to the vast amounts of heavy metals present at the site.
Based on the ﬁndings of this study, phytoremediation is not an appropriate remediation
technique for the Aalborg Portland site, due to the high toxicity of microﬁller and the
little diﬀerence plants have on the overall amounts of heavy metals present at the site.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The Danish cement factory Aalborg Portland A/S was founded more than 125 years

Humans have utilized heavy metals since ancient times, but utilization increased with
industrialization and anthropogenic activities have increased the presence of heavy
metals in the environment (background concentrations in Danish soils can be seen in

steel and Tl was once used in pest control products. But heavy metals also originate
from natural sources such as weathering and degradation of organic material (Jensen,
2000).
Furthermore atmospheric deposition contributes to heavy metal content in
soils (Ellermann et al., 2015) (Jensen et al., 1996). Most deposited heavy metals in
Denmark originate from anthropogenic sources abroad and are carried long distances
with the wind (Ellermann et al., 2015). Natural sinks of heavy metals include leaching
to ground water and surface water and uptake by plants, but regulations have also

Heavy metals are non-degradable, bio-accumulative and somewhat immobile (some
more than others) (Jensen, 2000). Metals such as Cr, Cu and Pb adsorb very strongly
to soil making them immobile (Jensen et al., 1996). Other heavy metals such as Ni
and Cd adsorb less strongly and are likewise more mobile. Ion exchange happens on
the soil particle surfaces, and soils with large total surface area (such as clay) have the
capacity to bind heavy metals more strongly than other soils such as sand. The surfaces
of most soil particles are negatively charged and therefore mainly cations (including
Pb, Cd, Ni, Cu and Zn) bind to them. Anions (including Se, Mo, Cr(VI)) do not
bind as strongly to soils due to the fact that they are negatively charged. Anions,
however, can still be retained in soils, for example due to precipitation (McLaughlin
et al., 2011).
pH and Cation Exchange capacity (CEC) are the two main factors that inﬂuence
adsorption of heavy metals to soil (Jensen et al., 1996). pH has the largest eﬀect on
the partition coeﬃcient, Kd, and hereby heavy metal mobility (Sauvé et al., 2000),
which increases when pH decreases (does not apply for Mo). This is due to the fact
that H+ competes with heavy metals in binding with soil particle surfaces under acidic
conditions (Jensen, 2000). Decreasing pH will increase the mobility of Cd, Cr(III),
Ni and Zn, and decrease mobility of Cr(VI). Under alkaline conditions Se, however,
becomes water soluble (as selenite or selenate) (Fishbein, 1983).
The levels of Cr, Ni and Zn and to some extent Cd in soils also depend on soil texture.
This, however, does not apply for Pb (Jensen et al., 1996). Soil organic matter also has
a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on heavy metal availability (Sauvé et al., 2000), which increases
with organic matter due to retention of cations (Bot and Benites, 2005). Capillary
rise in soils can cause contaminants to be transported upwards through the soil layer.
Capillary rise is slowest in ﬁne grained soils, but the water can be transported over
longer distances than in coarse grained soils, where capillary rise occurs at a higher
rate but over shorter distances (Brouwer et al., 1985).

2.3 Plant Processes & Phytoremediation of Heavy Metals
2.3
Plant Processes & Phytoremediation of Heavy Metals
In order to compensate for water lost through transpiration plants take up water
from their surroundings - mainly from soil (Larcher, 1995). Transpiration occurs like
evaporation and is driven by a deﬁcit of water vapour between soil and air, where
plants act as the transport route (Trapp et al., 2000).
Water for transpiration is
taken up through the roots from where it is transported to the transpiring surfaces
(including leaves) mainly through the xylem (McLaughlin et al., 2011) (Trapp et al.,
2000). The water evaporates from the leaves through the stomata, which can open
and close, depending on the conditions in the surrounding environment. The stomata
will open when the supply of light and water is high and the concentration of CO2 in
the plant is low. The stomata close when the water potential in leaves is below the
transpiration threshold (Trapp et al., 2000).
As mentioned, plants mainly absorb water from soil, which is saturated during pre-
cipitation. The rain water slowly inﬁltrates vertically towards the groundwater, but
some water is withheld in the soil layer due to capillary action. This water is termed
capillary water and contributes to the Field Capacity (FC), which is the water in the
soil, after saturation is obtained and inﬁltration through the soil layer has occurred.
FC is highest in ﬁne-grained soils such as clay and lowest in coarse-grained soils such
as loam. Plants will thrive best in soils with high FC because this ensures that there
will be water for transpiration even in dry periods (Larcher, 1995). Under dry condi-
tions plants also have the ability to extend their roots to deeper soil layers in order
to reach water for transpiration (Candell et al., 1996). Roots will however not extend
into compact layers where the soil bulk density is high. This applies for layers con-
sisting of shale and rocks (Dobson and Moﬀat, 1995). Furthermore the water table
limits the vertical growth of plant roots (Candell et al., 1996). Most of the plant root
biomass is located in the top 50 cm of the soil layer, but in areas with limited soil
depth, vegetation may be extra sensitive to strong winds (Dobson and Moﬀat, 1995).
As mentioned in Section 2.2 heavy metals dissolved in pore water can be absorbed by
plants during transpiration (McLaughlin et al., 2011). The diﬀerent processes involved
in uptake of metals can be seen in Figure 2.7. Using plants to absorb contaminants
from soil and water is called phytoremediation and is a cheap, non-invasive and sus-
tainable remediation technique. Phytoremediation is not at new concept and has been
used for over 300 years, for instance in wastewater treatment (Saier Jr. and Trevors,
2010) (Cunningham and Berti, 1993). Plants with accumulating properties and high
transpiration rates are preferred, and ﬂowers are therefore not an appropriate choice
for phytoremediation due to their lack of functional stomata (Larcher, 1995), which
prevent suﬃcient uptake.
Phytoremediation can be used to remove both organic and inorganic compounds -
including heavy metals (Salt et al., 1998) (Algreen et al., 2014), which are taken up
as ions through ion channels (McLaughlin et al., 2011). Most plants do not take up
more heavy metals than necessary and several plants use regulatory mechanisms to
overcome high concentrations. These mechanisms include sequestration in cell walls
and conducting heavy metals to specialized cell compartments. Furthermore plants

2.3.1
Plant Stress
Besides heavy metal toxicity, plant growth can also be aﬀected by other parameters,
including salinity and pH. High salt concentrations in the pore water will change the
osmotic pressure around the roots, decreasing the osmotic potential and the uptake of
water. This will in turn inhibit the plant growth (Shabala and Munns, 2012; Munns,
2002; Lãuchli and Grattan, 2012).
In addition high salinity will damage the plant
cells in the leaves inhibiting transpiration and in this way decreasing the growth rate
(Shabala and Munns, 2012). High pH aﬀects the nutrient availability in the soil. A
decrease in the availability of the essential elements can also decrease growth rate.

3.1
The Willow Tree Acute Toxicity Test
The willow tree acute toxicity test was developed by Professor Stefan Trapp (Trapp
et al., 2000) and is based on the ﬁndings of Thompson et al. (1998). The toxicity
is determined based on the inhibition of the transpiration of the willow trees (Trapp
et al., 2000), and was in this case used to determine the toxicity of microﬁller. The
willow tree acute toxicity test was done according to the laboratory manual which was
prepared by Trapp (2016) based on the article made by Trapp et al. (2000).
3.1.1
Preliminary Test
Prior to the toxicity test a preliminary test was run to gain knowledge about the
toxicity of microﬁller, which could be used to determine if and how much dilution was
necessary. In the preliminary test, only one concentration of 100 g microﬁller/L was
tested in ﬁve replicates. Furthermore ﬁve controls, containing water and nutrients,
were made to compare with.
The microﬁller solution was a mixture of a nutrient
solution and crushed microﬁller.

3.1.3
Running the Test
As in the preliminary test, the transpiration was determined at the beginning of the
test period, before exposing the trees to the diluted stock solution. On the ﬁrst day
of the test, the weight of the willow tree cuttings was determined before placing them
in 500 ml Erlenmeyer ﬂasks. Aluminum foil was rolled up and wrapped around the
willow trees, keeping the cuttings in place so that only the roots were submerged
in the solution. To ensure that the only passage from the ﬂasks to the surrounding
atmosphere is through the plant, paraﬁlm was used for sealing. (Sealed Erlenmeyer
ﬂasks can be used due to the fact that willows have a pressurized ventilation system,
which supplies the roots with oxygen (Trapp et al., 2000).) The whole apparatus was
wrapped in aluminum foil to prevent algae growth in the ﬂasks. Before placing the
trees under the lights, the whole apparatus was weighed and marked to ensure that
the trees would be placed in exactly in the same way each time they were weighed.
On the second day the apparatus was weighed again, and the transpiration determined
by subtracting the weight from the start weight that was found on the previous day.
The trees were ranked with respect to their weight loss giving the cutting with the
highest weight loss/highest transpiration a ranking of 1. The cutting with the highest
transpiration was used for a control, the second highest was given the highest dose
of microﬁller, the third highest transpiring tree was given the second highest dose of
microﬁller and so forth, to get an even distribution of the best and worst transpiring
trees. The tree with the lowest ranking is also a control (Trapp, 2016).
The diluted stock solutions were then transferred to the Erlenmeyer ﬂasks. In total 17
Erlenmeyer ﬂasks were used - four for each of the three dilutions and ﬁve controls, and

Transpiration
To evaluate the inhibition of the treated tree’s transpiration the Normalized Relative
Transpiration (NRT) can be calculated. This calculation compares the transpiration of
the treated trees with the transpiration of control trees (Trapp, 2016) and is calculated
as follows

1
n · Pn
i=1 Ti(C, t)/Tj(C, 0)
1

A plant uptake model was set up for each of the metals of most interest (Cd, Pb, Se

4.4
Modelling Results
Eight diﬀerent models were set up to simulate the transport and plant uptake of Cd,
Pb, Se and Zn. These metals were chosen due to the fact that Pb and Cd are non-
essential metals for plants, Zn showed the highest concentration in microﬁller and Se
has been stated by Rambøll (2012) to be of special concern in regards to leaching. The
models were made for both grass and tree cover to determine the impact of vegetation
type.
Figure 4.14 shows the annual water balance over a two year period for both grass
(left) and tree (right) cover respectively, including precipitation, transpiration and
leaching of water from the lowest layer deﬁned in the model (Layer 5). The leaching of
metals follow the same trend as leaching of water. The ﬁgures show that transpiration
begins in the spring and ends in early autumn for both models. Furthermore it can
be seen that leaching only occurs in the months where the plants do not transpire
and ﬂuctuates with precipitation, increasing when precipitation increases. This occurs
because the water content in the soil layers is higher than FC and water will therefore
leach to the underlying layers, ultimately leaching out of the last layers.
Figure 4.14: Annual water balance (L/d) with grass (left) and tree (right) cover.
Figure 4.15 shows the water content in the ﬁve soil layers that have been deﬁned in
the models. Like the annual water balance, the water content is shown over a two
year period and for both grass and tree cover. The initial water content is set equal
to PWP to simulate that the layers initially are empty. The layers start to ﬁll up in
September where the precipitation increases and the transpiration is low. The water
content is equal to FC until the plants start to transpire in March, emptying the top
layer ﬁrst and gradually taking up water from the deeper layers.
For the grass models the water content for layers 4 and 5 is constant because the
remaining transpiration in layer 3 is set to zero, in order to simulate that the grass
roots do not extend to the underlying layers, and therefore can not take up water

5.1
Willow Tree Acute Toxicity Test and Heavy Metal Uptake
5.1.1
Toxicity Test
The willow tree acute toxicity test was carried out to determine whether microﬁller
is toxic to plants. Firstly a preliminary test was carried out in order to assess the
toxicity before a dilution of the microﬁller solution was made (see Section 3.1).
The results of the preliminary willow tree acute toxicity test showed that a microﬁller
concentration of 100 g/L was very toxic to the willow tree cuttings and that a dilution
series was necessary to evaluate at which concentrations of microﬁller the willows could
survive. Findings by Trapp et al. (2008) have shown that salinity concentrations above
1 g/L, corresponding to approximately 1900 µS/cm (calculated from linear calibration
curve stated in Trapp et al. (2008)), inhibits the growth of plants. Based on the fact
that the conductivity of the solution in the preliminary test was measured to 4800
µS/cm, it is fair to assume that the growth of the willow tree cuttings was severely
inhibited due to high salt concentrations in combination with high pH (12.71), and not
due to the toxicity of individual heavy metals in the solution. Findings by Trapp et al.
(2008) have also shown that the uptake of salt is higher than the uptake of water at
high salt concentrations, increasing toxicity, which contributes to the conclusion that
high salt concentrations were the main toxicant.
The results of the deﬁnitive willow tree acute toxicity test showed that the willow tree
cuttings could overcome the conditions when the microﬁller solution was diluted in
the ratio 1:4. In this solution the salt concentration will have been diluted ﬁve times
as well giving a conductivity of approximately 1000 µS/cm, which corresponds to the
levels of salts that willow trees can endure. This contributes to the conclusion that
the willow trees die from high levels of salts before they are aﬀected by the toxicity of
individual heavy metals in the microﬁller solution.
The roots of willow tree cuttings exposed to diluted doses of microﬁller were shorter
than those observed in the control trees. This could possibly indicate that the willows
are aﬀected by the heavy metals that were taken up by the plants during the test,
since this is a toxicity symptom of all the heavy metals of interest (see Table 2.2).
Based on these symptoms, the growth rates of the plants at the site could potentially
be lower than under conditions with non-contaminated soils.
==PAGE:78==
58
Since the test showed that the willow tree cuttings can endure diluted microﬁller,
plants will be able to grow on the NGAs, under the right circumstances. If the plant
roots extend deeply into the topsoil layer, microﬁller may be present in the pore
water. The microﬁller, however, will be diluted due to precipitation and plants will
most likely be able to survive, based on the ﬁndings of the test. If the roots extend
further into the underlying microﬁller, the plants will die from high salt concentrations.
The rooting depth depends on the vegetation type and the soil environment. Plants
with short roots will not be exposed to toxic levels of microﬁller, while other plants
such as Populus Tremula (aspen) with root lengths of up to 2 m may (Candell et al.,
1996). In addition, drought may cause the roots to extend into the microﬁller, where
salt concentrations will be higher and toxic. The high density of the byproduct may
however prohibit the roots from extending into into the microﬁller layer, and in this
way the plants may be able to survive. If the roots do not extend into the microﬁller,
the level of metal content will however not be reduced.
5.1.2
Willow Tree Uptake
The results of the heavy metal analysis of the solutions from the willow tree acute
toxicity test showed relatively high uptake of heavy metals. Especially high uptake
of Cr, Ni, and Cu was detected. The higher uptake of Cu and Ni can be explained
by the fact that these heavy metals are essential for plants (see Section 2.2) and have
nutritional value. The uptake of Zn did not show the same dose dependent relationship
that was found for the other metals. If plants are grown in a nutrient solution with
limited space, like the willow cuttings in the willow tree toxicity test, the concentration
of ions in the external solution will change over time due to ion selective uptake (White,
2012a). This may be the reason for the negative uptake that was found for Zn in the
willow trees. If the plants take up water faster than the metal, the concentration of the
metal in the solution will increase. Another explanation could be that plants may be
able to extrude unwanted solutes from root cells by using energy and enzymes (Trapp
et al., 2008). The unexpected uptake may also be due to measurement uncertainties,
and more tests should therefore be run to ﬁnd the true uptake of Zn.
The percentage change from initial concentration to concentration at the end of the
test was calculated (Tables 4.2, 4.3 and 4.4). If an error of approximately 5% is applied
for both initial and end concentrations it can be assumed that a percentage change
under 10 % is insigniﬁcant and plant uptake cannot be assumed. The results of the
calculations showed that there was a signiﬁcant change in concentration over time
for Cr, Ni, Cu and partly Pb. A signiﬁcant change in concentration of Se was not
observed for the diluted solution containing high microﬁller content. This can explain
the odd uptake tendency shown in Figure 4.8. The percentage change between the
initial and end concentration of Mo is quite low for the solution with high microﬁller
content, indicating that there possibly isn’t a signiﬁcant uptake of Mo. This and the
large standard deviations and conﬁdence intervals (which can be seen in Table K.1)
explain the random uptake tendency observed for Mo, which can be seen in Figure
4.8. Other conditions may also have aﬀected the uptake of heavy metals in the willow
tree cuttings including the fact that microﬁller contains high levels of Ca2+ (Rambøll,

5.2
Heavy Metal Analysis of Collected Samples
The heavy metal analysis of microﬁller, soil, plants and water samples from the Rørdal
site was carried out in order to assess the current conditions at the site, and to generate
results that could be used as input in the plant uptake model.
5.2.1
Soil and Microﬁller
The results of the heavy metal analysis of soil samples collected from the site showed
that concentrations in general where highest in the top section of the topsoil layer.
This tendency can be attributed to the organic carbon content, which is highest in
the top layer. Organic matter retains cations in the soil making them available for
plants to utilize (Bot and Benites, 2005). Furthermore atmospheric deposition of heavy
metals may have contributed to the elevated concentrations in the top section of the
soil. A study prepared by Al-Khashman and Shawabkeh (2006) found relatively high
concentrations of Pb, Zn and Cd in the topsoil near a cement factory in southern
Jordan. This indicates that the higher concentrations in the top layer of the topsoil
could be caused by emissions of heavy metals from the cement factory located close
to the NGAs.

it can be observed that none of the concentrations in the samples exceed the thresh-
old values, and all concentrations correspond to Danish background concentrations
(see Table 2.1). This indicates that the high concentrations of heavy metals in the
underlying microﬁller layer does not aﬀect the topsoil.

were analyzed for heavy metals. The sites diﬀered in contamination, from the least
contaminated site 1, which was a school garden, to the most contaminated site 3,

metals in the soil was assessed, including Cd, Cr, Cu, Ni, Pb and Zn and can be seen
in Table 5.1. When comparing the results from Warming et al. (2015) with the results
found in this study, it can be seen that the values resemble those found at the least
contaminated site (site 1).

Statistical Uncertainties
Standard deviations, conﬁdence intervals and t-tests were calculated to assess the va-
lidity of the obtained results. Some uncertainty is associated with the statistical tests
due to the fact that the number of microﬁller, soil, plant and water samples taken at
the site and analyzed for heavy metals, is rather small (2-3 samples). This makes the
statistical test more unsure than if the sample size had been larger (eg. n=50). This
is due to a lower degree of freedom (n-1) and the fact that the t-distribution will be
ﬂattened out, which increases the chance of making statistical errors. Another uncer-
tainty is associated with the assumption of normal distribution, which was assumed
based on the fact that the samples are taken in the same location and with the same
method.

5.3 Interpretation of Model Predictions
5.3
Interpretation of Model Predictions
When comparing the annual water balance for the two models (grass and trees) it
becomes evident that the vegetation type has a large eﬀect on the transpiration, and
the leaching of heavy metals accordingly. This suggests that planting trees on top
of the NGAs will have the greatest eﬀect on leaching in comparison to grass.
In
addition, trees give good conditions for wildlife habitat, increase biodiversity, ﬁx CO2
and increase the recreational value (Algreen et al., 2014; Dobson and Moﬀat, 1995).
Tables 4.9 and 4.13 show that the leaching from layer 5 is highest for Pb and Se and
lowest for Cd for both grass and tree models. The mobility and hereby also leaching
and plant uptake are closely correlated to the Kd values. The Kd values of both Pb
and Se are very low in the microﬁller, which explains the high level of leaching from
layer 5.
It was found that the change of concentration in soil over ﬁve simulation events was
greatest for Se (see Table 4.11). The mass balance (Table 4.13) for Se showed that
leaching is the dominating process and this means that the reduction in metal concen-
tration in the soil is not primarily due to plant uptake, indicating that phytoremedia-
tion of Se will not be successful. This was also observed for both Pb models and the
grass model for Zn. This means that Se and Pb may potentially leach to the chalk lake
or the groundwater no matter which of the two vegetation types are planted in the
topsoil. It should, however, be emphasized that the applied Kd values for the metals in
the microﬁller are calculated and the simulated predictions may therefore not be rep-
resentative for the actual processes occurring at the site. Further analysis is therefore
necessary to determine the extent of leaching of these metals including measurements
of Kd in the microﬁller. On the contrary, plant uptake was the dominating process for
Cd for both vegetation types and for Zn with a tree cover, but the reduction of metals
by either process (leaching or plant uptake) is in any case insigniﬁcant compared to
the vast amounts present at the site.
The simulations of metal concentrations in plants, showed very high levels of Pb,
Se and Zn (see Table 4.12). This can be explained by the high initial heavy metal
concentrations in the microﬁller layers, from which the plants transpire from in the
tree model. Furthermore the low Kd values calculated for Pb, Se and Zn contribute
to the prediction of high concentrations in the plants. When comparing the predicted
concentrations in plants with the toxic leaf concentrations presented in Table 2.3, it
can be seen that the concentrations of Pb, Se and Zn exceed the concentrations that
the plants can overcome, which suggests that the plants at the site would most likely
die if the roots extend into the microﬁller layers, prohibiting phytoremediation.
Diﬀerent variations of topsoil and vegetation types were simulated in order to assess
how leaching most eﬀectively could be limited. The topsoil thickness was increased
with 10%, and the largest eﬀect was found for the scenario where grass is used as cover.
In the scenario with grass cover and a topsoil thickness of 50 cm, layer 3 reaches FC
almost immediately. Increasing the thickness of the topsoil layer delays the time before
FC is reached resulting in a lower level of leaching to the next layer. Changing the
==PAGE:84==
64
soil layer thickness in the scenario with tree cover does not aﬀect the water content
in layer 3 as much, and does accordingly not have as large an eﬀect on leaching in
the tree model. The topsoil layer at NGA1 is approximately 50 cm deep meaning
that an increase of only 5 cm is necessary to decrease leaching. Furthermore a thicker
topsoil layer will ensure that vegetation with deeper rooting depth can grow at the site
without being aﬀected by strong winds. These ﬁndings should be taken into account
in the further planning of the rehabilitation of the chalk pit, but it should again be
mentioned that, in comparison to the initial metal mass, the leaching and plant uptake
are of such little importance that changing the topsoil thickness will only have a tiny
eﬀect on the overall amount of heavy metals present at the site.
A crop coeﬃcient of 1.1 was used for trees, but increasing this coeﬃcient to 1.2 elimi-
nates leaching from layer 5 already in year two. As can be seen in Figure 4.15 the tree
roots extend into the lower lying layers (layer 4 and 5 in the model) in order to have
suﬃcient amount of water for transpiration, which also means that heavy metals will
be taken up from these layers decreasing leaching and increasing the concentration in
the plants.
Comparison
The plant uptake model was also used to simulate the uptake of Cd and Pb by maize
and wheat, in a study carried out by Legind et al. (2012). This study found simulated
concentrations of 0.025-0.085 mg Cd/kg dw and 0.22-0.89 mg Pb/kg dw in plants.
These ﬁndings correspond well with the concentrations predicted by the grass models.
The metal concentrations in plants predicted by the tree models are much higher.
When comparing the predicted concentrations with the concentrations measured in the
plant samples from the Rørdal site it is easy to see that the measured concentrations
are much lower than those simulated with the tree model.
The concentrations in
plants predicted with the grass model, however, do not diﬀer quite as much from those
measured in the samples. The concentration in the plant samples are actually higher
than those predicted by the model, which could indicate that the level of deposition
used in the model does not correspond to the actual deposition at the site.
Uncertainties, Limitations and Future Work
The modelled fate of heavy metals in the topsoil and microﬁller layers is associated
with some uncertainties and overall the simulated results serve as an a illustration of
the processes aﬀecting the fate of the metals and not the actual processes taking place
at the NGAs.
In order to make the modelling results more representative for the site, actual pre-
cipitation and transpiration data should be measured at the site. The data used in
the model is a mixture of reference values from 2001-2010 and local measurements for
2013. In addition, crop speciﬁc evapotranspiration is calculated based on crop coeﬃ-
cients for only the initial growth stage, making the simulated leaching and uptake of
metals non-site-speciﬁc and somewhat uncertain. If calculated evapotranspiration is
used in the model, crop coeﬃcients for the entire growth period should be applied and
can be found for most vegetation types in Allen et al. (1998). This would increase the
accuracy of the simulated processes included by the model. Furthermore it could be
==PAGE:85==
5.3 Interpretation of Model Predictions
relevant to take climate change into consideration, when modelling for an extensive
amount of simulation events.
The deposition applied in the model is the same for all years, which is a simpliﬁcation.
Deposition changes from year to year, and mainly depends on the emission from the
source areas (Ellermann et al., 2015). It is assumed that deposition of some heavy
metals may be higher at this site due to the fact that it is located close to the cement
factory and using measurements from the site, would give a more reliable prediction
of the concentrations in plants and soil. This is of highest importance for Pb because
it was found that deposition of this metal has a higher eﬀect on the concentration in
soil compared to the others.
Kd for all metals in the microﬁller have been estimated instead of measured, which
may give some uncertainties in regards to the partitioning of the metals in the soil.
Using measured Kd values for the metals instead will increase the accuracy of the heavy
metal mobility. The calculated Kd for Pb is very low compared to Kd in soil (see Table
3.3). The reason for this is unknown and a test of Kd would make simulations more
accurate. A measurement of Kd can for example be assessed with a column test (see
USEPA (1999)). Kd varies considerably with pH, which is not taken into consideration
by the model. This gives arise to rather large uncertainties due to the fact that the
pH in the microﬁller is quite high compared to uncontaminated soils.
The model assumes that plant roots only grow downward towards water, which in
reality is not the case. This simpliﬁcation gives arise to some uncertainty. Furthermore
the high density of microﬁller may prevent the roots from growing downwards into
layers 4 and 5. This is not taken into account by the model and leaching from these
layers may in reality be higher due to lack of transpiration from these layers.
In
addition, the model assumes passive uptake of all metals with transpiration water,
which in reality is only the case for non-essential metals (Legind et al., 2012).
Se could not be analyzed by the ICP-MS. Therefore half the detection limit was used
for input concentration in the models (Legind et al., 2012), which will give results that
may not be consistent with the actual scenario.

Chapter 6
Recommendations for Aalborg Portland
A/S
Based on the ﬁndings of this study, phytoremediation is not recommended for the
Rørdal site at Aalborg Portland A/S, due to the little diﬀerence the plants can make
compared to the amount of heavy metals present at the site. The plant roots will most
likely not extend into the microﬁller layer under the topsoil, due to high density, and
if they do, the plants will most likely wilt and die because of the high salinity and
alkalinity of the microﬁller.
It is however recommended that Aalborg Portland A/S should plant trees in combi-
nation with the already present grass, since this can reduce leaching of the metals and
increase biodiversity and contribute to the recreational value of the area. Furthermore
it is recommended that the thickness of the topsoil layer is increased, in order to ensure
that other vegetation species than grass can grow at the site, without being sensitive
to powerful winds. Increasing the topsoil layer will also ensure that humans will not
come in contact with the toxic microﬁller.
It is not recommended that Aalborg Portland A/S chooses ﬂowers as main vegetation
species due to the fact that ﬂowers have low transpiration rates and will therefore not
reduce leaching. In addition, Aalborg Portland A/S should refrain from planting edible
fruits and vegetables at the site due to the fact that the heavy metal concentrations in
the plants may be elevated due to both uptake from soil and atmospheric deposition.
Adding fertilizer, however could increase the growth of other plants at the site and in
this way contribute to a reduction of heavy metal leaching.
The model simulations showed that the FC of the soil layers has a large eﬀect on
the amount of metals that leach from the lowest lying layer. Aalborg Portland A/S
should therefore prevent actions that can lead to soil compaction, such as use of heavy
machinery, to ensure that the FC is as high as possible. Furthermore a gravel layer at
the bottom of the topsoil layer could prevent a potential upward ﬂux of contaminants,
caused by capillary forces.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The toxicity of microﬁller was determined by the willow tree acute toxicity test. A
preliminary test showed high toxicity when willow cuttings were exposed to solutions
containing 100 g microﬁller/L. pH and conductivity measurements of 12.7 and 4800
µS/cm respectively, revealed that the growth inhibiting eﬀect of microﬁller most likely
could be attributed to high salinity and alkalinity, and not the toxicity of the individual
heavy metals present in the byproduct. A dilution series was made to examine at which
ratios the willow trees could survive. The results showed a dose-dependent eﬀect, where
the strongest eﬀect was seen for the trees exposed to a microﬁller-nutrient solution ratio
of 1:1. Furthermore the results showed that at a microﬁller - nutrient solution ratio of
1:4, the growth of the willows was not aﬀected.
A heavy metal analysis of the diluted microﬁller solutions at the end of the willow
tree acute toxicity test was carried out, in order to determine the willow tree uptake
of each individual metal. The results showed high uptake of Cr (55-208 mg/kg fw),
Ni (42-185 mg/ kg fw) and Cu (84-447 mg/kg fw), where highest uptake was observed
for cuttings exposed to the highest dose of microﬁller.
A heavy metal analysis of
microﬁller samples from the Rørdal site showed quite high levels of heavy metals,
resembling concentrations found at highly contaminated sites (former landﬁlls and
sludge disposal sites). The highest concentration was found for Zn (603 mg/kg dw)
and the lowest for Tl (2.3 mg/kg dw).
Comparison with QC, Danish background
levels and other studies showed that heavy metal concentrations in the soil from the
Rørdal site were quite low and not of any danger to humans. The concentrations in the
plant samples were however higher than those found at other sites, including urban
gardening sites. In addition the concentrations of Pb and Cd in some of the plant
samples exceeded the tolerated concentrations for fruits and vegetables.
Predictions simulated by the plant uptake model showed that leaching was highest for
Pb and Se, which was due to low Kd values in the microﬁller layer, and plant uptake was
highest and leaching lowest for the scenarios with tree covers. Furthermore, predictions
showed that leaching was the dominating process for Pb and Se with both grass and
tree cover, and for Zn with grass cover. The dominating process for Cd with both grass
and tree cover, and for Zn with tree cover was plant uptake. Based on the predictions
of the model, neither leaching nor plant uptake had a large eﬀect on the overall change
in the amount of each individual metal present in the soil and microﬁller layers at the
site. In conclusion, phytoremediation is not recommended for the Aalborg Portland
site. The amount of metals that will be removed by plant uptake is insigniﬁcant in
comparison the the amount present at the site. In addition, the plants would most
likely wilt and die if roots extend into the microﬁller layer containing the highest levels
of metals.
