Abstract
Accurate forecast of soil temperature plays an important role in areas such as land-atmosphere
interaction and in agriculture. However, due to the non-linear and chaotic nature, it is not
easy to accurately forecast temperature in soil. Several machine learning techniques has been
reviewed by time on the forecasting of ambient temperature, but in this thesis it will be investi-
gated whether it is possible to predict temperature in soil with machine learning. A comprehen-
sive review takes place in this thesis, reviewing how well the Artiﬁcial Neural Network (ANN) is
able to forecast temperature in soil. The focus on this review is in the period 2020-2022, where
the data used for the implementation is measured in this period. The review clearly shows that
it is possible to forecast soil temperature by using antecedent meteorological data as input. In
other words, the machine learning model has the potential to capture complex relationships
between many different factors that in combination contribute to a certain temperature. The
results shown in this thesis are computed with data provided by DTU Diplom in Denmark. The
content of this thesis is meant to be understood by a an audience who are new to machine
learning, but who are interested in learning about Artiﬁcial Neural Network (ANN).

Introduction
In the farming industry, it is highly desired, almost a necessity, to measure soil temperatures in
ﬁelds, as the temperature of soil is a key to regional climate variation, as well as to climatic ef-
fects on agriculture [48]. Soil temperature plays a critical role in land–atmosphere interactions,
and therefore also land surface attributes. Air temperature, soil moisture and topography have
signiﬁcant effects on the variability of soil temperature. In other words, observed soil temper-
ature records are useful for understanding regional eco-environmental conditions and climate
change [105], where prediction of weather parameters like soil temperature, air temperature,
wind speed, relative humidity, rainfall, etc. are useful for agricultural purposes [37].
It is assumed that farmers do not generally use devices to measure the soil temperature, but
instead they use common logic to determine the health of their soil based on good air tem-
perature, rain and other air meteorological observations. Independently, the farmers, in most
cases, do not have the necessary technological knowledge to deploy devices, nor to exploit the
potential of these devices. In addition, soil temperature devices ﬁll the ﬁelds, since the sensors
are dug into the ground, and therefore might stand in the way of daily farming operations. For
these reasons, it is desirable to predict soil temperature by using antecedent measurements of
soil temperature and several land surface attributes from e.g. a deployed weather station. Thus
removing extra work load, as the farmers no longer would be responsible of device handling, of
deploying sensors or any other device related operations that interferes with their daily opera-
tions.
Among other things, the desire to solve these challenges has led to a number of studies explor-
ing simple and powerful tools for such estimations and modelling, where the Artiﬁcial Neural
Networks (ANN) approach has become one of the commonly used and powerful alternative
techniques to deal with time series forecasting [98]. Various previous studies show that it is
not only possible to predict soil temperatures with ANN, but even describes that it is possible
to predict soil temperatures with decent results and estimations [8] [9] [1]. Short-term predic-
tions of soil temperature are most reliable, and therefore the predictions of soil temperatures
for this paper are based on a period of 7 days with hourly predictions, where several different
models for the ANN are investigated and compared to ﬁnd the model with best estimates and
results.
Conﬁguring the neural network to optimize a model is ensured with hyperparameter optimiza-
tion, where the hyperparameters are manually tuned. The best hyperparameters are different
from each predictive modelling problem, but it is possible to ﬁnd the most optimal hyperpa-
rameters through trial and error with exhaustive searching, such as a grid search [5].
When conducting a prediction of soil temperature, a regression value, it is highly desired to
evaluate the performance of the learning with metrics, such as Mean Absolute Error (MAE),
Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), Coefﬁcient of Determination (R2) and more [34]. Simply
building a predictive model is not just the motive, it is about selecting the model with highest
performance through the given metrics. Optimizing the model and choosing the model with
best performance results in better prediction of soil temperature in the ﬁelds and thereby also
ensuring the best conditions for the farmers desiring a prediction of soil temperature.

1.1
Problem Formulation
As mentioned, soil temperature is a critically important agricultural and environmental factor
[110], but placing devices on the ﬁelds interferes with work of the farmers. Therefore, it is de-
sired to predict the soil temperature without having any devices placed on the ﬁelds. Artiﬁcial
Neural Network is the main machine learning algorithm to use for the prediction in this thesis,
due to the success of results documented in various other projects [8, 83, 34, 1, 98].
The main advantage and reason why artiﬁcial neural networks in many coincidences lead to
good results, is the advantage of the neural networks ability to represent both linear and non-
linear relationships directly from the data being modeled. There exists several traditional linear
models in machine learning, but they are simply inadequate when it comes to modeling data
with non-linear characteristics. An artiﬁcial neural network is all in all a powerful data model-
ing tool that is able to capture and represent complex input and output relationships [4].
In the recent years, most researchers are using artiﬁcial neural networks when predicting weather
parameters due to the nonrestrictive assumption and data-driven model. Furthermore, the ar-
tiﬁcial neural network has the ability to generalize and is efﬁcient in training large size samples
[52].
Optimally, it would be most appropriate to design and implement several different algorithms
such as linear regression, decision tree, random forest, etc. and then compare them with the
neural network. Thereby, one can ﬁnd the best algorithm for predicting temperature in the soil.
However, due to the time limitations, it has been decided that this master thesis is limited to a
single algorithm, namely the artiﬁcial neural network.
Therefore, the aim of this study is to design and implement an artiﬁcial neural network to pre-
dict temperature in soil using several meteorological parameters as input. The research ques-
tion in this master thesis is deﬁned as the following:
- How well does an Artiﬁcial Neural Network (ANN) predict soil temperature, when given
antecedent soil temperature and antecedent land surface attributes, such as air temper-
ature, relative humidity, wind speed, solar radiation, etc. as input to the ANN.

1.3
Machine Learning Workﬂow
When presented with a problem in machine learning, it is important to begin with understand-
ing exactly what type of problem is desired to solve. At the initialization stage, it needs to be
understood clearly whether the problem is a supervised learning problem, an unsupervised
problem or another type of problem. Additionally, if for instance the problem is a supervised
problem, the question needs to be asked whether it is regression or classiﬁcation. Secondly, the
available data needs to be understood clearly. For example, it needs to be considered if there
are any issues within the data set that needs to be handled. The reason why this is so important
is that data is fundamental for any machine learning method, and it is therefore difﬁcult to pro-
ceed, if having no knowledge of the data used. After data and the problem has been analysed,
the next step is to select an appropriate method, and this is where it is ensured that a method is
implemented correctly and where an output is produced. The last step is to evaluate the model
on how well the model performs. In other words, how well the model generalizes on previously
unseen data [46]. These are the steps that the structure of the report will be based on.

1.4
Step-by-step Prediction Flowchart
As mentioned, constructing the machine learning models requires ﬁrst and foremost to under-
stand what type of problem there is, particularly whether it is a supervised problem or unsuper-
vised problem [46]. Already, the soil temperature prediction problem has been determined to
be a supervised learning problem, due to the reason that an output is desired. When knowing
that it is desired to predict output values, it is important to clarify what type of output is desired
to predict. In supervised learning, values are divided into either classiﬁcation or regression,
and knowing that a soil temperature is a continuous value, it belongs to the regression category
[74].
Having established that the prediction problem is a supervised problem and furthermore know-
ing that it is a regression problem, it is possible to follow the following ﬂowchart 1.2. The entire
machine learning workﬂow is described in the ﬂowchart, including all steps, to build a proper
model for the prediction of soil temperature, from start to end of the whole process.

3.5
The Artiﬁcial Neural Network Method
An artiﬁcial neural network, often also simply called a neural network, is a computational or
mathematical model that is inspired by the structure and function of biological neural networks
in the brain [96]. A network is made up of a number of interconnected neurons arranged in
three layers: one input layer, one output layer and one or several hidden layers [25]. There
are also other variations of the neural network topology, but these three layers are the layers
relevant for this thesis. Each of the neurons are connected to neurons of an adjacent layer via
coefﬁcients called weightings. A neural network can “learn” a task by adjusting its weights [28].
As mentioned, an artiﬁcial neural network is made up of interconnecting artiﬁcial neurons that
are programmed to mimic the properties of a biological neurons. These artiﬁcial neurons are
working in parallel and unison to solve a speciﬁc problem [92]. In ﬁgure 3.3 a biological neu-
ron is illustrated in (a), while an artiﬁcial neuron is illustrated in (b). For the artiﬁcial neuron
x1,x2...xn corresponds to the input and w1,w2...wn to the weights. Any neuron can receive
multiple inputs, but only produces a single output. Furthermore, on the artiﬁcial neural net-
work, an activation function is illustrated. In an artiﬁcial neural network, the sum of inputs and
their corresponding weights are calculated, and ﬁnally an activation function is applied to get
the output of that particular layer, and supply it as the input to the next layer. The accuracy of
the prediction of a a neural network is dependent on the type of activation function used [64].

3.6
Number of Layers and Neurons
For a neural network, one of the big challenges is to design the architecture to achieve optimal
performance. To achieve this, the question that needs to be asked is, how many hidden layer is
needed to achieve good results? And how many neurons in these layers are required?
Since there are no ﬁxed solutions on the amount of hidden layers or neurons in the hidden
layers, it is difﬁcult to answer the above questions, but in practice one or two hidden layers
should be sufﬁcient and this amount is widely used. Additionally, for selecting the optimum
number of neurons in the hidden layers, there are common rules of thumb available [60].
• The number of hidden layer neurons are 2/3, or between 70-90%, of the size of the input
layer [11]
• The number of hidden layer neurons should be less than twice of the number of neurons
in input layer plus the number of neurons in the output one [68]
• The size of the hidden layer neurons is between the input layer size and the output layer
size [10]
Since the rules of thumb provided above are not proven to be correct in all domains, they should
be considered as suggestions for the initial design of a neural network architecture. Tradition-
ally, the identiﬁcation of topology is based on a primitive path, where trial and error is used to
establish the optimal number of neurons and hidden layers for a particular model. Automatic
methods to identify the optimal number of neurons and hidden layers has been explored for
instance exhaustive searching [101, 94]. Other than this, there is also the possibility to observe
the architecture of other similar projects, using the same source of data or similar, and where
the objective is identical to the objective that is being compared with.

3.8
Cross-Validation
Cross-Validation (CV) is a statistical method of evaluating the performance and comparing ma-
chine learning algorithms by dividing data into two segments: one segment used to train a
model, and the other is used to validate the model. In other words, some instance of the dataset
is trained upon the model, while the other part is tested upon the model. In a typical cross-
validation model, the training and validation sets must use cross-over in successive rounds.
Hereby, using the cross-validation method it is ensured that each data point has a chance of
being trained upon and validated against, thus utilizing all data in the dataset [81]. The main
use of all cross-validation techniques are roughly speaking the same, and that is to measure
how well are predictive model performs on new independent data from the same distribution
[58]. How well a predictive model performs on unforeseen data is achieved by calculating the
generalization error, which is a crucial metric used to evaluate how well a model generalizes.
A simple, yet efﬁcient, validation technique is the hold-out method, where a dataset is simply
split into training data and test data. Figure 3.6 illustrates how a dataset is split into two parts,
when applying the hold-out method in a dataset. In the ﬁgure, one part is the training data
that is used to train the model, and second part is the test data that the model is tested upon.
The split can have different percentages for instance 80% training data and 20% test data, 70%
training data and 30% test data or just any split is possible. Although any split in theory is possi-
ble, it for instance needs to be considered that too much training data and not enough test data
may result in overﬁtting. Note that this technique is not that computationally expensive, and
therefore hold-out may take much less time to do than K-fold or leave-one-out cross-validation.
Hold-out is used where there is colossal amount of data in the dataset, since other techniques
may take too long time to run. In some cases it is therefore better to use hold-out instead of any
other cv technique, even though other cv methods may perform more efﬁciently [107].

In contrary to the simple split between training data and test data in the dataset, there exist a
more complicated, but also more efﬁcient technique that generally results in a less biased or
less optimistic estimate, namely K-fold cross-validation. In K-fold cross-validation, a dataset
is partitioned into K equal or nearly equal sized folds, where (K - 1) is used to train a model,
whereas the left-out sample is used to test [53]. This process is repeated K times, while iterating
through the test folds, until testing has been done on all folds. While iterating through the test
folds, the model is constructed upon the remaining K-1 folds. Figure 3.7 illustrates an example
of how a 5 fold cross-validation is partitioning data into training data and test data, in an iter-

but it is possible for K to be any value, as long as equal sized folds are ensured in the split.

Lastly, arguably one of the most important resampling methods to describe in the thesis is the

with the lowest generalization error and use the estimation of generalization error as an es-
timate of how well the model performs. This is a tempting approach, however it is also too
optimistic with respect to the estimate of generalization error. Therefore, it is highly recom-
mended to use two-level cross-validation to select the optimal model MS∗and to estimate the
generalization error of for this optimal model [46]. One of the downsides of two-level cv is that
it is very computationally expensive, contrary to the other cross-validation techniques.

4.4
Feature Scaling
When training an artiﬁcial neural network, there is two clear options, to either use original
data or to use normalized data. There are several reasons on why to use normalized data, and
that could for instance be due to algorithm requirement, sometimes for improved learning and
sometimes even for no reported reason. There are many transformations that could be applied
to the data, but the two most common are normalization and statistical standardization [86].
The objective of normalizing data, is to ensure equal attention for all variables during the train-
ing process, and that the variables are scaled in such a manner that they are commensurate
with the limits of the activation function in the output layer. Min-max Normalization is espe-
cially useful, when the data does not contain outliers, as it does not handle outliers that well
[14].

Data preprocessing is the process where raw and noisy data is cleansed and organized to en-
hance the quality of the data, and to eventually make the data suitable for training a machine
learning model. Generally, real-world data contains missing values, noises, redundancy and
the like that inevitably affects the machine learning models negatively. Therefore, data prepro-
cessing is required for cleaning the data, and hereby ensuring that the data is suitable for ma-
chine learning models with increased accuracy and efﬁciency. When beginning on a machine
learning project, it is rarely the case that the data is clean and formatted already, and therefore
while doing any operation with data, it is mandatory to clean it and to put in a formatted way.
To begin with, the relevant dataset has been modiﬁed to hourly observations, where before
there could be several observations in an hour, it was then modiﬁed to average all observa-
tions within and hour. This was done to make sure that the observations for both devices were
aligned synchronously. Furthermore, statistical measures of the data has been taken to give an
insight on the data to better understand and interpret the data used to train the ML model.
Improving the quality of the dataset has been handled with two crucial machine learning pre-
processing tasks. First, outliers have been removed from the dataset, but in a manner where
only extreme and isolated values are removed. A moving average has been applied to remove
the outliers, since a value should be compared to the neighbouring values before making any
assumptions that a value is an outlier. Secondly, feature selection has also been applied to re-
move unnecessary features from the dataset, and to only select those features who indicated a
linear relationship with the target feature. This was achieved with Pearson’s correlation coef-
ﬁcient, but the results showed that only features that initially were not wished to be used had
indication of linear relationship with the target feature. Therefore, although they showed bet-
ter results, these features were not used in the training of the machine learning model, and
only relevant features with no clear indication of relationship were used to train the machine
learning model.
To further improve the performance in the training of the neural network, feature scaling has
been applied on the dataset. Speciﬁcally, the data has been scaled with standardization (z-score
normalization. This is a process where every value in the dataset is normalized with the mean
and standard deviation, which results in all data in the dataset to be in same scale.

6.4
Avoiding Overﬁtting A Model
A central problem in machine learning and supervised learning is the issue where a model does
not generalize well on observed data to unforeseen data. In other words, a machine learning
model performs perfectly on a training set, but ﬁts poorly in the test set. This is in machine
learning referred to as overﬁtting. The reason why it is difﬁcult for the model to ﬁt well on the
test set is that the model learns the detail and noise in the training data to the extent that it
negatively impacts the performance of the model on new data. Various strategies are available
to reduce the effects of overﬁtting, and a few are highlighted with the following list of techniques
[109]:
1. Ridge Regression: Adds squared magnitude of coefﬁcient as a penalty term to the loss
function. By adding bias to the regression estimates, quality errors are reduced. Addi-
tionally, solving the multicollinearity problem through shrinkage parameter λ [84].
2. DropOut: A regularization technique that randomly ignore units, along with their con-
nections, during training. Applying dropout to an artiﬁcial neural network prevents units
from co-adapting too much, and fundamentally results in sampling a thinned network
consisting of all the units that survived the dropout [93].
3. Early Stopping: Early stopping is a method that allows for training to stop before perfor-
mance starts to degrade. A problem with training neural networks is in the choice of how
many epochs to set, as too many epochs can lead to overﬁtting the model [19].

6.5
Data Leakage
Another crucial aspect to discuss is data leakage in predictive modelling. Data leakage is when
information from outside the training dataset is used to create the model, and is caused by the
use of information in the model training that is not expected to be available at the prediction
time. When data leakage occurs, it may lead to overly optimistic training, or in other words
overﬁtting in the model [108]. A sign of data leakage is for instance when achieving too extreme
performance, and therefore, if not considered, data leakage might lead to misleading perfor-
mance.
In the beginning of the implementation of the neural network, overly optimistic performance
was computed with the NN, and it was not discovered before later in the process. At that time,
soil temperature was predicted on the same data, which it was trained upon, not knowing that
data leakage was occurring in the predictive model. Figure 6.3 illustrates the period on which
the predictive model was trained, and furthermore shows the observed and predicted values.

def compute_closest_to_zero(myList):
""" Solution found from stackoverflow, but I have made minor edits: https://
stackoverflow.com/a/64185041/9794574 """
positive = []
negative = []
if len(myList) == 0:
print('0')
else:
for i in myList:
if i >= 0:
positive.append(i)
# print(positive)
else:
negative.append(i)
# print(negative)
# print(min(positive))
if positive and negative:
if min(positive) + max(negative) < 0:
return min(positive)
else:
return max(negative)
elif positive and not negative:
return min(positive)
elif not positive and negative:
return max(negative)
