Abstract
This thesis explores the development and performance evaluation of room temperature
sodium-sulfur (RT-Na-S) batteries, with an emphasis on the use of liquid electrolytes
and a comparative analysis of a solid-state battery configuration. The study employs
advanced electrochemical characterization techniques, specifically Galvanostatic Cycling
with Potential Limitation (GCPL) and Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS),
to investigate the irreversible processes occurring within the cathode during battery
cycling.
Utilizing tetraethylene glycol dimethyl ether (TEGDME) as the liquid electrolyte, two
different cell configurations are fabricated and investigated: Swagelok and coin cell.
The coin cell configuration demonstrates superior performance compared to Swagelok,
likely attributed to its superior sealing and more uniform electrolyte distribution.
To investigate their performance, a spray-coated sulfur cathode and a doctor-blade-coated
sulfur cathode are utilized. The batteries using the doctor-blade-coated sulfur cathode
exhibited poor performance overall, with substantial disturbances observed in the
charge-discharge profiles.
The experiments demonstrate that the coin cell with a
spray-coated cathode achieves a specific capacity of 650 mAh/g at 0.5 V for the first
discharge cycle, using a C-rate of C/200. A specific capacity of 550 mAh/g at 0.5 V
during the first discharge curve is observed using a C-rate of C/10. The discharge
curves reveal distinct regions, including sloping and plateau regions. At a C-rate of
C/200, plateaus are observed at 1.75 V and 1.2 V. During full discharge, elemental
sulfur in the cathode transforms into sodium polysulfides. However, these compounds
do not revert to elemental sulfur upon full charging, leading to polysulfide shuttling
and consequent capacity degradation over time.
The solid-state RT-Na-S battery exhibit significantly reduced polysulfide shuttling
compared to their liquid electrolyte counterparts, resulting in enhanced stability
in capacity retention. Moreover, the solid-state battery investigated demonstrates
a notably higher specific capacity compared to the liquid electrolyte cells.
This
improvement underscores the potential of solid-state configurations to achieve superior
performance metrics in RT-Na-S technology.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Metal-sulfur batteries are emerging as a promising contender in the realm of energy
storage, offering a cost-effective alternative to traditional lithium-ion (Li-ion) battery
technology.
Lithium-sulfur (Li-S) batteries hold considerable promise, driven by
their impressive specific energy of 2300 Wh/kg and energy density of 2600 Wh/L [1].
The potential to offer considerably greater energy density at a reduced cost relative
to Li-ion makes these batteries an attractive option for markets like grid storage
and electric vehicles [1]. Sulfur (S) is an electrochemically active material with the
capability of accepting two electrons per atom at approximately 2.1 V compared to
the standard lithium (Li) electrode Li/Li+ [2]. Consequently, S possess a theoretical
specific capacity of 1675 mAh/g [3]. When S is in its stable form, it forms a molecular
structure by the stacking of eight atom rings and a density of 2.07 g/cm
3 [2]. Other
metals, such as sodium (Na) and magnesium (Mg), can also be paired with S and have
the potential to offer substantially higher energy densities compared to conventional
Li-ion batteries. The implementation of Na rather than Li in metal-sulfur batteries,
holds potential to effectively counteract the potential Li shortage in a cost-eﬀicient
manner for several reasons. The Na content in the earth’s crust and water measuring
28 400 mg/kg and 11 000 mg/L, respectively, compared to the presence of Li of 20
mg/kg and 0.18 mg/L [4].
The traditional high-temperature sodium-sulfur (HT-Na-S) battery was invented by
the Ford Company in 1966 [5]. The HT-Na-S battery, utilizing molten salt technology,
comprises a positive electrode coated with molten sulfur and negative electrode with
molten sulfur [5].
These batteries boast a high theoretical specific energy of 760
Wh/kg and exhibit a low rate of self-discharge [6]. A significant challenge faced by
HT-Na-S batteries is their reliance on high operating temperatures, exceeding 300°C.
This requirement not only increases the risk of explosions and corrosion but also
demands substantial power consumption to maintain the batteries at their required
temperature levels [7]. In response to these challenges, efforts are made to develop
sodium-sulfur (Na-S) batteries that operate eﬀiciently at room temperature [7].
This thesis aims to investigate the various challenges known to arise in room-temperature
sodium-sulfur (RT-Na-S) batteries, with a primary focus on the mechanisms responsible
for the reversible conversion of Na polysulfides during battery charge and discharge
cycles. Leveraging electrochemical characterization methods, the investigation aims

to uncover the irreversible processes occurring within the S cathode during battery
cycling. By gaining insights into these mechanisms, the goal is to devise and optimize

Room-Temperature
Sodium-Sulfur Batteries
The RT-Na-S battery operating under ambient temperature (25 °C) were developed
in 2006, and has since undergone extensive investigation [7] [8]. In a typical RT-Na-S
cell configuration, the setup comprises a Na metal anode, a cathode composed of a
sulfur-carbon composite, and organic liquids acting as the electrolyte [9]. The battery
reaction typically begins with the discharge phase when elemental sulfur (S8) is used
as the cathode material [10].
Figure 2.1 shows the operating principle of a RT-Na-S battery.
As the discharge
process unfolds, the Na metal at the anode undergoes oxidation, yielding Na ions
and electrons. These Na ions move through the electrolyte and separator to access
the S composite cathode. Simultaneously, the electrons move to the cathode through
the external circuit, generating electrical energy in the process.
On the cathode,
S undergoes reduction by accepting the Na ions, leading to the formation of Na
polysulfides [9].

2.1
Cathode
In addition to a remarkable theoretical capacity, S offers several advantageous properties
as a cathode material. S can achieve high energy densities that significantly surpass
those of expensive transition metal oxides [14]. S also functions as an electrical and
thermal insulator with an exceptionally low electrochemical potential of 0.407 V vs.
standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) [15]. S8 is highly insulating, making it unsuitable
for direct use as a cathode material [16]. A carbon matrix is often used as a conductive
filler to enable elemental S to function as a cathode material [17].
2.2
Anode
Within the alkali metal group, Na has similar chemical characteristics to Li owing to
their common element group [8]. Na has a high theoretical capacity of 1166 mA/g
and a low redox potential [18]. The redox electrode potential of the Na metal anode is
-2.71 V vs. the SHE, whilst it is −3.040 V vs. the SHE for the Li metal anode [8] [19].

2.3
Electrolyte
The electrolyte serve as the essential conduits in sodium-ion batteries, facilitating
the movement of ions between the cathode and anode.
Their composition and
characteristics significantly influence battery performance, encompassing factors such
as eﬀiciency, cycle life, safety, and self-discharge [20]. Cycle life refers to the number
of charge-discharge cycles a battery can undergo before its capacity drops below a
specified threshold value [21], usually 80% of its initial capacity [22]. The electrolyte
used in Na-S batteries can be both solid and liquid, and this affects the working
principle of the battery [23].
2.3.1
Liquid Electrolyte
The liquid electrolyte in a Na-S battery consists of a solvent and the Na salt. The
selection of Na salts hinges on several factors, including solubility and dissociation
ability in the solvent, electrochemical stability within a specified electrochemical
window, and chemical inertness to other electrolyte components and electrodes [20].
Commonly used Na salts in Na-S batteries are sodium hexafluorophosphate (NaPF6),
sodium perchlorate (NaClO4), sodium trifluoromethanesulfonate (NaCF3SO3) and
trifluoromethylsulfoxide amine (NaTFSI) [23]. Sodium nitrate (NaNO3) and fluoroethylene
carbonate (FEC) are commonly used as electrolyte additives [23]. Additives constitute
the third main component of organic liquid electrolytes, albeit in minor quantities
(usually less than 5%) [20]. These compounds can significantly improve the electrochemical
performance, without imposing additional costs on production or necessitating changes
to existing manufacturing processes [24]. The use of NaNO3 and FEC improve the
cycle stability and fostering the development of a superior solid electrolyte interphase
(SEI) film [23].
The solvent must fulfill several essential criteria, including stability, non-toxicity,
cost-effectiveness, and wide electrochemical stability [20]. The ideal organic solvent is
expected to possess several key attributes. Firstly, it should exhibit a high dielectric
constant, ensuring eﬀicient ion solvation and ion transport within the electrolyte
solution. Secondly, low viscosity is desirable to minimize resistance to ion movement,
thereby enhancing electrolyte conductivity [25]. Additionally, a strong electron-donating
capacity of the solvent can favor the dissolution of electrolyte salts [23]. The solvent
can be esters, ethers, etc.
2.3.1.1
Ester Electrolyte Solvents
Conventional ester electrolyte solvents are ethylene carbonate (EC), propylene carbonate
(PC), diethyl carbonate (DEC) and dimethyl carbonate (DMC) [23]. These solvents
are all carbonate-based electrolytes, which have been showing good cycling performance
in Na-ion batteries, in addition to the ability to work at high pressures. The solubility

of sodium polysulfides (NaPSs) is also lower in ester electrolytes than in ether electrolytes,
which could weaken the shuttle effect of polysulfides to a certain extent [23].
2.3.1.2
Ether Electrolyte Solvents
Conventional ether electrolytes are monoethylene glycol dimethyl ether (MGDE),
diethylene glycol dimethyl ether (DGDE), triethylene glycol dimethyl ether (TREGDME)
and tetraethylene glycol dimethyl ether (TEGDME) [23].
The ether electrolyte
can fully dissolve NaPSs, unlike ester electrolytes. This helps the reaction to fully
proceed [23].
2.3.2
Solid Electrolyte
RT-Na-S batteries using solid-based electrolytes have high safety and less dendrite and
polysulfide shuttle problems than organic electrolyte systems. However, the problem
with the solid electrolyte for RT-Na-S batteries is that it has a poorer aﬀinity with
the electrode material [23]. The poor interfacial contact between the electrolyte and
the electrode leads to an increased internal resistance, resulting in a lower capacity of
the Na-S batteries [26]. The insuﬀicient contact also leads to formation of deep voids
at solid–solid interfaces, leading to fast degradation and very low Na utilization [27].
2.4
Challenges
While RT-Na-S batteries exhibit a high theoretical energy density of 1245 Wh/kg
based on the aforementioned reactions [28], several challenges must be addressed for
practical implementation.
2.4.1
Polysulfide Shuttle
The long chain polysulfides exhibit high solubility in electrolytes, facilitating easy
migration between the anode and the cathode. As high order polysulfides migrate
towards the Na anode, they react to form low order polysulfides.
The low order
polysulfides can then convert back into high order polysulfides as they migrate back
to the S cathode. This process, known as “polysulfide shuttle”, occurs repeatedly.
However, the dissolution of Na or S polysulfides into the electrolyte and the irreversible
reduction from sodium sulfide to elemental sulfur at full charge leads to loss of active
materials, consequently reducing the discharge capacity [29].
To address the challenges posed by polysulfide shuttling, researchers have developed
two strategies aimed at improving the cycling stability and capacity of (RT-Na-S)
batteries [12]. In recent work, inserting a nanostructered, carbon-based interlayer
between the S cathode and the separator have been introduced. The interlayer is
==PAGE:20==
8
2 Room-Temperature Sodium-Sulfur Batteries
designed with features that effectively trap long-chain Na polysulfide species, preventing
their migration towards the anode. Being electrically conductive, the carbon-based
interlayer acts as a secondary current collector, capturing and retaining polysulfide
species through electrochemical deposition of sulfur and sulfides at the interface of
the interlayer and cathode during charge and discharge. Additionally, the interlayer
can withstand stress and volume expansion during S discharge and accommodate the
volume changes of the trapped active material throughout cycling [30].
Another
approach is the integration of Nafion as the ion-selective constituent within the
separator of RT-Na-S batteries.
By employing sodiated Nafion as a coating on
the membrane, a higher conductivity is achieved, in addition to reduced polysulfide
permeation [31].
2.4.2
Self-Discharge
Another significant technical challenge of this system is the issue of high self-discharge.
This problem arises from the high solubility of metal polysulfide reaction products,
which exacerbates self-discharge [32]. When active materials easily dissolve in the
electrolyte, it creates concentration gradients within the system.
These gradients
facilitate the migration of polysulfides towards the anode, leading to pronounced
self-discharge [33].
2.4.3
Dendrite Formation
The SEI plays a critical role in RT-Na-S batteries by serving as a barrier between
the anode and the electrolyte. During the first cycles of charge and discharge, the
organic electrolyte is decomposed and form an unstable SEI on the surface of the
Na anode [34]. This layer is predominantly made up of inorganic compounds, which
form through the reduction of the electrolyte and the side reactions between the
electrolyte and electrodes [35]. This layer acts as a solid electrolyte between the Na
anode and the electrolyte, thanks to its ionic conductivity and electrical insulation
properties.
Additionally, the interphase forms a protective barrier on the anode,
preventing corrosion and suppressing the formation of sodium dendrites [36]. However,
this uneven deposition of Na introduce dendrite problems. The growth of Na dendrites
represents a fundamental issue encountered in all Na-metal batteries for a safe and
stable operation [37]. The presence of Na dendrites can lead to repeated breaking and
reconstruction of the SEI, thereby diminishing the coulombic eﬀiciency of RT-Na-S
and reduced capacity [8].
2.4.4
Sluggish Redox Kinetics
The low electronic conductivity of sulfur leads to sluggish electrochemical reaction
kinetics and low utilization of active sulfur during discharging [8]. Efforts are made to
boosting the reaction kinetics of S cathodes to improve the electrochemical performance,
==PAGE:21==
2.4 Challenges
9
by introducing the catalytic effect of metals and compounds for accelerating or
altering the conversion process [38]. However, the full potential of RT-Na-S batteries
still remains to be developed, and more efforts on further investigation is needed [38].
2.4.5
Volume Expansion
Managing the volume change of the S electrode is a major challenge for the RT-Na-S
battery system, potentially more severe than in the analogous Li-S system [12].When
sulfur (S) undergoes sodiation and desodiation, it experiences a significant volumetric
change.
This change can be quite large and unevenly distributed throughout the
material, ∼260% volume expansion upon full discharge [12]. When this occurs repeatedly
during the charging and discharging of the battery, it can lead to pulverization of
the sulfur cathode.
The pulverization results in the loss of active material since
the pulverized particles can no longer effectively participate in the electrochemical
reactions of the battery, and is one of the main reasons why a rapid capacity fade
is often observed [8]. In liquid electrolyte based batteries, the fluid electrolyte can
penetrate the pores of the electrodes, ensuring ion-conductive pathways for the exchange
of ions during charge and discharge cycles. This allows the electrode materials to
accommodate volumetric changes eﬀiciently [39].

CHAPTER 3
Experimental procedure
The experimental process involves fabricating and testing several battery cells. The
first battery made is a Swagelok cell with a spray-coated cathode. Following this,
four coin cell batteries are constructed: two with a spray-coated cathodes and the
other two with a doctor blade-coated cathodes.
An argon-filled (ultrahigh purity 5.0 argon, Airgas) MBraun Labmaster SP glove box
with oxygen and humidity content below 0.1 ppm is used for battery preparation. It
is a sealed container equipped with gloves that allow the user to manipulate objects
inside the box without directly exposing them to the external environment.
The
primary purpose of a glovebox is to provide a controlled environment where sensitive
or hazardous materials can be handled safely. The glovebox are maintained under
inert atmospheres, using argon gas, to prevent oxidation or other chemical reactions.
3.1
Cathode
Two different samples of cathode materials were provided, each coated using a distinct
method. The cathode components consist of sulfur powder, carbon black, and polyvinylidene
fluoride (PVDF) binder, mixed in a mass ratio of 70:20:10, respectively. A 5 wt%
PVDF binder solution is made bt dissolving PVDF powder in N-Methyl-2-pyrrolidone
(NMP) solvent. The PVDF binder solution is then added to a dry sulfur and carbon
mixture, which have been ground, mixed and sonicated in dry state. To achieve a
solvent-to-binder ratio of 75 ￿L/mg, additional NMP is added to the mixture. The
slurry is finally sonicated for 10 minutes and stirred for 20 hours at 650 rpm, to ensure
a well-mixed, homogeneous slurry. The slurry were coated onto an aluminium (Al)
foil, functioning as the current collector [40].
Two distinct coating methods are applied spray coating and doctor blade coating. The
spray-coated cathode have a sulfur loading of 1.496 mg/cm2, and the doctor-blade-coated

Performance Characterization
A various number of electrochemical methods are available for detailed characterization
of an electrochemical cell.
In this project, Galvanostatic Cycling with Potential
Limitation (GCPL) and Potentiostatic Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (PEIS)
are employed by using the EC-Lab software. The cells are placed in a multi channel
potentiostat/galvanostat, which is connected to a computer to monitor the system
using the EC-Lab software. GCPL is a standard protocol for studying the behavior
of battery cells being cycled.
The performance of the battery is determined as a
function of its charge and discharge conditions, which are generally a given rate and a
potential range [42]. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) provides valuable
insights into the kinetics and mechanisms of diverse electrochemical systems, finding
applications in corrosion research, semiconductor studies, and the development of
energy conversion and storage technologies [43]. EIS is a technique used for battery
cells to identify the diverse electrochemical processes that occur within the system
during operation and storage [44]. EIS is a non-destructive technique that utilizes
an alternating current (AC) signal to excite or perturb a system, while capturing the
response [45]. Utilizing this data, both real and imaginary impedance are plotted

3.6.1
PEIS Parameters
The following sections explains how the PEIS program is set up, and which parameters
are used. The PEIS experiment perform impedance measurements into potentiostatic
mode in applying a sinus around a direct current (DC) potential.
In the PEIS
measurements, the working electrode potential (Ewe) is maintained at 0 V relative to
the potential value reached at the end of the previous open circuit period (Eoc).
The frequency sweep is performed over a wide range, spanning from the initial
frequency (fi) of 1 MHz to the final frequency (ff) of 1 Hz. Number of points per
decade (Nd) is set to 12. This range provides insights into the frequency-dependent
impedance characteristics of the battery system. A sinusoidal voltage signal with
an amplitude of 10 mV is applied during the measurement, ensuring accurate and
consistent excitation of the system.
Additionally, the measurements are averaged
over 10 measures per frequency. To cover a wide potential range and capture various
electrochemical processes, the potential applied to the working electrode is swept from
-10V to 10V.

Figure 4.6 shows the Nyquist plot for the liquid electrolyte Swagelok with a spray-coated
S cathode. The Nyquist plot serves as a common method to display impedance data.
In the Nyquist plot, the imaginary part of impedance (-Z’) is plotted against the real
part of impedance (Z) across various excitation frequencies [43]. The Nyquist plot
typically exhibits both a semicircular and a linear region. The semicircle corresponds
to the frequency range where charge transfer phenomena dominate, and the linear
region, called the Warburg impedance, represents the frequency range where mass
transfer phenomena govern the electrochemical process [43]. The Nyquist plot shows
semicircles at the high-frequency region which is assigned to charge transfer resistance
and an inclined line corresponding to Warburg impedance at the low-frequency region.
The initial larger arc in the first measurement reflects the initial formation and
stabilization processes within the battery. The reduced arc in the second measurement
indicates an initial improvement and stabilization. However, the subsequent gradual
increase in arc size for subsequent measurements, shows progressive degradation of
the battery, likely due to polysulfide shuttling, electrode degradation, and electrolyte
decomposition. This results in increasing charge transfer resistance and worsening
battery performance over time. The Warburg impedance indicates mass transport
limitations related to the migration of polysulfides between the electrodes, where a
longer line indicates more significant mass transport resistance, which can lead to
slower ion diffusion and reduced battery eﬀiciency.

Solid vs. Liquid Electrolyte
While this project primarily focuses on RT-Na-S batteries utilizing liquid electrolytes,
a solid-state battery cell have been briefly investigated to provide a comparative
analysis. These comparisons aim to highlight the differences and potential advantages
or disadvantages of each electrolyte type. The challenge in comparing the solid-state
and liquid electrolyte cells lies in the fundamental difference in cathode materials used
between these two types of batteries. Unfortunately, specific details about the cathode
material used in the solid-state battery cannot be disclosed due to confidentiality
reasons.
Figure 4.33 illustrate the first discharge cycle at a rate of C/10 for both the solid-state
Swagelok cell and the liquid electrolyte coin cell, which uses a spray-coated cathode. It
is apparent from this figure that the lower cut-off voltage differs between the two types
of cells. The solid-state cell uses a cut-off voltage of 1.7V, while the liquid electrolyte
cell uses a significantly lower cut-off voltage of 0.5V. Despite this discrepancy, the
solid-state cell demonstrates markedly superior performance compared to its liquid
electrolyte counterpart.
Figure 4.33. Nyquist plot for the second coin cell with a doctor-blade-coated S cathode.
Both solid-state and liquid electrolyte RT-Na-S batteries have potential, but they each
come with distinct advantages and challenges. Liquid electrolyte RT-Na-S batteries
offers enhanced sulfur utilization and good rate performance, but suffer from safety
concerns, poor rechargeability and polysulfide shuttling [12] [32] [52].
Solid-state
RT-Na-S batteries promise enhanced safety, along with reduced polysulfide shuttling
[53] [54].
However, the practical applications of solid-state RT-Na-S batteries are
hindered by low ionic conductivity and instability at the interfaces [11].

4 Results and Discussion
The experiments reveal that liquid electrolyte battery cells experience greater capacity
loss with cycling compared to solid-state batteries. The decline in battery capacity
with liquid electrolytes points to several degradation mechanisms. This difference is
likely attributed to polysulfide shuttling, a phenomenon reported to be less problematic
in solid-state batteries [54].
This shuttling effect, combined with potential side
reactions and decomposition of the liquid electrolyte, leads to significant performance
degradation over time.
The experiments also indicate the formation of dendrites in the second set of coin
cells tested, observed for both cathodes. This phenomenon is a recognized challenge
in liquid electrolyte RT-Na-S batteries, occurring due to the decomposition of the
organic electrolyte and the subsequent formation of an unstable SEI on the surface of
the Na. The dendrite issue is exacerbated at higher currents [55], which could explain
why this phenomenon is not observed in the first set of coin cells tested at a C-rate
of C/200.
4.8
Swagelok vs. Coin Cell
The liquid electrolyte Swagelok cell demonstrates inferior performance compared to
the coin cells utilizing the same type of cathode. Several factors may contribute to
this discrepancy. Unlike in the coin cells, the electrolyte solution in the Swagelok
is not sealed and is more dispersed.
Additionally, the Swagelok contains a larger
volume of electrolyte solution, which may affect the overall cell performance. Results
indicate that the Swagelok cell is unable to achieve full charge. Consequently, a much
slower C-rate is chosen as the baseline for the subsequent coin cell testing, to improve
charging eﬀiciency and overall performance. Another possibility is that the specific
Swagelok cell tested was defective. Conducting tests on several Swagelok cells could
provide more reliable results, and necessary to conclusively determine the reasoning.

However, one notable issue observed in the experimental results, are the presence of
significant irregularities while cycling. This can affect the accuracy and consistency
of the performance measurements of the cell, making it challenging to assess the true

4.11
Variations in Fabrication Methods
Potential inconsistencies may arise due to human error in the battery fabrication
process. Variations in manual techniques, can lead to discrepancies in final battery
performance. These factors could affect the overall cell integrity, potentially influencing
the reproducibility and reliability of the experimental results.
Ideally, all the coin cells would have been made using identical procedures. However,
this was not possible due to a lack of equipment. This is why the size of the cathodes,
anodes, and separators differ for the four coin cells made. Consequently, the amount
of electrolyte used also varies, due to making sure that the separator is thoroughly
soaked.
The electrolyte to sulfur ratio (E/S ratio) significantly affect the battery
performance by influencing the dissolution and activity of polysulfides [58]. From the
full cell point of view, the most important parameter for practical RT-Na-S batteries is
the E/S ratio [59]. Several previous studies have reported the cycling performance of
S cathodes with an E/S ratio exceeding 20 µL. In these cases, the electrolyte weight
surpasses the total weight of all other battery components combined [59]. From a
practical standpoint, cell performance should be evaluated under realistic conditions,
using a lower E/S ratio.
A low E/S ratio can cause large polarizations and sluggish redox reactions. Conversely,
a high E/S ratio can result in high concentrations of polysulfides within the electrolyte,
which decreases the overall energy density of the battery [58]. Throughout all conducted
experiments, a consistent amount of electrolyte is used, ranging from 100-200µL for
the coin cells and 500µL for the Swagelok battery cell. The impact of the E/S ratio
on performance remains unclear from the results obtained. Despite small variations
in the E/S ratio across different experiments, particularly for the coin cells, it is
diﬀicult to determine how these variations influenced performance from the conducted
experiments. Additionally, investigating the E/S ratio was not a primary focus of this
study.
Another variable in battery fabrication is the method used to cut out the cathodes.
The best approach for cutting out the cathodes is to use the hydraulic press outside
the glove box. However, this requires an additional step of drying the cathode inside
the glove box after transferring it back to ensure it remains free of moisture. Due
to time constraints, several cathodes were cut out inside the glove box using a disc

cutter. This method proved challenging due to the manual effort required and the
diﬀiculty of maneuvering within the confined space, resulting in some rough edges on
the cathodes. Ideally, this source of error should have been eliminated by cutting all

Upon analyzing the results from the first coin cell equipped with a doctor-blade-coated

this cell was cut outside the glove box using a manual hydraulic press. Subsequently,
the cut-out piece remained in a glove box environment for four days before being

within the cathode, likely due to exposure outside the controlled glove box environment.
Consequently, for the subsequent two coin cells, the cathodes were cut inside the glove

to using a hydraulic press, it effectively eliminates the humidity problem.
The galvanostatic charge-discharge profiles for the second coin cell with a doctor-blade-coated
cathode also exhibit poor results, particularly noticeable at C/10. This suggests that
humidity may not be the primary cause of these unsatisfactory outcomes.
4.12
Testing Conditions
A testing temperature of 40C◦is employed for all batteries tested, despite them
being referred to as room-temperature batteries. The decision to use a temperature
of 40C◦is grounded in the understanding that a slight increase in temperature
can significantly enhance the test results.
Elevated temperatures promotes faster
electrochemical reactions within the battery cells, leading to more observable performance
characteristics and potentially revealing nuances that might not be as apparent at
lower temperatures.
4.13
Time Constraints
The project revolves around developing a battery cell, conducting tests, and analyzing
the results. Based on these findings, adjustments are implemented concerning fabrication
and testing parameters. Due to the time-consuming nature of battery testing, which
varies depending on the chosen C-rates, there are significant constraints on the
number of experiments that can be performed.
Due to time constraints, some of
the battery programs could not be completed as originally planned, resulting in a
lack of important data.
Conducting multiple experiments using the same setups is essential for drawing
conclusive findings, alongside employing additional investigative methods, which unfortunately
not were feasible due to time constraints.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This thesis has provided insights into the development and performance evaluation
of RT-Na-S batteries, mainly focusing on liquid electrolyte configurations.
Using
Na TEGDME as the electrolyte yields varied outcomes depending on the setup and
the type of cathode employed. Specifically, the coin cell configuration exhibits superior
performance over the Swagelok setup, potentially due to its better sealing and more
uniform distribution of electrolyte. The coin cells employing a doctor-blade-coated
cathode experience notable disturbances during cycling, impacting the accuracy and
consistency of the results. Further investigation is required to uncover the underlying
reasons behind these performance issues.
The coin cell with a spray-coated cathode achieves a specific capacity of 650 mAh/g
at 0.5V during its initial discharge cycle C/200.
The discharge profile exhibits
distinct regions, including sloping segments and plateaus noted at 1.75V and 1.2V.
These plateaus and sloping regions in the discharge profiles originate from a class of
sodium-polysulfide intermediates. Upon discharge, elemental sulfur in the cathode
converts into sodium polysulfides such as Na2S4, Na2S3, and Na2S2. The discharge
capacity for the subsequent cycles drops significantly, amounting to less than a third
of the initial discharge capacity. This indicates polysulfide shuttling, which diminishes
capacity by causing a loss of active sulfur material in the form of soluble polysulfides.
The solid-state Swagelok cell exhibits significantly enhanced performance compared to
its liquid electrolyte counterparts. The findings underscore the promising potential
of solid-state RT-Na-S batteries in minimizing polysulfide shuttling and achieving
higher specific capacities relative to liquid-based electrolyte cells. However, under
faster C-rates, the battery experiences fluctuations during charging, which could be
attributed to the instability of the Na anode and volume expansion of the cathode.
These challenges are recognized as primary obstacles for solid-state batteries.
Further investigations are necessary to determine the reasons for the inconsistencies
observed in the experiments.
In this study, cells were cycled for up to 25 cycles
only. Future investigations should extend the cycling duration of each battery cell
to thoroughly evaluate capacity retention.
Additionally, X-ray diffraction (XRD)
analysis should be employed to examine the crystal structure of the samples. Complementing
this, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) can be utilized to observe changes in
the surface morphology of the electrodes over time. To analyze the oxidation-state

Appendix A: Risk Assessment
Risk assessment is a crucial process used to identify, evaluate, and mitigate potential
risks associated with various activities, processes, or substances. It involves systematic
analysis and evaluation of hazards, their potential consequences, and the likelihood of
occurrence. The primary goal of risk assessment is to ensure the safety and well-being
of individuals, the environment, and assets by identifying and implementing appropriate
control measures to manage and reduce risks to an acceptable level. The assessments
for the different substances are based on information retrieved from Kemibrug.dk.
Tetraethylene glycol dimethyl ether
C10H22O5 (CAS no.:143-24-8), also known as Tetraglyme or Bis(2-(2-Methoxyethoxy)ethyl)ether
poses several hazards and requires careful handling. It may cause fire or explosion due
to its strong oxidizing properties. Precautionary measures include keeping it away
from clothing and combustible materials.
The substance should be stored tightly
sealed in a well-ventilated chemical cabinet. Regular testing for peroxide formation
is recommended, and if levels exceed 1000 ppm, the substance should be discarded.
C10H22O5 is an organic solvent that can affect the central nervous system if inhaled,
leading to symptoms such as headaches, dizziness, nausea, and possible loss of consciousness.
C10H22O5 may also damage fertility and the unborn child. Skin contact may cause
defatting, resulting in dry, chapped skin. It can also cause mild eye irritation. It is
important to ensure effective ventilation in work areas and use appropriate respiratory
protective devices if necessary. Safety goggles and gloves should be worn to minimize
skin and eye contact.
Emergency showers and eye wash bottles must be readily
accessible.
In summary, caution should be exercised when handling C10H22O5 to minimize the
risk of adverse health effects and potential formation of explosive peroxides. Proper
ventilation, personal protective equipment, and storage practices are essential for safe
handling and storage of this substance.

Sodium perchlorate
NaClO4 (CAS no. 7601-89-0) poses certain risks to human health and the environment.
NaClO4 is classified as a strong oxidizer, which means it has the potential to cause
fires or explosions when in contact with combustible materials. This highlights the
importance of handling and storing NaClO4 with care to prevent accidental ignition.
Ingestion of NaClO4 is considered harmful, as even small amounts of the substance
can lead to toxicity and health complications. It is crucial to avoid swallowing NaClO4
and to seek medical attention immediately if ingestion occurs. Exposure to NaClO4
can cause serious eye irritation , leading to discomfort, inflammation, and potential
damage to ocular tissues. To minimize the risk of eye irritation, it is essential to use
appropriate eye protection when working with NaClO4 and to rinse eyes thoroughly
with water if they come into contact with the substance.
Prolonged or repeated
exposure to NaClO4 may also cause damage to internal organs, highlighting the
importance of minimizing exposure and implementing appropriate safety measures to
protect against long-term health risks.
Overall, strict adherence to proper safety protocols and precautions is essential when
handling NaClO4 to minimize the risks of fire, explosion, toxicity, and injury associated
with this chemical compound.
This includes implementing appropriate personal
protective equipment, avoiding direct contact with the substance, and following established
emergency procedures in case of accidental exposure or ingestion.
Sodium nitrate
NaNO3 (CAS no.: 7631-99-4) presents specific hazards and precautionary measures
that should be considered.The compound has oxidizing properties, which means it
may intensify fires and increase the risk of combustion when in contact with flammable
materials. It is therefore important to keep NaNO3 away from ignition sources to
prevent accidental ignition and minimize fire hazards. In the event of a fire involving
NaNO3, use a powder fire extinguisher or dry sand to extinguish the flames. This is
recommended to effectively control and suppress fires fueled by NaNO3.
Additionally, NaNO3 can cause serious eye irritation upon contact. Exposure to the
substance may lead to eye discomfort, inflammation, and potential damage to ocular
tissues. Protective gloves and eye protection must be worn when handling NaNO3 to
prevent skin and eye irritation or injury. In case of eye contact with NaNO3, rinse
the eyes cautiously with water for several minutes. If contact lenses are present and
easy to remove, take them out and continue rinsing to minimize eye irritation.
Adherence to these precautionary measures is crucial to minimize the risks associated
with handling NaNO3 and ensure the safety of individuals and the surrounding
environment.

Sodium
Na (CAS no.: 7440-23-5) reacts violently with water, producing flammable hydrogen
gas and heat, which can ignite spontaneously. It is crucial to store Na away from water
or other reactive substances. Special precautions are necessary during handling and
storage to prevent accidental contact with water or moisture.
Na can cause severe skin burns and eye damage upon contact.
Inhalation of Na
dust or fumes may irritate the upper respiratory mucosa, leading to symptoms like
coughing and shortness of breath. Handling Na should be done under inert gas or in a
moisture-free environment. Effective ventilation, such as fume cupboards, should be
ensured during processes involving the substance. Safety goggles, protective gloves,
and a face shield should be worn to prevent skin and eye contact. Emergency showers,
eye wash bottles, and sinks with soap must be easily accessible in case of exposure.
Dry chemical powder or dry sand should be used to extinguish Na fires.
Water
should never be used as it can react violently with Na, releasing flammable hydrogen
gas and causing explosions. Na burns can produce hazardous decomposition products
like carbon monoxide and sodium oxide.
Na must be stored tightly sealed in a well-ventilated chemical cabinet, away from
water or other substances that react with Na. It should be stored under inert gas or
paraﬀin oil to prevent reactions with moisture in the air. Special safety notices apply
to the storage of large quantities of Na, as per regulations from the Danish Working
Environment Authority.
