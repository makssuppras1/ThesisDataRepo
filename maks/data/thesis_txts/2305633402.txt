Abstract 
 
- 2 - 
 
Abstract 
It is estimated that traditional microbiological methods for cultivation only recover approximately 
1 % of the total microbial diversity. However, bringing more prokaryotes into culture is essential 
for future biodiscovery of e.g. antibiotics, hydrolytic enzymes, and anticancer agents, since 
cultivation is still regarded the most powerful tool to achieve comprehensive understanding of the 
biology, ecology and bioactivity of microbes.  
New approaches for cultivation of the large number of yet uncultured bacteria rely primarily on 
growth in dilute nutrient media, prolonged incubation times, and different solidifying agents 
among others.   
The marine environment contains a vast resource of prokaryotes with great potential for 
biodiscovery of novel bioactive metabolites e.g. due to their potential for producing brominated 
compounds. The present study investigated the culturability of bacteria from seawater on a high 
nutrient medium (Marine Broth) and a low nutrient nature-mimicking medium made with filtered 
seawater, both solidified with agar or the anionic polymer gellan gum, as well as the effect of 
different incubation condition. The culturability was overall highest on the high nutrient gellan 
gum-based medium (2.0 x 105 ± 8.0 x 104 CFU/ml). When comparing media with same nutrient 
composition, the only statistical significant difference (P < 0.05) of agar versus gellan gum between 
all incubation conditions was seen for the two low nutrient media (seawater with agar and gellan 
gum, respectively). Furthermore, addition of two different concentrations of acyl homoserine 
lactones (0.5 μM and 50 μM) in high- and low nutrient media, respectively, did not increase the 
culturability on any of the two media. 
Amplicon sequencing of the V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene from colony material from the various 
growth media and from filters from direct filtration of seawater showed a difference in 
distribution of bacterial classes between the different nutrient level, solidifying agent, and 
incubation conditions. Marine Broth solidified with gellan gum was shown to resemble the 
distribution of classes from the seawater best. Calculation of β-diversity showed that this medium 
clusters with the filters, suggesting gellan gum to mimic the diversity of seawater better then agar.

Resumé 
Det er estimeret at kun ca. 1% af den totale mikrobielle diversitet er repræsenteret ved 
traditionelle mikrobiologiske dyrknings metoder. Det er derfor nødvendigt at kunne dyrke flere 
prokaryoter for fremtidig bio-discovery af f.eks. antibiotika,  hydrolytiske enzymer og anticancer 
stoffer, da dyrkning stadig anses som den bedste måde at opnå omfattende forståelse for biologi, 
økologi og bioaktivitet af mikroorganismer. 
Nye metoder til dyrkning af det store antal ’endnu-ikke-dyrkbare’ bakterier afhænger 
hovedsageligt af forsøg med vækst i medier med reduceret næringsindhold, en forlænget 
inkubations tid samt brugen af forskellige geleringsmidler. 
Det marine miljø indeholder enorme ressourcer af prokaryoter med et stort potentiale inde for 
bio-discovery af nye bioaktive metabolitter som f.eks. i kraft af deres produktion af brominerede 
molekyler. 
 
Dette studie undersøgte dyrkbarheden af bakterier fra havvand på et næringsrigt medium (Marin 
bouillon) og et næringsfattigt natur-efterlignende medie lavet af filtreret havvand gelleret med 
agar eller den anioniske polymer gellan gum. Effekten af forskellige indkuberingstider blev 
ligeledes undersøgt. 
Dyrkbarheden var højest på det næringsrige medie gelleret med gellan gum (2.0 x 105 ± 8.0 x 104 
CFU/ml). Sammenligning af medier med samme næringsindhold viste kun at være statistisk 
signifikant forskellige (P < 0.05) for de to næringsfattige medier (havvand med agar eller gellan 
gum). Derudover viste tilsætningen af to forskellige koncentrationer af acyl homoserine lactoner 
(0.5 μM and 50 μM) på næringsrigt og næringsfattigt medie ingen øget dyrkbarhed. 
Amplicon sekventering af V4 regionen af 16S rRNA gen af dyrket koloni-materiale fra forskellige 
vækstmedier og filtre fra direkte filtrering af havvand, viste en forskel i fordelingen af bakterielle 
klasser imellem de forskellige næringsindhold, geleringsmidler og inkuberingstider. Marin bouillon 
gelleret med gellan gum havde en fordeling der lignede fordelingen af klasser for havvand bedst. 
Udregningen af β-diversiteten viste, at dette medium grupperedes sammen med filtrene, hvilket 
antyder at gellan gum er bedre til at efterligne havvandets bakterielle sammensætning end agar.

Introduction

1 Introduction 
In the 21st century the field of biotechnology has become an important tool in reaching solutions 
to combat diseases, malnutrition, environmental pollution and many more worldwide challenges. 
Since biotechnology is based on the use of Nature’s living systems and organisms to develop new 
products, the microbial ecosystem holds a huge capacity for biodiscovery. The terrestrial and 
marine microbial niches are, due to their richness in so far undiscovered microorganisms and 
chemical compounds, key environments of future antibiotics, hydrolytic enzymes, anticancer 
agents, and other bioactive molecules with great value for industry, the human health, and the 
surrounding environment (Rosenfeld & Zobell 1947; Fenical 1993; Horn et al. 2000; Hahn-Hägerdal 
et al. 2006; Chavan & Deshpande 2013; Gupta et al. 2013). 
 
The largest ecosystem on Earth is found in the oceans, which cover 70 % of the surface of the 
planet. The marine microbial ecology is estimated to have existed for 3.7 billion years and is the 
greatest pool of microbial life on Earth (Joint et al. 2010). The oceans are thought to contain 
approximately 1029 bacterial cells (Whitman et al. 1998), and consequently the marine 
environment offers a huge diversity of bacteria with great potential for discovering of the e.g. 
antibiotics, hydrolytic enzymes, anticancer agents (Bull et al. 2000; Glöckner & Joint 2010). The sea 
makes up fundamentally different environments than the soil-based environments, which is in 
part due to the salinity, nutrient poorness, temperature range, and pressure. The marine 
organisms have evolved to ensure their survival under these environmental stresses and are found 
to have some unique metabolic and physiological capacities (Fenical 1993; Engel et al. 2002). 
Some unique metabolites of marine organisms is the halogenated molecules, where the 
brominated antibiotic, brominated pyrrole antibiotic (pentabromopseudiline) could be mentioned 
(Lewenza et al. 2002; Lovell 1966). 
 
Despite large efforts, the vast majority of the marine microorganisms (90-99 %) are yet 
unculturable under standard laboratory conditions (Glöckner & Joint 2010).  For long time the 
phenomenon of ‘uncultured bacteria’ was referred to as the ‘great plate count anomaly’ by Staley 
& Konopka (1985), which describes the difference in number of colonies developed on laboratory

medium and the total number of bacteria cells counted by epifluorescence microscopy of DAPI-
stained samples. The introduction of cultivation-independent approaches such as molecular 
biology techniques like polymerase chain reaction (PCR), rRNA gene cloning, and novel sequencing 
capabilities, contributed to precise knowledge of the classification of bacterial groups inhabiting 
the marine ecosystem. On the basis of molecular techniques it has been revealed that 35 % of all 
cells in the ocean surface are Candidatus ‘Pelagibacter ubique’ or SAR11 (Morris et al. 2002), a 
bacterium of which only a small number of species has been brought into culture (Morris et al. 
2002). Analysis of 16S rRNA gene sequences showed that the small fraction of cultivated bacteria 
did not reflect the phylogenetic diversity present in the natural habitat (Curtis et al. 2002; Gilbert 
et al. 2009). In general Gammaproteobacteria dominates cultivated marine isolates, whereas 
molecular techniques showed other classes as Alphaproteobacteria to be the most abundant 
bacterial class (Gilbert et al. 2009). 
 
Through the last decade there has been focus on the development of culture-independent 
molecular techniques such as rRNA gene cloning and sequencing of in vivo-isolated bacteria from a 
natural environmental population. These techniques have opened up new opportunities to exploit 
genetic information from a wider phylogenetic range than the cultivated isolates permitted 
(Heidelberg et al. 2010). Genomic and metagenomic information enabled the study of the overall 
activity and function of a specific environmental microbial community (Venter et al. 2004; Tringe 
et al. 2005; Delong et al. 2006; Moran et al. 2007; Dinsdale et al. 2008; Gilbert et al. 2008). From a 
biotechnological perspective these novel approaches should be identifying functional genes and 
enzymes interesting for applications in for instance the industrial and/or pharmacy, but there is a 
long way from the metagenomic screening of potentially useful genes to a fully functional product 
expressed by a host organism is produced (Steele et al. 2009; Mühling et al. 2013). Despite many 
years of research in the field of molecular biology techniques, isolation remains the best approach 
for comprehensive understanding of biology, ecology and bioactivity of prokaryotes, and it is 
essential for the future discovery to bring a wider phylogenetic range of bacteria into culture (Joint 
et al. 2010; Mühling et al. 2013).  
 
After nearly two centuries of microbiology as a science only a small proportion, approximately 1%, 
of the enormous microbial diversity on the planet is recovered (Kogure et al. 1979; Staley & A.

The common approach for cultivation of novel species has traditionally been isolation by 
manipulation of the macro- and micronutrients of the growth medium combined with changes in 
the parameter of cultivation conditions such as temperature, light, supply of oxygen, etc. Since 
this approach has only been sufficient for culturing a very small proportion of the extant microbial 
diversity, novel approaches have been introduced. 
Many theories regarding the ‘not-yet-cultured’ bacteria and the reason of their unculturability 
have been put forward. Some of the most frequent theories are based on the composition of the 
artificial substrates used for cultivation and the disruption of the interactions that occur in the 
community of organisms in the natural environment (Joint et al. 2010; Epstein 2013). The 
==PAGE:9==
 
Introduction 
 
- 8 - 
 
knowledge about the marine bacteria themselves is too insufficient. The choice of substrates is 
therefor an obvious bottleneck in cultivation. The standard laboratorial substrates are generally 
high nutrient media, whereas the marine environment’s content of nutrients is poor. The high 
nutrient media could have a toxic impact on bacteria evolved under oligotrophic conditions (Joint 
et al. 2010).  
For the last decade researchers found that mimicking the natural conditions enhanced the 
cultivation efficiency of the former ‘unculturable’ microbes. The isolation of the ubiquitous marine 
clade, SAR11, was based on this strategy. Rappé and colleagues (2002) facilitated the growth of 
SAR11 by use of a medium with a low concentration of nutrients and seawater from the sampling 
site. Center of Marine Biotechnology and Biomedicine, University of California in San Diego has 
investigated the use of seawater and marine sediments for mimicking the natural marine 
environment for in vitro cultivation, and long-termed incubation in low nutrient media containing 
different marine sediments followed by agar plate incubations has resulted in cultivation of former 
‘unculturable’ marine bacteria (Jensen et al. 1996; Jensen et al. 2005; Choi et al. 2013; Choi et al. 
2015). A change in media composition is not only favourable in the sense of a increased cultivation 
efficiency, it could more over change the bioactivity of the isolated bacteria as pointed out by 
Wietz et al. (2011) and Gram (2015). 
 
For the last two decades scientists have tried to solve the mystery of the ‘not-yet-culturable’ 
species’ vital needs. Commensalism has been proposed as a popular explanation for why many 
microbes are not able to grow in isolation. D’Onofrio and colleagues (2010) showed by co-
culturing experiments, that some marine microbes only grown when paired with a specific 
combination of other species. They found the exchange of siderophores to be a critical growth 
factor. The limited numbers of enzyme-coding genes in each marine species could be a mark of 
the dependency species in between, where a molecular by-product of one species could be 
essential substrates for another species (Joint et al. 2010). A way to compensate the insufficient 
knowledge of the critical growth factors of ‘not-yet-culturable’ bacteria is to look to the natural 
environment. Several research groups have used the idea of growing cells in simulated natural 
conditions in a diffusion chamber (Pörtner & Märkl 1998; Kaeberlein et al. 2002; Ferrari et al. 
2005; Gavrish et al. 2008; Aoi et al. 2009; Ben-Dov et al. 2009; Sizova et al. 2012). The diffusion

The diffusion chamber allows a free diffusion of chemicals while it restricts the movement of cells. 
This method has allowed cultivation of up to 40 % of bacterial cells from a marine sediment 
environment, as compared to the 0.05 % that grew on the traditional Petri dishes (Kaeberlein et al. 
2002). The higher cultivation efficiency of the in situ based cultivation methods appear logical, but 
for indescribable reasons small numbers of the former ‘uncultivated’ species isolated in the 
diffusion chamber, suddenly domesticate and grow on traditional laboratorial cultivation media 
(Bollmann et al. 2007; Nichols et al. 2008; Bollmann et al. 2010). One explanation for this 
phenomenon is the Scout model of the microbial life cycle (Epstein 2009). This model explains the 
bacterial state where cells are viable but non-culturable (VBNC) (Xu et al. 1982; Lleó et al. 2007; 
Oliver 2010). The Scout model, shown in Figure 3, divides a population of bacteria into two 
phenotypes: the dormant and the active. The dormant cell will stochastically become a ‘scout’ by 
some essentially random events, such as change in the expression or repression of master 
regulatory genes. Under persisting adverse conditions the scout will die, but in growth-permissive

1.1 Bacterial communication 
Being an isolated bacterium is probably not the preferred state of most bacteria, since microbial 
landscapes are dominated by biofilms and aggregates. In their natural environment, most 
microorganisms live as a part of a community, which is able to adapt to the fluctuating 
environment. Within the community microorganisms share and exchange metabolites, compete 
for limited resources, and communicate by exchanging specialized signaling molecules in complex 
networks. 
 
The process of communication among microorganisms is called ‘quorum sensing’ and is mediated 
through the synthesis, secretion and perception of small chemical signal molecules called 
autoinducers (Bassler & Miller 2006). The first autoinducer was described in 1965 as an external 
product that regulated the competent state of Streptococcus pneumoniae (Tomasz 1965). In a 
growing population, the individual microorganism secretes autoinducers into the extracellular 
environment and thus the cell density is correlated with the concentration of external 
autoinducers. These secreted autoinducers enable the microorganisms to ‘count’ each other and 
when the concentration reaches a certain threshold they regulate the expression of specific target 
genes accordingly (Miller & Bassler 2001). This communal coordination of gene expression that 
synchronizes the population is used in a diverse array of physiological activities such as symbiosis, 
virulence, conjugation, motility, sporulation, biofilm formation, and production of antibiotics 
(Bandara et al. 2012; Dobretsov et al. 2009; Milton 2006; Henke & Bassler 2004; Miller & Bassler 
2001).  
Quorum sensing is not restricted to interspecies communication. In the natural environment, 
biofilms are most often consisting of a mixture of bacterial species, fungi and/or algae.  Cross-
communications plays a crucial role for development and maintenance in such a living structure. 
Cross-communication has been shown for Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Lactobacillus casei 
(Kawarai et al. 2007) and for Fusobacterium nucleatum with Staphyloccus epidermidis or 
Porphyromonas gingivalis (Saito et al. 2008). Communication across kingdoms has also been 
reported (Joint et al. 2002; Tait et al. 2009). 
 
Quorum sensing systems and their autoinducers are different in Gram-negtive and Gram-positive 
bacteria. The most common class of autoinducers in Gram-negative bacteria are the N-acyl

The AHLs consist of a conserved homoserine lactone ring (HLS) as a backbone with an amide (N)-
linked acyl chain (Fig. 4a). Natural AHL compounds have an acyl chain consisting of four to 18 
saturated or unsaturated carbon atoms. The third carbon atom of the acyl chain can contain an 
oxo- or hydroxo-substituent (Uroz et al. 2005). The synthesis of AHL is found in more than 50 
different bacterial species (Safari et al. 2014), predominated by Proteobacteria (Visick et al. 2005). 
 
The quorum sensing system can be divided into two paradigmatic classes: LuxI/LuxR–type quorum 
sensing systems in Gram-negative bacteria and oligopeptide/two-component–type quorum 
sensing circuits in Gram-positive bacteria. As the membrane of Gram-positive bacteria is 
impermeable to oligopeptides, they have dedicated two-component systems consisting of cell-
surface oligopeptide transporters and sensor kinases to enable secretion of oligopeptides into the 
external environment and detection of the oligopeptides, respectively (Fig. 5). Detection of the 
external oligopeptides by the sensor kinase mediates phosphorylation of a response regulator 
protein, which regulates the transcription of target genes upon promoter binding (Fig. 5). The two-

component-system is found in a wide variety of both Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria. 
Besides quorum sensing, these systems are used for the bacterial sensing and responding to 
fluctuations in the environment. This is done like in the quorum sensing tow-component system 
by detection of extracellular cues followed by transducing information into the cell, where the 
response is a modulation of gene expression (Hakenbeck & Stock 1996).

The LuxI/LuxR type of quorum is believed only to exist in Gram-negative bacteria (Federle & 
Bassler 2003). This system is named after the very first quorum sensing circuit discovered in the 
bioluminescent marine bacterium Vibrio fischeri (Engebrecht et al. 1983). V. fischeri lives as a 
symbiont in the light organs of e.g. the Hawaiian squid Euprymna scolopes (Visick & McFall-Ngai 
2000). The quorum sensing proteins LuxI and LuxR control the expression of the luciferase operon 
luxICDABE required for production of light in V. fischeri (Waters & Bassler 2005a). The bacterium 
will only emit light when the cell density and thus the autoinducer concentration reach the critical 
threshold for activation of the expression of genes in the luxICDABE operon.  
==PAGE:15==
 
Introduction 
 
- 14 - 
 
Homologues of the LuxI and LuxR are found in more than 25 Gram-negative bacterial species and 
regardless of some differences in the genetic arrangement of regulatory and/or target genes, the 
actual mechanism of regulation is conserved in all cases (De Kievit & Iglewski 2000). Every LuxI-like 
protein produces specific autoinducer AHLs: In the case of V. fischeri it is N-3-oxohexanoyl-L-
homoserine lactone. In contrast to the Gram-positive quorum sensing system, the autoinducers of 
LuxI/LuxR systems can diffuse freely over the cell membrane (Kaplan & Greenberg 1985), which 
results in a balanced internal and external concentration of autoinducers. The cognate response 
regulator LuxR, which is an intracellular cytoplasmic receptor, becomes a DNA-binding 
transcriptional activator upon binding of its specific autoinducer AHL molecule. This activator 
complex binds to promoter DNA of certain target genes and alters the downstream gene 
expression (Fig. 6). In V. fischeri promoter binding of specific AHL-LuxR complex activates 
transcription of the luxICDABE operon, which in turn results in light production. 
Previously, the synthesis of autoinducers was thought to be uniquely controlled by a certain cell-
density threshold, but it is now known also to occur at different stages of growth (Federle & 
Bassler 2003; Waters & Bassler 2005a; Lee et al. 2007). This complicates the understanding of 
these widespread and vital systems of microorganisms’ communication.

In theory the use of quorum sensing for bringing new former ‘non-cultivated’ bacteria into 
laboratory cultures could sound promising. In spite of great progress in the study of quorum 
sensing in laboratory cultures, the understanding of the interactions between different bacteria in 
the natural environment is still incomplete. A few research groups have investigated whether 
addition of signaling molecules related to cell-cell communication could trigger microbial growth 
of hitherto uncultivated bacteria (Olson et al. 2000; Bussmann et al. 2001; Bruns et al. 2002). 
Bussmann and colleagues (2001) reported a slight increase in culturability upon addition of a 
mixture of N-acyl homoserine lactones to a basic mineral liquid medium with low concentrations 
of inorganic nutrients. Bruns et al. (2002) found an increase in culturability of bacteria from the 
Central Baltic Sea on 20 % by addition of N-(butyryl)-DL-homoserine lactone (BHL) to a liquid 
medium. Apart from these studies the use of quorum sensing molecules to enhance culturability 
and isolation of hitherto uncultured bacteria is still an uninvestigated area.

1.2 Impact of gelling agent on culturability 
 
Parallel to the culture-independent exploration of the unknown microbial diversity, the traditional 
cultivation methods of plating and batch liquid culture have continued to result in the isolation of 
new microbial species. This is from invention of new media, but also by optimization of routinely 
used media by changes in energy source, extended incubation time and substitution of gelling 
agent. Regardless of the amount of alternative polysaccharides found for solidifying media, for 
over 120 years agar has been the preferred gelling agent for preparation of solidified medium for 
cultivation of bacterial colonies (Tanaka et al. 2014). Agar is the main product the cell walls of the 
red algae Rhodophyta (McCandless 1981) and is a natural polymer build of repetitive units of β-D-
glucose and 3,6-anhydro-α-L-galactose (Araki 1956) (Fig. 7). Agar is known for its thermal stability, 
excellent gelling strength and easy handling. Derivatives of agar such as agarose and carrageenan 
are also known for their gelling properties but they are less studied.  
 
 
Fig. 7. Chemical structure of agar 
 
Agar conctitute a naturally part of the oceans and it has been shown to be degradable by some of 
the bacteria inhabiting the marine environment (Kurahashi 2004; Nedashkovskaya et al. 2004; 
Jean et al. 2006; Miyazaki et al. 2008). Since the discovery of agar in 1882, a handful of alternative 
gelling agent has been discovered: Xanthan gum (1950’s) from Xanthomonas campestris (Paul et 
al. 1986; Kubo et al. 2003), Carrageenan (1977) from Chondrus crispus (Lines 1977), gellan gum 
(1978) from Sphingomonas sp. (Shungu et al. 1983), Isubgol (1997) from Plantago ovate (Jain et al. 
1997), and Guar gum(2005) from Cyamopsis tetragonoloba   (Jain et al. 2005). 
Gellan gum (Gelrite® or Gelzan™) formerly known as PS-60 and S-60 (Shungu et al. 1983) was 
discovered in 1978 by Kelco Div. (Merck & Co., Inc., San Diego, California) as a product of

Sphingomonas elodea (originally designated Pseudomonas elodea, also referred to as 
Sphingomonas paucimobilis) produced during fermentation of glucose (Kang et al. 1982). Gellan 
gum is a anionic linear repeating tretrasaccharide unit composed of two residues D-glucose, one 
residue D-glucuronic acid, and one residue L-rhamnose (Harding et al. 2004; Morris et al. 2012; 
Masondo et al. 2015) (Fig. 8). 
 
Figure 8. Chemical structure of gellan gum.  
 
Gellan gum forms a very firm and clear solid gel at room temperature in the presence of 
multivalent cations like magnesium and calcium (Kang et al. 1982; Morris et al. 2012). The 
properties of gellan gum resemble those of agar and kappa-carrageenan in thermal stability and 
gel strength (Kang et al. 1982; Lin & Casida 1984). The high thermal stability of gellan gum makes it 
ideal for isolation and growth of several thermophiles as e.g. Methanobacterium sp., 
Methanobrevibacter sp., thermophilic Bacillus sp., and bacteria belonging to the genus 
Thermotogales (Shungu et al. 1983; Lin & Casida 1984; Harris 1985; Marteinsson et al. 1997). The 
gel strength of gellan gum is, like agar, influenced by pH; higher pH promotes harder gels. The 
basal nutrient formulation of a medium also influences the solidity of the gellan gum gel (Huang et 
al. 1995). Gellan gum was thought to be resistant to enzymatic degradation, but a few gellan gum-
degrading bacteria have now been reported: Kennedy & Sutherland (1994) found a red pigmented 
Gram-positive unclassified bacteria, that cleaves the β-D-glucosyl-(14)-β-D-glucuronosyl 
sequence of gellan gum by use of lyases. Muchová and co-workers (2009) found a Paenibacillus 
sp., which cleaved gellan gum nut did not posses extracelular free gallan depolymerases. 
With regard to price, gellan gum is also a much more economical choice than agar. The used 
amount in gram per liter is around half the amount of agar (Shungu et al. 1983) and the price in 
DKK per kilo is also half the prize of agar (prizing information from Sigma-Aldrich©), reducing the 
total price to around 25% compared to agar.

There is a number of reports on the use of gellan gum in a variety of ordinary, differential, 
selective and enrichment media for isolation and growth of bacteria from different natural 
environments (Shungu et al. 1983; Lin & Casida 1984; Harris 1985; Rule & Alexander 1986; Janssen 
et al. 2002; Tamaki et al. 2005; Tamaki et al. 2009). Shungu and colleagues (1983) described the 
substitution of agar with gellan gum tested with use of 50 bacterial species on selected media 
commonly used for the isolation, identification, and enumeration of microorganisms in clinical 
laboratories. They concluded that gellan gum compared quite favorably to agar and for some 
media it gave higher viable cell recoveries. 
Gellan gum is used as a substituent for agar in plant tissue culture media, since it promoted higher 
growth (Huang et al. 1995). It is furthermore used in isolation and cultivation of soil bacteria (Lin & 
Casida 1984; Gavrish et al. 2008; Stott et al. 2008; Hara et al. 2009; Chang et al. 2015) and has 
resulted in isolation of former ‘non-cultivable’ bacteria (Janssen et al. 2002; Sait et al. 2002). 
Investigations of the use of gellan gum for isolation of aquatic bacteria are few but promising since 
Tamaki and co-workers (2005, 2009) showed a increased culturability and growth of novel hitherto 
uncultured bacteria on gellan gum substituted media for cultivation of bacteria from freshwater 
sediments. To the writer’s knowledge there is no published studies on the use of gellan gum in 
isolation of marine bacteria.

1.3 Next-generation sequencing  
Characterization of microbial communities in aquatic, as well as other ecosystems has received 
more attention over the last decades and as a consequence of this new analyzing methods are 
invented. First generation of molecular tools like PCR, Sanger sequencing and different 
fingerprintings (Fuhrman & Hagstrom 2008) allowed the direct extraction of DNA and RNA from a 
environment, the determination of ecological and functional relationships and interactions, and 
provided insight into a huge diversity at both the taxonomic (Pace 1997) and the metabolic levels 
(Venter et al. 2004). These first generation techniques captured only the most abundant 
microorganisms and left a large majority of taxa undiscovered (Pedrós-Alió 2006). Knowledge of 
the diversity in a natural community was greatly improved by the application of high-throughput 
sequencing technologies (HTS) coupled to amplification of the 16S rRNA subunits. The most 
popular sequencing platforms for HTS are 454 Life Sciences/Roche and Illumina (Logares et al. 
2012). The field of microbial ecology has been revolutionized the last nine years as due to the 
popularization of these new sequencing technologies. The difference between first generation 
techniques and next-generation technologies is the throughput, which has gone from 102 
sequences of 600-900 bp lengths for Sanger sequencing to 106-109 sequences of 100-700 pb 
length for the next-generation techniques (Glenn 2011; Scholz et al. 2012). This throughput is 
conducted by massive parallel sequencing and produces a large amount of data, which demands 
powerful computers for the processing (Logares et al. 2012). Furthermore, this high throughput 
has made it possible to sequence more than one sample in a single sequencing reaction, which is 
termed multiplexing (Goya et al. 2011). Multiplexing or tags can be done by use of short DNA 
barcode sequence in the primers that enables separation of multiplexed primers post-sequencing. 
Primers can be designed with specific adaptors making sequencing-ready template molecules 
(Kozich et al. 2013; Logares et al. 2012). The Illumina platform is a chip-based amplification 
procedure, where the adaptor-containing template is bound to a glass-flow cell and amplified by 
the so-called ‘bridge amplification’ (Bentley et al. 2008; Metzker 2010), which uses incorporation 
of dye-labeled nucleotides, one at a time. The dye-labeled nucleotides contain cyclic reversible 
terminators, so after incorporation of the dye-labeled nucleotide, imaging are done followed by a 
cleavage step that removes the dye and regenerate –OH group for one more cycle of nucleotide 
incorporation (Fig. 9).

The general analysis of pre-processed (i.e. sequenced, image processed, de-multiplexed and 
quality scored) Amplicon data can be done on ordinary desktop computers with hardware 
consisting of four-core multithreaded processor with 16 Gigabytes of RAM and 2 Terabytes hard 
drive (Logares et al. 2012). 
 
The out-put data of the Illumina platform can be analyzed by use of various bioinformatics 
approaches in different software program packages. One open-source software package has 
integrated algorithms of various preexisting tools in a single software platform called Mothur 
==PAGE:22==

(Schloss et al. 2009). The following data analysis is performed in Mothur. The pre-processed data 
needs be assembled for forward and reverse reads. Then data have to go through a number of 
quality steps. Firstly, reads are screened for ambiguous base-calls and wrong lengths followed by 
an alignment to a reference database as SILVA (Pruesse et al. 2007), a rRNA sequence database. 
Poor alignments, sequences with gaps and/or homopolymers (sequences of identical bases) are 
removed. A pre-clustering is made to remove PCR noise and further PCR chimeras. These chimeras 
are formed as a result of incomplete extension of template sequence during the PCR process. They 
can be of similar or highly different sequences, but together they form novel sequences that are 
significantly different from the parent sequence. Amplicons of unwanted eukaryotic origin such as 
mitochondrial 16S, chloroplast 16S and 18S, and also amplicons from Archaea are removed.  
After the quality check, the remaining sequences are clustered in defined Operational Taxonomic 
Units (OTU), a way of grouping bacterial species at specific sequence similarity of rDNA or rRNA, 
commonly at 97% similarity. A graphic depiction of the number of observed OTUs as a function of 
the number of sequences sampled gives the rarefaction curves (Fig. 10). This is a picture of the 
sample’s richness and is used to set a cutoff number of the least sampled sequences that covers 
the diversity of all samples best. The cutoff is set where the curves are flattening and coverage is 
calculated to ensure that the diversity is covered good enough to proceed the data processing. All 
sequence data are then subsampled to the selected cutoff value and the α-diversity richness is 
found by looking at the observed number of OTUs. An estimated richness op the samples, Chao1 is 
also found. Other indices for α-diversity are Shannon and Simpson's reciprocal index (Inverse 
Simpson’s) both take the distribution in-between OTUs (evenness) into account. For comparisons 
of β-diversity the Bray-Curtis dissimilarity matrices can be used (Bray & Curtis 1957).

2 Purpose of this project 
As described earlier, it is still possible to recover novel hitherto uncultured bacteria using 
traditional plating methods with modifications of the media composition (Janssen et al. 2002; Sait 
et al. 2002; Davis et al. 2005; Tamaki et al. 2005), and emphasis is put on the importance of 
bringing more bacteria into culture for studying their physiology and for discovery and utilizing of 
new bioactive compounds with value for industry, pharmacy etc. 
The literature showed promising results of the use of gellan gum-solidified media for an enhanced 
diversity of isolated bacterial phyla (Tamaki et al. 2005; Gavrish et al. 2008; Stott et al. 2008; 
Tamaki et al. 2009). Previous unpublished work by (Rygaard 2015) showed likewise increase in 
bacterial growth (detected as CFU/ml) from a seawater sample on a minimal medium solidified 
with gellan gum. These observations formed the background for this Master Thesis. 
The purpose of this thesis was to use a traditional approach of isolating bacteria on solid growth 
media combined with one of the novel next-generation high-throughput-technologies of amplicon 
sequencing to investigate if cultivation efficiency of marine bacteria from a seawater sample could 
be changed/increased by modification of growth media. A commercial high nutrient medium 
Marine Agar (DifcoTM) and a low nutrient and environment-mimicking medium made of glass 
wool-filtered seawater, were used. The diversity of the natural community was likewise 
investigated by amplicon sequencing of PCR products from filter-captured biomass.  
Furthermore, the intense investigation on the quorum sensing paradigm of living microorganisms 
and the emphasis put on its roles and importance for sensing and responding in microbial 
communities, led to investigation of a ‘floating’ idea of supplementation of growth media with 
quorum sensing molecules to increase culturability. To the writher’s knowledge no studies are 
published on AHL addition to solid growth media. The number of four AHLs were selected based 
on Rasmussen et al. (2014), who determined the most common AHLs produced by 32 strains of 
the Vibrionaceae family. The four most common AHLs of these was selected and added to the high 
nutrient agar-solidified medium and to the low nutrient agar-solidified medium.

3.5.1 Extraction of genomic DNA 
To extract genomic DNA (gDNA) from cultured bacteria on solid media one plated dilution 
(containing less than 200 separated colonies) of the three samples (A,B, and C) at each incubation 
condition (sampling 1: 10° C, 10 weeks and  25 °C, 5 weeks and sampling 2: 10 °C, 5 weeks; 10 °C, 
10 weeks; 25 °C, 2 weeks; and 25 °C, 5 weeks) was selected for each growth medium. For 
extraction of gDNA, a protocol made by partners in the MaCuMBA Project was applied with a few 
changes. Following the protocol, all plates were washed with 5 ml lysis buffer (40 mM EDTA, 50 
mM Tris pH=8.3, and 0.75M Sucrose). Each filter from the 3 L seawater filtration (5 μm, 0.2 μm, 
and 0.02 μm) was cut into 5 mm strips and 5 ml lysis buffer was added. Filters and the washed-off 
colony growth were incubated at RT for 1 hour with regular agitation, and from here on treated 
identically. Samples were boiled at 100 °C for 10 min and cooled to RT. Lysozyme (Sigma; L6876) 
was added to a final concentration of 100 mg/ml and tubes were incubated at 37 °C for 45 min

10 min in-between each extraction. The upper aqueous phase was retained leaving behind any 
precipitate at the interface and extracted once using 1 volume of Chloroform/Isoamyl Alcohol 
(24:1, vol/vol), mixed and centrifuged at 3000 x g for 10 min. The upper face was retained and

3.6 PCR amplification 
A nested PCR protocol in which the 16S rRNA gene and subsequently the V4 region of this gene 
were amplified, were used (Staley et al. 2015; Kozich et al. 2013). Extracted genomic DNA from 
plates and filters was used as template DNA to amplify the 16S rRNA gene using the KAPA HiFi 
DNA Polymerase Kit (Kapa Biosystems; KR0369) with the universal 16S primers 27F and 1492R 
(Table 1). The master mix for one PCR reaction consisted of 0.5 U KAPA HiFi HotStart DNA 
Polymerase, 1 X KAPA HiFi Fidelity buffer, 0.75 μl 10 mM dNTP mix, 0.75 μl of each 10 μM primer 
(27F and 1492R). One μl of 10-100 x diluted template was used. Sterile Milli-Q was added to a 
total reaction volume of 25 μl. PCR reactions were performed in a Veriti Thermal Cycler using 
following program; an initial denaturation step (95 °C, 3 min), 25 repetitive cycles of denaturation 
(98 °C, 20 sec), annealing (52 °C, 15 sec), and extension (72 °C, 45 sec) followed by one final 
extension (72 °C, 6 min). PCR products were analyzed by gel-electrophoresis as described above.  
 
The amplified 16S rRNA gene product was used as a template for the nested PCR amplification of 
the hypervariable V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene. Primers were prepared in accordance with the 
dual-index paired-end sequencing approach by (Kozich et al. 2013) for use at the Illumina MiSeq 
platform. The forward primers had a p5 adapter-sequence, an i5 index, a pad-sequence and a link 
upstream the V4 forward primer sequence. The reverse primers had the same design just with a 
p7 adapter sequence and an i7 index instead of p5 and i5 index (Table 1).

Amplification was performed by use of the TEMPase Hot Start 2x Master Mix C Blue (Ampliqon; 
A290807). The master mix for one reaction contained: 50 μl TEMPase Hot Start 2x Master Mix C 
Blue, 4 μl of each 10 μM forward and reverse primer (Table 1), 4-8 μl template DNA (16S PCR 
product) and sterile Milli-Q water to a final volume of 200 μl. Samples were run equally divided in 
two tubes (due to the capacity of the machinery) and pooled after the PCR reaction to achieve a 
final volume of 200 μl PCR product for each 16S rRNA gene PCR product. The PCR program was 
adapted from a SchlossLab protocol on the preparation and sequencing of 16S rRNA gene 
sequence libraries using the Illumina MiSeq sequencing platform, as described in Kozich et al. 2013 
(SchlossLab protocol 2013). The following program was applied; an initial denaturation step (95 °C, 
15 min), 30 repetitive cycles of denaturation (95 °C, 20 sec), annealing (55 °C, 15 sec), and 
extension (72 °C, 2 min) followed by a final extension (72 °C, 10 min). The PCR products were 
subsequently analyzed by gel-electrophoresis as described above. 
3.6.1 Purification of PCR-products prior to high-throughput sequencing  
Amplicons of the hypervariable V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene were purified using the Agencourt 
AMPure XP PCR Purification Procedure (Beckman Coulter) as per the manufacturer’s instructions. 
A volume of 100 μl PCR-product was transferred to a round bottom 96-well Nunc® MicroWell™

(Sigma; P6866) and added 180 μl Agencourt AMPure XP solution (Beckman Coulter; A63880) to 
each well and pipette mixed 10 times. During 5 minutes incubation at RT, DNA fragments of 100 
bp and larger bound to the magnetic beads in the AMPure XP solution. The reaction plate was 
placed onto an Agencourt SPRIPlate 96 Super Magnet Plate for 2 min for separation of the beads. 
The clear solution was aspirated and discarded without disturbing the separated ring of magnetic 
beads. Beads were washed twice with 280 μl of 70 % ethanol with 30 sec incubation between each 
wash, and dried until all traces of ethanol were removed. The reaction plate was removed from 
the magnet plate and DNA was eluted in 40 μl sterile dH2O for each well. Concentrations of 
purified DNA was measured on a Qbit ® 2.0 Fluorometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Due to a total 
number of samples 58 nested PCR products, the V4 primers were used twice and consequently 
pooled in equal number and amounts of product in two tubes (29 samples in each). Pooled 
samples were quantified by Qubit®, DeNovix DS-11 FX Spectrophotometer (DeNovix Inc.), and gel-
electrophoresis before sequenced on the Illumina MiSeq platform at BGI Tech Solutions (Hong 
Kong) Co., Limited. 
3.6.2 Sequence processing 
The overall process of cluster generation, sequencing, image processing, and de-multiplexing was 
performed on the Illumina MiSeq platform at BGI Tech Solutions. All further sequence processing 
was performed using Mothur ver. 1.33.3 (Schloss et al. 2009). De-multiplexed sequencing reads 
were downloaded as fastq files from BGI’s webpage and a stability.file was made in Excel® for 
pairing sample ID with forward and reverse sequence. This file was used for assembling reads by 
the ‘make.contigs’ command in Mothur (below ‘ ‘ refers to a command in Mothur). Sequences 
then went through a de-noising pipeline of several steps. Sequences were aligned against the 
SILVA database ver. 102 (Pruesse et al. 2007) by ‘align.seqs´ and sequences exhibiting poor 
alignment to the database were excluded. Subsequently, the sequences were trimmed to 253 
nucleotides and sequences with ambiguous base calls more than 8 homopolymers were discarded 
by ´screen.seqs’. Sequences were filtered for removal of gaps by ‘filter.seqs’ and duplicates were 
merged into unique sequences for each sample, for a faster work process by ‘unique.seqs’. To 
ease the processing further, sequences were clustered into bins with using a cutoff of 1 difference 
for every 100 bp by ‘pre-cluster’. Chimeras were identified using the UCHIME algorithm (Edgar et 
al. 2011) through Mothur by ‘chimera.uchime’ and removed using ‘remove.seqs’. Then, sequences 
==PAGE:35==
 
Materials and Methods 
 
- 34 - 
 
of 18S rRNA gene origin as well as 16S rRNA gene sequences from Archaea, chloroplasts, and 
mitochondria were removed by ‘classify.seqs´ and ‘remove.lineage´. After de-noising, the 
sequences were clustered into operational taxonomic units (OTUs) at a 97 % nucleotide identity 
level by splitting sequences into bins at the taxonomic level of Order and subsequently clustering 
to the designated sequence identity. This was done using ‘cluster.split’. The taxonomy and 
number of sequences from each sample in each OTU was found by ‘classify.otu’ and 
‘make.shared’, respectively. 
Data output from Mothur was processed in Excel®. Intra-rarefaction curves of each sample were 
made by ‘rarefaction.single’ and on the basis of these, datasets were subsampled down to 8500 
sequences per sample and α-diversity indices of Chao1 were generated by ‘summary.single’. Bray-
Curtis dissimilarity matrices (Bray & Curtis 1957) were used for comparisons of β-diversity by 
‘dist.shared’ and ‘tree.shared’. The Newick-formatted output from Mothur was visualized using 
the Interactive Tree of Life (iTOL) (Letunic & Bork 2007; Letunic & Bork 2011).

3.7 Statistical analysis 
Data were presented as means ± standard deviations (SD). Significant differences among the level 
of culturable bacteria (logarithm of CFU/ml) between the different growth media were 
determined using the Student’s t-test (Hald 1952) with Microsoft® Excel® software ver. 14.1.0 
(Microsoft Corp., Redmond, Wash.), employing a two-tailed test and assuming that the two 
samples had unequal variance, evaluated at a 5 % significance level. The α-diversity indices of 
observed OTUs and Chao1 were analyzed by similar t-tests.

4.1 Estimate of total bacterial number by SYBR Gold staining 
SYBR Gold staining was used to estimate the total number of planktonic bacteria from the two 
seawater samplings; S1 and S2. The fluorescent dye detects double- and single stranded nucleic 
acids by exhibiting >1000-fold fluorescents enhancement upon binding to DNA or RNA (Tuma et al. 
1999). This method detects both living and dead cells without any option for distinguishing 
between them. The 0.02 μm pore-size filters stained with SYBR Gold yielded a bright and stable 
yellow-green fluorescence of both bacteria and viruses when examined under the fluorescence 
microscope (Fig. 15). The seawater sampling 1 and 2 contained 8.4 x 105 and 5.8 x 106 bacteria/ml, 
respectively (Appendix B).

4.5 HTS data processing  
The taxonomic diversity of bacteria growing on the different media after different periods of 
incubation was assessed by 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing of the complete colonial growth found 
on the filters.  Processing of the out-put data from the sequencing was as following: Assembly of 
forward and reverse sequencing reads in Mothur gave a total of 16,529,539 sequences. Out of 
these, 39 % were discarded due to quality issues (length, ambiguous bases, homopolymers) and 
poor alignment to the V4 region of the 16S rRNA genes in the SILVA reference database (Pruesse 
et al. 2007). Further 4 % were identified as chimeras and after sequences of misamplified taxa 
were discarded, a total of 9,251,646 high quality sequences were retained and analyzed. In order 
to compare diversity across samples they were subsampled to 8,500 sequences per sample by use 
of the rarefaction curves (Fig. 18 and Appendix E). This eliminated two samples: Filter.S2.0.2μm.3 
(3815 sequences sampled) and MBG.S2.10C.5w.B (4800 sequences sampled). Rarefying at 8,500 
sequences covered 94.4 - 99.2 % of the diversity in the 56 remaining samples (9 sampled from 
filters and 47 samples from plates).

5 Discussion 
Cultivation of the hitherto uncultured bacteria is important for the discovery of new bioactive 
compounds as antibiotics, hydrolytic enzymes, anticancer agents, among others with industrial, 
pharmaceutical, or environmental value, since the distance between genomic screening of 
potentially functional products and a fully functional product expressed by a host organism is long 
(Steele et al. 2009; Mühling et al. 2013). Bringing microbes into culture is essential, since it is 
almost the only way to discover their physiology, in the sense of which organic substrates they 
use, what secondary metabolites they might produce and which biotransformations they are able 
to produce. Furthermore cultivation is important form a scientific point of view for basic 
understanding of prokaryote biology and ecology (Joint et al. 2010; Mühling et al. 2013).  The 
marine environment contains a huge and complex diversity of microbes, due to the variations in 
temperature, oxygen content, pressure and salinity (Fenical 1993; Engel et al. 2002). These 
conditions presumably have allowed the evolution of bacteria with unique metabolites only seen 
for this environment (Burkholder et al. 1966). Cultivating such bacteria would, as outlined, be of 
both basic and applied interest. 
 
The aim of this study was to investigate if the cultivation efficiency could be changed by: i) 
substitution of gelling agent, ii) addition of quorum sensing AHL compounds naturally produced 
and excreted by marine bacteria, or iii) use of a environment-mimicking media made of filtered 
seawater. The efficiency was reported by count of colony-forming units and compared to direct 
microscopy counts and the bacterial diversity was investigated by next generation high-
throughput technologies. 
 
In the pipeline of this thesis, choices were taken on several levels, and all of which have likely 
affected the final results. For diversity studies, DNA has to be extracted, and a number of 
commercial extraction kits exist e.g. Metagenomic DNA Isolation Kit for Water (Epicentre 
Biotechnologies, Madison, WI, USA) and MoBio PowerSoil kit (MoBio Laboratories, Inc., Carlsbad, 
CA, USA). All of these were recently evaluated by Staley and co-wokers (2015) and found to be 
comparable in the majority of ecological conclusions, but analysis on OTU-level must be done 
using data generated from same extraction method, since differences below genus level was

found for the different kits. These kits would likely be useful for extraction of water samples. 
However, we chose a phenol/chloroform extraction method that was developed and used by a 
partner in the EU project MaCuMBA. Our rationale was that this would ensure a uniform and 
comparable extraction of biomass from filters as well as from colony biomass from plates. 
However, Boström et al (2004) demonstrated a loss of 30-40 % of DNA using phenol/chloroform 
compared with non-phenol/chloroform method (this was not analyzed for the three kits used by 
(Staley et al. 2015). The phenol/chloroform was assumed to be easier to use for the present study 
than extraction kits, where cells have to be pelleted (Staley et al. 2015), which was not possible 
from the filters..  
 
High-throughput sequencing at Illumina Miseq platform was chosen for phylogenetic analysis of 
bacterial diversity because of its cost efficiency and the opportunity to sequence pooled 
multiplexed samples at the same time. Compared with one of the other next generation 
sequencing platforms 454 (Margulies et al. 2005), Illumina-based technologies generated a 10-fold 
increase in sequencing depth and the largest amount of sequences per dollar (Bentley et al. 2008; 
Kozich et al. 2013).  
Another crucial choice that affects the final result of the present study was the choice of 16S rRNA 
gene region used for input to the Illumina MiSeq platform for high-throughput sequencing. There 
are nine hypervariable regions of the 16S rRNA gene, where the V4 region was chosen. It has a 
short length that is suitable for the capacity of the platform (~ 250 bp) and enable full overlapping 
of forward and reverse reads. This region are commonly used in literature (Kozich et al. 2013; 
Połka et al. 2015; Razzauti et al. 2015; Staley et al. 2015; Xie et al. 2016). The choice of other V 
regions in the 16S rRNA gene might have given other taxonomic results than presented here, as 
the V regions influences the operational taxonomic unit (OTU) clustering (Schloss et al. 2011). This 
difference has to be taken into account in the comparison with studies using different V regions. 
 
Different media and incubation conditions were used in an attempt to grow more or different 
compositions (diversity) of marine bacteria in this study. Marine Agar was chosen as a high 
nutrient growth media and this substrate is routinely used for cultivation of heterotrophic marine 
bacteria (Jensen et al. 1996; Choi et al. 2013; Neu et al. 2014). In contrast, the low nutrient 
==PAGE:51==

- 50 - 
 
medium made of seawater from the sampling site was chosen to promote growth of rarely 
cultivated species as oligotrophic marine bacteria, incapable of growth at substrate concentrations 
above a few mg C/liter (Ishida & Kadota 1981; Horowitz et al. 1983). Using a similar low nutrient 
medium, Jensen and co-workers cultivated and named two new families of marine bacteria, the 
Mooreiaceae and Catalimonadaceae and isolated additionally previously uncultured marine 
bacterial taxa within the phyla Bacteroidetes and Proteobacteria (Jensen et al. 1996; Choi et al. 
2013; Choi et al. 2015). A low nutrient media would select for opportunistic fast-growing 
organisms, (lab weeds) to a lesser extent than a high nutrient media (Watve et al. 2000) and this 
could increase the likelihood of isolating more slow growing bacteria that may have been 
overlooked in studies relying on marine agar and similar high nutrient media. The use of seawater 
as medium is based on the strategy of retaining the composition of compounds in the natural 
environment. The coarse filtering of seawater will remove some larger particles that could serve 
as source of nutrient and/or surface area for growth of some marine bacteria. The autoclaving 
process will alter (probably also destroy) larger fractions as particulate organic matter (POM) and 
dissolved organic matter (DOM) and other compounds that are heat sensitive. Still after these 
treatments it may contain a lot of the original vital compounds. 
Two incubation times and temperature was chosen to investigate the difference in culturablilty 
and diversity of the marine bacteria. The long incubation time of 10 weeks at the nature-
mimicking temperature (10 °C, like sampling temperature) showed a changed distribution of 
bacterial classes compared to the short incubation time at same temperature. In literature a 
prolonged incubation coupled to changes in media compositions have shown to be successfully in 
the incubation of another distribution of bacteria known and hitherto unknown bacteria: Sait et al 
(2002) isolated novel globally distributed soil bacteria (of the sub-classes Actinobacteridae and 
Rubrobacteridae) by small changes in cultivation strategy as addition of polymeric growth 
substrate and longer incubation. Stevenson and co-workers (2004) cultivated novel members of 
Acidobacteria and Verrucomicrobia from agricultural soil by use of low nutrient agar and 
incubation more than 30 days.  
 
The substitution of agar with gellan gum resulted in an increased culturability evaluated by CFU 
counts compared to microscopically determined total cell count: 1 % on MBA to 4 % on MBG and

0.02% on SWA to 0.3 % on SWG. However, the difference in CFU counts between MBG and MBA 
were only statistical significant for MBA incubated at 10 °C for 5 weeks. Previous studies by Sait et 
al. (2002) on the soil ecosystem, showed a slightly differences viable counts from gellan gum-
solidified and agar-solidified media. Tamaki et al (2005) reported likewise an increased 
culturability from 0.1% of the microscopically determined total cell count on agar-solidified 
medium to 1.3 % on gellan gum-solidified medium for bacteria from freshwater sediment on a 
moderately low nutrient medium. Janssen et al. (2002) showed an increased culturability from 
5.2% to 7.5 % of the microscopically determined total cell counts for agar-based and gellan gum-
based media, respectively. The distribution of phylogenetic classes of the cultivated bacteria was 
different on media solidified with agar compared to gellan gum in the present study. Gellan gum-
solidified media promoted growth of some of the low percentage classes found in the filter 
samples, as Betaproteobacteria and Actinobacteria. In a previous study by Gavrish et al (2008) 
they used diffusion chambers for isolation of soil bacteria and suggests that agar and gellan gum 
selects for different actinobacterial strains. This suggestion could be an explanation for the 
different distribution seen in the present study. Tamaki and colleagues (2005) reported also and 
alterations of phylogenetic distribution of bacteria from freshwater sediment upon substitution of 
agar with gellan gum in different growth media. They found the diversity and distribution of 
bacteria growing on gellan gum resembled the naturally occurring microbial community to a 
higher degree than the agar grown microbes. This present study suggests the same. These 
published studies suggests that the gelling agent is an important tool for increasing the 
culturability and chancing the diversity towards a more environmentally true distribution, what 
the present study also indicates. 
 
In biodiscovery a particular focus has been on members of the Actinobacteria, as they are 
responsible for around 50 % of the bioactive secondary metabolites as antibiotics (Bérdy 2005), 
anti-tumor agents (Cragg et al. 2005), immunosuppressive agents (Mann 2001), and enzymes 
(Pecznska Czoch & Mordarski 1988; Oldfield et al. 1998). This phylum predominately isolated from 
soil, have large genomes of which a significant proportion is dedicated to secondary metabolism 
(Bull et al. 2000; Fenical & Jensen 2006; Fiedler et al. 2005; Lam 2006; Blunt et al. 2011; Bull & 
Stach 2007; Suneetha & Khan 2011). Actinobacteria of marine origin also appear to have a major 
==PAGE:53==
 
Discussion 
 
- 52 - 
 
source of biotechnological relevant enzymes (Lam 2006; Bull & Stach 2007; Trincone 2010; 
Trincone 2011). The increased amount of cultivated members of the class of Actionbacteria on 
gellan gum-solidified media (15 % of all sequences found in MBG.S1.10C.10w compared to around 
1 % on MBA, SWA, and SWG in the same sampling) is therefore a very interesting finding and work 
should pursue on further culturing of these organisms.  
The high nutrient media solidified with gellan gum (MBG.S1.10C.10w) also allowed cultivation of 
members of the Verrucomicrobia phylum (0.005 % of all sequences). These organosms are 
commonly isolated form soil (Janssen et al. 2002; Sangwan et al. 2005) but also recognized in 
almost all marine environments across a range of environmental conditions, where they have 
been found by culture-independent methods to constitute approximately 2 % of the water column 
and 1.4 % of the sediment bacterial communities (Freitas et al. 2012). A number of studies have 
cultivated marine originated Verrucomicrobia and they are suggested to have a important role in 
the cycle of carbon (Bano & Hollibaugh 2002; Cho & Giovannoni 2004; Jackson and Weeks 2008; 
Gilbert et al. 2009; Freitas et al. 2012). Isolating members of the Verrucomicrobia phylum and 
studying their ecophysiology is therefore important for our understanding of biogeochemical 
cycles. Results of the present study suggest that gellan gum could increase the success of isolation 
members of this class. 
Other studies have found that addition of signal molecules related to cell to cell communication in 
combination with modifications of traditional cultivation techniques can increase culturability of 
bacteria (Bussmann et al. 2001; Bruns et al. 2002; Olson et al. 2000). Bruns and co-workers (2002) 
and Bussmann and collegues (2001) investigated the influence of adding quorum sensing 
molecules in liquid media, where the present study used the compounds added to solid plates. 
Bruns et al (2002) found an increase in culturability of 20% by supplementing a liquid medium with 
BHL N-(butyryl)-DL-homoserine lactone (BHL) and a slightly increase was reported on addition of 
OHHL (N-(oxo-hexanoyl)-DL-homoserine lactone) both in a concentration of 10 μM. In the present 
study, no change was seen in the overall culturability by adding an AHL mixture to the high 
nutrient media (MBA) and the seawater mimicking media (SWA). The AHLs used in this study were 
selected as they are commonly produced by members of the Vibrionaceae family (Rasmussen et 
al. 2014). The Vibrionaceae are ubiquitous in the marine environment and constitutes up to 3.2 % 
==PAGE:54==

of the bacterial community of surface water around the world (Wietz et al. 2010) and AHL 
produced by this family may therefor be commonly distributed the marine environment. These 
four specific AHLs are likely also autoinducers in other bacterial families, as several AHL molecules 
are produced by different bacterial genera and species (Eberhard et al. 1981; Fuqua et al. 2001; 
Gram et al. 2002; Waters & Bassler 2005a) and can also be used in interspecies communication 
(Kawarai et al. 2007). The autoinducing peptides used for communication among Gram positive 
bacteria were not tested, as Gram-negative bacteria dominates the marine environments (Leiva et 
al. 2015). Alternatively a mixture of AHL compounds and autoinducer-2 (the quorum sensing 
molecules detected by a wide range of both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria (Federle & 
Bassler 2003; Vendeville et al. 2005; Waters & Bassler 2005b)) could have been used. The decrease 
in bacterial recovery (colony-forming units per milliliter seawater) seen on the 50 μM AHL-
containing media may be a result of bacterial ‘poisoning’. To the writher’s knowledge no such 
decrease by addition of high concentration of AHL is published. Therefor it would have been 
interesting to analyze the V4 region of 16S rRNA gene from colonies growing on these AHL-
containing growth media too to investigate if the distribution of classes is the same as for the 
media with no AHL added. Further more it could be interesting to analyze the bioactivity of the 
colonizing bacteria of this high AHL-concentrating medium. 
The β-diversity comparison of sequences from plate samples and filter samples found that the 
diversity of filters was close to diversity of gellan gum-solidified high nutrient media (MBG), 
whereas the remaining media were clustered more distant from the filters. This indicated that the 
diversity of bacteria isolated on MBG would have a closer resemblance to the natural community 
than on the other growth media used. Gellan gum substitution of agar for cultivation of soil and 
sediment microbes were previously reported to increase viable counts but have no influence on 
diversity (Sait et al. 2002; Davis et al. 2005). Tamaki et al (2005) investigated the diversity of 
cultivable microbes on gellan gum vs. agar-solidified medium. They found a different distribution 
of microbes on the gellen gum-based compared to the agar-based medium by sequencing of 30 
randomly selected colonies of each media. Furthermore 10-times more colonies from the gellan 
gum-based medium compared to agar-based medium showed more than 95 % sequence similarity 
with the 16S rRNA gene clone library of the total community. They further found Bacteroidetes 
and Planctomycetes to be isolated on gella gum-based medium but nor agar-based medium and 
==PAGE:55==

microbes classified into Firmicutes and Actinobacteria on agar-based but not gellan gum-based 
medium. The present study found members of the Actinobacteria classes in both agar- and gellan 
gum-based media, but with 14-times as many sequences in gellan gum (MBG). The difference 
between distribution in Actinobacteria on agar- and gellan gum-based medium in the present 
study and Tamaki et al. (2005) could simply be due to the very different analysis method of a small 
number of randomly selected colonies compared to a high-throughput deep sequencing. 
To the writer’s knowledge no other studies have compared viable counts of isolated marine 
bacteria on gellan gum-solidified medium with HTS sequencing diversity out-put. 
 
To conclude, the results of this study suggest gellan-gum as a promising tool for future 
experiments for higher recovery and/or enhanced culturability of hitherto unknown species 
presented in the marine environment.
