Abstract
This thesis investigates how the load bearing capacity of masonry panels can be de­
scribed using homogeneous stress fields and the yield criterion. The fundamental me­
chanical properties of masonry are first presented as a basis for assessing its structural
capacity. By applying the theory of homogeneous stress fields, expressions for normal
and shear stresses were derived for different zones within a generic standard masonry
panel. In addition to the yield criterion for masonry, introduced by Findsen [1], several
analytical models were developed to estimate the capacity of the masonry panels.
Relevant data was collected from multiple experimental studies in the literature, all of
which involved tests on brick masonry subjected to in­plane loading. These experimental
results were compared with the predictions from the proposed models, allowing for an
evaluation of the expected versus observed failure loads and mechanisms. Based on the
experimental data, the expected horizontal failure loads were calculated, and a generally
good agreement was found with the observed results. By applying a safety factor of
κ = 0.8, most of the experimental results came to the safe side, with only a few instances
where the observed failure load was lower than expected, which in these specific cases
added a lack of safety.
Further analysis of the observed failure mechanisms for each experiment allowed for a
comparison with the failure modes predicted by the models, which took into account the
geometry and stress distribution of the panels. This comparison revealed that in most
cases there was good correspondence between predicted and observed failure mecha­
nisms, such as stepped line failure, toe crushing, and sliding failure.
In general, the theoretical models showed strong agreement with the experimental find­
ings, providing a solid foundation to conclude the study. Thus, it was concluded that the
project was successful due to the consistent alignment between the theoretical predictions
and the experimental data. Finally, several avenues for future research were proposed,
with the aim of further developing and expanding the understanding of the behavior of
masonry panels.

Introduction
Masonry has served as a fundamental construction material in Denmark for more than
800 years. Its widespread use can be attributed to its high compressive strength and
the accessibility of its primary constituents in clay and lime. These materials have histori­
cally been abundant and relatively easy to process, contributing to the material’s enduring
popularity.
Structurally, masonry comprises individual bricks arranged in specific bond patterns, which
influence both structural performance and appearance. These bricks are joined using
mortar, which serves to unify the assembly and contribute to the overall strength and
durability of the structure. Beyond its mechanical properties, masonry is often favored
for its aesthetic versatility. A wide variety of bond patterns and brick colors, ranging from
deep reds to pale yellows, allow for significant architectural expression. The final col­
oration of the bricks is mainly influenced by the type of clay used and the conditions of
the firing process.
Until the 17th century, the design and construction of masonry structures relied solely on
empirical knowledge and practical experience [3]. In contrast, modern masonry construc­
tion is governed by comprehensive standards and codes, which aim to ensure safety and
performance. To improve the understanding of masonry as a load bearing system, this
study focuses on the behavior of masonry panels subjected to in­plane loading, a critical
aspect in evaluating their structural capacity and failure mechanisms.
This project aims to investigate a plasticity­based formulation of the failure criterion for ma­
sonry panels. The approach is based on inspiration from the yield criterion of reinforced
concrete panels and the known solutions of concrete panels stated in section ”4.5.2 A
Survey of Known Solutions” in ”Limit Analysis and Concrete Plasticity” [2]. This new solu­
tion for masonry panels will be based on homogeneous stress fields and the yield criterion
for masonry stated by Karsten Findsen in his Ph.D thesis ”Murede skivers styrke” [1].
To assess the validity and practical applicability of the established solutions, they will be
evaluated against experimental results reported in the literature. The focus is to develop
an improved model of in­plane shear behavior of masonry panels, which describes the
capacity as a homogeneous yield capacity.
The project seeks to address the following key research questions:
1. How can the failure criterion for reinforced concrete be used to further develop mod­
els for the capacity of masonry?
2. Can the failure criterion, in conjunction with lower­bound solutions based on homo­
geneous stress fields, be applied with sufficient accuracy to model the load­bearing
capacity of masonry panels subjected to in­plane actions?
Ultimately, this project aims to contribute to a deeper understanding of the yield behavior
of masonry panels and to encourage further research and development in this field.

2
Literature study
The purpose of this literature study is to establish a solid foundation of the theoretical
knowledge necessary for the analyses presented later in this thesis. Particular attention
is paid to the fundamental material properties of masonry, its uniaxial strength parameters,
and the concept of homogeneous stress fields.
2.1
Fundamental material properties of masonry
This section seeks to describe some of the fundamental material properties, as well as
the general characteristics of masonry.
2.1.1
The three fundamental parts of masonry
The three fundamental components of masonry are the bricks, the mortar joints, and the
interface between them. The interface is considered a critical part of the masonry, as it
represents a weak zone where failures are likely to occur. For this reason, the interface
is often treated as a distinct material in its own right [3].
Brick
A brick is typically shaped as a right­angled prism, most commonly made of fired clay,
with standardized dimensions for height, length, and width, as illustrated for a standard
Danish brick in fig. 2.1.
Figure 2.1: The designations and dimensions of a Danish standard brick [3].
The designations of the brick faces are shown in fig. 2.1. The upper face is called the
bed face, the smallest face is the header face, and the face that is typically visible on
a wall is the stretcher face. In Denmark, two main types of bricks are commonly used.
The standard brick, with dimensions as shown in fig. 2.1, and the bredsten (translated as
“wide brick”), which is characterized by its increased width of 168 mm.

Bricks generally come in two main colors: red and yellow. The color is determined by the
composition of the clay used in the manufacturing process. Two types of clay are typically
used: red clay, which produces red bricks, and blue clay, which results in yellow bricks.
The color variation arises from differences in the ratio between iron oxide and calcium
carbonate in the clay. In Denmark, red clay is typically found within the top 2–3 meters of
the soil, while blue clay is found beneath this layer.
During the firing process, clay bricks gain their strength by being subjected to high tem­
peratures over a sustained period. Due to the manufacturing process and the nature of
the firing, the outer surface of the brick becomes harder than the core, which remains rela­
tively weaker. As a result, bricks are considered as inhomogeneous materials. The use of
hollow bricks allows for a more uniform firing process, resulting in a more homogeneous
material. In addition, hollow bricks require less raw material, offering environmental ben­
efits. However, this thesis will focus primarily on masonry constructed with solid bricks.
Joint
The joints are the general designation of the mortar joints between bricks. The joints
are divided into horizontal and vertical joints, where the horizontal joints are called bed
joints, and the vertical joints are known as head joints. The nomenclature of the joints is
illustrated in fig. 2.2.
Figure 2.2: The nomenclature of joints for standard masonry [11].
The joints are made with mortar, which secures the overall strength of the masonry, as
it bonds the bricks together. The thickness of the joints vary from the bed to the head
joints, where the bed joints in Denmark have a thickness based on the principle of ”3 on
20”, which means that the height of three courses should be 20 cm. Based on this, the
bed joints in Denmark have a thickness of approximately 12.67 mm. The thickness of the
head joints in Denmark is by standard 12 mm thick. Due to the construction method of
the masonry, it can’t be assured that the head joints are fully filled with mortar. Due to
this, there are differing opinions within the masonry community on horizontal load transfer
in the head joints, as the bond strength is reduced due to these joints often only being
partially filled [11].
In Denmark, the most common types of mortar used are ”funktionsmørtel”, roughly trans­
lated as design mortar, and ”receptmørtel”, translated to prescribed mortar. Similar for
both types are that they are usually made by different compositions of mainly: Lime, ce­
ment, sand and water. The design mortars are made in factories with an unknown com­
position, but with a given strength property of the mortar. The prescribed mortar states
the composition of the mortar, for example KC 20/80/550, which states the composition of
a lime­cement mortar with 20 parts lime, 80 parts cement and 550 parts sand measured
by weight.

Interface
The interface, in Danish known as ”skillefladen”, is the interface between the bricks and
joints. As this is seen as a weak part in masonry, it is often described as its own material.
The interface is where most failure lines are observed during failure, especially in terms
of stepped line failures.
Different references state that the strength of the interface between the bricks and joints
depends on different parameters such as: The initial rate of absorption of the brick, the
mortars ability to retain its liquid, the ratio between the composition materials in the mortar
and other parameters [3].
2.1.2
Material properties
Strength parameters
The strength parameters for masonry can be divided into the strength of the brick, joint
and interface. Each component has a compression and tension strength. The bricks
are classified by the suppliers by their compression strength in MPa, usually in the range
of 15 to 30 MPa, which is determined based on test procedures stated in the European
standards. The tension strength of the brick can be estimated to be a fraction of the com­
pression strength. Klavs Feilberg Hansen states the relation between the compression
and tension strength of bricks as

The tension strength of the interface between the brick and joints is estimated to be zero,

and the composition of the mortar, which is why this is usually determined by tests [1].
Bond strength
Bond strength describes how well bricks and mortar adhere to each other within a joint. It
is determined by the strength of the interface between these two materials. Factors such
as the type of mortar, the surface texture of the brick unit, and the curing conditions all
influence the resulting bond strength.
Initial Rate of Absorption
The Initial Rate of Absorption, also known as the IRA, is a material property of the brick,
which states how much water the brick absorbs in a minute and is often measured in
[kg/m2/min].
Studies by Zenke Hansen and Gudmand­Høyer state that there is a correlation between
IRA and the bond strength between bricks and mortar [12].
Friction angel
The friction angle for a material specifies the angle at which the friction is no longer enough
to prevent sliding. The friction angle for both bricks and joints in this thesis is estimated
to be 30 degrees, unless otherwise stated.

Bond pattern
The bond pattern describes how the bricks are laid in relation to each other. There exist
many different types of pattern based on geography and culture. In Denmark, building
tradition states that two consecutive courses must always be offset from each other by at
least a quarter of a brick to prevent alignment of the head joints. If alignment of multiple
head joints were to occur, this would form a vulnerable area of the masonry where cracks
would be expected to form through.

Homogeneous stress fields
Homogeneous stress fields are used in the design of concrete panels, as they can be
used to determine zones that need reinforcement. Homogeneous stress fields are about
dividing the panel into triangular zones based on the geometry, support conditions and
loading of the panel. This procedure requires a constant stress state in each of the zones.
The three equilibrium equations are used for each zone to determine normal stresses (σx
and σy), and shear stress (τxy). In addition, the triangular zones should satisfy statically
boundary conditions and statical conditions along the interfaces of adjacent triangles [2].

3.2
Yield criteria of masonry by Findsen
Findsen derived 14 yield criteria for masonry, based on the theory of yield criteria for con­
crete presented in ”Limit Analysis and Concrete Plasticity” [2]. These criteria were formu­
lated for orthotropic panels, as the degree of reinforcement in the two principal directions
differ for masonry because of the differences in the uniaxial strengths, as discussed in
section 2.2. The 14 yield criteria, which consist of cones, cylinders, and planes, were
developed under the assumption that masonry can be modeled as a modified Coulomb
material. Furthermore, the bond pattern in masonry is considered to form an internal
structure similar to reinforcement in concrete, which contributes to its directional strength
behavior.
Findsen emphasized that the derived yield criteria are only valid for unreinforced masonry
constructed with solid bricks. However, he also noted that it is possible to develop similar
criteria for reinforced masonry and for masonry composed of hollow bricks. In fact, he
later proceeded to derive the yield criterion for reinforced masonry.

4.3
Sources of error
Several different sources of errors and modifications were found during the analyzing of
the different articles. The sources of error cover important observations made during the
examination of the different test series. It can be about the experimental setup, or in
regard to the established models which would be used later to predict the behavior of the
masonry panel.
In general, a source of error, which occurs for all experiments, is the fact that each ex­
perimental setup is loaded in terms of a uniformly distributed line load, which is converted
into an equivalent point load located at the center of the panel in order to compare the
experimental data to the established theory. By doing this its ignored that a line load be­
haves differently and that it cannot be converted into a point load as done throughout this
thesis.
In addition, all experiments only state the compression strengths of the materials used,
which is why the tension strengths are approximated by using eqs. (2.1) and (2.2). The
error that can occur due to this approximation is stated in section 2.2.6.

[19]. As mentioned earlier, the yield criterion derived by Findsen only covers masonry

articles will not suit the established models at the same level as the rest of the articles.

5
Conclusion
Throughout this thesis, the capacity of masonry panels has been described and investi­
gated. Looking into the properties of masonry and the previous work performed by Find­
sen and others, it was possible to establish the theory needed to predict the behavior of
masonry panels.
A generic standard solution for panels subjected to vertical and horizontal loads. This
solution was made generic to predict the behavior based on the geometry and the magni­
tude of the loads. In order to create this solution, homogeneous stress fields were used.
The panel was divided into different triangular homogeneous stress fields to determine
the normal and shear stresses for each zone of the panel. The solutions were created
such that the disposition of the compression line would be a demanding factor to divide
the zones. This was done by introducing the distance, c, which was the length of the
fourth zone, which was made dependent on the geometry­ and the load ratio. This new
distance, c, was limited to ensure that the compression line would not extend through the
side of the panel, to prevent crushing at the panel toe.
The yield criteria for masonry, derived by Findsen were examined in order to use the
theory. Findsen stated that the yield criterion derived is only valid for unreinforced masonry
consisting of solid bricks but that the theory can be expanded to cover reinforced masonry
and hollow bricks. The yield criterion was used to define the yield surface, which was used
to determine the capacity of masonry panels subjected to in­plane loading.
By combining the homogeneous stress fields and yield criteria, it was possible to establish
models such as the P­α diagram and the utilization diagram. These two diagrams made
it possible to predict the behavior of the masonry, as the capacity was stated in the P­α
diagrams. The actual load actions could then be plotted in the P­α diagram to determine
the utilization of each of the homogeneous stress fields in the panel. These utilization’s
were used in order to create the utilization diagram which stated the geometry of the panel
and its homogeneous zones, where a color code was used to illustrate the utilization level.
In order to use the theory and models established, experiments from the literature were
investigated. Here, five different articles covering masonry panels subjected to in­plane
shear were reviewed. The main data were collected from the 21 individual experiments
in total. After a brief introduction to each experimental paper, the observed experimental
data in terms of the failure mechanism and the magnitude of the horizontal load at failure
were compared with the established models. Using the models, it was possible to estimate
an expected value for the maximum horizontal load and to obtain information about the
possible failure mechanism.
When the observed and expected values were later compared, it was found that there was
a good relation. It was seen that when the values for the horizontal load at failure were
compared, most of the experiment observed higher values than expected, which provided
some sort of safety to the model. Some experiments observed lower values, but it was
stated in section 4.3 that the affected experiments had used hollow bricks, which could
be one of the reasons for the disagreement. By introducing the safety factor of κ = 0.8, it
was observed that almost all the experiments were in the safe area with a deviation of ±

Further work
In order to ensure further development of the work performed in this thesis and to facilitate
future extensions, several areas where a broader knowledge is desired is stated below.
This is done to encourage future readers to take on this task.
In order to gain more knowledge of the behavior of Danish masonry, it is desired to perform
more experiments on Danish brick masonry. As described throughout this thesis, Danish
masonry generally consists of a standard brick type with different types of mortar used for
the joints. This would make it easier to compare experiments on Danish masonry as they
in general are more similar. During this thesis many different types of bricks and mortars
were used in the different tests, with different properties. One of the main differences was
the composition of the bricks, as some of the bricks were hollow bricks with different hole
patterns.
The yield criterion derived by Findsen [1], could be expanded to cover hollow bricks and
solid bricks to expand the coverage of the theory, as it was found in this thesis that many of
the articles in the literature cover experiments performed on masonry consisting of hollow
bricks.
It would also be interesting to investigate the impact of the different bond patterns used,
as it was discovered that many different types of bond patterns were used in the articles
investigated in this thesis. As mentioned, one of the bond patterns of the articles created
a scenario where three head joints would line up, creating a vulnerable area of the panel,
where a failure would be expected to pass through. During the calculations of the uniaxial
strengths in this thesis, the strengths were not dependent on the bond pattern, but it would
be interesting to investigate whether this is valid in reality.
It would be interesting to investigate the behavior of masonry under different types of
loading. In this thesis a vertical uniform line load has been investigated as the resulting
point load acting in the center of the panel, in addition to a horizontal point load acting
at the top of the panel. It could be interesting to investigate different types of loading
scenarios, such as eccentric vertical point loads at the top, where the load is not located
at the center but drifted towards the corners of the panel. In addition, other scenarios
could be investigated along different types of failure mechanisms as splitting failure as
stated by Hagsten in his PhD thesis ”Plasticitetsteori for murværk ­ Del 3: Koncentreret
last” [6].
As the last example, it could be interesting to investigate the yield criterion of reinforced
masonry as described by Findsen [1]. Findsen states the modified yield criterion for re­
inforced masonry, and this theory could be used to investigate other types of masonry
as brick lintels. Brick lintels behave like a tall beam, supported at both ends, making it
a standard beam solution subjected to different types of vertical load, like the example
stated in fig. 2.8a.
