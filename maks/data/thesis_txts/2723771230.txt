Abstract
Challenge and objective
Increasing environmental stress over the last century led to
various calls for action, which pressured companies to act responsibly and reduce their
impact on climate change. Whereas these organisations can base their efforts on several
target-setting frameworks, the options for a standardised and satisfying method to plan
the achievement seems to fall short. Hence, this work aims to support companies on
their decarbonisation journey with a methodology to develop GHG emission reduction
roadmaps.
Approach
The research design of this study is based on the Design Research Method-
ology and an underlying hypothesis. After the initial research phase, a structured literature
review is conducted to gain subject knowledge. This enables the validation of the hypoth-
esis, the identification of concrete research gaps as well as the derivation of research
questions. Next to the structured literature review, the knowledge-gathering process is
supplemented by expert interviews on which basis the conceptualisation and develop-
ment of the methodology are pursued. In addition to a case study, various criteria analy-
ses and expert interviews support the validation of the developed approach at the end of
the study.
Findings
The key finding of this study is a methodology that proposes the decarbonisa-
tion roadmap development in three thematic clusters, which are split into seven steps. An
additional pre-study and a follow-up process complete the method. The approach starts
with the thematic cluster of status quo analysis. During this step, the corporate carbon
footprint of a company is created and analysed. This is followed by the countermea-
sure analysis cluster. Here, emission reduction measures are identified, prioritised and
prepared for further processing. This leads to the final thematic cluster, called strategic
roadmapping in which the decarbonisation roadmap creation is detailed and a commu-
nication strategy is developed. Finally, high-level implementation planning is pursued in
the follow-up process.
Conclusions
The developed approach generates the desired results and may be used
by companies to create their decarbonisation roadmap. Hence, it has the potential to
guide companies in developing a clear and well-structured action plan to reach the decar-
bonisation target. Therefore, it resolves the uncertainty of companies. Nevertheless, the
limitations becomes apparent in high resource utilisation, especially when the project’s
complexity is rising. Thus, future work is required to enable the application for big enter-
prises also.
Keywords
Corporate decarbonisation roadmapping methodology, strategic decarbon-
isation management, climate change mitigation, corporate environmental sustainability,
greenhouse gas emission reduction, carbon emission reduction pathways for companies

Introduction
The first chapter of this thesis serves as an introduction to the subject area and outlines the
boundary conditions including the objective of this work. To this end, section 1.1 describes
companies’ initial requirements and challenges to reduce their impact on climate change.
This impact additionally forms the motivation of this thesis. Based on this, section 1.2
presents the objectives of the research project, while the scientific approach is outlined
in section 1.3. This is followed by defining the research boundaries in section 1.4. At the
end of chapter 1 in section 1.5, the structure of the thesis is described and illustrated by
a schematic outline.
1.1
Current situation and motivation
The anthropogenic change of the earth’s climate is scientifically proven [1] and in 1972 the
Club of Rome already warned in their study, ”The Limits to Growth” [2], that the rates of
economic growth cannot be maintained forever. They further called for action to reduce
the environmental stress of the planet [2]. In 2009 Rockström et al. [3] published the
framework of planetary boundaries, describing the limitations of the impacts of human
activities in nine environmental areas. These boundaries frame a point beyond which
the environment can not self-regulate itself anymore impending severe and irreversible
changes. One of those areas is climate change, caused mainly by the greenhouse gas
(GHG) concentration in the atmosphere [3]. With an update of the concept in 2015, the
researchers [4] concluded that the boundary for climate change has been exceeded and
several environmental catastrophes like the destruction of the summer polar ice cap are
almost certain [4]. Moreover, in 2015, the Paris Agreement [5] was passed by the United
Nations (UN) to ensure drastic GHG emission reduction globally. This aims to restrain the
”global average temperature to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and pursuing
efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5°C ” [5]. Despite all the calls for action and
warnings over the last 50 years, the 6th assessment cycle of the Intergovernmental Panel
on Climate Change (IPCC) showed peaking annual GHG emissions between 2010 and
2019 in comparison to the previous decades [6]. It is specified that considering the global
goals of the Paris agreement, GHG emission reduction requires immediate acceleration
and to be reduced by 43% by 2030. Further, it is stated that this is still considered possible
with drastic reduction actions across global industries [7, 8]. The key enablers for this
transition are policies fostering GHG emission reduction and access to finance for the
entities in these industries. Next to scientific pressure, public awareness also accelerated
over the past decade. Amongst others, this combination led to various climate mitigation
policies and regulations internationally [8]. One of those is the Green Deal of the European
Union (EU), including the EU climate law from 2021 [9], that aims to increase climate
change mitigation efforts and deliver ”on the implementation of the Paris Agreement” [9].
Hence, the emission reduction ambitions were defined for 2030 and 2050 with 55% and
100% respectively in relation to 1990 levels [9, 10]. One of the key measures of the
EU to reach this goal is the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), which
specifies sustainability reporting requirements for companies, aligning these entities with
the Paris Agreement. Regarding the environmental reporting requirements for climate
change, next to annual GHG emissions also reduction targets and transition plans need
to be disclosed.
Whether the scientific, political, public or the resulting customer pressure, companies face
increased calls to act and reduce their GHG emissions [11]. In order to claim ambition

1.2
Objective and research questions
The overall objective of this work is to support companies on their decarbonisation jour-
ney with a methodology to develop GHG emission reduction roadmaps. To this end, the
hypothesis is first tested to identify existing research gaps in strategic decarbonisation
guidance for companies across sectors. Specifically, this study focuses on emission re-
duction trajectories to reach corporate decarbonisation targets. Hence, it aims to propose
steps to be followed to create a decarbonisation roadmap. It is focused on holistic cor-
porate GHG emissions including emissions occurring at the value-chain level. Following
this, the work is based on reducing the occurrence of these GHG emissions and guides
companies with potential sources of the required information. In addition, it intends to
prioritise the necessary GHG emission reduction actions based on various sources such
as scientific references, political goals as well as opinions and assumptions. This leads
to the following main research question:
RQ I: What can a methodology for the successful development of a decarbonisation
roadmap for companies look like?
The outcome of the overarching research question is achieved by answering the following
sub-research questions:
• RQ II: What needs to be done in which order to create a strategic carbon reduction
pathway for a company?
• RQ III: Which steps can be helpful to define suitable decarbonisation measures for
a company?
• RQ IV: How can emission reduction initiatives be prioritised and which aspects need
to be considered doing so?
• RQ V: How can reduction profiles be created for emission reduction initiatives?

1.4
Limitations
This study targets the development of a method to create corporate roadmaps that are
focusing on GHG emissions and the impact of companies on climate change. Hence, it is
not focused on other environmental impacts, like biodiversity or water use, and also not
on holistic sustainability. In addition, the work centres on companies’ holistic emissions
(Scope 1, 2 and 3). Thus, the process is designed to structure the decarbonisation on a
company level, but not on a product level. A company reduction target of e.g. 20% does
not imply the reduction for specific products to the same amount. Whereas the attention
of this work relies on decarbonisation management, it does not aim to provide reasons
and convince companies on reducing their emissions. Therefore, the ambition and vision
to reduce the GHG emissions are prerequisites for the application of the results of this
work. Emission reduction initiatives are exemplified and sources for further actions are
named, but it is not the ambition of this study to propose a holistic overview of decarbon-
isation measures. As a consequence of the objective to reduce the emissions caused by
a company, greenwashing is explicitly excluded. Offsetting is only pursued to diminish
residual emissions that can not be reduced otherwise. However, it is not the purpose of
this study to guide the selection of specific offsetting projects. While this thesis aims to
be a strategic guidance for decarbonisation management, organisational change man-
agement and the detailed implementation of the strategy are clearly demarcated from this
work. Hence, this study won’t recommend any company restructuring measures including
degrowth and does not aim to change the current business model of a company.

2.1.1
Sustainability & ESG
For the concept of sustainability, different definitions within and across scientific disci-
plines exist. The word itself can be described as ”the quality of being able to continue
over a period of time” [17]. One of the most common definitions of sustainable develop-
ment was shaped in 1987 by the World Commission on Environment and Development, a
UN sub-organization. The organization was shaped to unite the world in sustainable de-
velopments and published the report ”Our common future” [18]. It narrowed sustainable
development down to actions that ”meets the needs of the present without compromising
the ability of future generations to meet their own needs” [18]. In order to have an overview
of the various sustainability definitions, Waseem and Kota [19], worked on an analysis of
sustainability definitions in 2017. As a result, most definitions are referring to a balance
between the three dimensions of economical, societal and environmental matters [19].
This also leads to another popular concept for sustainability in management, the triple-
bottom-line. This paradigm focuses on a company’s balance between social, economic,
and environmental impact [20]. Most companies report on their economic performance
traditionally through performance statements like financial reports. In order to focus also
on non-financial activities, the most dominant approach in recent years is the ESG con-
cept. Here, non-financial sustainability is defined by the topics of ESG. It is mostly used
to report on related risks and opportunities [21]. This framework is often related to sus-
tainable finance, which takes ESG considerations in investment decisions into account.
This roots in the assumptions, that ESG-focused investment will boost strategic financial
support in sustainable economic undertakings [21, 22, 23]. With the European Green
Deal, sustainable finance is one of the focus areas of the EU and is mostly reflected in
the CSRD. [24, 21, 25]. In the following the three parts of ESG will be explained and can
also be seen in fig. 2.1 [26, 27]:
• Environmental: This factor relates to the impacts of a company on the natural bal-
ance of the earth. This includes the usage of natural resources, pollution of the
environment and many more. Further, organizations would disclose which environ-
mental risks and opportunities derive from their operations. Specific examples of
that can be the disclosure of the carbon footprint or resource usage (e.g. water,
metals...).
• Social: This form of impact describes the influence of firms on Human Rights. It
includes fair wages and treatment as well as health and safety measures amongst
others. In the first place, this aims at the company itself but covers also the influence
along the supply chain.

2.1.1.1
Climate change and greenhouse gases (GHG)
This study is focusing on environmental sustainability and within this impact domain on
climate change.
Whereas weather can be referred to as the atmospheric state of the earth at a specific
point in time, the climate is the long-term perspective of weather. Therefore, climate can
be defined as the state of the atmosphere over several years or decades. Following this,
climate change can be defined as the statistical change of the atmospheric state over a
long period of time and includes averages as well as local extremes. As it is unequiv-
ocally that the climate changed in the last 150 years it is certain that it will do so in the
future. Further, it is evident that this is caused due to the increased pollution of GHG into
the atmosphere by humanity. GHGs are emitted into the atmosphere and can be related
to most of human activities. The most harmful actions can be named as the burning of
fossil fuels and deforestation [29, 1, 30, 31]. According to the UN (Kyoto Protocol [32]
and Doha Amendment [33]), the most important GHGs for climate change are: carbon
dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs), perflu-
orocarbons (PFCs), sulphur hexafluoride (SF6), nitrogen trifluoride (NF3) [32, 33]. The
most polluting, abundant and therefore important GHG for anthropogenic climate change
is CO2. In order to compare the impact of the different gases their impact related to a ref-
erence gas, dominantly CO2, can be calculated. This impact factor is also called Global
Warming Potential (GWP) and is mostly used for the impact of the gas over 100 years.
The related GWP to the above-mentioned GHGs can be seen in table 2.1 [34, 35, 36, 37].
In practice, the impact of gases, systems, products or companies would be described as
carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e). To quantify this for gases, the GWPs are multiplied
by the mass of the gases, which are aimed to assess. For the impact assessment of
systems on climate change, the number of gases emitted to the atmosphere is multiplied
by the GWP. The result is the impact of the system in relation to CO2. The two most
applied concepts of carbon impact assessment are product carbon footprints (PCF) and
corporate carbon footprint (CCF). According to the focus of this study, the next section is
concentrating on CCF. Within this work CO2, the words carbon dioxide and carbon always
refer to CO2e, unless not specified differently.

2.1.2
Relevant key concepts of corporate carbon footprints
A CCF is a quantification of carbon equivalent emissions caused by a company, directly
and indirectly. The relevant key concepts of CCFs are described in the following part. This
includes the operational boundaries, the impact quantification as well as the limitations.
Operational boundaries:
Operational boundaries are divided into direct and indirect
emissions of a company. Whereas direct emissions are occurring from owned or con-
trolled sources, indirect ones are the immediate consequence of the actions of the com-
pany. Therefore, direct emissions are emitted by the respective company and indirect
emissions are emitted by a second or third party. In order to refer to specific emissions
sources and to avoid double counting the GHG Protocol defined three different scopes
for the differentiation of operational emissions [38, 39, 40]:
• Scope 1: Direct emissions, which arise from a company’s own actions (e.g. emis-
sions from direct heating, transportation or use of chemicals)
• Scope 2: Indirect emissions from utilities like the purchase of electricity, steam,
heating, cooling
• Scope 3: Other indirect emissions which are not covered by Scope 2 and are a
consequence of the company’s activity. Covering emissions of the whole supply
chain, upstream and downstream. Examples of important categories are purchased
goods and services, capital goods, business travel, and use of sold products.
Impact quantification and emission factor (EF):
The impact quantification of a com-
pany can either be done with primary or secondary data. These data are values which
represent the emissions of a product, material or service in relation to carbon emissions.
Whereas primary data are product and supplier-specific and result from a Life Cycle As-
sessment (LCA) or a PCF, secondary data are average data. All of these values are
normally stated as the amount of CO2e (e.g. t of CO2e) in relation to an amount of the
product. This can either be activity-based (e.g. kg, t or L) or spend-based (e.g. €). The
impact data are used to assess the carbon-related impact of activities of companies on
climate change. The accuracy of these data varies in descending order from primary to
activity-based data and is lowest for spend-based data. An overview of the differences
in these data can be seen in fig. 2.3. The impact quantification is done by multiplying the
relevant material flows by the primary or secondary data of the material. The combination
of all assessed impacts of a company can then be seen as the overall impact on climate
change, which is also referred to as CCF [41, 40, 38, 39].

Limitations of carbon accounting:
CCF calculation methods have several limitations.
First, CCFs only measure GHG emissions and lack of accounting for environmental and
also social impacts, which can be harmed instead. More specifically, CCFs take carbon
emissions into account, but the impacts of water usage, on human rights or biodiversity
are neglected [43, 44, 45]. This is illustrated in fig. 2.4. Second, data availability and
quality can be critical, especially for Scope 3 emissions, where the use of average data
(EF) is mostly unavoidable. Hence, CCF but especially Scope 3 calculations should be
interpreted with caution [43, 46]. Kaplan and Ramanna (2021) [46] even state that these
values are often unreliable, unverifiable and often contradictory [46]. In 2022 Marlow and
Clark [47] concluded in 2022 two main gaps in carbon footprinting; ”incompleteness and
a lack of transparency” [47]. The latter is described due to not reporting sources of data
and accounting decisions like the chosen boundaries. They further conclude that carbon
disclosures are not comparable [47]. Regarding the lack of transparency, they found that
many are not accounting for all the emissions caused [47]. This coincides with the findings
of the Climate Responsibility Monitor in 2023 [11], where the 24 assessed companies only
showed limited reporting on Scope 3 emission categories [11].

2.1.3
Climate change mitigation activities
Climate change mitigation activities can be split in direct emission reduction activities by
the emitter as well as indirect reduction by a third party in favour of the emitter. In the
following subsection, first the direct and afterwards the indirect emission reduction concept
will be explained. In general, all decarbonisation actions should underlay the concept of
additionality. This means that the reduction of carbon emissions would not have occurred

==PAGE:24==
without the action. This concept is mostly known for indirect emission reduction projects as
direct activities are controlled by the emitter and through their characteristics additionality
is already given [50, 51]. Within this study, emission reduction activities will also be called
countermeasures, decarbonisation activities, actions, initiatives as well as measures.
Direct emission reduction strategies:
Direct decarbonisation efforts are mostly dis-
tinguished between efficiency, consistency and sufficiency measures, as seen in fig. 2.5
[52, 53, 54].
Figure 2.5: Direct emission reduction strategies [Adapted from [54, 55]]
Efficiency or more specifically eco-efficiency, initiatives are characterized by creating the
same performance with minimum efforts and resources used. Regarding decarbonisation
efficiency, the focused resources are (raw) materials and energy. Due to the reduced in-
puts, it is not only environmentally but also economically beneficial to enhance efficiency.
Examples are the use of LED light bulbs instead of conventional ones, improvements of
the building insulation (and therefore less demand of temperature modifications (heating/-
cooling)) or the use of an electrical appliance with a better scale on the EU energy label.
Improvements are typically measured in a relative decrease in inputs used to create the
same output [56, 57, 58]. The related decrease of inputs can often cause (direct or in-
direct) an increased use of the same or another resource, the so-called rebound effect.
In this way, the energy efficiency yields of a product often lead to a higher total energy
consumption of this product or decrease the success of efficiency wins [59, 60, 56, 57].
10

==PAGE:25==
Consistency, the second strategy, can be defined as metabolic nature-integrated indus-
trial ecology and focuses on the quality of resource flows [52]. It is also known as eco-
effectiveness [54, 58] and groups the transformation into nature-integrated processes,
often progressing towards renewable solutions. In practice, this is represented by the
substitution of current products or materials for more sustainable alternatives. Consis-
tency can be focused on changing the supplied product or the supplier (by consumer of
product) but also on innovating a system in a nature-integrated approach (by manufac-
turer of the product). The latter is very time-consuming and strategically oriented which is
mostly caused by technical modifications but can also require organisational changes [56,
57]. Examples of sustainable consistency initiatives are the supply of renewable energy,
local products or the substitution of virgin materials with recycled ones [53, 57]. Efficiency
and eco-effectiveness measures should always be considered together. On the one hand
as not demanded energy can be used somewhere else and decreased the stress of the
total energy demand. On the other hand, as for now, also renewable products like re-
newable energy sources rely heavily on non-renewable systems in their supply chain and
pollute the environment severely [54].
Sufficiency describes a change in consumer behaviour and focuses on using fewer as well
as different resources and goods. It can further be framed as a strategy which follows
the fact, that if a good is not manufactured it does not require resources and will not
emit emissions which can harm the environment [61, 58]. Sufficiency measures can be
categorized in the following three areas [62]:
• Reduction: Reducing the quantitative redundant usage of a resource after realizing
the actually needed demand. This can cause expanding use and service cycles (e.g.
using a product longer or washing it less), reducing the use of a resource (e.g. using
a machine less) or choosing a smaller size of a product (e.g. choosing a small car
over the not needed SUV).
• Substitution: Qualitative modification of practices which lead to a more sustainable
lifestyle (e.g. using public transport or a bike instead of a car or preferring the local
supplier)
• Adaptation: Conscious adjustment of products to the specific use. This could entail
the shutdown of unused modules or areas (e.g. turning off appliances and lighting
which are not used at a time). The realisation of adaptation can be supported by
sensors and smart meters.
Sufficiency is seen as encountering the disadvantages of efficiency (rebound effects) and
consistency (long-term innovation developments) [56] while also being accused of a pes-
simistic, unrealistic and backwards-looking approach due to its renunciation-based core
[54].
These strategies can help to limit the harm and stress caused by organisations and in-
dividuals. Whereas these approaches have different potentials, with efficiency of around
10-20%, consistency of 50-80% and sufficiency of around 10-40%, the biggest opportu-
nity lies in the combination of the strategies to yield the highest emission reduction returns
[56]. When it comes to energy efficiency, the paradigm of ”energy efficiency first” [63] is
dominant. It means considering energy-efficient investments before choosing a more ex-
pensive and complex energy supply option. If the efficiency measure is cheaper, it will
have the advantage that less energy supply is needed and can be utilized at another point
[64, 65, 66]. A summary of the presented strategies can be found in table 2.2.

Indirect emission reduction:
Emission reduction by a third party in favour of the emitter
can be called carbon crediting or carbon offsetting. Some examples of these actions can
be renewable energy certificates (REC), carbon sinks (e.g. reforestation or maintenance
of marshes and peatland), carbon capture of not yet emitted (at e.g. production plants)
or of already emitted carbon (direct air capture (DAC)) [67, 68, 69, 70]. While carbon
credits in general are not covered by leading GHG accounting frameworks, REC can be
currently used to decrease the GHG emissions of companies [39, 71]. Opposed to the
majority of offerings, indirect electricity emission reduction through the procurement of
REC can potentially reduce responsible emissions, whereas other offers often remove
already emitted CO2 or support others to emit less. As mentioned above, especially in
indirect emission reduction projects, additionality is key to ensuring the effectiveness of
the claimed impact. While this is also the case for the procurement of green electricity,
RECs are not necessarily ensuring additionally [71, 72]. This increases the complexity of
green electricity procurement, raises critics and results in the creation of green electricity
labels which ensure additionally [73, 72]. In addition to that, the SBTi [74] define in their
corporate net-zero standard, that only unabated emissions, which can currently not be
reduced, should be compensated by such neutralization projects [74].
Greenwashing:
As the environmental performance of products and companies is rising
in importance in consumer decision-making, some companies try to appear more environ-
mentally friendly than they actually are. This behaviour can be described as Greenwash-
ing. This can also be defined as misleading external stakeholders about the environmental
impact or performance of companies, products and services. Greenwashing can happen
in various forms, some examples can be seen below:
• Claims which can not be seen in actual actions of companies
• Claiming of not defined expressions
• Working with pictures of nature which have the intention to create an environmentally
friendly appearance
• Working with not existent labels
From an environmental and ethical perspective, those marketing stunts should be avoided
in any way and should not be pursued in a company’s sustainable transition [75, 76, 77,
78].

Climate Disclosure Project (CDP):
CDP stands for Climate Disclosure Project and is
an NGO, which offers a global system for climate-related disclosure. Companies, cities,
states and public authorities can disclose their sustainability-related data and key perfor-
mance indicators (KPIs) on a yearly basis to CDP. Here, the interested stakeholders can
see and compare the performance of the different companies. On a yearly basis, CDP is
also rating the disclosed bodies in order to incentivise action and improvements among
them [79, 80, 81].
Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi):
The SBTi aims to support companies limiting
their impact on global warming. It is a collaboration of the United Nations Global Com-
pact (UNGC), CDP, the World Wide Fund for Nature (WWF) and the World Resources
Institute (WRI). The initiative demonstrates companies how much they need to reduce
their emissions to align their operations with the Paris Agreement of the UN. From this,
companies can commit and set targets, which are reviewed and validated by the initiative.
Following this, firms would reduce their impact on the severe risks of climate change and
report progress updates annually [82, 83, 84, 85].

Decarbonisation strategy
After analysing several strategy definitions, Nickols comes to the conclusion that a strat-
egy can be a lot of things. In his view, a strategy can be a plan for how to reach goals, it
can be a perspective from which purpose, directions, decisions and actions are derived,
but can also be a pattern of decision-making. Nickols concludes that strategy can be all
of that, but it describes the pathway towards these goals and not the goals themselves.
Further, it is clarified that strategy is part of a four-part structure: Goal definition, strategy
to reach the goals, tactics how resources are deployed to reach the goals as well as the
resources themselves. According to Nickols’ conclusion, for this study, a decarbonisation
strategy is the defined pathway for how the emission targets are aimed to be reached [86].
2.2.2
Decarbonisation roadmap
A roadmap can be seen as a strategic tool, which helps deepen the understanding and
describes a possible trajectory to achieve outlined mid to long-term targets over time.
In addition, a roadmap typically states the desired objectives, actions and milestones
while also clarifying priorities and the gaps between the status quo and the goals. With
these characteristics (fig. 2.7), it is difficult to create a roadmap without measuring the
current situation and without involving all key stakeholders and decision-makers. It is
an appropriate tool for uncertain and complex environments and bears the benefit that
all participants of the roadmap creation will agree on the understanding of the situation,
including the status quo, needed actions, temporal trajectory, challenges and risks [87, 88,
89, 90]. According to Poduval’s perspective [91], a roadmap should not be regarded as
an unalterable plan, but rather as a tentative outline. This implies the need to respond to
technological advancements or setbacks by staying adaptable and flexible in the process
[91]. According to The Natural Step [92], successful sustainability roadmapping is only
possible with the concept of backcasting (fig. 2.7).
Figure 2.7: Backcasting [Adapted from [92]] and roadmapping principles
This is a method, where the roadmap creator starts to think from the goal backwards and
subordinates every consideration of actions in the present to achieve these goals. While
doing this the vision and the gap are constantly in mind and the situation is improving
action by action towards this objective [92]. In contrast, forecasting is the creation of future
scenarios by considering the past, which will not lead to the needed and drastic changes

Criteria set I: Baseline
The first criteria set focuses on creating a carbon emission baseline for the company for

determine the progress on the way to the goal and to analyse the success of strategy and
roadmap after the defined time horizon. The set entails and defines the three criteria:
1. Baseline creation: This part intends to evaluate if the approach considers creating

2. Holistic Baseline: As described in section 2.1.2 the scopes of a company’s carbon
footprint can highly vary. Accordingly, for an organisational decarbonisation trans-
formation, a methodology should at least mention the differences and derive the
scope following from the emission reduction targets of the company.
3. Baseline analysis: After the baseline creation this criterion focuses on the critical
reflection of the results. This could comprise a hot-spot or leverage analysis where
an impression of the biggest emission sources is formed. It could also contain further
analysis of the biggest or easiest emission reduction potentials.

The focus of strategic orientation of the methodology derives from the goal of this study -

1. Decarbonisation vision: As there are a lot of topics covered in corporate sustain-

is necessary to assess the strategic focus of a method. Thus this criteria aims to

2. Flexibility of method: This criterion aims to test if the method is versatile for the

Criteria set III: Decarbonisation initiatives
As a strategic plan is aiming to create an orientation for all stakeholders it is crucial to
include actions leading toward the targeted vision. Hence this set targets the proposed
climate actions of the source, which intend to reduce the emissions of the company. It is
split into the below specified seven criteria:
1. Proposing of initiatives: In order to give the reader a solid understanding of what
can be done to reduce emissions, it is seen as important to name categories and
specific examples of emission reduction levers. This can be done for one or several
specific sources of emissions or for different sectors.
2. Sources of measures: In addition to the above, it is crucial to elaborate on where
to find further emission reduction initiatives, which are not included as examples.
This also covers industry-specific sources and is identifying if the approaches give
insights into how to identify decarbonisation actions.
3. Reduction actions: As it is highly crucial in today’s decarbonisation-oriented world
to actively reduce emissions from activities yourself, the word reduction needs to be
stressed and focused. The opposite would be to pass on this responsibility, which
leads to the following criteria.
4. Only residual offsetting: As just framed it is not feasible that all companies are
relying on third parties to reduce emissions on their behalf. Therefore, this objective
entails that third-party carbon offsetting, including carbon crediting and removal, is
only considered for residual emissions which are not yet possible to reduce.
5. Avoid greenwashing: This matter is also building up on the one before and should
secure that greenwashing is avoided by the approach. Examples of that can be that
not only residual emissions are offset and not emissions which are already possible
to mitigate. Further, avoiding greenwashing can mean that things are chosen for
branding and marketing reasons and that the actual situation is not featured better
than it actually is.
6. Reduction profile: Finally, this object identifies whether the literature considers
quantifying the impact of the measure over the years to come including the option
to cover regional differences. It is insufficient for this criteria if it is only stated that a
certain impact can be created in total by the initiative.
7. Prioritisation: The last criteria of this set aims to assess whether the identified
carbon emission mitigation options are ranked according to their suitability.

ferent groups and departments within the company or whether they rather introduce
a top-down created plan. The stakeholders can be internal (e.g. decision-makers,
experts in departments...), but also external (e.g. investors, suppliers or customers).
2. Consider ambition: Specifying the above, it can also be important to include the
opinion and ambition (e.g. resources like money or employees, but also the de-
carbonisation speed) of key stakeholders to arrive at specific actions, timelines or
emission reduction profiles.
3. Involving of experts: Specifying the first criteria of this set, in order to derive certain
decarbonisation measures it can be of fundamental importance to discuss options
with experts outside the target company. This can be technical or strategical experts,
but also financial or legal professionals who can give more insights in e.g. feasibility,
prioritization, possibilities and others.
4.1.5
Criteria set V: Roadmapping
Following the title of the thesis, this can be seen as the core of the work, the actual creation
of the roadmap. This set can be understood as the translation of the decision-making into
a plan as well as the process visualization. The three derived criteria are explained below:
1. Roadmap creation: A criteria which determines if the emission reduction potential
is visualized over the corresponding time frame.
2. Backcasting: This point specifies the creation of the roadmap, which should follow
the concept of backcasting (described in section 2.2 & fig. 2.7). This can be derived
from [92], which states that the needed drastic changes for sustainability transfor-
mation can only be derived with the concept of backcasting (orienting always at
future scenario) instead of forecasting (the past gives the orientation). This criterion
is therefore satisfied if the emission reduction planning starts with the vision in mind
and moves from the baseline slowly towards the defined target. This simulation will
continue as long as the goal is reached.
3. Iterative approach: The criterion of iteratively describes a roadmap method where
there are opportunities to track the progress along the way and therefore leave room
for adjustments as well as developments (e.g. technical, legal...) over time.
4.1.6
Criteria set VI: Communication
The last criteria set focuses on the communication of results. After creating the decarboni-
sation roadmap it is important to spread the word about what the company plans to do. On
the one hand, creating awareness internally and externally is important to motivate and
engage employees, investors and customers. On the other hand, communication creates
the opportunity to get feedback and improvement ideas along the way. The communica-
tion can be focused on the plan but could also entail progress updates and improvements.

4.3.1.1
GHG Protocol
The ”GHG Protocol Corporate Accounting and Reporting Standard” [39] provides a frame-
work to measure GHG for organizations holistically from their supply chain to their oper-
ations [106]. Similar to the ISO 14064:1 it specifies rules and describes specifics as the
ISO standard the following [39]:
• Organizational boundaries
• Operational boundaries
• Base year development
• Transparency
• Accuracy
• Consistency
• Completeness
• Relevance
The GHG protocol is a combined effort of the World Resources Institute (WRI) and the
World Business Council for Sustainable Development (WBCSD), which started their ef-
forts in 1998 [107] and aims to support organizations to quantify their GHG emissions in
a true and fair way while simplifying this at low costs and creating transparent and con-
sistent guidance [108]. It was then first published in 2001 [39] and was amended several
times with relevant guidances and updates including the GHG Protocol Scope 2 Guidance
(for indirect purchased heat and electricity) in 2014 [109] as well as the ”Corporate Value
Chain (Scope 3) Accounting and Reporting Standard” in 2011 [42, 106]. The framework
covers the quantification of CO2, CH4, HFCs, PFCs, N2O, SF6 and NF3 [108, 39].

the following ”3 Strategic Prioritisation Questions” [117]:
• ”Is this action moving you toward or away from sustainability vision?” [117]
• ”Is this action a flexible platform toward your sustainability vision?” [117]
• ”Will this action offer an adequate return on investment” [117] (not limited to financial,
but also environmental)?

123-Zero Method for Net-Zero Carbon Manufacturing at Net-Zero Cost by
Smith et al.
Within this paper, Smith et al. [66] are describing a framework to support manufactur-
ing plants to reach net-zero emissions of energy consumption at net-zero costs. It is
named the ”123-Zero Approach to Net-Zero Carbon Manufacturing” [66] and is a three-
step method. The first step promotes increasing the energy efficiency of the plant, which
will require an investment in the beginning but will save money over time. After the invest-
ment is paid off, the authors recommend using this positive cash flow to invest in on-site
renewable energy systems in step two. It is further elaborated that these systems would
have the benefits of reduction of energy purchasing costs, option to sell renewable energy
certificates (REC) from the build system as well as being less vulnerable to energy prices.
Therefore, the time until the company would have profited from the on-site renewable sys-
tem investment is dependent on the cost of electricity, the cost and the performance of
the renewable energy system as well as the price of RECs. After implementing efficiency
measures, on-site renewable energy and both investments having a positive cash flow,
Smith et al. [66] are proposing to buy RECs to offset the rest of the carbon emissions
resulting from the energy use from the manufacturing plant. This practice is justified with
the argument that manufacturing companies produce goods that will be used outside the
plant and within thermodynamic thinking it would be unreasonable to think of a net-zero
plant as a net-zero on-site energy plant. Therefore the authors conclude that when prod-
ucts are produced for places outside the plant, the plant should also be allowed to use
outside REC to be net-zero [66].

A methodological framework for supply chain carbon footprint

Aivazidou et al. [120] are describing ”A methodological framework for supply chain car-
bon footprint management” [120], which is proposing a two-layered framework with nine
sub-steps. The two layers are divided into a strategic and a tactical one, where the lat-

is analysing the internal and external environment and setting strategic goals. The first
action in the strategic process is a legislative framework and trend analysis, where inter-

EU ETS). This is followed by a benchmark and best practice screening, aiming to learn
from ambitious green practices and policies from decarbonisation leading companies. Af-
ter the external analysis is completed, the authors recommend setting strategic emission
reduction targets to manage the carbon footprint effectively. The next strategical step en-
tails the identification of emission-critical operations in the supply chain and the life cycle
of the products. This includes first the mapping of activities and the assessment of cli-
mate change crucial hot spots and secondly the identification of the role of the supplier.
In the fifth step, a CCF will be developed according to ISO or GHG Protocol standards.
Within this step, the cruciality of the data gathering process is highlighted, which can be
both (CCF and data gathering) supported by tools and software. The final step within the
strategic phase is the evaluation of the CCF as well as the strategic process. Therefore,
the targets and the critical operational areas will be critically analysed and if necessary
adjusted. Further, in this step, the identified and reviewed focus areas will be specified to
know where and with whom will be collaborated as well as whether it must happen inter-
nally or externally. After the strategic process is finalized, the tactical one will be started.
Aivazidou et al. [120] are proposing to work on three steps, decision-making, monitoring
and re-evaluation. Within decision-making, it is described to assign the responsibility of
the overall program to a person or group and deciding who is in charge of which parts of
the reduction of the direct emission and should select appropriate external supply chain
partners to collaborate with. The last two parts are closely connected, where the progress
will be frequently monitored to assess the status quo of the emissions internally and ex-
ternally, the strategy should be periodically evaluated to decide whether changes should
be made or new technologies considered to be implemented [120].

up- and downstream as well as all possible tiers from the raw material extraction to the

the products originate from and where they go as well as what product is exchanged.
This value chain mapping will then result in the identification of the different raw mate-

and materials. A follow-up action of this is to undertake a hot-spot analysis to discover
the most carbon-impactful steps and materials as well as investigate the parts with the
most emission reduction potential. This is a crucial differentiation as the most impactful
area of emissions does not need to be an area of actual emission reduction potential in
the near term, as current processes of farming, apparel or steel are emitting a certain
amount of emissions. If a company does decide not to do so they risk wasting resources,
resulting mainly in money and time, while LCAs, geographical emission factor (EF)s as
well as sectoral initiatives can support in this manner. For now, most companies are trying
to tackle these value chain emissions by engaging first with the direct (tier 1) supplier and
encouraging them to reduce their direct emissions, with the aim that the tier 1 supplier is
doing the same until the raw material provider is reached. According to BSR [122] this
approach results in three problems: Raw materials themselves won’t be changed (includ-

less on the following and the upstream suppliers may have limited knowledge about emis-

to this approach, the authors propose the model of ”reverse sourcing” [122], where the
raw material providers are targeted in the first place. In order to increase the feasibility
of that long-term supply contracts and partnerships are proposed as well as collaboration

Ten Steps for Creating A Successful Roadmap by Poduval
In his article, Poduval [91] describes a ten-step process for a successful decarbonisation
roadmap. The author starts by considering to start implementing low-hanging fruits from
the beginning, in order not to waste time and get a head start on the decarbonisation jour-

is proposed out of a similar objective to step one, to start decarbonising and not getting
slowed down by details, which might change over time. Poduval [91], therefore, argues
to plan and start as well as come back to the decision and correct the plan to the newest
insights. Step three focuses on the need for decision-making to in or outsource actions,
processes and knowledge. Therefore, it is recommended to partner up with experts in
strategy, technology and regulation. Accordingly, it is described in step four to be aware
of legislation changes such as limitations of existing approaches (e.g. CO2 taxation) and
the encouragement of climate change mitigation efforts (e.g. funding of green technolo-
gies). Step five focuses on the importance of digitisation and how it can enable easy and
fast data consolidation and decision-making. In the next step, Poduval [91] recommends
analysing all currently available technologies (e.g. wind, solar, hydro and geothermal
power generation or electric vehicles) as well as future developing technologies (e.g. am-
monia, hydrogen and bio-fuels) which help the specific company with emission reduction.
Adding onto this, step seven is focusing on gaining needed information like timeline, cost
curve and resilience of the technology to prioritize the identified solutions in step eight to
know which technology to apply in the first place. The latter assessment is further spec-
ified with characteristics like risks, costs, opportunities and scalability of the technology.
Steps nice and ten then first address the importance of adaptability, similar to step two,
before the roadmap and status updates along the way are appropriately communicated
and published to the stakeholder of the company [91].

RQ I: What can a methodology for the successful development of a decarbonisation

In the following chapter, the developed methodology for decarbonisation roadmapping for

study within the research design (section 3.2.3). The limitations of the displayed method-

between companies’ decarbonisation target setting and the implementation of concrete
and meaningful reduction actions. This focus can also be seen in the overview of the
methodology in fig. 5.1. The method focus is accompanied by a pre-study and a follow-
up process, which are essential to describe boundary conditions and an outlook process.

boundary condition of the company that wants to apply the method. This is done in the pre-

a prerequisite to using the methodology, while the emission reduction target can be set at

1. Status quo analysis
2. Countermeasure analysis
3. Strategic roadmapping

The status quo analysis is more precisely illustrated in section 5.2 and distinguishes be-
tween the baseline creation (step 1.1.) and analysis (step 1.2.). In the first part, the
capture of the current situation is described with the creation of a CCF. The results are
analysed for specific insights into the company’s carbon reduction opportunities and lim-
itations. The status quo analysis completed, the countermeasure analysis targets the
identification (step 2.1.) and prioritisation of decarbonisation actions (step 2.2.) as well
as the creation of an overview per measure (step 2.3.). Details of this thematic cluster
can be found in section 5.3. While the identification step describes how the practitioner of
the company can come up with the measures, the prioritisation part focuses on ranking
and excluding certain measures with the help of the analytical hierarchy process. The
countermeasure analysis cluster will be concluded with the creation of a fact sheet for
the highest-ranked action, including the reduction profile creation. Here, the emission re-
duction potential caused by the decarbonisation initiative at which point in time at each
location will be determined. The last thematic cluster, the strategic roadmapping (sec-
tion 5.4), is divided into two steps. The first step, the roadmap creation (step 3.1.) will
use the reduction profile of the highest-ranked option and simulate the implementation on
a timeline diagram after verifying the consequences of the actions. It is then evaluated
if additional decarbonisation measures are needed to reach the decarbonisation target
of the company. This initiates an iterative process between steps 2.3. and 3.1. which
lasts until the strategic decarbonisation target is reached. Once the target is reached the
second step (3.2.) of the strategic roadmapping cluster, communication follows. After
creating a communication strategy with objectives, focused target groups and channels,
the content will be created and shared.
The methodology is concluded with an outlook into implementation planning, the follow-
up process (II.) - described in more detail in section 5.5. Here, it is recommended to
create a detailed implementation plan with responsibilities and processes in line with the
company’s policies (e.g. risk management or financing) as well as a systemic improve-
ment of the carbon management. Further, it is crucial to stress the iterative character of
roadmapping exercises, meaning that the progress should be monitored along the way to
counteract if needed. This can lead to additional decarbonisation measures or updating
the information to given technical innovations.

5.3.1
Identification
In this step, the identification of possible decarbonisation measures is described. It aims to
determine options to reduce the carbon footprint of the company per emission category to
get an idea of how the target might be reached. To do so, step A. describes two pathways
of how the identification can be pursued. Here, a decision between these two pathways
per emission category or source is needed and will be outlined in a detailed fashion.
Step B. sets out how decarbonisation initiatives can be identified, whereas step C. gives
concrete examples of those measures and suggests a way to cluster these.
Figure 5.13: Overview of step 2.1. Identification
A. Pathway decision
Depending on the type and culture of the company (e.g. decision-
making processes and risk aversion) as well as the emission source, a company can dis-
tinguish between an analytical and operational pathway (fig. 5.14). The analytical path-
way symbolises the identification of solutions for an emission source in an elaborate way.
Here, a company creates a long list of possible options to reduce the emission of a certain
activity and will list all the decarbonisation possibilities without constraints. In the second
step (2.2. in section 5.3.2) it is then needed to work on pointing one or a combination of
solutions out to counteract the emission [134, 144, 145, 132, 141]. Contrary to that, the
operational path is characterised by only choosing the best (feasible or the most feasible)
solution to reduce or eliminate the root of the emission. This can be the dominant option
in the market or based on an expert opinion. It is important to frame that this can vary
between the different emission sources, depending on the level of control or the available
solutions to tackle a specific emission etc. [136, 141, 146, 135, 144]. The analytical path
should be considered for companies with a very investigative decision-making culture,
but especially for emission sources with a high level of control and rather a complex and
technical character. This can be for example manufacturing processes like the large-scale
production of energy or heat. The operational pathway on the other hand can be preferred
for emissions with a low level of control and rather low complexity for the company itself.
This is therefore rather for service-based companies or supply-oriented solutions. An ex-
ample can be the reduction of fossil fuel energy use. Options to reduce this emission
source can entail a variety of solutions, depending on the level of control, space and own-
ership. For a service-based company, the switch to a green electricity tariff might be the
best solution. In order not to miss a crucial point a verification with a relevant expert is
recommended. In fig. 5.14 an overview of the characteristics of the two pathways can be
found.

B. Identification of countermeasures
After the best available solution to counteract

Before elaborating on how to come up with these actions it is important to keep the follow-

this method focuses on direct emission reduction measures of a company, which will di-
rectly reduce the emissions of the company. Therefore, this aims to avoid greenwashing
as well as considers carbon removals and offsetting activities only for residual emissions -
after every possible direct reduction action is exhausted [134, 132, 145, 146, 135]. Keep-
ing the limitations of the thesis and this method in mind, choosing these activities will
not be part of the method. Third, while identifying emission reduction actions, especially
for the analytical path, it is important to leave restrictions like cost, feasibility and others

aside, as these characteristics per measure, are evaluated in section 5.3.2. Finally, it is
crucial to consider regional differences in the identification of countermeasures for vari-

boundaries as well as differing local conditions at the specific locations (e.g. space for
machines, requirements for EV charging stations or conditions for a solar photovoltaic
(PV) energy system) need to be considered. [146, 144].
Within this thesis, several ways of identifying countermeasures are identified. They cover
internal and external knowledge of the company and aim to create a broad decarboni-
sation foundation. The different ways are the following (these will be explained in more
detail afterwards):
• Aiming at the practitioner
– Brainstorming
– Desk research
• Aiming to include internal knowledge of the company
– Interviews within the company
– Workshops within the company
– Surveys within the company
• Aiming to include external knowledge of the company
– External interviews with subject matter experts
The first identification area is aimed at the practitioner, who can be one or more employ-
ees of the company or a group of external advisors, and consists of brainstorming and
desk research. Whereas brainstorming is targeting the already existing knowledge of the
practitioners directly, desk research focuses on gaining additional knowledge. As said,
brainstorming mostly aims at already existing knowledge and might include insides about
current improvement projects of the company and if they can be rolled out on a bigger
scale [145, 134, 136, 135, 118]. The identification activity of desk research covers dif-
ferent aspects like best practices, market research and scientific literature. Within best
practices, it could be focused on the activities of the direct competitors [128, 127, 141],
but also on the industry focus [141] and what are related industries doing to reduce their
emissions [120, 136, 141]. The identification of best practices will mostly be concentrated
on web research of peers and associations, but can also be expanded to conferences,
journals and magazines. In a similar fashion general market research should be pursued,
here it can be focused on available alternatives per emission source [128, 141, 136]. The
analysis of scientific research aims mostly at possible reduction measures, that are ex-
pected to arise in the near or medium-term future. This can for example also cover the
discussed literature in chapter 4, where at least half of the authors are proposing some
chosen emission reduction initiatives. Additionally, scientific sources can demonstrate
technology roadmaps when, where and to which extent the technology might be avail-
able [126, 91].
Interviews, workshops and surveys are tools to spot internal expert knowledge and ideas
to decarbonise the company. Surveys can ensure the inclusion of the holistic workforce.
This can increase acceptance and sensitivity across the organisation which is needed
on all levels to achieve drastic and radical change [145, 141]. Contrarily, interviews and
workshops are targeting specific groups or functions in the organisation and should not
Decarbonisation roadmapping methodology development
69
==PAGE:84==
only cover the core working group of this process but moreover all functions which are di-
rectly or indirectly responsible for emission sources [145, 146, 135, 134, 141, 87]. These
formats can identify actions that are already in progress and discover the ones that can
be scaled up. Whereas interviews retrieve expert knowledge in a safe space environ-
ment where the interviewee does not need to be afraid to harm his standing towards any
decision-maker, workshops are more discussion oriented [145, 146, 135]. Furthermore,
workshops can be directly used to discuss several actions and might discover restrictions
and opportunities due to contractual situations and other characteristics.
Another relevant source to uncover decarbonisation initiatives is interviews with subject
matter experts. This should definitely be pursued for complex or technical actions and
can help to discover options and possibilities that would otherwise need lots of research
efforts. External interviews can be used in the analytical path to get a broad overview
of different options, but also in the operational pathway to get to know the best available
technologies for a specific source. Experts can help in different ways and in various areas.
Technical experts can mostly support by pinpointing emission reduction actions including
associated details like timing, costs, feasibility and maturity [136, 91]. Further relevant
expertise to include in the consultation can be of strategic, legal and regional knowledge
[120, 91, 136].
C. Classification of direct reduction actions
When various countermeasures are iden-
tified, it can be recommended to cluster them, which can be of course done in a narrow
point of view matching the emission sources and in a bigger perspective the emission
categories. Another recommendable grouping exercise of the direct emission reduction
measures are the three strategies presented in section 2.1.3: Efficiency, Consistency
and Sufficiency. This can support the stimulation of creativity and connection of different
strategies and to increase the identified measures. Some examples of the inspiration of
countermeasures according to the different strategies can be found in fig. 5.16.
Besides the three strategies, it can be recommended to identify emission reduction activ-
ities in the categories of quick wins and engagement initiatives. Quick wins are described
in step 1.C. and should be listed so that they can be implemented one by one [141, 134].
Engagement activities are focusing on collaborating with partners along the entire value
chain. This can entail, on the one hand, supporting customers to decarbonise their ac-
tivities, for example simplifying the identification of low carbon products with PCF [121].

Prioritisation

successful with a long-term visionary as well as financial backup. The tier 2 decision-

A. Selection of evaluation criteria
In the first step of this thematic cluster, the prioriti-
sation assessment criteria will be selected. This is crucial to be able to evaluate and rank
the identified measures in the next step. Moreover, it is necessary as every company is
different and therefore values different objectives. There is only one criterion that is al-
ways necessary to consider in the evaluation process, which is the impact on the carbon
footprint of the company, called return on environment (ROE) from here onwards [128,
123, 91, 132, 131, 145, 134, 136, 141]. Next to the ROE, an organisation might want to
select some or all of the following criteria, the selection is not limited to the initial selection
and can be seen as exemplary:
• Costs: Considering monetary resources necessary to implement the measure. It is
important to consider the option to include life-cycle costs and carbon prices in the
equation [91, 123, 132, 133, 131, 141, 134, 136].
• Return of investment ROI: This measures the amount of the return of an invest-
ment and is a profitability metric that can be used to distinguish the different mea-
sures from each other [146, 147, 148].
• Maturity and Risk: Assessing the risk of implementing a measure can be impor-
tant to know how big the possibility of negative effects (incl. e.g. technical, legal
and cultural) are [132, 91, 134]. One way to assess the risk can be Technology
Readiness Framework (TRL). Developed by the NASA, the method assesses the
72

==PAGE:87==
maturity and differs between 9 different levels. For detailed information on the TRL,
and the deployment of the assessment, refer to [149, 150, 151].
• Feasibility: When it comes to uncertain endeavours companies can evaluate the
feasibility of possible actions. It helps to include technical challenges in the process
and be aware of if a measure fits to the problem, company’s situation [132, 145,
146, 134]. For example, it is rather feasible for a service-based company without
ownership of their premises to buy green electricity, and rather infeasible to install
renewable energy systems themselves.
• Ease of implementation: This criteria can support to distinguish between complex
and simple project ideas [136, 145, 141].
• Availability: This can be used to identify supply chain issues at a given time to
estimate the lead time of certain projects from selection to implementation [91, 146,
141]
• Level of control: Including the dependency can be of interest if an organisation
does not want to increase or decrease its reliance on others [120, 136].
• Level of stakeholder engagement: As a lot of organisations are transforming their
businesses not purely out of intrinsic motivation and good will, it might be important
to incorporate the impact on stakeholder in the assessment [141, 134, 136].
• Regional differences: If a company wants to harmonise its operations across dif-
ferent locations regional differences might counteract. Therefore, the availability
across the organisational presence might be of relevance [135, 144].
• Opportunity to scale: Similar to the factor before, it might be crucial for companies
to scale up implemented technologies and limit small scale solutions. If that is the
case this criteria can be included [91].
• For supplier changes: Sustainability engagement of supplier: Especially for
organisations with low levels of control over their supply chain and suppliers in par-
ticular, it might be a big lever to improve its scope 3 emissions, favouring suppliers
with a high sustainability engagement. This indicates the suppliers’ carbon footprint
reduction over time. This includes the products of the supplier and can be another
option to include supplier selection in the process [136].
As mentioned in the introduction to this cluster, it is recommended to choose the crite-
ria in a workshop with a variety of stakeholders to limit biases and increase discussions
amongst the stakeholder. It can also be beneficial to set thresholds for each of the chosen
criteria in this workshop to streamline the expectations of the stakeholders regarding the
measures.
B. Evaluation & sequencing of countermeasures
Following the selection of the pre-
ferred evaluation criteria, the next logical step is the evaluation of the different initiatives
according to the criteria. This will lead to a ranked list of prioritised actions. To do so the
Analytical Hierarchy Process (AHP) will be used [131]. AHP is a multi-criteria decision-
making method, which is part of the operations research discipline and was developed
in the 1970s. It is designed to organise and analyse complex decision problems through
weighting of criteria to a goal and assessing the performance of alternatives to the criteria.
This is performed through pairwise comparisons. It helps to structure the complexity of
decision-making processes. The AHP is widely tested and applied also in group decision-
making processes and has various use cases amongst others the choosing, ranking and
Decarbonisation roadmapping methodology development
73
==PAGE:88==
prioritising of alternatives in business environments [152, 153, 154, 155, 156]. AHP can
further be used with diverse criteria, from qualitative or subjective to precisely quantifiable
ones [131, 157]. The proposed AHP to rank decarbonisation initiatives for a company
according to multiple criteria can be explained in the following steps:
1. Defining the system hierarchy
2. Collecting data
3. Weighting the criteria’s importance (pairwise comparison)
4. Weighting the measure’s strength (pairwise comparison)
5. Calculating of final results
6. Sorting of final results
7. Preparing results, excluding measures and repeating the evaluation
Defining the system hierarchy
The first step defines the system hierarchy, which has
three elements goal, criteria (optional with sub-criteria) and alternatives. All elements
are directly connected with all of the elements in the direct level above and beneath.
This means that the goal is directly connected with each criterion and so are all alterna-
tives directly connected to the criteria [152, 153]. A typical system in the decarbonisation
roadmapping process consists of the overall goal to choose the best measures to reach
the carbon reduction targets, the defined criteria which are the result of step 2.2. as well as
the identified carbon reduction initiatives out of step 2.1. The connections and hierarchy
of an exemplary system for decarbonisation roadmapping can be seen in fig. 5.18.
Figure 5.18: AHP hierarchy for decarbonisation roadmapping [Adapted from [158, 131]]
Collecting data
In the second step, all the necessary information according to alterna-
tives and criteria will be collected and evaluated [152]. As mentioned before the whole
step 2.2.B. has an iterative nature and should be supported with workshops. Therefore,
in the first iteration, the information can be rough and increases the level of information
with every repetition. In this manner, the first iteration may entail a qualitative level of

information, whereas the second one may be of quantitative ranges and the third of exact
values (only possible for quantifiable measures, qualitative ones could be investigated in
more detail with every repetition). The specific information (details on ROE, cost but also
opportunities, risks and others) for each measure and each iteration can be gathered in a
variety of ways including experiences, desk research and expert interviews (internal and
external of the company). The decisions on the evaluation for each measure should be
conducted and discussed in workshops, especially for the rather qualitative and intangible
measures.
Weighting the criteria’s importance (pairwise comparison)
The third step aims to
identify the relative importance of the criteria in order to reach the defined goal. This
will be achieved through pairwise comparisons of the criteria and the calculation of the
relative importance with the help of a matrix. The comparison will be performed for each
pair of criteria and the importance to reach the goal will be evaluated. This part of the
AHP is dominantly performed following a nine-level Likert scale from equally important
to extremely more important. The Likert scale is indicated in fig. 5.19. This means, the
stronger the criterion is indicated the higher the importance of reaching the overall goal
of decarbonising the company’s operations [158, 152, 153].
Figure 5.19: Likert scale used for AHP, including reciprocals [Adapted from [152, 153]]
When all the pairwise criteria comparisons are done (three evaluations in the given exam-
ple in fig. 5.18), the comparison matrix can be set up. This matrix has a diagonal of 1 and
the other values can be complemented with values from the Likert scale as well as with
the reciprocals of these values. The reciprocals are used when a criterion is less impor-
tant than another criterion. An exemplary matrix can be seen in fig. 5.20. In this example,
criterion I is decided as marginally stronger (numerical value of 3) when compared with
criterion II, therefore criterion II is scoring with 1/3 compared with criterion A. [152, 153].
Figure 5.20: Indicative criteria weighting [Adapted from [158, 152]]
As a last step of the criteria weighting, the relative importance of the criteria is calculated.
These values are serving as weights and are the key inputs for step five [158, 154, 152].
In order to decide on the importance of the criteria, workshops will be helpful to discuss
and decide together with the key decision makers of a company on the different pairwise
comparisons. This will be crucial to produce profound results from which reliable decisions
can be made.

Weighting the measure’s strength (pairwise comparison)
The fourth step contains
the weighting of the measures, which are the different alternatives that are being decided
on. This step follows the same procedure for all the different measures as step three
for the criteria. Hence, the pairwise comparison between alternatives of the system, the
creation of the comparison matrices and the calculation of the relative strength of the
alternatives are pursued. The comparison will be performed for each pair of measures
in relation to their strength of fulfilling each criterion. This evaluation follows the same
Likert scale, illustrated in fig. 5.19. For the measure comparison this means the stronger
the argument is indicated the higher the probability that the alternative is fulfilling the
overall goal compared to the other measure and the respective criteria [158, 152, 153].
When all the pairwise comparisons are finalised, the comparison matrices will be set up.
This results in one matrix per criteria in which the pairwise scoring of the measures to
this criteria will be displayed. This process will be identical to the one for the criteria.
Therefore, the matrix has a diagonal of 1 and the other values can be complemented
with the values from the Likert scale as well as with the reciprocals of these values. The
reciprocals are used when a measure is less important than another measure. Hence,
when measure A is decided on one of the levels when compared with measure B, then
measure B has the reciprocal value when it is compared with measure A. An exemplary
matrix can be seen in fig. 5.21 [152, 153]. As a last step, for each of the matrices, the
relative strength of the elements will be calculated. These relative strengths are serving
as priorities or weights and are the key inputs for the next step [158, 154, 152]. For the
weighting of the measure’s strength, workshops are less important as the scoring will
follow the collected data of step 2.
Figure 5.21: Indicative weighting of the measure’s strength [Adapted from [158, 152]]
Calculating of final results
In the following step, the final results per measure of the
AHP are calculated. This is done per measure by multiplying the respective values from
steps two and three. Here, per measure, the sum of all weights of the criteria (results of
step 3) multiplied by the rating of the measure per criteria (results of step 4) is created. This
is shown in fig. 5.22, where U, V and W are representing the final results, which are always
between 0.0 and 1.0. These results are weighted results per measure deriving from the
judgement of the company and experts for each measure over the other measures with
respect to the defined criteria. The sum of all the final results is always 1.0. [152, 158].
Sorting of final results
Due to the sum of all the final results to 1, the maximum re-
sult of an alternative in an AHP is theoretically 1 (requires all other alternatives to be 0).
Therefore, the highest numbers of the final results are best. Hence, measures should be
arranged in descending order of their final results (from highest to lowest goal-supporting
measure). This ranking corresponds to the order of the best decarbonisation initiatives
for a company. Therefore, this sequence will be the input for further steps in this method.

Preparing results, excluding measures and repeating the evaluation
As prepara-
tion for further steps, it should be indicated which emission category the measures are
targeting. At the end of each AHP sequence, a workshop should be used to evaluate the
results together with the executive management. Here, a AHP organisation-dependent

thresholds - for a company to consider implementing. The next iteration of the evaluation
should then be continued with a consolidated list of actions, updated and more detailed
information as well as a validation of the comparisons.

Synthesis and future work
This chapter intends to give a concise overview of the results of this study, therefore it is
split into findings, conclusions and future work. In the first part, in section 8.1, the results
of this study are summarised. In the conclusions section, these results are interpreted
and critically reflected. This is detailed in section 8.2. The study as a whole as well as
this section are complemented by the elaboration on potential future work in section 8.3.
8.1
Findings
In the following section, the results of this study are presented. In the first part, the initial
situation and results of testing the hypothesis are described. This is supplemented with the
overall findings of this study, the developed decarbonisation roadmapping methodology.
Here, the research questions are also being addressed.
Initial situation and research
The initial situation of this study was driven by the hy-
pothesis, that there is no current standardised and satisfying decarbonisation roadmap-
ping framework for companies (introduced in chapter 1). Based on an extensive literature
review, the hypothesis was confirmed and a clear research gap was identified, which is
characterised by a lack of sufficient methodology for decarbonisation roadmapping for
companies. Additionally, it showed the need to incorporate the creation of reduction pro-
files for the planned emission reduction initiatives. This led to the concrete demand for
scientific action of creating such a satisfactory method, which resulted in the following
overall research question:
RQ I: What can a methodology for the successful development of a decarbonisation
roadmap for companies look like?
The outcome of the overarching research question is achieved by answering the following
sub-research questions:
• RQ II: What needs to be done in which order to create a strategic carbon reduction
pathway for a company?
• RQ III: Which steps can be helpful to define suitable decarbonisation measures for
a company?
• RQ IV: How can emission reduction initiatives be prioritised and which aspects need
to be considered doing so?
• RQ V: How can reduction profiles be created for emission reduction initiatives?
For further insights into this process and the derivation of the results refer to chapter 4
and section 4.4 respectively.
Corporate decarbonisation roadmapping methodology development
The overall
result of this study will be described on the basis of these research questions. While
the overall research question RQ I and the sub-research question RQ II are answered
throughout the next paragraph, the responses to the sub-research questions RQ III, RQ
IV and RQ V are highlighted respectively.

==PAGE:140==
The key finding of this thesis is a methodology which proposes the decarbonisation roadmap
development in three thematic clusters, that are split into seven steps. An additional pre-
study and a follow-up process complete the method. This method starts with the thematic
cluster of status quo analysis, which is presented in detail in section 5.2. The CCF of a
company is created (section 5.2.1) and analysed (section 5.2.2) during this step. This is
followed by the countermeasure analysis cluster in section 5.3 which entails three steps.
First, the identification step describes in detail how the roadmap practitioner may iden-
tify decarbonisation measures. The specific insights are set out in section 5.3.1 and are
related to RQ III. Consequentially, in section 5.3.2 the prioritisation step is focusing on
ranking these measures and excluding insufficient ones, which targets RQ IV. In the last
step of the countermeasure analysis cluster, RQ V is answered. Here, amongst others,
the reduction profiles per chosen measure are created (section 5.3.3). This leads to the
final thematic cluster, called strategic roadmapping (section 5.4), in which the decarbon-
isation roadmap creation is detailed and a communication strategy is developed. For
further insights refer to section 5.4.1 and section 5.4.2 respectively. Finally, high-level
implementation planning is pursued in the follow-up process (section 5.5).
8.2
Conclusions
This section has the objective of critically reflecting upon the results of this study. Thus,
the first part critically discusses the developed methodology and interprets the outcomes
of chapter 7. Further, the research design of this study is discussed.
Validation of the results
The validation analyses of chapter 7 revealed three main con-
clusions. First, the two identified research gaps are closed. Hence, the methodology can
be classified as satisfying in relation to the content-specific criteria of this study and fur-
ther includes the creation of reduction profiles. The second finding is that the developed
approach is plausible, applicable and entails a logical progress sequence to create a
decarbonisation roadmap. Finally, there is a further need to focus on the utilisation of
resources necessary to perform the method. This is particularly noticeable in two as-
pects. On the one hand, sub-steps may be classified as optional. In that way, the level
of complexity of the method will be partially determined by the practitioner choosing to
include those optional steps. Further, it was identified that the prioritisation process AHP
is highly resource intense and complex. Especially with an increasing amount of activities
and potential countermeasures, the scientific-based AHP limits the manageability of the
method. Hence, the prioritisation process may be further investigated for future optimi-
sation. Some possible improvements were discussed in section 7.2 and will be further
addressed in section 8.3.
Summarising, the developed approach generates the desired results and may be used by
companies to create their decarbonisation roadmap. Hence, it has the potential to guide
companies in developing a clear and well-structured action plan to reach the decarboni-
sation target. Nevertheless, the limitations became apparent in high resource utilisation,
especially when the complexity of the project is rising.
Critical reflection on the research design of the study
The critical reflection of the
scientific process of this study is focusing on three aspects. The first one is related to the
evaluation of existing approaches in chapter 4. The defined and clustered content-related
research criteria may have been weighted. With this distinction of the importance of the
different requirements it would have been possible to not only define the satisfaction rate
but moreover rank the best current available approaches. The second aspect is concern-
ing the number of interviews, for both the knowledge-gaining but also validation interviews

more experts may have been involved. This would have led to a broader opinion basis
and a more solid foundation. The last identified improvement potential of the research
design is focusing on the case study. On the one hand, more case studies may have
been performed, so that the identified improvement aspects were verified and addition-
ally checked upon. On the other hand, a contemporary case study based on a current
event may have led to increased reliability of the results and potential improvements. This
is further addressed in the next section.
8.3
Future work
Based on the findings (summarised in section 8.1), the validation and critical reflection
(chapter 7), as well as the conclusions (section 8.2), a series of recommendations con-
cerning the direction of future work are proposed. These are clustered in five areas and
presented in the following.
Resource reduction utilisation investigation
Previously mentioned in section 8.2 and
chapter 7, the methodology and its entailing steps and sub-steps are very comprehensive
and elaborate. Hence, it was noticed that further differentiation of necessary and op-
tional steps makes sense. In future work, it is essential to investigate the sub-steps of
the methodology to frame more of them as optional. These steps are increasing insights
or unleashing a broader potential, but may not be essential to define the decarbonisa-
tion roadmap or are redundant due to certain decisions along the process. Further, the
AHP prioritisation scheme with its pairwise comparisons is getting highly complex with
an increased amount of evaluation criteria and countermeasures. Hence, the number of
pairwise comparisons becomes difficult to perform in a quantitative matter as well as to
maintain the consistency between the comparisons. Therefore, future work requires in-
vestigating a differentiated prioritisation scheme. As proposed in section 7.2, there are
different options such as using an AHP software to ensure consistency, reducing the num-
ber of measures before performing the AHP or shifting the scientific-based AHP to a more
pragmatic way. This may be for example a scoring system that is further detailed in sec-
tion 7.2. Moreover, the prioritisation options are not bound to the proposed concept of
AHP and may be tackled by a broader variety of sequencing processes.
Intensification of methodology validation
As a consequence of the validation analy-
ses in chapter 7 and the interpretations of the results in section 8.2, some analyses may be
intensified to include new information or increase the certainty of the results. First, further
case studies may be performed to validate the findings of the first case study. This may
also include a contemporary case study, based on a real-life event. Second, the formal
requirements of manageability and low effort need further analysis to conclude the judge-
ment on criteria fulfilment. Finally, the CSRD ESRS E1 requirement of section 7.1.3, that
a qualitative study of potential embodied carbon from the company’s key products and
investments are included in the decarbonisation roadmap may be further investigated.
This is to be done especially when the EU is publishing an update with increased de-
tails regarding this requirement. The here-mentioned validation analyses may optimally
be performed after the improvements resulting from the realisation of the previous para-
graph are already incorporated in the decarbonisation roadmapping methodology.
Creation of additional supporting material
The third direction of future work is the
creation of additional supporting material. As mentioned in section 7.3, this entails on
the one hand increased guidance per step as well as specific supporting material of the
methodology in general. The latter is aiming to develop additional tools and templates
Decarbonisation roadmapping methodology development
127
==PAGE:142==
per step to simplify the application. This may also lead to platform development, where
the roadmap practitioner is educated to perform the steps with do-it-yourself templates.
This may enable a semi-automated process for the mass creation of decarbonisation
roadmaps for various companies. In addition, this has the potential to be supported by
sector-specific guidance that proposes specific decarbonisation measures for certain lo-
cations and emission categories. This may entail the potential ROE of the measure, which
can then be transferred to a company-specific reduction profile. The increased guidance
per step may lead to an increased level of knowledge in terms of the output per step, but
also the resources needed like employees or time and may further entail a timeline of
proposed workshops along the process of the method.
Adjustments to the methodology
Regarding adjustments to the methodology, the pre-
study may be augmented by the recommendation of an agreement between the project
commissioner and the roadmap practitioner on the level of required details and accu-
racy. Further, the methodology may be complemented with the incorporation of company
growth and uncertainty in the roadmap. Moreover, scope 3 focus and guidance may be
more stressed in the presentation of the methodology. These mentioned future aspects
of work are related to the outcomes of the validation interviews, elaborated on in more
detail in section 7.3. In addition, one of the peer discussions [144] (documented in ap-
pendix F.3) resulted in the recommendation to use the developed approach to create a
flexible method, that queries the practitioner if certain steps are done and will then refer
to the relevant and logical next step. For example, it may prompt the practitioner: Is a
decarbonisation target set? If yes, go to baseline creation; if not, set a target; or is a holis-
tic baseline created? If yes refer to baseline analysis; if not, go to baseline analysis. In
connection to this, it will be crucial to investigate the differences in the process when the
target is not set in the pre-study but along the process, as mentioned in the introduction to
chapter 5. A last point of the potential adjustments is to include more detailed guidance on
the grouping of sites and locations of companies as a basis for counteracting the impact
of the organisations.
Adaptation and extension of the methodology
The last area of potential future work
is targeting the potential expansion of the methodology. This covers the elaboration of
details within the follow-up process, focusing on the implementation plan as well as the
performance monitoring. Another aspect may be the adaptation of the method to other
environmental sustainability areas like water use or biodiversity or the holistic consider-
ation of environmental sustainability. Moreover, it may also be investigated to include
social sustainability within the process. Another methodology adaptation aspect may be
the adjustment to the product level.
