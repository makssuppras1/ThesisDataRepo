Abstract
Safety is a prime concern in operating high-speed machinery.
However
with the digitalisation of the industry and the rise of automation, new
tools are emerging to ensure better security and eﬃciency.
The use of
an active-tilting-pad journal bearing (ATPJB) is investigated as a way to
reduce rotor vibrations through the well-timed injection of high-pressure
oil through the bearings pads (active lubrication). To do so, a state-space
model of an existing rotor-foundation-ATPJB test-rig is further developed
and validated against experimental results from 1000rpm to 6500rpm for
both passive and active lubrication.
The state-space model is based on
the work of (1) but extended, by better modelling the shaft, the discs,
the ATPJB the foundation as well as the servovalves. Using the feedback
provided by the displacement sensors on the shaft, a Simulink LQG control
loop of the oil injection is designed to control the opening of the servovalves
for the high-pressure ﬂow.
Results show strong eﬀectiveness in reducing oscillations over a wide range
of speeds, with shaft vibration amplitudes being reduced by up to (70%)
at low velocity. Pump failure prevented the testing of the control loop at
higher-operating speeds. Nevertheless accounting for a good model ﬁt from
[1000rpm-6500rpm] and eﬀective damping at lower speeds, it is believed the
control loop would be able to dampen vibrations at higher speeds, more
particularly in damping the conical modes which becomes predominant
above 4000rpm.
The improvements and new experimental results developed here solve the pre-
vious discrepancies between experimental results and theoretical state-space
model at higher operating speeds encountered in (2)(1). By highlighting and
ﬁtting the transition from a 2 pad conﬁguration with resting upper pads to a
4 active pad TPJB at 3000rpm as speed increases a more reliable model was
developed. Eﬀort should be put in developing a better TPJB model, that
doesn’t need manual ﬁtting to represent the transition.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Safety is an ever-growing concern in the conception and operation of high-speed ma-
chinery, present in a great number of various industries. However such mechanisms often
bear multiple components functioning at high temperature and high velocity. This poses
a paramount risk to human and material capital that must be monitored and carefully
accounted for so as to avoid potential disastrous consequences.
However the growth of the 4.0 industry has challenged current technological limits
by profoundly changing the way engineers approach machinery. Introducing digital tools,
intelligent technology and automation into machinery allows engineers to both minimise
risks but also improve eﬃciency, lifespan, reliability or ergonomy.
These technological developments bear promising applications for high-speed rotat-
ing machinery, and must be further investigated and developed. By coupling actuators
and sensors as well as new digital tools it is feasible to reduce the vibrations of a shaft.
raising the bar for safety standards deﬁned by current limits set on turbo-machinery.
At the heart of these innovation lies the active tilting pad journal bearing (ATPJB).
Oil-based bearings are based on a thin ﬁlm of oil pressurised between two solids. This
thin ﬁlm can be sustained by the rotation of the journal, or from an external supply of oil.
Tilting-pad journal bearings are oil based bearings comprised of multiple pads wrapping
around the shaft. Their angle is adjusted depending on the load and journal position. Due
to the design of such bearings, they oﬀer unique advantages such as vibration damping,
improved stability or better load distribution.
The ATPJB is a tilting pad journal bearing that was altered by drilling one or multiple
holes in the pads. High-pressure oil can then be injected through such holes to alter the
pressure of the pad’s oil ﬁlm, and therefore act on the shaft.
By implementing such an actuator (ATPJB) and proximity sensors (induction sensors
for instance) on a rotating machinery, it is possible to create a self-monitoring and self-
regulating system. Creating a feedback loop between actuator and sensors, allows great
improvements in safety by the minimising of the shaft oscillations.
Such a system could be well suited for particularly risk-averse applications such as
the an overhung centrifugal compressors of a gas turbine. It would be a great tool when
switching rotational speeds and having rotational velocities coeinciding with modes.

1.2. Previous work
1.2
Previous work
To this day, extensive work has been carried out on the ATPJB, on both a theoretical
and experimental standpoint.
Here key contributions are summarised to identify the
diverse advances in the ﬁeld, as well as identify key points to further investigate.
In 1886 O. Reynolds derived the Reynolds equation from the Navier-Stokes equations
by studying ﬂuid ﬂows within the boundary of ﬂuid thin ﬁlms. This equation is therefore
fundamental to the function of oil bearings which rely on a pressure ﬁeld generated by a
thin-ﬁlm of oil.
In 1998, Santos and Russo (8) included an injection term to the Reynolds equation
to obtain the Modiﬁed Reynolds Equation (MRE). This alteration modelled how injecting
high-pressure oil through a hole in a pad of a TPJB could alter the pressure distribution,
and change the properties of the bearing. Further work was carried out to model the servo-
valves as second order low-pass ﬁlters. From this initial body of work, many contributions
have improved and reﬁned the model of the ATPJB.
Notably, Kristiansen (7) aimed to determine the optimal injection hole geometry to
improve the damping of the rotor-foundation-TPJB system. It was concluded that 4 holes
with a common diameter of 4mm placed symmetrically at the pad center provided the
best results.
Eﬀort on modelling the pivot stiﬀness of the pads was carried out by Mønsted (9).
Radial and tangential motion of the pivot was added to the code. Moreover, the pivot stiﬀ-
ness was modelled using Hertz contact theory. Results underlined the signiﬁcant inﬂuence
of the pivot stiﬀness on the system model.
Salazar and Santos (10) experimentally characterised the injection of oil on the system
dynamics. It was found out that constant injection improves the system’s response com-
pared to passive condition. Moreover shaft vibration damping was most signiﬁcant when
oil was from the bottom pads. A PID control as well as a model based Linear-Quadratic-
Gaussian Controller (LQG) were then developed in (11) to model the high-pressure oil
feedback control. Signiﬁcant system damping was achieved for both controllers, however
the LQG controller failed to damp all frequencies, this was attributed to unmodelled
dynamics of the foundation.
To solve this, Rasmussen(12) aimed at developing a ﬁnite-element model of the foun-
dation using plate elements. Unfortunately, the model had important discrepancies with
the experimental data.
Another approach to modelling the foundation was developed by Gani(13). The plate
elements were switched for 3D elements using the commercial software ANSYS. However,
foundation model size greatly increased, need a redution method.
This new approach
provided a signiﬁcant improvement to model, closely matching experimental data.
The foundation model was further improved by Jensen (2) who used non-physical
values. Using the existing model, an LQG controller was implemented on the test-rig.
Signiﬁcant damping was achieved at lower speeds, however at higher-speeds the model
became uncorrelated to the experimental results.
To solve this, Rasmussen (5) further improved the ATPJB model by including the
inﬂuence of temperature on the oil thin-ﬁlm for both adiabatic and non-adiabatic cases.
A new model was developed including the temperature dependency of the oil viscosity as
well as the thermal dilation of the pads. However, these implementations came short of
explaining the discrepancies between experimental and theoretical results.
A revisition of the foundation model was done by (? ). A previously unseen groove
under the foundation was added into the model. Moreover the various pedestals were
included into the foundation model using 3D elements and solid-solid stiﬀeners to join

without needing non-physical values. The rotor misalignment was also added to the AT-

Active Tilting-Pad Journal Bearing (ATPJB)
The ATPJB P is the centrepiece of the system, In fact, this bearing is an extension
of common tilting-pad journal bearing (TPJB). Such bearings use multiple pads facing the
shaft where in between a thin ﬁlm of lubricant is sustained via the rotation of the journal
in what is refereed to as hydrodynamic lubrication. The angle of the pads can adapt on
the journal position, which can vary depending on load and speed. This setup displays
remarkable stability, thanks in part to the damping brought by the oil.
The speciﬁcity of the ATPJB is that the pads posses one or more holes in which
high-pressure oil can be injected. This high-pressure oil is then susceptible to inﬂuence
the local pressure ﬁeld, and therefore the journal’s motion.
For the given tilting-journal bearing here, it is designed as a 4 pad, LBP (Load
between pad), tilting pad bearing. The pad have a radius of Rs = 50mm, a length of
w = 100mm. The aperture angle (β) corresponds to the angle that the curvature of the
pad forms. The high-pressure injection holes bear a diameter d0 = 0.25 and a length
l0 = 0.25mm.

2.2. Hydraulic system review
2.2
Hydraulic system review
2.2.1
Overview
The hydraulic part of the testing facility is a multi-component system designed to
meet all of the ATPJB’s lubricating needs as well as its ﬂuid power requirements. Key
components are highlighted and identiﬁed in Fig.2.7.
One part of the setup, referenced as the low-pressure circuit (LP circuit) guarantees
a continuous ﬂow of oil to the Active-tilting-Pad-Journal Bearing (ATJPB). This ensures
the bearings’ ability to sustain hydrodynamic lubrication by keeping a thin ﬁlm of lubri-
cant in between the journal and the pads. The high pressure circuit (HP-circuit) provides
the high-pressure oil needed to inject through the holes in the bearing pads. The aim is
to alter the pressure ﬁeld and inﬂuence the bearing dynamics.
Both circuits share the same oil tank P , and the returning oil is cooled using the
fan of the motor driving the high-pressure pump I . The oil used is the Mobil DTE 22
hydraulic oil provided by ExxonMobil®.
Figure 2.7: Representation of the hydraulic system.

==PAGE:26==
2.2. Hydraulic system review
2.2.2
Passive lubrication
For the ATPJB to function, a ﬂuid-ﬁlm must be maintained between the rotor and
the pads. This ﬂuid ﬁlm is maintained due to the rotation of the shaft itself, hence this
form of lubrication being referred to as ”hydrodynamic lubrication”.
To get the oil from the oil tank P to the ATPJB, it is ﬁrst pumped out by the LP
pump L , driven by the LP motor K . Depending on the ball valve position A , ﬂow
can be directed to the TPJB J after ﬂowing through a ﬁlter and manometer, or else be
redirected to the oil tank.
Oil is pumped backed thanks to the LP return pump N , driven by the same motor
as the inlet pump. Oil is then cooled down using the HP motor fan H and its heat
exchanger I .
The low-pressure oil circuit is monitored using two temperature sensors: one for the
ﬂow of low-pressure oil and one for the return oil, the latter being shared by the active
lubrication circuit . A ﬂow and pressure sensor is also used to monitor the ﬂow returning
oil.
2.2.3
Active lubrication
The goal of the active lubrication is to inﬂuence the rotor dynamics, this is done
through injection of high-pressure oil through 1mm holes at the center of the pads. The
oil is pressurised using a high-pressure pump K and a pressure release valve E . Oil
pressure can be set between 10bar and 100bar using the pressure release valve. Similarly
to the low-pressure circuit, a ﬁlter O before the valve prevents particles from damaging
the system, particularly the injectors and servovalves.
A temperature sensor attached to the circuit ensures an accurate monitoring of the
high-pressure circuit and ensures safety of the system.
This hydraulic circuit is optional to the running of the test-rig, whereas the running
of the low-pressure circuit is essential, since it prevents solid-solid contact between rotor
and the bearing pads by ﬂooding the bearing.

2.4.2
Run-out compensation
The surface of the shaft isn’t perfectly smooth. This roughness introduces errors
when monitoring the displacement of the rotor : displacement sensors may register the
surface roughness as the actual displacement of the rotor. Moreover, if the shaft is prone
to misalignment (its not perfectly aligned, with its axis of rotation) the shaft will periodi-
cally move towards, and then backwards to the sensor, inﬂuencing the measurements.
To counter this issue, run-out compensation is performed on all 10 displacement sen-
sors. This method relies on measuring the distance from each sensor to the rotating shaft
with no excitation from the shaker. The results are then averaged on a single 360° cycle
to obtain a function of the surface roughness to be used for each sensor. These ﬂuctua-
tions are then subtracted to real-time measurements to obtain the true displacement of
the rotor. An analog method is used on the force signal.
Here run-out compensation is carried-out at a sampling frequency of 3k Hz, for 120s.
It is carried-out regularly, and redone each time motor speed is changed or the temperature
has changed signiﬁcantly.

2.4. Instrumentation and data acquisition
2.4.4
Data acquisition & data processing
Further insights into data acquisition and processing are required to obtain a high-
quality result. Aliasing issues appear when the sampling rate isn’t insuﬃcient to capture
high-frequency phenomenons. During sampling, such high-frequency components appear
as low frequency ones. To solve this issue, the sampling rate should be at least double
the frequency of the highest-frequency phenomenon, according to the Nyquist-Shannon
sampling theorem. Another solution is to use a low-pass ﬁlter on the signal before the
sampling, to remove unwanted high-frequency components. The position sensors do not
have such ﬁlter expect sensor 3. However the signal conditioner of the force transducer
has a 1k Hz low-pass ﬁlter (lowest setting possible).
Spectral leakage occurs when carrying out a Fourier transformation on a non-periodic
signal. In fact, the experimental signals are intrinsically non periodic since they are sam-
pled over a ﬁnite amount of time. The phase of the signal at the start of the recording
might not match the phase at the end of the recording. The Fourier transformations as-
sumes the signal is periodic, therefore any phase mismatch would be read a sharp transient,
leading to erroneous frequencies.
To solve this issue window functions are used to render the signal periodic. Such
function have resemble the geometry of a bell, starting at 0, increasing to 1 and then
decreasing to 0. If a signal is multiplied onto such a function, it will appear periodic (since
the start matches the end, being 0). In practice, it is common to split the signal into
multiple blocks, and then apply the window function to each. The window chosen here is
a Hamming window, which corresponds to a cosine function.
Each block can then be Fourier transformed and averaged accross all over blocks.
Further noise reduction can be obtained by overlapping the blocks. This is called Welch’s
method. A 50% overlap is commonly used, and is the setting used in the data transforma-
tion.

3.2. Flexible Rotor
3.2
Flexible Rotor
In this section, a Matlab FEM model of the shaft is developed to obtain its mass,
damping, stiﬀness as well as gyroscopic matrices according to translation and rotation
DOF. Having previously determined the physical properties of the discs, comparison to
EMA results (6)(4) obtained without discs, with 1 disc and with 2 discs will be conducted.
Compared to the model in (1)(2), results are compared to more accurate EMA results
and the model includes modal damping, as well as various model corrections. Given that
the shaft contributes greatly to the system dynamics, a particular attention is given to
develop a highly accurate model with minimum nodes, which was achieved here.
Figure 3.5: Illustration of the ﬂexible shaft. The shaft has been stripped of all attach-
ments (bearings, discs and locks). Units are in minimeters.
3.2.1
Mathematical Model
Prior to develop a model of the shaft, several assumptions are to be made.
© The shaft is considered a homogeneous, isotropic solid
© The amplitude of shaft vibrations are minor compared to the shaft length.
This is experimentally conﬁrmed as all amplitudes fall under 100µ m.
© The geometry of the rotor is assumed radially symmetrical
Local absence of mass in the form of threaded holes and key seat are reverberated on the
geometry of the local element, by reducing its radius so as to preserve the mass of the
element.
© All shaft displacement is purely transversal
Both shaft longitudinal and torsion motions are omitted : there are no twisting moments
or longitudinal loads applied on the shaft when it is static or spinning at constant speed.
© The rotor speed (Ω)is constant
The rotor speed is guaranteed within a 1% error margin from the PI controller developed
in section.
The following theory is an extension of what was carried out in (? )(2) and applies
Euler-Bernoulli beam theory on a ﬁnite-element model to explicit the mass, damping, stiﬀ-
ness and gyroscopic matrix of the shaft according to well deﬁned generalised coordinates.

The shaft is ﬁrst discretised into 26 ﬁnite elements, and between each element a node
is placed as shown in Fig.3.6. The sectioning of the shaft is carried out to accommodate
the various sections of diﬀerent radius of the rotor, as well as the position of the sensors,
the bearings and the discs. This undoubtedly leads to varying element sizes. The optimal
discretisation was ﬁrst carried out by (2), who found the meshing to provide accurate
enough results for the ﬁrst 3 modes, with errors under 3%.

the Young Modulus of the foundation to 60GPa, a value under was is to be expected for
grey cast iron.

dation was noticed, reducing the foundation thickness by cm.

node connectivity. The assembly and coupling of the components was instead carried out

3.4. Pad pivot
3.4
Pad pivot
The manner in which the bearings couple the foundation to the rotor has a paramount
inﬂuence on the system dynamic response. By adjusting the stiﬀness and damping of such
coupling links, the modes and FRF can be greatly inﬂuenced.
In an analogue manner, the pivot liaison which links the pad to the foundation has
strong inﬂuence on the response of the model. Here an eﬀort is made to better represent
the stiﬀness of that liaison by making it load dependant : the greater a pad is pushed back
into its casing, the greater its stiﬀness will be.
3.4.1
Model Development
The theory developed here is based largely on (15). The aim is to establish a rela-
tionship between the load applied on a pad of the TPJB and the deformation of the pivot
liaison. This will provide an estimate of the of the pivot stiﬀness. The stress-deformation
theory is based on Hertz stress theory.

3.7. ATPJB Model
3.7
ATPJB Model
The rotor is coupled to the foundation through a self-aligning rolling-element bearing
and a tilting-pad journal bearing. The latter bearing bears the majority and of the load of
the shaft and its attachment. An accurate modelling of its behaviour is therefore crucial
to the validity of the system model.
The model is ﬁrst deﬁned by expliciting the ﬂuid ﬁlm height, according to the many
geometric parameters of the bearing. By knowning the thickness of the ﬂuid ﬁlm, it is
possible to evaluate the pressure proﬁles present at each of the 4 pads. From these pressure
proﬁles, it is possible to determine the forces and moments that are created, and hence it is
possible to determine the stiﬀness and damping coeﬃcients of the bearing using Allaire’s
method. These coeﬃcents may then be assembled into a dynamic mass, damping and
stiﬀness matrix that may be implemented into the system model.
3.7.1
Mathematical Model
To develop an accurate and reliable model, it is necessary to rely on practical and
realistic assumptions. Hereby are listed the key hypotheses on which the model will be
developed.
© The ﬂuid is Newtonian and incompressible
The hydraulic oil used here lies well within the deﬁnition of a Newtonian ﬂuid, moreover
the oil is considered incompressible over the range of pressures investigated here (¡30bar).
© The oil ﬂow is laminar (Re < 1000).
This was proven in the previous section, with the maximum Reynolds number achievable
to be around 800, at 7000rpm
© The inﬂuence of inertial forces on the oil ﬂow is negligible.
Inertial forces are proportional to the mass on which it is applied, which in the case of
the ﬂuid ﬁlm is extremely small.
© Pressure gradients in the radial direction are neglected, ∂p
∂x = 0.
This is a direct consequence of the Navier-Stokes equation, and is valid when studying
a thin ﬂows.
© The oil ﬁlm is much smaller than the journal radius
This hypothesis is guaranteed as Cp
R = 5−3 << 1.
Process description
To obtain the dynamic matrices of the TPJB, multiple steps are required depending
on the assumptions carried out. The process is an iterative one, where a guess is ﬁrst made
on the TPJB equilibrium position xATPJB = {xJ, yJ, βJ, ΓJ, α1, ..., α4, ξ1, ..., ξ4, η1, ..., η4, qv,1, qv,2}.
From that guess, the MRE may be solved, and the pressure ﬁeld can be integrated to de-
termine the forces acting on the bearing, if they match the external resultant (weight and
moment of the shaft), then this iteration is concluded, or else, a new guess for the ATPJB
equilibrium is proposed. Extensive work was carried out on the thermal eﬀects inﬂuencing
the properties of the ATPJB by (12). From a 2D ﬂow, the ﬂuid ﬁlm was modelled as a
3D ﬂow, moreover the isothermal oil assumption was lifted. Heat can ever be completely
dissipated by the ﬂuid ﬂow (adiabatic conditions) or also be evacuated from the pad. This
thermal load on the pad is susceptible to deform it, and therefore inﬂuence the ﬂuid ﬁlm
shape in turn. All the previously mentioned steps are detailed in Fig.3.21.

TPJB 2-4 pad Behaviour Transition
It is observed while trying to ﬁt the model for the passive lubrication over the range
of speed [1000rpm-7000rpm] is rather diﬃcult. In fact noticeable characteristics transpire
at low and high speed, that are unable to be conciliated into a single model. Essentially
the model can either be ﬁtted for higher speeds, using a high preload mp = 0.61 and
no changes to the nominal clearance, or can be adapted to lower speeds by reducing the
nominal clearance by 60µm and lowering the preload to mp = 0.20.
It is observed that around 3000rpm the upper pads of the TPJB engage, as they were
previously resting and didn’t develop any downward load. It is likely the modelling of the
TPJB is accurate when all 4 pads are engaged, when only the two lower ones are, model
corrections are necessary.
Interestingly, no model correction is necessary when considering active lubrication.
The initial ﬁt stays consistant up to 6000rpm. This is likely because the injection of high-
pressure ﬂow from the lower pads generates upwards force without the need to develop an
important ﬂuid ﬁlm and raising the journal.

To understand the evolutions presented on the diagram, is it important to remember
that as the rotor velocity increases, the stiﬀness of the ATPJB increases, and its damping
decreases. In other words, both foundation and rotor become more coupled.
It can be observed that the modes associated with the foundation are fairly indepen-
dant of the rotor velocity. An exception can be made for the second torsion mode which
seems most impacted by the coupling of the shaft.
4.6.2
Critical speeds
The black line in Fig.4.6 represents points where the excitation frequency matches
the frequency of the rotor revolution. Speeds at which modes intersect the black line are
called critical Speeds. At such speed the system experiences important vibrations due to
that mode. This is due to natural imbalances in the shaft that generate excitation forces
at that shaft velocity frequency (Ω).
From Fig.4.6, it can be observed that the system presents multiple critical speeds are
present within the range [0rpm;7000rpm]. It should however be noted that they do not
all bear the same danger. In fact critical speeds associated with highly undamped modes
are particularly dangerous.
The shaft crosses many modes of the foundation, without ever causing too much of a
disturbance. The rotor conical modes seems to appear near 2000rpm, this is likely because
they are over-damped, and therefore do not oscillate before that. Oscillations appear as
the system gets stiﬀer thanks to the ATPJB becoming stiﬀer itself.
One major concern is the ﬁrst horizontal rotor bending mode, which seems to form a
critical speed near 9000-1000rpm, above the range studied here. This is dangerous as this
mode is highly undamped, and may endanger the system’s integrity.
Designing a powerful control loop to dampen these critical speeds is therefore essential
in ensuring run-up and run-down of the system. This will be investigated in next chapter.

5.5. Observability & Controllability
5.5
Observability & Controllability
Depending on the position of the sensors and actuators, a given mode can be, to a
varying degree, observed or acted on. To understand this, it is important to understand
that mode-shapes correspond to well-deﬁned geometrical deformations of a given structure.
At one or more point of the geometrical deformation, some points may be unimpaired :
these refer to nodes: points that remain undisturbed in a given mode. For a given mode,
if the point in front of a sensor is a node, then that mode will not be observable, similarly,
if a node is present at an actuator, then the actuator will not be able to act on that given
mode.
This notion of observability and controllability is crucial when developing a controller.
For the controller to be able to dampen a given mode, it must be able to observe the mode,
as well as act on it.

reason that the disc model was reﬁned.
The foundation model was updated owing to recent EMA investigations carried out
by (4). Removing the rotor from the EMA provided better modal parameters to model
the system with.
Most of the improvement carried out here were on the servovalve and the ATPJB.
The dynamic behaviour of the servovalves was poorly modelled, and the corrections pre-
sented here allow for the development of a control loop. Moreover, many previous projects
struggled to model the test-rig behaviour under 3000rpm. Here the origin of this issue was
likely pinpointed to the transition between 2-LBP tilting pad bearing to a 4 pad tilting
pad bearing as the speed increases. The model used here functioned as intended for 4 pad
conﬁgurations, but must be adapted when only two pads of the bearing are engaged.
A controller using 4 inputs and 2 outputs was designed and implemented on the
test-rig. The controller showed displayed good damping abilities, however a pump failure
prevented the running of the shaft at higher speed. Given the accuracy of the model, and
the success of damping at lower speeds, it is believed the given controller here can dampen
said system at high speed. But it remains yet to be proven.
7.0.1
Future work
Investigations should be further carried out concerning the ATPJB, more precisely,
the transition between a two-pad TPJB to a four-pad TPJB should be further delved into.
One idea would consist in completely removing the two upper pads from the model and
see if the model a 2 pad (LBP) TPJB would more accuretely model a 4 pad (LBP) TPJB
with disengaged upper pads.
Moreover, the controller proposed here should be tested out at high-operational
speeds.
This could prove valuable as a critical speed is predicted near 11krpm.
It is
the belief that components such as the rotor, the foundation and the discs need not be
improved upon further, as the limitations encountered here were from other components.
At last the investigations carried out here were mostly interested in damping hori-
zontal disturbances. Even if the model seems to predict with respectable precision the
vertical dynamics, further work is required. This could then be combined with the the
work carried out on the horizontal control, to develop a control system able to dampen
vibrations at any angle.
