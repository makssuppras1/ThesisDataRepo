Abstract
Graphene patterning is regarded as one of the areas of interest due to its necessity for
applications. Owing to the recent developments in large area production of CVD grown
graphene, it has now become more accessible to study the emergent nanoscale properties
of graphene in larger scales. However, transferring such nanoscale properties to large
scales by using conventional graphene patterning methods has remained a challenge. The
current graphene micro-patterning techniques, including electron beam lithography (EBL),
optical lithography, and nanoimprint lithography are time consuming and scale with sample
size. In such methods, graphene is patterned in a plasma environment through dry etching
reactions with gases like O2. These reactions lead to rough edges and no preferred crystal
alignment in patterned graphene, which prevent a systematic approach in understanding some
of graphene’s mechanical behaviors like tearing. Consequently, there is a need to provide a
reliable method for largescale graphene micro-patterning, which will further constitute a way
for studying some of graphene’s properties such as tearing and cracking.
This MS.c thesis presents hot punching as a novel method for largescale micro-patterning
of CVD graphene. CVD grown graphene sheets are prepared for hot punching by being
transferred from the Cu foil to 30 µm thick PVA layers. A nickel stamp with micro-structures
is used to replicate 300 µm micro-patterns in graphene-PVA stacks by applying pressure and
temperature. A set of experiments are designed to optimize the hot punching process. Hot
punched samples are characterized by using optical profilometry and optical microscopy. It
is shown that by optimizing relevant parameters, large-area CVD graphene micro-patterning
with hot punching is possible.
Micro- and nanostructures of micro-patterned graphene sheets are characterized by using
Raman spectroscopy, AFM, and TEM techniques. The Raman characterization results show
that as a consequence of hot punching with optimized conditions, highest strain is applied in
graphene sheets, which in turn has increased its charge carrier density. AFM scans show the
optimized hot punching conditions, lead to formation of graphene ribbons with an average
width of 0.70±0.21 µm, parallel to the edges of the punched areas. TEM results confirm that
the formed ribbons show novel properties such as clean and disorder-free edges, makeing
them an ideal candidate for electronic devices fabrication.

Chapter 1
Introduction
G
raphene, a flat honeycomb monolayer arrangement of sp2 bonded carbon atoms
[1], was first theoretically explored in 1947 by P. R. Wallace as a starting point
for understanding the electronic properties of 3D graphite [2]. Before its isolation and
characterization in 2004 by Andre Geim and Konstantin Novoselov [3], it was believed that
graphene does not exist in the free state, and is unstable with respect to the formation of
curved structures such as fullerenes and nanotubes. Later it was demonstrated that from
the topological point of view, graphene can be considered to be the basic unit for carbon
materials of all other dimensions, such as 0D buckyballs, 1D nanotube, and 3D graphite [4].
In their groundbreaking study in 2004, which later led them to win the 2010 Nobel
Prize in Physics, Geim and Novoselov were able to successfully extract single-atom-thick
crystallities from bulk graphite, and characterize its field electric properties. Graphene layers
were pulled from graphite and transferred onto thin SiO2 on a silicon wafer in a process
known as micromechanical exfoliation (or the Scotch tape method) [3]. Their breakthrough
in the discovery of graphene electrical properties, rapidly kindled the motivation within the
research community, and increased the number of scientific articles related to graphene from
161 in 2004 to more than 30000 by the end of 2018 (figure 1.2). This perpetually growing
research in graphene is indeed owed to its superior properties such as mechanical stiffness,
strength and elasticity, very high electrical and thermal conductivity, optical transparency,
impermeability, and many others [4][5].

1.1 Graphene Properties and Applications
3
1.1
Graphene Properties and Applications
A truly 2D material, graphene is highly flexible, and is known to be the thinnest material
while being the strongest one as well, with a Young’s modulus of 1.02 TPa and intrinsic
tensile strength of 130 GPa [6]. For instance, the strongest steel has a Young’s modulus
of 200 GPa. This extraordinary mechanical strength and stability is due to the sp2 orbital
hybridization between each of the carbon atoms. Sp2 is a combination of s, px, and py orbitals
that form the σ bond [5][7].
Often referred as the Wonder Material, graphene exhibits unusual electronic excitations
and is an interesting mix of semiconductor and metal, with the electrons having a very
long mean free paths [8]. For instance, it was found that few layers graphene flakes, that
were produced by mechanical exfoliation on Si/SiO2 wafers, have electron mobilities of
15,000 cm2V−1s−1, independent of temperature T between 10 and 100 K [9]. Further
measurements had revealed that hexagonal Boron Nitride (hBN) encapsulated graphene, has
electron mobility exceeding 100,000 cm2V−1s−1 at room temperature [10]. In a study by
Moser et al. in 2007, it was reported that graphene is able to endure extremely high densities
of electric current up to 109 A·cm−2. For a comparison, copper can sustain 103 A·cm−2 [11].
Regarding the thermal properties, graphene is known to have a thermal conductivity
in the range of 4,800-5,300 W·m−1 ·K−1, based on a measurement on suspended single-
layer graphene by Balandin et al. in 2008 [12]. This thermal conductivity of graphene, for
instance, is higher compared to that of copper (385 W·m−1 ·K−1). Even though later studies
reported that thermal conductivity of graphene with different lengths ranges between 1,500
to 2,500 W·m−1 ·K−1 [13], graphene is still considered to be an excellent thermal conductor.
Moreover, theoretical calculations have estimated the melting temperature of 4150 K for
graphene [14][15].
A perfect single layer of graphene is also found to be highly impermeable to all gases, due
to π orbitals forming a dense non-localized cloud. This dense non-localized cloud blocks the
the holes in aromatic rings, and prevents molecules, even the smallest ones such as Helium
or Hydrogen, from passing through graphene [16][17].
Despite being one atom layer thick, graphene monolayer shows unusually high absorption,
causing a linear transmittance drop of more than 2 %. Studies by Nair et al. (2008) and Zhu
et al. (2014) reported that in a few-layer graphene, each single layer adds 2.3 % to the light
absorption [18][19]. The latter also means that graphene is nearly transparent to the human
eye.
It should be noted that all of the aforementioned points are a brief selection of graphene’s
unique properties. Additionally, such versatility in properties has not been observed in any
of the other materials yet, and has made graphene the ideal candidate for wide range of

applications in the next generation of technologies including electronics, energy systems,
sensors, composites and coatings, biomedical technologies, and several others [20]. Such
applications are driven both by the scientific communities and commercial demands. Con-
sequently, two major science and technology roadmaps for graphene were presented, one
by Novoselov et al. (2012), and the other one by Ferrari et al. (2015) [5][21]. These two

The first study that reveal the superior electrical properties graphene, was based on
graphene applications in field effect transistors [3] Additionally, graphene’s high electrical
conductivity and mechanical strength, combined with its flexibility as an atom layer thick
material, has made it as the ideal candidate for flexible electronic systems as well. Graphene
can be used as sensors and devices that require adhesion and interaction to the human body
[21].
Another application of graphene which utilizes its mechanical and electrical properties,
lies within the field of Nanoelectromechanical Systems (NEMS). The enormous stiffness and
low density of graphene make it an ideal material for nanoelectromechanical sensors [22].
Since graphene does not oxidize in air or biological fluids, it is a desirable material to be
used as a biosensor [23]. A graphene circuit can be configured as a field effect biosensor by
applying biological capture molecules and blocking layers to the graphene, then controlling
the voltage difference between the graphene and the liquid that includes the biological test
sample.
Graphene’s light weight provides relatively good frequency response, suggesting uses in
electrostatic audio speakers and microphones [24]. Due to its transparency, atomic thickness,
and mechanical properties, monolayer graphene can establish itself as the potential choice
for transparent and flexible loudspeakers.
1.2
Micro- and Nano-pattering of Graphene
A wide range of the applications that are based on graphene, are hinged on how graphene’s
properties and shape can be controlled. Patterning graphene into finite structures with various-
scale confinement is important for tuning the transport properties of graphene-based devices.
Hence, it is essential to learn how to create patterned structures in graphene, and by doing so,
controlling the properties of graphene. As such, graphene patterning is established itself as
one of the areas of interest.

progress in this area have developed a variety of practical routes to patterned graphene sheets,

The lithography method is perhaps the most common technique for patterning. It has been
stimulated by the micro-electronic industry, where it is used for the fabrication of integrated
circuits (IC). The advantages of this technique, such as high resolution and accuracy, allow
the fabrication of patterns with features down to several-hundred nanometres in size [26].
In 2009, Cong et al., reported using the colloidal lithography as an elegant and emerging
technique for creating nanoscale patterns on graphene [27]. In another study, Kumar et al.
(2011) stated that the patterning of graphene sheets was prepared by using a metal etch mask
[28].
Nano imprint lithography (NIL) is another type of lithography that is used for patterning
of CVD grown graphene. Traditionally NIL uses a combination of a rigid mold and a
resist. Under pressure and temperatures above the glass transition temperature of the resist,
micron/nano sized structures can be created on the surface of the resist as it conforms to the
shape of the patterns on the mold. Later, the graphene is etched around the areas that are
patterned on the resist. Changing the structure types is dependent on the mold used during
the imprint step and the size of the imprinted area is limited only by the size of the mold
used. In 2013, Ng et al. employed this technique to achieve shape tunability with sizes
ranging from micrometer to nanometer [29]. It was demonstrated that their technique have
preserved the quality of the CVD grown graphene and offered the versatility of transferring
the graphene array onto any rigid or flexible substrate. The process was then expanded to
fabricate a graphene based microelectrode array. Later in 2017, Mackenzie et al. used the
thermal NIL technique to pattern wafer-scale CVD graphene on a 4" wafer [30]. In their
study, it was reported that the nanopatterning process had introduced a modest decrease in
carrier mobility and a minor change in residual doping.
The other type of lithography that is used for graphene patterning is electron beam
lithography (EBL). EBL can be used to create graphene nanoribbons (GNRs), with width
down to ∼20 nm, with a roughness on the order of a few nanometres [31]. The method
employs conventional EBL negative resist to form a protective pattern on graphene, which
is subsequently exposed to plasma oxygen. The unprotected portion of graphene is then
chemically removed upon the attack by the reactive plasma and carried into the vapour phase.
The pattern on the mask (and the e-beam resist) then is “printed” into graphene. Recently,
Jessen and Gammelgaard et al. (2019), successfully employed EBL as the nano-patterning
technique to engineer band structure of graphene [32]. Although EBL may be used directly

Graphene Synthesis and Preparation
2.1.1
Graphene Synthesis by CVD
Synthesis of graphene refers to any process for depositing or extracting graphene, depending
on the desired size and purity of the specific product. Since its isolation in 2004, several
techniques for monolayer graphene production have been developed. These methods are
divided into the so called ”top-down” and ”bottom-up” synthesis techniques, some of which
are mentioned in a paper by A. K. Geim in 2009 [36]. Examples of top-down synthesis include
mechnical exfoliation, liquid exfoliation, and unzipping carbon nanotubes (CNT). Each of
these methods have got advantages and disadvantages. For instance, mechanical exfoliation
provides some of the best samples for fundamental research due to its high crystallinity,
low amount of defects, and low cost of production [37]. On the other hand, mechanical
exfoliation is not scalable and identifying a single layer of graphene is time consuming
[38]. The bottom-up synthesis approach includes methods like on-surface synthesis, thermal
decomposition of SiC, and chemical vapour deposition (CVD) [38]. Several points can be
mentioned as the advantages and disadvantages of the bottom-up method. For example in
on-surface synthesis, high quality graphene is aquired that can be tailored down to atomic

level. However, it requires ultra-high vacuum conditions that are expensive and not scalable
[39].
Among all the bottom-up graphene synthesis techniques, CVD is the most common
one. CVD is the process of deposition of solid material onto a heated substrate through
decomposition or chemical reaction of molecular compounds, which are heated up and
changed to a gaseous state (precursor), passing over the substrate (catalyst) to produce the
desired thin film deposit [40]. The CVD process is schematically illustrated in figure 2.1.

In a CVD process, the precursor dissociates on the hot surface of the catalyst. After
this step, several physical phenomena like diffusion and desorption take place. Finally the
products of dissociation of precursor start to react and chemically transform into the final
product. Various hydrocarbons like methane, ethylene, acetylene, and benzene have been
used as precursor, and were decomposed on various metal substrates for CVD production of
graphene [41]. Among all the substrates that have been used as the catalyst layer to produce
graphene via the CVD process, nickel (Ni) and copper (Cu) are the most common ones [42].
In 2009, Li et al. reported the first CVD growth of large area and uniform graphene on
polycrystalline Cu foils by using thermal catalytic decomposition of methane [42].
Hydrocarbon and other chemicals ratio, temperature, and pressure are the factors that
determine the quality and size of a CVD grown graphene on Ni and Cu substrates [43]. For
instance, temperature plays a determinant role in the size and quality of graphene grains
and the number of layers. Higher temperatures correlates to obtaining monolayer graphene
[44]. As another example, a study in 2010 by Bhaviripudi et al., revealed that graphene
growth using using Cu catalyst under atmospheric pressure CVD (APCVD) conditions with
higher methane concentration is not self-limiting compared to the case of low pressure CVD
(LPCVD) [45]. Later in 2013, Hao et al. studied the role of surface oxygen in the growth
of large single-crystal graphene on Cu [46]. Their results had shown that the control of

2.1.2
Graphene Transfer Techniques
Regardless of the synthesis method, once the graphene is obtained, it needs to be transferred
from the growth substrate to a target substrate for most of the applications. During the past
years, graphene transfer has been one the main challenges due several reasons. Being only
one atom layer thick, how is it possible to handle graphene without damaging it? How
can one achieve an even transfer, i.e., being sure that the entire grown graphene layer is
transferred from the growth substrate to the target substrate? Such issues imply that even
though quality improvements can be made during synthesis steps, it is important to note that
most of the time degradation of quality occurs during the transfer process [51]. It should be
noted that while transferring graphene from the growth substrate, the goal is to weaken the
interaction between the substrate and the graphene layer. This weakening in the interaction
could eventually cause the least amount of damage to the graphene [51]. For instance,

theoretical calculations have shown that the adhesion energy for the as-grown graphene on
Ni and Cu is approximately 13 J/m2 and 73 J/m2, respectively (for comparison, adhesion
energy of graphene on SiO2 is 0.1-0.5 J/m2) [52]. These all have led into developing various
techniques for graphene transfer.
Etching transfer, bubbling transfer, and etch-free transfer are among the examples of
graphene transferring techniques which have been studied [51]. For example, one of the
major drawbacks in the etching transfer is that the growth substrate is etched away after the
transfer [49]. In the case of the bubbling transfer, the transfer process should be performed
at a low speed to prevent excessive bubbling, which can damage graphene due to surface
tension forces [53]. In an etch-free process, the Cu substrate can be reused several times
for graphene growth without significant degradation of quality. In such a technique, it is
required to transfer graphene to a substance that act as a glue substrate. However, the previous
studies that have used the etch-free method, have not provided a step that removes the glue
substances [54][55].
Recently, an etch-free CVD graphene transfer method from Cu foil to Poly(vinyl alcohol)

employing the roll-to-roll technique. The office laminator acts as the roll-to-roll device for
transferring the CVD grown graphene on Cu to the PVA film (figure 2.3). This method has
also further enabled a fast and easy transfer of graphene from the glue substance (PVA) to

2.1.3
Poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA)
Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) is a water-soluble synthetic polymer with the idealized formula
of [CH2CH(OH)]n and an amorphous structure [57]. PVA has unique strong cohesiveness
and film toughness, as well as other features, including good smoothness, oil and solvent
resistance, air barrier properties, abrasion resistance, and the property of protective colloid
[58]. However, these properties are dependent on humidity, meaning that with higher
humidity, more water is absorbed. Water, acting as a plasticiser, will then reduce PVA’s
tensile strength, but increases its elongation and tear strength [59]. Later in 2014, another
study which was performed on mechanical assessment of PVA nanofiber sheets, concluded
that electrospun PVA nanofiber films have a maximum yield strength of 57 bar [60]. It should
be pointed out that such high yield strengths are for biomedical applications, and other types
of commercially available PVA have lower yield strength, meaning that such PVA films can
deform under lower tensile stresses.
Regarding the thermal properties, in some of the literature it is reported that PVA is
known to have a glass transition temperature (Tg) of 85 ◦C and melting temperature (Tm)
of 180-190 ◦C [61]. One should carefully consider that the reported values correspond to a
pure PVA, and also depend on its the molecular weight (Mw). In other words, different types
of commercially available PVA can have impurities that can alter the thermal properties,
and consequently affect the Tg and Tm of the material. In order to investigate the thermal
properties of the PVA films, thermoanalytical characterization techniques such as Thermal
Gravimetric Analysis (TGA) or Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) are performed.
DSC is the measurement of the change of the difference in the heat flow rate to the sample and
to a reference sample while they are subjected to a controlled temperature program. During a
DSC analysis both the sample and reference are maintained at nearly the same temperature
throughout the experiment [62][63]. In this study, DSC was performed to measure the Tg
and Tm of the PVA films that are used for the CVD graphene transfer.

2.2.1
Hot Embossing
The main technique of this work is hot punching, which is a combination of hot embossing
and mechanical punching. Before moving on to describing hot punching, it is required to
introduce hot embossing as a microfabrication technique. Hot embossing is considered
as a flexible, low-cost microfabrication method for polymer microstructures. As such,
hot embossing is the technique to fabricate high precision and high quality plastic micro-
structures [64]. In the hot embossing process, a micro-structured master, a so called mold
insert or stamp, is replicated in polymer. Polymers are cheap and are available in different
modifications with a wide range of properties. Therefore, micro-structures with a large
bandwidth of properties can be replicated, suitable for the application for which they are
needed [65][66]. Moreover, micro hot embossing of thermoplastic polymers is a cost-
effective replication technology to transfer micro-structure patterns from a master mold onto
a polymeric substrate at proper pressure and temperature.
In both academia and industry, micro hot embossing is regarded as one of the most
promising processes to fabricate high-precision and high quality features in micro/nano-
scale [67]. The embossing process consists of the following major four steps, which are
schematically shown in figure 2.4: (1) heating the mold and substrate to the molding
temperature, (2) embossing microstructure patterns at embossing temperature (isothermal
molding), (3) cooling the molded part to demolding temperature, and (4) demolding of the
component by opening the tools.
Molding parameters include all parameters and influencing factors that control the process
or affect the quality of the molded part. Together, they influence the quality of the microstruc-
tured molded parts. Process parameters include the molding temperature, embossing force,
holding time, and demolding temperature. Another group of parameters is related to the
properties of the molding material. They largely determine the process parameters and the
processing windows.
The specific process parameters depend on polymer and mold materials [65]. Thermo-
plastic polymers experience two stages of deformation in the whole process: one is a stress
concentration and strain hardening stage occurring in heating and applying pressure steps,
and the other is a stress relaxation and deformation recovery stage which occurs in cooling
and demolding steps [68]. Glass transition temperature Tg, forming pressure, and holding
time are the most important parameters for the micro hot embossing process of thermoplastic
polymers.

The advantages of hot embossing are low material flow, avoiding internal stress which
induces e.g. scattering centers infavorable for optical applications, and low flow rates, so more
delicate structures can be fabricated, such as free standing thin columns or narrow oblonged
walls. Moreover, polymer hot embossing in microfabrication offers other advantages, such
as facile manufacturing, versatile geometries (rounded, rectangular, hexagonal, high aspect
ratios, etc), and low material cost for high-volume fabrication (for instance, compared to
other materials such as silicon, glass, and quartz) [64].
There can be three different configurations for the molding tools, which constitute the
micro hot embossing into three different categories including roll-to-roll (R2R), roll-to-plate
(R2P), and plate-to-plate (P2P) (figure 2.5) [65]. R2R is characterized by two rotating
cylinders with a polymer film in between. One of the cylinders is microstructured„ with the
contact areas between the polymer and the other cylinder being only a line. The advantage of
R2R is a lower press force compared to P2P embossing. In R2P, there is a rotating cylinder
against a flat area. R2P is principally similar to R2R, with the difference that the mold can be
integrated in the plane area. In the P2P method, a relatively flat polymer sample is placed
between a structured mold and a flat mold surface, typically FDTS coated Ni stamps.

The principles of P2P is as followed. In a parallel press or with a hydrostatic pressure,
the stack is heated above the glass transition temperature (for amorphous structures) or the
melting temperature (for crystalline structures) of the polymer and pressure is applied. The
polymer deforms to the shape of the mold and after a specific amount of time, the temperature
is lowered below the glass transition or the melting temperature, while maintaining the
pressure. Finally and after the cooling stage, the pressure is released and the stack is
separated mechanically (e.g. with a razor). In the P2P, the press force over the whole
micro-structured area allows the use of polymer melts, which is a requirement for molding
microstructures with high aspect ratios. The other advantage about the P2P method that needs
to be mentioned, is the way the microstructures demolding is performed. In this method
structures can be demolded by a motion parallel to the structure, which decreases the risk of
damaging the structures. Being the most conventional method, P2P covers a wide range of
applications in industrial fabrications such as compact disc (CD), digital versatile disc (DVD)
[67], as well as production of biodegradable microcontainers for oral drug delivery [69].
Based on all the aforementioned properties and advantages of P2P method, in this study,
P2P is also used as the technique for large area CVD graphene micro-patterning. However,
since graphene is only one atom layer thick and has a melting temperature of 4150 K, hot
embossing of graphene is not possible.

2.2.2
Mechanical Punching
Mechanical punching is a technique which is used for fabricating holes in metallic sheets.
A punching configuration is schematically illustrated in figure 2.6. The sheet is aligned
between the punch and the die. Once the sheet is positioned, the punch moves downward
and thrusts into the die. Further, the sheet is cut by the parallel movement of the edges of the
punch and the die, while passing each other. The punching process undergoes three phases:
1) when the punch and the sheet come into contact, the sheet is deformed, 2) later the punch
penetrates the sheet and the sample is cut, and lastly 3) the shear stress within the material is
high enough to fracture the sheet along the contour of the cut [70][71]. Generally, for a sheet
of material with thickness of ts, material multiplier MM, and ultimate tensile strength UTS,
the total punching force with a punch perimeter of L is calculated as followed [72]
F = MM·L·ts·UTS
(2.1)
Depending on the material, MM ranges from 0.5 to 1.4. The value is reported 0.7 for metal
and 1.4 for hard steel [72].

2.2.3
Hot Punching
As mentioned previously, hot punching is the main patterning technique used in this study.
Hot punching is a combination of hot embossing and mechanical punching. Prior to this study,
hot punching has been used as a process to fabricate individual biopolymer microcontainers
for oral drug delivery applications [73]. Hot punching of polymers is done in three stages: 1)
the stamp forms a conformal contact with the substrate spatially enclosing the polymer to
be punched, 2) the polymer is cut by the highest protursion on the stamp, and finally 3) the
tensile stress within the polymer becomes high enough to fracture the film along the contour
==PAGE:38==
20
Materials and Methods
of the cut. In order to hot punch the polymers, polymers should be heated up above Tg to
enable the material to flow [70].
Fig. 2.7 Schematic illustration of hot punching for fabrication of microcontainers [73].
Generally, hot punching has got some benefits compared to hot embossing. The most
significant advantage of hot punching is that due to the complete conformal contact of the
stamp with the substrate, penetration to residual layer would be possible. In hot embossing the
latter is nearly impossible [73]. Hot punching also is advantageous compared to mechanical
punching. In mehcanical punching, no temperature is involved in the patterning process,
which requires large forces to be applied in order to replicate the patterns. However in hot
punching, the samples are heated above the Tg. The latter implies that since the substrate
will be reached to a viscoelastic state, it would be thus easier for the mold to penetrate into
it. In other words, less forces are required to replicate the patterns during the hot punching
process. Additionally, hot punching is a versatile process, where the residual layer can be any
elastic polymer such as PDMS or PVA, while the device material typically is a thermoplastic
polymer [73].
In this study a modified version of hot punching is used. Compared to the conventional
hot punching process, the residual layer is not of importance in this case. But rather, it is
needed to replicate the patterns on the graphene layer. In order to make the patterns onto
graphene, graphene is mechanically punched while the polymer layer is hot embossed above
its Tg. Consequently, the process that is used in this study for patterning graphene sheets,
can be regarded as temperature-assisted mechanical punching. This process, would enable
patterning of graphene sheets (device layer) with mechanical punching, as they are on a
polymer layer (PVA), only by applying pressure and temperatures above Tg of PVA. The
embossing equipment that is used for in this study, is ”P 300 SV Hot Embosser” manufactured
by Collin GmbH.

2.4
Graphene Characterization
Once the micro-patterned graphene sheets were acquired by hot punching, the impact of
hot punching on graphene’s micro- and nanostructure was studied. In materials science
terminology, characterization is the fundamental process by which a material’s structure and
properties are probed and measured. With graphene being on the front line of the 2D materials,
several spectroscopy and microscopy characterization techniques have been developed to
study its structure. These methods include optical microscopy, Raman spectroscopy, Atomic
Force Microscopy (AFM), Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM), Transmission Electron
Microscopy (TEM), etc.[1]. In the following, the theory and fundamentals of these techniques,
which are also employed in this study, are presented.
2.4.1
Optical Microscopy
Optical microscopy is an inexpensive and quick way to observe domain boundaries of
graphene. One of the ways to detect graphene by using optical microscopy, is to transfer
it to a layer of SiO2. In 2007, Blake et al. studied graphene’s visibility and showed that it
depends strongly on both thickness of SiO2 and light wavelength. It was found that by using
monochromatic light and for any thickness of SiO2, graphene can be isolated. 300 nm and
100 nm were reported as the most suitable SiO2 thicknesses for visualizing graphene [78]. In
this study, the micro-patterned CVD graphene sheet is visualized by being transferred to a
90 nm thick layer of SiO2. The optical microscope setup which is used to characterize the
graphene, is a Nikon Eclipse L200N, equipped with a Nikon DS-Fi2 camera, Nikon CFI Plan
Fluor lens with various objectives, and Priori Scientific programmable stage.

Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is a type of electron microscopy that examines and an-
alyzes the physical information obtained by generating focused electron beams and scanning
the surface of the sample in order to determine the structure, composition, and topography
of the sample. The electrons interact with atoms in the sample, producing various signals
that contain information about the surface topography and composition of the sample. In a
typical SEM, an electron beam is thermionically emitted from an electron gun fitted with a
tungsten filament cathode. Tungsten is normally used in thermionic electron guns because it
has the highest melting point and lowest vapor pressure of all metals, thereby allowing it to
be electrically heated for electron emission, and because of its low cost.
The electron beam, which typically has an energy ranging from 0.2 keV to 40 keV, is
focused by one or two condenser lenses to a spot about 0.4 nm to 5 nm in diameter. The most
common imaging mode collects low-energy (<50 eV) secondary electrons that are ejected
from conduction or valence bands of the specimen atoms by inelastic scattering interactions
with beam electrons. Due to their low energy, these electrons originate from within a few
nanometers below the sample surface [79].
SEM can be used to measure the flake size, grain boundaries, crystal defects, and other
properties of graphene. SEM is a useful method to directly observe the CVD growth of

2.4.3
Raman Spectroscopy
Raman spectroscopy is a spectroscopic technique typically used to determine vibrational
modes of molecules, even though rotational and other low frequency modes of the systems
can be determined. Raman spectroscopy relies upon inelastic scattering of photons, known as
Raman scattering. A source of monochromatic light, usually from a laser in the visible, near
infrared, or near ultraviolet range is typically used, although X-rays can also be used. The
laser light interacts with molecular vibrations, phonons or other excitations in the system,
resulting in the energy of the laser photons being shifted up or down. The shift in energy
gives information about the vibrational modes in the system. The magnitude of the Raman
effect correlates with polarizability of the electrons in a molecule. It is a form of inelastic
light scattering, where a photon excites the sample. This excitation puts the molecule into a
virtual energy state for a short time before the photon is emitted. Inelastic scattering means
that the energy of the emitted photon is of either lower or higher energy than the incident
photon. After the scattering event, the sample is in a different rotational or vibrational state
[81].
There are two methods by which a Raman spectrum is obtained; dispersive Raman
and Fourier Transform(FT)-Raman. Dispersive Raman has become the largest and most
widely used of the Raman systems, the instrument will typically consist of a monochromator
==PAGE:43==
2.4 Graphene Characterization

(spectrometer or spectrograph), a charge-coupled device (CCD) detector, and various laser
sources- ranging from UV to NIR. A microscope is often used to enable micron scale analysis
[81]. It should be noted among all the mentioned parts of a dispersive Raman spectroscopy
setup, the laser is often considered as the most vital component. There are light sources with
different wavelengths such as 488 nm, 532 nm, 780 nm and etc. that have been used in Raman
spectroscopy measurements. In most of the literature within the graphene research, 532 nm
is the most frequently used light source. An example of a dispersive Raman spectrometer is
schematically shown in figure 2.12.
Considered as a versatile and integral tool within the graphene research [82], Raman
spectroscopy is used to determine the number and orientation of layers [83], the quality and
types of edges [84], and the effects of perturbations, such as electric and magnetic fields,
strain [85][86][87], doping [88], disorder, and defects [89]. All these properties can be
probed by studying the variations in the corresponding Raman spectra. For example, in 2009
Casiraghi et al. showed that Raman spectroscopy is a sensitive tool for edge orientation,
since the D peak is zero for zigzag orientation and large for armchair. It should be pointed
out that the D peak intensity is only related to the degree of disorder and not the number of
graphene layers [83].

2.4.4
Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM)
AFM is a very-high-resolution (on the order of fractions of a nanometer) type of scanning
probe microscopy, used for imaging surfaces by mechanically scanning their surface contours.
In AFM, the deflection of a sharp tip sensing the surface forces, mounted on a compliant
cantilever, is monitored. An AFM forms a map of the height of the sample surface. The data
enables a very simple and quick way to measure the height, width, or volume of any feature
in the image [90].
The typical AFM possesses the following configuration: A small spring-like cantilever
is carried by the support. A piezoelectric element (typically made of a ceramic material)
oscillates the cantilever. The sharp tip is fixed of the free end of the cantilever. The detector
==PAGE:45==
2.4 Graphene Characterization
27
records the deflection and motion of the cantilever. The sample is mounted on the sample
stage. An XYZ drive permits to displace the sample and the sample stage in x, y, and
z directions with respect to the tip apex. According to the described configuration, the
interaction between tip and sample, which can be an atomic scale phenomenon, is transduced
into changes of the motion of cantilever which is a macro scale phenomenon. Several
different aspects of the cantilever motion can be used to quantify the interaction between the
tip and sample, most commonly the value of the deflection, the amplitude of an imposed
oscillation of the cantilever, or the shift in resonance frequency of the cantilever. The detector
of AFM measures the deflection (displacement with respect to the equilibrium position) of
the cantilever and converts it into an electrical signal. The intensity of this signal will be
proportional to the displacement of the cantilever [90].
When using the AFM to image a sample, the tip is brought into contact with the sample,
and the sample is raster scanned along an X-Y grid. Most commonly, an electronic feedback
loop is employed to keep the probe-sample force constant during scanning. This feedback
loop has the cantilever deflection as input, and its output controls the distance along the
z axis between the probe support and the sample support. As long as the tip remains in
contact with the sample, and the sample is scanned in the X-Y plane, height variations in the
sample will change the deflection of the cantilever. The feedback then adjusts the height of
the probe support so that the deflection is restored to a user-defined value (the setpoint). A
properly adjusted feedback loop adjusts the support-sample separation continuously during
the scanning motion, such that the deflection remains approximately constant. In this
situation, the feedback output equals the sample surface topography to within a small error
[91].
The tapping mode is the most common dynamic mode used in atomic force microscopy
(figure 2.14). In dynamic mode AFM, the cantilever is oscillated with (or near) its resonance
frequency near the sample surface. Using a feedback electronic, the cantilever-sample
distance is controlled by keeping either the amplitude or the phase of the oscillating cantilever
constant. Since lateral tip–sample forces are avoided by this technique, the resolution is
typically higher compared to the classical contact mode AFM, where tip and sample are in
direct mechanical contact [92]. In this mode, the cantilever is vibrated close to its resonant
frequency near the sample surface. Due to the tip–sample interaction the resonant frequency
(and consequently also amplitude A and phase φ) of the cantilever changes with the cantilever
sample distance d. Therefore, the amplitude, as well as the phase, can be used as feedback
channels. At a certain set-point for example, the amplitude is given, and the feedback loop
will adjust the tip–sample distance such that the amplitude remains constant. The cantilever
==PAGE:46==
28

sample, and the scanned height essentially represents the surface topography.
The deflection of the cantilever is typically measured with the laser beam deflection
method. During operation in conventional tapping mode, the cantilever is driven with a fixed
frequency and a constant excitation amplitude using an external function generator, while
the resulting oscillation amplitude and/or the phase shift are detected by a lock-in amplifier

2.4.5
Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)
Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) is a microscopy technique in which a beam of
electrons is transmitted through a specimen to form an image. The specimen is most often
a thin film section less than 100 nm thick or a suspension on a grid. An image is formed
from the interaction of the electrons with the sample as the beam is transmitted through
the specimen. The image is then magnified and focused onto an imaging device, such as a
fluorescent screen, a layer of photographic film, or a sensor attached to a charge-coupled
device (CCD) [95]. The electron energy is in the range of 60-150 keV or 200 keV-3 MeV in
the case of high voltage electron microscope. Electrons are accelerated in the electron gun
by thermionic emission from tungsten hairpin cathodes. A two-stage condenser-lens system
permits variation of the illumination aperture and the area of the specimen illuminated. The
electron-intensity distribution behind the specimen is imaged with a three- or four-stage lens
system, onto a fluorescent screen. Finally, the image can be recorded by direct exposure
of photographic emulsion inside the vacuum [95]. Electrons interact strongly with atoms
by elastic and inelastic scattering. Hence, the specimen must be very thin, 5 nm- 0.5 µm
for 100 KeV electrons depending on the density and elemental composition of the object
and the resolution needed. TEM can provide high resolution because elastic scattering is an
interaction process that is highly localized to the region occupied by the screened coulomb
potential of an atomic nucleus [96].

2.5
Image Analysis: Hough Transform
For the purposes of this project, Hough transform is used for accurate analysis of the
orientation of the lines and wrinkles in the acquired AFM measurements. Hough transform
is a feature extraction method for detecting simple shapes such as circles, lines and etc. in
an image. A "simple" shape is one that can be represented by only a few parameters. For
example, a line can be represented by two parameters (slope, intercept) and a circle has three
parameters — the coordinates of the center and the radius (x, y, r). Hough transform does an
excellent job in finding such shapes in an image [99].
The simplest case of Hough transform is detecting straight lines. In general, the straight
line y = mx+b can be represented as a point (b,m) in the parameter space. However, vertical
lines pose a problem as they would give rise to unbounded values of the slope parameter m.
Thus the following transform was suggested to use
ρ = xcos(θ)+ysin(θ),
(2.2)
where ρ is the distance from the origin, usually the top left corner, to the closest point on
the straight line, and θ is the angle between the x axis and the perpendicular line connecting
the origin with that closest point. It is therefore possible to associate with each line of the

image a pair (ρ,θ). The (ρ,θ) plane is sometimes referred to as Hough space for the set of
straight lines in two dimensions. Given a single point in the image plane, then the set of all
straight lines going through that point corresponds to a sinusoidal curve in the (ρ,θ) Hough
plane, which is unique to that point. A set of two or more points that form a straight line
will produce sinusoids which cross at the (ρ,θ) for that line. If the line is passing below the
origin, it will have a positive ρ and angle less than 180. If it is passing above the origin,
instead of taking angle greater than 180, angle is taken less than 180, and ρ is taken negative.
Any vertical line will have 90 degree and horizontal lines will have 0 degree.

The main aim of this section is to investigate whether it is feasible to hot punch CVD graphene

and the molding material parameters [65]. Among all the variables that were discussed in

study: (1) Height of the Ni stamps, (2) Molding temperature, and (3) Molding pressure. It
shoudl be clarified that the molding temperature and the molding pressure values refer to
their setpoint values on the hot embossing machine. Through out the rest of this section,
the influence of each of these parameters on micro-patterning of CVD graphene sheets with
hot punching will be investigated. The entire optimization process is centered around fine
tuning of these parameters, considered as the most relevant ones, while the other ones are

The obtained results from figures 4.5 to 4.8 can be interpreted as follows. At the glass
transition temperature, PVA begins to soften, and its viscosity decreases as the temperature
increases. By going above the Tg in the hot punching process, the PVA polymer will be thus
in a viscoelastic state [65]. At this point, the Ni stamp is in contact with the viscoelastic PVA
layer, resulting into PVA film deformation under the applied molding pressure [69]. The
latter also can be be supported by comparing figures 4.5(c-f). The height profiles clearly
show that the Ni stamp has passed the PVA layer (the thickness of the PVA film is 30 µm),
reached the supporting paper layer by moving deeper, and created an imprint on that layer as
well. The higher the temperature, the more viscous the PVA film, and hence the Ni stamp
will be able to move further inside the layers of the PVA film (figure 4.5b).
As the highest protrusion of the Ni stamp proceeds more into the PVA film, it is thus
more likely for the graphene to move to the underlying paper layer as well. This eventually
decreases the possibility of the graphene returning on hot punched areas to the surface of the
PVA film, during the roll-to-roll transfer to SiO2 layer. As it is evident in figures 4.5(c) and
4.6(a), the micro-patterns are replicated on the CVD graphene sheets, but the graphene is not
removed on those areas. This might be due to the insufficient penetration of the Ni stamp in
the PVA layer, which enables the graphene to move back to the surface of PVA during the
transfer to SiO2.<ioi
The circles that are observed in figure 4.6 can also be reasoned by the same argument on
the PVA viscoelastic behavior at higher temperatures. The less viscous the PVA film, the
more likely it becomes for the micro-cavities between the highest structures on the stamp to
==PAGE:69==

be filled with viscoelastic PVA. As a result, despite the anti-sticking FDTS coating on the
Ni stamp, the PVA is partly remained in the Ni stamp micro-cavities while it is peeled off
from the stamp. Besides the mentioned point, one should also note the role of air bubbles in
causing irregularities in formed shapes. When the micro-patterns are made in PVA with hot
punching, during transfer the process onto SiO2 layer, the micro-patterned areas can act as
preferential sites for air bubbles to be trapped. In other words, the deformities in the circular
shapes, and adjacent holes joining up to form larger holes, can also be due to trapped air
bubbles.
The results obtained with studying the impact of the temperature, suggest the need for
applying the molding temperatures that are above 100 ◦C. Due to the viscoelastic behavior
of the PVA, such temperatures ensure the sufficient movement of the Ni stamp into the PVA
film. However, an upper threshold should be taken into account as well for the temperature.
After the demolding of the Ni stamp, with hot punching at the molding temperature of 130
◦C, it was seen that the PVA had overflowed outwards, in the area between the stamp array
and the PVA film. Such a phenomena was not observed at other temperatures.
In conclusion, a molding temperature is needed that prevents the PVA film from over-
flowing outwards out during the hot punching process, while provides the highest penetration
depth of the Ni stamp. This molding temperature will lead to a successful micro-patterning
of CVD graphene sheets with hot punching. Based on the mentioned factors, the molding
temperature that will be used further on will be set to 120 ◦C.

4.3 Conclusion
55
A consistent correlation between the results acquired from the optical profilometry
measurements (figure 4.9) and optical microscopy characterization (figure 4.10) can be
noticed. For instance, at the molding pressure of 2 bar, the Ni stamp starts moving into
the PVA, however not that deep that for the graphene to be entirely removed. This can
be interpreted in the manner that graphene is displaced into the bottom layers of the PVA.
However, similar as for the influence of temperature, it has moved back near the surface of
the PVA during the roll-to-roll transfer onto the SiO2 layer. Once the SiO2-PVA-graphene
stack is put in the DI water, to etch the PVA layer, the whole graphene sheet, including the
hot punched areas, remained on the SiO2 layer.
By increasing the molding pressure, the Ni stamp will move further inside the PVA layer.
As a result, this increase in the penetration leads to a higher accuracy in micro-patterning
graphene sheets. The latter indicates that graphene will not be removed while being hot
punched, even at a temperature above the glass transition temperature of PVA, unless the
applied pressure has reached a certain point. At such a pressure, the Ni stamp will move
deeper into the graphene-PVA stack, which in turn provides a higher chance of graphene
removal with the hot punching process [65].
To conclude, by increasing the molding pressure in the hot punching, higher fidelity
for graphene micro-patterning will be provided. On the other hand, the molding pressure
should be lower than a level that will result in destruction of graphene sheet during the tensile
deformation of PVA layer. Thus, the molding pressure of 4 bar returned the most desirable
outcome.
4.3
Conclusion
In conclusion, a set of experiments were designed and performed to investigate the influence
of some of the parameters on hot punching of CVD graphene sheets. The parameters that
were studied include the Ni stamp height, the molding temperature, and the molding pressure.
The impact of each these factors were analyzed by utilizing optical profilometry and optical
microscopy techniques.
In correlation to the first point in the thesis statement (section 1.3, figure 1.4), it is
now revealed that large scale CVD graphene micro-patterning by hot punching is possible.
This requires using a Ni stamp with a protrusion higher than the thickness of the PVA film,
combined with applying an appropriate molding temperature above the Tg of PVA, and a
sufficient high molding pressure.

structural properties of graphene sheets after being hot punched. A method is thus required to
measure variations in edges orientation and defects, strain, and charge carrier density, caused

defects in the hot punched areas. At the center of the hot punched area, the D peak intensity

graphene. Such a phenomena implies the presence of small islands of intact graphene, where
most of the material has become defective [103]. At the same time, smaller peaks such
as D′ and D+D′ are also rising, which are other indicators of the defects in graphene [82].
Moreover, since edges are also categorized as structural defects, the orientation of the edges
can be detected based on the D peak intensity. For ideal edges, D peak is zero for zigzag
orientation and large for armchair [84].

increase in the charge carrier density can be correlated to the induced tensile strain [105][88].

density has increased as well. These changes in the strain and carrier density can happen as a
result of hot punching process, the transfer process, or a combination of both. The value for
the unpatterned pristine CVD graphene that is used in this study, is reported to be 2.42·1012
cm−2, according to the Terahertz Time Domain Spectroscopy (THz-TDS) measurements

5.2
AFM Characterization
In section 5.1, Raman spectroscopy measurements identified structural disorders in the
micro-patterned CVD graphene sheets. As the next step, it is required to determine how
these structural disorders look like on the micro- and nanoscale. In this section, atomic force
microscopy (AFM) is used as the technique to extract high resolution topographical images
of the samples. Similar to optical microscopy and Raman spectroscopy, AFM does not
require any sample preparation. The micro-patterned graphene samples can be characterized
while they are supported by the 90 nm SiO2 layer.
In order to reduce the damages, that can be caused by the mechanical contacts with the
samples, the AFM is operated in the non-contact tapping mode in air [92]. All the scans are
performed by setting the X-Y scan range to 10×10 µm2 with a resolution of 512 samples
per line and the cantilever’s resonance frequency of 0.5 Hz. The scanning angle is set to 90◦.
Before proceeding to the AFM results, it is required to introduce the clock nomenclature,
which is frequently referred to during this section. Since the replicated micro-patterns are in
circular shapes, it is possible to perform AFM scans by positioning the AFM cantilever in
different angles, relative to the edge of the micro-patterned areas. In order to simplify the
understanding of the cantilever’s positioning in each angle, a one-to-one correspondence is
defined between the scanning positions and a clock. For a better understanding, an example
of the clock nomenclature is schematically illustrated in figure 5.11. As it can be seen in
figure 5.11(a), the cantilever is positioned at an angle, which can be referred to as the "clock
3" position according to the clock nomenclature (figure 5.11(b)).

Furthermore, by looking into the results obtained from the AFM scans in figure 5.12,
periodic bright lines, which are appeared on the surface of the graphene, can be identified. A
group of these lines are marked with rectangles in the AFM results. These lines are known
as wrinkles. Wrinkles are formed as a result of the negative thermal expansion of graphene
and the transferring process for the surface-grown graphene samples on metallic substrates
[106][80]. The interaction between the substrate and graphene strongly influences the wrinkle
formation. Graphene grown on metallic catalysts exhibits high density of wrinkles due to the
opposite polarity of thermal expansion coefficient of graphene and the metal [106].
Comparison between the different AFM results in figure 5.12, clearly demonstrates a
change in structure. The uniform graphene sheet in figure 5.12(a) is divided into graphene
ribbons, and wrinkles have all changed their orientation normal to the edges of the ribbons.
Collateral ribbons are formed parallel to the of micro-patterned areas.
By the virtue of the AFM measurements in figure 5.12, it has become evident that
the stamp with the height of 21 µm, not only has caused damages to graphene sheet via
transferring surface contamination, but also has not resulted in ribbons formation. This is in
the direct contrast when the other stamp (75 µm high) is used. For this stamp, the graphene
surface does not contain additional residues, and ribbons are formed normal to the edges
of the hot punched region. In order to assess the occurrence of the latter, it is required to
investigate how the different molding temperatures and pressures will influence the ribbons
formation and wrinkles behavior.

5.2.3
Hough Transform Analysis
AFM results in figure 5.15 showed that the graphene ribbons follow an angular distribution
around the edges of the micro-patterned areas. Since the wrinkles are normal to the edges
of the ribbons, Hough transform analysis can be used to determine at which angle each
array of wrinkles is distributed. In order to execute the Hough analysis on the scans that are
acquired by AFM, images need to be binarized first. In other words, colored images should
be converted to their black and white versions. By doing so, solid lines become detectable
for the algorithm by which the Hough analysis is ran. For binarizing the images, Fiji, an
open source image processing software, is used. Figure 5.16 presents an example of an AFM
image and its binarized version.
One should note that the wrinkles are detected in the binary image and not the edges of
the graphene ribbons. This is due to the higher optical contrast of wrinkles in the original
image. A set of wrinkles in the original image and its binarized version are marked with
a circle in figures 5.16(a) and (b). Wrinkles are visible as a solid line in the binary format,
making them detectable for the Hough transform algorithm. After images are transformed to
the binary format, the Hough transform will thus find the angle at which the wrinkles are
oriented [99].

5.3
TEM Characterization
Graphene and the graphene ribbons that were identified in the AFM scans, need to be
characterized further. This characterization should be performed to investigate how clean and
free of disorder the graphene is at its edges. Such properties of edges are of importance since
they can determine some of the electrical properties of graphene. Roughness in graphene
can result in scattering effects that decrease the carrier mobility [110][111]. By using
transmission electron microscopy (TEM) technique in this section, it will be attempted to
determine qualitatively how clean the micro-patterned graphene is at the edges.
Unlike Raman spectroscopy and AFM characterizations, the graphene cannot be charac-
terized with TEM while it is supported on the SiO2 layer. The thick Si will block the electrons’
transmission through the sample, and no images will be acquired. Thus the graphene should
be suspended on a grid.

Based on the obtained images in figures 5.21 to 5.24, several information can be derived.
In figure 5.21(a), a graphene ribbon can be observed. Consistent with the AFM results that
were presented in figure 5.15, the wrinkles have become normal to the edges of the ribbons.
Figure 5.21(b) is the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) of a spot on the edge of the
graphene. The black dots represent the crystal structure of graphene in a reciprocal space
(Fourier transform of the real space) [98]. For a single layer graphene, these dots appear with
a weak and monotonous intensity in the SAED results. For bi- or few layers, additional dots
appear as well. The latter can be also attributed to folded graphene layers [98]. Additional
dots will appear in the SAED patterns if graphene is folded on top of itself. As it is seen in
figure ??(b), the intensity of dots is low and no additional dots can be seen either. This may
imply that the edge of the graphene ribbon is not folded.
In figure 5.22(a) a graphene can be seen, which is on top of amorphous hydrocarbon
structures. The presence of such hydrocrabon structures can be due to the insufficient
dissolving of the PVA during the graphene transfer process to the grid. Even though the
latter can make it more challenging to detect the edge of graphene, graphene seems to have a
clean edge on the area where the image is taken. In the corresponding SAED image, it can
be seen that some dots have appeared, however the intensity of the dots is not strong. Since
the exact spot on the TEM image, where the SAED is taken from, is not determined, it is
hard to confirm if the graphene is folded at its edge. However, the detected edge in the TEM
image is free of disorder on the presented scale.
In the cases of figures 5.23 and 5.24, since graphene is hanging from the edges of the holy
carbon membrane, it is easier to determine whether it is folded at the edges by measuring the
thickness. Each graphene layer has a thickness of 0.33 nm, and the distance between each
of the graphene layer is 0.15 nm [4]. The measured thickness is more than ∼0.8 nm, which
indicates more than one layer, and graphene has folded on itself. This self folding tendency
in graphene is due to the Van der Waals interactions between the graphene layers [112]. The
identified folded edges in figures 5.23(a) and 5.24, are consistent with the acquired SAED
pattern in figures 5.23(b) and 5.24(b). The intensity of the dots has increased, which implies
the presence of more than one layer of graphene.
The above discussions on the TEM results can lead to the preliminary conclusion that
the fractured graphene sheets and formed ribbons, tend to have clean and free of disorder
edges. Some issues such as graphene self-folding, the presence of hydrocarbon structures,
and the limited amount of graphene edges that should be hanging on from the holey carbon
membranes, hindered a more thorough investigation in TEM images. By solving such issues,
a better understanding based on the TEM measurements can be obtained.

5.4
Conclusion
To conclude, this chapter provided a more detailed investigation and understanding of the
structural properties of micro-patterned CVD graphene sheets. By using the respective
techniques for graphene characterization, the impact of hot punching parameters, such as the
temperature and the pressure, was investigated.
The derived results addressed the 2nd thesis statement as well (figure 1.5 in section 1.3).
In accordance with each other, the Raman spectroscopy measurements, AFM results, and
TEM images confirm that some novel behaviors in mechanical properties of graphene are
observed. As a direct consequence of hot punching of graphene with specific parameters,
CVD graphene tends to tear itself and form arrays of ribbons. The resulting ribbons are
formed parallel to the edges of the micro-patterned areas and have clean edges, which implies
that graphene is naturally cracked in a preferred direction.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Outlook
6.1
Conclusion
This MSc. thesis described a novel approach for large area CVD graphene micro-patterning
by using Ni stamps in the hot punching technique. The results that were obtained through the
course of this thesis, demonstrated the concepts that were initially set as thesis statements.
Large area CVD graphene was successfully micro-patterned by employing a Ni stamp, and
performing the hot punching process in an optimized manner. Furthermore, it was observed
that not only graphene is removed on the hot punched areas, but also the regions that are not
hot punched have remained intact. This means that with this approach, hot punching process
has not damaged the other areas of the graphene sheet. Except for the regions near edges
of the hot punched areas, which had led to formation of graphene micro-ribbons, the rest of
the structure of graphene sheet was preserved. Additionally, it was observed that graphene
is torn and cracked on its lattice special direction. The latter is a direct consequence of the
hot punching, and provided some new insight on mechanical behaviors of graphene. These
outcomes indeed lied within understanding and optimizing the parameters that are involved
in the hot punching process.
As an integral component of the hot punching of thermoplastic polymers, the PVA film
was characterized. The DSC results showed that the 30 µm commercial PVA films, on which
the CVD graphene was transferred, has a Tg of 61 ◦C. The Ni stamps which were used in
this study were characterized in statistically relative manners by using SEM and optical
profilometry techniques.
The influence of the parameters that are the corner stones of a desired hot punching
process were examined by designing a set of experiments. These parameters included the
height of Ni stamp structures, the molding temperature, and the molding pressure. It was
shown in order to acquire micro-patterns in graphene-PVA stacks, the height of the features
==PAGE:108==
90
Conclusion and Outlook
on the Ni stamp should be larger than the thickness of the PVA film. Designed experiments
concluded that hot punching graphene-PVA stacks is not possible at every temperature above
the Tg of PVA. Furthermore, it was observed and concluded that there is a specific range at
which PVA-graphene stacks can be micro-patterned by hot punching.
Finally, the micro- and nanostructures of micro-patterned CVD graphene sheets at differ-
ent hot punching conditions, was investigated by using the corresponding characterization
techniques for graphene. The characterization methods included Raman spectroscopy, AFM,
and TEM. 2-dimensional spatial Raman spectroscopy results confirmed that depending on
the hot punching conditions, graphene is successfully removed on the hot punched areas.
Moreover, Raman measurements showed that by increasing the pressure and temperature,
the induced strain in graphene increases, which leads to increasing its charge carrier density.
The induced strain was measured to have the largest value, when CVD graphene sheets are
hot punched with 75 µm tall Ni stamp, under the punching conditions of T=120 ◦C and
P=4 bar. The AFM results revealed that at the mentioned hot punching conditions, graphene
exhibits novel mechanical properties by forming graphene micro-ribbons. The TEM results
confirmed that the formed graphene ribbons have clear and disorder-free edges, implying
that graphene is cracked along the lattice preferred directions.
6.2
Outlook
The outcomes of this MSc. thesis can be extended to a wide range of future scientific
studies and industrial applications. The main scope of this MSc. was to prove the concept
of graphene micro-patterning with hot punching technique. Now that the feasibility of this
technique is proven, the followings can be considered as the future works for to continue this
study in larger extents.
Since the structure of the CVD graphene sheet has been preserved in the areas that
were not hot punched, the micro-patterned graphene sheets can be a candidate for for
device fabrications and further characterizations. For instance, some methods such as
micro-four point probe measurement and cyclic voltammetry, can be used to characterize
the electrical and electrochemical properties of hot punched CVD graphene sheets. By
using such characterization techniques, the applicability of the hot punched graphene sheets
for device fabrication would be assessed. The hot punched CVD graphene sheets can be
considered to fabricate devices such as sensors and antennas.
Owing to the other methods of hot punching such as roll-to-roll, fabricating of such
micro-patterns can be taken to a large-scale production, by increasing the rate of samples
==PAGE:109==

that are fabricated. It will be further intriguing to observed whether nano-patterning with this
technique would lead to similar results, on the nano-scale.
The fabricated ribbons still require a more thorough characterization by using TEM and
other electrical characterization techniques, such as Hall measurements. Moreover, by using
other shapes rather than circles, it should be assessed whether such formation of ribbons is
consistent and reproducible. For instance, if the ribbons are produced by using square shaped
stamps, then it could be possible to obtain a large array of ribbons, which are all oriented in
the same direction, normal to the edges of the micro-patterned areas. These investigations
require to be done, since such ribbons can possess the potential to be used in a wide range of
applications electrical devices. Additionally, a theoretical solution to the novel behavior of
the wrinkles, that was observed in both AFM and TEM images, would be able to provide a
thorough understanding of this physical phenomena.
