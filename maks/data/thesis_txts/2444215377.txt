Abstract
This Master's thesis details the implementation of an advanced model for predicting hydrogen-
assisted cracking in an Abaqus user element. The model makes use of the phase eld fracture
model, higher order strain gradient plasticity, and a stress-eld coupled, hydrogen transport
model. The eect of hydrogen on fracture is assumed to occur by the hydrogen-enhanced
decohesion mechanism. The quantitative degree of reduction for the cohesive energy is based
on quantum mechanical calculations from the literature.
A discussion of some of the numerical considerations for obtaining valid results with the
model is included. The implementation of a chemical boundary condition at the moving
crack surface is presented. In addition a discussion is included on obtaining fracture in agree-
ment with Grith's model for brittle fracture using the phase eld model.
The model has been utilized using the concluded best practice, to study crack growth. Quasi-
cleavage is a failure mode which is often seen in experiments when studying hydrogen assisted
cracking. However, few models are able to capture this failure mechanism at realistic ma-
terial parameters. The results presented here shows quasi-cleavage to be a plausible failure
mechanism, when plastic strain gradients and hydrogen embrittlement are considered.

Introduction
More than a hundred years have passed since the embrittling eect of hydrogen in metals was
rst studied in a scientic manner [1]. Since then, the eld of fracture mechanics has formed
and evolved, bringing insight regarding the most common failure mode in history. However,
environment-assisted cracking (EAC) remains a eld where very little is properly understood
and most models are highly empirical and require several parameters to be calibrated ex-
perimentally [24]. The simple reason for this is that EAC is highly complex, spans many
disciplines, and occurs through a varying degree of interaction between multiple mechanisms.
For example, some metals and environments might primarily see stress-corrosion cracking
(SCC), while others might be dominated by hydrogen embrittlement (HE). Within HE, some
metals such as titanium might form hydrides at grain boundaries, which can act as a brit-
tle fracture path, while others, such as steel seem to experience a reduction in the cohesive
strength of the lattice [5]. When no general way of predicting the strength or longevity of a
structure in an aggressive environment exists, designers are often forced to be overly conser-
vative and HE-related failures can still be observed due to unpredicted environmental eects.
Traditionally, hydrogen embrittlement has been an issue in the oil and gas industry, as well
as in ships and o-shore structures, where aggressive environments are present. But over the
last few decades, tremendous progress has been made in designing increasingly strong and
tough alloys. As the alloys get stronger, many also become more susceptible to hydrogen
embrittlement, which is now observed even at very low hydrogen concentrations, such as in
humid air.
Another eld where hydrogen embrittlement is of immense interest, is the eld of hydrogen
energy systems. While the need for reducing carbon emissions becomes increasingly clear,
hydrogen fuel and energy systems are becoming increasingly promising. Hydrogen energy
systems oer solutions for zero-emission combustion engines as well as energy storage for
sustainable energy sources, both of which are desperately needed. However, the deleterious
eect of hydrogen in metals is a serious challenge in developing and implementing these tech-
nologies and insucient understanding of hydrogen embrittlement can lead to catastrophic
failures in components which are exposed to highly concentrated hydrogen gas.
The main objective of this Master's thesis is to further the development of a mechanism-based
model for predicting the initiation and growth of cracks in structural elements exposed to
hydrogen. A framework which utilizes the rather novel and very general phase eld fracture

augments this framework with higher order strain gradient plasticity (SGP) to better pre-
dict crack tip stresses, which is vital for predicting hydrogen concentrations [7, 8] and the

remarks and suggestions for future improvements of the model are suggested.

Introduction
Hydrogen assisted cracking is a complex problem. At least three components are needed for a
HAC model for elastic-plastic materials: A hydrogen transport model, a deformation model,
describing elastic and plastic deformations and, nally, a fracture model. Several models
have already been developed with the goal of predicting crack growth in metals aected by
hydrogen embrittlement. Examples of such models may be found in [7,10,11], which include
dislocation based models, statistical weakest-link approaches and cohesive models. Gener-
ally, these models are unsuited for either the size or the complexity of engineering components.
In [6], a promising model for HAC was presented by Martínez-Pañeda and co-workers. This
model features a stress-eld coupled hydrogen transport model, which reduces the fracture
energy based on data from quantum-mechanical calculations. For a fracture model, [6] uses
the phase eld model of fracture, which is a promising new continuum damage model, based
on variational methods of energy minimization proposed by Francfort and Marigo [12].
The model by Martínez-Pañeda et al. was implemented for linear elasticity. For HAC in
metals, plasticity plays a signicant role. Since hydrogen embrittlement is most critical at
the crack tip and hydrogen transport is dependent on the stress eld, accurately predicting
the stresses in the crack tip region is critical. The present work incorporates higher order
strain gradient plasticity as presented in [13] and [14] to capture the micron-scale eects of
geometrically necessary dislocations (GNDs). GNDs aect work hardening and yield strength
in regions of high plastic strain gradients and must be considered to accurately determine the
stresses at the crack tip and in the fracture process zone. Thus, the present work incorporates
the phase-eld model of fracture, strain gradient plasticity, and a stress-eld coupled hydro-
gen transport model, similar to those in [6] and [11]. The result is an advanced model, which
incorporates several important physical mechanisms to accurately predict hydrogen-assisted
fracture.

advanced continuum model of damage under the inuence of hydrogen embrittlement. Sec-

1.1
The phase-eld model fracture model
When the goal is to predict structural failure, predicting crack growth is often paramount.
The work of Grith in 1921 [15] created a foundation for the theory of fracture in brit-
tle materials by considering the rst law of thermodynamics and concluding that unstable
fracture occurs when the rate of change in strain energy becomes greater that the surface
energy of the material. Later, many notable contributions were made to the eld of fracture
mechanics, here among the works of Irwin [16,17], some of which built upon the earlier work
of Westergaard [18]. These works extended the theory to consider plasticity, and made it
possible to estimate stresses and displacements near the crack tip.
In modern applications, fracture is often a problem which is approached numerically and as a
result, several numerical frameworks have been established for prediction of fracture. Finite
element methods are by far the most prevalent numerical approach to fracture and several
models have been proposed over the years, approaching the problem in dierent manners.
Succesful approaches include cohesive models [1921], which dene elements or interfaces
that act as nonlinear springs and local enrichment strategies [22,23], which allow for mesh-
independent crack growth. Finally, gradient damage models [2426] are also an established
approach, where the crack is approximated as a sharp gradient rather than a discontinuity.
The phase-eld model of fracture belongs in the latter category and is the model which will
be used here. The phase eld method is based on a variational formulation of energy mini-
mization rst proposed in [12], with important additions from Bourdin and co-workers [27]
and Miehe and co-workers [26,28]. The main advantage of the phase eld model is prediction
of crack growth of arbitrary topological complexity, including branching cracks and crack
initiation. This is a massive advantage, which is oered by few other fracture models. In ad-
dition, it can readily be combined with material models such as visco-elasticity and plasticity
and the phase-eld model has been succesfuly applied to large deformations analyses [29,30],
multi-physics applications [31,32] and dynamic analyses [33,34]. The wide array of applica-
tions prove that the phase-eld model is truly versatile and well-suited for a general model.
This section details the basic theory of the phase eld model, including the choice of a sti-
ness degradation function and a derivation of the phase eld balance equations.

1.2
Strain Gradient Plasticity
In the last 30 years, there has been signicant interest in modelling plastic behaviour at small
scales. This interest was sparked by a number of experiments, for example those by Nix and
Gao [38], Fleck and co-workers [39] and Stölken and Evana [40]. Those experiments found
that at suciently small scales, test samples undergoing non-uniform plastic deformation
showed increasing strength and hardening with decreasing size. The underlying mechanism
is tied to plastic strain gradients. A large gradient in plastic strains requires a high con-
centration of geometrically necessary dislocations (GNDs). While the presence of GNDs are
guaranteed by the presence of a plastic strain gradient, they do not directly contribute to
plastic strain, which is driven by randomly trapped dislocations, referred to as statistically
stored dislocations (SSDs). As a result, the presence of GNDs cause an elevated density of
dislocations for the amount of plastic strain, which means that the material work hardens
more than it otherwise would. These eects can be studied and predicted by several methods,
but only continuum level methods are viable for analysis of engineering components, due to
the computational requirements of, for example, dislocation dynamics. Continuum plasticity
theories that incorporate a length scale to account for these gradient eects are known as
strain gradient plasticity theories. Several formulations of strain gradient plasticity have been
proposed over the years. Examples of these theories are [13,4143], a good overview of the
current state-of-the-art can be found in [44].
Strain gradient plasticity is signicant for a number of applications, such as thin-lm appli-
cations and micro-electromechanical systems. The reason that it is important here, is that
it also predicts signicantly more accurate stress levels at crack tips, compared to linear
elasticity or conventional plasticity, by providing a more realistic description of hardening
behaviour. Accurate estimations of crack tip stresses is pivotal in the current model, as an
accurate prediction of stress levels is crucial in hydrogen embrittlement [7].
For this project, Gudmundson's model [13] is used. The model incorporates both energetic
and dissipative higher order stresses, to ensure that the model is thermodynamically consis-
tent. In addition, the model is formulated such that it can be implemented with relative ease
into a nite element framework. The implemenation here follows the one in [14].
This section serves to introduce the balance equations of the strain gradient plasticity model,
along with the formulation of the constitutive relations and the choice of a visco-plastic func-

1.3
Hydrogen embrittlement
Environment-assisted cracking (EAC) is a broad term, which includes a large number of
mechanisms through which engineering components fail prematurely. Most of these mech-
anisms are not fully understood and their individual importance is often unclear. Two of
the most studied mechanisms are stress-corrosion cracking [52, 53] and hydrogen embrittle-
ment [10,11], with the latter being the focus of this work.
Hydrogen embrittlement is signicant for a great number of industries and applications, such
as oshore structures, high-pressure pipelines and vessels for hydrogen energy applications,
and the oil and gas industry. Multiple mechanisms have been proposed as underlying mecha-
nisms for hydrogen embrittlement. Some of those which have received the most attention are
hydrogen-enhanced localized plasticity (HELP) [54,55], hydride formation [5] and hydrogen-
enhanced decohesion (HEDE) [11,56]. In this work, the latter is assumed to be the primary
mechanism. The reduction in cohesive energy is supported by quantum mechanical calcula-
tions, as is done for example in [5759]. These calculations will also be used to provide a
relation between hydrogen coverage and fracture surface energy for dierent metals.
In addition to determining a relation between the amount of interstitial sites which are occu-
pied by hydrogen and the subsequent reduction in cohesive energy, the transport of hydrogen
must also be described. Whether it is the diusion of hydrogen from the surrounding at-
mosphere or from other parts of the solid, transport of hydrogen must be included in the
model, as the transport is dependent on the stress-eld. This also indirectly introduces a rate
dependence to the model, as hydrogen cannot reach steady state concentrations near the tip
of a rapidly growing crack.
This section details the theory of modelling stress eld coupled hydrogen diusion in a solid
body. The approach to a continuum model for hydrogen diusion and embrittlement pre-
sented here is similar to the approach in [6]. Considerations regarding strain gradient plastic-
ity will be discussed in section 1.4. In addition transport of hydrogen, the subsequent eect
on fracture energy is considered in section 1.3.2.

Combining strain gradient plasticity and phase-eld fracture with hydrogen transport and

Boundary conditions
Working with a well-established commercial nite elements code such as ABAQUS comes
with the advantage that simple boundary conditions are easy to apply in the preprocessor
and require no special consideration besides whether the conditions are appropriate. In ad-
dition, they may be applied to any degree of freedom. However, some specialized boundary
conditions require more careful consideration and may have to be implemented either as an
independent user subroutine or directly inside the element. This section discusses the imple-
mentation and necessary considerations for chemical boundary conditions on newly formed
crack surface and boundary conditions on higher order plastic strains.
2.2.1
Chemical boundary conditions
When a crack grows, it is to be assumed that the surrounding uid or gas will come into
contact with the newly formed surface immediately. As a result, the newly formed crack
surface is promptly exposed to the conditions of the environment and should therefore, from
a modelling perspective, have appropriate boundary conditions assigned. However, tracking
the formation of crack surface can be a rather complex task, depending on the formulation of
the fracture model. In the case of the present model, no actual surface or boundary is formed,
as the phase eld model is a gradient damage model. Unlike actual boundaries however,
zones of damaged or broken material are simple to track within the element. A simple way
to implement a boundary condition for new crack surface, is to enforce it on each suciently
damaged integration point. Each point where φ is above a threshold value is prescribed a
hydrogen concentration, which correpsonds to the concentration of the environment. This
can be achieved in several ways. For this implementation, the penalty approach has been
chosen for its simplicity and versatility. The boundary condition is implemented by adding

Loading rate
Multiple rate dependencies have been built into the model and as a consequence, it is highly
important to consider the nature of these dependencies and how they are expressed in a given
model. For the purpose of this section, the rate dependent phase eld term is not considered,
as it only serves a regularizing function to assist convergence.
The rst rate dependence which should be discussed is the time dependence of hydrogen
transport. The diusion process is rather slow and for sub-critical loads, the rate of crack
growth may be limited by the rate of hydrogen transport towards the crack tip. The eective
hydrogen transport rate is controlled by the size of the specimen and the diusion coecient,
D. The time-dependence of hydrogen is directly controlled by the time-scale of the problem.
As such, it does not require the analyst to directly control for it, as it will yield the appropriate
response for the time-scale of the problem specied.
The other important rate dependency is the viscoplastic model which was introduced in
section (1.2.3).
This model has two parameters to control the rate dependency, namely
the reference strain rate, ˙ε0, and the rate-sensitivity parameter, m. The rate dependence
of the viscoplastic model can have a signicant impact on convergence and on the solution
itself. The strain rate near the crack-tip is highly non-linear. As a result, approaching rate
independent results may require allowing some rate dependence in the bulk of the model.
The rate dependence of the viscoplastic function can be controlled completely independently
from the rate dependence of hydrogen transport by changing ˙ε0 proportionally to any change
in time-scale to accommodate hydrogen transport. Due to the highly non-linear nature of the
eective strain rate, comparing the degree of rate dependency is not straight forward. The

3.1.4
Hydrogen embrittlement
Hydrogen is now introduced to the boundary layer model. The primary goal of this study is
to investigate the inuence of HEDE on fracture initiation and steady-state crack growth. It
is well known that hydrogen-assisted fracture occurs at sub-critical loads, but a quantitative
understanding of how sub-critical the loads can be is highly important.
All simulations are run such that the material is assumed to contain no hydrogen at the
start. The outer boundary has a prescribed ux ρ = 0, since the boundary layer represents a
small fraction of an innite plate. In addition, the outer boundary is far from the crack tip,
such that any boundary condition would have no noticeable eect. The prescribed chemical
potential condition from section 2.2 is active of the crack which is prescribed through the
phase eld. Due to issues with convergence, only the part of the crack closest to the tip is
prescribed through the phase eld, corresponding to the mixed crack in gure 2.4. When
hydrogen is included, the rate of transport is proportional to the hydrostatic stress. Because
of this, the choice of E becomes signicant. For these simulations, E = 210GPa is used. The
partial molar volume is assumed to be V H = 2000mm3/mol along with a diusion coecient
D = 0.0127mm2/s, which is tting for an iron-based material. Accordingly, χ = 0.89 is used.
Results will be normalized by the initial values of K0 and R0, as it is not meaningful to
attempt to report results relative to their eective values, which varies with the hydrogen
concentration in a non-linear manner.
Inuence of hydrogen concentration
Resistance curves are computed with varying concentrations of hydrogen in the environment.
The concentrations range from Cenv = 0.1 wppm, to Cenv = 1 wppm, which correpsonds to
a 3% NaCl solution. The computations are done with ℓ/R0 = 1/110, which implies a critical
stress level as one would expect in cleavage fracture. The plastic strain gradient sensitivity
is set as Lp = 0.001R0, which is a realistic value for a ductile steel. The simulations are
carried out over a timescale of 116 days, to ensure that equilibrium hydrogen concentrations
are obtained at all times.

The padeye is an engineering component which is frequently used for lifting in the oil and
gas industry. Padeyes are typically welded or bolted to a structure, to provide an attachment
point for lifting equipment. In this section, the capability of the phase eld fracture model
for capturing arbitrary crack growth in an engineering component is demonstrated, using the
example of a padeye.
Figure 3.8: Geometry of the padeye, with the boundary conditions applied
3.2.1
Model description
The geometry can be seen in gure 3.8. The lower boundary of the model in constrained in
both directions corresponding to a welded connection. To simulate a lifting situation, load
is prescribed on the hole in the padeye, where a shackle of wire might be attached. Ideally,
the load should be added as a surface traction resembling a Hertz pressure, which would
correspond to the contact pressure of a shackle being pulled. Unfortunately, a downside of
working within Abaqus is that user elements are not compatible with surface tractions. In-
stead, a displacement is prescribed to half of the hole. The displacement is prescribed to be
20 degrees o of vertical, to simulate an asymmetric load. Such a load gives rise to large
compressive stresses at the rim of the hole. Thus, for this problem, the decomposition of the
elastic strain energy, such that only tensile stresses drive the phase eld is critical.
Mesh
The mesh needs to be suciently ne to resolve the crack in all areas where it might extend
to. One way of achieving this is to use a very ne mesh for most of the model. A computa-
tionally cheaper approach is to rene the mesh in the area where crack growth is expected,
and update the mesh based on the trajectory of the crack in the previous simulation. Here,
the second approach is used. The nal mesh can be seen along with the results in gure 3.9.
The mesh uses approximately 20000 elements. It is ensured that the crack does not reach
the edges of the rened zones, such that results are aected.

CONCLUSION
Conclusion
A novel mechanism-based model for predicting hydrogen assisted cracking based on the
hydrogen-enhanced decohesion mechanism has been presented and implemented. The model
incorporates hydrogen transport, the phase-eld fracture model, and higher-order strain gra-
dient plasticity. The resulting model accurately predicts stresses and hydrogen concentrations
at the crack tip, to predict crack growth in a physically sound manner.
The current implementation of the model has been applied to a boundary layer model, to
supplement previous ndings in the literature [14,68,71] on the role of elastic and plastic ma-
terial parameters in fracture. It is concluded that quasi-cleavage, as is seen in experiments,
is a plausible failure mechanism in a ductile metal, if it is exposed to even low concentrations
of hydrogen. The stress levels required for quasi-cleavage are reached by the joint action of
plastic strain gradients and hydrogen. The strain gradients increase the stresses in the plastic
zone, while the hydrogen reduces the required load level for fracture.
Finally, the capabilities of the phase eld model for predicting arbitrary crack growth were
shown, by simulating asymmetrical loading till failure on a padeye. Padeyes are a common
engineering component in the oil and gas industry and in oshore applications, where aggres-
sive environments are common. A realistic crack path was obtained, featuring simultaneous
growth of two cracks.
Predicting simultaneous crack growth in directions which are not
known in advance is something which most fracture models are unable to do. This makes
the phase eld fracture model uniquely qualied for a general predictive model for HAC.

FURTHER WORK
Further work
An important addition to the current model would be to lower or remove the sensitivity to the
increment size, which may require introducing a fully implicit solution scheme. The reason
such a solution strategy was not used in the rst place is that it is far less robust and may
not converge as easily [28]. This drawback would in that case have to be addressed as well.
While it is easy to minimize the inuence of increment sensitivity, it takes many increments,
which would not otherwise be needed. More importantly, it may lead a careless analyst to
signicantly overestimate the steady state fracture toughness of a structure.
The stress degradation function should be modied to always preserve compressive stresses
and degrade tensile ones. The current implementation is not suitable for predicting hydro-
static stresses for problems which include both compressive stresses and hydrogen. Such a
stress degradation can be introduced by calculating the stresses as follows:

Appendix B: Usage instructions
The purpose of this section is to provide an overview of the inputs and variables of the custom
element user subroutine that has been implemented here. In addition peculiarities regarding
its use in ABAQUS are discussed.
The element is organized in nodal block structure, meaning that the seven dofs of the rst
node are the rst seven dofs of the element and the dofs of the second node are the subsequent
seven dofs of the element. This is in contrast to an element block structure where for example
the displacement degrees of freedom of all nodes are listed rst, then the plastic strain and
so forth. It must be noted that ABAQUS is capable of working with a mixed form of these
structures.
