Abstract 
 
As the full-scale commercial release of 5G mobile networks is a few years around the corner, 
there is a growing need for new technologies that will offer substantial capacity enhancements 
to the existing networks and help in meeting the high 5G requirements that are driven by the 
constantly increasing mobile traffic volume and number of connected devices. 
Non-Orthogonal Multiple Access is an alternative to the existing multiple access schemes that 
shows great potential in terms of network capacity and spectral efficiency. NOMA is based on 
multiplexing several users into the same resource block (non-orthogonality) and takes advantage 
of high channel gain differences between users. It can result in increased total data rate without 
requiring additional spectrum or other resources. 
A simulation model was implemented for this project using the MATLAB environment, in order 
to investigate and quantify NOMA’s performance gain over traditional orthogonal multiple access 
schemes. While varying the power allocation technique and the user pairing algorithm, results 
showed that NOMA provides significant throughput gain over a traditional OMA scheme, both in 
single cell and full scale network scenarios.

1 Introduction 
 
The sheer volume of mobile data traffic, driven by the increasing number of active subscriptions 
and the rising average data volume per subscription, is putting a strain on modern mobile 
networks. This tendency is primarily fueled by the constant demand for viewing and streaming 
video content on mobile devices. According to Ericsson’s 2018 Q3 mobility report [1], mobile data 
traffic grew by almost 80% on a yearly basis, which was the highest growth since 2013. This trend 
is expected to continue in the near future. 
 
Figure 1: Yearly Mobile Data Traffic [1] 
As mentioned above, the primary factor responsible for augmenting the mobile data traffic is the 
number of mobile subscriptions. As of November 2018, there were 7.9 billion active mobile 
subscriptions worldwide, with 120 million of those being added in most recent quarter. The 
countries with the most new subscriptions were China and India. 
Due to the nature of the content (mostly video and streaming), there is a constant shift to newer 
technologies that can facilitate the transmission of larger amount of data. 5G is currently being 
standardized and some service providers have already launched 5G services. It is expected that 
==PAGE:10==
2 
 
it will keep gaining momentum and full-scale deployments will start taking place in 2020. By the 
end of 2024, 1.5 billion 5G subscriptions are projected for enhanced mobile broadband. 
 
Figure 2: Mobile Subscriptions by Technology (in billions) [1] 
Based on the aforementioned trends, it is of paramount importance to develop and implement 
mobile network enhancements that will enable operators to provide the required services and 
meet the ever-growing QoS demands. 
Conceptually, the main part of a mobile network where such enhancements are possible is the 
radio access network (RAN), between the user equipment and the core network. One of the 
major factors that impact the network’s capacity is the employed multiple access (MA) scheme. 
The MA scheme essentially decides how the network resources are distributed to the users, 
which in consequence affects the bandwidth allocated to each user. 
There has been a lot of ongoing research and developments suggesting that Non-Orthogonal 
Multiple Access (NOMA) is the most promising MA option for the future 5G mobile networks. 
Unlike the most recent mobile technologies, NOMA allows for multiplexing several users in the 
same resource block, leading to higher data rates and better utilization of the available spectrum. 
Nevertheless, there are still several issues that need to be addressed and further specifications 
are required on how to best implement NOMA as a 5G feature. There is thus, a need for a detailed 
investigation of any parameters that might affect NOMA’s performance and a way to tackle any 
challenges that NOMA poses for existing network infrastructures.

2 From 4G to 5G: Requirements and Expectations 
 
As mentioned in the introduction, the constant rise of the traffic carried by modern mobile 
networks, combined with the ever-increasing number of connected devices, is pushing the 
existing infrastructures to the limit. As these trends continue, it is expected that in the near future 
the existing 4G mobile networks will be unable to meet the demands for digital content and 
mobile services. With the 5G standardization being an ongoing process, there is a lot of discussion 
over what the expectations and the formal requirements of future mobile networks should be. 
One of the corners of the infamous triangle that portrays the requirements and use cases of 5G 
mobile communications (depicted in figure 4, based on [3]) is dedicated to enhanced mobile 
broadband. It is therefore important to explore and develop techniques and methods that enable 
improvements on the capacity of the existing mobile networks. These improvements frequently 
take place in the radio access network (RAN) and one feasible way of implementing them is by 
modifying the employed multiple access scheme of the network.

2.1 Mobile Network Architectures and Radio Access 
 
The architecture of the modern 4G mobile networks, specified by in 3GPP release 10 [4] for LTE-
A, is depicted in figure 5. It consists of two core areas, the radio access (evolved UMTS terrestrial 
radio access network, E-UTRAN) and the cellular core network (evolved packet core, EPC). The 
eNodeBs located in the RAN are interconnected via the X2 interface. They also have backhaul 
connections to nodes in the EPC, known as the S1 interface. 
 
Figure 5: 4G Mobile Network Architecture 
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
The radio access network is responsible for all radio-related functionalities, such as the 
scheduling, radio resource handling and allocation, MIMO schemes, retransmissions and coding. 
On the other hand, most back-end network functions occur in the core network, including 
authentication, end-to-end connection setup, billing, handover assist to eNodeBs etc. 
In regards to the 5G network architecture, its standardization is still an ongoing process that is 
not yet fully finalized. The 3GPP technical specification that monitors it is TS 23.501 [5]. The core 
network (EPC) remains similar to 4G, with the main difference being the interconnections 
between various internal nodes. 
The interesting change for the scope of this project lies in the RAN part of the network. 5G 
introduces the concept of New Radio (NR). The basic principle for the future 5G architecture is 
heterogeneity. The multi-connectivity and backwards-compatibility of 5G will facilitate the 
coexistence of 5G NR, 4G and other access technologies (e.g. Wi-Fi). 
Due to the impending network densification, especially in urban areas, further deployment of 
cells will be needed for 5G, in order to meet the growing coverage and capacity needs. As finding 
a place for deploying new cells is becoming increasingly difficult, it is anticipated that network 
multi-layering will grow in popularity, with a full-scale layer of small cells being added on top of 
the existing macro cell architecture. 
This creates a new issue for operators, whether the new cells will be deployed in-band (operating 
in the same frequency as the macro cells) or out-of-band (different frequency from macro cells). 
While the first scenario might appear simpler and would not require additional spectrum, it 
would demand more sophisticated and efficient interference mitigation and coordination 
techniques to combat the added inter-cell interference. The second scenario, which looks more 
promising for 5G, allows the utilization of new frequency bands (e.g. millimeter waves – 
mmWaves) and has the added benefit of possibly separating the control and the user plane [6].

2.2 Multiple Access Schemes and Spectral Efficiency 
 
One of the factors that directly impact the capacity when designing a mobile network is the 
multiple access scheme (MA). MA techniques are employed in order to allocate the available 
resources of a mobile network to multiple users/devices in the most efficient way, with the goal 
of maximizing the network capacity, both in terms of data rates and number of users serviced. 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
Another important concept when it comes to increasing a network’s capacity is that of spectral 
efficiency. Increasing the spectrum that a mobile network operates on usually directly increases 
the capacity. However, spectrum is a limited and rather expensive resource for network 
operators to acquire. It is thus important to employ a multiple access scheme that can increase 
the network capacity by efficiently utilizing the available spectrum [2]. 
MA schemes can be categorized into orthogonal and non-orthogonal approaches. In orthogonal 
multiple access techniques (OMA), the different user signals are orthogonal to each other, which 
means that their cross-correlation is zero. Classic examples of such methods include time-division 
multiple access (TDMA) and frequency-division multiple access (FDMA). These two were 
prevalent during the first generations of mobile networks, while orthogonal frequency-division 
multiple access (OFDMA) is currently used in most 4G networks. OMA schemes have the 
advantage of no intra-cell interference. However, they require careful cell planning and sufficient 
distance between reused channels to mitigate inter-cell interference. This results in low spectral 
efficiency. 
On the other hand, non-orthogonal multiple access schemes allow for non-zero cross-correlation 
between different user signals. A classic example of a known non-orthogonal MA scheme is code-
division multiple access (CDMA), which is used in 3G mobile networks. NOMA techniques, unlike 
their orthogonal counterparts, are very robust against fading and inter-cell interference. They 
are, however, prone to intra-cell interference [7]. 
The main advantage of NOMA over orthogonal multiple access techniques is the higher spectral 
efficiency it provides. Allowing the operators to multiplex two or more users in the same resource 
block leads to increased network capacity, without the need for additional spectrum. This 
establishes NOMA as an attractive solution for satisfying the 5G mobile network requirements 
and will be further analyzed in the next chapter.

2.3 Chapter Summary 
 
The growing mobile data traffic demands along with the increasing number of connected devices 
are putting a strain on modern mobile networks. As the transition from 4G to 5G is expected to 
fully materialize in the near future, the requirements and expectations of the 5G technologies 
are currently being specified and standardized. Network improvements and enhancements are 
definitely needed to meet the new coverage and capacity demands. 
Altering the basic architecture of mobile networks is a prime way to boost the network´s 
capabilities. Although the core network is not expected to change drastically between 4G and 5G, 
the radio access part will experience significant adjustments. 
As 5G will support vastly different types of devices and has to facilitate high data rates for most 
of them, the 5G RAN will be a heterogeneous network. It will consist of different layers, tiers and 
cell sizes, operating on different frequency bands. Along with this network densification, new and 
promising technologies are needed to help coping with the extended traffic demands and 
increase the network capacity. 
One such technology is NOMA, which appears to be a high-potential alternative to the traditional 
multiple access schemes that are employed in the existing mobile networks. By facilitating non-
orthogonality between resources allocated to different users, NOMA provides very high spectral 
efficiency, directly enabling network capacity increases without requiring additional spectrum.

3 Theory behind NOMA and Core Principles 
 
Apart from increased spectral efficiency, NOMA has several significant benefits that make it 
desirable for 5G networks. Those include higher cell-edge throughput, relaxed channel feedback, 
low transmission latency and massive connectivity. Additionally, it can achieve a good operating 
point where both spectral and energy efficiency can be optimized [8]. 
Two main categories of NOMA have been broadly defined in the literature: power-domain and 
code-domain NOMA. In the former the signal of each multiplexed UE is separated in the power 
domain while in the latter, the multiplexed signals are differentiated by using user-specific 
spreading codes. The core principles of power-domain NOMA will be analyzed in the rest of this 
chapter, while code-domain NOMA schemes are briefly investigated in chapter 4. 
 
3.1 The Primary NOMA Concept 
 
As mentioned earlier, the core concept of NOMA is multiplexing UEs in the power domain, 
something that has not been adequately explored in the previous generations (TDMA, FDMA, 
CDMA, OFDMA).  
In orthogonal MA schemes transmissions are usually done with full power but split bandwidth, 
meaning that UEs are multiplexed in the time and frequency domains but resource blocks always 
get allocated in their entirety. This often essentially results in wasted resources due to the 
quantized nature of the allocated power, which usually depends on the channel gain of the UE 
(for LTE it depends on CQI – Channel Quality Indicator). NOMA, however, allows for overcoming 
this issue [9]. 
NOMA lifts the necessity for orthogonality in the resources (time, frequency, spreading codes) of 
the network.  The main principle in NOMA is to select UEs with high difference in channel 
conditions and multiplex in the same time/frequency resource block, but with different 
transmission power levels. This lets UEs with high channel conditions access the resources 
allocated to UEs with poor channel conditions, and therefore increasing the spectral efficiency 
and the overall capacity of the system [10]. 
A comparison between the user multiplexing in OMA and NOMA is depicted in figure 7. 
Superposition transmission is used on the transmitter side to multiplex the signals from different 
UEs and the power of each UE is defined based on dynamic power allocation techniques. The 
level of the allocated power depends on the channel conditions of each UE. The UEs with high 
channel gain get the larger fraction of the total power dedicated to each resource block. Despite 
the fact that power-sharing reduces the power allocated to each multiplexed UE, the overall 
bandwidth they have access to is higher as they are being scheduled more frequently [11]. 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
 
Figure 7: UE Multiplexing, OMA vs NOMA 
On the receiver side, successive interference cancellation (SIC) techniques are used to separate 
the signals and mitigate intra-cell interference. Even though the number of multiplexed users per 
resource block is not restricted, most of the related literature emphasizes in two-user NOMA 
methods. This is because intra-cell interference is proportional to the number of multiplexed UEs 
and additionally, the decoding of the signal on the receiver is significantly more complex when 
more than two users are multiplexed, hindering the performance of the network. Superposition 
transmission and successive interference cancellation are analyzed in depth in the following 
sections.

receiver. Once the signal is received, the far UE, UE2, simply decodes the stronger signal, whereas 
the near UE, UE1, applies SIC before decoding its signal [6].

3.4 Standardization 
 
As is the case for most of the 5G technologies that are currently under development, NOMA has 
not been yet fully standardized. However, the more primitive version of it that was mentioned in 
section 3.2, MUST, was standardized for LTE and its specifications are included in the 3GPP 
technical report TR 36.859 [14].  
Three different network deployment scenarios were targeted, varying between homogeneous 
and heterogeneous networks, with in-band and out-of-band deployment. Additionally, three 
==PAGE:23==
15 
 
different categories of MUST schemes were proposed with varying power ratio, gray mapping 
and label-bit assignment. 
After defining the metrics for evaluating MUST in system-level, it was concluded that it can 
significantly increase system capacity and improve user experience. It is more beneficial in a high 
traffic load environment and users in the cell edge experience bigger benefits. 
 
 
Figure 9: MUST Network Deployment Scenarios 
==PAGE:24==
16 
 
Although NOMA is not fully standardized for 5G, 3GPP maintains another technical report, TR 
38.812 [15] that includes any available NOMA specifications for 5G and gathers all technical 
outcome of the study on NOMA´s performance in 5G NR. 
Three different deployment scenarios are defined. The mMTC (massive Machine-Type 
Communication) scenario includes a huge connection number, low control signaling overhead 
and both synchronous and asynchronous transmission. The URLLC (Ultra Reliable Low Latency 
Communication) scenario requires both high reliability and low latency for the transmission, 
which is again both synchronous and asynchronous. The eMBB (enhanced Mobile Broadband) 
scenario includes grant-based and grant-free transmissions, while the latter can be synchronous 
or asynchronous. It is no coincidence that these three scenarios correspond to the three corners 
of the 5G requirements triangle mentioned in chapter 2. 
After defining evaluation metrics both in terms of link level and system level, NOMA is studied in 
the aspects of transmitter side processing, receiver complexity, related procedure and 
performance assessments. Some early simulations results that are included in the report appear 
very promising, particularly for the enhanced mobile broadband case.

resource blocks, NOMA can prove effective in overload scenarios (which are very likely in 5G 
networks), as it can facilitate the “extra” users without requiring additional spectrum or

Furthermore, it can support massive connectivity, another one of the three main 5G 
requirements. Due to NOMA’s nature, the number of scheduled UEs is not directly limited by the

orthogonal MA schemes in the same time period, NOMA can increase the number of served UEs 
within one sub-frame, again, without additional resources. 
Lastly, as discussed in section 3.1, NOMA takes advantage of the heterogeneity that traditionally 
appears in wireless channels. In most mobile networks so far, this attribute proves to be a great 
challenge for operators, as it makes loss modelling and resource management significantly 
harder. In NOMA´s case though, this attribute is essentially a privilege. 
Nevertheless, implementing NOMA involves trade-offs and comes at a cost, mainly due to the 
loss of orthogonality and the user multiplexing. Power allocation and user pairing are two factors 
that greatly affect the final outcome of NOMA and require specific and dynamic 
implementations. This, however, usually comes at the cost of increased computational 
complexity, which puts more strain on the network. 
These methods also require detailed and accurate knowledge of the channel. This is not always 
a feasible task in modern high-speed scenarios and reduces the cases where NOMA is applicable. 
Additional considerations are needed for newly-imposed interference. Although inter-cell 
interference is minimal with NOMA, mitigation techniques (i.e. ICIC, COMP) are still required. 
Intra-cell interference heavily depends on the success of SIC, which is a technique that becomes 
increasingly complex for the high channel gain users. 
Finally, security can also prove to be a concern with NOMA, since the signals of the low channel 
gain users are also transmitted to the high channel gain users due to the nature of superposition 
transmission. Traditional security methods (such as encryption) can help mitigate this problem 
[9].

3.6 Chapter Summary 
 
NOMA appears to be a promising solution for multiple access in the future 5G networks. It 
provides higher spectral efficiency and increased capacity by multiplexing different users in the 
same resource blocks. The key principle it is based on is that the users need to have a large 
difference in their channel gain. Superposition transmission is applied on the transmitter by 
combining the signals. On the receiver side, SIC is applied in order to decode and isolate the 
different signals. 
Although an earlier version of NOMA, MUST, was standardized for LTE, the 5G standardization 
for NOMA is currently under way and not yet complete. NOMA has advantages that facilitate 
many 5G requirements including enhanced mobile broadband and massive connectivity. 
However, it also encompasses challenges that need to be tackled, mainly related to power 
allocation and user pairing, two factors that will be further analyzed in the next chapter.

4 Power and Code Domain NOMA Techniques 
 
Due to the nature of transmission and reception in power domain NOMA that was analyzed in 
chapter 3, the two factors that have potentially the biggest impact on the system’s performance 
is power allocation and the user pairing scheme. 
The selection of the power ratio directly affects the data rates of the multiplexed UEs. The power 
allocated to each UE affects not only its own data rate, but also those of the UEs it is multiplexed 
with, due to the nature of superposition transmission and successive interference cancellation. 
The two general categories of power allocation techniques are static and dynamic power 
allocation. In static power allocation, a certain value of transmission power is allocated to each 
UE based on some predefined coefficients (usually a fraction of the total available power). On 
the other hand, when dynamic power allocation is used, the power allocated to each UE is 
different for every pair and usually depends on the SINR difference between the two UEs. 
Similarly, the selection of what UEs should be multiplexed together is not always clear. Apart 
from following the core principle of pairing users with large difference in channel conditions, 
there are additional considerations about how to maximize the total achievable capacity of a 
system and how to perform user pairing in an efficient but also non-complex way. Another 
important condition that needs to be met is that the data rate of each user when using NOMA 
should be higher than if OMA was used (both for the high channel-gain and the low channel-gain 
UE in each pair). 
Based on these contemplations, power allocation and user pairing are not easily standardized 
and are usually implementation specific. There is a lot of ongoing research and discussion in the 
literature in regards to these two issues. Some of the suggested methods and algorithms are 
summarized in the next two sections.

The following three methods are presented in [11] under the broader category of multi-user

allocated to any user affects the throughput of not only that user, but also of those he is

 Full Search Power Allocation (FSPA) 
This technique is essentially an exhaustive search of user pairs and transmit power allocations, 
which achieve the best performance of NOMA. All possible combinations of power allocations 
are considered for each user. FSPA has very high computational complexity, rendering it 
unusable. This method also does not take into account user pairing and more specifically, the fact 
that users with large channel gain difference are paired with higher probability in NOMA. It is 
therefore more effective to follow a dynamic and simplified power allocation scheme. 
 Fractional Transmit Power Allocation (FTPA) 
In order to reduce the computational complexity, this suboptimal fractional transmit power 
allocation algorithm is proposed, that is similar to the transmission power control method used 
for the uplink channel in LTE. The basic principle here is that transmission power allocated to 
each user is dependent on a decay factor. This is essentially an optimization parameter that is 
decided a priori by computer simulations that attempt to maximize the target performance 
evaluation metric. 
 Pre-defined user grouping and per-group fixed power allocation (FPA) 
This method entails a user grouping and pairing algorithm. The users are divided into different 
user groups according to their channel gains. In this pre-defined user grouping, the users can be 
paired together only if they belong to different user groups. Dynamic power allocation according 
to channel conditions of multiplexed users achieves the best performance because of its efficient 
utilization of the power resources. With the pre-defined user grouping, however, power 
allocation can be simplified even further by applying fixed power assignments to users belonging 
to the same group. Pre-defined user grouping and fixed power allocation can effectively decrease 
the amount of downlink signaling related to NOMA. For example, the order of successive 
interference cancellation (SIC) and information on power assignment do not need to be 
transmitted in every subframe but rather on a longer time scale.

The performance of proportional fairness (PF) in NOMA is discussed in [17]. The objective of PF 
is to maximize the long-term averaged user rates, thus targeting long-term fairness. The PF 
scheduler gives each user a priority inversely proportional to its average throughput, and since 
users’ instantaneous rates are not considered in the scheduling metric, some users do not gain 
access to available resources whenever their historical rates are high. This can be problematic for 
demanding applications. Moreover, the PF scheduler does not take advantage of the fact that 
the sum rate of NOMA systems increases when increasing the difference in channel gains of 
paired users. An allocation technique that attempts to address these issues is proposed, based 
on suboptimal power allocation using an iterative waterfilling process. 
Lastly, a novel power allocation algorithm is proposed in [18] that attempts to fully exploit the 
characteristics of a NOMA-based cognitive radio network to improve its performance. The users 
are separated into primary and secondary. The algorithm is separated into two parts. The first 
one ensures the QoS requirements of primary users by setting a total power value for all the 
secondary users (and thus ensuring the interference to the primary users is kept below a 
maximum tolerable level). The second part conducts power allocation of the available total to all 
the secondary users with the goal of maximizing the number of total admitted secondary users.

4.2 User Pairing Approaches and Algorithms 
 
The impact of user pairing on NOMA performance is examined in [19]. Fixed power allocation 
NOMA (F-NOMA) and cognitive-radio-inspired NOMA (CR-NOMA) are examined in comparison 
to conventional MA schemes. The probability that NOMA achieves a lower total sum rate 
compared to traditional MA is used as a performance metric, after conducting user pairing by 
adhering to the principle discussed in chapter X, which suggests pairing users with high difference 
in channel gain. The results show that both investigated NOMA techniques provide significantly 
larger sum data rates compared to orthogonal multiple access, especially when the user pairing 
meets the suggested criteria. 
Conventional near-far user pairing for NOMA is evaluated in [20]. The following user pairing 
schemes are proposed after concluding that any pairing scheme employed should be based on 
maximizing the overall sum of in-pair users channel gain differences for all the pairs and that the 
channel gain difference between any two in-pair users should be greater than a threshold. 
 Uniform channel gain difference pairing 
Uniform channel gain difference (UCGD) pairing focuses on accommodating the cell mid users by 
maintaining a relatively UCGD between in-pair users of all pairs, assuming two users per pair. 
Channel gains of users present in the cell are divided into two groups, followed by inter-group 
paring in such a way that cell mid users are well accommodated. UCGD pairing does not focus on 
pairing users located in the cell center and cell edge with each other. The technique actually finds 
users from the cell mid that can be paired with either the cell center or cell edge users. In this 
way, the proposed technique accommodates these mid users by efficiently pairing them with cell 
center and cell edge users, thereby allowing them to avail high capacity gains provided by NOMA 
and still avoiding or minimizing the SIC performance issue.

 Hybrid pairing 
Hybrid user pairing scheme follows conventional near–far pairing for the extreme end users with 
high channel gain differences, but when the channel gain difference between users starts to 
decrease, it switches to the UCGD pairing. This scheme continues to achieve the same data rates 
for extreme low-gain and high-gain users as in conventional pairing while still being able to 
minimize the mid users’ issue by making some trade-offs.

 M-users UCGD pairing 
The main contribution in [20] is a generalized M-user pairing model that extends the concepts of 
UCGD pairing, where M represents the number of users in each pair. This model provides a basis 
for pairing the maximum number of users together, while keeping the threshold conditions 
satisfied. It constitutes an example of multi-user NOMA, which will be further analyzed in section 
4.3. 
The aforementioned user pairing algorithms are the ones that were implemented for the 
simulation part of this project (with the exception of M-users UCGD pairing). They will be 
examined in detail in chapter 6. 
The work in [6] is based on dividing the users of the system in two groups, one that includes users 
that were selected by the scheduler to transmit, and one that includes those that need resources 
but were not allocated any during the pre-scheduling. A cost matrix between the users of the 
two groups is computed that is based on their SINR difference and theoretical achievable data 
rates with NOMA. Four different user pairing algorithms are then proposed: 
 Hungarian pairing 
This method reduces the user pairing problem to running a combinatorial optimization algorithm 
used for solving a two-sided one-to-one matching problem. The complexity of this algorithm is 
==PAGE:32==
24 
 
calculated as the number of iterations needed to find the optimal pairing. The execution of each 
step defined by the algorithm is considered an iteration. 
 Gale-Shapley pairing 
The Gale-Shapley algorithm uses the stable marriage criterion to find stable pairs. The complexity 
of this algorithm is calculated as the iterations for the user ranking plus the number of proposals 
done (accepted and rejected) until the stable solution is found. The solution provided by the Gale-
Shapley algorithm is always stable, but not necessarily optimal (unlike with the Hungarian 
pairing). 
 Random and exhaustive pairing 
In the random method, users from one group simply choose the best unpaired user from the 
other group based on the costs of the available pairs. The complexity of this method is calculated 
as the number of iterations needed until all the pairs have been found. This approach has 
obviously very low complexity but there are no guarantees for finding the optimal solution. 
In the exhaustive pairing, all possible pairs are evaluated to find the combination of pairs that 
yields the minimum total cost. The solution derived from this method is always optimal. However, 
its complexity makes is computationally expensive and non-feasible in a realistic network 
scenario. Each iteration corresponds to the evaluation of one of the possible permutations.

4.4 Code Domain NOMA 
 
Up until now, NOMA has been investigated only in the power-domain, by examining its basic 
principles and applications. Even though most of the recent literature focuses on power-domain 
NOMA and considers it to be the optimal multiple access scheme for future 5G networks, there 
has also been ongoing research and developments in code-domain NOMA (CD-NOMA). 
CD-NOMA uses spreading sequences to spread the data of each user over multiple subcarriers to 
realize overloading. It can be also transformed to codebook-based schemes, in which the 
codewords can be generated by those spreading sequences known by both the transmitter and 
receivers [22] 
==PAGE:34==
26 
 
In other words, code domain multiplexing, similarly to how CDMA works in 3G networks, is 
assigning different spreading codes to different users and then multiplexing them on the same 
time/frequency resources. Unlike power domain multiplexing, code domain multiplexing can 
achieve certain spreading gain and shaping gain at the cost of additional signal bandwidth [23]. 
In the rest of this chapter, the code-domain multiple access schemes that have gained the most 
attention recently will be briefly analyzed. 
 
4.5 Low-Density Spreading CDMA 
 
The basic concept behind LDS-CDMA is to use sparse spreading sequences (unlike the dense ones 
used in traditional CDMA) to reduce the interference at each chip. In this way, LDS-CDMA can 
improve the system performance by efficiently decreasing the interference between multiple 
users with appropriate spreading sequence design, and overloading can be achieved. 
At the receiver, a message passing algorithm (MPA) can be used to realize multiuser detection 
(MUD). MPA is very efficient for the factor graph, which is a bipartite graph including variable 
nodes and factor nodes as illustrated in figure 14. Messages are passed between variable nodes 
and factor nodes over edges, which can be interpreted as the soft values that represent the 
reliability of the symbol associated with each edge. The marginal distribution of a variable node 
can be regarded as a function of the messages received by that node [23].

4.6 Sparse Code Multiple Access 
 
SCMA is an enhanced version of LDS-CDMA. Unlike the latter, SCMA directly maps different 
bitstreams to different sparse codewords, where each user has a predefined codebook. This 
method is depicted for six users in figure 15. All codewords in the same codebook contain zeros 
in the same two dimensions, and the positions of zeros in different codebooks are distinct to 
make possible the collision avoidance between any two users. For each user, two bits are mapped 
to a complex codeword. Codewords for all users are multiplexed over four shared orthogonal 
resources (for instance OFDM subcarriers). 
 
 
Figure 15: SCMA Encoding and Multiplexing [23] 
The key difference between LDS-CDMA and SCMA is that a multi-dimensional constellation for 
SCMA is designed to generate codebooks, which introduces the aforementioned shaping gain 
that is not possible for LDS. It refers to the gain in the average symbol energy when the shape of 
a constellation is changed. In general, the shaping gain is higher when the shape of a constellation 
is closer to a sphere, and the maximum achievable shaping gain by the optimization of a multi-
dimensional constellation is 1.53 dB. For the concatenated approach in high modulation order, 
the multi-dimensional constellation can be optimized to obtain shaping gain, and then codebooks 
are generated based on the multi-dimensional constellation [23, 24]. 
 
4.7 Multi-User Shared Access 
 
In the uplink MUSA system shown in figure 16, each user randomly picks a spreading sequence 
from a selected pool. Each user may also use different spreading sequences for different symbols, 
and further enhance the performance through interference averaging. All spreading symbols are 
==PAGE:36==
28 
 
then transmitted over the same time/frequency resources. The spreading sequences should have 
low cross-correlation. At the receiver, codeword-level SIC is used to separate the data coming 
from each user. 
 
 
Figure 16: Uplink MUSA System [23] 
 
In downlink MUSA, users are separated into groups. In each group, symbols from each user are 
mapped to different constellations in a way that ensures Gray mapping in the combined 
constellation of superposed signals. The combined constellation is determined by the modulation 
order of each user and the transmit power partition between multiplexed users. Orthogonal 
sequences can be used to spread the superposed symbols to get time or frequency diversity gain. 
Gray mapping of the combined constellation reduces the reliance on advanced receivers, so less 
processing- intensive receivers such as symbol-level SIC can be used [23].

4.8 Chapter Summary 
 
The two primary factors that affect the performance of power domain NOMA are arguably the 
power allocation technique employed and the user pairing algorithm. It is up to the mobile 
operator to select a desirable approach, as each method entails several trade-offs in terms of 
outcome, and there is no definitive standardization as of yet. 
The two main power allocation categories are static and dynamic PA. In static PA, each user in a 
NOMA pair receives a predefined fraction of the total available power. On the other hand, 
dynamic PA allows for different power ratios in each NOMA pair, which usually depend on the 
channel gain difference between the two users of the pair. Additional considerations when 
deciding how to allocate power in NOMA involve fairness and QoS requirements (minimum data 
rate for every user). 
User pairing can also be done based on a variety of different approaches. The common 
denominator between all of them is the basic NOMA principle, which exploits the channel gain 
differences between users to increase the total network capacity. Some algorithms attempt to 
maximize the channel gain difference of each pair, which results in higher data rates for a certain 
number of users. Others, maintain uniformity between all pairs to ensure each user accomplishes 
a certain data rate threshold. Regardless of the selected approach, the employed algorithm 
should also account for complexity, as the scheduling and resource allocation process happens 
really fast in a real mobile network. 
Unlike in the power domain, code domain NOMA is based on assigning different spreading codes 
to different users and multiplexing them on the same resource block, similarly to how CDMA 
works in 3G mobile networks. Code domain multiplexing achieves spreading and shaping gain at 
the cost of additional signal bandwidth. 
Some of the traditional code domain NOMA techniques are LDS-CDMA, SCMA and MUSA. LDS-
CDMA uses sparse (low-density) spreading sequences to reduce the interference at chip. SCMA 
employs a direct mapping of different bitstreams to sparse codewords, while each user has a 
predefined codebook. In MUSA, each user transmits after randomly picking a spreading sequence 
from a selected pool. SIC is applied at the receiver on a codeword-level to separate and isolate 
the data originating from each different user.

5.1 Conceptual Model Analysis 
 
A single cell model was selected as the cornerstone of the overall analysis, as it constitutes good 
foundation for an initial investigation (due to NOMA’s properties) and can be easily expanded to 
larger scale scenarios. 
The basic premise is that an eNodeB is centrally located in a cell. It is responsible for scheduling 
and allocating resources to users requiring them. It is capable of allocating bandwidth both 
orthogonally and non-orthogonally (multiple users on the same resource block). The users are 
randomly distributed in the cell based on their SINR (signal-to-noise-plus-interference-ratio), a 
metric that alludes to the channel gain of each user. The maximum SINR of a user (near the cell 
center) is 20 dB while the minimum SINR of a user (near the cell edge) is 0 dB. 
The following assumptions are made: 
 Inter-cell interference is negligible and it is thus possible to make an analysis for an 
isolated single-cell scenario. This can be considered reasonable based on NOMA’s 
properties discussed in chapter 3. 
 Intra-cell interference is mitigated by performing optimal successive interference 
cancellation (SIC). Since the analysis concerns two-user NOMA only, it is safe to assume 
that is the case. 
 User locations in the cell follow a normalized random uniform distribution. This 
represents the general case cell load used for early simulation sets. More realistic and 
extreme cases will be examined in some of the simulation sets described in chapter 6.

6.2 Impact of User Pairing Algorithm 
 
This section investigates the impact of different user pairing algorithms on NOMA’s performance. 
Dynamic power allocation is used for all simulations and 4 different algorithms are implemented. 
 Random Pairing 
After sorting the users in ascending SINR, each low channel gain user is randomly paired with a 
high channel gain user. 
 Near-Far Pairing 
The algorithm used in the previous section. The lowest SINR user is paired with the highest SINR 
user, second lowest with second highest, etc. 
 Uniform Channel Gain Difference (UCGD) Pairing 
This algorithm ensures that the SINR difference between the two users of each pair is as even as 
possible (uniform). This is essentially accomplished by pairing cell mid users with cell edge and 
center users. What happens in practice after the SINR sorting is that user 1 gets paired with user 
501, user 2 with user 502, etc. 
 Hybrid Pairing 
This approach combines the last two algorithms. Near-far user pairing is applied to a certain 
number of pairs while UCGD pairing is applied to the rest of the pairs. In our case there is an even 
split (50%) between near-far and UCGD user pairs.

6.6 Chapter Summary 
 
The chapter includes all the simulation results and all the relevant discussion and conclusions 
reached from them. Five different simulation sets are included, where OMA and NOMA are 
compared. 
The first set investigates the impact of static and dynamic power allocation. It is concluded that 
dynamic PA provides significantly higher gain, but for only half the users, the ones with higher 
channel gain. Static PA provides lower gain, but spread more evenly across all users. The NOMA 
gain over OMA is higher than 27% for the first and higher than 18% for the latter. 
The second simulation set examines NOMA’s performance while employing 4 different user 
pairing algorithms, random, near-far, UCGD and hybrid pairing. All algorithms output significant 
gain over OMA, in the range of 27 to 28%. Near-far user pairing provides the highest overall 
throughput between the four. UCGD pairing outputs the lowest (but still significantly higher than 
OMA), but it ensures all high channel gain users’ throughput is above certain threshold, by 
eliminating “bad” pairs and keeping the channel gain difference in all pairs somewhat even. 
Hybrid pairing is an efficient compromise between the two. 
While the previous scenarios assume uniform cell load, the third set studies how NOMA performs 
in extreme cases, where the load is heavily skewed towards the cell center or edge. Results show 
than even in such scenarios, NOMA provides significant throughput gain over OMA. 
The fourth simulation scenario involves an implemented single-tier network consisting of 7 cells 
with varying user number and load. Near-far user pairing is applied in some cells and hybrid 
pairing in others. Results show an 18.4% throughput gain when NOMA is employed. Over 60% of 
the users experience higher throughput with NOMA than they do with OMA. 
Finally, the last simulation explores the effect of implementing NOMA in mobile network 
dimensioning. Results show that a significantly lower number of cells is required to achieve a 
certain network traffic volume when implementing NOMA instead of OMA. Similarly, the same 
number of cells can carry around 27% additional traffic by switching to a NOMA implementation.

7 Conclusion 
 
As the transition to full scale 5G mobile networks is materializing, there is a lot of ongoing 
research and experimentation to complete the specification of the technologies that will be 
featured in the final 3GPP standards. As the data traffic and the number of connected devices 
are constantly increasing, there is an imminent need for network capacity enhancements that 
will facilitate the 5G requirements. The primary area of the network where such enhancements 
can take place is the radio access. Specifically, the multiple access scheme implemented decides 
how the network resources are allocated to the serviced users. It is therefore necessary to select 
an MA scheme that distributes the existing bandwidth as efficiently as possible. 
NOMA is a technology that seems to be a high-potential alternative to the existing multiple access 
schemes. Unlike OFDMA which is used in 4G mobile networks, NOMA allows for non-
orthogonality between multiplexed user signals. This can directly increase the network capacity 
without requiring additional spectrum. The primary NOMA concept is based on selecting users 
with high difference in channel conditions and multiplexing them in the same resource block. 
Essentially, a user with high channel gain accesses the resources allocated to a user with poor 
channel conditions, resulting in increased spectral efficiency and total system capacity. 
In power domain NOMA, the multiplexed users have different transmission power levels, which 
usually depend on their channel gain. The two primary factors that affect how power domain 
NOMA performs is the selected power allocation technique and the user pairing algorithm. The 
two primary PA categories is static and dynamic. In the first one, the power ratio between the 
users of each pair is fixed and predetermined. In the latter, the power ratio varies depending on 
the channel gain difference between the users of the pair. Several power allocation and user 
pairing techniques have been proposed so far in the existing literature, each involving trade-offs 
in terms of throughput gain, complexity and implementation cost. 
A simulation model was implemented within the scope of this project, in order to investigate and 
obtain quantifiable results on how NOMA performs in realistic scenarios, in comparison to a 
traditional OMA scheme. The impact of several parameters was investigated, including the power 
allocation approach, the user pairing algorithm and heavily skewed cell load. NOMA’s 
performance was examined in single cell and full network scenarios, as well as its impact on 
network capacity dimensioning. 
Results showed significant throughput gain from implementing NOMA over OMA in all cases. 
Dynamic power allocation provides higher total gain than static PA, although the gain only 
appears for the users with good channel conditions. Similarly, near-far user pairing provides 
higher throughput than the other examined user pairing algorithms (random, UCGD, hybrid). 
Hybrid pairing outputs slightly lower, but ensures the gain is more evenly distributed to all the 
high channel gain users. NOMA outperforms OMA even in extreme case scenarios, where the cell 
is unevenly load, either skewed towards the cell edge or the cell center. Lastly, the simulations 
==PAGE:61==
53 
 
imply that even in a full scale mobile network with varied conditions and parameters between 
each cell, it is beneficial for network operators to implement NOMA. In terms of capacity 
dimensioning, a significantly lower number of cells is required to achieve certain network traffic 
volumes with NOMA than with OMA. 
All in all, the simulations that took place during this project confirmed the findings of the existing 
literature. Despite the challenges it entails, Non-Orthogonal Multiple Access can yield remarkable 
benefits for mobile operators, as long as enough analysis goes into its implementation in terms 
of the resource allocation and the user pairing. 
 
7.1 Future Work 
 
Despite the promising results, there is definitely room for additional research in several areas, 
the impact of which was not within the scope of this thesis project. 
Intra-cell interference is a factor that can negatively influence NOMA’s performance if it remains 
unmitigated. NOMA’s success presupposes optimal SIC on the receiver side, a task that is not 
always easy to accomplish. 
Additional study can also go into multi-user NOMA, as most of the existing literature assumes 
two users per pair. Multiplexing more than two users can provide even higher throughput gains, 
although it results in the decoding at the receiver being significantly more complicated. 
Additionally, there are questions on whether NOMA can be successfully implemented in 
scenarios where high channel gain difference between users cannot be achieved. Load balancing 
between cells can be an interesting way to artificially accomplish that when necessary. 
Finally, even though most of the ongoing research and this project consider power domain 
NOMA, perhaps code domain NOMA can also be a viable alternative. Since CDMA is used for 3G 
networks, it is possible that a lot of the existing infrastructure and resources can be used to 
enhance it and ensure it can facilitate the 5G requirements.
