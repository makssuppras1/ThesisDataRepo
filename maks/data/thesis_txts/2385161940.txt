Converting Schedule Based Buses to Demand Responsive Public Transport

Abstract
Smart phones and other Information Communication Technologies (ICT) have created opportunit-
ies to create a new form of dynamic Demand Responsive Public Transportation (DRPT) services,
that have the potential to act as an alternative to the schedule based Conventional Public Trans-
port (CPT). This study analyzes the feasibility of moving from a schedule based bus system to
a demand responsive system that is able to respond to dynamic requests in real time in order to
move passenger from origin to destination, much like a taxi would, while requiring passengers to
share the ride.
In order to model this sort of a service the dynamic Dial-A-Ride Problem (DARP) is solved in a
Discrete Event Simulation model. For that purposes a pickup and delivery dispatching algorithm
is constructed.
Best insertion construction heuristic is implemented coupled with local search
using random destruction and best reinsertion with limited solving time per request.
Key Performance Indicators (KPI) are identiﬁed that are comparable between the two systems.
The main measure introduced is the Transit-Auto Travel Time (TATT) ratio measuring expected
ride times of each request against its direct ride time.
Ride times are extracted using Traﬃc
Analyst [4] tool in ArcGIS using industrial standard methods.
The case study analyzed speciﬁcally is for the town of Roskilde, Denmark. Results are reported
for up to 1462 requests and up to 500 request active in the solution space at any given simulation
time.
Results show that for denial ratio ∼5% the amount of vehicles needed would result in
around 2 times the cost of buses in any of the scenarios. A future scenario with cost estimates for
driverless vehicles however shows positive results.
The aim of the study is to provide a ﬁrst order approximate of the eﬀects of transforming the
whole CPT system into DRPT, it is concluded that the costs provided give an upper bound on
what is achievable since assumptions are designed to give CPT the beneﬁt of the doubt. For
Roskilde it is not recommended to shift from CPT to DRPT given current cost structures. Future
implications are that driverless vehicles will be more cost eﬃcient for at least a part of the bus
lines once technology has matured, but CPT will still in the foreseeable future be the cheapest
option for transportation while dial a ride will be used by passengers requiring better QoS than
the alternative.
Keywords: Dynamic Dial a Ride Problem, Demand Responsive Public Transportation, Schedule
Based Buses, Key Performance Indicators, Heuristics, Discrete Event Simulation

Background
Transportation is on the verge of revolution. Those are the words of Lyft’s founder John Zimmer
who talks about the 3rd revolution of transportation [77] in the company’s vision for the future.
The 1st revolution having been the building of the network of canals and railroads in the 19th
century, the 2nd being the triumph of the private vehicle in the 20th century.
The private vehicle is today by far the most prevalent mode of inland transportation throughout
the world; around 83% - 84% of all passenger-kilometers (pkm) traveled within Europe (time
period 2003-2013)[5][24] and around 88% - 91% passenger miles in the United States (period 1990-
2009) [49] have been with a private vehicle as opposed to the public transit alternative. Of those
trips 76% in 2009 were single rider trips [69] in the US and private vehicles are estimated to sit
empty in a parking lot 95% of the time [55]. An average city driver furthermore spends 6-14
minutes ﬁnding a parking spot a day and approximately 30% of traﬃc in an average business
district are cars looking for parking[63]. So apart from low utilization of vehicles these cars also
take up signiﬁcant amount of land area that could otherwise be used for parks and buildings for
denser cities[77]. Congestion also continues to be a problem. According to the The INRIX 2016
Global Traﬃc Scorecard [30] Americans spend for example an hour a week on average stuck in
congestion, with drivers in Los Angeles peaking at more than 100 hours on average stuck in traﬃc.
The slowest studied city in the world, Dublin, furthermore has average peak hour speeds (5.5 kph)
that are slower than that of a horse and cart [30]. Conditions in Denmark are not as bad, with 24
hrs./year spent in congestion on average in Copenhagen, ranking at 160th in Europe, and around
14.8 hrs./year in Roskilde as an example [30]. Danes can thank this relatively low congestion
levels to their good infrastructure in public transportation and bicycles being the mode of choice
for approximately 20%-30% of commuting trips as of 2009[72]. From a global perspective these
statistics suggest that it is simply not sustainable or feasible anymore that the majority of the
population goes about most of their trips alone in private vehicles, so alternative methods have to
be found.
Zimmer’s 3rd revolution addresses these challenges by the introduction of eﬃcient carpooling and
ride-sharing options facilitated by modern smartphone technology and dispatching algorithms.
Companies such as Uber with Uber Pool[37], Lyft[77], Sidecar [64] and Via [75] are in the fore-
front providing so-called Mobility on Demand (MoD) [2] or Mobility as a Service (MaaS) [65] as
an alternative to owning a vehicle, taking public transport or a taxi. The services provided then
work as a taxi service within dense city areas where customers go from one place to another but
can expect to share the ride with another customer [75][64][42][73].
This trend is a part of the recent rise of the sharing economy / collaborative consumption, which
can be deﬁned as ”Peer-to-peer-based activity of obtaining, giving, or sharing the access to goods
and services, coordinated through community-based online service” [28] (p. 2). The sharing eco-
nomy has been on the rise in recent years because of the increased awareness among consumers
with relation to sustainability and ethical business practices. However factors such as maximizing
utility of access to service that might even be cheaper than ownership and more eﬃcient use of
resources from sharing provide an economically rational reasoning for this trend[28].
Several examples of sharing economies with a mobility concept exist in the Nordic Region. UbiGo
in G¨oteborg [37][65] made an experiment with a system where users would have diﬀerent options
depending on their activity; bus, bike or carpooling to get to and from work, car for out of town
trips and dropping kids oﬀto soccer practice, taxi after a night out; all paid for via one sub-
scription. The ﬁeld experiment was reported as a success with 93% of users satisﬁed with their
trips and 97% claiming they would continue to use the service, however the conclusion was made
that further development of business models would have to take place that would beneﬁt diﬀer-

agents [33], but never in the same scale.
What the scale and speed of autonomous vehicle adoption and use of ride sharing services will
be exactly is debatable but one thing is clear; the 3rd transport revolution is happening and it is
happening now.
1.2
Problem Speciﬁcation and Delimitations
This thesis is motivated by the emerging MoD technologies and some of the emerging business
models previously described that have the potential to disrupt both people’s relationship with the
private vehicle and other public transportation.
The aim of the project is as following;
 Assess whether dial-a-ride service oﬀered for the general public can be a feasible alternative
to conventional schedule based bus system in a small city, with Roskilde as a case study.
The research objectives are then as following:
 Model a Demand Responsive Public Transport system by solving the dynamic Dial-a-Ride
problem using heuristics in order to understand its behavior.
 Gather and analyze data for the bus system in the area of interest (Roskilde) in order to
decide which lines should be replaced by DRPT.
 Deﬁne and implement a set of Key Performance Indicators that can be used to compare the
two systems in terms of Quality of Service and operational eﬃciency.
 Design and solve a set of scenarios in order to give a ﬁrst order approximations of the costs
and QoS-levels attainable for the DRPT with relation to the CPT.

Literature Review
2.1
Demand Responsive vs. Schedule Based Public Transport
2.1.1
Terminology
This paper compares two kind of bus transit systems that will from now on be referenced as Con-
ventional Public Transport (CPT) and Demand Responsive Public Transport (DRPT) as is done
by Diana et al. (2007)[19] and Kashani et al. (2016)[36]. CPT is the conventional schedule based
bus transit system with ﬁxed routes. Demand responsive transportation (DRT) however is an um-
brella term for an array of solutions that all have in common that a passenger is picked up from one
location and delivered to another according to a speciﬁc request. The service thus responds to the
demand. Examples of this is a traditional taxi, dial-a-ride services for disabled and elderly where
most requests usually come in the day before [68][47] and more recent ride-sharing services such
as UberPool, Lyft and Via, also referred to as Mobility on Demand (MoD) [2]. The term DRPT
as used here refers to a dynamic dial-a-ride service provided for the general public (as opposed
to only disabled and elderly in paratransit). Furthermore DRPT is used rather than MoD to
emphasize the focus of this project, that is MoD as a public transport alternative with the vehicle
capacities of a van or minibus, but current MoD systems are typically limited to two passengers [2].
Neither MoD or DRPT should however be confused with ride-sharing, which has the notable dis-
tinction that drivers act as independent actors and the objective is then to match passengers with
drivers that are headed from one location to another and make a detour to pick up passengers [1].

Key Performance Indicators
2.2.1
Background
A Key Performance Indicator (KPI) is by deﬁnition a measure that says something signiﬁcant
about the performance of a system that is being analyzed. Choosing the correct indicators and
connecting them with clear objectives is important. A lot of measures can be deﬁned for any
system describing its’ behavior but if one is not careful they can be either redundant or worst
case; distracting. A KPI that is not connected to a clear objective might lead whomever is using
it in the wrong direction. ”Key” is the key word to look out for (no pun intended). An inﬂuential
paper by Doran (1981) [20](p. 36) on the subject of performance measures for a business operation
states that any objective to be used in performance measurement should be:
Speciﬁc - target a speciﬁc area for improvement.
Measurable - quantify or at least suggest and indicator of progress.
Assignable - specify who will do it.
Realistic - state what results can realistically be achieved, given available resources.
Time-related - specify when the results can be achieved.
These are some general considerations to keep in mind when developing a good KPI. These criteria
however were designed for the context of business operation and measures how well a company
or individuals within it perform over time compared to stated objectives. This is diﬀerent from
the measures that can be deﬁned for a simulation study such as this. The goal is to measure the
performance of a theoretical DRPT system vs. a current CPT while the transformation period or
implementation process needed to get there is not considered. Therefore the criteria assignable
and time-related are not as important in this context as the goal is not to develop an implement-
ation strategy. It is however important that, by moving from CPT to DRPT, the KPIs developed
indicate speciﬁcally what should be improved, that they are quantiﬁable and within a realistic
context to be usable [20].
So how explicitly should KPIs be deﬁned for a public transit system? The Transit Capacity and
Quality of Service Manual (TCQSM) published by the American Transportation Research Board
of The National Academies (2013) [71] was highly inﬂuential for deﬁning the KPIs. Although
the manual does not use the term KPI it does use the term performance measures. They can
be either Quality of Service (QoS) related indicators, referring to the performance of the system
from the passenger perspective; or operational, referring to the factors that more directly inﬂuence
operational costs and use of resources.
These indicators are usually intertwined however and
propose a trade-oﬀbetween maximizing QoS while minimizing operational costs. Section 2.2.2
summarizes relevant concepts in terms of QoS from TCQSM (2013) [71].

Operational Indicators
Operational indicators measure how well resources, vehicles in this case are utilized. The primary
measure for this is capacity utilization. Another one can be the request serving eﬃciency of a
given vehicle, that is how many requests a vehicle serves per hour.
Capacity of vehicles in DRPT systems is typically 8-seater vans [19] [5]. Even for higher capacity
vehicles cases occur rarely where no. passengers within a vehicle at any given time exceeds 8 and

have lower capacity (6-7) for example if room has to be made for a wheelchair, but if it goes much
lower it starts to reach typical taxi/private vehicle category. In some cases it can however be
beneﬁcial to have a ﬂeet of various types of vehicles, some with lower capacity. Mulley and Nelson

2.3.1
Classifying DRPT as a dial-a-ride problem
The mathematical problem to be solved in order to provide a dynamic demand responsive public
transport is the dynamic Dial-A-Ride Problem. The Dia-A-Ride Problem (DARP) is a speciﬁc
version of the Vehicle Routing Problem with Pickup and Delivery (VRPPD) where a passenger
has to be delivered from an origin point to a speciﬁc destination. The objective is then usually
to minimize cost of the vehicle routes while satisfying constraints such as time windows, vehicle
capacity, duration (such as a day of operations), pairing, precedence and ride-times for each pas-
senger [9] [16]. The problem is thus quite similar to the the Vehicle Routing Problem with Pickup
and Deliveries and Time-Windows (VRPPDTW) with cargo [17], [9], with the added complexity
of taking into account passenger inconvenience.
In a survey by Berbeglia et.al. (2007) [9] several static pickup and delivery problems are classi-
ﬁed according to three criteria: [Structure ∣Visits ∣Vehicles]. The structure can be one-to-one
(1-1) where a commodity/passenger has one speciﬁc origin and destination, one-to-many-to-one
(1-M-1) where a vehicle drives from one point of origin (depot for example) and drops oﬀand
picks up goods from multiple customers before returning to the depot and ﬁnally many-to-many
(M-M) where any node can act as a customer demand point or supplier. Visits can then either
be combined (P-D) where goods/passengers can be picked up and delivered at the same location
(a delivery of fresh beer is swapped with the recyclable bottles for example), exclusive (P/ D)
where each point can only demand or supply a good/passenger or free (PD) with no restrictions
on visit type. Finally the Vehicle criteria simply refers to number of vehicles, which can be one
(1), many (M) or non-speciﬁed (-) [9]. DARP and the dynamic case by extension can be classiﬁed
as [1−1∣P/D∣−] by this deﬁnition since each customer has to go from exactly one point to another,
each point of visit is exclusively either pickup or delivery and ﬁnally it can be speciﬁed for both
one or many vehicles. This applies to the DRPT described here so it can indeed be classiﬁed as a
dial-a-ride service.
The DRPT system to be designed is a dynamic one, as opposed to a static one, since information is
revealed during the day of operations. However it is assumed that once requests and information
is revealed it is deterministic, and does not change once it becomes available.
Therefore the
system can be deﬁned as dynamic and deterministic, and not stochastic. That is events such as
cancellations and no shows, breakdown of vehicles or variable driving times due to for example
congestion is not taken into account. That would make the problem stochastic. As would the
same problem be demand pattern in terms of probability is known before hand and the heuristic
designed ins such a way that would take advantage of that. This is in compliance with Psaraftis
et al. (2016) [54] deﬁnition of static vs. dynamic and deterministic vs. stochastic VRPs.

Many approaches to the static version of DARP have been tried. Exact methods have been tried
but the use of heuristics and meta-heuristics is far more common, especially for the multi-vehicle

algorithms by for version of the the problem where capacity and time window constraints where
ignored [60], eﬀectively reducing the problem to the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) with ad-

For larger instances heuristics have proven to give good results. Cordeu and Laporte (2003) [17]
review a couple of early methods. That includes Jaw et al. (1986)[32] who made an early con-
tribution to the multi vehicle case, proposing an insertion algorithm able to handle large-scale
instances, from 250 up to 2617 users and 28 vehicles. Several papers regarding clustering are
reviewed, where customers are grouped together according to spatial and temporal dimensions,
routes are then created for each group using a single vehicle algorithm before swaps are made
between the resulting routes [11] [31]. A real life problem in Bologna was solved [70] using local-
search and tabu thresholding where requests where assigned to routes using parallel insertion
procedure before inter- and intra-route exchanges.
Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) heuristic was later developed by Ropke and Pi-
singer (2006) [58] extending the Large Neighborhoods Search (LNS) by introducing a randomized
adaptive procedure into the algorithm, such that it learns what removal and insertion procedures
to use more often than others while it computes the solution and has provided some really prom-
ising results [9]. Li et al. (2016)[40] used the ALNS to solve the Share-a-Ride Problem (SARP)
benchmarked against classic instances for DARP solved with a mixed integer programming (MIP)
solver. It gave new best values for two out of twenty instances developed by Cordeu and Laporte
(2003) [18].
2.3.3
Solving the Dynamic DARP
Less work has been conducted with regards to the dynamic version of the problem. Most solution
approaches depend on heuristics and recent papers indicate that eﬃcient algorithms for this prob-
lem are still lacking [2] [10]. All these algorithms have in common though that they have make
two sets of decisions [10]:
1. Should a vehicle wait or go, and
2. Should we accept or reject a request.
In a survey by Berbeglia et.al. (2010) [10] several solution methods for the dynamic pickup and
delivery problem is introduced, that deal with those questions. One basic approach is to solve
the problem as a static problem each time a new request enters the system. This can be thought
of as a reactive approach where the algorithm reacts to new information being revealed, and the
algorithm has to be modiﬁed such that new solutions are feasible with regards to past events
as well. A common method is then to insert the request with an insertion heuristic, such as by
greedy construction. Then the request is added to a position within a route that gives the lowest
additional cost. One way to do this is to reoptimize everything each time a new request is revealed.
Another way is to update the solution; running the static algorithm only once in the beginning.
The latter approach is more generally used [10].

deletion, interchange moves and local search algorithms that improve the initial feasible solution
found. If a request can not be inserted it has to be rejected, typically incurring a penalty in the

 Psaraftis (1980) [52] did one of the ﬁrst studies on dynamic DARP. The single vehicle
case is solved where passengers wish to be served as soon as possible. An exact dynamic
programming algorithm is presented for the static case with a high computational complexity
of O(n23n). This approach is thus only usable for small cases.
 Madsen et al. (1995) [44] presented an eﬃcient insertion algorithm for a “real-life multi-
vehicle dynamic DARP consisting of up to 300 daily requests [. . . ] in Copenhagen.” [10].
The insertion algorithm called REBUS is a four step algorithm where a list of unassigned
jobs J is added to a position within the vehicle that gives the least additional cost. If no
feasible insertion exists however then the unassigned job is added to another list of requests
that cannot be served, in which case a decision is made to reject the request. This is iterated
until list J is empty.
 Attanioso et al. (2004) [6] made a two phase algorithm where static requests where allocated
to vehicles using a Tabu Search procedure. Then the dynamic requests were handled with a
parallel insertion algorithm. Computational results where reported for up to 144 requests.
Results indicated that parallel computing could be beneﬁcial for real time problems but
suggested that further research in simulated lookahead should be conducted.
 Xiang et al.
(2008) [76] took into account stochastic elements along with the dynamic
component, reporting solutions for up to 610 requests. Requests were inserted to routes
using best insertion before a local search procedure was applied to improve upon the solution
based on inter trip moves.
Berbeglia et.al. (2010) [10] also covers when some information about the future is known and
requests can to some degree be anticipated beforehand. This can include information about where
and when what demand can be expected, such as predictable behaviors because of commuting
patterns. Often the requests follow a distribution, such as the Poisson, but in many cases the
exact distribution is unknown and then it has to be modeled with historical data. Bent and Van
Hentenryck [8][7] and Hvattum et al. 2007 [29] are commonly referred to in that context [10][54]
[57]. When some information about the future is known the problem becomes both dynamic and
stochastic. Algorithms then have to allocate vehicles strategically in such a way that anticipates
the most likely future outcome.
Ritzinger et al. (2016) [57] surveyed dynamic and stochastic VRPs. A conclusion was made that
taking stochastic information into account can yield up to 60% better solutions than if it’s not
taken into account for when decisions are made on-line in real time. That speciﬁcally applied
to DVRP with Stochastic Customer (DVRPSC). That is when locations of customers and aware
time of request is stochastic. Bent and Van Hentenryck (2004) [8] introduced the multiple scenario
approach (MSA) where solutions are calculated for several potential future scenarios and put into
a solution pool.
More recently metaheuristics have been applied to solve the dynamic case. Schilde et al. (2011)
[62] proposed a Variable Neighborhood Search (VNS) to solve DARP with stochastic elements
focusing on transporting patients and elderly people, using a ﬁxed vehicle ﬂeet and information
about return trips. Two VNS algorithms where compared, one taking into account the stochastic
information about future demand, the other ignoring it. Then each time after a solution has been
found (after request insertion and shaking solution), future requests are sampled and decision is
made whether to change the solution according to the most likely future scenario or not. That
decision is built on a sample average estimator that estimates average cost of a given sample. It

and research on the topic is really active at the moment. Algorithms based on Large Neighborhood
Search seem to be the most common approach based on these ﬁndings, yielding competent results
for a variable size of instances.
However it does seem sampling future inputs for the coming
rollings stock horizon does give better results than only reacting to requests. Finally the waiting
and relocation strategies can have signiﬁcant eﬀect on results. A combination of these results is
likely to yield some good results.

2.4.3

Van Lon et al. (2016) state that ”knowing the dynamism of a problem does not give any extra
information on the predictability of events”([74],p. 617). A counterargument to that could be that

evenly distributed, making the instance more predictable. The results presented by van Lon et
al. (2016) in fact indicate this. Instances with low dynamism give poorer results, especially if
urgency is decreased (measured in minutes). This can be explained by the fact that if all events
happen unexpectedly at the same time it is harder to handle than if events are evenly distributed
one by one over time. It is thus concluded that while dynamism measure can be useful to get

3.2.2
The Dynamic Dial a Ride Problem - Discrete Event Simulation
The dynamic DARP as explained in Section 2.3.3 extends the static DARP by having requests
revealed over time. These requests are revealed at discrete time intervals and thus a discrete event
simulation model was used based on java-based framework developed and provided by Rune
Larsen [39].
Discrete Event Simulation is a simulation process where events occur at discrete time intervals,
as opposed to continuously like one would experience in the real world. Events then occur ac-
cording to either a certain probability distribution or a schedule. If the events occur according to
a pre-determined schedule the system is said to be deterministic. That is the behavior is known
before hand. A stochastic system however is the opposite where events can not be predetermined
or behavior known before hand, since they occur randomly. Typically a simulation process models
a ﬂow of entities that go through a system subject to one or more processes. These processes will
aﬀect the entities, the input, and change the state of the system and/or the entities will undergo
state changes [59].
The ﬂowchart on Figure 3 shows the general idea behind the discrete event simulation implemen-
ted for the dynamic dial a ride. Each time a new event triggers the arrival of a new request, the

Before any of this can happen (request inserted into the solution) locks have to be updated. These
locks suggest whether constraints allow rescheduling of any particular visits, that is if visits have
already been served, the time has passed. Here visits are locked if a vehicle is already on its way
to a visit. In other words rescheduling of a vehicle while driving to a visit is not allowed. This
means that if a vehicle has been scheduled for a pickup visit and arrives before the opening of a
time window for example it can not be scheduled to go anywhere else between, even if it would
be technically feasible. It should be noted that even if a request has been picked up the delivery
time can still be modiﬁed. A request does thus not exit the solutions space until the delivery has
been locked as well as the pickup.
The policy of when to lock a visit can serve as a service level design feature for a dial a ride service.
A customer might for example want to know with some time in advance when to expect to be
picked up. This might be 15 minutes, 30 minutes or more, depending on policy. As stated earlier
arrival times are locked as soon as vehicle departs from a previous visit unless stated otherwise.
That is the service level equivalent of stating ”we are on our way, ETA: [tnow +DRT]” where tnow
is the current time and DRT is the direct ride time.
Another design feature for the discrete event simulator is how often to call the solver. This is
related to how fast the solver should answer a request. Alonso-Mora 2017 [2] for example in their
DRPT study for NYC gather incoming requests during 30 second time interval and then solve for
average of 30 seconds. That would mean a user might have to wait 1 min. for an answer. Thus
instead of calling the solver each time a request is revealed, the solver is called between longer
time intervals. The requests are then queued up and instead of serving them on a First Come
First Serve basis or through other similar policies the request that ﬁt best into the solution are
added ﬁrst based on an optimization heuristic (requests if the heuristic is parallel).

with an event e ∈E. The set of requests U in this case with an associated aware time τu of the
request is in eﬀect equivalent to having a set of events E since no events except requests entering
the simulation can happen. If other types of events like cancellation or vehicle malfunction were
allowed the set E would have to be introduced more formally along with type setting for each event.
The dynamic parameters of the problem diﬀer from the static ones by the fact that they are due to
change each time a new event e ∈E is triggered by its’ aware time τe. While the static parameters
stay the same throughout the simulation, the dynamic parameters are inﬂuenced by the solution
constructed at the previous event. Therefore sets such as which visits are locked depend on both
the simulation time and the decisions made by the solver in previous steps.
The solution then at any given event time τ consists of ∣K∣routes each containing an ordered list
of visits Vk, in which order the vehicle serve each visit. Associated with each of those visits a
decision has to made on when to arrive at and when to depart from a visit, setting values for Ai
and Di for each visit i ∈V that satisfy constraints. Finally if that is not feasible a request has to
be rejected and added to a list R of rejected requests.
A ﬁnal note here is that it is true that boolean variables could be deﬁned as xijk ∈{0,1} whether
visit i is located at position j in route k but that would not be a practical representation of routes
that are variable in size and are due to change all the time. Similarly a variable yu ∈{0,1} could
be deﬁned for whether request u is rejected or not. This does however not provide any beneﬁt for
the solver and distract from the fact that the model is not an MIP-model.

3.3
Solver
This section describes the solver implemented in order to solve the dynamic DARP. To implement
a heuristic solver methods have to be deﬁned where requests are inserted into the solution and
move actions where requests are moved from one route to another.
3.3.1
Insertion Method
Greedy Construction is a common way to create a feasible solution [58]. The insertion method
implemented was greedy construction using best insertion. The general idea is to insert requests
into at the place within a route that adds the least cost to the solution.
The approach taken in the implementation of the heuristic was to introduce a data structure called
candidate consisting of the following information: [route k, request u, pickup position p, delivery
position d] and a delta value δ associated with putting a request u into position (p,d) within
route k. The ﬁrst step then as can be seen in Algorithm 3.3.1 is getting those candidates via a
function getGreedyCandidates(U,K,cf) that returns the best candidates (best pickup/delivery
position) for each route/request combination given existing routes K, unplanned requests U and
cost function cf. Then the cheapest insertion is chosen until there are no more unplanned requests
or the remaining requests can’t be inserted and have to be denied.
The way that greedy candidates are generated (Algorithm 3.3.1) is by ﬁrst getting all candidate
positions (all unlocked positions), calculating the δ-value for each of the pickup/delivery positions
and sorting the list according to that value. After that the best candidate for each route/request
combination is found by getting the ﬁrst feasible candidate in the list; if there is one.

3.3.4
Waiting Strategies
At any given time when a vehicle has arrived a at visit or is located at a depot a decision has to be
made whether to wait for some time or drive right away to the next customer given the available
information. The two main strategies proposed for dealing with this are [45]:
 Drive First: Start driving to the next customer as soon as possible. This means that a
vehicle can arrive before the start of the time window and will wait at that location for the
customer to be ready.
Waiting time of the vehicle is then max(0,ai −¯Ai), whereas customer does not have to wait
at all. Main drawback is that vehicles get locked early on leaving less room for optimization
later in the day as information is revealed. If vehicle arrives in the middle of a time window
then it leaves again as soon as possible.
 Wait First: Wait as long as possible such that passenger is picked up right before the end of
his time window. This gives the operator more information to optimize routes since action
is taken later in the day. However by deﬁnition the approach eats up all slack within each
route, speciﬁcally in the beginning leaving no ﬂexibility to react to new request after a certain
time when all slack has been ﬁnished. The wait ﬁrst strategy is not a good approach when
dealing with passenger problems such as the DARP, since passenger ride time also have to
be taken into account. It can be assumed that a passenger would not be happy sitting idle
at a certain location in the middle of his trip eating up slack just in case that maybe a new
request will appear that might save the operator small amounts of gas.
Figure 5: Comparison of three diﬀerent waiting strategies. Adapted from Mitrovic-Minic (2004)
[45].
These two strategies are two extreme options available to deal with this decision, but anything in
between can be applied as well. A third option is therefore proposed where a vehicle is scheduled
to arrive at the earliest when the time window of a given visit i begins. The vehicle therefore
waits at the previous visit until there is only the time it takes to drive there left. This Approach
will be referred to as Just In Time strategy from now on; that is vehicles are scheduled to arrive
at a customer just in time of the opening of the time window. Figure 5 illustrates the diﬀerence
between the three strategies.

3.3.5
Relocation strategies
Relocation strategies have to do with whether vehicles should move to another location or stay
where they are at any given time.
This is typically applied when a vehicle for one reason or
another is idle. In this project if vehicles stay idle for any signiﬁcant period of time that simply
means there are too many vehicles on the ﬁeld. This can occur speciﬁcally late in the day when
less requests are coming in to the system. Therefore the only relevant relocation strategy is when
vehicles should return to the depot. For those purposes simply as a policy issue a time τreturn
should be speciﬁed for any given instance when vehicles should think return home. That is return
home as soon as it stands idle. Another approach is to return when suﬃcient idle time has passed,
say 10-15 min. for example.

3.4.2
Bus Network and Estimating Bus Ride Times
Ride times for the CPT have to be estimated for any given OD trip generated for the dial a ride
in order to be able to compare the two systems. This section covers how those ride times were
estimated.
Stop to Stop Bus Ride Time Matrix
The ArcGIS extension Traﬃc Analyst [4] from Rapidis was used to estimate travel times between
all two stops within the Roskilde region. The speciﬁc tool used is the Public Assignment tool which
is schedule based, meaning that it models public transportation operations using ”full timetable
information without any aggregation or simpliﬁcation” (p. 169 [4]). The Rejseplanen data Rapidis
used in the model has information for the whole Movia service region, containing all of Sjælland.
The tool can be used to model the passenger ﬂows and travel patterns but only the aspects of the
model that go into calculating the expected transit ride times will be explained here. The inputs
required to calculate the public transit ride times are:
 set of stops that serve as Origin/destination points that ride times should be calculated
between,
 lines, along with their respective service type (bus, train etc.),
 trip matrix denoting when lines serve each stop during the day,
 time intervals, having these values 0-23 for example will yield an average travel time for each
hour of the day, this can be calculated with shorter intervals or longer giving more or less
level od detail for traﬃc modeling.
The tool is then used to ﬁnd the shortest path between stops where transfers are taken into account.
The transfers are modeled with edges and transfer time as an example of i shown in Figure 7.
The result is then a large table where the columns of interest are FromZoneID, ToZoneID, and
TimeInteID as the origin, destination and time pointers respectively, along with InVehicleT and
ﬁnally WalkT and WaitT which are the walk and wait time within a route combined representing
total transfer time. Empty values in the table means there are no connections between those pair
of stops at that speciﬁc time.

Key Performance Indicators - Mathematical Formulation
In Section 2.2 the concept of key performance indicators was introduced, along with some criteria
that need to apply to a good KPI and ﬁnally an overview of what performance measures are
commonly used for both a DRPT and CPT. A conclusion was made that KPIs need to comply
with a well deﬁned objective. Here the objective is to compare the QoS of DRPT vs. CPT given
the context of operational cost. Therefore the KPIs have to reﬂect that.

is sharper than the evening.

ˆµt ± 2 ⋅ˆst where ˆµt is the average relative demand and ˆst is the standard deviation of the relative

5
Discussion
Discussion of the results goes from analyzing the outcome from the scenarios, to discussing the
performance of the heuristics and assessing input data quality and approximations to reality.
Potential future use of the model proposed along with business development opportunities are
then discussed along with some indications for future research.
5.1
Scenario Analysis
The main outtake from the results is that DRPT would not be cheaper than CPT for Roskilde in
any of the scenarios devised. It was shown analytically that the requests serving eﬃciency needed
in order for the dial a ride to be less expensive than the bus system was unreasonable. Therefore
scenarios were set up where the most utilized lines were taken out of the equation. To break even
the system however would still have to be able to serve 20 requests per vehicle hour on average
at the peak. That goal is near impossible for the dial a ride to reach given the assumption, for
example, of 1 min. service time per visit. That is 20 min. out of an hour gone before counting
any driving time if the system were to reach those goals. The break even points for the driverless
future however was more likely to be in reach. This proved to be the case for the low to medium
cost estimates of the ’T50’ scenarios (scenarios with 50% probability of going to Roskilde St.).
For the four diﬀerent area based scenarios with 50% of morning traﬃc going to Roskilde St. and
the rest of the OD-trips uniformly generated the service proved to be cheaper with a low estimate
for the driverless case. It was however noticed that a signiﬁcant proportion of the vehicle ﬂeet was
standing idle once the morning peak had been covered. Assuming that part of the ﬂeet could be
put to rest in the low demand periods the operating hours could be brought down yielding cost
reductions at the aggregate level. That would result in the medium driverless estimate becoming
competitive but not cheaper for all scenarios. This shows that a relocation strategy where vehicles
go to depot and rest during slow periods could yield some operational cost beneﬁts.
But how realistic are the driverless cost estimates? The low estimate is truly at the lower end
by simply taking the driver costs out of the equation. There will always be cost of technology
additional to the pre-existing costs. Added purchasing price of a vehicle due to self driving capab-
ilities (cost of technology) is projected to go below $ 7000 by 2025-2030 [34] which is less than two
months worth of driver salary. Assuming those vehicles are durable the low and medium estimate
are likely to be realized by 2030 if not sooner, thus making the driverless dial a ride a feasible
alternative to the CPT in the future.
Before taking the costs at face value it is important to understand what QoS level is gained reached
for those costs. In terms of availability the service frequency and service span vary between bus
lines and some of the lines the dial a ride is set to replace only operate part of the day. This
is worth to take note of when the results are interpreted. The service span for the DRPT is 16
hrs. which is much higher than for many of the lines in question. Thus puts the DRPT at a
disadvantage in the cost comparison but indicates a higher QoS in this regard. Accessibility also
increases by deﬁnition for addresses that previously were out of walking distance from a bus stop
by the nature of the service. Availability for DRPT is also measured in terms of response time,
which is 0.5-2.0 hours. This technically is a reduction in QoS since bus users can use that ser-
vice more spontaneously as they can walk to the next bus stop at the precise moment they want
to travel. Testing for the feasibility of reducing the response time to ∼0 is ground for more research.
In terms of comfort and convenience the travel time was the primary measure implemented for
comparison of DRPT and CPT with how it relates to the direct ride times via the TATT-ratio.
Actual ride time of the dial a ride turned out to be guaranteed by the deﬁnition of maximum
ride time to be lower than what can be achieved on average by the CPT. It is surprising that the
diﬀerence is so high and initially leads to the conclusion that the maximum ride time constraint

5.2
Algorithm Performance
The algorithm developed to solve the DARP was in principle a Greedy Best Insertion coupled with
a local search using random removal. This is not to say that the search procedure implemented
was not LNS as described in method Section ??, simply that the no. perturbations per iteration
did not exceed 1, eﬀectively reducing the algorithm down to local search.
The few no. iterations can be explained by the fact that requests will alway be inserted using
best insertion, rather than for example based on ﬁrst improvement. This means that all potential
insertion candidates have to be checked and evaluated before the insertion is completed. First
improvement would try to insert a request as soon as potential for improvement is found, so not
necessarily the best neighboring solution but the ﬁrst one that improves the objective. While ﬁrst
improvement might in some cases prove to be quicker it would require more feasibility checks,
which are more computationally heavy than delta-evaluating the insertion candidates.
Another explanation for low no.
iterations is that at any given time once the simulation has
overcome the warm-up period, no. active requests is quite high. It ranges from 100 for the ’town’
scenario in the slow period up to 500 request for the ’aBig2’ scenario at it’s peak. Compare this
to recent studies where total no. requests for largest instances reported range from 610 [76], 720
request [62] for a full instance [MORE REFS]. Alonso-Mora (2017) [2] has instances with no.
requests ranging between ”382,779 (Sunday) and 460,700 (Friday) requests, and the running pool
of requests contains up to 2,000 requests at any given time” for the case of Manhattan. That is
signiﬁcantly higher than ever come across here. So while the instances being dealt with in this
project are larger than typical instances in the literature emerging studies such as [2] are pushing
the boundaries of solvable sizes with new exact methods. It should be noted however that the
study by Alonso-Mora et al. (2017) [2] takes 30 sec. to gather incoming requests, and another 30
sec. to insert them. This is a reasonable approach where ∼

5.3
Input Data Quality and Approximations to Reality
Any conclusions that can be drawn from the results are contingent upon the data quality, assump-
tions made and approximations to what would be needed to model a truly realistic case.
5.3.1
Service Area
The deﬁnition of the service area was not an easy one to make. In the ﬁrst attempt an arbitrary
rectangle around Roskilde town was chosen, but that did not provide nice system boundaries so
areas from the National Traﬃc Model were used instead to provide a more ’natural’, more oﬃcial
boundary, in the sense that borders have been deﬁned by public authorities.
It is likely that
a public service would follow such deﬁnitions. It was of interest to include the whole Roskilde
Municipality, but because of its peculiar long shape from North to South, some zones are in closer
proximity to Roskilde town than the far reaches of the municipality, therefore external areas were
also included. It was furthermore hard to decide which bus lines to include in the scenarios. The
rural areas around Roskilde had to be included since they provide more cost reduction opportun-
ities with their lower utilization levels than the town. The problem with those areas however with
regards to the bus lines is that they cross multiple municipalities and NTM-zones, since Movia
services all of Sjaelland. Which bus lines to replace in any given scenario based on how much of the
line goes through that area is thus admittedly prone to bias. Further work would be required to
include Movia and the municipalities involved to grasp bus lines should be targets for replacement.
To conclude the system area boundary for Roskilde is hard to deﬁne due to its central location
in Sjaelland and is hard to isolate from the surrounding world. It is thus not the lowest hanging
fruit in terms of a choice for a case study. Perhaps a more isolated area could have been chosen.
5.3.2
Ride Time Estimates
The direct ride times were calculated using industrial standard techniques using commercial soft-
ware from Rapidis ApS [4] which is used by several municipalities and service providers. Therefore
it can be concluded that the ride times as such are as close to reality as one can get. However
with the clustering of addresses distances between each individual OD-pair (on the address level)
was approximated within 250 m × 250 m areas. That distance in terms of automotive driving
time contributes negligible error since in reality other factors would aﬀect the realized driving time
more explicitly. Those factors include congestion, luck with traﬃc lights, speed of the vehicle and
the road network. For a truly realistic case driving times would also have to be slower at rush
hour than at other parts of the day. Another extension to the problem could be to apply variable
driving times. Then the model would have to respond each time vehicles deviate from the schedule.

5.3.3
Demand Patterns
The demand patterns used as input to the model in terms of relative no. trips over the period of a
typical weekday came for the National Traﬃc survey data [15], with data ranging from 2006-2016.
The data includes all registered trips in the survey from within East Sealand. The data from all
modes was used rather than the ﬁltered data for public transport demand as it had much lower
variance over time. The mean ﬂuctuations of the demand pattern for the public transport how-
ever was much more than over all modes, with higher relative peaks. This means that the actual
relative morning peak could be considerably higher than the one modeled. In such an event the
no. vehicles needed to cover that peak would increase from what was achieved. This might be
the case since the demand assumed for the proposed dial a ride is the same as for current public
transit. However demand for public transit might be somewhat biased towards the schedules of
the public transit where higher QoS is oﬀered during the peaks. The overall demand pattern
for all modes might better show when people actually want to travel, and use a service such as
dial ride. Regardless conﬁdence interval for afternoon peak of public transport at 15:00 as an
example ranges from 5%-20% approximately rendering that data practically unusable compared
to the overall transport demand pattern.
Another source of uncertainty is how many people use the public transport per day. The data used
was oﬃcial headcount from Movia [46] for each bus line over the year 2016. Then the average no.
passengers per day is that number divided by 365 days. This gives no information on the diﬀerence
between type of days; work days, weekends, holidays etc. or the variance between individual days
regardless of type. An average demand for day might thus for example be 5000 but one day a week
it is 6000. Should the system then be designed to serve 5000 or 6000 requests? So if the aim is to
serve 95% of requests it is important to be accurate in terms of over what time period; 95% over
the course of a week or 95% for each day for over 95% of all days in a year. Having information
on the variance could thus mean the system has to be designed for a higher no. requests per day.
The bus line passenger counts from Movia [46] do however not have any information on origin-
destination patterns, only total no. people for each line. Thus information on what stops attract
and generate most demand was not included and some assumptions had to be made about origins
and destinations. The approach was then to distribute demand uniformly between addresses for
the base scenarios. This as Hjalmar Christiansen at the National Traﬃc Survey [13] pointed out
in a conversation is not a particularly realistic approach. An area with a lot of small apartments
might be densely populated with addresses generating a lot of demand in the model where as
locations such as the hospital, shopping centers, etc. have only one address but in reality will
generate much more demand than modeled. Including such patterns would likely beneﬁt the dial
a ride given the out-performance of the ’T50’ scenarios over the ’uniform’ scenarios.
5.3.4
Additional Considerations for a Real Case
There were several aspects not taken into account in the discrete event simulation and solver that
would have to be taken into account for a fully ﬂexed commercial product. Considerations include:
 Driver shifts: Driver union rules and contracts have to be taken into account. Drivers have
to take breaks and shifts can not be too long, overtime might have to be taken into account
if contracts allow for it.
Department of Management Engineering, Technical University of Denmark, DTU
73
==PAGE:80==
Converting Schedule Based Buses to Demand Responsive Public Transport
-
 Refueling: Vehicles have to stop for gas / recharging if electrical at regular time intervals.
If electric vehicles become dominant battery life and the time it takes to recharge will have
a constraining eﬀect on the operation.
 Cancellations / no shows: If the model is to be used as a real time operational tool it has
to be able to take into account cancellations and no show events, when passengers do not
show up at the agreed time and location. Cancellations/ no shows add an extra dimension
to the solving process. In those cases at least the delivery visit is taken out of the route
which means that later visits within it could be scheduled earlier than previously planned.
A policy to charge users for no shows could mean taking these events into account does not
negatively eﬀect the outcome.
 Vehicle breakdown: Although rare events, a policy has to be in place to handle when vehicle
breakdowns occur. Mitigations could include getting an external operator/contractor to step
in or keep a backup vehicle.
 Flexible time windows: Rapidis ApS include options for ﬂexible time windows for their clients
[3]. So instead of ﬂat out denying a request the model could try to insert the requests outside
the time window and suggest an alternative pickup time for the user.
 Hard delivery time windows: The model developed assumes that all customers announce a
desired pickup time. However, on many occasions it is more important for users to want to
be guaranteed to arrive before a certain time, like work or school, then the delivery time
window becomes hard rather than the pickup.
 Variable vehicle types: A commercial product would have to take into account diﬀerent
vehicle types and assume a non-homogeneous ﬂeet.
 Wheelchair accessibility: A decision would have to be made whether the service would include
wheelchair accessibility. A vehicle with capacity for 8 passengers could be modiﬁable and
capacity reduced to 6 passengers if space for wheelchair is to be included [3]. This would incur
additional qualiﬁcations required of the drivers, possibly increasing service times of those
customers beyond what non-disabled customers would be happy with. Driverless vehicles
would have to be designed speciﬁcally with this need in mind if the goal is to serve it.
 Group orders and carpooling: The number of vehicles needed speciﬁcally for the peaks could
be reduced signiﬁcantly if riders where incentivised to order in advance and commute in
groups if they live close to one another. This would make the problem partly static and the
solution could be improved by the additional solving time that implies.
 Multi delivery/pickup at same location: Rapidis ApS take into account in their models when
multiple operations are conducted at the same location in a row. In the current model the
service time per visit is 1 min. and for example delivering 5 customers and picking up 5
others ready to go at the same time at Roskilde St. will take 10 min. in the current model,
which is overkill. Dial a ride would beneﬁt by taking this into account.
 Variable driving times: In real life vehicles will take shorter or longer times to go from A
to B than the DRT matrix implies. In other words there will be some deviations from the
plans. A commercial product would have to mitigate when vehicles hits unexpected delays
by perhaps swapping a request planned at a later stage in the delayed route to another
vehicle. Adding variable driving times would add stochasticity to the problem.
Each of these diﬀerent dimensions would aﬀect the performance of the dial a ride diﬀerently. Driver
shifts, refueling considerations and vehicle breakdowns would all add to utilization ineﬃciencies
over time. Cancellations/no shows add to the complexities of the problem and added ineﬃciencies
if customers are not charged for a fare, particularly in the case of a no show. Flexible time win-
dows would reduce no. denied requests and thus increase requests served per vehicle, but on the
Department of Management Engineering, Technical University of Denmark, DTU
74
==PAGE:81==
Converting Schedule Based Buses to Demand Responsive Public Transport
-
other hand reduce QoS with regard to waiting time. Hard delivery time windows is just a diﬀerent
setup in the instance generation and would not eﬀect the constraints or the solver as such, the
same maximum ride time constraint would apply as before. Adding diﬀerent vehicle types might
beneﬁt the dial a ride in terms of cost by introducing smaller cheaper vehicles as a part of the
ﬂeet, as average capacity utilization does not go beyond 3.5 pass./veh. in the results (Figure 29).
Adding group orders and taking into account shorter service times per passenger where multiple
passengers are picked up/ delivered to the same location would increase the capacity utilization of
the vehicles. Carpooling and oﬀering routine commuting trips would also help increase eﬃciency.
That would make the problem partly static, providing more time to optimize, and the service
closer to being schedule based. Such a fusion between the systems is likely to be cheaper than
what is reported here. Finally wheelchair accessibility would incur some costs for the system but
on the other hand, the already in place para-transit could be merged with it bringing the total
cost of both down.
To summarize if all these additional considerations were to be taken into account it would have
unpredictable eﬀects on the outcome. Some would beneﬁt and other handicap the dial a ride, but
on aggregate possibly balance out its performance. Further studies could analyze the impacts of
these diﬀerent factors on the solution quality.
5.4
Potential Use of Model and Further Development
5.4.1
Use of Model in its Current State
The model in its current state models driving times well because of the realistic drive time matrix.
It can be used as decision support tool for operators and municipalities thinking about imple-
menting a dial a ride service. It will give an upper bound on the no. vehicles needed to reach
a certain QoS regarding denial ratio. That upper bound would be contingent upon the quality
of demand estimates provided as input to the model. This both refers to quantity of users per
day and OD-location patterns. Assigning weights to addresses would be a way to do this, where
weight could be added according to state of the art transport modeling techniques. In the current
implementation each address-point would be multiplied according to those weights as was done in
the T50 scenario. If the possible weight settings in that case would be too many the number of
address data-points however grow exponentially, so an empirical distribution would likely be the
way to model this much like was done for the demand pattern over time, avoiding multiplication
of data points.
The primary feature of this model however is its’ ability to compare ride times of three diﬀerent
modes of transportation; automobile, dial a ride service and conventional public transit. After
sampling enough test estimates for OD-pairs could be gathered into a cost matrix that could be
used as an input to route choice models for example. The model also gives a detailed capacity
utilization information for the dial a ride showing the behavior of the system over time. Several
additional steps however would be required to build a viable product.

There are two sets of products that could be developed from the current model

In case of the decision support tool the program is pretty close to being usable. It runs a simulation
running instances that the user can set up with diﬀerent assumptions and pumps out a solution
along with several text ﬁles with relevant computational results. It however in its current form
has to be done within the source code. The ﬁrst feature therefore that would have to be added is

standardized input, preferably in a Graphical User Interface (GUI). Then the second feature could
be a standardized report generator generating graphs, tables and a visualization of the solution
potentially in a pdf or even a modiﬁable tex ﬁle where the end user could add his own comments
to the report. Additionally outputs in the form of text ﬁles would have to be generated as well

A dispatching solution would need more work. First of all drivers need instructions of how to
get from A to B. The direct ride time matrix does not have that routing information. Storing
such information in memory would not be feasible but retrieving routing from Google for example
via JSON - query [27] after the route has been optimized could be an approach. Then addresses
would be connected via a database to a clusterID which would be the origin destination index
in the cost matrix itself that very well might be stored in memory like was done in this project.
Storing a matrix the size of what was used here should not be a problem for a commercial server.
A matrix with the same level of accuracy could also be applied in Copenhagen for example. The
Copenhagen area is not signiﬁcantly larger than the service area deﬁned for this study although
the number of addresses is signiﬁcantly higher and there are less empty areas that would reduce
the size of the matrix. The matrix for Copenhagen would likely be smaller than the bus ride
time matrix for Roskilde which was stored in memory during the tests. The main limits is in the
number of active requests the algorithm can handle, not the size of the cost matrix (if deﬁned in
this way). Thus improvements to the algorithm would have to be made if the demand reaches
numbers signiﬁcantly higher than 400-500 active requests at any given time. An operator with
less demand than that can use the implemented tool without much modiﬁcations.
Additionally a GUI would have to be developed for the operators for them to be able to manually
see and possibly change plans made by the algorithm if something comes up. The model would
likely never be able to react to every single situation even by taking all additional aspects from
Section 5.3.4 into account. In such a scenario the end user has to be able to make real time
modiﬁcations to the plan.
To summarize there is clear business potential for the product. A dispatching solution made from
scratch would also meet signiﬁcant competition in the Nordic region from companies such as the
Danish Drivr [22], Swedish UbiGo [37] and Finnish MaaS [43] along with the international giants
Lyft [77] and UberPool [73] to name a few.
Lastly one must remember that although the DRPT proved to be signiﬁcantly more expensive
than the CPT, that does not disprove it as a valid business concept. Had the angle been taken
to oﬀer the service as an alternative to the private vehicle there is next no doubt that it would
prove really competitive. The Kutsluplus experience proves that people are willing to pay more
for demand responsive service [56] but taking away the cheapest option, conventional buses, would
not be likely to be a popular decision among those less aﬄuent. A privately run business however
would likely be more proﬁtable since it could market itself as a cheaper alternative to taxis and
private vehicles, and quicker than a bus. It would also not be bound by as strict requirements
regarding level of service as a public option would, although denial ratio could not exceed much
more than 5%-10% for it to be deemed reliable [71]. Any business trying to go intu this market
however would have the challenge of becoming large enough to work [67], as was the case for the
town scenario where the number of vehicles were not many enough to be utilized eﬃciently.

the moment. What became evident during the work on this thesis is that it was a large problem

5.5.1
Operation Research Opportunities
At the start of the implementation process for the algorithm the idea was to develop an ALNS
or a VNS algorithm resulting in a LNS which in the end was reduced to a greedy best insertion
coupled with local search. While VNS has successfully been used to solve dynamic DARP [62]
it had signiﬁcantly less no. dynamic requests in general. So using heuristics truly eﬃcient and
scalable insertion method is called for capable of answering requests in real time such as the one
proposed in this study. What would be most likely to give good results is introducing stochasticity
to the problem and using simulated lookahead to optimize with regard to the most likely future
scenario [62][10].
A special mention is the Alonso-Mora et al. (2017) [2] paper that served as a key inspiration for
the presentation of results in this thesis and shows some really promising results for a large scale
DRPT system using exact methods. A study that would recreate the results of that paper using
their method and applied in another setting, such as Roskilde or Copenhagen, and compared to a
heuristic would be an interesting topic.
5.5.2
Empirical Studies
The methods presented could be applied empirically to multiple areas around Denmark and else-
where. Then DRT and BRT matrices could be gathered from various places and the aim of the
study could be to identify what properties an area and a pre existing bus service must have for
DRPT to be a cheaper option. Results from that could be used by the likes of Movia to systematic-
ally identify areas where DRPT is feasible. It is proposed that areas relatively isolated, containing
bus lines less eﬃcient than the ones in Roskilde, should be analyzed ﬁrst. Roskilde is concluded
to be too dense, with public transport network too eﬃcient to have it replaced by DRPT thus
providing an upper bound to the properties an area can have to be ripe for replacement.
5.5.3
Transport Modeling
The results with expected ride times and cost estimates for the DRPT could be used as input to
a route choice model in a Transport Modeling study. That could model whether passengers would
choose to use the DRPT over a private vehicle for example. The study could give insight into the
real eﬀects on demand when DRPT is added as a new source of transport supply.

6
Conclusion
The aim of this study was to assess whether dial-a-ride service oﬀered for the general public can
be a feasible alternative to conventional schedule based bus system in a small city, with Roskilde
as a case study.
Four scenarios were set up for the morning traﬃc of Roskilde city and surrounding areas, with
two diﬀerent assumptions in the OD-location trip distribution; uniform between all addresses and
50% probability of going downtown to Roskilde St. in the morning. For each scenario the demand
for the bus lines within it were used as input into a dial a ride solver that was implemented in a
discrete event simulation model. The bus lines targeted for replacement were all bus lines within
their respective service areas excluding two of the most utilized lines in Roskilde municipality. It
became clear analytically that the eﬃciency required of the dial a ride would be beyond feasibility
if any cost reductions were to be made by replacing those popular lines.
Replacing the less utilized lines however was shown to be feasible for future low-medium cost es-
timates for driverless vehicles, probable by the year 2030. Those conclusions were reached for the
scenarios with 50% probability of going to the station after that scenario had proven to give more
positive results than the uniform alternative, with enough no. vehicles to cover approximately
95% of requests. Using current cost structures however will yield approximately twice the cost
of bus system if dial a ride is to be implemented. It is thus not recommended that the city of
Roskilde adopts a purely dynamic dial a service until self driving technologies mature, if cost is
the main criteria.
The quality of service given the assumptions made however are signiﬁcantly higher for the dial a
ride than the bus system. This was speciﬁcally true for the ride times of dial a ride which were
lower than 50% of expected ride times with CPT for the same request. The reason for this is that
the transit-auto travel time ratio (TATT) was quite high (2.3 −2.9 without taking into account
walking time, 3.3 −4.1 taking it into account, where > 2.0 without walk time considered tedious
for all users [71]) for the CPT whereas the dial a ride was between 1.9−2.3. TATT > 2 is often the
only feasible service level for a small city with surrounding rural areas which proved to be the case
here. It was also argued in terms of QoS that the accessibility of the system would be increased by
deﬁnition; service can be ordered to any address, whereas accessibility is constrained by proximity
to stops for CPT. Allowing for denial however does reduce the reliability of the system vs. the
bus system proposing a trade-oﬀin that regard.
Capacity utilization of the vehicles was up to 2.5 −3.5 passengers per vehicle for the peak with
examples of the vehicles reaching top capacity of 8, proving the heuristic promotes real sharing.
The heuristic developed to solve the problem was greedy construction using best insertion coupled
with local search using random removal and the same best insertion to plug it back in, local search
was allowed to iterate for 1 sec. per request. Solutions were reported for up to 500 active running
requests and 55 vehicles at any given simulation time. There were signs that the heuristic was
reaching the limit of its capabilities to solve the problem within a second for the largest instance
sizes used.
All in all the cost estimates this projects provides gives a ﬁrst order approximate of what the
eﬀects might be of converting from buses to demand responsive public transport. Although the
dial a ride is not deemed feasible to replace CPT in the modern context it is projected to be so in
the driverless future. This is given assumptions where the CPT every step of the way was given
the beneﬁt of the doubt. First of all the bus lines the DRPT was meant to replace in many cases
did not operate for a full day, compared to 16 hrs. service span for the DRPT. Secondly QoS
as discussed was higher than can be expected of CPT. Thirdly the low response time the DRPT
has to comply with by design is much lower than modern dial a ride services typically comply
Department of Management Engineering, Technical University of Denmark, DTU
78
==PAGE:85==

with [47][68], often not accepting request unless at least 2 hrs. in advance. Furthermore, only
1 passenger was allocated per request, taking group orders and any such patterns into account
would beneﬁt the dial a ride. That brings up the ﬁnal point that likely if the dial a ride was
truly to be oﬀered as public option it would likely be partly static, partly scheduled, where users
would be incentivised to plan shared commuting trips. It is thus concluded that the cost estimates
presented here provide an upper bound to what is possible.
Future studies could focus on the demand side, both to model the input to the problem for example
taking into account stop to stop demand. It could also model the possible extra demand DRPT
might generate from vehicle owners and bicycle riders which was not considered here but would
have a big impact on the results. The demand side of the project would also be interesting use
for a solving approach that uses simulated lookahead and perhaps machine learning techniques
based on past data. Finally empirical studies could be made where the aim is to identify the
characteristics of a town and a bus system where DRPT might be a cost eﬃcient alternative using
similar techniques to what is presented here.
Wider implications of this study is that CPT is not going anywhere anytime soon. The two most
utilized bus lines were far from feasible to replace, even for driverless vehicles. This implies that
in the cities of the future buses, trains and other means of schedule based public transport will
continue to be a dominant cheap option for travelers. DRPT will however as it gets cheaper replace
less utilized lines and will in a wider context prove much cheaper than private vehicle providing
competitive QoS attracting users from those modes. These diﬀerent modes of transportation will
co exist in the future with CPT still providing the cheapest option but DRPT attracting users
willing to pay for higher QoS.
