1.1
Project Background and Motivation
The candidate worked with Beta Technologies, a company committed to designing the next generation of ﬂight:
electriﬁed vertical-take-oﬀ-and-landing (eVTOL) aircraft. There are two primary classes of VTOL aircraft: wing-
borne and rotor-borne (helicopter or gyrocopter designs). Wing-borne VTOL virtually requires a transition stage
between VTOL and cruise modes, which has traditionally made stabilization and control of the aircraft diﬃcult.
With the ever-increasing capabilities of modern computing, aircraft control is no longer an issue, thus the beneﬁts
of range and safety during ﬂight of a wing outweigh the diﬃculties of transition. An example wing-borne VTOL
aircraft is seen in Figure 1.1:
(a) Hover
(b) Cruise
Figure 1.1: NASA’s Greased Lightning, focus on dual-mode operation [Bowman, 2016]
There are many limitations to electriﬁed ﬂight, similar to electric vehicles (EVs). The primary limiting factor in
a land-borne electric vehicle is range, which is normally increased by adding battery mass. In an aircraft, mass is a
primary limiting factor, so adding battery mass may actually result in a reduced range, as the other components of
the aircraft have to be improved to carry the additional battery mass. As these components are increased, the aircraft
mass similarly increases, thereby increasing the power requirements, causing a positive feedback loop and potentially
making the aircraft design diverge. The other primary method of increasing an electric vehicle’s range is to increase
the system eﬃciency, which is more applicable to an eVTOL design. Increasing the system eﬃciency creates a more
eﬀective use of the existing battery mass, increasing the range and reducing the necessary cooling mass. A small
change in eﬃciency can have a multiplicative eﬀect on the system mass, similar to the positive feedback in the initial
situation.
The electric ﬂight industry is pursuing a wing-borne eVTOL design, with two primary modes: hover and cruise.
Contrary to an electric car, which uses a large range of torque and RPM, eVTOL ﬂight has a speciﬁc operation
envelope, with comparatively little variation in torque or speed within these modes. The predicted cruise and hover
power usage of the proposed aircraft can be seen in Figure 1.2, overlaid on an Emrax 228 motor torque-speed
versus eﬃciency plot. The proposed aircraft has a relatively large1 envelope around the ideal VTOL operating point
to account for control and stabilization manoeuvres, as well as diﬀering environmental conditions (temperature,
elevation, humidity, etc). The cruise mode is more constrained, as the motors and propellers are not critical for
control of the aircraft, so the envelope primarily varies with environmental conditions.
The large diﬀerence between cruise and hover operation makes motor design for this application diﬃcult. The
overall energy usage is dominated by the cruise portion of the ﬂight, but it is infeasible to size the motor where the
cruise envelope is within the eﬃciency peak, due to the high power requirements of hover.

2.1
Electric Power and Propulsion System Architecture
Prior to discussing the individual components, it is important to understand the overall system architecture. The
baseline EPPS, seen in Figure 2.1, has eight primary motors for both hover and cruise use. The two motors at each
“corner” of the aircraft are coaxial and counter-rotating.
Figure 2.1: Proposed Dual Energy Storage EPPS Architecture [Beta]
5

For the purposes of this study, the eight motors will be treated equally, with an even distribution of the total
aircraft load. The motors are directly attached to the propellers, in a direct-drive system. Each power distribution
bus attaches one energy storage system to eight of the 16 three-phase motor controllers. Each motor is thus driven
by two motor controllers (one from each energy storage system) for maximum redundancy.
2.2
Conceptual Machine Design
The machine topology is critical to meeting the input requirements for the study, namely fault tolerance and power
density. The candidate considered many diﬀerent AC motor topologies, including Synchronous Reluctance (SynR),
Switched Reluctance (SR), Induction (Asyn), Surface Permanent Magnet (SPM), and Interior Permanent Magnet
(IPM) machines.
Since the motor mass is important to the overall aircraft performance, PM machines are the
primary focus of this analysis due to their inherent speciﬁc power advantages over the other designs. PM machines
do introduce another failure method—demagnetization—but this typically occurs gradually over multiple thermal
cycles and can be accounted for in maintenance of the aircraft.
2.2.1
Machine Control
All of the proposed machine topologies are Permanent Magnet Synchronous Machines, producing a sinusoidal or
approximately sinusoidal back EMF. Thus the technique to drive the machine involves synchronous, aligned magnetic
ﬁelds in stator and rotor. This is typically represented by using a direct-quadrature rotating axis, as seen in Figure
2.2, where the direct axis (d-axis) is aligned with the permanent magnet in the machine, and the quadrature axis
(q-axis) is leading the d-axis by 90 electrical degrees, which is 90 mechanical degrees in a two-pole machine. In this
ﬁgure, the a′ coil is just past the peak change in ﬂux, as the q-axis has just rotated past it. This implies it is near
the peak EMF, as the EMF E is proportional to the negative change in ﬂux per time, −dB
dt .

2.2.2
Surface Permanent Magnet Machine
In a Surface-mounted Permanent Magnet machine (SPM) the rotor structure has a soft ferrous material under the
surface mounted magnets, as seen in Figure 2.4. SPMs are known for their relatively high eﬃciency and speciﬁc
torque. These advantages make SPMs a strong contender for EV application.
Figure 2.4: Surface Permanent Magnet Electric Machine [Upadhyay, 2015]
The primary failure mode of a PM machine is demagnetization, which can occur suddenly due to impact, or
over time due to thermal cycling. High heat in the motor is the most common cause of demagnetization. Once
demagnetization begins, the current through the motor must be higher to supply the same power, thereby causing
higher internal temperatures, further increasing demagnetization in a positive feedback loop. Cooling of PM motors
is critical to safe operation. The other most common failure modes are coil failures and mechanical failures. Coil
failures typically occur due to vibration causing wear in the coil insulation. A coil failure can cause inter-winding
linkage, leading to faults in other phases. Mechanical failure occurs due to bearing failure or rotor eccentricity,1 but
due to the mechanical nature it will not be considered in this study.
General Advantages of SPM:
• High torque and power density
• Low torque ripple, low electromagnetic noise and acoustic vibration
• Moderate-high peak eﬃciency
General Disadvantages of SPM:
• Uses shaped rare earth magnets, so high material cost

rotor angle. In extreme cases, this can cause rotor/stator contact, potentially destroying the motor.

• Small “island of eﬃciency.” Very good at operating at a nominal RPM/torque, but eﬃciency drops oﬀwith

2.2.3
Interior Permanent Magnet Machines
Interior Permanent Magnet Machines (IPMs) use soft ferrous rotor with embedded permanent magnets, as seen in
Figure 2.5. They often have ﬂux-concentration properties by utilizing rotor saliency, which allows for very high ﬂux in
the air-gap between stator and rotor. By increasing the ﬂux density (B), it is possible to increase the overall torque,
as torque is proportional to ﬂux density as seen in Equation 3.3. IPMs often have negative saliency (high resistance
to ﬂux in the q-axis) due to addition of air-gaps in the rotor core, which improves operation in the constant-power
region. Unfortunately, this reliance on rotor salience to induce reluctance torque typically limits the number of
magnetic pole pairs in and IPM design to four or fewer, as the reluctance torque is proportional to the inductance
in the windings, which is itself proportional to the square of the number of magnetic poles [Soong, 2008].
Figure 2.5: Interior Permanent Magnet Electric Machine [Upadhyay, 2015]
Demagnetization in an IPM due to mechanical contact is nearly eliminated, due to the magnets being embedded.
However, the embedded magnets have less cooling than in an SPM design, but the large thermal mass of the rotor
back iron helps to reduce this impact. Demagnetization can still occur under a high load condition for a long period
of time. Coil failures in an IPM behave similarly to those in an SPM. Interior permanent magnet machines nearly
eliminate the potential for eccentricity issues aﬀecting the magnets, enabling a smaller airgap and thereby increasing
the ﬂux density.
General Advantages of IPM:
• Moderate-high torque and power density
• Resistance to demagnetization
• Robust and cheap rotor construction
• Moderate peak eﬃciency
General Disadvantages of IPM:
• Limited to interior-rotor designs
• Reluctance torque limits the number of magnetic pole pairs, typically ≤4
• Requires smaller air-gap than a surface-mount PM
– Higher tolerance construction, so higher assembly cost
– Slightly higher potential for mechanical failure due to rotor-stator contact
• Small island of eﬃciency

2.2.5
Fault Tolerance
In aerospace application, fault tolerance is a primary design consideration, especially for safety-critical components
such as the drive and communications systems. According to the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) regulations
for General Aviation, no single-point-of-failure may cause loss of control of the aircraft. For a commercial aircraft
the regulations are more strict: during any single failure or combination of failures not shown to be “extremely
improbable” the aircraft must be “capable of continued safe ﬂight and landing . . . within the normal ﬂight envelope,
without requiring exceptional piloting skill or strength” [FAA, 2017].
This design-for-failure idea extends to the electric machine design for the aircraft. A direct-drive motor design
eliminates the mechanical gearbox—a single-point-of-failure component—from the drive system, greatly increasing
the potential for fault tolerance, in addition to increasing the system eﬃciency. While the aircraft design, including
number and distribution of propellers, can reduce the impact of a single (or possibly multiple) total motor failure,
it is preferable to include a fault-tolerant motor design to increase the number of concurrent failures before loss of
control. The most common electrical failures in a PM motor are coil short/open-circuit failures (approx. 40% of all
faults) and demagnetization failures (approx. 15% of all faults) [W.Thanmke et al., 2015].
There are a number of methods to to reduce or eliminate the chances of certain failures occurring. Within the
motor, utilizing one phase winding per slot maximizes thermal isolation and minimizes mutual magnetic coupling be-
tween phases. This prevents phase-to-phase faults, which are more serious than single phase faults. Additionally, the
designer can wind each phase around a single tooth (in adjacent slots) to further prevent magnetic coupling between
phases, due to there being minimal or no overlap of the phase windings at the base of the motor. Demagnetization
is more diﬃcult to abate in the motor electromagnetic design, but the primary cause is increased temperature so a
good cooling design will reduce the chances of demagnetization [Gopalarathnam et al., 2000, Zhang et al., 2017].
2.2.5.1
Phase Conﬁguration
There are two primary failure modes in the motor windings: a short circuit and an open circuit. A three-phase motor
will produce approximately zero torque in event of a symmetric single-phase short-circuit event [Welchko et al., 2003].
In the event of an open-circuit failure, a three phase motor can continue to operate if it is already spinning, due to
a pulsating torque output from the two operational phases, but with a drastically reduced average torque output.
Additionally, this causes an increase—up to 200%—in the phase currents [Giangrande et al., 2016].
The addition of electrical phases will increase the number of phase failures required to cause loss of torque
production. Five and six phase motors are the most common high-order motors, which require three and four faults
prior to loss of torque, respectively. The eVTOL industry is interested in the six phase design due to possibility
of two symmetric three-phase drives from the two energy storage systems in the aircraft for additional system-level
fault-tolerance, as seen in Figure 2.1.
For a multi-phase machine to produce rated power in event of phase failure, each phase must be overrated by
n/(n −1) where n is number of phases. This creates a much higher peak power capability during normal operation,
and serves to decrease the coil losses during rated operation. The high phase-order motor controller design is equally
important to a system-level fault tolerant design [Gopalarathnam et al., 2000]. The controller design is discussed
further in 2.2.5.2.
While most motors are connected in a star or delta conﬁguration, it is possible to utilize individual, isolated
windings to improve the fault tolerance of the machine, as seen in Figure 2.8. While this increases the total number
of conductors required between the controller and the motor, the use of isolated wires allows for better fault-mode
behaviour and allows for a non-zero fault-mode current.
As in a standard electric machine with overlapping windings, magnets in the rotor must induce balanced ﬂux in
order to generate balanced EMFs during operation. In a segmented motor, the number of pole pairs and phases must
be relatively prime, which is most easily achieved using Equation 2.1, where P is pole-pairs and n is the number of
phases [Baudart et al., 2012a]:
P = n ± 1
(2.1)
Due to this diﬀerence in pitch of the pole-pairs and coils, a given phase will never fully intersect the ﬂux produced
by a given magnet; if the ﬂux will either be opposed by the opposite magnet (when P = n + 1) or the ﬂux will ﬂow
through the adjacent stator tooth between the phases (when P = n −1). This does reduce the magnetic eﬃciency
10
==PAGE:21==
of the machine, but is oﬀset by the shorter windings—reducing copper losses—and the higher pole pair count, which
reduces the maximum ﬂux in the stator and rotor back-iron, allowing for less back-iron material or lower iron losses
[Baudart et al., 2012a].
Additionally, in a concentrated, single-winding-per-tooth design, the standard induced voltage waveform is non-
sinusoidal, but can be modiﬁed by changing the ratio of inter- to intra-winding tooth width. A higher intra-winding
width (pushing the positive and negative areas of coil in a single phase farther apart) creates a more sinusoidal EMF.
Increasing the width of the intra-phase tooth reduces the higher-order harmonics in the EMF waveform, as well as
reducing the cogging torque, but does reduce the overall peak of the waveform [Zheng et al., 2013].
2.2.5.2
Controller Conceptual Design
While not the primary focus of this study, the controller design is still important to fault tolerance of the system
and must be linked with the motor design. Since the eVTOL industry is most interested in a six-phase design, the
controller must coordinate those phases under normal and fault operation. The ideal controller allows for independent
control of each phase, and each phase is electrically isolated to allow for fault isolation. There are two primary control
methods, a star-connected machine using a traditional inverter and an isolated H-bridge, as seen in Figures 2.7 and
2.8 respectively. As discussed in Section 2.2.5.1, most most motors utilize a star connection to reduce the number of
conductors necessary. This requires the fault mode currents to sum to zero, which gives fewer control options and
increases the overall torque ripple and losses during fault [Baudart et al., 2012b]. Thus, the H-bridge conﬁguration
in Figure 2.8 is preferable for fault tolerance, but will increase the overall mass of the system.
Figure 2.7: 6-phase motor control using 6-leg inverter [Baudart et al., 2012b]
Figure 2.8: 6-phase motor control using isolated H-bridges [Baudart et al., 2012b]
There are a number of methods to produce fault-mode torque output in the event of a failure, such as continuing
operation as a ﬁve-phase machine, or disabling the entire set of three phases that contain the fault. The former
option will generally have a higher fault-mode torque (up to 90% with no increase of phase currents) but will have a
large torque ripple if the phase current angles are not changed to compensate for the failure. Continuing operation as
a three-phase machine will still have a slightly increased torque ripple over normal operation, but drastically reduced
output torque (approximately 50%) with no increase in phase currents.
As discussed in Section 2.2.5.1, the conductors in the phases can be oversized to allow for a higher fault-mode
current, thereby increasing the fault-mode torque output. This comes at the cost of mass and volume, which will
decrease the non-fault performance of the machine [Chen et al., 2016]. Compensating for a single-phase open-circuit
fault using the ﬁve-phase method is preferable for this reason. A 20% increase in coil mass allows for nominal torque
production in the event of a single failure. Given multiple phase failures, a phase-phase failure, an inverter failure,

3.2.3
Material Selection
Material selection is important to the physics calculations in COMSOL. COMSOL takes parameters from each
material deﬁnition to calculate diﬀerent model values, ranging from ﬂux potential to current to losses to mass. In
practice, the material selection is not entirely critical to the accuracy of the simulation, as many important parameters
are deﬁned within the physics, as described in Section 3.2.4, or accounted for manually, as described in Section 3.4.2.
The within each model, there are two necessary material deﬁnitions. The back iron encompasses the grey domains
in Figure 3.6, and air can be used for all other domains. The candidate used COMSOL’s built-in “soft iron (without
losses)” and “air” for these materials. The speciﬁc material parameters used are shown in Appendix 6.7. Soft iron
without losses can be used, as the iron losses are calculated externally in MATLAB, using real material parameters.
The soft iron material is primarily used to give the relevant magnetic hysteresis characteristics to the metal. Air
is a basic material, with material parameters that can be overridden by the physics deﬁnitions. This model is not
perfectly accurate, but is within a reasonable error for accurate results.
3.2.4
Physics Selection
The physics deﬁnition in COMSOL is where the user dictates how each domain behaves. The primary deﬁnitions
used with the RMM module are Amp`ere’s Law, Magnetic Insulation, Coil, Continuity, and Force Calculation. Each
operation has a number of parameters that can be used to deﬁne diﬀerent domain properties, such as magnetization,
magnetic hysteresis, and coil conductivity.
3.2.4.1
Amp`ere’s Law
Amp`ere’s Law is used to deﬁne the calculations of the air, magnet, and back iron domains. It employs the equations
discussed in Appendix 6.2.1. In the COMSOL RMM module, there is a necessary Amp`ere’s Law deﬁnition that
aﬀects all domains. This deﬁnition can be overridden by other physics deﬁnitions, so in practice it is used for the
air domains. This deﬁnition takes the relative permeability, relative permittivity, and electrical conductivity from
the domain material deﬁnition discussed in Section 3.2.3 to calculate the relevant electromagnetic properties at each
mesh element within the domain.
Amp`ere’s Law is also used to deﬁne the magnet domains, where magnetization is used instead of relative perme-
ability. This deﬁnition utilizes the cylindrical coordinate system described in Figure 3.3 to ensure the magnets are
directed radially, or in the φ direction for Halbach ﬂux-focusing magnets, at all points of rotation. The candidate
used a moderate magnetization for neodymium magnets of 850[kA/m] for all magnet deﬁnitions.
3.2.4.2
Magnetic Insulation
Magnetic insulation is used to limit the simulation space in COMSOL. While in reality any magnet or magnetic eﬀect
will aﬀect any object that is not inﬁnitely far away, the eﬀect is negligible beyond a given distance. To account for
this, COMSOL assumes that the outer boundary of the ﬁnal assembly is perfectly magnetically insulated, meaning

machine, one and a half times the OD of the machine. Reducing the simulation space to this external air allows

nodes.

Force Calculation
COMSOL’s force calculation operator uses Maxwell’s Stress Tensor, described in Appendix 6.2.2. While the candidate
used Arkkio’s Torque Method for two of the designs, Arkkio’s Method gave inaccurate results for the AWM design.
COMSOL’s Force Calculation operator calculates the MST at the external boundary of the set of domains selected.
In the case of the rotor domains, this boundary is within the airgap5. The airgap requires at minimum two domains:
one for the rotor and one for the stator. Simply using two domains can cause some issues internal to COMSOL, where
the tensor cannot be calculated properly at the boundary pair, so for accuracy of torque calculation the candidate
created four domains within the airgap (detailed in Figure 3.7). This allows the torque to be calculated for the overall
machine. The accuracy of MST calculation in COMSOL is strongly aﬀected by the time step of the simulation, with
a smaller time step giving a smoother curve, with a smaller torque ripple.

3.2.5
Mesh Construction
While the model geometry and materials both aﬀect the physics calculations of each domain, the mesh aﬀects the
accuracy of calculation within said domain. In general, the mesh can be coarse or ﬁne, with a coarser mesh giving
less accurate results than a ﬁne mesh, but taking less computation time. COMSOL can generate a mesh, simply
accepting an approximate element size as input. In practice, this often creates a mesh that is slow to simulate and
not as accurate as a more involved mesh.
COMSOL has user options to construct an application-speciﬁc mesh. There are a number of operations, but the
candidate only employed four: distribution, edge, edge copy, and free triangular. The distribution and edge are used
in conjunction to create an even distribution of boundary nodes, edge copy is used to copy said boundary nodes to
another boundary, and free triangular uses COMSOL’s built-in mesh generation to create a distribution of triangular
elements in the remaining domains. The operations are shown in Figure 3.7:

The airgap is most critical object to mesh in an electric machine. As discussed in Section 3.2.4, the airgap is
comprised of four separate domains, with an edge between each, plus an additional edge known as the identity pair,
that COMSOL uses for moving the mesh to simulate machine rotation. It is possible to create four distributions and
four edge meshes on each of the airgap boundaries, but it is faster to create one and use the copy edge operator to
create the same mesh on the other boundaries. To accomplish this, the candidate created a distribution of nodes along
one boundary within the boundary pair, as seen in Figure 3.7a. The candidate used 150 nodes for this distribution,
as it gave a clean ﬁnal mesh and thus accurate results without increasing simulation time overly much. This edge
distribution can be copied to the additional edges within the airgap, as seen in Figure 3.7b, and then the remaining
geometry is ﬁlled in using the free triangular operator as seen in Figure 3.7c.
The free triangular operator has a number of parameters: maximum element size, minimum element size, max-
imum growth rate, curvature factor, and resolution of narrow regions. Maximum and minimum element size are
self-explanatory; they limit the range of element edge lengths. The smaller these are, the ﬁner the mesh. The can-
didate chose 5[mm] and 0.01[mm], respectively. This allows for accurate representation of the areas near the airgap,
and in narrow regions like the corners of the magnets and the IPM bridges. Maximum element growth rate is the
maximum ratio of side length within a given element, and limits the growth of element area. A low growth rate
leads to a ﬁner mesh over the whole machine. The candidate found that a 1.5 maximum growth rate allowed for
an even mesh throughout the back iron and air domains, while maintaining a ﬁne mesh in the magnets, coils, and
airgap. The curvature factor limits how large a mesh element can be along a curved region; a low value leads to a
better approximation of the curve, instead of a series of line segments. The candidate selected a curvature factor of
0.15, but changing the value had a minimal eﬀect on the ﬁnal mesh. Due to the low element sizes and growth rate,
the elements along the curved boundaries in the design approximated said curves acceptably. Finally, the resolution
of narrow regions controls the number of layers within narrow regions. Again, this value had minimal eﬀect on the
ﬁnal mesh, as the ﬁne mesh already created multiple nodes in any narrow regions in the design. The candidate used
the default value of one. The parameters used are listed in Appendix 6.8, in Table 6.8.

Chapter 4
Design Optimization
Mathematical optimization is the process of selecting a near-optimal design from a large set of possible designs.
Mathematical optimization generally involves a computer program1 to ﬁnd input values that maximize or minimize
a given function, typically called the objective function. Many subdivisions of optimization exist, each of which
are used to solve a speciﬁc type of problem.
Optimization problems range from simple, single-variable, known
functions to convex unknown functions, to complex, multivariate, unknown functions with multiple local extrema.
The COMSOL simulations discussed in Section 3.2 fall into the last category of optimization problems, as they are
eﬀectively a multivariate black-box objective function.
4.1
Selecting an Optimization Method
4.1.1
Fast Optimization Methods
Fast optimization methods, also known as local optimization, such as Gradient Descent or Newton’s Method, are
most commonly used to ﬁnd extrema for a function that is known to be derivable and convex. These methods are
generally the fastest of all optimization methods [Borwein and Borwein, 1987]. With a derivable and convex function,
the fast optimizer will ﬁnd the global extrema. In a larger global function, that is not convex, a fast optimizer may
ﬁnd the global optimum, or it may get “stuck” on a local extrema. These local optimization methods can be used
to approximate the global solution of a non-convex function by running the algorithm multiple times with diﬀerent
starting points, to ﬁnd multiple local extrema, returning the best as the global “optimum”. This is not guaranteed
to give the true optimum and suﬀers in accuracy if the objective function is non-derivable.
There is no guarantee that the outputs of the COMSOL simulations are convex or derivable, so fast local methods
are discounted due to the uncertainty of an accurate solution.
4.1.2
Global Optimization Methods
Another subset of optimization algorithms, called global optimizers, use various methods to ﬁnd or approximate the
true global optimum. They often include derivative-free algorithms, to allow for convergence even with the presence
of cusps or discontinuities. These are often split into deterministic algorithms (DA) and heuristic algorithms (HA).
Deterministic algorithms for global optimization, while guaranteed to converge to the optimal solution, are often too
slow for practical use. Given the length of time that even a single simulation in COMSOL takes2, implementing a
DA—requiring thousands to millions of simulations—would be infeasible. Heuristic algorithms do not necessarily
give the true optimal solution, but provide a result that is approximately optimal much faster than a DA [Ting,
2005]. Thus, the candidate selected a HA, as speed is of greater importance than perfect accuracy.
There are other non-deterministic algorithms, such as the Monte-Carlo method which employs a pseudo-random
number generator to generate inputs into the function to be optimized, thereby approximating the optimal solu-
tion. HAs utilize some heuristic to improve upon these random methods, thus requiring fewer objective function
computations.
Genetic Algorithms (GAs) are a subclass of HAs that employ a set of heuristics inspired by natural evolution
to more quickly converge to an approximately optimal solution. Genetic algorithms create an initial population
of potential solutions, typically by varying the input parameters randomly within the parameter constraints. The
random parameters corresponding to a simulation are known as genes. A portion of this initial population is selected,
generally by the “most ﬁt” parents, to “breed” the next generation. The most ﬁt parents are selected from the

for this reason. These parents are “bred” by randomly selecting a pair of ﬁt parents and using crossover and mutations
to create a unique child.
Crossover is the process of combining multiple parent genes into a single child. Typically, this is done at the bit
level or at the byte level. The child is a combination of the parents’ genes, as seen in Figure 4.1[Eshelman, 1991]:
Figure 4.1: Gene Crossover
Mutation is the process of changing the child’s genes, often by swapping random bits in the gene information,
in order to maintain genetic diversity.
Mutation causes GAs to avoid genetic stagnation and local extrema, by
preventing generations from becoming too similar. Most GA solvers have a mutation parameter that aﬀects the
chance of mutation. The chance of mutation should be kept low, or the GA will become a more computationally
expensive random-search algorithm [Ronco and Benini, 2013].
Mutation and crossover heuristics allow the GA to converge in a relatively low run time. Generally, the population
size depends on the number of input parameters, and number of generations relates to the shape of the ﬁtness function.
The population size and number of generations generally remain exactly and close to constant, respectively, within
a given type of optimization, so after one initial optimization future run-times can be predicted.
The candidate selected a genetic algorithm to approximate the optimal parameters for each motor design, due to
the relatively fast and predictable runtime, and compatibility with the simulation software, COMSOL.
4.2
Implementing the Optimization Method
As each motor design is simulated using COMSOL, the options for parameter optimization using a genetic algorithm
are limited by the program interface. COMSOL interfaces with MATLAB using COMSOL LiveLink as discussed in
Section 3.3, enabling usage of MATLAB’s large body of optimization options. Speciﬁcally, the candidate implemented
the Genetic Algorithm from MATLAB’s Global Optimization Toolbox.
The simulation of each population member is the same process as described in Section 3.2, the only diﬀer-
ence being that the GA in MATLAB uses MATLAB commands to reference the model in COMSOL, such as
model.param.set(ParamName, ParamVal) to change parameter values and model.sol(SolutionName).runAll to
compute the model solution. MATLAB constructs the entire model at each iteration of the optimization process,
which can lead to some issues with domain and boundary selection. Reconstructing the model with a diﬀerent set
of machine parameters can cause COMSOL’s internal domain labels to change, making deﬁnitions relative to the
geometry components during geometry construction very important.
MATLAB’s GA behaves similarly to a generic GA as described in Section 4.1.2, with some key diﬀerences. The
GA function terminates upon reaching either of two conditions: the maximum number of generations are reached,
or the population of a given generation is within a speciﬁed tolerance. Additionally, MATLAB’s GA breeds parents
somewhat diﬀerently than described in Section 4.1.2. The MATLAB GA uses a set of functions to select ﬁt parents
and breed children: tournament, mutation, and crossover functions.
The MATLAB tournament function selects the ﬁttest parents from the previous generation to breed the next
generation. It accomplishes this by staging noParents/tourneySize tournaments, where a set of tourneySize parents
are randomly chosen from a generation, and then the ﬁttest parent is chosen from each tournament.
The MATLAB mutation function takes a random number from a Gaussian distribution with mean zero, and adds
it to each entry of the parent gene vector. The Gaussian distribution begins with an initial range set by the GA
function input parameter bounds, the initial standard deviation is set to one, and the standard deviation decreases
each generation linearly until the standard deviation is zero at the maximum generation. This occurs before using
the crossover function to breed the parents.
The MATLAB crossover function selects entire post-mutation genes from the parents to crossover, rather than
crossing bits or bytes. It accomplishes this by creating a pseudo-random binary vector, and selects the gene from
parent one if the binary value is 1 and from parent two if the value is 0. If the parents are p1 = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5] and
p2 = [a, b, c, d, e], and the binary vector is [0, 1, 0, 1, 1], then the function returns a child vector child = [a, 2, c, 4, 5].
25
==PAGE:36==
In addition to breeding, MATLAB’s GA also allows a set of “elite” individuals to pass unchanged to the next
generation. This can help to save time by ensuring that the GA does not waste time rediscovering old candidates, and
helps to maintain variability in the gene pool. This can cause some issues with local extrema, so the elite population
is typically kept to a small portion of the total population. The candidate allowed 5% of each generation to pass to
the next. This portion allows enough gene variation to provide stability of results, while still avoiding local extrema
and repeated gene simulation.

5.6
Suggestions for Future Research
The future of ﬂight is electric, and thus further research into the subject is critical. This thesis builds a strong base
of knowledge, however, there are a number of areas where additional research could lead to a better overall system
design, and increase the performance of future eVTOL aircraft.
The designs in this thesis were primarily optimized for a component parameter; optimizing for the system param-
eter would likely further increase the system performance. While it is much more diﬃcult to optimize for a system
parameter (ﬂight range in this application), the results will be more eﬀective for the overall system than optimizing
for a component parameter (eﬃciency in this thesis). System optimization requires many more constraints, such as
the output power to ensure each design can meet the hover and cruise portions of ﬂight. System parameter optimiza-
tion would require a large body of simulations for each iteration of the optimizer to accurately portray the motor
design’s torque-speed-eﬃciency behaviour to accurately calculate ﬂight range, however full system optimization is
certainly possible, given enough time, and will lead to a better aircraft design.
In addition to system design optimization, there are a number of other design changes that could be implemented
to further improve the motor performance. Motor speciﬁc power is critical to aircraft performance, which can be
improved by increasing current density, increasing ﬂux density, and increasing RPM. The current density can be
increased through better machine cooling techniques. As hover is very short during a standard mission, using non-
conventional, short-term cooling techniques such as liquid nitrogen or phase-change cooling could enable a much
higher current density than in the machines presented in this thesis, allowing for much higher speciﬁc power. The
current density could additionally be increased through the use of specialized conductors, such as rectangular ribbon
conductors, which allow a much higher packing factor than standard round conductors. The ﬂux density can be
increased through the use of stronger magnets. The extra expense of specialized conductors and magnets would
mean a small increase in the overall aircraft cost, with a large increase in performance. Finally, it is likely worthwhile
to pursue a high RPM design, either through the use of a gearbox between the motor and propeller, or through a
diﬀerent propeller design. Addition of a gearbox does introduce a single-point-of-failure component, but allows for
a much higher EPPS speciﬁc power through the use of a high RPM motor with a similar speciﬁc torque. Changing
the propeller design does alter many of the other requirements, as the propeller hover power P is related to the
swept area of the propeller A as P ∝
q
1
A, so a small change in propeller size has a large eﬀect on the hover power
requirement. A smaller propeller is more mechanically eﬀective during cruise however, allowing for an increased
cruise speed or reduced cruise power. A small propeller design may allow for a better-performing implementation of
a direct-drive motor, increasing system fault-tolerance and potentially increasing ﬂight range, due to the increased
cruise speed.
5.7
Conclusion
The candidate was successful in increasing the range of the aircraft over the baseline design within the constrained
design space using the surface permanent magnet design. The high-speed design derived from the direct-drive airgap
winding machine results were also competitive with the baseline design, and shows the large impact drive-system
mass has on overall aircraft range. The candidate found that both the AWM and the SPM machine were applicable
to an electric ﬂight application, but at diﬀering design points.
The AWM design has a distinct advantage over the baseline design at very high speed operation, due to the
drastic increase in iron loss with increasing frequency in an SPM machine. Thus implementation of an AWM in
an aircraft using a high reduction-ratio gearbox or high-speed propellers could have higher eﬀectiveness than the
34
==PAGE:45==
baseline design. The primary drawback of a high-speed dual-rotor AWM, like the design presented in this thesis, is
the complex mechanical design, which increases construction costs and possibility of mechanical failure.
The primary advantage of the SPM design is a much higher speciﬁc torque, which allows for a greater battery
mass with a given motor torque requirement. The SPM design is competitive for a low-speed machine, and could
potentially be used in a direct drive application where fault tolerance is critical.
This thesis presents potential electric machine designs for aviation application, with a focus on improving the
system range and fault tolerance over a baseline design with high-speed machine and gearbox. This thesis, and
research like it, will enable the eVTOL industry to increase aircraft performance and usher in a new era of ﬂight.
