Abstract
Traditional spacecraft navigation relies on Earth-based tracking systems such as GPS
and the Deep Space Network (DSN), which have limitations in deep-space missions. This
thesis investigates the use of X-ray pulsars as natural celestial beacons for autonomous
spacecraft navigation. By analyzing pulsar signals and applying advanced signal pro-
cessing techniques, such as Fast Fourier Transform and cross-correlation, demonstrates
the feasibility of precise positioning in space. The simulations indicate that pulsar-based
navigation can achieve an accuracy of 2.9 km. Additionally, a preliminary design of an
X-ray pulsar navigation (XNAV) system has been developed, focusing on detector selec-
tion, signal processing requirements, and system integration. These findings suggest that
X-ray pulsar navigation could enable more autonomous deep-space missions, reducing
reliance on Earth-based infrastructure.

1
Introduction
Navigation is the process of determining one’s position and guiding movement from one
location to another. Whether traveling across the Earth, exploring the depths of the ocean,
or venturing into space, knowing one’s precise location is essential. Traditional navigation
systems, such as GPS, rely on satellites orbiting Earth, but this becomes impossible for
deep space missions where the signals are unavailable.
Many spacecraft have employed active transmitters to be used for determining orbits and
trajectories. It works by the spacecraft receiving a ping from an observation station on
Earth and re-transmit the signal back to Earth. However this method is only usable when
the spacecraft is in the line of sight from Earth and require a large workforce.
In this context, pulsar-based navigation presents a compelling alternative. Pulsars are
rapidly rotating neutron stars that emit highly stable periodic X-ray, radio, or gamma-ray
signals. These signals behave like a natural cosmic beacon, and can serve as reliable
reference points for an autonomous spacecraft navigation system. By measuring the
Time-of-Arrival (TOA) of the pulses and comparing it to the predicted arrival time, it is
possible to determine the position in space with remarkable precision.
Pulsar navigation has the potential to revolutionize deep space travel. GPS relies on a
network of satellites, while Deep Space Network (DSN) depends on ground-based sta-
tions. In contrast, pulsar navigation is entirely self-sufficient and does not require any
external infrastructure.
By using the natural precision of pulsars, a spacecraft could navigate autonomously. This
reduces the dependency of Earth-based tracking systems and enabling more efficient,
independent exploration of the cosmos.
As the exploration of the solar system continues, methods of increasing the navigation
performance while reducing system complexity are becoming attractive to many coming
expeditions.
1.1
Problem statement
This paper address the development of a novel navigation system which is suitable for
use in deep space travel and satellites in orbit.
The proposed navigation system aims to address the limitations on existing navigation
systems by leveraging novel navigation ideas to calculate the 3D position of a spacecraft.
The scope of the project includes evaluation of the methods of position determination and
implementation for minimizing the position error.
While this paper presents a comprehensive conceptual design and theoretical analysis of
a novel navigation system, it still acknowledges certain significant limitations. Firstly, the
project will not advance to the stage of creating a physical prototype of the navigation sys-
tem, which indicate that the design and theoretical models will not be tested or validated
through empirical means. Additionally, the navigational performance characteristics such
as quantum efficiency, gain, and signal processing will not be experimentally verified.
Another limitation is the lack of operational validation in space-like conditions. The naviga-
tion system will not be tested in an environment that simulates space, where factors such

1.2.1
Nicer/SEXTANT
Nicer/SEXTANT uses X-ray signals from millisecond pulsars, to demonstrate the feasi-
bility of autonomous spacecraft navigation in deep space, where the SEXTANT mission
demonstrated the feasibility of X-ray pulsar navigation (XNAV). SEXTANT stands for Sta-
tion Explorer for X-ray Timing and Navigation Technology, which uses the observations of
X-ray pulsars to determine the absolute position in space in the energy range of 0.2 keV
to 12 keV. NASA developed an Extended Kalman Filter to determine the Time-of-Arrival
(TOA) of the pulsar pulse by evaluating the timestamps of individual X-ray photons and
comparing it to a known pulse template. The SEXTANT software autonomously deter-
mines the location in three dimensions of the instrument to within approximately 5 km for
a 2-day period. After the mission, there were some updates to make it usable for a real
case mission, where power and mass is a constraint. Hence, it was stated that the ulti-
mate goal, is to develop detectors and other hardware to make pulsar-based navigation
readily available for future spacecraft. To achieve this goal the focus is on reducing the
size, weight, and power requirement and improving the sensitivity of the instruments. [1]
1.2.2
DARWIN
DARWIN is a proposed mission from ESA, which was meant to be a constellation of free
flying spacecrafts. The number of free flying spacecrafts have changed over the years. It
began with a total of nine spacecrafts, but at the moment the estimated number of space-
craft is between four or five. The mission idea is to search for earth-like planets around
other stars, where it should analyze the atmosphere for chemical composition that emu-
lates life signatures. Three (or four) of the spacecrafts from the DARWIN constellations
would have to carry a 3 m to 4 m telescope, which would have to redirect the light to the
central hub spacecraft. This spacecraft would be able to construct a high resolution im-
age of distant objects. To meet its objective of finding and investigating Earth-like planets,
it would use a technique called ’nulling interferometry’, which is a method of slightly de-
laying light to cancel out incoming light from nearby stars in the image. The light from
planets is naturally delayed between each of the telescopes, because the planet is offset
from the direct line of sight. The clever aspect lies in delaying this light a second time,
which allows the light from planets to be combined constructively. This technique will en-
hance the planet’s signal while effectively cancel out the overwhelming brightness from
the starlight. If this delay is not applied, the planet’s faint glow will be obscured by a much
stronger light from the nearby stars. [2]
To achieve the comparable imaging capabilities, it is necessary for the DARWIN mission
to be part of a constellation of spacecrafts. Without this constellation setup, a telescope
with a diameter of several hundred meters would be required. At the moment this would
be impractical due to the limitations in rocket sizes. Hence, it would need a navigation
system with good precision and also be autonomously to work. [2]

Theory and Methods
2.1
Deep Space Network
One of the solutions at the moment for position determination is Deep Space Network
(DSN). DSN works through a combination of advanced radio tracking techniques, precise
data analysis and the application of orbital mechanics. It consists of a global network
of large antennas located in California, Spain and Australia, this provides a continuous
coverage for spacecrafts as the Earths rotates around itself.
The estimation of a spacecraft’s position relies on radio signals, which are exchanged
between its antennas and the spacecrafts’ antenna. One of the key methods involves
the measuring of the Doppler shifts in the radio signal, which gives the spacecrafts’ radial
velocity, so moving toward or away from Earth. This is complemented by range measure-
ments, which are achieved by looking at the time delay between the transmission and
reception of a signal, this will determine the spacecraft’s distance from Earth. These two
methods will provide crucial information about the Spacecraft’s motion.
When these measurements are collected, they are then integrated into navigation mod-
els, that will account for the spacecraft’s velocity, thrust events, and the gravitational in-
fluences of nearby celestial bodies. The combination of these techniques will allow for
a position estimation with accuracy that is often within a few kilometers for a spacecraft
that is millions of kilometers from Earth, and will be even better for closer mission. The
accuracy of DSN can be calculated with eq. (2.1). [6]
Poserror = 0.75 · AU [km]

However, there are some issues with DSN, these being the need for the spacecraft to be
in view of Earth, which means that it cannot be used when the spacecraft is on the other
side of the Sun compared to Earth. Another issue is also the worsening of the accuracy
as it depends on the distance from Earth, this will give a risk for mission which is going
for deep space and need a high position estimation.
2.2
Navigation
Positioning is the process of determining one’s location by using a signal from a known
reference point. One of these system is GPS, it enables navigation on Earth by measuring
the distance between the user and multiple different reference points, to triangulate the
position of the user.
GPS is based on a constellation of satellites orbiting Earth. These satellites do contin-
uously transmit radio signals, which contain information regarding their position and the
exact time the signal was sent. A GPS receiver on the ground will then use these signals
to calculate its position in a three-dimensional space.
The process for the GPS receiver begins with measuring the time delay between the sig-
nal being transmitted by a satellite and when it is received. As radio signals travels at
the speed of light, can the time delay be converted into a distance between the receiver
and the satellite. To determine the exact position, the GPS receiver will need signals
from at least four different satellites. The need for the four measurement, lies in the less
4

==PAGE:21==
accurate clock at the GPS receiver compared to the satellite clock. With these four mea-
surements, the receiver can use a process called trilateration to calculate the position in
three dimensions: latitude, longitude and altitude. [7]
There exist different kinds of positioning, these being the absolute position, relative posi-
tion and delta correction position.
2.2.1
Absolute position determination
Absolute position determination is the process of an onboard navigation system that can
operate in an absolute (cold-start) mode, which is very desirable for many applications in
space missions. Since there would not be a need for a position estimate from an already
existing position estimator such as DSN. In the absolute position mode, the navigation
system will generate a complete three-dimensional position solution by using its equip-
ment and source measurement. Absolute position determination allows a spacecraft to
navigate and guide itself over to its intended target with complete autonomy. This would
also be an advantageous mode to run with, if suddenly there would come a computer
reset or the spacecraft would have a power failure. [8]
To actively determine the absolute position from variable celestial sources is it necessary
to determine which specific pulse period is being detected at a certain time, basically
which pulsars are being detected. A celestial source does not uniquely identify each of its
pulses, so a technique must be developed to identify a detected pulse relative to a chosen
reference pulse. [8]
2.2.2
Relative position determination
Relative position determination is the determination of its relative position with respect to
another known object, which could be other spacecrafts, observation stations, planetary
bodies and etc. This could also be important for a mission where the relative position is of
high importance. This method allows relative navigation with respect to the object’s frame
of reference, without requiring a full inertial positional knowledge. [8]
One of the advantageous with this method is the need for only a relative position difference
or range difference, instead of a full three-dimensional solution. The techniques from the
absolute position determination can also be implemented into this solution. Here it would
be the computation of the number of pulse profiles between the spacecraft and the known
object to determine the range between the two positions. [8]
2.2.3
Delta-Correction to position solution
A more subtle method is to correct an existing approximate position solution. The delta-
correction technique is used to update a working solution of position such that an improved
solution is produced. One of these methods to update would be the range measurements
from DSN. It would use the signals emitted from variable celestial sources to measure
the error within the estimated solution, with successive measurements it would refine the
estimated solution to an acceptable level of accuracy. [8]

by placing spacecraft all around our galaxies to navigate around. Therefore, it would
make sense to go a bit back in history and use one of the oldest navigation system which
were the optical lighthouse. They were used for warning mariners of potential dangerous

2.3.1
Pulsar classification
In the study of pulsars, they are categorized according to their primary source of energy,
where the two most common types of pulsars are Rotation-Powered pulsars (RPSR) and
Accretion-Powered pulsars (APSR).
2.3.1.1
Rotation-Powered Pulsars
For X-ray pulsars many are of the Rotation-Powered pulsars (RPSR) type where the en-
ergy source is from the stored rotational energy of the star itself. There are two mech-
anisms behind X-ray emission from pulsars i.e. magnetospheric and thermal emission,
where some pulsars can emit X-ray using both mechanisms. The strong magnetic field
that pulsars have as described by Equation 2.3 will accelerate the charged particles along
the field lines, which results in electromagnetic waves being radiated from the magnetic

==PAGE:24==
poles across the spectrum. Due to the misalignment between the magnetic axis and the
rotational axis, a distant observer will detect a pulse as the beam crosses the line-of-sight,
similar to the lighthouse. [13]
As the pulsars’ are created by a supernova they are extremely hot and as they age, local
areas cool at different rates, leaving some areas hotter than others. This thermal energy
causes the electrons to accelerate and collide with each other, and thus also radiates
electromagnetic energy out. Due to the rotation of the pulsar, the beamed X-ray radiated
from the hot spots can be observed as pulses.
As these types of pulsar becomes older, their energy is slowly radiated away, causing the
star to spin down to longer and longer rotation periods. The RPSR can shine for around 10
million year, after they have been slowed down to a period of above 10 s, which causes
it to no longer generate the strong field to accelerate the particles which produces the
beam. Once pulsars reach this stage where they no longer can produce a radiated beam,
they are categorized as “turn off” and are placed in the “pulsar graveyard” since they no
longer can be detected. [13]
These pulsars make for a good clock due to their periods change to be around 100 ns,
which makes them well-suited for navigational uses and calibrate clocks. Due to the
slowing down of the rotation of the pulsar, the crust of the pulsar becomes out of sync with
the super fluid interior, causing the pulsar to go out of equilibrium. This causes variation in
their rotation period, i.e. gradual (The long-term stochastic wandering of the phase, also
known as the “timing noise”) and sudden (Known as glitches and is caused by build up
strain on the crust that suddenly releases). [8, 13, 14] Figure 2.2 shows the main principle

2.3.1.2
Accretion-Powered Pulsars
An Accretion-Powered pulsars (APSR) is a pulsar located in a binary system, in which
the pulsar is powered by material, which is transferred from the companion star. The flow
of material from the companion star is channeled by the magnetic field of the pulsar onto
the companion star. As the pulsar accretes mass from the companion star, it will not only
increase in mass but also transfer its angular momentum to the pulsar. Due to this transfer
of angular momentum it will start to spin-up to a period of a few milliseconds which also
weakens the magnetic field. The mass transferred also heats up the pulsar to be bright
sources of X-ray. These types of pulsars are divided up in two main groups, depending
on the size of the companion star.
• High-Mass X-ray Binary (HMXB): Are binary systems where the companion star of
the pulsar is a high-mass main sequence star of at least 10M⊙−30M⊙. In these
systems the gravitational pull of the pulsar is not strong enough to absorb matter
from the companion star and thus the energy is mainly transferred by stellar winds.
This is also shown in Figure 2.3a.
• Low-Mass X-ray Binary (LMXB): These binary systems have a companion star with
a much lower mass, possibly less than 1 solar mass, or may be white dwarfs. For
these systems, the pulsars gravitational pulls are strong enough to transfer matter
from the companion star. The accretion transfers results in an accretion disk sur-
rounding the pulsar, and X-rays are created when matter from the accretion disk is
transferred to the star. This is also shown in Figure 2.3b.
Although most of the pulsars in binary systems produce X-rays, their pulsation is more
difficult to model since the pulsar rotates around the companion star and the companion
star can eclipse the observer’s line of sight. While these problems exist, the stability of
their periods for these systems remains so high that they cannot be ignored.[8]

2.3.1.3
Millisecond Pulsars
Lastly is the Millisecond Pulsar (MSP) class that is formed from binary systems when the
pulsar has transferred all the mass from the companion star which causes the accretion
process to stop. This causes the pulsar spin-up to a period of milliseconds and the mag-
netic field to decay by a factor of up to 10,000 as what happened for APSR. The reduced
magnetic field causes them to have a much higher stability (period changes of 1 ps per
year) and live for tens of billions of years, which far outlives the two other types of pulsars.

2.5.2
Blackbody radiation
Black-body radiation is the thermal electromagnetic radiation that is emitted by a black
body. A black body is a body that exist in thermodynamic equilibrium, which means it is
an idealized physical body that absorbs all incident electromagnetic radiation.
The different kind of stars can be simulated as a blackbody. A blackbody is basically a
body that emits the maximum amount of heat for its absolute temperature.
The different kind of stars that are going to be simulated for their X-ray are neutron stars,
Supernova remnants, white dwarfs and the sun.
Neutron stars are extremely dense and compact stars which are thought to be composed
primarily of neutrons. Their diameter is typically around 20 km with masses that ranges
from 1.18 to 1.93 times that of the sun, which the majority of around 1.35 times that of
the sun. This gives them a mean density which is extremely high, about 1014 times that
of water. It is still not known what is in the center, where the pressure is the greatest, of
these kinds of stars. The intermediate layers are mostly neutrons, which is probably in
a super fluid state. The outermost 1 km remains solid despite the extreme temperatures,
which can reach up to 1 million Kelvin. This solid layer’s surface, in which the pressure is
at its lowest, consists of an extraordinarily dense form of iron. [30]
Another notable feature of neutron stars is their incredibly strong magnetic fields, ex-
ceeding 1012 gauss—vastly stronger than Earth’s magnetic field, which measures just 0.5
gauss. These intense fields cause the surface iron to polymerize into long chains of iron
atoms. The atoms are compressed and stretched along the magnetic field’s direction,
binding together end-to-end. Beneath the surface, however, the pressure becomes so
extreme that individual atoms can no longer exist. [30]
Supernova remnants are nebulas that are left behind after a supernova, which is a spec-
tacular explosion in which a star will ejects most of its mass in a violently expanding cloud
of debris. At the brightest phase of the explosion, will the expanding cloud radiate as
much energy in a single as the sun have done in the past 3 million years. These kinds of
explosion will occur around every 50 years within a large galaxy. [31]
Supernova remnants play a crucial role in shaping galaxies. They significantly heat in-
terstellar gas through the magnetic turbulence and violent shocks they generate. These
remnants are the primary source of most heavy elements, starting from oxygen. When a
massive star explodes while still embedded in its original molecular cloud, the resulting
expanding remnant can compress nearby interstellar gas, potentially triggering new star
formation. Supernova remnants also produce strong shock waves that generate filaments

this energy to regions thousands of light-years above the plane. [31]

XNAV position determination
As it has now been determined that the position will be estimated by the X-ray pulses
from pulsars, will it be important to know how the position will be calculated. The main
idea behind X-ray pulsar navigation is timing the arrival of pulses from multiple pulsars,

2.6.1
Reference frame
To accurately compute the arrival times of pulses, measurements must be done relative
to an inertial frame. The inertial frame represents the three-dimensional inertial position
coordinates, and also the coordinate time which is the fourth dimension. The data col-
lected while in the moving frames must be transformed into an inertial frame and then
subsequently transferred to where the pulse model is defined. There exist a couple of
different reference frames, one of these is the Solar System Barycenter frame. The SSB
frame is a suitable coordinate system for a spacecraft navigation, since it is a common
inertial reference system, which have been chosen for many pulsar observations, and
would simplify some of the general relativistic equations that are used for high accuracy
time transfer. [8]
The SSB is generally referred to as the International Celestial Reference Frame (ICRF),
where its axes are aligned with the equator and equinox of epoch J2000. [8]
ICRF originate as the position of the center of mass for the whole solar system, which is
called the barycenter. The sun and Jupiter are the ones which contributes the most to this
location, where all other subsequent planets, moons, and asteroids contributing with only
a secondary effect. Since the sun have a large mass, comparable to the other bodies, is
the barycenter position very near the surface of the sun. The sun’s center rotates around
the barycenter position with a period that is equal to the ≈12 yr orbit period of Jupiter. [8]
For operations and measurements of pulsar timing that are done within the solar system,
must the Earth’s motion on the detectors be removed to produce accurate models. There
exist two different time scales which does support the solar system timing, both have
their origin in the SSB. The two time scales are either the Temps Coordonnée Barycen-
trique (TCB, Barycentric Coordinate Time) or the Temps Dunamique Barycentrique (TDB,
Barycentric Dynamical Time). Most of the current pulsar observations are computed with
the TDB timescale. [8]

2.6.3
Spacecraft positioning
The absolute position for a spacecraft is its three-dimensional location which is specified
in an inertial frame. By determining the absolute position of a spacecraft will allow the
vehicle to immediately recognize the relationship to other nearby objects. This will allow
the vehicle to safely control itself around potential obstacles, and also achieve the most
important task which is to assist the spacecraft in pursuing the intended mission position.

for the spacecraft. [8]

pulses be identified by the way a set of pulses coordinate with respect to the orientation
of a set of pulsars. [8]

2.8.1
Scintilator
In the following section, scintillators composed of gas and inorganic will be introduced.
Scintillators are materials that will emit light when it absorbs ionizing radiation such as X-
rays, and charged particles. This light can then be detected and measured, which makes
scintillators a critical component in a radiation detection system. They work by an ionizing
radiation will interact with the scintillator material and excites the atoms or molecules that
are within the material. These excited states are unstable and will therefore quickly return
to their lower-energy states, and hence release the energy in the form of photons (light).
2.8.1.1
Gas scintillator
There are two types of X-ray gaseous detectors, this being the Gas Proportional Scin-
tillation Counters (GPSC), and the gas proportional ionization avalanche counter. The
X-ray absorption and primary electron drift process is basically the same for both types of
gaseous detector. The incident X-rays will interact primarily in a region where the electric
field is weak, also called the drift region, and the resulting primary electron cloud will drift
into a region with a stronger electric field. [38]
In proportional ionization counters, each primary electron triggers an electron avalanche
in the strong electric field near the anode, generating a substantial number of secondary
electrons during the amplification stage. In GPSC, however, the strong electric field is
configured such that primary electrons excite, rather than ionize, the gas atoms. This
excitation results in a light pulse during the de-excitation of the gas atoms, a phenomenon
known as secondary scintillation. During the amplification stage, this process produces
many photons per primary electron, enabling signal amplification without the accumulation
of space charge, as occurs in proportional counters. [38]

Inorganic scintillators
The scintillation mechanism in inorganic materials depend on the energy states deter-
mined by the crystal lattice of the material. As shown in Figure 2.12, electrons are only
available in discrete bands of energy in materials which are classified as insulators or
semiconductors. The lower energy level, known as the valence band, contains electrons
that are essentially bound to lattice sites, while the conduction band consists of electrons
with enough energy to move freely throughout the crystal. Between these bands lies the
forbidden band, a range of energies where electrons cannot exist in a pure crystal. When
energy is absorbed, an electron can be excited from its normal position in the valence
band, crossing the energy gap into the conduction band, which leaves behind a hole in
the valence band. In a pure crystal, the process of an electron returning to the valence
band and emitting a photon is inefficient. Additionally, the typical energy gap widths result
in photons with energy too high to fall within the visible spectrum. [39] To increase the

impurities, known as activators, are often introduced into inorganic scintillators. These

allowing electrons to de-excite back to the valence band through intermediate steps. Be-

de-excitation sites are referred to as luminescence centers or recombination centers, and
their energy structure within the host crystal lattice determines the scintillator’s emission

Photodiode
In semiconductor detectors, photodiodes are one of the simplest form, and there are two
main types: PN and the variation PIN. There are two layers in the PN photodiode: a
positive P-layer that is filled with holes and a negative N-layer that is filled with electrons.
By diffusing holes from the P-layer to the N-layer, a depletion region is formed between the
two layers, resulting in no free carriers in the region. Photons absorbed in the depletion
region creates an electron-hole pair, where the electrons will drift towards the N-layer and
the holes towards the P-layer, due to the applied electric field. [43]

being an intrinsic I-layer placed between the P- and N-layer. As a result of the intrinsic
layer of the PIN photodiode being highly resistive, the electric field is increased, increasing
time resolution and depletion region size. [43]

Challenges at high count rate
Normally, SDDs are able to handle high input rates of photons, but when the rate exceeds

accounted for.

Figure 2.19: Electron drift distance and rise time for a SDD detector. It can be seen the

Ballistic deficit happens because when an incoming photon hits the detector, this creates
an electron cloud that spreads out and diffuses as it moves towards the anode. As the
photon hits further away from the anode will the electron have to travel further, which
increases the diffusion of the electron cloud. As the electron cloud gets closer to the
anode, it begins to induce an output signal, however it only outputs the full signal once
the whole electron cloud has been collected by the anode. This means that the rise time
of the signal is dependent on the distance from the anode, as seen in Figure 2.19. In
order to achieve a high count rate, a fast processing time is required, but a too short
processing time may result in the pulse not reaching full peak, resulting in a deficit in the
measurement. This is known as the ballistic deficit. [44]
The other issue that happens when the count rate is increased is pulse pile-up. This
happens when the photons arrive so close to each that the signal from the two pulses can
not be discriminated, and thus the two signals are counted as one. [44]

2.9
Clock
In navigation, time keeping plays a crucial role, as a nanosecond’s uncertainty corre-
sponds to roughly 1/3 meter uncertainty in distance. According to Eric Burt, an atomic
clock physicist for the mission at JPL, GPS clocks must be calibrated several times a day
to keep a high accuracy and currently NASA is developing the Deep Space Atomic Clock
(DSAC) which would only need to be calibrated once a week. [48]
Quartz crystals used for everyday appliances on Earth are by the space navigation stan-
dards not very stable, since after just an hour, the quartz crystal oscillator be off by a
nanosecond, and after a couple of weeks be off by a full millisecond (300 km).
Atomic clocks works by measuring the natural oscillation of atoms, since it acts like a
pendulum, just much more precise, due to the high frequency and stability of the atoms.
For example, the International System of Units (SI) defines a second as the time it takes
a caesium-133 atom to oscillate exactly 9,192,631,770 times. [49]
By blasting the atoms with microwaves, it can cause an electron to rise to a higher orbit.
The electron must receive the correct amount of energy to jump to a higher orbit, oth-
erwise called the resonant frequency of the atom. Every element has a unique energy
requirement for making an electron jump to a higher orbit, and this energy remains the
same no matter where it is measured. The most used elements for atomic clocks are
cesium and rubidium, however the DSAC uses mercury-Ion. In the atomic clock it will use
the frequency from a quartz oscillator to excite the atoms, and if the frequency is correct
many electrons will jump to a higher state, but if the frequency is different from the res-
onating frequency of the atom far fewer electrons will jump to a higher state. A correction
can then be made to the quartz oscillator to get it back to the correct frequency. [50]

3.7
Detectors
It has been decided to observe the pulsars in the X-ray band due to the large size the
antenna dishes would have to be for it to achieve a positional error of less than, 10 km
along with requiring additional data processing to account for the dispersion that the ISM
causes. As mentioned above, using X-ray optics requires a long focal length along with
limiting the FOV, thus to abide by the requirements set for the instrument, the detector
will have to work without optics to focus the X-ray towards a detector.
3.7.1
Microchannel plates
Microchannel plate (MCP) have the possibility of reaching a timing resolution of ns, how-
ever they have the disadvantage of having to be under low pressure or vacuum to operate
along with being complex to manufacture, since each MCP pore is typically between 5 µm
to 25 µm. In addition, they require a voltage of multiple kV to operate, which would require
a separate power supply.
3.7.2
Proportional chamber
Proportional chambers were used as some of the first detectors for X-ray detection, but
in recent time they have been phased out for semiconductor detectors. The reason for
this is due to the limited lifetime of the gas, due to diffusion and the possibility of a single
point of failure for the escape of the gas. The Proportional chamber also requires high
voltages to operate.
3.7.3
Charge-Coupled Devices (CCD)
For the CCD, the limiting factor is the speed of the read-out electronics, and the deep
depletion method to clear the pixel after it have been hit by a photon, which is often limited
to µs time resolution. As the CCD becomes larger, they will begin to lose more of the total
charge from a pixel, per transfer, typically 10−5 per transfer from pixel transfer.
CCDs are susceptible to radiation damage, which can lead to permanent degradation.
While annealing can be used to repair the damage, this process introduces additional
complexity to the system.
3.7.4
Calorimeter
Calorimeters are good at measuring single photons, however as they require being super-
cooled, the detector’s power usage is high and have a high mass. Along with this, the
detector’s size is limited to the capabilities of the cooling system, which often limits it size
to <1 cm. To accommodate the desire of requirement REQ-NAV-03 in Table 3.1 that says
the minimum active area has to be at least 100 cm2, which requires the use of optics to
reflect the photons to the detector.
3.7.5
Scintilator
Inorganic scintillators change the incoming X-rays into optical light, which could introduce
optics such as a camera lens to direct the light down to a photodiode. Hence, a large area
of detections could be achieved with a small photodiode.
Inorganic scintillators offer a high light yield, which will produce a substantial amount of
photons for each detected X-ray. However, scintillators are most efficient at higher ener-
gies, where they produce more light rays, while at lower energies, the reduced light output
can limit detection efficiency at low energies.
This characteristic allows for the effective use of photodetectors to convert the optical
signals into electrical pulses with a high sensitivity.

3.7.6
Photodiode
Photodiodes have the advantage of having one of the highest time resolutions due to
the short distance the electrons have to drift towards the cathode. However, they have
the disadvantage of the capacitances is proportional to the detector area as stated by
eq. (2.48). Thus, a large area photodiode will have a high capacitance that leads to a
slower response time and an increase in the electronic noise.
When paired with scintillators, they efficiently detect the optical photons generated by X-
rays emitted from pulsars. This is because the optical light output from the scintillator
can be focused into a small area, allowing the photodiodes to exploit their superior time
resolution compared to other detectors. Their fast response times are great for capturing
the rapid pulsations characteristic of pulsars
They are also robust and consume a minimal amount of power, when a reverse bias
is applied, which is suitable for space application where reliability, efficiency and power
consumptions are critical.
3.7.7
Silicon Drift Detector (SDD)
For the SDD, the two main parameters affecting the time resolution are the size of the
detector and the applied voltage to create the applied electric field, as stated by Equa-
tion 2.42 and Equation 2.44. Which states that if the area is increased, the applied electric
field would also be needed to increase to keep the same drift time. Because the anode col-
lects charges in a smaller area than the other semiconductor detectors, the capacitance
of the SDD remains the same even as the area of the detector increases.
Figure 3.9 illustrates this as well, where the drift time increases as the radius increases.
Based on Requirement REQ-NAV-02 in Table 3.1 regarding time resolution, it is necessary
to ensure that the resolution remains below 1 µs. As shown in Figure 3.9, to meet this
criterion, the maximum radius is 3 mm for 12 V and 40 mm for 2000 V at a temperature of
150 K.

3.7.7.2
Teardrop formed SDD
The Teardrop formed SDD is mainly used when the SDD has an integrated FET, to protect
the FET from irradiation of the incoming X-ray photons is done by using a collimator.
However, by forming it as a teardrop, it means that the maximum distance the electrons
must travel is nearly tripled compared to circular or rectangular SDD. This also typically
limits the maximum size of the detector while still having a high time resolution to about
10 mm2. [44]
3.7.7.3
Mini-SDD
Rather than having one large detector with a single anode, a new type of SDD splits up into
multiple smaller cells (mini-SDD), each with an anode, giving multiple pixels in addition
to high time resolution without the need for strong electric fields. With the SDD divided
into multiple pixels, the detector can have a much bigger area while still maintaining a
high time resolution, since electrons need to drift for a shorter distance (µm compared to
mm/cm). An example of this technology is the Mini-SDD [68] that uses hexagonal pixels
of 204 µm×236 µm with a side length of 136 µm to make a detector ladder of two monolithic
sensors of 3 cm×6.2 cm cm each that contains 128×256 pixels. They managed to acquire
a Quantum Efficiency (QE) of > 52%, > 88%, > 97% for E = 0.25 keV, E = 0.5 keV,E =
1 keV, respectively. The hexagon shape of the pixel is chosen due to on average there
is a shorter distance to the center anode than for a square pixel of similar area, resulting
in higher time resolution. In addition, the hexagon shape of the pixel also helps with
minimizing the fraction of split events that happens when photons are absorbed along the
border of the pixels. However, the biggest disadvantage this model of the mini-SDD have
the high power usage of ≈263 W at the current state. The mini-SDD consist of 1024×1024
pixels which results in a power usage per pixel of 250 µW. [68]

It is desired that the detectors does not contain a gas chamber, as this would lead to a
gas leakage over time and adds a single point of failure. Therefore, Proportional cham-
bers do not provide an optimal solution for pulsar navigation, although they have a good
efficiency, since the system would be required to be resilient for the many years that are
necessary to complete the mission.
It is also advantageous to exclude any solution that has a low QE (below and around
20 %), this will therefore exclude Microchannel plates and CCD semiconductors. This
is advantageous since a low QE would require a larger detector size to achieve the same
signal levels as other designs with a high QE.
Since SDD time resolution is proportional to the drift distance and voltage applied, which
results in high voltages (+1000 V) to maintain a time resolution of 1 µs while increasing
the area to the required size for this project. This can be seen in the large area SDD
that requires 2.4 kV to have a drift time of 4.3 µs while still having an area of 53 cm2 which
is below the minimum requirement for area as stated in Table 3.1. Hence, the SDD and
large area SDD can be excluded as a solution for an instrument for pulsar navigation. The
Mini-SDD, do work around the issue that the large area SDD has, by breaking the large
area up into small SDD points, which would take care of the time resolution issue and
lower the required voltage to operate down to 28 V and still maintain ns time resolution.
Even though the scintillator have a low to medium QE, they could still be advantageous
to work with. This comes down to the fundamental idea behind a scintillator, which is

3.8.1.2
Detector
The photodetector is an important part of this design, hence it needs to be able to achieve
the needs with as minimal instrument noise as possible. Therefore, it is important to select
the correct mode with which the photodiode is running. For a photodiode, there exist two
different modes in which it could run, this being either photovoltaic or photoconductive.
The main difference between the two modes is their biasing, where the photovoltaic have a
zero-bias, meaning the anode and cathode have the same potential. The photoconductive
have a reverse bias, which means that the cathode have a higher potential compared to
the anode. [74]
The reverse bias will cause the depletion region to become wider, which have two advan-
tageous. This being a more sensitive photodiode, meaning it will produce more output
signal relative to the illuminance, and reduce the junction capacitance. However, the
higher the reverse bias becomes, the higher the dark current will also become. Dark cur-
rents are the current that flows through a photodiode even when no illumination is present.
Hence, the total current is a combination of the current generated by the illumination and
the dark current. The dark current can be mitigated to some extent by subtracting the
expected dark current from the total current, however the dark current also comes with
some noise (dark noise), which is observed as a random variation in the magnitude of the
dark current. The photocurrent can therefore only measure light which generate a higher
current than the dark noise. [74]

3.9.2
Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC)
The ADC job is to convert the analog signal to a digital signal that a microcontroller can
read. For choosing a ADC there are 2 main considerations that need to considered i.e.
the sampling rate of the system and the resolution of the ADC. The ADCs sampling rate
determines how often it turns the analog signal into a digital signal. When the sampling
rate is much lower than the input frequency of the analog signal, aliasing starts to occur,
which means the input signal is not recognizable in the converted digital signal. A good
rule of thumb is to use the Nyquist frequency, which is that the ADCs sampling rate should
be at least twice as high as the highest frequency of the input analog signal. [77] Filters
are often placed before the ADC and used to remove the aliasing by setting a cut-off
frequency, that removes undersampled higher frequencies in the ADC. [78]
The next important design choice is the resolution of the ADC and is described in bits. The
bits describe how many levels the output signal have, and the higher the bit, the more it
resembles the input analog signal. For example, a 1-bit ADC only have 2 levels and can
be used for on-off or Boolean needs. As for the ADC for this mission, the accuracy of

Another method that could be used is to use the stable nature of the pulsars period to

can still search for another pulsar or the same. This means that the maximum observation

to recalibrate the atomic clock at a maximum rate of once a day.

Shielding
In the design of X-ray detectors, shielding plays a critical role in ensuring the longevity
and functionality of the system. There are two key shielding components: the protective
window in front of the detector and the radiation protection layer behind it.
4.2.1
Protection window
The protective window acts as a barrier between the detector and the external environ-
ment, protecting it from physical damage, contamination, and environmental fluctuations.
Even though it must protect the detector from the environment, it must be transparent to
X-ray in the 1 keV to 10 keV range to allow the photons to reach the detector without at-
tenuation. The most used material for it is Beryllium, due to its low atomic number (Z=4),
making it transparent to X-ray along with it being a strong and durable material. Another
option is Boron Carbide (B4C) windows that is often chosen when there is a concern
of health hazards, due to Beryllium being toxic in powder form, however it needs to be
thicker to have the same performance of Be windows. [87] From eq. (2.31) the attenua-
tion of photons through a material can be calculated. Graphene windows shows promising
results as they can be made ultra-thin along with being close to 100 % transparent to X-
rays. However, as it is a quite new technique there are still some problems regarding its
fabrication and thus not suitable at the current moment. [88]

4.3
Pulse time transfer and comparison process
Before the data processing can be done, it is important to understand the steps required
to be done for the incoming photons at the spacecraft detector. The first step at the
spacecraft detector is to collect and time pulsars X-ray photons at the spacecraft’s detector
using the onboard clock. Then correct the spacecraft clock to the proper time at the
SSB coordinate system TDB time using the standard corrections and spacecraft velocity
effects. The second step is the time transferring to the SSB, here it is going to use the
spacecraft position, velocity and the gravitational potential of the solar system. Then
correct the measured photon TDB arrival time for offset of vehicle from the SSB. The last
step is to do the TOA measurement and offset, which is going to be coherently folding
the photons into an observed pulse profile (Epoch folding). Then compare the observed
pulse profile to the standard template profile of pulsar to determine the measured TOA
of detected pulse, as it would arrive at the SSB origin. Then using the pulsar timing
mode, compare the predicted TOA of pulse closest to the measured TOA to determine
the difference in pulse arrival time. The last step is also the point where the epoch folding
and cross-correlation is done to determine the σTOA.

5.3
Position calculations
The position is calculated by measuring the arrival time of pulses from at least three dif-
ferent pulsars and then comparing this with their predicted arrival time, which makes it
possible to work out a position in three-dimensional space. The predicted TOA at SSB
is calculated with eq. (2.24). In the formula, there are two constants, which is the speed
of light in vacuum, c, and the gravitational constant times the mass of the source, µs.
Therefore for the time transfer formula, the unit vector towards the pulsars, ˆn, the base-
line distance, D0, baseline vector, b, and also a position vector of the spacecraft, r. When
calculating the position, the position vector of the spacecraft will be continually updated
during the calculations, to obtain the correct position. [60]
The TOA which are derived from the different pulsars, is the TOA offset between the TOA
at the spacecraft (which is going to be time transferred over to SSB) and the predicted
TOA at SSB. Hence when doing the calculations for the position at this step, it is possible
to give an estimate on the position by estimating a time transfer function, which will be
the distance away from SSB for the different pulsars used. Afterward, it is possible to
determine the position error for the different pulsars and the overall position error.

Conclusion
The research, analysis and simulations conducted in this thesis has explored the potential
of X-ray pulsar navigation (XNAV) as a viable alternative for autonomous spacecraft navi-
gation in deep space. By using the periodic signal of the pulsars, XNAV provides a method
of position determination, independent of Earth-based infrastructures. In this study, the
findings demonstrate the feasibility of XNAV, demonstrating its ability to complement or
even replace existing deep-space navigation systems such as the DSN.
The thesis covers the fundamental principles of pulsar navigation, methods for detect-
ing and processing pulsar signals, and the performance of XNAV based on simulations.
The results of this thesis indicate that time resolutions can reach hundreds of nanosec-
onds even at large areas with the current advancements in pixelated Silicon Drift Detector
(SDD). This rendered it highly suitable for XNAV, coupled with a Quantum Efficiency (QE)
of close to 95 %.
A background noise in the range of 1 keV to 15 keV was found to be 0.17 photon
cm2 s however,
with a FOV of 130◦will the pulsars within FOV provide a mean background noise from
other X-ray sources of 185 photon
cm2 s . To filter and separate the incoming signal with the high
background noise within FOV, a coded-aperture mask was deemed necessary with an
angular resolution of 2◦, to increase the SNR.
Another important part of XNAV is timekeeping, where two atomic clocks fitted the cri-
teria for a low- and high-mass mission. For the low-mass mission, a Rubidium Atomic
Frequency Standard (RAFS) from SAFRAN took 1.5 days to reach a time error corre-
sponding to 100 m. For a high-mass mission the Deep Space Atomic Clock (DSAC) from
NASA took 125 days to reach a time error corresponding to 100 m. It was concluded that
for using pulsars as a clock, it requires the maximum time error from the clock to not
exceed the time it takes to lock onto the pulsar signal.
A preliminary design of the detector have been made based on the WFM onboard the
eXTP satellite[91] to give an estimate on the size of 70 cm×70 cm×33 cm and the weight
of the instrument at 16 kg.
In the test and simulation chapter it was concluded that the position can be calculated
for the proposed solution. From the simulations an accuracy of 466.2 km was achieved
with the largest error in the y-direction, however some errors have been identified and
discussed in the data generation.
However, under the assumption that the σTOA for
B1937+21 can be estimated to the same degree as B1821-24, it would lead to an ac-
curacy of 12.5 km. For the theoretical calculation, an accuracy of 6.24 km were obtained
after 115 days with the same parameters as the simulation. The paper concludes that the
geometrical location of the pulsars contributes to the positional accuracy of the naviga-
tion system. Theoretical calculations, using the same parameters, yielded an accuracy
of 5.78 km for geometrically spaced pulsars with an observation time of 11 days. This
highlights the necessity for further investigations to determine the parameters and pro-
files of pulsars beyond the galactic plane. By using a coded mask, it was concluded that
the required time to achieve a positional accuracy of less that 10 km would be less than
a single day, with an accuracy of approximately 4 km. Including historical data, the final
range error was calculated to be 2.9 km. It is stated that the proposed solution will have
better accuracy compared to DSN at Saturn from a single observation period.

7.5
Kalman filtering
In regard to some future steps required for a navigation system, is the prediction of the
next position based upon the data received for the set position. This would mainly attract
two different Kalman filters, one that is running while the detector is observing and gath-
ering data to improve the σTOA and another that is running based upon the historical data
gathered after a set amount of observation time to improve the accuracy of the navigation
system. These Kalman filter are not going to be the same, the one running while it is
observing the pulsars would be an extended Kalman filter, while for the historical data it
would be an unscented Kalman filter. The reason behind not using the same Kalman filter
is based upon the large time difference between each point in the historical data, where
the unscented Kalman filter will generally perform better with less frequent data.
