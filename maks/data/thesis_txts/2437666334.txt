Biomechanical Modeling of the Left Ventricle in a Cardiac Phantom
by Marion LOPEZ
s162366
keywords: cardiac phantom, left ventricle, structural analysis, computational ﬂuid
dynamics (CFD), system coupling, ANSYS Mechanical, ANSYS FLUENT
Several ﬂuid-structure interaction (FSI) models of simpliﬁed or realistic human
left ventricle have been developed throughout the years. In this study, a biomechan-
ical model of a cardiac phantom left ventricle is developed. The cardiac phantom
at stake in this project is from Shelley Medical Imaging Technologies. A geometric rep-
resentation of this phantom including both the structural wall and the ﬂuid cavity
is generated. A translation motion is prescribed to the wall, for the duration of a
cardiac cycle, while the circulation of the ﬂuid in the ventricle is maintained. The
displacement response of the inner part of the structural domain is transferred to
the ﬂuid domain boundary at each time step. The maximum magnitude of the struc-
tural mesh displacement is 10.6 mm and it is observed around the apex. The results
are discussed in terms of ﬂuid ﬂow patterns. They show that, for a ﬁlling velocity
of 0.9 m s−1, the outﬂow velocity reaches 4.22 m s−1. The ﬂuid is going out of the left
ventricle at the same time as it is getting in. Some vortexes are formed during the
ﬁlling and they are located in zones where the pressure is lower.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Motivation
With the rise in novel three-dimensional (3D) ultrasound (US) techniques, there
is a need to develop veriﬁcation tools. Numerical models are developed to validate
these new measurement techniques.
Imaging phantoms are objects that are scanned and used to assess the perfor-
mance of an imaging device. Phantoms are designed to respond to the different
imaging modalities, like the tissue or organ they are representing will do. A car-
diac phantom is a replicate of a human heart, usually made of a hydrogel material,
whose purpose is to mimic the biomechanical behavior of the heart. Using these
imaging phantoms, instead of living subjects or cadavers, presents the advantage
of being always ready to be used and provides more consistent results. Moreover,
the use of a phantom instead of a living subject avoids putting the subject at risk [11].
Objective
In this master thesis, Shelley Medical Imaging Technologies dynamic heart phantom
DHP-01 (a two-ventricle dynamic heart phantom) is the object to be modeled. The
scope of this thesis is limited to the model of the left ventricle.
The objective is to develop a numerical simulation model of the biomechanics
of the cardiac phantom left ventricle and to a larger extent (outside the scope of
this thesis) to develop a numerical simulation model of the entire cardiac phantom
biomechanics. This project is the ﬁrst step towards the creation of a complete FSI
model of the cardiac phantom.
A translation motion is applied to the wall of the left ventricle phantom to rep-
resent the stretching and compression of the phantom. The motion from the wall is
transferred to the ﬂuid. The simulation purpose is to observe the velocity ﬁeld and
the pressure ﬁeld of the ﬂuid present in the left ventricle during a cardiac cycle.
The simulations are performed under ANSYS 18.2 Workbench. All structural
simulations are performed using ANSYS Mechanical and the ﬂuid ones using AN-
SYS FLUENT. The displacement data transfer is made possible by the use of the
System Coupling feature from ANSYS 18.2 Workbench. Moreover, SolidWorks is
also used for the creation of the geometric model.

Chapter 2
Background
2.1
Physiological background
2.1.1
Heart anatomy
The heart is a four-chamber organ. It can be divided into two hemispheres, left
and right. Each hemisphere is composed of an atrium that sits on top of a ventricle,
each chamber being linked to the other by valves (see Figure 2.1). The wall of the
heart is made of three layers: the endocardium, which is in direct contact with the
blood, the myocardium, which is the muscular layer enabling the pumping func-
tion of the heart, and the outer layer, the pericardium which is the protective sac
surrounding the heart [18].
FIGURE 2.1: Representation of the heart anatomy and blood path in
the heart. Adapted from [18].
2.1.2
Heart physiology
The heart works like two pumps placed in series where each hemisphere corre-
sponds to a pump. The right part receives the de-oxygenated blood from the vena
cava and sends it to the pulmonary system through the pulmonary arteries to expel
the carbon dioxide and to recharge the blood with oxygen. The right part of the
heart is represented in blue in Figure 2.1. Once the blood has been re-oxygenated, it
enters the left part of the heart, which is represented in red in Figure 2.1 through the

pulmonary veins and it gets propelled to the different organs of the body through
the aorta [18].
The pumping action of the heart follows a certain rhythm described by the so-
called cardiac cycle. This cycle is divided into two periods, the systole that corre-
sponds to ventricular contraction (blood expelled) and the diastole corresponding
to the relaxation of the ventricle and the ﬁlling of the chamber (see Figure 2.2). The
systole lasts one third of the cycle whereas the diastole represents two thirds of the
cycle. One cycle length corresponds to one heart beat that is about 0.8 s [18].
FIGURE 2.2: Representation of the cardiac cycle in terms of pressure
and volume evolution (top) and blood circulation (bottom) [18]. The
maximal value of pressure reached is 120 mmHg which is equivalent
to 16000 Pa.
The systole is additionally subdivided into two periods: the iso-volumetric con-
traction and the ejection. The iso-volumetric contraction period occurs right after
the closure of the atrio-ventricular (mitral for the left side and tricuspid for the right
side) valve, the outlet valve remains closed for about 0.02 s while the contraction is
starting. During this period both valves are closed, so the volume of the chamber is
not changing. At the same time, the contraction is happening which causes a sudden
increase in pressure in the ventricle. The pressure in the chamber keeps increasing,
and when it becomes higher than the pressure in the outlet artery (pulmonary or
aorta depending on the side), the valve opens and it is the onset of the ejection pe-
riod. At the end of the ejection period, the ventricle relaxes which generates a drop
in the intra-ventricular pressure and the closure of the outlet valve to avoid back-

2.2
State of the art
Studying the blood ﬂow in the left ventricle is of great importance to detect pos-
sible cardiac dysfunction. Numerical method, named computational ﬂuid dynamics
(CFD), is used to solve the ﬂow equations. The inﬂuence of boundary motion on
ﬂow patterns in the left ventricle during diastole has been studied by S.S Khalafvand
et al. [13]. In their article, they have obtained the ﬂuid boundary motion by mag-
netic resonance imaging (MRI) of the heart in motion. This motion has then been
prescribed to the model boundary. It is a geometry-prescribed CFD method. They
have shown the formation of core vortexes which is also demonstrated by Q.Long et
al.[15].
Many of the articles are only focusing on the heamodynamics and do not take
into considerations the structural inﬂuence on the ﬂow. The FSI method is more and
more used to also take into consideration the forces and deformations undergone by
the cardiac wall. The FSI method models the structure by Finite Element Method
(FEM), the ﬂuid using CDF and a coupling algorithm. B. Chahboune et al.[6] devel-
oped a FSI model for a two-dimensional (2D) simpliﬁed ventricle. Y. Cheng et al.
[7] have carried out FSI analysis on a simpliﬁed three dimensional model of the left
ventricle with a very thin wall. Krittian et al. [14] have developed a coupling concept
that enables an iterative data exchange between the structural analysis and the ﬂuid
one at each time step. Hiroshi Watanabe et al.[22] even went further by including the
cellular mechanism in the FSI simulation.
FSI simulation can also be performed on a model of a cardiac phantom. So far, it
appears that only a structural study has been performed by Y.Zhu et al.[24] on Shelley
Medical Imaging Technologies cardiac phantom model. Their study objective was to
compare the computed strain and MRI-based results. Complete FSI simulation of a
cardiac phantom still need to be performed. The objective of this thesis is to start
this process.

2.5.2
Flow pressure-based solver
This section is based on ANSYS FLUENT theory guide [2].
ANSYS FLUENT solver used in this thesis is the pressure-based solver. The ve-

extracted by solving a pressure correction equation obtained by manipulating both
the continuity and the momentum conservation equations as it is described in sub-
section 2.5.3 .
The algorithm behind this solver follow these three steps:
1. the ﬂuid domain is divided into discrete control volumes.
2. the governing equations (2.3) and (2.5) are integrated on each control volume
to construct algebraic equations for an unknown ﬁeld variable such as the ve-
locity or the pressure.
3. the discretized algebraic equations are linearized.

3.3
Material properties
3.3.1
Blood
Blood is a ﬂuid that consists of erythrocytes (red blood cells), leukocytes (white
blood cells) and thrombocytes (platelets) and a liquid called plasma. Water repre-
sents 90 to 93% of plasma. The rest of the plasma is mostly proteins. Erythrocytes
are between 500 to 1000 times more numerous than the other types of blood cells
and they represent between 40% to 50% of blood volume (hematocrit) [18].
When considering only the plasma, blood can be seen as a Newtonian ﬂuid be-
cause it is mainly made of water and the coefﬁcient of viscosity remains indepen-
dent of the shear rate. Since blood is a liquid containing deformable particles in
suspension, it is actually a non-Newtonian ﬂuid. This mechanical behavior is really
important if the blood is ﬂowing through small channels, such as capillary vessels,
whose dimensions are close to the dimension of blood cells (erythrocytes long axis
dimension is around 7 µm). But in larger channels, the diameter (bigger than 1 mm)
is large compared to the blood cells dimension, then it can be assumed that the blood
has a constant viscosity given that the shear rates are high enough so that the viscos-
ity is independent of them [16]. Therefore, the blood is assumed to be a Newtonian
ﬂuid for the simulation of ventricular cardiac circulation.
Regarding the compressibility of blood, it can be shown that, in medium to large
arteries, blood can be considered as an incompressible ﬂuid. Indeed the Mach’s
number (Ma), which is deﬁned as the ratio between the ﬂuid ﬂow velocity and the
speed of sound in that ﬂuid (see (3.1)), determines the compressibility effects since
the ﬂuctuation of density can be scaled with the Mach’s number squared (see (3.2)).
Ma = |⃗V|
c
(3.1)
where |⃗V| is the ﬂuid ﬂow velocity and c the speed of sound in the ﬂuid.
Ma2 ∼∆ρ

(3.2)
where ∆ρ

Given that the speed of sound in blood is around 1540 m s−1 [12] and the blood
ﬂow velocity varies between 0.01 to 1 m s−1 usually in large arteries, the Mach num-
ber is very small (about 0.0006 if the velocity is 1 m s−1) which will give density
ﬂuctuation down less to 1% [17]. A ﬂuid is considered to be incompressible if its
Mach’s number is less than 0.3. Thus blood ﬂow can be modeled as an incompress-
ible ﬂow in large arteries. This is extended to the left ventricle.

Dynamic mesh
The wall of the ﬂuid domain will move based on the motion of the structural

the boundary of the ﬂuid domain to allow mesh deformation. The option System
Coupling needs to be speciﬁed in order to be able to use ANSYS System Coupling
feature. Moreover, the interior of the ﬂuid domain is also assigned to a dynamic
mesh by specifying that the interior will get deformed.
4.3.6
Solver settings
A transient analysis is performed in ANSYS FLUENT. A pressure-based solver
is used to perform the simulation. The pressure-velocity coupling algorithm used
is the SIMPLE scheme. The spatial discretization is performed using the second or-
der upwind scheme. The gradients are evaluated using the Green-Gauss Cell-Based
method. All these methods have been deﬁned previously in section 2.5.
The time step is set to ∆t = 0.01 s and the number of steps is set to 240 so that
three cardiac cycles can be visualized. The maximum number of iterations for the
iterative-time advancement procedure described in Figure 2.7, is set to 200. The
convergence is considered to be reached, for a given time step, when the residuals
for both the mass and the momentum equation decrease by less than 10−3.

Chapter 6
Discussion and conclusion
6.1
Discussion
The model developed in this thesis is effective. Indeed the displacement data
transfer between the structural wall and the ﬂuid domain has been performed suc-
cessfully and the CDF results are consistent with the conservation of mass and mo-
mentum.
The results clearly show that, for this model, the ejection is occurring at the same
time as the ﬁlling of the heart. Indeed, during the ﬁlling phase, that is the ﬁrst 0.4 s
of the cardiac cycle, the velocity vectors at the outlet are oriented outward and can
reach magnitude of about 4 m s−1. This gives an outgoing mass ﬂux a bit higher than
8 L min−1. This ejection ﬂux is still realistic because a normal human cardiac output
is varying between 4 and 8 Lmin−1. However, in a real heart, the ejection should
occur during the systole, that is during the last 0.4 s of the cardiac cycle. It is not
possible to observe this phenomenon in this model because no valve system is im-
plemented to prevent the ﬂow from going through the outlet and the volume change
is not big enough (0.5%) to prevent the ﬂow from going out. Even though a pressure
proﬁle is applied at the outlet, it does not play the same role as the valve system. As
it has been shown, this outﬂow is the translation of the mass conservation.
A small volume change is observed and it can be explained by the fact that a
longitudinal displacement of 10 mm is applied at the apex and no radial dilation is
prescribed. It is consistent with what has been found by Y. Zhu et al., as they have
obtained a volume change of 1% for an apex longitudinal displacement of 10 mm
for the same cardiac phantom (and a radial displacement was applied) [24].
6.2
Future work
Due to time limitations, only an in-vitro recording of the phantom motion has
been performed. Flow velocity measurements need to be carried out. Hence this
model, even though it has been veriﬁed (mass conservation principle respected, con-
sistent velocity ﬁeld and pressure ﬁeld), has not been validated yet. The next step
with this model will then be to compare it, especially the velocity pattern obtained,
to the results of an in-vitro measurement on the cardiac phantom undergoing stretch-
ing and compression cycle.
Moreover, the scope of this thesis was limited to transferring the displacement
data at the ﬂuid-structure interface from the structural domain to the ﬂuid domain.
A further step of this project would be to add the transfer of force from the ﬂuid
==PAGE:72==
54
Chapter 6. Discussion and conclusion
domain to the structural domain to perform a complete 2-way FSI and thus, obtain
a closer model of what is happening in a human heart.
Finally, to generate a model as complete as possible, the twisting motion that is
possible to apply to the cardiac phantom should be added as a prescribed displace-
ment to the model.
6.3
Conclusion
This work mainly focuses on the creation of a biomechanical model of the cardiac
phantom left ventricle. It is a ﬁrst step towards the realization of a complete FSI
model. Using the System Coupling feature from ANSYS 18.2 Workbench, it has
been possible to perform a one-way displacement data transfer from the structural
wall to the ﬂuid boundary to numerically simulate the stretching and compression
of the cardiac phantom. The results obtained are consistent with the ﬂow governing
equations. These results are not what is expected on a physiological point of view
since ﬂuid is entering and leaving the ventricle at the same time, but this can be
explained by the absence of the valve system in the model.
