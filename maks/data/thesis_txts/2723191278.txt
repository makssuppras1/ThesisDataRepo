Abstract
Surfing has become a prominent use of coastal areas and has experienced widespread
growth globally, including in Denmark, but as interest grows, the surfing resources can
become limited and surf spots can become overcrowded. Establishing Artificial Surfing
Reefs (ASRs) is an increasing priority in many countries, and successful implementations
have been conducted in Europe and Australia, among other places. In recent years the
interest in multi-purpose reefs, which can provide advantages to both shoreline manage-
ment, biodiversity, and surfing has increased on the northern coast of Zealand, Denmark.
As such, it is critical with thorough evaluations of wave conditions in Southeast Kattegat
to determine if multi-purpose reefs realistically can include wave breaking which supports
improved surfing amenity. Despite the close proximity between the Swedish and Danish
coasts in the South Kattegat Sea, it is known that surfing amenity is of considerably
higher quality in Sweden. This study focused on evaluating similarities and differences
in surfing amenity between the two locations by utilizing state-of-the-art numerical wave
models. Additionally, limitations of the numerical models to resolve very short period
waves were also investigated due to the nature of rough sea-states related to surfing in
Kattegat. The study found that the surf spots in Sweden generally offered better surfing
amenity, with lower directional spreading, higher frequency of surfable waves in offshore
wind conditions, and greater wave steepness attributable to steeper bed slopes compared
to those found in northern Zealand. Conflicting conclusions were found in the analysis
of the peakedness of the frequency spectrum, indicating less clean conditions in Sweden,
which highlights the need for further investigations. The most critical difference between
the two coastlines was found to be the bathymetry. High-quality bathymetry surveys
revealed that the northern coast of Zealand lacks the complex bathymetry formations
that can focus wave energy and increase wave heights at surf spots. Contrarily, it was
found that one surf spot on the Swedish coast had very impressive bathymetry with high
levels of wave focusing. A 3D wave model simulated wave breaking at this spot indicat-
ing extremely clean waves and surfable wave peel angles, but simultaneously the analysis
showed significant numerical dissipation due to resolution limitations. Overall, surfing
frequency of surfable waves were similar on the Danish and Swedish coast, which suggest
that constructing an ASR in northern Zealand that imitates bathymetry features found
in Sweden could enhance surfing amenity in the area.

Introduction
The water sport surfing has become a prominent use of coastal areas, and has experi-
enced widespread growth globally, including in Denmark. As participation levels in the
sport increases, the surfing resources become limited and suitable surf spots can become
crowded. Establishing and protecting surfing spots in populated coastal areas is a growing
concern for several councils and local communities since these surf breaks are a significant
tourist attraction. To improve surfing amenities, several countries have adopted the use
of artificial surfing reefs (ASRs), with the worlds first ASR build in Perth Australia in
1999 (Rendle and Rodwell, 2013) and Europe’s first ASR in Boscombe UK in 2009 (Mead
et al., 2011).
Reefs can be multi-purpose in nature by providing advantages to both
shoreline management and surf amenities, and such reefs have in more recent year gained
more interest. Several examples of these exist with varying degree of success (Silva et al.,
2020, Tinoco et al., 2014). Danish Hydrological Institute (DHI) designed a multi-purpose
reef in Palm Beach Australia which was constructed in 2019 and has been a great success
(Mortensen et al., 2015).
Successful surf reefs in Danish waters are yet to be accomplished. In 2014, a sandy surf
reef design was developed for improvement of surfing amenity in Hvide Sande, Denmark.
However, the project was short lived because the reef was not of hard-bottom material, and
due to sediment transportation processes, continuous sand fillings for the spot was required
to maintain its quality. Recently, the project BARREEF by DTU Aqua has been exploring
multi-purpose reefs in Kattegat and the inner Danish waters. BARREEF initially included
stakeholders with surfing experience, but the projects final aim is to provide coastal
protection as well as increasing biodiversity by construction of submerged reefs. In 2022
the community driven association Foreningen Stenrev Nordkysten (Gribskov Stenrev)
was founded.
The association includes surf stakeholders and in cooperation with the
municipality it aims to work for the establishment/rehabilitation and later maintenance
of a stone reef along the coast in Gribskov Municipality on the northern coast of Zealand.
With increasing interest for multi-purpose reefs on the northern coast of Zealand it is crit-
ical with thorough evaluations of wave conditions in South Kattegat to determine if such
reefs realistically can include wave breaking which supports improved surfing amenity.
While a thriving surfing community is present on the northern coast of Zealand the surf-
ing amenity is much lower quality compared to Cold Hawaii in Jutland. Interestingly,
Höganäs Municipality in Sweden, located very close to the northern coast of Zealand
has quite good surfing amenity. This dissimilarity in surfing experience between two surf
locations in close proximity (i.e. same offshore wave conditions) raises the question of
differences in underlying nearshore conditions.
No scientific studies have been conducted on the wave conditions for surfing on these
coastlines in Southeast Kattegat. In the literature, surfing amenity has mostly been eval-
uated at coastlines which are susceptible to incoming long period ground swell (Espejo
et al., 2014). Consequently, ASRs have been modelled on clean long period wave spectra
using Boussinesq wave models or 3D wave models in software such as OpenFoam. How-
ever, wave conditions in Kattegat are primarily wind swells with short period and less
clean and less organized wave fields. This complicates numerical wave modeling because

it requires more computational resources to simulate these rough sea states with sufficient
accuracy. As an approximation, most wind swell in small water bodies such as Kattegat
will be resembling a JONSWAP spectrum, which includes a longer tail of very short pe-
riod waves typically referred to as "chop" in surfing - perhaps very similar in size to white
caps. The nature of spectral wave models allows for these high frequencies (i.e. low wave
periods) to be resolved, but such numerical models are phase-averaged and mostly limited
to non-linear wave theory. Consequently, non-linear 3D phase-resolving wave models are
a necessity to accurately model non-linear wave transformations in the intermediate water
zone and into the breaking zone. However, to model such wave transformations, includ-
ing wave chop, models require incredibly detailed spatial resolution. The state-of-the-art
MIKE 3 Wave model, developed by Danish Hydrological Institute (DHI), include detailed
wave breaking capabilities, but has not yet been utilized for wind swell applications, which
provides an interesting topic for research.
1.1
Purpose
The purpose if this study is to function as a preliminary study which evaluates surfing
amenity in northern Zealand (Denmark) and in northeastern Skäne in Höganas District
(Sweden). The hope is that these findings will guide stakeholder decisions and advance
efforts towards establishing a comprehensive multi-purpose artificial reef on the northern
coast of Zealand, whether or not surf capabilities are included. Furthermore, the study
can inform participating engineers about the strengths and limitations of numerical wave
models when utilized for simulating wind-forced rough sea states such as Kattegat Sea.
1.2
Scope
The scope of this project is limited to surfing amenity in South Kattegat Sea at the
coastline of northern Zealand and at the northeast coastline of Höganas District. The
aim of the study is to compare surfing amenity at these two coastlines. It encompasses
field work collection of high quality bathymetry data, utilization of spectral wave models
to compare surfing amenity including historical wave conditions, and finally utilization of
a state-of-the-art 3D phase-resolving wave model to simulate wave breaking in high detail
at a surf spot in Sweden.
1.3
Motivation
Motivation for picking this project is interest in wave dynamics and surfing, as well as
state-of-the-art numerical modelling software. Waves in spatially limited water bodies
such as Kattegat in Denmark are generally not considered to provide surfing conditions
of good quality, however a thriving surf community is present both in Eastern Denmark
and Western Sweden.
These coastlines provide surf of very different quality which is
hypothesised to be a result of differences in bathymetry. This thesis will hopefully give
insight into what constitutes good wave conditions in these waters, and how especially
bathymetry affects the waves, and potentially how artificially altering the bathymetry can
increase the surfing amenity in low quality areas.

Theory
This chapter introduces several factors related to the quality of surfing, including wave
characteristics such as height and period, wind conditions, bathymetry, and spectral
shape. The specific numerical modelling tools used are presented as well as their strengths
and limitations. First the MIKE 21 Spectral Waves model (DHI, 2017b) is discussed, and
then MIKE 3 Wave model which is a Non-Volume Over Flow (VOF) 3D Flow Model
(DHI, 2019b).
2.1
Quality of surf waves
2.1.1
Basic wave parameters
The quality of waves in surfing is greatly influenced by various basic wave parameters
such as significant wave height (Hs), peak period (Tp), wave direction and wind direction
( Butt and Russel, 2014).
Significant wave height
The significant wave height (Hs) is commonly used to describe wave height, and refers
to the average height of the highest one-third of the waves. Usually higher Hs lead to
larger and more powerful waves which are more ideal for surfing, since the number of
surfable waves increases, and due to each individual wave presenting greater possibilities
for advanced surf maneuvers. Smaller wave heights may result in less challenging waves.
Wave heights between 1 m to 20 m are generally considered surfable on shorter boards.
However, it does depend on wave shape and size of surfboard, as waves smaller than 1 m
are regularly surfed on longer boards with more volume, or by highly skilled and lighter
surfers (Scarfed et al., 2009).
Peak period
The peak period (Tp) refers to the period with highest wave energy. In linear wave theory
the dispersion relation relates wave length, L, and wave period, T:
ω2 = g · k · tanh kh

where h is water depth, k = 2π

In deep water the wave length depend only on the wave period, whereas in intermediate
and shallow water it depends on both period and water depth. Generally, shorter peak
period (i.e. shorter wave length) can result in unorganized and less suitable waves for
surfing, while longer peak periods may lead to smoother, more consistent waves. Peak
wave periods below 5 s are generally very poor wave quality and are often not considered
surfable due to the short wave length (Surfertoday, 2023).

Wave direction
The wave direction refers to the direction in which the waves are traveling, and can be
represented as an angle ranging from 0◦to 360◦. Some surf locations, such as California,
are exposed to swell waves from several direction, as a result of storms in both the North
Pacific and South Pacific, as well as wind-generated waves from nearby wind systems.
Wave direction can impact the quality of the waves for surfing, as waves traveling in a
certain direction may interact with underwater features such as sandbars or reefs to create
favorable conditions for surfing (Butt and Russel, 2014).
Wind direction
The wind direction can also have a significant impact on wave quality in surfing. Onshore
winds, which blow from the sea towards the land, can result in sea state, where smooth
long period swell suitable for surf, is mixed with short period swell producing less smooth
waves and hence less suitable waves. These onshore wind often produce what surfers refer
to as "chop". Off-shore winds, which blow from the land towards the sea, can create more
favorable conditions for surfing by smoothing out the waves and reducing the amount of
chop from short period waves (Butt and Russel, 2014).
2.1.2
Advanced wave spectrum analysis
The wave parameters presented in the section above are the most popular used in forecast-
ing of surf waves, and are useful to provide a basic understanding of the incoming waves
at a given time and place. However, these parameter are a simplification of the actual
spectrum of waves in the ocean. A Hs = 2 m, Tp = 12 s swell with light offshore winds can
be extrapolated from vastly different sea states, since these values are simply statistical
summaries calculated from the spectrum of the waves. Two spectra with identical peak
period can have different spectral bandwidth and directional spread, which lead to great
differences in wave quality for a surfer.
Frequency spectrum
The frequency spectrum represents the distribution of wave energy on the sea surface as
a function of frequency. In the context of surfing this is usually in the range 0.04 Hz to
1 Hz corresponding to 1 s to 25 s periods. Peak period (Tp) informs where in the frequency
spectrum the waves have most energy, but it does not tell how the frequencies are shaped
around the peak, or if there are secondary peaks with significant energy. In Figure 2.1 the
bandwidth also called peakedness of the spectrum is illustrated by two swells (Butt and
Russel, 2014). The broad curve is less peaked and periods are spread out over a wide range
of periods, which will make the sea messy due to differentiating wave lengths interfering
with each other, resulting in low quality surf. The narrow curve is more peaked with
a concentration of periods around the peak frequency, which will make the sea appear
organized and clean, resulting in high quality surf.

Directional spectrum
The directional spectrum represents the distribution of wave energy as a function of
direction in the range 0◦to 360◦increasing clockwise, with North as 0◦degrees and East
as 90◦. The spread of wave energy across directions is called the directional spread. When
directional spread is narrow, you will experience a clean swell with straight wave fronts,
as most of the waves originate from a similar direction. On the other hand, a broad
swell direction results in a chaotic, inconsistent swell, as the waves come from various
directions causing interference. Analogous to peak period in the frequency spectrum the
peak direction describes the incoming wave direction with most energy. This statistical
measure can provide basic information as to where the most energetic wave components
are coming from, but does not show the directional spread.

Refraction
When waves travel over water of different depths at different speeds, they bend, which is
known as refraction. This bending occurs because the part of the wave that travels over
shallow water moves more slowly than the part that travels over deeper water, causing
the wave to bend towards the shallower water. Refraction can have a significant impact
on the characteristics of a surf break due to variations in sea-floor bathymetry and the
angle at which waves approach (Butt and Russel, 2014).
Bathymetric focusing and defocusing
Refraction can have different effects on waves depending on the type of bathymetry it
propagates over. When wave crests hit shallow areas with a relatively deep trench in
the middle, they experience convex refraction also called bathymetric defocusing. This
is illustrated in Figure 2.4 where the wave energy is spread over a wider area in a cove,
resulting in weaker and smaller waves. In contrast, when waves hit a shallow reef/ridge
with deeper water on either side, they experience concave refraction also called bathymet-
ric focusing. In the illustration it is observed how wave energy is focused onto the reef
(headland), which concentrates the energy resulting in a more powerful peak with larger
waves. Refraction plays a critical role in shaping wave breaking at a surf spot and hence
the quality of surf. Here are some typical types of surf spots (Butt and Russel, 2014):
• Point-breaks: waves wrap around a point or a headland with a reef running along
the side. They tend to defocus and reduce the size of waves, but make them long
and walled up.
• Reef breaks: the waves are focused onto a protruding reef or a rock, resulting in
bigger and more powerful but shorter and more concentrated waves.
• Beach breaks: waves are typically determined by refraction on existing sandbars,
which focus the waves on shallow areas and create peaks and channels along the
beach. However, often beaches have very uniform bed which results in no bathy-
metric focusing or defocusing and waves collapse simultaneously along the beach as
a "close out".

Peel Angle
The surf ability of a wave is significantly influenced by the peel angle. The peel angle
is defined as "the angle between the peel-line and a line tangent to the crest-line at the
breaking point", according to Scarfed et al. (2009). The peel-line is the path of broken
white water left behind the wave. It extents from the breaking point at wave crest one
(C1) and to the breaking point at wave crest two (C2), as shown in Figure 2.6. The peel
angle plays a significant role in determining the speed with which the wave breaks along
the wave crest, where low peel angles lead to waves breaking too fast to surf. Surfers
with intermediate and advanced skill levels can ride waves with peel angles between 30°
to 55°, whereas professional surfers can surf peel angles below 27°. A wave crest with a 0°
peel angle breaks simultaneously, creating a closeout, which is suitable for beginners, but
do not create an enjoyable ride for more experienced surfers. Additionally, peel angles
between 60° to 70° are also suitable for beginners as the wave does not break too fast
(Scarfed et al., 2009).

2.2
MIKE 21 Spectral Wave FM
In this section and in section 2.3 the two wave models which will be used in this study
are presented. MIKE 21 SW is a phase averaged spectral wave model, which will be used
to simulate wind-generated waves, while MIKE 3 Wave is a phase-resolving 3D model,
which will be used to simulate wave breaking at a specific surf spot.
In short MIKE 21 SW is a state-of-the-art 2D spectral wind-wave model. The model
can spectrally simulate growth, decay and transformation of wind-generated waves and
swells in offshore and coastal areas (DHI, 2017b). As mentioned in section 2.1 spectral
wave models are a representation of ocean waves that uses the spectral approach. This
considers the wave motion in terms of its frequency and direction using wave components
rather than considering individual waves, and is a relatively computationally cheap model.
With this in mind spectral wave models are mainly utilized for evaluating wave conditions
in offshore and coastal regions, both in forecasting and retrospective analysis (hindcast).
From the wave spectrum, statistics such as significant wave height, peak wave period, and
mean wave direction, are calculated in all grids in the domain. In Figure 2.7 a global
application of the model is shown from the SW model user guide DHI, 2017b.
2.2.1
How does MIKE 21 SW work?
The model has two different formulations:
• Fully Spectral formulation
• Directional Decoupled Parametric formulation
The fully spectral approach is based on the wave action conservation equation, where the
directional-frequency wave action spectrum is the dependent variable. The directional
decoupled parametric approach involves a parameterization of the wave action conserva-
tion equation in the frequency domain using the zero and first moment of the wave action
spectrum. This formulation was not used in this report and will not be discussed further.
Physical effects included in the model are as follows:
• Wave growth by action of wind
• Non-linear wave-wave interaction
• Dissipation due to white-capping
• Dissipation due to bottom friction
• Dissipation due to depth induced wave breaking
• Refraction and shoaling due to depth variations
• Wave-current interactions
• Effect of time-varying water depth and flooding and drying.
The model uses a cell-centered finite volume method for discretization in geographical
and spectral space. It uses an unstructured mesh technique in the geographical domain
and a fractional step approach with a multi-sequence explicit method for time integration
of propagation of wave action (DHI, 2017b).

2.3
MIKE 3 Wave FM Model
In short MIKE 3 Wave FM is an advanced 3D phase resolving non-VOF wave modelling
application based on the 3D Navier-Stokes equations.
Comparing this to the MIKE
21 Spectral Waves Model which is a phase-averaged spectral model, MIKE 3 Wave is
capable of simulating individual waves including complex phenomenon such as wave run-
up, over-topping and most importantly for this project wave breaking. The model is fully
non-linear and models fully dispersive surface gravity waves and flow (DHI, 2019a). An
important distinction to make is that the model is not a Volume Overflow (VOF) model,
but instead utilizes a simple height function as the water surface. This allow for much
faster computation compared to traditional Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD) VOF
models.
2.3.1
How does it work?
The model is based on the numerical solution of the three-dimensional in-compressible
Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations. Hence, it comprises continuity and
momentum equations in 3D, and it also incorporates a turbulent closure scheme (DHI,
2019a). As mentioned the surface elevation is taken into account with a height function,
using a sigma coordinate transformation approach, which is illustrated in Figure 2.8.
Figure 2.8: MIKE 3 Wave Model illustration of sigma-level vertical grid. ((© - DHI, 2019a))
15
==PAGE:23==
The numerical solution technique solving the governing equations are as follows (DHI,
2019a):
• Spatial discretization: Governing equations are discretized using cell-center Finite
Volume Method (FVM).
• Time integration: Performed using semi-implicit scheme.
• Convective and diffusion terms: An implicit scheme is used to eliminate stability
issues related to vertical resolution.
• Remaining terms: Discretized using a second-order explicit Runge-Kutta scheme.
• Interface convective fluxes: calculated using a shock-capturing HLLC approximate
Riemann solver. It enables stable simulation of shocks or discontinuities, such as
bores, hydraulic jumps, and breaking waves (key feature for transformation of waves
all the way to the swash zone (wave breaking, undertow etc.).
• Non-hydrostatic pressure: projection method (Chorin (1967) for pressure-velocity
coupling.
• Flood and dry algorithm to simulate over topping effects.
• Turbulence closure with k −ϵ model.
Overall, MIKE 3 Wave Model is a complex state-of-the-art modelling software with many
applications in ocean engineering.
Perhaps the most unique feature is the ability to
model wave transformation all the way from deep water, through the surf zone, and into
the swash zone in one single model. Figure 2.9 illustrates an example of a vertical cross
section with surface elevation and horizontal velocity. As seen on the figure, the model

the wave at breaking. In that regard MIKE 3 Wave is inferior to a traditional CFD VOF
model, but it is also magnitudes faster in computation time. In a comparison conducted
by DHI between MIKE 3 Wave and an OpenFOAM VOF model, it was found by that to

Flood and dry
In MIKE 3, flooding and drying is an approach for treatment of areas in the domain
where flooding and drying occur.
For example in the coastal zone where water wash
up on the beach, also known as over topping effects. In this approach a threshold for
the drying water depth and a wetting depth is specified. When the water depth in the
simulation is less than the specified wetting depth, the problem is reformulated by setting
the momentum fluxes to zero and only consider the mass fluxes. If the water depth is less
than the drying depth the cell is completely removed from the simulation. This approach
will help minimize reflection by reducing over topping effects (DHI, 2019b).

3.2.2
Classification of surf conditions
In this section the offshore wave conditions are described which can produce poor, good,
and excellent surf. It is important to make the distinction that these conditions are used
to extrapolated wave quality at the beach, but actual wave breaking height at the surf
spot may vary according to conditions. These offshore conditions are classified into three
classes as follow:
• Class 1 offshore - Poor surf conditions
• Class 2 offshore - Good surfing conditions
• Class 3 offshore- Excellent surfing conditions
Class 1 offshore conditions describe offshore wave conditions which can produce break-
ing waves exceeding the minimum threshold of being surfable at breaking. Wave condi-
tions in this class produces surfable waves of poor quality, and individual surf rides will be
25
==PAGE:33==
low performance; low speed, no significant maneuvers, and short rides. Bigger surfboards
with more volume are needed to catch waves.
Class 2 offshore conditions describe offshore wave conditions which can produce good
surf conditions. Individual surf rides are high performance with high speeds for board
planning, possible wave maneuvering, and longer rides which occur with higher frequency.
Mid length and short boards with less volume can be utilized.
Class 3 offshore conditions describe offshore wave conditions which can produces ex-
cellent surf conditions. Individual surf rides are very high performance and will not be
suitable for all level of surfers due to increased difficulty from wave heights and wave
steepness at take of (start of ride). Waves require speed management on the board, mul-
tiple maneuvers are possible due to increased size of wave face, and rides will be longer.
These conditions require a combination of large waves with higher wave period as well as
clean conditions.
Using thresholds in Table 3.2 surf was classified for each location based on the 23 year pe-
riod from 1995-2018. Significant wave height thresholds were based on surfers experience
from the author and others in the surfing community in Zealand. A minimum wave pe-
riod of 5 s is required for the wave to be surfable, since smaller wave periods will produce
very short length waves of low surf quality. Finally, only hours with daylight occurring
between yearly average sunrise 7:00 AM and average sunset 7:00 PM were included.

Gilleleje

Spectral Wave Model
While data provided by DHI (MOOD) is very useful to analyse offshore wave conditions
it does not provide sufficiently high nearshore resolution for analysing specific surf spots
and the wave transformation processes such as refraction, diffraction and shoaling. In this
Chapter a detailed comparative analysis of the surf spots is conducted using a MIKE 21
Spectral Wave (SW) model. It includes collecting bathymetric data, setting up the model
domain with a flexible mesh, configuring model parameters, and a systematic analysis of
the wave conditions via application of both a quasi-stationary model as well as a stationary
model forced with 5 years of wind data.
4.1
Methodology - Bathymetry
4.1.1
Bathymetric Data
Surf quality at specific surf spots are very dependent on the quality of the swell, however,
local ocean floor conditions are equally important, if not significantly more important.
Therefore, bathymetric data of high quality and resolution is critical for understanding
the wave conditions at a particular surf spot, and hence necessary for numerical wave
modelling. The data provides information about the physical processes that transforms
the wave field, such as refraction, shoaling, and wave breaking.
Presentation of bathymetric data sets
The entire model domain consists of Kattegat and East Skagerrak. The resolution of the
data required depends on the vicinity to the surf spots. Four different data sets were
utilized to interpolate the mesh for the model:
1. Data provided by DHI covering entire domain of Kattegat and East Skagerrak. The
data scatter data was extracted from a large scale mesh covering Danish waters.
The data is low horizontal resolution (>1000 m). No vertical or horizontal accuracy
were found for this data set. See Appendix Figure 8.3 for a visual presentation.
2. Data provided by European Marine Observation and Data Network (EMODnet)
covering a subpart of the domain in southeast Kattegat. Data is medium horizontal
resolution (∼100 m).
Vertical accuracy is 0.5-5% of water depth and horizontal
accuracy <20-500m. See Appendix Figure 8.4 for a visual presentation.
3. Data provided by DHI. The data is satellite-derived and covers North Zealand near
shore and Swedish coastline from Skäret to Svanshall. Data is high horizontal reso-
lution (10 m), but does only cover to 6 m depth in Sweden. Vertical accuracy is 10%
of water depth, and horizontal accuracy is estimated to be less than 5 m because
the data is satellite derived. See Appendix Figure 8.5 for a visual presentation.
4. Data surveyed using an Unmanned Surface Vehicle (USV) covering Skäret and Plan-
tagen surf spots. Very high horizontal resolution (∼0.5 m). Vertical accuracy is 0.5%
of depth and horizontal accuracy is given as 3 m. See Appendix Figure 8.6 & 8.7
for a visual presentation.

4.2.3
Basic equations and solution technique
• The fully spectral formulation was selected. This method is more computa-
tionally heavy but it is also more accurate. A directionally decoupled parametric
formulation can also be used, but it does not include wind-wave generation processes,
which is essential for this investigation. Therefore, the fully spectral formulation has
been opted for because it excels in larger domains where simultaneous modelling of
wind-waves at large (Kattegat) and local scale (surf spot) is needed (DHI, 2017b).
Default settings was used: number of max Newton-Raphson iterations 500, toler-
ance 0.001 m (i.e. max-norm of change in significant wave height), and relaxation
factor 0.1.
• The quasi-stationary formulation was selected. The wind forcing was setup to
examine waves at specific wind speeds and wind directions, and therefore not how
the waves behave in a time-series.
This matches a quasi-stationary formulation
which calculates a stationary solution for each time step. It is often used when
winds are slowly varying in time and space and when wind-waves are fetch limited
(DHI, 2017b).
4.2.4
Spectral and directional discretization
The recommended logarithmic frequency distribution was used. It is given by:
fj = f0 · cj,
j = 0, 1, ..., n −1
(4.1)
where fj is the frequency, f0 minimum frequency, c the frequency factor (=1.1 default set-
ting) and n is number of frequencies. Standard settings in MIKE 21 SW is 25 frequencies
ranging from 0.54 Hz to 0.055 Hz (e.i. 1.85 s to 18.2 s). This range covers storm generated
long period waves, and was therefore reduced to ranging from 0.54 Hz to 0.07 Hz (e.i.
1.85 s to 14.3 s). The number of frequencies was increased to 50 and the result can be seen
in Figure 4.8. The directional discretization was chosen as 48 directions (default setting

16) distributed on a 360 degree rose, which correspond to a directional wave component
for every 7.5 degree.

4.2.5
Model calibration
In this section a calibration of the spectral wave model is presented. A discussion about
the validity of the calibration can be found in Section 4.7.5 in the discussion. The model
was calibrated using 27 days of wave data measured in the summer of 2020 at Hornbaek.
A spectral wave model was forced with winds ECMWF ERA5 reanalysis data with an
hourly time resolution and a grid resolution of 0.25◦X 0.25◦. The model was rerun with
several different configurations of the bottom friction and white capping.
Bottom friction
A Nikuradse roughness of kn = 0.04 m is the default setting in MIKE 21 SW and is
typically used for offshore applications. However, it is usually too high for near shore
applications (DHI, 2017b).
By reducing kn the bottom friction decreases resulting in
larger waves. In Appendix Figure 8.10 kn was varied from 0.004 to 0.4 which shows that
changing the parameter one order of magnitude results in a 0.15 m change in significant
wave height at the largest wave event.
White capping
In MIKE 21 SW white capping is primarily controlled by the dissipation coefficient, Cdis.
By reducing Cdis the overall white capping dissipation is reduced resulting in larger waves
(see DHI, 2017b). In Appendix Figure 8.11 results of varying Cdis in the range 0.665 to
2.66 are show. By decreasing Cdis by 50% the wave heights increased 0.2 m at the largest
wave event.
Final model configuration
In Figure 4.9 the wave data is plotted together with the default model settings of kn =
0.04 and Cdis = 1.33 and the final model settings of kn = 0.01 and Cdis = 0.665. It is
evident that decreasing the white capping and bottom friction constants by 75% and 50%
results in 0.35m larger waves, which fit the measured wave data more accurately, though
not perfectly.

Spectrum transformation
In this section the spectral shape is analyzed at Skäret and Svanshall. These two surf
spots are interesting because they exhibit very low peakedness contrary to the typical
surfing experience at these locations. Nonetheless, it is highly likely that findings from
doing the same analysis at North Zealand would be very similar. The analysis consists of a
comparison between the original wind-forced full scale model spectrum and a local model
which only is forced by a boundary condition (output from full scale model) and does
not include wind forcing. The idea is to determine how much the wind locally affects the
spectrum and quality of waves by analysing the spectrum without local winds. The local
model domain is shown in Figure 4.19, and red boundaries indicate where output from the
full scale model (wind-forced) are used as input for the local model. Specifically, Hs, Tp,
DSD, and peak wave direction was varied in time and in space along a line (boundary)
according to wave conditions in the full scale model. Mesh resolution and general model
setup remains identical in both models.

Results - In-stationary SW Model
The data was analyzed as follows: For each area output at a given time step the grid cell

4.5.1
Classification of surf conditions
Using the same methodology as Chapter 3 the surf conditions was evaluated using a
classification system. However, here waves are evaluated much closer to the to the surf
spots. Therefore, the thresholds (i.e. Hs, Tp) used to define classifications are adjusted
from offshore conditions to wave heights better resembling breaking heights (e.g. lower
Hs threshold). Classifications are as folows:
• Class 1 surf - Poor surf conditions
• Class 2 surf - Good surfing conditions
61
==PAGE:69==
• Class 3 surf - Excellent surfing conditions
Class 1 surf conditions describe nearshore wave conditions which can produce breaking
waves exceeding the minimum threshold ( 0.75m - 1.0m) of being surfable. Wave condi-
tions in this class produces surfable waves of poor quality, and individual surf rides will be
low performance; low speed, no significant maneuvers, and short rides. Bigger surfboards
with more volume are needed to catch waves.
Class 2 surf conditions describe nearshore wave conditions which can produce good surf
conditions. Wave heights are approximately waist to head high (i.e. 1.25m to 1.75m)
generally.
Individual surf rides are sometimes high performance with high speeds for
board planning, possible wave maneuvering, and longer rides which occur with higher
frequency. Mid length and short board types with less volume can be utilized.
Class 3 surf conditions describe nearshore wave conditions which can produces excellent
surf conditions. Generally, wave heights are head high (i.e. above
1.75m) and above.
Individual surf rides are very high performance and will not be suitable for all level of
surfers due to increased difficulty from wave heights and wave steepness at take off. Waves
require speed management on the board, multiple maneuvers are possible due to increased
size of wave face, and riders will be longer. These conditions require a combination of
large waves with higher wave period as well as clean conditions.
Using thresh-holds in Table 4.3 surf was classified for each location based on the 5 year
period from 2018-2022. Peak wave period should consistently be above 5 s to provide
wave lengths sufficiently long to surf. Only hours with daylight occurring between yearly
average sunrise 7:00 AM and average sunset 7:00 PM were included. Thresholds were
based on typical wave breaking heights at the surf spots, see surf spot descriptions in
Section 3.3.

4.7
Discussion
In this section both spectral wave models are discussed as well as wave breaking types,
data uncertainties and the model calibration.
4.7.1
Quasi-stationary model
The utilization of the quasi-stationary model made it possible to reach very high cell res-
olution, and thus to very accurately simulate phase-averaged waves at each surf spot in
very controlled wind directions. Resolution was sufficiently high to model the bathymetric
focusing on the ridge at Skäret. This confirms one of the reasons to why this surf spot
creates higher quality waves in comparison to Danish surf spots which lack these bathy-
metric formations that can precondition the incoming wave field thus increasing wave
heights. Overall, simulated results clearly demonstrated larger waves in northern wind
directions and decreased waves heights at more sheltered spots further away from the
headlands (i.e. Gilleleje and Kullen). At wind directions below 280 degrees longer peak
periods were observed to dominate closer to the coast, and this pattern was attributed
to the transformation of the frequency spectrum due to winds. A local model without
wind forcing confirmed simultaneously that directional standard deviation is mostly cor-
related to bathymetry, and also that peakedness Qp was strongly correlated to local winds
showing up to 100% increases in Qp at the most south wind directions from 230◦to 240◦
in 15 m/s wind speeds. In windy conditions peakedness was at all surf spots observed
to increase with distance along wave propagation directions (i.e. stream lines), and the
rate of increase was highest from 500m-1000m offshore and into the breaking zone, which
illustrates that the clean up of the frequency spectrum is very affected by depths. Espe-
cially, Skäret and Svanshall were seen to show higher rate of change in peakedness in the
breaking zone from 25m offshore to the 1 m contour line. Surprisingly, the peakedness
was in windy conditions generally observed to be lower for sheltered spots in southwest
wind direction especially Skäret and Svanshall. Likewise, Hornbaek and Plantagen were
seen to have lower peakedness compared to Nakkehoved and Gilleleje.
Helsingoer on
the contrary showed extremely high peakedness in southwest wind directions, perhaps
indicating that these sheltering effects can be expressed in increased peakedness, when
sheltering is very high and wave high very low. Generally, these results are opposite to the
expectations from surfers experience, where waves should be smaller but also significantly
cleaner in lower wind directions. Additional investigations may be required to address this
72
==PAGE:80==
discrepancy. Directional standard deviation does not bring any insight to this discrep-
ancy, because it is not very correlated to wind direction. However, it was observed that
Skäret and Svanshall had slightly lower directional spread. One very significant limitation
to this modelling method is the wind forcing. Every water surface in the entire model
domain is forced by a wind speed of 15 m/s, which is not a realistic depiction of the wind
speeds locally at each surf spot. Local wind speeds are affected by surface roughness and
sheltering from jetty’s, forests, and elevated land surface elevation. For example Kullen
and most of the Swedish coast is actually quite elevated and vegetated, and therefore
realistically provides lower wind speeds on the lee side when winds are offshore. Wind
speeds would be relatively small on the shoreline and then gradually increase in the off-
shore directions as the wind roughness is reduced in open ocean. Assuming winds are
less strong near the coast in western directions the shape of the spectrum would resemble
something in between the less peaked wind-forced model spectrum and the more peaked
local model without wind forcing. Such a spectrum would be relatively peaked, but still
have a slight shift towards longer peak periods along the shoreline due to medium strength
winds locally.
4.7.2
In-stationary model
The comparison between Danish and Swedish surf spots in south Kattegat was made
possible by simulating 5 years of wind data. According to the findings, the surfable hours
in a year for different surf conditions are as follows: Approximately, 300hr to 400hr for
Class 1 surf conditions, which consist of small surfable waves around waist high, i.e. 0.75m
to 1.25m wave heights. Approximately, 80hr to 115hr for Class 2 surf conditions, which
include larger waves around waist to head high, i.e. 1.25m to 1.75m wave heights. And
finally, approximately 15hr to 40hr for Class 3 surf conditions, which involve very large
waves above head high, i.e. greater than 1.75m. Skäret, Svanshall and Helsingoer showed
signs of occasionally longer period clean swell above 8 s and between 0m to 1.5m wave
heights, which is an indicator for higher quality waves. Unfortunately, the peakedness
of the wave spectrum were not analyzed, but directional standard deviation did show
directionally cleaner waves in Sweden with DSD 5◦to 10◦lower compared to the Danish
surf spots. Especially, Gilleleje which is exposed to many wave directions showed high
directional spread, while more southern surf spots like Hornbaek and Plantagen had lower
directional spread. Generally, it was found that the Swedish surf spots exhibited a quite
narrow range of mean wave direction and directional standard deviations. Perhaps the
most interesting findings where the frequency of surf in offshore wind directions at the two
Swedish spots. At 10◦offshore wind directions these two spots had disproportionately
high frequency of surf compared to Hornbaek and Plantagen in North Zealand. These
findings are perhaps even conservative estimations and if utilizing a more local coast line
orientation surf conditions are even more skewed towards frequently good waves at Skäret
and Svanshall with percent-wise increases ranging from 50% to 175%. It should be noted
that the orientation of harbour jettys at Gilleleje, Hornbaek and Helsingoer likewise could
have been used to adjusted using this method which would have increased frequency of
offshore wave conditions. Therefore, local adjustments like these are best compared to
Nakkehoved and Plantagen. Overall, these results show better surfing amenity in Sweden
compared to Denmark using parameters which does not include peakedness. As such,
the analysis is somewhat flawed because the quasi-stationary findings indicated that on
average the peakedness was lower at the Swedish spots.

4.7.3
Wave breaking types
The evaluation of surfing amenity between surf locations in northern Zealand and north-
east Höganäs Municipality were in the MIKE 21 Spectral Wave found to be somewhat
conflicting, because peakedness was not correlated with winds as expected. More fre-
quent offshore wind conditions and lower directional deviation did however suggest better
surfing amenity. Similarly, better surfing amenity was found from a wave steepness per-
spective. It was evident that the Swedish coast had considerably steeper waves, because
of the rocky bed slope that is up to three times steeper than the sandy beds found on
the Danish coast. This is an important distinction because steeper waves provide more
possibilities for higher level of performance surfing such as high speed maneuvers which
require speed management. Additionally, sandy bed types also called "beach breaks" are
not as reliable for surfing because sedimentation processes move the sand around dur-
ing storms. This is particularly observed when surfing at jetties such as Hornbaek and
Gilleleje, where sediments (i.e. sand) accumulated on the lee side of the structure can
shift around significantly.
4.7.4
Uncertainties
In this study simulating ocean waves spectrally involved some uncertainties due to the
resolution of the model, the resolution of wind data, and the resolution and accuracy of
bathymetry data. The spatial resolution of the mesh was significantly reduced to be able
to simulate many years of wind data. This definitely reduced the accuracy in very shallow
water and around complex bathymetry such as the ridge at Skäret, which consequently
was not resolved at all. However, it is not expected that the resolution affects the results
very significantly because data is mostly extracted from grid cells located quite far off-
shore where the complexities of the seabed is of less importance. Perhaps, more important
was the resolution of wind data and bathymetry data. Due to complications with data
formats lower quality wind data with a 15 x 15 km resolution was used instead of more
detailed 6 x 6 km resolution data available. The largest contributor to uncertainties was
the satellite bathymetry data, which was found to be mismatching other data sets. This
was particularly evident in nearshore areas, such as the case at Nakkehoved, which is
problematic because the effects of waves on the seabed are more pronounced in shallow
water. Collecting high quality data for all surf spots in the study using the USV method
would have increased the quality of results, but it would simultaneously have required sig-
nificantly more resources. Furthermore, the relatively uniform seabeds mostly consisting
of sandbar in North Zealand would perhaps not have benefited from better data.
4.7.5
Calibration
Later in the work process regarding this study it was discussed that the white capping
constant Cdis is sufficiently and thoroughly calibrated by DHI and is not a parameter which
should be changed. Furthermore, the model should also have been calibrated on wave data
from a beach profile with bottom roughness (i.e. solid rock) and bed slope more similar
to Skäret and Svanshall to determine a reasonable kn value. It is very likely that wave
heights have been overestimated in the spectral wave models, especially in North Zealand
where beach slopes are more gentle, which perhaps disproportionately increases bottom
friction at these surf spots compared to the Swedish spots. The possible calibration error
has most likely overestimated frequency of surf in Class 1, Class 2 and Class 3 overall, but

Mike 3 Wave Model
In Chapter 4 phase averaged results for waves were analyzed using a MIKE 21 Spectral
Waves model. It could not be concluded using parameters such as directional standard
deviation DSD or peakedness Qp, that surfing conditions from western directions are
more clean compared to northern directions at the two coast lines. The SW model was
useful for covering a larger domain with smaller focus areas, and it was capable of showing
bathymetric focusing at Skäret. However, the SW model is limited to linear shoaling and
refraction processes, and therefore a phase resolving model such as a MIKE 21 Boussinesq
Waves model or a MIKE 3 Waves model can be used to accurately model non-linearities
and individual wave breaking. Therefore, this Chapter presents a detailed analysis of

convergence analysis of wave statistics and model resolution, setting up the model domain
with an adjusted bathymetry profile, as well as a comparative analysis with MIKE 21 SW
results, and finally a visual presentation of wave breaking.

Convergence of wave statistics
In this section an analysis of wave statistics convergence (i.e. significant wave height)
is conducted through a simple numerical modelling scheme. When simulating individual
wave in MIKE 3 wave, sufficient time steps are required for accuracy in significant wave
height. If simulation time is not sufficiently long, wave statistics may not converge and
results can be unreliable, when compared with results from a spectral wave model.

using kurtosis, skewness and atiltness. Kurtosis describes the peakedness of the wave
shape. High kurtosis indicate narrow peaks and longer troughs. Typically, linear waves
has a kurtosis of 3, but MIKE 3 Wave shows linear waves at a kurtosis of 0. Skewness
describes the asymmetry of waves. Positively skewed waves have a heavy tail such that
the mean value is above the median value, Atiltness describes the asymmetry in the
direction of propagation. Positive atiltness describes non-liner waves in shallow water
which tend to have a steep front slope and a less steep back slope. From subfigure (a)
and (b) it is evident significant wave height is identical for each spectrum. Subfigure
(c) to subfigure (h) indicate the non-linearity of the waves in the area ranging from
the end of the ridge in intermediate water and into the breaking zone in shallow water.
Skewness is approximately equal between the two spectra. However, for the long period
waves peakedness (i.e. kurtosis) increases further offshore compared to the northern short
period waves and peakedness is generally higher along the shore. Similarly, atiltness is
90

significantly higher for the long period waves signifying steeper waves and perhaps more
powerful waves of the plunging type. This can be deduced from the definition of atiltness
which includes the steepness of the front and rear slope. Highly plunging waves on the
limit to surging waves have very gentle rear slopes and extremely steep front slopes.

Dissipation of short period waves
Figure 5.17 shows 3D surface elevation in the northern corner of the model in the wave
generation zone. It is very notice how quickly high frequency wave (i.e. short period) are
dissipated outside of the wave generation zone. This was also evident from the cumulative
wave statistics, but here it is visible how fast the process happens for the smaller "ripples"
waves. From a small assessment of the convergence analysis the following can be found:
The wave periods T = [2 s, 3 s, 4 s, 5 s] waves will have a deep water wave length of
L0 = [4.6 m, 10.4 m, 18.5 m, 28.8 m]. Using 25 cells per wave length (c/w) it can be
calculated a horizontal resolution of [0.18 m, 0.42 m, 0.74 m, 1.15 m] is required to resolve
these waves, and this resolution was found to always diverge from higher resolutions very
quickly. This means that since the model domain was computationally limited to a 1 m
cell size in the the wave generation zone all waves smaller than ∼5 s will numerically
dissipate with the shortest periods dissipation at the fastest rate. This confirms why Hs
from MIKE 3 Wave is smaller compared to MIKE 21 SW. MIKE 3 Wave model results
could be forced to match spectral wave results by introducing a limit to higher frequencies
such as fmax =∼4 s and then rescaling the spectrum. This would preemptively cut out
non-resolvable wave lengths, but at the same time this would artificially provide cleaner
waves, exactly as numerical wave dissipation does in the model.

Peel angle
The quality of a wave is significantly influenced by the peel angle. As defined in the
theory section the peel angle is "the angle between the peel-line and a line tangent to
the crest-line at the breaking point". The peel-line can be approximated following the
turbulence trail in the model and the crest-line can be found by the surface elevation.
Figure 5.21 visually shows the peel angles at two instants for both spectra. The angles
range from 40◦to 60◦, and after analysing 30 time steps (i.e. 30 s) of wave breaking for
each spectrum the mean peel angle for both were found to be 50◦. According to Scarfed
et al. (2009) this classifies the waves as suitable for intermediate level surfers where some
of the waves in the lower range around 40◦are more suitable for more advanced surfers.

5.5
Discussion
The investigations carried out in this chapter shows that MIKE 3 Wave is a powerful tool
for modelling non-linear wave transformations such as shoaling and refraction as well as
96
==PAGE:104==
wave breaking in the surf zone. Both the MIKE 21 SW model and the MIKE 3 Wave model
was capable of accurately describing the complex bathymetric focusing at a high quality
surf spot such as Skäret. However, it was found that MIKE 3 compared to MIKE 21 SW
could model 2.5% - 5.5% larger significant wave height on the ridge, which is significant
because the model simultaneously was negatively affected by numerical dissipation due to
limitations to spatial resolution. It was concluded from a convergence analysis that the
model resolution necessary for resolving wave spectra from two typical wave directions,
W and NW, was only sufficiently high to accurately simulate peak periods in the wind
swell, and shorter period waves were unfortunately numerically dissipated. Here, a typical
modelers dilemma occurs, where increased resolution is desirable, but is only attainable
by increases to computational resources. For this project computational resources were
maximized, and therefore other options were to spatially reduce the domain or to decrease
the simulation time. Reducing the domain was difficult due to the large spatial extent
of the ridge bathymetry in the domain, and the simulation time was already reduced
such that significant wave height was statistically converged, but maximum wave height
was not converged. Ultimately, the constraints to the resolution resulted in dissipation
of waves around the lower periods effectively cleaning up the wind swell artificially by
increasing the peakedness around the peak period. This effect was most pronounced in
the northwest wind swell, which had a peak period of 6.2 seconds and produced slightly
more messy waves compared to the longer period swell from the west, although the quality
of the waves was very comparable. Generally, wind is one of the factor affecting surf
quality the most, because it produces unwanted choppy and messy waves in the extreme
high frequency tail of the wave spectrum. A rather wide spectrum without wind chop
in the most extreme tail of the spectrum will for most surfers be preferable to a narrow
spectrum with wind chop. These “wind chops” typically disappear quickly in cross-shore
or slight offshore wind conditions, but it is almost impossible to model such small-scale
waves without requiring astronomical computing resources.
This makes it difficult to
interpret to what degree the cleaner conditions closer to the surf spot can be attributed to
refraction and shoaling processes or to numerical dissipation. Nonetheless, MIKE 3 Wave
was effectively used to simulate waves that replicate the breaking pattern at the surf spot
quite accurately, with peeling angles which from the literature and from surfers experience
matches with intermediate level surfing. However, additional quantitative analysis of the
3D wave outputs is still necessary to confirm if surfable waves were modelled or not. This
includes quantifying terminology such as wave sections length, wave section duration,
detailed pocket points/area, wave breaking intensity and of course wave breaking heights
to determine if the wave is surfable and additionally what surfing level is required. Scarfed
et al. (2009) can be consulted for further inspiration into these characteristics of surfing
waves. Some Algorithms such as OPTISURF by DHI, has already been developed for
this such purpose, and simulation-based algorithms can be compared with camera-based
algorithms which track real world waves (Thompson2021).
Wave tracking algorithms
were not utilized for the scope of this project, but is an exciting new area of research.
Overall, MIKE 3 shows strengths in simulating non-linear wave transformations especially
in the breaking zone where non-linear wave transformations can be quite dominating, but
in relation to short period wind waves it is quite limited, and spectral wave model are
perhaps preferred depending on the goal at hand.

Improving surfing amenity on the
north coast of Zealand
The evaluation of surfing conditions in Southeast Kattegat revealed that Swedish surf
spots were of generally higher quality than Danish ones, primarily due to significant dif-
ferences in bathymetry, which included steeper bed slopes and complex bathymetric wave
focusing. However, the analysis also showed that the frequency of good wave conditions
across all wind directions was identical if not higher at Danish compared to Swedish surf
spots. An Artificial Surf Reefs could potentially provide an solution for increasing the
surfing amenity on the Danish coast by imitating the characteristics observed in Sweden.
Naturally, it is not feasible to increase the depth to 15 m to match close shore conditions
at Skäret, but it is possible to improve breaking steepness by designing an ASR close to
the breaking zone. This would requires extensive research to determine the ideal location,
ASR dimensions, offshore depth of ASR, orientation to the coastline of ASR and addi-
tionally other considerations such as shoreline management and biodiversity to include
the many stakeholders interested on the northern coast of Zealand.

Conclusions
Applications of two numerical wave models, a MIKE 21 Spectral Wave model and a MIKE
3 Wave 3D phase-resolving model, was used to study the surfing amenity in Southeast
Kattegat and to compare surf quality between Danish and Swedish surf spots in the
geographical area.
Overall, the spectral wave models determined that the surf spots in Sweden offered better
surfing amenity, characterized by lower directional spreading, higher frequency of surfable
waves in offshore wind conditions, and greater wave steepness attributable to bed slopes
two to three times steeper than those found in northern Zealand.
The study found that the directional spreading was higher at the surf spots in Denmark
and was strongly correlated to bathymetry but only slightly correlated to wind direction.
The peakedness parameter, represented by Qp, led to conflicting conclusions, as it indi-
cated lower spectral "cleanliness" at the Swedish surf spots. Generally, during offshore
and cross-shore wind conditions peakedness was low, which is typically associated with
lower quality waves. This contradicts the surfers experience, as these wind directions
are commonly considered ideal for clean surfing conditions, and the spectral peakedness
therefore requires further investigation.
Furthermore, the study found that the surf spot "Skäret" had a very complex bathymetry
in the form of a ridge, which increased wave heights due to the focusing of wave energy.
In contrast, the bathymetry data for Danish surf spots such as "Plantagen" showed a lack
of these complex bathymetric formations that can precondition the incoming wave field.
The MIKE 3 Wave model was found to be a powerful tool for 3D modelling non-linear
wave transformations such as shoaling, refraction and wave breaking in the surf zone.
Compared to the MIKE 21 SW it found 2.5% - 5.5% larger significant wave heights
on the ridge, which likely is a conservative estimate because the model simultaneously
was negatively affected by numerical dissipation. Unfortunately, it was not possible to
accurately resolve short period wind chop waves, and as a results, the model effectively
cleans up the wind swell waves artificially. Nonetheless, MIKE 3 Wave was effectively
used to simulate waves that replicate the breaking pattern, including peeling angle, at the
surf spot quite accurately.
When accounting for all wind directions, the yearly surf hours across all spots in southeast
Kattegat was found to be very similar with an average of:
• 300 hours to 400 hours with small waist high waves ranging from 0.75m to 1.25m.
• 80 hours to 115 hours with waist to head high waves ranging from 1.25m to 1.75m.
• 15 hours to 40 hours with very large waves above head high i.e. 1.75m.
The similarity in surf frequency suggests that constructing an ASR in northern Zealand
to replicate the bathymetry features found in Sweden could enhance the surfing amenity
in the area.
Finally, significant uncertainties are related to the resolution of the wind data, and es-
pecially the bathymetry data which was found to locally have large errors in the water
depth. The calibration process has likewise probably contributed to overestimation of the
wave heights.
