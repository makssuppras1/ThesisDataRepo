Abstract
This work explored the fertigation value of two pilot-scale AnMBR permeates: (i) a 225:25 co-
digestate of municipal waste water (MWW) with food waste (FW) and (ii) a 1:1 co-digestate of
brewery waste water (BWW) with MWW. Their agronomic performance was tested in parallel
trials on the biomass crop Salix viminalis and the vegetable Capsicum annuum. Chemical char-
acterisation showed that the MWW/BWW permeate carried a higher nutrient load but also a
greater salt content, necessitating a higher dilution than the milder MWW/FW permeate. Treat-
ments compared a modified ISO 8692 nutrient solution (NS) with undiluted digestates (D100),
water-diluted digestates (DD) and nutrient-solution digestate blends (NSD) in hydroponics for S.
viminalis and in soil for C. annuum. Across both crops the NSD mixtures consistently outper-
formed either NS or D100 at comparable total-nitrogen levels, an effect attributed to synergistic
uptake at favorable NH4 : NO3 ratios. In willows, diluted NS blended 1:1 with MWW/FW digest-
ate diluted 1:1 with de-ionized water achieved the highest normalized relative transpiration (NRT),
reaching 148% of the NS control at an NH4 : NO3 ratio of 1.83. The MWW/BWW digestate,
diluted 1:3 and blended with diluted NS, delivered 168% of control NRT, the greatest water-use
efficiency (WUE) and a high relative weight gain (RWG) at a NH4 : NO3 ratio of 0.41. In pep-
pers, a 1:9 blend of BWW/MWW digestate with full-strength NS doubled fruit mass relative to
pure NS and yielded the highest WUE and nitrogen-use efficiency (NUE) at NH4 : NO3 = 0.36.
Additionally, diluted digestate (DD) outperformed the NRT of the NS in the S. Viminalis hy-
droponic trials conducted with the MWW/FW digestate, displaying a higher RWG and WUE. In
the hydroponic trials with the MWW/BWW digestate, the DD treatment achieved similar NRT
values and a higher WUE. The C.
Annuum soil experiment saw the DD treatment displaying
similar transpiration and stomatal conductance values as NS, as well as a higher WUE and NUE.
The experiments confirmed both digestates as applicable for fertigation, either as co-fertilizers or
by themselves, when nutrient levels and salt risks were taken into account. A risk assessment
conducted with the generic plant-uptake model by Trapp, Shi et al. (2023) indicated that the
concentrations of organic micro-pollutants (OMP) and personal-care products (PPCP) present in
the MWW/BWW effluent would be taken up in the C. Annuum cultivars at levels well below any
threshold of health concern.

Introduction
Water scarcity is becoming an increasingly bigger problem in Europe, a problem, that contrary
to popular belief is less the consequence of an inexorable climatic drying trend than of the ways
societies extract, divert and drain water. High-resolution studies show winter precipitation across
northern Europe climbing by roughly 1.6 % per decade (Haruna et al. 2025) and Danish gauges
logging 40 mm yr-¹ more rain than the 1981-2010 norm (Danish Meteorological Institute 2025).
Satellite and probe data further reveal that surface-soil moisture has risen over 57% of the contin-
ental United States since 2011, identifying increasingly unstable precipitation patterns as the main
factors driving such trends. Furthermore, increasing surface soil driven by more efficient water use
by plants will largely compensate for, and in some cases overcome, decreasing surface soil moisture
trends driven by anthropogenic warming (Lucas R. Vargas Zeppetello 2024).
Yet rivers run dry and aquifers decline, when society drains water faster than clouds can replace
it. Roughly 16% of German farmland is reportedly laced with subsurface tile drains, deflecting
rainfall straight to the nearest ditch or river, wasting valuable water groundwater resources and
polluting in the process (Steidl et al. 2022). A global system of monitoring wells reveals persistent,
human-driven groundwater declines even in basins where precipitation has stayed flat or risen,
because withdrawals systematically outpace recharge (Fatemeh Karandish 2025). Agriculture then
pumps what is left, claiming about 70% of global freshwater withdrawals, and is the only major
sector in Europe where abstractions keep climbing (Ingrao et al. 2023; European Environment
Agency 2024). In short, scarcity here is less about a hostile climate than about how we capture,
drain and misuse our water resources.
Recycled wastewater can serve as a reliable and effective water supply, offering benefits such as
water conservation, reduced dependence on water resources, pollution reduction and nutrient re-
covery (Extension 2017). Despite successful implementations in several EU countries, water reuse
remains underutilized, with only 1 million m3 being reused every year, a volume the The European
Commission suggest could be increased sixfold, identifying limited stakeholder awareness and the
absence of a coherent framework as main barriers for expansion of water reuse practices (European
Commission 2025b). To address these challenges, further research and literature is essential to
raise awareness, develop regulatory frameworks and promote public acceptance on the topic.
Anaerobic membrane bioreactors (AnMBRs) unite the biogas efficiency of classic digesters with a
membrane separation step that screens out solids, yielding a clear, nutrient-rich effluent. Recent
reviews report 85–95% COD removal, while capturing most of the liberated carbon as methane,
conserving nutrients in the process (Aslam et al. 2022). Long retention times allowing thorough
bacterial degradation, and fermentation processes occurring in anoxic environments, render nutri-
ents mineralized into plant-available forms like ammonium (NH+
3 ), phosphates (PO3−
4 ) and hy-
drogen sulfide (H2S). The same processes reportedly knock vegetative pathogens down by roughly
3 −5 log10CFU (´Alvarez-Fraga et al. 2024), resulting in permeate suitable for direct agricultural
use (Alburquerque et al. 2012; Pou et al. 2022; M¨oller et al. 2012). A 2019 report by Jain et
al. (2019) states that 5-7% of inorganic fertilizer could be replaced by digestate globally, if the
potential from the biogas industry were utilized.
At the same time, packing more biomass behind a membrane drives up membrane pressure and
increases fouling, so capital and cleaning costs remain higher than in conventional digesters (Chang
et al. 2002). However, life cycle studies show AnMBRs outperform their aerobic counterparts when
the effluent itself is reused (Xu et al. 2020), suggesting integration of AnMBRs in circular economy

initiatives as a credible route of water reuse, provided agronomic value is realized and risks are
managed.
Most studies, however, feed the reactor either municipal sludge or a single industrial stream, and
few explore the nutrient balance or contaminant profile that emerges when urban wastewater is co-
digested with residues such as food waste and brewery waste water residues. This is the set-up at
the EU-funded SYMSITES “ecosite” on Bornholm, containing a community-scale AnMBR treating
mixed municipal and industrial streams aiming to close local water-nutrient loops (European Com-
mission 2025a). Whether this mixed feed permeate can safely replace synthetic fertilizer, either as
a co-provider of nutrients or replacing it entirely, is the core objective of this thesis.
Several studies report such co-digestion of food waste or brewery effluent with municipal wastewa-
ter in AnMBRs to lift both energy yields and the fertilizer value of the permeate. A two-stage
AnMBR fed with kitchen waste plus wastewater delivered 80 % COD removal, methane yields
up to 249 ± 7L kg CH4/CODremoved while leaving ammonium- and phosphate-rich permeate con-
sidered suitable for fertigation after dilution (Le et al. 2022). At demo scale, a plant treating
roughly equal parts domestic sewage and food waste held stable fluxes at ambient temperatures,
and cut net greenhouse-gas emissions by 85% versus activated-sludge, while still exporting an ef-
fluent with most nutrients contained (Lei et al. 2024). System-level work backs this up. LCA
assessments shows trading activated sludge for food waste in an AnMBR beat landfilling, com-
posting, or separate digestion of the matter, decreasing both Global Warming Potential (GWP)
and eutrophication footprints (Becker et al. 2017).
For brewery sidestreams, anaerobically digested effluent has already been used as the sole nutrient
provider in hydroponic solution.
Mustard greens and lettuce grown in pH adjusted digestate
yielded similar produce as that of the inorganic fertilizer, demonstrating valid potential for nutrient
recovery (Riera-Vila et al. 2019). These studies position the SYMSITES concept, demonstrating
how decentralized co-digestion of urban wastewater with food and brewery wastes can act as a
realistic route to community-scale circularity, provided that nutrient balance, salinity and pH are
managed.
An equally important, but sometimes overlooked aspect when exploring the field of fertigation
with waste water, is the possible accumulation of organic pollutants in crops (Colon 2015; Zheng
et al. 2021). Municipal waste water, being a large fraction of water entering WWTPs as well as
the water treated in this study, is frequently contaminated with organic micropollutants (OMPs)
encompassing pharmaceuticals and personal care products (PPCPs), as well as chemicals released
from industries. Identified as a gap in conventional engineered treatment plants, OMPs and PPCPs
largely pass untreated through waste water treatment plants (WWTPs) and enter water streams,
posing harm to flora and biota, as confirmed by several Recent Danish surveys. Aggerbeck et al.
(2024) found drugs and therapeutic compounds to be by far the most abundant group measured in 3
Danish WWTP effluents, identifying 114 individual compounds of which several harmful to aquatic
biota, while Kilpinen et al. (2023) measured 21 pharmaceuticals above limit of quantification (LOQ)
across eight plants, with several exceeding predicted no-effect concentration (PNEC) values.
This thesis seeks to investigate effluent from the pilot-scale AnMBR installed on Bornholm within
the EU-funded SYMSITES project, evaluating its suitability for fertigation of two contrasting
crops: Salix viminalis (a woody bioenergy species) and Capsicum annuum cv. Padr´on (a high-
value vegetable). The experimental work compares growth of these species with standard ISO 8962
nutrient solution with blends containing AnMBR digestate, measuring transpiration, biomass and
yield while monitoring the fate of pharmaceuticals measured in the effluents. Plant accumulation
is interpreted through a generic plant uptake model by Trapp, Shi et al. (2023), implemented
with measured data on the peppers as well as literature data. By coupling water-reuse initiatives,
nutrient recovery from AnMBRs and OMP risk in a single study, the thesis aims to investigate
how decentralized anaerobic treatment coupled with water-reuse can be a viable path for existing
and future circularity initiatives.

Site description
The SYMSITES pilot consists of a 2.5 m3 anaerobic digester coupled to an external membrane
tank mounted on the same frame, the digester being fed by three insulated feeding tanks of 1
m3 each, dosing municipal wastewater (MWW), brewery wastewater (BWW) and macerated food
waste (FW). Permeate is then withdrawn through the membrane unit, consisting of three flat-sheet
polymeric ultrafiltration membranes (Alfa Laval, Lund, Sweden) housed in a tank adjacent to the
digester. The structure has semi-open design for biogas safety reasons, allowing venting in case of
over-pressure in compliance with ATEX standards. The system is connected to a pre-treatment
container, containing the BW shredder, the feeding tanks, a drum filter filtering the slurry, dosing
pumps, a regulation tank and the electrical cabinet. A flow chart of the structure can be seen in
fig. 1.

0.1.2
Permeate analysis
The permeates were delivered frozen and were thawed under a fume hood for a day before analysis.
Pathogen removal
Before characterization, the effluent was autoclaved by use of a CertoClav
Classic (Leonding, Austria) at 121 °C for 90 minutes, ensuring total removal of residual pathogens.
Nutrients
Effluent samples were analyzed in duplicate with Hach Lange LCK sealed tube cuvette
tests, which place reagents for each parameter in glass vials with bar codes, read automatically
by the DR 3900 spectrophotometer (Hach Lange, Germany). Total nitrogen was determined with
LCK 238 (alkaline persulfate digestion, ISO 11905-1), nitrate-N with LCK 339 (2,6-dimethylphenol
colour reaction, ISO 7890), ammonium-N with LCK 304 (salicylate indophenol, ISO 7150-1), ortho-
phosphate-P with LCK 348 (Phosphormolybdenum, ISO 6878-1-1986) and total P after persulfate
digestion with LCK 350 (molybdenum-blue, EN 1189), and sulphate with LCK 153/353 (turbi-
dimetric BaSO4, ISO 9280). The barcode uploads method, calibration and expiry data to the
photometer, measuring at wavelengths from 365 nm (NO3 −N) to 880 nm (PO4 −P). Additional
laboratory analysis (SKALAR) was done on NOx, nitrates NO2 and ammonia NH3, as well as
orthophosphate PO4. Samples were prepared and sent to the lab for Total Organic Carbon (TOC)
and metals analysis (ICP) by filtering 10ml with a syringe (Chiruna, Slovakia) through a 0.2µm
nylon filter (Agilent, USA) into 10 ml plastic vials for SKALAR and ICP, and glass vials for TOC.
COD & BOD5
Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD) was measured with the Hach Lange LCK
314 test kit, based on sealed-tube sulphuric digestion (ISO 15705; DIN 38409-H41-H44) reading at
λm = 400nm. Biochemical oxygen demand was measured respirometrically with 500 mL brown-
glass bottles equipped with OxiTop-i measuring heads (WTW/Xylem, USA). Firstly, each bottle
was filled with 250ml sample based on a predicted BOD value of 200mg/L, and one drop of allyl-
thiourea (strength) was added per 50ml of liquid. Then, an alkali cartridge (NaOH) was added to
a sleeve sitting below the lid to absorb CO2 released during respiration. Finally, the Oxitop lid
was mounted on the bottle, and scanned with the controller where sample amount and predicted
concentration was put in, before the flasks were put the incubation chamber. As microorganisms
consumes dissolved O2, the resulting CO2 is stripped from the gas phase, producing a proportional
decline in head-space pressure.
The integrated sensor logs this pressure drop every 15 min at
20 ± 1°C and the instrument then converts the ∆P to BOD (mg O2L−1) using the ideal-gas law.
TSS & VSS
The TSS and VSS measuerements were determined by gravimetric analysis, i.e.
weight differences of filters after filtration of a known amount of waste water. Glass fiber filters
with 0.45 µm pore size (Advantec, Japan) were preconditioned by wetting with DIW, and placed
in a drying oven (VWR/memmert, Germany) at 105°C for an hour. They were then left to reach
ambient temperature in a desiccator (Aldrich, USA) and weighed at 0.001g accuracy with an
analytical balance (Mettler Toledo, USA). 50ml sample was filtered through the filters by use of
a Pyrex bottle system (Millipore, USA) coupled to a vacuum extractor (Heto waterjet, Holm &
Halby , Danmark) after which the filters were put back in the drying oven at 105°to evaporate
residual water. After 24h, the filters were taken out and left in a desiccator (Aldrich, USA) before
being weighed again. TSS was then calculated by formula 1.

0.1.3
Willow transpiration experiments
The willow toxicity experiments were carried out as described in the manual ’The acute toxicity
test’ by Trapp, Zambrano et al. (2000), available online (DTU Environment, Technical University
of Denmark 2004). The test follows the principle that healthy trees transpire more water than
sick trees. Cuttings of Salix Viminalis are grown hydroponically in sealed flasks, first in nutrient
solution and then in the solutions that are tested for. The outcome of the experiment is based on
the change of transpiration in the trees between the treatments.
Willows for the first digestate experiment were delivered from the willow plantation at DTU Risø,
and cut manually outside DTU building 101 for the second digestate. After collection, they were
measured with a caliper (Vernier, USA) to diameters 10-14 mm, cut into lengths of 40 cm and
placed upright in a bucket with water 10 cm up the stem in a south-facing windowsill until root
and foliage growth was observed, approximately one week for roots and two weeks for the foliage.
After two weeks, the willows were given numbers 1-30, weighed with an electronic balance of
0.001 g accuracy (Mettler Toledo, Columbus, USA) and put in 500 ml erlenmeyer flasks filled
with 400 ml of modified ISO8692 solution (composition in appendix), diluted 1:1 with de-ionized
water to halve the solution concentrations. The flasks were then covered with aluminum foil to
minimize light seepage and reduce algae growth, while openings were clogged with aluminum foil
and sealed with laboratory film (parafilm PM-996, amcor, USA) to ensure water loss was solely
from transpiration and no evaporation would take place. The flasks were weighed with the balance
and placed uniformly on a lighting table with 10 overhanging Philips TL-D 36W/840 light tubes
(Philips, NL) delivering 3100 lm, each spaced 20 cm apart. As the middle light tube was defect,
the flasks were placed on either side to ensure uniform lighting conditions. The placements of the
flasks were marked on the table with a whiteboard marker as well as the stems of the trees in the
facing directions. The setup can be seen in figure 2.
Plants were weighed with flasks every morning, and daily weight/transpiration loss was noted.
After 7 days, the average transpiration was calculated, and plants were ranked from most to least
transpiring. The bottom 5 were discarded. The remaining 25 were weighed without bottles to
determine biomass growth, and were then put back in the bottles, refilled with solution based on
their transpiration. 5 treatments were employed in total: the top 5 were put in Nutrient Solution
(NS) as control, the 5 second most transpiring in Tap Water (TW), the middle 5 in pure digestate
(D100), second least transpiring in diluted digestate (DD) and the 5 least transpiring in nutrient
solution mixed with digestate (DNS). Care was taken to put plants facing the exact same directions,
and be in the same place by use of the markers on the tables and the stems, to decrease variability.
The plants were then weighed every day Monday to Friday for two weeks, and the weight loss was
noted each day. After two weeks, a clear trend in transpiration between the treatments could be
seen, and the experiment was terminated. The trees were weighed without bottles, and leaves and
roots were harvested. Solutions were sampled into 50 ml propylene falcon tubes for analysis.

Fertilization strategy, Digestate 1 FW/MWW
The first digestate dilutions were based on
matching the nitrogen concentration to that of the nutrient solution.
Having a total nitrogen
concentration of 31.8 mg-N/L compared to 19.76 mg/L in the nutrient solution, the digestate was
diluted by 2 parts DIW to one part digestate for the diluted digestate treatment (DD), rendering
a nitrogen concentration of approximately equal amount (21.2 mg/L). The nutrient solution with
digestate mix was selected as 50/50 diluted nutrient solution and diluted digestate 2:1, rendering a
concentration of 20.48 mg/L. The pure digestate treatment (D100) was chosen to determine possible
toxicity effects of the permeate, and tap water (TW, treatment 4) as a nutrient-less baseline. A
summary of the dilutions used in the first willow experiments can be seen in section 0.4.
Fertilization strategy, Digestate 2 MWW/BWW
The second digestate of MWW/BWW
was diluted based on its salinity content to limit toxic effects. For the diluted digestate treatment
(DD), the digestate was therefore diluted with two parts water, reaching an EC of 7.12 mS/cm.
Based on Hangs et al. (2011), reporting a limit value of 8 for salt tolerant willow species, and a
value of 5 as the limit for most species, the dilution was chosen to maximize nutrient content as
well as to test salinity resistance. The nutrient solution-digestate mix (NSD) was chosen as 3 parts
diluted NS (1:1) to one part of digestate rendering an EC value of 5.32, to maximize nutrients but
limit salinity effects. The pure digestate treatment was again chosen to determine possible toxicity
effects of the permeate, and tap water as a nutrient-less baseline. A summary of the dilutions used
in the second willow experiments can be seen in section 0.4.
Working hypothesis
The working hypothesis was that the mix of digestate with nutrient solu-
tion (DNS) would perform the best due to its beneficial mix of ammonia and nitrate - N, followed
by the nutrient solution (NS), the pure digestate (D100), the diluted digestate (DD) and finally
the tap water (TW), due to nutrient contents. The same prediction of the DNS mix was applied
for the second digestate, but that the trees in pure and diluted digestate solutions would transpire
the least despite high nutrient contents, due to salinity effects.

Field capacity, wilting point and watering schedule
The watering schedule for the peppers
was based on theory of pepper irrigation from Tiffany Maughan (2015) and water stress in pot
experiments from Imakumbili (2019). The maximum available water level in the soil, determined
as the soils field capacity (FC), is calculated in each pot, which is then given a water level for
which it cannot drop below, determined as a 40 % loss in total available water. The formula is
given by relationship 3.
WL[g] = FC −0.4 · AW = FC −0.4 · (FC −WP)
(3)
Here, WL [g] is water level at which the plant should be watered, and field capacity (FC) [g]
is weight of the soil when its holding the maximum amount of water, after excess water have
percolated out due to gravity . Available water (AW) [g] is the difference between field capacity
and wilting point (WP), the point at which no water is available for plant uptake and the plant
will die.
To determine the field capacities, each plant/pot was thoroughly watered until water started seep-
ing from the bottom, done three times to ensure the soil was fully saturated, i.e. both the micro-
pores and macro-pores in the soil were completely filled with water. The pots were then left to
drain for 24 hours on a thin layer of LECA balls in the bottom of the plastic crates, after which
excess water had percolated, and the soil was at field capacity (Imakumbili 2019). For finding
the permanent wilting point, one plant with soil was dried in a drying oven (VWR/memmert,
Germany) at 105°C for 24 h, after which it was put back in the pot and its weight was noted.
Available water was calculated as the difference between the field capacities and the wilting point,
and an allowable water depletion of 40 % was determined, based on Tiffany Maughan (2015).
Plants were weighed each day, and once coming close or below water levels (approximately every
3. day), all pots as well as controls were watered through light dripping on the topsoil with a 100
ml syringe (Chiruna, Slovakia), bringing them as close to field capacity as possible. Pots at field
capacities before and after re-potting as well as control weights and weights to water can be seen
in table 2.

Generic plant–uptake model for ionizable PPCPs
Pepper uptake of ionizable pharmaceuticals and personal-care products (PPCPs) found in the ef-
fluent was simulated with the analytic spreadsheet model of Trapp, Shi et al. 2023. The model
consists of two versions, with the classical/standard method, developed for neutral organics, con-
sidering partition processes and unidirectional water flux from roots to leaves/fruits within the
xylem (the central tissue responsible for transport of water/soil nutrients), setting the diffusion
end point between root cells and water. Most PPCPs are however ionized at environmental pH,
and an extended model was thus developed, taking into account (i) acid–base dissociation, (ii) dif-
fusion across cell membranes for ions, (iii) protein sorption in plant compartments (roots, leaves,
fruits), and (iv) bidirectional phloem flux, i.e. from leaves to fruits and leaves to roots. Main
equations defining the model are described below.

Literature on co-digestion of FW/MWW mixes used for fertigation is sparse and variable. Song et
al. (2021), examining the fertigation potential of anaerobically treated food waste from a canteen,
found ammonium at 4.70 g/L, P at 171.90 g/L, Mg at 6.46 g/L, and Na at 2 270.05 g/L, with the
digester operated at 40 kg volatile solids (VS) per week (Song et al. 2021). Dur´an et al. (2018),
studying an AnMBR treating kitchen waste for 536 days found ∼40 to ∼70 mg/L of NH4-N, P
at ∼5 to ∼8 mg/L, TSS at 12 - 30 mg/L and COD levels of ∼23 to ∼50 mg/L. Addition of
food waste was not seen to increase ammonium in the permeate, but increased hydrolytic activity

4

themselves. The first digestate, despite having modest nutrients compared to the second, overall
rendered reasonable amount of nutrients for use in the fertigation trials also when undiluted.
Digestate 2: MWW/BWW
Conversely, brewery waste water is typically characterized by
nitrogen deficiency and high carbon to nitrogen ratios (∼50-53 vs. ∼3-5 for traditional MWW,
Li, Xiao et al. 2025). A lower ratio was seen in the permeate, attributed to half the feedstock
being MWW. The parameters being roughly twice that of the first period otherwise coincides well
with the loading rates. The significantly higher VSS and TC can be attributed to organic carbon
in spent grains, constituting a large part of brewery waste (Jagiello et al. 2022), which constituted
half the feedstock in digestate 2 compared to 10 % for the FW in digestate 1.
Studies on co-digestion with brewery waste water and MWW were not found, as most studies
focused on either treatment on BWW alone, or BWW added to sludge digesters rather than the
main liquid line. Chen et al. (2016), treating pure brewery waste in an AnMBR saw permeate
COD ∼171 mg/L, TN ≃80–90 mg/L, NH+
4 ≃8 mg/L and PO3−
4
≃55 mg/L. Most organic N
was converted in the digester, but already soluble P and N managed to pass the membrane. Seeing
as the second digestate is 50% BWW, these findings match well for the COD and N, but reported
higher phosphate levels. A study treating brewery waste water with FO-AnMBR reported ≃40
mg/L ammonium, COD at ∼5 mg/L and a pH of 5.5 (Schneider et al. 2021) in the brewery effluent
to be treated, but post-treatment values were not reported, as methane yield and flux control was
the main objective of the study. The same conclusion can be drawn as for the first digestate, that
literature on co-digestion is sparse, and values must be estimated from reactors treating single
streams. Also, it is important to note that nutrients were not in focus in any of these studies,
but rather biogas yield. In studies where the fertilizer potential of brewery digestate was the sole
objective of the study, such as Taylor et al. 2018, nutrient values were notably lower (∼19 g TN,
∼2 g TP) or additional nitrogen was added to the digester to increase nutrient values (Riera-Vila
et al. 2019).
None of the studies examined saw BWW counter the electrical conductivity of the MWW+BWW
permeate in this study. Measured at ∼21–22 mS/cm, it sits far above the 0.5 - 3 mS/cm usually
reported for brewery waste waters (Espinoza-Qui˜nones et al. 2021; Wang et al. 2016) and the
1-10 mS/cm band typical of ordinary municipal or industrial effluents (Apure Instrument 2025).
Ion concentrations proved no explanation to this, as sulphate was measured similar to the first
digestate, and the concentration of Na and Mg (6) is not enough to drive the EC up two two-digit
levels. The explanation was hypothesized to be CIP chemicals such as NaOH, KOH, H3PO4 and
HNO3 which might be discharged at several hundred litres in a brewery cleaning cycle, as caustic
or acid to drain (Renfrow 2021). A 2% NaOH solution is already 80 mS/cm, so an unintended spill
makes the numbers plausible. Due to salinity, nitrogen levels were initially determined to be very
low in the second effluent, as inhibitory effects by salts on the test kits hindered the measurements

Legislations on agricultural reuse of WW
Agricultural reuse of waste water is regulated in
Denmark in the same manner as the EU regulations, providing limits to microbiological substances
such as E. coli as well as BOD and TSS/turbidity, but no limits on salinity, Na, NH4 −N, P
or heavy metals (U.S. Environmental Protection Agency 2023). The overall classification on the
Symsites reactor effluent was B in this regard, considered safe for application on edible crops not
in touch with the soil. The same is stated in the danish emission guidelines (Danish Environmental
Protection Agency 2000), but permission must be applied for in each project. General guidelines
on fertigation water for vegetables sets the EC requirement at 2.25 and all above 3 unsafe for most
crops (Ayers et al. 1985).
Autoclaving effect on digestate chemistry
Digestate samples 2.1 and 2.2 were taken from
the same MWW + BWW AnMBR batch, but 2.2 was autoclave-sterilized at 121 °before analysis,
seemingly having a noticeable impact on the sample chemistry. The most obvious change was
pH, as the autoclaved sample’s pH rose to 9.2 (from
7.2 in the non-autoclaved), likely due to
CO2 being expelled during heating, eliminating carbonic acid and thus raising pH. A similar rise
in pH was found after autoclaving neutral digestate at the same temperature in Cheong et al.
2020. Autoclaving also affected solids and carbon measurements, as digestate 2.2 showed nearly a
doubling of total suspended solids (115 to 220 mg TSS /L). This suggests that upon sterilization,
some dissolved or colloidal constituents might have precipitated or aggregated into measurable
solids. The higher inorganic carbon (from 141 to 173 mg IC/L) could hint at precipitation of
carbonates (e.g. CaCO3) when CO2 was driven off at high pH. These newly formed particulates
would increase TSS but not VSS (since CaCO3 is inorganic). While COD remained similar, it
usually rises during treatment at high temperature due to lysis of cell structures (Cheong et al.
2020). The fact that such a rise was not seen, could simply be due to the test kit chemistry not
registering such a change. These changes underscore that while originally identical, autoclaving
chemically alters chemical composition which is important to account for as they are not reflective
of the actual reactor condition, but rather a post-treatment effect.

High canopy development supported the transpiration data.

second experiment, where trees in NSD landed at a final NRT of 168% compared to trees in NS,
while also displaying the highest WUE among the dilutions. The change in solution chemistry
reflected the plant uptake, as ammonium NH+

NS (24.4 mg/L) but a salinity at the limit for willow trees (6.77 mS/cm). Determined as a salinity
test to see if the willows would benefit from the higher nitrogen despite the salt content, the lower
relative transpiration indicated the opposite. Hangs et al. (2011), examining the salt tolerance of
Salix bioenergy species found S. Viminalis to have a relatively greater salinity tolerance among
37 examined species, only surpassed by India & Tully Champion in an hydroponic solution with
EC equal to 8.0 mS/cm. This was attributed to the species accumulating ABA when salt stressed,
increasing water-use efficiency by supporting plant morphological and physiological responses (Ran
et al. 2022). Elevated RSR and high WUE (10 b. & c.) supports this, suggesting the plants relied
on extensive rooting to compensate for possible salt and ammonium stress. Additionally, purely
supplying ammonium for nitrogen has been shown to stimulate the root-elongating hormone auxin
(Li, Rennenberg et al. 2011). Toxicity indications were however seen, as chlorosis (yellowing) of
the leaves and suppressed canopy growth (see picture 11, second to right). The dilution showing
significant acidification as well as the biggest decrease in solution EC in the second experiment
(table 11) however suggests ammonium synthesis and cation uptake. The samples ending at a
similar relative transpiration to controls by the end of the experiment, supports statements of
S-Viminalis as a salt tolerant species. The DD of the first digestate demonstrated potential as a
sole nutrient provider, but the second digestate would seemingly require further dilution to limit
toxicity responses. Instead of increasing due to the common upconcentration, EC declined by half
to one unit in the DD as well as the NSD, both presenting high initial ion loads. This can have
lead EC to drop as roots consumed salt, while the AT remained low (8).

Takeaway
In short, the depth of the early NRT depressions scaled with the initial ammonium
loads and osmotic jumps, whereas the long term performances seemed mainly dependent on how
much assimilable N (NH3+NO3) the solutions delivered. The NSD mixtures, offering NH+
4 /NO−
3
ratios below 1 and moderate salinity, delivered the most sustained transpiration, and by implica-
tion, growth potential. Nitrate-ammonium fertilization has on several occasions been confirmed to
prove the most balanced nitrogen uptake, as the combination of both ions act synergistically for
nitrogen uptake (Rewald et al. 2014; Kawai et al. 2022). Plants synthesize nitrate (NO3) by step-
wise reduction into ammonium, requiring significant amounts of energy supplied by photosynthetic
products. Conversely, ammonium, can be directly synthesized into amino acids, and conserves
metabolic energy by bypassing the the nitrate reduction steps (Muratore et al. 2021; Yoneyama
et al. 2020). Growth promotion triggered by an appropriate NH4/NO3 ratios has been coupled
to accumulation of more carbon and nitrogen, a higher leaf area and photosynthetic rate, higher
chlorophyll concentration, increased auxin synthesis, improved absorption of nutrients, improved
root growth and a lower root/shoot ratio (Hao et al. 2023). Trials exploring ammonium/nitrate
ratios report 10% additional biomass growth in willows, and over 100% additional gains on centi-
pede grass as well as increased nitrogen-use efficiencies at NO3 : NH+
4 ratios near 1 compared to
either ion alone (Rewald et al. 2014; Hao et al. 2023).
Explicit studies on using co-digested MWW with FW or BWW with MWW as fertilizer are scarce,
however, ammonium-rich digestate — particularly anaerobically digested food waste, has been
investigated on several occasions. Heinsoo et al. (2019) reported that moderate applications of
permeate from biogas production outperformed traditional pig slurry on willow fields and matched
the value of mineral fertilizers.
Similarly, Holm et al. (2014) observed that Salix plantations
treated with AD produced more than twice the biomass of controls supplied with water or mineral
fertilizer, attributing the increase to greater nutrient availability in the digestate. In leafy-vegetable
production, NPK fertilizer diluted with 40 % food-waste AD increased xiao bai cai (Brassica rapa)
yield by approximately 8 % compared with NPK alone (Cheong et al. 2020). Using liquid digestate
as the sole nutrient source, Ntinas et al. (2021) recorded higher mineral uptake and a 10 % rise
in chlorophyll content in hydroponically grown lettuce. Likewise, digestate diluted to 20–40 %
28

performed on par with NPK in trials with four leafy vegetables (Song et al. 2021). Examining
brewery waste, Riera-Vila et al. (2019) found mustard greens grown with digested brewery waste
yielded similarly to those receiving commercial fertilizer, while Taylor et al. (2018) noted that
cabbage irrigated with brewery-waste AD grew significantly larger than with wetland effluent,
albeit 13 % smaller than plants given a standard nutrient solution and fresh water. Taken together,
these studies indicate that, when nutrient balance and salinity are managed, fertilizers diluted with
ammonium-rich digestates can equal or exceed the performance of conventional fertilizers.

The predicted distribution of each pharmaceutical in pepper plants varied by compound chemistry.
Acetaminophen (paracetamol), a relatively polar, hydrophilic compound, was predicted to trans-
locate with the transpiration stream, reaching shoots and possibly fruits to a moderate extent.
The extended models higher fruit prediction of acetaminophen can be attributed to the models
continuous water-translocation to fruits from the leaves via phloem. This increases the fruit con-
centrations by water gain, while the leaves lose concentration through growth dilution (0.1/d for
leaves vs. 0.7/d for peppers). However, acetaminophen is reported to rapidly metabolize both in
planta (e.g. conjugation to glutathione in cucumber) (Zheng et al. 2021), and in soil, rendering
large fractions of the molecule as degradation products unavailable for uptake (Li, Ye et al. 2014).
Degradation rates were not included in the model due to unavailability in literature, hence uptake
rate of a degradable compound like acetaminophen might be over-predicted.
1H-benzotriazole and its 5-methyl derivative (5-MeBTA) are small, weakly acidic molecules with
moderate soil sorption abilities (log KOC 2.1 & 2.6), leading the model to accordingly project
root uptake and xylem transport to shoots. Studies on peppers were not found, but a study on
uptake of benzothiazoles in carrots found six examined BTZs to be readily taken up by roots, but
exhibiting poor translocation into shoots (Wu, Lai et al. 2024). The models however predicted
high fruit concentrations relative to roots and leaves for both thiazoles, attributed to the molecules
carrying larger ionic fractions in the alkaline phloem sap (8.0) than at root pH (6.5), leading to
ion trapping and subsequent translocation into fruits.
35
==PAGE:43==
For the two basic compounds found in the effluent, namely metoprolol (beta-blocker, pKa ∼
9.5) and venlafaxine (antidepressant, pKa ∼8.4), the standard model as expected over-predicted
shoot/fruit concentrations by treating them as essentially neutral organics (based on moderate
log KOW values). In reality, at neutral pH these bases are almost entirely in their cationic form,
strongly binding to negatively charged sites in soils and root tissues. The PPCP model incorporates
this by simulating ion trapping and sorption to plant components. As a result, the extended model
predicted higher root concentrations, and a slightly lower fruit and leaf concentration for both
compounds. Empirical data support this: in a study on root vegetables fertigated with waste
water, metoprolol was detected in carrot and sweet potato leaves, but not in the edible roots
and tubers (Malchi et al. 2014), indicating root retention and minimal transfer to harvestable
parts.
Venlafaxine, having the lowest inital soil concentration showed negligible uptake in all
compartments, however, increasing the soil concentration in the model displayed notably higher
accumulation in roots compared to shoots in the PPCP model vs the standard model. The standard
model misleadingly predicts more venlafaxine in fruits due to its higher log KOW ∼3. Venlafaxine
has mostly been studied in aquatic environments due to its toxicity effect on benthic vertebrates,
where it is shown to accumulate in fatty tissues like liver and brain (Maulvault et al. 2018), in line
with a slightly higher log KOW (∼3.28) and moderate sorption to protein coefficient. The extended
model as such predicted its concentrations predominantly in compartment with waxy cuticles such
as leaves and stems. Despite lower translocation to fruits, venlafaxine has been reported to be
toxic to plant growth, inhibiting growth and reproduction, and leading to mortality at µg doses
(Feito et al. 2013). The low initial soil concentration in this case however makes such predictions
infeasible.
HCTZ have been detected in reclaimed-water irrigation experiments, but mainly in leafy vegetables
rather than fruits. For instance, hydrochlorothiazide was found in 100 % of lettuce leaf samples (at
low ng/g levels) under long-term wastewater irrigation in Manasfi et al. (2021). The higher uptake
of HCTZ in leaves and fruits compared to roots is consistent with previous model predictions
on other polar compounds such as sulfolane and imidacloprid, also exhibiting very low logKOW s
(Trapp, Shi et al. 2023). Losartan, having a logKOW of 4.1 and high logKHSA due to its nature
as an antihypertensive, was the only chemical to show lower overall uptake in the extended model.
Sorbing well to organic carbon and as such staying predominantly in soil, its plant uptake can be
treated as negligible (Andrade et al. 2020).
With an exception for losartan, the PPCP model steadily predicted higher concentrations in roots
and lower ones in leaves than the standard approach, as sorption to proteins reduces upwards
translocation in xylem, and addition of phloem flow leads to transport downwards. Despite this,
leaves and fruits generally show the highest uptake, consistent with known trends of leafy vegetables
generally accumulating more PPCPs than root vegetables, also exceeding vegetables like peppers
and tomatoes (Christou et al. 2019).
Takeaway
In evaluating the model predictions against empirical data, the conclusion was that
pharmaceutical uptake into pepper fruits was extremely low for all compounds studied.
The
extended models improved predictions on the behavior of ionizable compounds made its predictions
more realistic, correctly anticipating cationic substances such as metoprolol and venlafaxine to a
larger degree to be confined to roots and less so in fruits. Polar compounds like acetaminophen
were somewhat more mobile, but as it is shown to degrade rapidly both in the soil and in the plant,
its predictions could de deemed exaggerated. Benzothiazoles also exhibiting degradation in water
and sunlight (Felis et al. 2016) further emphasizes the need for development of degradation rates
for more accurate model predictions.
Nevertheless, diluting the wastewater (1:5) ensured pollutant loads to be very low, diminishing any
serious accumulation in edible tissues for any compound. Uptake in plants is roughly proportional
to exposure concentration, so such low inputs yield extremely low residues in plant tissues. The
model-predicted pepper fruit concentrations would quite possibly be below analytical detection
limits or only in the picogram to low nanogram per gram range. Fruit contaminant levels are
as such likely beneath health concern thresholds by several orders of magnitude, given that even
much higher exposures produce negligible risk quotients in crop intake studies (Zheng et al. 2021).
Improved data on degradation rates, protein-sorption coefficients and measured soil concentrations

rather than estimates would have provided a more realistic uptake scenario. However, considering
the low effluent concentrations as well as its dilution, the predicted pepper fruit residues are likely
far below any known ecotoxicological effect thresholds and present no tangible risk to consumers
or the environment.

Shortcomings and limitations
A control treatment with tap water was not kept for the peppers. This would have made for
valuable insight with a nutrient-less baseline. The EC meter in the chemistry lab made for faulty
measurements which proved unuseful.
More holistic data on EC development in the soil and
percolate could have provided insights on the plant parameters. The ICP analysis machine was
broken for most of the thesis and results were not available before the end of the thesis period.
This did not allow time for analysis of micronutrients. Different nitrogen values in digestate 2 were
measured with test kits, skalar and ICP. The actual nitrogen content was uncertain, and trust was
put in the results from ICP analysis. The nutrient analysis of the treatments for the second willow
experiment arrived by skalar the day of the thesis delivery, and showed higher ammonia values
than the TN measured in the same effluent by ICP. Store bought plant soils are to the authors
knowledge exclusively pre-fertilized, meaning added nutrients will come on top of those already
provided by the soil. As such, adding dilutions could have provided nutrients in excess by the end
of the treatment, when the peppers were just landed in the new soil. The control pots were not
uniform in soil amounts, but provided similar evaporation values. Caution should be taken to keep
even controls for more accurate measurements.

Research and future outlook
Throughout the thesis work and literature examined, few studies were found on large-scale ex-
periments on waste water reuse from AnMBRs. Most studies were found at pilot-scales, treating
singular feed stocks and focusing on biogas yields rather than fertilizer potential. The results in
this thesis pointed to the fact that waste water reuse can be a valuable resource, able to remedi-
ate fertilizer need, provide circularity perspectives and diminish pollution. All digestates can be
detoxified by dilution, leaving large potential for digestate reuse. Lastly, a large gap was identified
when addressing chemical degradation rates, found to be severely lacking in literature (Arnot et al.
2012; Muir et al. 2006). Degradation rates are crucial to predict uptake of PPCPs more accurately,
and provide realistic risk assessments.

Conclusion
This study successfully demonstrated fertilization potential for both the examined digestates,
provided nutrient values and salinity levels were appropriately addressed.
NS mixed with the
digestates consistently outperformed the NS by itself across all fertigation trials, indicating proper
dilution as the only factor governing the fertigation potential. Both digestates were seen as valid
fertilizers when diluted with NS or DIW, both for the biomass crops S. Viminalis, and for the
vegetable C. Annuum. The modeled chemical uptake of OMPs in the peppers was found insigni-
ficant due to low initial soil concentrations, but demonstrated how uptake models can be used in
site-specific occasions for conducting risk assessments.
