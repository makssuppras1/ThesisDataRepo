Abstract
In this thesis a recently proposed design concept for keyed shear joints is investigated. The new
joint design has the promise of being more construction-friendly than the current best practice.
The new design involves designing the keyed shear joint as a symmetric indented joint in which
the reinforcement is laid out as overlapping loops in the vertical plane of joint as opposed to best
practice where the loops are overlapping in the horizontal plane. The strength and ductility of
the new conceptual joint design are investigated through a series of experiments, and theoretical
models are developed for strength prediction. Push-off tests of keyed shear joints with varying key
depths were carried out to investigate the inﬂuence of key depth on failure mode. Digital image
correlation was used to identify the key failure modes and to determine at which load point in the
load-deformation curve key failure occurred. In order to investigate the strength of the keyed shear
joint in the transverse direction, a tensile test of the speciﬁc U-bar loop design was performed.
Upper bound models were developed to predict the pure tensile capacity of the U-bar loop
connection. These models perform in a satisfactory manner for the loop connection, however the
same models underestimate the transverse capacity of keyed shear joints due to simpliﬁcations in
the model. Therefore it is concluded that if the loop connection is designed to fail according to
yielding of the U-bars and not by concrete core failure, the transverse capacity of the keyed shear
joint is also governed by yielding of the U-bars.
In the push-off tests, two different failure modes were observed in the keyed areas. In joints with
small key depths there were crushing and shearing of the key corners, while joints with larger key
depths experienced complete key cut off. Diagonal lines of fracture developed across the connection
at time of failure, most likely due to the boundary conditions of test specimens. Rigid-plastic upper
bound solutions for the shear strength of keyed joint are found to agree well with the tested shear
capacities and these models successfully capture the inﬂuence of key depth on the failure mode.
The shear joints displayed a good ductile behaviour.

Introduction
1.1
Background
This thesis focuses on keyed shear joints between precast concrete wall elements. On-site cast
joints between elements are often the weakest link in precast concrete element buildings, and
therefore often governs the strength of the joint and the design of the structural system. Current
best practice is keyed shear joints with overlapping U-bars which are inserted with a locking bar
running the entire length of the joint as shown in Figure 1.1. The joint is grouted with mortar. The
current practice is challenged by narrow constructions zones during the installation process which
can lead to inadequate structural continuity across the narrow zones. Wall elements often have to
be installed vertically which means that the U-bars must be bent 90 degrees up or down to enable
this operation. After the element is installed, these U-bars are straightened to form overlapping
loops again. This process is time consuming and requires small U-bar diameters for the workers to
be able to straighten the U-bars. Since the diameters are small, the shear strength is often governed
by the yielding of U-bars and consequently the U-bar connection can be regarded as a ductile
connection. In summary, the reinforcement layout of the current best practice prevents optimal
and fast installation of the precast concrete elements. For these reasons, a new design concept is
proposed.
1.2
Objectives
In this thesis a recently proposed design concept for keyed shear joints between precast concrete
wall elements is investigated. The proposed concept is shown in Figure 1.2. The aim of this new
design concept is to establish a conceptual joint conﬁguration which is construction friendly, highly
ductile and can withstand large shear forces which can occur in e.g. high rise structures. The new
joint design may replace the conventional in situ cast approach, which currently is preferred due to

The new concept is to design the keyed joint as a symmetric indented joint in which the reinforcement

Chapter 2
Theory of plasticity
In this chapter some insight into the theory of rigid-plasticity will be given. The different concepts
will be described in brief, however a more elaborate depiction can be found in Nielsen and Hoang
(2011). Plastic theory is a branch of continuum mechanics which was developed in order to deal
with the determination of the collapse load and collapse mechanism. Plastic analysis is consequently
only used for ultimate limit state (ULS) calculations.
This chapter also presents available strength prediction methods for keyed shear joints.
2.1
Theory of rigid-plasticity
A rigid-plastic material is deﬁned as a material in which there are no deformations for stresses
up to the yield point. Once the yield point is reached, arbitrarily large deformations are possible
without any change in stresses (Nielsen and Hoang, 2011). Such a material does not exist in reality,
however the model is applicable for use when the plastic strains are much greater than the elastic
strains.
2.1.1
Yield condition
The yield condition for a rigid-plastic material may be expressed as
f(σx,σy,σz,τxy,τxz,τyz) = 0
(2.1)
where (σx,σy,...,τyz) is an arbitrary stress ﬁeld in the material. For stresses satisfying Eq. (2.1), the
material can experience unlimited plastic strains without any change in stresses. These deformations
are assumed to be plastic for a rigid-plastic material. If the yield function f < 0 it means that the
material can endure the stress ﬁeld without deforming and thus the strains will be zero. Stresses

2.1.3
Extremum principles for rigid-plastic materials
In order to determine the collapse load of rigid-plastic bodies the extremum principles can be
very helpful. The ﬁrst formulation of these principles were given by Gvozdev (1936) and later
independently in the Western world by Drucker et al. (1952). The formulation of the extremum
principles are presented in the following:
The Lower Bound Theorem:
The load determined from a statically admissible and safe stress ﬁeld will be smaller or equal to
the actual collapse load of the structure. This means that if the stress ﬁeld satisﬁes f ≤0, the
equilibrium condition and the statical boundary conditions for the actual load, this load will not be
the actual collapse load. There can be several stress ﬁelds that satisfy these conditions, however the
largest lower bound load will be closest to the load-carrying capacity of the structure.
The Upper Bound Theorem:
The load determined from using the work equation on geometrically possible collapse mechanism
will be greater or equal to the actual collapse load of the structure. The strains corresponding to the
collapse mechanism must conform with the normality condition. The lowest load found from the
consideration of several collapse mechanisms will be closest to the load-carrying capacity of the
structure.
The Uniqueness Theorem:
There will only be one load that satisﬁes all conditions from the lower bound and upper bound
theorem. Thus if the two procedures give equal load, then this result will be the exact load-carrying
capacity of the structure according to plastic theory.

2.4
Models for strength prediction of keyed shear joints
In this section three different methods of predicting the strength of keyed shear joints are presented.
Two of the methods are based on the theory of plasticity. Upper bound methods are based on the
assumption that failure occurs along lines in which energy is dissipated. A lower bound solution
based on a strut-tie method was developed by Christoffersen (1997). In addition there is a method
included in Eurocode 2 (2005) based on the theory of shear-friction which can be used to calculate
the shear capacity of an indented/keyed joint.
2.4.1

these lines of discontinuity, which are called yield lines, there is a dissipation of plastic energy. It is
an upper bound approach since the collapse load is found from a geometrically possible collapse
mechanism. The energy dissipated can be found using dissipation formulas. These formulas are

Chapter 3
Experimental investigations
In this chapter, the experimental investigations are presented. Two different large scale tests were
performed; a push-off test to investigate the shear capacity of keyed shear joints and a tension
test to determine the tensile capacity of U-bar loop connections. Specimen geometry and material
parameters are presented and summarised, and thereafter the experimental setup is described for
both tests. Digital image correlation (DIC) was used to identify the failure modes and this system
is described in short. Finally the experimental results are presented and discussed.
3.1
Specimen geometry and material parameters
3.1.1
Push-off test - Keyed shear joint
The general layout of the push-off test specimen is shown in Figure 3.1 in plan and section. The
specimen was a keyed shear joint in which two L-shaped precast elements were joined together
with U-bar loop connections. A lacer bar was placed inside each loop and the joint was grouted
with mortar. In addition a locking bar was placed in the longitudinal direction with locking plates
at both ends. Each joint consisted of three shear keys (nk = 3) on each precast element and four
two-on-two U-bar loop connections. The test series were divided into ﬁve pairs in which each pair
had respectively a key depth of dk = 10, 16, 20, 25 and 28 mm. This was the only geometrical
parameter that varied for the test series. All geometrical and material parameters of the specimens
are summarised in Table 3.1. The keys extended all the way to the surface to enable failure
identiﬁcation by use of digital image correlation.

Test setups
Two different test setups were necessary to test the relevant specimens in shear and tension loading:
1) Push-off test to investigate the shear capacity of keyed shear joints
2) Pure tension test to investigate the tensile capacity of a two-on-two U-bar loop connection
3.2.1
Push-off test
The test setup for the push-off test is illustrated in Figure 3.3 and 3.4. The specimen was placed
horizontally within a steel frame. A 1.2 MN Instron compression machine is installed at one of
the ends. The load was applied through a piston moving with a constant displacement rate of 2.5
mm/min. It is assumed that the steel frame was inﬁnitely stiff.
Figure 3.3: Test setup for push-off test
The specimens were placed in the centre of the frame and level to the ground to avoid any eccentric
loading. As shown in Figure 3.4(b), pallets were used to raise the element to its vertical position.
Smooth wooden boards were placed on top of the pallets at both sides of the connection to limit
surface friction and to allow the joint to displace vertically. 12 mm Celotex plates were used at the
piston and support end to evenly distribute the cylindrical pressure to the element. A square steel
block was used as a spacer at the support end as the test specimen was too short for the steel frame.
Figure 3.4(a) shows the cantilevered steel beam to which artiﬁcial lightning and a high deﬁnition
camera were attached. The camera was used for digital image correlation which is explained
in greater detail in Subsection 3.2.3. The connection was painted in a black and white speckle
pattern as seen on Figure 3.4(c). Strain gauges were installed on the lacer and locking bars
in six test specimens. Displacement sensors were installed at both ends of the connection to
record the relative transverse and longitudinal displacement. The position of the strain gauges and
displacement sensors can be seen in Figure 3.14.

3.2.3
Digital Image Correlation
Digital image correlation (DIC) systems can be used to accurately determine strains and displacements
on deforming structures. Compared to other measurement techniques such as extensometers and
strain gauges, DIC systems are capable of measuring non-uniform full-ﬁeld deformations without
requiring any contact with the specimen. In this thesis a DIC photogrammetry software called
ARAMIS was used. ARAMIS can be used to track deformations on the test specimen surface by
identifying features on a set of images. The software breaks the image up into subsets called facets
and by locating the positions of these facets in each image, can determine the displacement.
ARAMIS was used on all ten push-off test specimens. The images were acquired by a high-deﬁnition
==PAGE:48==
26
CHAPTER 3. EXPERIMENTAL INVESTIGATIONS
digital camera and thereafter analysed in ARAMIS. ARAMIS was used identify the failure modes
of the keys and to correlate the time of failure with a speciﬁc load point on the load-displacement
curve. Images were taken of the tension test specimens, however the cracks on the surface did
not represent the failure modes in the concrete core. Therefore ARAMIS was not used on these
specimens.
The test procedure using the ARAMIS system is explained in the following:
• Preparation of test specimen surface:
– In order to get suitable photographs a stochastic speckle pattern was painted on the
connection surface. This is done so ARAMIS can track each unique facet within the
image.
– The surface was ﬁrst sprayed with an uniform white layer which was subsequently
covered with random black dots.
– These dots must be adequately small such that there are several dots inside the perimeter
of each facet of the computational mask applied by ARAMIS. The droplet should be
between 1/3-1/2 the size of a facet. The default facet size of 15x15 pixels was used.
• Camera setup:
– The images were captured by a Nikon D800 with a 24 mm lens which resulted in a
resolution of 7360×4912. The camera light sensitivity was set to ISO 100.
– The camera was mounted on a cantilever steel beam above the element. Vibrations
due to cracking in the specimen might have caused the steel beam and thus camera to
move. This could have had an adverse effect on the results. The distance between the
connection surface and the camera was chosen such that the connection ﬁlled up most
of the camera’s ﬁeld of view. This yielded the highest possible image resolution. The
camera was oriented such that the connection was horizontal in the image frame.
– The camera was mounted so the lens was parallel to the test specimen surface. This
was necessary to avoid measurement errors in the post-processing stage.
– Artiﬁcial lighting was used to avoid shadows and thus increase the quality of data.
• Testing and data acquisition:
– In order to calibrate the software in the data analysis stage an image was taken of a tape
measurer placed in the joint. This allowed for the actual distance to be correlated with
the digital measurements.
– A signal sent from the load cell to a display placed in the image frame provided load
information for each stage.
– During the test, the time lapse rate was set to 15 images (stages) per minute (4 seconds
interval). After some time the rate was increased to 6 images per minute in order to
limit the size of the data. Each test yielded around 250-300 load stages.

• Data analysis:
– The image series was imported into ARAMIS which processes the data to compute
strain and displacement for each stage. For more details about the post-processing
stage see ARAMIS User Manual (ARAMIS, 2005).

The relative displacement between the precast elements both parallel and transverse to the load

ARAMIS was also used to validate the measurements from the displacement sensors. In general
the relative displacements determined by ARAMIS were less than that of the displacement sensors.

are affected by bending deﬂections in the precast elements due to eccentricity. ARAMIS is
insensitive to such deﬂections since it only measures the relative displacement in the joint. Another
advantage of ARAMIS is that the relative displacement can be measured at virtually any point on
the connection surface.

3.4
Analysis of experimental investigations
In this section the test results are analysed for the inﬂuence of some design variables. In the test
program, the keyed shear joints had a varying key depth and one of the objectives of this thesis is
to investigate the importance of this design parameter in relation to the failure mode. The ductility
of the keyed joint is also analysed. Additionally, the inﬂuence of varying lacer bar diameter will be
analysed for the U-bar loop connection loaded in tension.
3.4.1
The inﬂuence of key depth on failure mode
A main objective of this thesis is to investigate the inﬂuence of key depth on the failure mode of the
keyed area. It was observed that joints with small key depth failed by crushing and shearing of the
key corners, while complete key cut off was the governing key failure mode for joint with large
key depth. This is consistent with the experiences of Hansen et al. (1974). Corner crushing was
only observed in the two specimens with key depth dk = 10 mm and in specimen G1 (dk = 16 mm).
This indicates that the transition between the two different key failure modes occurs for key depth
of approximately 16 mm for the tested keyed shear joints.
The tested cracking load, Pu,test, is shown in Figure 3.19 as a function of key depth. As seen in the
ﬁgure the cracking load is lower for joints with the smallest key depth (10 mm) than for joints with
larger key depth. For specimens G-J, the cracking load is insensitive to increasing key depth.

Chapter 4
Upper bound plasticity models
In this chapter, rigid-plastic models for predicting the shear capacity of keyed joints and tension
capacity of U-bar loop connections are presented.
4.1
Shear failure
The experimentally observed failure modes are used as inspiration for the development of upper
bound plasticity models for strength prediction of the keyed shear elements. The theory behind the
upper bound models is described brieﬂy in Section 2.4.1.
Mainly two types of mechanism will be evaluated based on the test results. The ﬁrst mechanism
is referred to as "mechanism with complete key cut off". Different variations of this mechanism
will also be presented. The most elementary variation is a mechanism without diagonal yield lines
which only involves vertical yield lines over the keys. The other variations are mechanisms which
involve one or more diagonal yield lines that divide the connection into segments which undergo
rigid-body displacements. These are referred to as mechanisms with diagonal yield lines. The other
main mechanism is a "mechanism with inclined key cut off" which was observed in shear keys
with small key depths. This mechanism also has the same variations as the ﬁrst main mechanism.
The developed plastic models are upper bound models, thus the lowest upper bound solution will
be closest to the actual collapse load of the structure.
The main assumptions for the plasticity models are listed below:
• Concrete is treated as Modiﬁed Coulomb material
• Tensile strength of concrete is neglected, ft = 0
• Problem is treated as plane strain problem, i.e. α ≥ϕ.

4.2
Loop connection subjected to pure tension
A rigid-plastic upper bound model has been developed for the speciﬁc two-on-two U-bar loop
connection which was tested in tension. A basic model for concrete core failure is developed along
with an additional model for the case of small or no transverse reinforcement in the joint.
4.2.1
Basic model for concrete core failure
The model for concrete core failure is inﬂuenced by observed failure modes in tests and moreover
it is also strongly inﬂuenced by the basic model developed by Jørgensen and Hoang (2013).
The main assumptions for the basic model are as follows:
• Concrete is treated as Modiﬁed Coulomb material
• Tensile strength in concrete is neglected, ft = 0
• Dowel action is neglected
• Yielding of U-bars are not governing failure
• Surface between joint mortar and precast elements is considered perfectly smooth
The failure mechanism is shown in Figure 4.12. This mechanism is a simpliﬁcation since the failure
of the concrete core in a loop connection involves a three-dimensional failure surfaces (Jørgensen
and Hoang, 2013). In the simpliﬁed mechanism there are plane diagonal yield lines which stretch
between the looped ends of adjacent U-bars. Two inclined yield lines run from the looped ends of
the outer U-bars to either the vertical edge or the lower precast element surface. These yield lines
can have an inclination with the n-axis between 0 and π/2. Since it is possible, the inclination is
chosen such that u becomes normal to the yield line, i.e α = 90◦, and there is no energy dissipation.
Therefore these two yield lines are in fact separation yield lines without energy dissipation. Lastly,
there are two yield lines which run parallel to the n-axis from the outer U-bars to the upper precast
element surface.
The yield lines divide the joint into three rigid parts (I, II and III). The lower precast element
and part I are at rest, while the upper precast element along with part III have an upwards motion
described by the vector uelement. The remaining rigid segment II is undergoing a rigid body
displacement described by the vector u. The vectors are:
uelement =(un,0)

the U-bars are not at yield. Therefore the upper precast element and segment II have the same

Chapter 5
Comparison with test results
In this chapter, experimental results are compared with the strength predictions from the upper
bound plasticity models developed in Chapter 4.
The friction angle of mortar is an important parameter in the plasticity model due to the assumption
of plane strain. An effectiveness factor must also be introduced for the problem of keyed shear
joints and U-bar loop connections, where a modiﬁcation is necessary to account for use of mortar
in the joints.
5.1
Friction angle of mortar
It is assumed that the friction angle of mortar lies in between the friction angle of structural concrete
and cement paste as mentioned in Section 2.2. Hansen et al. (2004) adopts a value of ϕ = 30◦as
the friction angle of masonry mortar, which is the value used for the calculations in this thesis. It is
important to determine the friction angle as accurate as possible as it inﬂuences the failure mode
signiﬁcantly due to the assumption of plane strain in the joint.
Relative displacements measured in ARAMIS can be used to estimate the friction angle. The
assumption of plane strain implies that the angle α between the failure surface and the displacement
vector must be greater or equal to the friction angle. If this assumption is adhered to, the
displacement angle determined in ARAMIS might provide an estimate of the friction angle.
The displacement angle may be found from the inclination of the relative displacement in the
longitudinal (x-dir) versus the transverse direction (y-dir) for the push-off test specimens. The
inclination of the curve will give the angle between the longitudinal axis and the relative movement
since α = arctan(y/x). An example is shown in Figure 5.1(a) for the relative displacement between
two segments measured over a diagonal crack. The inclination is found for two separate phases in

In phase 1 cracks are propagating up to the point where there is fracture in the joint. The inclination

Therefore a more exact estimate of the friction angle can be determined from phase 2 since this
phase should capture the actual fracture as idealised in Chapter 4. However, since ARAMIS was
only provided with data points every 4 seconds it is uncertain if the point of fracture was captured.
This means the inclination is only an average of the development between the data points. α is
found to vary between 10◦and 30◦for phase 2.
According to the plastic models, an inclined key cut off mechanism should lead to a greater
displacement angle than a complete key cut off mechanism since the angle between u and the
l-axis is (α + γ) when there is corner crushing in the keys. However, similar angles were found
for all specimens. It is possible that the slanted fracture causes a conﬁnement effect in the key.
As reported by Nielsen and Hoang (2011), the friction angle of a highly conﬁned mortar may be
much smaller than that of concrete which may explain why the measured angle is not greater. This
potential conﬁnement effect has not been included in the theoretical models.
Several considerations must be taken into account when comparing the determined displacement
angles to the actual friction angle of mortar. It is not certain that the assumption that mortar behaves
as an ideal rigid-plastic material is entirely met. Furthermore, if the assumption of plane strain is
not met, the problem may be treated as a plane stress problem in which the restriction α ≥ϕ does
not need to be adhered to.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the load-carrying capacity of a newly proposed design
for keyed shear joints between precast concrete wall elements which has the promise of being
construction friendly, highly ductile and can withstand large shear forces. The capacity and ductility
were investigated through a series of experiments in which only the key depth varied. Moreover,
the inﬂuence of key depth on the failure mode in keyed area was examined. Push-off tests have
been carried out to determine the shear capacity of the keyed joints. Upper bound plasticity models
have been developed and compared with test results. Additionally, tests of the speciﬁc two-on-two
U-bar loop connection was performed to determine the tensile capacity of one loop. By correlating
the tensile capacity of one loop with the strength of the keyed shear joint in the transverse direction,
the ultimate load-carrying capacity of the keyed shear joint could be determined.
In all the tension tests concrete core fracture was observed. The results reveal that the tensile
strength of the connection increases when the amount of transverse reinforcement (lacer bars)
increases. Plastic models were developed for the calculation of the tensile strength of the loop
connection. With these models, the connection can be designed with a safety margin between the
capacity related to concrete core failure and the yield capacity of the U-bars. It has been shown
that the models predict the tensile capacity of the U-bar loop connection in a satisfactory manner.
Both the tested and theoretical tensile capacity of the two-on-two U-bar loop connection with a 16
mm lacer bar were governed by fracture of the joint mortar. However, the shear capacity of the
keyed shear joint appears to be related to the yield capacity of the U-bars. The theoretical tension
model may fail to predict the correct failure mode due to simpliﬁcations in the model related to the
activated concrete area. In reality the surrounding concrete and the longitudinal locking bar may
contribute to the transverse capacity of the keyed shear joint, while the basic model only takes into
account the concrete in the core conﬁned by the U-bar loops. Therefore, it may be concluded that
81
==PAGE:104==
82
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
if the U-bar loop connection is designed so the tensile capacity is governed by yielding of U-bars
and not by concrete core failure, it is likely that the transverse capacity of the keyed shear joint will
also be governed by the yield capacity of the U-bars.
ARAMIS was used to identify the failure mechanisms in the push-off tests and to correlate the
observed failure with a speciﬁc load point on the load-displacement curves. Two different failure
modes were observed in the keyed areas. In joints with small key depths there was crushing and
shearing of the key corners, while joints with larger key depths experienced complete key cut off.
In both failure modes, diagonal lines of fracture developed across the connection at failure due to
the boundary conditions of test specimens. A model for predicting the shear capacity has been
developed based on the observed failure modes. It was seen that the cracking load was insensitive
to a variation in key depth in specimens where complete key cut off was the critical mechanism.
It has been shown that shear joints were quite ductile since µ = 0.8-1.0, where µ is a measure of the
rigid-plastic ductility in the joint. Joints with the smallest key depth had a load-deﬂection behaviour
closest to that of a rigid-plastic material.
The upper bound model predicts the load-carrying capacity of the shear joints and the inﬂuence of
key depth on the failure mode in a satisfactory manner. The effectiveness factor used is determined
from an existing formula modiﬁed to ﬁt the problem of shear keyed joints grouted with mortar. In
calculations a friction angle of 30◦has been used for the joint mortar.
6.1
Future work
Available research on the proposed design for keyed shear joints is limited and in the future more
extensive experimental studies could be performed. It would be valuable to investigate the inﬂuence
of other design parameters such as key length, height of key and amount of transverse reinforcement
(U-bars).
In addition to varying more design parameters, it would also be of great interest to perform more
tests with smaller intervals key depths. The shift between the two key failure modes occurred for
relatively small key depths and a variation around this limit would be relevant to investigate further.
It was shown that the friction angle of mortar inﬂuences the failure mode signiﬁcantly. For that
reason a study in which greater care is taken to determine this value more accurately would be very
valuable.
To further explore the advantages of the newly proposed design for keyed shear joints compared to
current best practice, reference tests with best practice design could also be performed.

B.1.2
Concrete
The joint mortar was made from ordinary Portland cement with a maximum aggregate size of 4
mm. Both the precast element concrete and joint mortar for the keyed shear joint specimens were
made at Expan AS. A total of 8 cylinders were prepared for the precast element concrete and 24
cylinders for the joint mortar containing all three batches. The joint mortar for the tension test was
made by students at DTU and a total of 12 cylinders were prepared for the two batches made.
The cylinders were tested in two different machines. An Instron 8508 5 MN servo-hydraulic testing
machine was used to test cylinders JC32 to JC41. This machine is shown in Figure B.7(a). The
cylinders were tested with displacement control at a rate of 0.15 mm/min. Displacement sensors
were used to record the displacement of the piston. The load is outputted as a function of the
oil-pressure in the hydraulics so even without a cylinder in the machine, load is about 30 kN when
the piston is moving downwards due to friction. However, the friction in the oil pressure is assumed
to be negligible at the maximum load.
All of the other cylinders were tested in Toni 3000 kN compression machine as seen in Figure
B.7(b). For some of the cylinders a load-displacement relationship was obtained by performing a
displacement controlled test at 0.5 mm/min until the test is stopped manually. In some of the tests
only the compressive strength was obtained with a load-controlled test at 3.1 kN/s. For these tests
the machine stopped automatically when the decrease in load exceeded 7% of the maximum load.

In this section the results for each tension test specimen are presented in greater detail than in the

was prepared by bachelor students while the connections were cast by master students, the author
included. Pictures from the work process can be seen in Figure B.70.
