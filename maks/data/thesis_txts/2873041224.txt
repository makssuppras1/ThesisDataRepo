Abstract
Deep ensembles (DEs) are widely regarded as state­of­the­art for uncertainty quantifi­
cation in neural networks. Despite their strong empirical performance, they lack a solid
theoretical foundation. A common lens for studying DEs is Bayesian machine learning,
where DEs are interpreted as an approximate posterior represented by a mixture of Dirac
delta distributions, placing all probability mass on a finite set of parameter point estimates.
To improve this approximation, Bayesian methods such as the Laplace approximation and
SWAG learn Gaussian­based approximations to the posterior distribution. However, re­
cent findings suggest that when the ensemble size becomes sufficiently large, many of
these approximate methods underperform the simpler Dirac­delta approximation in terms
of test expected log predictive density (ELPD).
In this project, we explore an alternative strategy for enriching the approximate posterior.
Specifically, we initialize a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampler at each DE mode
to locally explore the posterior landscape. Our results show potential benefits: In some
cases, this approach yields a 30% improvement in test ELPD compared to standard DEs,
while in others the gains are marginal (<1%). However, across all cases, we observe that
the performance benefits consistently diminish as the ensemble size increases, though
they remain non­negative, suggesting that the MCMC­based approach does not harm
predictive performance even in large ensembles.
On CIFAR­10 with a WRN­16­4 model, the MCMC­based DE performed comparably to
the baseline, with no observed improvement or degradation. In experiments using last­
layer MCMC sampling on this model, the samplers exhibited restricted movement, with
posterior samples concentrated near the initial DE modes. While this limited exploration
may be due to the last­layer constraint, other factors such as insufficient hyperparameter
tuning may also play a role. Importantly, none of the MCMC chains in our experiments
showed signs of convergence, even in cases where performance improved. This sug­
gests challenges in sample quality and exploration remain, highlighting key limitations
and offering directions for future work.

Introduction
Artificial intelligence (AI) is increasingly being embedded into modern society. From writ­
ing assistants like ChatGPT and Copilot to smart systems in cars and healthcare devices,
AI has become deeply integrated into our daily lives. As a result, we are growing more
reliant on its capabilities and judgment. Yet, despite this growing dependence, our un­
derstanding of how trustworthy AI truly is remains limited and requires further exploration.
For example, imagine a neural network trained to predict house prices and suppose that
it estimates the value of a particular house to be $600,000. On the surface, this prediction
appears to be precise, but without any additional information, how much confidence can
we place in this estimate? Would you trust placing a $600,000 bet based on this single
number?
Unlike our house­price model, modern AI tools often provide confidence scores along­
side their predictions. However, a major problem is that these models can be extremely
confident in their outputs, even when those predictions are incorrect (Papamarkou et al.
2024). In other words, they often overestimate their certainty and fail to recognize when
they are likely to be wrong. This overconfidence poses serious risks, especially as AI sys­
tems are increasingly deployed in critical areas such as healthcare (Kompa et al. 2021;
Jiang et al. 2017), courtrooms (Brunette 2024), and autonomous driving (McAllister et al.
2017), where poor decisions can have severe consequences.
Thus, proper uncertainty quantification (UQ) is crucial for these models. One of the sim­
plest yet effective “tricks” for more robust UQ is to train multiple instances of the same
model. Due to the complexity of neural networks and the inherent randomness during
training, different instances will converge to slightly different solutions.
Returning to our house price example, if we predict the price using all these model in­
stances, we will obtain a variety of estimates. The spread of these predicted prices pro­
vides valuable information about the model’s confidence. For instance, if all instances pre­
dict prices tightly clustered between $600,000, we can be relatively confident in the esti­
mate. On the other hand, if the predictions range widely from $500,000 to $800,000, it sig­
nals considerable uncertainty. Formally, we call this method deep ensembles (DE) (Lak­
shminarayanan et al. 2017), as it involves training an ensemble of deep neural networks.
We will discuss DE in much greater detail in Section 2.2.6.
Despite its simplicity and strong empirical performance (Ashukha et al. 2021), DE has
relatively little theoretical justification. In contrast, Bayesian machine learning (BML)
offers a principled framework for modeling uncertainty by placing a probability distribution
over the model parameters. In mathematical terms, the goal of BML is to learn the poste­
rior distribution p(θ|D), over the model parameters θ given the dataset D. Thus, within
the BML framework, the posterior allows us to quantify uncertainty and summarize what
we know about the model and its parameters.
Now, the challenge for Deep Ensembles (DE) arises when we attempt to represent a
proper distribution over the multiple model instances, as the resulting distribution tends to
be very crude. This is illustrated in the left plot of Figure 1.1, which shows the posterior
density for different parameter values using three model instances.

Naturally, this has motivated numerous approaches to improve the approximate posterior
of DE. The most common approach is to fit a Gaussian distribution around the parameters
of each ensemble member, as illustrated in the right plot of Figure 1.1. With these Gaus­
sian approximations, the resulting approximate posterior becomes much richer, forming
a proper distribution. However, as the plot also reveals, the Gaussian approximation has
its limitations and can deviate noticeably from the true posterior. This is particularly clear
in the last peak of the true posterior, where the Gaussian approximation is considerably
wider. Furthermore, a recent study by Jordahn et al. (2025) finds that many of the ap­
proaches proposed to improve DE fail to surpass the baseline DE when the ensemble
size is sufficiently large.
Their main result is shown in Figure 1.2, where the performance of four approximate
Bayesian methods, LLLA (Kristiadi, Hein, et al. 2020), SWA (Izmailov, Podoprikhin, et al.
2019), SWAG (W. Maddox et al. 2019), and LA­NF (Kristiadi, Eschenhagen, et al. 2022),
is compared to that of a baseline Deep Ensemble (DE) with the same ensemble size K
across five different settings. The performance is measured as the expected log pre­
dictive density (ELPD) (Andrew Gelman et al. 2013) (see Section 3.2.1), reported as a
percentage change relative to the ELPD of the corresponding DE. Notably, scores below
0 indicate that the method performed worse than the vanilla DE of the same size. As
shown in the figure, the number of such negative results increases as K grows to 20.
These results motivate us to explore a different approach to improving DE. Rather than
directly refining the posterior approximation, we aim to enrich the approximate posterior
by incorporating samples from the true posterior itself.

Therefore, in this project, we will study DE and MCMC­augmented DE across a variety of
MCMC methods, datasets, and neural network architectures. We begin by examining the
effect of MCMC sampling applied to a single small network (i.e., K = 1) using relatively
small datasets. Next, we gradually increase complexity by exploring MCMC sampling on
larger DEs. Finally, we study the MCMC­augmented DE using one of the models and
datasets also used in Jordahn et al. (2025) to compare our method with other Bayesian
approaches, and investigate whether our method exhibits similar tendencies.
Thus, our project can be summarized with the following research questions:
1. How is the predictive performance of single neural networks and deep ensembles af­
fected by augmenting them with post­hoc MCMC sampling across different datasets?
2. Do MCMC­augmented deep ensembles exhibit the same behavior observed in other
Bayesian methods from Jordahn et al. (2025), and how does the absence of Gaus­
sian constraints influence this?
3. How does the choice of the specific MCMC algorithm impact the predictive perfor­
mance and convergence of the MCMC sampling process?
4. What performance metrics are used to evaluate deep ensembles, and how do their
values and behavior change across different settings?
The report is structured as follows: In the remainder of this chapter, we briefly review
related research relevant to the core areas of this project. In the following chapter (Chap­
ter 2), we introduce the fundamental theory behind Bayesian machine learning, including
deep ensembles, MCMC methods, and other approximate Bayesian approaches. Chap­
ter 3 presents our MCMC­augmented DE method, followed by an empirical evaluation in
Chapter 4 using a variety of datasets and models.
Chapter 4 is divided into three sections, one for each experiment. In the first experiment
(Section 4.1), we study the MCMC augmentation in a simple setting using a single neural
network model. In the second experiment (Section 4.2), we extend this analysis to deep
ensembles by increasing the ensemble size to K ∈{1, 2, 5, 10, 20}, while using the same
models and datasets. Finally, Section 4.3 evaluates the MCMC­augmented models on
the CIFAR­10 dataset (Krizhevsky 2009) using a Wide ResNet architecture (Zagoruyko
and Komodakis 2017), one of the model–dataset combinations also studied in Jordahn
et al. (2025).
Lastly, in Chapter 5, we summarize our findings, discuss limitations, and outline direc­
tions for future work.

1.1
Literature Review
This section provides a non­exhaustive review of recent research relevant to the core ar­
eas of this project.
Importance of Bayesian Machine Learning (BML): The recent paper Position: Bayesian
Deep Learning is Needed in the Age of Large­Scale AI by Papamarkou et al. (2024) advo­
cates for the importance of Bayesian Machine Learning (BML) / Bayesian Deep Learning
(BDL) in the current era of advanced AI systems. They provide a comprehensive dis­
cussion of why BML is important, the current challenges it faces, and potential future
directions and solutions. Another earlier paper by Wilson (2020), named The Case for
Bayesian Deep Learning, also argues for the importance of BML. It emphasizes that the
key property of BML is marginalization (Eq. (2.9)), which is especially important for large
models such as Bayesian Neural Networks (BNN), where the optimal parameter configu­
ration is not unique.
Deep Ensembles: Following the paper Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017) and the em­
pirical success of DE (Ashukha et al. 2021), many studies have explored their theoretical
foundations and connections to Bayesian machine learning. Among the earliest was Iz­
mailov, Vikram, et al. (2021), who proposed that DE approximates the predictive posterior.
This perspective was later reviewed by Hoffmann and Elster (2021) in their work Deep
Ensembles from a Bayesian Perspective. B. He et al. (2020) connect DE to Gaussian
processes (Rasmussen and Williams 2006), a classical model for Bayesian inference, in
the infinite­width limit, and Wild et al. (2023) link DE to Bayesian Variational Inference
using Wasserstein Gradient Flows. Other works by Garipov et al. (2018), Draxler et al.
(2019), and Benton et al. (2021) have studied the posterior loss landscape of BNN and
DE. Their primary finding was the existence of flat regions in the loss landscape where
the modes are connected. Furthermore, Garipov et al. (2018) and Benton et al. (2021)
suggest methods to utilize these regions for faster ensembling of networks.
Lastly, some studies have investigated methods to increase diversity among individual
members of the ensemble for better generalization (Fort et al. 2020). Some studies by
D’Angelo and Fortuin (2023) and D’Angelo and Fortuin (2023) based on Stein Variational
Gradient Descent (Liu and Wang 2019) incorporate a repulsive term during optimization
to encourage divergence among the weights of individual ensemble members.
Approximate Posterior of Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs): Many works have ex­
plored extending traditional neural networks to Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs). One
of the earliest approaches by MacKay (1992) proposes to fit a Gaussian distribution at
the maximum a posteriori (MAP)/maximum likelihood estimate (MLE) using the Laplace
Approximation (LA). Later work by Ritter et al. (2018) extends this to larger models by
approximating the Hessian of the posterior at the MLE/MAP using the Kronecker product.
Another work by Kristiadi, Hein, et al. (2020) introduces Last­Layer Laplace Approxima­
tion (LLLA) by using a subset of the parameters, such as the last layer of the networks,
to compute the LA. Differently, W. Maddox et al. (2019) propose to extend Stochastic
Weight Averaging (SWA) (Izmailov, Podoprikhin, et al. 2019) to derive a Gaussian ap­
proximate posterior (SWAG). These methods were then extended to DE by works such
as Eschenhagen et al. (2021) and Wilson and Izmailov (2022) with a Mixture of Laplace
Approximations and MultiSWAG, respectively.
Recently, Kristiadi, Eschenhagen, et al. (2022) proposed to refine the Gaussian approxi­

mate posterior using Normalizing Flows (NF) (Rezende and Mohamed 2016a) optimized
using Variational Inference. This was extended to DE by Jordahn et al. (2025) in their
study on local posterior structure in DE. Other works in Bayesian Variational Inference
(BVI) have explored subspace and sparse subspace methods for improved scalability (Li
et al. 2024; Izmailov, W. J. Maddox, et al. 2019) or function­space inference for more
intuitive BNN priors (Rudner et al. 2023; Sun et al. 2019).
Sampling Methods: As Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) and especially Hamilto­
nian Monte Carlo (HMC) (Brooks et al. 2011; Hoffman and Andrew Gelman 2011) are
often considered the ”gold standard” for Bayesian inference, many works have explored
methods to improve convergence speed and scalability to larger models. Welling and
Teh (2011) introduced Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), a combination of
Stochastic Gradient Descent (Robbins and Monro 1951) and Langevin Dynamics (Brooks
et al. 2011). Later T. Chen et al. (2014) proposed stochastic gradient HMC, scaling the
HMC by approximating the gradient using mini­batching. Other works suggest improving
convergence by subspace inference (Izmailov, W. J. Maddox, et al. 2019) or using control
variates (Baker et al. 2017). Lastly, R. Zhang et al. (2020) extended the SGHMC with an
amortized second­order Langevin algorithm (AMAGOLD). Notably, they show that their
method converges to the target distribution despite performing the Metropolis­Hastening
(MH) correction infrequently.
The theoretical properties of SGMCMC methods have been studied in works such as C.
Chen et al. (2016) and Ma et al. (2015), the latter of which presents a unified framework
for SGMCMC by parameterizing the dynamics with diffusion and curl matrices. Further­
more, non­asymptotic convergence bounds for the un­adjusted Langevin algorithm (ULA)
were studied in the works of Durmus and Moulines (2016) and Durmus and Moulines
(2018). Lastly, in the context of DE, Łatuszyński et al. (2025) discuss three fundamental
challenges for MCMC methods for multi­modal distributions. They argue that standard
MCMC methods have problems with finding the modes, switching modes, and efficiently
sampling from the modes.
Finally, Monte Carlo Dropout by Gal and Ghahramani (2016) is a popular non­Markovian
sampling approach for its simplicity. However, recent studies have shown cases where
the approximate posterior from this method violates properties of the true posterior (Foong
et al. 2020; Osband 2016) or cases where the approximate posterior assigns zero prob­
ability to the true posterior (Folgoc et al. 2021).

networks. For instance, as we will discuss later, posterior sampling algorithms can also
be configured to sample only a subset of the model’s parameters and keep the rest of
them fixed.

2.3
Markov Chain Monte Carlo
In the previous sections, we introduced several standard Bayesian methods for neural
networks. A common feature among most of them was that they explicitly learned an
approximate posterior, whether in the form of a Gaussian, as in LA and SWAG, or as a
mixture of Dirac delta distributions, as in DE. In this section, we introduce another class
Enriching deep ensembles with local structure

of Bayesian methods: Markov Chain Monte Carlo4 (MCMC). Rather than learning an
explicit approximation of the posterior, MCMC methods are constructed in such a way
that they allow us to sample directly from the true posterior itself.
The basic idea behind MCMC methods is to construct a chain of samples, also known as
states, θ0, θ1, . . . , which eventually converge to samples from a given target distribution
p∗(θ). In this project, we will assume that our chain is Markovian. Formally, this means
that the transition kernel from the current state θs to the next state θs+1 satisfies (Murphy
2023):
p(θs+1|θ0, . . . , θs) = p(θs+1|θs).
(2.71)
In other words, the generation of a new sample θs+1, depends only on the current state θs.
As we shall soon see, one of the key properties of MCMC methods is that we only need to
evaluate the target distribution up to a normalization constant to generate these samples.
For instance, when the target distribution is the posterior p∗(θ) = p(θ|D) ∝p(D|θ)p(θ),
we only need to evaluate the product p(D|θ)p(θ) instead of the intractable posterior itself.
The remainder of this section is organized as follows: We begin by introducing the Metropolis­
Hastings (MH) algorithm (Metropolis et al. 1953), one of the simplest MCMC methods,
and provide a brief proof to illustrate why it produces samples from the target distribu­
tion. We then explore several variants of MH, starting with the Random­Walk Metropolis
(RWM) algorithm (Metropolis et al. 1953). Next, we take a brief detour into Hamiltonian
mechanics, which motivates the Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) algorithm (Brooks et al.
2011). Finally, we examine simplified variants of HMC, including the Metropolis­Adjusted
Langevin Algorithm (MALA) (Roberts and Tweedie 1996), the Unadjusted Langevin Al­
gorithm (ULA) (Brooks et al. 2011), and Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD)
(Welling and Teh 2011).
In particular, RWM, MALA, ULA, SGLD, and a variant of SGLD using control variates
(SGLD­CV) will be especially important for the remainder of this project. These algo­
rithms form the core set of methods we will use to enrich the crude posterior of the DE,
as we will see in Chapter 3.

2.3.4

3.2
Performance diagnostics
To properly evaluate the predictive performance of our models, we need to choose the
right performance measure. As these measures aggregate the performance of the entire
model into a single value, the choice of performance measure is crucial. We will in this
project advocate for the expected log predictive density (ELPD) (Andrew Gelman et al.
2013) as our measure­of­choice. However, we will also discuss more common choices
such as negative log­likelihood (NLL) and root mean square error (RMSE), as they are
used in many of the previous works. Selecting an appropriate performance measure to
evaluate models remains an important challenge (Gneiting 2010). The issue is that all
our performance measures try to accomplish the impossible, as stated by Gelman et al.
(2013) in their paper on predictive information criteria for Bayesian models:
One difficulty is that all the proposed measures are attempting to perform what is, in
general, an impossible task: to obtain an unbiased (or approximately unbiased) and ac­
curate measure of out­of­sample prediction error that will be valid over a general class
of models and that requires minimal computation beyond that needed to fit the model in
the first place. When framed this way, it should be no surprise to learn that no such ideal
method exists. (Andrew Gelman et al. 2013)
Consequently, we are left with many different performance measures, each with distinct
properties and trade­offs, all striving to approximate this unattainable ideal (Andrew Gel­
man et al. 2013).

tic is computed separately for each dimension of the samples. Thus, when θnm ∈Rd,
we obtain one ˆR score per dimension, with some falling below the convergence criteria,
while others may exceed them.
One limitation of the standard ˆR statistic is that it requires multiple MCMC chains initial­
ized from different starting points. This poses a challenge in our setting, as each MCMC
chain will be initialized at a MAP estimate, and thus, the starting point is always fixed. Fur­
thermore, even when using different MAP estimates for initialization, the symmetry and
permutation invariance in neural networks make it difficult to compare the resulting chains.
To address this, we use a slight variation known as the split­ ˆR statistic, which can be
applied to a single MCMC chain (Murphy 2023). The idea is to split the chain in half and
treat the first and second halves as if they originated from separate initializations. The ˆR
statistic is then computed using these two segments, enabling an approximate conver­
gence diagnostic when multiple chains are unavailable or misaligned.

We will now introduce our experiments to examine the empirical behaviors of DE and DE

refer to models using the MAP estimates as MAP­based models, and these will generally

models in this project were initialized at the MAP estimate. Thus, we sometimes also
refer to these models as augmenting the baseline MAP­based model. The standard DE
will be referred to as MAP­based DE, and the MCMC augmented DE will be referred to
as MCMC­based DE.
We will first study the impact of augmenting the MAP­based models, trained on small
regression datasets, with posterior samples from the MCMC samplers. Then we will ex­

periments by Jordahn et al. (2025) with our MCMC­based models to see if we observe
similar phenomena.

4.1.5
Discussion
From our experiments, two particularly interesting results emerge: first, there is a no­
table difference between the scores of the y­averaged and θ­averaged metrics; second,
performance generally improves when augmenting the MAP­based models with posterior
samples from the MCMC samplers.
Different variants of RMSE: We begin by examining the discrepancy between the two
metric variations. As noted, the RMSE values are generally lower (i.e., better) in Table 4.3,
which reports the y­averaged RMSEs, compared to the θ­averaged RMSEs presented in
Table 4.4. Interestingly, this improvement is not reflected in the rank summary in Table 4.6,
as the ranking metric considers only relative performance across methods. As a result,
the RWM­, MALA­, and SGLD­based models appear to achieve better ranks under the
θ­averaged RMSE, even though their actual RMSE values are worse.
The difference in the two variants of RMSE is further illustrated in Figure 4.1, which dis­
plays the distribution of the RMSE difference, defined as ∆RMSE = RMSEθ −RMSEy,
across individual experiments. On the left side of Figure 4.1, we present boxplots of
∆RMSE grouped by dataset, allowing us to assess whether the discrepancy depends
on the data. For the smaller datasets (N < 1500), over 75% of the differences are posi­
tive, indicating that the y­averaged RMSE generally yields better (i.e., lower) performance
scores. For the larger datasets, the differences are negligible, with only a small fraction
being negative. This limited variation aligns with the results from Section 4.1.4; however,
Figure 4.1 further reveals that the higher variability observed in smaller datasets is pri­
marily skewed in favor of the y­averaged RMSE.

mately 95% of the ∆RMSE values are positive for the ULA­, SGLD­, and SGLD­CV­based
models, while only about 75% are positive for the RWM­ and MALA­based models. Thus,
these results suggest that the performance of MCMC­based models deteriorates when
posterior samples are used individually rather than averaged. One possible explanation
is that the posterior mean is not representative of the entire posterior distribution, as illus­
trated in Figure 3.3 from the previous chapter. Consequently, while individual posterior
samples may perform well, their average may not achieve the same level of performance.
Another option is to consider how each variant approximates the posterior predictive dis­
tribution (Eq. (2.33)). When we average over y, it essentially corresponds to a Monte
Carlo approximation (Eq. (2.19)) using the MCMC samples. In contrast, when averaging
over θ, it amounts to a Poor Man’s Bayes approximation (Section 2.2.1) using the poste­
rior mean ¯θ. From this viewpoint, it is expected that the scores of RMSEy are better than
the RMSEθ scores. However, it is important to note that evaluating RMSEθ is significantly
more efficient in both memory and computation time as it requires only a single evaluation
using ¯θ. For example, computing the y­averaged RMSE in this study was approximately
30,000 times slower than computing the θ­averaged RMSE, since it involved evaluating
the network for each of the posterior samples. Therefore, RMSEθ may be viewed as a
more practical approximation to RMSEy when computational resources are limited, par­
ticularly in the case of large models or datasets.
One possible explanation for the large variation in ∆RMSE observed for the ULA­ and
SGLD­CV­based models is the potential bias in their limiting distributions. As discussed in
Section 2.3.5, both ULA and SGLD require a decreasing step size for asymptotic conver­
gence to the true posterior, but in practice, a constant step size is often used to accelerate
convergence. In this project, we chose to fix the step sizes for ULA and SGLD­CV, while
Enriching deep ensembles with local structure
51
==PAGE:60==
applying a decreasing step size schedule only to the standard SGLD. In hindsight, it would
have been more consistent to treat all samplers similarly. However, we initially believed
that a constant step size for ULA and SGLD­CV, and a decreasing schedule for SGLD,
would best reflect their typical usage and practical capabilities. As a result, estimates of
¯θ for ULA and SGLD­CV are likely more biased, which may explain their poorer perfor­
mance and the increased variance in ∆RMSE. In contrast, the decreasing step size used
in SGLD likely led to a more accurate estimate of ¯θ. Even so, this remains speculative
and should be examined more thoroughly in future work.
Performance gain of MCMC sampling: Our next key observation is that performance
improves when augmenting MAP­based models with MCMC sampling. This is evident
from the ELPD values in Table 4.5 and the corresponding ranks in Table 4.6. However,
the magnitude of the performance gain appears to vary significantly across datasets. For
instance, the MCMC­based models outperform their MAP counterparts by up to 31.5%
on the Yacht dataset, whereas the improvement is only 0.28% on the Year dataset. This
suggests that the effectiveness of MCMC augmentation may be correlated with the size of
the dataset, N, or more specifically, the ratio N/dX, which serves as an indicator of how
well­specified the dataset is. Consequently, we examine the percentage gain in ELPD
from the MAP­based results to the MCMC­based results as a function of N/dX as shown
in Figure 4.2. In the top­left plot of Figure 4.2, the percentage ELPD gain is shown for each
experiment as a function of N/dX, with points colored by dataset. We observe that the
variance of the gain is much higher for small values of N/dX, which is expected. Interest­
ingly, these gains also tend to be higher on average compared to those associated with
larger N/dX values. This pattern is further illustrated in the top­right plot of Figure 4.2,
where boxplots are sorted in decreasing order of N/dX. Here, both the variance and
median ELPD gain generally decrease as N/dX increases.
A notable exception is the Kin8nm dataset, which displays a much higher average ELPD
gain than the Wine and Concrete datasets, despite having a significantly larger N/dX. It
is also worth noting that the Yacht dataset shows extremely high variability in ELPD gain,
even though its N/dX ratio is not the smallest among the datasets.
These results suggest a weak negative relationship, which is further supported by the
Pearson correlation test (Pearson 1895) computed across all points, yielding r = −0.265
and p = 0.000. However, the relationship is likely more nuanced. In summary, there ap­
pears to be a potentially significant gain from augmenting MAP­based models with MCMC
samplers when the dataset is small or the ratio N/dX is low. In contrast, for larger datasets
such as Protein and Year, the performance gain is negligible, indicating that MCMC aug­
mentation is not recommended in such cases due to the added computational cost. One
possible explanation is that for datasets like Protein and Year, the posterior distributions
are sharply peaked and well­defined around their modes. Consequently, sampling around
these modes yields minimal additional information, as most of the posterior mass is con­
centrated in this narrow region. In addition, the neural networks used in this study are
relatively small, which further simplifies the posterior landscape and potentially limits the
gains from MCMC exploration.

4.2
Regression analysis of the MCMC­based DE
For our second study, we extend the MAP­based models to DEs of sizes K = {1, 2, 5, 10, 20}.
From the first study, we observed that augmenting the MAP­based models with MCMC
samples increased the performance for most of the datasets. Now, we want to study
whether the same effect occurs when sampling from the DE. In particular, we are inter­
ested in whether the performance deteriorates when applying MCMC samplers to the DE.
Following the first study, we will limit our performance metrics to ELPD and y­averaged
RMSE, focusing mainly on the ELPD. Furthermore, we will consider our MAP­based mod­
els and MAP­based DE as the only baselines, and since we only use a single RMSE
variant, it will simply be referred to as ”RMSE”. Finally, we can view the first study as
the special case of this study where K = 1. This means that our training and sampling
procedures remained mostly the same.
4.2.1
Training
The main difference in the training process was that we now computed up to 20 MAP esti­
mates for each DE using different random seeds. Then, each DE of size K = {1, 2, 5, 10, 20}
was constructed using a random combination of the 20 models10 without replacement. To
ensure reproducibility, the data split, training, and construction of the DE were repeated
ten times for a total of 200 MAP estimates per dataset. This meant that none of the MAP
estimates of the 200 models used the same seed for training. Importantly, the seed con­
trolling the data split was fixed for each DE to ensure that each member within the DE
was trained and tested on the same data11.
4.2.2
MCMC sampling
For MCMC sampling of the DE, ˆα and ˆβ were also computed using Eq. (4.3), and the
same step sizes were used as in the previous experiments. The main difference in this
study was how we managed the number of samples for each member of the ensembles.
We wanted to ensure that the number of samples S used to compute performance met­
rics was independent of the ensemble size K. Specifically, we wanted to avoid a situ­
ation where an MCMC­based DE with K = 20 would use 20 times more samples than
an MCMC­based model with K = 1. One option was to draw only S/K samples from
each ensemble member. However, we were concerned that this would negatively impact
convergence for larger ensembles, as each chain would be 20 times shorter.
To address this, we ran the MCMC chain for each ensemble member for 10,000 itera­
tions. Then for each member k = 1, . . . , K we extracted a subset Sk of the chain such
that evaluation set for the entire ensemble became the union S = S1 ∪· · · ∪SK. We
decided to fix S = |S| = 2000 and distribute the subset sizes Sk uniformly across mem­
bers, so that on average, |Sk| = S/K. Rather than assigning a fixed number of samples
per member, we allowed |Sk| to vary randomly to introduce additional variability. It also
handles the case where S is not divisible by K without ad hoc adjustments. Lastly, the set
Sk was extracted by selecting samples at evenly spaced intervals from the corresponding
10,000­sample chain. This ensured that Sk was representative of the entire trajectory.

4.2.4
Discussion
Our results suggest that the performance of the MCMC­based DE is consistently at least
as good as, and in some cases better than, that of the MAP­based DE. For certain
datasets, the improvement is substantial, while for others it is minimal. Furthermore,
this difference seems to diminish as the ensemble size grows. For instance, when the
gain is marginal at K = 1, as with the Protein and Year dataset, it is even more so at
K = 20. Despite this, the performance of the MCMC­based DE does not appear to un­
derperform relative to its MAP­based counterparts, in contrast to what was observed for
other Bayesian DE methods in Jordahn et al. (2025).
56
Enriching deep ensembles with local structure
==PAGE:65==
One possible explanation for the observed decline in the performance gap as K increases
is that the additional information provided by posterior samples may be relatively minor
compared to what is already captured by the MAP estimates. For example, Kristiadi,
Eschenhagen, et al. (2022) argues that a few “high­quality” samples can yield strong pre­
dictive performance. If the MAP estimates are themselves of sufficiently high quality, the
posterior samples may offer only limited additional value. This would imply that the ben­
efit from local exploration around the modes is marginal, making further enrichment of
the ensemble through posterior sampling less impactful. However, this interpretation is
speculative and requires further investigation before drawing any firm conclusions.
As in the previous study, we observe that the ULA­ and SGLD­CV­based DEs achieve the
highest overall performance. As discussed in that work, this may be due to our handling of
the hyperparameters, since we would otherwise expect the MALA­based methods to per­
form best. However, the differences in performance among the MCMC­based methods
may also be influenced by the convergence behavior of the corresponding chains. This
could also help explain the variability in performance across datasets, as convergence
may be slower for larger or more complex datasets.
To investigate this, we ran 20 chains using RMW, MALA, and ULA for each dataset, each
for 10,000 iterations, using the same hyperparameters as in the previous experiments.
For each chain, we computed the split­ ˆR values (see Section 3.2.3) for each dimension
dθ, and then averaged these values across the 20 chains. In Table 4.8, we report the
25th, 50th, and 75th quantiles of the resulting ˆR values across dimensions, as well as
the maximum ˆR value per dataset and sampler. Additionally, we report the proportion of
dimensions satisfying ˆR < 1.01 and ˆR < 1.1 to assess how many dimensions appear to
have converged under each threshold.
Surprisingly, we observe from the table that most ˆR values are well above both conver­
gence thresholds. Only about a quarter of the dimensions satisfy ˆR < 1.1, and just 6–7%
meet the stricter criterion of ˆR < 1.01 across all datasets and methods. This has two key
implications. First, it suggests that none of the MCMC chains achieved full convergence.
Second, the performance differences observed across datasets and samplers cannot be
attributed to convergence behavior. For instance, although MALA achieves a notable per­
formance gain on the Yacht dataset, the proportion of dimensions below the convergence
thresholds is smaller than for MALA on the Year dataset, where the performance gain was
negligible.
Given that all chains were initialized at the MAP estimate, one would expect relatively
fast convergence due to the favorable starting point. However, the low proportion of con­
verged dimensions indicates that good initialization alone is insufficient. This raises the
question of whether the lack of convergence is due to actual sampling issues or limita­
tions in the diagnostic itself. It is also likely that more thorough hyperparameter tuning
could improve convergence. Nevertheless, the overall lack of convergence observed is
unexpected and requires further investigation.
This concludes our study of MCMC­based DEs on the regression datasets. We will now
proceed to analyze these models using one of the datasets and models from Jordahn et al.
(2025) to determine whether the patterns they observed also occur for the MCMC­based
models.

4.3
Experiments on CIFAR
For our final study, we extended the analysis of MCMC­based deep ensembles (DE) to
more complex datasets and models. Building upon the work of Jordahn et al. (2025),
we investigated whether our MCMC­based DE exhibits the same performance degrada­
tion they observed for other Bayesian neural network DE methods. Thus, we consider
our own MAP­based DE alongside the DE variants presented in Jordahn et al. (2025) as
baseline models for comparison. Specifically, our results will be compared to their re­
ported results for SWA (Izmailov, Podoprikhin, et al. 2019), SWAG (W. Maddox et al.
2019), LLLA (Kristiadi, Hein, et al. 2020), normalizing flow refinement of Laplace ap­
proximation (LA­NF) (Kristiadi, Eschenhagen, et al. 2022), IVON (Shen et al. 2024), and
MCDO (Gal and Ghahramani 2016). We covered all these methods in Chapter 2 ex­
cept Improved Variational Online Newton (IVON). Essentially, it is a variational method
that optimizes the mean µ and diagonal covariance σ of a Gaussian approximate poste­
rior q(θ) = N(θ|µ, diag(σ)2) using an optimization process similar to ADAM (Diederik P.
Kingma and Ba 2017) but with natural gradients (Shen et al. 2024).
As in previous experiments, we will use the Expected Log Predictive Density (ELPD) as
our primary performance metric. Additionally, since this is now a classification task, we
will also report the average prediction accuracy.
4.3.1
Dataset
We use one of the same datasets as Jordahn et al. (2025), namely the CIFAR­10 dataset
(Krizhevsky 2009). It consists of 32 × 32 RGB images divided into a training set of 50,000
images and a test set of 10,000 images, each belonging to one of ten classes12. Thus,

For training and MCMC sampling, we extracted 2,000 images from the training set to

4.3.2
Neural Network Architecture
Following Jordahn et al. (2025), we used a Wide Residual Network (WRN) (Zagoruyko
and Komodakis 2017), a wide extension of the Residual Network (ResNet) architecture (K.
He et al. 2016), to fit the image data. Specifically, we used a WRN­16­4 model, where
the depth l = 16 refers to the total number of convolutional layers (including those in the
shortcut connections), and the widening factor k = 4 determines the number of feature
maps, thereby controlling the width of the network.
An overview of the WRN­16­4 model is shown in Figure 4.4, with annotated tensor shapes
illustrating the flow from input image to predicted label. A more detailed view of the resid­
ual block, highlighted in the dashed box, reveals that it consists of two smaller blocks,
each comprising two consecutive convolutional layers and a shortcut connection. While
dropout is typically applied between the two convolutional layers, we have omitted dropout
in our implementation following Jordahn et al. (2025). The convolutional layers use a 3×3
filter, and have 16k, 32k, and 64k channels for each residual block, respectively. After the
final residual block, the feature maps are aggregated using global average pooling and
then passed through a final 256 × 10 linear layer, followed by a softmax activation to pro­
duce a vector of class probabilities.
Lastly, normalization is applied before each convolutional layer, but it is omitted from the
diagram for clarity. While the original WRN paper uses Batch Normalization (BN) (Ioffe
and Szegedy 2015), we instead adopt Filter Response Normalization (FRN) (Singh and
Krishnan 2020), as recommended by Jordahn et al. (2025). The motivation is that BN
poses challenges for Bayesian methods as it introduces dependencies between individ­
ual data points in the likelihood (Jordahn et al. 2025; Izmailov, Vikram, et al. 2021), and its
statistics must be recomputed for each posterior sample (Jordahn et al. 2025; W. Maddox
et al. 2019).
4.3.3
Training
To reproduce the results of Jordahn et al. (2025), we closely follow their training setup.
Specifically, we use the same SGD optimizer with a learning rate of 0.1, weight decay of
5 × 10−4, Nesterov momentum of 0.9, and cosine annealing. Models are trained for 100
epochs with early stopping based on the validation ELPD.
The DEs were constructed as in the previous experiment, using K = {1, 2, 5, 10, 20}
distinct MAP estimates per DE. This procedure was repeated ten times, resulting in a
total of 200 MAP estimates. This is the only difference in our training process compared
to Jordahn et al. (2025), who trained only 30 models. Their DEs were then constructed
by randomly sampling combinations (without replacement) from these models for each
ensemble size K = {1, 2, 5, 10, 20}. While their approach is considerably more efficient
in both time and memory, we chose to ensure that no MAP estimate was reused across
runs in our study.

4.3.6
Discussion
At first glance, the MCMC­based results appear promising, with an average test ELPD
only 0.001 lower than that of the MAP­based DE. While augmenting the DE with MCMC
sampling does not lead to performance gains, it also does not result in any degradation.
Furthermore, the results suggest that the performance of MCMC­based DE also does not
degrade to the same extent as the ensemble size K increases compared to most other
Bayesian DE methods. One possible explanation for the lack of both performance gain
and degradation in the MCMC­based DE is that the sampler may not move far from the
initial MAP estimate in the state space. Then it is likely that the additional posterior sam­
ples provide little new information from the MAP estimate, resulting in behavior similar to
that of MAP­based DE.
To investigate this behavior, we begin by examining how the performance metrics evolve
as the number of samples increases. Specifically, we analyze how test accuracy and
ELPD change with the number of evaluation samples, for each ensemble size K, using
MALA. The results for one of our runs are shown in Figure 4.5, along with the scores of
the corresponding MAP­based DE at K = 1 and K = 20 for comparison. From the figure,
it is clear that the largest performance gains are due to the diversity of the MAP estimates.
This effect is particularly noticeable for ELPD, where small ”jumps” can be observed each
time samples from a new chain are introduced. For instance, the ELPD scores at K = 1
remain nearly constant, compared to the other scores. Despite these ”jumps,” the ELPD
scores are not piecewise constant, suggesting a subtle effect and slight movement of the
MCMC samples in the state space.
Another way to assess how much the samples move in the state space is by examining
trace plots of the MCMC chains. To provide a meaningful scale for comparison, we ex­
tracted the parameter covariance from one of the LLLA models provided by Jordahn et al.
(2025), along with its corresponding network parameters. We then ran MALA, initialized
at the model’s MAP estimate, using the same procedure as in our previous experiments
(Section 4.3.4). We present the resulting trace plots alongside histograms of the samples
in Figure 4.6, for five randomly selected dimensions. From the figure, it appears that the
samplers move on a very small scale, with estimated variances of 5.20×10−6, 1.92×10−5,
1.22 × 10−5, 8.46 × 10−6, and 9.13 × 10−6, respectively. This is further highlighted by the
corresponding LA variances of 6.31, 6.31, 6.31, 6.20, and 6.31, which are on a completely
different scale. Thus, compared to the LLLA, the variation of the MCMC samples in the
state space is very insignificant.

explained by the fact that only the parameters of the final layer are sampled. Since this
corresponds to less than 0.1% of the parameters in the full model, the sampled subset
may remain restricted or implicitly constrained by the fixed parameters in the preceding
layers. As such, it would be interesting to investigate whether sampling a larger propor­
tion of the model’s parameters leads to greater deviation from the MAP estimate, and in
turn, improved performance. However, this would significantly broaden the scope of the
study and is therefore left for future work.

5
Conclusion
In this project, we introduced the fundamentals of Bayesian machine learning and deep
ensembles (DE). We reviewed the theoretical limitations of standard DEs, particularly the
fact that their approximate posterior is extremely crude, placing all probability mass on a
finite set of points. Prior work has proposed several popular approximate Bayesian meth­
ods to improve this posterior by introducing more local structure. However, a recent study
suggests that these approaches fail to surpass the standard DEs when the ensemble is
sufficiently large. We argued that this limitation may arise from restrictive assumptions
about the form of the approximate posterior. In response, we proposed an alternative
approach: augmenting DEs with Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling to enrich
the local structure of their posterior. The method was then evaluated on multiple datasets
and models.
5.1
Reviewing the research questions
We now summarize our findings by addressing the research questions:
5.1.1
How is the predictive performance of single neural networks and
deep ensembles affected by augmenting them with post­hoc MCMC
sampling across different datasets?
From the experiments in Sections 4.1 and 4.2 on various regression datasets, we ob­
served that the predictive performance of both MCMC­based models and deep ensembles
(DEs) varied significantly across datasets. For example, on the Yacht dataset, MCMC­
augmented models achieved around a 30% increase in ELPD, while the improvement
was less than 1% for the Year dataset.
Furthermore, the benefit of MCMC augmentation tended to diminish as the ensemble size
increased. Despite this decline and the negligible improvements for some datasets, it is
notable that none of the MCMC­augmented DEs performed worse than their MAP­based
counterparts. This suggests that MCMC augmentation does not harm predictive per­
formance, and can in many cases offer meaningful improvements.
We also explored whether dataset characteristics influenced performance gains. Our
results suggest a weak inverse relationship between the improvement from MCMC aug­
mentation and the ratio N/dX (number of data points over input dimensionality), indicating
that performance gains may decrease as this ratio increases. However, additional inves­
tigation is required to support this claim.
5.1.2
Do MCMC­augmented deep ensembles exhibit the same behavior
observed in other Bayesian methods from Jordahn et al. (2025), and
how does the absence of Gaussian constraints influence this?
Based on the experiments in Sections 4.1 and 4.2, our results suggest that MCMC­
augmented DEs do not exhibit the same negative performance trends for large ensem­
ble sizes as those reported for other approximate Bayesian methods in Jordahn et al.
(2025). This was initially supported by the results in Section 4.3.5, where we evaluated
the MCMC­augmented DE on the CIFAR­10 dataset using the WRN­16­4 architecture,
one of the model–dataset pairs also studied in Jordahn et al. (2025). In that setting, the
MCMC­augmented DE achieved performance nearly identical to the baseline DE.
Enriching deep ensembles with local structure
67
==PAGE:76==
Upon closer inspection, however, we found that this apparent stability was likely because
the additional MCMC samples remained very close to their corresponding MAP estimates.
That is, the MCMC samplers showed minimal movement from their initializations. We hy­
pothesize that this was driven by the very small tuned step size (1 × 10−8) and the strong
prior regularization (precision set to 9000). To investigate this further, we experimented
with larger step sizes. While this encouraged the samplers to explore further, it led to a
decline in predictive performance, particularly for large ensemble sizes, thus mirroring the
trends observed in Jordahn et al. (2025).
These findings suggest that the situation is more nuanced than simply attributing the
decline in performance to Gaussian constraints. Other factors, such as dimensionality
reduction via last­layer methods, likely play a significant role. For instance, they may
contribute to the sampler’s limited movement around the MAP initialization. At the very
least, our results highlight that configuring MCMC­based DEs is non­trivial and requires
thoughtful tuning of hyperparameters such as step size and prior strength to avoid de­
grading predictive performance.
5.1.3
How does the choice of the specific MCMC algorithm impact the
predictive performance and convergence of the MCMC sampling
process?
From a theoretical perspective, one would expect MALA­based augmentation to yield the
best predictive performance, given its strong convergence guarantees. However, across
all our experiments, we found that methods based on ULA and SGLD­CV generally out­
performed MALA in terms of predictive performance. This result was unexpected, but, as
discussed earlier, it can likely be attributed to our suboptimal hyperparameter tuning. In
particular, for the experiments in Sections 4.1 and 4.2, the MCMC samplers were used
with hyperparameters derived from the training process with no additional fine­tuning.
As for the convergence behavior of the samplers, we observed surprisingly little variation
between the different MCMC algorithms across datasets. More importantly, our results
suggest that none of the sampling methods achieved effective convergence, even in the
simpler neural network settings. This was unexpected and highlights both the importance
and the difficulty of properly tuning these samplers in practice.
Finally, because convergence behavior remained similar across datasets and samplers,
it does not appear to directly explain the large variations in predictive performance we
observed from MCMC augmentation. These findings highlight the need for further inves­
tigation into alternative factors that may influence predictive performance.
5.1.4
What performance metrics are used to evaluate deep ensembles,
and how do their values and behavior change across different
settings?
In this project, we explored several metrics to evaluate the predictive performance of the
different DE variations. Our primary focus was on the expected log predictive density
(ELPD), as it captures the expected log­probability under the predictive posterior distribu­
tion. However, we also considered variations of RMSE and NLL.
In Section 4.1, we examined two aggregation methods: one that averaged model param­
eters and another that averaged the individual model predictions. The results showed
that RMSE performance was consistently better when averaging predictions rather than
parameters. Additionally, the ELPD scores were more closely aligned with the prediction­
68
Enriching deep ensembles with local structure
==PAGE:77==
averaged metrics, further supporting the effectiveness of this approach.
We argued that this difference could be attributed to how each approach approximates
the posterior predictive distribution. While averaging of the parameters corresponds to a
simplistic Poor Man’s Bayes approximation, averaging of the predictions is more akin to
Monte Carlo integration, resulting in a much richer approximation.
5.1.5
Final Remarks
Overall, our results indicate that the performance of the MCMC­augmented DE is com­
parable to, or better than, that of the baseline DE. However, hyperparameter tuning and
convergence of the MCMC chains proved challenging, even for relatively simple networks.
This suggests that while the method is promising, it introduces additional complexity that
must be carefully managed in practice.
5.2
Limitations
One of the main limitations of MCMC­augmented deep ensembles lies in their computa­
tional cost. Deep ensembles are already resource­intensive, and incorporating MCMC
sampling further increases the burden. Specifically, introducing S additional parameter
samples per model multiply both inference time and memory usage by a factor of S. This
raises a natural and important question: Is the added computational cost of MCMC­based
DE justified by its performance gains? Based on the results presented in this project, we
believe the answer is no, at least in its current form. Nevertheless, studying these models
remains valuable, as they offer insight into the behavior and structure of DEs, particularly
with respect to posterior approximation.
Regarding the limitations of this project itself, there is room for improvement in how the
MCMC augmentation was implemented and evaluated. In hindsight, it may have been
more fruitful to focus on fewer experimental settings and instead conduct a more in­depth
analysis. For example, dedicating more effort to fine­tuning the hyperparameters of each
MCMC algorithm on the regression tasks, as well as thoroughly investigating the con­
vergence behavior and parameter­space movement of the samplers, could have yielded
deeper insights.
5.3
Future work
There remain several promising directions for future work to be studied for MCMC­based
DE. A key direction is to revisit the performance of MALA­based augmentation under more
carefully tuned hyperparameters. Given MALA’s strong theoretical properties, it would be
particularly interesting to see whether proper tuning allows it to outperform other samplers
as expected
Further investigation into the convergence behavior of the MCMC chains is also needed,
especially to understand how convergence (or lack thereof) affects predictive performance.
Additionally, in our implementation for WRN­16­4, only a small subset of the model’s pa­
rameters were included in the MCMC sampling process. Exploring how the number and
choice of sampled parameters influence both the diversity and effectiveness of the pos­
terior approximation would be a valuable extension. It may also help explain why the
samples remained so tightly clustered around the MAP estimates, as each posterior sam­
ple was conditioned on a large number of fixed parameters.
