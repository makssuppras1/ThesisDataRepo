Abstract
The present work reviews recent numerical results and discoveries concerning passive sup-
pression of vortex-induced oscillations of laminar and transitional ﬂow past the circular
cylinder using nonlinearly coupled mass dampers, ﬁndings that have been made over the
last decade, many of which have been related to the investigation of the rotational mass
damper (NES) as an alternative to traditional tuned mass dampers (TMD). As opposed
to previous work done, we make a critical and direct comparison of the NES and TMD
type dampers at similar speciﬁcations, for ﬂow around the sprung circular cylinder at
Re = 100 and ﬂuid-structure mass ratio m∗= 10, with special attention to the interac-
tion between the cylinder and the ﬂuid ﬂow caused by the attached mass. We present
results that highlight the importance of the fore-aft degree of freedom when analysing
exotic dampers such as the NES, which has traditionally been ignored in numerical and
empirical experiments due to its insigniﬁcance. We ﬁnd that both the TMD and NES
suppress the motion of the cylinder, while the latter also reduces the aerodynamic loads
and consequently the interaction with the ﬂow, the former is superior in terms of damping
performance in the region around the critical wind speed.

Abstrakt
I det nuværende værk gennemgås nyfundne resultater og opdagelser fra numerikken angående
passiv dæmpning af hvirvel-inducerede svingninger i laminar strømning og strømning i
transition omkring en cirkulær cylinder ved hjælp af ikke-lineært koblede massedæm-
pere, fund der er gjort indenfor det seneste årti, i forbindelse med undersøgelser af den
roterende massedæmper (NES) som et alternativ til traditionelle tunet massedæmoere
(TMD). I modsætning til foregående værker, laver vi en kritisk og direkte sammenligning
mellem NES og TMD dæmperne ved lignende speciﬁkationer, for strømningen omkring
en fjederophængt cirkulær cylinder ved Re = 100 og ﬂuid-struktur masseforhold m∗= 10,
med ekstra opmærksomhed på sammenspillet mellem cylinderen og ﬂuid-strømningen
forårsaget af den fæstnede ekstra masse.
Vi præsenterer resultater som tydeliggører
vigtigheden af agter-bov bevægelsesfrihedsgraden, hvilken traditionelt set er blevet ig-
noreret i numeriske og empiriske eksperimenter grundets dens ubetydelighed, når ekso-
tiske dæmpere såsom NES’en undersøges. Vi ﬁnder at både TMD’eren og NES’en dæm-
per cylinderens bevægelse, mens sidstnævnte ligeledes reducerer aerodynamiske laster og
dermed formindsker interaktionen med strømningen, så er førstnævnte dog overlegen i sin
dæmper præstation i området omkring den kritiske vindhastighed.

Foreword
The following work concludes a master degree in wind energy with specialization in me-
chanics and aerodynamics at DTU Wind Energy, Technical Universty of Denmark. The
project was carried out in collaboration with DTU Mechanical Engineering under super-
vision of Professor Jens Honore Walther.
Structure
This master’s thesis written presentation follows the IMRAD report structure with em-
phasis on results and discussion, while only introducing the reader to the very essentials
of the background theory, presuming the reader possesses some knowledge on dynamical
systems, ﬂuid mechanics and numerical methods.
Acknowledgement
I am greatful to Professor Jens Honore Walther ﬁrst and foremost for accepting the request
for collaborating on a project initially of my design, and for providing excellent support
throughout the project.
Inspiring discussions on the subject of vortex-induced vibrations and stability with my
former colleague Teis Schnipper lead to the initial project idea.
The project was only made possible by the vast computational resources granted by
Niﬂheim Linux supercomputer cluster (Niﬂheim7) and the Central DTU HPC cluster
(LSF 10).

Introduction and Background
Flow around circular cylinders is a wide ﬁeld of research spanning several regimes and
scales of ﬂow, from boundary layer development over convex surfaces to large scale vortex
shedding in the wake. The following chapter introduces the reader to the problem in
question and the background theory most relevant in order to solve it.
1.1
Outline
The problem to be presented is based on investigations conducted within the last decade,
while research into ﬂow around circular cylinders goes many years back. This section
covers the topic in a brief historical overview followed by a summary of current research
and an outline of the present work.
1.1.1
Historical Perspective
Investigation of ﬂow around bluﬀcylindrical bodies has been a prime interest of scientists
and engineers in ﬂuid mechanics since the dawn of aviation, as general interest in ﬂuid
dynamics rose with the growing industry of aerospace engineering. Where the ﬂat plate
geometry has served as a generic case for sharp thin bodies such as bridges, wings and
rotor blades, the circular cylinder has been the generic case for almost every other bluﬀ
and slender body geometries. The ﬂow around circular cylinders is divided into several
regimes of steady and unsteay ﬂows depending on the Reynolds number, historically
identiﬁed through empirical work, analytical and numerical solutions to the Navier-Stokes
equations.
The history of circular cylinder ﬂow and consequently vortex-induced vibrations is in-
herently tied to that of ﬂuid dynamics, starting with the early scientiﬁc work of Aristotle
and Archimedes, introducing concepts such as continuum, pressure and drag [13]. Find-
ings of Galilei Galileo and Isaac Newton set the framework for other great scientists of the
time, such as Daniel Bernoulli and Jean le Rond d’Alembert, to produce a mathematical
formulation of the conservation laws. With the advancement of calculus the mathematical
formulation was improved leading to the inviscid Euler equations and potential ﬂow at
the contribution of Leonhard Euler, Joseph Louis Lagrange and Pierre-Simon Laplace,
which set the theoretical foundation of ﬂuid mechanics in the 18th century. However, in
the idealized potential ﬂow theory1 the ﬂow is inviscid, incompressible and irrotational,
and given these conditions d’Alembert found that ﬂow around a circular cylinder induces
zero drag, a puzzle he never solved and thus became known as d’Alembert’s Paradox.

Although a strong mathematical formulation of ﬂuid mechanics had been developed, the
true nature of a ﬂuid ﬂow was not well-understood at the time.
Independently Claude-Louis Navier and George Gabriel Stokes understood the need of
a friction term in the already complex partial diﬀerential equation Euler formulated, which
lead to the viscous and rotational formulation of the Navier-Stokes equations that set
the framework for modern ﬂuid mechanics and computational ﬂuid dynamics. However,
analytical solutions to said equations are not in abundance, hence a closed-form solution
to d’Alemberts Paradox and the circular cylinder ﬂow was not simply obtained. Research
was shifted towards obtaining approximate solutions to the Navier-Stokes equations which
lead to the concept of vorticity coined by Hermann von Helmholtz and consequently the
Kutta-Joukowski theory of circulation of lift and the boundary-layer theory developed by
Ludwig Prandtl, the latter which along with Osborne Reynolds’ work on ﬂow regimes,
improved understanding of drag on the circular cylinder due to viscosity.
Unsteady ﬂow around circular cylinders in the form of harmonic vortices being shed
into the wake was ﬁrst thoroughly described by Theodore von Kármán [7] after whom
it was originally named The Kármán Vortex Street, and he recounts2 the origin of his
interest in the circular cylinder ﬂow when working as a graduate assistant under Prandtl,

problem, but every morning when I came in the laboratory I asked him, Herr
Hiemenz, is the flow steady now? He answered very sadly, It always
oscillates. (Hiemenz later succeeded in making the ﬂow almost steady by

properly adressed. The result caught the attention of Lord Rayleigh [2, 3] who thought

laptop and an interest in ﬂuid mechanics, investigating complicated ﬂows no longer re-
quires a wind tunnel or access to vast resources. The current research on VIV suppression
using a rotational mass damper, is conducted solely using numerical methods, which has
been advantageous since the system is highly non-linear, thus unpredictable and sensitive
to initial conditions, hence motivating extensive analysis into parameter space, a task
easily solved using numerical methods.

Kárman-Bérnard Vortex Street
Vortex shedding is the ﬁrst instability that breaks up the disturbance-free and fully sym-
metrical creeping ﬂow and develops into the laminar regime. Creeping ﬂow is character-
ized by symmetries, usually such that it is not easy to tell the ﬂow direction. For the
circular cylinder the ﬂow resembles that predicted by potential ﬂow shown in Figure 1.4,
where the streamlines are fully symmetrical about both axes, however potential ﬂow is
not able to predict drag, as it is at a maximum in creeping ﬂow, exactly due to viscous
drag. Creeping ﬂow loses its identity at about Re ≈5, where two symmetrical attached
vortices are formed on the trailing side of the cylinder, thus breaking the symmetry about
the vertical axis while retaining symmetry about the horizontal axis. As the Reynolds

number increases the vortices grow and elongate downstream creating a wake which is
approximately the width of the cylinder. Approaching Re = 48 an instability in the far
wake causes a shear-layer between the vortices to oscillate harmonically. As the ampli-

propagate upstream into the near-wake symmetrical vortices which then start shedding
harmonically, thus breaking the symmetry about the horizontal axis as well. This is the

starts at Re ≈200 [14]. Von Kárman used the terminology that below the threshhold
Reynolds number of about 48 the symmetrical vortices were the stable solution and above
the threshhold the asymmetric vortices were stable [7]. In this range the Strouhal number
increases from zero to about 0.2.
Transition
As indicated by Figure 1.6 the transition range spans a wide range of Reynolds numbers.
Transition to turbulence starts in the wake and propagates upstream to the near-wake
shear layer [14]. In this range the vortex shedding remains qualitatively unchanged. Thus
one could make the argument that the results found from the investigation at Re = 10000
might not diﬀer much in the rest of the range. Turbulent vortices in the wake are vastly
diﬀerent from the laminar vortex street, as there will be gradients across the ﬂow in
the cylinder direction as well, due to momentum advection in all directions, a trait of
turbulent motion.
Hence three-dimensional eﬀects must be accounted in this regime
in order to properly model the actual physics of the ﬂow, however at a heavy cost of
computation time [20, 42], thus most of the preliminary investigation is conducted in the
laminar regime where two dimensional ﬂow reasonably catches the essential physics of the
ﬂow. The Strouhal number approximately constant at 0.2 in this range.
Critical Transition
Drag is signiﬁcantly reduced over a short Reynolds number range, Re ≈[2·105, when the
transition point reaches and propagates upstream the boundary layer [14]. The separation
points move downstream thus narrowing the wake, consequently reducing drag. The sharp
gradient in CD(Re) shown in Figure 1.6 is not easy to model and predict, as the exact
critical Reynolds number depends on surface roughness and external factors as well. As
the wake has narrowed signiﬁcantly in this regime, harmonic vortex shedding is annihilated
and the wake consists mostly of a steady stream of disorganized turbulent structures.
Fully Turbulent Regime
In the post-critical regime, Re > 5·106, the boundary layer is fully turbulent and harmonic
shedding of turbulent vortices is resumed, although at a slightly higher Strouhal number
[14].

Advanced Analytical Methods
Among advanced analytical methods tested by Tumkur and Blanchard is the method of
proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) [33, 35], where the ﬂuid ﬂow is divided into a
ﬁnite number of modes which are superimposed to create the complete ﬂow ﬁeld [39].

1.3
Computational Fluid Dynamics
The governing equations of ﬂuid mechanics may be solved in various alternative ways to
the analytical approach, either through further assumptions or rigorous approximations
based on empirical results, or they may be solved by discretization of the second order par-
tial diﬀerential equation that is the Navier-Stokes momentum equation. The discretized
system of equations are of course solved most eﬃciently by computer, as there will be at
least one calculation per discrete element per timestep, depending on the method used,
which can easily amount to millions of ﬂoating-point operations per second. Solving the
governing equations numerically on discretized form fascilliates manipulation of any pa-
rameter anywhere in the domain, which itself might be manipulated, on a timestep basis
or even every at iteration, of course while considering numerical stability. This enormous
ﬂexibility in numerical methods makes solutions of complicated systems attainable, for
instance ﬂow through a turbojet engine in operation or even turbulence. The circular
cylinder ﬂow is rather simple compared to those cases, hence numerical methods in ﬂuid
mechanics has its merits and advantages where analytical methods come short, in a ﬁeld
traditionally dominated by empirical investigation.
Numerical, Empirical or Analytical Method
Numerical experiments can be conducted by any individual with access to a computer
and reliable CFD software, free or proprietary. Simple cases such as laminar ﬂow around
the circular cylinder and the Kárman vortices can be simulated using relatively low com-
putational eﬀort.
Computational Fluid Dynamics is cost eﬃcient in this example, as
setting up an actual experiment to show the existance of Kárman vortices or other ﬂow
phenomena is not easily done at home. Parameter space is not nearly as conﬁned for CFD
as it is for actual experiments. For instance how does one investigate weather systems
on other planets, ﬂow around a two-hundred meter wind turbine or a space shuttle reen-
tering the upper atmosphere, in a controlled environment? The numerical world is not
limited by physical or technological restraints in the same sense. One can easily model
a mountain emerged in helium or argon, or simply a full-scale wind turbine in air at the
right Reynolds number. Furthermore the method yields a solution at every discrete point
in the simulation domain. In an actual experiment it would require advanced methods
and measurement equipment to obtain three dimensional pressure and velocity ﬁeld in a
large domain, and for scales in the order of a full scale wind turbine wake, it is well nigh

impossible.
One of the prime advantages of CFD over experiment is probably the absolute control
of initial and boundary conditions and the ease of sweeping several parameters while
keeping others constant, such as running through several Reynolds regimes at the same
Mach number, without changing ﬂow velocity or geometry. Examining Reynolds number

Although the numerical branch has several advantages and merits over the analytical

1.3.1
Numerical Model
The following lightly discusses the fundamentals behind the numerical model used in the
present work, such that the reader gets an idea about the challenges of CFD in the context
of the present work.
Flow Solution
There are several ways to solve a PDE numerically and in ﬂuid mechanics the Navier-
Stokes equations are solved using the method of ﬁnite volume. The gradient terms in the
partial diﬀerential equation of (1.5) are approximated by n-order diﬀerence schemes. The
discretized equations of conservation are then solved numerically for each cell in the do-
main. There exist various methods for accomplishing this and one common method used
for incompressible viscous ﬂow is the semi-implicit method for pressure linked equations
(SIMPLE) algorithm, that solves the pressure-velocity ﬁelds in a number of steps. It is
beyond the scope of the current work to introduce the reader to further details of CFD
algorithms, which is a vast subject in itself.
Turbulence Model
Turbulence is caused by gradients in the ﬂow that promote advection in any direction.
In the boundary layer turbulence increases momentum transport toward the surface thus
energizing the near wall ﬂow consequently changing the velocity proﬁle and shear stress.
However turbulence is associated with ﬂow structures in many scales, in fact as far as we
know as of now, all the way down to thermodynamic motion at the molecular scale [31].
Thus in order to properly model turbulence one must resolve its motion all the way down
to an adequate level. This is termed Direct Numerical Simulation or DNS, which is also
the algorithm used for laminar ﬂow in the present work at Re = 100, as the absence of

turbulence allows a much lower resolution. For turbulent ﬂows DNS requires higher order
methods and extremely high resolution, at the cost of computational eﬀort.
There exist a number of alternatives to modelling the ﬁnest scales of turbulence,
for instance Large-Eddy Simulation, where only the largest turbulent ﬂow structures are
simulated while the rest is modelled.
The most widely used and computationally eﬃcient turbulence models are RANS-
based, Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes, where all turbulence is modelled based on gra-
dients in the ﬂow. For the present work the common k−ωSST model is used to model
turbulence at Re = 10000.
The k−ωSST turbulence model is a so-called eddy viscosity model, that is based on
an analogy between molecular diﬀusion and turbulent motion. The two-equation model
expresses the Reynolds stress tensor in terms of two parameters, k and ω, turbulent kinetic
energy and speciﬁc dissipation rate respectively.
Transition Model
Transition from laminar to turbulent ﬂow is highly inﬂuenced by small-scale instabilities
in the shear- and boundary-layer. Thus proper simulation of transition, which can be
very important to predict correctly in order to catch the right ﬂow characteristic, requires
very small-scale motion to be resolved, which is computationally heavy. Thus one may
invoke a transition model, of which many diﬀerent types exist. Usually if it is known a
priori where the transition point will take place, the computational domain is divided into
a region of laminar ﬂow and one of turbulent ﬂow. However, such transition models tend
to force transition, potentially leading to an unreasonable ﬂow solution.
The γReθ transition model is a two-equation model, which introduces the scalar inter-
mittency to the ﬂow that varies between 0 and 1 depending on whether the ﬂow is laminar
or turbulent. The model requires the location of the free-stream edge to be deﬁned, that
is the approximate edge of the boundary layer [25].

1.3.2
Work Flow
The process of a succesful CFD experiment consists of several interconnected yet diﬀerent
steps, precisely like an actual experiment that is more or less divided into three parts;
setup of experiment, gathering observations and analysis of observations. In an actual
experiment the execution of each of these steps may be carried out by diﬀerent individuals
at very diﬀerent times, however in CFD all steps are usually carried out in quick succession,
sometimes even using the same software package [44].
Pre-processing
Fluid domain
is set up ﬁrst and foremost, such that it suits the object or ﬂow phe-
nomenon, usually an empty box for external ﬂows and for internal ﬂows any shape that
ﬁts the geometry in question.

Geometry of body
is inserted next, which has to be generated using CAD8 in an
appropriate format such as .stl. The geometry is subtracted from the computational
domain, leaving an additional surface behind in the domain, representing said body.
Mesh of discrete cells
is generated within the domain using an appropriate meshing
algorithm. This is one of the most challenging steps in CFD, as it will most likely always
have an inﬂuence on the ﬁnal result, and it is of course the objective to reduce the inﬂuence
of the mesh. The mesh has to be suﬃciently ﬁne to resolve important physics, and this
can be done in several ways. The mesh can be altered locally through reﬁnement or type.
Boundary conditions
for the domain must be set appropriately to ensure physical
ﬂow. Setting the correct boundary conditions is vital, as the ﬂow within the domain
can not converge to a physical solution, although it obeys Navier-Stokes equations, if the
boundaries are unphysical.
Initial conditions
are arguably as important as the boundary conditions. Proper ini-
tial conditions ensure numerical stability and faster convergence to a physical solution,
especially for transient simulation. For steady simulations the best initial condition is of
course the one that is identical to the steady solution.
Solving
The solving step is the most straight forward process in CFD from the operators point
of view, one simply executes the simulation, which has previously been initialized, and
monitors its progress. However, writing the algorithm that solves the problem fed to the
machine from the pre-processing is undoubtedly the most exciting and challenging process
in CFD. The operator must possess knowledge of the machinery that delivers the solution,
in order to pick appropriate models for a given problem.
Temporal resolution and iteration criteria are controlled in this step, along with speciﬁc
model parameters when required.
Post-processing
Although the entire ﬂow ﬁeld is solved simultaneously and full ﬁeld solution exists at
any one time, it is quite memory demanding, thus in almost any case only the current
timestep is saved. However, a number of monitors might be set up to save data in each
timestep, such as velocity proﬁles, pressure distributions, snap-shots of scalars in planes
or three-dimensional ﬂow structures.
Post-processing is translating a three-dimensional ﬂow ﬁeld of various scalars into
digestable tables and visuals.

1.5
Coupled Fluid-Structure Model
The ﬂuid-structure problem consists of two systems solved simultaneously with a coupling
step that depends on the problem in question. For the rigid cylinder in ﬂow the coupling is
quite simple. The ﬂuid solution depends on the motion of the cylinder, that is the motion
of the cylinder boundary. The motion of the cylinder imposes a new boundary condition
in the ﬂuid domain at each time step. The cylinder motion conversely is coupled to the
ﬂuid ﬂow through the total aerodynamic force.
Thus at each timestep the ﬂuid domain, that is geometry and boundary conditions, is
updated with respect to the current cylinder position and previous positions, accounting
for the velocity of the body, which changes the ﬂow ﬁeld. The resulting pressure and
shear stress distribution constitutes the external force that determines the motion of the
cylinder based on the resulting acceleration.
֒→Flow Domain →Flow Field →Aerodynamic Loads →Cylinder Motion
The coupling can be solved explicitly or implicitly as an integrated or separate part of
the CFD algorithm. For the present work the motion equations are solved simulatenously
at every inner iteration, whereas for the work by Blanchard and Tumkur it was solved
explicitly with a very low timestep.
In general the dynamics of a multi degrees of freedom system may be expressed as;
M¨q = f,
(1.32)
where q are the generalized coordinates of the bodies of which M describes the inertia
and f is a generalized force that hold all external forces, such as body forces, for instance
due to gravity, aerodynamic loads, internal couplings between the bodies, for example a
spring or a rigid pivot arm [44].

It was found that the Courant number should remain relatively low in order for the vortices
to properly build up to maximum amplitude, especially for the cases with the dynamical
sprung cylinder. For explicit time-stepping the Courant number should not exceed 1,
however for an implicit second order time scheme, as used in the present work, depending
on the ﬂow case and number of inner iterations the Courant number may exceed 5 or
more. For the present work the Courant number is set to Co = 2, which determines the
timestep, ∆t, based on the lowest cell size, ∆x0, and the maximum theoretical velocity
Umax. The minimum cell size is 1% of the basesize. Taking a conservative approach the
maximum velocity is the sum of the cylinder potential ﬂow velocity maximum, that is 2,
and the velocity of the cylinder.

Mechanical Energy
The time series of mechanical in the system is a good measure of the kinetic energy
transferred from the ﬂow to the cylinder. The quantitiy E∗of the given body is the
mechanical energy normalized with 1/8ρU 2
∞πD2, the kinetic energy of ﬂuid occupying the
same volume as the cylinder. Figure 3.55 shows the mechanical energy of the cylinder
and TMD masses separately. After the initial transient there is almost no ﬂuctuation in
E∗, indicating a very steady transfer of energy from the ﬂuid ﬂow to dissipation in the
TMD through the cylinder motion.
The result is quite diﬀerent for the NES case. Figure 3.56 shows signiﬁcant ﬂuctuations
in energy indicating the unsteady nature of the system. For the 2-DOF case the steady
decline in mechanical energy for the cylinder corresponds to a constant energy level for
the NES mass, which suggests that energy is indeed being dissipated in a one-way transfer
to the NES. Furthermore, when the energy level of the cylinder reaches the steep rise the
energy also increases for the NES, contrary to linearly coupled multibody systems for
which energy may be exchanged back and forth between the degrees of freedom. This is
an essential feature of the nonlinear energy sink.

Frequency Spectra
Consuling the frequency spectra of Figure 3.57 the single peak in the motion spectrum
reﬂects a simple harmonic response, and the loads are correspondingly simple with a single
harmonic. For the TMD a simple analytical model with a periodic excitation would likely
suﬃce as an initial approximation, given the linearity of the mass body system. The
dominant frequency is slightly higher than the corresponding vortex shedding frequency
for the stationary cylinder.
However the spectrum of the NES tells a diﬀerent story as depicted by Figure 3.58.
The motion spectrum is full of noise and the dominant frequency has decreased. The
transervse load spectrum is also full of noise and multiple higher frequency peaks not
harmonics of the motion frequency, suggesting a much more complex interaction with the
ﬂuid ﬂow. Thus a simple harmonic excitation in an analytical model would probably not
catch the essential dynamics of the NES system, as the interaction with the ﬂuid ﬂow
due to nonharmonic motion becomes signiﬁcant, and the aerodynamic loads can not be
determined analytically. The spectra for both the 1-DOF and 2-DOF cases carry similar
features, the dynamical modes are just completely diﬀerent.

Turbulent Flow
In order to complete the comparison of the TMD and NES, comparable simulations in
the transitional regime at Re = 10000 are attempted, which includes fully turbulent wake
vortices with laminar ﬂow over the cylinder. In this regime the ﬂow topology varies to
a lesser extent as a function of Reynolds number, where drag coeﬃcient and Strouhal
number remain fairly constant.
Despite strong eﬀorts to retain a high accuracy relative to benchmark results, the
discrepancy was deemed unacceptable using the coarset possible temporal and spatial
resolution in order to simulate an appropriate time series of at least 20 oscillations for ﬁve
cases, namely ﬁxed, 1-DOF, 2-DOF, 2-DOF TMD and 2-DOF NES.
In the following is presented the results obtained, highlightning the discrepancy found,
using RANS, as opposed to the spectral DNS and empirical benchmarks, and the com-
putational eﬀort and necessity of including the spanwise direction for three-dimensional
ﬂow.

Conclusion
In the present work, we review many fundamental results of the ﬁxed and sprung cir-
cular cylinder in laminar ﬂow using a two-dimensional ﬂuid domain, which was found
suﬃcient compared to three-dimensional ﬂow at Re = 100, and ﬁnd excellent correla-
tion with previous work, numerical and empirical, in regards of predicted aerodynamic
loads and maximum attainable amplitude for the dynamic cases, comprising a 1-DOF
(Y-only motion) and 2-DOF (XY motion) system in comparison, of which the former
has traditionally been preferred, which have been found to have a subtle diﬀerence in ex-
treme loads and displacement of about 4%, while the overall dynamics remain unchanged
from a qualitatively. Furthermore the three cases (ﬁxed, Y, XY) are simulated over an
extended Reynolds number range from 5 to 200, to investigate the inﬂuence on aerody-
namic loads and the wake dynamics, and in accordance with previous results [12] there is
a Hopf bifurcation at ∼48, at which the ﬂow topology changes from a symmetrical wake
of bound vortices to a harmonic vortex shedding wake known as The Kárman-Bérnard
Vortex Street. The system is found to be especially hysteretic for the dynamic cases,
in which the exact location of the bifurcation point is undetermined, and may depend
highly on initial conditions, as it has been found that vortex shedding can be sustained
in a steady condition at Reynolds numbers down to ∼25, well outside the lock-in region,
that is at Strouhal numbers diﬀerent from the non-dimensional natural frequency of the
sprung cylinder. Possibly vortex-induced vibrations can be sustained at a much lower
Strouhal numbers than the lower bound of ∼0.14 found at the onset of vortex shedding
for the ﬁxed cylinder, if the cylinder natural frequency is changed accordingly to promote
resonance, while keeping attention to initial conditions. Variations in Strouhal number
and aerodynamic loads change signiﬁcantly with Reynolds number in the laminar range,
which extends into the ﬁrst wake transition range at Re ∼400, thus dynamic behavior
found in this Reynolds range may not exist at higher Reynolds numbers, not only due to
the inﬂuence of turbulent phenomena but also due to the vanishing gradients in aerody-
namic loads and Strouhal number, which remain fairly constant over the majority of the
transition range and post-critical range.
The succesful coupled dynamical model is then extended to include multibody dynam-
ics, attaching an additional mass with a speciﬁc coupling resembling either a rotational
mass damper (NES) [35] or a tuned mass damper (TMD), which are thoroughly compared
for a speciﬁcally chosen set of similar conﬁguration parameters, that is similar damping
and relative mass. Both types of dampers are modelled in the 1-DOF and 2-DOF conﬁg-
uration and compared to the ﬁxed and undamped cases, considering aerodynamic loads
and resulting motion of the cylinder. The dynamics of the TMD coupled system is found
to behave tediously linearly to such a degree that proportional damping in the cylinder
spring coupling would be a suitable substitute. The dynamics of the undamped cylinder

simple superposition of few harmonics, thus a simple analytical model would be suﬃcient

by three quarters in transverse amplitude, at the expense of an increase in transverse
loads exceeding those of the ﬁxed and undamped cases. The TMD conﬁguration in ques-
tion is seen to perform better than the NES in almost every aspect, even as a broad-band
damper, however a restriction of the motion of the TMD mass, which is seen to exceed the
radius of the cylinder. On the contrary while the NES ranks lower as a damper, it does
oﬀer an interesting dynamical interaction with the cylinder ﬂow system, which seemingly
reduces motion amplitude without a corresponding penalty in terms of increased trans-
verse loads, and it is found that the nonlinearly coupled mass induces chaotic behavior,
with quasi-periodic responses of multiple harmonics and inharmonics. The extreme loads
and displacements exceed those of the undamped case, while the average is lower, the
dynamics vary over long time scales as identiﬁed by Blanchard and Tumkur. Further-
more, in the present work it is found that the dynamical response for the NES cases vary
signiﬁcantly when accounting for the streamwise degree of freedom. Qualitatively the
dynamics diﬀer for the 1-DOF and 2-DOF cases by a considerable amount.
In previous work by others the streamwise direction has improperly been ignored,
which is a fair approximation for linear dampers or the undamped case, but not for
the rotational damper, which by its very deﬁnition exerts forces in the entire xy-plane.
Additionaly in response to previous work on the NES as a VIV suppression device, it is
found that ﬂows at Reynolds numbers close to the Hopf bifurcation are sensitive to initial
conditions and the resulting semi-chaotic dynamical interaction induced by the NES might
not exist in the transition regime, far from the symmetrical ﬂow solution where VIV is
completely suppressed. Furthermore the evaluation of the rotational NES as a damper
for hamonic excitations, such as vortex shedding, should be compared with traditional
damper solutions, such as the TMD, which has proven to be superior in every aspect.
