Abstract
Recalcitrant secondary biomass that is not efficiently utilized by the conventional and well­
established process of anaerobic digestion, can be alternatively gasified to produce a gas
mixture (syngas), composed mainly by CO/H2/CO2/CH4. The mixture is of poor quality
and can be either used as low quality fuel, be upgraded by removing CO2 or converted to
biomethane. That can be done biologically, using the syngas as substrate for mixed mi­
crobial communities to metabolize it to biomethane. Different reactor configuration were
investigated for the syngas biomethanation, with trickle bed reactor providing the highest
potential. The context of the specific process has not been sufficiently studied but it has
attracted a lot of attention lately because of its high potential for scaling­up. The present
study constitutes an effort of process optimization regarding the operational parameters
of inlet gas composition and gas residence time (GRT). Specifically sets the baseline for
further optimization. The mass transfer coefficient (kLa) of the reactor was firstly deter­
mined to explore the systems potential. The reactor achieved kLa values of 380 ± 4.9 and
204 ± 18.5 for H3 and CO respectively at the highest recirculation rate of 500 ml/min. The
effect of gas composition at high GRT=3h and low recirculation rate, 20ml/min, on produc­
tivity, substrate conversion efficiency and exit gas composition was explored at the first
phase. The best performance was exhibited at a syngas quality index (SQI)=4 with 2.10
±0.29 mmol/lbed/h and full substrate conversion. The purity of biomethane reached 80%.
Following, an effort to decrease gradually the GRT was made in order to investigate the
threshold after which conversion efficiency drops. Finally, the threshold was at GRT=1.5h
with an increased productivity of 4.45 ± 0.03 mmol/lbed/h with full conversion of CO and
H2. The maximum productivity was achieved at GRT=1h of 5.77 ± 0.14 mmol/lbed/h at the
expense of substrate conversion efficiencies and lower purity.

Introduction
Anthropogenic activities have affected the Earth’s system, at a scale that the abrupt global
environmental change cannot be argued. For this is reason, it is urgent and main respon­
sibility of the industry sector to move with a fast pace and implement sustainable tech­
nologies that will allow humanity to survive and thrive without trespassing the planetary
boundaries[1].Moreover the human population is projected to reach 9.7 billion by 2070,
following significant increase in energy consumption and waste generation that may have
detrimental effects in air, soil and water quality[2]. Consequently it is crucial to consider
the energetic utilization of residual biomass as an imperative solution.
The bioenergy sector appears to have tremendous potential, estimated at a range of 139­
530 EJ[3], accounting over 6% of the global energy supply in 2022[4]. Taking also into
account that the energy sector is susceptible to geopolitical issues that arise, European
commission established the REPowerEU plan [5]. The plan targets to fill underground
gas storage to 80% of the capacity and a new marketplace (AggregateEU) to trade gas
in Europe has opened.
To address firstly the environmental challenges and simultaneously ensure the supply of
gas, secondary biomass can be employed. Residual lignocellulosic biomass and other
carbon containing wastes that cannot be biologically degraded due to their complex struc­
ture can be gasified, as an alternative, in an oxygen limited environment to produce syn­
thesis gas, or syngas trough gasification or pyrolysis. The occuring syngas consists of
CO, H2 and CO2 in varying composition depending on the biomass feedstock and oper­
ational parameters. Other than syngas, biochar and tars are also produced with yields
depending also on the same factors[6]. That results in complete utilization of the recal­
citrant biomass and adds value on the process[7]. Syngas can be used as a building
block for added­value chemicals, can be partially upgraded and used as a low quality fuel
or converted to biomethane and injected to the natural gas grid. The last solution has
attracted lately a lot of attention because of the high potential to commercialize and the
opportunity to partially substitute the fossil derived methane[8].
The conversion of syngas to methane is conducted conventionally through the Sabatier
process by employing metal based catalysts in high or medium temperatures (maximum
conversion at 350 oC) and high pressures. Extreme operational conditions are necessary
to overcome the thermodynamic limitations of the process. Besides that, the catalytic pro­
cess requires specific H/C ratios and high gas purity since the catalysts are susceptible
to impurities[9]. Another way to convert syngas to biomethane is to combine thermo­
chemical with biological conversion. The thermochemically converted syngas is fed to
microorganisms which act as biocatalysts and metabolize the gaseous substrate (syn­
gas) to the desired product (biomethane). The process has been a strong candidate for
syngas conversion, due to the cost­effectiveness potential[10]. This is due to the fact that
the process operates at mild temperatures and pressures, can utilize different H/C ratios
and the microorganisms exhibit higher resilience to gas impurities. The main drawbacks
of the syngas biomethanation process is the gas­to­liquid mass transfer limitations due to
the low solubility of H2 and CO in the liquid phase and the low cell concentrations since
the syngas fermentation can be run only under anaerobic conditions[11].
Despite the toxic nature of syngas, because of the CO presence, several bacteria and
archaea have been reported to be able to utilize syngas as a substrate with methane

The synergistic effect of co­cultures or mixed microbial consortia favour the process in
many ways. Mixed microbial cultures exhibit higher robustness to inhibitory compounds
since there will be present strains that metabolize these compounds or are able to easily
adjust to them. In terms of process, no sterilized environment in needed, reducing the risk
of process failure due to contamination. Last but not least they present high resilience to
environmental disturbances, rendering the operation consistent and stable[14]. The main
disadvantage working with mix cultures is the complexity, adding freedom degrees in the
system. There can be problems identifying the source of system malfunction or possible
microbial competitions lowers the carbon yield to the desired product[15].
The major bottlenecks of syngas fermentation, as discussed before, is the gas­to­liquid
mass transfer limitations and the low cell concentrations. Those problems have been
perceived since three decades ago [16],[17], but the rapid developments on alternative
processes like fermentation of sugars or Fischer­Tropsch process pushed the gas fermen­
tation process aside. A key parameter to the process is the reactor configuration. Three
main types of reactors have been reported as potential candidates: the continuous stirred
tank reactor (CSTR), bubble column reactor (BCR) and the trickle bed reactor (TBR). In
a CSTR the mass transfer limitations are tackled by implementing effective mechanical
stirring to achieve uniform conditions. The power input for mechanical stirring though and
the long hydraulic retention times to avoid wash­out of the cells undermine significantly

the cost­effectiveness of the process. BCR eliminates the stirring requirements by intro­
ducing the gas in the form of bubbles providing the surface are for the gas­liquid contact,
but coalescence and backmixing resulted in several folds lower mass transfer rates[17].
On the other hand trickle bed reactors are gas phase reactors, bringing a packed bed col­
umn that allows biofilm to grow and higher surface area for gas liquid mass transfer. By
gas flowing co­ or counter­currently to the liquid phase, mass transfer is significantly im­
proved compared to a CSTR or a BCR [18] while biofilm formation on the packing material
addresses the low cell concentration challenge.
The focus on trickle bed reactors for the biomethanation process was initiated in 1990 and
touched again after 2013, when the power­to­gas concept started emerging[19]. Hence,
the process of syngas biomethanation in TBR can be considered immature and the fac­
tors and how they affect the performance must be comprehended and investigated in
greater extend. However, there is rapid development on research and increasing interest
that had driven the process on pilot scale tests for syngas biomethanation and biogas
upgrade[20][21].
The factors that have major influence on the reactor performance can be divided into three
main sections reactor design, operational parameters and microbial community selection.
For reactor design, H/D ratio, type and size of packing material, the way that liquid and
gas phase are introduced in the reactor are important parameters that need to be eval­
uated. The selected cell strain or the source of the microbial community is crucial since
it comprises the core of the process. Culture enrichment techniques, bioaugmentation,
cell adaptation prior to inoculation are some the factors determining the system’s perfor­
mance. However, the focus of this study is on investigating crucial operational parameters
that have been previously proven to affect significantly the reactor performance and will
be discussed further below.
1.1
Temperature
The parameter of temperature has already been studied and found that affects the choice
of metabolic pathways and reactor kinetics. Previous studies have already stated that
under mesophilic conditions the acetate pathway for methanogenesis is selected, while
at thermophilic conditions H2/CO2 are the main precursors for methane production[22].
That happens because of carboxydotrophic hydrogenogens appear higher diversity at el­
evated temperatures when at 37 oC acetogenic bacteria are more abundant[22]. That can
also be explained by the fact that the minimum Gibbs free energy for methanogens to uti­
lize the H2 is two times lower than the one for homoacetogens[23]. Regarding the overall
process the positive impacts on CO conversion and methane productivity by elevating the
process have been presented by several studies. Where 5 times higher CO conversion,
at 60 oC, was achieved compared to the mesophilic conditions even if at higher tem­
peratures the gas­to­liquid mass transfer is poorer[24]. The highest CO conversion was
reported at 55 oC by [25]. While [26] exploring the effect of temperature on reactor per­
formance, achieved, under thermophilic conditions the highest methane producticity until
now in literature. Nonetheless,increasing the temperature, the solubility of the gaseous
substrates decreases, leading to mass transfer limitations under thermophilic operation.
1.2
pH
The pH is also an important parameter that influences the microbial metabolism and the
growhth of the employed cells. Methanogens that are the vital organisms for the biometha­
nation process grow optimally at a range of pH 6.8­8.5 and are partially inhibited in lower
pH values[27]. Hydrogenic bacteria also grow optimal at a neutral pH, while the aceto­

pH decreases[28][29]. Hence considering the optimal growth for the most important cells
to the process and to avoid carbon diverted to byproducts the preferable pH is at 7.
1.3
Syngas Composition
Syngas composition is one of the main parameters that can affect the process of syngas
biomethanation for the following reasons. The partial pressure of the gas components
first and foremost determines the maximum methane content in the exit gas composition.
High excess of carbon­moles will result in unconverted CO2 at the exit of the reactor, while
high excess of electron­moles will give high unconverted H2. Syngas composition can be
described according to [30] by the Syngas Quality Index
SQI = %H2 + %CO
%CO2 + %CO
(1.1)
which shows the ratio of the moles of electron donors to the moles of carbon donors in
the syngas. The percentages are the molar fractions of each component in the inlet gas.
H2 and CO release 2 electron­moles each from the oxidation reaction per C­mole. Sub­
sequently, CH4 as the target product requires 1 C­mole and 2x4 e­moles per C­ mole
resulting in SQI=4 from eq 1.1. That means that inlet syngas composition with SQI=4
satisfies the stoichiometrical ratio of e­moles required per C­mol for the CH4 production
assuming no carbon losses to cell growth, maintenance and byproducts.
Following, although CO can be an excellent energy source in terms of reducing power for
anaerobic microorganisms, a few of them can utilize it as their sole energy source. Hy­
drogenogens, and some acetogens have already been proved that can metabolise CO,
while some methanogens can grow with CO as their sole energy source[31]. However car­
boxyditrophic methanogens are more sensitive and require lower CO partial pressures[32]
.CO conversion efficiency and methane productivity is directly related to the CO partial
partial pressure because of the inhibitory effects on methanogenic mixed cultures[33].
The inhibitory effect of CO on mesophilic mixed culture was investigated by [34]. They
showed that methane productivity at CO partial pressures lower than 0.2 atm was increas­
ing, while after that limit started declining until PCO2 of 1 atm, where it was totally blocked.
H2 is also a source of reducing power in syngas biomethanation and no direct inhibitory
effect has reported until now. On the contrary, the addition of H2 was shown to pro­
mote the hydrohenotrophic methanogenic community on biogas upgrade[35]. However,
[32] observed that adding H2 in the presence of CO lowers the CO consumption rate,
because it was firstly reacting with the bicarbonate, lowering the pH and inhibiting the
activity of CO­consuming microorganisms. Moreover, H2 affects the metabolic interac­
tions in mixed microbial consortium by thermodynamic driven limitations which change
the catabolic routes from hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis towards homoacetogenesis
or SAO.
1.4
Gas residence time
The gas loading rate of 19.68% H2, 9.67% CO2, 55.62% CO and 14.82% Ar was firstly
investigated by [16] in two different sizes of trickle bed reactors, for the performance as­
sessment of biomethanation process in a triculture. Regardless the size of the reactor,
methane productivity was increasing linearly with increasing gas flow rate and the oppo­
site effect was exhibited for the CO conversion. Following ,gas residence time was proven
to affect the selectivity to CH4, diverting the carbon and energy to products. Also, it was
observed to improve the productivity of the system[19]. During the successful scale­up of
a trickle bed reactor by [20] from 180ml to 5000ml the highest reported CH4 productivity

1.5
Liquid recirculation
Another operational parameter that, if it will be taken into account, can improve signif­
icantly the performance of the TBR is the liquid recirculation rate. Unlike many cases
that the biofilm formation is connected to biofouling, in the trickle bed reactors due to
the low organic rate that does not cause problem. The flow through preferential paths
called chanelling is the main drawback, resulting in not evenly distributed biofilm and
lower methane productivities.By increasing the liquid recirculation rate channeling is dis­
rupted and better wetting of the packing material is achieved.[36] assessed optically the
channelling phenomena and the effects on reactor performance when highering the liquid
recirculation rate in a TBR for syngas biomethanation. The results were more uniform
trickling of the liquid and the area available for biofilm formation was increased along with
the conversion efficiencies and the productivity. The only drawback was a slight increase
in VFA concentrations. However, in an effort to explore nutrient supply methods in a
biotricking filter reactor for biogas upgrade. It was observed that highering the liquid flow
rate changes the hydrodynamics and enhances the resistance to mass transfer of the
feed gas making it harder to reach the biofilm[37].
To conclude there are a few efforts on syngas biomethanation in trickle bed reactors. Ev­
ery attempt investigated the effects of different factors like temperature, GRT, liquid recir­
culation, HRT and are summarized in the table. On this study was made an effort of strate­
gic optimization of the conversion efficiency, productivity and the purity in biomethane for
two types of inocula in three identical reactors. Based on literature the three reactors
operated at 55o, atmospheric pressure, pH 7 and an HRT of 20 days. At the beginning,
the reactor was characterized to determine the potential and limits of the gas­liquid mass
transfer. At the next stage the optimum gas composition for the inlet gas was chosen,
at a high GRT, in order to achieve the maximum conversion for each composition and
at the lowest recirculation rate in order have room for mass transfer improvement later.
For the last stage was planned to increase liquid recirculation at the optimum GRT and
progressively pushing the system to lower GRTs and higher recirculation rate for achiev­
ing the maximum productivity along with high conversions and yields. Unfortunately, the
time was restricted and this stage is not included in the report. On the table below are
summarized the results of reaserch on operational parameters for syngas biomethantion
in TBRs.

Objective of the study
The TBR has already been proved a strong candidate to address simultaneously the gas­
to­liquid mass transfer limitations and the low cell concentrations associated with low con­
version efficiencies and low productivities. Hence, the key performance parameters and
operation optimization potential of the TBR was investigated. The experimental design
aims to strategically optimize the performance of the process in terms of exit gas compo­
sition, conversion efficiency, methane yield and productivity affected by induced distur­
bances in feed­gas composition, gas residence time (GRT) and liquid recirculation.

3.1
Inoculum
Two types of thermophilic cultures were employed to inoculate the three reactors. INCO2
used in the duplicates reactors R1 and R2 was derived from a bioreactor for biogas
upgrade.It was propagated in batch bottles with BA­medium and fed continuously with
CO2:H2 (1:4 ratio). The batch bottles were stored an incubator at 55oC for a significant
period. R3 was a reactor on standby for syngas biomethantion. Biomass was already
attached and it was restarted with thermophilic inoculum, INCO from its former operation
at 55oC.
3.2
Nutrient media
Liquid fraction of digestate (LFD) was used as a source of nutrients. The digestate was de­
rived from an industrial­scale anaerobic digester (Solrød Biogas) treating mainly seaweed
and manure and operating at thermophilic conditions. To extract the liquid fraction, the
digestate, which was degassed and stored in the incubator at 55oC, was filtered through
a 2mm and 1mm sieve. In that way, large solids were removed to avoid/reduce potential
clogging of the bed. The Total solids (TS) and Volatile solids (VS) corresponded to 1.07%
± 0.00 and 0.51% ± 0.03 respectively. NH+
4 ­N was measured at 3.25 g/L and regarding
Volatile fatty acids (VFA) concentration Acetate: 206 mg/L ±5.24, Propionate: 55 mg/L
± 1.51, Isobutyric acid: 14.8 mg/L ±0.43, Butyric acid: 3.01 mg/L ±0.09, Isovaleric acid
26.4 mg/L ± 1.05, Hexanoic acid: 1.45 mg/L ± 0.05 .

Reactor configuration
Three identical TBRs were set up. The reactor configuration is presented in figure3.1.The

As already mentioned, the major limitation of a gas fermentation process is the gas­to­
liquid mass transfer, meaning that the diffusion rate of the gases to liquid phase is slower
than gaseous substrate (H2, CO2 and CO) consumption rates by the microbes. As a
result the mass transfer rate is slower than the metabolic biochemical reactions and the
system is not limited by the reaction kinetics but by the mass transfer rate and that yields
to lower substrate conversions. Trickle bed reactor provides with high interfacial area
that facilitates significantly the diffusion of the gaseous substrate to the liquid phase, as
a result higher part of the inlet gas is accessible to the microorganisms. A parameter to
quantify the gas­to­liquid mass transfer is the volumetric mass transfer coefficient (kLa)
from eq.4.5 according to the two film theory [40] where the gas­liquid mass transfer rate
is modeled by describing the flux through each film according to

bulk gas phase and the gas concentration in the bulk liquid phase. Also the gas mass
transfer rate per unit of reactor volume (N) is obtained by multiplying the overall flux (Jo)

4.4
Conclusions
In the present study a successful strategic optimization of syngas biomethanation in a TBR
system was demonstrated and shed light on potential challenges on further optimization
of the system. First and foremost, the benefits of operating the system at an SQI=4 for the
inlet gas composition was confirmed. 80% purity was achieved and full conversion of the
gaseous substrates. The achieved productivity was 2.10 ± 0.29 mmol/lbed/h. Important
outcome from the SQI experiments is, when working at different syngas quality indexes is
the H2 partial pressure that inhibits the system, probably carboxydotrophic hydrogenesis,
and not the partial pressure of CO. When the effect of lowering the GRT investigated
nothing unexpected happened. Specifically, the productivity increased to a maximum of
5.77 ± 0.14 mmol/lbed/h with a drop in the conversion efficiencies from full H2 and CO
conversion at 1.5h to 0.86 ± 0.04 and 0.43 ± 0.01 at 1h. While the productivity was
slightly lower at the higher GRT 4.45± 0.04 mmol/lbed/h. Most important that the series of
the experiments conducted, constitute the baseline for further optimization of the system
since it was operating at the low kLa that reactor is able to give. It should be noted the
increase trend of the kLa values is not the same for CO and hydrogen and that should be
definitely taken into account when increasing the liquid flow.
4.5
Future perspectives
The results that came up from this project were quite promising to encourage further re­
search on the syngas biomethanation field, rendering the TBR configuration an attractive

solution. However, in depth analysis of the evolution of the microbial community during
operation and a thermodynamic analysis would make clearer the reasons of the systems
response on induced disturbances. While a better control system could improve the stud­
ied parameters. In general, considering the maximum achieved productivity of 3.10 l/lbed/d
of the studied system, to reach the methane productivity of an average biogas plant which
produces 3 m3 of methane per day, it will need a reactor 1000 times larger. The main
challenge of the scale up is the high H/D ratio that is required. Moreover that does not
guarantee that the reactor will exhibit the same performance and this is only one of the
main challenges when trying to scale­up. Moreover, a technoeconomic analysis along
with sensitivity analysis to assess the trade­off between conversion and productivity is
suggested, since it will reveal the parameter that affects the profitability of the technol­
