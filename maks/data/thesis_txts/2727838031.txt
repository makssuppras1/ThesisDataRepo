Abstract
Despite the number of significant technologies and developments in the wind energy
industry, there are still several unresolved challenges. One such challenge relates to
minimizing the operation and maintenance (O&M) costs of the wind turbine blades
by implementing maintenance only when necessary. A recent development to tackle
this issue is the integration of Digital Twin technology, through facilitating decision-
making regarding the timing of required O&M activities. This is achieved by virtually
representing an actual wind turbine blade called the digital twin to monitor its current
condition by transferring damages in real-time. Thus, the performance of the physical
blade can be monitored virtually, facilitating decision-making for O&M.
In this project, a novel method for modeling damages from a physical wind turbine
blade to its digital twin is developed. It is assumed that the damage in this case
are complex cracks, available either in an image form or else through non-destructive
techniques (NDT) and the digital representation is a high-fidelity finite element model
developed in ABAQUS. Since wind turbine blades are complex structures having
multiple components like the spar cap, shear web, trailing edge adhesive, etc. The
novel method is further divided into two sub-methods, each with a specific focus and
based on the extended finite element method (XFEM) within ABAQUS. The first
sub-method focuses on modelling complex cracks only on the skin layers of the blade,
whereas the second sub-method focuses on modelling complex cracks in the adhesive
section at the trailing edge. Both the developed sub-method consists of an image
processing tool and a crack construction tool. The image processing tool is used to
analyse the cracks from an image to create a crack geometry. This crack geometry is
then analysed by the crack construction tool to be defined as a level set code in the
output. As the level set code can be integrated with the input file of the original finite
element model, new crack geometries can be updated in real-time without the need
for remodelling the original finite element model. Moreover, since the crack modelling
is based on the XFEM approach, there is no need for the crack geometry to align with
the mesh.
The developed sub-methods are later tested on a case study to check if the level set
code generated is compatible with the XFEM method in ABAQUS. Simulation results
show that the level set code is compatible, and no convergence issues were detected
during the analysis. Furthermore, conducting progressive failure analyses (PFA) on
various crack scenarios allows for a comparison of the global structural response to
assess the structural integrity of the blade in each case. Thus facilitating accurate
decision-making for the O&M activities.
Keywords: XFEM, Fracture modeling, Level set, Finite element modeling, com-
plex crack reconstruction, Progressive failure analysis (PFA), composite wind turbine
blades

Chapter 1
Introduction
In recent years, there have been a number of significant advancements in wind tur-
bine technologies, making it an affordable and clean energy source [1]. Hence, many
countries view Wind Energy as a crucial step toward achieving sustainability and a
greener future which has resulted in a growing demand for the development of wind
farms. Despite the progress made through research and technological developments
in the past few years, there is still a need for further innovative ideas and technologies
as a number of challenges remain unsolved.
For instance, the rotor blades are one of the most critical components of a wind
turbine as throughout its operation phase the rotors are in direct contact with the
harsh environmental conditions and have to sustain major loads from the wind in order
for it to rotate and produce energy. In [2], it is mentioned that the structural health
of each blade in the rotor varies due to different manufacturing defects. Therefore,
it is crucial to inspect each blade individually to ensure proper assessment. As of
now, wind farms are scheduled for operation and maintenance (O&M) inspection in
intervals of roughly 2 to 3 years [2] with additional unscheduled maintenance in the
case of critical damages.
These O&M account for 25-35% of the levelized cost of
energy (LCOE) which is a useful indicator for evaluating the competitiveness of an
energy generation technology [3]. Hence a number of technologies have developed over
the years to reduce the O&M cost for wind farms.
Facilitating Decision-Making for O&M using Digital Twin:
One such technology which has recently been introduced in the wind industry to reduce
the O&M cost is the Digit Twin (DT) [4]. The main objective of introducing DT is to
simulate the structural health of each wind turbine blade to facilitate decisions-making
of when an O&M inspection is necessary. To do this, the DT should be integrated with
the structural health monitoring (SHM) systems [2] such that the actual damage can
be updated in real-time on the DT model facilitating a much more accurate decision.
A closer look at the DT and its functioning is necessary to better understand this
integration.
Figure 1.1 gives a detailed overview of how the DT technology can be integrated
with wind turbine blades. The life cycle of a wind turbine blade consists of three
primary stages: manufacturing, operation and monitoring, and maintenance as il-
lustrated in the figure with a physical blade on the left and its corresponding dig-
ital twin on the right.
As discussed earlier, manufacturing defects have been ob-
served to develop further during the operational phase, leading to a gradual reduc-
tion in the structural integrity of wind turbine blades.
Therefore, communicating
the manufacturing damage during the manufacturing stage itself is crucial. These
manufacturing defects can then be tracked during the operational phase for their

Phantom nodes

the elements are lost, thus displacement and stresses are not possible to compute.
To solve this problem ABAQUS introduced the concept of phantom nodes which are
superimposed on the original node before an element is split. Once the crack slips
through an element, the real nodes and phantom nodes are no longer tied to each
other. Thus the element would now form a combination of real and phantom nodes

3.2.1
Composite material failures
To simulate progressive failure in composites, it is necessary to account for stiffness
degradation of the material when the corresponding failure criteria are met. In this
project, this is done by reducing the material properties of the composite in a specific
direction to zero as the associated failure criteria are reached. This allows for stress
distribution and gradual degrading of the structural integrity of the composite material
until ultimate failure occurs. As previously mentioned regarding dividing the failure
mechanism, the in-plane failures are checked against the Tsai-Wu criteria, while the
out-of-plane failures are checked by the quadratic strain-based failure criteria.
In
ABAQUS, this progressive failure modelling is implemented through a FORTRAN
subroutine, which can be used during simulation [25].

Crack construction tool (with fixed Y-direction)
The aim of the crack construction tool is to generate a code based on the level set
method that represents the crack geometry and allows the user to model it at a specific
location on the skin layer of the FE blade section model. The output of this tool is a
file called levelsetcode.inp, which contains the generated code. The only input required
for this tool is the set of ‚Äėpoints‚Äô defining the crack geometry obtained either from
the image processing tool or equivalent tools. By connecting successive ‚Äėpoints‚Äô with
straight lines, the crack geometry is defined, as shown in figure 4.4(c). Separating this
algorithm from the image processing tool offers the advantage of easily integrating
advanced image processing software in cases where the image processing tool fails to
analyze the crack in the image
To obtain the level set code for the crack geometry, consider an element from the
skin layer 1 with two faces, F1 (upper surface) and F2 (bottom surface), as depicted
in figure 4.2(a). Using the ‚Äėpoints‚Äô C1 and C2 obtained from the image processing
tool, a crack geometry is constructed by connecting these two points with a straight
line, (depicted as a solid brown line in figure 4.2(b)). When the crack geometry passes
through face F1 of an element, two crack planes are generated: crack plane 1 and crack
plane 2. Crack plane 1 is defined by the crack geometry and projected inwards until
it reaches face F2. Crack plane 2, on the other hand, is defined to be perpendicular
to crack plane 1 at the point where crack plane 1 ends (i.e., face F2). Once the crack
planes are generated based on the crack geometry, they are further defined within the
element from each node. Crack plane 1 is represented by the parameter Ōē, while crack
plane 2 is represented by the parameter Ōą for each node, as clearly depicted in the
figure 4.2(b). These Ōē and Ōą values for each node correspond to the signed distance
function values described in the equation 2.7, which form the level set code used in the
input file for ABAQUS. Similarly, if the crack points C1 and C2 pass through two or
more elements, the Ōē and Ōą values are computed separately for each of those elements.
The elements are identified by their element label in the input file for ABAQUS. For
more detailed information on the level set method for XFEM in ABAQUS, refer to
chapter 2.

surface detected in the image. These points are visually represented by red circles,
as shown in figure 4.5 (b).
2) Polyfit degree: The ‚Äėpoints‚Äô placed on the crack surface are governed by the
degree of fit. For a complex crack with curves, it is recommended to increase
the number of points and polyfit degree accordingly. However, there are no pre-
defined values for these parameters, and a trial-and-error approach is typically
used to determine suitable values based on the specific crack geometry.
3) Rescale Z and X range: As described in section 4.1.1, the objective is to extract
the Z and X coordinates of the ‚Äėpoints‚Äô when they are fitted to the crack in the
image. However, the extracted data is initially in the pixel coordinate system and
needs to be transformed into a cartesian coordinate system. This transformation
is done by specifying the rescale Z and X range parameter.
For instance, consider a scenario where a crack needs to be modelled at the top
right corner of the skin surface, as depicted in figure 4.4 (d). To achieve this, the
input to the Rescale Z and X range parameter should be set to a specific range.
In this case, setting the X range from 0.8 m to 1.1 m and the Z range from 22.5 m
to 23.5 m would rescale the crack geometry to the desired location and coordinate
system. By doing so, the crack geometry can be scaled to any preferred location
and size, providing flexibility in the modelling process.
III The crack image from step I is initially converted to a binary image using the
imbinarize function in MATLAB. A binary image consists of pixels with only
two colours, namely black and white. Each black pixel is represented as 0, while
white pixels are represented as 1. This binary image is stored in a matrix format,
with rows and columns, in MATLAB. In this case, the white pixels correspond
to the crack surface, while the black pixels represent the background.
To isolate the crack surface from the binary image, the bwboundaries function
is used. This function separates all the white pixels and extracts the indices of
the boundaries, which are then stored. These boundary indices are depicted in
figure 4.5 (a) and are highlighted in blue.
The stored indices essentially represent the Z and X coordinates of the crack
surface in the image, in the pixel coordinate system. These coordinates are fed
into the polyfit function in MATLAB, along with the parameters Number of points

2) Element type
‚ÄĘ In this case since the skin layer 1 is made up of single elements along its thickness,
it is reasonable to consider the entire element as a triaxial composite laminate.
Hence using the solid homogeneous element C3D8R is valid in this case. How-
ever, when using layered composites that have different material properties in
each layer, the solid composite element would be the preferred choice due to its
ability to model different layers in a single element saving on huge computational
time.
‚ÄĘ This however is not possible when using XFEM as solid composite elements
cannot be used for modelling crack. Thus each layer in the composite lami-
nate should be modelled as a solid homogenous element leading to a significant
increase in computation time.
2) Stationary crack modelling
‚ÄĘ The XFEM method in ABAQUS, as discussed in section 2.2, does not consider
the singularity function. Therefore, in order to analyze crack propagation, either
VCCT or CZM must be defined along with XFEM. For example, if CZM is
selected, crack initiation and crack propagation parameters need to be defined
based on the material type.
‚ÄĘ In composite material, crack initiation and propagation are not as simple as
in homogeneous materials. It is necessary to consider different damage mecha-
nisms such as matrix cracking, fibre kinking, fibre splitting, and fibre tension,
each with its own initiation criteria based on various user-defined strength pa-
rameters.
Similarly, the crack propagation through damage degradation (d)
would also change based on the different mechanisms, which in this project is
not considered. Hence the complex crack is modelled as a stationary crack to
understand how the structural response changes in the presence of pre-damaged
elements due to a crack.
‚ÄĘ ABAQUS does provide the option of defining custom initiation and propagation
criteria using the User Defined Material Damage Initiation (UDMGINI) sub-
routine, allowing users to incorporate various damage mechanics. Hence, the
developed sub-methods would prove to be an efficient tool for crack modelling
in such cases.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
To conclude, a novel method is developed for modeling complex cracks from the
physical blades to its finite element model. Since the complex crack on the physical
blade is available in an image form, the image processing tool is developed. This
tool aims to analyze the crack image and fit ‚Äėpoints‚Äô along the crack surface based on
user-provided inputs.
Although the image processing tool performed well for the complex crack cases in
this project, it is essential to note that its accuracy may vary. The ‚Äėpoints‚Äô fitted on
the crack surface in the crack image are based on a polyfit function, which may not
always provide a precise representation of the crack image for certain complex cases.
In such situations, additional advanced tools like OrginPro, as explained in appendix
B, might prove to be useful. The OriginPro tool is just an example and a similar tool
can be employed as long as the ‚Äėpoint‚Äô representing the crack geometry is obtained.
Further, to map the crack geometry on the FE model the crack construction tool is
developed. Utilizing XFEM for crack propagation analysis, the crack construction
tool represents the crack geometry using a level set code. This approach offers several
advantages, as XFEM models the crack within each element by splitting it into two
parts.
As a result, there is no need for mesh refinement or providing a solution-
dependent path. Hence by just updating the level set code in the original input file
of the FE model the crack geometry can be modelled with the existing mesh and the
need for remodelling or re-meshing is eliminated.
By integrating the image processing tool with the crack construction tool, this project
successfully achieves its first objective of modeling complex cracks from a physical
blade to its FE model.
Furthermore, the utilization of XFEM and defining crack
geometries as level set codes enables real-time updates of crack geometries in the
original input file itself, thereby fulfilling the second objective of the project as well.
To account for different crack types, the novel method developed for crack modelling
is divided into two sub-methods: XFEM crack modelling for the skin layer and XFEM
crack modelling for the adhesive. While both sub-methods use the same two tools,
their internal workings and objectives are different. For XFEM crack modelling on the
skin layer, the primary aim is to model complex cracks on the FE model‚Äôs skin layers.
This method allows crack modeling in all four skin layers and efficiently models various
crack types, including transverse, longitudinal, and other complex cracks. However, it
lacks through-thickness dimension control, which is acceptable for the thin skin layers
in this case but may be limiting for thicker skin layers.
On the other hand, the XFEM crack modelling in the adhesive aims to model cracks in
the adhesive section at the trailing edge. Delamination or debonding in the adhesive
==PAGE:80==
62
Chapter 6. Conclusion
layers are common failure types in wind turbine blades. Thus by provides the capa-
bilities of controlling the through-thickness dimension of the crack through defining
multiple mesh grids. It enables efficient and precise modelling of delamination and
debonding cracks. Although this approach offers an advantage in terms of through-
thickness control, it does have a drawback when it comes to automation. Unlike the
successive sub-method, the points obtained from the image processing tool cannot
be automatically transferred to the crack construction tool. Instead, they must be
manually inputted in a tabular format for each mesh grid.
Based on the conducted case study, both sub-methods produced compatible level set
codes that were successfully integrated with the ABAQUS solver, and the simulation
displayed no convergence issues. However, it was noted that the crack geometries and
dimensions were slightly inaccurate. This is due to the assumption that the crack
would split an element completely.
In addition, the conducted PFA for various crack cases illustrates how the global
structural response can be compared under specific loading conditions, enabling a
better understanding of their impact on the blade‚Äôs structural integrity. Thus the
bigger picture of facilitating decision-making regarding the O&M activities is made
possible.
All thought the work in this project has covered the entire scope, a few points on the
future work for further improvements in the two developed sub-methods can be stated
as follows;
‚ÄĘ Modifying both the developed sub-method to handle the complex transverse and
complex longitudinal cracks within the same code.
‚ÄĘ Modifying the second developed sub-method for the adhesive section to allow
for crack propagation from face F3 to face F2 in the X-Y plane.
‚ÄĘ Link the image processing tool and the crack construction tool for the adhesive
method such that the manual process of entering the ‚Äėpoints‚Äô in a tabular format
is completely automated.
‚ÄĘ Extending the existing developed method to modelling cracks on other blade
components like spar cap, foam layer, etc.
