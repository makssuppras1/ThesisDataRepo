Abstract
GNSS are essential for precise positioning, but their accuracy is compromised in urban
environments due to multipath errors from signal reflections. This thesis examines these
errors using a dual­polarized antenna system along with an attempt to reduce the multi­
path error with tighter chip spacing settings.
The study covers the differences between Galileo and GPS, focusing on multipath errors,
details the inner workings of a GNSS receiver and the origins of the multipath errors.
Experiments with various reflectors, brick, metal and glass walls, using a dual circularly
polarized GNSS antenna compare multipath errors in GNSS signals focusing primarily on
the L1 and L5 bands. In the analysis a comparison of the constellations GPS and Galileo
is also included. A software receiver is used to assess the differences in chip spacings in
correlation functions to investigate the effect of a tight chip spacing.
The results show that dual­polarized antennas can effectively measure the strength of
multipath errors due to the shift in polarization. These antennas provide valuable infor­
mation about the presence and intensity of multipath signals. By optimizing chip spacing
settings, GNSS receivers can achieve higher reliability and accuracy, especially in chal­
lenging urban environments where multipath effects are more prevalent.
Key findings include the increased strength of multipath signals and the varied reflection
patterns of different materials, with metal and brick showing overall linear reflections and
glass showing random reflection patterns. The general signal geometry is also discussed
for the measurement locations. The overall settings for the software receiver are dis­
cussed, and how it can be improved in the future. Suggestions for future research include
methods for calculating the distance between an antenna and a reflector and determining
material properties based on multipath.
The project concludes with that it is possible to reduce the multipath error by minimizing the
chip spacing in a software receiver, thus it should also work in a normal GNSS receiver.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Relative carrier­phase based Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) positioning can
be used as a highly accurate method to determine precise locations. However, this tech­
nique is susceptible to various error sources [2]. One of these is the multipath error, which
is the focus of this project. Multipath occurs especially in urban environments, where the
signal will be able to reflect off buildings before being processed by a GNSS receiver.
These buildings are made of different materials, where the signals will be affected in dif­
ferent ways depending on materialistic properties [3, p. 444 ­ 447]. Multipath affects
positioning accuracy, and while there are many causes of errors, multipath is considered
the most significant. As multipath is site­dependent for the receiver, this removes the
possibility of most real­time applications [4].
As cities become bigger and buildings become taller, multipath has a bright future. The
typical scenario in which a user experiences multipath is in an urban environment, us­
ing their smartphone for navigation. Some may have experienced sudden jumps in their
position. This may have been the result of multipath.
In order to analyze the effect of multipath in urban environments, this study has focused
on data collection using a dual polarized GNSS antenna. By capturing both polarizations,
it is possible to differentiate between direct and reflected signals [5], providing more infor­
mation on the behavior of multipath. Furthermore, the study will compare the multipath
effects on different GNSS signals and constellations, namely the American Global Posi­
tioning System (GPS) and the European Galileo. These constellations have signals with
different characteristics and frequencies, which may have varying susceptibility to multi­
path.
Moreover, a GNSS software receiver will be used to potentially reduce and mitigate mul­
tipath through various settings. Software receivers offer the flexibility to process signals
and test different algorithms and settings, which makes them an invaluable tool for this
research.
This study will perform the analysis and possibly mitigation of multipath in a static setting.
This is to develop a deep understanding of the different materials causing reflections and
what possible dependencies there are for strong multipath.

Chapter 2
Theory
The following chapter introduces the theoretical concepts used in the study.
The first part will introduce the basic concepts of GNSS. In this part, the constellations
and signals used will be explained, including how a position is calculated using GNSS
and which error sources to consider when calculating this position. In the next part, the
inner workings of how a GNSS receiver will be explained, as well as the basic concepts
of how satellites are acquired and tracked. In the final part, the multipath error source will
be explained in­depth and how it affects the received signal.
2.1
Global Navigation Satellite System
GNSS signals are electromagnetic waves propagating at the speed of light. The frequen­
cies of these signals are in the radio spectrum, between approximately 1.2 and 1.6 GHz
[3, p. 3­10], which is a part of the L­band [6]. These frequencies are characterized as
being able to resist interference from most weather conditions [6]. While it is true that
some atmospheric effects cannot be disregarded, this has led to the adoption of multiple
frequencies, a topic that will be elaborated on in section 2.1.4.2.
Currently, there are four major GNSS constellations, and two regional GNSS constella­
tions, of which GPS was the first system to be implemented [7]. The frequencies of the
signal bands may be close to other signals or even of the same frequency; however, they
may then use different modulations [8]. The overlapping frequencies can be seen in fig­
ure 2.1, which shows the frequencies for GPS, Galileo and the Russian Global Navigation
Satellite System (GLONASS).

CHAPTER 2. THEORY
An important feature of all GNSS signals is the modulation of the harmonic radio wave with
a characteristic Pseudo­Random Noise (PRN) code [3, p. 3­10]. These codes are essen­
tially a binary sequence without an obvious pattern, which ensures that the given signal
can be tracked, even if the signals transmitted from different satellites share the same
frequency [3, p. 3­10]. This concept will be explained in more detail in section 2.1.1.1.
2.1.1
GNSS constellations and signals
In this study, it was essential to narrow the focus from analyzing all constellations to an
examination of only two specific constellations. The two constellations of interest have
been chosen to be GPS and Galileo. GPS is of great interest due to the modernized L5
signal band for civil use and the legacy signal L1, which gives a great basis for comparison.
Since the Galileo constellation uses a different modulation on the E1 signal [3, p. 251],
it is also an interesting constellation to compare with the GPS constellation, where it is
expected to see a reduction in the multipath strength.
2.1.1.1
GPS (L1, L2 and L5)
The GPS constellation consists nominally of 24 satellites in a circular orbit with an orbital
radius of 26559 km. Currently, there are 31 active satellites, and it is possible for the
constellation to have up to 32 satellites active at the same time. This redundancy is used to
ensure global coverage, even if there are failures on some of the satellites. Satellite orbits
are inclined 55° with respect to the equatorial plane. There exist six orbital planes, with
four satellites in each of them for the nominal setup of the constellation. This configuration
of the constellation ensures that there is always a minimum of four satellites in view when
a receiver is trying to compute a position. The revolution period for each satellite is 11
hours and 58 minutes [3, p. 197­216].
The GPS satellites have undergone continuous development since their launch in 1978
[3, p. 197­216], and have increasingly added better clocks and more signals, among
other implementations. This development can be seen in table 2.1, where it can also be
seen that the L5 signal has been introduced in block IIF. Currently, L5 is not available on
all satellites that are operational today, but it broadcasts from 18 satellites at the time of
writing this report [9].

The PRN code for each signal is generated at 1.023 Mchips/s and belongs to the Gold
family. A unique PRN code is used for each signal type transmitted by each satellite,
to distinguish the received signal between the available satellites. For the PRN code

The Galileo constellation nominally consists of 30 satellites, to ensure that a minimum of

2.1.4.3
Receiver noise
The receiver noise is a complex error generated in the receiver while measuring satellite
signals. It covers a broad spectrum of noise types, including noise introduced by system
components (such as cables, antennas, etc.) and signal quantization noise, among other
factors to increase receiver noise [18].
Receiver noise is considered white noise and cannot be completely avoided. With newer
equipment, modern receiver technologies estimate that this is about 0.1 to 1% of a cycle
in the carrier phase and up to centimeters in pseudorange measurements [18].
2.1.4.4
Multipath
Multipath errors are caused by the signal arriving in different ways at the antenna. Its prin­
cipal cause is the antenna being close to reflecting objects. The receiver will experience
higher multipath errors from a low­altitude satellite.
The errors caused by multipath will be different for different frequencies and will have
an effect on both the carrier­phase measurements and the code measurements. In the
code measurement, there will be a theoretical upper limit of 1.5 times the wavelength of
the chip3. In the carrier­phase measurements, it will have a theoretical upper limit of a
quarter of the wavelength [19].
This explanation will not suffice for this study; therefore, the multipath error source will be

2.2.2
Tracking of signal
To track a signal, several measures are necessary to ensure the accuracy and stability
of the tracking process. Tracking is based on the generation of an internal replica of the
received signal. The replica is generated itself by several code and carrier Numerically­
Controlled Oscillators (NCO). The NCO counters are converted into the code and carrier
pseudoranges and the Doppler, which are passed together with the signal power to the
navigation processor. If the signal power drops below a certain threshold, the channel
declares a loss of lock and will restart with the acquisition. During the tracking phase, the
channel synchronizes the navigation message in the signal and decodes it.
Figure 2.6 illustrates the schematic block diagram of a generic GNSS receiver, focusing on
the internal functions of a signal processing channel. The signal is received at an antenna,
which are then filtered, amplified and downconverted to an intermediate frequency by the
RF signal chain in the front end. Then it is applied to an analog­to­digital conversion at
the end of the RF front end. In a channel, digital processing starts with the detection of
a signal, which is explained in section 2.2.1, where coarse estimates of code delay and
Doppler of the signal are determined. The channel then switches to code and carrier
tracking, to refine the estimates in a feedback structure [3, p. 402­440].
After the coarse Doppler estimates and the code delay are estimated, the signal tracking
will obtain fine estimates of the signal parameters. Within the signal tracking, PLL is used
for carrier­phase tracking and Delay Locked Loop (DLL) is used for code delay tracking
[3, p. 402­440], which are the two tracking loops that will be focused in this study.
The main purpose of a signal tracking loop is to adjust the input of local replica signal
generators to match the incoming signal. This is done by adjusting the input of the NCO,
which is the rate of change of the signal parameter of interest. The signal tracking loops
are implemented in a feedback system, which is closed by the NCO. The single tracking
loop in a single channel of a GNSS receiver is composed of correlators, discriminators,
loop filters and NCOs for the code and carrier tracking loops [3, p. 402­440].

2.3
Multipath error
Signals broadcast from a given GNSS satellite will be received on a large fraction of the
globe. Specifically, the fraction visible to the satellite. The signal illuminates all given
objects in the vicinity of a GNSS receiver, which makes the signal able to bounce off the
surface of the objects, simultaneously with the signal received by the GNSS receiver. The
received signal is therefore a composite signal, which is the combination of a direct signal
and multiple other reflected or refracted signals. This may lead to receiver tracking errors
and can cause errors in both code and carrier­phase measurements [3, p. 443­468].

2.3.3.2
Effect on Signal­to­Noise ratio
In static GNSS applications, carrier­phase multipath errors will typically show themselves
as sinusoidal functions in the SNR, with the theoretical maximum amplitude being of a
quarter of the observed wavelength. The amplitude will primarily depend on the reflective
surface causing the multipath, where objects close to the antenna will cause multipath
errors present in the SNR with a short period. Conversely, distant objects will cause a
multipath error with a long period [4].
As described earlier, each reflected signal has a relative carrier phase offset. Tracking
errors due to multipath are maximized when the carrier phase offset is 0° and 180°. Mul­
tipath interference will be constructive when the carrier phase offset is between ­90° and
90°, and will be destructive otherwise [32, p. 245­257]. This constructive and destructive
signal interference is what causes the error in the correlation function in figure 2.15a, and
this ultimately results in an error present in the SNR [3, p. 443­468].

impact of outliers and errors in individual measurements, providing a more robust estimate
of the position. Using the median rather than the mean reduces the influence of extreme
values, which is particularly important in the presence of multipath effects and ionospheric
delays.

3.4
Multipath mitigation
To mitigate multipath, is not as simple as mitigating other error sources. Since the mul­
tipath effect occurs only at the receiver, some algorithms like DGPS will not be able to
remove the error source, as this will only remove similar error sources for both receivers,
where multipath will not be one of them.
In order to combat the errors caused by multipath, different multipath mitigation technolo­
gies have been introduced. This includes the design of the L5 signal band using a faster
chip rate and the Galileo constellation using the CBOC modulation [37].
This project does not utilize all of the methods explained in this section. However, it is
deemed important to have knowledge of different mitigation techniques.
3.4.1
Post processed multipath mitigation
There are several applications in which multipath is unavoidable, and the most challenging
scenario is the need to mitigate multipath in a dynamic setting, especially if the mitigation
is needed in real time. The simplest technique involves an elevation­angle dependent
measurement weighting in a least­squares solution or in a Kalman filter. However, this
will not remove all multipath signals, as it is not only low­elevation satellites that will result
in multipath [3, p. 455 ­ 459].
For situations where mitigation is not required in real time, different techniques can be

3.4.2
Antenna configuration
One of the mature technologies to mitigate multipath is the choke ring antenna. The de­
sign idea is to reduce the antenna’s low elevation angle and backward gain by design
choices for the antenna pattern. This is done to ensure that the gain of the receiving an­
tenna in the direction of the reflected signal is small, resulting in a reduction of the number
of reflected signals in the composite signal. This antenna design will also effectively sup­
press the reflected signals from below ground level [37]. However, the design of a typical
choke ring antenna is not designed with portability in mind. The first design of the choke
ring antennas was with an outer diameter of 36 cm with a typical weight of more than 4

3.4.3
Chip spacing in receiver
The final method, and the method that will be used in this study, is to decrease the spac­
ing between the early and late branches used in the code discriminator, as explained in
section 2.2.2. In [3, p. 455 ­ 459] it is proposed to use two early and two late correla­
tor functions in the receiver settings discriminator function, which will decrease the error
caused by multipath. This method is called the double­delta correlator, since it effectively
consists of the difference of two differences.
The typical early to late branch spacing in the correlator of a GNSS receiver is set to be
0.5 chips. If the correlation interval of the correlator is smaller, the early to late branch will
be closer to the peak value of the correlation. This should result in a multipath error that is
mitigated to a maximum of 1/10 of the original value in the normal receiver settings [37].

for the narrow early and late branches. The wide setting uses a 0.8 chip spacing for the
early and late branches and a 0.3 chip spacing for the narrow early and late branches.
The tight setting uses a 0.2 chip spacing for the early and late branches and a 0.1 chip
spacing for the narrow early and late branches.

and, crucially for this study, the ability to modify otherwise static constants, such as chip
spacing in the early and late branches. The software receiver is based on GNU Radio
[49], which is a well­established framework that provides the signal processing runtime
and processing blocks to implement software radio applications [47].

to adapt the receiver to any front­end or data file that is used. The processing output
can then be stored in a RINEX file. For this project, the software receiver was used to

In this chapter, various aspects of the results and the process are discussed. The dis­

The first part will examine the dependency of the elevation angle on the strength of mul­

are discussed. In the second part, the geometry of the composite signal is further com­
mented on. In the third part, there will be a brief discussion of the use of the Brewster
angle.
In the fourth part, the use of the software receiver will be discussed. This includes the

Finally, in the sixth and seventh part, some thoughts on various aspects that have been

6.1
Elevation angle dependency
Throughout the result section, elevation conditions for the selected satellites have been
mentioned. As described in section 2.3.1.1, a general observation of the multipath strength
is that it will increase as the elevation angle decreases. This is further shown through a
clean measurement from the Galileo satellites, seen in table 5.3, where larger maximum
signal values appear when the elevation angle decreases. The maximum values for the
best elevation conditions are much lower than the maximum values for the worst elevation
conditions. Therefore, the values indicate that the multipath errors vary depending on the
elevation angle. The same effect applies to the azimuth when dealing with non­horizontal
reflectors, which is true for this study. Therefore, it is obvious that the position of the satel­
lite is very relevant for the strength of the multipath from a given reflector. This introduces
some aspects which must be considered from this study.
The first aspect is the strength of a given reflector. Although table 2.5 introduces that
different materials will attenuate the signal differently, depending on the reflection angle,
it is also true that the strength depends both on the azimuth and the elevation angle of
the satellite. Since this study aims to analyze the difference between materials in an ur­
ban environment, it has been crucial to be able to assume the same multipath conditions.
However, this assumption is challenged by the differing elevation and azimuth angles of
the chosen satellites. This will affect the strength of the reflected signal, but it must be
noted that it should still be possible to notice a pattern in the reflected signal. The cho­
sen satellites are also chosen to mostly reflect similar azimuth and elevation conditions;
however, it has not been possible to compare satellites with the same conditions for every
scenario.
When calculating a spectrogram, it should reveal various reflection patterns. If one reflec­
tor exhibits a clear linear reflection, it is likely that other reflectors will also display similar

6.2
Signal characteristics
In section 2.1.1 the signal bands for both GPS and Galileo are presented. It is noted
that the legacy bands L1 and L2 are modulated with BPSK. They are therefore excellent
signals for comparison, given that wavelength influences performance in multipath envi­
ronments. This will be true because of the Fresnel zones explained in section 2.3.1.1. If
the wavelength changes, so will the size of the first Fresnel zone, and therefore the wave­
length will define how much energy is reflected on the incidence surface. Thus, even if the
modulations of L1 and L2 are the same, there will be a difference in the calculated errors
between the signal bands. L5 also has the BPSK modulation, but with a higher code rate,
being 10 times faster than the L1 signal. Throughout the results, L5 has proven itself to
be better at mitigating multipath, especially compared to L1.
The comparison of Galileo and GPS in table 5.1 attempts to compare the two constella­
tions. There is an issue with this comparison, due to the discussion earlier regarding the
position of satellites. The chosen satellites do not have the same elevation or azimuth
conditions. Because of this statement, the comparison is very difficult to make. Through­
out the presentation of the results, different aspects of both signal modulations can be
seen. One of the major comparison results does not happen in estimating the multipath
error estimates in themselves. This happens when the SNR effects based on distances
are presented in section 5.4.2. Figures 5.19 and 5.20 both show promising results for mul­
tiple issues. The most interesting result for this happens for the brick wall. The Galileo
signal bands do not have a strong 10m (blue) signal in the image; however, the GPS im­
ages do. This will suggest that the Galileo signal modulations are better at mitigating the
reflected signal due to the different variations of BOC modulation. This comparison, once
again, is not going to be a perfect comparison as the elevation and azimuth angles are
not exactly the same. The satellites used in section 5.4.2 are very similar, but not directly
equivalent. These small differences may do more than one expects to the composite sig­
nal, as the geometries at the different measurement locations are not clean, thus minor
changes in the satellite position can in fact change many elements.

Geometry of composite signal
The geometry of the locations, which can be seen in section 3.1, is not as simple as first
assumed. The different locations have different geometries, but in this part of the discus­
sion, the brick wall will be taken as an example. As seen in figure 6.1, the direct signal
(black line) can travel directly to the antenna. However, since the satellite illuminates the
entire area with its transmitted signal, any object can reflect the signal. The initial analysis
only assumes that there is a reflected component from the brick wall (red line). The entire
composite signal will contain many more components than the reflected component from
the brick wall. In fact, any object large enough to hold the first Fresnel zone and is in view
of the antenna, may contribute to a reflected component in the composite signal. This
includes the lamp post (green line) and the car (blue line). Figure 6.1 do not include all
components in the composite signal and will serve only as a simple illustration. For any
given satellite in the sky, this may be true.

be reflected signals from somewhere in the measurement, possibly from the ground. The
same can be seen in the spectrogram in figure 5.20 for the L1 in the brick wall. The blue

[50]. This would normally not be an issue; however, in this study it has been, if not all

improper values that cause RTK­LIB to think that it is not possible to calculate a position
with the given information.

6.6
Chip spacing settings
The software receiver allows the user to configure the chip spacing of the early and late
branches. As explained in section 2.2.2, the traditional GNSS receivers will use a 0.5­
chip spacing between the early and late correlators. The spacing between the early and
late replica is what allows any given amount of error to slip into the receiver. Narrowing
this chip spacing will in theory reduce the maximum allowed error caused by multipath,
due to the tracking error and the correlation error caused by multipath, which is shown in
figures 2.15a and 2.15b. As illustrated in figure 5.32, the choice of chip spacing has an
impact on the amount of multipath error estimates. It is especially present for the L1 band,
where it is seen that tight settings will, in fact, decrease the multipath error estimates.
Improvements can also be seen when inspecting position calculations, mainly for the L1
signal. This is very present in figures 5.26, 5.27 and 5.30 where all three different settings
have been used. The wide settings perform the worst, while the tight settings perform the
best in terms of stability of the position calculated.
In this study, only three different settings have been tested, as can be seen in table 3.2.
It has been chosen not to adjust the settings further than the given values to ensure the
best possible results. This may be improved according to each situation, as each reflect­
ing surface (and urban environments in general) will give a different composite signal. The
chip settings may be seen as a possible weighted variable. If more of the signal is desired
to be computed, then the wide settings can be used. This is most relevant in situations
where a clean measurement can be obtained, but it is also valuable for approximating po­
sitions in environments with significant multipath interference. This will most likely cause
a worse position, but that may be deemed more important than a precise position in some
situations.

6.8
Determining materialistic properties
During the analysis, it has been discovered that different materials will have different char­
acteristics depending on their reflection coefficients or attenuation factors. The difference
or ratios in RHP and LHP SNR data will possibly be able to determine the materialistic
properties of certain materials. Figure 5.12 shows the ratios between the two polarisa­
tions. In this figure, it is possible to see that the brick ratio is the lowest value for all signal
bands, thus causing the strongest reflections. The second strongest reflector is the metal
reflector, whereas the clear measurement and glass reflector are very similar. Note that
this is possibly also caused by differences in the elevation conditions of the satellites.
However, it seems rather constant for the ratios and there does not seem to be a clear
indication of an elevation dependency. The spectrograms in figures 5.13 and 5.14 show
different patterns for the materials glass and brick. These patterns will change depending
on the material that causes the reflections. Therefore, this study suggests further explo­
ration of the potential to determine certain material properties using GNSS measurements
by leveraging the multipath error source.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This project can in broad terms be divided into two subprojects. The first part has focused
on data analysis, where the impact of different signal modulations and material properties
has been the focus of the multipath error. The second part has focused on utilizing a
software receiver to reduce multipath errors.
Three different materials have been analyzed to determine the potential strength of the
multipath errors caused. The three materials have been glass, metal, and brick buildings,
in order to analyze the data as if it were in a typical urban environment. Through this
analysis, the strongest reflective material has been found to be the brick building. From
this project alone, it is not possible to confirm these results completely, as there is a strong
dependence of the position of the satellite, and also the effects of the non­simple geom­
etry of the measurement locations. It has been shown that it has not been possible to
assume a simple geometry, as several signals have been present in the data.
The signals compared have been for two constellations, GPS and Galileo. The GPS
satellites use the BPSK modulation at different chip rates, and the Galileo satellites use
different variants of the BOC modulation. Through this study, it has been shown that in­
creasing the chip rate for the BPSK modulation has been crucial in reducing the multipath
errors present for the GPS satellites. There is also a great difference in the amount of
multipath errors present for Galileo compared to GPS, which is due to the different mod­
ulation. The BOC modulation has been proven to also reduce multipath errors, as the
different signal bands from Galileo in general have less multipath present than GPS. The
study has also shown a difference for the signals in terms of wavelength.
With the chosen software receiver, GNSS­SDR, it has been possible to reduce multipath
errors for the L1 signal band. The tighter chip spacing settings utilized in this project have
not mitigated the multipath but have been proven to reduce the multipath. The use of
different chip spacings has been discussed and it has been deemed that there is not one
perfect setting of chip spacings. A tighter chip spacing will reduce the error; however,
a wider chip spacing will be able to give more signal in a clean scenario. Therefore, a
receiver with a dynamic chip spacing setting will be a good all­round GNSS receiver.
7.1
Future work
While significant progress has been made in this project, several areas for future work
have been identified.
In order to properly analyze the effect of multipath in different constellations, a compara­
ble elevation and azimuth angle is required. Therefore, it is suggested to implement an
algorithm to predict the time for a given position for a satellite based on a given position for
a receiver. This will make it possible for an analysis of comparable elevation and azimuth
conditions between Galileo and GPS to properly analyze the effect of the different mod­
ulations. This should be a rather simple approach using almanac data for the satellites
given in [33].
This project has only performed an analysis for static data. The findings of the reduction

In order to confirm the assertions regarding a dependency of elevation and azimuth an­
