Abstract
In the new design norm for cathodic protection of offshore wind structures, ISO 24656:2022,
sea currents are granted large influence on design values, but are evaluated with a sim-
plified approach. It is therefore of great interest to investigate how time variation of sea
currents, and hydrodynamic effects from water flow around cylindrical structures, affect
the corrosion protection of structures, including the formation of protective calcareous de-
posits.
These effects are investigated through a methodical series of experiments in still water,
constant flow, and lastly in a cyclic combination of the two. The experiments are con-
ducted in synthetic seawater using cylindrical steel specimens with a diameter of 30 mm
and height of 30 mm. The potential of the steel specimens are kept at -1000 mV (Ag/AgCl)
for both 6 and 11 days with continues logging of current density. Formed calcareous de-
posits are inspected visually for influence of hydrodynamic effects and analysed in SEM-
EDS for insights on micro structure and chemical composition.
It is found that time variation of flow is of influence with regards to formation of calcare-
ous deposits and the protective effect of these. Flow speeds are found to be of influence
with regards to the initial electrical current demand for ICCP. The formation of calcareous
deposits on cylindrical specimens exposed to low flow speeds are found to show clear
influence of hydrodynamic effects.

1
Introduction
On the 24th of April 2023 nine European countries signed the OSTEND DECLARATION
ON THE NORTH SEAS AS EUROPE’S GREEN POWER PLANT. With this agreement
the countries committed to a target for expanding the existing 30 GW offshore wind in the
North Sea to at least 120 GW by 2030 and at least 300 GW by 2050 [1].
These plans of expanding the capacity of offshore wind in the North Seas will involve
structures placed at locations with more challenging conditions than normal. This calls
for more research in order to design optimised structures with long life time and minimum
environmental impact.
There are several different types of foundations used for offshore wind turbines. The
choice of foundation depends on water depth, seabed composition, and other factors. The
structural concept known as monopiles is used for more than 80% of the wind turbines
installed in the North Sea [2]. A monopile consists of a single large steel pipe driven
into the seabed. Atop of the monopile a transition piece is installed which serves as
connection to the actual wind turbine tower. Corrosion protection of these structures is
done by means of Impressed Current Cathodic protection (ICCP). Normally, anodes for
ICCP are installed on the transition piece but for deeper waters, or severe environments,
additional ICCP systems are needed near the seabed.
Figure 1.1: Principal drawing of a monopile founded in the seabed offshore.

Problem Statement
2.1
Background and Motivation
The new design norm for cathodic protection of offshore wind structures, ISO 24656:2022,
states strict requirements with regards to the influence of sea currents (flow currents) [3].
Even though the requirements leads to more conservative design values for electrical
current densities than previous norms, both sea currents and the related effects are ap-
proached in a simplified way. The norm acknowledge the variation of flow speed around
cylindrical structures due to hydrodynamic effects, but these complex effects are taken
into account by adding a simple factor of 1.5 as an ”engineering approach”.
Despite the large cyclic time variations in sea currents, caused by tidal currents, the norm
regulates solely based on average flow speeds and hereby neglect the influence of time
variation.
As sea currents are granted large influence on design values, but evaluated with a simpli-
fied approach, it is of great interest to investigate how time variation of sea currents, and
hydrodynamic effects from water flow around cylindrical structures, affect the corrosion
protection of these, including the formation of protective calcareous deposits. In today’s
world of engineering with strong simulations tools available this might serve as input for
more advanced simulations in future design norms.
2.2
Project Content
This project investigates the basic principles related to development of protection and
formation of calcareous deposits on cylindrical steel structures with impressed current
cathodic protection exposed to seawater flow. The aim is to contribute to a better un-
derstanding of the effect involved in this complex phenomena. The project continues the
extensive work performed in series of master projects [4], [5], [6], [7] working with a cus-
tom build flume setup at DTU.
The content of this project is to:
• Investigate the influence of flow speed and of periods without flow, within a tidal-like
flow cycle, with regards to current demand for ICCP and formation of calcareous
deposits. This is done through a methodical series of experiments both without
flow, in constant flow, and lastly in a cyclic combination of the two.
• Furthermore, it is studied whether the formation of visual calcareous deposits is influ-
enced by hydrodynamic flow effects around the cylindrical test specimen (diameter
of 30 mm).
• The formed calcareous deposits are examined with regards to micro structure and
chemical composition.

3
Theory
3.1
Corrosion
In the book Corrosion and protection the Norwegian corrosion scientist Einar Bardal de-
scribes corrosion as:
“An attack on a metallic material by reaction with its environment” [8].
This work will only focus on wet corrosion which is the corrosion of metals in a conducting
liquid (electrolyte).
3.1.1
Corrosion Mechanism
The corrosion process is an electrochemical reaction consisting of two half-cell reactions,
an anode and a cathode reaction. At the anode site metal is dissolved in an oxidation
reaction and free electrons are produced, as seen in reaction 3.1. These electrons are
conducted through the metal to the cathode site where they are used in a reduction reac-
tion [8]. The mechanism is illustrated in figure 3.1:
Figure 3.1: Wet corrosion of a divalent metal M in an electrolyte containing oxygen [8].
The dominant cathode reaction under normal conditions in seawater, and most other nat-
ural environments, is oxygen reduction as seen in reaction 3.2. Under certain conditions
the hydrogen developing reaction 3.3 will however take over.
Anode : M →M2+ + 2e−
(3.1)
Cathode : O2 + 2H2O + 4e−→4OH−
(3.2)
Cathode, alternative : 2H+ + 2e−→H2
(3.3)
The conditions where hydrogen development will be dominant are in solutions with low
pH where H+ ions will be present in abundance and in solutions with little or no oxygen
available [8].
In order to be able to quantify the tendency for a certain metal to oxidise, the concept
half-cell potential or electrode potential has been introduced. The idea is to choose a
certain electrode reaction, and measure half-cell potentials with reference to this. This is
normally referred to as the potential of an element. Unless anything else is stated, the
potentials in the following work will be given solely with reference to a Ag/AgCl reference
electrode measured in seawater.

3.1.2
Factors Influencing Corrosion
3.1.2.1
Oxygen
Oxygen reduction is one of the main cathode reactions and the corrosion rate is therefore
influenced by the amount of dissolved oxygen in the solution and the diffusion of oxygen
towards the cathode site.
3.1.2.2
Temperature
Temperature influences the corrosion process in multiple ways. Higher temperature will
lead to faster reaction kinetics which increase corrosion rate. It will also lead to lower
levels of dissolved oxygen in water which can decrease corrosion rate. The overall effect
in typical conditions is however that higher temperature leads to higher corrosion rates
[8].
3.1.2.3
Water Flow
An increased flow will help supply oxygen to the metal surface, leading to higher cathodic
reaction rates. Higher flow velocities can further cause erosion of surface deposits or
oxide layers, which is expected to increases anode reaction rate [8].
3.1.2.4
Salinity
The presence of dissolved salts in the electrolyte highly influences the corrosion rate.
Higher chloride concentrations leads to decreased oxygen solubility water which cause
lower cathode reaction rate. For lower concentrations this effect is however overruled by
the effect of increased conductivity, which is expected to increases anode reaction rate.
A maximum corrosion rate is found at salinity just above 3% [8]. This is more or less
corresponding to the salinity in the North Sea, as this is 3.3 wt% on average [9].
3.1.2.5
pH
pH is a factor of great influence with regards to corrosion in aqueous solution as it affects
the equilibrium potentials of most reactions and the solubility of different salts. In general
lower pH leads to higher corrosion rates and higher pH leads to lower corrosion rate.
The lower corrosion rates at pH>10 is explained to be due to passivation and partly to
formation diffusion-limiting surface deposits [8].
3.1.2.6
Langelier Saturation Index
The Langelier Saturation Index (LSI) is a measure of the saturation of calcium carbonate
in a solution and thereby a indicator of the solutions ability to precipitate calcium carbon-
ate. The precipitate is dependent on different factors including pH, total alkalinity, and
dissolved substances in the water. LSI is expressed as [8]:
LSI = pH–pHs
(3.4)
pH refers to the actual pH of the water, and pHs is the pH value at saturation with respect
to CaCO3. The saturation pH for CaCO3 in seawater can be derived from Hoover’s align-
ment chart in appendix A.2, based on temperature, pH, calcium content, and carbonate
hardness.
A positive LSI means that the water is supersaturated with calcium carbonate which can
precipitate.

3.1.3
Polarization
During a corrosion reaction the electrode reactions 3.1 and 3.2 are usually shifted away
from equilibrium. This results in a potential shift away from equilibrium potential. This
deviation from equilibrium is denoted polarization and the electrode is hereby polarized
[8].
3.1.3.1
Concentration Polarization
The equilibrium potential is dependent on the available concentration of the reactants tak-
ing part in the reaction. This means that the mass transport within the solution can have
a significant influence on corrosion rate. In case the concentration of a reactant becomes
the controlling parameter for a corrosion process, the potential shifts away from equi-
librium is called concentration polarization. This is often the case with oxygen diffusion
through a solution, to the cathode reaction [8].
3.1.3.2
Resistance Polarization
Surface layers or calcareous deposits on metal surfaces may result in an ohmic resis-
tance. This is the case for oxides on eg. aluminium and for calcareous deposits on steel.
Due to the resistance, an ohmic drop will occur when a current passes the surface layer.
This is know as resistance polarization, as it changes the potential of the metal. Based
on ohms law the size of the ohmic drop (η) is given by:
η = R · I
(3.5)
with R being resistances of the layer and I the current. The ohmic potential drop is also
known as a IR-drop and can be used to describe the electrical properties of the calcareous
deposits [8].

3.1.4
Pourbaix Diagram
As pH have a huge influence on potentials, the Belgian scientist Marcel Pourbaix have
made the so called Pourbaix diagram which are pH/potential diagrams for different metals
under different conditions. These are graphical illustrations of Nernst equation and hereby
gives a qualitative illustration of what is thermodynamically possible at a given pH and
potential.
Figure 3.2 shows the Pourbaix diagram for iron in water. The regions, passivity, corrosion
and immunity indicates the states of iron at different conditions. The dashed lines, e and
f that seperates the diagram in three regions are the stability zones of water from the
Pourbaix diagram for water (see appendix A.1). It is noted that for practical applications
a certain over-potential is recognised, meaning that the potential of a steel surface needs
to be more negative than the value given in the Pourbaix diagram, for a certain reaction
to take place.
As seen on the diagram in figure 3.2, iron have three zones being: corrosion, immunity,
and passivisation. In the immunity and passivisation zone, corrosion rate is reduced to a
bare minimum and the iron can be considered protected.

3.1.5
Cathodic Protection
The principle of Cathodic Protection is to lower the electrode potential of a metal down
into to the immune region of the Pourbaix diagram in order to hinder corrosion [8].
This can be done in two ways:
• By connecting the metal to a less noble metal (denoted sacrificial anodes) that then
will act as anode in a galvanic cell. This method is know as Galvanic Anode Cathodic
Protection (GACP).
• By means of a external power source exposing the metal to a impressed current
and thereby creating a electrochemical cell with the metal that needs protection as
the cathode. The metal is hereby polarized cathodically. This method is known as
Impressed Current Cathodic Protection (ICCP).
This work will focus solely on protection of offshore steel structures with ICCP.
The principle of this setup is illustrated below:
Figure 3.3: Principle setup for ICCP of a metal structure in seawater [10].

decomposition of water or oxidising of dissolved chloride ions [10]:

3.1.5.1
Purpose and Requirements
In the book Cathodic Protection: Theory and Practice the author B.S. Wyatt describes
the requirement of cathodic protection systems for offshore structures as:
“The requirement of a cathodic protection system is to provide sufficient current density
to all submerged and buried components of a offshore structure to prevent corrosion
without excessive over-protection leading to a possible reduction in the fatigue limit” [11].
In order to meet these conditions, different standards states criterion to the potential that
steel structures must be polarized to. The figure below from DS/EN 12473:2014 gives an
overview of the states of steel in seawater at different potentials:
Figure 3.4: Electrode potentials of steel in seawater and corresponding degree of protec-
tion. Modified from [10].
The potentials at which the steel is protected are often denoted Ep and are mainly defined
upon on empirical testing. The main criterion for protection is that the cathode potential
should be more negative than Ep. Under normal conditions a steel structure in seawater
is considered protected at potentials more negative than -800 mV [10].
8
==PAGE:18==
When steel potentials become very negative the risk of hydrogen embrittlement arises.
At these potentials the cathode is located in the H2 stability zone at the Pourbaix diagram
(see appendix A.1) and hydrogen gas will be produced in critical amounts at the steel
surface by cathode reaction 3.9. Hydrogen formation can lead to severe damaged of steel
structures as it can be absorbed in the steel micro structure and reduce fatigue strength.
Potentials in this range are often referred to as over-protection and usually defined as
potentials more negative than -1100 mV [10], [11]. Between the anode and cathode a
potential drop will occur, due to resistance in the water and at calcareous deposits. This
means that the anode potential will have to be more negative than the potential of the
structure. This means that the current density at areas near the anode will be higher
than elsewhere on the structure. The risk of over protection is therefore biggest near the
anodes.
3.1.6
Calcareous Deposits
During cathodic protection the whole steel surface acts as cathode. The OH−produced in
the cathode reaction 3.2 contributes to a local change of the pH close to the surface mak-

both based on theoretical and experimental work [12], [13], [14], [15]. As mentioned in
section 3.1.2.6 a higher pH will result in a higher LSI and therefore greater chance of
deposit formation. This is caused by the pH-change shifting the balance of carbonate/bi-
carbonate equilibrium in the water in the favour of CO2−

3.2.1
Hydrodynamics Around Cylindrical Structures
Steady flow regimes around smooth cylindrical structures can be described as functions
of the Reynolds number, given as
Re = D · U
ν
(3.14)
In which D is the diameter of the cylinder, U is the flow velocity and ν is the kinematic
viscosity [17].
Changes in Re will change the regime of flow around the cylinder as illustrated in figure
3.5 below. With an increasing value of Re the flow goes from a creeping flow with no sep-
aration, to the subcritical regime with a laminar boundary layer, separation and turbulent
vortex shedding in the wake and lastly to the transcritical regime with turbulent boundary
layer and separation.
Figure 3.5: Regimes of flow around a smooth, circular cylinder in steady flow. Modified
from [17].
When looking at the area close to the cylinder surface, the local areas of interest are
denoted as seen in figure 3.6:
Figure 3.6: To the left: Definition of the different areas of interest at a cylinder located in a
flow [17]. To the right: definition of the angle α used to describe locations along the edged
of the cylinder and location of separation point.
Here the boundary layer, the wake region and the separation point are illustrated. It is
noted that the thickness of the boundary layer increases towards the separation point.
The point at which the incoming flow line hits the cylinder surface perpendicularly is de-
noted as the stagnation point. At this point the flow speed is locally zero. Based on
10
==PAGE:20==
potential flow theory the flow speed goes from zero to being equal to the speed of the
incoming flow at a location of α=30°, to being the double of the incoming flow speed at
α=90°(approximately at the separation point in the sub- and transcritical flow regime). Up
to the separation point in the sub- and transcritical flow regime, this flow speed distribution,
relative to the incoming flow speed, remains practically unaffected by change of the Re as
implied by the pressure distributions shown in Figure 2.5 in [17] and the Bernoulli equation.
The location of the separation point will in the subcritical flow regime be located in the area
of approximately α=75°to α=90°depending on the specific Re and surface roughness.
A very significant feature of the flow regime around a cylinder is the Vortex shedding in the
wake region. Two vortices are created in the wake of the cylinder, starting from each of the
two separation points. The vortex formation is an unstable process resulting in one vortex
being dominant and hereby drawing the opposite vortex across the wake, whereupon the
process is reversed resulting in a continuously oscillating mechanism as seen in figure
3.7 below. The mechanism facilitates that the fluid from the boundary layer around the
cylinder is mixed in the wake of the cylinder. As the wake is continuously oscillating, the
mean flow velocity is close to zero.
Figure 3.7: The figure shows how vortices are alternately drawn across the wake during
shedding [17].

4.2
State of the Art Research on Formation of Calcareous
Deposits During ICCP of Steel in Seawater
The study [22] investigates the composition and structure of calcareous deposits formed
on steel specimens exposed to ICCP at -910 mV (Ag/AgCl) for 8400 minutes (5.8 days).
The specimens are cylindrical with a height of 25 mm and a diameter of 13 mm. The test
setup is placed in a beaker and includes a system for water exchange that provides a
slight flow around the specimens.
Experiments with variation in flow rate showed, that the flow had a direct proportional
relation with the current density reached within the initial minutes of the experiment, and
no direct relation to the current density plateau towards the end of the experiment where
deposit formation has taken place.
The study finds that a thin uniform magnesium rich deposit layer are formed within the first
minutes of polarization that significantly reduced the current density. Atop of this layer a
thicker deposit of CaCO3 was formed.
The initial formation of a Mg-rich layer followed by Ca-rich deposits is widely documented,
but the full mechanism behind is poorly understood.
The process of deposit formation on a cathode surface consists of an initial precipitation
of the calcareous compounds in the surrounding solution followed by a deposition on the
cathode surface.
The study [22] states a possible chemical explanation of the initial precipitation of Mg(OH)2
before CaCO3 as: Mg(OH)2 is under OH−and not Mg2+ concentration control and will pre-
cipitate as soon as the pH near the steel surface reach the pH of saturation. This process
is chemically faster than precipitation of CaCO3. The formation of Mg(OH)2 is however
limited by the modest distance in which the OH−is abundant and pH high, resulting in
only thin layers of deposits.

form are given in table 4.1. The compounds can be expected to precipitate in a solution
if pH>pHs.

• ICCP towers and reference electrodes were installed in the flumes and connected

• Potentiostats were turned on.
• Camera was turned on.

7
Discussion
All electrode potentials in the following work are given with reference to a Ag/AgCl refer-
ence electrode in seawater unless otherwise stated.
7.1
Experiments Without Flow for Determination of Optimal
Potential
Figure 6.1 shows steel specimens after 3 days at -900, -1000 and -1100 mV. From here
is seen that -900 only resulted in a slight layer of calcareous deposits, where -1000 mV
resulted in a visually dense and solid layer, that was relatively durable. -1100 mV produced
a lot of deposits, but these were flaky and fell off. The flaky structure is most likely caused
by high formation of hydrogen at the surface, that breaks the structure of the calcareous
deposits. These observations align with observations from previous studies [6]. The
observations contributed to a good understanding of the formation of calcareous deposits
at different potentials and based on these observations, -1000 mV was chosen as the
setting to be used in the flume setup.
7.2
Experiments in Flume Setup With Different Water Flows
7.2.1
Development of Cathodic Protection
From the measured current densities presented in figure 6.3 to 6.7 it can be seen that
higher flow speeds tends to result in higher initial current densities. This is expected as
higher flow speed will decrease the diffusion layer thickness and hereby increase the oxy-
gen consuming cathode reaction with higher current density as a consequence. This also
explains why high initial current densities are not seen in experiments without flow. The
effect have also been documented by other studies [22], [28].
The current densities seen in the conducted experiments have been as large as 3500 mA
m2
for the initial period of the experiments. These current densities have an unrealistic mag-
nitude compared to a real life ICCP cases with current densities around 200-400 mA
m2 . This
is an effect of having a small cathode area, compared to anode area, in the experiment,
as the potentiostat can easily deliver high current densities. This is not possible in real
life, where cathode areas will be much larger compared to anode area. The effects seen
in the experimental setup are therefore not directly relatable to real life conditions.
The high current densities are partly a result of the low potentials used in the experiment.
These have been chosen with the aim of generating visual calcareous deposits within a
short time.
After the initial peak all experiments with water flow experience a significant decrease
in current density. The experiments conducted in flume 2 have a more exponential de-
crease, whereas the results from flume 1 have a slightly step wise decrease at low flow
speeds and a more exponential decrease at higher flow speed. The step wise decrease
can be a sign of oxygen for the cathode reaction being limited, first by diffusion in solution
and later on by calcareous deposits. This step wise development have also been seen in
other studies with minimal water flow [22]. The exponential like decrease is similar to that
seen in previous studies with high flow speed in the flume setup, as on figure 4.2 and 4.7.
When looking at the cyclic flow in figure 6.6 and 6.7 it can be seen that the periods with
water flow have a significantly higher current density than periods without flow in the be-
ginning of the experiment. Towards the end of the experiment this difference seems to be
55
==PAGE:65==
reduced to almost nothing. As with constant flow, higher flow speed will decrease the dif-
fusion layer thickness and hereby increase the oxygen consuming cathode reaction with
higher current density as a consequence. In periods without flow, this effect will not be
present. That the difference in current density between flow and no flow fades out, and
that the current density generally gets lower, are both caused by the build up of calcare-
ous deposits limiting oxygen diffusion. The same trend have been observed in a previous
study conducted in the flume setup, as seen on figure 4.2.
It is further seen that the current density from the experiments with cyclic flow seems
to continue to decrease, whereas the experiments with constant flow seem to be more
stable towards the end. As the duration of the experiments have only been 6 to 11 days,
stabilised conditions have not been reached within the given time period. This work there-
fore only gives indications of the effects that will occur and not the full understanding. The
results hereby serves as a preliminary understanding to be used for further studies.
At figure 6.8 the experiments have been arranged according to ICCP tower, as the visual
calcareous deposits have been consequently greater with tower I and the current density
higher in all experiments. It is noted that except from experiment 1, all experiments with
ICCP tower I have been conducted in flume 1. The results from this figure might therefore
be influenced by the flumes as well.
From the figure it is clear that the two ICCP towers, or flumes, act differently and that no
consistent trends can be seen across the two. It is therefore not possible to derive any
conclusions on current densities at end of experiments. This difference in the results will
be discussed further in section 7.3.
The previous project with cylindrical structures conducted in the flume setup [7] found
current densities in the range 200-300 mA
m2 at day 6 with flow speeds around 25-30 cm
s .
Compared to those results, the results from tower II and flume 2 comes closest.
7.2.2
Calcareous Deposits
7.2.2.1
Current Densities
Based on the results from this work, it is difficult to relate the visual amount of calcareous
deposits to current demand for ICCP. It have been assumed that more deposits would lead
to lower final current densities. Taking all results into account, there are however no clear
tendencies indicting that this or the opposite should be the case. It should be noted that
the visual evaluation is not ideal for this analysis, as it does not take the properties of the
layer into into account with respect to electrochemical properties and diffusion resistance.
It has however not been within the frame of this work to investigate these properties further.
7.2.2.2
Hydrogen Formation
Large amount of bubbles were seen to rise from the steel specimens within the first min-
utes of experiments. At this time high current density have been documented in all ex-
periments, indicating excessive cathode reaction taking place.
Initially the pH of the water around the surface will have a pH equal to that of the bulk wa-
ter (around 8), but with time the pH will rise due to the cathode reaction producing OH−.
Based on this increase in pH, a decrease in hydrogen bubble formation can be expected
by looking at the Pourbaix diagram for water in figure 7.1:
56
==PAGE:66==
Figure 7.1: Pourbaix diagram for water with respect to Ag/AgCl reference electrode. The
intersection of the orange lines indicates the starting point for the cathode reaction, de-
noted point 1, point 2 indicates the state after increase in pH due to cathode reaction
taking place.
As seen in figure 7.1, the initial situation for the cathode reaction, point (1) is located
well below the line (a) which is separating the area in which H2O is stable and the area
of hydrogen liberation. Hydrogen formation in the cathode reaction is therefore to be
expected. With the increase in pH the cathode is dislocated to point (2) and hereby closer
to line (a). As pourbaix diagrams are made for ideal conditions, a certain over-potential
is needed in reality for the cathode reactions to take place at a steel surfaces [8]. It is
therefore expected that hydrogen formation will be less in the zone just below line (a).
This shift from (1) to (2) explains the observed reduction in hydrogen formation.
Hydrogen will however still be formed at lower rates, as it is seen on pictures taken during
the experiments like figure 6.19. This continuous hydrogen formation might explain the
crater-like holes in the calcareous deposit seen on figure 6.20. If the formation of hydrogen
is intense at a certain location, or the initial calcareous layer weak, the physical presence
of hydrogen bubbles might hinder the formation of calcareous deposits.
7.2.2.3
Protection Against Depolarisation
Looking at figure 6.10 it is seen that all experiments with more than one measurement,
except 1.2, shows a natural decrease in the ∆potential over time. This can be explained
by the growth in calcareous deposits in time, as these lower the oxygen diffusion to the
steel surface and thereby hinder the depolarisation. Furthermore, the experiments without
flow and cyclic flow all show a low ∆potential and hereby a great diffusion resistance.
This is likely related to the fact that the calcareous deposits on these are distributed all
around the cylinder and not just focused on one side. Calcareous layers formed under
cyclic flow shows better protection against depolarisation, in a situation with the same
direction of flow. In real life the flow direction will however vary, which will likely lead to a
less significant influence.
7.2.2.4
Electrical Properties
The IR-drops seen on figure 6.9 are mostly located in the interval 0-30 mV which relates
well to results obtained after 6 days in previous studies [6]. No consistent relation have
been found between IR-drop and calcareous deposits, flow speed or current density.
It have been difficult decide upon how to measure the IR-drop. It has been attempted
57
==PAGE:67==
to make curve fits on the measurements from the first 10 seconds and then extrapolate
backwards. This method did however not return meaningful results. In the end it was
decided to rely on the first measurement the potentiostats did on the open circuit potential
to get consistent and comparable measurements.
7.2.2.5

a more uneven surface with small craters. The deposits upstream are lighter, thinner and
more even.
In the wake there will be a mix of the fluid from the boundary layer (containing OH−) likely
leading to a permanent higher pH, as fluid in this zone is not removed in the same way as
on the up stream side. This was also seen in the visualisation test with phenolphthalein
as seen on figure C.1. The situation might look as the one sketched on figure 7.5:

During the experiment, the hydrogen bubbles formed on the steel surface downstream of
the separation point are not removed by the incoming water flow, as seen on the pictures
taken during the experiment. This permits the hydrogen to form larger bubbles that might

==PAGE:69==
lead to the ”craters” as described above. It is noted that this effect is not scaled correctly
compared to real life conditions, as hydrogen bubbles most likely will not grow as large
relative to the size of the structure as seen here.
For this flow speed, the calcareous deposits formed just upstream of the separation point
were unexpected. At this location potential flow theory suggest that the flow speed will
be up to two times larger than that of the the incoming flow, likely creating unfavourable
conditions for deposit formation. That the deposits are seen can potentially be explained
by the fact that the thickness of the boundary layer increases along the surface of the
cylinder, as it approaches the separation point. Furthermore, as the separation point is
approached the gradient of the velocity profile at the bed is reduced. Lastly the fluid with
higher pH from the cathode reaction on the steel surface further upstream will pass this
location. These three effects will likely counteract the effect of the increased flow speed.
An important aspect to the results seen in this work is that the flow direction remains
unchanged. In a tidal system the flow will change direction multiple times a day, which
will likely result in more uniform calcareous deposits.
7.2.3
Chemical Composition of Calcareous Deposits
SEM-EDS pictures from experiments 1.2, 3.1, and 4.1, (0 cm
s and constant flow of 1.5 cm
s
and 5.3 cm
s ) are seen in section 6.2.5. All pictures show a layer consisting of two phases:
a darker phase with a needle shaped structure and a lighter phase with a dense struc-
ture. The darker phase covers the steel surface at all locations with visual deposits and
are in some locations spanning all the way to the deposit surface with a thickness up to
around 150 µm. The lighter phase is located within the darker phase towards the deposit
surface. At some locations it grows close the steel and in others it is located as drops on
top of the darker phase. EDS analyses clearly indicated that the darker phase consists
of Mg, whereas the lighter phase clearly consists of Ca. The initial formation of Mg-rich
deposits on the steel surface have been documented in various studies, as discussed in
section 4.2 and 4.1. These studies however only detects the Mg-rich deposits as very thin
films on the steel surface, whereas the results in this work indicate much larger amount
of Mg-rich deposits.
The study [24] suggests that CaCO3 will not deposit at the cathode surface for pH >9 and
Mg(OH)2 will not deposit for pH >11 due to the surface charge of particles. Measurements
of the pH locally at the steel surface have not been performed in this project, but previous
research have found pH values close to the steel surface around 11 [12], [13], [14], [15]. A
relative high pH at the steel surface relates to excessive cathode reaction. This requires
high current densities, which is exactly what have been documented for experiment 1.2,
3.1, and 4.1 as seen in figure 6.2, 6.4 and 6.5. It is therefore likely that high pH have been
present near the steel surface in the conducted experiment and similar to the findings in
[24] this could lead to the relatively large amount of Mg-rich deposits.
The composition of the calcareous deposit can be related to the one presented in the
step-wise model seen on figure 4.9. Here small groups of Ca- and Mg-rich compounds
are formed atop of a Mg-rich layer, and these will start to grow together. The results differs
from the last phase of the model, as the composition of the deposit contains less Ca-rich
phases as discussed above.
All three specimens analysed in the SEM have been from experiments with high current
and large amount of calcareous deposits. It could therefore be of great interest to anal-
yse the composition of thinner deposits formed with lower current densities. This have
however not been possible within the frame of this work.

7.3
Validation of Flume Setup
The results with regards to current density and visual appearance of the specimens clearly
indicate that there is a difference in behaviour between the two flumes or the two ICCP
towers. Some difference was already observed in some initial tests before experiment
one, but it was expected to be ruled in the experiments with water flow. Except from
experiment 1, all experiments have been performed with tower I in flume 1 and tower II
and flume 2, with the aim of being able to compare the results. Based on this, it have
been observed that experiments with tower I and flume 1 reach current densities at the
end experiment that are much greater than those observed with tower II and flume 2.
Furthermore, the specimens from tower I and flume 1 reveals much greater formation
of calcareous deposits than those from tower II and flume 2. There is a documented
difference in the flow speed between the two flume, but this have been relatively small
compared to the difference in formed deposits and current density. This behaviour is
therefore unexpected and further investigation have therefore been conducted.
A chronopotentiometry test with a fixed current density at 550 mA
m2 have been conducted
to see the behaviour at lower initial current densities. From figure C.11 it is seen that the
cathodic polarisation takes place at a higher current density before the chronopotentiom-
etry for flume 2 with tower II than after. This means that the cathode reaction before was
plenty which can also be seen from the steep slope of the curve, indicating that reaction is
strongly limited by oxygen diffusion. After the chronopotentiometry the cathode reaction
is less, which is likely caused by the formation of a diffusion limiting layer of calcareous
deposits. The form of the curve after also indicate a diffusion limited reaction, however
not as strongly as before. When looking at figure C.10 from tower I in flume 1 it is seen
that the situation is reversed. The cathode reaction seems to draws a higher current after
chronopotentiometry. No explanation of this phenomena have been found.
The insights above illustrate that there a difference between the two flumes and that there
are some poorly understood and unexpected effects taking place. When looking at figure
C.12 it is seen that the chronopotentiometry is almost identical for the two flumes. Based
on the results from the chronoamperometry previous in the project (figure 6.2-6.7) indicat-
ing different behaviour, this is unexpected as the same setup is used. It might however
be due to the lower initial current of 550 mA
m2 , whereas the initial currents in experiment 1-6
have been as high 3500 mA
m2 .
The results from the simple Test of flumes (section B.5.3) presented in table C.3 clearly
indicates that the effects follow the towers and not the flumes. These are however con-
ducted in without flow, whereas most experiments have been conducted with water flow.
That the towers should act differently without flow is however countered by the experiment
test of towers where the two towers perform identical. The potentiostats have been tested
as well, with the results in figure C.7 indicating equal performance between the two.
Based on the results presented in figure C.10 and C.11, flume 2 with tower 2 seems to
produce the most reliable results. Flume 1 with tower 1 have on the other hand seemed
to be produced high amount of calcareous deposits and therefore been good with regards
to visualisations of hydrodynamic effects in short time.
The reason behind the different in behaviour between the two flumes/ICCP towers re-
mains unknown and comparison of results between the two seems difficult. Future studies
in the flume setup should pay special attention to this.

8
Suggestions for Future Work
8.1
Testing Under Realistic Conditions
In order to fully understand this topic investigations like these are to be carried out on
real offshore structures. If studies however are to be performed in a lab, the environ-
ment should simulate what is seen in real life conditions. This includes temperature, flow
speeds and time variation of these, oxygen content etc.
It have not been possible to reach these conditions in the current flume setup at DTU.
8.2
Chemical Analysis of Deposits
A thorough chemical analysis of the formed calcareous deposits would be of great value.
This could be done with XRD analysis of deposits formed under varying conditions.
8.3
Investigation of Local Effect
As seen in this project the local variations in pH, oxygen content and hydrodynamic effects
are of great interest. It is therefore recommended for future work to focus more local
effects than ”global” effects in the setup, as this will likely bring more value to the work.
8.4
Choice of Flume
For future investigations in the flume setup at DTU, it is recommended to only use flume 2
with ICCP tower II, as the results obtained in this combination seems to be most reliable
and relatable to real life conditions. Formation of calcareous deposits can however be
done in short time in flume 1 with tower I in case that is of interest.
8.5
Cathodic Polarisation Curves
Cathodic polarisation curves seems to reveal a lot of information on the processes hap-
pening in the flumes. It is therefore suggested to actively use these measurements in
future studies.

9
Conclusion
Cylindrical steel specimens placed in synthetic seawater have been exposed to ICCP
at fixed electrode potential of -1000 mV (Ag/AgCl) for 6 and 11 days. These have been
exposed to no water flow, constant flow, and a cyclic combination of the two. The following
can be concluded:
• For specimens exposed to cyclic flow, the periods with water flow resulted in higher
current density than periods without flow. This trend was strong in the initial period
of experiments and faded out towards the end. Maximum flow speeds are therefore
of influence with regards to the initial electrical current demand.
• Periods without flow, within a tidal-like flow cycle affected the formation of calcareous
deposits, as more area was visually covered in deposits compared to constant flow
with the same mean flow speed, leading to better protection against depolarisation.
The time variation of flow is therefore of important influence.
• For constant flow, higher flow speed resulted in higher initial current demand and
less formation of visual calcareous deposits.
• The formation of calcareous deposits on cylindrical specimens exposed to low flow
speeds showed clear influence of hydrodynamic effects.
• SEM-EDS analyses have shown, that the calcareous deposits, formed in experi-
ments with high current density consisted of a Mg-rich phase with a needle shaped
structure and a thickness of up to 150 µm. Within this phase areas of dense a Ca-
rich phase were seen. The Mg-rich phase was deposited first as this fully covered
the steel surface.
• The study have not been able to produce unambiguous repeatable results on how
different flow speeds or calcareous deposits relates the steady state current de-
mand.
Time variation of water flow and hydrodynamic effects from water flow around cylindrical
structures, are hereby shown to affect the corrosion protection of these, including the
formation of protective calcareous deposits. The obtained results contribute to a better
understanding of the effect involved in this complex phenomena while emphasizing the
need for further research.
