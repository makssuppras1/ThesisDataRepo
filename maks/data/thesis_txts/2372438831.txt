Abstract
Topology optimization and dynamic contact with friction are both well-
established research ﬁelds. However, the two have not yet been uniﬁed within
the literature. This thesis takes the ﬁrst steps towards a uniﬁcation of the
two methods, by showing that density based topology optimization can be
performed for structures experiencing frictional impacts with a rigid and
straight obstacle.
The Lagrange multiplier based unilateral contact condition is enforced
in a nodal sense. The elastic structure is discretized and modeled by the
standard ﬁnite element method, in which the stiﬀness of an element follows
the modiﬁed SIMP method, mass is linearly dependent on the ﬁltered design
variables, and Rayleigh damping is invoked. Time integration of the govern-
ing equations is mainly achieved by an implicit Backwards Euler scheme, and
the optimization is performed by the Method of Moving Asymptotes. Density
ﬁltering is applied to avoid mesh-dependencies and checkerboard structures.
The sensitivities of a general objective function with respect to the design
variables have successfully been derived by the adjoint method. The deriv-
ation allows the objective function to depend on displacements, velocities,
accelerations, and contact forces. Only dynamic structural compliance has
been investigated in this work.
For optimization of a frictional impact situation, the obtained designs
resemble the designs obtained for test cases without contact, although ex-
tra features emerge as a consequence of the impact. The direct implications
of including friction forces are illustrated but have not been thoroughly ex-
amined.
To conclude the work of this thesis: a uniﬁcation of density based topology
optimization and frictional contact methods is hereby shown to be possible.

Part I
Introduction
This thesis examines the eﬀects of applying density based topology optimiz-
ation to dynamic structural contact problems with friction.
Friction has been known to man for a long time and we’ve exploited it
since Homo Erectus to ensure survival by lighting bonﬁres from the friction
generated by spinning a pin against a log. After learning to control ﬁre, we
started playing music. Percussions were one of the ﬁrst instruments to be
played; these are sounded by being struck by a beater and the sound emitted
is highly dependent on the shapes of the clashed bodies.
In our days, understanding impact situations leads to increased collision
safety in the transportation sector. In civil engineering friction between tires
and roads is of major importance to maintain safety standards. In extreme
situations, friction forces may lead directly to mechanical failures of essential
parts of constructions.

Impacts and contact situations are present in almost any real dynamical
system; therefore it is of great engineering interest to understand and invest-
igate these problems. Because of its ubiquity in classic mechanics, frictional
contact has been of interest to researchers for a long time, and numerous
approaches to solving such problems have been proposed. A full dynamic
frictional model has, to the author’s best knowledge, not yet been combined
with the newer, but rapidly growing, ﬁeld of density based topology optim-
ization. The aim of this thesis is to take the ﬁrst steps towards a uniﬁcation
of the two research ﬁelds.
By applying and combining mathematical models from the literature, it is
possible not only to account for the contact forces but also take this inform-
ation into account during structural optimization. One may even optimize a
structure to give a desired behaviour of the contact forces.
The author of this study has previously conducted a study during the
fall of 2016 titled ”Finite Element modeling and optimization of contact
problems”[1]. The study dealt with static contact problems only and fric-
tional eﬀects were neglected. The goal of the study was to solve the vertically
loaded L-problem sketched in ﬁgure 2a and obtain a design which resulted in
an even distribution of pressure forces at the lower side L-block. The result
included in ﬁgure 2b demonstrates how an even distribution of pressure forces
were obtained. The study then went on to apply a compliance constraint and
employ projection methods to drive the design towards a solid-void state.

Time integration by the Backwards Euler Method
As (1.1) is a second order diﬀerential equation, a time integration scheme has
to be implemented to obtain a time varying solution from t = 0 to t = T1.
If (1.1) is reformulated into a set of ﬁrst order diﬀerential equations, stand-
ard methods such as Matlab’s ode solvers may be utilized to obtain the
response solution from t = 0 to t = T1. As an alternative to Matlab’s ode
solvers the implicit Backwards Euler scheme is investigated in this work. This
scheme is one of the most simple time integration schemes, as accelerations

next timestep; which is often the case for other time integration schemes such
as the Newmark scheme. The Backwards Euler scheme is beneﬁcial in cases
where contact may occur as velocities and accelerations change abruptly in
such case. The Backwards Euler scheme has proven to be very robust but at
the cost of accuracy as errors seems to accumulate with time, causing the res-
ult to appear algorithmically damped. The Backwards Euler approximations
to acceleration and velocity at timestep t are:

1.2
Solving the governing equations
To solve (1.1), two diﬀerent approaches have been investigated. (1) Mat-
lab’s built in ode45 (for cases without contact) and (2) a Backwards Euler
scheme with an underlying Newton-Raphson method. A relative tolerance of
RelTol = 10−12 has been used with the ode45 solver throughout this work.
The Backwards Euler method has proven to be less accurate than the
ode45 solver but more robust, as the method solves geometrically non-linear
problems in which contact occurs, without any numerical problems.
The two methods require two diﬀerent reformulations of (1.1). The ode45
solver requires the problem to be posed as a set of ﬁrst-order diﬀerential
equations, whereas the Newton-Raphson solver requires the introduction of
the system residual and derivation of the Jacobian. The system residual and
partial derivative with respect to Dt are presented in the following section.
The total residual R and the total system Jacobian J for an implementation
that includes frictional unilateral contact is covered in section 2, after the
contact formulation has been presented.

1.3
The unilateral contact formulation with friction
The following sections present the frictional contact formulation used in this
thesis. The following section contains a review of the literature in the ﬁeld
of contact mechanics, especially in relation to the ﬁnite element method.
The necessary extensions to include a contact condition is described, and the
gap function is presented followed by a review of classic contact mechanics
and the presentation of the Lagrange multiplier based unilateral contact and
friction condition.
1.3.1
Previous studies of contact mechanics
Contact mechanics has long been studied due to its ubiquity within mech-
anical engineering and its intriguing non-linear behavior.
Naturally, the
ﬁeld is well established, and the amount of literature describing methods,
algorithms, the physics and practical examples is almost incomprehensible.
In 2005 Bourago and Kukudzjanov published an elaborate review of the nu-
merical methods of contact mechanics [2], which describes the work of contact
algorithms throughout the last four decades.
The methods capable of dealing with frictional contact in large deforma-
tion settings are brieﬂy described in [3], which groups the available methods
into three categories: (1) Node-to-segment methods, (2) Mortar methods and
(3) Contact domain- and intermediate surface methods.
The Node-to-segment and Mortar methods are both asymmetric formula-
tions due to the master-slave surface treatments. Domain- and intermediate
surface methods are naturally symmetric but suﬀer from the fact that an in-
termediate surface cannot be guaranteed for arbitrary three-dimensional geo-
metries [3]. In 1993 the node-to-segment method was presented and demon-
strated for two-body contact of large deformation by Laursen and Simo in
[4].
The mortar methods were originally proposed by Bernardi in 1994 [5]
to overcome problems of potential contact between dissimilar meshed struc-
tures. The mortar methods were recognized for their applicability by numer-
ous authors, and have been investigated with respect to kinematically linear
contact for small strains [6, 7] and large deformation settings [8, 9, 10] as
in 2006 when Fisher and Wriggers presented a penalty formulation based on
the mortar concepts [11].
In 2015 Poulios and Renard proposed an integral approximation of the
contact and friction complementary condition in the context of an augmented
Lagrangian formulation, in which the contact, friction and complementary
conditions are included in the weak formulation of the problem.

1.3.4
The unilateral contact and friction condition
This section presents the nodal based unilateral contact and friction condition
[3] of this work, simply referred to as the contact condition. The condition is
necessary to ensure gj ≥0 and obtain the contact forces. Classic mechanics
typically distinguishes between static and dynamic friction, as illustrated in
ﬁgure 6. Consider the box resting on a surface in ﬁgure 6a. The box is
vertically loaded by Fw but penetration of the surface is prevented by the
normal contact force Fn. The force Fs is applied to move the box and Ff is
the friction force that prevents motion. Accelerating the box requires a non-
zero horizontal net force, which is present when the applied force exceeds the
maximum attainable static friction force. The maximum attainable static
friction force depends on the friction coeﬃcient between the box and the
surface and the compression of the two. In classic mechanics, dynamic friction
is always less than the maximum attainable static friction.

3
Topology optimization
The following sections give an introduction to the ﬁeld of structural optim-
ization. Three diﬀerent methods are brieﬂy presented, however, the rest of
this section focuses speciﬁcally on topology optimization as it is the method
used in this work. The aim of structural optimization is to iteratively and
systematically improve the performance of a structure. The performance of
a structure is measured by an objective function which is to be minimized.
Three methods of structural optimization are illustrated in ﬁgure 7.
(a) Size optimization
(b) Shape optimization
(c) Topology optimization
Figure 7: Three types of structural optimization methods. Upper: Initial
design guess. Lower: optimized structures
Each of the three diﬀerent approaches to structural optimization presen-
ted in ﬁgure 7 has strengths and weaknesses. The use of size optimization is
limited because the optimized design is a scaled version of the initial guess.
For size optimization, the design variable is typically a structural dimension.
With shape optimization, the performance of a structure is increased by al-
tering the contours of the initial guess. Shape optimization results in designs
that are topologically equivalent to the initial guess. Topology optimization
is the agilest form of the presented optimization methods; it is well suited in
cases where designers have no pre-existing knowledge of the distribution of
topological features for an optimal design.
The following section presents the basic ideas and solution strategies in
relation to the topology optimization part of this work. A brief review of the
literature is presented followed by an introduction to the general optimization
problem and a sequential presentation of all the algorithmic steps during a
design iteration, including the derivation of the design sensitivities.

3.1
Previous studies
As topology optimization helps to answer a fundamental engineering ques-
tion of ”how to distribute material to obtain better structural performance?”
it may not be much of a surprise that the research ﬁeld emerged from mech-
anical design problems. Since the publication of the seminal paper on nu-
merical topology optimization by Bendsøe and Kikuchi [12] computer power
has allowed the methodology to be applied to a wide range of ﬁelds including
electromechanics [13], acoustics [14], photonics [15], ﬂuids [16], vibrations
[17] and many more. The ﬁeld has spread in so many directions that a full
review is almost incomprehensive. The book by Bendsøe and Sigmund [18]
covers the classical theory and methods for topology optimization. Since the
release of this book Sigmund and Maute has produced a great review article
[19] which covers trends and directions of the ongoing, and suggestions for
future, research.
3.1.1
Topology optimization of elasticity problems
The foundation for modern topology optimization was laid by Bendsøe and
Kikuchi when they in 1988 published their seminal paper [12]. At that time
shape optimization was more popular, but this method is only capable of
producing topologically equivalent structures to the initial design guess, and
remeshing is often required to obtain a stable convergence. The proposed
method of the seminal paper circumvented both of the two drawbacks of
shape optimization. Since 1988 the method has spread in many diﬀerent
directions including ”density”, ”level-set”, ”topological derivative”, ”phase
ﬁeld”, ”evolutionary” and several others.
Of all the diﬀerent methods, the density based method seems to be (one
of) the most popular method(s). The density based method relies on the
modiﬁed SIMP interpolation of (3.1) to relate ˜ρ to element stiﬀness. p is the
so-called penalization factor and E1 and E0 are Young’s moduli of solid- and
void respectively.

3.1.2
Topology optimization of contact problems
Topology optimization of contact problems is just a tiny branch of all the
ﬁelds to which topology optimization has been applied, and hence the avail-
able literature is very limited. In 1995 Klarbring et. al. [20] extended the
ground structure approach to include a unilateral frictionless contact con-
dition. The extensions were used in size optimization of bridge-like truss
14
==PAGE:22==
structures, to ﬁnd structures that provided an even contact force distribu-
tion at the outset of the structures. In 2009 Stromberg and Klarbring [21]
applied density based topology optimization to static linear elastic structures
unilaterally constrained by a frictionless rigid support. The objective of this
paper was structural compliance, and the eﬀects of diﬀerent load cases were
investigated. In 2015 Lawry and Maute [22] applied level-set topology optim-
ization methods to geometrically linear two-component problems with sliding
contact interfaces. In this paper, the contact condition was enforced by the
stabilized Lagrange multiplier methods and an active-set strategy. The inter-
action between two solid phases with possible sliding- and separable contact
at the phase boundaries are investigated, and responses were obtained quasi-
statically. The paper studied only sliding contact. However, the level-set
method of XFEM is proposed to extend to optimization of frictional prob-
lems conveniently. In 2016 the author of this thesis [1] investigated density
based topology optimization for static frictionless contact problems.
The
objective of the optimization was a uniform pressure distribution at the con-
tact interface, subject to compliance and volume constraints. At the same
time of 2016, Maury [23] submitted a Ph.D. thesis on shape optimization for
contact and plasticity problems by the level-set method. Maury rightfully
argues that the continuous density based approach to topology optimization
suﬀers from the drawbacks of occurring intermediate densities in the optim-
ized designs. However, numerous methods are available to force the design
into a solid-void state.
As evident from the brief literature review above, the ﬁeld of topology
optimization for structural contact problems is subject to ongoing research.
Of the mentioned studies above only the ones by Lawry, Maute, Maury and
the author of this thesis included a separable contact condition. The level-
set method of the study by Lawry and Maute [22] and Maury [23] allowed
the shape of the contact interface to change as a consequence of the shape
optimization methods used, whereas all the other studies dealt with ﬁxed
contact areas. To the author’s best knowledge there is, at the time of writing,
no literature available on the subject of density based topology optimization
of dynamic structural contact problems with friction.

3.4.6
The optimization algorithm: the Method of Moving Asymp-
totes (MMA)
Structural compliance optimization is a convex problem when there is no
contact and p = 1. By including a contact condition, the problem most likely
becomes non-convex. This means that multiple local minima are present and
that the solution depends on the initial design guess. For such problems,
a well-tested and robust optimization algorithm is essential for reaching a
minimum. The optimization algorithm used throughout this thesis is the
Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA) developed by Svanberg in 1987 [29].
The MMA decomposes the optimization problem into smaller sub-problems
which are locally strictly convex approximations to the original problem.
The generation of the subproblems is controlled by the so-called moving
asymptotes, which control the step size of the optimizer and hence may act
to speed up and stabilize convergence. It is important to note, that the MMA
22
==PAGE:30==
never reaches an exact minimum, as the step size is reduced as the minimum
is approached. Even for problems subject to multiple constraints the MMA
has a very good convergence rate and is very stable.

Part IV
Discussion, conclusions and
future work
6
Discussion and conclusions
This thesis set out to demonstrate that a uniﬁcation of the research ﬁelds of
density based topology optimization and the frictional contact dynamics is
possible. A uniﬁcation of the methods of both ﬁelds has successfully been
demonstrated. With this work, it has been shown to be possible to account
for contact forces that may arise from impacts and contact during a struc-
tural optimization process. All of the optimized designs presented in this
work outperforms their unoptimized counterparts by far. The importance
of investigating various initial design guesses has also been illustrated, as
designs optimized for problems without friction in some cases outperformed
the designs optimized with friction. In other cases, it has been shown to be
possible to achieve a performance increase of up to 5% by accounting for the
contact forces during the optimization.
The implicit Backwards Euler approach has been utilized to achieve time
integration of the governing equations for many of the problems presented.
This scheme has proven to be very useful as it does not use accelerations of
the previous timesteps to determine the location at the next timestep; this
is beneﬁcial in cases where impacts occur. With this scheme, no numerical
instabilities have been observed around impacts, even though such events
make dynamic problems very stiﬀ. The Backwards Euler approach has been
compared to Matlab’s ode45 which proved to be more accurate, but much
slower than the Backwards Euler integration. The Backwards Euler scheme
produces results that approach the solutions obtained with ode45 as the
number of timesteps is increased, however even for a relatively low number
of timesteps it has been possible to obtain the sensitivities of the design
problems and obtain sensible designs.
The contact formulation used throughout this work is a nodal form of
the integral formulation given in the reference. As the restricting boundary
is straight throughout all of this work, a nodal based implementation suﬃ-
ciently hinders element sides from penetrating the restricting boundary. It is
important to realize that the contact formulation overestimates the friction
forces compared to classic mechanics as the formulation does not distinguish
between static- and dynamic friction. The augmentation parameter a of the
45
==PAGE:53==
formulation should be chosen to satisfy a > 0. This parameter may aﬀect
the convergence of problems, but should not aﬀect the ﬁnal result. It has
been observed that for optimization problems with friction, this parameter
should be chosen to be very low, to avoid convergence problems in the forward
problem.
In conclusion, it has been demonstrated how contact forces such as normal
pressure forces and coulomb friction forces can be resolved at every timestep
during a dynamic ﬁnite element analysis. Further more, it has proved to
be possible to conduct a destiny based topology optimization for a structure
undergoing frictional impacts with a rigid straight obstacle.
7
Future work
As it has been proven possible to conduct density based optimization of
frictional impact problems, the forward research may go in many diﬀerent
directions. As a ﬁrst thing, it would be interesting to solve more problems
to conduct further investigations and assessments of the direct implications
of including friction forces into the design problems, as the eﬀects have only
brieﬂy been examined in this work due to time limitations. It would also
be interesting to solve cases where friction forces are more dominant. One
could, e.g., solve the case in which friction causes ﬂicking of a structure and
minimize the structural compliance of such a setting.
Also, it would be of great interest to extend the contact formulation to
allow for non-straight obstacles and enforce the contact condition in an integ-
ral sense. This would allow for slightly more exciting geometric conditions of
the design problems. By also including geometrical non-linearities in the op-
timization, interesting results are likely to be obtained. The inclusion of such
non-linearities should follow the usual approach, which is well documented
in the literature.
Naturally, it is very intriguing to optimize for something else than com-
pliance. With the derivations given in this thesis, it should be possible to
come up with an objective function for the contact forces, to achieve desired
properties or behavior of these.
As the topology optimization framework itself is the same, as for ordinary
transient structural optimization without contact, the framework should be
directly compatible with recent developments within the research ﬁeld. This
means that methods developed for e.g. increasing discreteness, accounting for
manufacturing uncertainties and so on should be directly applicable frictional
impact problems as well.

A.1
Introduction to the ﬁnite element method
The Finite Element Method is a mathematical tool that may be applied to
approximate a set of ﬁeld variables. A ﬁeld problem is a type of problem in
which some distribution of a quantity is of interest. Quantities of interests
could be, but are not restricted to, structural displacement, temperature
or pressures. To derive the ﬁeld quantities the structure is discretized into
a ﬁnite number of elements.
The arrangement of elements is referred to
as the mesh. Elements are connected at nodes. Within each element, the
ﬁeld quantity is allowed to have only very simple variation. The variation
is usually described by low-order polynomials that interpolate nodal values
across the element. The polynomials used for interpolation are referred to
as the shape functions. The physical variation of ﬁeld quantities across an
element is undoubtedly not described by a low-order polynomial; thus the
Finite Element method provides an approximation to the real distribution of
the ﬁeld quantities. Nodal values of ﬁeld quantities combined with the shape
functions completely describes the spatiality of the ﬁeld quantity, element by
element. The solution represents an approximation, but the solution may be
improved by mesh reﬁnement.

Filtering methods
Filtering is a methodology used in relation to topology optimization to avoid
the well-known problem of occurring checkerboard- and mesh dependent
structures. Checkerboard regions are identiﬁed as areas of alternating solid
and void and non-physical nodal connections between elements, as shown
in ﬁgure 40. The checkerboard problem occurs as nodal connections have
artiﬁcially high stiﬀness that is captured by the ﬁnite element model and
exploited by the optimizer. Mesh dependent designs are encountered as the
optimizer can create smaller features as the mesh is reﬁned, which is usually
beneﬁcial.

freedom increase. A simple continuous square structure meshed into a grid
of two-by-two Q4 elements would have 18 degrees of freedom - meaning that
the global matrices would be 18×18 elements large with 324 entities. For the
sake of simplicity the conventional assembly process is instead illustrated for
the global stiﬀness matrix of the three bar truss structure of ﬁgure 43a. This
structure has 3 elements, 3 nodes and 6 degrees of freedom. Each element
has 4 degrees of freedom so element stiﬀness matrices are 4×4 entities large.
Even though the process is illustrated for a truss structure, the analogy for
assembly of a global matrix for a structure of Q4 elements is the same.

The damping matrix
All physical structures are damped. The concept of damping is well known
from everyday life as amplitudes of any vibration tends to decay with time -
that is energy is slowly dissipated. Damping also acts to limit the response of
a structure that is harmonically loaded by a load whose frequency matches a
natural frequency of the system. Damping that is proportional to velocities,
as in (A.18), is referred to as Rayleigh damping. Rayleigh damping may
