Preface 
This master thesis, dedicated to the research field of antimicrobial resistance in the food animal 
production, represents the final component in the completion of my MSc in Food Technology. 
I was engaged in the project from August 2023 to January 2024, and it was undertaken in 
collaboration with the Research Group for Genetic Epidemiology at the National Food Institute 
at the Technical University of Denmark. The scope of this project was shaped in collaboration 
with my main supervisor Per Håkan Vigre, who provided both general guidance and valuable 
input throughout the entire duration of the project. The main learning objectives devised for 
this thesis was to 1) model the effect of antimicrobial usage on antimicrobial resistance in the 
Danish pig production by applying time series analysis, and 2) use the developed time series 
models to forecast the future occurrence of antimicrobial resistance.  
The project was based on data collected by the Danish Integrated Antimicrobial Resistance 
Monitoring and Research Programme (DANMAP) from 2015 to 2022. The laboratory analysis 
and bioinformatics processing of the samples were completed by the research group before the 
commencement of my project work. Thus, it should be understood that any mention of the 
sampling, laboratory and bioinformatic methodology is an outline of the efforts made by others, 
which formed the foundation for my data analysis. In the initial phase of my project, I focused 
on data management, involving thorough preprocessing and quality assurance of the data, as 
well as other necessary steps to prepare the time series data for analysis. This stage ended up 
consuming more time than anticipated, largely due to the effort needed to correct potential 
systematic errors identified in the data. The initial groundwork was essential for the following 
analysis, where I explored a range of analytical techniques to best interpret and derive 
meaningful conclusions from the data.  
Taking the role as the first author of a scientific manuscript was one of the devised learning 
objectives. Therefore, the presented work was drafted by following the guide for authors from

Abstract  
7 
Antimicrobial usage (AMU) is recognized as a driving factor for the occurrence of bacterial 
8 
antimicrobial resistance (AMR) both in humans and food animal productions. Currently, 
9 
regulatory measures concerning AMU in the Danish pig production are implemented in 
10 
response to the occurrence of phenotypic AMR detected in selected microorganisms isolated 
11 
from caecum samples collected from slaughter pigs in the Danish Integrated Antimicrobial 
12 
Resistance Monitoring and Research Programme (DANMAP). Advancements in high-
13 
throughput metagenomic sequencing has provided the opportunity to estimate the abundance 
14 
of antimicrobial resistance genes (ARGs) from the microbiome DNA in preserved DANMAP 
15 
samples pooled on a monthly basis from 2015 to 2022. To support the development of a 
16 
knowledge-based and predictive decision-making tool for authorities, this study aimed to 
17 
assess the forecast accuracy of dynamic regression time series models quantifying the effect of 
18 
AMU on the mean relative abundance of ARGs in the gut microbiome of slaughter pigs. The 
19 
AMU was measured as the lifetime exposure to eight specific antimicrobials (AMs) by 
20 
calculating the relative number of standard doses ordinated throughout the rearing period 
21 
(piglets, weaners, and finishers) to pigs slaughtered each month. The dynamic regression time 
22 
series models were computed using the ARG abundance training dataset from 2015 to 2021 
23 
==PAGE:6==
Forecasting Antimicrobial Resistance Gene Abundance  
 2 
 
and was based on the isolated time series trends. The models were developed to assess both the 
24 
immediate and long-term effect of lifetime AMU, separately on the resistance developed 
25 
towards the specific AMs. Using the models, the abundance of ARGs was predicted for 2022 
26 
and evaluated against the ARG abundance test dataset from the same year. The results shows 
27 
that the use of all the investigated AMs was identified as a significant predictor for the 
28 
corresponding AMR. Increased lifetime AMU was found to result in an increase in the ARG 
29 
abundance after 5 to 9 months. The forecasts were able to capture the variation in the observed 
30 
ARG abundance trends from 2022, with an average error in the monthly predictions of 2-14%. 
31 
The forecasts were generally underestimated, likely attributed to the models not including co-
32 
selection effects of other AMs. In conclusion, this study demonstrated that leveraging 
33 
population-level time series data generated DANMAP for dynamic regression time series 
34 
modelling enabled the prediction of the long-term impact of real-time AMU on the abundance 
35 
of ARGs. Such forecasting of future ARGs abundance trends could potentially enable 
36 
authorities to respond prior to an increase in the occurrence of AMR and evaluate the impact 
37 
of potential AMU interventions.

1. Introduction  
45 
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a leading global health threat, compromising the prevention 
46 
and treatment of bacterial infections in humans (WHO, 2021). The selective pressure of 
47 
antimicrobial usage (AMU) – preventing the proliferation of susceptible bacteria but not 
48 
resistant bacteria – is considered the main driver of the emergence and increased prevalence of 
49 
AMR (DANMAP, 2022; WHO, 2021). Resistant bacteria may be transmitted to humans from 
50 
the environment, animals, and food of animal origin, emphasizing the need for a One Health 
51 
approach to ensure the continued effectiveness of antimicrobials (AMs) (Aarestrup, 2015).  
52 
In Denmark, AMR and AMU has been monitored by The Danish Integrated Antimicrobial 
53 
Resistance Monitoring and Research Programme (DANMAP) since 1995. In samples collected 
54 
from different sources, the annual occurrence of AMR is determined by means of phenotypic 
55 
testing of specific zoonotic and indicator bacterial species (DANMAP, 2022). Monitoring data 
56 
plays a crucial role in facilitating the establishment of regulations and policies concerning 
57 
AMU to reduce the occurrence of AMR. As approximately 75% of AMU in Denmark is linked 
58 
to the pig production, regulatory measures are primarily focused on this sector (Danish 
59 
Veterinary and Food Administration, n.d.). To date, these measures, implemented as a response 
60 
to the observed occurrence of AMR detected by DANMAP, rely on qualitative knowledge 
61 
about the effect of AMU on AMR (Andersen et al., 2023). Therefore, the development and 
62 
implementation of a quantitative approach, aimed at ensuring the desired impact of AMU 
63 
interventions, could contribute to a more efficient mitigation of the human health risk linked to 
64 
the prevalence of AMR in the Danish pig production. 
65 
The attribution of AMR in the food animal production to the human AMR burden has not been 
66 
established (Aarestrup, 2015; Fastl et al., 2023). However, it relies on the likelihood of the 
67 
horizontal transfer (i.e., gene transfer between bacteria) of antimicrobial resistance genes 
68 
(ARGs) to human pathogens, which depends on the prevalence of ARGs in the microorganisms 
69 
==PAGE:8==
Forecasting Antimicrobial Resistance Gene Abundance  
 4 
 
within the gastrointestinal tract (the microbiome) of the animals. With advancements in high-
70 
throughput metagenomic approaches it has become possible to estimate the abundance of 
71 
ARGs from the total-community DNA in any given samples, defined as the resistome (Pillay 
72 
et al., 2022). Studies have shown that metagenomics outperformed culture-dependent 
73 
phenotypic testing to detect resistance in pig herds (Duarte et al., 2023; Munk et al., 2017). 
74 
Moreover, it has been demonstrated that the effect of changes in AMU are more easily detected 
75 
in the resistome (Duarte et al., 2023), and that it depends both on the specific AMs and ARGs 
76 
(Abuoun et al., 2021; Andersen et al., 2017, 2023; Bangsgaard et al., 2021; Birkegård et al., 
77 
2017; Gompel et al., 2019). 
78 
Metagenomics constitutes a promising future approach within AMR monitoring, improving the 
79 
foundation for quantitatively assessing the impact of national AMU interventions. Moreover, 
80 
as the AMR monitoring by DANMAP inherently produces time series data, it enables the 
81 
utilization of time series analysis techniques. In a recent study, monthly samples from Danish 
82 
slaughter pigs from 2015 to 2018 were analysed by metagenomics, and subsequently the 
83 
association between AMU and the corresponding AMR – i.e., the resistance developed towards 
84 
the specific AMs – was investigated in a time series cross-correlation analysis (Duarte et al., 
85 
2023). The study revealed that changes in AMU in the Danish pig production had a delayed 
86 
effect on AMR, manifesting as a change in the abundance of ARGs in slaughter pigs within 3 
87 
to 11 months. The development of time series models of the effect of AMU on AMR also 
88 
provides the opportunity to forecast the future occurrence of AMR. Contingent upon the 
89 
predictive performance of these models, the impact of AMU interventions can be evaluated. 
90 
Time series models based exclusively on historical AMR data (i.e., ARIMA models) have been 
91 
the focus of other studies on time series analysis of AMR in food animal productions (Boireau 
92 
et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2023), but the potential predictive performance of these models is 
93 
limited by their inability to account for the effect of changes in AMU. In hospital settings, the 
94 
==PAGE:9==
Forecasting Antimicrobial Resistance Gene Abundance  
 5 
 
use of dynamic regression time series models (DR models), which account for both the effect 
95 
of external predictors, such as AMU, and historical temporal dynamics, have proven to be a 
96 
statistically robust technique to model and potentially forecast AMR (Laffont-Lozes, Larcher, 
97 
et al., 2023). 
98 
Building upon these findings, the objective of this study was to investigate the forecast 
99 
accuracy of DR models quantifying the immediate and delayed effect of AMU on AMR in the 
100 
Danish pig production from 2015 to 2021. By means of metagenomic analysis, AMR was 
101 
measured as the abundance of ARGs in pooled cecum samples collected monthly from 
102 
slaughterhouses. The DR models were developed separately for the effect of eight AMs 
103 
(aminoglycosides, amphenicols, extended-spectrum penicillins, narrow-spectrum penicillins, 
104 
lincosamides, macrolides, pleuromutilins, and tetracyclines) on the corresponding AMR. The 
105 
predictive performance of the DR models was assessed for the forecasted ARG abundances for 
106 
2022 by external evaluation. Ultimately, this research seeks to support the development of a 
107 
framework for predicting the impact of national AMU interventions by means of existing AMU 
108 
and AMR monitoring data.

2.1. Estimating lifetime AMU 
111 
The lifetime AMU – i.e., the AMs ordinated throughout the entire rearing pathway (consisting 
112 
of three periods: piglet, weaner and finisher) of slaughter pigs – should be considered when 
113 
studying the effect of AMU on AMR (Andersen et al., 2017, 2018). Therefore, lifetime AMU 
114 
was estimated from data of purchased AMs in the Danish pig production. Data on AMU in 
115 
Danish pig farms was obtained from the Danish Veterinary Medicine Statistic Program 
116 
database (VetStat), which contain records of purchased veterinary drugs. Data records on AMs 
117 
purchased for pigs from 2012 to 2022 were extracted from VetStat (on 28th of September 2023). 
118

2.2. Estimating ARG abundance 
192 
Data on the abundance of ARGs in Danish slaughter pigs from 2015 to 2022 was obtained from 
193 
metagenomic analysis of samples collected by DANMAP. The DANMAP program entails the 
194 
monthly collection of caecal samples from individual healthy pigs in slaughterhouses by meat 
195 
inspection staff. The sampling is stratified by slaughterhouse, allocating more samples to those 
196 
with a greater annual throughput, and by herd size, with larger herds being sampled more 
197 
frequently, followed by the random selection of a slaughtered pig from each of the selected 
198 
herds  (DANMAP, 2022). The caecal material is frozen and stored at the Technical University 
199 
of Denmark. The preserved samples were pooled on a monthly basis from March 2015 to 
200 
December 2022 (89 samples in total). The pooled samples contained between 4 to 25 individual 
201 
samples each (median: 25, IQR: 17-25), varying based on monthly sample availability. The 
202 
pooling procedure is described in detail elsewhere (Duarte et al., 2023).  
203 
The ARG abundance was determined by shotgun metagenomic sequencing of the pooled 
204 
samples. An elaborate description of the DNA extraction, sequencing and bioinformatic

2.3. Time series analysis   
239 
The time series analysis focused on investigating the effect of lifetime AMU on the abundance 
240 
of ARGs in slaughter pigs and subsequently forecasting the future occurrence of ARGs, 
241 
utilizing dynamic regression time series models (DR models) and monthly time series data. 
242 
The analysis was made separately for the eight AM-classes and the corresponding AMR-
243 
classes. The analysis was carried out in R (version 4.2.1).  
244 
An overview of the data analysis is presented in Figure 2. Initially, the time series data was 
245 
processed to handle data gaps and systematic errors, decomposed to remove seasonality and 
246 
random errors, and made stationary to comply with underlying statistical assumptions to 
247 
compute reliable DR models and accurate forecasts. The ARG abundance time series was 
248 
divided into a training dataset used to compute the DR models – from June 2015 to December 
249 
2021 (79 months) – and a test dataset used for external evaluation of the forecasts made with 
250 
the DR models – from January to October 2022 (10 months).

2.3.2. Time series decomposition  
296 
The lifetime AMU and ARG abundance time series data were intrinsically affected by annually 
297 
occurring patterns arising from various factors, such as disease cycles and, among others, 
298 
vacation and harvesting periods influencing drug purchases (known as the seasonal time series 
299 
component, St). Additionally, the time series were influenced by random data and sampling 
300 
errors, as well as variations in laboratory analysis (known as the residual time series 
301 
component, Rt). To avoid that these components masked the effects of lifetime AMU on the 
302 
abundance of ARGs in the analysis, they were removed to isolate the underlying gradual 
303 
variation of the AM use and resistance over time (known as the trend component Tt). 
304 
==PAGE:19==
Forecasting Antimicrobial Resistance Gene Abundance  
 15 
 
The seasonal components were identified by means of the STL (seasonal and trend 
305 
decomposition using locally weighted regression, loess) function of the R package stats 
306 
(version 4.2.1). The STL function determined the variation in the time series that originated 
307 
from the seasonal component, by analysing the seasonal sub-series (the time series of all values 
308 
from the same month across all years). The seasonally adjusted time series were estimated by 
309 
subtracting the values of the seasonal component from the original time series values, as the 
310 
STL function assumes an additive time series composition (i.e., each value in the time series is 
311 
the sum of the three components: Tt + St + Rt).  
312 
The lifetime AMU trend components were derived from the seasonally adjusted lifetime AMU 
313 
time series by means of the SMA (simple moving average) function from the R package TTR 
314 
(version 0.24.3). The SMA function smoothed the time series by computing the average of 
315 
each value in the time series with the values from the two past months, to reduce noise from 
316 
the residual component. The first two months, where the lack of past values resulted in 
317 
incomplete smoothing, were omitted from the time series. 
318 
Using the STL function, the ARG abundance trend components were derived from the 
319 
seasonally adjusted ARG abundance time series, by means of locally weighted regression 
320 
(loess, i.e., a weighted moving average with both past and future values). The STL function 
321 
smoothed the time series by computing the weighted average of each value in the time series 
322 
with the values from the two past and future months, to reduce the noise from residual 
323 
component. The weights were assigned using the tricube weight functions (Cleveland et al., 
324 
1990), giving the central value and the values one and two months before and after the central 
325 
value a weight of 1, 0.90, and 0.36, respectively. The first and last two months (March and 
326 
April 2015, and November and December 2022), where the lack of past or future values 
327 
resulted in incomplete smoothing, were omitted from the time series.

2.3.3. Time series stationarity  
329 
By visual inspection, the lifetime AMU and ARG abundance time series trends were identified 
330 
as non-stationary (characterized by a non-constant mean and heteroscedasticity). The time 
331 
series were made stationary to comply with underlying statistical data assumptions to compute 
332 
reliable DR models and accurate forecasts. Stationarity of the time series was obtained by 
333 
differencing – that is, estimating the difference between each value in the time series and the 
334 
value the month prior. Thereby, the values in the time series represented the absolute change 
335 
in lifetime AMU and ARG abundance from the month prior. As a consequence of differencing, 
336 
the first value was omitted from the time series trends. Stationarity was verified by means of 
337 
the Kwiatkowski-Phillips-Schmidt-Shin (KPSS) and Augmented-Dickey–Fuller (ADF) tests 
338 
for trend stationarity, using the kpss.test and adf.test function of the R package tseries (version 
339 
0.10-54), and by visual inspection to ensure homoscedasticity. 
340

4. Discussion  
506 
In this study, the abundance of ARGs in the gut microbiome of slaughter pigs was forecasted 
507 
by means of the estimated effect of lifetime AMU in the Danish pig production, using dynamic 
508 
regression time series models (DR models). The computed DR models were based on national 
509 
monitoring data (DANMAP) from 2015 to 2021 and developed by means of the ARG 
510 
abundance (measured by means of metagenomic sequencing of pooled caecum samples) and 
511 
lifetime AMU time series trends. By including lifetime AMU as a time-lagged predictor in the 
512 
DR models, it was possible to identify the time between the full effect of a change in lifetime 
513 
AMU in the Danish pig production on the average abundance of ARGs in slaughter pigs. The 
514 
DR models – linear regression and SARIMA error models, estimating the effect of time-lagged 
515 
lifetime AMU and the remaining temporal dependencies respectively – accounted for most of 
516 
the variation in the ARG abundance time series trends, equivalent to, as presented in a recent 
517 
systematic review, DR models analysing the effect of AMU on AMR in hospital settings 
518 
(Laffont-Lozes, Larcher, et al., 2023).  
519 
Using the DR models and the identified predictive time-lagged lifetime AMU, it was possible 
520 
to predict the ARG abundance time series trends for 2022, which subsequently were evaluated 
521 
against the observed ARG abundance trends. The forecasted ARG abundance trends captured 
522 
the variation in the observed ARG abundance trends for most classes. However, the forecast 
523 
did not incorporate an error measurement of the inherent accumulation of inaccuracies in the 
524 
predictions over time. Nevertheless, the findings of this study suggest that utilizing population-
525 
level time series data generated by DANMAP together with DR models enables the prediction 
526 
of the long-term effect of real-time national AMU. This ability to predict the long-term effects 
527 
of changes in AMU could enable authorities to respond promptly and with due diligence, before 
528 
the AMR is affected in the slaughter pig population.  
529 
==PAGE:31==
Forecasting Antimicrobial Resistance Gene Abundance  
 27 
 
To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study to: 1) forecast AMR time series based on 
530 
the effect of AMU in food animal production, and 2) utilize metagenomic AMR data in DR 
531 
models to forecast the effect of AMU on the abundance of ARGs. The computed univariate 
532 
DR models analysed the response in the abundance of ARGs to changes in the lifetime AMU 
533 
of the corresponding AM-class exclusively. In accordance with several studies, the use of all 
534 
the investigated AMs was identified as significant predictor for the corresponding AMR 
535 
(Andersen et al., 2020; Bangsgaard et al., 2021; Birkegård et al., 2017; Duarte et al., 2023; 
536 
Gompel et al., 2019). The forecasts computed by means of the univariate DR models 
537 
underestimated the future ARG abundance trends for most of the classes. This is likely 
538 
attributed to co-selection, observed for the usage of the majority of the AMs (Andersen et al., 
539 
2020, 2023), which was not accounted for in the predictions as the effects of other AMs were 
540 
not included in the DR models. Moreover, the underestimated predictions may also be 
541 
attributed to an underestimation of the regression coefficients, which is a likely consequence 
542 
of regression dilution caused by variation in the rearing pathway of slaughter pigs introducing 
543 
errors in the lifetime AMU estimates. Some autocorrelation persisted in the innovation errors 
544 
of some of the models, which suggest that all temporal dynamics were not accounted for, 
545 
inevitably affecting the forecast accuracy as well. Generally, the actual impact of AMU on 
546 
AMR is believed to be more substantial than what the results have indicated. 
547 
The forecasts were generated using the identified time-lagged lifetime AMU, which was found 
548 
to be predictive of the abundance of ARGs for each class. The identified time-lag, representing 
549 
the long-term effect of lifetime AMU on the abundance of ARGs, is likely determined both by 
550 
the delay of the full effect of a change in lifetime AMU, as well as the time it takes for the 
551 
monitoring system to detect the resulting change in the abundance of ARGs. The identified 
552 
time-lags imply that the full effect of a change in lifetime AMU in the Danish pig production 
553 
is detected in slaughter pigs within a year. These results align with the results from a recent 
554 
==PAGE:32==
Forecasting Antimicrobial Resistance Gene Abundance  
 28 
 
Danish cohort study of lifetime AMU in pig farms (Andersen et al., 2023), indicating that the 
555 
effects detected at population-level are consistent with the effects identified at farm-level in the 
556 
pig production in Denmark. This suggests that DANMAP captures the effects of the changes 
557 
in AMU on AMR in the gut microbiome of slaughter pigs and that the time series monitoring 
558 
data is suitable for developing models to predict national AMR trends.  
559 
The significant lags identified for each AM are contingent upon the specific time series data 
560 
and the period in which it was analysed, as well as how it was processed and included in the 
561 
models. Prior to modelling, the ARG abundance time series were adjusted to reduce an 
562 
observed systematic increase from 2018 to 2019 for all AMR-classes. The increase is suspected 
563 
to be attributed to the identified 48% increase in the average total FC from the 2015-2018 to 
564 
the 2019-2022 samples, which were sequenced in separate years. Though the increase was 
565 
inherently reflected in the abundance of fragments mapped to bacterial genes as well, 
566 
normalization of the ARG abundances by means of the bacterial gene abundances did not 
567 
significantly reduce the increase. The ARG abundance time series were adjusted by aligning 
568 
the 2015-2018 and 2019-2022 data to remove the observed increase and reduce the potential 
569 
effect it would have on the identification of the significant lags. The adjustment was justified 
570 
considering that the systematic increase was not observed in the annual phenotypic AMR 
571 
monitoring of the same samples reported by DANMAP (DANMAP, 2022). However, this 
572 
adjustment might also bias the assessment of significant AMU lags by potentially removing 
573 
true variation in the data. As demonstrated by several studies, macrolide usage results in an 
574 
increase in the abundance ARGs of many different AMR-classes (Andersen et al., 2020, 2023). 
575 
Therefore, the effect of the notable increase in macrolide usage from 2017 to 2018, which, 
576 
according to this and other studies (Andersen et al., 2023; Duarte et al., 2023), would typically 
577 
manifest in AMR after approximately a year (i.e., between 2018 to 2019), could inadvertently 
578 
have been omitted from the AMR time series data. This might have impacted the assessment 
579 
==PAGE:33==
Forecasting Antimicrobial Resistance Gene Abundance  
 29 
 
of the identified significant time-lag of lifetime AMU for all the AMR-classes and might 
580 
explain why the predictions of macrolide resistance failed to reflect the actual trend patterns 
581 
observed in 2022. Given that macrolides are among the most used antimicrobials in Danish pig 
582 
production and one of the most frequently observed resistance classes in slaughter pigs, 
583 
minimal forecast errors, like those demonstrated by the tetracycline model, was expected. 
584 
Nevertheless, as the systematic increase does not manifest gradually over time, but occur from 
585 
December 2018 to January 2019, it suggests that most of the increase is likely due to a 
586 
systematic error rather than a simultaneous large increase between two consecutive months 
587 
across all classes due to the effect of the increase in macrolide use. Given the uncertainty about 
588 
the true ARG abundance time series trends from 2018 to 2019, using this data to develop a 
589 
forecasting tool requires careful considerations and may potentially require resequencing of 
590 
some of the DANMAP samples from each period (2015-2018 and 2019-2022) to ascertain 
591 
whether the observed increase represents a systematic error or true variation.  
592 
The DR models were based on the ARG abundance and lifetime AMU time series trends, which 
593 
were isolated by identifying and removing seasonality, and subsequently smoothing the 
594 
seasonally adjusted time series to remove random data and sampling errors. The number of 
595 
past and future values included in the moving average smoothing was based on the assumption 
596 
that: 1) the national AMR change gradually over time and therefore similar values are more 
597 
likely to occur than abrupt fluctuations between consecutive months, and 2) the potential 
598 
discrepancies between reported date of purchase and the actual data of ordination may extend 
599 
up to three months. By modelling the trends rather than the observed values, the predictability 
600 
of future values increases as unpredictable random errors were removed. However, true 
601 
variation may have been reduced by the smoothing as well, which would affect the assessment 
602 
of significant lifetime AMU lags. The trends may also have been affected by the linear 
603 
interpolation of the data gaps in the ARG abundance time series, which was based on the first 
604 
==PAGE:34==
Forecasting Antimicrobial Resistance Gene Abundance  
 30 
 
assumption as well. Though this only concerns five data points in the beginning of the ARG 
605 
abundance time series, more advanced imputation methods could be applied to increase the 
606 
reliability of the data and resulting forecasts.  
607 
In summary, the findings of this study demonstrated the predictive performance of the 
608 
computed DR models of the effect of AMU on AMR. This has only been demonstrated in one 
609 
prior study assessing the performance in predicting AMR from the effect of AMU in a French 
610 
hospital between 2014 and 2019 (Laffont-Lozes, Salipante, et al., 2023). In all DR models in 
611 
this study, only the effect of the corresponding AM was estimated. Developing DR models 
612 
where the effects of several AMs are included may results in better predictive performance. In 
613 
addition, multivariate DR models would be necessary as the basis of a national AMU 
614 
intervention impact assessment tool, to account for co-selection effects, both to improve 
615 
forecast accuracy and to account for the potential counteracting effects of consequential rises 
616 
in the usage of other AMs when the usage of one AM is reduced. Though studies have shown 
617 
that the detection of the effect of AMU on the abundance of ARGs is enhanced when samples 
618 
are pooled and aggregated to class level (Andersen et al., 2021), improved forecasting accuracy 
619 
might also be achieved by increasing the number of samples, which call for an extension in the 
620 
existing monitoring program. The DR models developed and evaluated in this study were built 
621 
on monitoring data from the Danish pig production, and a similar approach may be applied to 
622 
other animal species as well. In the future, when developing a tool for impact assessment of 
623 
national AMU interventions, the DR models must regularly incorporate new monitoring data 
624 
to adjust for changing temporal dependencies, ensuring the model's ongoing accuracy in future 
625 
predictions.  
626 
5. Conclusion 
627 
This study showed, that utilizing DR models to quantify the long-term effect of AMU in the 
628 
Danish pig production on the corresponding AMR, enabled the prediction of the impact of real-
629 
==PAGE:35==
Forecasting Antimicrobial Resistance Gene Abundance  
 31 
 
time AMU on the future occurrence of ARGs in the gut microbiome of slaughter pigs. These 
630 
findings showed that utilizing population-level time series data generated by national 
631 
monitoring systems was suitable for developing models aimed at assessing the impact of 
632 
national AMU interventions. The DR models, which focused exclusively on the effect of the 
633 
corresponding AM, explained most of the variance in the abundance of ARGs by accounting 
634 
for residual temporal dependencies in the data, signifying the statistical robustness of these 
635 
models in studying and predicting AMR. Developing DR models where the effects of several 
636 
AMs are included to account for co-selection could ensure increased forecast accuracy of the 
637 
future occurrence of AMR in the Danish pig production.
