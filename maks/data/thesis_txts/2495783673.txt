Abstract
The worlds largest particle accelerator, the LHC at CERN, is going to be up-
graded by 2026. The LHC accelerate protons to near speed of light and collide
them inside diﬀerent detectors. The goal of the upgrade is to increase to lu-
minosity which will drastically increase the amount of data collected in the
detectors. To handle this increase, the trigger and data acquisition system will
need to be redesigned.
This thesis details the design and implementation of two digital circuits on
the Stratix 10 2100 MX FPGA that performs operations on the particle track
data coming from the detector. The ﬁrst circuit implements a path searching
algorithm on detector data, which will increase the data rate from 80 MHz to 742
MHz. The second circuit perform linearized helix parameter calculations used
for further processing the particle track data. For the second block, multiple
DSP circuits were designed in order to evaluate the best ﬁtting in terms of
timing and logic utilization.
Furthermore an investigation on the FPGA platform under heavy utilization in
terms of DSP count was performed in order to understand how it would behave
under stressed conditions.
Two working designs have been described and implemented in VHDL. Both
are able to run at 200 MHz as required, and uses less resources than initially
allocated. They were designed using a minimum logic and switching activity,
resulting in a very low power consumption.

Synopsis
Verdens største partikel accelerator, LHC i CERN, er planlagt for en opgradering
i 2026. Målet er at øge styrken af kollisionerne, hvilket vil øge datamængden som
detektorerne indsamler drastisk. For at kunne behandle den øgede datamængde,
vil man være nødt til også at opgradere data indsamlings systemet.
Denne afhandling beskriver design og implementeringen af to digitale kredsløb
på en Stratix 10 2100 MX FPGA, som udfører matematiske operationer på
partikeldataen fra detektoren. Det første design udfører en sti ﬁnder algoritme
på detektor dataen for at ﬁnde den korrekte sti som partiklen rejse. Dette sker
ved en data rate på 742 MHz. Det andet design udfører en linariseret helix
parameter udregning, som skal bruges til yderligere analyse og udvælgelse af
partiklerne. For design nummer to blev der udviklet og testet fem designs, for
at ﬁnde det bedst egnede til dette projekt. Udover de to designs blev der også
undersøgt hvordan FPGA platformen opfører sig når den er fyldt udnyttet.
To fuldt fungerende kredsløb blev designet, implementeret og testet i VHDL. De
kan begge køre ved 200 MHz som kravet foreskrev, og bruger færre ressourcer
end oprindelig allokeret. De blev designet til at bruge et minimum af hardware
og switching, hvilket har resulteret i et meget lavt strømforbrug.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The largest particle accelerator in the world, the LHC, is due to an upgrade in
2026. The upgrade will result in a steep increase in data generation at each of
the proton-proton collision points in the ATLAS detector. An important part of
the ATLAS detector is the trigger and data acquisition system, which is doing
online ﬁltering of the data from ATLAS. The current trigger and data acquisi-
tion system will not be able to keep up with the increased data rate and will
need to be redesigned.
This thesis have designed and implemented two VHDL designs. They are part
of a larger design which will perform a selection of the collision data that are rel-
evant for scientiﬁc analysis. The ﬁrst design, the track distributor, implements
a path ﬁnding algorithm on detector data which will increase the data rate from
80 MHz to 742 MHz. The paths that are found will go through a χ2 calculator
and then to the second The second block, the Helix Parameter Calculator, is
computing ﬁve helix parameters that describe the path of the particle travelling
inside the detector. This is used to analyse the trajectory of the particles in
order to determine the type of particle and its energy.
The Helix Parameter Calculator design has high computational requirements,
and it was important to ﬁnd the optimal solution within the speciﬁed con-
straints. Therefor ﬁve diﬀerent designs where proposed, each with its own ad-
vantage and disadvantage, before the best suited design was implemented.
Before the two design process began, a performance test of the FPGA platform

that the ﬁrmware will use was performed. A simpliﬁed edition of the equation
used to compute the helix parameters was implemented, in order to relate the
results to this circuit. The purpose was to investigate how the FPGA performed

This thesis successfully designed two fully operational digital circuits.
They

Chapter 2

This is a common chapter between Magnus Høegh Jensen and Mathias Braa-

This chapter present the background information about the CERN experiment

2.1
Introduction to CERN
2.1.1
LHC and ATLAS
The CERN is a joined European research organisation, which has the main re-
sponsibility of operating and maintaining a particle laboratory consisting of par-
ticle accelerators and detectors. CERN maintains and runs the largest particle
accelerator in the world; LHC. It is a circular accelerator with a 27 km circum-
ference consisting of superconducting magnets as well as accelerating structures
to increase the energy level in the LHC [1]. The particles inside the LHC travels
in opposite direction in two separate tubes, where they travel in complete vac-
uum [2]. The particles are accelerated up to 99.9999991 % the speed of light and
guided into each other for collision. There are 4 diﬀerent detectors at the LHC:
ATLAS [3], ALICE [4], CMS [5] and LHCb [6], that carries out diﬀerent exper-
iments. The collision between the particles is highly controlled by the scientists
==PAGE:20==
4
Background - common work
to happen inside one of the detectors, which then gathers data for research.
The LHC has lead to ground breaking physics discoveries, with the observation
of the Higgs boson in 2011 and 2012 as one of the most important ﬁndings by
both ATLAS [7] and CMS [8]. The Higgs boson was predicted by the standard
model, and it is necessary for the standard model to be correct. The observation
has thereby validated a part of the standard model, that had not been proved
before.
The LHC has proven to be capable of providing valuable scientiﬁc data, but
it is now reaching its limit of capabilities. To extend the possibilities of the
LHC, it has been decided to upgrade the LHC to increase the luminosity in the
accelerator. The project is called The High-Luminosity Large Hadron Collider
(HL-LHC), and it is expected to be operational mid 2026 [9].
The project aims to increase the luminosity with a factor of 10 beyond the orig-
inal design values of the LHC [1]. The increased luminosity will increase the
count of collisions that happens in a time slot. This result in larger quanti-
ties of data being collected, which will increase the likely-hood of rare scientiﬁc
observations. But the increased data-rate causes a challenge to the detectors,
that needs to measure the collisions and process the data. The detectors will
therefore need to undergo diﬀerent upgrades and modiﬁcations to be operative
during HL-LHC. This master thesis is a part of the upgrade of the ATLAS detec-
tor, which needs a new trigger system for online sorting and ﬁltering of the data.
2.1.2
The ATLAS experiment
The ATLAS (A Toroidal LHC Apparatus) experiment is one of the four major
experiments connected to the LHC [3]. It is the largest detector at CERN, with
a length of 46 m, a diameter of 25 m and a weight of 7000 tonnes. It is a general-
purpose detector that consist of diﬀerent components to be able to exploit the
full potential of the LHC [10], which has lead to the size of the detector. Figure
2.1 shows an illustration of the detector.
The detector consist of 4 major components:
• The Inner Detector: Measures the momentum of the charged particles
• The Calorimeter: Measures the energy carried by neutral and charged
particles

• The Magnet System: Bends the trajectories of the charged particles to

The detector also consist of two major integrated components. The ﬁrst com-
ponent is the TDAQ, that ﬁlters away noise and irrelevant data to reduce the
amount of data in the rest of the system [12].
The other integrated component is the Computing System, that consist of a dis-
tributed network of computers across 130 countries to store, process and analyse
the data from the collisions [13]. It is of great importance that a good ﬁltering
is done by the TDAQ to reduce the resources needed for storage and further
analysis of the data.
The TDAQ uses the data supplied by the Inner Detector to do the ﬁltering, but
the Inner Detector will not be able to function when the HL-LHC phase begins,

The Inner Detector
The Inner Detector is the ATLAS component located closest to the collision
point. The current implementation of the Inner Detector will not be able to
handle the increased rate of interactions inside the detector, and will be dam-
aged by the increased radiation in the LHC. It is therefor being completely
redesigned and replaced.
Figure 2.2: View of ITk as implemented in sections [14]
The new design will be an all-silicon system called the "Inner Tracker" (ITk),
which will consist of two types of modules: pixel [15] and strip [16], that are
able to measure where a particle intersect the module. The pixel modules mea-
sure the location of intersection point in two-dimension, while the strip modules
measure the intersection point in one-dimension.
Arrays of pixel an strip modules are placed on the ITk layers to allow the recon-
struction of charged particle trajectories. A visualisation of the ITk is shown in
ﬁgure 2.2. The modules are placed both in barrel layers, which are around the
collision point and in the end cap layers, which are placed perpendicular to the
LHC beam pipe in both ends of the detector. They are placed in this manner
to ensure that all possible tracks of the particles are measured correctly. Figure
2.3 shows the placement of the pixel and strip modules.
==PAGE:24==
8

Figure 2.3: A diagram showing the placement of the pixel and strip modules.

modules.
The LHC beam pipe is placed along the bottom axis.[17]
When a particle passes through the ITk each layer detects the intersection point
between the sensor and particle. The intersection point is referred to as a ’hit’.
The hits in each layer are used to reconstruct the trajectory of the particle,
and thereby analyse the type of particle. The particles are traveling in bunches
in the LHC which results in many collisions happening simultaneously. When
a collision happens many particles are shattered out through the detector. It
results in lots of hits detected, which is referred to as an ’Event’.
In ﬁgure 2.4 an expected collision is shown with the green lines representing the
tracks of particles send out from the collision point.
To be able to detect all the hits the ITk is build by many sectors, each measuring
the tracks of the particles. Each section has its own ﬁltering circuit to be able
to keep up with the high data-rate.

Since the three computational blocks are part of a larger ﬁrmware design made

The FPGA is going to be running with diﬀerent clocks for the diﬀerent sec-

The TFB is allowed latency of about 300 ns , which corresponds to 60 clock
cycles at 200 Mhz.

Power consumption
The power consumption of the FPGA have been estimated in a preliminary
research using Intel Early Power Estimator Tool[26]. It is based on an estimate
of the utilization of the diﬀerent building blocks of the FPGA, the input data
and the clock frequency.
The Current Best Estimates (CBE) of the PRM FPGA power consumption is
72 Watt of which the TFB consume 30 Watt including the static power con-
sumption of the FPGA. The implemented TFB design will need to stay within
this power budget.
Logic utilization
The ﬁrmware consists of many blocks with diﬀerent resource budgets. The TFB
is the only block that is estimated to utilize DSP blocks, thus all 3.960 DSPs
on the Stratix 10 MX will be available for the designs in this thesis. However
the DSPs have a large power consumption, and the utilization should be taken
into consideration when choosing a design.
The CBE for TFB logic utilization is at 10% of the available resources such as
registers and ALMs.

Conclusion
This preliminary test of the Stratix 10 MX platform was done in preparation of
the actual project, and should thus be viewed as such. It helped us to under-
stand how the FPGA will behave under heavy DSP usage and under diﬀerent
timing constraints.
We believe the drastic decline in the clock frequency as a function of number of
DSPs is due to the interconnect wire delay. However we believe that this will
be less of a problem for the actual implementation, as it will use fewer DSPs
per sub-circuit, and can thus utilize the DSPs on the FPGA in a faster way.
There was a signiﬁcant diﬀerence of the maximum frequency between the two
test circuits, even though the circuits are similar. The diﬀerence between the
two circuits is the placement of the DSPs. In the HPC test circuit all DSPs
are independent of each other, while the DSPs are grouped in pairs in the χ2
test circuit. It suggests that dependencies between DSP blocks may cause an
issue in the design. It might be because the synthesis tool has more diﬃculties
performing an eﬃcient ﬂoor-planning. Thereby longer wires are used which is
known to have a big impact on the performance of an FPGA.
Research [31] shows that the interconnect delay causes 50% of the total delay in
some designs, which is not far from what we experience during the tests. How-
ever as the test designs has several thousands DSPs in parallel, it is somewhat
diﬀerent from what we will implement in the project. Therefor we do not expect
the problem to be this vast, however it should be taken into consideration in
the design process.
The logic utilized in the test was used primarily for the multiplexers that chooses
to output the result from one of the DSPs. They ensures that the synthesis tool
does not trim away some of the DSP blocks during the tests.
It was evident that the multiplexers were the main contributors to the logic uti-
lization because exactly half the logic was used in the χ2 tests compared to the
PC tests. In the χ2 test circuit two DSPs were used to generate a result, while
one DSP was used to generate a result in the HPC test. The χ2 test circuit had
therefor half as many outputs as the HPC at a certain count of utilized DSPs.
Hence half the count of multiplexers was required by the χ2 to chose a result
for output.
The tests also showed an approximated linear increase in compile time.
To
gather the test results we had to perform more then 100 compilations adding
up to many hours of run time. It showed not to be feasible to do on a laptop
and instead a workstation was used to run the tests. It gave a great reduction
in compile time. It made it clear that a well equipped machine was required
for the project, hence we prioritized to setup a working directory on a server
==PAGE:51==

with remote access. It reduced the compile time signiﬁcantly and increased the
eﬃciency through the thesis.

maximum number of clusters per layer. For example if one layer contains three
clusters and the rest contains two, the latency for loading all the clusters is three

the circuit starts to output tracks immediately after the ﬁrst column of clusters
have been loaded.

Data conversions
The cluster data is represented as ﬁxed point numbers and need to be converted
to 32 bit ﬂoating point numbers to ﬁt the DSPs used by the χ2 block and the
HPC. Figure 2.12 shows how the pixel and strip layers are packed when received
from the DO. The conversion is done using the ﬁxed-to-ﬂoat IP core [25] pro-
vided by Intel. It is a fully pipelined block which receive the tracks computed
by the algorithm block and convert the cluster data to IEEE-754-2008 standard
for ﬂoating-points.
As there are four layers of strip data and four layers of two dimensional pixel
data, they will get routed to diﬀerent converters. Each of the four strip layers
have their overhead removed and are wired to a 4.8 ﬁxed point to ﬂoating point
converter
Each of the four pixel layer also get their overhead information removed and
each of the two pixel coordinated are routed to a 4.14 ﬁxed point to ﬂoating
point converter.
This results in 12 32 bit ﬂoating points. These are outputted to the χ2 block
together with the stalled IDs as speciﬁed in section 4.4.
Since all the layers 12 in all four TD computational blocks would need a con-
verter, this result in a total of 48.

The FIFO controller is implemented using a mealy state machine. It keeps track

6.2.2
Cascading
A dot product can be calculated by a cascade of blocks multiplying two sig-
nals and adding the result of the previous block. This is the idea behind the
cascading design shown in ﬁgure 6.2. The ﬁrst block of the cascade data line
is a multiplier implemented with a DSP that perform Ci0 · x0. The following
DSPs utilize the chain-in data line in the DSP in order to do the accumulation
computation such that block number two performs (Ci0 · x0) + Ci1 · x1. This is
done for all 12 indexes of equation 6.2.
This design exploits the hardware very well because both the adder and the
multiplier inside the DSP is used, except for the ﬁrst DSP.
The test results can be seen in table 6.1. Due to the very high achievable speed
of the DSPs and because there is no combinational logic in between, this design
can reach the highest clock frequency of all the designs tested. The latency is
very high as the data will have to pass through all the DSP in the cascade data
line. However it is possible to alter the DSP and remove some of the pipeline
registers such that both the latency and maximum achievable clock frequency
decreases.
The circuit is fully pipelined and can receive data at every clock cycle. This
makes it a very suitable circuit for computing dot products when clock frequency
and logic utilization is a high priority, and the latency of the circuit is not.
However there will need to be implemented some control logic delaying the
inputs. As the design is currently, the input signals have to be steady for 48
clock cycles. This could be solved by inserting a triangle of pipeline registers
delaying the input according to the layer.
The critical path is through the adder inside the DSP, and is therefor not possible
to make any shorter as there are no available pipeline registers left.

6.2.3
Parallel
The parallel design shown in ﬁgure 6.3 was made to decrease the latency of the
cascade design by computing values in parallel. However a dot product compu-
tation can never be fully parallelized as it contains dependencies.
The ﬁrst columns of DSPs compute all the multiplications in parallel as these
are independent. The multiplication results are passed on to a layer of adders
that adds the results in pair, which again is passed on to a new layer of adders.
This forms a pyramidal adder.
This decreased the latency to 24 clock cycles as seen in table 6.1. The design is
fully pipelined and can operate at 466 MHz. Another advantage is the lack of
control logic, since all 12 vectors can enter the pipelined circuit simultaneously.
This reduces the logic usage and the power consumption.
However the DSPs are not fully utilized since they do either an addition or
a multiplication, but not both like the cascade design, which is why the DSP
count is increased to 115.
As with the cascade design it is possible to reduce the latency and maximum
achievable clock frequency, by removing some of the pipeline registers inside the
DSP.
The critical path is from the output register of one DSP to the input register
of another. There is no logic between the DSP which was the same case in the
cascade design, but the maximum achievable clock speed is lower. This suggests
that the interconnect wire delay is setting a limit on the clock frequency. This
is similar to the behavior experienced in the χ2 test circuit in chapter 3.3.

Comparison
Each of the ﬁve designs has its own advantages and disadvantages, and which
one to chose highly depend on the project speciﬁcations and constraints.
As described in chapter 2.3.2 there were several constraints on the entire TFB.
• It have to run at a minimum of 200 MHz
• Use less than 3960 DSPs in total
==PAGE:86==
70
Parameter calculation
• have a latency of less than 300 ns (60 clock cycles at 200 MHz)
• Use less than 10% of the available logic
Furthermore the HPC will have to handle a data rate of 58 MHz.
The scalar implementation is the design that ﬁts these requirements best. It has
a latency of 16 clock cycles which is lowest of the pipelined designs, and it does
not need any additional control logic as the cascade and sequential design will.
It uses 60 DSPs which is within the budget of the HPC. By selecting a design
that can not be pipelined, there would need to be a queuing circuit after the
HPC block similar to what is after the χ2 block. This is because parallel blocks
will experience race conditions when each block produce a result simultaneously
if not proper designed. It is estimated that the HPC will have a lower power
consumption and logic utilization if a pipelined design is implemented, as there
is no need for a block handling the outputs of parallel blocks.
Digital designers that need to implement a vector dot product on an FPGA can
use this result as well.
If a high clock frequency is the most important factor in deciding on a design, I
suggest the cascading design that can reach 638 MHz. It has a large latency, but
since it is pipelined this might not be a problem depending on the constraints
of the design.
If latency on the other hand is the most important factor in deciding on a de-
sign, the vector dot design can compute a result in 9 clock cycles. It is however
quite expensive in terms of ALM’s and registers.
If DSP utilization is important and the data rate is diﬀerent from the clock
frequency, the sequential is a good choice. This is because it can handle 14 MHz
of data at 200 MHz per DSP and can be implemented in parallel in order to
adjust to the data rate of the design.

calculations blocks are placed in parallel, one for each parameter. The track

7.1
Combining the circuit blocks
7.1.1
Design
The TFB consists of the three components: The TD, the χ2-calculator and the
HPC, which all have been developed during our thesis.
During the development of the circuits it became evident that the TFB will have
to process data at a high data-rate. The components were therefor designed to
be pipelined, which ﬁts well to the high data-rate. The pipelined circuits also
make the integration between the parts more straight forward as each circuit
will be able to receive new data each clock cycle. It reduces the need of control
signals between the components.
==PAGE:92==
76
Integration - common work
The three blocks are integrated as shown in ﬁgure 7.1.
The track distributor consist of 4 parallel blocks to process the tracks at a suf-
ﬁcient rate. The χ2-calculator is also build by 4 parallel circuits that calculates
the χ2 for a track each in parallel. It allows a simple connection between the
two components as the tracks from each TD are connected directly to a corre-
sponding χ2-block. As both components are pipelined no control signals/ready
signals are required from the χ2-blocks to the TD-blocks.
The χ2-calculator calculates the χ2 of all 4 tracks in parallel. The HPC can
handle only a single track at a time hence a queuing circuit was designed as
part of the χ2-calculator.
The queuing circuit puts the track, the IDs related to the track and the χ2 re-
sult into a set of FIFOs. Only the tracks that passes the χ2 selection are saved
while the rest are discarded and not processed further. It reduces the data-rate
between the χ2-calculator and the HPC. The parameter calculator is pipelined
hence it can handle one new track each clock cycle. The queuing circuit is there-
for always providing a new track from the FIFOs to the parameter calculator
to be processed.
The HPC can process 1 track pr. clock cycle, but simulation shows that an aver-
age of 3 clock cycles will occur between accepted tracks. It is therefor expected
that the HPC is able to keep the number of tracks in the FIFO at a minimum
or empty. A valid signal is used to indicate if valid data is available to the HPC.
Figure 7.1 shows the top level design of the entire TFB.
Both the χ2-calculator and the HPC require constants from memory to calculate
the results. It requires a HBM to retrieve the data, hence the constants are saved
in HBM blocks as explained in the 2.3.1.
The HBM interface will be developed by other people and we have therefor not
used it in our thesis. For our thesis, the constants are saved in a ﬁle, from where
the data can be retrieved.

7.3
Results
7.3.1
Timing
The maximum achievable frequency of the integrated implementation with four
TFBs is 200.7 MHz. The clock frequency where set at 200 MHz as the con-
straints described in chapter 2.3.2 dictated. The model used for the synthesis
was slow 900mV and 100 C.
When only a single TFB was synthesised it could reach a clock speed of 217.6
MHz. This conﬁrms that the maximum achievable clock speed indeed will be
decreased when more logic is utilized on the FPGA.
This might become a problem when the rest of the PRM are implemented on
the FPGA, however the TFB is the only part that uses DSPs. The critical path
==PAGE:95==

of the design was between the output register of the TD and the pipeline regis-
ter between the TD block and the χ2 block. If the maximum achievable clock
frequency should drop even more when the rest of the PRM are implemented,
it would be possible to pipeline the critical path.
Figure 7.2: A slack histogram of the full implementation with four TFBs.
Figure 7.2 show a slack histogram of the implementation. It show a right ori-
ented distribution. Ideally it would be best to have all edges at 0 slack as it
would imply that the design is fully optimized and processing at the entire clock

increase the clock speed by pipelining only a small number of edges.
The latency of the χ2 block is 30 clock cycles, the latency of the HPC is 23 clock
cycles, and the latency of the TD is 0 clock cycles. This is not including the
FIFOs as the latency depends on the burst rate of the data. In total it takes 53
clock cycles for the data to propagate through the TFB which is lower than the
60 clock cycles speciﬁed by the constraints.

Chapter 8
Discussion
This thesis have explored the design and implementation of the Track Distrib-
utor and the Helix Parameter Calculator.
The test of the Stratix 10 2100 MX under heavy utilization provided interest-
ing correlations, and investigated the performance of a state of the art FPGA
that are not widely used yet. The TCL script developed during this project
can be used by other digital designers in many applications. We used it to test
the FPGA platform, however a more common application would be automated
scaling test of a design or comparison between two designs. For instance when
comparing two circuits that are designed to run in parallel, the TCL script can
be used to get data for comparison not only at a single number of parallel blocks,
but a wide range.
The wire delay between interconnected DSPs as described in section 6.2.3 and
when the TD and χ2 block was connected as described in chapter 7.1 was similar
to the wire delay experienced during the Stratix 10 test we performed.
The vector dot product designs using DSPs had very diﬀerent performances in
terms of clock speed, latency and logic utilization. This result can be used by
other digital designers implementing a vector dot product computation. Digital
design is very often about ﬁnding a balance between clock frequency, latency,
logic utilization and power consumption. Depending on the design constraints,
each of the vector dot product designs presented would be a good ﬁt.
==PAGE:100==
84
Discussion
The TD, however fully functioning, has potential for being optimized before the
integration with the rest of the ﬁrmware. For instance it would be possible to
implement two register matrix instead of one as proposed in this thesis: one
that handles the average case of 1-3 clusters per layer, and a larger register
matrix that can handles up to 10 clusters per layer. With the current design,
a single register matrix dimensioning will have to handle all cases from 1 to 10
clusters per layer. That implies that most of the registers are very rarely used.
By having two diﬀerent sized matrices, it could potentially save power as the
large matrix could be switched oﬀwhile not in use.
The limited memory bandwidth is the bottleneck in many FPGA applications,
and might also become a problem in the ﬁnal ﬁrmware design. The design of
the two blocks implemented in this thesis does not cover the HBM interface,
but does however use large quantities of bandwidth. When the TD and HPC
will be integrated in the rest of the ﬁrmware, some redesigning might have to
be done even though the bandwidth usage was greatly reduced by placing the
HPC after the χ2 calculator. For example by implementing a stall function,
making it possible to pause either the χ2 calculator or the HPC while the other
is retrieving data from the HPC. This would increase the latency of the circuit,
but as it is already below the constraint of 60 clock cycles, this could be an
option.
As the ﬁrmware is still in the design phase and not due to be ﬁnished until
2025-2026, there have been many unknowns regarding data rates, bit lengths
and data formats. The designs presented in this thesis have used the currently
available information, however they are still up for changing. This have been
partly solved by making dynamic VHDL code using global constants that auto-
matically scale the circuits accordingly. However if the bit length or data rates
changes from what has been speciﬁed in chapter 2.3.2, it might aﬀect the per-
formance of the designs in such a way that it will no longer meet the constraints
in terms of clock speed, latency, logic utilization and power consumption. This
includes the depth of the FIFOs as discussed in the respective chapters. This
will have to be handled when the ﬁnal design of the ﬁrmware is performed.
The slack experienced by the TD and HPC was very high as seen in ﬁgure 6.8
and 5.7. Since the designs are able to reach 316 Mhz and 408 Mhz respectively,
the slack could be used by increasing the clock frequency to the lowest of the
two (316 MHz) or to the same frequency as the DO in order to remove any clock
synchronizer. However since the χ2 block was not able to reach 250 MHz which
is the clock speed of the DO, the TFB design will have to adjust to the lowest
common denominator.
==PAGE:101==
85
We wanted to do a thorough comparison with the current implementation of
the TDAQ. However as this is implemented on a diﬀerent brand of FPGAs and
are dimensioned for lower data rates, a direct comparison in terms of timing
and logic utilization was not possible. It would have been possible to compare
the path searching algorithm and architecture of the HPC, but that was feasible
within the time frame of the thesis.
The design proved to be working correctly, however further testing would be
needed before it is ready to be implemented with the rest of the PRM. A more
thorough test could be designed that implemented random vectors and auto-
matic veriﬁcation, that would also increase the data rate in order to test the
limits of the design. Using realistic data and data rates would also provide a
more realistic power consumption estimation, as it is possible to inject test vec-
tors in the Quartus Power Analyzer.
The performance and hardware utilization for the TD and HPC were within the
speciﬁed constraints and fully functional. However when connected with the χ2
block, they were not fully utilized as they had to use a lower clock frequency.
Furthermore the full design with four TFBs was not able to use less than 10%
of the registers as speciﬁed by the constraints. The best solution would be to
do a new iteration of the design process and select a χ2 design that uses less
registers, and possibly more DSPs.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
In this thesis I have successfully designed and implemented two fully operational
circuits that meet the requirements after the ATLAS detector is upgraded.
I managed to keep the overhead latency of the TD block to zero such that it
could meet the required output data rate and not create a bottleneck. The HPC
was implemented with a minimum of DSPs and logic while still maintaining a
high clock frequency and low latency.
The diﬀerent dot product designs presented in the HPC can be used as a guide
for other digital designers, as they showed very diﬀerent behaviour each with
their own advantages and disadvantages.
The performance test of the Stratix 10 MX will help the digital designers of
the ATLAS collaboration in deciding how to optimally utilize the hardware.
Furthermore the behaviour experienced in the test was also seen when the TD
and HPC design were implemented and could thus be explained.

variable tmp : positive;

tmp := 1;

end loop;
return log;
