Summary 
Due to the increasing requirements, both legislatively and economically, for energy 
consumption and efficiency in buildings, causing increasingly complex building systems and 
requiring increasingly sophisticated design, building owners need a method of making sure 
that their buildings actually comply with the design specifications and legislative 
requirements. 
For this purpose, Building Commissioning is extremely useful, both when applied during the 
design and construction phase of the building project, so-called “New Building 
Commissioning”, and when applied after the building has already been construct, so-called 
“Existing Building Commissioning” or “Retro-commissioning”. However the scope of such 
commissioning projects are by definition very extensive due to the holistic approach 
requiring the investigation of the entire building, which can limit the implementation of 
Building Commissioning in the building sector. 
The topic of this study was to apply a new approach to Retro-commissioning at the Energi 
Fyn domicile in Odense, Denmark, implementing occupant questionnaires in the 
commissioning process in order to focus the process to the most problematic areas, thereby 
reducing the scope of the project by utilizing the occupants’ experience with the building. 
At the beginning of the project, occupant questionnaires were sent to the occupants of the 
Energi Fyn domicile, resulting in indications that the problems with the indoor climate were 
most frequent during the cooling season and were most severe in respectively the southern 
and northern open-plan offices. Additionally the types of problems that was shown to be 
higher than the reference values, draught and too varying temperatures, gave indication that 
the cause could be found in the control system of the building. 
Based on the results from the questionnaire, measurements and trend data analysis was 
carried out, to investigate the problems causing dissatisfaction with the indoor climate and 
inefficient operation of the building systems. Here it was found that the ventilation system 
was unable to achieve the set-points in the control system, because the set-points were set 
too low. This in turn caused relatively constant air flows, due to the inability of the ventilation 
system to achieve set-points even in periods with small cooling loads, restricting the ability 
of the plant to react to varying conditions and cooling loads. Additionally it was shown 
through local thermal environment measurements, that the supply air temperatures could be 
decreased to increase the cooling capacity of the ventilation system, without adversely 
affecting problems with draught, since these could not be connected to the ventilation system 
after the measurements. 
The project resulted in several conclusions. The first of which was that there was evidence 
that the questionnaire did in fact help identify the problems in the building. However the 
monitoring showed additional problems that required attention, which were not identified in 
the questionnaire, challenging the thoroughness of the approach. Due to this, the 
questionnaires’ ability to reduce the scope of the process without impacting the quality 
negatively could not be verified. Instead however, since the questionnaire can be practically 
automated, the questionnaire can still serve as a valuable tool in the retro-commissioning 
process, both as part of the monitoring phase and as part of composing the building’s indoor 
climate requirements, with little impact on the work required.

1 Introduction 
In recent years there has been an increasing focus on sustainability and green energy in 
society. As a consequence of this focus, low-energy buildings have become a priority in the 
building sector, both legislatively, but also as a means to secure a positive image of a 
company. Sustainable buildings with modern technology are able to add to a positive 
perception of the occupants.  
Green design however is not necessarily comfortable design. One of the ways green design 
manages to conserve energy is to eliminate in- and exfiltration, and instead use controlled 
ventilation. Combined with increased insulation of the building envelope, it creates a big 
potential for heat exchangers in the ventilation system, reducing the heat consumption. This 
de-incentivizes proper air change rates, since it will both increase the power consumption of 
ventilation and heating systems. With strict requirements for energy consumption in modern 
buildings, it is tempting to prioritize conserving energy over providing good comfort. 
Especially in the first decade of the 21st century many new buildings have been designed with 
a primary focus on energy consumption and sustainable with low priority on comfort. With 
the majority of work-places being offices, and a continuing proportion growth, office-
buildings also provide one of the largest market shares for new non-residential construction. 
Clearly poor comfort is not a problem in all modern buildings, and green buildings are not 
inherently uncomfortable, but in the cases where comfort is low, this provides a big issue. 
[1] 
It has been shown in several studies that the thermal comfort and air quality can affect the 
productivity and motivation of the occupants in a building. Higher ventilation rates and 
appropriate temperatures have been shown to be able to provide between 5 and 10% 
increased performance. Additionally, increasing ventilation rates has been shown to 
positively affect the amount of sick-days and absence rates. Noise distraction has also been 
shown to provide a significant decrease in productivity, meaning that while open-plane 
offices are a good way to reduce building area, precautions have to be made to reduce noise 
pollution. Since salaries account for approx. 92% of the costs over the lifetime of a typical 
office building, even a small change in productivity will have a big impact on the bottom 
line. This only stress the fact that building have to be designed to provide optimal conditions 
for the occupants, and that reducing the energy consumption comes in second. [2]   
When large building projects are undertaken it is usually necessary to split the project into 
several smaller, manageable parts. Each subcontractor will then only focus on their own 
project, and with initial investment costs taking precedence over later expenditure, 
integration with the other segments are not always a top priority. Building services should be 
integrated in a comprehensive system in order to be efficient. If the heating and ventilation 
system are not connected, there is a risk of one counteracting the other. 
Since there are few requirements for the indoor climate, strict requirements for the energy 
consumption from a legislative standpoint, and usually very strict requirements for the budget 
from the investors, the indoor climate of the building is at risk of taking a back-seat in the 
design process.

2 Ongoing commissioning 
In recent times there has been an increasing focus on climate change and the transition from 
fossil fuels to renewable energy. To ensure that the transition to sustainability is successful 
the transition needs to be economically feasible and this is where the need for energy 
optimization becomes interesting, since a lower energy consumption will inevitably make 
the transition cheaper. 
Since the need for transition to renewable energy sources has been widely accepted all over 
the world, the main issue is making this transition in the best way possible, and this requires 
an optimization of the energy consumption. Energy optimization has been incentivized 
differently in different areas, from legislation in the building sector, making the requirements 
towards energy consumption more stringent, to providing subsidies for investments.  
Either way the energy requirements for new buildings, whether they are provided by the 
building owner(s) or legislation, are becoming increasingly stringent. At the same time the 
building occupants are demanding a comfortable and healthy indoor environment. Fulfilling 
both the requirements to decrease or sustain a low level of energy consumption and providing 
a comfortable and healthy indoor environment is indeed problematic, since the two 
requirements inevitably will work against each other. On top of these two requirements the 
design team also have to adhere to the economic boundaries of a project. All these 
requirements pose a big challenge to the design team, and designers will use calculations and 
simulations to corroborate the building meeting the given requirements. Experienced 
building owners however, have known for a long time that the performance of new buildings, 
in regards to energy-use and comfort, is often not as intended, as stated in the introduction to 
the bi-monthly series “Technical vs. Process Commissioning” in the Ashrae Journal. [3] 
Previously buildings would perhaps be designed with over-dimensioning in key areas with 
increased attention, to ensure that the finished building would perform “at least” as well as 
promised. However since the financial crisis in 2008, leading to a more competitive bid 
market contractors are having to cut prices, combined with more and more stringent building 
regulations, simply “over-dimensioning” is no longer a feasible solution. 
It becomes apparent that there is a clear need for a method of ensuring that the performance 
of the finished building complies with the intended design and requirements. This issue was 
already identified and addressed in 1977 by Public Works Canada, when they began using 
building commissioning (Cx) in their projects. While this is still not common practice in 
Europe, it has been gaining in popularity in the U.S. with the help of organizations such as 
The Building Commissioning Association (BCA), by integration in standards, codes and 
policies and endorsement of certification programs in the building field, such as the LEED 
Certification process from the USGBC. 
2.1 New building commissioning 
The concept “commissioning” comes from the shipbuilding industry, where a 
“commissioned” ship signifies that the ship is ready for service. The commissioning in this 
sense means that the equipment has been tested and corrected, the prospective crew has been 
trained to operate the ship, and everything has gone through an extensive quality assurance 
process. This means that a commissioned ship has been tested and is almost certain to 
perform in accordance with the design [4].

This same approach was what Public Works Canada started applying to the building process 
in 1977. Since then, the process has been improved by many organizations, companies and 
institutions, since the benefits of such a quality assurance process is apparent to both 
customers, designers and contractors, namely increased quality. 
According to the Building Commissioning Association: 
“New construction commissioning is the process of ensuring that 
systems are designed, installed, functionally tested and performing in 
conformity with the design intent. Existing building commissioning, or 
retrocommissioning, applies of systematic process for improving and 
optimizing a building’s operations and supporting those improvements 
with enhanced documentation and operator training.”[5] 
The commissioning process varies slightly between the different guidelines, but common for 
all of them is phase structure. This following phase structure is from the California 
Commissioning Guide: New Buildings [4], but similar a structures can be seen in the IEA 
ECBCS1 Annex 40 – Commissioning Tools for Improved Energy Efficiency [6] and The 
Building Commissioning Guide [7]; 
1. Pre-design phase: 
• 
To be carried out before the actual design of the building starts. 
• 
Where the parties meet and leader is selected, 
• 
the Owner’s Project Requirements (OPR) is clarified and documented, since 
this document will help guide the entire process, and will clarify the 
objectives, 
• 
And where the initial outline for the Commissioning Plan is developed. 
2. Design phase: 
• 
Carried out simultaneously with the design of the building. 
• 
Where the design documents are reviewed to ensure that it complies with 
both the acceptance criteria in the OPR and other regulatory requirements.  
• 
Additionally it is important to ensure that the reasoning and assumptions 
made for design decisions are documented properly. 
• 
The Commissioning Plan is updated throughout this phase to include 
documentation about the structure, methods and design, as well as 
preparation for the Construction phase. 
3. Construction phase: 
• 
Carried out simultaneously with the construction of the building. 
• 
Where the verifications and functional tests are performed and documented, 
• 
The training of the prospective technical staff is performed and reported, 
• 
The Systems Manual is completed and reviewed, 
• 
And where the first draft of the finished Commissioning Report is made. 
4. Operation phase: 
• 
Carried out after the turnover of the building.

2.2 Existing Building Commissioning 
There are several reasons why commissioning on existing building is an important addition 
to new building commissioning. As buildings become more complex, with increasingly 
interactive systems becoming more prevalent, the potential for unforeseen problems 
increases and with the integration of sophisticated control systems the causes and solutions 
can be extremely difficult to identify. Even if the staff is successful in solving most of the 
most obvious operational problems, having to do this without proper documentation and/or 
information about the building systems, many of the solutions will not solve the underlying 
problems or potentially even lead to other building problems. 
This example highlights the value of one of the benefits of commissioning, improved 
documentation and increased expertise of the crew responsible for operating the building 
systems. Additional benefits are highlighted in the California Commissioning Guide: 
Existing Building [10]. These highlights include: 
• 
Improved Equipment Performance. 
Since each piece of equipment is tested to be functioning properly throughout the 
commissioning process, and for each of the pieces of equipment that fail to comply 
with the requirements a solution is found, whether that is replacing the equipment or 
mending errors. 
• 
Improved System Operation and Energy Savings. 
An essential part of the commissioning process is taking a holistic view on the 
building systems, which enables better synergy between the different systems, such 
as heating and ventilation. There is also a focus on optimizing operation, reducing 
the need for maintenance, and assessing the performance of the control system. All 
of which can result in O&M2 as well as energy savings. 
• 
Improved Indoor Environment. 
Another important driver behind commissioning process on existing buildings is the 
improvement of the indoor environment. When considering the holistic view of the 
building, the comfort, health and productivity of the occupants plays a significant 
role. Since a poor indoor environment can lead to reductions in productivity as well 
as tenant retention, simply resulting in lost earnings.

in the report “The Cost-Effectiveness of Commercial Buildings Commissioning”[11]. Here 
the results show that for existing buildings the energy savings is the most prominent benefit.

the decision to perform commissioning, with 94% of the sample stating that energy savings

2.3 Current practice 
As should be evident thus far, is that there is no specific procedure to existing building 
commissioning that should be regarded as the definite choice. Additionally there are no 
regulations either in the United States or in Denmark that require any certain procedure or 
steps to be performed for existing or new constructions. Instead the process should be tailored 
to the situation, building and desired scope of the owners.  
However in the United States it is worth considering Building Commissioning if there are 
ambitions for a LEED certification. A total of 7 credits can potentially be rewarded towards 
the LEED certificate as a result of existing building commission and ongoing commissioning. 
In the rating system, 2 credits are awarded for developing and executing a commissioning 
plan that complies with the requirements. Additionally 2 credits are awarded for 
implementing the commissioning process requiring: 
• 
Implementing low-cost operational improvements, and developing a five-year plan 
for equipment replacement and major modifications or upgrades based on the results 
from the execution of the commissioning plan. 
• 
Confirming training of the building staff. 
• 
Developing a tracking and verification program for the projects implemented as part 
of the commissioning process. 
In addition to the 4 points potentially awarded for the existing building commissioning 
process, 3 points can be awarded for implementing ongoing commissioning that includes; 
planning, point monitoring, system testing, performance verification, corrective action 
response, ongoing measurement and documentation.[12]

In Denmark there are also several certifications that incorporate elements from building 
commissioning. One such certification is the DGNB3 certification that was launched in the 
spring of 2012, created by the DK-GBC4, a Danish non-profit organization founded in 2010 
with the goal of “improving sustainability in buildings and the built environment in 
Denmark”[13]. This certification is based on the German certification of the same name, but 
adapted to Danish norms, standards and legislation. The DGNB certification involves much 
more than just commissioning however, much like the LEED certification, and includes 40 
evaluation criteria spread over 6 main areas. The 6 main areas include: Environment, 
Economy, Social, Technology, Process and Region. The certification includes three different 
ratings; Silver, Gold and Platinum, with the ratings requiring respectively a 50%, 65% and 
80% of the total available points, and a minimum of 35%, 50% and 65% of available points 
in each main area.[14]  
With commissioning and “documentation of quality in construction” both being in the 
Process main area, worth 10% of the total score for all areas, and only providing 3 points 
each out of a total 21 points in the Process area, the focus on commissioning when going for 
the certification, is relatively small compared to the certification process as a whole. 
Other Danish certifications that include small elements of building commissioning are ISO 
50001:2011[15] and ISO 14001:2015[16], respectively Energy and Environment 
management, while not directly focusing on the building, involves effective operation of the 
company as a whole, including the building and building systems.  
2.3.1 Overview of the phase structure in retro-commissioning 
Due to the lack of a definitive guideline or even regulatory requirements for the existing 
building commissioning process, discussing the current practice presents a challenge since 
the process differs from company to company and from project to project. Instead the 
different potential elements from the California Commissioning Guide: Existing Buildings 
[10] and the report Best Practices in Commissioning Existing Buildings by the BCA5[17], 
will be reviewed. 
The Planning Phase:  
As opposed to the Building Commissioning process for new buildings, there is no Pre-design, 
Design or Construction phases since obviously the building is already standing. Therefore 
the RCx process starts simply with a planning phase.  
The first task when starting the commissioning process should be for the building owner to 
define the main objectives of the project, such as improving the indoor environment, since 
clear well-documented objectives will help guide the entire project. 
When the main objectives are defined, the Operating Requirement’s should be documented, 
similar to the OPR6 in the new building commissioning process. If the OPR already exists 
from a previous commissioning project, they should be updated; otherwise new requirements 
should be documented. These requirements are essential to the commissioning process, since 
they represent the framework for the investigation and measures.

The last task in the planning phase should be to develop the commissioning plan including

for the rest of the project, but should be considered flexible so that it can be revisited when

The Investigation Phase: 
After the commissioning plan has been formulated, the first part of the investigation phase 
should be to review the documentation for the building facilities. The goal should be to obtain 
and review as many of the key documents as possible. The key documents include: Original 
design documentation, equipment lists, control system documentation, operation and 
maintenance manuals, TAB7 reports and drawings for the building services. This phase 
should give the commissioning team a thorough understanding of the building as a whole. 
After the facilities documents have been reviewed the next step to the investigation phase is 
performing diagnostic monitoring, which includes gathering time-series data for different 
variables, enabling the commissioning team to calculate key metrics to evaluate the 
performance of the building, and to verify whether or not the building systems operate 
correctly. Typical trends that are gathered are: Energy consumption, temperatures, flow rates, 
weather data, damper positions and runtimes. 
The diagnostic monitoring will, a lot of the time, only indicate a problem, but not uncover 
the root cause. Therefore the diagnostic monitoring needs to be followed by functional tests, 
which are aimed at identifying these root causes, usually done by manually controlling the 
problematic components to observe the response. 
When the measurements are completed and the results are analyzed it is helpful for the 
building owner, that the findings of the investigation phase are compiled in a systematic 
deliverable. This document will ultimately serve as a tool for deciding which measures need 
to be implemented to comply with the Operating Requirements and main objective 
documented in the planning phase.  
The Implementation Phase: 
This phase involves, as the name suggests, the implementation of the improvements. The 
typical procedure for this phase involves making a plan detailing which and how measures 
are to be implemented, implementing the improvements, and finally verifying that the 
measures are working as intended. 
The Hand-Off Phase: 
In the final phase of the commissioning process, there are two important deliverables that 
need to be completed. These are the final report and the systems manual. 
The final report is a record for the entire building commissioning process, compiling the 
documentation developed in each step and giving a summary for the process as a whole. It is 
a critical resource for the building staff, and is important for the persistence of improvements 
made as part of the commissioning project, as well as for future projects. Additionally the 
report is a requirement for the LEED certification credits.

2.4 Involvement of Occupants 
When reviewing the current practice in relevant guidelines and reports, the importance of 
involving the occupants as a valuable resource in the commissioning process is highlighted 
intermittently. Mostly this involvement includes meetings to inform the occupants about the 
project, keep them up-to-date or ask for feedback. One of such highlights is included in the 
report, Best Practices in Commissioning Existing Buildings [17], where the idea is brought 
up to include occupant surveys during the diagnostic monitoring phase. 
Since the occupants are the ones actually experiencing the indoor environment inside the 
building and the entire purpose of a building is to provide optimal conditions for its 
occupants, their perception and satisfaction should be regarded as the most important 
parameter when diagnosing building problems. Supporting the statement that occupant input 
is undervalued in the current commissioning processes, is the low priority it is given in the 
different guidelines, compared to the value it is attributed in regards to e.g. post-occupancy 
evaluation backed up by studies.  
One such study is presented in the article “Listening to the occupants: a Web-based indoor 
environmental quality survey” [18]. This study was performed at the Center for the Built 
Environment, Berkeley, California, and the topic was development and testing of a web-
based survey and accompanying online reporting tools. From this study it was concluded that 
the survey was a helpful, inexpensive tool for assessing the performance of buildings, as well 
as being helpful for detecting and solving problems.  The CBE Occupant IEQ8 Survey, has 
subsequently been used in over 600 buildings and in numerous research projects and 
publications, solidifying the validity of using occupant surveys to quantify the performance 
of buildings as well as identifying and solving problems [19]. 
The purpose of this study will be to investigate the application of occupant questionnaires in 
the retro-commissioning process, by performing a case study on the Energi Fyn domicile in 
Odense. The drivers for including occupant surveys in the retro-commissioning, is in order 
to reduce the scope of the process without disrupting the holistic approach. 
The holistic approach was preserved in implementing the questionnaire to the building as a 
whole at the start of the project, and the responses from this survey was then used to focus 
the subsequent activities (such as diagnostic monitoring and functional tests) to areas 
indicated by the occupants as experiencing problems.

3 Approach 
As was described in the literature review, while building commissioning is an important 
tool to ensure efficient energy consumption, acceptable indoor climate and efficient 
operation, management and maintenance of building, the tool has still not been 
implemented widely in the Danish building sector. 
The biggest barrier of entry for this tool seems to be the associated costs and resources that 
have to be allocated to either the existing or new building commissioning process. While 
many of the benefits in the building commissioning process comes from the holistic view, 
that ensures a complete overview and a thorough quality assurance, this is also a big reason 
why the process becomes costly and time consuming. 
3.1 Purpose 
The purpose of this thesis was to decrease the scope of the retro-commissioning process, 
without compromising the benefits that give the process value. This was done by making the 
process more focused through the use of occupant questionnaires, which help indicate 
problematic areas that require special attention quickly and cheaply. 
This approach to building commissioning was then applied to a real case at the Energi Fyn 
domicile in Odense, Denmark. This building was a good candidate for retro-commissioning 
due to the numerous problems that presented itself, such as odours and insufficient cooling, 
since the time it was finished in 2009. 
3.2 Thesis structure 
The structure of this thesis includes the previous literature review and introduction, a 
description of the building 
In order to properly address the objective of the thesis in a structured fashion, the report was 
split into three components: 
The Questionnaire section, including the background for the questionnaire, the method, the 
results, as well as a discussion of these results and a summary of the conclusions that can be 
drawn from these. This section includes the method for the questionnaire and finally the 
results from questionnaire as well as analysis of these. All of this results in the information 
needed to continue a focused approach to monitoring and tests. 
The Monitoring and Tests section took impetus in the questionnaire results, resulting in a 
number of measurements based on the findings in the questionnaire. The monitoring and 
tests, which included both active measuring and passive logging of data, were then expanded 
based on the findings with a focus on addressing the issues highlighted in the questionnaire. 
This section includes the separate purpose and methods for each of the measurements, as well 
as the results, discussions and conclusions that could be found from these measurements. 
The description of the building, site and buildings as well as the proposals for improvements 
serve as parts of the applied retro-commission approach, with the former acting as the starting 
point for the process, and the ladder as part of the result. 
Finally the case study of applying the retro-commissioning approach to the Energi Fyn 
domicile in Odense, Denmark, was evaluated, assessed and summarized in the overall 
discussion and conclusion sections.

4 Objective 
Summarizing the approach, the objective of this thesis was to: 
1. Apply the approach to building commissioning that implements occupant 
questionnaires in the monitoring and testing phase of the retrocommissioning process

5.3 Ventilation, heating and cooling 
The ventilation system of the Energi Fyn domicile in Odense is constituted by 3 separate 
systems, with completely separate ducting and plants, all controlled by a single control 
system. The three systems are: LA1, LA2 and LA3. 
LA01 provides ventilation to the northern open-plan office, for all of the “silent rooms” and 
for approx. half of the west-facing offices. This corresponds to the dark-blue zone, approx. 
the northern half of the purple zone, and approx. the northern half of the light-green zone.  
LA2 provides ventilation for the southern open-plan office, the control room, all of the 
meeting rooms, the lobby and the remaining west-facing offices. This corresponds to the pink 
zone, the remaining half of the purple zone and the remaining half of the light-green zone.   
Finally LA3 provides ventilation for both of the east-facing open-plan offices, as well as the 
two smaller offices on the eastern side of the 1st floor. This corresponds to the light-blue, 
orange, yellow and red zones. 
The three ventilation plants are for all intents and purposes separate from each other, and are 
only connected in the control interface. They are however very similar in components and 
control strategies, and only differ in capacities. Therefore describing one plant should suffice.

6.1 Methods 
6.1.1 Structure of the questionnaire 
The purpose of the questionnaire was to listen to the building occupants, and understand their 
perception of the indoor climate. This is especially important when analyzing a building of 
this size with a floor area of approx. 6000 m2 that accommodates approx. 215 people.  
The questionnaire should allow the building owner/manager to get a better idea about what 
requirements should be set based on the priorities of the occupants, which problems are most 
severe and therefore require most attention. 
Additionally the questionnaire should help to focus the diagnostic monitoring process by 
identifying which areas experience problems, and which areas do not. This should ultimately 
save a significant amount of work, since the monitoring activities can be focused on the areas 
in need. 
This format for the questionnaire was inspired by the Ӧrebro model and the MM040 NA 
Office questionnaire [23][24]. The reason for basing the questionnaire on this model served 
two purposes; providing a solid tried base on which to build, and because the questionnaire 
manual provided reference values for 5123 participants that have completed the standard 
questionnaire, which will be used to compare the results during the data analysis. 
The questionnaire was created using Google Forms, and a print out of the entire survey in 
Danish can be seen in Appendix 1. The survey was distributed via a custom link attached to 
an internal e-mail sent to all employees at Energi Fyn on Monday February 1 2016, and ran 
from the time of distribution until the evening of February 8 2016. 
The time frame of the questions in the survey include the last three seasons. The reason why 
the time frame included summer, fall and winter, and not the spring of 2015, is that spring 
was thought to be too far in the past for the participants to give any reliable answers to the 
questions, and similar problems to those experienced in spring were expected to appear in 
the fall. Even the summer of 2015 was perhaps too far in the past, to receive accurate results, 
but the period is too important to neglect, and the results was analyzed critically. 
Since the time frame of the questionnaire is approx. 8 months (June-2015 through January-
2016), getting precise answers from especially the summer of 2015 is a concern for the study.  
The first page of the questionnaire included the following background questions: 
• 
Age and gender, to understand the demographic of the building and for individual 
zones. 
• 
Type of work, to get a picture of the activity level and time spent at the primary 
working space. 
• 
Building zones, where the occupants could specify inside which zone their primary 
working space was located. 
In the question regarding building zones the two pictures in Figure 6.1 was shown, and the 
occupants were asked to specify the color of the zone in which they primarily worked. This 
question was included to be able to split up the answers following the questionnaire, so that

6.3 Discussion 
6.3.1 Demographic 
From the results of the demographic questions, it can be seen in Table 6.4 that there was an 
even distribution between males and females as expected. It can also be seen on Figure 6.2 
that the age distribution of the participants was consistent with the age distribution in the 
company. Based on the distribution of gender and age it could be concluded that neither 
gender nor any age groups are overly represented in the questionnaire, since both of these 
distributions match the expected distribution of the employees. 
For the building zones most zones were represented corresponding to their relative size. The 
exceptions being the Red zone, where the response rate was significantly lower than 50% at 
approx. 20% and the Orange zone, where the response rate was significantly higher than the 
average at approx. 80%. The reason for this is hard to deduce, but it is assumed that there is 
a connection between the problems occupants are experiencing and the interest in 
participating. 
The distribution of the type of work for participants seems appropriate when considering an 
office building, but the answers for this category is mostly disregarded due to feedback 
suggesting that the question was not clear.  
After the questionnaire, feedback was given from participants that some had problems 
finding the relevant building zone and decide which type of work was appropriate for them. 
In future versions of the questionnaire these comments will be considered, and additional 
explanatory text will be included.

6.3.2 Overall satisfaction 
As can be seen in Table 6.6 the medians for the overall satisfaction for each of the seasons 
and for the meeting rooms during the last three months are all 3, corresponding to a “Neutral” 
response in the questionnaire, i.e. neither satisfied or dissatisfied.  
It can also be seen that the median was largely influenced by the responses in the Pink zone, 
which make up approx. 28% of the total answers, where the median was 2 corresponding to 
“Dissatisfied”, indicating that at least half of the occupants in the zone were dissatisfied with 
the indoor climate. Additionally it can be seen that the satisfaction is higher than the median 
in the Light Blue and Light Green zones.  
While this shows that there may not have been any urgent critical problems with the indoor 
climate, it also showed that there was a lot of room for improvement, and that the Pink zone 
should be a focus going forward with the diagnostic monitoring phase. Worth remembering 
is that a median response of 3, corresponding to “Neutral” means that at least half of the 
responders answered 3 or below, corresponding to neutral, dissatisfied and very dissatisfied, 
not exactly indicating a satisfactory indoor climate.  
When considering also the percentage of occupants that were dissatisfied with the indoor 
climate, meaning the share of occupants that either answered “Dissatisfied” or “Very 
Dissatisfied”, the share was relatively large. 
Considering the effects of the age distribution, there were only negligible differences in the 
responses of the different groups, and the unequal distribution of the age groups in some of 
the smaller zones, makes drawing any meaningful conclusions based on age distribution 
almost impossible. As for the gender distribution, the overall satisfaction was slightly lower 
for females relative to the male responders. This means that the response for the Pink zone 
could be understated slightly since approx. 2/3 of the responders in that zone are men. 
Reversely the response in the Dark Blue zone could be exaggerated slightly since approx. 2/3 
of the responders in this zone are women.  
6.3.3 Meeting rooms 
As was shown in Table 6.6 and Table 6.7 the satisfaction with indoor climate was higher 
inside the meeting rooms compared to the participants primary working place. This can be 
explained by the fact that each of the meeting rooms are completely closed off from the 
surroundings with glass walls, requiring individual ventilation, in the core of the building, at 
least 8m away from the nearest external window. This makes it easier to control the indoor 
climate, compared to an open-plan office without external shading in the north and south 
façades.  
While the participants were not given any options to submit the frequency of the different 
indoor climate problems, as with the questions to each season, the participants who answered 
“Dissatisfied” and “Very Dissatisfied” were prompted to give a reason for their answer in the 
form of a comment. All of these comments can be seen in Appendix 2 with both original and 
translated comments. In summary, the most frequent comments regarding the indoor climate 
in the meeting rooms, were about either too high temperatures, too low temperatures or too 
varying temperatures.

The comments about too low temperatures however indicated a too high air flow. From the 
comments it therefore seemed like the problem with the indoor climate inside the meeting 
rooms is the result of a poorly functioning (or non-existent) control scheme for the ventilation 
to these rooms. 
6.3.4 Frequency of indoor climate problems 
Summer 
From what could be seen on Figure 6.4 the most frequent problem seems to be with too 
varying temperatures which was the most prevalent problem both weekly and daily with over 
20% of the occupants saying that they experienced this problem daily and 34% of the 
occupants saying that they experienced this problem weekly or more often during the 
summer. Similar frequency could be seen with draught and lack of concentration. Since the 
occupants both experienced draught and too high temperatures there seemed to be 
inconsistencies in the control scheme of the ventilation plant.  
Additionally it could be seen that many occupants experienced bad odours during the 
summer. There was also a disproportionate number of comments concerning odours, which 
could mean that this problem affected the occupants’ experience of the indoor climate more 
negatively than the other problems. Some of the comments even went further into detail, 
commenting that the odours seemed to be most common during days with high humidity and 
heat. 
Too high temperatures also seemed to be a common problem during the summer which is to 
be expected, especially with no automatic external shading, however it could be seen that it 
was less frequent, or at least less memorable, since few occupants answered that they 
experience this problem daily during the summer. 
It seems likely that the problem with lack of concentration is connected to either the odours 
or too high temperatures.  
Looking at the individual zones in Table 6.8, it could be seen that especially the large open-
plan offices, constituted of the Pink and Dark Blue zones, were affected by the respective 
problems, since the frequency in these zones were higher than for the building as a whole. 
From these results it could also be observed that overheating problems were not more 
frequent in the south-facing open-plan office, the Pink zone, however it could be observed 
that draught, odours and lack of concentration were more prevalent. These results were 
consistent with increased ventilation rates in the Pink zone compared to the Dark Blue zone, 
which would be expected due to the increased cooling load provided by the many south-
facing windows. 
Seeing these problems, it would be a good idea to investigate the set points, control scheme 
and temperatures during the cooling season, since the issues could point to problems with the 
controls of the ventilation system when dealing with larger cooling demands. 
Comparing the results for the summer of 2015 to the reference values in Table 6.1 and Table 
6.2 it could be seen that for all the problems except “Too low temperature” the prevalence in

Fall

It could also be seen that too varying temperatures and a lack of concentration were still some

It can also be seen that odours were now the least frequent problem, indicating that the

temperatures was reduced greatly, but the problem was replaced with occupants experiencing 
too low temperatures.

the building as a whole in all categories as in the summer, giving further incentives to focus 
on the large open-plan office areas in the further investigation.

it could be seen that the prevalence of the problems regarding too high and too low 
temperatures does not differ significantly from the reference values. This indicated that the 
responses to these two questions can simply be explained by the occupants having different 
preferences to the temperature and it being impossible to satisfy everyone. It can also be seen 
that the prevalence of odours had been reduced to coincide with the reference values 
indicating that the problem with odours was either periodic or seasonal. 
Additionally it could be seen that the prevalence of draught was still significantly above the 
reference value which indicated that this problem was not necessarily dependent on high 
heating loads, meaning that this problem was more likely caused by faulty regulation of the 
supply air or faulty design of the ventilation system. This increased the incentive to further 
investigate the ventilation system and diffusers, especially in the large open-plan office areas 
since draught was more prevalent in these zones.

6.3.5 Frequency of symptoms of SBS 
The main reason for asking about these symptoms was to investigate whether there was a 
problem with the air quality, stemming either from pollutions in the ventilation plant and/or 
ducts or from building materials, such as the carpeting that is used throughout most of the 
office areas.  
When analyzing the data, it became apparent that counting the responses that participants 
who experienced the symptoms only monthly or more seldom was unlikely to provide any 
useable results, since a large share of occupants experiencing the symptoms at least once in 
three months is very likely even with perfect IAQ12 inside the building. Instead it would have 
been interesting to count the share of occupants experiencing the symptoms daily, since this 
could potentially help identify problems with the IAQ. 
Additionally a question about whether the experience of symptoms change depending on the 
occupants’ presence in the building, could have been a great indicator for whether or not the 
symptoms were connected to the building. 
When looking at Figure 6.7 and Table 6.11 it could be seen that there was a high prevalence 
of both headache and lack of concentration with respectively 24% and 25% experience these

6.3.6 Frequency of work environment problems 
To include issues that could influence the indoor environment for occupants without being 
connected to the air quality, the occupants were asked about the frequency with which they 
experienced problems with insufficient lighting, glare and noise. 
Especially noise was expected to be a major issue due to the large open-plan office areas and 
as could be seen on Table 6.12 was something that a large share of the occupants was 
bothered by, with 30% experiencing problems with noise daily, and 45% of the occupants 
experiencing problems with noise at least weekly. Compared with the reference value on 
Table 6.1, it could also be observed that the share of occupants experiencing the problem at 
least weekly was over 3 times higher than the 13% reference value. 
Considering the two other work environment problems, it could be seen that only 8% of the 
occupants experience problems with insufficient illuminance. This could likely simply be 
explained by the fact that different people prefer different levels of lighting, and sometimes 
will experience insufficient lighting when the external shading is activated to spare some co-
workers from glare. This combined with the fact that only the leadership have control over 
the artificial lighting at their workplace, could explain this 8%. 
As for the 18% having experienced glare during the last three months, it could likely be 
explained in the same way as with insufficient illuminance, since occupants have no personal 
control over the external shading, and activating this would in turn cause other occupants, 
further away from the windows, to complain about insufficient lighting. 
6.3.7 Overall 
Some concerns for the questionnaire as a whole was the method used for evaluating the 
statistic uncertainty of the responses to the questionnaire. While the group size for the 
building as a whole was big enough for statistical irregularities to have a significantly lower 
impact, some of the zones that were investigated are not. It was therefore necessary to account 
for the group size when comparing the frequencies both between seasons and between zones, 
but also when comparing to the reference values.

CHI-2 analysis to the entire questionnaire was deemed too large for this thesis, and instead 
Table 6.3 from the MM040 manual, that is based on CHI-2 analysis but only provides a rough

This compromise clearly affected the reliability of the responses and required additional 
caution when attempting to identify indications of indoor environmental problems and zones 
that experienced problems more frequently. 
Additionally the reliability of the responses regarding the work environment problems in the 
summer of 2015 can be questioned, since occupants will have a hard time remembering the 
actual situation more than 6 months in the past. While this issue with reliability was 
acknowledged, the results are still useful, since they were the only possibility of getting an 
indication of the indoor climate during summer. 
Ideally, alternatives, such as questionnaires at the end of each season would be preferred, but

considered.

6.4 Conclusion 
It could be seen from the overall satisfaction results, that the focus areas of further 
investigation of the indoor climate should be the large open-plan office areas since these had 
shown below average satisfaction. Additionally it could be seen that the biggest problems 
with indoor climate seem to during summer.  
While the questionnaire indicated that the most severe complaints about the indoor climate, 
was during the summer, the timeframe of this particular project does not allow for specific 
measurements during this season. To compensate for this, an investigation of historical data, 
assumed to be available in the CTS-system was planned. It turned out, however that this 
information was not available. Instead an attempt was made to perform measurements during 
operation with cooling in the building, since similar, but perhaps less severe, problems should 
occur during periods in the spring with high temperatures. 
Looking at the results from the Fall and Winter seasons it could be seen that draught, a lack 
of concentration and too varying temperatures were the main culprits of poor indoor climate 
during these periods. Along with the SBS symptom responses, there was an indication that 
the problems could be caused by improper control of the ventilation system. 
The timeframe of the project does allow measurements of the indoor climate during the 
spring, which was assumed to experience many of the same problems of the fall season. 
Based on the results from the fall and winter periods there will be an increased focus on 
identifying the severity and causes for draught and too varying temperatures in the large 
open-plan office areas. 
While the indoor climate in the meeting rooms was better than in the winter season, which 
should cover approx. the same time period, there is still a high percentage of dissatisfied 
among the occupants, with comments indicating that there is malfunctioning or non-existent 
control of the airflow to and from these rooms. Therefore a further investigation of the airflow 
was also performed.

7 Monitoring and tests 
Through the analysis of the questionnaire answers and the investigation of the ventilation 
system several problems had emerged, specifically draught and too varying temperatures in 
the meeting rooms and large open-plan office areas. 
The approach to the monitoring and tests was, as part of the approach to retro-commissioning 
utilizing the occupant questionnaires, altered slightly compared to conventional retro-
commissioning as described in the literature review. 
Instead of simply starting out by monitoring everything and then using functional tests to 
investigate the root causes of the identified problems, the monitoring took impetus from the 
questionnaire results, in order to decrease the scope, and therefore work-load of the retro-
commissioning process. The monitoring activities were then followed by functional tests and 
trend data analysis to investigate the root causes, based on the problems indicated by both 
the questionnaire results and the measurements. 
7.1 Methods 
7.1.1 Measurement 1: Temperatures in open-plan offices 
As described in section 7.1.3 the air flow to the large open-plan office areas are controlled 
by a temperature sensor in the exhaust ducts. This means that the control variable for the air 
flow control is based on the mixed exhaust air from the entire control zones as also described 
in section 7.1.3.  
This can be especially problematic for the south-facing open-plan office, since the south-
west control zone includes the exhaust air from the lobby. However, the ventilation 
requirement for these two rooms are completely different. One part of the south-west control 
zone includes an approx. 300 m2 office area with a room height of 3 m and approx. 60 
occupants and the other part includes a 250 m2 lobby area with a room height of 8 m and 2 
occupants. Even though the two areas are completely different the air flow to the two zones 
are being controlled, based on the combined exhaust air from the two zones.  
Similarly the south-east control zone controls the air supply and exhaust for both the 
remaining 360 m2 of the south-facing open-plan office area and for a connected but closed 
off “Control Room”. The “Control Room” is approx. 70 m2 with 8 workplaces and is filled 
with large flat-screen monitors, with south-facing windows, separated from the open-plan 
office with a floor-to-ceiling glass wall. As with the south-west control zone, the ventilation 
requirements for these two separate rooms are completely different, but the air flows are 
being controlled by the same two dampers. 
7.1.1.1 
Purpose 
The purpose of this measurement was to investigate the temperatures inside the open-plan 
offices as well as the central exhaust ducts, in order to analyze whether ventilation 
requirements in the zones were being met, or if the centralized control scheme caused the 
ventilation requirements in parts of the offices to be under- or overestimated due to the 
temperature sensor point being in the exhaust duct.  
Additionally another purpose of the measurement in the central exhaust duct was to 
investigate the operative temperatures inside the rooms, to assess whether or not the set-
points in the control system were appropriate.

Tinytag Datalogger – TGP-4020 
The TGP-4020 data logger is a battery powered data logger, and is designed for use with a 
thermistor probe to log temperatures in the range of -40°C to +125°C. 
The data logger can be configured to log with difference intervals, from each second to 
several days, and has a capacity for approx. 32500 measuring points. The clock in the data 
logger is synchronized with that of the computer, which means that each logger was 
synchronized to the same clock. 
The data loggers can also be configured to a delayed start, which was utilized to synchronize 
the measurements from all the data loggers so that all data loggers started measuring at 8 am 
with the same interval. 
Tinytag Thermistor Probe – PB-5006-1M5 
The data logger was equipped with a thermistor probe (Tinytag Thermistor Probe – PB-5006-
1M5). The thermistor probe is a probe with a 1.5m cable which allows for more flexible setup 
combined with the battery powered data loggers. 
The thermistor measures the temperature at the point by the use of a resistor that is more 
dependent on temperatures than normal resistors. The resistance in the resistor will decrease 
with the temperature at a given relationship between the two variables, which is utilized to 
measure the temperature. 
The PB-5006-1M5 is designed to measure temperatures in the range of -40°C to +125°C and 
while the producer does not specify an accuracy of the probes, the typical accuracy for this 
type of thermistor is between -3% to -6%.[25][26] 
The data sheet for the thermistor probe can be seen in Appendix 4. 
For the purpose of this measurement, the operative temperature was more appropriate for the 
measurements inside the office areas, since this should also account for the mean radiant 
temperature that also affects the occupants’ perception of the thermal environment.

7.1.2 Measurement 2: Meeting rooms 
In the analysis of the questionnaire responses it could be seen that there was a significant 
share of occupants dissatisfied with the indoor climate in the meeting rooms. The 
respondents, who indicated dissatisfaction with the indoor climate in the meeting rooms, 
were asked to make elaborative comments describing the reason why they were dissatisfied. 
The majority of comments had to do with the thermal climate, i.e. draught, too high, too low 
or too varying temperatures. These comments, given the range of complaints, could indicate 
a problem with the ability of the ventilation system to appropriately control the indoor climate 
in the meeting rooms.  
The investigation of the ventilation system and control scheme showed that all meeting rooms 
except one, that shares a damper with the printing room, had individual dampers, meaning 
that the ventilation to the meeting rooms could be controlled independently. The dampers for 
these rooms were controlled based only on a temperature sensor placed between the exhaust 
device and exhaust damper inside the duct. 
Additionally there were concerns about the placement of the supply and exhaust ducts inside 
a majority of the meeting rooms, since, as could be seen on Figure 7.3 showing the placement 
of the sensors inside the specific meeting room, the rectangular supply and exhaust grilles 
were less than 2 m apart. 
7.1.2.1 
Purpose 
The purpose of this measurement is to investigate the ability of the ventilation to meet 
ventilation demands inside the meeting rooms. 
7.1.2.2 
Method 
The temperature and CO2 concentrations was measured at two different points inside the 
room. The first point was be at the center of the occupied zone, to get an approximation of 
the average temperature and CO2 concentration inside the occupied zone. The second point 
was at the exhaust grille, which gave the exhaust temperature and CO2 concentration. The 
supply temperature was measured inside the main duct by the control system, which was 
logged every minute, and was exported for the measurement period. For the supply air CO2 
concentration, the assumption was made, that it corresponds to the background CO2 
concentration of 395.5 ppm.[27] 
This allowed evaluation of the ability of the ventilation system for the meeting rooms, to 
remove cooling and pollution loads. 
The measurement was started on March 30th 2016 with all equipment being in place at 2 pm,

7.1.5 Trend data assessment of outdoor temperatures and main fan speed 
During the investigation of the control scheme for the ventilation plant several potential 
problems were detected, one of which was the control of the main ventilators. 
As was described in section 5.3, the main ventilators are controlled based only on a time 
schedule (5am-20pm during weekdays) that provided an on/off signal and a differential 
pressure in the ducts. Since the fan speed could be adjusted by frequency converters, the 
ventilation system was practically of the VAV type, being able to adjust the fan speed to the 
amount of air needed. 
However the only variables that affected the fan speed in the control scheme was the 
differential pressure, which in turn was only affected by the position of the dampers inside 
the ducts, which again in turn were only controlled based on the exhaust air temperature for 
the given damper and the room set-point. This meant that if the ventilation system for some 
reason was not able to accomplish the zone-level set-points, whether because of the set-points 
being set too low, or because the supply air temperature was insufficient to provide cooling 
towards the set-point, the dampers will stay completely open, causing the main fans to be at 
maximum speed at all times. 
Ideally a system with VAV should be able to adjust the air flow to match the cooling need, 
meaning that the fan speed should be reduced as the outdoor temperature drops. 
In order to observe whether or not this was the case in the building, the fan speeds were 
logged in the control system of the ventilation plants. Simultaneously the outdoor 
temperatures were logged, and the two were plotted against each other, only including trend 
data during times of operation. This trend data was gathered between April 26th and May 10th.

7.3 Discussion 
7.3.1 Measurement 1: Temperatures in the open-plan offices 
To provide some better structure to this discussion section, considering the number of 
different results used from the first measurement, the discussion was split into 4 segments 
that were based on their respective results section, and also the respective results in relation 
to the results as a whole as well. 
Preferably the measurements should have been carried out with a longer duration and during 
different seasons to observe the effect of different temperature loads, however due to time 
constraints this was not possible. 
7.3.1.1 
Temperature difference within control zones 
Differences in operative temperature within the control zones was problematic in regards to 
controlling the air flow to the zone. Since the air flow to the zones are controlled based simply 
on the exhaust air temperature, which was an aggregate of all of the exhaust air flows from 
the zone, and should therefore provide the best idea about how much cooling is needed to 
reach the set point. However if the operative temperatures, and thereby conditions for 
cooling, are not uniform inside the control zone, the conditions for cooling will not be either.

This means that one part of the zone can experience satisfying operative temperatures and 
another part experience too high operative temperatures. If the part of the zone experiencing 
too high temperatures makes up a larger share of the exhaust air, the air flow to the zone to 
the zone will increase to reach the set-point, perhaps improving conditions for the occupants 
experiencing too high temperatures, but at the same time cause too cold temperatures for the 
occupants that were previously satisfied. 
Differences in operative temperature between different parts of a control zone can be caused 
by several things such as amount of direct sunlight and non-uniform air distribution. 
Prior to the measurements, the biggest temperature difference were expected to be found in 
the two southern control zones, due to the inclusion of the “control room” and lobby in 
respectively the south-eastern and south-western control zones. Since the “control room” is 
not open to the rest of southern open-plan office, with south-facing windows and a lot of 
equipment, it was expected that the operative temperature inside this room would be a lot 
higher than the remaining control zone.  
While there was clearly some significant temperature difference within the zone, as can be 
seen by the top whisker for the south-eastern control zone on Figure 7.13, the 75th percentile 
for the temperature difference was only approx. 1.5°C. While even a difference 1.5°C can 
cause problems with maintaining satisfactory thermal conditions for all occupants, it is hard 
to avoid even within smaller and more secluded zones. 
The same problem was expected with the south-western control zone, since it includes the 
lobby. The lobby is open to the 1st floor with a large west-facing window façade, both 
presenting challenges to maintaining a temperature throughout the day, and a clear 
temperature difference was therefore expected. As can be seen on Figure 7.14 however, the 
temperature differences for the south-western zone were not substantial enough to be a cause 
for concern. Additionally Figure 7.14, showing the time within different temperature 
intervals for the south-western zone, shows that the temperature is relatively uniform in this 
zone compared to the others. 
The biggest temperature difference within a control zone could be observed in the north-
eastern control zone, and looking at Figure 7.11, showing the temperature intervals for the 
measuring points in the north-eastern control zone, it could be seen that the temperatures 
were significantly higher for measuring point 1, located close to the eastern edge of the 
northern open-plan office, closer to the center of the building, than for measuring point 5 in 
the center of the zone. This indicated clear problems with non-uniform air flow in the north-
eastern control zone, and that especially the edges of the control zone experienced 
insufficient ventilation. 
Similarly to the south-west control zone, the north-western control seemed to experience 
relatively uniform operative temperatures, indicating a uniform air flow in the zone. 
7.3.1.2 
Difference between operative temperatures and exhaust air temperature 
The differences between the operative temperature and the exhaust air temperatures 
indicates, as the temperature difference within a zone, non-uniform thermal conditions within 
the zone. It also, however, indicates areas within the control zone that are not being properly 
controlled. If the difference between the exhaust air temperature and the operative 
temperature is much greater for one of the points compared to the other points in the same 
control zone, it indicates that the thermal environment in that area is not represented in the 
==PAGE:75==
Nicolaj Yde Sørensen 
Master’s Thesis 
Spring 2016 
Sustainable Energy  
DTU-BYG  
04-07-2016 
65 
 
control scheme, and therefore it cannot be expected that the ventilation in that area is 
appropriate. 
The two temperatures, operative and air temperature, cannot be directly compared since they 
are measured differently, with the operative temperature also taking into account the mean 
radiant temperature. That means that it is pointless to look at the temperature difference by 
itself, since it would not be possible to interpret this without knowledge about the mean 
radiant temperature at the measuring point. Looking at the temperature differences for the 
different measuring points, however, can give some indication about the suitability of 
controlling the air flow centrally for the sizable control zones. If the differences are 
significantly larger for some of the measuring points that for others, it does give an indication 
that the central control is not representable for all areas within the control zone, which could 
mean that splitting up the control zone into smaller parts would be advisable. 
From Table 7.3 it could be seen that the operative temperature was much higher than the 
exhaust air temperature (median 2.06°C) and significantly higher than the difference between 
the other points and the exhaust air. This indicated that the area around measuring point 1 in 
the north-eastern zone was not represented properly in the control scheme, and therefore does 
not experience appropriate ventilation. 
From Table 7.4 it could be seen that the temperature differences between the operative and 
exhaust air temperatures were relatively uniform, only being slightly higher in measuring 
point 2, which was located at the center of the zone. 
From Table 7.5 it could be seen that the temperature difference between operative and 
exhaust air temperature, were significantly higher for measuring points 1 and 2 than for the 
3 other measuring points. Measuring point 1 is inside the “control room”, where it was 
expected that the temperature would be significantly higher than the rest of the south-eastern 
control zone, due to the amount of equipment inside the room, and the fact that it was isolated 
from the open-plan office.  
Measuring point 2 in the south-eastern control was located near the south-facing windows, 
and it could be seen from the results in Table 7.5 that the temperature here was significantly 
higher than the rest of the open-plan office. From the local thermal environment 
measurements it was also found that the air velocities in the eastern part of the southern open-
plan office, near the windows, measured at measuring points S1 and S2, were very low 
compared to many of the other measurements, indicating that the air flow to these parts of 
the office was obstructed in some way. 
Regarding the south-west control zone, it can be seen from Table 7.6, that the temperature 
differences between operative and exhaust air temperature was relatively uniform inside the 
office, with the difference being significantly higher at measuring point 4, which was located 
in the lobby / reception area. Additionally it could be seen that the temperature difference 
was relatively uniform for the rest of the control zone, varying only slightly between the 
remaining measuring points. 
7.3.1.3 
Temperature difference in each measuring point over time 
In the questionnaire, problems with too varying temperatures were reported during summer 
operation. To investigate this problem, the operative temperature drifts in the open-plan 
offices was measured and calculated.

While small fluctuations in the operative temperature through the day is to be expected a rate 
of temperature change above 2K/h can potentially adversely affect the comfort level for 
occupants. Small temperature differences will not be noticeable for occupants. When 
temperatures differ from the optimal operative temperature as can be seen in Table 7.1, 22°C 
during winter and 24.5°C during summer, occupants are able to adjust clothing levels are 
other variables within their control, to improve their comfort. However when the operative 
temperature drifts significantly over short periods of time, occupants will have a harder time 
adjusting to the present thermal environment. 
As could be seen in Table 7.7 the rate of temperature change was above 2 K/h for 23.44% of 
the time at measuring point 1 in the south-eastern control zone. Additionally it can be seen 
that there is also problems with temperature drifts in the both the north-eastern and north-
western control zones, at measuring point 4 and 1 respectively. While it could be seen that 
there are periods with significant temperature drift at several areas in the open-plan offices, 
it hardly seemed like a pronounced problem as could be indicated by the questionnaire. This 
could be due to temperatures during the measurements not being as high as during summer 
operation, but testing this would require a much longer period in which to perform 
measurements, or existing trend logs of the operative temperature. The temperature drifts that 
could be observed in the results are most likely due to occupant activity (e.g. gathering for a 
meeting or traffic), or the opening of windows nearby.    
7.3.1.4 
Operative temperature during working hours 
In order to assess the operative temperature levels, and investigate the problems with too high 
and too low temperatures, the operative temperature compliance with the categories in 
DS/EN ISO 7730 that could be seen in Table 7.1, for the cooling season was investigated. 
Compliance with the cooling season requirements were chosen due to the outdoor 
temperatures experienced at the time of the measurements, and the operation mode of the 
ventilation plant. 
As could be seen on Figure 7.11 and Figure 7.12 the compliance with the categories in DS/EN 
ISO 7730, is more or less successful, with most of the measuring points in the northern open-
plan office showing compliance with category A for most of the time. The clear exception to 
the general trend in the northern open-plan office is measuring point 1 in the north-eastern 
control zone. This area has been shown to experience higher than average operative 
temperatures, and there is clearly a problem with insufficient ventilation in this area. The 
median temperature for the measurement point, 26.74°C, also gives a clear indication of non-
compliance, and an unacceptable indoor environment. 
It is worth remarking here, that the set-point for the exhaust air temperature in both the 
northern control zones was 22°C and that the dampers were fully open for almost 100% of 
the time of measurement. This should be enough to question whether this compliance with 
category A, was perhaps not the result of proper operation and controls, but instead simply a 
result of the outdoor conditions at the time of the measurement. 
For the southern open-plan office, that could be seen in Figure 7.13 and Figure 7.14, there 
were clearly more challenges with sustaining an acceptable operative temperature, compared 
to the northern open-plan office. It could be seen that measuring points 3, 4 and 5 in the 
south-eastern control zone, showed the same general trend as the northern office.

7.3.2 Measurement 2: Meeting rooms 
As could be seen on Figure 7.3 showing the position of ST45 and the layout of the exhaust

the occupant questionnaire could be explained by the air flow “short-circuiting” causing 
ineffective ventilation inside the room and therefore temperatures that varied based solely on 
the heating/cooling loads from the roof windows and heating loads from the 
occupants/equipment. 
The results from the measurement, specifically the results for CO2 concentration in the room, 
indicated that the room is well ventilated due to the relatively low concentrations of CO2 and 
only very slight peaks with problematic concentration levels. Normally it would not be likely 
to measure higher concentrations of CO2 at the exhaust compared to the occupied zone, 
unless the ducts were closed letting the CO2 build up inside the room, however the differences 
in CO2 concentration between the occupied zone and the exhaust, were so low (less than 70 
ppm, approx. 97% of the time) that this difference can be attributed to measuring 
uncertainties. 
Additionally the temperatures inside the room also indicated some “short-circuiting” since 
the temperatures that could be seen in Figure 7.15, showed that temperatures at the exhaust 
were distinctly lower than the temperatures at the center of the occupied zone. 
Additionally it can be seen that the temperatures inside the room was lower than in the open-
plan offices, with significant periods of time below 23.5°C which was within the lower bound 
for category A compliance, something that was almost never seen in any of the open-plan 
offices, which could indicate either significant heat loss through the roof windows, or a 
relatively high air flow compared to the size of the room. The ladder of which seemed more 
likely when looking at the CO2 concentration levels inside the room.

7.3.5 Trend data assessment of outdoor temperature and main fan speed

under-dimensioned in regards to the building loads.

7.4 Conclusion 
In conclusion the monitoring and tests, a clear indication that the plant was unable to provide 
enough cooling to deal with the cooling loads inside the offices was found. This was partly 
due to the fact that the operative temperatures inside the open-plan offices, in part due to the 
trend data assessment of outdoor temperatures and main fan speed, and finally decidedly 
indicated by the trend data assessment of the exhaust air temperatures compared to the set-
points. 
In the final trend data assessment it was shown that the ventilation system was not able to 
maintain an exhaust air temperature within 0.5 K of the set-point at any point during 
operation in any control zone. The measurements of operative temperatures also showed 
compliance with at least category C during cooling season (24.5 ± 2.5°C), from DS/EN ISO 
7730, through the entire measurement except for measurement point 4 in the south-west 
control zone and measurement point 1 in the north-east control zone. Combining these two 
findings, through measurements performed simultaneously, it was concluded that while the 
ventilation system clearly could not sustain the set-point, it did not need to, as the operative 
temperatures were mostly acceptable, and that this set-point could easily be increased without 
impacting the operative temperatures at all. 
Through the measurement of the CO2 concentrations and temperatures within the meeting 
room ST45, it was found that while there was some “short-circuiting” due to the placement 
of the supply and exhaust diffusers in the room, the cause for the complaints about the thermal 
environment inside the meeting rooms, were more likely caused by solar cooling loads from 
the roof windows and an excessive air flow (also due to the set-points) as opposed to 
insufficient ventilation due to “short-circuiting”. 
It was found through the measurement of the balancing of the supply diffusers, that the air 
flow to the southern open-plan office, was respectively +11 % and +16 % for the south-east 
and south-west control zones, compared to the design values found when investigating the 
documentation of the ventilation system. Combining this with the trend of operative 
temperature compliance being more within category B and C, compared to the measuring 
points in the northern open-plan offices, and the differences between exhaust air temperature 
and set-points, there is evidence that the air flow required to ventilate the south-east and 
south-west control zones were grossly underestimated during the design phase. 
Additionally it was found that due to the proportionally excessive air flows to the southern 
open-plan office, that there should be zones connected to LA2 (The southern plant), that 
receive insufficient air flow compared to the design values. Since most of these zones are 
made up of the larger meeting rooms in the building, while not being able to identify the 
exact zones that experience insufficient supply air, there is a clear indication that the results 
from the measurements in ST45 are not indicative of the other meeting rooms, requiring 
additional measurements. 
Finally it was found through the measurements of the local thermal environment at different 
points inside the 3 big open-plan offices, that the local air velocities from the ventilation 
system were not the cause of the draught problems, since most of these were relatively low 
(below 0.12 m/s), combined with high air temperatures, resulting in draught rating only 
exceeding 10% at 5 points out of a total of 84 measurement points, with the highest draught 
rating at 16%. This suggests that air flow could potentially be increased to the zones to 
increase comfort, without resulting in draught problems.

8 Proposals for improvements 
Based on the knowledge gained from studying retro-commissioning, investigation of the 
building systems documentation and from monitoring and tests of the buildings systems, 
several problems have shown themselves. As a conclusion to the work in the building, 
proposals for improving the performance of the building is presented here. 
8.1 Building systems documentation 
After reviewing literature on the building commissioning process, and from the experience 
gained while investigating the building systems and attempting to diagnose the building, the 
importance of readily available exhaustive documentation is made clear. 
Going through the diagnostic process without clear documentation about the assumptions 
and design decisions, makes finding problems, and their root causes, extremely difficult, 
since conscious design decisions, can sometimes be perceived as errors without a proper 
overview of the system. 
Trying to diagnose a system without a proper overview of how the entire system works, is 
like trying to solve a puzzle without all the pieces. Assumptions are made, that can lead to 
treating the symptoms of a problem instead of actually finding and removing the root cause, 
which in some cases can solve one problem only to create another. Therefore it is important 
for the staff responsible for operation and maintenance, to have proper knowledge about how 
the system is supposed to work, to be able to identify equipment not working as intended and 
understand why it is happening, and what to do about it. 
The alternative to the staff being able to identify and solve problems to ensure efficient 
operation, is outsourcing this job. However even when this is the case, having the exhaustive, 
clear documentation reduces the workload significantly of having to start over with gathering 
information each time a new person commences work on the system. 
Gathering and organizing all of the relevant documentation should therefore be a top priority, 
before any further work on the building systems is performed, since this will save a lot of 
resources for everyone having to start from square one, trying to work with the building 
systems. 
Another important aspect of organizing the documentation is clarifying the design criteria for 
the building systems, providing a baseline for the operation of these. This allows the 
maintenance staff to identify when operation of the system drifts from the requirements, and 
if strategies are in place, whether they be contacting external companies with service 
contracts or fixing the problem with internal personnel, ultimately improving the operation 
of the building systems and potentially reducing maintenance costs. 
8.2 Overhaul of the control system 
In the current control system, there are several problems that should be addressed. The 
problems consist mainly in oversimplification of the system, which can only be attributed to 
reducing the cost of the plant.  
One of the problems manifested in the weather compensation curve for the supply air 
temperature combined with the control zone temperature set-points. Here the weather 
compensation curve would result in supply air temperatures above 22°C when the outdoor 
temperature went below -5°C. Since the dampers in the zones are only controlled based on

Master’s Thesis

the respective exhaust air temperature and set-point, situations where the outdoor temperature 
is below -5°C but the room temperature is above the zone set-point (constant 22°C) the 
dampers will open to 100% attempting to cool the room with air at the set-point temperature, 
resulting in a situation where there is maximum air flow in all zones. 
This is clearly not the intended purpose of the weather compensation curve, which instead 
probably was to reduce energy consumption from the radiators by increasing the heat 
recovery. But due to the set-points within the zone, the result is increased energy 
consumption from the radiators due to the excessive air flow. 
This example highlights another problem with the control system, the fact that there is no 
implementation of the other building systems. Ideally, the heating systems, windows and 
external shading should all be included in a central control system, promoting synergy 
between the systems. 
Currently the only central control of the heating system, is a valve connected to an outdoor 
temperature sensor, which shuts off heat supply when the outdoor temperature goes above 
25°C. The individual radiators are controlled by individual thermostats that can be changed 
by the occupants. The control of the windows happens by central switches, the same as the 
external shading, which therefore should not be too difficult or expensive to implement in 
the central control system. 
Overhauling the control system to include ventilation, heating, external shading and windows 
would require building a new control scheme from the ground up as well as new equipment 
for the radiators allowing remote control, but should be worth it by improving the efficiency 
and functionality of the building systems. 
Integrating all of the building systems would allow the automatic use of natural ventilation, 
external shading, and ensure efficient heating and cooling by coordinating these two systems 
preventing them from counteracting each other, as is periodically the case currently. 
Another part that is missing in the current control system is preventative measures, such as 
internal equipment functionality checks. An example of such a preventative measure would 
be a temperature measurement on both sides of e.g. the heating panel, comparing the intended 
effect, heating if that was the case, with the measured temperature on both sides of the heating 
panel. If a signal was sent to the valve supplying the heat panel with DH17 to open up, but the 
air temperature was not increased across the heating panel, an alarm would go off, notifying 
the staff of the problem. These kinds of measures should of course be applied to all 
components in the ventilation plant, allowing problems to be discovered immediately. 
8.3 Short-term solutions utilizing the current control system 
While the optimal solution would be to overhaul the control system completely, 
implementing the other building systems as well as preventative measures, this improvement 
would be a somewhat expensive investment. And while the investment would probably be 
worth it in the long term, alternatives to this extensive initiative should be explored first.

8.4 Increasing the cooling capacity 
After investigating the ventilation plant it is clear that there are 3 components vital for the 
cooling of the supply air.  
1. The evaporative cooling unit on the supply side,  
2. The evaporative cooling unit on the exhaust side supplying indirect cooling through 
the rotary heat exchanger  
3. And the desiccant wheel used to dry the supply air in preparation for direct 
evaporative cooling. 
Using evaporative cooling naturally limits the cooling capacity, since the air can only absorb 
so much humidity, meaning on a hot day with high humidity it would be impossible to cool 
the air further. This is where the desiccant wheel enters the picture, since by drying the air 
prior to the direct evaporative cooling the cooling capacity increases.  
As described in section 5.1, the description of the building site, the climate in this area entails 
warm summers, no dry seasons and a daily probability of rain between 40% - 60% throughout 
the year and a relative humidity ranging between 64% in May and 89% in December. 
These humid conditions reduces the potential for cooling by direct evaporative cooling and 
makes drying the air in the desiccant wheel imperative for the cooling process. 
Additionally the use of direct evaporative cooling means that the supply air has a very high 
RH, and therefore the potential of using evaporative cooling on the exhaust side, for indirect 
cooling, is relatively small. This again emphasizes the importance of drying the air 
sufficiently in the desiccant wheel. 
The desiccant wheel works by first blowing the “wet” air, the supply air in this case, through 
the wheel, where the adsorbent material inside the wheel adsorbs the humidity from the air. 
As the wheel rotates the area that just adsorbed the air humidity comes in contact with another

air flow, the “dry” exhaust air in this case, where the adsorbent material releases humidity to 
the air flow with a low relative humidity. 
So in order to transfer the moisture from one stream to the other, there needs to be a 
significant difference in relative humidity, and the way this is accomplished, is by simply 
heating the exhaust air.  
The exhaust air is heated through a heating panel with district heating with a supply 
temperature of 65°C. During summer, when the cooling loads are high, and the need for peak 
operation in the desiccant wheel are present, which means that a lot of heating is necessary. 
Assuming the exhaust air has a temperature of 26°C and 60% RH, it would require approx. 
26 kW/m3/s to heat this air to 55°C reducing the RH to 9.8%, not even considering the 
efficiency of the heating panel. With a peak air flow in the building of approx. 20 m3, the 
power needed for heating the exhaust air would be 520 kW. Supplying this amount of heating 
by use of only DH requires a massive flow, and thereby pipes that dimensioned accordingly. 
Based on all of these considerations, increasing the cooling capacity by increased drying is 
not recommended, since this would require additional DH capacity, which is expensive by 
itself, and also incur high operation costs in regards to DH consumptions.  
Instead it is recommended that a cooling coil is implemented as an additional component in 
the ventilation system, to complement the current evaporative cooling during peaks or hour 
periods with very high outdoor humidity. While an initiative such as this, would require a 
significant investment, both in reworking the control system, and the physical 
implementation, it should improve the functionality of the ventilation system and increase 
comfort in the building. 
9 Overall discussion 
As the overarching objective of the thesis, was to investigate the usefulness of implementing 
questionnaires in the retro-commissioning process to reduce the scope and cost of the 
process, without compromising the results. 
Evaluating this is difficult, since no two commissioning projects are exactly the same, and 
the scope changes to fit the owner’s expectations and the resources available. Most of all the 
difficulty in evaluating the approach, is that there was not enough time for another project, 
with a more conventional approach, to be carried out, to which the new approach could be 
compared. Even if there had been sufficient time for both projects, they would have had to 
be carried out simultaneously so lessons learned in one project did not affect the other. The 
only way to assess the approach, is to evaluate the effect of the questionnaire on the project 
as a whole. 
In evaluating the effect of the questionnaire on the project the question can be asked; “Did 
the questionnaire assist meaningfully in the commissioning process?” The questionnaire 
results indicated that there were areas in the building with more severe problems that others, 
as well as periods of operation that were more problematic. 
The questionnaire showed that the summer period was most problematic overall, due to 
complaints about draught, too high and too varying temperatures, odours and a lack of 
concentration. This lead to a focus in the monitoring process of problems associated to the 
cooling operation mode of the ventilation system and the problems associated with this.

10 Overall conclusion 
The questionnaire showed clear evidence that there was significant dissatisfaction with the 
indoor climate in the building with respectively 46%, 38% and 35% of participants generally 
dissatisfied with the indoor climate during summer, fall and winter. Additionally the 
questionnaire was successful in isolating specifically problematic areas inside the building, 
namely the northern and southern open-plan offices, due to increased frequency of thermal 
environment complaints compared to the rest of the building. 
The measurements indicated several problems with the ventilation system, however the most 
pronounced was the insufficient ability of the ventilation plant to maintain sufficient cooling 
to comply with the given set-points. Combined with the measurement of the actual operative 
temperatures during the period, the results showed that the assumptions used to set the set-
points either overestimated the capacity of the ventilation system or the ventilation needs in 
the offices, since the exhaust air set-points on zone level could be increased significantly 
without adversely impacting the operative temperature levels. Additionally it was shown 
through the measurements of the local thermal environment that the supply air temperatures 
could be lowered, which when combined with the increased zone-level set-points, should 
decrease air flow and thereby fan speed, decreasing overall energy consumption. 
Several proposals for improvements were given, two of which concerned the control system 
of the ventilation plants, and the lacking implementation of the remaining building systems, 
such as heating, shading and windows. Another proposal concerned improving the building 
documentation, as a lack of this severely affects the ability of both the managerial staff and 
external consultants to operate and maintain the building systems. The final and proposal 
concerned adding components or scaling the current ones responsible for cooling the supply 
air. While these proposals should remedy most of the identified problems, there are still 
problems to which the root causes could not be identified, and therefore improvements could 
not be proposed.  
In conclusion there seems to be evidence that the questionnaire did in fact help focus the 
effort of the project, and while there was some work in preparing the questionnaire and 
analyzing the results, much of this work could potentially be automated, leaving little effort 
required to utilize the questionnaire to focus the diagnostic monitoring phase. 
While the questionnaire helped focus the monitoring phase significantly, there was still 
evidence that the monitoring phase had areas that needed further investigation to be properly 
concluded. One of such being the balancing of the southern zones, since evidence from the 
measurement phase showed disproportionate air flow in the southern open-plan office. 
What this showed, was that while the questionnaire could help focus the monitoring phase, 
there is still areas in need of thorough monitoring and tests, reducing the potential for the 
questionnaire to reduce the scope of the project as a whole, since this showed that the quality 
of the retro-commissioning project suffered, due to compromising the holistic investigation 
of the building.

project, since no firm requirements had been documented for either the building or the 
building systems. However, the questionnaire showed itself as a good indicator of the

owner/manager creates the OPR18. 
While this project focused mostly on the identification of potential problems with the 
building systems and indoor climate, the retro-commissioning process, and building 
commissioning in general, puts a large focus on other things such as the gathering and 
organization of building documents, training of staff and providing a clear strategy for 
implementing initiatives. 
These areas would not be affected by the implementation of the questionnaire, which means 
that the effect of implementing the questionnaire as a means to reducing the scope of a 
building commissioning process, could prove to be rather insignificant in the greater scheme 
of the process.  
Since the questionnaire can be partly automated and the analysis of the questionnaire can be 
practically fully automated, the effort required to implement the questionnaire as a tool in the 
retro-commissioning process can be reduced very significantly. And since the results shows 
that, while the questionnaire cannot be used to reduce the scope of the retro-commissioning 
process significantly, there are still benefits to using it. Therefore it can be concluded that 
while implementing questionnaires may not be revolutionary, it can still be implemented as 
a very valuable tool for the retro-commissioning team.

Appendix 3: Calibration of operative temperature loggers 
In this section, the method used for cross-calibrating the operative temperature with the more 
accurate thermos-anemometers will be described and the results from the calibration 
presented. 
Method: 
Both the operative temperature loggers, created by attaching the grey balls to the thermistor 
probes as described in section 7.1.1, and a thermos-anemometer, of the same type as were 
used in Measurement 4, were placed inside a polystyrene box to smooth out the temperature 
variations, and the box was placed in a window. A picture of this set-up can be seen below: 
 
Figure A 1: Picture showing the setup used for cross-calibrating the operative temperature loggers. Thermo-
anemometers can be seen fixed to a stand, and wires from the operative temperature loggers can be seen 
protruding from the polystyrene boxes. 
Assuming that the air temperature and operative temperatures are equal and uniform inside 
the polystyrene box, the temperature measured by the thermo-anemometer can be assumed 
as the “actual” temperature, allowing the correction of the measurements from the operative 
temperature loggers accordingly. 
Both the operative temperature loggers and the thermos-anemometer then measured the 
temperature at an interval of 1 minute between 5 pm on May 30th and 8 am on June 3rd in 
order to have as much data, and as wide a range of temperatures as was possible with 
relatively strict scheduling constraints.
