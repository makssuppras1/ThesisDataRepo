Abstract
This thesis investigates the application of Polarimetric SAR interferometry on land ice data
in order to estimate penetration depths of the radar signal into the ice sheet. It has been
done on P­band data from West Greenland monitored in the spring of 2012 during the
IceSAR 2012 campaign. The focus has been on two test sites since they hold different
geophysical characteristics of the ice sheet. The SHR site is situated in the ablation zone
at an elevation of 712 m. While the S10 site is more inland at an elevation of 1853 m and
in the percolation zone.
Both polarimetric and interferometric analyses have been made with the data. An Entropy­
Alpha decomposition revealed that surface and volume scattering were present at both
test sites.The highest coherences were seen for the HH + VV channel whereas the com­
plex coherences of the HH −V and 2XP were almost identical. The coherence profiles
did not show any distinct subsurface layers within the ice. However, it was shown that co­
herences from the S10 site followed the expected behaviours for a Random­Volume, RV
model and Random­Volume­under­Surface, RVuS model. Therefore model inversions of
both the RV and RVuS models have been performed on each test site to estimate the
penetration depths.
Results showed for both sites that in the complex plane many pixels did not follow the
behaviour of the RVuS model. Those pixels were therefore masked out. For the other
pixels an estimated penetration depth was found through 3­stage model inversion of the
RVuS model. Both the RV and RVuS models estimated highest penetration depths for
April and lowest for June at both test sites. This could be explained by a more wet snow
in June yielding higher extinction coefficient. However the models did not show any clear
difference between the two test sites.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The Greenland ice sheet covers an area of 1.700.000km2 which corresponds to 80% of
the Greenland surface [1]. It is the second largest ice sheet on this planet just after the
Antarctic ice sheet. They are both highly affected by climate change through increasing
temperatures causing melting. Though the changes seem local, the effects will have
impact on a global scale, since the ice sheets serve as massive water reservoirs. A total
melt of the Greenland ice sheet would lead to a global sea level rise of around 7m. This
would affect hundreds of millions of lives around the world [2]. After a potential tipping
point the damage of the climate changes in the polar regions seem to be irreversible
due to the many positive feedback mechanisms in the system. However it is difficult to
estimate this tipping point will occur. Therefore constant monitoring of the ice sheets is
important since it provides real time updates of their states. To do so a measuring method
is needed which ensures high temporal and spatial resolution in any weather conditions
and during both day and night. For this purpose a Synthetic Aperture Radar, SAR mounted
on a satellite would be valuable. With such a system SAR interferometry, InSAR could be
used to perform Digital Elevation Models, DEM of the ice sheets, hence used to predict the
mass balance of the ice sheet over time. Unfortunately the SAR signal penetrates into the
ice introducing a negative bias. Therefore the SAR measures more than the distance to
the ice surface, and the additional depth known as the penetration depth changes locally
depending on radar and ice characteristics. This creates a high uncertainty in the DEMs
and hence the mass balance estimations.
Fortunately Polarimetric SAR interferometry, Pol­InSAR has the potential of deriving the
true surface elevation of an ice sheet by estimating the penetration depth. It is done in
a coherent combination of polarimetry, interferometry and modelling. An ESA satellite
mission, BIOMASS, is scheduled for launch in 2023 with a main objective to produce
Pol­InSAR data across the world [3]. This will allow for monitoring the whole ice sheets
with Pol­InSAR over a longer time span. The satellite could be used for better estimates
of the mass balance in regions where traditional methods like altimetry fails. There the
estimated penetration depths from Pol­InSAR can be used to correct the InSAR DEM
and hence lower the uncertainty on the the mass balance, which would improve climate
monitoring of the delicate polar systems.
1.2
State of the art
Pol­InSAR was first proposed in 1998 by S. Cloude and K. Papathanassiou [4]. Since
then the research focus has mainly been on vegetation volumes to apply the most suit­
able physical model and most applicable model inversion [5],[6],[7]. A main objective has
been to retrieve forest heights with a long wavelength radar, same as BIOMASS will use.
For that purpose a Random­Volume­under­Ground, RVoG model [8] has proven to be
sufficient with the 3­stage model inversion technique [6].
However the method can be used on any scattering volume such as an ice sheet [9]. A
scattering model for dry ice was first derived in 2000 by E. Hoen and H. Zebker [9]. They
modeled the subsurface of the ice as a uniform lossy volume with constant extinction and
infinite depth. This allowed the measured InSAR coherence at C­band to be related with
the penetration depth of the InSAR signal. The method only included the absolute value

The first publication of Pol­InSAR used for glaciological application was in 2003 by J. Dall,
K. Papathanassiou and H. Skriver in [10]. Where L­band data over a Greenland ice cap
situated in the percolation zone were presented. It was demonstrated that the data had
a polarimetric and interferometric dependency since the coherence amplitude and phase

studied the same data in the polar plane and compared it to different land ice scattering
models. The models were similar to those used in vegetation studies however adapted to
ice applications. Both uniform and non­uniform ice models were tested, although no phys­
ical realistic model was found since none were consistent with both the ice and reflector
observations.
In [12] J. Dall studied the relation between the penetration depth and elevation bias to
conclude that they cannot be assumed identical if the penetration depth is large compared
to height of ambiguity.
In recent years more studies have been made on the application of Pol­InSAR on ice
volumes [13], [14], [15]. The increased interest can be due to climate change, and due to
the prospects of obtaining vast amounts of Pol­InSAR data of the ice sheets in the future
through BIOMASS and due to Pol­InSAR’s unexplored potential to retrieve important ice
sheets parameters such as penetration depth.
J. Sharma et al. [14] applied polarimetric decomposition on both P­ and L­ band Pol­
InSAR data to remove the surface scattering from the volume scattering. This was then
used with the Uniform­Volume­under­Ground model to retrieve extinction coefficients.
In [15] G. Fischer suggested a multiple­layer­plus­volume model based on coherence pro­
files for several frequencies and for each polarimetric channel. A model with 2 layers, first
one at the surface, turned out to be efficient enough to describe the measured coherence
undulation for almost all frequencies except the L­band, where a third layer was needed.
A model fit of the L­band was made with the coherence profiles and the GPR data to re­
trieve the layer­to­volume scattering ratio and layers depth. A full model inversion was not
made since even the simplest model, 2­layer­plus­volume model, has 9 model parameters
while single­baseline Pol­InSAR provides only 6 observable parameters. Therefore triple­

[16]. It turned out that triple­baseline data provide a more robust estimation of the pene­

However, a full model inversion of the Random­Volume, RV and Random­Volume­under­
Surface, RVuS model has not yet been applied on Pol­InSAR ice sheet data in order to
retrieve the penetration depth.

Theory
The application of Pol­InSAR over ice sheets requires a combination of several topics
including glaciology, remote sensing, polarimetry, interferometry, Polarimetric SAR inter­
ferometry, and inversion of models. This chapter provides a description of each topic and
its necessary theory.
2.1
Ice sheets
The cryosphere includes all the areas on Earth covered by ice, including the poles with
the Greenland and the Antarctica ice sheets. They are the two most massive ice sheets
on Earth covering an area of respectively 1.7 mio km2 and 14 mio km2 [17]. Together
they hold more than 99 percent of the freshwater ice on Earth. Antarctica itself contains
30 mio km3 of ice [17].
The global mean sea level of Earth is highly connected to glaciers and the ice sheets
as they behave as large water reservoirs which makes them crucial to the global water
cycle. Water is stored during winter as snow whereas some are released during summer
by calving or melt­off. The interaction between the water cycle and the glaciers can be
related to the mass balance of the glacier, which tells its total change of volume within
one year. The mass balance is calculated as the sum between accumulation and ablation
both given in volume of water pr. unit surface area. Accumulation covers the gains of ice
through snowfall, which eventually is transformed in to firn and ice. Whereas ablation
explains how much snow and ice is lost due to sublimation, calving, surface melting, and
run­off. The mass balance of a glacier is a main characteristic of the individual glacier
and a crucial variable in climate modelling.
The 6’th IPCC [18] reports that mass losses from glacier and ice sheets can explain 42
% of the global mean sea level rise during 1971­2018, while the rest is manly due to
thermal expansion. Especially Greenland is predicted to continue melting and it might
even increase due to cumulative CO2 emissions. Monitoring of the mass balance of the
Greenland ice sheet is therefore important in order to predict and mitigate future global
sea level rises.
2.1.1
Glaciology
A fundamental knowledge of glaciology is needed to interpret the SAR images of ice and
for understanding the scattering models presented later on.
The ice sheet of Greenland and Antarctica can be divided into different glacier zones which
impact the radar backscatter response differently. In Greenland the ice zones ranges
from the ablation zone to the wet­snow zone at low elevations while at higher elevations
it ranges from the percolation zone to the dry­snows zone. Antarctica is almost only the
dry­snows zone [10]. The different zones and their characteristics can be seen in fig. 2.1,
where the overall areas are the accumulation and ablation area [19].
In the accumulation area more snow is falling than melting so the area has an positive
mass balance. Whereas in the ablation area more snow is melting than falling so there a
negative mass balance is present.
The two areas can be divided into further zones. The zone situated at the highest elevation
is the dry snow zone, where usually no snow is melting even during summer. Instead more
snow accumulates which compacts the lower snow layer to firn and even further down to
ice. Since there is no melt in this zone the firn density is lower than other glacier zones

is dated back to the Medieval Warm Period [22]. Even though this was an exceptional
year an increase in melt has been monitored for the Greenland ice sheet. In the 1990s
Greenland’s ice mass loss was monitored to be 25 bio tons pr. year while in 2019 the
average was 234 bio tons pr. year. This increase means that the Greenland ice sheet
is melting 7 times faster nowadays compared to the 1990s [23]. Therefore a continuous
monitoring of the ice sheets is important to document the rapid changes.
2.1.2
Monitoring of the cryosphere
Originally the ice sheets were measured by in situ techniques, which are an expensive,
dangerous, challenging, and time consuming task due to the vast size of the ice sheets
and the harsh climate. However it does provide data with high detail of the ice subsurface

The method has been followed up by remote sensing techniques applied both from satel­
lites and airborne platforms that ensures easily coverage of a large area.
Remote sensing monitoring of the cryosphere covers several methods such as; aerial
photography, spaceborne sensors in the visible range, gravimetry, passive microwave
technology, and radar techniques which include altimetry, scatterometry, and other active
imaging sensors like SAR. The main constrain on monitoring in the polar areas are the
dark polar winters and a massive cloud coverage throughout the year. This makes optical

Also the penetration depth of the ice sheet vary with the systems.
A gravimetry like the GRACE satellites, measure and use their small relative displacement
to each other along with precise location data to derive the gravity field, hence mass
beneath them. Therefore they have been used for mass balance calculation of the entire
ice sheets. However the resolution of such a system can be very coarse, even up to a
few hundred kilometers [24].
Satellite radar altimetry is one of the most used techniques to measure the mass balance
of an ice sheet. It is done by using a nadir­looking radar and through altimetry algorithms
the distance to the surface can be found. It therefore enables measuring of the surface
elevation changes over time, whereby the mass balance can be derived. The spatial
coverage of the altimeter is limited to areas with flat terrain and smooth surface, which
limits its use to the center of the ice sheet.
2.1.3
Ice monitoring by SAR
Synthetic Aperture Radar, SAR is an active microwave imaging radar system. The active
sensor can operate independently of daylight and since it measures in microwave range
it can be used in all­weather conditions even if its cloudy. This makes it very suitable for
monitoring in polar regions. It also has the ability to penetrate into the dry snow and ice
and hence observe subsurface structures and ice properties.

When ice is monitored by SAR only the top layer of the ice sheet is of interest due to
the penetration depth of the SAR system extending to several tens of meters [21]. The
penetration depth is proportional to the wavelength, such that longer wavelengths provide
deeper penetration but it also varies with the polarisation. The penetration depth is also
depending on physical properties of the snow and ice like its snow density, grain size,
structure and dielectric properties [27].
Characteristic backscattering signatures are seen for the individual glacier zones. The dry
snow zone usually has low backscatter intensity since the zone consists of relatively small
snow and firn grains which interact little with the long wavelength microwave signals [28].
On the other hand the percolation zone exhibits strong backscatter amplitude if ice pipes
and lenses with a comparable size to the wavelength are present [1]. Dominant surface
scattering can also be present due to surface melt which also introduces low backscatter
intensity. Whereas a refrozen surface melt would increase the signal [28].
No current satellite can perform the Pol­InSAR data over ice sheets nor any operating at
P­band allowing a high penetration depth, but it will come in the future with the BIOMASS
satellite.

CO2 in the atmosphere leads to global warming [2]. The carbon cycle is thus an important
system in climate models which is used to estimate the future impacts of climate change.
A forest can undergo several changes that will change its biomass and hence its amount
of carbon such as deforestation, fire and growth. However it is not well monitored and

2.3.1
Scattering mechanism
When measuring a natural media the backscattered SAR signals, in (2.1), are stochastic
signals as they are a combination of contributions from several point scatters within the
resolution cell. The scatters can be situated at various elevations and originate from dif­
ferent scattering mechanisms. SAR polarimetry enables the distinction and identification
of the main scattering mechanism within each resolution cell which can be used to extract
geophysical information of a scene.
The channel combinations in the Pauli vector, (2.3) each represent a main scattering
mechanism. Where the first element SHH + SV V is characterized as contributions from
surface scattering which mostly depends on surface roughness and permittivity. The sec­
ond element SHH−SV V represents double bounce scattering which is made from dihedral
structures. Such structures do exist naturally in nature like the forest surface and a tree
stem. Further on the first element will be referred to as HH + VV and the second as
HH −VV. The last element 2XP indicates volume scattering which can originate from
both forest, ice volumes or deserts. The volume scattering depends on the size, shape,
density, dieletric constant and orientation of the individual scatters within the volume.
2.3.2
Entropy­Alpha polarimetric decomposition
Another way to reveal the scattering mechanisms within a SAR image is by Entropy­Alpha
polarimetric decomposition. The method is based on a decomposition of the coherency

2.4
Radar Interferometry
Interferometric SAR, InSAR is a technique that allows for mapping of topography, ex.
DEM or detecting surface deformations. It requires at least two SLC SAR images of
the same scene. They should be recorded from different locations or at different times
and afterwards co­registered. A differential phase term can then be found between the
images and it holds the unique differential ranging information. The InSAR method used
in this thesis will be the across­track interferometry where the SAR antennas are spatially
displaced but with a short temporal separation. This allows for monitoring height of the
effective scattering center for each resolution cell.
The measurement principles of InSAR will be presented here along with the complex
interferometric coherence. Different decorrelation sources are listed and how to correct
for them.
2.4.1
Complex Interferogram
The geometry of the InSAR technique is shown in fig. 2.4. Two images of the same scene
are required, and they are known as the interferometric pair. In the following they will be
denoted as the first and second image. As seen the radars are geometrically separated
which introduces a spatial baseline, here shown as the normal baseline, Bn. A temporal

2.6
Scattering models for Land ice
Several scattering models with varying complexity exist for land ice, some of which will
be explained in the following. The purpose of the scattering model is to relate the mea­
sured interferometric coherence with geophysical ice parameters of the measured land
ice. The scattering model needs to be a compromise between a simple model that al­
lows for inversion and complex enough to describe the structure of the ice subsurface
sufficiently.
The models are similar to those used for vegetation studies [4] ,[8], [6]. However the key
differences when applied for land ice instead, are that the volume can be assumed to be
infinitely thick when using microwave wavelength [12]. Also the surface scattering occurs
at the top of the volume instead of at the bottom like in a vegetation scenario.
In the following the class of uniform models will be presented which include both random
and oriented volumes.

2.6.1
Uniform Volume models
The simplest used scattering model is the Uniform Volume model, UV. It assumes a uni­
form distribution of scatters and that the signal is uniformly damped through the volume
meaning a constant extinction coefficient. The vertical backscattering function, σuv then
becomes exponential and may depend on both depth and polarisation.
The UV model can be divided into 4 different models depending on 2 model characteris­
tics [11];
1. is the extinction coefficient depending on the polarisation , resulting in either a ran­
dom or an oriented volume if true.
2. is the surface contribution either negligible or significant.
A combination of these characteristics results in 4 different uniform models [12].
• The Random Volume, RV model
• The Oriented Volume, OV model
• Random Volume under Surface, RVuS model
• Oriented Volume under Surface, OVuS model.
The models have individual trade offs between being invertible with a low number of ob­
servables and being sufficient enough in describing the physical structure of the ice. So
since the oriented models introduces additionally 2 unknown parameters, it has been cho­
sen to only use the random models in the following. Also [14] shows that at P­band the
ice volume can be assumed to be random.

3.1.2
Data pre­processing
The data used in this thesis is processed to Level 1c, L1. This means that the raw data
have gone through several processing steps prior to becoming the L1 SAR focused data.
First the raw data were sent through the standard POLARIS level 0c processor [43]. It
performs range compression, equalization, internal calibration, and interpolation. The
navigation data are also transformed to the radar data times and coordinate system.
The L1 processor takes as input the data processed from the level 0c processor. A SAR
focusing in the along­track direction is then made which improves the along­track resolu­
tion while decreasing the surface clutter [26]. Afterwards radiometric calibration is made.
All acquisitions of the same scene are focused to the same grid, which means that if the
navigation data are assumed perfect the data will already be co­registered [43].

Implementation and pre­processing
This chapter covers the various implementations made in MATLAB. It includes a review
of how the kz maps were generated based on the navigation data and InSAR geometry.
Followed by the several pre­processing steps that need to be made before the data can
be used for model inversion and parameter estimation. These steps involve correcting
for different decorrelation terms as stated in section 2.4.2. The main goal is to remove
decorrelation from all other sources than the volume decorrelation which will be used for
the model inversion. How to filter decorrelation terms away from the measured coherence
and the effect on the coherence after filtering will be shown here. Furthermore, a way to
implement the Vector scattering interferogram is shown.
In the end an overview scheme of the processing steps is given since the order of the
steps are important. All the code written for this thesis have been done in MATLAB in a
combination of both scripts and functions. They have been provided in a zip­file along
with the thesis.
4.1
Vertical wavenumber maps
The first investigation of the data were through vertical wavenumber maps which were
produced from navigation data of the flight tracks, InSAR geometry and (2.47).
Each data acquisition have a navigation file named _sch file which contains the position
of the flight track in a (s, c, h)­coordinate system [46]. It is a curvelinear coordinate system
where s is an along­track coordinate, c is an across­track coordinate and h the altitude
above the reference ellipsoid [41]. It is a relative coordinate system where the center of
the s­ and c­coordinate is placed at the center of the scene. This allows an easy way

the two images.

The mean shifts from table 4.4 are used to co­register the image pairs. For a better co­

been needed if the data were to be used for example ice velocity estimation which needs
a high accuracy. However in this case less accuracy is required and it simplifies the
implementation by only using the mean shift for the whole image. The mean shift is applied
in the frequency domain by using the time delay theorem [49]:
f(t −τ) ↔e−iτωF(ω),
(4.7)
where f(t) is the function in time and F(ω) its impulse response. ω is angular frequency
and τ is the desired time delay. In this case the desired time delay is equal to the mean
shift times the sampling time, Ts which is the reverse of the sampling frequency, fs. This
is due to the mean shift being in units of pixels and Ts in the unit of time between each
pixel, hence their product resuls in the time delay.
So after the shifts are calculated by using Ulriks MATLAB function icesarproc.m, an
array with the angular frequencies is made. It is generated from a linear frequency, f
going through zero and equally spaced from −fs/2 to fs/2. The angular frequency is
then equal to ω = 2πf.
The time delay theorem, (4.7) is first performed in the range direction and afterwards in
the azimuth direction. So given the direction, the lines of the image in that direction are
converted into the frequency domain by the use of a Fourier transform, FFT. Afterwards the
spectrum is shifted such that the spectrum is centered around the zero angular frequency.
This allows for an easy multiplication with the angular frequency array as the frequencies
are ordered in the same manner as the angular frequency components. The frequencies
are then multiplied by the exponential component in (4.7) before the spectrum is shifted
back to its initial position. Finally the spectrum is converted back to the time domain
through an inverse FFT.
The co­registration is only applied to the second image such that no changes are made
to the first image.

4.4.1
Characteristics of a trihedral reflector
A trihedral radar reflector, as used, is expected to have the same bright backscatter am­
plitude in the co­pol channels while the cross­pol channel should be zero. Similar should
be seen for the phase of the backscattered signal.
So initially the amplitudes of the SHRot data were investigated to find pixels that meet
these requirements. This meant studying the backscattering of various pixels with a high
amplitude in the co­pol channels. Unfortunately natural effects can have the same char­
acteristics as described above for the trihedral reflector. This was also seen especially
for bright pixels in near range which followed a snow like pattern as seen in the bottom of
fig. 4.20a.
It is a natural radar phenomena that higher backscatter intensity occurs in near range
where the incident angle is low. Therefore more of the signal is reflected back to the radar
than away, which is opposite in far range for higher incident angles. To take care of this
phenomena a normalisation can be made in the azimuth direction of the image. Ideally
this will damp the high intensity in near range such that high intensity pixels in far range
can be investigated as candidates for the radar reflector. The intensity profile in range
direction prior to the normalisation can be seen in fig. 4.20b where a clear difference in
intensity is seen between near and far range pixels.

4.5
Model inversion of RVuS
The 3­stage model inversion of RVuS was implemented in MATLAB based on (2.61) and
section 2.7.2. The process can be carried out on an image pair of the same scene in the
MATLAB script RVuS_and_three_stage.m.
The script holds a double main loop that runs through each pixel for estimating the pen­
etration depth individually. First it makes a least squares linear line fit between the Pauli,
HH and VV complex coherences in the complex plane. The two intersections between the
linear line and the unit circle in the complex plane are found. The intersection closest to
the HH+VV complex coherence is chosen as the topographic surface point which is used
to phase rotate all the complex coherences. Two intersections are then found between
the rotated linear line and the extinction curve, parameterized as a half circle with a radius
of 0.5 and a center in [0.5, 0]. One intersection will be at the topographic surface point in
[1, 0] and the other one is chosen as the volume only complex coherence, γvol. The γvol is
assumed to only hold volume scattering, and can therefore be used to calculate the pen­
etration depth through (2.62). The procedure is then carried out for each pixel resulting
in an estimated penetration depth map.

After investigation of the data it was clear that the 3­stage inversion method had to undergo

An other criteria is that the complex coherences lie within the extinction curve. This can
be assumed since they hold some surface scattering which pulls them towards the to­

value with an uncertainty depending on NL. Therefore a margin is set so that the com­

in which the complex coherences can lie in, is within the orange boundary in fig. 4.23b.

6
Discussion
This chapter evaluates how well the measured complex coherences can be explained by
the stated models.
6.1
Data acquisition
A main limitation of the data have been that it was not acquired with the main purpose of
being used for Pol­InSAR model inversion. Therefore large baselines are seen for some
data sets which affect the kz and the measured complex coherences. Even kz values up
to 2.5 rad/m are seen for S10ot June data. Very small baselines around zero are also seen
for other data sets. Even baselines that change sign within the same scene are present.
The small baselines result in pixels with very high unrealistic estimated penetration depths.
While the large baselines result in low coherences which introduce more errors in model
inversion.
The extreme baselines limit the available useful data sets and makes it harder to conclude
whether a RV or RVuS model are sufficient enough to explain the data.
More radar reflectors monitored on the ice surface would have been useful for validating
the model inversions. They are easy to use as validation points as they hold the phase
of the true ice surface. Unfortunately it is all a matter of finances as more data sets and
more reflectors are expensive.
6.2
Trends in the Pauli complex coherences
After investigating the Pauli complex coherences for the different data sets it was clear
that the complex coherences of HH −VV were almost identical with the 2XP complex
coherences. The same trend is not seen in vegetation applications and therefore the 3­
stage inversion technique has been slightly changed in this thesis to make it applicable for
ice application. However, the trend makes physical sense since in a vegetation volume
like forests the HH −VV channel is affected by double bounce scattering between the
forest surface and tree stems. While the 2XP channel, volume scattering, would interfere
with the tree crowns. So the two channels would not necessarily have identical complex
coherences since they are affected by different physical parts of the forest. Whereas in
an ice volume the double bounce and volume scattering are expected to occur beneath
the ice surface instead of above as in the forest case. So it is likely that the channels have
similar complex coherences as they are affected by the same ice structures such as ice
lenses and inclusions. Though it does not mean that their vertical distribution of scatters
are similar as the complex coherence is a statistical measurement.
In general for all data sets higher coherences are seen for SHR than for S10. Also the
SHR coherences fluctuate less rapidly from pixel to pixel than in S10. This could indicate
more volume scattering at S10 and a more superficial scattering for SHR.
The coherence profiles for June S10ot and S10if follow the expected behaviour of the
RV model even though they have very different kz values. The RV model cannot explain
the coherence profiles completely like the increasing coherence for large kz values. A
more complicated model might be needed. For SHRif the coherence profiles showed an
inverse proportionality with the kz value which cannot be explained by the models, instead
it could be due to artifacts. The coherence profiles of SHRot were almost independent of
kz with a more or less constant coherence, especially for the HH + VV channel. In the
extreme case of only surface scattering the complex coherences would be drawn towards

6.3
Evaluation of the model inversion results
The main limitation of the 3­stage inversion method is the assumption that the 2XP­
channel only contains scattering from the volume. None of the polarisation states contains
only one kind of scattering mechanism. However, when using the 2XP­channel the es­
timated depth is a lower bound of the actual depth. Also the accuracy of the line fit is
highly dependent on both the number of complex coherences and the relative position of
the complex coherences in the complex plane.
For all data sets many pixels were masked out during the RVuS model inversion as the
observations did not follow the model assumptions. The observed complex coherences
for specific pixels were shown in the complex plane in fig. 5.25 and 5.26 to reveal two
common errors.
The main reason for masked out pixels for SHRif and S10ot June was Error 1. This
indicates that the RVuS model inversion had performed better if the sign of kzV ol was
changed in those pixels. The spatial distribution of Error 1 for SHRot June revealed that
those pixels also have a large negative difference between HH + VV and 2XP. Such a
clear connection is not seen for the SHRif June and S10ot June data, seen in Appendix
E.
A reason for Error 1 could also be that the wrong topographic phase was chosen since
Error 1 occurs in pixels with low coherence. A low coherence would place the complex
coherences near origo of the complex plane. This would bring them closer to each other
and there will be a higher statistical chance for them to switch places. This increases the
chance of choosing the wrong topographic phase which will result in complex coherences
that after phase rotation are not within or close by the extinction curve.
For the June data the Error 2 was mainly seen for SHRot. The Error 2 could indicate that
volume scattering was more dominant than surface scattering as the complex coherences
were drawn further away from the topographic surface point. The number of masked out
pixels due to Error 2 could have been lowered if the threshold on 0.2 was increased.
An other source of error would be if the incident angles are not calculated correctly. This
would effect the kz value and increase its uncertainty. This error would have the highest
impact at large incident angles, so in far range, as the difference between the vertical and
oblique distance would be the largest.
For the model inversions a constant kzV ol has been used for each pixel which controls
the extinction curves exponential distribution of d2. This effect was visualized for two dif­
ferent kzV ol values in fig. 2.8. A small kzV ol value introduces a higher increase of d2 in
the last half of the curve compared to a higher kzV ol value. Generally for all data sets the
predicted γvol lies on the last half of the extinction curve. Therefore it is highly affected by
the size of kzV ol. If kzV ol is small a higher d2 can be estimated. Also a small change in
γvol would lead to a very different d2 for small kzV ol due to high sensibility in the last half

In the model inversion all the decorrelation is set equal to the volume decorrelation. Even

A clear difference is seen in the estimated d2 between April and June for both test sites.
This seasonal change can be explained by an increase in melting such that more wet snow
can be expected for June. Wet snow would increase the extinction coefficient and hence
lower the penetration depth. The April data for SHR and S10 have a higher average d2 but
they also have a very small kzV ol. Some scenes have both negative, zero, and positive
kzV ol which introduces high average d2 values, whereas the June data have larger kzV ol.
Also the highest coherences are seen in April which affect the high average d2. The
high coherence could be explained by less melting between the two acquisitions in April
compared to June.
The same geophysical behaviour was expected to be seen between SHR and S10 as
more dry snow was expected for S10 yielding a deeper penetration. This is not seen in
the average d2 for neither the RV or RVuS model.
A tomographic analysis of the same data was made by Benda F. et al. in [50] 2013.
The Capon tomograms showed a distinct surface with changing elevation in the azimuth
direction for both test sites. At S10 a strong indication of volume scattering was seen but
also distributed scattering beneath the surface. Penetration depths were estimated to be
20m for SHR and 60 m for S10.
The reflector could have been investigated for other SHRot data sets with the hope of
getting a better validation point for the models. The reflector found in SHRot April got an
estimated d2 up to 46.50 m by RVuS. Though the pixel of the reflector did not have the
backscattering characteristics as expected for a trihedral reflector it was still used as the
candidate for the reflector due to its (s, c, h)­coordinates.
The surface plot with respect to the reflector in fig. 5.28 showed high variations of d2 for
neighboring pixels of the reflector. This could be expected for SHR as d2 could have high
local variations giving a less smooth surface plot. However, a smooth surface plot might
have been seen for S10 due to the more homogeneous scene, even though no reflector
was placed there due to higher cost of placing a reflector more inland.
6.4
Future and related work
For future work it would be necessary to state a model, that suites the observations even
better. This might require more data in order to better characterize the true behavior
of an ice sheet monitored with Pol­InSAR. A more complicated model might be needed
which requires more observables, hence multiple baseline interferometry. Therefore other
model inversion methods may need to be used.
Much research has been made on different inversion methods for forest applications.
Some of these techniques might be useful within ice applications in the future.
Shi et. al. [51] proposes a new method were dual­baseline is used so more observables
are included. Beside that the ground­to­volume scattering ratio are identical for both base­
lines. This method is compared with the traditional 3­stage inversion and a method where
the extinction coefficient is fixed. The new proposed method turns out to estimate the tree
heights closest to the validation data.

Machine learning introduces a whole new branch of applications within model inversion.
In [52] Sun X. et. al. used machine learning to find the most suitable extinction coefficient
for P­band single­baseline data of forests. The extinction coefficient was then kept fixed
during a geometric inversion process in order to obtain a unique solution. The method
was then compared to a three­baseline inversion method and a null­extinction inversion
method where the RMSE to the Lidar data was improved by 22.72% and 17.63%, respec­
tively.
In [1] locations of snow facies of the Greenland ice sheet were found based on an un­
supervised classification method, named the c­means fuzzy clustering algorithm. It was
applied on X­band TanDEM­X intererometric SAR data of Greenland. Beside the classifi­
cation the mean snow densities and permittivities were derived for each facies such that
the penetration depth could be estimated from the interferometric model suggested by
Weber Hoen and Zebker [9]. Two­way penetration depths for each facies were estimated
and with mean values racing between 4.18 −2.34 m which was slightly lower than the
reference data.
Another method to estimate the penetration depth from InSAR data was proposed in
[53]. There a simple linear regression model between penetration depth, coherence, and
backscatter intensity was used. The linear relationship was derived from data in the North­
ern Greenland monitored by TanDEM­X and IceBridge in April 2012. The results showed
a clear connection but further investigation into the effect of Ha is needed.
Rott et al. [54] estimated the penetration bias of InSAR DEM over a glacier from the inter­
ferometric coherence. They did it by assuming a uniform volume with constant scattering
and absorption properties. The inversion method was based on [12] and the relation be­
tween the coherence phase and amplitude. A simple model was used since the inversion
was only performed with a single parameter, the coherence. Their results correlated very
well with their validation data, however the model seemed to overestimate the penetra­
tion bias in areas with high wind exposure and low accumulation rates, while the model
underestimated in areas with high accumulation.
Based on the data and analysis in this thesis a multiple layer­plus­volume model will not be
applicable to explain the coherence profiles as it was in [15]. A main difference between
the two results are that the acquisition of the data for this thesis was made in 2012 while
[15] used data acquired from 2015. So the distinct layer seen in [15] might be due to the
record high surface melt in 2012.

7
Conclusion
In this thesis Pol­InSAR P­band data from the IceSAR 2012 campaign in Greenland have
been used to estimate penetration depths of SAR signals into an ice sheet. Unwanted
decorrelation terms have been filtered away such that the measured complex coherences
should only contain volumetric decorrelation and the topographic phase. These have
been used to retrieve the penetration depths through model inversions of the RV and
RVuS models.
The Entropy­Alpha decomposition revealed that surface and volume scattering were pre­
sent for both test sites though not necessarily double bounce scattering.
The highest coherences were generally seen for the HH+VV channel. While the complex
coherence for HH −VV and 2XP channels were almost identical for every data set. The
coherence profiles showed that for SHR the coherence was almost constant and inde­
pendent of kz especially for the HH+VV channel indicating high surface scattering. They
also indicated no distinct layers within the subsurface of the ice for both sites. For S10ot
June data the coherence profiles followed nearly the same pattern as expected for RV
and RVuS models though with small variations for small and large kz values.
The RV model inversions were highly dependent on kzV ol, where low d2 were estimated
for high kzV ol values. The average estimated d2 for June was 7.56 m for SHRif, 8.63 m for
SHRot, and 5.82 m for S10ot.
The RVuS model inversions were highly dependent on high coherences for the 2XP chan­
nel and small phase differences between the HH + VV and 2XP channels. If this was not
present the pixels were masked out as they did not fulfill the assumptions of the 3­stage
model inversion method. The main error for masked out pixels for SHRif and S10ot June
was that the complex coherence of 2XP did not lie within or near the extinction curve. The
same error was also seen for SHRot June along with an other error where the complex
coherence of 2XP was to far away from the extinction curve. For pixels that were not
masked out the RVuS model inversion averaged d2 for June with 42.14 m for SHRif, 31.14
m for SHRot, and 49.62 m for S10ot.
Both models estimated highest d2 values for April and lowest in June which follows the
assumption that the extinction is higher later in the melting season due to more wet ice.
Also a deeper d2 was expected for S10 data due to its position in the dry snow zone
though this was not seen for the RV or RVuS model inversion.
For future work a new and improved data set would be appropriate. One where the main
objective is to use Pol­InSAR data on ice, as it would ensure suitable baselines. The
BIOMASS mission will provide vast amounts of data such that the Pol­InSAR data of
an ice sheet can be characterized sufficiently. This would give an improved foundation
to choose or make a new suitable scattering model for the ice sheets. Other inversion
techniques might be needed such as machine learning or deep learning, due to the low
number of 6 observables for single­baseline data.
Pol­InSAR applied on an ice sheet still has a large unexplored potential, where this thesis
