Abstract
The growing global demand for energy and the urgent need to reduce greenhouse gas emissions
have driven the adoption of hybrid power plants (HPPs) that integrate renewable energy sources
(RES) with energy storage systems (ESSs). This thesis investigates the optimal design and
operation of islanded HPPs, focusing on two case studies: Ikaria in Greece and the Faroe
Islands. A model developed using HyDesign is employed to evaluate various ESSs, including
Li-ion batteries, Pumped Hydro Energy Storage (PHES), Compressed Air Energy Storage
(CAES), Liquid Air Energy Storage (LAES), Gravity Energy Storage (GES), and Pumped
Thermal Energy Storage (PTES) and to model the operation of an islanded conﬁguration. The
study assesses the technical and economic performance of these systems in oﬀ-grid applications,
taking into account the unique weather conditions and electrical grid demand load of each case
study. Moreover, a multi-objective function is applied to identify the optimal sizing of the
HPPs, with a focus on minimizing diesel generator usage and/or maximizing the net present
value (NPV) relative to capital expenditures.
The trade-oﬀs between these objectives are
thoroughly examined and discussed. The ﬁndings contribute to the broader goal of sustainable
energy development in remote communities, oﬀering valuable insights into the deployment of
RES and ESS to achieve energy independence and environmental sustainability.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The global push towards sustainable energy solutions is driven by signiﬁcant economic growth
and increasing industrialization, which have drastically elevated energy consumption over the
past decade. According to a recent report by the International Energy Agency (IEA), global
energy demand rose by 4.5% in 2021 [7]. This increase has consequently led to a rise in carbon
emissions, which grew by more than 5% in the same year. This alarming trend underscores
the urgent need for a transition to renewable-based energy systems to mitigate environmental
impacts and reduce greenhouse gas emissions.
In response, the European Union (EU) has set ambitious climate targets as part of its European
Green Deal and Climate Law, aiming to achieve net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050. To
reach this goal, the EU has committed to reducing emissions by at least 55% by 2030 compared
to 1990 levels. This transition is critical not only for environmental sustainability but also for
enhancing energy security and reducing dependence on fossil fuels.
Renewable energy sources (RES), such as solar photovoltaics (PV) and wind turbines are piv-
otal to this green transition. By 2030, projections indicate that non-fossil fuel sources must
generate 57% of global energy to limit global temperature rise to 2◦C, aligning with the Paris
Agreement’s targets[19]. In Europe, RES are expected to account for two-thirds of electricity
generation by 2030, an increase from 39% in 2022, with wind and solar PV contribution to over
85% of the new capacity added during this period [23].
However, the intermittent nature of renewable energy sources presents challenges. Solar pan-
els are less eﬀective in cloudy conditions, and wind turbines are unproductive during calm
weather. These intermittencies can lead to periods of both excess and insuﬃcient electricity
production, complicating energy management and grid stability. This is where energy storage
systems (ESSs) play a crucial role.
ESSs are essential for both on-grid and oﬀ-grid applications. A hybrid power plant (HPP)
with a storage system meets the energy demand and ensures energy continuity. Hybrid renew-
able energy systems have important eﬀects on reducing greenhouse gas emissions for a cleaner
environment and reducing dependence on external sources from an economic point of view.
Oﬀ-grid applications, such as remote communities or isolated facilities, rely on ESSs to provide
a reliable power supply, reducing dependence on diesel generators and other fossil fuels.
Over the past decade, advancements in energy storage technologies have signiﬁcantly expanded
the capabilities of HPPs. Various storage options, including batteries, pumped hydro storage,
compressed air energy storage, and thermal energy storage, oﬀer diverse characteristics in terms
of capacity, eﬃciency, response time, and cost. Selecting the appropriate energy storage tech-
nology is critical for the successful implementation and operation of HPPs.
1.2
Research objective
This study aims to develop a method for modeling an islanded conﬁguration, meaning for HPPs
connected to microgrids and provide energy to a remote area or island independently from the
main electrical grid. It also includes a comprehensive evaluation of diﬀerent ESSs, examining
the technical and economic aspects of various storage options to identify the most suitable
1
==PAGE:12==
technologies for enhancing the performance of hybrid power systems in oﬀ-grid applications.
The developed method will be applied to real-life cases: one in Faroe Islands, where wind re-
sources are abundant, and one on a small Greek island named Ikaria which has moderate wind
resources and strong solar irradiance.
1.3
Research Questions
The research questions the current study will try to resolve are the following:
• How can islanded conﬁgurations be modeled?
• How do diﬀerent energy storage technologies in hybrid power plants compare in terms of
their suitability for oﬀ-grid applications?
• How do the wind or solar resources aﬀect this comparison?
• How do diﬀerent energy storage technologies impact the sizing of wind, solar, and storage
to obtain an optimal techno-economic performance of hybrid power plants?
1.4
Research process
The main approach of this study is described in the following steps:
• Research on the thesis objective
• Literature review on diﬀerent energy storage technologies
• Identiﬁcation of key variables to model diﬀerent energy storage technologies
• Collect input required such as eﬃciency, power rate, life time, self discharge rate etc.
• Problem formulation of the HPP islanded mode operation
• Integration of this model to the already built optimization model
• Development of the multi-objective function for the optimal sizing
• Data collection regarding the two case studies
• Results interpretation and comparison between diﬀerent energy storage technologies
• Sensitivity analysis to identify and evaluate the key parameters
1.5
Contributions
The main contributions of this study are the following:
• Implementation of an optimization model for an islanded wind-solar-storage HPP
• Identiﬁcation of technical and economic aspects of diﬀerent energy storage technologies
• Implementation of a multi-objective function for the sizing optimization
• Analyses of two case studies

2
Literature review
2.1
Hybrid Power Plants
2.1.1
Introduction
A HPP is deﬁned as a power system that combines multiple energy technologies to improve
the reliability and eﬃciency of power generation. These systems integrate various renewable
energy sources, such as solar, wind, and hydro-power, with energy storage solutions like batter-
ies, pumped hydro storage, or thermal storage to maintain a stable power supply. The primary
objective of HPPs is to address the intermittency of renewable energy sources by storing sur-
plus energy produced during periods of high generation and releasing it during times of low
generation or increased demand.
In recent years, the installation of HPPs has increased worldwide, driven by the growing need
for sustainable and reliable energy systems. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the deployment of HPPs
has expanded across various regions, showcasing their critical role in advancing renewable en-
ergy integration and enhancing grid stability. This trend underscores the importance of hybrid
power plants in the global transition to clean energy and the eﬀorts to reduce dependency on
fossil fuels.
Figure 1: World’s operational and pipelined HPPs. [37]
2.1.2
Taxonomy of HPPs
Based on the linkage and operational integration, HPPs can be categorized into three main
types. These types are illustrated in Fig. 2:
• Co-located Resources: These systems involve multiple energy technologies that are
physically located together but operate independently. The primary advantage of co-
located resources is the potential for cost savings through shared infrastructure and in-
terconnection. However, the lack of integrated operation can limit the overall eﬃciency
gains.
• Virtual Power Plants: These systems involve dispersed energy technologies that are
coordinated operationally to optimize their collective output. VPPs can provide increased
4
==PAGE:15==
operational value by leveraging complementarities in resource characteristics and services.
The operational coordination can enable these systems to participate in energy markets
and provide ancillary services more eﬀectively.
• Full Hybrids: These systems combine both locational and operational linkages, involv-
ing shared components and integrated control strategies. Full hybrids oﬀer the potential
for the highest net economic beneﬁts through co-optimization of the constituent technolo-
gies. This category can include shared inverters in PV and battery systems or combined
control systems in wind and thermal storage hybrids.
Figure 2: Taxonomy of HPP. [10]
2.1.3
HPP Conﬁgurations in islanded areas
Developing hybrid and renewable energy projects in islanded areas such as the Faroe Islands
and the Greek islands is critical due to their geographical isolation and unique energy challenges.
In the Faroe Islands, the need for sustainable energy solutions is driven by their dependence
on imported fossil fuels and the ambition to achieve energy independence. The region has
signiﬁcant wind and hydro resources, making it well-suited for renewable energy projects. By
integrating these resources with energy storage systems in HPPs, the Faroe Islands can ensure
a stable and reliable power supply. This reduces the reliance on imported fuels, lowers opera-
tional costs, and minimizes the environmental impact of energy production [11].
The Greek islands face similar challenges, including high energy costs and environmental con-
cerns due to their reliance on oil-burning power plants [31]. These plants are not only expensive
to operate but also contribute signiﬁcantly to pollution. The isolation from the mainland grid
exacerbates these issues, making energy security a pressing concern. Implementing hybrid re-
newable energy systems on these islands can mitigate these problems. By combining renewable
energy sources like wind and solar with energy storage technologies, the islands can achieve a
more consistent and sustainable energy supply [12]. This transition is crucial for reducing fuel
import costs, improving energy reliability, and protecting the environment.
Furthermore, hybrid renewable energy systems enhance energy independence and support eco-
nomic development in islanded areas. They allow for the eﬃcient use of local renewable re-
sources, reducing the need for expensive and polluting fossil fuels. This leads to lower electricity
costs and better environmental outcomes, which are essential for the sustainable development
of these regions. For example, projects on Greek islands such as Tilos [28], [25], Donnousa [30]
and Sifnos [15] have demonstrated the viability and beneﬁts of integrating renewable energy
systems, showcasing signiﬁcant improvements in energy stability and sustainability.
5
==PAGE:16==
In conclusion, the development of hybrid and renewable energy projects in islanded areas like
the Faroe Islands and Greek islands is vital for achieving energy security, reducing costs, and
minimizing environmental impacts. These projects leverage local renewable resources to create
a more sustainable and resilient energy system, addressing both economic and environmental
challenges eﬀectively.
2.2
Techniques of energy storage
2.2.1
Introduction
As of 2021, there were 1,693 energy storage projects worldwide, with a total capacity of approx-
imately 180 GW. Fig. 3 shows that 80% of these projects are operational, 11% are announced,
and the remaining share comprises oﬄine, contracted, under-construction and de-commissioned
projects.

2.2.2
Pumped Hydro Energy Storage (PHES)
A PHES system operates by using gravitational energy to store electricity. It typically includes
two reservoirs at diﬀerent elevations, connected by pipes, and a system consisting of pumps,
turbines, electric motors, and generators. In some setups, the pump and turbine are combined
into a reversible pump-turbine, and the motor and generator are integrated into a single mo-
tor/generator unit.
7
==PAGE:18==
The operation of PHES is relatively simple. During times of low electricity demand, surplus
power from the grid is used to pump water from the lower reservoir to the upper reservoir, stor-
ing energy as gravitational potential energy. When electricity demand is high, the stored water
is released from the upper reservoir, ﬂowing through turbines to generate electricity, which is
then supplied back to the grid. A sketch of the PHES system is depicted in Fig. 6.
Figure 6: Pumped Hydro Energy Storage system.
PHES is the oldest and most widely implemented large-scale energy storage technology. As
shown in Fig. 7, it dominates the large-scale energy storage market, representing 99% of the
world’s capacity in this category. There are currently around 340 PHES facilities in operation
globally, with a combined installed capacity of 178 GW [16].
Figure 7: Cumulative sum of globally installed PHES system.
The technology features low self-discharge rates which depend on factors such as evaporation
and leakages, the round-trip eﬃciency measuring the ratio of energy output during discharge
to energy input during charge ranges from 70% to 85%. PHES systems are known for their
large power ratings, up to 5000 MW, and can store over 1000 MWh of energy. They also have
long lifespans, usually up to 60 years. These systems can respond within a few seconds and
8
==PAGE:19==
achieve full load in under three minutes. However, they require signiﬁcant water resources and
suitable geography. Furthermore, for greenﬁeld plant it is requires a high capital cost reaching
up to e3M per MW [13]. To mitigate these costs, new PHES installations are often feasible
in regions with favorable topography or by upgrading existing hydroelectric plants, potentially
reducing costs to e0.6M per MW and increasing power ratings beyond 5000 MW.
2.2.3
Compressed Air Energy Storage (CAES)
Another large-scale energy storage technology, following PHS, is CAES. The concept of CAES
was ﬁrst introduced in the early 1940s, but the ﬁrst operational CAES plant was not built until
30 years later.
The working principle of CAES is straightforward, similar to PHS. During periods of low power
demand, excess generation capacity is used to compress air and store it in an underground
cavern (e.g., hard rock caverns, salt caverns) or in above-ground man-made tanks, pipes or
containers. When electricity demand peaks, the stored compressed air is released, heated (for
conventional CAES using natural gas), and expanded through an air turbine. The turbine,
mechanically coupled to an electric generator, converts the potential energy of the compressed
air back into electricity. This type of CAES plant, which wastes the heat generated during
compression, is known as D-CAES.
The two operational CAES plants today use this working principle, making them D-CAES
units. The ﬁrst CAES plant was built near Huntorf, northern Germany, in 1978. The second
operational CAES plant is located in McIntosh, Alabama, USA, and has been in operation since
1991 [29]. Based on practical experience, D-CAES is characterized by a round-trip eﬃciency
in the range of 40 to 70%, a lifetime of up to 60 years, very low self-discharge rates (0.003 to
0.05%/day), and a start-up time of approximately 10 minutes.
Despite these characteristics, D-CAES relies on fossil fuels, leading to a non-negligible environ-
mental impact during the discharging phase. Therefore, researchers have explored alternative
conﬁgurations to eliminate the need for fuel consumption by implementing Thermal Energy
Storage (TES) and recovering the heat generated during the compression process [4, 17]. Such
a system is illustrated in Fig.8. This upgraded version of D-CAES is called Adiabatic CAES (A-
CAES). Several pilot applications have been reported in the literature, including the ADELE
project by the RWE Power Company in Germany [34].
9
==PAGE:20==
Figure 8: Compressed Air Energy Storage system.
It is expected that these A-CAES systems will be commercially available with rated power ca-
pacities ranging from 0.1 MW to 1000 MW, storage capacities in the range of 1 to 1000 MWh,
a higher round-trip eﬃciency of nearly 75%, and signiﬁcantly lower start-up times. However,
these advancements generally come with higher capital costs.
Despite these technological updates, CAES shares a common drawback with PHES: both re-
quire speciﬁc geographical conditions for installation.
2.2.4
Gravity Energy Storage (GES)
GES systems have been designed to overcome the primary limitations of PHES, such as the
need for continuous water ﬂow and speciﬁc geographical conditions for installation.
One type of GES operates as a closed-loop system using water as the working ﬂuid. A large
piston, equipped with sliding seals to prevent leakage, is suspended in a deep storage shaft.
During periods of low electricity demand, oﬀ-peak electricity is used to pump water from the
deep storage shaft to the return pipe, lifting the piston from the bottom to the top of the
shaft. When electricity demand is high, the piston descends, pushing water along the return
pipe to drive a hydraulic turbine, thus generating electricity. Although there are no existing
installations of this technology, it is expected to achieve a round-trip eﬃciency of over 80% and
have a lifespan exceeding 30 years. A schematic of a GES unit is shown in Fig.9.
10
==PAGE:21==
Figure 9: Gravity Energy Storage system.
Compared to PHES, this system does not require a continuous water ﬂow, as it recirculates wa-
ter in a closed loop. Additionally, it does not rely on a natural waterfall since the deep storage
shaft and return pipe are man-made. However, similar to PHES and CAES, it is advantageous
to place this system in a site with stable geological conditions. Due to the complexity of the
system, the investment costs are comparable to those of PHES and CAES.
Another GES concept has been described in the literature. This system uses oﬀ-peak electricity
to lift buckets of gravel from a low to a high elevation. When electricity is needed, the buckets
are released to descend, generating electricity. The expected eﬃciency of this system is around
90%. While the working principle is simple, the system requires a suﬃciently high hill, needing
a signiﬁcant elevation diﬀerence between the top and bottom of the storage unit.
The Advanced Rail Energy Storage system is another type of GES. This system involves loading
train wagons with concrete blocks. During periods of low demand, the wagons are moved from
the bottom to the top of a hill or mountain. When electricity is needed, the wagons descend
under gravity, generating electricity. The round-trip eﬃciency for this system is expected to be
in the range of 75 to 86%, with a lifespan reaching 40 years.
2.2.5
Liquid Air Energy Storage (LAES)
LAES is a promising new technology for large-scale energy storage, where electricity is stored
in the form of liquid air (or nitrogen) at cryogenic temperatures. The operating principle of
LAES is illustrated in Fig.10. During the charging phase, ambient air is puriﬁed, compressed
using excess electricity, and cooled down to its liquid state. The liqueﬁed air is then stored in
near-atmospheric pressure vessels. When electricity is needed, the liquid air is pumped, evap-
orated, and expanded through a series of turbines to generate electricity. The LAES process
produces hot and cold thermal strams during air compression (charging) and evaporation (dis-
charging). These thermal streams can be captured and reused within the system to enhance
energy eﬃciency. Therefore, LAES systems often include TES devices, a hot and a high-grade
cold one, in addition to liquid air tanks. This design signiﬁcantly reduces the required volume
11
==PAGE:22==
of storage vessels, making a LAES plant signiﬁcantly smaller than a CAES and a PHS plant.
Figure 10: Liquid Air Energy Storage system.
The round-trip eﬃciency of LAES systems ranges from 45% to 70% [3], operational lifespans
of 20 to 40 years, the response time is expected to be between 5 and 10 minutes, with a very
low self-discharge rate. One major drawback of LAES is the high cost reaching similar capital
costs with PHES and CAES.
2.2.6
Pumped Thermal Energy Storage (PTES)
Pumped Thermal Electricity Storage is an innovative technology designed for large-scale en-
ergy storage. PTES works by using a high-temperature heat pump cycle to convert oﬀ-peak
electricity into thermal energy. This energy is then stored in two thermally isolated vessels:
one containing hot material and the other cold. When electricity demand peaks, a thermal en-
gine cycle converts the stored thermal energy back into electrical energy. Many diﬀerent plant
schemes have been examined and proposed aiming to improve eﬃciency by reducing losses [1,
5]. Fig.11 depicts a conﬁguration of a PTES system.
12
==PAGE:23==
Figure 11: Pumped Thermal Energy Storage system.
The working ﬂuid for PTES is typically a gas such as air or argon. The system stores elec-
tricity as sensible heat using inexpensive solid materials like concrete, gravel, or other common
minerals. PTES oﬀers a low self-discharge rate (1% per day), a round-trip eﬃciency of 48-75%
and a lifespan of 25-30 years [3]. Additionally, no geographical constraints are required for such
installation as with PHES and CAES.

Methodology
3.1
Background
A HPP operating in remote areas is designed to ensure a reliable and continuous energy sup-
ply to meet the demand load at all times. Such a plant typically incorporates a mix of non-
dispatchable and dispatchable technologies to achieve this goal. The non-dispatchable technolo-
gies include wind and solar power plants, which generate electricity based on natural conditions
that cannot be controlled. These renewable energy sources are complemented by dispatchable
technologies, primarily energy storage systems, which store excess energy produced during pe-
riods of high generation and release it when production is low or demand is high. This ensures
that the energy supply remains stable despite ﬂuctuations in generation capacity.
To further ensure energy reliability, a diesel generator or gas turbine is included in the system
as a backup power source. These generators can quickly be activated to provide electricity
when renewable sources and stored energy are insuﬃcient to meet the demand. The primary
objective of operating such a HPP is to maximize the utilization and storage of the renewable
energy produced, thereby minimizing the reliance on the diesel generator or gas turbine. This
approach not only ensures a stable power supply but also reduces operational costs and envi-
ronmental impacts associated with the use of fossil fuels.
By eﬀectively managing the balance between renewable energy generation, storage, and backup
power, the HPP aims to optimize its performance and provide a sustainable and reliable energy
solution for remote areas.
This study aims to model a HPP with the conﬁguration depicted in Fig. 12. The proposed
HPP will include wind and/or solar energy sources combined with an ESS, such as a Li-ion
battery or an alternative storage technology. The HPP is designed as a fully hybrid plant,
meaning that the renewable energy sources and the storage system are physically co-located
and their operations are integrated. This integration allows the storage system to be charged
directly by the renewable energy sources, ensuring eﬃcient and cohesive operation of the plant.

3.2
Assumptions
To simplify the modeling process and ensure the feasibility of this study, several key assumptions
have been made. These assumptions are critical for understanding the scope and limitations of
the results derived from the model:
• The eﬃciencies during the charging and discharging phases of the ESSs are considered
constant. This assumption reduces the complexity of the model but may not account for
variations in eﬃciency due to changes in operating conditions.
• While some ESSs exhibit response delays of several minutes, this delay is ignored in the
model, and an immediate response is assumed. This simpliﬁes the calculations and the
model for ESSs.
• For weather, price, and demand data, a perfect forecast is assumed. This means that the
model operates on the premise that power generation and demand are based on exact
given data, without any deviations or forecasting errors. The model uses hourly data for
one year and duplicates these to represent the entire project lifetime. As a result, any
ﬂuctuations in weather or demand beyond the provided one-year data are not considered.
• A constant electricity price is assumed with no variations over time and through the years
of operation. This simpliﬁes the ﬁnancial modeling but may not reﬂect real-world price
ﬂuctuations.
• The HPP is assumed to operate for a period of 25 years. Although many storage tech-
nologies have a longer lifespan, it is essential to account for additional costs associated
with necessary replacements and maintenance to ensure extended operation over this du-
ration. These costs should be factored into the overall economic analysis to provide a
more accurate assessment of the projects long-term feasibility.
• For the islanded conﬁguration model, a diesel generator (DG) is included to operate
whenever the HPP cannot generate suﬃcient power to meet electricity demand. The
fuel consumption of the diesel generator is assumed to be constant per unit of energy
produced. Moreover, the lifetime of the diesel generator has been considered to be 25
years, and the possibility of replacement has not been considered.

3.4.2
Degradation model
Following the literature review, it was concluded that Li-ion batteries experience signiﬁcant
degradation over their lifetime.
This degradation is closely related to the battery’s usage,
speciﬁcally the charge-discharge cycles. Therefore, including a battery degradation model when
considering a HPP that includes a Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) is necessary.
For the other ESSs examined in this study and reviewed in Section 2.2, it is concluded that
degradation does not need to be considered, as their performance does not signiﬁcantly decrease
over time. Especially, when considering the expected operational and maintenance costs, the
impact of degradation is negligible, as indicated in [9].
As a result, for the modeling conducted in this thesis, the degradation component is included
when a Li-ion battery is used as the ESS. This component is already implemented in HyDesign
and described in [26]. However, for all other ESS types, degradation has not been factored into
the calculations.

is set to 1, the focus is entirely on maximizing the ﬁnancial return, emphasizing the economic
feasibility and proﬁtability of the HPP. Conversely, when λ is set to 0, the priority shifts to
minimizing DG usage, thus focusing on maximizing renewable energy usage and reducing car-
bon emissions. This ﬂexibility allows stakeholders to tailor the optimization process based on
speciﬁc project needs, regulatory requirements, or sustainability goals, making it possible to
address conﬂicting objectives.
The use of a multi-objective function is important for several reasons. Firstly, it provides a
holistic approach to optimization, considering both economic and environmental objectives,
which is crucial for designing eﬃcient and sustainable HPPs. Secondly, it facilitates strategic
decision-making by quantifying the trade-oﬀs between ﬁnancial performance and environmental
impact, enabling stakeholders to make informed decisions that align with their goals.
In summary, the multi-objective function is a powerful tool for optimizing the conﬁguration of
an islanded HPP, ensuring that it is both economically viable and environmentally sustainable.
By carefully balancing the objectives through the adjustment of λ, the model identiﬁes the
most eﬃcient and cost-eﬀective conﬁguration for the hybrid power plant, guaranteeing long-
term sustainability and resilience against ﬂuctuating energy demands and generation capabili-
ties. Therefore, the diﬀerent values of λ indicate where the focus of the sizing optimization is
directed, whether it is towards minimizing the use of the DG or maximizing the NPV/CAPEX
value of the HPP.

4.6
Discussion
The results presented in the previous section lead to several key conclusions across all case
studies. Starting with the ESSs, it is evident that LAES is the most expensive option among
all the technologies considered. In nearly all scenarios, LAES fails to provide a viable solu-
tion. Conversely, PHES and CAES consistently perform similarly across most conﬁgurations,
emerging as the more economically feasible options. However, both technologies have signif-
icant geological and geographical limitations that restrict their installation in certain areas.
Additionally, the performance of Li-ion batteries falls short technically, primarily due to their
limited energy storage capacity. This limitation makes it diﬃcult for Li-ion batteries to achieve
high levels of RES penetration, rendering them less comparable to the other ESSs in terms
of their ability to support extensive renewable energy integration. This is particularly evident
in case studies like the Faroe Islands, where the correlation between renewable resources and
grid electricity demand is weak, necessitating a storage system with a larger storage duration
capacity. Even the 8-hour GES is insuﬃcient in this context, unlike Ikaria, where GES achieves
RES penetration levels similar to the 20-hour capacity PTES.
The Pareto fronts discussed, based on a PPA price of 150 EUR/MWh for the Faroe Islands’
main grid and 200 EUR/MWh for Ikaria and the Faroe Islands’ Southern grid, indicate that the
Faroe Islands’ main grid case study tends to produce higher NPV/CAPEX values compared
to Ikaria and Suduroy. This suggests that the Faroe Islands represent a more economically
eﬃcient case study, likely due to the smaller ﬂuctuations and reduced vulnerability associated
with larger grids, in contrast to the smaller and more volatile grids of Ikaria and Suduroy.
Moreover, the Ikaria case study achieves better NPV/CAPEX values for λ = 0 than either of
the Faroe Islands case studies. This diﬀerence could be attributed to the nature of the energy
resources in each area; Ikaria beneﬁts from excellent solar resources, leading to high solar PV
eﬃciency, whereas the Faroe Islands are known for their wind resources. However, solar PV
parks are signiﬁcantly cheaper than wind turbines, which is reﬂected in the ﬁnal results.
Interestingly, all case studies converge on a peak RES penetration of around 70%. This out-
come is signiﬁcant, as it suggests that the optimization process does not favor over-sizing wind
and solar power plants to achieve higher RES penetration. This result could be inﬂuenced by
the limits set during the optimization process, as outlined in Table 6. It would be worthwhile

to investigate whether broader limits could enable higher RES penetration. Another potential
explanation for this result is that the current EMS modeling focuses on the ﬁrst year of op-
eration, with the assumption that, over a 25-year period, any degradation in solar and wind
power output would be compensated by increased diesel generator usage. This simpliﬁcation in
the modeling may unfairly skew the system’s long-term operation and underestimate its true
RES penetration potential. As such, this calculation might not fully represent the system’s
long-term operation and its ability to achieve higher RES penetration.

5
Conclusion
A key parameter in designing an oﬀ-grid HPP capable of meeting the demand of an isolated
area is the weather conditions and demand load proﬁle of the region. Each application site
has unique patterns and characteristics that critically inﬂuence the ﬁnal design of the HPP.
The correlation between wind and solar resources and the local demand load is particularly
important, as it directly impacts the required size and capacity of the storage system. A strong
correlation can reduce the reliance on storage, while a weaker correlation necessitates a larger
storage capacity, and particularly duration capacity, to manage discrepancies between supply
and demand.
Moreover, as demonstrated in the case studies presented in this study, small grids are more
susceptible to ﬂuctuations in grid’s demand load. These grids tend to experience greater vari-
ability in demand peaks due to local activities or sudden changes in weather conditions, posing
additional challenges in achieving high renewable energy penetration and in designing a con-
ﬁguration that is both economically viable and technically reliable.
The availability and quality of renewable resources in the area are also crucial factors. If a par-
ticular resource, such as solar or wind, oﬀers strong performance and is paired with relatively
inexpensive technology, it can signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the design strategy. For example, in the
Faroe Islands case study, the optimization process favored oversizing solar power plants instead
of expanding storage capacity. This is because solar technology, despite being less eﬀective
in the local climate, is still a more cost-eﬀective option than other, more expensive storage
technologies.
In terms of technology selection, storage capacity, especially in terms of duration, is a critical
factor for achieving high renewable energy penetration. Technologies like Li-ion batteries and
GES, which have lower duration capacities compared to others, generally achieve lower RES
penetration. This limitation reduces the dispatchability of energy produced by renewables,
meaning that surplus energy generated during periods of high renewable output cannot be
stored for later use when production is low.
Additionally, the charge/discharge eﬃciency and ﬁnancial parameters (CAPEX and OPEX)
of each technology signiﬁcantly impact the economic performance of a HPP. Lower eﬃciency
necessitates the slight oversizing of renewable energy sources to oﬀset the higher losses incurred
during the charge/discharge process.
Moreover, the PPA price and the cost of diesel fuel
are crucial factors inﬂuencing the ﬁnancial performance and optimal conﬁguration of the HPP.
When the cost of diesel fuel is much higher than the electricity price, relying on diesel generation
becomes prohibitively expensive, making investments in renewable and storage technologies
more attractive. Conversely, when the electricity price and diesel operational costs are more
closely aligned, diesel usage becomes more preferable. This balance is inﬂuenced not only by
the PPA price but also by the CAPEX and OPEX values of the various energy technologies
and the overall cost of diesel fuel.

6
Future work
A key area for future work that could signiﬁcantly extend the ﬁndings of the current study is
the consideration of an extended project lifetime. Given that the majority of the technologies
examined have very long lifespans, it would be valuable to explore a project timeline extend-
ing up to 50 years. This extension would necessitate incorporating some replacement costs
for solar, wind, and/or storage systems. Extending the projects lifetime is anticipated to sub-
stantially lower the PPA prices required to achieve positive NPV/CAPEX values, compared
to the results shown in Section 4. Additionally, as mentioned in the assumptions, including
replacement costs for the diesel generator, which were excluded in the current analysis, would
provide a more comprehensive economic assessment. Accounting for these replacement costs
could further shift the balance in favor of renewable energy penetration over diesel usage.
Another important aspect for future investigation would be a thorough examination of the fac-
tors that could be adjusted to achieve higher RES penetration rates, potentially exceeding 80%.
The results of the current study, as discussed in the Discussion section, reached a maximum
of 70% RES penetration. It would be interesting to further investigate whether larger energy
storage systems are required or if other adjustments could facilitate higher RES penetration.
Such an analysis would oﬀer critical insights into the strategies needed to approach 100% RES
penetration, which is the ultimate goal for isolated areas aiming for energy independence.
