Abstract
Purpose: Diagnostic errors are frequent and can lead to patient harm from no or wrong treatment;
it affects numerous people but are difﬁcult to identify. This thesis proposes a deep learning model
that learns disease patterns and thereby can identify diagnoses that appear in unusual contexts.
Materials and methods: The proposed model was inspired by the Bidirectional Encoder Rep-
resentation from Transformer (BERT), a pre-trained language model created by Google. The
framework from BERT was adapted to learn patterns of diagnoses from disease trajectories. The
model is pre-trained using 5.2M patient trajectories from the Danish National Patient Registry.
The model’s predictions were evaluated on chapter level and in case studies of selected individual
diseases; Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), Alzheimer’s disease, angina pectoris,
acute myocardial infarction and chronic ischemic heart disease. As part of the evaluation, the pre-
trained model was ﬁne-tuned on a binary classiﬁcation task of detecting COPD trajectories.
Results: The pre-trained model performed best on diseases related to pregnancy and worst on
diseases related infections. Further analysis of individual diseases implied that the model learned
disease patterns to distinguish diagnoses from each other. The pre-trained model estimated 78% of
the patients with a COPD diagnoses reported in DNPR to be misdiagnosed and 64% of the patients
that the model predicted as COPD patients to be underdiagnosed. The ﬁne-tuned model estimated
23.9% of the observed COPD patients to be misdiagnosed.
Conclusion: The proposed model can be applied as a systematic approach for predicting faulty
diagnoses within DNPR. The model learned the context of the diseases to identify under- and mis-
diagnoses. The estimates of faulty diagnoses should be interpreted as the number of patients at
risk of an under- or misdiagnosis. Further work is required, to exploit the full potential of the
model.

1
Introduction
A medical diagnosis is a process to determine the cause of a patient’s health problem. The diag-
nosis is determined through interviews, physical examinations, and diagnostic tests. Based on this
diagnosis, the healthcare professionals decide the treatment course. Thus, for patients to have the
best opportunity for a positive health outcome, a correct diagnosis is essential [1]. An inaccurate
diagnosis, also referred to as an diagnostic error, can lead to both wrong and unnecessary treatment
or absence of the necessary treatment. Diagnostic errors can cause unplanned hospitalization, psy-
chological distress and death. Although diagnostic errors have been a problem for decades, it was
not until recently the World Health Organisation (WHO) identiﬁed it as a high-priority issue for
patient safety, and it has been estimated that everyone will experience a diagnostic error in their
lifetime [2][3][1].
Diagnostic errors include under- mis- and overdiagnosis. Deﬁnitive deﬁnitions of the terms have
not yet been agreed upon, and each term is therefore used in different contexts. Diagnostic errors
are not only challenging to deﬁne but also challenging to detect [4]. One common approach to
identify diagnostic errors is based on reviews of medical records and clinical data [5]. The diag-
nosis determined upon the review is compared to the assigned diagnosis recorded in a patient reg-
istry. This approach has been used to estimate the rate of diagnostic errors for speciﬁc diagnoses.
Reviews of medical records are very time-consuming and often only include few diagnoses and
patients. Therefore new, more systematic methods to identify diagnostic errors are needed.
This study aims to provide a systematic approach to identify faulty diagnoses within the Danish
national patient registry (DNPR) by using deep learning. The deep learning model will be trained
on patients’ time-ordered disease histories, also called disease trajectories, in order to learn cor-
relations of diseases. The learned patterns will enable the detection of diagnoses that appear in
unusual context and thereby potentially faulty diagnoses. For this purpose, we adapted the BERT
framework, which is well known and recognized. There are currently two other studies within the
clinical domain that have adapted the BERT framework: BEHRT [6] and Med-BERT [7].

Theory
2.1
Danish National Patient Registry
The DNPR was established in 1977 with the ofﬁcial aim to monitor the utilization of hospitals
and health care services as well as diseases [8]. To address these aims, information about patient
contacts with Danish hospitals are reported to the DNPR. Patients are linked to the information in
DNPR with a personal identiﬁcation number (CPR-number) given to all residents in Denmark from
1968 [9]. The information registered is both administrative and clinical and includes, among other
things, the CPR-number of the patient, date of hospital admission, diagnoses and procedure codes
[8]. The diagnoses were coded according to the International Classiﬁcation of Diseases, version
8 (ICD-8) until 1994, where International Classiﬁcation of Diseases, version 10 (ICD-10) was
adapted [10]. ICD-10 consists of codes for classiﬁcation of diseases and related health problems.
In addition to the diagnosis, the registry also contains information about the diagnosis type; eg.
primary, secondary or referral. From 2007, all contacts with Danish hospitals have been reported
to the DNPR, including encounters at somatic, psychiatric and emergency wards [10].
2.1.1
ICD-10 CODES
ICD is a system developed by the WHO to classify health-related problems, such as diagnoses,
procedures, or injuries. ICD-10 is the tenth revision of the ICD system, replacing the former ICD-
9 to introduce additional combinations and more precise classiﬁcation. In clinical and research
settings, ICD-10 codes are considered the diagnostic classiﬁcation standard [11]. The codes con-
sist of three to seven characters, combining letters and numbers. The alphanumeric codes are
hierarchically structured, with 21 chapters at the top level. The ﬁrst character in the code is a letter
associated with a chapter, which is displayed as j in ﬁgure 1. The chapters are divided into 227
blocks consisting of more speciﬁc disease categories. The disease categories within each block are
of similar characteristics. The categories are identiﬁed by the ﬁrst three characters in the ICD-10
code and cover 1,702 different diseases. If the disease code is longer than three characters it can
be followed by a decimal point. [12][11]. The fourth character identiﬁes the subcategory that de-
scribes the disease’s site or variety. The subsequent ﬁfth and sixth characters identify the location
and laterality [11], see ﬁgure 1. This study will work with disease codes at the category level,
therefore only including the ﬁrst three characters and will be based on the Danish version of the
ICD-10 system due to the use of DNPR. The Danish version of the ICD-10 system includes more
detailed disease codes regarding clinical information than the international version [13].

2.1.2
Quality of DNPR
DNPR is considered the best of its kind and is almost complete in terms of activity at the public
hospitals and public ﬁnanced activity at private hospitals [10][15]. However, registrations from
encounters at private hospitals and clinics that are not publicly funded have been incomplete de-
spite it being mandatory since 2003. Therefore, some encounters are missing from the registry
[10]. If an error is detected in the reported encounter, it is corrected at the hospital before it is
registered in DNPR. The error can be identiﬁed through the systematic quality control of DNPR,
which includes 500 checks for errors such as incorrect or missing digits and inconsistencies in
the CPR-number [15]. Furthermore, information reported using coding systems can be affected
by changes in coding practices. In particular, the inconsistencies of disease codes can lower the
quality since the diagnosis code assigned can depend on the individual physician and hospital. Pa-
tients with identical phenotypes can therefore be assigned different disease codes [15]. Overall,
the quality of DNPR is in general considered to be high and can be examined in terms of validity
and completeness [8].
Validity of DNPR
The validity of a diagnosis refers to the extent that the disease is accurately measured. The posi-
tive predictive value (PPV) is a metric to estimate the validity and is deﬁned as the percentage of
patients registered with a diagnosis; who actually have the disease [8]. Many studies have vali-
dated diagnoses in DNPR through reviews of medical records [5]. One study found that overall
PPV for all medical diagnoses in DNPR at category level was 81% including both the primary
and secondary diagnosis. The PPVs for the individual diagnoses ranged from 15%-100% [8]. An-
other study found the PPV within a diagnosis also varied according to subcategory, age and sex
[16].

Completeness of DNPR
The completeness of a diagnosis refers to the percentage of a population with a disease that is
recorded in the registry [8]. An estimate of completeness is conditioned by knowing the true
number of people with the disease in the population. Hence, diseases that require treatment at
a hospital, such as hip fractures, have high completeness in DNPR. In contrast, diseases usually
treated by the general practitioner, such as hypertension, have low completeness [8].
2.2
Diagnostic process
A medical diagnosis is a process to determine the cause of a patients’ health problem. In the
diagnostic process, the physician usually considers multiple diseases and possibilities that could
explain the patients’ symptoms. Through interviews, physical examinations and diagnostic test-
ing, the number of potential diagnoses are reduced until a ﬁnal diagnosis is made. Based on this
diagnosis, healthcare professionals make clinical decisions and determine the treatment course.
[1].
2.3
Diagnostic errors
Diagnostic errors mainly occur due to human or clinical mistakes in the diagnostic process. How-
ever, the diagnostic process is complex and multiple factors can lead to an erroneous diagnosis
[1][17]. For example, completely different diseases can be presented with the same symptoms.
Moreover, the symptoms can evolve differently as the disease progresses, depending on the pa-
tient, comorbidities, and disease-medication interactions [1]. The different causes of diagnostic
errors have led to inconsistent deﬁnitions. However, a diagnostic error is usually deﬁned as the
failure to establish an accurate and timely explanation of the patient’s health problem [1][17].
Diagnostic errors include under-, mis- and overdiagnosis.
2.3.1
Underdiagnosis
Underdiagnosis is used synonymously with undetected or missed diagnosis and is deﬁned as the
failure to diagnose a disease [18]. Underdiagnosis can occur when the patient have not been able
to explain the symptoms or when the physician has failed to assign a diagnosis to the symptoms
[19]. In this study, underdiagnosis will refer to the failure of diagnosing a disease at the hospital
within the patient’s recorded time in the DNPR. Thus, the term will not include delayed diagnoses.
An underdiagnosis can lead to inadequate treatment and therefore have a serious impact on the
patient’s quality of life and public health in general [20]. Examples of underdiagnosed diseases are
Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) and ischemic heart disease [19][20][5].
An underdiagnosis of COPD might occur in younger people, as the diagnosis depends on a lung
function test and the lung function decreases with age [21]. Studies have estimated that 35-80%
of COPD to remain underdiagnosed [5][22][19][23][24][25][26][27][28] Underdiagnosis of is-
chemic heart disease mainly concerns women as their symptoms are often atypical and their risk

of ischemic heart disease is consistently underestimated, which can lead to underdiagnosis [29].
Studies estimate ischemic heart diseases to be underdiagnosed in 34-50% of all cases [20][5].
2.3.2
Misdiagnosis
The terms misdiagnosis and diagnostic error are often used interchangeably [17][30]. However,
misdiagnosis is a type of diagnostic error and is deﬁned as an incorrect diagnosis [18][31][32][33]
[34][17]. Misdiagnosis occurs when a diagnosis is assigned, but the patient does not meet the
clinical deﬁnition of the disease [33]. Misdiagnosis often results in patient harm and a decrease
of life quality as the underlying disease is insufﬁciently treated or a wrong treatment is given
with unfortunate effect [1][18][35]. Examples of misdiagnosed diseases are Alzheimer’s disease
and COPD [36][5]. A range of conditions are presented with symptoms similar to Alzheimer’s
disease, and therefore might be misdiagnosed as Alzheimer’s disease [37]. Misdiagnosis of COPD
is mainly caused due to the absence of a proper diagnostic test [38][39]. Studies estimates that
5-62% of COPD cases are misdiagnosed [40][41][42][43]. One study found that a misdiagnosis of
COPD might actually be patients suffering from lung cancer [33].
Misdiagnosis can be difﬁcult to distinguish from overdiagnosis as both can result in unnecessary
treatment. However, misdiagnosis is a wrong diagnosis, opposite to overdiagnosis which is a
correct diagnosis.
2.3.3
Overdiagnosis
There exists no formal deﬁnition of overdiagnosis, and the term has therefore been used in differ-
ent contexts [35]. A common deﬁnition, which this study will make use of, deﬁnes overdiagnosis
as a correct diagnosis, given to a patient who meets the clinical criteria of the disease, but where
the diagnosis will induce more harm than beneﬁt for the patient [31]. Overdiagnosis mainly occurs
due to overdetection which is the detection of a disease that was not destined to cause any harm
[32][31]. Overdetection is often a result of screening, where asymptomatic people are tested, and
diagnosed even though the disease never progresses to cause symptoms or death [32][31]. Another
common cause of overdiagnosis is overdeﬁnition. There are two reasons for overdeﬁnition: one
is shifting the clinical threshold for a diagnosis without proof that it will beneﬁt the patients. The
other is, widening disease deﬁnitions, such that patients with mild symptoms are included [31].
Overdiagnosis through overdetection or overdeﬁnition increases the prevalence of diseases by in-
troducing new low-risk patients. Moreover, overdiagnosis often leads to unnecessary monitoring or
treatments that can impair a patient’s life both physically and mentally [31]. Different studies have
estimated the frequency at which overdiagnosis occurs at the population level, based on incidence
data prior to and after introducing screening in a population [44][45]. It is, however, challenging
to identify overdiagnosis at an individual level, as it includes the counterfactual scenario of no
diagnosis and the unattainable knowledge of how the disease will affect the patient in the future
[46].
Due to the difﬁculty of identifying overdiagnosed diseases at an individual level, this study will
only focus on under- and misdiagnosed diseases.

2.4
Machine learning
Machine learning is a study of algorithms that learns from experience. The experience is gained
by attempting to solve variations of the same task. Thereby the algorithm learns a generalizable
process of overcoming that task. The algorithm is then able to predict the outcome of new situations
of a similar kind based on the learned experience. Essentially, the objective of machine learning
is to improve at a certain task. The tasks can be categorised into supervised-, unsupervised- and
semi-supervised learning [47]
When using Supervised learning the algorithm learns from labeled data. The learning is accom-
plished by adjusting the variables of the algorithms to minimize the error produced by comparing
the output of the algorithm and the true labels. Hence, the learning process is supervised, as the
algorithm knows the correct answers, and can modify accordingly. When the algorithm reaches
an acceptable performance, the model can be used to predict new unseen examples. This type of
machine learning can further be divided into regression and classiﬁcation tasks. It is a regression
task when the output is a numeric continuous variable and a classiﬁcation task when the output is
categorical [48].
Unsupervised learning is a type of machine learning that can be used to discover groupings of
similar characteristics within the input data. This type of task is known as clustering. Unsupervised
learning can additionally be used for establishing relationships between variables in the data. For
example, people diagnosed with disease X are often also diagnosed with disease Y. This type
of task is known as an association. The algorithm only learns from the input data and not the
correspondent labels when using unsupervised learning [48].
Semi-supervised learning combines supervised and unsupervised learning with a large amount
of unlabeled data and a small amount of labeled data. This type of machine learning is often
used in real-world tasks since labeled data can be expensive and time-consuming to access, unlike
unlabeled data. For semi-supervised learning tasks, the unsupervised part is used for learning the
underlying structure of the data. The supervised part can then be used for training the algorithm
with labeled data to make predictions [49].
Self-supervised learning is similar to the supervised learning, however this method uses unlabeled
data and applies algorithms to generate pseudo-labels based on attributes within the data. These
labels are used for training in the same way as for the supervised learning task [50].

2.6
Deep learning
Deep learning is a subarea within machine learning. Deep learning is able to extract and discover
more complex nonlinear interactions between the features in the input data than other methods
in machine learning [49]. Deep learning techniques are essentially neural networks with two or
more hidden layers, thereby yielding a deeper and larger network, than the shallow one-layer
neural networks. Deep learning is widely used to solve tasks such as image recognition, speech
recognition, and Natural Language Processing (NPL) [53]. NLP uses algorithms to read, listen and
understand spoken and written language. These types of models are used to perform tasks such
as translation, question answering and text summarization. One state of the art NLP model is the
Bidirectional Encoder Representation from Transformer (BERT). BERT is developed by Google
and trained on text data from BookCorpus and the English Wikipedia; therefore, the examples in
the following sections will be based on sentences. The architecture of the BERT model is based
on the Transformer [71]. BERT is described in more detail in section 2.7 BERT.
2.6.1
Transformer
Transformer is a deep learning model architecture proposed for sequential tasks in the paper Atten-
tion in all you need [72]. The Transformer has an encoder-decoder structure, commonly used for
sequence to sequence tasks. The encoder learns a representation of the input, which the decoder
predicts from. The learning of the encoder distinguishes the Transformer from other architectures
as it is entirely based on the self-attention mechanism. In self-attention, the whole input sequence
is read at once, which enables modelling of dependencies regardless of the distance within the
input sequences. However, it also means that the model initially lacks a sense of order in the input
sequence. Thus, working with data, where the order of the elements is essential, such as sentences
or disease trajectories, requires embedding [72][73].

Encoder
The encoder part of the Transformer consists of multiple encoder blocks stacked. The embedding
of the input example is fed into the ﬁrst encoder block, and the output of each subsequent block
is then fed into the next block. The last output yields the representation of the input example,
which is then used in the decoding part. Each encoder block’s structure is identical and consists
of two sublayers, one multi-head attention layer followed by a feed forward layer. The output of
each sublayer is then transformed by an add and norm component, which is a residual connection
followed by layer normalization. The residual connection combines the input and output of each
sublayer by adding them. This is to pass on information from previous layers to future deeper
layers [73]. The structure of all the components of the encoder is visualized in ﬁgure 9.

2.7
BERT
BERT is a pre-trained deep learning model published by Google AI [71]. BERT is based on the
Transformer model, however only the encoder component is included. The decoder is not part of
the structure as BERT aims to create a language model that can be applied to various downstream
tasks. Thus, BERT is pre-trained on a huge unlabeled dataset, and the weights of that model are
then ﬁne-tuned in relation to a new task. Many variants of the BERT framework have been created
since its development. The variations include different conﬁgurations and data types [73]. The
change of conﬁgurations aims to optimize the original BERT, whereas the change of data will alter
the learned vocabulary and the optimal conﬁguration.
2.7.1
Pre-training
The primary purpose of pre-training is to train a model on an extensive unlabeled dataset, and then
use the learned weights to initialize new downstream tasks. Thereby, the weights of the new model
are not randomly initialized, which provides the model with a better starting point for learning.
The google BERT is pre-trained using two approaches; Masked Language Modelling (MLM) and
Next Sentence Prediction, of which only MLM is implemented in this study [73].
Masked Language Modelling
MLM is a task where the model randomly masks out 15% of the input words. The masking is done
by replacing the token with a [MASK] token, which the model predicts. This approach is based
on self-supervised learning, since the model generates a pseudo-label, the token that is replaced by
[MASK], which the model uses for learning. BERT is a bidirectional auto-encoder and therefore
predicts the masked token by reading the context of the input sentence from both directions. This
enables BERT to make a better prediction, as it learns the context of the whole sentence. However,
only replacing tokens with the [MASK] token will result in a mismatch between the pre-training
and ﬁne-tuning task, since the masked token is not a part of the ﬁne-tuning [73]. To avoid this
problem BERT applies the 80-10-10% rule. When this rule is applied not all 15% of the masked
tokes are replaced by the actual [MASK] token. The tokens selected to be masked will be replaced
by the [MASK] token 80% of the time, a random token 10% of the time and remain unchanged
10% of the time, see ﬁgure 10. The ratios of the masking strategy have been selected using a trial
and error approach [71].

The parameters in the pre-trained BERT model can be adjusted to a speciﬁc task; this process is

need to be trained over a large number of epochs. Hence, ﬁne-tuning is computational inexpensive
compared to pre-training [71].

2.7.3
Variations of BERT
The two variations of the BERT model described in this section are both within the clinical domain
and pre-trained on diagnosis data.
BEHRT:
The pre-trained model BEHRT is created with the aim of “providing improved accuracy for the
prediction of future diagnoses” [6]. The model is trained using data from the Clinical Practice
Research Datalink, which consists of data from general practitioners in the United Kingdom. 1.6M
patients are included in the dataset. The diagnoses in the data is coded in Caliber code, a disease
classiﬁcation code developed by a college in London [6]. The vocabulary size for this coding
system is 301. The BEHRT model is pre-trained using the MLM approach [6].
Med-BERT:
The Med-BERT study aims to create a pre-trained model for general disease prediction down-
stream tasks. The model was pre-trained using data from the Cerner Health Facts, consisting of
over 600 hospitals and clinics in the United States. The dataset included approximately 20M pa-
tient trajectories. The Med-BERT study included ICD-9 and ICD-10 disease codes, which resulted
in a vocabulary size of 82K. When pre-training the Med-BERT model two approaches were used:
MLM and prediction of prolonged length of stay in the hospital [7].

3.2
Data cleaning
The raw DNPR consists of assigned diagnoses from 1977-2018 for Danish registered patients.
This study includes all patients with a vital status of either dead or alive and living in Denmark,
see ﬂowchart in ﬁgure 13. The diagnoses were encoded in both ICD-8 and ICD-10, from which
all ICD-8 encoded diagnoses were excluded. In practice, encounters registered before 1994 when
ICD-10 was adapted were removed to ensure continuity across the whole dataset. The diagnosis
codes from the last three ICD-10 chapters; Lesions, poisonings and certain other consequences of
external inﬂuences (19), External causes of injury (20) and Factors of importance to health status
and contacts with health care (21), were assumed to be unrelated to the objective of this study and
thus excluded. The same assumption was behind the exclusion of every additional code concerning
labour. Furthermore, encounters labeled with diagnose type ‘H’ were removed, as these are refer-
ral diagnoses from other hospital wards and can therefore not be truly conﬁrmed. The included
diagnoses were examined at the category level, meaning disease code at higher levels were trun-
cated. The truncated diagnoses were only allowed to be assigned once to each patient; therefore,
the encounters were only preserved the ﬁrst time a patient was diagnosed with the disease. Lastly,
patients were required to have more than one diagnosis and less than 32. The remaining patients
and hospital encounters were used for data analysis, model testing, and training. A ﬂowchart of
the data cleaning process can be found in ﬁgure 13.

3.3
Data for ﬁne-tuning
The pre-trained model and a random initialized model was ﬁne-tuned on a binary classiﬁcation
task of detecting COPD trajectories. The training and test set used for ﬁne-tuning was a subset of
DNPR, split the exact same way as for the pre-training. Both the training and test set was prepared
for ﬁne-tuning by ﬁrst creating a new binary column, based on whether the patient trajectories con-
tained COPD (j44) or not. Then all the patient trajectories containing COPD were selected, as well
as a sample of non-COPD patients of equal size to the number of COPD patients. The sample of
non-COPD patients was semi-randomly selected based on the distribution of the trajectory length
of the COPD patients, by splitting the distribution of trajectory lengths for patients with COPD
into four ranges by the three quartiles. A sample of non-COPD patients equal to the sample size in
the corresponding range for COPD patients was then selected for each range. The selection within
each of the ranges was random. The ﬁnal train and test set was balanced in terms of COPD and
non-COPD patients. Lastly, the disease code ‘j44’ was removed from the COPD trajectories.

3.4

The architecture of the proposed model was inspired by Google’s NLP BERT model. However,
instead of sentences of words, the model in this study was based on disease trajectories of ICD-

consisted of 6 stacked encoders with 12 self-attention heads, a feed forward layer of size 512 and
hidden size of 288 hidden units. The model was trained using the MLM approach. When using the
80-10-10 rule, the selected tokens were replaced by a [MASK] token 80% of the time, a random
token 10% of the time and unchanged 10% of the time. An example of the three different events is

3.6
Fine-tuning model
The ﬁne-tuning was structured as a binary classiﬁcation task. The classiﬁer was created by adding
a feed forward layer followed by a softmax function to the pre-trained model, illustrated in ﬁgure
18. The ﬁrst dense layer consisted of 64 hidden units and a relu activation function. The second
dense layer, which yielded the output, consisted of a single unit and a softmax activation function.
The ﬁne-tuning was optimized using the Adam algorithm and cross entropy as the loss function.
The described structure was used to ﬁne-tune two models: one pre-trained model and one randomly
initialized model. Both ﬁne-tuned models were trained using a batch size of 32, learning rate 1e-5
and over 25 epochs. The models were evaluated using accuracy.

5
Discussion
This study aims to provide a systematic approach to identify faulty diagnoses within DNPR using
deep learning. This is a novel approach, making it difﬁcult to compare the results with other
studies.
5.1
Study design
Study assumptions
In order for the model to learn the true disease relations, the training of the proposed model was
based on the underlying assumption that the diagnoses in DNPR are correctly assigned. However,
the study’s objective was to detect the diagnoses that were falsely assigned. This study, therefore,
assumes that faulty diagnoses exist within DNPR. These two assumptions are contradictory, as the
diagnoses recorded in DNPR are considered the ground truth during training and when analyzing
the results, the model predictions are considered the ground truth. Ideally, the model should only
have been trained on the correctly assigned diagnoses and then tested on a mixture of faulty and
correct diagnoses. The rationale behind this study is that there currently is no systematic approach
to detect which diagnoses in DNPR are inaccurate and which are accurate. Therefore this study
was based on the assumption that the vast majority of diagnoses in DNPR are correctly assigned,
and the correct diagnoses constitute a large enough proportion for the model to learn the true
correlations between diseases rather than correlations between diseases and faulty diagnoses.
Verifying faulty diagnoses
The current approach of identifying faulty diagnoses is through reviews of health records. This
approach provides a golden standard to verify whether the diagnosis recorded in DNPR is accurate
[5]. However, it is very time-consuming and therefore not scalable. Moreover, the reviewer and
their ground truth vary between the studies. The diagnosis is dependent on the physician assigning
it, which makes their ground truth less valuable [15]. The ideal validation to conﬁrm a diagnosis
are diagnostic tests, which would inform whether the patient meets the diagnostic criteria for the
diagnosis. The proposed model does not validate whether the faulty diagnoses are in fact faulty
diagnoses as this is currently impossible with the data available. Thus, additional studies are
needed to conﬁrm the faulty diagnoses found by the model, but the approach is the ﬁrst of it’s kind
and is consistent and systematic and includes many patients and diagnoses.
46
==PAGE:56==
5.2
Data
Size
The dataset used for pre-training is relatively small compared to other studies based on the BERT
framework. After cleaning the data, it consisted of 5.3M. patients’ trajectories and 41.7M dis-
ease codes. In comparison, the original Google Bert was trained using BooksCorpus and the En-
glish Wikipedia, consisting of 800M words and 2,500M words, respectively [7]. Furthermore, the
dataset used for pre-training the Med-BERT model consisted of 20M trajectories. The dataset used
in this study is approximately four times smaller than the dataset used in the Med-BERT study.
BEHRT pre-trained on an even smaller dataset consisting of 1.6M patients, which is criticized as a
limitation in the Med-BERT paper [7]. In general, working with complex deep learning structures
requires a large dataset [7]. Therefore, it is difﬁcult to evaluate the model’s full potential in this
study.
Inconsistencies in DNPR
DNPR consists of diagnoses from 1994 to 2018. The time range spans over 24 years, which is much
lower than the average danish life expectancy [77]. Thus, it is possible to conclude that most of the
disease trajectories represented in the DNPR do not fully reﬂect the patients’ disease trajectories.
Thus, some diagnoses might not be included from the beginning of the trajectories, the end or
both. This is a disadvantage for the model since it is not able to process the entire context of the
disease codes in the trajectories. Hence, the model is missing important information to understand
the underlying structure when predicting the masked or replaced disease codes. Additional data
from before 1994 could have been included to overcome part of this issue. It was not done due to
the inconsistency of disease codes. Furthermore, DNPR does not include diagnoses assigned by
the patient’s general practitioner [8]. Diseases such as dementia and COPD which can be assigned
by the general practitioners, will therefore not be fully represented in the dataset. Hence, the
completeness might be low. There are studies that try to determine the completeness of the various
diseases. However, it can be very difﬁcult to estimate [8]. For these diseases, mostly the more
severe cases will appear in DNPR, which raises the same problem as for the limited period in the
dataset.
The frequency of diagnosis over the years for the diseases from literature, are available in appendix
ﬁgure C.1 in appendix. The diagnostic criteria might be revised or improved over the years, along
with coding practice and how frequent the diagnosis is assigned. The evolving of diagnostic crite-
ria, as well as coding practice, affect the disease patterns that the model aims to learn. The decrease
in diagnosis frequency might be related to better prevention, whereas an increase of assigned di-
agnoses such as Alzheimer’s disease, might be related to the increment in the population’s life
expectancy [77]. However, the years in which a diagnosis is rarely assigned might also reﬂect un-
derdiagnosed diseases. These inconsistencies might affect the model’s ability to learn true disease
correlations.

5.3
Pre-trained model
5.3.1
Methods
BERT framework
Deep learning-based methods in general, require a large number of samples, to learn the complex
interactions in the input. However, many deep learning algorithms lack a deeper understanding
of context within the input and are therefore limited when predicting [7]. Pre-trained models
can overcome this problem by obtaining a complex understanding of context through learning the
underlying structure of the data. This knowledge can then be transferred to speciﬁc tasks [7]. The
proposed model needs to learn the structure within disease trajectories from DNPR, for which a
pre-trained model is advantageous. This study adopts the BERT framework, which is a pre-trained
model with proven success.
Metrics for evaluation
The model with the highest performance reached an overall F1-score of 0.1015, which is relatively
low. However, the overall F1-score accounts for all of the diseases. the model is not expected to
be able to predict the many diseases that lacks structure and context, which will be discussed later.
Whether a performance around 0.1 can be considered a good prediction depends on the prediction
tasks. In this study the F1-score was only used to compare and evaluate the pre-trained models’
performances, for which the tasks were the same.
The pre-trained model in this study was optimized using the cross entropy loss. The best epoch
selection for the model was evaluated based on F1-score and the epoch yielding the highest F1-
score was used for further analysis. This study aimed to maximize F1-score, as the interest lies in
whether the model predicts the correct disease and not how close the prediction is. The F1-score
is not differentiable and could not be used as a loss function. Furthermore, using the cross entropy
loss to select the best model, does not directly align with the F1-score. Thereby, the lowest loss
does not necessarily yield the highest F1-score. Hence, a combination of metrics was used for
model training and selection.
Med-BERT parameters versus BEHRT parameters
The pre-trained models in the study were based on the parameters from Med-BERT [7] and BEHRT
[6]. The models based on Med-BERT hyper-parameters yielded a higher F1-score than the model
trained using BEHRT parameters. This was expected since the data for this study is more similar to
the data used in the Med-BERT study than the BEHRT study, which were trained on a completely
different classiﬁcation of diseases system called Caliber code [6]. The most signiﬁcant difference
between data used for this study and Med-BERT is the inclusion of ICD-9 and all levels of disease
codes. The reason for the inclusion was, as mentioned in the Med-BERT paper, to make the model
“easily deployable to different institutions and clinical scenarios” [7]. This however, was not
relevant for this study since the focus only lies on detecting faulty diagnoses within DNPR.
48
==PAGE:58==
All models were pre-trained over 100 epochs. This number was set based on the BEHRT model
since the size of the BEHRT dataset was most similar to this study compared to the Med-BERT
and BERT models [7]. The epoch yielding the best performance was 96, which might imply that
the training error for the model had not yet converged. This is supported by ﬁgure 20 and 21,
where it seems that the performance of the chapters and diseases is still improving at the end
of training (after 100 epochs). The model should be trained over a larger number of epochs to
examine whether the model has converged. This was not done due to the long execution time and
the limited time frame for this study.
Due to the limited time available, the hyper-parameters for the trained models were based on the
pre-trained BEHRT and Med-BERT models [6][7]. These hyper-parameters were assessed to be
the most suitable for this study due to the similarity of data used for training. However, a more
legitimate approach for selecting the optimal hyper-parameters would be to apply a random or grid
search. In order to deploy these methods, several models must be trained over a wide range of
different hyperparameter combinations. It was not feasible for this study due to the long training
times. Applying one of the mentioned methods would be an obvious step towards optimizing the
model if more time was available.
Gender speciﬁc models
In addition to the general models trained on the entire DNPR, two gender speciﬁc models were
trained on only men and women’s trajectories, respectively. The results for these models can
be found in appendix table B. The intention of training these models was to potentially make it
more simple for the model to understand the general structure of the trajectories. Some diseases
are gender speciﬁc such as ovary and prostate cancer [78]. Moreover, the trajectory structure
for women and men can vary greatly. Some disease codes can appear in one context and order
for women and in a completely different context and order for men [79]. The gender speciﬁc
models potentially allow for easier learning, as the disease codes will appear in a more uniform
context. The model trained on female data yielded higher performance than the model trained
on male data. The pregnancy chapter can mainly explain this. The pregnancy chapter showed to
be the best performing chapter in both the women and general model. The low performance of
the model trained on men compared to the model trained on women can therefore be explained
by the absence of the good performing pregnancy chapter. One approach to overcome this and
ensure a fair comparable performance for both models, could be, to only predict disease codes that
occur in both mens’ and womens’ trajectories. In this way, disease codes such as those relating
to pregnancy, would be excluded in the evaluation for both models and not only the model trained
on male data. Another way to overcome the gender bias, would be to train a model with an extra
gender input, which would allow the model to differentiate between women and men without
splitting the model into two separate models.

5.3.2
Results
Sensible chapter performance
Several factors can explain why the models are better at predicting diseases from one chapter over
another. Chapter 1, contained many different diagnoses which were rarely assigned, observed in
ﬁgure 19. This was also the worst performing chapter on average, which could be explained by
the fact that the model does not predict the diagnoses from the chapter enough to learn a pattern.
However, chapter 2, concerning neoplasms, was the third best predicted chapter. This chapter
also contained various diagnosis codes which were rarely assigned. Therefore, the performance
of chapters are not only explained by the frequency of diagnoses predicted. Hence, the model
learns some correlations between the diseases. The model predicted diagnoses from the chapter
related to pregnancy well and the infections chapter poorly. This is expected, as the process of
pregnancy and childbirth are very well-documented at the hospital with a regular or deﬁnite struc-
ture. Furthermore, patients diagnosed with diseases within the pregnancy chapter, were commonly
assigned more than one diagnosis from the same chapter. Pregnancy was common and with high
completeness, as many women goes to hospitals for scans, examinations, delivery and so forth.
The model learned patterns from these repeated structures and predicted the diagnoses from this
chapter well. One chapter the model was not able to learn well, was the chapter related to infec-
tions. This was expected since infections are caused by various transmission pathways. Another
chapter the model was not able to learn well, was the chapter of mental disorders. Mental disorders
are very challenging to diagnose. Although there exist diagnostic criteria for the diagnoses, there
are few biological tests to determine a mental disorder. Instead, the diagnoses of mental disorders
are based on observation and patient reports [1]. As mental disorders are difﬁcult to diagnose, it is
expected that the model also would ﬁnd it challenging.
The chapter performance provides relevant information. Namely, for which kind of diseases, using
the BERT framework is a valid approach to detect the context within trajectories. The model
learned the context, for disease which are structured and with a sufﬁcient number of patients.
Patterns and associations
HIV resulting in other conditions: Among the diseases in ﬁgure 21, HIV resulting in other
conditions (b23) stands out, as this disease is from the chapter that on average performed lowest.
The majority of patients diagnosed with both HIV resulting in other conditions (b23) and HIV
disease leading to other infectious and parasitic diseases (b20) were correctly predicted. These two
diagnoses are closely related as b20 can be thought of as a subcategory of b23 and therefore are
expected to co-occur often. The close relation between diseases could also explain that unspeciﬁed
HIV and AIDS (b24) was a good predictor for HIV resulting in other conditions (b23). Hence, it
makes sense that the model is able to predict b23 even though it is from the infection chapter as
b23 diagnosis is often given in a context of very similar codes b20 and b24. Nevertheless, sexually
transmitted diseases (a63), were equally distributed between patients that the model was able and
unable to predict with a diagnosis of HIV resulting in other conditions (b23). This implies that the
model does not weigh this diagnosis as an important predictor for HIV resulting in other conditions
50
==PAGE:60==
(b23), despite it being a predominantly sexually transmitted disease [80].
The TWP predictions were excluded when examining the co-occurring diagnoses in trajectories
with the diseases from the literature. The reason for this was the high number of diagnoses in these
trajectories, where many diagnoses unrelated to the investigated disease might be frequent.
COPD: Pneumonia (j18) often co-occurred in trajectories with COPD, which coheres with the
fact that COPD increases the risk of severe pneumonia, for which hospitalization is required [81].
Conversely, pneumonia can also increase the risk of COPD [82]. Pneumonia (j18), and senile
cataracts (h25), occurred frequently both in trajectories in which the model was able and unable
to predict COPD. The high frequency of senile cataracts (h25), can be explained, as both senile
cataracts and COPD are often diagnosed at an old age [20]. Diseases that are known to be as-
sociated with a COPD diagnosis, respiratory failure (j96), chronic bronchitis (j42), asthma (j45)
[83][84][85], were exclusively present in the TP trajectories. This indicates that the model learned
the association between COPD and these diagnoses.
Alzheimer’s disease: The diseases the model learned to associate with Alzheimer’s disease were
disorientation (r41) and dementia in Alzheimer’s disease (f00) which are established associations
[86][87]. However, unspeciﬁed dementia (f03) was only part of the FN patients trajectories. Thus,
if the patient was diagnosed with unspeciﬁed dementia (f03) the model would not predict the pa-
tient to be diagnosed with Alzheimer’s diseases. This was also one of the diseases that Alzheimer’s
diseases was most frequently predicted as, which implies that patients with unspeciﬁed dementia
(f03) and Alzheimer’s disease have similar disease trajectories. Another study have also found,
these patients to have similar patterns of diseases [88].
Overall: The patterns that the model learned are likely complex combinations of several diseases.
Combinations of diseases that yield a high F1-score are not examined due to the number of possible
combinations. Investigating the similarity of trajectories, in which the model predicted a diagnosis
correctly and falsely, might be a fairer comparison than the naive approach of frequency count.
Nevertheless, the conducted analysis did provide useful information about the learned disease as-
sociations.
Similar diseases
The patients diagnosed with one of the ischemic heart diseases from literature, are very similar in
terms of other frequently assigned diagnoses. Furthermore, the physiology of some of the ischemic
heart disease are very similar. If the myocardial infarction is acute it is coded as i21, if it is old it
is classiﬁed as chronic and coded as i25 [13]. Thus, the same disease is coded with two different
ICD-10 codes. To accommodate this, three approaches were considered; merging similar diseases
into one ICD-10 code, training the model on block level, and training the model on a higher level
of ICD-10 including more speciﬁcation. Merging similar diseases would introduce bias, as only
the diseases from literature would be merged and not all diseases. Training on diseases at block
level would be less biased, but this approach might not provide enough information for the model
to make predictions. Including more characters of the ICD-10 would reduce the number of patients
assigned to each disease. Therefore, neither of these approaches were applied in the study. They
51
==PAGE:61==
would, however, be relevant to consider for further work.
Time from diagnosis till death
In general, patients with a TP or TWP predicted diagnosis, seemed to live longer than patients
that were predicted as FN. Patients with a falsely predicted diagnosis might be misdiagnosed,
or difﬁcult to determine the true diagnosis of. Patients who are misdiagnosed might not receive
the appropriate treatment for the true underlying disease, which could explain their earlier death.
When the diagnosis is difﬁcult for the doctor to determine, the diagnosis might be assigned later
than usual. As a result, the time between diagnosis and death is shorter. A survival analysis would
uncover if the correctly predicted patients in fact live longer than false predicted patients [89]. This
could indicate that they receive the correct diagnosis and thereby the adequate treatment to prolong
their lives.
Young COPD patients
The patients diagnosed with COPD were diagnosed at a wide range of ages. When the patient
was diagnosed with COPD as a child, the model was not able to predict the diagnosis as COPD.
The majority of patients assigned a COPD diagnosis as a child have the sub-diagnosis “chronic
asthmatic bronchitis” (j44.8b) [33]. A previous study have stated that the trajectories for children
diagnosed with j44.8b are dissimilar to typical COPD trajectories [33]. This explains that the
model is not able to predict the COPD diagnosis amongst patients diagnosed early, as the learned
pattern does not align with these patients.
TWP and number of diagnoses
The median number of diagnoses were higher for TWP than TP and FP predicted patients for all
the diseases from literature, except Alzheimer’s disease. This could simply be explained by the
fact that longer trajectories have more positions for the model to insert or replace the disease codes
at than in shorter trajectories. Therefore, the probability of predicting the disease code at the wrong
place is greater for long trajectories.
Estimates of mis- and underdiagnosis
Misdiagnosis: The predictions labeled FN, are diagnoses reported in DNPR, which the mod-
els does not predict correctly and can therefore be interpreted as the model’s suggestion of a
misdiagnosis. Previous studies have estimated COPD to be misdiagnosed in 5-62% of all cases
[40][41][42][43]. The model prediction insinuates that a greater proportion of the COPD diagnoses
in DNPR are misdiagnosed, namely 78%. However, this estimate should rather be interpreted as
the number of patients at risk of a misdiagnosis as their trajectory differ from the usual patient diag-
nosed with COPD. Therefore, the group of patients classiﬁed as FN, might be considered a patient
group relevant for screening. The proportion of misdiagnosed patients is probably overestimated
as the predictions are classiﬁed as FN, when a diagnosis is not predicted at the exact position as in
DNPR. Moreover, the position of a COPD diagnosis in DNPR in relation to the other diagnoses in
52
==PAGE:62==
the trajectory might be inconsistent from patient to patient. Although diagnoses can have a physio-
logical order, as some diseases are risk factors for COPD and some are sequelae, these might have
been reported in another order in DNPR. One scenario could be that the patient is diagnosed with
COPD at the general practitioner and then hospitalized with sequelae of COPD, and the diagnosis
recorded in the registry will be the sequelae. Later the underlying reason for the hospitalization,
COPD, might be noted in the registry. In addition, patients diagnosed at their general practitioner
will only be present in the registry if hospitalized, therefore the diagnosis might not be registered
in DNPR or registered later than it was actually diagnosed.
In general all diseases from literature were estimated to be frequently misdiagnosed (j44: 78% g30:
95%, i20: 75%, i21: 78%, i25: 59%). In addition to the explanations given for COPD, the high
estimates might also be explained by the diseases being interchangeable with other diagnoses, such
as Alzheimer’s disease and Alzheimer’s in dementia, or Angina pectoris and chronic ischemic heart
disease. However, a prediction of Alzheimer’s disease as Alzheimer’s in dementia is considered a
FN prediction.
Part of the FN predicted COPD patients, were predicted to have lung cancer, in which case the
model insinuates that these patients are misdiagnosed with COPD and underdiagnosed with lung
cancer. These patients were more similar to lung cancer patients than COPD patients in terms
of number of diagnoses and age of time at diagnosis. This implies that the model is able to
learn the overall context of the lung cancer and COPD trajectories and distinguish them from
each other.
Underdiagnosis: The number of underdiagnosed COPD is estimated in other studies to lie within
the range 35% to 80%. [5][19][20]. The TWP prediction are considered TP, as this study deﬁne
underdiagnosis as the failure of diagnosing a disease within a record time of the patient. The model
estimates 64% to be underdiagnosed with COPD. This estimate lies within estimates previously
established. However, the percentage of underdiagnosis is overestimated. The model predicts
the COPD diagnosis based on prior and subsequent diagnoses. Therefore, the patients, which the
model predicted to have COPD, but COPD was not observed in DNPR (FP), could both be patients
who are at risk of developing COPD later in life, but also underdiagnosed patients. Given that the
model only predicts based on diseases prior to the prediction of COPD, the patients are at risk of
developing COPD and could be invited for screening. If model prediction of COPD is based on
the patients’ subsequent diseases these patients might be underdiagnosed, and could be a patient
group to screen.
The model’s estimates of underdiagnosed Alzheimer’s disease and the three types of ischemic
heart diseases (g30: 84%, i20: 62%, i21: 69%, i25: 54%) were higher than estimates based on
medical record reviews. Previous studies have estimated Alzheimer’s disease to be underdiagnosed
in 4% to 75% [5] of all cases and ischemic heart diseases to be underdiagnosed in 34-50% of cases
[5][20]. The same explanation as for COPD can be used to explain the models overestimation for
these diseases.

Evaluate by comparing with Med-BERT
A comparison with the similar Med-BERT model could have been conducted to further analyze
the pre-trained model’s performance. This can be done by ﬁne-tuning both models on the same
downstream task. This was not possible for this study since the pre-trained Med-BERT model
is only available upon request and with permission, which might be a long process [7]. Another
method would be to ﬁne-tune the pre-trained model from this study with one of the downstream
ﬁne-tuning tasks used in the Med-BERT study. The comparison would not be completely fair due
to the differences in the datasets and vocabulary. However, it would provide an extremely rough
estimate of how the pre-trained models perform compared to one another. This was not done for
this study, due to the limited time.
5.4
Fine-tuned model
Fine-tuning performance
Other studies relying on the BERT framework, evaluate their pre-trained models’ potential by
comparing their performances when being applied for different downstream tasks [71]. Thereby,
the performance of the pre-trained model itself is not evaluated, but instead its ability to transfer
its learning to more speciﬁc downstream tasks. This study only applies the ﬁne-tuning task on the
pre-trained model yielding the best performance. This is not ideal, since a different pre-trained
model may have a better understanding of COPD, than the chosen.
For this study, the ﬁne-tuning task was used as part of evaluating the selected pre-trained model.
This was done by comparing the results when ﬁne-tuning the pre-trained model and a random
initialized model as a baseline. In general, both the pre-trained and random initialized model
performed well. The pre-trained model was expected to perform much better than the random
initialized model due to the pre-trained model’s prior knowledge. However, this was not the case.
The similar performances could be explained by the different distribution within characteristics
for the COPD and non-COPD sample. The method used to sample the non-COPD class was not
completely random. A completely random sampling would likely result in the majority of the
selected samples being healthier with fewer diagnoses than the average COPD patient. The reason
behind this was that the overall data set has a lower median number of diagnoses of 11 than the
COPD patient which has a median of 13. In general, COPD patients are sicker than the wider
population, assuming that more diagnosis equals more sickness. Therefore, the sampling of the
non-COPD class is conducted with respect to the distribution of the number of diagnoses of the
COPD class. However, even though the uneven distribution of trajectory length is encountered
for, other distributions such as diseases often related to older patients might affect the model’s
performance. COPD patients have a median age of 70 when diagnosed, therefore it can be assumed
that these patients in general have other diagnoses related to older age. Non-COPD patients are
generally younger, and might not be diagnosed with diseases relating to old age. This enables the
model to easily distinguish between the two classes. This gives the model an unfair advantage
when predicting COPD cases, which might explain the high and similar performance between the
pre-trained and random initialized model. Another explanation of the high performance of both
54
==PAGE:64==
models, could be the simplicity of the classiﬁcation task. One method to further understand why
both models are performing well, and which part of the disease trajectories the ﬁne-tuned model
mostly base the predictions on, would be by comparing the performance of two models. One model
trained on a dataset consisting only of the diagnoses that appear prior to COPD in the trajectories
and another on the subsequent diagnoses. Given that one of the models outperformed the other, a
deeper understanding of what the model actually learns would be obtained.
Fine-tuning estimates of mis- and underdiagnosis
The trajectories labeled as COPD cases, which were predicted as non-COPD cases (FN) can be
assumed to be misdiagnosed based on the ﬁne-tuning, whereas trajectories labeled as non-COPD
cases that were predicted as COPD cases (FP) can be assumed to be underdiagnosed. The per-
centage of patients the ﬁne-tuned model proposes to be misdiagnosed is 23.9%, this lies within the
estimated interval of 5-62% from literature [40][41][42][43]. Furthermore, the model predicted
that 18.6% of COPD cases to be underdiagnosed. This is lower than what other studies estimate,
which ranges from 35% to 80% [5][22][19][23][24][25][26][27][28]. The estimated range from
other studies of under- and misdiagnosis is wide, thus making it difﬁcult to fully evaluate the
model’s performance only based on this. Therefore, it would be necessary to further analyze and
verify the trajectories the model deﬁnes as faulty diagnoses. However, this was not done due to
this study’s limitations and time constraints.
The ﬁne-tuned model’s estimate of underdiagnosis can not be compared with the estimate found
when pre-training, since the calculation of underdiagnosis is based on the number of COPD pre-
dictions. The ﬁne-tuned model only evaluates the COPD patiens and a corresponding non-COPD
group, whereas the pre-trained model evaluates on the whole population available. In contrast,
the estimates of misdiagnosis can be compared, since this calculation is based on the number of
observed COPD patients in the registry. The number of observed COPD patients used when pre-
training is the exact same as when ﬁne-tuning. The ﬁne-tuned model estimates of COPD misdiag-
nosis were lower than the estimates obtained when pre-training. As the pre-trained model focuses
on all diseases and ﬁne-tuning is restricted to COPD patients, the estimate from ﬁne-tuning were
expected to be more correct. Moreover, the estimate of misdiagnosis from the ﬁne-tuned model
are more similar to other studies than the estimates by the pre-trained model.

5.5
Strengths and limitations
Strengths
One strength of the study is the data used for training and testing the proposed model. DNPR
is comprehensive in terms of the number of patients and information included in the registry.
Moreover, the information can be linked to other registries, as DNPR contains CPR-numbers.
Lastly, DNPR is in general considered to be of good quality [8].
Another strength is the novel concept of systematically detecting faulty diagnoses, as well as sug-
gesting an approach to perform the detection. The approach relies on the well-recognized language
model BERT, but applies it in the context of predicting diseases based on trajectories. Furthermore,
the proposed model not only predicts if the disease is wrongly assigned, but also suggests another
diagnosis that ‘should’ have been assigned. This is advantageous for the purpose of identifying
faulty diagnoses. In addition it enables the model to be applied in numerous downstream tasks
regarding diagnoses.
Limitations
This study has several limitations. Although the data is considered a strength, there are inconsisten-
cies and missing information, such as diagnoses assigned by the general practitioner. Moreover,
the faulty diagnoses could not be veriﬁed, limiting the evaluation of the model’s performance.
Directly comparing the model performance with other studies was not feasible, as the proposed
approach of detecting faulty diagnosis has not been attempted before. Other types of veriﬁcation
were not possible due to the limited data and time. The greatest limitation was the deﬁnite time
frame, which constrained possible optimization of the model and explorations of the results.
5.6
Utility
The proposed model has various opportunities that can be exploited. The pre-trained model can
identify commonly misdiagnosed diseases, which should be diagnosed with caution. In addi-
tion the model can provide information about patterns of diseases within patients who are mis-
diagnosed. The model can also detect associations between diseases that are not yet discovered.
Thereby identifying risk factors, sequelae or more complex interaction between diseases. Identi-
fying patient groups who should be screened, either because they could be at risk of developing a
disease or being under- or misdiagnosed is also a possible application of the model. On an individ-
ual level, the model is able to identify a possible false diagnosis, which can be utilized to correct
and improve the diagnoses in DNPR, for research purposes. Lastly, the pre-trained model can be
applied to numerous downstream tasks, within the clinical domain.

5.7
Future perspectives
There are several perspectives of this study that could be implemented and improved. Tuning
hyper-parameters might improve the performance and would be valuable to explore. In addition,
the pre-trained model evaluation could also be improved by being less restrictive about the posi-
tioning of the disease. One way of implementing this could be by incorporating a loss function
that was weighted with regard to the distance of diagnosis. Such that, the TWP predictions would
be evaluated in terms of how far the predicted disease’s position was from the true position of that
disease.
This study adapted the perspective of examining which types of diseases the model was able to
learn. However, speciﬁc types of patients or disease trajectories could also explain that the model
was able or unable to predict the diseases. To investigate this, all diagnoses in the disease trajec-
tories would, at turn, be masked and predicted. Thereby the patients with few correctly predicted
diagnoses and patients with many correctly predicted diagnoses could be compared. Furthermore,
additional data could be included in the study. Clinical data of operation- and procedure codes are
available in DNPR; this information could be applied both as additional input variables and as a
kind of validation. If a clinical test has been conducted the diagnosis is more likely correct.

6
Conclusion
This study aimed to provide a novel systematic approach for identifying faulty diagnoses within
the Danish national patient registry by using the BERT framework. The proposed model was pre-
trained with the task of predicting a disease based on the other diseases in the trajectories. The
diseases that the model was able to predict were thereby assigned in a common context, whereas
the diseases that the model was unable to predict appeared in an uncommon context and could
therefore be interpreted as a faulty diagnosis. Hence, the proposed model can be applied as a sys-
tematic approach for discovering faulty diagnoses within DNPR.
The model was able to predict the diagnoses in the pregnancy chapter, and unable to predict the di-
agnoses within the infection chapter. This is coherent with the expectations as pregnancy requires
hospitalization, is well documented, and occurs structured in the registry. In contrast, infections
often does not require hospitalization and are expected to be unstructured in terms of related diag-
noses. This validates that the model learned the context of the diseases, which are used to detect
unusual diagnoses and thereby identify under- and misdiagnoses. The pre-trained model estimated
underdiagnosed cases of the investigated diseases (j44: 64%, g30: 84%, i20: 62%, i21: 69%, i25:
54%). All of these estimates were higher than those of other studies. The model also estimated
misdiagnoses at a high rate (j44: 78% g30: 95%, i20: 75%, i21: 78%, i25: 59% ). The patients the
model suggested to be misdiagnosed had a tendency to have less years between diagnosis and death
than the patients diagnosed correctly. Furthermore, the COPD patients the model predicts as lung
cancer, have more similar characteristics to other lung cancer patienter than COPD patients. When
ﬁne-tuning the model on the downstream classiﬁcation task of detecting COPD trajectories, the
number of underdiagnosed was 18.6%, which was lower than the number found in other studies.
The number of misdiagnosed was 23.9%, and within the range found in other studies. However,
it was not possible to validate whether the faulty diagnosis is truly a faulty diagnosis, which made
it difﬁcult to fully evaluate the model. Therefore, the estimates should only be interpreted as the
number of patients at risk of a misdiagnosis or underdiagnosis as their trajectory is different from
the usual patient diagnosed.
Even though the proposed model showed potential in discovering patterns in disease trajectories
and thereby also identifying trajectories where diseases occurred in unusual patterns, further work
is needed to exploit the model’s full potential.
