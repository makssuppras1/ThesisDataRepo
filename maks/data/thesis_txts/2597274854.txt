Summary
This project investigates the utilisation of generative adversarial networks in
order to augment image training data to increase the accuracy of segmentation
networks. A signiﬁcant problem in many diﬀerent domains is the amount of
annotated data for the training process of segmentation networks. It is shown
that image segmentation networks generalise better if more labeled image data
is provided. Human-labeled datasets are costly because the labeling process is
very time-intensive and need expertise. Therefore, segmentation datasets are
usually small, and state-of-the-art networks are hard to train. Furthermore, im-
age datasets in diﬀerent medical areas often suﬀer from a severe class imbalance
which hinders image segmentation network from generalising for speciﬁc classes.
In order to achieve the required performance in segmentation networks even with
small amounts of data, state-of-the-art solutions either use a model pre-trained
with another data set or attempt to augment the dataset artiﬁcially with classi-
cal image manipulations. Both solutions show limitations in their applicability
and also in their quality when used within across datasets. This thesis aims
to overcome this problem by using generative adversarial networks to augment
image datasets artiﬁcially.
For this purpose, two generative adversarial networks (GANs) have been imple-
mented. One GAN performs image generation in order to create realistic label
maps and the other performs image-to-image translation in order to generate
realistic images to the synthetic label maps, which has a similar style compared
to the original images. For both generative models, several modiﬁcations were
implemented and compared, in order to either increase the individual image
quality, stabilise the training or increase the mode variation within the gener-
ated data. The modiﬁcations for the label map generation task include changes
in the architecture, such as self-attention layer (SAGAN), history buﬀering and
sample packing (PACGAN). The modiﬁcations also include changes in the loss
==PAGE:4==
ii
function, such as Wasserstein GAN with gradient penalty (WGAN-GP). The
modiﬁcations in the image-to-image translation task include changes in the
discriminator architecture, such as Patch-GAN discriminator and Multi-Scale-
Discriminator and generator architecture, such as replacing the U-Net generator
with a ResUnet-a or with a Multi-Scale-Generator, as well as diﬀerent loss func-
tions.
The diﬀerent frameworks and modiﬁcations were tested using the image dataset
provided by the University of Warwick within the "The GlaS Challenge Con-
test".
It is shown that the addition of GAN-generated images does not improve the
segmentation accuracy, but decreases it slightly, even if the image quality is
very close to the original data. The generated images contain small artefacts,
noise and incomplete objects which hinder the segmentation performance from
increasing. When looking at the problem in an isolated way, the results indicate
that performance could be increased with improved image quality. Especially
the inﬂuence of the label map’s quality is severe.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In digital image processing, image segmentation describes the process of assign-
ing content-related regions to an associated class. A distinction is made between
semantic segmentation, where all connected regions are assigned to the corre-
sponding class, and instance segmentation, where diﬀerent instances within the
class are also identiﬁed. The outputs of both approaches are objects with precise
object boundaries. In diﬀerent ﬁelds, the task of segmentation is highly rele-
vant. In the medical ﬁeld, the identiﬁcation and localisation of objects, such as
cells, vessels or anomalies, is essential in order to make decisions about therapy.
The same applies in the ﬁeld of autonomous driving, where the goal is to get a
precise scene understanding in order to make decisions on driving behaviour.
A signiﬁcant problem is the amount of annotated data for the training process
of segmentation networks. Human-labeled datasets are costly because the label-
ing process is very time-intensive and need expertise. Therefore, segmentation
datasets are usually small, and state-of-the-art networks are hard to train. Fur-
thermore, image datasets in the medical area often suﬀer from a severe class
imbalance which hinders image segmentation network from generalising for spe-
ciﬁc classes.
In order to achieve the required segmentation performance even with small
amounts of data, state-of-the-art solutions either use a model pre-trained with
another data set or attempt to augment the dataset artiﬁcially with classical
image manipulations (see Figure 1.1. Both solutions show limitations in their
applicability and also in their quality when used on diﬀerent datasets.
==PAGE:18==
2
Introduction
In order to solve the problem of small datasets, this project aims to investigate
the utilisation of generative adversarial networks to generate artiﬁcial data. In
particular, we want to investigate whether it is possible to use generated data to
augment an existing medical dataset to increase the accuracy of segmentation
networks. This approach is divided into two parts, in which synthetic label maps
and afterwards matching synthetic photo-realistic images are to be generated.
The generation of synthetic label maps is realised with generative adversarial
networks. The generation of corresponding realistic images is realised with the
help of conditional generative adversarial networks.

1.1
Related work
The related work area is divided into three parts. First, an overview of image
segmentation networks is presented. Then currently available data augmenta-
tion options and generative models in relation to synthetic image generation are
presented.
1.1.1
Image segmentation networks
In digital image processing, image segmentation describes the process of assign-
ing content-related regions to an associated class. A distinction is made between
semantic segmentation, where all connected regions are assigned to the corre-
sponding class, and instance segmentation, where diﬀerent instances within the
class are also identiﬁed.
==PAGE:19==
1.1 Related work
3
The traditional methods of digital image segmentation are based on individual
pixel information such as colours, colour intensities and pixel positions. The
interpretation of a global picture context, which a human observer is able to
perceive, is not possible with this traditional method. A more modern approach
for the segmentation task are neural networks, which usually use a convolutional
network architectures in image processing. For segmentation tasks, image seg-
mentation networks are used in image analysis for pixel-wise classiﬁcation.
Image segmentation networks are used in various areas. They can be used any-
where where objects on an image need to be located very accurately.
They
are used to interpret scenes and can help to quantify an image and its content
[LSD]. If an image can be segmented with suﬃcient accuracy, applications and
methods for further image analysis such as counting, sizing or locating can be
built upon it. A large ﬁeld of application lays in autonomous mobile robotics,
which also includes the autonomous driving of cars. There, the moving vehicle
must be able to locate and classify traﬃc objects and thus obtain an under-
standing of the scene. Another large ﬁeld of application is the medical sector.
In this sector, there are a large number of tasks in which pixel-wise analysis
of images is necessary. Examples include cell analyses or the analysis of MRT
or CT scans. It can help to analyse large amounts of data for which generally
experts are needed, within a short time frame.
Image segmentation networks show problems in the generalisation when seg-
menting outside of their training dataset. This eﬀect, also called over-ﬁtting,
can have various reasons. Firstly, there may be too little training data available
in general. Another reason may be that there is a lack of invariance within the
training dataset.
1.1.1.1
Convolutional neural networks for semantic segmentation
Image segmentation based on convolutional neural networks (CNN) is currently
roughly divided into three categories. The encoder-decoder neural networks, di-
lated/ atrous convolutional neural networks and image pyramid neural networks
[XHLL20].
In encoder-decoder networks, the spatial dimensions are gradually reduced with
pooling layer in the encoder part and then recovered to the original image size
with the up-sampling layer in the decoder part. Initial approaches only use one
layer in the decoder part to up-sample to the original size. Long et al. [LSD]
show that a fully convolutional network (FCN) can make accurate predictions
for per-pixel tasks, such as semantic segmentation. Here, fully convolutional
classiﬁcation networks are used as a starting point, and instead of making a
prediction for the whole image, the last layer is up-sampled to the original size
of the output image. This provides a prediction for each learned class as well
as for the background. The results are generally still very rough, depending
==PAGE:20==
4
Introduction
on the number of down samplings within the network. Newer approaches re-
place the single up-sampling layer by several up-sampling steps. The networks
usually contain an encoder-decoder structure. The encoder is used obtain an
eﬃcient feature representation of the input and the decoder is utilised to make
a decision by processing the feature representation. Two common architecture
within this category are the U-Net [RFB15] and SegNet [BKC16]. The SegNet
transfers pooling indices from the down-sampling operations in the encoder to
up-sampling operations in the decoder. The U-Net transfers the whole feature
map from diﬀerent scales from the encoder to the decoder side, in order to pro-
vide detailed information for the reconstruction in the decoder. Both approaches
enable a much more accurate image segmentation than a basic encoder-decoder
structure.
Dilated/atrous convolution neural networks, such as Deeplab [CPK+16], lever-
age more spatial information for the segmentation than the FCN, by using the
atrous spatial pyramid pooling (ASPP) model and the fully connected condi-
tional random ﬁeld (CRF).
Image pyramid neural networks, such as PSPNet [ZSQ+16], use diﬀerent pool-
ing proportions to obtain features with diﬀerent scales.
Furthermore there are many other improvements through the use of e.g. resid-
uals [ZWZ+20, HZRS15, DWCW20] or attention [ZWZ+20, FLTL].
1.1.1.2
One-shot-learning
Shaban et al. [SBL+17] try to perform a pixel-wise classiﬁcation for a new class
with only one image and one CNN, which is pre-trained on a small dataset.
The implementation consists of two branches. The ﬁrst branch "Conditioning"
receives the image-label pairs and produces a set of parameters for the logistic
regression layer using a VGG [SZ15]. The segmentation branch is an CNN that
receives a query image as input and outputs strided features of a convolutional
map.
The predicted map is generated by classifying the pixel-level features
through the output of the ﬁrst and the second branch, which is then up-sampled
to the original size [SBL+17].
1.1.1.3
Semi-supervised segmentation networks
Hung et al. [HTL+18] propose a method for semi-supervised semantic segmen-
tation using an adversarial network. They model a discriminator that is able to
segment images without knowing the corresponding ground truth. Mondal et al.
[MADD19] slightly improves the approach in regards to cycle consistency based
on the Cycle-GAN introduced by Zhu et al. [ZPIE18]. Compared to the U-Net

and SegNet, this approach can be used with a minimal amount to training data,
where the accuracy of the data drops only very slightly.
1.1.2
Data augmentation
A key factor for the generalisation of image segmentation networks is the number
of existing training images and their corresponding ground truth segmentation
maps. Since the annotation process is time-consuming and costly or requires
certain expertise [ZBD+19], in many cases, not enough images are available for
the training. Furthermore, in the medical ﬁeld, many datasets suﬀer from severe
class imbalance due to the rare nature of some pathologies [KBK+19]. For this
reason, it is necessary to augment the existing dataset in order to generalise
models. This method, described in the literature as data augmentation, can be
performed in various ways.
1.1.2.1
Classical data augmentation
The classical approach to enlarging the existing dataset is to modify the images
with, e.g.
rotation, cropping, change of colours/intensities, distortion, verti-
cal/horizontal ﬂip. The goal of data augmentation is to teach the model invari-
ances in the data domain [CZM+19]. The images are changed in such a way
that they are new for the network but at the same time can still be assigned to
its original class. Images containing digits or letters demonstrate the limitation.
Images with the numbers 0, 3, 8 can be horizontal ﬂipped and keep their mean-
ing. All other numbers, however, change or lose their meaning for the human
observer (see Figure 1.2). The advantages of classical data augmentation are
Figure 1.2: Example of classical data augmentation’s limitation.
Red sur-
rounded digits lose their meaning and green surrounded digits keep
their meaning
that the methods are relatively easy to apply and are not based on a model that
needs to be trained. Therefore, the application of classical data augmentation
results in low time-eﬀort. The correct use of classical data augmentation usually

leads to a signiﬁcant increase in accuracy and less over-ﬁtting in the training
process. The disadvantage is that despite the random selection of parameters,

1.1.2.2
AutoAugment and Neural Augmentation
AutoAugment contributed by Cubuk et al. [CZM+19] uses the various meth-
ods of classical data augmentation but tries to ﬁnd the optimal settings for the
methods based on the target dataset using a search algorithm. The search al-
gorithm that is used in the paper uses reinforcement learning, and consists of
two main components: a controller (RNN) and a training algorithm (Proximal
Policy Optimisation algorithm). There are typically 30 steps of the training al-
gorithm in which the controller generates a decision that leads to optimal data
augmentation settings for a given network and dataset. A comparable approach
has been performed by Perez et al. [PW17], which also utilise classical data
augmentation. Instead of using a reinforcement learning, they use an additional
classiﬁer which provides feedback for diﬀerent data augmentation types during
training.
With the help of AutoAugment, the parameters for data augmentation are au-
tomatically found and adjusted according to a target dataset. This property
solves the problem of classical data augmentation, namely that it is relatively
rigid.
A problem would be the time required for a model that needs to be trained. An
RNN/CNN is positioned in front of the segmentation network, and it is trained
multiple times until optimal settings are found, which is very costly.

1.1.2.4
Synthetic simulated environments
Many approaches in robotics use synthetic images created using Computer Gen-

to make them usable for image classiﬁcation or segmentation, two diﬀerent ways

1.1.3
Generative models
Generative models try to learn a data distribution either explicitly or implicitly
and are then able to generate samples from the learned model. The quality
of the samples strongly depends on the chosen approach. In order to provide
segmentation networks with enough images to generalise for a speciﬁc class,
besides classical data augmentation, completely new realistic-looking images and
corresponding label maps can be created using generative models [KBK+19].
In the following the most common approaches used for image generation and
translation applications are presented.
1.1.3.1
Variational autoencoder
Variational autoencoder (VAE) aim to learn a probability density function and
restrict it to a multivariate normal distribution. The encoder part of the VAE
predicts reasonable distribution parameter, and the decoder network maps en-
codings from the interpretable to a hidden distribution and ﬁnally, to the image
space [Han19].
The training of VAE is generally stable.
A disadvantage is
that images generated by VAE often have a low image quality, as it is limited
in spatial sharpness [Han19]. Pearlstein et al. [CEP+19] used VAE generated
images to augment the training data for classiﬁcation tasks and increased the
performance for the model.
1.1.3.2
Generative adversarial networks
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) are a framework to learn models that
are able to generate natural images by learning from an adversarial game (min-
==PAGE:24==
8
Introduction
imax game) between a generator and a discriminator. The generator tries to
transform a noise vector into a fake sample, which matches the distribution of
real images. The discriminator gets to see both the real and the fake images and
has to distinguish between both. The training is completed, when the genera-
tor can fool the discriminator [GPAM+14]. The most frequent applications of
GANs in literature are in the ﬁeld of image generation, image-to-image transla-
tion, super-resolution tasks or semantic segmentation and are described in the
following.
GANs for image generation
The basic GAN model, introduced by Goodfel-
low et al. [GPAM+14], tries to map a latent random variable drawn from a prior
distribution to generate data which is supposed to follow the target distribution.
In other words, images are generated from random variables based on a training
dataset. An extension of this is a deep convolutional GAN (DCGAN) [RMC16],
which used only convolutional layer in their architecture. Further extensions
use diﬀerent loss function such as the Wasserstein distance [ACB17, WZW+19],
or utilise spatial awareness for the generative models [ZGMO19].
Most ap-
proaches contribute to solving the three most common problems encountered
during GAN training: training instability, mode collapse and the evaluation of
the model. Mode collapse describes a phenomenon where GANs map all in-
put distributions to very similar synthetic samples covering only a single mode
of the data distribution or missing a subset of the existing modes. The most
relevant solutions for the mode collapse are contributed by Goodfellow et al.
[Goo17] and Lin et al.
[LKFO18] which show the discriminator in diﬀerent
ways several images at once and force the discriminator to see a higher variance
within the data. Qin et al. [QMW19] try to stabilise the training procedure
with the scaling of the domain to decouple the eﬀect of Lipschitz regularisation
on the discriminator network and the loss function. Heusel et al. [HRU+18]
using diﬀerent learning rates for the discriminator and generator to achieve a
convergence of the training – Two time-scale update rule(TTUR).
GANs for image-to-image translation
Image-to-image translation aims
to learn a unidirectional mapping from a source domain to a target domain
[IZZE18]. Costa et al. [CGM+17] try to create new images of retinal images
and corresponding vessel tree segmentation masks by training a conditional
GAN to create alternative images for existing image/ label map pairs. Based
on this approach, the segmentation network has image/ label map pairs where
the label map occurs several times in the training dataset, and the images are
individual. Image-2-Image translation can help to increase the performance of
segmentation networks by creating new realistic images based on a mask, for
example. Therefore more training images are available, which can lead to less

over-ﬁtting in the training process.
A problem could arise from the lack of
diversity in the masks, which could lead to a worse generalisation of the model.
Concatenation of image generation and image translation GANs
Two

nearly at the same time, propose a solution that might overcome this problem.
They use both a GAN and a conditional GAN to create image/mask pairs in

perform an image-to-image translation between the mask and the real image.
The newly created images can then be used to train an image segmentation

training masks and matching realistic images are generated for the training, and
there is no bottleneck in either the masks or the realistic images. A disadvantage
of this approach could be that two models have to be trained for an improvement,
which is very time-consuming and also challenging to train.
Cycle-GAN
The Cycle GAN approach tries to allow an image-2-image trans-

is the domain transfer between a horse and a zebra. It should be possible to
make a horse look like a zebra and vice versa. This approach has the advantage
that no paired images are needed to train the model. Instead, only images of
both domains are needed. Similar approaches for unsupervised learning have

quantitatively. Zhang et al. [ZPL19] introduces a harmonic function to enforce
consistent mappings during the translation between two domain to reduce the
number of artefacts and corrects major failures in multiple domains such as in
medical imaging.

2.1.1
Convolutional layer
A CNN is composed of convolutional layers that perform convolutional oper-
ations. These convolutional operations perform an element-wise matrix mul-
tiplication between a kernel (ﬁlter) and the pixel area corresponding to the
kernel size. The kernel converts the two-dimensional input matrix into a two-
dimensional output matrix. This output matrix, also called feature map, con-
tains the weighted sums of the input matrix, where the weights represent the
values of the kernel. Once this process is completed, the kernel moves one po-
sition further and continues the process until all possible positions have been
reached.
Common operations are padding and striding (see Figure 2.1).
A
padding operation adds elements to the input at its edge. This allows the spa-
tial dimension of the input to be maintained without changing the meaning of
the input itself. With striding, individual positions are skipped while the kernel
is sliced. This allows the spatial dimensions of the input to be reduced.
2.1.2

The calculated output of the element-wise matrix multiplication of the convolu-
tional operation would create a linear model. Since most of the real-world data
is non-linear, non-linearity is introduced into the model using activation func-

the activation function. Commonly used activation functions are the sigmoid

2.3
Generative Adversarial Networks
Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN) is a framework that allows estimating
a generative model using an adversarial process. In this process, two models are
trained – a generative model and a discriminative model. The generative model
G tries to capture a given data distribution. The discriminative model D tries
to determine if a sample is from the given data distribution or not [GPAM+14].
The training process is designed so that the generator tries to generate samples
that the discriminator classiﬁes to belong to the real data distribution.
At
the same time, the discriminator trains to correctly classify real and generated
images. A theoretical equilibrium is achieved when the discriminator can predict
with a 50% conﬁdence, whether an image created by the generator is fake or
real.

2.3.2
Problems of GAN training
The following are the three most common problems that can occur within GAN
training. The problems include training instability, generalisation (mode col-
lapse) and evaluation of GANs.
2.3.2.1
Training instability
GANs may exhibit signiﬁcant instability during training, usually due to the al-
ternating updates of generator and discriminator, which is visible in oscillating
values of the two network’s losses. According to Arjovsky et al. [ACB17], the
problem is caused by the fact that the real distribution and the learned dis-
tribution on lies on disjoints manifolds in a high dimensional space. For this
reason, the discriminator learns the correct distinction between real and gen-
erated samples. According to Lin et al. [LKFO18], this is the reason why the
==PAGE:37==
2.3 Generative Adversarial Networks
21
gradient of the discriminator loss function has a very strong inﬂuence compared
to the gradient of the generator loss function.
Currently, two ways are suggested in the literature to help stabilise the training
of GANs [LKFO18]. One way is to change the optimisation objective function. A
change is either made in the distance metric [ACB17, NCM+17, NCT16, NK18,
LCC+17, AGL+17] , because the original Jenson-Shannon divergence, which is
expressed by the Equation 2.1 if the discriminator is trained optimally, behaves
unreliably in regions without overlapping support, or is made by adding regular-
isation to the objective function [GAA+17, HRU+18, MNG18, MKKY18]. The
other way to stabilise the GAN training is to change the architecture, involv-
ing several heuristic tricks, such as minibatch discrimination/packing, reference
batch normalisation and feature mapping.
2.3.2.2
Evaluation problem
A fundamental problem of Generative models is that they are challenging to
evaluate. It is a matter of comparing the samples generated by the Generative
model and the original distribution and evaluating them for their quality and
diversity. Visual inspection is a standard method where externals evaluate the
quality of the images. This method is, of course, very complex and not easily re-
producible. The most common evaluation technique is the Inception Score (IS),
which describes how precisely a classiﬁer classiﬁes generated images. However,
according to Heusel et al. [HRU+18] the IS does not evaluate how syntheti-
cally generated samples look in comparison to real samples. For this reason,
the Frechet Inception Distance (FID) is often used to evaluate computer vision-
speciﬁc features of an input image.
2.3.2.3
Mode collapse
The term mode collapse generally describes a lack of divergence in the generated
samples. There are usually two diﬀerent categories of mode collapse [LKFO18].
1. Missing mode – The trained generator is missing one or more of the
modes when the training data is a multimodal distribution. In the example
of MNIST, a GAN suﬀering from mode collapse could not generate some
individual numbers at all.
2. Intra-mode-collapse – The trained generator is mapping two latent vec-
tors z, that are far apart from each other, to a very similar data sample.
==PAGE:38==
22
Fundamentals
Also, in the example of MNIST, the generator would map multiple dis-
tant latent vectors to the same number. Therefore, the generator would
provide samples that do not have a high variance in one speciﬁc sub-mode
(E.g. all sevens look very similar). Lin et al. [LKFO18] also describes this
as intra-mode-collapse.
Lin et al. [LKFO18] distinguishes the mode collapse from the phenomenon de-
scribed as mode augmentation. In this case, the generator will create samples
from a newly created mode. For MNIST, this could be a mixture of diﬀerent
numbers that have similarities to the images in the dataset but has no meaning
in terms of a number.
Currently, there are three ways described in the literature on how to overcome
mode collapse – (1) Joint Architectures, (2)Augmented Discriminators, (3) Op-
timisation based solutions
1. Joint Architectures – In order to mitigate mode collapse in encoder-
decoder architectures, latent representations are added in joint architec-
tures to the discriminator in addition to high dimensional representations
of each data point [KW19a, SVR+17, DKD17].
2. Augmented Discriminators – It is a technique to show the discrimina-
tor more information than one sample at a time, as it has been shown that
the discriminator makes incorrect predictions with only one unlabeled sam-
ple presented at the same time. There are diﬀerent techniques to augment
the discriminator to increase discriminative performance. In the work of
Che et al. [CLJ+17], it is shown that the discriminator achieves better
results with labelled data. Other methods, which are also successful for
unlabeled data, provide the discriminator with two or more samples at the
same time [LKFO18] or provide the discriminator with all the information
given in one minibatch [SGZ+16]
3. Optimisation – Another method to alleviate mode collapse is to improve
the optimisation algorithm. One method is to skip updating discrimina-
tors every n-th step compared to the generator, or to update the generator
and discriminator at diﬀerent times per epoch. In this way, optimisation
process can balance the two models to convergence and avoid to end up in
mode collapse. Another method is proposed by Metz et al. [MPPSD17],
where k gradient steps for updating are considered to see how the discrim-
inator reacts.

According to Vaswani et al.

signiﬁcant the value is. This attention module is inserted twice between the
existing convolutional layers. The two attention modules are inserted after the

the three feature spaces f (query), g (keys) and h (value). The resulting fea-

map. Then we matrix multiply the attention map with the value feature map h
and apply a 1x1 convolution again. The output is self-attention map, which is
inserted as a residual connection between the already mentioned convolutional
layer.
With the help of the attention layer, we achieve an attention-driven,
long-range dependency within the training of the generative model.

the discriminator. This method has shown to stabilise the training of adversarial
training [SPT+17].

2.4
Supervised Image-to-Image Translation
Image-to-image translation essentially is the task of learning a mapping between
an input image and an output image. This is used in various areas such as
style transfer [IZZE18] and photo enhancement/ upscaling [WLZ+18], and im-
age completion [ISSI17]. The focus within this thesis is on the use of conditional
generative adversarial networks with the task to synthesise realistic photos from
label maps. We try to obtain matching pairs of synthetic images and label maps
by generating the synthetic image counterpart to the label map. In the following,
three diﬀerent approaches based on conditional generative adversarial networks
(cGANs) are presented to solve the image-to-image translation task. The basic
approach is the pix2pix framework [IZZE18] with a U-net generator. This ap-
proach was then modiﬁed with respect to its generator architecture with a novel
architecture for image-to-image tasks, ResUnet-a (see Section 2.2.2). This ap-
proach combines the advantages of U-net and ResNet-a. Finally, the extension
of pix2pix, pix2pixHD [WLZ+18] was tested, where two diﬀerent generators are
responsible for either global structures or local details.
2.4.1
pix2pix
pix2pix is a GAN for image-to-image translation, based on a conditional GAN
(see Section 2.4.1.1).
The generator is trained to generate an image that is
conditional on the source image. The discriminator receives both the source
and the target image and evaluates whether the transformation from source to
target is plausible or not.
2.4.1.1
Conditional generative adversarial networks
In their original form, GAN models are not able to control their output. If
samples are to be generated, they may be plausible and could belong to the
underlying dataset, but the classes are generated randomly. Conditional GANs
(cGAN) can solve this problem. cGANs are an extension of the Generative Ad-
versarial Networks, in which the generator and the discriminator are conditioned
to a speciﬁc output using additional information (see Figure 2.12). This addi-
tional information can be of various types (e.g. class labels, one-hot-encoded
image labels or source images) [MO14].

2.4.1.3
U-Net generator
The generator in the pix2pix network is built according to an encoder-decoder
structure.
In this structure, the encoder samples an input down by passing
it through a series of convolutional layers until the bottleneck is reached. In
the decoder after the bottleneck, the process is reversed, and the image is up-
sampled to its original size. According to Isola et al. [IZZE18], many image-to-
image problems lack low-level details that get lost through the basic encoder-
decoder structure.
For this reason, skip connections like in the U-Net (see
Figure 2.3) are added to the network. The skip connections allow to pass the
entire output from the respective levels of the encoder to the decoder side and
concatenated with the input from the lower levels. By this shortcut passing of
low-level information, the network is able to generate much more detailed and
realistic images. In principle, it is possible to replace the generator within the
pix2pix framework by other segmentation networks such as the ResUnet-a (see
Section 2.2.2).

2.4.2.1
Coarse-to-ﬁne generator
The coarse-to-ﬁne generator is a multi-scale generator that consists of two gen-
erator networks. One is the global generator, that is responsible for the global
context. The other one is the local enhancer network that is responsible for local
details that achieve photorealism. The global generator and the local enhancer
network both consist of three building blocks a: a convolutional front-end, a set
of residual blocks and a transposed convolutional back-end. The main diﬀer-
ences between the two generators is that the operate in diﬀerent resolutions – the
global generator operates e.g in 1024x512 and the local enhancer in 2048x1024
– and the input to the residual blocks of the local enhancer is the element-wise
sum of two feature maps. One Feature map comes from the global generator
and one from the previous local enhancer residual block (see Figure 2.14). The
coarse-to-ﬁne generator that operates on multiple scales can utilise global as
well as local information. It enables higher quality images with clear object
boundaries and local details.

3.2.2
Hyper-parameter optimisation
In machine learning, hyper-parameters are parameters that are used to control
training algorithms. In principle, these values are set before the actual training.
Since the values have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the course of the training, it is
essential to determine the correct values for these parameters. The search for
the optimal values for the parameters is called hyper-parameter optimisation
==PAGE:55==
3.2 Experimental Setup
39
[YZ20]. There are diﬀerent procedures and strategies to choose the right pa-
rameters. Common strategies are manual search, random search, grid search
and Bayesian optimisation. Usually, the search is done by selecting the hyper-
parameters, then the algorithm is applied based on the hyper-parameters, and
ﬁnally, a performance metric is used to determine how good the learned model
is. Theoretically, this process is then repeated until the optimum is found. The
methods diﬀer mainly in how the hyper-parameters are chosen for the individual
test runs.
In the grid search, a set of hyper-parameters is deﬁned with a restriction to a
part of the room, and the values are performed in all combinations. This search
is very elaborate, and the higher the dimensionality of the parameters, the more
complex the search becomes [YZ20].
The random search is an improvement of the search algorithm compared to the
grid search. Instead of trying out all combinations, a random search is per-
formed by randomly selecting values within the given hyper-parameter space.
In contrast to the grid search, no discretisation of the space is required. Usu-
ally, random search outperforms grid search if only a small number of hyper-
parameters is searched [YZ20].
Bayesian optimisation is an optimisation method that creates a probabilistic
model of the function between hyper-parameter values and the chosen evalua-
tion metric. In this process, hyper-parameter combinations are iteratively tried
out which are judged to be the most promising according to the current model.
After each step, the model is adjusted again. This more targeted search for new
parameters has shown in practice that signiﬁcantly fewer tests are required than
with the grid or random search.[YZ20]
In general it is diﬃcult to determine the contribution of individual hyper-
parameters to the accuracy of obtained model quantitatively[YZ20]. Therefore,
manual optimisation is used to gain an intuition for the eﬀect of the diﬀerent
hyper-parameters. For the systematic search for "optimal" hyper-parameter,
Bayesian optimisation is used on a limited set of parameters. Due to limitations
of computational resources and the long training time of the GAN training, the
individual training runs are only partially carried out when using Bayesian op-
timisation. Thus, the range for the individual hyper-parameters can at least be
limited, even if not utterly determined.
3.2.3
Evaluation metrics and methods
Generative Adversarial Networks, as well as other Generative Models, are chal-
lenging to evaluate (see Section 2.3.2.2). Ideally, one would measure the dis-
tance between the distribution based on real data and the distribution learned
in training. Based on this distance measurement, the quality of the generative
==PAGE:56==
40
Materials and Methods
model could then be evaluated. However, this is a general problem, because
Generative Models do not generate samples from the real distribution, but only
from the learned one.
The real distribution is usually unknown.
Therefore,
no direct measurement between the distributions is possible. However, there
are several qualitative and quantitative Evaluation methods, which provide in-
sights into the models quality [Bor18]. In the following, the methods used for
the evaluation of the GAN models within this thesis are described. The selec-
tion is based on what is most commonly used in the current literature. The
qualitative methods include visual inspection and the quantitative methods in-
clude two classiﬁcation-based metrics – Inception Score and FID Score. The
Inception score measures the diversity and quality of generated samples. The
Frechet Inception Distance measures the distance between the generated and
real distributions [Bor18].
3.2.3.1
Visual inspection
The most commonly used evaluation technique is visual inspection, where hu-
mans compare a collection of generated images with images of the real dataset
side by side [LKFO18, GPAM+14]. Furthermore, generative models are often
checked, if they can create semantically meaningful interpolation between two
generated images. In general, both methods are costly and not necessarily reli-
able. Nevertheless, they provide insight into the quality of the images and can
detect certain features faster than metrics [LKFO18].

Chapter 5
Conclusion
5.1
Summary and Discussion
This thesis investigates the possibility of using generative adversarial networks
as data augmentation method for the training of segmentation networks, in or-
der to increase their segmentation performance. It has shown that segmentation
networks generalise better if more training data is provided. Classical data aug-
mentation techniques that try to provide more training data are usually very
rigid and must be tailored to each dataset. The utilisation of generative adver-
sarial networks for artiﬁcially augmenting training data sets oﬀers a more generic
approach and compared to traditional data augmentation a higher number of
possible artiﬁcial samples. For this purpose two generative adversarial models
were implemented, one of which creates realistic label maps (image generation
task) and another generates a realistic image to the synthetic label map (image
translation task), which are similar to the original images. For both generative
models, several modiﬁcations were implemented and compared in order to either
increase the individual image quality, stabilise the training or increase the mode
variation within the generated dataset. The modiﬁcations include changes in
the architecture and the loss function.
The label map generation task based on a DCGAN showed that the quality of the
generated images could be signiﬁcantly improved by using self-attention modules
==PAGE:84==
68
Conclusion
(SAGAN) and Wasserstein loss with gradient penalty (WGAN-GP). The use of
both components leads to a stabilisation of the training so that the generator
generalises much better and generates synthetic images that look similar to
the training dataset. The training stability is also signiﬁcantly improved by
history buﬀering. Furthermore, the packing framework (PACGAN) successfully
prevents mode collapse. However, it can be seen that with increasing packing
factor, the quality of the images suﬀers. The combination of a SAGAN using
the packing framework with a factor of 2 and history buﬀering shows the best
image quality.
In the image-to-image translation task, it is shown that conditional GANs are
able to generate realistic-looking images that are very similar to the original
images. With the use of ResUnet-a in the pix2pix framework, a novel approach
was examined. It led to a signiﬁcant improvement in the accuracy of the outlines
of the objects as well as in the depth details. With the use of the pix2pixHD
framework with the coarse-to-ﬁne generator, images with further improvements
in detailed structures but mostly in colour variety could be generated.
The
generated images show high similarity to the real images in terms of colour,
structure and composition. Unfortunately, however, they are not suﬃciently
similar to the real dataset, as they contain artefacts or incomplete objects. The
pix2pixHD framework with history buﬀering showed the best image quality.
It is shown that the addition of the Dual-GAN-generated images does not im-
prove the segmentation accuracy, but slightly decreases.
In order to verify
whether it could only be due to the complexity of the tested dataset (GLAND),
the approach was also veriﬁed on the DRIVE dataset. However, the statement
was found to be conﬁrmed.
In further investigations, it turns out that both the label map and the corre-
sponding images are very similar to the real data, but the image quality remains
below the level of the real data. One question is whether it is possible to achieve
GAN-based data augmentation with improved image quality. For this, the in-
creasing image quality would have to correlate with an increasing segmentation
performance. An isolated examination of the image-to-image task indicates this
possibility.
Another possibility could be that the segmentation performance
does not increase, despite increasing image quality and is limited by the ac-
tual segmentation performance achieved with the real data. The reason for this
could be that the generated images do not contain more information than the
real images, and a segmentation network does not beneﬁt from generated data.
A big problem within the GAN domain is the evaluation problem presented in
Section 2.3.2.2. It shows, especially in the case of label map generation, that
the metrics (FID, IS) used in the GAN domain often do not reﬂect qualitative
results. Therefore, the evaluation problem does not allow for a fair comparison of
quantitative models. Furthermore, it is challenging to perform hyperparameter
==PAGE:85==
5.2 Future Work
69
optimisations because they optimise an objective function based on a metric
which reﬂects the performance of the GAN.
5.2
Future Work
As described in Section 4.3, it remains to be investigated whether the segmen-
tation performance can be improved by a better image quality of the generated
images. For this reason, it should be explored how the image quality of addition-
ally generated images relates to the segmentation performance of a segmentation
network.
Assuming that GAN-based data augmentation with better image quality can
help segmentation networks to generalise, the following ideas can be considered.
Schönfeld et al. [SSK20] can generate more globally and locally coherent images
using a U-Net discriminator by deciding whether the image is real or synthetic,
not only for the whole image but also for each pixel. The experiments show that
this simple change results in maintaining global and local realism. A further
improvement could be brought about by following the approach of Karras et
al. [KALL18] by changing the architecture during the training. The training
starts with a real ﬂat architecture that allows generating 4x4 large images. As
the training progresses, more layers are then gradually added until the desired
image size is reached. This approach leads to a much more stable and faster
converging training and a higher image quality, especially at higher image res-
olutions, compared to a ﬁxed number of layers.
In the area of image-to-image translation, there are also optimisation possibili-
ties that can increase image quality. One possibility is to apply the ResUnet-a
architecture used in the pix2pix framework to the Coarse-to-ﬁne generator of the
pix2pixHD framework since the combination of ResNet blocks and the U-Net
skip connections has already led to qualitative and quantitative improvement.
A further improvement of the pix2pixHD network is presented in the CC-FPSE
network introduced by Liu et al. [LYS+20]. Here a multi-scale discriminator is
extended by a decoder section and the feature map of the decoder is compared in
diﬀerent scales with the original semantic label map. This comparison leads to
more ﬁne details in the images and increases the semantic alignments of objects.
Post-processing the generated images using classical image manipulation could
lead to higher image quality. Noise, incomplete objects and artefacts can be
reduced, and the similarity to the original dataset can be increased.
