Abstract
As the most massive structures in the early Universe, protoclusters play an important role
in the formation of massive galaxies and of galaxy clusters in the local Universe. In this
thesis, I present the selection and identification of distant galaxy protoclusters and galaxy
groups using unique datasets of multi-wavelength observations. Firstly, I developed the
public code GalCluster, to automatically search for protocluster candidates via overden-
sity mapping. Secondly, by applying GalCluster to multi-wavelength observations in deep
fields, I identified three significant overdensities of galaxies at 3.5 < z < 5.3. The first
structure, HPC1001, is a protocluster at z = 3.61 in the COSMOS field. NOEMA spectro-
scopically confirmed the redshift of this structure via the CO(4-3) emission line, which is
in excellent agreement with the photometric redshifts from the COSMOS2020 catalogue.
It is hosted by a massive dark matter halo of ∼1013 M⊙. Remarkably, the most massive
member galaxy in HPC1001 has a low specific star formation rate, which indicates that it
is likely on the way to quenching its star-formation activity. These factors indicate that
HPC1001 is a protocluster in the maturing phase at z = 3.61, and its core is likely already
collapsed. The second overdensity CGG-z5 is a compact galaxy group at z ∼5.2 revealed
by JWST observations in the CEERS field. The final overdensity, also revealed by JWST,
is CW-z4OD at z ∼4 in the COSMOS-Web survey. With this thesis, I have identified
three more structures in the early universe, adding to the scarce sample of high redshift
galaxy protoclusters and galaxy groups. Future follow-ups with advanced telescopes will
robustly reveal the structures and characterize the interstellar medium properties and dy-
namic states, pushing our understanding of massive galaxy formation and the evolution
of large-scale cosmic structures.

Introduction
The study of astronomy is one of the eldest disciplines of science, but the study of extra-
galactic objects is a relatively recent addition to the scientific community. The stars and
the great beyond, fascinating humanity for thousands of years. This thesis studies the
secrets of the early universe, through the dense environments of progenitors of massive
galaxy clusters.
1.1
Cosmology
Cosmology is the study of the structure of the Universe itself, and combines elements of
theoretical physics and observations. The Lambda Cold Dark Matter model (ΛCDM) of
cosmology, often refered to as the standard model of cosmology, is a cosmological model
consisting of three distinct elements
• Λ - The cosmological constant, describing the accelerating expansion of the universe,
driven by dark energy.
• CDM - Cold dark matter
• Normal baryonic matter
The cosmological constant, Λ, first put forward by Einstein (1917), is described as a
universal constant ”[...] an der an sich nicht beansprucht, ernst genommen zu werden”,
meaning, ”which does not in itself claim to be taken seriously”. In Einstein (1917) the
cosmological constant is used as a means to an end, and is not physically motivated to ex-
ist. It was not until the acceleration of the expansion of the universe was measured, using
type Ia supernovae, that the cosmological constant had concrete observational evidence
(Riess et al., 1998).
”Dunkle Materie”, Dark Matter, was first proposed by Zwicky (1933), to describe the ex-
cess of rotation velocity of galaxies around the Coma cluster. This theory of dark matter
was further corroborated by Rubin et al. (1970), measuring an excess of rotation velocity
of Hα emitting regions in the Andromeda Galaxy.
Further studies revealed that cold
dark matter, i.e., dark matter with negligible thermal velocity compared with the Hubble
flow, best describes the galaxies and structures observed in the universe (Riess et al., 1998).
The smallest contributor to the total energy budget of the universe, baryonic matter, en-
compasses all visible materials, such as gas, stars, galaxies, and you and I (Riess et al.,
1998).
1.1.1
Cosmic Microwave Background
Due to the observed expansion of the universe, it was theorized by Gamow (1948), that
there should have been a homogeneous extremely hot and dense gas, billions of years ago.
Later, the cosmic microwave background (CMB) was measured directly by Penzias et al.
(1965) and Dicke et al. (1965) simultaneously. The CMB fits with a near-perfect black-
body at T = 2.725 K. Coupling this with the temperature of the universe at recombination
(3000 K, Hu et al., 2002), puts the CMB at redshift z = 3000 K

As seen in Fig. 1.2 the overdensities in the primordial light grow as time progresses. And
the voids between the cosmic filaments increase in size.
In the beginning, there were many, very small, dark matter haloes. Due to the dark mat-
ter being cold, it was able to contract and merge, forming increasingly larger haloes. This
scenario of hierarchical merging, also called “bottom-up”, is a crucial part of the ΛCDM
models (Boylan-Kolchin et al., 2009).
After accruing enough mass, these large haloes
were then able to gravitationally attract the pristine gas of the early universe intergalac-
tic medium (IGM). The gas inside the halo eventually collapsed to form a rotationally
supported disk. Small perturbations in the gas density created local gravitational wells,
forming the first stars of the universe (Benson, 2010).
If these large dark matter haloes, were in an extremely overdense region of the early uni-
verse, smaller haloes would continue merging into the largest haloes as time progresses.
This continuous merging will, in turn, fuel the largest halo with new gas and galaxies
formed in smaller subhaloes (Benson, 2010).
This process has been ongoing since the epoch of recombination (z ∼1050 −1100), con-
tinually creating larger and larger dark matter haloes. The largest virialized dark matter
halos in the Universe host systems of galaxies, and constitute present day massive clusters
of galaxies. The evolution of these galaxy clusters should be traceable back to the earliest

1.2
Protoclusters
The progenitors of these massive and virialized systems, protoclusters, contain galaxies
that are more evolved than field galaxies in the same epoch. Protoclusters are thought to
be as rare as nearby clusters, but even more diﬀicult to detect due to the vast distance
to them. As mentioned previously, protoclusters inhabit areas of increased spacial galaxy
density as compared to the field. Galaxies in protoclusters have been shown to not evolve
more quickly than field-galaxies, rather, they formed earlier, and are thus at a later stage
of development (Overzier, 2016).
The definition of a protocluster is still in debate, however, it is commonplace to define
a protocluster as a structure residing in a dark matter halo that is massive enough, to
be able to form into a present day virilized cluster. Adopting the definition of Overzier
(2016), a protocluster is a structure residing in a dark matter halo, that will grow to a
virial halo mass of log(MDM/M⊙) > 14 at z = 0. Any structure that does not fulfil this
requirement, with (MDM/M⊙) > 13 at z = 0, will be classified as a galaxy group.
Due to the overdensity of dark matter, and the inflow of pristine gas from the IGM,
there is much more gas available to galaxies in protoclusters to form stars. The large gas
reservoirs allow star-forming galaxies (SFGs) to form at earlier times, this in turn means
that massive galaxies are more prevalent in overdense environment. Further, it means that
the earliest population of quenched galaxies can be found in protoclusters, in turn driving
the observed increased fraction of quiescent galaxies (QGs) in protoclusters compared to
the field (Overzier, 2016).
1.2.1
Protoclusters’ contribution to the Cosmic Star Formation Rate
Density
Due to the earlier evolution of SFGs in overdense environments and the higher likelihood
of mergers due to the high density of galaxies, many massive star-bursts (SBs) inhabit the
centres of these massive overdense systems. The high fraction of SBs in protoclusters has
been shown in simulations (Chiang et al., 2017), contributing a significant amount of the
cosmic star formation rate density (CSFRD), i.e., the amount of stellar material formed
per unit volume, in the early universe. The contribution to the CSFRD of protoclusters
and protocluster cores can be seen in Fig. 1.3.

1.3
Observed protoclusters
High redshift clusters and protoclusters have been discovered within the last decade (e.g.,
Wang et al., 2016; Oteo et al., 2018; Miller et al., 2018), mostly characterized by SBs and
SFGs in their cores. Through these observations of protoclusters at different epochs, three
simplified stages of protocluster evolution have been put forward.
1.3.1
Simplified stages of protocluster evolution
Protoclusters are believed to undergo three distinct phases of evolution, before becom-
ing massive virialized clusters containing primarily quiescent galaxies in the centre. Shi-
makawa et al. (2018) proposes three distinct phases, the growing phase, the maturing
phase, and a mature phase, a sketch of these phases is shown in Fig. 1.5.

As the illustration in Fig. 1.5 shows, the earliest phase of protoclusters is the growing
phase, which is characterized by several small groups of massive galaxies in separate non-
virialized dark matter haloes, that will eventually merge and form a protocluster core.
This phase of evolution is theorized to occur earlier than redshift three, when the universe
was approximately two billion years old. The growing phase is where most of the massive
SFGs are formed (Shimakawa et al., 2018).
The next phase is called the maturing phase, characterized by a nearly virialized core dark-
matter halo encompassing many galaxies at different evolutionary stages. These galaxies
include, SBs, SFGs, QGs, and active galactic nuclei (AGN). The maturing phase is thought
to occur in the one billion years between redshift two and three. Maturing phase proto-
clusters, should be the ideal environment to form the earliest massive quenched galaxies
in the universe (Shimakawa et al., 2018).
The final phase, the declining phase or mature phase, is characterized by a large virilized
dark matter halo, surrounding a core of massive QGs. The cold gas in the intracluster
medium (ICM) has been depleted, and only a superheated plasma is left. The declining
phase is the most recent phase of protocluster evolution, and is thought to have started
ten billion years ago (Shimakawa et al., 2018).
It should be noted that the epochs of these phases are purely meant as a guide, and not
as definitive epochs.

et al. (2012). The protocluster consists of three distinct clumps of galaxies, containing
many Hα-emitters (HAEs) and distant red galaxies (DRGs).

In early 2023, the discovery of a z = 2.77 protocluster dominated by massive QGs, QO-

might be able to detect the superheated plasma in the ICM.
1.4
How to find protoclusters
Several different methods of identifying protoclusters exist, some are based off of tracers,
i.e., individual galaxies associated with protoclusters. Others, are based off of environmen-
tal effects, and the final methods are based on the spacial density of galaxies (Overzier,
2016).
1.4.1
Methods
In this section is detailed some of the multiple methods of protocluster candidate detection.
1.4.1.1
Tracer methods
Tracer methods utilize the fact that protoclusters are dense environments, and thus, har-
bour more evolved and dusty galaxies than the field at the same redshift (Overzier, 2016).
8

==PAGE:17==
Submillimeter galaxies
Submillimeter galaxies (SMGs) are usually highly star-forming and very dusty galaxies.
SMGs are thought to trace protoclusters, as they are theorized to be the most massive
galaxies in the early universe. If other galaxies at similar redshift can be found surrounding
these extreme galaxies, a new protocluster candidate has been identified. This method
was used by Capak et al. (2011) to discover a protocluster of galaxies around the SMG
COSMOS AzTEC-3 at redshift z = 5.3.
Radio galaxies and Quasars
Radio galaxies and quasars stem from the same astronomical phenomenon, AGN. These
AGN form when the super-massive black hole (SMBH) of a galaxy has an accretion disc of
material, that is superheated to emit X-rays and relativistic jets of charged particles from
the poles of the nucleus. Overzier et al. (2006) used this method to discover a protocluster
at redshift z = 5.2, around the radio galaxy TN J0924-2201.
1.4.1.2
Environmental methods
Environmental methods exploit the dense environment of protoclusters, by observing
the effects of the massive dark-matter haloes associated with protoclusters and clusters
(Overzier et al., 2006).
The Sunyaev-Zel’dovich effect
The Sunyaev-Zel’dovich (SZ) effect, presented in Sunyaev and Zeldovich (1970), is inverse
Compton scattering of CMB photons, due to the hot plasma of the ICM. Three new
clusters at 0.4 ≤z ≤0.8 were discovered by Staniszewski et al. (2009), by performing a
large scale (40 square degree) SZ survey. The SZ effect as of now cannot be used to detect
high-redshift protoclusters, the current frontier of SZ observations is z ≈2 (Overzier,
2016).
Extended X-ray emission
The hot plasma in the ICM cools by way of thermal bremsstrahlung, detectable as an
extended X-ray emission tracing the distribution of hot gas bound to the dark-matter
halo of the cluster. The massive galaxy cluster XMMXCS J2215.9-1738 was discovered by
Stanford et al. (2006) as an extended X-ray source in XMM-Newton archive data.
Lyα blobs
Lyα blobs are extended regions of cool ionized hydrogen gas, usually associated with
galaxy groups and clusters. Prescott et al. (2008) investigated the environment of the lyα
blob LABd05, determining it to be associated with a protocluster at z = 2.65.
Lyα tomography
Using a dense grid of background galaxies, a tomographic map of Lyα absorption can be
used to identify large clouds of intergalactic gas. These absorption features trace the ICM
of massive group and cluster halos. Lyα tomography was used by Newman et al. (2022)
to discover a large sample of new protocluster and group candidates at z = 2.2 −2.8.
1.4.1.3
Density methods
The final collection of methods to discover protoclusters, involve mapping the spacial
density of different types of galaxies (Overzier, 2016).
Distant Red Galaxies
DRGs are optically red galaxies that are far away, the red colour of these galaxies could
Exploring the nature of the first protoclusters in our Universe

==PAGE:18==
either be due to an ageing stellar population or dust obscuration. Wang et al. (2016)
searched for overdensities of z > 2 galaxies brighter in UVISTA Ks than J, corresponding
to red and violet colours at redshift two, discovering the X-ray cluster CL J1001 at z =
2.51.
Star-Forming Galaxies and quiescent galaxies
Similarly, searching for overdensities of star-forming or quiescent galaxies, based either on
rest-frame colour selection or physical properties, can yield discoveries of protoclusters.
Ito et al. (2023) searched for overdensities of QGs discovering QO-1000 at z = 2.77, and
Steidel et al. (1998) used spacial overdensity of star-forming galaxies to discover a large
protocluster at z ∼3.
General galaxy density
To alleviate biases when searching for passive or active galaxies, general galaxy overdensity
can be used to search for protoclusters with both types of galaxies. This was done in
Sillassen et al. (2022) finding an overdensity of both red and blue galaxies at z ≈3.7 in
the COSMOS field.
1.5
Motivation
As mentioned above, protoclusters are regions of overdensity, both in terms of gas, dust,
and galaxies, i.e., baryonic matter, but also in terms of dark-matter. This dense environ-
ment has been shown to not change how galaxies evolve (Koyama et al., 2013), but rather,
galaxies in overdense environments formed at earlier times in the universe (Overzier, 2016).
Furthermore, protoclusters reside in large filaments of dark-matter, probing both how mas-
sive clusters assemble, and act as a testing ground for cosmological models. Thus, finding
and studying protoclusters is thus important to understand how massive galaxies evolve,
massive structures form, and how the Universe was formed and changes with time.

2
Finding protoclusters in practice
As described in Section 1.4, multiple methods of searching for protoclusters exist, look-
ing either for tracer galaxies, protocluster environments, or overdensities of galaxies. The
latter of these methods is the only one not requiring extra observations, making it the
quickest method, however requiring deep multi-wavelength photometric data and reliable
photometric redshifts.
When reliable data is available, two different methods of density estimation can be used,
either fixed physical radius or nearest-neighbour density approaches (Overzier, 2016).
Fixed physical radius approaches rely on the number of galaxies within the approximated
physical size of protoclusters or protocluster cores at given redshifts, of each galaxy (e.g.,
Strazzullo et al., 2015).
A nearest-neighbour approach relies on the distance to the Nth nearest neighbour of each
galaxy, which is used in the program GalCluster.
GalCluster takes a catalogue of
galaxy positions and redshifts as input. Large photometric redshift catalogues increase
the likelihood of correctly calculating the density of the field population, which is crucial
to correctly identify overdensities.

Overdense regions are selected as protocluster candidates based off the redshift provided

to verify the protocluster candidates as being real.
2.2
Sanity checks and verification
The easiest sanity check to perform is looking at images of the protocluster candidates.
This way, things like stars can be removed, and looking at the sizes and colours of the
galaxies, one can infer whether these could be a part of the same structure. An example
of a good and bad detection is shown in Fig. 2.1.

2.3
Spectroscopic follow up
To solidly verify these protocluster candidates, spectroscopic follow-ups are needed to
verify the redshift of all galaxies, to verify that the galaxies are close in physical space and
not just projection in line of sight.
Utilizing the knowledge of emission lines from galaxies, and the estimated redshifts from
photometry, high spectral resolution telescopes can be used to observe targeted emission
lines, to solidly confirm the redshift of the galaxies.

3.1.3
Evolutionary stage
Following the three simplified stages of protocluster evolution from Shimakawa et al.
(2018), it can be argued how far along HPC1001 is in its evolutionary track.
Comparing HPC1001 to the most distant X-ray detected cluster to date, CL J1001 (Wang
et al., 2016), HPC1001 is more compact, hosts a greater number of lower mass galaxies,
and has a much lower IR SFR. Shimakawa et al. (2018) proposed that CL J1001 is in the
maturing phase, due to the high SFR, > 3000 M⊙yr−1 and low quenched fraction ∼20%.
HPC1001 would be classified in the same phase as CL J1001, due to the low total SFR,
the core hosting both quenching and star-bursting galaxies, and a massive dark matter
halo. HPC1001 is approximately at the same epoch as the protoclusters of Oteo et al.
(2018) and Miller et al. (2018), but hosts ∼6 times lower integrated SFR, suggesting that
HPC1001 is at a later stage of evolution than either of the others. It should be noted,
however, the dark matter mass estimate of HPC1001 is lower than both of these massive
protoclusters.

Discussion
4.1
A brief review of high redshift protoclusters in
COSMOS
As stated in Chapter 1, dense environments affect how galaxies grow and evolve, and
dominate the formation of stars and galaxies in the early universe. Galaxy clusters are
also the sites of the most massive quiescent galaxies, and their progenitors should host
the earliest quenching galaxies. A still standing question in modern astronomy is when
the progenitors of these massive structures first collapsed, which could be answered by
spectroscopically confirming high-redshift protoclusters.
Considering the COSMOS survey, which is the most complete survey to date, only a
handful of protoclusters and massive groups have thus far been identified (Capak et al.,
2011; Spitler et al., 2012; Cucciati et al., 2014; Yuan et al., 2014; Chiang et al., 2015;
Casey et al., 2015; Diener et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2016; Lemaux et al.,
2018; Pavesi et al., 2018; Darvish et al., 2020; Mitsuhashi et al., 2021; Daddi et al., 2021;
Koyama et al., 2021; Polletta et al., 2021; McConachie et al., 2022; Sillassen et al., 2022;
Ata et al., 2022; Ito et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023), an overview of the properties of
these protoclusters and groups is shown in Table 4.1. These protoclusters and groups are
very diverse, both in terms of the number of member galaxies detected, and the types of
galaxies that reside in them.
In terms of the evolutionary stage of these protoclusters, examples of all the three simpli-
fied phases are present in the COSMOS region.
The AzTEC-3 cluster is the second earliest protocluster at z = 5.298, hosting one large
star-burst and 5 smaller galaxies in the core, with the full system spanning > 13 Mpc.
AzTEC-3, together with PCl J1001 and CRLE, represent the first stage of protocluster
evolution, growing massive galaxies in separate dark matter haloes (Capak et al., 2011;
Lemaux et al., 2018; Guaita et al., 2022). Looking at Table 4.1, it is clear that AzTEC-3
is hosted by the smallest dark-matter halo, and contains much lower stellar content than
later protoclusters.
Maturing-phase protoclusters, like MAGAZ3NE J0959, MAGAZ3NE J1000, HPC1001,
G237, PCL1002, and CL J1001, show both the results of massive galaxy growth, i.e., all
cores of these protoclusters contain very massive galaxies. Further, the large redshift span,
2.16 < z < 3.61 (1.3 Gyr), hints at the huge amount of time required to assemble a galaxy
cluster. This epoch of maturing protoclusters occurred before the peak of CSFRD, i.e.,
cosmic noon (Madau and Dickinson, 2014).
Interestingly, though it is at higher redshift than some of the maturing phase protoclusters,
QO-1000 at z = 2.768, appears to be in the mature phase, hosting a large overdensity of
quiescent galaxies, and a 3x higher quenched fraction than the field (Ito et al., 2023).
With the very low number of confirmed high redshift protoclusters, the observational evi-
dence supporting the current theory of massive cluster formation is very limited. Looking

4.2
When do the first virialized galaxy haloes appear?
This is an extremely diﬀicult question to answer, and requires careful definitions of what
qualifies as clusters, protoclusters and groups. With the best estimate halo mass of RO-
1001 consistent with a Coma like progenitor, Daddi et al. (2021) refers to the structure as
both a group and a cluster, settling on massive galaxy group. With the definition adopted
in Overzier (2016), due to the large halo mass of the system,RO-1001 would be classified
as a protocluster, regardless of the relatively low number of galaxies.
CL J1001 is the most distant X-ray detected protocluster identified to date at z = 2.506.
With an estimated halo mass of MDM = 8 × 1013 M⊙and a virial radius 0.373 Mpc, CL
J1001 inhabits one of the most massive, i.e., rarest halos, which should have a number
density comparable with local rich clusters 10−6 Mpc3 h−3 based on simulations (Bahcall,
1996; Lukić et al., 2007). This number density is not consistent with what is being ob-
served, assuming the estimated dark matter halo masses of the individual protoclusters
are correct. As calculated previously, at 2.45 < z < 2.55 only 0.94 haloes of this mass are
expected in the entire COSMOS field, however, four separate haloes at this mass or higher
are reported in the literature (Chiang et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2016; Darvish et al., 2020;
Dong et al., 2023).
CLJ1001 is usually reported as a cluster due to the hot ICM of the structure, as traced
by X-ray observations. This structure shows features of both the maturing phase and
declining phase, with SB and AGN members being a prominent feature of the maturing
phase, and the hot ICM being a feature of a mature/declining phase protocluster.
Another extreme case is that of SPT2349-56 from Miller et al. (2018), showing a very dense
and starbursting core. Recent observations have identified more SMG members, as well as
sub-haloes located out to ∼1 Mpc away from the core (Hill et al., 2020; Hill et al., 2022;
Apostolovski et al., 2023). Interestingly, nearly all galaxies in the SPT2349-56 core are
expected to merge into one central BCG at z ≳3.3 (Rennehan et al., 2020). SPT2349-56
shows most of the signs of being a growing phase, with massive galaxies growing in sepa-
rate haloes (Hill et al., 2020; Hill et al., 2022), and a hot gas ICM traced by an extended
Lyα halo (Apostolovski et al., 2023). However, it also shows signs of having AGN activity
(Chapman et al., 2023), a trait of the maturing phase.
HPC1001 shows signs of being the earliest protocluster in the maturing phase, with a
large diversity of member galaxies, massive dark matter halo, and extremely dense core

(Sillassen et al., 2022). It is still uncertain if the HPC1001 core is compatible with a sin-
gle virialized or collapsing dark matter halo, with several follow-up observations required

CL J1001 is the earliest (proto)cluster detected in X-ray, suggesting it is the first virialized

5
Summary
Early universe protoclusters are ideal laboratories to study the evolution of galaxies, prob-
ing the validity of cosmological models, and observational verification of large scale simu-
lations.
This thesis introduces core concepts about how density fluctuations, traced by the primor-
dial light of the Universe, grow over time and eventually form the first protoclusters in
the early universe. The effects of dense environments on galaxy evolution are presented,
and what characterizes the three simplified stages of protocluster evolution, the growing,
maturing, and declining phases. Several protocluster identification methods are then pre-
sented, including tracer, environmental, and density based methods.
1. A very quick overview of the protocluster candidate identification code GalCluster,
originally developed by the author of this thesis, is given, with concrete steps to find
and verify detected candidates.
2. Three objects found with the GalCluster program are presented in detail
• the maturing protocluster core HPC1001 at redshift 3.61
• the compact galaxy group CGG-z5 at z ∼5.2, thought to be the progenitor of
the present day massive galaxies, revealed by JWST observations in CEERS
• the CW-z4OD group candidate, possibly one massive group, or two groups in
projection.
All of which were impossible to discovery without the unprecedented completeness
and depth of the COSMOS2020 catalogue, and JWST data.
3. The most important discovery in the context of protoclusters is obviously HPC1001,
it is very massive, extremely dense, and is another critical data point in the obser-
vational efforts of early universe protoclusters. HPC1001 is even more interesting,
looking at the properties of the protocluster members and underlying dark mat-
ter halo. With a possibly quenching galaxy, a starbursting galaxy, several ”normal”
main sequence SFGs, and a 1013 M⊙possibly collapsing dark matter halo, HPC1001
looks to be in the maturing phase of protocluster evolution, which would make it
the earliest to date discovered maturing protocluster core.
4. Of pure relevancy to galaxy evolution, CGG-z5 shows a clear picture of massive
galaxy growth at a very short-lived stage, observationally revealing the picture pre-
dicted by simulations. Massive galaxies have been predicted to form in early times by

not clear, further observations are needed to fully understand this structure. If this
is indeed one group, or even two groups, they would be some of the first of a number
of low mass galaxy groups that will be revealed with the capabilities of JWST.
