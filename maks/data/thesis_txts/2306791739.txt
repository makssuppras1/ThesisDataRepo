Abstract
Dept. of Civil Engineering, Section of Building Energy
Department of Civil Engineering
Master of Science in Architectural Engineering
Numerical Parametric Studies of Diﬀuse Ceiling Ventilation
by Michael Hykkelbjerg Nielsen
The main purpose of this thesis is to analyse the beneﬁts and disadvantages of diﬀuse
ceiling ventilation. The results are from CFD simulations, and when possible compared
with earlier studies. Plenum design, design of diﬀuse ceiling plates and diﬀerent room
types are simulated. The room types are a two-person oﬃce, a landscape oﬃce and a
laboratory.
The results showed that it is possible to establish key performance indicators that quan-
tiﬁes the performance in relation to draught, since the highest draught rating is located
away from the occupants. Correct placement of the in- and outlet creates a uniform air
distribution across the diﬀuse ceiling. Micro-jets occur when the ventilation rate is at
or above 6 l/s/m2 for non-diﬀuse ceiling plates. The heat load conﬁgurations and room
heights impacts the draught rating in oﬃce environments. Diﬀuse ceiling ventilation
was able to handle high ventilation rate requirements in for example a laboratory.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The primary function of buildings has always been to protect the occupants from the
outdoor environment. The ﬁrst buildings only protected the occupants against the wind
and rain. Today, the requirements of buildings are increased and in some places buildings
must heat, cool, provide hot water and better indoor air than outside the building.
Time spent indoor have increased during time and at the moment it is estimated that
people spend around 87 % of their lifetime indoor [Klepeis et al., 2001]. The indoor
air quality (IAQ) greatly aﬀects our health and productivity at work. Improving IAQ
decrease the risk of asthma and allergy in homes [Fanger, 2006]. As an example poor
IAQ can reduce the performance of oﬃce works by 6-9 % [Wyon, 2004].
In Denmark ventilation is a requirement in new buildings [BR15, 2015]. The main func-
tion for ventilation is to provide fresh air into the building, and remove moisture, heat,
odour etc. The inlet air can be ﬁltered, cooled, heated humidiﬁed and dehumidiﬁed,
and should be provided to the room without destroying the indoor environment qual-
ity (IEQ), which is often done by creating draught, high air speeds or a bad vertical
temperature distribution.
Most ventilation systems are mechanical, except a few which relies on natural forces. The
typical ventilation systems are mixing and displacement ventilation, but in the recent

years a new type of ventilation called diﬀuse ceiling ventilation is installed in buildings
occupied by humans.
1.2
Diﬀuse ceiling ventilation
Diﬀuse ceiling ventilation (DCV) is commonly used in livestock buildings. In Denmark
approximately 90 % of the livestock buildings are ventilated with DCV. The air is
supplied with pressure over a suspended diﬀuse ceiling (DC). It provides fresh air with
high level of comfort to the animals, at low energy and investment cost for the farmer.
[Jacobsen, 2006].
More and more buildings for humans have been installed with diﬀuse inlets through the
ceiling plates instead of traditional diﬀusers in the recent years. The system has aesthetic
beneﬁts, because it gives a ceiling without inlets, and studies have shown that DCV is
able to handle higher heat loads, compared with traditional ventilation systems [Nielsen
and Jakubowska, 2009]. However limited design tools are available when designing DCV.
Computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) can be used to simulate the indoor environment
in buildings. It simulates the whole room and can give a detailed picture of eventual
problems with the IAQ and IEQ. This is important as more and more equipment are
installed in buildings and the IAQ and IEQ requirements rises. These simulations can
give guidelines helping building designers implement DCV in more buildings.

Chapter 2
Literature review
This chapter describes the diﬀerent DC types and which material they can be made of.
State of the art studies of the properties of DCV and how it performs in diﬀerent room
types and with diﬀerent heat loads are also shown.
2.1
Description of diﬀuse ceiling ventilation
DCV is in this report ventilation with the supply air is going through the whole or an
area in the ceiling. There are three ways the supply air can be provided to the room.
The ﬁrst method is the case when the plates are tight and the air goes through the
cracks in the ceiling, the second scenario is when the plates are perforated and the air
goes through both the cracks and the plates, and ﬁnally the case where the ceiling is
fully diﬀuse where the air is supplied through a uniform diﬀuse ceiling. The last solution
is not a system based ceiling system as the two others.
The plates can be made in diﬀerent materials and their properties are listed in this
section.
2.1.1
Aluminium plates
These plates are normally used for acoustic ceilings and are produced in square tiles. On
the upper side a textile is glued to the plate, which gives the plate acoustic performances
3

and the possibility to be used as DC. The aluminium plates are fastened in a proﬁle
which ensures a relative close connection.
2.1.2
Gypsum plates
As the perforated aluminium plates, these are also used for acoustic and DCs with a
textile glued to the upper side and produced in square tiles. The gypsum plates are
normally mounted on reverse T-proﬁles, which result in minor openings along the edges
of the plates.
2.1.3
Cement-wood plates
These plates can be made as passive and active plates. The plates are made as diﬀuse
cement-wood plates, where wood shavings are mixed with cement. Using cement-wood
to construct the plates results in a low particle and gas emission. The plates are fastened
with screws at the suspended ceiling.

2.2.1
Reverse ﬂow in plenum
Reverse ﬂow through the ceiling should be limited to avoid mixing between the dirty
air in the room and the fresh air from the inlet. This problem has been investigated
theoretically and experimentally. Studies from [Hviid and Svendsen, 2013] measured
tracer-gas in the plenum and the room and only observed a concentration increase of
5.1 % with the inlet speed of 1.3 m/s and between 1.3 and 2.6 % at inlet speeds from
1.7 to 3.3 m/s. An article [Zhang et al., 2016] performed a theoretical investigation of
the topic and observed reverse ﬂow close to the inlet, which was also above a radiator
that produced a high thermal plume.

2.2.2
Pressure drop
A low pressure drop across the diﬀuse ceiling plates is desired because it leads to lower
energy consumption. Earlier studies have tested the correlation between the pressure
drop and the airﬂow for the mentioned diﬀuse ceilings. A study from [Zhang et al., 2014]
compared diﬀerent DC types, some of the results are shown in Figure 2.2.

lowest pressure drops are from the gypsum and aluminium ceiling systems due to crack

2.2.3
Cracks in the ceiling and micro-jets
The amount of air going through the cracks depends on the type of suspended ceiling and
the preciseness from the workers mounting the ceiling. Work from [Hviid and Svendsen,
2013] shows that the crack ﬂow can be 17 or 64 % of the total ventilation rate for an
aluminium or a gypsum suspended ceiling respectively.
The crack ﬂow in cement-wood ceiling plates have been investigated by [Zhang et al.,
2016]. The study also shows the amount of air going through the cracks. However, an
exponential correlation with the ventilation rate is observed, whereas in the case with
the aluminium and gypsum plates, which had a linear correlation.

The airﬂow through the air gaps can create microjets with high air velocities up to 0.5 m
below the ceiling [Nielsen et al., 2010]. Therefore the article suggests, that the minimum
distance from the ceiling to the occupied zone is 0.5 m. The study is based on smoke

2.2.4
Uneven loads
[Zhang et al., 2016] concludes that the heat loads create the dominant ﬂow in the room.
Therefore the location of heat sources is of great interest when analysing the indoor envi-
ronment. The study investigated four diﬀerent combinations of heat load distributions.
The results showed a strong recirculation of air ﬂows opposite the heat sources, when
they were placed in one side of the room. When the heat loads was placed evenly no
clear air circulation was observed. The highest draught risk was observed at the ﬂoors.
The same conclusions are made by [Nielsen et al., 2015].
2.2.5
Ceiling heights
Ceiling heights are an important parameter when designing ventilation systems. For
example displacement ventilation should be avoided in rooms with a low ceiling and
ventilation with supply from the ceiling might give problems in rooms with a high
ceiling. Diﬀerent ceiling heights have been investigated theoretically by [Zhang et al.,
2016] with an evenly placed internal load. The results showed draught problems, when
the room height was increased. Experimental results from [Nielsen et al., 2015] show
the same tendency.

Chapter 3
Aim of the project
The majority of earlier studies of DCV has been based on experiments and experimental
measurements. It has given results without structure. This study will investigate DCV
with a theoretical approach by using CFD simulations.
This chapter summarizes previous work, lists hypotheses and name the limitations.
3.1
Hypotheses
The limitations and potentials of diﬀuse ceiling ventilation to provide a good IEQ and
IAQ in rooms will be investigated, with the results being evaluated in a way, that are
clear and easy to compare. Therefore it is necessary to deﬁne clear key performance
indicators (KPI) for, in this case, diﬀuse ceiling ventilation. Earlier studies of diﬀuse
ceiling ventilation shows that the heat loads create the dominant air ﬂows in the occupied
zone. The heat loads are placed opposite the occupants in the room. Traditional KPIs
for ventilation are deﬁned for the whole occupied zone. This might be too conservative
method for DCV since the worst IEQ is always placed away from the occupants.
Hypothesis 1:
Is it possible to establish key indicators for DCV that quantiﬁes the perfor-
mance in terms of draught (KPIs)?
The distribution of air in the plenum has been investigated by [Zhang et al., 2016]. Their
studies were theoretical and showed a reverse ﬂow through the diﬀuse ceiling close to the

3.2
Limitations
When investigating full-scale rooms, this thesis focuses on oﬃces and laboratories. While
two diﬀerent oﬃces, a two-person and a landscape oﬃce, are simulated, one simulated
laboratory is used.
The study is based on CFD simulations, which require a lot of user operations and input
information to give reliable results. Simpliﬁcation will be made for the inputs and will
resolve in results which can vary from the reality. Therefore the results should be veriﬁed
by full-scale experiments.
The results from CFD simulations are based on a speciﬁc moment or a short period of
time and do not take long therm inﬂuence on the indoor climate into count.
Furthermore relative humidity and CO2 concentration will not be investigated in eval-
uation of the IEQ and IAQ.

Chapter 4
Method
This study will investigate diﬀerent geometries to analyse the air movements from the
air is blown into the plenum and to the air leaves the room. The following scenarios and
models are made.
• Distribution of air, temperature and pressure in the plenum
• Diﬀerent diﬀuse ceiling types and their inﬂuence on micro-jets in the room
• Diﬀerent room types; two-person oﬃce, landscape oﬃce and laboratory
4.1
Plenum
The temperature of the air in the plenum and to the room will be investigated. The
heating of the air from the inlet to the plenum and in the room is important, because
uneven temperatures in the room and at the ceiling can create thermal discomfort for
the occupants.
The desired plenum for DCV has an uniform pressure, which is slightly higher than in
the room. A uniform pressure gives evenly distributed air to the room and the pressure
should only be slightly higher to give as low as possible pressure drop, which reduces
the energy consumption for the ventilation system.
Figure 4.1 shows the model for the plenum simulations. Diﬀerent plenum heights and
distances from the diﬀuse ceiling to the inlet are used in the models.

4.9.2
PMV and PPD
Predicted mean vote (PMV) is an index that sets a comfort value of a large group of
persons. It is based on a sensitivity scale from -3 to 3, where zero is the best.
The measurements will be made 10 cm behind the occupants and the height is at the
centrer of their gravity, 0.6 and 1.1 m for seated and standing occupants respectively.
The overall PMV calculation is complicated and relies on many parameters. A table
from the standard [ISO-7730, 2005] (Annex E) will be used to calculate PMV. In the
table, some assumptions are made, meaning that the PMV can be calculated based
on the temperature and relative air velocity. The relative air velocity is found by the
following Equation 4.3
var = va + 0.005 · (M −58)
(4.3)
va is the air velocity [m/s]. The activity level is set at a Metabolic rate (MET) at 1.2,
and therefore M=69.6 W/m2 (oﬃce work) [ISO-7730, 2005].

5.4.3
Age of air
The age of air is inserted as an additional variable in CFX-Pre. Then a sub-domain is
created in the room, with the age of air as a source. Then at any boundary conditions
to the room, the age of air value is set to 0 s.
5.5
Meshing
A good mesh is important to minimize errors in the solver, which leads to incorrect
results. It is important to have a good mesh resolution, distribution and quality. Every
volume of the model is divided into at least three elements in the ﬂow direction to
ensure correct calculations. The model is sliced into proper volumes to ensure a good
distribution, where the size of the elements varies, according to size of the model volumes.
This also reduces the number of elements and therefore the calculation time.
The mesh is constructed with a denser mesh at the inlets and outlets and mesh inﬂation
is applied at the boundaries towards the plenum and the walls.
Statistics for the mesh can be seen in Appendix A.1
5.6
Solver control
The simulations showed the velocities and temperatures ﬂuctuated at the same point.
To reach convergence, transient simulations were used. At ﬁrst, 100 simulations were
performed at steady state with a time-scale factor at ten or one (depending on the angle
at the convergence curve), and thereafter a transient simulation over 10 seconds with ten
time-steps were performed. The ﬁnal simulated results are used for the result section.

Discussion
7.1
Plenum
Little counterﬂow is observed for the standard Glyptone DC plates, meaning that it is
necessary to verify the results by detailed experiments. Prior tracer-gas experiments
have shown a minor concentration increase around 5 % in the inlet [Hviid and Svendsen,
2013], and it would be interesting to redo this experiment, to verify the results of the
CFD simulations. Another CFD study [Zhang et al., 2016] showed counterﬂow above a
heat source (radiator at 1650 W) with a strong thermal plume. This study provides no
indications that the heat sources can create counterﬂow, since the counterﬂow is only
observed close to the inlet. The CFD study from [Zhang et al., 2016] used a cement-
wood ceiling plate with a pressure drop coeﬃcient at 0.04 Pa/l/s/m2, where the DC
in this study has a pressure drop coeﬃcient at 0.65 Pa/l/s/m2. The diﬀerent pressure
drop coeﬃcients and the fact that the heat sources in this thesis are lower for the same
pressure drop at the DC are possible reasons of the diﬀerent results.
Only combinations with a single inlet and outlet, were both are placed in the wall are
simulated. It would be interesting to test combinations with the outlets placed in the
ceiling and/or with multiple in- and outlets.

Chapter 7. Discussion
7.2
Micro-jets
An earlier experimental study [Nielsen et al., 2010] shows micro-jets with a length of
0.5 m from a diﬀuse ceiling with an ach at 6 h-1. Similar results are not found in this
study. The length of the micro-jets observed at 6 h-1 (4 l/s/m2) was around 0.1 m. In
[Nielsen et al., 2010] the air speed of the micro-jets was not measured, which might be
the reason for the diﬀerent results.
The simulations in this study were performed with a uniform crack size along all the
edges. This is a theoretical approach, which will most likely not exist in reality, where
the plate will not be plane and therefore touch the grid in speciﬁc points or along an
edge. The approach used in this study, however gives the longest micro-jets, because
it produces the most ideal interference below the middle of the plate. It is therefore a
conservative approach.
The size of the crack in the simulations is based on assumptions and the results show
that the length of the micro-jet is depending on the crack size. This adds uncertainty
to the results as the results will be diﬀerent for other crack sizes.
The length of the micro-jets were analysed based on the ventilation rate.
Another
approach could be based on the pressure diﬀerence and the size of the cracks. This
could especially be interesting in situations where a desired pressure drop is wanted over
the DC.
The simulations were performed for a single tile with 0.7 to 1.0 m air below and symmetry
planes at the sides and an opening placed at the bottom. The optimal would be to
simulate a detailed room with all the plates modelled and with an outlet, inlet and
internal gains. This was not modelled due to the large amount of cell numbers which is
too heavy for the programme to handle and it gave long simulations times, which was
diﬃcult to converged. Therefore only a few simulations were performed with 9 plates.
As future computers are likely to be more powerful, it will be possible to produce more
accurate results.

7.3
KPIs
To have speciﬁc KPIs for DCV it is necessary to prove that the highest air speeds are
located away from the occupants.
In the simulations with a negative heat ﬂux the DR at the feet of the occupants is the
highest in the occupied zone. These results would have been diﬀerent if radiators were
installed. Then the highest DR in the occupied zone would probably be located close to
the inlet away from the occupants.
7.4
Oﬃces
All PMV values at the occupants were positive, which means that they are feeling warm.
This could be changed by setting the temperature lower than 23 oC. This would also
give higher DR in all simulations.
The air speed at 0.1 m from the ﬂoor is also evaluated in diﬀerent heating and cooling
scenarios in [Hviid and Petersen, 2014]. The same tendencies are seen with high air
speeds close to the occupants (around 0.2 m/s) for the winter scenarios and high air
speeds (0.3 m/s) away from the occupants in the summer conditions. There is also a
scenario with a radiator, with prevents the downwards ﬂow from the cold window.
Around the occupants the local air change index was around 90 %, which means that
there there must be supplied 11.1 % more air to have the same ventilation rate as a
ventilation system with ideal mixing.
This study showed high air speeds (above 0.3 m/s) for rooms with a high ceiling. The
same results are found at [Zhang et al., 2016].
[Zhang et al., 2016] also investigates the placement of heat sources and the inﬂuence it
has on the air speeds in the room. [Zhang et al., 2016] ﬁnds a more clear connection
between the placement of the heat sources and the high air speeds, which is, that the
high air speeds, are located away from the heat sources. The diﬀerence in the results
may be because of the diﬀerent placement of the in- and outlet. [Zhang et al., 2016] use
one in- and outlet, which is placed opposite each other, while this study use two in- and
outlets placed on the same wall. This gives diﬀerent temperature distributions at the
==PAGE:102==
86

DC, that might also inﬂuence the air speeds in the room. The heat sources (occupants)
in [Zhang et al., 2016] are also placed closer than in this study.
7.5
Laboratory
The simulations with the modiﬁed inlet did not show a signiﬁcant improvement com-
pared to the reference simulation even though it was expected to. The reason might
be that the modiﬁed inlet is to simpliﬁed, and does not remove the dynamic pressure
eﬀectively.
Other designed solutions will give better results, which is a more evenly
distributed air in the laboratory, that will give higher air change eﬃciency and better
comfort in the occupied zone.
The results showed the highest air change eﬃciency for the simulations with the highest
pressure drop at the middle part of the ceiling. This installation will result in a slightly
increased energy cost at the ventilation system corresponding to a pressure increase at
around 9 Pa.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
8.1
Plenum
The results showed that it is possible to create a uniform air distribution across the
ceiling.
The in- and outlets should be placed in the middle of the wall, because it gives the
lowest counterﬂow and the most uniform distribution of the inlet air. The temperature
diﬀerences at the bottom of the DC are also lowest at these positions.
To avoid counterﬂow from the inlet the distance from the inlet to the DC can be in-
creased. An increased pressure drop will give the same result.
8.2
Micro-jet
For ventilation rates below 6 l/s/m2 no critical micro-jets are seen for any DC types. At
higher ventilation rates, it is recommended to make the DC with diﬀuse ceiling plates
or with a vertical direction of the crack air ﬂow. DC plates with no cracks and the
alu-plate system, showed no and micro-jets below 0.05 m for ventilation rates up to 16
l/s/m2 respectively.
The length of the micro-jets varies with the crack size. At low ventilation rates a small
crack gives the longest micro-jet and at higher ventilation rates a bigger crack gives a
longer micro-jet.

Chapter 8. Conclusion
8.3
KPIs
It is possible to make KPIs for DCV. The heat loads placed diﬀerent locations in the
landscape oﬃce showed that the DR at the occupants where 3-4 % lower than in the
occupied zone for all scenarios.
At higher internal gains the diﬀerence between the highest DR in the occupied zone and
at the occupants is larger, which is because the heat gains produce air ﬂows going up to
the cold ceiling.
8.4
Oﬃces
The locations of the heat sources inﬂuence the air speeds in the occupied zone.
In
general the air speeds are located away from the occupants, but in some scenarios the
high air speeds are at the outer area of the heat sources. Other parameters such as the
temperature distribution at the plenum and total heat ﬂux from the heat source will
also inﬂuence the air speeds.
The simulations with diﬀerent room heights showed similar results for the PMV/PPD
and air change eﬃciency. The air speed at 0.1 m above the ﬂoor changed according to
the room height. The DR is increased with the room height and at a room height of 3.5
and 4.2 m the air speeds was above 0.3 m/s 0.1 m from the ﬂoor.
8.5
Laboratory
The design of the plenum, design of the inlet and pressure drop coeﬃcient inﬂuences the
air distribution in the laboratory. From the results it can be seen that the lowest local
air change index at the tables is 0.4/40 %.
The results show that it is possible to deliver an even air distribution if the pressure
drop coeﬃcient is increased at the DC, this scenario also gave the highest air change
eﬃciency for the laboratory.

8.6
Future research
This study showed that the air change eﬃciency was around 50 % or ideal mixing. This
is about the same as for traditional mixing ventilation with a diﬀuser [CR-1752, 1998].
A comparison between DCV and mixing ventilation with a diﬀuser in relation to the
air change eﬃciency would be interesting to investigate. It should test diﬀerent inlet
temperatures and diﬀerent ventilation rates. The higher air change eﬃciency the lower
ventilation rate is needed.
Another interesting approach could be to test diﬀerent outlet positions (also in the
ceiling) and test the local air change rate in the occupied zone. This could be made with
CFD or in full-scale experiments with CO2 emitters from dummy occupants.
The highest DR in the occupied zone was observed close to the inlets. These should be
placed away from the occupants. A study which resulted in guidelines for placement of
the inlet (and outlet) would be a useful tool for designers.
