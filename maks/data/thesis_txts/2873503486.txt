Abstract
The expansion of offshore wind farms (OWFs) in European waters raises questions about
their potential impacts on marine ecosystems and commercial fisheries. Trawlers operating in
the Gulf of Lion are among the fleets potentially affected. This study develops an agent-based
model (ABM) to simulate trawler behaviour and fishing effort distribution under different
OWF scenarios, integrating spatially explicit biomass, regulatory constraints, operational
limits, and behavioural patterns derived from workshops with experts and literature on
trawling practices. Observed fishing strategies were synthesised into key operational rules
and formalised as decision-making algorithms, allowing the model to capture daily routines,
species targeting, and spatial navigation choices.
Simulations for the 2034–2036 period revealed that OWF exclusion zones prompted local
redistributions of effort, often concentrated along the perimeters of the wind farms, driven
by navigation patterns rather than perceived ecological effects. Harbours closest to OWFs
experienced slight increases in the average distance from harbour to the first fishing ground
(≤0.1 km) and modest reductions in the number of trawl hauls performed per day, while
more distant harbours reallocated effort closer to shore. These changes had little effect on
total catch but could lead to the loss of areas of cultural or operational importance to fishers.
Beyond these spatial results, the study establishes a methodology for integrating social
science data on trawling fisheries into ecosystem modelling in the Gulf of Lion, paving the
way for coupling a fleet dynamics model with an Ecospace trophic model. Such integration
will improve the capacity to assess the cumulative impacts of offshore renewable energy
development in the Mediterranean Sea.

Introduction
1.1
Offshore wind energy and its deployment in the Mediterranean
Sea
In the context of climate change, the increasing demand for sustainable energy sources
is driving the expansion of Offshore Wind Farms (OWFs) worldwide. France has outlined
its national objectives in several strategic policy documents, notably the Programmation
Pluriannuelle de l’Énergie (PPE) (Ministère Transition Écologique et Solidarité, 2019), the
French Energy and Climate Strategy (Ministère de la transition énergétique, 2023), and
the Government–Industry Pact on Offshore Wind Energy (Gouvernement, 2022). These
documents define key milestones for 2028, 2030, and 2035, as well as long-term scenarios for
2050.
Among the main targets are the commissioning of seven OWFs by 2030 and an average
annual deployment of 2 GW from 2025 onwards. The Government–Industry Pact formalises
the State’s commitment to scaling up offshore wind development, with a specific objective of
reaching 18 GW of installed capacity by 2035.
Offshore development in the Mediterranean Sea is not as advanced as on the Atlantic and
Channel coasts, primarily due to greater depths that necessitate floating wind technologies
(RWE, 2025).

1.2
Fisheries in the Gulf of Lion
The GoL hosts a culturally and economically important fishing sector (Bechec, 2023).
The fleet is composed of a wide range of métiers, including small-scale coastal fisheries (petits
métiers) and larger industrial activities such as bottom trawling and tuna seining. The petits
métiers segment is composed of vessels generally less than 12 meters in length, often operating
close to shore and using passive gears such as nets, traps, and lines (Bechec, 2023; Ifremer,
2018). In contrast, trawlers typically use bottom or pelagic trawl nets and operate further
offshore, often in deeper waters and over longer trips. Tuna seiners represent another distinct
métier, which is highly regulated with quotas regarding the size and number of individuals,
seasonal closures and fishing licenses (Légifrance, 2024).
The region is governed by a mix of formal and traditional institutions. Three recognised
Producer Organisations (Organisation de Producteurs (OP)) are active in the Gulf and are
responsible for managing quotas, coordinating landings, and implementing certain aspects of
the Common Fisheries Policy on behalf of their members (Ministère de l’Agriculture et de la
Souveraineté alimentaire, 2025).

==PAGE:12==

Alongside these institutional structures, the Mediterranean coast is also home to
prud’homies, traditional fishers’ collectives with medieval origins that remain active to-
day (Blayo, 2021; Decugis, 2015). Each prud’homie is associated with a coastal city and
exercises localised governance over its maritime territory. Their roles include the allocation of
fishing zones and quotas among registered members, resolution of conflicts between fishers, and
the application of internal rules and sanctions. Registration with the prud’homie is mandatory
for patrons-pêcheurs ("owner-operators", meaning fishers that own their vessel), who must also
pay a contribution to be allowed to fish under its jurisdiction. The prud’hommes themselves
are experienced fishers, elected by their peers, and recognised for their deep knowledge of the
sea and fishing practices (Blayo, 2021; Decugis, 2015).
In the context of offshore wind development, the métiers most likely to be directly
affected are those practiced beyond the coastal zone, such as trawlers and tuna seiners, as
they operate in offshore zones overlapping with future wind farm areas. In contrast, the
petits métiers, whose activity is generally restricted to the 3-nautical-mile coastal band, may
be less exposed to direct spatial impacts. However, indirect effects may still emerge due
to displacement of effort, changes in access to port infrastructure, or cascading effects on
resource availability and social dynamics within the fishing community (Bechec, 2023; Projet
PROSPECT, 2021).
Anticipating such complex interactions requires integrated modelling approaches that
consider both ecological and human dimensions. Improving the representation of fishing
activities — and how they might evolve in response to Offshore Renewable Energies (ORE)
deployment — is essential to more accurately anticipate the cumulative impacts on marine
ecosystems. Understanding how spatial exclusions or environmental disturbances interact
with pre-existing pressures — such as overfishing, climate change, or habitat degradation
— requires models that can integrate both ecological feedbacks and behavioural plasticity.
Exploring more realistic representations of fisher behaviour within ecosystem models can
therefore improve our understanding of the complex dynamics at play in socio-ecological
systems.
1.3
Trophic models as tools to assess the ecological impact of
offshore wind farms
Modelling trophic interactions, in particular, is central to assessing ecosystem responses
to such pressures. These models require complex datasets and detailed knowledge of local
biodiversity and human-induced pressures. At France Energies Marines (FEM), researchers
have developed spatialised ecosystem models along several French coasts using the Ecopath
with Ecosim (EwE) and Ecospace suite (Christensen and Walters, 2004; Polovina, 1984).
These models allow the simulation of trophic dynamics and the evaluation of different
scenarios, including the deployment of ORE infrastructure.
In the GoL, the GOLEM model (Seyer et al., 2023; Seyer, 2024) — developed by Thomas
Seyer and currently used by France Énergies Marines — is one such spatialised trophic model,
parametrised for the local ecosystem. It simulates biomass, energy flows, and fishing activities
across a 12,172 km2 area of the continental shelf. The model includes 68 functional groups,
Page 3 of 38
==PAGE:13==
Master’s Thesis – Lise Dulieu
representing the major components of the local food web — from primary producers to marine
top predators such as dolphins and pelagic sharks. It incorporates seven fishing gear types
and reflects the average state of the ecosystem over the period 2010–2019, following major
changes in species composition and size structure (Colloca et al., 2017; Hidalgo et al., 2022).
The model was calibrated with extensive time series from scientific surveys and fisheries
statistics.
However, this modelling approach has notable limitations. In Ecospace, effort allocation
is based on a gravity model, although the exact drivers — such as catch rates, prices, or
travel costs — depend on the input data provided (Walters et al., 1999; de Mutsert et al.,
2024). Empirical studies suggest that fishers’ behaviour is influenced by many other factors,
including risk avoidance (e.g. bycatch or gear conflicts), cultural attachment to specific fishing
grounds, historical fidelity to locations, social norms, and perceived — not just expected —
revenues (Poos and Rijnsdorp, 2007; van Putten et al., 2012; Wijermans et al., 2020). These
socio-cognitive dimensions are difficult to capture in a purely gravity-based framework and
reduce the model’s ability to simulate adaptive behaviour under novel or uncertain conditions,
such as the spatial exclusion zones created by offshore wind farms (Walters et al., 1999).
Some methodological workarounds have been proposed, such as using species preference
indices based on more factors than profitability in lieu of the selling price in the gravity
equation, or modifying the sailing cost map based on historical effort rather than only
geographical distance to ports (Christensen and Walters, 2024). These adjustments can
improve the representation of fishing effort in Ecospace, but they are not designed to fully
capture behavioural and socio-economic drivers. Because Ecospace models rely on spatially
and temporally explicit data, they benefit from frequent updates to reflect changing patterns
of effort and abundance, which can be challenging when high-resolution datasets are limited.
In this context, complementary approaches such as Agent-Based Models (ABMs) can
improve the fishing component by explicitly modelling fisher behaviour and socio-economic
dynamics, thereby providing additional perspectives that can be coupled with trophic models.
1.4
Integrating social sciences into fisheries modelling
Integrating social data into fisheries modelling remains a persistent challenge. Despite
increasing recognition of the importance of fishers’ behaviours, values, and knowledge, qualita-
tive insights are often used only for post hoc validation or adaptation of quantitative models.
Decision-making still relies primarily on ecological and economic indicators, usually developed
by natural science teams with limited incorporation of behavioural science perspectives
(Burgess et al., 2020).
One recurring difficulty lies in translating information from interviews and field obser-
vations into variables that can be used in numerical models. Schlüter et al. (2017) offer
a conceptual framework to address this issue, summarising fishers’ behaviours into four
categories: Motivation (e.g. income satisfaction vs. profit maximisation), Ability (skills,
access to capital or knowledge), Livelihoods (degree of dependency or diversification), and
Social Interactions (such as imitation, information sharing, or avoidance).
Several models have incorporated these dimensions in different ways. Garavito-Bermúdez
and Boonstra (2023) simulated satisfaction thresholds as a driver of effort, while Bastardie

group strategies or social norms. These studies demonstrate that moving beyond simplistic
assumptions of rational profit-maximising agents can improve the predictive and interpretative
power of models — especially when used in policy contexts.
1.5
Agent-based modelling for socio-ecosystem dynamics
ABMs are bottom-up simulation tools centred around individual decisions. In fisheries
modelling, agents can be a fishing vessel, a fisher, or even a groups of fish when looking more
in depth into the biological components (Shin and Cury, 2004). Decision-making processes
are explicitly coded, that way each agent makes — at each time step — its own decision and
actions, by considering their environment, goals, and personal strategies. It is particularly
interesting when looking at social interactions. In an ABM, agents can interact, retain and
share information, and effectively learn with time, and adapt to their environment. Groups of
trust or distrust can be formed, and individual decisions can lead to global patterns depending
on the agents’ strategies.
While ABMs exist for many fisheries, for example the German North Sea (Letschert
et al., 2025), Swedish Baltic Sea (Wijermans et al., 2020), the Danish fleet: North Sea,
Skagerrak, Kattegat and Baltic Sea (Bastardie et al., 2013), or in the Australian Coral reef
Fin Fish fishery (Little et al., 2009), no existing ABM was identified in the Gulf of Lion.
As there is no wide-spread software such as the Ecopath suite, and motivations as well as
expected outputs for creating an ABM vary, the methods used to create the models (coding
language, social data gathering, etc.) also vary. And so with them the inputs necessary, and
the parameters used for calibrating the agents’ actions.
In this work, an ABM was developed to simulate the behaviour of individual fishing
vessels in the Gulf of Lion, integrating spatially explicit environmental, regulatory and socio-
economic data. As offshore wind energy projects expand in this region, particularly through
floating wind farms, there is growing concern about how such infrastructure may affect the
spatial dynamics of fishing activities. Yet, limited empirical data is currently available on
how fishers might respond to exclusion zones or changes in accessibility around offshore wind
farms. This presents a significant knowledge gap in the context of marine spatial planning.
Agent-based modelling offers a useful framework to explore these potential responses
by explicitly representing individual decision-making processes. While such models rely on
assumptions and simplifications, they allow the incorporation of contextual knowledge — such
as existing spatial patterns, regulatory constraints, and inferred behavioural responses — to
simulate plausible adaptation strategies. In doing so, they can help anticipate indirect effects
of offshore developments on both fishers and the ecosystem, and support a more integrated
approach to managing marine space.
This study aims to explore how spatial restrictions associated with offshore wind farms
could influence the behaviour of trawlers operating in the Gulf of Lion, and to what extent
such effects can be captured and analysed through an agent-based approach.

2
Research objectives and scope
This study aims to integrate socio-cultural information on fishers in the Gulf of Lion
into an ABM of fleet dynamics, in order to assess the potential impacts of OWF development
on fishing effort allocation and catch. This work contributes to bridging a methodological
gap in ecosystem-based management by incorporating behavioural and social heterogeneity
into spatialised fisheries models.
Focus was given on the allocation of fishing effort, both in time and space. The model
simulates individual vessels that make daily decisions to fish or not, and if so, where to
operate, depending on environmental, economic, and socio-cultural variables.
The main objectives of this project are as follows:
• Identify a fleet of interest that targets one or a few species and is expected to be
directly impacted by OWF development in the Gulf of Lion. This simplifies the case
study and focuses the analysis.
• Translate social and cultural characteristics of the fleet into quantifiable model
variables and rules of behaviour, using information from anthropological interviews and
relevant literature.
• Develop an Agent-Based Model that simulates daily fishing decisions and spatial
effort allocation across the Gulf of Lion, accounting for social preferences and constraints
as well as technical and regulatory ones.
• Integrate spatio-temporal environmental and fisheries data (e.g. fishing zones,
ports, stock layers, wave conditions, trawl percentages, etc.) to ensure that the model
reflects realistic constraints and variability.
• Simulate scenarios with and without OWF to evaluate changes in fishing effort
patterns and potential redistribution or exclusion effects.
• Present results to stakeholders and experts, including fishers, to validate the
realism of the simulated dynamics and refine the assumptions for future developments.
Ultimately, the model and the method developed to translate qualitative social data
into a computational framework will be made available to France Énergies Marines. The
goal is to provide a foundational case study that identifies both methodological opportunities
and challenges in integrating socio-economic information into spatially explicit fisheries and
ecosystem models.

3
Materials and methods
3.1
Fleet definition and selection
This project was conducted in close collaboration with an anthropologist, whose mission
at France Énergies Marines (FEM) is to redefine the concept of “fleet” beyond technical
or administrative classifications. Inspired by Boonstra and Hentati-Sundberg (2016) and
Wijermans et al. (2020)’s fishing styles, their objective is to study the fishing community in
the GoL and to identify groups of fishers who share values, behavioural traits, and fishing
strategies.
As their work is embedded in a longer-term project at FEM, and since their full results
were not available by the end of this study, only partial information and selected findings
from field interviews could be integrated. To complement this, a combination of grey and
scientific literature, SIH reports, and interviews with entities involved in the professional
fisheries field was used to document the fleets present in the Gulf of Lion, including their
gear types, target species, catch profiles, and quotas.
Since the transversal definition of the Gulf of Lion fishing fleets developed by the
anthropologist was not yet available for use in the model, the choice of which group to
represent was guided by literature review and expert consultation. The fleet to be modelled
needed to be directly affected by the construction of offshore wind farms, target a limited
number of species or species groups, practise few métiers, and have sufficient data available
on both the fleet itself and the targeted resources.
Applying these criteria led to the exclusion of petits métiers, whose diversity of gears,
target species, and fishing strategies would make them difficult to schematise into consistent
fishing styles within the time frame of this project. In contrast, trawlers showed relatively
homogeneous behaviour across the fleet and met all the required criteria. They were therefore
selected as the fleet to be modelled in this study.
3.2
Selected fleet: trawlers
3.2.1
Characteristics of trawling vessels
Trawling vessels are large fishing boats, usually between 18 to 25 meters in length in the
Mediterranean Sea (Ifremer, 2024; TMT and IMCS Network, 2023). They typically operate
between the 3-nautical-mile (NM) coastal limit and the outer continental shelf. Trawlers use
towed nets, which may be deployed in mid-water (pelagic trawling) to target small pelagic
species such as sardines and anchovies, or along the seafloor (bottom trawling) to catch
demersal and benthic species (Figure 3).
In France’s Mediterranean fleet classification, trawling gears are codified by SIH under
the EU-standard gear codes:
• Bottom Otter Trawl (OTB): This is the most common gear type used by demersal
trawlers in the Gulf of Lion. It consists of a single conical net towed along the seabed,
held open horizontally by otter boards (trawl doors) and vertically by floats and weights.
Page 7 of 38
==PAGE:17==
Master’s Thesis – Lise Dulieu
(a) Bottom trawl
(b) Midwater trawl
Figure 3: Examples of Trawling Nets – from NOAA (2022)
It targets a wide variety of demersal fish and benthic species, such as red mullet, hake,
and sole.
• Twin Otter Trawl (OTT): A variant of OTB, this setup uses two nets towed side by
side, with each net held open by its own set of doors. Twin trawling increases the swept
area and can enhance catch rates, particularly for species that are patchily distributed.
This gear is typically used by larger vessels operating in deeper waters.
• Bottom Pair Trawl (PTB): This gear consists of a single bottom trawl net towed by two
vessels (a pair). The horizontal opening of the net is achieved by the distance between
the boats, eliminating the need for trawl doors. Although less common, some vessels in
the Gulf of Lion use this technique, especially when targeting dense aggregations of
demersal species.
• Midwater Otter Trawl (OTM) and Pelagic Trawl Midwater (PTM): These codes
correspond to pelagic trawling gears. OTM involves a single pelagic net towed by one
vessel using doors to maintain the horizontal opening, while PTM refers to a similar
net towed by two vessels. These gears are used by a small number of vessels in the Gulf
of Lion targeting pelagic species like sardines and anchovy, mainly in winter and spring
when these species form dense shoals.
Each gear type is chosen based on the vessel’s technical characteristics, crew experience,
targeted species, and local seabed morphology. In practice, most trawlers in the region use
OTB as their primary gear, occasionally switching to OTT or PTB during specific seasons or
for certain target species (Mihanovic, 2022).
In this study, all fishing effort and catches from OTB, OTT and PTB will be assimilated
as bottom trawling, and all fishing effort and catches from OTM and PTM will be assimilated
as pelagic trawling. The data extracted from SIH reports and construct and parameterise
GOLEM corresponds to the areas MT11, MT12, MT21, MD11, MD12, MD21, MLGO and
MLGE (Figure 4).

3.2.2
Legislation and fishing practices
In the gulf of Lion, trawling vessels embark for daily sea trips, limited to a maximum
of 15 hours at sea, though some vessels benefit from a derogation allowing up to 18 hours
(CNPEM, 2024). Fishing is prohibited within 3 NM of the coast under French legislation
dating back to 1983 (Code rural et de la pêche maritime, Article R. 922-42, Legifrance
(2015)), and further restrictions apply in protected habitats: bottom trawling is banned
over Posidonia oceanica meadows and coral reefs, particularly within Natura 2000 marine
protected areas, which often overlap with the 3 NM coastal zone.
In 2023, 42 bottom trawling vessels were registered in the Mediterranean sea, most of
which operate in the Gulf of Lion, and a few in Corsica. Only 3 of them were recorded
to practice pelagic trawling in 2023 (Ifremer, 2024). This sector of fisheries is currently
facing major structural changes, leading to considerable uncertainty regarding its future, and
widespread mistrust toward both scientific institutions and European regulations. Trawlers
operate under increasing pressure from dwindling fish stocks and ever-tightening restrictions,
which together threaten the economic sustainability of their activity (Vaca, 2024; Wendling
and Bãnaru, 2025).
In the Mediterranean sea, trawling effort is primarily regulated through effort quotas: a
limitation of days at sea, and through minimum individual landing sizes, rather than catch
Page 9 of 38
==PAGE:19==
Master’s Thesis – Lise Dulieu
volumes. The only species with individual Total Allowable Catch (TAC) quotas are the
Atlantic bluefin tuna and the Mediterranean spearfish , which are targeted by longliners
rather than trawlers (ICCAT, 2025). These regulatory constraints significantly affect how
trawlers organise their activity — from the strategic use of their effort quota (defined as a
maximum number of fishing days per year) to the geographic distribution of effort and the
associated fuel costs needed to reach permitted areas.
A central component of these constraints is the WestMED Multiannual Plan, adopted in
2019 and implemented between 2020 and 2025, which restructured the spatial and temporal
organisation of trawling activity in the Gulf of Lion. The plan includes seasonal closures in two
areas: the 90–100 m isobath zone (closed from September to May) and the GFCM “CGPM
box” (closed from November to May), both intended to protect the stocks of European hake
and red mullet.
3.3
Implementation of the agent-based model
3.3.1
Programming environment: Python and Mesa
The model was developed using Python 3.11.9 and the Mesa framework 3.4.2 (Ter Hoeven
et al., 2025), chosen for its flexibility and compatibility with other models used by FEM.
Mesa allows for agent-based modelling using Python language, in a similar way to
Netlogo (Wilensky, 1999). It is an object-oriented framework that comes with an integrated
system of classes and inheritance, as well as spatial grids and dynamic visualisation.
The model simulates the fishing effort in a 2D environment, with agents representing
different groups within the fishing community. The fishing zones, the OWF zones, and other
relevant geographic features are included in the spatial grid. Several target species are also
represented, with use biomass data inputs from France Energies Marines’ existing models.
3.3.2
Architecture of the agent-based model
The model aims at representing the trawling fleet in the Gulf of Lion. The spatial grid,
extracted from GOLEM, encompasses the Gulf, from the shore to the canyons limited by the
isobaths 200 m (the fishing effort is generally limited to the continental shelf and almost no
vessels fish in the canyons). The cells are square with a 2.5 km side length. Agents represent
fishing vessels, which can practice either bottom trawling, or mixed trawling (both bottom
and pelagic). Fishing agents are given a set of parameters, including a fishing efficiency per
metier, as well as a harbour, a cruising and a trawling speed, etc. The harbours’ coordinates
were extracted from GOLEM and adjusted to place them as accurately as possible on shore.
Spatial closures mentioned above, such as the 3 nautical miles zones, or the WestMED zones
are represented. These areas were adapted from their continuous polygon format to a discrete
grid — a cell was considered inside the area if more that half it’s surface was inside the
polygon (Figure 5).

Fishers
If they decide to fish on a given day, mixed trawlers face an additional choice compared
to bottom trawlers. At the start of each week, they decide whether they will operate using
bottom trawling or pelagic trawling for the week. Once this choice is made— and for bottom
trawlers as well—the next step is to determine where to fish. This decision-making process
is summarised in Figure 8. Each day, when selecting their target fishing grounds, they go
through the following sequence of decisions:
• Seasonal Context: The fisher first determines the current time of year. The calendar
month is converted into a quarterly period (Q1 to Q4), as fishing opportunities vary by
season.
• Identify Possible Fishing Targets: Based on the current quarter, the fisher consults
a predefined list of possible target species and associated depth ranges, with one or
two species per option. This seasonal targeting strategy was defined in consultation
with various members of the fishing industry. The association between species and
season was based on catch data reported in SIH quarterly summaries. Sardines were
included in the model but were not listed among the target species, as their stock has
been declining and very few individuals currently reach the minimum landing size. This
decision was also made following discussions with stakeholders, who confirmed that
sardine fishing is now largely avoided in practice due to these constraints.
The depth range at which each species is assumed to occur was derived from GOLEM’s
species functional response curves to bathymetry, by extracting the interquartile range.
Species targeted during the same season and sharing overlapping depth ranges were
grouped in pairs and included in the list of possible targets.
• Choose a Target: A single combination of species and depth range is randomly
selected among the available options for the current season.
Page 13 of 38
==PAGE:23==
Master’s Thesis – Lise Dulieu
• Look for a Fishing Spot: The fisher searches the grid for suitable locations and
selects one randomly within the cells that:
– are within the acceptable distance from port,
– fall within the selected depth range,
– are fishable and unoccupied.
• Consider Preferred Zones: When the WestMED conservation zones are not closed to
bottom trawling, the fisher gives priority to these specific zones, as they have historically
been productive for regionally preferred species such as red mullet and hake. If such
zones are available, there is a higher probability that the fisher will select a spot within
them.
• Go Fishing: Once a valid location is identified, the fisher moves to the spot to perform
fishing. If no suitable area is found, the fishing trip is skipped.

3.8
Calibration and validation
3.8.1
Behavioural model
In regards with the decisional trees and the assumptions made on the fisher agent’s
behaviour, a co-construction of the model with an active participation of fishers and entities
involved in the industry would have been preferable. However, the timing of this internship did
not allow for direct engagement with fishers. In the Gulf of Lion, the social climate remains
tense between the fishing and scientific communities, and building trust-based relationships
that enable meaningful information sharing takes time.
Several workshops were carried out as part of FEM’s projects, during which the model
was presented to various stakeholders of the industry, including research engineers in fisheries,
members of fishing committees, and members of producer organisations. This allowed for
discussion and partial validation of the model’s main behavioural assumptions.

and métier.

5
Discussion
The modelling approach implemented in the present study yielded realistic results in
terms of spatialised fish effort, while developed in a data constrained environment, and offered
a first approach at modelling Gulf of Lion fisheries centered on the human dimensions of
those dynamics.
5.1
Biological data integration and trophic model perspectives
The biological compartment of this model was developed to prepare for a future coupling
with an Ecospace model. As such, output data from the existing version of GOLEM were
used as inputs, albeit containing some approximations and known uncertainties. The version
used in this project is the result of a doctoral project and is currently undergoing revision at
France Énergies Marines. Compartments such as marine mammals and birds have since been
improved, and fishing pressure on certain species — notably octopuses — was identified as
being overestimated. No specific selling price was provided per species, which led Ecospace
to apply a default price of €1/kg to all taxa in its gravity model. This likely influenced the
spatial distribution of fishing effort in GOLEM, favouring areas where high-biomass species
occurred. The quality of input data is expected to improve with upcoming versions of the
model and additional field campaigns, which will provide more comprehensive and up-to-date
information to support the further development of trophic models.
Several elements of the ABM’s biological dynamics could be improved by coupling
it with a fully operational trophic model, in particular migration and dispersal processes.
In the meantime, the redistribution step used in the ABM’s monthly biomass updates —
described in subsection 3.6 — offered a pragmatic workaround that prevented artificial
biomass accumulation in inaccessible cells. Coupling with Ecospace would also introduce
stock regulation through trophic interactions, which could help limit the stock instability in
further developments of the model and support a more integrated socio-ecosystemic approach.
These biological assumptions also influence the spatial distribution of fishing effort and
catches. In the current implementation, catch is computed as a fixed proportion of the biomass
available in each cell (Equation 3.3). As a result, scenarios with higher biomass levels logically
lead to higher catches. This proportional relationship remains visible at the grid scale, as
shown in Figure 30 to Figure 40. For species such as anglerfish, where biomass increased
significantly compared to GOLEM values, the divergence in both biomass and catches between
scenarios also grew over time. Greater variability observed in harbours hosting fewer vessels
— e.g. Figure 33, Figure 39 — is likely due to stochastic effects amplified by small sample sizes.
As mentioned in section 2, all biomass present in the model is considered available for
fishing, provided the cell is accessible to fishers. This assumption may introduce limita-
tions, especially for species subject to minimum landing sizes. When stock levels fluctuate
considerably, the ratio of adults (i.e. legally fishable individuals) to juveniles also changes,
and the current formulation may overestimate catches. For more stable stocks, however,
Page 32 of 38
==PAGE:42==
Master’s Thesis – Lise Dulieu
this ratio remains relatively constant, and the fishing efficiency implicitly accounts for
the portion of biomass that is not catchable. This issue is particularly relevant in the
Mediterranean, where decreasing individual sizes — especially for small pelagic species —
have led to a growing proportion of biomass falling below legal landing sizes, even though
overall stock levels have not necessarily collapsed (Beauvieux et al., 2022; Brosset et al., 2016).
The focus of this study was not to provide definitive conclusions on stock viability or
catches in the context of OWF, but rather to use the biological compartment as a support
for developing the methodology. In this respect, the model fulfils its purpose. While similar
approaches have been explored elsewhere (Bourdaud et al., 2025), their application to the
GoL represents a novel step. Using Ecospace outputs as inputs for this ABM provides a first
implementation toward a fully integrated module within the EwE suite, while acceptable
simulation times — with room for further optimisation — support its relevance for scenario
analyses and future extensions.
Ultimately, it is the way fishers respond to these biological dynamics — through their
perceptions, choices of target species, and spatial strategies — that determines the distribution
of fishing effort. The next section therefore examines the behavioural component of the model
and its implications for simulating fleet dynamics in the GoL.
5.2
Perceptions, strategies, and redistribution of fishing effort
Although detailed empirical information on the daily decision-making of trawlers is
scarce, a synthetic behavioural pattern could be identified and translated into the model,
producing plausible reconstructions of fishing effort distribution. The information available
on this fleet did not allow the identification of clear processes of learning and adaptation
on a daily basis, which explains why such dynamics were not implemented in the present
model. How such adaptations occur, under which conditions and at which spatial and
temporal scales, remains an ongoing subject of research. It is nevertheless likely that fishers
progressively stop targeting stocks that are depleting or composed largely of individuals below
landing size, as has been observed for sardines in the Mediterranean (Basilone et al., 2023;
Colloca et al., 2017), and instead shift to new target species or fishing grounds — processes
that should be integrated into future versions of the model. For the small-scale métiers,
which will also be represented in upcoming developments, adaptability and polyvalence were
repeatedly emphasised during workshops, with fishers switching target species or gears over
short time scales depending on resource availability, regulations, or traditions. Capturing
such behaviours will require not only more detailed knowledge of the fishing community, but
also a biologically consistent trophic compartment and an economic component to account
for profitability and operational costs in fishers’ decision-making.
Most trawlers are owner-operators, directly responsible for both operating costs and
income. While their daily decisions may not be explicitly expressed in terms of profitability,
economic dynamics structure the activity on longer time scales. Incorporating such features
would allow the agents’ choices to be influenced by key factors such as fuel price — a major
Page 33 of 38
==PAGE:43==
Master’s Thesis – Lise Dulieu
share of trawlers’ expenses (Daurès et al., 2013; France 3 Occitanie, 2020) — and the selling
price of catches. In fish markets where trawlers sell part of their catch, auction sales typically
follow a descending-price system, where the initial price is set by the auction manager
according to species, quality, and market demand, before decreasing until a buyer accepts the
lot. These fluctuations can be substantial and influence choices of target species on different
temporal scales, potentially leading to inter-annual variability in landings. Incorporating this
dynamic, even partially, could strengthen the behavioural realism of the model by linking
target selection more closely to economic outcomes.
Going further, integrating economic viability at the vessel level would also make it
possible to explore scenarios where activity becomes unprofitable and vessels exit the fishery.
Such exits are already a reality: decommissioning schemes, organised by the government
every few years, provide financial compensation for permanent withdrawal from the fleet
(Ministère de la Mer, 2022). Considering that the trawling sector is in a vulnerable state and
has experienced a nearly continuous decline over the past two decades, adding this option
would be particularly relevant. It would not only reflect the current dynamics of the fleet but
also provide a framework to anticipate how future pressures — such as spatial restrictions,
rising fuel prices, or market fluctuations — might accelerate structural changes in the fishery
(Wendling and Bãnaru, 2025; Valérie Luxey, 2025; Itsaso Aurrekoetxea Jover, 2025).
While several modelling assumptions were likely responsible for the decrease in fishing
effort along the western coast compared to Jadaud et al. (2015), the model simulated
interesting and relevant emerging patterns. Reduction of effort along the North-Western
coast could be attributed to:
• The representation of trawling hauls as straight lines of fixed length.
• A data extraction issue that resulted in a reduced number of target species having
defined fishing grounds within the GoL. For species such as octopuses or anchovy,
the entirety of the grid was designated as potential fishing ground, removing any
spatial preference. Only mackerel had specific spatial targets, which led to an artificial
concentration of effort in its associated area (Figure 41). Considering the spatial
coverage of this target area, the impact of this issue was likely limited to the western
coast effort diminution observed, as well as a lack of actual seasonal preferences.
• The number of vessels used in the model was derived from the 2023 fleet (Ifremer,
2024), and remained constant throughout the simulation. The reduction of the fleet in
vessels number in the past decades could have affected harbours such as Port-Vendres
and Port-la-Nouvelle more than others, which could explain some of the differences
observed with real-world effort patterns (Jadaud et al., 2015).
Separately, it is worth noting that the model applied a simple avoidance rule at the
start of each fishing day, preventing agents from initiating fishing in the same cell. This
assumption captures a basic form of mutual avoidance but is only one possible formulation,
and testing the sensitivity of results to this parameter would be valuable given the low vessel
Page 34 of 38
==PAGE:44==
Master’s Thesis – Lise Dulieu
density relative to the study area.
Apart from the artefact on the North-Western coast, the reallocation of effort around
wind farms revealed important behavioural responses. When wind farm areas became inacces-
sible, any haul path crossing a restricted cell was removed from the list of available options,
thereby limiting access to surrounding zones. This constraint led fishers to redirect effort
toward adjacent areas, often along the wind farm’s perimeter. Interestingly, this behaviour
was not the result of an anticipated spillover effect (Bourdaud et al., 2025; Halouani et al.,
2020; Nogues et al., 2021), but rather of fishers’ daily routines: fisher agents try to fish while
returning to harbour, following a straight path from their fishing ground (see Figure 6).
When this trajectory is interrupted by a wind farm, they likely redirect their return route
to pass along its edge. This redistribution of effort therefore reflects operational constraints
and navigation routines, rather than any deliberate response to biomass increase around the
wind farms.
These adaptations in space also had small but measurable effects on fishing performance.
Harbours such as Agde and Port-la-Nouvelle, located near the wind farms, experienced
a slight increase in average fishing distance and a minor decrease in the number of daily
hauls performed. Both harbours seemed to reallocate their effort further South and East
(Figure 16). On the other hand, harbours such as Marseille and Port-de-Bouc, for which
the wind farms were installed in the furthest part of their usual fishing grounds, reallocated
their effort to closer areas (Figure 16), which resulted in a slight increase of the average
number of hauls they could perform in a day. These effects remain modest but could become
more pronounced with the deployment of larger farms projected for 2050 (Éoliennes en mer,
2025b).
While results suggest an increase in available biomass and catch when wind farms are
installed, it is important to note that the development of OWF may also lead to the loss of
preferential fishing areas that hold cultural, traditional, or symbolic value for fishers. Such
dimensions are not directly reflected in the model outputs but they play a crucial role in how
fishers perceive spatial restrictions and in the acceptability of new developments (Utne-Palm
et al., 2025).
Workshop discussions also highlighted that some of the behavioural hypotheses imple-
mented in the model required adjustment. Fisher agents were initially modelled to attempt
4 hauls per day, with backup strategies allowing them to complete 3 if the fourth was not
feasible. However, one participant pointed out that this figure was unrealistic, noting that
trawlers rarely perform more than 3 hauls per day, with the average usually between 2 and 3.
The discussion underlined the methodological value of involving fishers in the co-construction
of the model, as their knowledge helps align assumptions with actual practices. Similarly,
the definition of target fishing grounds could also be refined. In the present implementation,
these were based on the fleet’s fundamental niche, but the realised niche — better accessible
through trophic modelling of the ecosystem — would likely better reflect fishers’ ecological
knowledge and preferences (Garavito-Bermúdez and Boonstra, 2023). Nonetheless, the mod-
Page 35 of 38
==PAGE:45==
Master’s Thesis – Lise Dulieu
elling approach developed in this project provides a finer spatial attribution of fleet-specific
fishing effort than what Ecospace can typically achieve. In Ecospace, distance from harbour is
included in the gravity-based cell attractiveness calculation, but it is computed as a straight
line that disregards landmasses and access constraints (de Mutsert et al., 2024). Although
distance maps can be provided as a workaround, these remain static inputs. Moreover,
Ecospace evaluates each cell independently, without accounting for spatial continuity or
coherent fishing movements. As a result, it cannot capture patterns such as towing along
consistent directions, local preferences for unobstructed areas, or other sequential behaviours.
Implementing these patterns would require extensive pre-processing, often beyond the in-
tended scope of the model. By contrast, the agent-based approach incorporates them directly
through individual-level decisions, path planning, and operational routines, allowing more
realistic and emergent spatial patterns of effort to appear — even when knowledge of fishers’
behaviour remains limited.
5.3
Scenario analysis and implications for fleet dynamics
The scenarios chosen offer a prospective take at the horizon 2040, focusing on the OWFs
already in the planning.Future legislation governing fishing activity in these areas is not
yet known, and may introduce further restrictions such as speed or vessel size limits. In
parallel, many vessels are currently equipped with oversized engines, leading to unnecessary
fuel consumption and higher emissions. This not only increases operational costs but also
undermines the environmental performance of the fleet (Bechec, 2023; France Filière Pêche,
2015). Exploring different adaptations in engine sizes, characterised in the model as cruising
and towing speeds, and fuel consumption if this parameter is included in further developments,
could be explored in future scenarios to better assess their implications.
Broader structural changes are already reshaping the trawling sector. In 2016, while
the trawling fleet represented only 5% of the total number of vessels and nearly 12% of
fishers at sea, it supplied between 85 and 90% of fresh seafood landings in the region. With
around 10,000 tonnes landed annually — equivalent to €35 million, trawling accounted for
75% of the turnover of fish auctions (Médieu, 2016). The potential decline of this activity
therefore has consequences that extend well beyond the fleet itself, raising questions about the
redistribution of effort and catches to other métiers, as well as the functioning of fish auctions
that depend heavily on trawled landings (Decressonnière, 2025; Médieu, 2016). Some stake-
holders argue that maintaining a minimum number of trawlers is necessary for the economic
equilibrium of the region, while others stress that a transition toward less intensive fishing
methods is not only possible but inevitable to ensure stock viability (Decressonnière, 2025;
Itsaso Aurrekoetxea Jover, 2025). These calls for transition are closely linked to longstanding
environmental critiques of trawling, which is widely denounced for its low selectivity and
significant impacts on the seabed (Decressonnière, 2025; Stergiou et al., 2003). As a result,
bottom trawlers are caught at the crossroads of environmental regulation and socio-economic
dependency: on the one hand, conservation strategies — such as the WestMED Plan — have
reduced their fishing time and access to traditional grounds, while on the other hand, coastal
communities relying on trawling often have few viable alternatives, fuelling tensions and
Page 36 of 38
==PAGE:46==
Master’s Thesis – Lise Dulieu
mistrust toward scientific institutions and European regulations (Wendling and Bãnaru, 2025).
One of the most prospective assumptions made in the model was therefore the contin-
uation of the WestMED Plan beyond 2025. While a second phase is under discussion, its
precise content and spatial restrictions are not yet known. Seasonal closures are unlikely to
be abandoned altogether, but their extent and timing could change, and new measures could
be introduced. Combined with the projected expansion of offshore wind farms in the Gulf of
Lion (Éoliennes en mer, 2025b), these uncertainties illustrate the difficulty of anticipating the
future of the fleet. The cumulative effect of spatial restrictions, fuel costs, stock fluctuations,
and regulatory change may accelerate structural transformations in the sector (Bechec, 2023;
Itsaso Aurrekoetxea Jover, 2025). Taken together, these elements emphasise that a wide
range of scenarios could be considered when assessing the future of Mediterranean trawling,
and that exploring such trajectories will require modelling approaches able to capture both
fleet dynamics and cross-métier interactions.

6
Conclusion
This thesis developed an agent-based model to represent the behaviour of trawlers
in the Gulf of Lion and explore the potential impacts of offshore wind farms on fishing
effort allocation. Despite operating in a data-limited environment, the model reproduced
spatially plausible fishing patterns and revealed how operational constraints, such as haul
routines and navigation paths, can drive emergent redistribution of effort around exclusion
zones. Importantly, the approach provided insight into how fishers’ strategies are shaped
by a combination of routines, regulations, and contextual factors, including environmental
conditions and operational constraints.
The methodology established here allowed the integration of social science data on fishing
practices into spatially explicit modelling frameworks, providing a basis for coupling fleet
dynamics models with trophic models such as Ecospace. This integration would improve the
capacity to assess cumulative impacts of offshore renewable energy development and other
spatial pressures. Regular, direct exchanges with the fishing sector, including fishers and
their representatives, will be essential to refine the behavioural assumptions of the fishers and
strengthen the robustness of the modelling framework. At the same time, these interactions
can help bridge the gap between scientific and fishing communities, encouraging mutual
understanding and creating the basis for collaborative tools to assess cumulative impacts in
the future.
