Abstract
In the context of indoor environments, the hue-heat hypothesis states that building oc-
cupants’ thermal responses are affected by visual stimuli. Recent studies have found
that daylight transmitted through colored glazing affects thermal response: blue colored
daylight induces lower thermal sensation in the summer, while orange colored daylight
induced higher thermal sensation in the winter. Applying these findings to energy con-
sumption, it could thus be assumed that the heating set-point temperature could be re-
duced in a winter scenario, and the cooling set-point temperature could be increased in
a summer scenario, maintaining the same levels of thermal comfort. This thesis investi-
gates the effect of set-point temperature change and the use of colored filters on annual
energy consumption in a single family house. The goal was to understand if the hue-heat
hypothesis could be used to reduce the total annual energy consumption in a heating sce-
nario in Denmark and in a cooling scenario in Spain as well as the key parameters that
affect this theory. Two models were built in the software IDA ICE: a single family house
and a reference room. Blue and orange filters were applied to the windows, increasing
or reducing the set-point temperature respectively when the filters were in use. Annual
energy consumption was analyzed and studied, comparing the scenarios with no filter to
the scenarios with filter. The results showed no annual energy savings from the orange
filter in the Copenhagen winter scenario due to the reduction in solar gains produced by
the filter. Annual energy reduction was found in the Madrid summer scenario due to the
double effect of the hue-heat hypothesis and the reduction in solar gains produced by the
blue filter. Of the parameters studied, location, orientation, window size, wall type, filter
properties and glazing type were found to have significant influence on the performance
of hue-heat hypothesis. Lighting profiles also had an impact on the performance of the
theory, albeit lower than other parameters. An additional study taking into account artifi-
cial lighting tested the combined effect of colored daylight and artificial lighting on thermal
response for long exposures. If the hue-heat hypothesis was proven to hold for such
long exposures, it would produce large reductions in total annual energy consumption.
These results motivate future studies to understand the specific parameters which would
optimize the implementation of the hue-heat hypothesis.

Introduction
Reducing energy consumption and improving indoor climate are crucial for achieving the
European Union’s (EU) commitment to reducing greenhouse emissions. By decreasing
energy use, not only can we decrease carbon emissions and slow the pace of climate
change, but we can also reduce the energy costs, and improve air quality[1]. Energy
efficiency can reduce both indoor and outdoor concentrations of air pollutants. In doing
so, energy efficiency drives a range of economic, environmental and health benefits as-
sociated with local air quality [1]. The EU’s goal is to reduce emissions by 55% by 2030
and Net Zero by 2050 [2], which will require significant changes in the way we use energy
in our homes, workplaces, and transportation [2]. Through implementing energy-efficient
technologies, using renewable energy sources, and changing our consumption habits, we
can work towards a more sustainable future.
The built environment plays a significant role in global emissions and energy consumption.
In Europe, buildings account for 40% of total energy consumption and 36% of greenhouse
gas emissions (GHG)[3]. The 36% of GHG mainly stem from construction, usage, ren-
ovation and demolition. 40% of total energy consumption is a significant portion of the
energy consumed in Europe, and it is only expected to increase as the population grows
and urbanization continues.
Globally, the situation is not much different, the building sector is responsible for around
40% of global energy consumption and a third of greenhouse gas emissions [4]. The en-
ergy consumed and the GHG emissions produced by the building sector can be divided in
two groups. The existing buildings, and the construction of new buildings and renovations.
The energy consumed in existing buildings is primarily used for heating, cooling, lighting,
appliances, and other building equipment [3]. In 2020, the energy consumed in the EU
was made up by five different sources[3]: petroleum products 35%, Natural gas 24% re-
newable energy 17%, nuclear energy 13%, and solid fossil fuels 12 %. Fossil fuels count
for a total of 71% which are the main source of CO2 emissions. At the same time, approx-
imately 75% of the total existing buildings are inefficient in terms of energy consumption
[3]. These type of buildings require significantly amounts of energy to maintain adequate
indoor air quality (IAQ) [3]. To achieve the mentioned before EU goals it is key to reduce
the energy consumption of the existing buildings.
There are many different ways of reducing the energy consumption in existing buildings.
This Master´s thesis looks into the possibility of reducing the energy consumption in single
family houses by using the Hue-Heat Hypothesis (HHH).
The HHH suggests that environments lighted with colors in the warm visible spectrum,
such as red and orange, are perceived as warmer than colors in the cold visible spec-
trum G.M.Huebner (2016) [5], such as blue and green. This hypothesis has been studied
in a variety of contexts, in fields such as visual perception [6], advertising [7][8], interior
design [9][10] and indoor environment [11]. Throughout the last 100 years, various re-
searches have investigated the effect of the HHH in the thermal perception which is a
subjective perception of the temperature, and the physiological responses in the body
which are objective measurements of the body temperature, with mixed results. Com-
bined, these two responses are referred as ”thermal responses”. Some of the studies
carried out from the past century, proved the influence of the HHH in the thermal re-

In recent times, different publications supporting the HHH have encouraged researchers

1.0.1
Objective and problem statement
If the HHH could be proven, we could assume that a subject placed in the interior en-
vironment under the effect of daylight transmitted through an orange warm filter, would
feel warmer. In that scenario, in winter period, we could then assume that the set-point
temperature of the heaters located in the room could be lowered, while maintaining the
same thermal perception and the same levels of PPD and PMV. In the case of the blue
cold filter, in summer period, we could also assume that the use of the blue filter could
allow an increase of the set-point temperature without changing the thermal perception
of the interior environment. These changes of set-point temperature could potentially re-
duce the energy consumption while keeping a stable thermal perception, leading to the
research questions on this paper:
• Can the HHH be used to reduce energy consumption in existing single family houses?
• What are the parameters that influence the performance of the HHH on the total
annual energy consumption and why?
This thesis aims then to motivate future studies on the effect of the HHH in thermal per-
ception based on the potential energy savings and economic impact of the use of colored
window filters to affect thermal perception, and is structured as follows: Section 2 provides
a literature review of previous investigations of the HHH. Section 3 presents the method-
ology used in the investigation of the questions above. Section 4 presents the results of
the analysis which are further explored in Section 5. Section 6 concludes and provides
suggestions for further investigation on the topic.

2.1
Origins of the Hue Heat Hypothesis
As mentioned in the introduction, The HHH suggests that the sensation of coolness and
warmness is influence by the perceived visual color, where environments with ambient
colors in the warm visible spectrum, such as red and orange, are perceived as warmer
than the ones in the cold visible spectrum, such as blue.
This hypothesis was first mentioned by Morgensen and English in 1926 (as cited in A.Bennet
(1972) [19]). They suggested that hues in the red-yellow range are perceived as warm,
while hues in the blue-green are perceived as cool. In their experiment, they had 25 sub-
jects judging the warmth of two cylinders with equal temperature and covered with papers
of different colors (purple, green, yellow, orange, and red). Even though they appreciated
a significant color effect by analysing the variance, they concluded that color preference
rather than judgment of warmth could act in the subject’s answers. They concluded that
their results showed no evidence of the effect of the HHH. The limitations of the study to
only 25 subjects and the fact that they focused on the perception of temperature of an
object instead of the thermal perception of the subjects itself, motivated researches for
future studies.
The HHH, as proposed by Morgensen and English in 1926 (as cited in A.Bennet (1972)
[19]), has been studied in many different angles by researchers throughout the past
century [6] [7][8] [9][10][11]. While the theory has been supported by several studies
[12][13][14][15][16][17][5][11], it has also been challenged on the basis of cultural differ-
ences [20][21], and the complexity of the relationship between color and warmth [22].
2.2
Studies questioning the Hue Heat Hypothesis
Throughout the last century, researchers have questioned the universality of the HHH.
Aiming to provide more insights into the HHH, Houghton, Olson, and Sucui (1940) (as
cited in A.Bennet (1972) [19]), conducted an experiment where two subjects spent seven
hours watching a screen illuminated by different colors (red,blue and white) each one in
sequence of 40 minutes period. Keeping thermal conditions constant, they gathered they
responses on thermal comfort, and they measured the oral and skin temperatures. In line
with the first results of Morgensen and English, they didn’t appreciate a hue effect in the
subjects responses on thermal perception and the temperature measurements taken.
After the studies from Morgensen and English 1926 and Houghton, Olson, and Sucui
(1940), Berry PC (1961) [18] studied the thermal perception under different colored lights.
In his experiment, he used different colored lights, including red, orange, yellow, green,
blue and purple, and had participants rate the perceived temperature of the room under
each lighting condition. The results also suggested that the intensity of the light source
had an effect on the perceived temperature with brighter lights being rated as warmer

that dimmer lights. The experiment did not reveal an interaction between the thermal
perception and the color of light. However, the subjects of the experiment persisted in the
conventional belief that green and blue colors are ”cool” colors.
These findings and the limitations of the study realized by Berry, motivated the study
from A. Bennet (1972) published in the article What’s so hot about red? [19]. Aiming to
explore the psychological and physiological effect of the red color, Bennet tested twenty-
one subjects responses while wearing red, blue and clear googles. Bennet stated that no
hue main effects were found and concluded that hue produces an intellectual effect, but
that it does not affect people’s thermal comfort.
Other studies suggest that the relationship between color and thermal comfort could be
context and cultural specific. A study by Jameson and Hurvich (1989) [20] found that
while participants in their study rated red-yellow hues as warmer than blue-green hues,
they also found that these ratings varied depending on the culture of the participants.
They suggested that the relationship between color and warmth may be culture-specific.
In a similar line, The paper by SE Palmer, KB Schloss et al (2016) [21], examined the
role of individual differences in perceptual preference. The authors studied how people’s
personal characteristics and experiences influence their visual preferences for different
images, colors, and patterns. They found that there is a wide range of individual differ-
ences in visual preferences, which can be influenced by factors such as age, gender,
culture, and prior visual experiences. These papers shows that the understanding of in-
dividual differences in perceptual preference is also important and could affect the HHH
bases of feeling warm under ”warm” light and cold under ”cold” light.
The perception of color and its translation to psychological effects are very complex, and
the knowledge on the topic is also limited. Researchers have suggested that this rela-
tionship is more complex than the one suggested by the HHH. In the study, conducted by
Palmer and Schloss (2010)[22], the researches found that the relationship between color
and temperature perception may depend on a variety of factors such as the intensity,
saturation, and surrounding colors and not only the CCT.
Another recent study conducted by Baniya, R (2016) did not provide support for the HHH
either. The study was conducted in a test room illuminated with an LED lighting system
with an adjustable correlated color temperature where air temperature, air velocity, and
relative humidity were kept constant. The CCT of the artificial lighting inside the test room
was changed gradually while keeping the color rendering index values greater than 90,
an illuminance level of 500 lx, and chromaticity difference (Duv) values within the limits
of ±0.005. Sixteen study subjects were exposed to a ”high room temperature” (25°C)
and a ”low room temperature” (20°C) on different days. The subjects were adapted to
low correlated color temperature (2700 K), medium correlated color temperature (4000
K), and high correlated color temperature (6200 K) lighting for 10 min and subsequently
completed a questionnaire about their thermal comfort and thermal sensation.
The results of the study did not provide support for the hue-heat hypothesis and indicated
that people felt thermally more comfortable in an indoor workplace at the correlated color
temperature of 4000 K than at the correlated color temperature of 2700 K or 6200 K.
There are many reasons for the inconsistency of the relationship between hue and per-
ceived temperature. Limitations on the experiment’s participants which are often under-
taken by students of a similar age group, in a relatively short period of time and with a
small number of participants could explain that there is not a consensus on the evidence
of the HHH among researches. It is also important to mention that the majority of studies

2.3
Studies supporting the Hue Heat Hypothesis
Other researches have however provided support for the hue heat hypothesis. The first
one founded is explained in the book The elements of color, by J.Itten (1970) [13], the
author examines the relationship between color and thermal sensation including physio-
logical and psychological effects. Itten argues that warm colors like red and orange can
make a space feel warmer, while cool colors like blue and green can create a sense of
coolness. In the same line, L.Clark (1975)[14] explores the use of color therapy in ancient
cultures and how it was still used at the time. These two texts support the HHH and give
insight into how color can affect humans perception of temperature, and how it has been
used throughout time for healing and therapeutic purposes. In addition to these studies,
Fanger P.O et al. (1977) [15] found that subjects exposed to extreme red colors preferred
a 0.4°C lower temperature than those exposed to extreme blue light. The findings are in
line with the HHH but seem small to have a practical effect.
A study by Ohno et al. (1987) [16] investigated a synthesized index that covers both ther-
mal and non-thermal environmental factors for designing and controlling an optimal indoor
environment and energy conservation. Through four experiments using a chamber with
red and blue walls at various room temperatures, the authors investigate the interactions
between thermal sensation and color event, a representative non-thermal environmental
factor. Participants’ psychological responses such as thermal sensation, comfort level,
and hue-heat impression of surrounding colors were measured using adjective pairs, and
mean skin temperature was calculated as a physiological response. The study found that
hue-heat impression of color is significantly proportional to room temperature, and that
thermal sensation is significantly affected by surrounding color in cases where room tem-
peratures are changing from uncomfortable to neutral conditions and during the period
after the change. These findings confirm the HHH and suggest that interactions between
different modalities are based on the intensity and/or the change of affection level aroused
by each event.
Two papers talk about the possibility of using color temperature lighting to reduce the
energy consumption in a room. In the paper by S. Lu et al. (2015) [17], they investigated
the user’s motivation to change the room temperature when the color temperature of the
room lighting was changed, in two experiments. They found statistical evidence that a
warmer light increased the motivation of users to reduce the ambient temperature, and
that cooler lights increased the motivation of users to raise the ambient temperature. They
also found that subjects would feel warmer under warm lighting and colder under cold
lighting in line with the HHH. One interesting result of the first experiment suggests that
subjects in the warm lighting condition at an average temperature of 22.9°C expressed
a motivation to decrease the room temperature by an average of 1.91°C. The ones in a
cold lighting condition wanted to increase the room temperature by an average of 1.13ºC.
In a similar experiment, G.M. Huebner (2016) [5] investigated the thermal responses of
participants under two cooling and warming scenarios. In the first one, the temperature
changed from 24ºC to 20ºC and in the second one, the temperature increased from 20ºC
to 24ºC. In each case the subjects were exposed to warm lighting (2700K) in one study,
and cold lighting (6500K). The results showed statistical evidence that the participants felt

slightly colder in both studies under cold lights and slightly warmer under warm light. It

In the line of the previously described studies, in 2015, the study ”In search of evidence
for the hue-heat hypothesis in the Ittenaircraft cabin” [11], investigated the HHH in the
context of aircraft cabins. By using two lighting scenarios (yellow and blue) 199 subjects
were asked about their thermal sensation in different temperatures. The results of the
study are in line with the previously described experiments, where the influence on color
in the thermal perception was supported.
Up to 2018, all the studies used artificial lighting as a parameter to try to look for evidence
of the HHH. G.Chinazzo et al. (2018) [12] was the first one to investigate the effect of
daylight transmitted through colored glazing (blue, orange and neutral) on the thermal re-
sponses and overall comfort at three different temperatures (19ºC, 22ºC and 26ºC) trying
to prove the influence of the HHH in the thermal responses.
The results showed the effect of the HHH on the thermal perception at temperatures of
22 and 26 degree both on blue and orange colored glazing. The subjects exposed to
the orange filter at 22ºC expressed a warmer thermal sensation, a higher thermal com-
fort, better thermal acceptability and a thermal preference closer to a no change level
compared with the blue filter but not to the neutral filter. On the other hand, subjects ex-
posed to the blue filter at 26ºC expressed a cooler thermal sensation, and a better thermal
comfort compared with the orange and neutral filter.
At the same time, it was found that the use of colored glazing can reduce the overheating
effect of direct sunlight, improving the thermal comfort of a room. However, the study also
found that the color of the glazing can negatively affect the overall comfort. The use of
colored glazing impacted negatively the subject’s overall comfort. The results indicated
a less comfortable environment under blue and orange filter compared to neutral. They
also found a significant difference on the thermal responses from the beginning of the
exposure and the end of the exposure. This is suggested to be due to color adaptation
of the subjects. The main reason of discomfort at the beginning of the exposure was
visual environment, and at the end was a combination of thermal environment and visual
environment. They noticed less complaints about the visual environment at the end of the
experiment specially at temperatures of 22ºC and 26ºC.
Even though the above mentioned studies provide evidence of the HHH, the effects of
this hypothesis seams to be small and limited to temperatures around thermal comfort.
Studies taking daylight into consideration [12], opens up a new line of investigation and
motivates the realization of this thesis. Could the simple installation of a colored filter
reduce the energy consumption in a single family house? By using the HHH, we could
reduce the set-point temperature of the heaters in a winter scenario, however, the use
of a filters placed in the window would also reduce the solar gains obtained through the
window. Would the decrease of energy consumed by the heating system compensate
the energy reduction obtained by solar gains? It is then logical to hypothesise that the
use of blue colored filters for the summer scenarios in warm climates will produce greater
energy reductions compared to the winter scenario. The filters located in the windows will
reduce the solar gains, and by using the HHH, the set-point temperature of the cooling
system will increase reducing the energy consumption for cooling.

Methods

single family houses and to investigate what variables influence the performance of the
HHH, two models were created in the software IDA ICE 4.9.9 (hereafter ”IDA”). This soft-
ware was chosen because it was considered the most suitable program to analyze and
compare annual energy consumption under different parameters, and because it has the
ability to run parametric studies, providing detailed outputs of energy consumption and
energy balance for each of the cases studied. These models include:
1. A single family house.
2. The dining room of the single family house, hereafter referred to as the ”reference
room”.

The values shown in table 3.6 were then introduced to IDA, and controlled by a Macro
3.5. This Macro, controlled the lighting signal. As an input, the daylight levels and the
occupancy were added. The daylight level in the zone was then controlled by a switch.
As input 1 to this switch 2000 (lux) was added, as input 2, the daylight was connected,
and as selector, the occupancy was filtered by a ”greather than 0.9” and added the value
with the adder. The idea was that in the case when the occupancy was lower than 0.9, the
value of the selector of the switch would be 1, and the illuminance level that would through
the switch would be 2000 lux. Then the 2000 lux would be connected to a thermostat,
with a Deadband of 1000 lux, and a set-point of 600 lux. The thermostat would be then
ON until a level of 1000 lux was reached, turning it OFF, and it would be turned ON again
if the input value was lower than 100 lux. So in the named case of no occupancy, the
signal would be OFF in the thermostat. In the case where the occupancy was larger than
0.9, the adder would give a signal of 2, and the switcher would give the luminous level in
the room (center of the room at 0.5 m height). The light would then be ON until it reaches
the level of 1000 lux where it will be turn OFF, turning ON again when the illuminance
was lower than 100 lux. The output signal was then multiplied by a ”sleeping” schedule
that provided a 0 output during ”sleeping hours” (assumed from 24:00 to 07:00). So if the
time was in between 07:00 and 24:00, the occupancy was ON, and the illuminance levels
were lower than 1000 lux, the light signal would be ON.

3.1.5
Filters
Two filters were defined following the properties described by G.Chinazzo et al. (2018)
[12]. An Orange filter , and a Blue filter. The spectral diffuse transmittance levels ob-
tained for both filters (see figure 3.6) ranged from 380 to 1005 nm in wavelenght. IDA
required values from 280 to 2500 nm. As both filter material were made from polycarbon-
ate, it was then assumed that the values of the diffuse transmittance for both Orange and
Blue filter outside 380-1005 nm were the same as the transparent polycarbonate (graph
in Appendix A.1) and that they were equal for both front and back surfaces in line with the
findings by Moretti et al.[28]. The direct component of total normal hemispherical trans-
mittance for the front, and back surface were assumed 0. The direct component of total
normal hemispherical reflectance, front surface and back surface were also assumed 0.
The diffuse component of total normal hemispherical reflectance, front surface and back
surface were assumed 0.08 (8% in accordance with the values founded for polycarbonate,
Appendix graph A.1). All these values were added to a .txt file that was then introduced
to IDA.
The filters were located inside the room, at a distance of 7 cm from the inner surface of
the glass. A gap of 1 cm was added to the top, both sides, and bottom, from the window
wall gap to the filter.

3.1.7
Set-point temperatures
Madrid, cooling
A set-point temperature of 25°C was decided according to the norm DSEN 16798 [30]
section B.2.4, default indoor temperatures for energy calculations, table B.5 for a cate-
gory II (values in between 23°C - 26°C). The studies supporting the HHH for the cooling
scenario have been studied at temperatures of 22.9°C [17],25°C [31], and 26°C [12], and
30°C [32]. Each of these studies suggested a different effect of the cold lighting in the
thermal responses. The results vary from 1.13°C [17] for the 22.9°C, to approximately
0.5°C [12] for the 26°C. Due to the variation in the results, it was assumed 1°C increase
of the set-point temperature under the cold lighting (under the Blue filter). The set-point
temperatures will be as follow:
• Madrid, No filter 25°C
• Madrid, Blue filter 26°C (+1°C)
Copenhagen, heating
A set-point temperature of 22°C was selected for Copenhagen, heating. In accordance
with the norm DSEN 16798 [30] for category II (values in between 20°C - 25°C). The
studies supporting the HHH for the heating scenario have been studied at temperatures
of 20°C [31], 21.5°C [11], 22.9°C [17], and 22°C [12]. The results vary from -1.91°C in the
case of 22.9°C [17], to approximately -0.3 °C for the 22°C [12]. A value of -1°C was then
assumed when the Orange filter was unrolled generating the following set-point scenarios:
• Copenhagen, No filter 22°C
• Copenhagen, Orange filter 21°C (-1°C)

In the lower left corner of the macro, the heating input was added. Then it was connected
to a ”greater than 1”, providing a value of 1 when there was a heating signal greater than
1 W, and a value of 0 when heating was OFF (or lower than 1 W). This was relevant so
that the Orange filter was only used in the winter period (when there was the need for
heating). Then the output was multiplied by the daylight signal received from the zone.
So it would be 0 when there was no heating, and the lux value of illuminance inside the
zone otherwise. Then the signal was connected with a thermostat with a deadband equal
to -200 lux and a set-point of 200. This gave an OFF signal until the illuminance levels in
the zone reached 300 lux [31]. Then it set to ON until the illuminance levels went under
100 lux. It was assumed than the filter would have an effect for illuminance values higher
than 100 lux (100 lux was decided, as the minimum illuminance levels which has been
reported to provided a partial effect in thermal responses (HHH) is 50 lux [33]). The output
of the thermostat is then connected to the signal of the filters.
To control the set-point temperature of the ideal heaters, the air temperature from the zone
was connected to a Proportional controller with a set-point signal. When the signal from
the shading was OFF, the set-point was set to 22°C as described in 3.1.7.
When the output signal from the thermostat controlling the filter was ON, it was then con-
nected to an ”ONOFFWDELAY”(on off with delay) setting the time-stop variable to 30
seconds. This delay was used to avoid an ON-OFF effect in the thermostat temperature
after few seconds from the input signal. After this, the delayed signal was multiplied by
-1°C and added to the 22°C signal. In accordance with the previously described in 3.1.7.
As a result, when the filter was unrolled in front of the window, the set-point temperature
of the thermostat was reduced by -1°C.

As a general rule for all graphs, the columns represent the annual energy consumption
in kWh and the lines represent the percentage difference in annual energy consumption
between the no filter case and the filter case, hereafter stylized as no filter - filter, for
each parameter. The percentage difference was calculated according to eq. (4.1). This
metric was chosen to asses the effect of the HHH on annual energy consumption, as
the difference between the no filter - filter case alone does not provide an idea of the
magnitude of the impact of the parameter on annual energy consumption. A positive
value for percentage difference refers to a lower value of energy consumption in the filter
simulation, and therefore, a greater effect of the HHH. On the other hand, a negative value
denotes higher energy consumption in the filter case, and therefore a negative impact of
the HHH. For this reason it was decided to show both results in the same graph. The left
Y axis axis refers to the results showed as columns, and the right right Y axis refers to
the results showed as lines.

5
Discussion and Conclusions
5.1
What are the parameters that influence the performance
of the HHH on the total annual energy consumption and
why?
5.1.1
Orientation
The Orientation was found to have a significant influence on the performance of the HHH,
although the impact varies by location.
Madrid, Spain
The highest total annual energy consumption occurs towards South, followed by East,
West, and North. The higher the solar gains, the higher the annual cooling consumption,
and therefore the higher the total annual energy consumption.
The largest effect of the HHH on the total annual energy consumption, as represented
by the percentage difference, was observed in Orientation East for every Window combi-
nation and Glazing type. This can be explained as using the filter towards East reduces
the solar gains from sunrise and reduces the use of cooling. Comparing with Orientation
South, the filter was unrolled later than in the East case. The outside temperature was
higher than in the Orientation East when the filter was unrolled. The filter was then less
effective as there has been more energy transmitted through the envelope at the time
when the filter is unrolled. Similar results were obtained in Orientations West and North.
Copenhagen, Denmark
The total annual energy consumption was affected by the Orientation resulting in higher
consumption towards North, where the solar gains were lower, and lower towards South,
where the solar gains are higher. Similar consumption in Orientation East and West were
found with values in between the ones obtained for Orientation North and South.
The Orientation in Copenhagen is not a significant factor effect of the HHH, as similar
effect was found for all Orientations. By applying the Orange filter, the solar gains were
reduce, and more heating was needed. So even if the set-point temperature was reduced
-1°C, the lower solar gains provided a higher total annual energy consumption for the
Orange filter simulations in all orientations.
This is due to the 300 lux set-point that controls the Orange filter, combined with the low
levels of daylight in Copenhagen. These produce a very low amount of time in which the
filters are unrolled. Adding the error produced by the results of IDA, it can not be concluded
that there is a significant difference in between filter and no filter cases depending on the
Orientation with the presented results.
5.1.2
Lighting profiles
The effect of the lighting profiles on total annual energy consumption was found to be low.
In Madrid, the effect of the HHH was greater under the 20W lighting profile than in the
40W. In Copenhagen, the results obtained don’t provide a conclusion on the impact of
the lighting profiles of the performance of the HHH. This is due to the low amount hours
where the filter was unrolled. This was expected, as lighting consumption corresponds to
1% to 6% of the total energy consumption depending on the Window size and Orientation
in the 20W and 40W lighting profiles.
The effect of daylight transmitted through colored filters on thermal perception (hue-heat hypothesis)
57
==PAGE:66==
As the use of filters reduces illuminace levels, the lighting scenarios have to be investi-
gated before the implementation of the HHH. Large lighting consumption profiles could
result in higher total annual energy consumption which would defeat the purpose of the
implementation of the HHH. These results are in line with the ones obtained by L. Bellia
et al. (2019) [31], where they stated that the application of the HHH needs to be evalu-
ated by integrated simulations associating the results for lighting consumption and energy
consumption.
5.1.3
Wall construction
The Gypsum wall reduced the total annual energy consumption due to its lower U value
for all Window combinations and all Orientations for both Madrid, and Copenhagen. The
higher consumption for Brick wall, resulted in a higher percentage difference of cooling
reduction for Madrid in Orientations North and West. For East and South, the effect of
the Brick wall in the percentage difference was still higher than in the Gypsum wall, but
the difference was lower than in the North and East case. From the results obtained,
it is not possible to analyze the effect of the difference in thermal mass of the materials
in contact with the room. The difference in performance of the different walls is partially
due to the thermal mass (of the Brick and the Gypsum surfaces in contact with the indoor
environment), and partially due to the U values of both walls. Further investigations where
the U value of the total wall is kept constant and different materials with different thermal
masses are tested would provide a better understanding of the influence of the material
thermal mass on the performance of the HHH.
5.1.4
Filter
The filters have a key role in the performance of the HHH.
Blue filter
The Blue filter reduced the solar gains, and allowed an increase of the set-point tempera-
ture under the assumptions mentioned in Methods. It would be interesting to investigate
the effect of different filters with different spectral diffuse transmittance in the thermal
responses. Could a filter with lower visual transmittance provide similar results in ther-
mal perception than the ones suggested by Chinazzo et al. [12]? In that way, the solar
gains would be reduced, and the set-point temperature of the cooling system could be
increased.
Orange filter
The Orange filter reduced the solar gains and allowed a reduction of the set-point tem-
perature. The results showed how the reduction of the set-point temperature did not
compensate the solar gains reduction by the use of the Orange filter. Further research
on Orange filters with high levels of visual and spectral diffuse transmittance should be
performed to maximize the solar gains in winter time.
5.1.5
Glazing type
The Glazing type has a significant influence in the performance of the HHH under colored
filters. The Glazing F resulted in higher effect than the Glazing H for every Orientation and
Location. Glazing with higher U, g, and τv, result in higher total annual energy consump-
tion, but also higher percentage difference in energy reduction. This indicates that the
implementation of the HHH would have a greater effect in rooms with ”poor” glazing types
(higher U, g, and τv). The interaction of each filter and glazing type should be further
investigated to asses the effect of the combination of these in the thermal responses.
Another line of future investigation could be the research of glazing types that would opti-
mize the effect of the HHH for both winter and summer period. This glazing would ideally
be orange in the winter period when illuminated by daylight, while maintaining high levels
of transmittance, and blue in the summer period with low levels of transmittance.

5.1.6
Window width
As a general rule, the larger window area, the higher total annual energy consumption.
The effect on the HHH was also higher for larger window area. In some scenarios, equal
total window area, but different placement of the windows, provided a higher effect in the
lighting consumption which affected the total annual energy consumption. The occupancy
played an important role in these scenarios, as the light is controlled by the occupancy,
but the filter is not. For that reason, the window location that provides higher illuminance
levels for each location and orientation at occupied times, provided higher percentage
difference reductions in total annual energy consumption.
The results obtained in this chapter show the potential of applying the HHH to reduce
the energy consumption in rooms with large window areas at Orientation East, South
and West. Further studies in rooms with large windows and in the named orientations
should aim to validate the effect on thermal perception under colored filter to identify the
temperature set-point change.
5.2
Can the HHH be used to reduce the energy consumption
in existing single family houses?
5.2.1
Madrid, Cooling
No filter - filter, Glazing F,H
The results showed how the combined effect of the reduction of the solar gains due to the
use of the Blue filter and the increase of set-point temperature led to a decrease in the total
delivered energy for both Glazing F and H. However the analysis of the energy balance
of the Glazing F showed how the impact of the Blue filter in the reduction of total annual
energy consumption, was 4 times higher than the increase of set-point temperature. For
the Glazing H case, the effect of the filter was 2.6 times higher. This is due to the better
properties of the Glazing H (lower U, g, and τv).
This findings are in line with the ones obtained in the Reference room, and motivate future
investigations on Blue colored filters that would reduce the solar gains even more com-
pared the one used in this study, maintaining sufficient levels of daylight so that the effect
on the thermal perception would still happen. In relation with this, it would be interesting
to study what would be the lowest illuminance levels where the HHH still has an effect
in the thermal perception. In that way, the solar gains could be reduced to the minimum,
maintaining the minimum levels in which the HHH functions.
Set-point not controlled by filter, Glazing F,H

vs the 0.53% of the no filter-filter for Glazing F, and 3.28% vs the 0.36% for Glazing H.
These results motivates further investigations on the influence of Blue artificial lighting on
thermal perception to find the optimal Blue lighting conditions that maximizes the effect
on the HHH in cooling periods.

5.3
Further considerations
Another interesting field to be studied could be the exposure duration. Most HHH studies
to the date have short exposure times ([36],[31],[37],[38],[39],[11],[32],[18]) ( <1h). A study
recently published by W. Lou et al. [40] tested the HHH in long exposures (>2h) under
artificial light. The results provide no support for the HHH; however, it was tested for
mild cold conditions (17°C), while the studies which have documented an effect of the
HHH have been conducted under temperatures above 20°C, [12],[13],[14],[16],[15],[17].
The results of Lou et al. [40] are aligned with the ones obtained by Chinazzo et al. [12],
indicating a higher effect of the HHH for shorter exposure periods. This indicates that the
benefits of implementing the HHH would be greater in rooms with shorter occupancy times
such as corridors or meeting rooms in office buildings, where the spaces are occupied for
medium to short time periods ( <1h).
At the same time, overall comfort should be taken into consideration before implement-
ing the HHH to reduce the energy consumption. Studies by [12] have demonstrated that
colored daylight has a negative effect on overall comfort, with higher levels of visual dis-
comfort due to the colored daylight occurring at the beginning of the exposure than after
30 minutes for both the Orange and Blue filter. There is potentially trade off between
economic savings and visual discomfort as produced by the HHH which should be further
analyzed for different building programs. The extent to which building occupants equally
bear the positive and negative impacts of the HHH will likely influence the decision to
implement HHH-based solutions and their potential benefits.
With regard to the set-point temperature change assumption of 1°C for both winter and
summer scenarios, futures studies should aim to identify the exact temperature change
in thermal perception as a result of the HHH and its potential variation between different
types of occupants. The assumption of 1°C is based on previous studies of the HHH, the
majority of which have been conducted on small sample sizes and with subjects of the
same age group (normally students) [12],[5],[15],[18],[17], [16]. This limits the validity of
these results, and therefore the assumption taken in this thesis.
Furthermore, it is important to note that the single family house model used for Copen-
hagen was also used for Madrid, which was based on Danish standards for Building type,
Wall construction and Occupancy. Only weather conditions were changed for the Madrid
scenario. Before the results of this thesis are implemented in a Spanish context, it would
be beneficial to repeat this analysis on a model based on a Spanish house typology using
local Wall construction types, Occupancy files and lighting profiles. Given that higher U
values for wall construction were required in the past in Spain, a higher effect of the HHH
in existing housing can be anticipated. In 2006, the requirements for U values in new
construction for exterior walls varied from 1.22 W/m2K to 0.74 W/m2K depending the lo-
cation [41]), while the U values used in this thesis were 0.35 W/m2K for the Brick wall and
0.20 W/m2K for the Gypsum wall). As was mentioned in 5.1.3, the higher the U values of
the exterior wall, the higher the total annual consumption, and also a higher effect of the
HHH.
In conclusion, this thesis provides support for the use of Blue colored filters to reduce
energy consumption from cooling in summer scenarios in warm climates, but not for the

==PAGE:69==
Orange colored filters in winter scenarios in cool climates for heating. Due to the limited
effect of the HHH on thermal response and the interdependence of the different param-
eters studied, the application of the HHH should be evaluated by integrated simulations
in the future. This will allow for a more holistic assessment of the potential benefits of
HHH-based solutions, enabling the positive impact of reduced energy consumption to be
weighed against negative impacts of the HHH such as reduced overall comfort.

Glossary
25
A
A
Formula symbol for a surface in the geometry
B
Background area
The background area borders the direct ambient area according to DIN EN 12464-1 and
reaches up to the borders of the room. In larger rooms, the background area is at least
3 m wide. It is located horizontally at floor level.
C
CCT
(correlated color temperature)
Body temperature of a thermal radiator that serves to describe its light color. Unit: Kelvin
[K]. The lesser the numerical value the redder; the greater the numerical value the bluer
the light color. The color temperature of gas-discharge lamps and semi-conductors are
termed "correlated color temperature" in contrast to the color temperature of thermal
radiators.
Allocation of the light colors to the color temperature ranges acc. to EN 12464-1:
Light color - color temperature [K]
warm white (ww) < 3,300 K
neutral white (nw) ≥ 3,300 – 5,300 K
daylight white (dw) > 5,300 K
Clearance height
The designation for the distance between upper edge of the floor and bottom edge of
the ceiling (in the completely furnished status of room).
Control group
A group of luminaires that are dimmed and controlled together. For each lighting scene,
a control group provides its own dimming value. All luminaires within a control group
share this dimming value. The control groups with their luminaires are automatically
determined by DIALux on the basis of the created light scenes and their luminaire
groups.
CRI
(color rendering index)
Designation for the color rendering index of a luminaire or a lamp acc. to DIN 6169:
1976 or CIE 13.3: 1995.
The general color rendering index Ra (or CRI) is a dimensionless figure that describes the
quality of a white light source in regards to its similarity with the remission spectra of
defined 8 test colors (see DIN 6169 or CIE 1974) to a reference light source.

Glossary
26
D
Daylight autonomy
Describes what percentage of the daily working time the required illuminance is met by
daylight. The nominal illuminance is used from the room profile, unlike described in EN
17037. The calculation is not done in the centre of the room but at the placed sensor
measuring point. A room is considered sufficiently supplied with daylight if it achieves at
least 50% daylight autonomy.
Daylight factor
Ratio of the illuminance achieved solely by daylight incidence at a point in the inside to
the horizontal illuminance in the outer area under an unobstructed sky.
Formula symbol: D (daylight factor)
Unit: %
Daylight quotient effective area
A calculation surface within which the daylight quotient is calculated.
E
Energy evaluation
Based on an hourly calculation procedure for daylight in indoor spaces, considering the
project geometry and any existing daylight control systems. Orientation and location of
the project are also considered. The calculation uses the specified system power of the
luminaires to determine the energy demand. A linear relationship between power and
luminous flux in the dimmed state is assumed for daylight-controlled luminaires. Times
of use and nominal illuminance are determined from the usage profiles of the spaces.
Switched-on luminaires that are explicitly excluded from control also consider the
specified times-of-use. The daylight control systems use a simplified control logic that
closes them at an outdoor horizontal illuminance of 27,500lx.
The calendar year 2022 is used as a reference only. It is not a simulation of this year. The
reference year is only used to assign the days of the week to the calculated results. The
changeover to summer time is not considered. The reference sky type used is the
average sky described in CIE 110 without direct sunlight.
The method was developed together with the Fraunhofer Institute for Building Physics
and is available for review by the Joint Working Group 1 ISO TC 274 as an extension of
the previous annual regression-based method.
Eta (η)
(light output ratio)
The light output ratio describes what percentage of the luminous flux of a free radiating
lamp (or LED module) is emitted by the luminaire when installed.
Unit: %

G
g1
Often also Uo (overall uniformity)
Designates the overall uniformity of the illuminance on a surface. It is the quotient from
Emin to Ē and is required, for instance, in standards for illumination of workstations.
g2
Actually it designates the "non-uniformity" of the illuminance on a surface. It is the
quotient of Emin to Emax and is generally only relevant for certifying the emergency
lighting acc. to EN 1838.
I
Illuminance
Describes the ratio of the luminous flux that strikes a certain surface to the size of this
surface (lm/m² = lx). The illuminance is not tied to an object surface. It can be determined
anywhere in space (inside or outside). The illuminance is not a product feature because it
is a recipient value. Luxometers are used for measuring.
Unit: Lux
Abbreviation: lx
Formula symbol: E
Illuminance, adaptive
For the determining of the middle adaptive illuminance on a surface, this is rastered
"adaptively". In the area of large illuminance differences within the surface, the raster is
subdivided finer; within lesser differences, a rougher classification is made.
Illuminance, horizontal
Illuminance that is calculated or measured on a horizontal (level) surface (this can be for
example a table top or the floor). The horizontal illuminance is usually identified by the
formula letter Eh.
Illuminance, perpendicular
Illuminance that is calculated or measured plumb-vertical to a surface. This needs to be
taken into account for tilted surfaces. If the surface is horizontal or vertical, then there is
no difference between the perpendicular and the horizontal or vertical illuminance.
Illuminance, vertical
Illuminance that is calculated or measured on a vertical surface (this can be for example
the front of some shelves). The vertical illuminance is usually identified by the formula
letter Ev.
L
LENI
(lighting energy numeric indicator)
Lighting energy numeric indicator acc. to EN 15193
Unit: kWh/m² year
Light loss factor
See MF

28
LLMF
(lamp lumen maintenance factor)/acc. to CIE 97: 2005
Lamp flux maintenance factor that takes the luminous flux reduction into account of a
luminaire or an LED module in the course of the operating time. The lamp flux
maintenance factor is specified as a decimal digit and can have a maximum value of 1
(no luminous flux reduction existing).
LMF
(luminaire maintenance factor)/acc. to CIE 97: 2005
Luminaire maintenance factor that takes the soiling into account of the luminaire in the
course of the operating time. The luminaire maintenance factor is specified as a decimal
digit and can have a maximum value of 1 (no soiling existing).
LSF
(lamp survival factor)/acc. to CIE 97: 2005
Lamp survival factor that takes the total failure into account of a luminaire in the course
of the operating time. The lamp survival factor is specified as a decimal digit and can
have a maximum value of 1 (no failures existing within the time concerned or prompt
replacement after the failure).
Luminance
Dimension for the "brightness impression" that the human eye has of a surface. The
surface itself can emit light thereby or light striking it can be reflected (emitter value). It
is the only photometric value that the human eye can perceive.
Unit: Candela per square meter
Abbreviation: cd/m²
Formula symbol: L
Luminous efficacy
Ratio of the emitted luminous flux Φ [lm] to the absorbed electrical power P [W] Unit:
lm/W.
This ratio can be formed for the lamp or LED module (lamp or module light output), the
lamp or module with control gear (system light output) and the complete luminaire
(luminaire light output).
Luminous flux
Dimension for the total light output that is emitted from one light source in all directions.
It is thus an "emitter value" that specifies the entire emitting output. The luminous flux of
a light source can only be determined in a laboratory. A difference is made between the
lamp or LED module luminous flux and the luminaire luminous flux.
Unit: Lumen
Abbreviation: lm
Formula symbol: Φ
Luminous intensity
Describes the intensity of the light in a certain direction (emitter value). The luminous
intensity is a matter of the luminous flux Φ that is emitted in a certain spherical angle Ω.
The radiation characteristics of a light source are presented graphically in a light
distribution curve (LDC). The luminous intensity is an SI base unit.
Unit: Candela
Abbreviation: cd
Formula symbol: I

MF
(maintenance factor)/acc. to CIE 97: 2005
Maintenance factor as decimal number between 0 and 1 that describes the ratio of the
new value of a photometric planning parameter (e.g. of the illuminance) to a
maintenance value after a certain time. The maintenance factor takes into account the
soiling of luminaires and rooms as well as the luminous flux reduction and the failure of
light sources.
The maintenance factor is taken into account either overall or determined in detail acc.
to CIE 97: 2005 by the formula RMF x LMF x LLMF x LSF.

P
(power)
Electric power consumption
Unit: watt
Abbreviation: W
R
R(UG) max
Measure of the psychological glare in indoor spaces.
In addition to the luminance of luminaires, the level of the R(UG) value also depends on
the observer position, the viewing direction and the ambient luminance. The calculation
is made according to the table method, see CIE 117. Among other things, EN 12464-
1:2021 specifies maximum permissible R(UG)- values R(UGL) for various indoor workplaces.
Reflection factor
The reflection factor of a surface describes how much of the striking light is reflected
back. The reflection factor is defined by the color of the surface.
RMF
(room maintenance factor)/acc. to CIE 97: 2005
Room maintenance factor that takes the soiling into account of the space encompassing
surfaces in the course of the operating time. The room maintenance factor is specified
as a decimal digit and can have a maximum value of 1 (no soiling existing).
S
Surrounding area
The ambient area directly borders the area of the visual task and should be planned with
a width of at least 0.5 m according to DIN EN 12464-1. It is at the same height as the
area of the visual task.

Working plane
Virtual measuring or calculation surface at the height of the visual task that generally
follows the room geometry. The working plane may also feature a wall zone.
