Summary
The goal of this project was to develop a hardware platform that makes use of the SiPo
(Silk-Laponite composite) sensor technology in order to facilitate the tracking of hand
movement. This could be used in a variety of applications ranging from medical reha-
bilitation and monitoring, to home entertainment purposes for Virtual Reality motion
detection.
First a substantive look into related technologies and sensing materials is conducted
with the scope of obtaining an understanding of the tasks and requirement of the project.
This is followed by a comprehensive analysis of the subsystems necessary to build the
platform from sensing to signal conditioning, to data acquisition and processing. After
obtaining the relevant knowledge a measurement setup is implemented in order to gain
insight into the sensor characteristics and later on build a circuit model to simulate and
estimate system behaviour. In order to determine the hardware setup, test circuitry and
equipment is developed which will help explore and evaluate different design options.
The knowledge gained there ultimately culminates in the design of the PCB prototype.
In the end tests are carried out to quantify the performance of the prototype design and
assess any potential faults and problems in the system.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The pervasiveness of technology and more specifically electronic devices in our day to
day life is increasing with each passing year.They are becoming an integral part of our
society aiding us in tasks as simple as brushing our teeth to the implanting of life saving
apparatuses that ensure the heart beats at the appropriate rate. At the same time the
existence of the internet has allowed us to have access to and transmit enormous amounts
of information on a level unparalleled in the whole of human history. This together with
the aforementioned devices form an infrastructure, as outlined in [1], that is becoming
more and more indispensable in our pursuit for a more efficient and healthier society. To
this effect the Eco friendliness of these devices is also a factor in the range of applications
and sustainability .
An area of interest where these new technologies are playing an increasingly impor-
tant role is in health-care systems [1][2] where the focus is not only in the treatment of
health issues but also in preventative care[3] which apart from potentially saving lives
by addressing the root of the problems before they occur also conserves time and energy
in the long run.
A category of these health monitoring devices are known as wearable sensors[3][2]
that need to fulfill a number of requirements in order to be useful data gathering equip-
ment.These devices need to be:
• Noninvasive (as much as possible)
• Non-obtrusive so that they do not impair the patient from doing the required
activity or test
• Non-toxic to the wearer
Although these types of devices will play an important role in the health industry their
usage is not limited to it.
Controller and feedback systems for VR(Virtual Reality)
or AR(Augmented Reality) for example are also an exciting avenue for these sorts of
technologies.

1.1
Scope and Overview
It was with these reasoning’s in mind that the motivation for this thesis came about.
The goal of this project was to develop a hardware platform that makes use of the
SiPo(Silk-Laponite composite) sensor technology in order to facilitate the tracking of
hand-movement.
SiPo is ”a Protein-Based, Water-Insoluble, Bendable and Ionically
Conductive Polymer”[4] that is an environmentally friendly, stretchable material which
can act as a substitute for a lot of the synthetic components present in electronics. A
more detailed explanation of the material will be outlined in section 1.3.
1.2
Literature Review
In the process of researching the topic of stretchable sensors and sensor gloves systems
a number of interesting articles were found that together give a comprehensive insight
into the types of materials used for sensing together with their properties and most
important characteristics.
As such there are a number of key factors by which the
quality and applicability of any given sensor can be assessed.
1.2.1
Main characteristics
Firstly it is worth noting that these types of senors can be categorized as strain gauges
since their resistance characteristic changes proportionally to the applied stress on the
material[5] .Therefore, arguably the most important characteristic of the sensor is its
gauge factor (GF will be used henceforth to describe this parameter).

• GF = gauge factor

As can be seen in equation (1.1) the GF is determined as being ”the ratio of
fractional change in electrical resistance to the fractional change in length
(strain)”[5].Typically the most widely known type of strain gauge sensor comes in the
form of a metallic wire network arranged in a zigzag pattern. The GF of these types of
strain sensors is 2(refer to [5]. Higher GF correspond to higher sensitivity to strain and
vice versa.

Another important parameter of a strain sensor is it’s strain factor or its stretch-
ability. This defines how much any given material can measure strain relative to its
resting length. Usually this term is presented in percentage figures(e.g. a material with
400% strain factor can detect strains up to 4 times its resting length)
Depending on the specific application the tensile strength of the senor can be im-
portant. For example even though a metallic strain gauge has a small strain factor it
typically has a high tensile strength(relatively speaking) making them good for measur-
ing forces applied on materials where the deformation in the material is small but the
applied force is large.
The influence of temperature on the material plays an important role in it’s behaviour.
Here it is important to note that the effects of temperature have to be seen from two
perspectives. One the one hand the temperature rating of the strain gauge refers to the
maximum temperature the material can work at before breaking down. On the other
hand and more importantly temperature change has a noticeable effect on sensor read-
ings since most of the materials used have temperature dependent behaviours. Lastly
the stability under cyclical stress of the sensor plays an important role in determining
its reliability over subsequent measures and over time.
1.2.2
Sensor Categorization
Before delving into the categorization of the sensors there is a discussion to be had
about the type of electric conductors they can be separated into. When discussing strain
sensors there are typically two types of conductive material, namely ionic conductors
and electron conductors. Electron conductors produce a potential difference through
the movement of the electrons in the material(e.g.
metallic conductors) while ionic
conductors produce a potential difference by the movement of ionic particles(e.g. sodium
chloride in water). The strain sensors can be essentially broken down into three main
categories:
• Capacitive - mechanical stress induces a proportional change in capacitance
• Piezoresistive - mechanical stress induces a proportional change in resistance
• Piezoelectric - mechanical stress induces a proportional change in voltage
The capacitive sensors in essence make use of two sensor sheets of a stretchable
conductive material separated by a dielectric as can be seen in[6][7].As the sensors are
stretched and pressed the surface area of the material is changed which leads to a change
in the capacitance of the device. Since this type of sensor deals in relative change in
capacitance equations (1.1) becomes (2.1).

of 300% but with with GF’s of bellow 4. A touch panel sensor was made based on a
polyacrylamide hydrogel composit[16] that can withstand strains of over 1000%.

movement outline different ways of approaching the same problem. Some evaluate dif-
ferent types of strain sensor materials in order to determine the best fit for cost vs
reliability [17][18],others chose to overcome limitations in separate sensing elements by
combining different types of sensors [12] and performing sensor fusion. One article deals
with using kinematics theory together with a specific methodology in order to find out
the most optimal sensor placement on a glove assembly [10].
1.3
SiPo sensor
A general downside among the strain sensors discussed in the literature review section
is that the used materials are synthetic and non-recyclable.As mentioned previously the
sensing element for the SmartGlove system is the SiPo(Silk Laponite composite) which
due to its protein based nature presents an eco-friendly,bio-degradable substitute for
many of the materials used in electronics. The composite is part of the ionic conductor
category since the flow of current through the material is facilitated by the movement
of ions.
The issue with devices made from these synthetic materials is that over time they
become more and more toxic to the environment and by extension to humans. There-

CHAPTER 2
Principle of Operation and
Design Considerations
The following chapter outlines the working principles behind the major components
in the SmartGlove platform together with the important design considerations that
have to be taken into account when designing this system. A general diagram of the
main components can be seen in figure 2.1. The platform can essentially be broken
down into three major parts.
The signal processing hardware that contains all the
necessary components for signal conditioning: acquisition, filtration, amplification and
conversion(analog to digital). The micro-controller block that handles data flow control,
data fusion and communication(to the PC). And finally the PC block which simply acts
as an interface between the hardware and operator. In this chapter the main focus will
be on the signal processing hardware block
Figure 2.1: General diagram of the system
2.1
Wheatstone Bridge
With traditional strain gauges that use metallic sensors the amount of strain would be
relatively small being in the order of 10−3. This means that the amount of change in
resistance that the sensing element experiences is very small which in turn would result
in a small signal reading that is hard to detect and sensitive to noise. In order to get
around this problem as with strain gauges the SiPo sensor is placed in a Wheatstone
bridge configurations.
The main idea behind using a Wheatstone bridge is that instead of measuring change
in current the system measures a change in voltage. This is essentially achieved by having
two voltage dividers as illustrated in figure 2.2.

Wheatstone Bridge Error Sources
There are a number of error sources that are inherent to any bridge measurement system
that has to be taken into account [20]. The first source is what is known as offset error
and it refers to the measurement signal produced when the bridge is balanced. Ideally
when the sensor is in its idle state the voltage response at the terminals should be zero.
Practically speaking this is of course impossible since due to variations in resistor values
and other non-linearities the idle response will always be nonzero. However this type of
error source can be easily accounted for. Since this is a constant error term(relatively
==PAGE:22==
10
2 Principle of Operation and Design Considerations
Figure 2.4: Wheatstone Bridge-full bridge
speaking) the system can be calibrated by compensating for it either in code or through
hardware design.
A second error source is the full scale error. Again ideally speaking it is expected
that the measurement reading slope is identical to the actual response of the measured
parameter. However there will always be a gain associated with any system that will
cause the measurement slope to differ from the actual expected slope. This issue much
like the previous one can be resolved by calibration. By applying a known signal source
to the system the difference in response can be determined and accounted for.
A third error source is determined by the drift of the system. This drift is usually dis-
cussed in terms of time and temperature and refers to how the signal under measurement
changes in relation to them. Drift with time can be especially hard to compensate for
because it depends more on the sensor itself and it’s physical characteristics. One way
to account for this error source is to use higher order bridges like a full-bridge since the
drift over time is roughly the same in each element and therefore the error is filtered out.
Another way to account for drift is by using a dummy sensor which is identical to the
ones used in the bridge that is placed in a permanent idle state, therefore transforming
the error source into a known value that can be accounted for.
Finally there are the error sources determined by the inherent non-linearity’s of the
sensor. These can be accounted for to some degree by using the methods outlined above
but cannot be eliminated completely.

of the SmartGlove project a quarter bridge configuration was chosen as the best option.

2.2
Amplification Stage
Due to the fact that the signal from the Wheatstone bridges is in the order of mV an
amplification stage is usually used in conjunction with it. The device used to achieve this
was a operational amplifier assembly [21]. The reasons for using operational amplifiers
are as follows:
• High Gain - operational amplifiers typically have open loop gains of over 100dB
• Very High Input Impedance - when analyzing or amplifying the signals an
important consideration is the loading effect the circuit has on the sensing device.
If the input impedance of the analyzing circuit is close to that of the Wheatstone
bridge assembly the signal will be attenuated due to the voltage divider principle.
• Very Low Output Impedance - conversely the output impedance of the am-
plifier is typically very low in order to not limit the current that is drawn by the
circuitry connected to it and therefore is a good property for signal strength.
• Voltage Difference - Ideally the operational amplifier amplifies only the voltage
difference between its input terminals which is what is desired for this particular
application.

2.2.2
Operational Amplifier Error Sources
As in the case of the Wheatstone bridge when using Operational Amplifiers a number
of key error sources had to be taken into consideration. As mentioned before the Op-
amp should ideally amplify only the voltage difference between the two inputs, however
regardless of the type of amplifier that is used there will always be a voltage offset present
at the input which means that when the two inputs are tied to the same potential the
output will be non-zero. Depending on the quality and type of amplifier this voltage
offset can range from a few mili-volts to micro-volts values and is typically specified
in the data-sheet. Fortunately since this is a systematic error it can be eliminated by
compensating for it either in code or in hardware design.
A second source of error is presented in the form of input current bias determined by
the accompanying circuitry around the Op-amp.This is caused by unmatched impedances
on the two pA input lines. Due to the fact that the signal is amplified by a certain gain
even small currents can be amplified to noticeable degrees.
Typically the best way to deal with this source of error is to simply match the
impedances so that both input lines have the same input current bias which ultimately
cancel themselves out because of the differential input.
Finally the third error source is connected to input offset current. Again because
of practical reasons the input impedance of the operational amplifier ,although very
large(mega to gig Ohm values), will still draw some amount of current in the nA to pA
range and is also specified in the data-sheet of the Op-amp. This, like the Voltage offset
error, can be accounted for.

Due to the fact that the signal coming from the sensor is analog in nature the design
had to incorporate a Analog to Digital Converter(ADC henceforth) to provide digitized
measurement data to the micro-controller board. Before processing the analog signal a
low pass filter was needed in order to eliminate some of the unwanted high frequency

2.3.1
ADC requirements
Before choosing the ADC an assessment of the requirements for the applications had to
be made. As such a number of key parameters had to be taken into consideration when
choosing the ADC type and assessing its features:
• Sampling Frequency- According to the Nyquist–Shannon sampling theorem in
order to recreate a signal from the continuous domain to a discrete domain the
sampling frequency(Fs) must be at least 2 times the highest frequency component
in the signal.
• Resolution- this determines the amount of detail that can be represented in the
signal.
• Number of channels- every ADC comes with a set number of analog inputs that
are used to analyze signals together with the type of configurations they can be
used in.
• Accuracy - it determines how accurate the measurement reading is to the ac-
tual value and therefore is closely related to the resolution of the ADC since the
quantization noise is one of the main error sources influencing the reading.
• Cost- though this is the least significant parameter in terms of priority it has to
be taken into consideration for developing a low cost platform.
For the purpose of the application at hand The highest frequency component of
interest is determined by the highest frequency at witch the SiPo sensors are expand-
ed/contracted. For a human hand this is determined by the maximum angular speed
a joint can can move, for the shortest possible angular distance it can travel. Unfortu-
nately due to the complex kinematic nature of hand movement(that has many degrees of
freedom) a comprehensive look into this matter was not possible and outside the scope
of the project. Additionally not a lot of research was found dealing with this particular
subject. Therefore based on [23] and a rough estimation the following numbers assume
a stretching/contracting frequency of maximum 10Hz.This means that the sampling
frequency according to the Nyquist–Shannon theorem has to be at least 20Hz.However
==PAGE:27==
2.3 Data Acquisition, Filtration and Processing
15
for a proper signal reconstruction a good rule of thumb is to have 10XFs and therefore
the minimum sampling frequency was chosen to be 100Hz.
For a hand sensing device a higher resolution corresponds to more fluent and precise
hand tracking and therefore it is desirable to have it as high as possible. Finally with
regard to the number of channels there have to be at least 5 for single ended readings
and 10 for differential readings.
2.3.2
ADC architecture
Given the many options for ADC architectures choosing the one that best fits this appli-
cation is dependent on the constraints outlined in section 3.3.1. Since hand movement
is a relatively low frequency activity the SmartGlove system requires an ADC architec-
ture that prioritizes resolution and accuracy to sampling rate. The two main options
that are widely used are the SAR(Successive Approximation Register) and Sigma-Delta
architectures. For the purpose of this project the Sigma-Delta ADC was chosen due to
the fact that it can provide higher bit resolutions with better noise reduction [24] while
having sampling rates high enough for tracking hand movements. The Sigma-Delta ar-
chitecture achieves this by a series of processes known as oversampling, noise shaping,
digital filtering, and decimation [25].

An avenue that was explored in the process of testing configurations was the possibility

internal PGA (Programmable Gain Amplifier) that can amplify from a gain of 1 to 32
and on its own the ADC provide two more levels of 2 and 4 which together come up
to 128. For the test presented here the ADS1261 was set in PGA bypass mode which
bypasses the PGA inputs and the input voltage range is extended all the way to the
analog supply.Thus only the 32 64 and 128 levels of amplification are used as a result.
Using the built test circuitry and equipment it became possible to ascertain the
capabilities of the sensor and the available hardware as well as the constraints imposed
by them. To this end there are a number of key tests that were carried out. They were
done for the epoxy isolated, copper strip, and platinum strip samples.

amplification of the ADC was used. Unless otherwise stated the tests are carried out

This means that for 5 channels the effective Sampling rate is 8kS/s.

can be seen in figure 8.5.
Figure 8.5: Voltage supply Fluctuation
For this test the ADC was left to run in continuous conversion mode. The supply
voltage fluctuates with roughly ±500mV . According to this reading in the worst case
scenario the lowest point of the reference voltage would be at around 4.5V which while
not ideal is no where near the 0.4 V necessary to trigger the reference voltage alarm. At
the same time however on its largest peak the voltage rises up to 5.5V for approximately
40ns.
According to the ADS1261 datasheet the recommended operating conditions

specify a maximum analog and digital voltage of 5.25V and an absolute maximum
rating of 7V . This means that while the overshoot may not be enough to damage the
ADC permanently it may be enough to interrupt normal operation and invoke damage
over time.
8.3
System Diagnoses
Given the information gathered so far it is clear that the faulty readings are due to
problems with the reference voltage. However as of the writing of this documentation
the root cause is unclear. Future investigation into fault detection include: examining
for bad solder connections on supply pins for the ADC and determining the cause of the
relatively high supply fluctuations

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The goal of the project was to design a hardware platform around the SiPo sensor to
facilitate data acquisition. A good deal of insight into the characteristics of the SiPo
sensor as well as knowledge in building measurement and data acquisition hardware was
gained throughout the process of development. In the beginning of the project journey
a comprehensive research into the SiPo sensor and related technologies was done. The
understanding gained about the properties and characteristics of strain sensors as well
as the technologies built around them formed the foundation of knowledge needed to
make the SmartGlove platform.
During the process of acquiring knowledge into the field of measurement and data
acquisition, information was gained on Wheatstone Bridge and strain gauge function-
ality. At the same time proper use of operational amplifier and ADC technology was
explored in order to gain a better understanding of signal conditioning and processing.
Before starting the task of designing the hardware it was necessary to first understand
the electrical characteristics and behaviour of the sensor material.
To facilitate this
an automated measurement acquisition and processing system was put in place. This
streamlined the task of understanding the SiPo material capabilities and limitations as
well as helped in manufacturing better sensors. Throughout this endeavour a greater
appreciation for measurement accuracy and error sources was obtained. Circuit models
of the sensor and different subsystems were made with the scope of building a predic-
tive model of the system. Together this helped create a picture of the overall system
architecture.
With the gained knowledge from research, as well as the insights from the circuit
simulations it was possible to build circuitry and equipment to test the different con-
figurations and hardware. Along this process the concept of modularity was heavily
employed which aided in fast prototyping. The use of mechanical and control circuitry
skills laid the foundations for building a controlled and repeatable test environment.
Ultimately these practices helped determine an optimal solution for the application
by reducing component count and deciding on system configuration. With all this it was
finally possible to design the actual hardware for the PCB prototype. Throughout this
a fair amount of experience was obtained about PCB hardware design. Using datasheet
information for design examples and best practices the final product began to take
shape. An appreciation for the skill of component placement, wiring logistics and space
management was gained.

9 Conclusion
Though the final product requires further performance analysis and fault assessment,
it establishes a solid milestone for future development.
9.1
Future Work
In regards to the progression of the project going forward the first priority is to solve
the issue with sensor readings. Upon resolving this, optimization’s can be done to the
design by removing some of the redundant signal pins on the header and the power switch
owning to the fact that their primary role for this current build was to allow for more
hardware and software debugging options. Furthermore a proper power consumption
analysis should be done in order to assess the portability aspect of the system.
