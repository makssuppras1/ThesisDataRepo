Abstract
The linear response function of a system is a quantity of great interest in con-
densed matter physics, and appears in GW calculations, correlation energy cal-
culations and in the dielectric matrix. One of the bottlenecks in calculating
this quantity is that it involves an inﬁnite sum over unoccupied orbitals; a sum
which converges slowly. The eﬀective energy technique allows the sums over
unoccupied states to be eliminated, potentially providing a large speed-up in
calculations. The goal of this thesis is to implement the eﬀective energy tech-
nique within the framework of the projector augmented wave method (PAW).
Benchmark calculations show the promised speedup in a variety of diﬀerent sys-
tems. Furthermore, using the eﬀective energy technique reveals an error speciﬁc
to the PAW formalism, and partially corrects it.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In computational materials science, the overarching goal is to accurately model
the physical and chemical properties of materials based on their atomic-scale
properties, using the framework of quantum mechanics. The ability to do this
would allow us to predict how existing materials would react in novel situations,
and even to design and create new materials with desirable properties, to be
used for example in solar cells or creating novel catalysts. The rapid increase in
computer power over the last years mean that it is possible to model more and
more systems realistically.
Many quantities of interest are described by the interactions and behaviour of
the electrons in the material, and in principle, the equations governing their
behaviour are well-known. Solving these equations exactly is only possible for
very simple systems, and is in general a very complex task. This is because
the electrons interact with one another, and so all the equations are coupled:
the behaviour of one electron is correlated with the behaviour of all the others.
Instead of having an equation in three variables for each of the N electrons,
we therefore have a single equation in 3N variables for all the electrons. There
is currently no known way to solve this many-body equation exactly, and even
representing the many-body wavefunction is a challenge. As an example, if we
want to represent the behaviour of 10 electrons on a 10 × 10 × 10 grid, we have
to deal with 1030 pieces of information. This is not likely to be practical in the
foreseeable future.
Density Functional Theory
Fortunately, there is a way of avoiding having to solve the many-body problem
known as Density Functional Theory (DFT)[1]. In this framework, we model
the electrons as non-interacting, and include the interactions by adding in an
eﬀective potential. All the diﬃculties of the many-body problem are hidden away
in this quantity, known as the exchange-correlation potential. The challenge
then lies in ﬁnding good approximations to this potential and to the related
exchange-correlation energy.
This quantity can be split into an exchange part and a correlation part, where all
of the diﬃculty can be placed in determining the correlation energy. A promis-
1
==PAGE:14==
ing approach to ﬁnding this is the random phase approximation (RPA), which
produces accurate results, but has the disadvantage of being computationally
expensive. One of the reasons for this is that it involves a sum over all the empty
states in the system, a sum which converges very slowly.
Recently, it has been suggested that the sum over states in the RPA can be
eliminated, by using a single expression to account for the contribution from all
the empty states: the eﬀective energy[2]. This technique transforms the sum over
all the unoccupied states into a sum which only runs over the occupied states,
can can therefore greatly reduce the computational cost of RPA calculations.
DFT in practice: Pseudopotentials and Projector
Augmented Waves
The DFT formalism says nothing about how it is to be implemented in practice.
One of the challenges which arises here is that the electronic wave functions have
very jagged features near the nuclei, and representing these features is compu-
tationally expensive. One possible solution is to treat explicitly only the valence
electrons and approximate the nuclei and the core electrons by pseudopoten-
tials. Another approach is to treat the regions of space near to the nuclei and
far from the nuclei separately. This leads to the Projector Augmented Wave
(PAW) method[3].
The eﬀective energy technique has previously been shown to work well using
pseudopotentials in the ABINIT software package, but has never been imple-
mented in PAW.
The aim of this work is to eliminate the sum over states in the RPA calculations
in PAW using the eﬀective energy technique. The transformations involved in
eliminating the sum over states also make it possible to map out some weak-
nesses which have recently been identiﬁed in the PAW formalism, and which are
diﬃcult to probe otherwise[4].
Outline
The remainder of the thesis is organised as follows:
• Chapter 2 contains a brief introduction to the theory of DFT, followed by
an explanation of the PAW method and the Eﬀective Energy Technique.
• Chapter 3 contains details of how the Eﬀective Energy Technique can be
implemented in GPAW
• Chapter 4 contains simulations in diﬀerent materials using both the stan-
dard RPA calculation and the Eﬀective Energy Technique.

2.5
The Projector Augmented Wave method
From functional analysis, we know that the (non-degenerate) eigenfunctions of
a Hermitian operator in a Hilbert space must all be orthogonal to one another.
In quantum mechanics, the Hamiltonian is one such operator, and so we would
expect the eigenstates of the Hamiltonian describing any physical system to be
orthogonal. This becomes a problem near the nuclei when doing DFT calcu-
lations in solid state physics: All the states; core, valence and conduction are
nonzero in this region, and hence the wavefunctions must exhibit very sharp
features to maintain orthogonality. Computationally, representing these sharp
features well in turn means that the calculations have to be performed on very
ﬁne grids in realspace, or equivalently with a very high plane-wave cutoﬀin
reciprocal space.
11
==PAGE:24==
A number of diﬀerent approaches to eliminating the computational burden posed
by the core region have been proposed; among these are the use of pseudo-
potentials, and the projector augmented wave method.
In the pseudopotential method, the whole system of core electrons and nuclei are
described by an eﬀective, much smoother, potential and the DFT calculations
are performed only for the valence electrons. The drawback of this method is
that all information on the full wave function close to the nuclei is lost; and that
there is no easy way of knowing when the approximation yields reliable results.
An alternative to this is to divide space into separate regions - spheres centered
on each atom, and a bonding region outside these spheres. In the bonding region,
the wavefunctions can be expected to be smooth, and numerically simpler to
deal with. We then solve the Kohn-Sham equations for the smooth function ˜

Using Kohn-Sham DFT requires knowledge of a complex functional known as the

We have then seen how the random phase approximation allows us to calcu-

Finally we have seen how calculations can be performed in practice, using plane-

The constant term approximates the contribution from all states - both those

The calculation of each term involves two separate components - ﬁrst, there is
the pair density between valence states and other states, and secondly there is
the calculation of the denominator, including the eﬀective energy δ.
In this chapter we will see how these components are calculated. We will start
by looking at how an RPA calculation in GPAW currently works, to gain an
understanding of how the diﬀerent parts of the code work together. Then, we will
see how to calculate the new quantities needed for the EET using the existing

3.1
RPA calculations in GPAW
The calculation of the RPA correlation energy is done in three steps. First, a self-
consistent DFT calculation is performed. For all calculations in this project, the
17
==PAGE:30==
Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) functional[8] will be used. The code to perform
the self-consistent DFT is
bulk_crystal = bulk(’Si, ’diamond’, a=5.421)
bulk_calc = GPAW(mode=PW(1200), xc=’PBE’,
kpts={’size’: (k, k, k), ’gamma’: True}
occupations=FermiDirac(0.0001))
bulk_crystal.set_calculator(bulk_calc)
e0 = bulk_crystal.get_potential_energy()
In ﬁnding the ground-state, we also ﬁnd the self-consistent DFT Hamiltonian.
This can then be diagonalised explicitly to ﬁnd the excited states and their
energies, which in GPAW is accomplished by calling the method
bulk_calc.diagonalize_full_hamiltonian().
The number of states which will be found by this routine will depend on the
plane-wave cutoﬀspeciﬁed in the call to PW: the Hamiltonian is diagonalised
explicitly, and hence there will be as many states as the dimension of the basis
set. This is given by the number of G-vectors, which is proportional to E−3/2
cut,DFT.
Once the Hamiltonian has been diagonalised and the excited states have been
found, the noninteracting response function can be calculated according to equa-
tion (2.2.6) and thence the correlation energy according to (2.2.5). This calcu-
lation should in principle be done self-consistently, by including this new corre-
lation energy in equation (2.1.6) and ﬁnding the ground state from that, but a
non self-consistent approach will be used instead[26].
As the calculations are performed in a plane wave basis, a central ingredient
is the plane-wave descriptor, which contains information about the reciprocal
lattice, including the G-vectors and the Fourier transform from realspace to
reciprocal space.
3.2
Pair Densities
The calculation of pair densities has already been implemented in GPAW[27]
and is used for the standard χ0 calculations. As long as we can describe which
G-vectors we want to ﬁnd the pair density for, it is therefore possible to use the
existing code for this without performing any changes.
The standard χ0 calculation also involves a sum over unoccupied states; the
EET sum can therefore be included termwise, by adding on the correction inside
the existing sum over states. As the EET sum runs over occupied states as well,
these terms have to be included explicitly. This can be done by manually setting
the lower limit in the sum to zero. For semiconductors, this will not aﬀect the
standard calculation of χ0, as the pair densities in equation (2.2.5) are multiplied
by the diﬀerence in occupation number between |v⟩and |n⟩. If both states are
fully occupied, this will give zero.
3.3
EET Denominator
In the zeroth order approximation, the EET δ is given by equation (2.3.4),
which is independent of the state involved. This means that the calculation

summations, instead of having to be performed once for every term.
The zeroth order δ can be programmed in python as

|G + q|2 for all the vectors which fall inside the cutoﬀradius. The third line
adds this to its transpose, creating exactly the symmetrised matrix in G and

3.4
Constant Term
Using the completeness relation in the deﬁnition of the eﬀective energy results
in terms of the form ρvv(G−G′). In the current GPAW code, it is only possible
to ﬁnd the pair density as a one-dimensional array, ρvv(G). To get from this
expression to a 2D matrix requires some index manipulation, which will be
described in the following section.
The second issue involved in the calculation of the pair density of the constant
term is selecting the appropriate plane wave cutoﬀfor the G-vectors. This will
be described in section 3.4.3.
3.4.1
Indices
GPAW can already calculate a pair density of the form ρvv(G), for any G-
vector we give it. The simplest way of constructing the matrix in G and G′
whose elements are the desired pair density is to calculate ρvv(Γ) for every Γ
which lies within a sphere with radius equal to twice the size of the largest
G-vector in our system. Once we have this 1D array, we know what the pair
density of G−G′ is for any G and G′ inside the small cutoﬀsphere: even when
G and G′ are antiparallel, they can be no larger than twice the radius of the
small cutoﬀsphere, and hence will be included in the 1D array.
The problem is identifying which elements of the 1D array correspond to which
element of the matrix, and the challenge then boils down to selecting the correct
elements of the array and placing them at the correct locations in the matrix.
To do this, an index array from the 1D array to the matrix is created, in such
a way that numpy’s clever indexing makes it possible to create the pair density
matrix simply by calling
pair_density_matrix = pair_density_array[indices].
Creating this index array turns out to be non-trivial, and proﬁling the code
shows that it takes a signiﬁcant amount of time. Consequently, diﬀerent ap-
proaches were used to calculating the array. First, a naïve implementation using
a double for loop was written, and this proved slow.
19
==PAGE:32==
To improve the calculation time, a way was found to generate the index array
once per calculation, instead of once for each q-point, resulting in a signiﬁcant
speedup. Finally, a further, smaller, speedup was achieved by using a more
sophisticated algorithm to calculate the array.
Naïve Calculation of the Index Array
Based on the deﬁnition of the quantities involved, the index array can be created
by using the following code:
for i in range(ni):
for j in range(nj):
k = ((vector - matrix[i, j])**2).sum(1).argmin()
indices[i, j] = k
.
Listing 1: Generating the index mapping
.Here ni and nj are the number of G and G′ vecotrs, vector is an array of
G-vectors inside the large ball and matrix is a matrix of the corresponding
G −G′ elements. k then represents the index in the array for which the vector
in vector is closest to the vector at the [i,j]-location in matrix.
This naïve approach to matching the G-vectors has the advantage of being
robust: if ﬂoating point errors cause the elements of vector and the elements
of matrix to diﬀer slightly, the index array will still be set to the correct value.
Unfortunately, this approach also has two disadvantages. Firstly, it is slow, as
it consists of a double for loop over all the (potentially very many) plane waves
included in the calculation, a for loop which cannot be parallelised over k-points.
Secondly, it introduces a risk of setting the some of the indices to nonsense values
- if for some reason a given G −G′ is not found in the array of G-vectors, the
code will just use the index for which the vector is closest, without complaining.
The second problem should never occur, as the cutoﬀs have been chosen so that
all of the G −G′ can be represented. The ﬁrst turns out to be a serious issue,
and indeed for the naïve solution, the calculation of the indices turned out to
take up to 80% of the calculation time - though only when the code runs on
many processors.
To speed up the calculations, two separate tricks can be employed. The ﬁrst
is to calculate the index array once, and then pass it in as a parameter where
needed. This can potentially result in a very large speed-up. The second is to
use a better algorithm to calculate the indices.

G-vectors ﬁt inside the |G + q|2 cutoﬀsphere depends on the value of q.

than the largest possible q-vector. This means that for the diﬀerent q values,

3.4.2
Using a hash map to calculate the index array
The second solution was to use a hash map (python dict) on the vectors, which
created the required vector →index map, and then use a dictionary lookup
to apply this map to the matrix. Implementing the index like this should have
better asymptotic complexicty than the double for loop. In the best case, the
naïve implementation shown in listing 1 is O(n3), where n is the number of
G-vectors inside the cutoﬀ: The double for loop contributes a factor of n2,
and then for each element in the loop, we have to at minimum examine every
element of a 1D array to ﬁnd which is the smallest, giving another factor of n.
The approach using a hash map has better asymptotic complexity: assuming
that generating the hash map is O(n), where n is the number of elements to
be hashed, and that each individual lookup is O(1), we can conclude that the
asymptotic complexity is O(n2), as n2 lookups must be performed.
The implementation of the hash map in python is as follows

and each vector value is replaced by the corresponding index. Finally, the re-

Using this implementation, only a slight speedup is actually achieved in the
index calculation. An explanation for this could be that the asymptotic analysis
completely ignores the constant factors in front of the order: The hash lookup
might be doable in constant time, but for the range of n which we are consid-

Using this implementation of the hash function also reduces the robustness of the
code, as the code now requires that the two sets of vectors be exactly identical,
which is not guaranteed when dealing with ﬂoating point numbers. Therefore,
to make the code behave sensibly, the naïve method of calculating the indices
is left in place as a fallback in case anything goes wrong.

3.4.3
Plane wave cutoﬀ
The other point of attention in the calculation of the constant term is which
plane wave cutoﬀshould be used for the constant term. If the full G-vector
ball of radius 2Gmax is used, that would require knowledge of the plane-wave
components of the states at a cutoﬀenergy of 4Ecut, where Ecut is the energy
cutoﬀused for the remainder of the χ0 calculation. In practice, this would mean
that to do RPA calculations in silicon with a cutoﬀenergy of 300 eV, one would
have to diagonalise the original Hamiltonian at a cutoﬀof 1200 eV - well beyond
the limit where the pseudo-wavefunctions have converged.
23
==PAGE:36==
It turns out that there is a way to avoid this. One would expect that the most
jagged features of the wavefunctions exist near the nuclei, inside the augmen-
tation spheres. This means that to a very good approximation, the smooth
wavefunctions have no plane-wave components at high cutoﬀs, and hence only
the parts inside the augmentation sphere contribute.
This makes it natural to split up the calculation of the pair density and per-
form two separate calculations: ﬁrst calculate the contribution from the pseudo-
wavefunction with respect to a G-vector cutoﬀof Gmax, and then add in a
contribution from the PAW corrections with respect to a cutoﬀof 2Gmax.
Because of this split calculation, it is necessary to work with two separate index
arrays - one mapping to the large ball centered at the origin, the other mapping
to a smaller ball centered at the origin. The PAW contribution can be added
exactly as described in the previous section, while the contribution from the
pseudo-wavefunctions has to be included in a slightly more clever way: when
the indices are calculated as before, there will be elements of G −G′ which do
not correspond to any vector in the smaller array. To avoid ﬁlling the matrix
with nonsense values, we can perform the following operation
mask = (matrix**2).sum(2) / 2.0 < ecut
pseudo_pair_density_matrix = pseudo_pair_density_array[indices]*mask
First a mask is created by ﬁnding the magnitude of the G −G′ vector and
comparing it with the small cutoﬀenergy. Then, the ﬁnal matrix is multiplied
by this mask so that only the elements which are smaller than the cutoﬀenergy
will be set; the rest will remain zero, exactly as desired.
The separation of the pair density calculation into two components also makes
it possible to examine the eﬀect of varying the cutoﬀof the pseudo-wavefunction
pair density calculation between Ecut and 4Ecut.
Figure 3.2 shows how the RPA correlation energy in silicon, diamond and silicon
carbide changes as the cutoﬀenergy of the pseudo-wavefunction pair density
is increased. It can be seen that there is an eﬀect - going from the smallest
cutoﬀto the largest cutoﬀchanges the correlation by some meV. Performing
the calculations at a higher energy cutoﬀmakes this eﬀect even less important.
For comparison, the absolute correlation energy per unit cell for the diﬀerent
substances is between 12 and 14eV, so the relative change in energy is less than
1‰. In comparison with this shift, the advantage of not having to diagonalise

to converge faster with respect to the number of empty states included, and
from section 4.1 we should not expect the EET and the sum over states RPA
calculation (SOS) to necessarily converge to the same value.

Chapter 5
Discussion and Outlook
The simulations performed have shown that the EET generally gives a faster
convergence with respect to the number of bands, but there are two questions
which remain.
The ﬁrst is the discrepancy between the EET and the SOS enerrgies - by con-
struction, the two should give exactly the same result in the limit of including
very many bands, so what is causing the diﬀerence?
The second question is with respect to the convergence of the correlation energy
with respect to the RPA cutoﬀenergy. The derivation of the EET presented in
section 2.3 xshows that the convergence with respect to the cutoﬀenergy should
be exactly the same for the EET and for the SOS. Understanding why the EET
converges more slowly for magnesium oxide than the SOS does is therefore also
desirable.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
We have implemented the zeroth order eﬀective energy technique in the GPAW
codebase, and improved the implementation so that calculations using the EET
run only 30% slower than those running without.
In the course of testing the implementation, a tool to verify the completeness of
the states generated by the existing PAW framework has been created. Using
this tool reveals that, particularly for the metal oxides, MgO and ZnO, there are
errors in the states created by GPAW. This is due to the fact that only a small
number of projectors and basis functions are used to describe the wavefunctions
inside the augmentation spheres.
Calculating the correlation energy of the tetrahedral semiconductors silicon,
silicon carbide and diamond is possible with signiﬁcantly fewer bands in the
EET than it is with the SOS calculation, and the converged values with and
without the EET are very similar.
The correlation energy for the metal oxides is very diﬀerent for the SOS and
the EET; a diﬀerence in excess of 1 eV per electron for zinc oxide. By using
setups with smaller cutoﬀradii it is possible to get signiﬁcantly better agreement
between the EET and the SOS for magnesium oxide. Extrapolating to perfect
agreement shows a ﬁnal value twice as close to the EET value as to the SOS
value.
For both the tetrahedral semiconductors and the metal oxides, the convergence
with respect to the cutoﬀenergy is seen to follow a power law at high cutoﬀs; for
both the EET and the SOS. For the metal oxides, the convergence with respect
to cutoﬀenergy is much slower with the EET than without. This appears to be
due to the violation of the completeness relation in the PAW framework.
Due to the observed diﬀerences in total correlation energies between the EET
and the SOS, the correlation energy as a function of lattice constant has also
been calculated. This showed that the main eﬀect of the EET is a constant shift
in correlation energy, but that a slight qualitative diﬀerence remains, a shift
which will manifest itself as a slightly diﬀerent equilibrium lattice constant.
Calculating this equilibrium lattice constant for the diﬀerent materials with the
EET and SOS shows a diﬀerence of less than 0.5% between the EET and the
45
==PAGE:58==
SOS for the tetrahedral semiconductors, and a diﬀerence of 0.7% for magne-
sium oxide, indicating that the diﬀerences between the EET and the SOS will
ultimately have observable eﬀects on the physics of diﬀerent systems.
Going to higher order in the eﬀective energy technique would enable calculations
to be performed with even fewer bands included explicitly. This would speed up
calculations, but also further reduce the PAW error, as the highly-lying states
are more aﬀected by it. A reduction in this error would also translate to a faster
convergence with respect to cutoﬀenergy.
Finally, an improvement in the index calculation routine can make the zeroth
order EET practical to use in all calculations.
