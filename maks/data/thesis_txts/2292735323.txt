Summary (English)
In many areas of industry and healthcare, 3D scanning is becoming an ubiquitous technology. Many
different technologies compete in the ability to digitize real world objects in various ways. While some
methods aim to provide high speed 3D scanning, others aim to perform an exceptionally precise digital
reconstruction of the subject. To obtain such accurate results, a system comprising motors, axes, cameras
and a source of light is often used. This system is able to meticulously position and orient the subject, while
capturing images of a pattern of light, often lines, projected onto the subject. In some scanners, this pattern
of light is translated across the subject by moving cameras and projector using a stepper motor and a linear
axis. This expensive, heavy and noisy component may potentially be replaced by a technology capable of
deviating the projected light in a controlled fashion. One promising method involves the use of a liquid lens
with variable-tilt functionality, e.g. the Baltic 617 made by Varioptic.
Over the course of this project, the Baltic 617 liquid lens has been modelled and simulated to establish
its theoretical qualities. Subsequently, the liquid lens has been characterized in controlled experiments
to determine its Ô¨Ātness for use in 3D scanning applications. Finally, an experimental scanner has been
assembled using parts from various other scanners, and Ô¨Ātted with a new projector. This new projector
incorporates the Baltic 617 liquid lens, and is thus capable of sweeping the projected pattern of light. The
software and procedures associated with calibration and scanning, using a 3Shape scanner, have been
amended to embody the functionality needed to use the new liquid lens.
The end result is a prototype scanner that employs a liquid lens in order to replace the conventional linear
translation with an angular sweeps. The scanner has been used to scan several objects to test its ability in
comparison to that of existing scanners. The experimental scanner is capable of scanning full size dental
casts with a performance sufÔ¨Ācient for use in industry.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
For many years, the idea of 3D scanning only existed in science Ô¨Āction settings. However, in the last half
of the 20th century, the Ô¨Ārst 3D scanning technology was created. While the early efforts were crude and
unreliable, the technology has been evolving rapidly ever since. Today, the science, materials and techniques
have grown so as to make 3D scanning a tool in industry as well as in our everyday life.
One Ô¨Āeld that has already proÔ¨Āted from the coming of industry-grade 3D scanners, is dentistry. Tra-
ditionally, casts are created from imprints of a patient‚Äôs teeth and the surrounding tissue. These casts are
painstakingly measured by hand, and used to design prosthetics that Ô¨Āt tightly. This procedure not only
contains many sources of error, but also forces the dentist to spend many hours carrying out trivial work.
In contrast, 3D scanning equipment allows for the generation of 3D models directly from the casts. These
models may be measured by software, thus allowing for extreme accuracy whilst removing the element of
human error.
In order to obtain a digital three dimensional mesh of a cast, many techniques may be used. In general, a
known source of light is shone at the subject, and the reÔ¨āected light measured and used in triangulation to
establish the physical shape of the subject. To fully scan an object, light must be shone on, and reÔ¨āected
by, every single point on the surface of the object. To facilitate this, the object must be moved and rotated
relative to the source of light. Whereas simple systems may require the object to be moved and rotated by
hand, precise measurements require higher precision in the movement of the object. Thus, high-precision
actuators such as stepper motors may be employed to translate and rotate the object.
The Danish company 3Shape produces high-precision 3D scanners, primarily intended for dental use.
One set of scanners designed by 3Shape is the Dental Lab Desktop Scanner series. In this report, one of
the newest models in this series, the D1000, will serve as the reference scanner, and will thus be referred
to as the desktop scanner, reference scanner or production scanner. The D1000 employs a Light-Emitting
Diode (LED) source and associated optics in order to emit a pattern of light in the form of 27 parallel lines.
These lines are photographed by four digital cameras to establish an overdetermined system, in which the
3D position of any single point on a line of light may be precisely found. To fully cover the surface of the
subject, three stepper motors are employed, two of which are used for rotating the object in two dimensions.
The third stepper motor is used to translate a mount on which the LED source and cameras are placed.
1.1
Problem and Motivation
Stepper motors allow for precise translation and rotation of the object under inspection, but consume
large amounts of power whilst doing so. This in turn induces electrical noise in other parts of the scanner,

movement in the motor will not only result in wear, but also create vibrations and sound, both of which are

the translation of the light pattern on the subject could be carried out without moving the entire LED source

1.2
Goal
A recent innovation, the liquid lens, utilizes the principle of electrowetting to create a variable-focus lens
with no mechanical movement. Some liquid lenses have multiple electrodes, allowing for incident rays of
light to be deÔ¨āected in a controlled fashion.
This project aims to replace the translation of the camera column in a 3Shape Desktop Scanner with a
controlled deÔ¨āection of the projected light. This will be done by employing a liquid lens, namely the Baltic
617 made by Varioptic. By integrating the Baltic 617 into an existing desktop scanner, the translation of
the projected light may be done without using mechanical actuators. The end result should be a prototype
scanner that is capable of scanning real objects and is comparable to the scanners currently in production.

2.2
3D Scanning
The act of 3D scanning cannot be conÔ¨Āned to a single method. This is because real objects appear in all
kinds of shapes and sizes, with many different textures and composed of many different materials. Some
materials scatter incident light in all directions in a diffuse fashion, whereas other materials reÔ¨āect a ray of
light precisely in a well deÔ¨Āned direction. Furthermore, objects may exhibit smooth surfaces, or feature
jagged contours and sharp edges.
The combination of the above-mentioned traits lead to a situation, in which no current method provides
full coverage of the spectrum of objects that may be interesting to scan. When the economical perspectives
of time, cost and precision are added, the complexity grows even further.
To correctly cope with all these different scenarios, 3D scanners come in many forms and sizes. Some
are capable of extracting a crude 3D model from just a few images captured in ambient light using a standard
digital camera. Others offer precision in the range of a few microns at the cost of specialized hardware,
optics and a controlled environment.
Common for almost all 3D scanners is that the result of a scanning session is a cloud of points, which
may be used in a reconstruction algorithm to determine the Ô¨Ānal 3D mesh. The key methods for obtaining
these 3D point clouds may be coarsely divided into three categories: contact-based, Time-Of-Flight (TOF)
and triangulation. Of these three, only the non-contact approaches, i.e. TOF and triangulation, will be
considered. These technologies will be detailed in the sections below, along with the pros and cons of each
method.

Recent developments in the triangulation approach has seen the number of cameras increased to two

position of a given point, thus reducing the variance associated with this point. Having multiple cameras
also alleviates the need for the rotation of the projector, őł, to be known, because the measurements provided
by the cameras are sufÔ¨Ācient for establishing the location of the point in 3D space. Finally, multiple cameras
can improve scan speed and coverage, as described by Hollenbeck et al. [2012].
The most simple form of triangulation scanning is depicted in Ô¨Āgure 2.2, namely that of line scanning. In
this approach, the light source projects a plane of light onto the subject, manifesting itself as a line across the
surface of the subject. This approach is sufÔ¨Ācient for making precise high-resolution scans, but requires that
the single line is moved across the entire surface of the object. Although this task is easily accomplished,
it comes at the cost of scanning speed. One solution to this problem is to project multiple lines onto the
subject, thus allowing the determination of the position of more points without having to move the subject,
camera or projector[Georgopoulos et al., 2010]. However, doing so increases the complexity of the point
matching between cameras due to correspondence issues. Any observed point must be mapped to a line
whilst factoring in occlusion, edges on the subject and changes in surface reÔ¨āectivity.
The correspondence problem can be solved in multiple ways. Common for almost all of these approaches
is the use of active structured lighting, i.e. light that is encoded to make any observed point unambiguous.
These patterns of active structured light are most easily produced by employing an active projector, e.g. an
Liquid Crystal Display (LCD) projector. By doing so, an arbitrary pattern may be projected onto the subject
and altered spatially in any thinkable way.
One of the simplest forms of active structured light is the projection of a sequential binary pattern as
described in [Geng, 2011, p. 134]. Figure 2.3 show an example of a sequential binary pattern. By projecting
each of the patterns shown in Ô¨Āgure 2.3 and capturing the resulting light reÔ¨āected by the subject, any point
can have its correspondence determined by considering the evolution of reÔ¨āected light in relation to the used
encoding.

Using a binary encoding will in many cases ensure a correct correspondence between points and lines,

even use a hybrid of the two.
If a scanner does not have a projector capable of changing its emitted pattern, then a search for correspon-
dence may be carried out using knowledge of the temporal behaviour of the scanning system. For instance, a
pattern may be translated across the subject at a known speed, while keeping track of the observed points
and their associated lines using a mathematical model of the entire system. This method is the one used in
the experimental scanner described in chapter 5.
In chapter 1, a number of arguments were put forth in favour of replacing the linear translation axis on
the 3Shape Dental Scanner with another mechanism. Because the scanner relies on a physical movement
of the projected line pattern, a suitable replacement for the linear axis must also be able to move the line
pattern relative to the subject. In section 2.3, a number technologies are considered for this exact use case.
2.3
Translation of Projected Line Pattern
When considering technologies for translation of a line pattern across the subject, several factors must be
taken into consideration:
Range
In order to achieve full coverage of the subject, the pattern of light must be translated a distance
equal to the spacing between adjacent lines in the projected pattern. In the experimental scanner described
in chapter 5, this corresponds to a translation of 3.1 mm or approximately 0.9¬į at a working distance of
190 mm.
Precision
To facilitate high quality scans, it is paramount that the pattern of light can be translated with
high positional resolution. Furthermore, the position of the projected pattern should be fully deterministic in
order to provide a basis for the mathematical modelling of the system.
Speed
The current linear axis technology, i.e. a geared stepper motor, provides a smooth and continuous
translation of the line pattern. Any replacement should be able to translate the projected pattern at an equal

2.3.1
MEMS Mirrors
A technology that has seen large improvements over the last decade is that of MicroElectroMechanical
Systems (MEMS) operated mirrors. MEMS mirrors are electromechanical mirrors that may be controlled
by one or more voltages. Some MEMS mirrors allow for movement along one axis[Greywall et al., 2003],
whereas others provide the ability to position the mirror along two or more axes[Bernstein et al., 2003].
Some MEMS mirrors are tiny, and are thus usually found in arrays or grids[Bernstein et al., 2003].
Figure 2.4 shows an example of a MEMS mirror array with one degree of freedom. As evident in the Ô¨Āgure,
a single mirror has been tilted.
Figure 2.4: Array of MEMS mirrors with one degree of freedom, adapted from Greywall et al. [2003]
By moving all the individual mirrors in a MEMS mirror grid, the effect of tilting a single large mirror
can be emulated. If the line pattern is projected onto such a grid of mirrors, a small change in mirror angle
can enable a translation of the reÔ¨āected line pattern across the subject. Such a setup is depicted in Ô¨Āgure 2.5.
MEMS mirrors may also be much larger, some in the range of 400 mm2 [Senger et al., 2015]. These
larger mirrors are not placed in grids, but are used as standalone components, each on a single chip.
MEMS mirrors are a well-understood component backed by many years of research. They are commer-
cially available, allow for a larger scan range than needed for this application and exhibit excellent precision
and speed. However, implementing a MEMS mirror grid in an existing scanner is a rather invasive procedure,
requiring a redesign of the existing mechanical and optical systems.

2.3.3
Piezoelectric Tilting Mirror
As discussed in section 2.3.1, a single mirror may be used to provide a translation of the emitted line
pattern. In order to control the rotation of the mirror precisely, a number of methods may be used. One
method that enables control without mechanical movement uses piezoelectric elements[Park et al., 2012].
Using this method, a 28 mm √ó 22 mm mirror may be moved in a range offering a sufÔ¨Ācient deÔ¨āection angle,
at very high speeds.
Another usage for a piezoelectric element could involve moving the actual projector, i.e. light source and
slit glass, relative to the imaging optics. Because of the magniÔ¨Ācation of the imaging system, a relatively
small travel distance is required to move the line pattern an adequate amount.
Piezoelectric elements are usually inexpensive, especially when compared to the price of a stepper motor,
DLP projector or a MEMS grid. Furthermore, piezoelectric elements are easy to acquire, with hundreds of
manufacturers and the potential for in-house design and production.
While a mirror may be used to deÔ¨āect the projected line pattern in a controlled fashion, another technology
seeks to eliminate the mirror all together. This approach uses a lens capable of changing its optical interfaces
dynamically. The following section will explore the theory behind the new liquid lenses.
2.4
Electrowetting Lenses
Wetting refers to the ability of a given liquid to remain in contact with a given surface as a result of
interactions between the molecules of the liquid and the surface. Consider a drop of water. The molecules
inside this drop are all equally attracted in every direction, resulting in an equilibrium. However, molecules
on the surface of the drop are all attracted toward the center of the drop, thus creating surface tension. The
surface tension is minimized if the drop assumes a perfect spherical shape, which is what almost everyone
consider as the natural shape of a very small body of water.
If such a drop is placed on a surface, a surface tension at the interface of the drop and the surface will
result. This surface tension depends on the properties of both materials, and determines to which extent
wetting will occur.

surface tensions[Shamai et al., 2008], namely those between liquid and solid, ő≥LS, between solid and air, ő≥SG

The Baltic 617 lens consists of two liquids with equal density but with different indexes of refraction.
These two liquids are immiscible, thus forming an interface that will refract incident rays. As discussed in
section 2.4, this enables the control of the lens using applied voltages.

but may also be forced toward one or more of the four electrodes. If a differential voltage is applied to two
or more electrodes, any rays of light entering the system will be tilted slightly upon leaving the liquid lens.
A mathematical description of this phenomenon will be given in section 3.3.

3.4.1
Introduction
Zemax is a program used for optical design and optimization. It allows the user to analyse and characterize
complex optical systems. The software works by carrying out ray tracing for a large quantity of rays. These
==PAGE:41==
3.4. SIMULATION IN ZEMAX
23
rays may be cast in several ways, one of which is the sequential mode. In the sequential mode, rays are
propagated through the elements of an optical system in a sequential fashion, allowing the designer to check
focus, contrast, vignetting and much more. The designer speciÔ¨Āes an optical system by creating a series of
surfaces with different radii of curvature, sizes and spacing.
Once a system has been established, Zemax is able to calculate the effects of various sources of optical
aberrations, e.g. spherical aberrations and diffraction. A system described by ray transfer matrices can easily
be converted to a Zemax model and then used for more in-depth examination and characterization.
For this project, Zemax was initially used to design an optical system consisting of a Baltic 617 as well
as additional optics required to create an imaging system with the correct focal length and magniÔ¨Ācation.
Later in the project, it was decided that the liquid lens should be combined with the lens used in the current
3Shape desktop scanners. The lens used in the current line of desktop scanners has a focal length of 12 mm
and a relative aperture of F8. In this conÔ¨Āguration, the liquid lens need only supply a tilt, because the custom
lens is already designed for this particular application. The custom lens is a composite optical system with
the limiting aperture placed approximately one-third into the lens. Seeing as the Baltic 617 also has a small
aperture, it might become the limiting aperture in a combined system, thus resulting in vignetting of the
projected line pattern.
Vignetting is usually seen as a rounded falloff in the intensity of light, dependant on distance from the
optical axis. Vignetting can severely hamper the effectiveness of a scanner due to differences in the intensity
of lines. To investigate the vignetting in different conÔ¨Āgurations, Zemax is used. The results are described in
the sections below. In each of these cases, the Zemax conÔ¨Āguration casts rays from the object plane, i.e. the
subject, through the optical system and onto the image plane, in this case the slit glass. This means that the
rays are traced in the opposite direction when compared to the actual projection from the slit glass and onto
the subject. However, seeing as the direction of propagation is arbitrary during ray-based simulation, casting
rays from the subject allows for a smaller part of the entire system to be plotted and examined.

Reproducibility
In order for a scanner to yield high precision measurements consistently, it is of great importance that any
motion can be recreated as many times as required. In the present case, this means that the liquid lens should
be capable of repeatedly translating the pattern of light to the same set of positions with high precision.
To examine the ability of the Baltic 617 lens to do so, an experiment has been conducted using the setup
described in section 4.2. The experiment uses the following procedure
1. Determine voltages associated with Ô¨Āve positions ranging from ‚ąí0.6¬į to 0.6¬į tilt with equal spacing
in between.
2. Start at leftmost position.
3. Move to next position and Ô¨Āre software trigger to capture image.
4. Repeat 3 until Ô¨Āfth position is reached.
5. Move to previous position and capture image.
6. Repeat 5 until Ô¨Ārst position is reached.
7. Repeat steps 2 to 6 100 times to ensure a proper sample size.

Interfacing with Baltic Development Board
In order to enable control of the Baltic 617 by way of the mainboard on the scanner, the development
board must be integrated into the scanner electrically. Furthermore, an interface for communication between
the scanner and the development board must be implemented using the FPGA. Finally, a set of routines for
controlling the tilt and focus of the liquid lens from the host computer must be developed.
As mentioned in section 2.5, the Baltic 617 may be controlled using the MAX14574EWL+ IC over
the I¬≤C protocol. In order to simplify the task of integrating the liquid lens into the scanner, the Baltic 617
Development Kit is utilized. The development kit contains a small Printed Circuit Board (PCB) with power
supplies, a MAX14574EWL+ and a small microcontroller. This development board allows for demoing
of Optical Image Stabilization (OIS), variable focus and tilt of the liquid lens. Furthermore, this board
contains both USB and I¬≤C interfaces for communicating with the microcontroller. Commands written over
any of these interfaces are interpreted by the microcontroller, and used to forward writes and reads to the
MAX14574EWL+.
During the experiments described in chapter 4, the USB interface is used to execute commands directly
from a host computer. However, seeing as commands must be sent from the mainboard of the scanner,
utilizing the I¬≤C interface of the development board is more practical. This is due to the simplicity of the
I¬≤C interface, and because the mainboard already has several other components that communicate using I¬≤C.
Thus, interfacing the mainboard with the development board is a matter of wiring the two PCBs together.
In order to establish the needed electrical connections between the mainboard and the Baltic development
board, a ribbon cable is used. To power and communication with the development board, a total of four
connections are required. These are
‚ÄĘ I¬≤C SCL: The serial clock signal used for I¬≤C data transmission.
‚ÄĘ I¬≤C SDA: The serial data signal used for I¬≤C data transmission.
‚ÄĘ VCC: A 3.3 V supply from the mainboard to the development board.
‚ÄĘ GND: A connection to the ground of the mainboard.
One end of the cable is attached to a free connector on the mainboard, whereas the other end is connected
to the development board using a 6-pin Molex picoclasp connector.
The actual control of the liquid lens is carried out at the user space level by the ScanLab process. Under
normal circumstances, the mainboard would be tasked with motor control in order to ensure real time
operation. However, seeing as this project is a proof of concept, the ability to read and write values over I¬≤C
is simply exposed to the user in ScanLab. This means that control of the liquid lens is no longer real time,
but saves precious development time. This issue can be mitigated to a degree, as discussed in section 5.4.

5.3
Calibration of Geometry
Once the experimental scanner is assembled, the 3Shape software must be adapted to function correctly
with the reduced hardware footprint and added liquid lens. Even though the positioning and orientation of
the various constituents of the system are known beforehand, the subtle mechanical differences between each
scanner mean that the precision of this initial estimate is not sufÔ¨Ācient. Thus, the goal of the calibration is to
reÔ¨Āne the starting guess whilst continually reducing a cost function. This cost is based on the correspondence
of the mathematical model of the system, to the actual scene. The free parameters in the cost function are the
relative position and orientation of each component, as well as error correction functions for lenses and axes.
As these free parameters are gradually Ô¨Ātted, the match between the model and the real scanner geometry
grows.
The calibration is executed in two steps; the Ô¨Ārst of which uses a known subject (dotplate) to estimate the
positioning and orientation of each component along with intrinsic parameters for both camera lenses and
motors. Figure 5.3 shows such a dotplate. This is done by presenting the dotplate to the cameras in a wide
range of positions and orientations using the motorized axes. The best results are obtained if the dotplate is
observed at different positions throughout the entire FOV of the cameras.
Figure 5.3: Dotplate used for Ô¨Ārst phase of calibration
The second calibration utilizes the knowledge garnered from the Ô¨Ārst in order to determine position,
orientation and intrinsic parameters of the multi-line projector. This section will describe the obstacles
faced, and overcome, during the Ô¨Ārst calibration pass. Section 5.4 will detail the calibration of the multi-line
projector with the addition of the liquid lens.
In order to establish the geometry of the scanner exactly, the different components that make up the
scanner must be positioned and oriented relative to one another. This relative setup lends itself to a
hierarchical description, which is given in Ô¨Āgure 5.4. As seen in the Ô¨Āgure, everything is relative to the world
coordinate system. The scanhead and rotation axis both have their position and orientation given in world

The actual calibration routine is proprietary and will thus not be discussed here. However, this is a

Because of the changes to the scanner mentioned in section 5.2, many changes had to be made to the
underlying software. The following will attempt to describe some of the problems and the solutions used to
overcome them.
First off, removing the linear and swing axes means that the system loses many of the dimensions
previously calibrated. This is both a good and a bad thing. It is good that the geometry of the scanner
is simpler, because the optimization routine will then have fewer unknowns to determine. However, it is
problematic that the rotation axis alone cannot position the dotplate throughout the full FOV of the cameras.
As a result, the acquired data is less informative than that gathered using a conventional scanner. To achieve
a successful and precise calibration, the geometrical hierarchy is changed to the one depicted in Ô¨Āgure 5.4.
Next, many changes are made to the orientations and positions included in the optimization. Finally,
images are captured at more different angles of rotation than usual. Some of these angles are more extreme
than the usual set, causing clipping of some of the features on the dotplate. However, this is easily Ô¨Āxed by
allowing a number of features to remain undetected in a given image using the scripting language.
Another, but less signiÔ¨Ācant, problem is also caused by the simpliÔ¨Ācation of the scanner. The regular
3Shape scanners are all built with a top light capable of illuminating the dotplate during this stage of
calibration. However, seeing as the scanner shown in Ô¨Āgure 5.1 has no chassis, there is nowhere to mount
such a light source. As a result, this phase of calibration must be carried out in ambient light. This means
that the lighting is often uneven with specular highlights and shadows cast across the dotplate. To remedy
this, a mobile phone with torch functionality is used for illumination during the image capture sequence.
The changes described above led to repeatable calibrations of relative positioning and orientation,
intrinsic lens parameters and rotational axis position compensation. Each of these calibration exhibited low
residuals.

Once the positioning and orientation of the rotational axis, cameras and dotplate have been found, the
position and orientation of the multi-line projector must be determined. Ordinarily, this is done by turning

because the width of the emitted pattern is larger than that of the dotplate, the linear axis is used to move
the scanhead relative to the dotplate. This ensures that all lines are seen by the cameras, thus providing the
highest amount of information to the subsequent optimization routine. This reliance on the swing and linear

As shown in Ô¨Āgure 5.4, the liquid lens is modelled as a rotational axis, providing only a one-dimensional

A successful calibration of the multi-line projector results in an update of the geometry Ô¨Āle mentioned
in the previous section. Once the orientation and position of the projector, relative to the scanhead, have
been determined, the scanner is ready for use. When scanning a subject, the knowledge obtained through
calibration is put to use. For each point on the subject that is illuminated by a portion of the line pattern,
the four images are matched with a calculated projection of the lines. If these are in agreement, the point is
accepted and stored as part of the Ô¨Ānal point cloud.
As mentioned at the beginning of this section, various issues arise from the simpliÔ¨Ācation of the scanner.

sufÔ¨Ācient for this proof of concept. In order for this approach to work, the allowed noise margin must be
increased. However, it seems that the resulting calibration is inÔ¨āuenced very little by this.

5.5
Sweep Scanning using Liquid Lens
In order to scan with a sweeping motion using the liquid lens, several changes must be made to the
scanning procedure and software environments:
‚ÄĘ The liquid lens must have its tilt changed between each image.
‚ÄĘ Each captured image must be associated with a given tilt angle.
‚ÄĘ The line tracking software must use the provided tilt angles to change the mathematical model before
attempting to align the points in an image.
‚ÄĘ The scripting environment must be able to reset tilt between scans at different rotations.
‚ÄĘ The scripting environment must be able to control the breadth of a liquid lens swipe.
To facilitate the changes listed above, a software component is created. This software component
describes the liquid lens as a motor capable of rotating around a given axis. this matches the model shown in
Ô¨Āgure 5.4. Once this component is inserted into the software architecture related to the scanner, the tilt angle
of the liquid lens for a given image may be propagated to the multi-line tracking software.
The next step is to actually change the tilt angle between images. Ordinarily, this would be accomplished
by issuing commands to the FPGA residing on the mainboard, which would then alter the tilt of the liquid
lens and send back packets containing the current state of tilt. To avoid the development time associated with
FPGA programming, this is instead done client-side. By synchronizing with End-Of-Frame (EOF) packets
from the mainboard, the tilt between each image may be altered according to a predeÔ¨Āned LUT. Every time
an image is received, the most recent index from this LUT is injected into the stream, which subsequently
Ô¨āows to the liquid lens motor component described above. Once the motor component receives an index, it
may then convert the index to a corresponding rotation of the multi-line projector. This rotation can then be
forwarded as described above.

6.1.1
Proof-of-Concept Scanner
The experimental scanner detailed in this report is very much a proof-of-concept. As detailed in chapter
5, many assumptions and changes to the underlying software had to be made in order for the scanner to
operate successfully. While the Ô¨Ānal implementation works in spite of these changes and simpliÔ¨Ācations,
only a very rough analysis of the implications of these changes have been made.
To summarize, the principal sources of implementation errors are
New Calibration Routines
To make the calibration routines function, images could only be captured
using different positions of the rotation axis. This greatly decreases the fraction of the FOV that is covered
during calibration, thus lessening the information provided to the optimization function. Because of the
complexity of the system, such an underdetermined calibration might cause errors that are very hard to track
down. For instance, a lens might exhibit distortion in the upper left corner, but if this corner is never utilized
during calibration, then the distortion will never be modelled, and never corrected for. This is not, however,
a problem pertaining only to the liquid lens scanner, but to any single axis scanner in general.
To see and characterise all of the 27 projected lines, a new dotplate target was used. This new dotplate
was only ever measured by hand, potentially injecting noise into the multi-line calibration routine. The usage
of two different calibration targets requires that no assumption regarding the likeness of these targets is made.
However, the current calibration routine relies heavily on the same dotplate being used for both calibrations.
When the traditional dotplate is swapped with the new dotplate, even minute differences in the two might
cause an increase in the levels of noise. This is indeed evident in the multi-line calibration noise tolerance,
which had to be increased by a factor of 10 for all lines to be found successfully.

The new calibration routines could only use the rotation axis to obtain information about the system.
While this seemed sufÔ¨Ācient, some dynamics and errors may have gone uncorrected. This was experienced
during calibration of the multi-line projector, where the height of the projector had a rather large deviation
between successive calibrations. This could indicate that a rotation of the subject is inadequate to fully
characterize the geometry and optics.
Modelling of Liquid Lens Tilt
As described in section 5.5, the sweeping motion of the liquid lens is
modelled as a simple, piecewise linear rotation of the multi-line projector. From the work and experiments
chronicled in chapters 3 and 4, this is known to be a faulty description of the dynamics. Figure 6.8 shows the
deviation from linearity of each recorded position during the experiment described in section 4.5.1. It is
immediately clear from the Ô¨Āgure that the tilt cannot be accurately described by a simple linear, or even a
piecewise linear, function. Because of the large differences between the model and the actual tilt of the lens,
many points are discarded by the tracking algorithm. Even worse, some points are accepted even though
they would have been discarded, had the modelling been accurate.

the size of the limiting aperture, having a larger aperture will decrease the volume usable for scanning. This
is due to the fact that defocus will set in much faster with distance from the focal plane. When attempting to
scan a larger object, the larger aperture will incur tracking errors due to parts of the object being signiÔ¨Ācantly
out of focus.

6.1.2
Liquid Lens Technology
Even though the principle of electrowetting has been known for many years, the liquid lens is still very
much in its infancy. This means that only few corporations have invested time and resources in liquid lens
research and development. As such, the capabilities of liquid lenses will likely expand and improve, as
competition increases. Because of the inherent simplicity of liquid lenses, the price of a single unit is already
quite low. Even though the price of a Baltic 617 remains unknown, other models are sold for as little as two
dollars. This price will likely decrease even further in the years to come.
A large array of different liquid lenses are already available on the market. However, most of these
devices are only capable of changing their effective focal length. The Baltic 617 is, as of yet, the only device
that features a tilting of the liquid interface. This may be due to the fact that only few machine vision tasks
beneÔ¨Āt from a tilt of only 1.2¬į.
The Baltic 617 employed for this project is an engineering sample and is not yet commercially available.
The manufacturer of the Baltic lenses, Varioptic, remains the only corporation to have expressed interest in
the production of variable-tilt liquid lenses. Thus, the future remains rather unclear for the tilting liquid lens
technology. However, Varioptic has recently announced the Visayan series of liquid lenses. This series does
not only feature a larger aperture than the Baltic 617, but also doubles the number of electrodes connected to
the liquid interface. This retains the tilting ability, whilst opening the possibility for control of astigmatism
and other higher order corrections. Furthermore, a Visayan lens might, due to its larger aperture, be suitable
for mounting in front of the existing 3Shape lens system.
This lack of enthusiasm from manufacturers, even Varioptic, should be taken seriously. Even though the
Baltic 617 was announced in January of 2011[LaserFocusWorld, 2011], the lens remains under reengineer-
ing[Varioptic, 2015]. If the same holds true for the Visayan series, then many years could pass before the
lens is made commercially available, if it is so at all.
The uncertainty of the Varioptic liquid lenses increases the risk associated with investing in them. Even
though the Baltic 617 provides an admirable performance at a low price point, it is simply too unlikely that
this lens can be sourced reliably for the next ten years. Thus, looking into other emerging beam steering
technologies, some of which are described in section 2.3, might provide the best medium- to long-term
investment.

Conclusion
At the onset of this project, the viability of liquid lenses, in regard to 3D scanning, was completely
unknown. Hence, there was no guarantee that a liquid lens scanner could function. Because of the inherent
complexity of the 3Shape Desktop Scanner, there were many obstacles that had to be overcome. The linear
translation axis has been a fast component for many iterations of the scanner, meaning that many software
components and calibration procedures are dependant on it. Yet, even during initial phase of this project,
where modelling and research were the main tasks, the liquid lens displayed great promise. The mathematical
modelling, in conjunction with simulation in Zemax, showed that the required translation could be attained

using the Baltic 617 partnered with a 12 mm lens. This hypothesis was subsequently put to the test through a
slew of experiments. These experiments further reinforced the professed potential of the Baltic 617, making
the realization of a liquid lens scanner seem plausible.
Thus, work began on the experimental liquid lens scanner. At Ô¨Ārst, it was discussed whether the scanner
should use a single-line projector to ease the implementation. This would have caused the Ô¨Ānal scanner
to only be capable of scanning very small objects, i.e. up to 3 mm across. Instead, it was decided that the
experimental scanner should use the latest hardware, the multi-line projector included. After much tinkering
and many changes to the existing software, the calibration routines started working. Once these were locked
down, the actual scanning using the liquid could commence. After many recalibrations and some trial and
error, the Ô¨Ārst cluster of lines were tracked correctly. From here on out, the scanner exceeded all expectations.
Scanning of different objects yielded results comparable to those produced by production scanners, with
greatly reduced noise, power consumption and at a lower price point.
Nevertheless, the implementation was not without its faults. Many assumptions and simpliÔ¨Ācations
degraded the performance of the experimental scanner, while the Baltic 617 disappeared from the market,
labeled as under reengineering.
This project has shown that 3D scanning using a tiltable liquid lens is a realistic and viable approach. If
this technology is invested in, it may potentially provide a reliable, silent and precise method of translating a
pattern of lines across the subject, at a fraction of the cost of current components. On the other hand, other
emerging technologies are promising similar results without the risk of being a Ô¨Ārst-mover.
The 3D scanning industry has never seen more competition, with many technologies vying to increase
resolution, speed and robustness even further. Whether the liquid lens will play an integral role in this
evolution is still uncertain. It is, however, certain that this evolution will merge our reality with its digital
counterpart, to extraordinary effect.
