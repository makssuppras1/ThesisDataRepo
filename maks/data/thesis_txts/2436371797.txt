Abstract
This study investigates the ice-storage on a solar powered vaccine cooler, to be used in
extremely hot climates in developing countries. Through this investigation, a dynamic model
of the vaccine cooler has been created, which is presented in detail along with the calibration
procedure, as well as relevant theory regarding control volumes and transient conduction.
By using the model for a numerical analysis, the study seeks to determine how diﬀerent
parameters of the vaccine cooler aﬀects the autonomy time. It is concluded that the mass of ice
is the most promising parameter to consider, along with the insulation between the ice-storage
and ambient. Furthermore, it is analyzed and discussed how much cooling capacity is needed,
in order to secure an eﬃcient freezing of the ice-storage. It is found that approximately 26 W
of cooling capacity is the minimum requirement to ensure a complete freezing of the ice, while
at least 100 W is required to ensure a relatively fast freezing. Finally, the study investigates
how the temperature of the vaccines behave, if the entire vaccine cooler is suddenly ﬁlled
with hot air, and it is determined that the current layout of the vaccine cooler ensures a
vaccine temperature within the acceptable range. By bringing the acquired knowledge into
consideration, the study proposes a possible way to optimize the existing vaccine cooler, and
gain a simulated autonomy time above 120 hours.

Introduction
In many developing countries, the electrical grid is either very unreliable or simply not existing,
especially in the rural regions and small villages. A study by the World Health Organization
(WHO) [2] revealed that 25% of health facilities in the sub-Saharan area of Africa did not
have access to any kind of electricity. Furthermore, 1/3 of hospitals in the area experienced an
unreliable grid connection. This of course leads to very limited use of electrical applications,
such as cooling of medical supplies and products.
In addition, medical supplies such as vaccines are critically needed in the developing countries.
A recent study by WHO [3] showed that the immunization program of the World Health
Organization saves about 2-3 million lives each year, but also that another 1.5 million lives
could be saved by reaching more people in the program. However, the global immunization
coverage (I.e. the amount of people with eﬀective vaccines in their blood) seems to have
stalled at around 86% and only very limited progress was attained last year.
One of the reasons why the progress has faded, is due to the fact that vaccines need to
be stored cold, in order to avoid spoilage and loss of potency. According to the WHO [4],
they have deﬁned the cold chain, which revolves around keeping all vaccines at a storage
temperature between 2 and 8°C at all times from manufacturing to administration, in order
to ensure the quality of the vaccines. But keeping vaccines cold, especially in rural regions of
developing countries is rather complicated, due to the lack of electricity to power refrigerators,
as well as astounding ambient temperatures, especially in areas such as the sub-Saharan part
of Africa.
To solve this issue several commercial coolers exists, which are able to operate without a grid
connection, often using Photovoltaic (PV) modules and a battery for storage. This thesis is
part of a project, trying to develop a new generation of vaccine cooler, from the manufacturer
Vestfrost Solutions. Their ﬁrst generation had great success with the combination of PV
and ice-storage, rather than a conventional battery. The new generation will thus be an
optimization of the ﬁrst, making it more eﬀective, thereby improving the business case of
Vestfrost Solutions.
1.1
Background and motivation
This section is written in compliance with the project description at Energiforskning.dk [5].
This master thesis is part of a project related to solar-direct-drive refrigerators for vaccine
storage. Solar-direct-drive indicates a solar powered refrigerator, which runs directly on
the DC current supplied by the solar panels, and therefore does not utilize a battery. The
project is led by Danish Technological Institute (DTI), with assisting partners being Vestfrost
Solutions, Secop and DTU.
The projects aims to improve an already existing vaccine cooler, in order to advance the
business case, by making the cooler more eﬀective, more reliable as well as cheaper. The
existing cooler is almost 15 years old, which means that improvements are needed, in order to
maintain a global market share.
4
==PAGE:11==
The ﬁrst as well as the new generation of vaccine coolers function without a battery, but
uses ice as an energy storage medium instead. The reason why batteries are not an optimal
solution, is because they tend to deteriorate quickly in the hot climates of most developing
countries, due to the high temperatures and deep discharging issues, Pedersen et al. [6].
By developing this new unit, the project hopes to eventually improve the general health of
developing countries, by being able to supply the reliable cooling needed to ensure the quality
of the vaccines.
1.2
Scope of this thesis
Using numerical analysis, dimension the ice-storage of a solar powered vaccine cooler, and
investigate how diﬀerent parameters of the ice-storage and cabinet aﬀect the autonomy time of
the vaccine cooler. By this investigation, also determine the required cooling capacity needed
in order to secure an eﬃcient freezing of the ice-storage. Finally, investigate how sudden
changes to the conditions within the vaccine chamber will aﬀect the usability of the vaccines.
To investigate these topics, an existing dynamical model of a vapour compression cycle
combined to a cold storage room will be used as a starting point. To this model, the ice-
storage will be implemented, while changing the geometry to better suit the vaccine cooler in
this project. Using data from tests on the vaccine cooler, the model will then be calibrated,
and its validity range will be assessed. The ﬁnal model will then be used to simulate the
vaccine cooler, and investigate the autonomy time, freezing time and door openings of the
vaccine cooler.
1.3
Terms and deﬁnitions
Throughout this report, a series of terms will be used. The following presents the most
common terms, as well as a brief deﬁnition. Some of the deﬁnitions are written in compliance
with the WHO [7].
Acceptable temperature range refers to the standardized range, in which all vaccines
must be kept for their entire lifetime, to avoid spoiling. As deﬁned by the WHO, this range is
from 2-8°C.
Autonomy time, as deﬁned by the WHO, refers to the amount of time, the vaccine cooler
can keep the vaccines within the acceptable temperature range during low solar radiation
conditions. This term is not to be confused with holdover time, which is the same as autonomy
time, except that the vaccine cooler is allowed no solar radiation at all. The primary diﬀerence
between the two is therefore that fans, electronics and other low-consumption equipment
are able to function when testing the autonomy time. When testing the holdover time, no
electrical equipment is able to function. Additionally, the autonomy time ends once the
vaccines reach 8°C, while the holdover time ends once they reach 10°C. Note that this thesis
investigates autonomy time, and does not further discuss holdover time.
Equilibrium temperature refers to the temperature of the air within the vaccine chamber,
5
==PAGE:12==
during the steady state phase where the ice-storage is melting.
Frame refers to the wall, separating the ice-storage from the vaccine chamber. This frame
is modelled as two outer layers of aluminum and an very small inner layer of polyurethane
(PU)-foam.
Freezing time refers to the time it takes for the vapour compression cycle to cool and freeze
the entire ice-storage from 10°C to a complete frozen state at 0°C.
Hot Zone refers to a climate condition, deﬁned by the WHO, in which the ambient tempera-
ture is 43°C.
Initial model refers to the model developed by Jonas Kjær Jensen, adopted from another
project. It is thus the model prior to any changes.
Product refers to the item(s), which are to be stored cold. In this thesis, it refers to the vials
of vaccine.
Temperate Zone refers to a climate condition deﬁned by the WHO, in which the ambient
temperature is 32°C.
1.4
Reader’s Guide
Section 2 presents the known literature on the subject, initially investigating the use of solar
power for cooling applications, then the use of ice-storage. The section ends by presenting
this thesis’ contributions to literature.
Section 3 then presents the methods used, starting with a brief presentation of the initial
model, followed by a detailed explanation of the changes made to the initial model, in order
to reach the ﬁnal version of the model. Following this, section 4 presents the calibration
process used, and discusses the validity range of the model. Afterwards, section 5 presents
the methods used to perform the various simulations.
Section 6 then continues to present the results of said simulations. At ﬁrst, a base case is
analyzed, in order to gain insight to the behavior of diﬀerent parameters throughout the
autonomy time. Afterwards, the results for autonomy time, freezing time and door-opening
simulations are presented and analyzed. The section ends by using the gained knowledge to
propose how an autonomy time of 120 hours can be reached. Finally, section 7 compares
the results to the known literature, while also accounting for the uncertainties in the model,
along with a sensitivity analysis. Based on these two topics, suggestions for further work is
discussed. In the end, section 8 summarizes the main ﬁndings and conclusions of the thesis.

2
Literature Review
2.1
Use of solar power in refrigeration
This section covers a literature survey on solar power used for refrigeration purposes. Since
solar energy is abundant in many developing countries, the option to be able to get electricity
oﬀ-grid seems like a viable and cost-eﬀective solution at ﬁrst glance.
In a study by Fatemulla et al. [8], a refrigeration system using PV modules and a battery for
energy storage was investigated. Their focus was to compare the costs of using PV, rather
than conventional grid electricity. The PV cells of course come with an investment costs,
but are then able to produce electricity practically for free (assuming negligible maintenance
costs). They found that a payback period of around 7 years was to be expected from the PV
modules. Since this was shorter than the expected lifetime, the authors deemed PV a feasible
investment. In addition, they reported a successful use of batteries as storage technology.
Severeal studies have investigated the use of thermoelectric coolers for oﬀ-grid cooling purposes
[9,10]. In the recent study by Hans et al. [9], a thermoelectric cooler driven by a PV system
including a battery for storage was investigated. Through experimental testing, it was found
that the cooler was able to keep the cold room temperature within the deﬁned boundaries of
10-15°C, while operating with a Coeﬃcient of Performance (COP) of 0.34.
Another study by Aktacir [11] also considered a refrigerator powered by a PV/ battery
combination. The goal was to investigate the system performance, both on a daily, as well as
a seasonal level in one of the warmest regions of Turkey. It was found that the PV solution
was able to deliver the required amount of energy needed to operate the refrigerator at desired
conditions.
Since PV modules generate DC current, it is often advantageous to select a DC compressor
for the refrigeration system. In a study by Ekren et al. [12], such a DC compressor was
investigated, to see it the energy usage could be reduced by use of a variable speed controller,
rather than an on/oﬀcontroller. It was found that the use of variable speed control led to
increase in COP as well as exergetic eﬃciency, particularly at high rotation speeds.
In a similar work, Jensen et al. [1] also compared the performance of a refrigerator depending
on the type of control used, through an advanced dynamic model. They conﬁrmed that the
use of variable speed control resulted in noticeable energy savings.
2.2
Use of ice-storage in refrigeration
This section present the literature on ice-storage used for refrigeration applications. Due to
the unreliability of solar power, energy storages such as ice becomes very attractive, due to
the ability of storing energy for periods with low solar radiation.
Pilatte [13] investigated a refrigerator powered by PV cells, which had the ability to produce
ice and use this for energy storage. The cooler was relatively large, with a vaccine storage
chamber of 70 litres, and an additional storage room of 70 litres, with a temperature of about
10°C. The ice-storage was 14 litres, and was able to produce 2 kg of ice within 24 hours.
7

Performing several tests revealed that the cooler was able to deliver the requirements set by
the WHO for vaccine coolers in tropical regions. During operation, the cooler consumed 370
Wh within 24 hours and achieved an average COP of 1.6.
In a bachelors project from MIT, Walker [14] investigated how to design a low-cost vaccine
cooler, focusing on a heat transfer regulating device, which purpose was to control the
temperature within the vaccine storage chamber. This cooler was also utilizing ice-storage,
in order to reach large autonomy times. (I.e. time where the storage temperature remains
within the acceptable temperature range, without any solar radiation.) Through modelling of
the system, openings of the cooler lid was investigated as a response in temperature of the
vaccine chamber. It was found that even though only one vial was present, and the entire
cooler was ﬁlled with air at 45°C, the single vial still remained within the boundary of the
acceptable temperature range.
Another study, led by Kumar et al. [15], investigated the use of ice-storage for peak-shaving of
a cooling demand. Since the system of interest had a very large peak demand, it was possible
to generate ice throughout the part-load periods, which then could be utilized in the daily
peaks. By using the ice-storage, it was found that the compressor did not have to run for as
many hours, thereby prolonging its lifetime, as well as reducing the operational costs of the
system.
Mammoli et al. [16] studied the use of ice-storage for residential purposes. It was concluded
that it was possible to develop a cheap ice-storage in the residential scale, with even better
performance than that of an equivalent battery.
In a recent work, Torres et al. [17] investigated a system using ice-storage and PV to cool
milk. They found the system to be operating with a COP between 1.15 and 1.89, with an
ambient temperature of 40°C.
2.3
Contribution to the literature
This thesis will make a contribution to the known literature. As seen in the ﬁrst two parts of
this section, it is already known that solar panels can function extremely well in the climates
of developing countries, and that ice-storage is an excellent method for energy storage, when
combined with unreliable energy sources, such as PV.
What is not covered by the current literature is an analysis on how the ice-storage and cabinet
parameters can be altered, in order to increase the autonomy time of the vaccine cooler.
Furthermore, there is also a lack in literature on how much cooling capacity is required to
ensure freezing of the ice-storage, as well as how diﬀerent parameters can keep the vaccine
temperature low in case of a sudden large intrusion of hot air. These three bullets, is where
this thesis will contribute to the literature.

This section introduces and discusses the methods used in this thesis. At ﬁrst, the initial

3.1
Initial model
For this project, a model from another project was used. This model will be denoted as ”the
initial model”, and was made by Jonas Kjær Jensen at the Technical University of Denmark.
The initial model was used, due to its similarities with the vaccine cooler, in the fact that the
initial model also simulated a refrigerating unit, operating using a simple vapour compression
cycle. Of course, the model was not speciﬁcally tailored to also simulate a vaccine cooler,
so a number of alterations had to be made. The remaining part of this section will present
the model as it was, when ﬁrst adopted by this project. Following this, it will be described,
which alterations had to be made to the model, in order to make it more suited to simulate a
vaccine cooler. Furthermore, section 3.3 will present the implementation of the ice-storage in
greater detail.
As mentioned, the initial model describes a refrigerating unit. The sides and bottom of the
cabinet consists of a layer of steel, followed by a layer of PU-foam and a another layer of steel.
On the top of the unit, the outer layer of steel is strengthened by an additional layer of wood
(MDF). Within the cabinet, the evaporator is placed and the product is stored. Around the
product and evaporator is air. The evaporator is connected to the remaining components
of the vapour compression cycle (Compressor, condenser and capillary tube). However, the
remaining components are of course located outside of the cabinet walls. The refrigeration
system is depicted on ﬁgure 1. It should be noted that a sort of collector tank is seen. This
tank symbolizes an internal heat exchanger, in the way that part of the suction line is brazed
on the capillary tube. In this way, heat is transferred from the hot liquid in the capillary tube
to the vapour coming out of the evaporator. This is installed to ensure that no droplets occur
in the compressor [18].
The diﬀerent components of the model interact with each other through the use of control
volumes. Thereby, the product control volume is able to interact (exchange energy) with the
adjacent control volumes, i.e the control volume of air. Likewise, the control volume of the
evaporator is able to interact with the control volume of refrigerant within the evaporator and
the control volume of air surrounding the evaporator. The same goes for the condenser; The
control volume of refrigerant within the condenser can interact with the control volume of the
condenser, which is then able to interact with the ambient. Finally, the control volume of air
within the cabinet can interact with the control volume of the walls of the cabinet, which are
then interacting with the ambient. All of the control volumes are presented in ﬁgure 1.

3.2.1
Transient conduction
Transient conduction is an important concept within the vaccine cooler model, as well as the
initial model. Transient conduction is described in great detail by Incropera et al. [19] ch.
5. It refers to conduction, for which conditions change as a function of time. This is clearly
the case for this model, since conduction appears through every layer of insulation, and the
temperatures on either side of the insulation can change with each timestep within the model.
The method for solving transient conduction problems described by Incropera et al. [19] is a
ﬁnite diﬀerence method, which revolves around deﬁning a set of nodes within the thickness of
the wall. More nodes results in greater detail regarding the temperature gradient within the
wall, but also causes longer computational times.

insulation (PU-foam) surrounded by a layer of steel on one side, and a vacuum insulation

are what constitutes the ”Wall”. Initially, the number of nodes is determined. Since the wall
is not very thick, it is not required to have a signiﬁcant number of nodes. An amount of 10
nodes was therefore used within the layer of insulation. Additional nodes were then applied
at each boundary between diﬀerent materials, as suggested by Jensen [21]. The nodes of the

therefore act very slowly to changes in the environment. It is clear to see how this parameter
can play a signiﬁcant role in transient conduction problems.

3.3
Implementation of ice-storage
As described earlier, the ice-storage is able to interact with the frame facing towards the
vaccine chamber, the wall facing the ambient, as well as the evaporator. A diﬀerential equation
then governs which way the heat to these three other control volumes will ﬂow, as well as
the resulting change in internal energy of the ice-storage. This internal energy must for each
timestep in the simulation be translated to a temperature of the ice-storage, which is needed
to determine relevant heat ﬂows. Had there not been any phase change during the operating
conditions, this translation would have been a simple function of the heat capacity. However,
the phase change is clearly present, and is responsible for the majority of stored energy.
Therefore, further steps had to be implemented, in order to model the ice-storage properly, es-
pecially due to the reported metastiblity in the water, causing it to freeze at -6°C, rather than 0.
As discussed by Aliotta et al. [23], the metastable, sub-cooled liquid acts as a regular liquid
when being cooled. When the liquid reaches the freezing point, a rapid phase change will
occur, but only for a certain fraction of the water. Knowing the speciﬁc heat of water, the
amount of energy stored in the sub-cooling can be determined. As the rapid phase change
23
==PAGE:30==
occurs, the energy must be conserved, and the mass of ice created can thus be determined by
knowing the amount of energy stored and the heat of fusion of ice.
To do this, a procedure was added to the model in EES. A procedure is merely a function, but
with the added ability to deliver multiple outputs. The procedure takes the following inputs:
• Tice,reference: Temperature of a frozen reference state. -15°C.
• uice,reference: Speciﬁc internal energy of ice at the reference temperature.
• uice: Speciﬁc internal energy of ice in the current timestep.
• Mice: Mass of ice, as determined from the volume of the ice-storage and density of ice.
• Tfreeze: Temperature, at which the water will freeze. -6°C.
• Tmelt: Temperature, at which the water will melt. 0°C.
• IceStateNumberlast: Integer number, corresponding to the physical state of the ice-
storage in the last timestep. 1 for frozen, 3 for liquid, 2 for a mixture.
Using these parameters, the procedure will evaluate which state of phase the ice-storage is
currently in, as well as the corresponding temperature. Considering ﬁgure 14, two possible
scenarios can exist in the ice-storage. Depending on whether energy has been removed or
added in a particular timestep, the ice-storage can either be freezing or thawing. When
thawing, the ice-storage will follow the classical phase change, starting as a solid with a
constant speciﬁc heat, turning into a mixed state of water and ice. This state is characterized
by its constant heat of fusion. When all of the ice has melted, the state is pure liquid,
characterized by another constant speciﬁc heat.
However, the same trend is not seen when the storage is freezing. Due to the metastability, the
liquid water will be sub-cooled below zero, until a certain freezing point. In this metastable
state, the ice-storage maintains its liquid properties. After reaching the freezing point, a
speciﬁc amount of the sub-cooled liquid is instantly turned to ice, leaving the ice-storage as a
mixture of water and ice. The two options are shown on ﬁgure 14.
By denoting certain internal energy levels, each of the diﬀerent phases can be characterized
as intervals. As seen on ﬁgure 14, a reference energy, Ereference, is deﬁned. This parameter is
a constant, corresponding to the amount of internal energy of ice at a certain temperature
below zero. It is thus found using the reference temperature of ice and ambient pressure, and
multiplying by the mass of ice, to get the amount of energy for the entire ice-storage.
Ereference = f(Tice,reference, P) · Mice
(3.37)
Next, E1 is deﬁned as the amount of energy required to reach the melting point, but still have
a complete solid.
E1 = Ereference + Mice · cice · (Tmelt −Tice,reference)
(3.38)

==PAGE:31==
Figure 14: The ice-storages behaviour at diﬀerent conditions.
Following the same procedure, E2 is deﬁned as the amount of energy at the melting point,
but having solely liquid.
E2 = E1 + Mice · LHphasechange
(3.39)
Where LHphasechange denotes the heat of fusion. Finally, the amount of energy needed to be
sub-cooled to the freezing point, but just before the sudden phase change, is deﬁned as E3.
E3 = E2 −Mice · cwater · (Tmelt −Tfreeze)
(3.40)
All of the energy intervals are thus deﬁned. It is recalled that the procedure imported the
speciﬁc internal energy of ice, uice. This speciﬁc internal energy can be used to ﬁnd the
total amount of internal energy in the ice-storage, by multiplying with the mass of ice. The
resulting number thus deﬁnes the total amount of energy in the ice-storage for each timestep.
The procedure then evaluates this level of energy to the speciﬁed intervals, through a series of
if-statements. It is clear that if the energy within the ice-storage is lower than E1, it must be
frozen. Likewise, if the energy is higher than E2, the ice-storage must be liquid. In both cases
the exact temperature can be calculated, since the speciﬁc heats of ice and water are well
known constants.
If the energy level is between E1 and E3, the ice-storage must be in a state of mixed ice and
water. The resulting temperature will thus be zero. Thereby only one interval remains, the
one between E2 and E3. This interval can have multiple states. If the ice-storage was liquid,
and currently in the process of freezing, this interval corresponds to sub-cooled. However,
if the ice-storage is thawing, the interval will correspond to a mixture of ice and water. To
25
==PAGE:32==
determine the true state, the parameter IceStateNumberlast is used. If this number is 3, the
ice-storage was in a liquid state in the previous timestep, and must thus be sub-cooled. If the
number is diﬀerent than three, the ice-storage is then a mixture of water and ice.
Thereby, any amount of energy in the ice-storage can be translated into a corresponding
temperature and state. The EES code for the procedure can be seen in Appendix C.
3.4
Validity of lumped capacitance assumptions
This section seeks to investigate the previously mentioned assumption about lumped capaci-
tance. The assumption means that the object of investigation has one constant temperature
at each timestep, rather than having a temperature proﬁle. The assumption thus implies that
heat moves away from the object at a rate, which corresponds to the rate, at which heat is
transported within the object. E.g. if heat is able to leave the object very fast, but moves
slow within the object, the side of the object will tend to have a lower temperature than the
middle. Thereby a temperature gradient is created, and the assumption is no longer valid.
This ratio of heat rates is generally termed the ”Biot” number, and is deﬁned in the following
way, Incropera et al. [19]:
Bi = α · L
k
(3.41)
With α being the heat transfer coeﬃcient between the object and the surrounding ﬂuid, L being
the characteristic length (usually wall thickness) and k being the the thermal conductivity of
the object. Thus, a ratio between heat leaving the object, and heat moving within the object.
As a rule of thumb, Incropera et al. [19] suggest that the lumped capacitance assumption is
valid as long as the Biot number is less than or equal to 0.1, thus:
α · L
k
≤0.1
(3.42)
Using the above mentioned methodology, an investigation is conducted to check the validity
of the assumption within the model. The assumption is made for the following four control
volumes in the model:
• Product, i.e. vaccines
• Ice-storage
• Evaporator
• Condenser

a negligible layer of glass. A thermal conductivity of 0.56 Wm−1K−1 is assumed, based on
the built-in property function of the simulation tool EES. As for the characteristic length,
this can be considered to be the radius of the cylinder shape of a single vaccine. However,
since a variety of diﬀerent cylinders might be placed in the cooler, a variety of diﬀerent radii
are investigated. As for the heat transfer coeﬃcient, this is very diﬃcult to estimate, and

Calibration and validation
This section describes the process of calibrating and validating the model. These steps are
important to ensure the validity of the model simulations. Note that the calibration was
performed on the light version of the model, and the results were implemented in the full
model as well.
With the model established as described in section 3, it was needed to ensure that the model
simulated something close to reality (validation) and maybe alter some parameters in the
model, in order to make it ﬁt reality better (calibration). This was done by the use of
two independent tests on the vaccine cooler. The two tests were made in order to measure
the autonomy time of the vaccine cooler. Both tests were performed at certain conditions
(temperature, humidity etc.) and yielded speciﬁc results. By simulating the test with its
known ambient conditions, the model should thus be able to predict the ﬁnal results, while
some uncertainty is expected.
One of the tests was carried out by the WHO at Hot Zone (HZ) conditions. This test yielded
an autonomy time of 72 hours and 24 minutes, with an equilibrium temperature of around
5.8°C [24]. Note that the equilibrium temperature denotes the temperature of the air, when
the entire system is in a steady state condition caused by the phase change of the ice-storage.
The other test was carried out by the Danish Technological Institute, at Temperature Zone
(TZ) conditions. Their test showed an autonomy time of 111 hours and 16 minutes, while
keeping the equilibrium temperature around 5°C [25]. The calibration procedure was per-
formed using both tests.
The equilibrium temperature and autonomy time were used as targets, in the sense that it
was desired to have the model simulate these two parameters as accurately as possible for
both tests. In order to make such a ﬁt, some of the parameters within the model had to be
changed. The following parameters were chosen, since they are parameters of high uncertainty,
and thereby rather diﬃcult to assume.
• UAthm,brg: The UA value corresponding to thermal bridges in the vaccine cooler. Since
this is impossible to assume without a more detailed invstigation, it is an ideal variable
to use for calibration.
• αin: The heat transfer coeﬃcient on the inside of the vaccine cooler. This can to some
extent be approximated, since it is known that the heat transfer within the vaccine
cooler is governed by natural convection. However, the heat transfer coeﬃcient still has
some impact on the ﬁnal results, and is therefore very useful for calibration.
• tPU,ice: The thickness of insulation between ice-storage and vaccine chamber. This
thickness was unknown at the time of calibration, and therefore good to include in the
calibration process.
• Tice,0: The initial temperature of ice. This parameter is not deﬁned clearly in either of
the test speciﬁcations, since the test begins with the end of the last compressor cycle.

Thereby, it is not possible to know the temperature of the ice without additional data.

5.1
Timestep simulations
Prior to any real investigations, it was chosen to perform a series of timestep simulations,
in order to determine if the timestep within the model had any inﬂuence on the simulation
result. The timestep indicates the resolution of the simulation. If a simulation time of 1000
seconds is desired, and a timestep of 500 seconds is chosen, the simulation will yield a solution
at three diﬀerent times. One at zero seconds, another at 500 seconds, and a ﬁnal solution at
1000 seconds. Had a timestep of 10 seconds been chosen, a solution would be deﬁned for 101
diﬀerent times. This naturally leads to a much smoother change of the diﬀerent variables,
but also induces comprehensive calculation times. It is therefore beneﬁcial to determine a
timestep, which yields an accurate result, but still manages to have a fast calculation time.
In order to determine the inﬂuence of the timestep, the autonomy time was simulated for
the Hot Zone, as described in the following subsection. Through a series of simulations, the
autonomy time was determined, with the only changing variable being the timestep, which
was varied from 30 to 360 seconds. The results of this investigation can be seen in ﬁgure 16.

As EES performs the simulation, it does not know what will happen in the following timestep.
Thus, when EES determines a heat ﬂow, it assumes that this ﬂow will continue for the entire
timestep. However, the heat ﬂow will must likely change with each passing second, maybe
even switch direction. So when EES reaches the next timestep, the amount of heat transferred
has been wrongly estimated. EES then needs to correct its estimation by reversing the heat
ﬂow in the following timestep. And thereby the heat ﬂows tend to ﬂuctuate up and down,
until gradually fading out.

5.2
Autonomy time simulations
Autonomy time is an important concept in the vaccine cooler terminology, since it describes
the amount of time the vaccine chamber can be kept within the acceptable temperature
range, without any active cooling from the refrigeration cycle. The autonomy time is per
deﬁnition reached, once the air within the vaccine chamber surpasses 8 degrees, since that
is the maximum allowable temperature, for the vaccines to avoid spoilage, according to the
WHO [4,7].
The concept is important, because it basically describes the amount of time, in which the
cooler can function without suﬃcient sunlight on the PV panels. Naturally, because the WHO
wants a certain security related to the coolers storing their vaccines, they have a requirement
of an autonomy time of 72 hours in the Hot Zone, according to their test procedure [7].
In order to be able to optimize a vaccine cooler to achieve a longer autonomy time, it is
important to consider which parameters could have an eﬀect. In this study, the following
parameters have been taken into consideration:
Mass of ice: With the ice-storage being the primary energy storage within the vaccine cooler,
the mass (and thereby size) of the storage is bound to have a large inﬂuence on the autonomy
time. Ideally, the phase change of the ice occurs at zero degrees, which should be suﬃciently
low to keep the vaccine chamber below 8 degrees through the entire phase change. Increasing
the mass of ice would thus increase the amount of energy required to overcome this phase
change, thus prolonging the autonomy time. Since the ice-storage in the model is considered
lumped (i.e. having one constant temperature for each timestep), a linear relation between
autonomy time and mass could be expected. For example, if the mass of the ice-storage was
doubled, so would the autonomy time.
In the model, the mass of ice was changed through the variable tice, which indicates the
thickness of the ice-storage. This variable was changed, in order to ensure that the area
between the ice-storage and vaccine chamber remained constant, in order not to inﬂuence
the results. tice was varied from 2.23 to 4.03 cm, resulting in masses of the ice-storage
corresponding to 8-15 kg.
Area between ice-storage and vaccine chamber: The area is an important parameter,
since it represents the eﬀective heat transfer area between the ice-storage and the vaccine
chamber. Considering equation (5.1) it is recalled that the convective heat transfer from the
ice-storage to the air is dependent on three factors; the convective heat transfer coeﬃcient, the
heat transfer area, and the temperature diﬀerence. The importance of the area thus becomes
clear. If the area was to be reduced by 50%, so would the available heat transfer.

If the area is set too low, less heat can be transferred between the ice-storage and the
vaccine chamber. The eﬀective cooling thus becomes smaller. This could result in increasing
temperatures within the vaccine chamber, potentially enough to reach the 8 degree limit prior
to the completion of the phase change. Likewise, having a too large area would make the
cooling too eﬃcient, potentially creating too low temperatures within the vaccine chamber.
In order to simulate the inﬂuence of the area, multiple parameters must be taken into account.
The area is varied by changing the parameter Hice, which represents the height of the ice-
storage. However, altering this parameter will also cause the volume, and thereby mass of
the ice-storage to change as well. Therefore, the thickness of the ice-storage, tice must be
changed correspondingly, in order to have a constant mass of ice throughout the simulations.
Changing Hice from 0.1 to 0.35 m, while changing tice from 5.61 to 1.86 cm, resulted in a
variation of the area of 0.18 to 0.64 m2.
Insulation between ice-storage and vaccine chamber: This piece of insulation in the
frame is also important to consider, since it controls the same behaviour as the above mentioned
area. Increasing the amount of insulation would eﬀectively reduce the temperature diﬀerence
between the frame, and the air within the vaccine chamber. Considering equation (5.1) again,
it is clear that this reduction in temperature diﬀerence would result in a decrease in heat
transfer. This leads to the conclusion that too much insulation would cause a too low cooling,
resulting in diﬃculties keeping the temperature within the allowable upper limit. Similarly,
too little insulation would potentially cause the vaccine chamber to become too cold.
The thickness of the insulation is varies between 0.2 and 1.8 mm. This relatively small
bandwidth indicates the importance of this parameter.
It should be noted that this parameter is a bit diﬀerent than in the real vaccine cooler.
The real vaccine cooler has a larger insulation thickness, but of a less insulating material.
This mismatch was caused by the real properties of the frame not being available when the
modelling and calibration was made. However, the thickness was included in the calibration,
as described by section 4, with the purpose of eliminating the error otherwise caused.
Insulation between ice-storage and ambient: This piece of insulation is essentially what
prevents the ice from being exposed to the blazing ambient temperatures of many developing
countries. Therefore, it is naturally of interest to investigate. More insulation naturally causes
less heat from the ambient to reach the ice-storage, while negligible amounts of insulation
would cause the ice to melt very fast, having only the VIP to protects against the ambient
conditions. It is therefore expected, that this parameter will have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on
the autonomy time. The insulation is altered by its thickness from 0.5-5 cm.
Insulation in the bottom of the vaccine cooler: This insulation is directly placed
between the ambient conditions and the air within the vaccine cooler. It is an interesting
parameter, since a small amount of insulation would allow higher amounts of heat ingress to
the air. Having too little insulation, allowing a very large heat ingress, could potentially cause
the temperature in the vaccine chamber to surpass the 8 degree limit, if the amount of heat
ingress was larger than the available cooling from the ice-storage. To test the importance of
this parameter, the thickness of bottom insulation was varied between 0.5 and 8 cm. Note

that the top insulation, as well as the side insulation below the ice-storage are similar to the
bottom insulation. Therefore, the investigation of the bottom insulation can be seen as a
guideline for the two other layers of insulation as well, since the same trends are expected.
Vacuum insulation panels: These panels cover all of the sides as well as the bottom and
top of the vaccine cooler. They contribute heavily to the insulation of the vaccine cooler,
due to the favourable properties of vacuumed space. Since these panels are available in three
diﬀerent thicknesses from the manufacturer, it seems appropriate to examine the eﬀect of
using the smallest, medium or largest panel. Since the properties of the three sizes of panels
are of conﬁdential nature, they will not be further described in this report. The results section
will show them labelled as ”Small”, ”Medium” and ”Large”, whereas the largest panels are
the ones used in the actual vaccine cooler.
Initial temperature of the ice: This ﬁnal parameter, could be considered irrelevant, since
the autonomy time is predeﬁned to start from a speciﬁc temperature of the ice, and end when
the air reaches 8°C. However, this parameter is still interesting to investigate, considering
its real life applications. Being able to cool the ice-storage further down would of course
result in longer autonomy times, since more energy would be required before reaching the
melting point. When dimensioning a refrigeration system to accompany the vaccine cooler, it
would be ideal to consider what could be gained in autonomy time, by being able to cool the
ice-storage down an additional degree. This is shown by this series of simulations. The initial
ice temperature was varied between -10 and -0.1°C in the simulations.
5.3
Freezing time simulations
The freezing time denotes the time it takes for the vaccine cooler to cool down the ice-storage
(starting as water with a temperature of 10°C) until reaching a state where the ice-storage is
completely solidiﬁed. The freezing time is important to consider, since too long freezing times
will make it diﬃcult to get the vaccine cooler ready for use. However, a very short freezing
time could suggest an overdimensioned refrigeration cycle. The freezing time thereby also
yields an indication of what cooling capacity the refrigeration cycle should be dimensioned for.
The freezing time was simulated by making a couple of minor alterations to the model. First
of all, the heat ﬂow interacting between the ice and evaporator, ˙Qevap,ice, was changed to be a
predeﬁned variable, rather than being determined as the product of a heat transfer coeﬃcient,
area and temperature diﬀerence. Thereby, the cooling capacity could be set as a constant
value. Additionally, ˙Qevap,ice was removed from the energy balance of the evaporator. This
was done to ensure that the temperatures of the evaporator and the refrigerant within did
not increase drastically, as a constant heat ﬂux was added. Had these temperatures been
allowed to grow too large, the simulation would crash due to convergence issues with the high
temperatures. This was of course not an issue with the light version of the model, since the
evaporator had been removed.
37
==PAGE:44==
With these minor alterations in place, the cooling capacity could be altered, and the freezing
time simulated. The cooling capacity (i.e. ˙Qevap,ice) was changed from 50-1000 W, in 50 W
intervals. The simulation were performed twice, to see the diﬀerence between the Temperate
Zone and Hot Zone temperatures. Simulations at lower cooling capacities than 50 W yielded
very long freezing times, and were thus not included. However, the theoretical minimum of
cooling capacity was investigated and will be further discussed in section 6.3.
5.4
Door opening simulations
A door opening implies a situation occurring during the daily operation of the vaccine cooler.
At certain times during a day, the lid to the vaccine cooler will be opened, because a vaccine
is needed. When this happens, a portion of hot ambient air will enter the vaccine chamber,
eﬀectively heating up the vaccines. Since the vaccines must remain within the acceptable
temperature range, it is of interest to examine the eﬀect of such door openings.
The door openings were simulated by changing the initial temperature values in the model.
Assuming a complete exchange of air, the initial temperature of air was set to 43°C, while
the initial temperatures of the walls were set to 7.3°C, except for the frame, which was set
to 1°C. These temperatures were chosen, based on an investigation of the wall temperatures
during the steady state operation with the ice-storage in the process of melting. Therefore,
the ice-storage was deﬁned to have an initial temperature of -0.1°C. Finally, the product was
set to have an initial temperature of 6.5°C, also based on the before mentioned investigation.
Having the initial temperatures in place, the model was able to determine the heat ﬂows
between the diﬀerent control volumes, and thereby determine the transient temperatures.
The simulation was made for a varying number of vials (1-241), in order to see the eﬀect of
having more mass to absorb the incoming heat. Additionally, an investigation was made to
see the eﬀect of the heat exchange area between the ice-storage and the air. This was made
by selecting a constant number of vaccines, and altering the area to see the eﬀect. The area
was altered by changing the height of the ice-storage between 0.05 and 0.3 m, which resulted
in areas between 0.09 and 0.55 m2.

Finally, the initial temperature of ice is considered. The results are shown in ﬁgure 28.
The autonomy time shows a linear trend, similar to the mass of ice. The reason for the
51

linearity is found by the constant speciﬁc heat of ice. The energy stored by being able to
cool additionally one degree, must be equal to the product of mass and speciﬁc heat (as
well as temperature diﬀerence, which was assumed unity). With the mass and speciﬁc heat
both constants, the excess energy available by cooling another degree will thereby also be
a constant. Additionally, the equilibrium temperature remains unchanged, just like for the
mass of ice. The changing parameter is the initial temperature of the ice, which will have an
eﬀect on the air temperature in the beginning of the simulation. But since the ice will melt
at 0°C no matter what temperature it had in the beginning, the steady state conditions will
thus be the same. And thereby, the equilibrium temperature will also remain constant.

As seen on the ﬁgure, the eﬀect on the autonomy time is quite small, at least compared to the
mass of ice. This suggests that the cooling below zero does not oﬀer as much energy storage,

one degree below zero, with a speciﬁc heat of ice of about 2 kJkg−1K−1, as suggested by [26].
The energy stored by this small sub-cooling would be 2000 J. Considering the phase change,
the latent heat is approximately 334000 J for that same 1 kg of ice, according to the built-in
property function of EES. The sub-cooling in this case constitutes 0.6% of the stored energy.
This example clearly indicates why the initial temperature of ice has such a low inﬂuence on
the autonomy time.

As seen, the graphs takes the form of a non-linaer declining function. Thus, increasing the

simulation of 50 W, yields a freezing time between 28-32 hours. Such a long freezing time is
of course undesired, since it would make it rather diﬃcult to get the unit up and running at
initial start-up, or following a period with low solar radiation. Therefore, a cooling capacity
of around 100-150 W seems suitable, as it would lead to a freezing time of around 10 hours,

53
==PAGE:60==
and a hot climate. As seen, the two graphs are almost identical for cooling capacities higher
than 200 W. However, as the cooling capacity drops below 200, the diﬀerence in freezing time
between the two temperature levels seems to grow rapidly, ending at a diﬀerence of three
hours for the simulations of 50 W cooling capacity.
This trend could be explained by considering the heat ingress to the vaccine cooler. With a
desired temperature below eight degrees in the cooler, and an ambient temperature of above
30°, heat ingress is inevitable. As heat enters the cooler, energy from the ice-storage is used,
in order to cool the air again. Thus, the more heat ingress, the more energy will be used of
the ice-storage, and thus more energy need to be compensated by the cooling capacity.
At high cooling capacities, the heat ingress constitutes a rather small amount, compared to
the cooling capacity, and the freezing time is therefore very short. Additionally, the diﬀerence
in ambient temperature does not create enough diﬀerence in heat ingress to be noticed in
such a short time-frame. But when considering the low cooling capacities, the heat ingress
suddenly makes up for a rather large part, compared to the cooling capacity. This leads to
increased freezing times. And as the freezing time increases, the diﬀerence in heat ingress
caused by temperature diﬀerence suddenly becomes very noticeable.
Additionally, the simulation timestep also plays a minor role. Considering ﬁgure 29b, it is seen
that the simulations with a cooling capacity higher than 400 W all show a diﬀerence of zero.
The ﬁrst simulation to show a real diﬀerence between the two zones is the one with 400 W of
cooling capacity. A closer look reveals this diﬀerence to be 0.033 hours, which corresponds to
exactly 120 seconds, which was the active timestep during these simulations. This suggest
that the simulations above 400 W all contained a diﬀerence, which was below 120 seconds,
and thereby seemingly the same due to the lumpiness caused by the timestep.

7.1
Comparison to litterature
This section seeks to compare the established results with what exist in the current literature.
Pilatte [13] studied a PV refrigerator, which was designed to store vaccines, and produce ice.
He investigated the freezing capabilities of this unit, and found that 2 kg of ice could be cooled
from 32°C and frozen within 24 hours of operation. Comparing this to the results from this
thesis in section 6.3, the entire ice-storage (well more than 2 kg) was able to be cooled from
10°C and frozen within 30 hours for the lowest cooling capacity. This suggests the vaccine
cooler from Vestfrost being far superior to that of Pilatte [13]. Considering autonomy time,
Pilatte reported between 30-40 hours for a test performed at 32°C. For comparison, this thesis
would estimate a bit more than 100 hours for a similar test on the Vestfrost unit. The main
reason for these signiﬁcant changes is most likely the technological advancement made in the
more than 30 years since Pilatte [13] published his article. For example, there is no mention
of any VIPs by Pilatte [13], which was determined in this study to have a signiﬁcant impact.
In a more recent study, Walker [14] investigated the eﬀects of opening the lid to a vaccine
cooler, and ﬂushing the entire storage room with hot air. The vaccine cooler in her report was
also using an ice-bank for energy storage. Similarly to this thesis, she made her investigations
with only one vial of vaccine in the chamber, in order to deﬁne a sort of ”worst-case-scenario”.
The vaccine and wall temperature were initially at rest at 3°C. When exposed to the hot air,
the temperature of the wall increased linearly to 4°before ﬂattening out. The vaccine followed
the same trend, but non-linearly. The vaccine experienced a rapid temperature increase in
the begging, with the rate slowly decreasing and stabilizing around 4°C, some time after the
wall temperature. The temperature change of the vaccine took approximately 300 minutes.
When comparing this to the results of section 6.4.2, the same trends can be seen, with some
deviations. In this thesis, the vaccine experienced a larger temperature increase, from 6.6
to 8°C, but the temperature proﬁle is the same as that of Walker [14]. However, this thesis
estimated the temperature to occur much faster. The maximum temperature in this thesis
is reached at about 100 minutes, which is one third of the time it took in the simulation
by Walker. A lot of factors can contribute to this diﬀerence. Walker [14] does not mention
anything regarding size of the vaccine chamber nor the vaccine, but these two geometrical
factors will without doubt play a role. Furthermore, the heat transfer coeﬃcient used by
Walker is unknown. But if she has estimated a very low heat transfer coeﬃcient, it will of
course take longer for the vaccines to respond to the temperature change. Other than that,
the two investigations were in agreement, regarding the results.
In addition, several authors have investigated the use of ice-storage for domestic sized
refrigeration, among others Mammoli et al. [16] and Torres et al. [17]. They both reported
successful results in using ice-banks as storage for these small-scale applications.
This
corresponds very well with the results from this thesis, which are also very promising for that
scale of application.
Finally, the vacuum insulation panels are considered. Wakili et al. [27] investigated VIPs
63
==PAGE:70==
experimentally, in order to determine their eﬀective thermal conductivity. They concluded
that the eﬀective thermal conductivity was a function of two parts: The thermal conductivity
in the centre of the panel, and the thermal conductivity on the joints. The centre of the
panel is naturally much lower, since it only concerns the vacuumed space, whereas the joints
also consider the framing of the VIP. These two factors are thus combined into an eﬀective
thermal conductivity. From their selection of experiments, the thermal conductivities ranged
from 4.5 · 10−3 to 8 · 10−3 Wm−1K−1. Since the exact numbers for thermal conductivity of
the VIPs from this thesis are conﬁdential, they will not be presented. However, it can be
concluded that they are within or close to the scale of eﬀective thermal conductivity given by
Wakili et al. [27] and thereby in compliance with current litterature. Furthermore, another
study by Strong et al. [28] also reports eﬀective thermal conductivities within this range.
7.2
Sensitivity analysis
As mentioned earlier, some of the parameters used as inputs for the model were unknown.
These parameters were used for calibration, in order to determine an estimated value. However,
it is still necessary to determine the sensitivity of the model in regards to these parameters,
in order to determine if further focus should be put on the determination of any of these.
Therefore, a sensitivity analysis was conducted. The following parameters were included:
• The UA value for thermal bridges, UAthm,brg.
• The convective heat transfer coeﬃcient on the inside of the cooler, αin.
• The convective heat transfer coeﬃcient on the outside of the cooler, αout.
• The initial temperature of ice, Tice,0.
• The amount of insulation between the ice-storage and air, tPU,ice.
• The humidity of ambient air.
Note that only one heat transfer coeﬃcient was used for calibration, while is has been split
into and internal and external parameter for the sensitivity analysis.
Also note that the humidity of ambient air was not included in the calibration, because it was
deemed to little inﬂuence to have any eﬀect. However, this assumption is tested by including
it in the sensitivity analysis. Furthermore, the uncertainty around this parameter originates
from the oﬃcial test procedure from the WHO, which mention that the humidity in the test
chamber must be within 45-75% relative humidity [7]. Since, a more speciﬁc value was not
given, 60% was used in the model, being the average of the span.
Each parameter in the sensitivity analysis was studied by increasing and decreasing its value
by 20%. The results of the sensitivity analysis can be seen in ﬁgure 39. It should be noted
that the sensitivity analysis was only carried out for the Hot Zone conditions.
As can be seen on ﬁgure 39, one parameter clearly stands out, which is the UA value for
thermal bridges. When decreased by 20%, the autonomy time is increased by 10.6%. This is
of course due to the decreased amount of heat ingress. Similarly, when the thermal bridges
are increased, the autonomy time drops severely, since the heat ingress is now increased. Since
this parameter has such a large inﬂuence, it is very important to have a better estimate of its
true value.
64
==PAGE:71==
Figure 39: Results of sensitivity analysis.
The second largest deviations were caused by the initial temperature of the ice. Note that
”-20%” in this case denotes a higher temperature than for the base case, since the base case
temperature was multiplied with 0.8. As seen, a higher initial temperature yielded a lower
autonomy time, but only by less than one percent. Similarly, a lower initial temperature
yielded a longer autonomy time. This is similar to the trend observed in section 6.2.8, and
caused by the additional gain in energy storage of being able to cool the ice further down.
Yet, since this parameter only aﬀects the autonomy time by less than an hour, it is not as
crucial to investigate as the thermal bridges.
Another observation in the sensitivity analysis is regarding the convective heat transfer coef-
ﬁcient on the inside of the cooler. It is seen that both increasing and decreasing this value
caused a minor drop in autonomy time. This suggest that the selected value for the heat
transfer coeﬃcient actually creates a local maximum value for the autonomy time. This might
be due to the calibration, in which the value could have found it’s maximum, if increasing
autonomy times were sought. The eﬀect of altering the value is however, still very small, but
it should be considered to proper determine this value, to check the validity of the lumped
capacitance assumptions.
This local maximum is created when multiple factors are working against each other. As the
heat transfer coeﬃcient goes down, less heat is transferred internally, which means that the
overall system experiences a longer time to reach a steady state. This delay also aﬀect the air,
which will be a bit slower at absorbing heat, meaning that the air can stay below 8°C for a
bit longer, thereby increasing the autonomy time. On the other hand, the low heat transfer
coeﬃcient will also alter the equilibrium temperature within the vaccine chamber. First of
all because less cooling is available, but also because less heat ingress is allowed through the
walls. If the equilibrium temperature goes down, heat ingress will be increased again, due
65
==PAGE:72==
to the thermal bridges, which are dependent on temperature diﬀerence between the air and
the ambient. With increased heat ingress, the autonomy time will drop. With these multi-
ple factors working against each other, a local point of maximum autonomy can naturally exist.
The convective heat transfer coeﬃcient outside also shows a minor eﬀect on the autonomy
time. As the coeﬃcient is increased, the autonomy time goes down, and vice versa. This
makes good sense, since a higher heat transfer coeﬃcient allows more heat to be transferred
to the wall. However, the heat transfer is not limited by the outside convection, but more by
e.g. the vacuum panels, which is why there is only such a small deviation observed.
The parameter with the second lowest inﬂuence on the autonomy time was the ice-insulation.
This parameter only changed the autonomy time by 0.1%, which only corresponds to 4-5
minutes. As seen, an increase in insulation yielded a minor increase in autonomy time, while
a decrease in insulation caused a shorter autonomy time. This can be explained, since the
increasing insulation thickness limits the heat ﬂow from the ice-storage, which eﬀectively
increases the equilibrium temperature. This reduces heat ingress, thereby increasing autonomy
time.
Finally, the last parameter, which was the ambient humidity, showed absolutely no change in
autonomy time, at least with the given timestep of 120 seconds. The only way the humidity
can have an eﬀect is by increasing the amount of water vapour in the air in the vaccine cooler.
More water content in the air means more heat capacity to store energy. However, with the
relatively low amount of air, this change in humidity evidently did not cause a reaction to the
autonomy time.
7.3
Modelling uncertainties
Even though the model was calibrated, it was only calibrated to ﬁt the autonomy time and
equilibrium temperature. Thereby, a good deal of uncertainty is still plausible for other
parameters in the model. Furthermore, it is unknown how robust the calibrated model is,
when certain parameters are suddenly changing. This section tries to quantify areas in the
model, where assumptions have caused uncertainty.
First of all, the light model is addressed.
This model was created, in order to reduce
computational time for the simulations. However, the use of this light model also caused
a small amount of uncertainty. The components of the vapour compression cycle were not
included in the light model. This means that the ice-storage did not have the option to
exchange heat with the evaporator. And this will add some uncertainty, because the evaporator
can acts as an energy storage, just like the ice. However, only a small fraction of the energy is
stored by the evaporator. But it is still enough to cause a minor uncertainty.
The other components also generate uncertainty. For example the condenser. The condenser
is included in the model, and is able to exchange heat with the refrigerant, and with the
ambient conditions. Similarly, the wall on which the condenser is placed, is able to exchange
heat with the ambient conditions, but is in no way aﬀected by the condenser. In order for a
66
==PAGE:73==
condenser to function, it must operate at a temperature higher than the ambient, because it
needs to be able to remove heat from itself. With a temperature higher than the ambient
conditions and placed on the wall of the vaccine cooler, it is bound to have an eﬀect on the
heat ingress through that particular wall. The same goes for the compressor, which is placed
inside the vaccine cooler unit. Even though the walls to the vaccine chamber are insulated,
the heat generation from a running compressor must increase the amount of heat ingress. Of
course, the compressor is not running during the autonomy time, making this consideration
indiﬀerent for the scope of this thesis. But very important to consider when trying to model
a running refrigeration cycle.
Next, the frame is considered. Recalling, the piece of insulation between the ice-storage and
air was included in the calibration, because the exact dimensions and material composition
was unknown at the time. The idea of using it for calibration was of course to reduce the
uncertainty associated with assuming that a small wall of PU-foam would have the same
eﬀect as a larger wall of a less insulating material. However, it would still be optimal to try
to include the correct composition of this wall, in order to make the model even closer to the
real vaccine cooler.
The lumped capacitance assumption is also considered. This assumption was presented and
brieﬂy discussed in section 3.4. It was found that the assumption of lumped capacitance was
very valid for the evaporator and condenser, but on the edge of validity for the product and
ice-storage. This of course generates some uncertainty.
When considering the door-opening simulations, the product temperature was in focus, and
should therefore be predicted as correct as possible. However, the products were assumed to
be only water, and have no temperature gradient. In reality, each vaccine is covered by a layer
of glass, which would acts as insulation, preventing the hot air from immediately exchanging
heat with the vaccines. This is not accounted for in the investigation, but would thus cause
smaller deviations of the product temperature, than those presented in section 6.4. When
considering the assumption about lumped capacitance, the vaccines would in reality have
a larger temperature close to the side of the vial, and a lower temperature in the middle.
However, since the vaccines are liquid, a natural ﬂow would occur as a temperature gradient
began to form. Thus, the assumption about lumped capacitance can be considered valid,
since this natural ﬂow would help equalize the temperature throughout the vaccine.
Next, the ice-storage is also addressed. As presented in section 3.4, the maximum allowable
heat transfer coeﬃcient was strongly dependent on the thickness of the ice-storage. However,
since the ice-storage thickness was chosen to be in the range of around 3 cm, the assumption
remains on the edge of validity, and thereby yields an uncertainty. In order to reduce the
uncertainty, the ice-storage could be discretized into a number of thinner layers. Each layer
would thus have its own control volume, and be able to exchange heat with the adjacent
control volumes. This will create a temperature gradient in the ice, which is most likely also
present in the actual cooler. However, this gradient will fade, as the vaccine cooler reaches
steady state conditions during the phase change of the ice-storage. It will thereby only have
an eﬀect when the ice is in a single phase state. And since heat will be able to be transferred
relatively fast within the ice-storage layers, compared to the natural convection limitations of
the rest of the model, the eﬀect of discretizing will most likely not be very noticable.
67
==PAGE:74==
Furthermore, a true discretization would also allow for exchange of mass between the layers,
while the storage is in a liquid state. This would however be fairly complex to implement.
The ﬁnal assumption to be discussed, is the assumption of mixing within the vaccine cooler.
In order to keep the model simple, it was assumed that the air within the vaccine chamber did
not have a temperature gradient, and could be described by only one control volume. Thus,
one constant temperature in each timestep. This assumption is of course not entirely true,
since only natural convection exists within the chamber. Thereby, air heated in one second
will not be able to release this heat to the vaccines in the next second, but a certain delay
must be expected before the natural circulation in the chamber has reached all of the vaccines.
However, considering the timestep of the model, the assumption might not be completely
false. The timestep is currently set to 120 seconds, which then becomes the time, the heated
air from one timestep has to reach other parts of the vaccine chamber. This at least yields a
more realistic picture, since it simulates a certain delay. However, if one desired an even more
realistic result, the air within the vaccine chamber would have to be split into diﬀerent control
volumes, each allowing for the exchange of heat and mass with each other. Determining
the governing equations and modelling this would be very time consuming, and also cause
heavy calculation times when simulating. It can therefore not be recommended, especially,
considering the small inner dimensions of the vaccine cooler (approx. 108 litres).
7.4
Further work
Since this thesis is a part of a much larger project, a lot of interesting work still lies ahead.
This section presents the most obvious tasks for further analysis, had the author had more
time to work on this project.
First and foremost, a more detailed investigation of the thermal bridges is needed. This
detailed investigation should yield an estimate of the thermal bridges, to be used in further
modelling. The reason why this is so important, is due to its large inﬂuence, as shown both
by the base simulation analysis and by the sensitivity analysis. This investigation of thermal
bridges could be conducted in some ﬁnite element modelling program, with the ability to
simulate heat ﬂow. Additionally, current literature should be consulted, perhaps revealing
methods for determining thermal bridges. Furthermore, the heat transfer coeﬃcient inside of
the vaccine cooler should also be further investigated, in order to examine if the assumption
about lumped capacitance can be validated.
Next, it would be interesting to tackle some of the uncertainties and simpliﬁcations in the
model. For example, as mentioned earlier, the assumption regarding lumped capacitance of
the ice-storage is on the verge of validity. Therefore, it could be of interest to model a layered
ice-storage, to see if it creates a diﬀerence to the ﬁnal results.
Another simpliﬁcation is regarding the control volumes of the model. Considering for example
the entire side wall. The wall is made up of two control volumes, on in contact with the
ice-storage and ambient, and another in contact with the vaccine chamber and ambient (Recall
ﬁgure 13 for more detail). These two control volumes have diﬀerent temperature distributions,
68
==PAGE:75==
but are unable to interact with each other, despite being located on top of each other. The
same goes for the ice-storage, which is not allowed to exchange heat with the insulation located
below itself. This means that two points in the model could have a temperature diﬀerence of
maybe 5-10°C, but not be able to exchange heat. It would therefore be rather interesting to
expand the model by implementing smaller control volumes, allowing the heat to ﬂow in a
more natural way, rather than being forced to only ﬂow in one direction. Additionally, more
control volumes could also be created for the air, to see if this would change the results.
It should also be considered to improve the overall geometry of the model. The model is
of course made to ﬁt the selected vaccine cooler from Vestfrost, but a lot of assumptions
and simpliﬁcations were made in regards to geometry. For example in relation to the frame,
which should be modelled with its true properties, rather than being a calibrated assumption.
Furthermore, the relatively complex geometry of the ice-storage should also be included in
the model.
In addition, an improved calibration should be carried out. The calibration in this thesis was
based on two independent tests, with limited access to the results. A more precise calibration
could be achieved, if more tests were used, and the tests revealed other results than just the
equilibrium temperature and autonomy time. In addition, some tests could be reserved for
a validation process, in which the calibrated model was used to predict the results of other
independent tests.
Finally, a great deal of work still exist in the implementation of solar panels to the model.
Once the solar panels are implemented, a large investigation will be conducted, in order
to see how the solar panels and eﬀects of changing weather will act together with the ice-
storage. Furthermore, it will also be possible to simulate the performance of the vaccine cooler
throughout a regular day, and determine further improvements from this.

Conclusion
Based on an initial model, the basic geometry of the vaccine cooler has been implemented to
this model. The implementations and changes have been presented, along with a detailed
explanation of the ice-storage and it’s functionality in the model. Furthermore, the methods
used to perform the various simulations have been presented. With a functioning model, a
calibration procedure has been used, in order to increase the accuracy of the model. This cali-
bration as well as the results have been explained and discussed. It has been concluded, that
the model was fairly accurate within the temperature span between 32 and 43°C, with an un-
certainty of about -1.8% to 1.3% on the autonomy time. With the calibration in place, several
investigations have been conducted, using the model for numerical analysis. First of all, it was
investigated how diﬀerent parameters aﬀected the autonomy time. Second, the freezing time of
the ice-storage has been analyzed, and ﬁnally it has been simulated how the internal conditions
in the vaccine cooler changed when the cooler was suddenly ﬂooded with hot air. The results
from all three investigations have been presented and discussed. Based on this discussion, it
has been suggested how to improve the vaccine cooler, and reach autonomy times of more than
120 hours. Finally, uncertainty in the model has been addressed, and it has been concluded
from a sensitivity analysis, that more work should be put in determining the thermal bridges
of the vaccine cooler. Furthermore, it has been concluded that the model could be further
improved by adding more control volumes, especially in the ice-storage and the walls around it.
The study has thereby successfully investigated the vaccine cooler and its ice-storage from
three main perspectives: The autonomy time, the freezing time, and the door openings. Based
on these three investigations, the main ﬁndings were:
• To increase the autonomy time of a vaccine cooler, the mass of ice is a crucial parameter,
since twice as much ice means twice as long autonomy time.
• The insulation between the ice-storage and ambient is also important to consider, while
the other parts of insulation still display a fair eﬀect on the autonomy time.
• The heat exchange area and ice-insulation has a large eﬀect on the equilibrium temper-
ature. This is important to consider when altering these parameters, since too much
alteration could cause the vaccines fall beyond the acceptable temperature limits.
• In general, storing the product at a lower temperature results in higher heat ingress,
which tends to lower the autonomy time.
• The main reason for heat ingress is found in the thermal bridges, and should therefore
be investigated further.
• In order to secure a complete freezing of the ice-storage, approximately 26 W of cooling
capacity is required. This will be able to freeze the ice-storage to a temperature of -6°C.
However, if a relatively fast freezing time is desired, a cooling capacity of about 100-150
W should be considered.
• The current layout of the vaccine cooler ensures the vaccines against sudden ﬂooding of
hot air, by being able to cool the hot air down fast enough. However, the heat exchange
area between the ice-storage and air has a strong inﬂuence on this, and should therefore
not be decreased any further.

If (ice_state_number_last < 3,5) and (ice_state_number_last > 2,5) Then
ice_state$
= 'Supercooled'
ice_state_number
= 3
add_energy
= energy_diff - E_3
T_ice
= T_freezing + add_energy/(M_ice*cp_water)
Else
ice_state$
= 'Mixed'
ice_state_number
= 2
T_ice
= T_melt
Endif
Endif
 
If energy_diff > E_2 Then
ice_state$
= 'Liquid'
ice_state_number 
= 3
add_energy
= energy_diff - E_2
T_ice
= T_melt + add_energy/(M_ice*cp_water)
Endif
End
