Abstract 
Monoterpenoid indole alkaloids (MIAs) are a significant class of plant-derived natural products 
recognized for their pharmacological properties, including notable compounds like vinblastine and 
vincristine, essential for cancer treatment. Despite their therapeutic importance, MIAs are present in 
very low concentrations in plants, making their extraction costly and unsustainable. Furthermore, they 
present very complex structures with multiple stereocenters and oxygenated functional groups 
complicating total chemical synthesis. Therefore, developing efficient alternative supply routes is 
critical. 
A promising approach to overcoming production challenges involves the use of yeast cell factories. 
However, optimizing these strains to produce MIAs efficiently has proven challenging. Thus, 
developing a high throughput screening platform capable of sensing MIAs could ease the optimization 
of MIA cell factories to reach commercial performances. 
The allosteric transcription factor (aTF) RamR from Salmonella typhimurium, known for its ability to 
bind to a wide range of molecules, offers a potential solution. Through a workflow called Seamless 
Enrichment of Ligand-Inducible Sensors (SELIS), RamR was engineered in Escherichia coli to 
selectively respond to five different MIAs: strictosidine, rauwolscine, quinine, mitragynine, and 
catharanthine.  
The primary aim of this thesis is to functionalize these RamR variants as biosensors in baker’s yeast 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae for detecting the target MIAs produced by the yeast cell factories available 
in the hosting lab at DTU Biosustain. This involves tuning aTF expression, codon optimization of the 
repressor protein, as well as engineering the reporter promoter and operator sequences. Ultimately, the 
goal is to enhance MIA production yields in yeast, contributing to the sustainable and cost-effective 
manufacturing of these valuable therapeutic compounds. 
In this work, we successfully established a RamR biosensing platform in yeast and demonstrated that 
the engineered sensors variants obtained in E. coli also showed improved affinity for the target MIAs 
in yeast. We show that tuning repressor expression level can lead to enhanced sensor performance, 
improving sensitivity and dynamic range. Genome integration of RamR could further standardize 
expression and reduce population variability, enhancing biosensor reliability for high-throughput 
screening of strain and enzyme variants. Future work involves testing biosensors in production strains 
and refining the system for practical applications in metabolic engineering and MIA production.

1. Introduction 
1.1. 
Importance of PNPs in historic and modern pharmacology  
Natural products from bacteria, fungi, and plants have significantly impacted human health and 
nutrition throughout history1,2. The importance of these natural compounds is particularly evident in 
the medical field, where numerous plant natural products (PNPs) have been identified and harnessed 
for their therapeutic potential against serious ailments to target various types of cancer3, infectious 
diseases such as bacterial, viral, and fungal infections4, neuromuscular disorders including conditions 
like multiple sclerosis5 and muscular dystrophy6, and cardiovascular conditions such as hypertension7, 
heart failure8, and atherosclerosis9. 
Pharmaceutically important classes of PNPs include terpenoids, polyketides, flavonoids, alkaloids, as 
well as other aromatic amino acid derivatives10,11. This structural diversity highlights the versatility, 
but also complexity of these compounds. Many of these PNPs have been approved as active 
pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) by regulatory bodies. Specifically, over half of drugs approved by 
the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) during the last four decades corresponds to natural 
products or derivatives thereof12. 
Among these, alkaloids stand out as a class of compounds with remarkable therapeutic potential13. 
Alkaloids are a diverse group of nitrogen-containing organic compounds found predominantly in 
plants14. They often exhibit pharmacological activity and have been extensively studied for their 
medicinal properties against cancer, pain and other diseases15. 
Alkaloids are further classified into various groups based on their chemical structures and origins16. 
One prominent group is the indole alkaloids, which contains the structural moiety of indole. Among 
these, monoterpenoid indole alkaloids (MIAs) form a major group in the plant kingdom, comprising 
over 3,000 identified compounds to date16,17. The MIAs have structures derived from strictosidine 
formed from the condensation of the indole-derivative tryptamine with the iridoid monoterpene 
secologanin (Figure 1)15,16. Examples of pharmaceuticals belonging to the MIA group and 
commercially isolated from plant materials include the antitumor agents vinblastine and vincristine 
from Catharanthus roseus (Madagascar periwinkle)18, camptothecin a potent antitumor agent 
extracted from Camptotheca acuminata19, or quinine an alkaloid extracted from the bark of the 
Cinchona tree used to treat malaria since the 17th century20,21. Another significant group of plant 
alkaloids is the benzylisoquinoline alkaloids (BIA) family that include therapeutically relevant 
compounds like the narcotic analgesics codeine and morphine22,23.  
Supplying safe, efficient, and enough (APIs) based on therapeutic phytochemicals for the 
manufacturing of medicines is, however, a major challenge24. Most bioactive compounds possess 
complex structures with multiple stereocenters and oxygenated functional groups, complicating, and 
often hindering total chemical synthesis as a means of production at industrial scale25. For this reason, 
extraction from natural plant resources has been the go-to method and remains indispensable for 
sourcing many bioactive plant compounds26. For instance, vincristine and vinblastine have been listed 
by the WHO as essential medicines27 . They are currently produced by semi-synthesis, in which the 
biological precursors vindoline and catharanthine are extracted from C. roseus leaves and 
subsequently chemically coupled in vitro28.

The use of plants as raw materials involves significant economic and environmental costs. Plant 
alkaloids are typically found at very low concentrations in planta, necessitating the processing of 
large quantities of raw material 29,30. Cultivating these plants on a large scale is also challenging due to 
their slow growth rates, which require extensive land and resources for mass plantations. For example, 
producing just 1 gram of the alkaloids vinblastine and vincristine requires approximately 500 kg and 
2,000 kg of dried Catharanthus roseus leaves, respectively31. Additionally, the presence of structurally 
similar metabolites in these raw materials complicates the extraction process, making it inefficient and 
energy-intensive29. Therefore, finding alternative methods to synthesize or extract these compounds 
more efficiently is crucial. 
The need for extensive plantations results in excessive resource consumption and ecological risks, 
making PNPs production emerge as a dominant cause of biodiversity decline globally32. Moreover, 
political instabilities can disrupt supply chains leading to shortages of essential medicines32.  
1.2. 
Microbial cell factories for PNPs production 
Faced with the considerable challenges associated with the extraction of PNPs, there is a growing 
need for alternative methods to supply these medicines33. One promising solution lies in refactoring 
the biosynthesis of PNPs within genetically tractable heterologous hosts, particularly microbial cell 
==PAGE:10==
Master Thesis | Cecília Castellví Domingo 
 
August 2024 
3 
 
factories. Over the past decade, microbial cell factories have emerged as robust alternatives for 
producing plant secondary metabolites34. Once the biosynthetic pathway of a plant compound is 
elucidated, the genes encoding the enzymes can be refactored into a microbial chassis to produce 
PNPs through fermentation, offering a more reliable, sustainable, and scalable production process 
utilizing relatively inexpensive sugar feedstocks33,34. 
Industrial microbiology workhorses like E. coli and S. cerevisiae are often chosen as hosts for these 
microbial cell factories. Both organisms display fast growth rates, well-characterized metabolisms and 
genetics, and are easily handled with scalable cultivation setups35. S. cerevisiae is favoured as a 
production host for PNPs due to its complete intracellular membrane systems and superior expression 
of membrane proteins compared to E. coli33. Its eukaryotic cell architecture enables post-translational 
protein modifications, compartmentalized metabolism, and the expression of endomembrane-
anchored enzymes like cytochrome P450s which are highly represented in plant secondary metabolite 
pathways35. 
Enzymes in PNPs pathways originate from plant secondary metabolism and have had a weaker 
selection pressure given that, as secondary metabolites, they are only synthesized under specific 
conditions, for short periods of time and at relatively low fluxes36. These evolutionary conditions have 
generally rendered enzymes with low activity and high promiscuity36.  
Fundamentally, increasing the yield of a microbial cell factory involves two main points of focus: to 
(1) push the precursor pool, by enhancing the supply of precursors, and to (2) pull the flux towards 
the product by having more efficient pathway enzymes. A variety of strategies can be employed to 
increase precursor supply including the overexpression of endogenous genes, deletion of competing 
pathways, and transcription factor engineering30,37. On the other hand, pathway efficiency towards the 
product can be increased by protein engineering of bottleneck enzymes and enzyme homolog 
screening.  
Significant advances have been made in the microbial production of BIAs using S. cerevisiae. 
Christina Smolke’s group at Stanford University has engineered yeast cells with multiple genes from 
various organisms, including plants, bacteria, and humans. They successfully engineered yeast to 
produce the alkaloids reticuline, thebaine, morphine, codeine, noscapine, and berberine amongst 
others22,23,37,38. Their approach included optimizing enzyme activities, balancing precursor supply, and 
enhancing flux towards the desired products39. They utilized techniques such as transcription factor 
engineering, the use of synthetic promoters, and pathway compartmentalization to improve yield and 
stability23. They have reached commercial viability for thebaine production using a yeast opioid 
biosynthesis strain that required the expression of 21 enzyme activities and demonstrated scalability in 
>100 m3 reactors22. 
In another example, the MIAMI project (refactoring Monoterpene Indole Alkaloids in Microbial Cell 
Factories) led by Michael K. Jensen at DTU Biosustain aimed, over the past eight years, at developing 
new tools and methodologies to elucidate biosynthetic MIA pathways and optimize their production in 
yeast cell factories. They achieved a significant milestone in refactoring MIA biosynthesis in yeast by 
reporting in 2022 a yeast platform capable of reaching titers of 25 mg/L of strictosidine, which 
represents more than 1000-fold increase compared to their initial strain31. As strictosidine is the 
precursors to >3000 MIAs, this milestone opens venues for the fermentation-based manufacturing of 
numerous MIAs in yeast40. Zhang. et al., (2022) used this platform strain to demonstrate de novo 
production of catharanthine and vindoline, precursors of anticancer MIA vinblastine31. They obtained 
this by performing 56 genetic edits, such as the expression of 34 heterologous genes, knock downs of 
yeast endogenous genes, and overexpression of ten yeast genes to improve precursor supply. Finally, 
using the same strictosidine producing yeast platform, Bradley et al. (2023) demonstrated the 
production of alstonine and serpentine18. Recently Holtz et al. (2024) demonstrated de novo 
production of mitragynine and speciogynine by utilizing a synthetic biosynthetic pathway based on 
fungal tryptamine 4-monooxygenase PcPsiH circumventing an unknown enzyme in the native kratom 
plant biosynthetic pathway41.

1.3. 
Challenges in the optimization of PNP cell factories 
Despite these significant advancements, achieving profitability at an industrial scale remains 
challenging. Developing new cell factories that meet economic requirements typically takes 6 to 8 
years and costs over 50 billion USD42. 
The MIAMI project highlighted these challenges. Their primary limitation over the last eight years 
has been the strain screening step using high-resolution liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry 
(LC-MS). Relying on LC-MS is considered a low-throughput screening that is both extremely 
expensive and limited in capacity, even in the best-equipped laboratories, where it can handle only 
thousands of samples per day. While many flux-controlling enzymes have been identified in the above 
MIA pathways, the lack of cost-effective high-throughput screening methods significantly hinders the 
rapid optimization of MIA strains and enzyme functions that are needed to achieve commercially 
relevant production performance. 
One promising approach to address this issue is the development of biosensors that respond to the 
MIAs of interest. A fluorescence-based biosensor could significantly accelerate the screening process, 
enabling more efficient enzyme engineering and strain optimization. 
1.4. 
 Biosensor characterization and identification 
The development, characterization, and application of metabolite biosensors has garnered significant 
interest due to the need for methods that enable multiplex testing of single-cell factory designs. One of 
the most prominent classes of biosensors applied for metabolic engineering is the allosterically 
regulated transcription factors (aTFs), which are abundantly present in prokaryotes.  
aTFs are one-component protein-based biosensors capable of binding to a target metabolite.43 Upon 
binding, aTFs undergo a conformational change, leading to a change in their DNA binding modalities 
resulting in a modification of the expression of genes controlled by the target promoter (Figure 
2ab)44. In their native hosts, this conformational change signals the need of a cellular response in 
relation to changing environment cues such as carbon source availability or toxin presence43 .aTFs are 
composed of a DNA binding domain (DBD) and an effector binding domain (EBD), linked by a 
flexible loop region45 (Figure 2c).

repressor aTF mechanism of regulation. RNA polymerase II (green) is unable to start transcription due to the 
transcription repressor (blue) binding to the operator sequence upstream of the reporter gene. B) Once a cognate ligand 
is present in the cell, the inducible aTF undergoes a conformational change, unbinding from the operator sequence and

Engineering aTFs to respond to specific molecules can significantly increase their utility, offering 
potential applications as intracellular biosensors for the directed evolution of metabolic pathways. In 
metabolic engineering, only a small number of molecules of interest have been found to possess either 
naturally occurring, well-studied aTFs or engineered protein variants, which are necessary for the fast 
establishment of biosensing platforms. Given this limitation, d’Oelsnitz et al., (2022) designed a new 
approach for biosensor evolution, based on the premise that a protein’s promiscuity correlates with its 
malleability. In this study, they utilize biosensors with wide substrate scopes, prokaryotic multidrug 
resistance regulators to be more specific, which have large substrate-binding pockets and can

recognize a series of structurally diverse lipophilic molecules. With this initial ligand recognition, they 
mutate and adapt the biosensor to react to ligands of interest more specifically and with higher 
sensitivity. Prokaryotic multidrug resistance regulators, often identified while studying mediators of 
broad-spectrum antibiotic resistance, include a variety of proteins. D’Oelsnitz et al., (2022) focused 
on RamR, a TetR-family transcriptional repressor of the gene-encoding the RamA protein, which 
controls the expression of the multidrug efflux system genes acrAB-tolC. RamR, an important 
multidrug-resistance factor in Salmonella typhimurium, binds to a diverse range of lipophilic 
molecules; d’Olesnitz et al., (2022) engineered highly specific and selective RamR sensors for 
multiple MIAs. In this study, they developed a directed evolution workflow named Seamless 
Enrichment of Ligand-Inducible Sensors (SELIS), which operates as follows (Figure 3):  
a) Library generation: A library of RamR variants is generated by combining site-saturation 
mutagenesis of residues lining the ligand binding pocket and error-prone PCR that introduces 
diversity in distal sites from the binding pocket.  
b) Negative selection: This library is transformed into E. coli carrying a GFP reporter 
controlled by a RamR-regulated promoter. A genetic circuit links the expression of functional 
RamR variants to cell survival through expression of a zeocin resistance gene. During this step, 
mutants can be counter selected for specificity to non-target ligands.  
c) Positive selection: The mutants that survive the first negative selection step are then plated 
on media containing the ligand of interest. Clones with the best induction profile are visually 
selected based on their fluorescence signal. Promising mutants are tested in an ON/OFF 
induction assay and the most promising ones are sequenced and further tested.  
d) Selection of optimal mutants: Colonies exhibiting the highest fluorescent signal in the ON 
state but lowest fluorescence signal in the OFF state are chosen and a dose-response assay is 
performed. The best performing mutant is then used as template for the next round of evolution 
until the biosensor reaches the target selectivity and sensitivity objectives. 
Figure 3: Seamless Enrichment of Ligand-Inducible Sensor (SELIS) approach for biosensor evolution. a) Library 
generation and transformation into E. coli, b) Cells are cultured in zeocin presence overnight such that cells containing 
the sensor variants that are unable to repress are eliminated from the population. c) The resulting culture is plated on 
LB-agar containing the ligand of interest; binding of the sensor variant to the ligand derepresses GFP expression, 
therefore highly fluorescent colonies are picked and cultured overnight in liquid media in a 96-well format. Cultures 
from each picked colony are split and grown either with or without the target ligand to determine ON-OFF states. d) 
Dose-response curves are obtained for variants that display high signal to noise ratio. The top variant will be the 
starting point for the next round of evolution. Figure taken from d’Oelsnitz et al., (2022)47 
This pioneering work highlights the potential to create semi-quantitative biosensors capable of 
providing a fluorescent signal in response to the accumulation of specific natural product titers, laying 
the groundwork for the development of a similar biosensor sensitive to different PNPs. Building upon 
this work, d’Oelsnitz et al. (2024) developed a highly specific and sensitive RamR-based sensor for 
the key Amaryllidaceae alkaloid intermediate, 4’-O-methylonorbelladine48.

1.5. 
Orthogonalization of bacterial biosensors in yeast 
The development of biosensors could be instrumental for the optimization of PNP production cell 
factories. And, as mentioned earlier, the eukaryotic nature of yeast makes them preferred hosts for 
metabolic engineering for alkaloid production. However, integrating bacterial sensors into yeast poses 
significant challenges. There are substantial transcriptional differences between eukaryotes and 
prokaryotes, which renders the functionalization of prokaryotic aTFs in yeast challenging35. 
Bacterial promoters and transcription factors do not function directly in yeast without significant 
modification. In E. coli, a single type of RNA polymerase is responsible for the synthesis of all types 
of RNA. The RNA polymerase core enzyme consists of five subunits (α2, β, β', and ω) and requires a 
sigma factor to initiate transcription 49. Sigma factors recognize specific promoter sequences, which 
are typically 50 – 300 base pairs long and include the -10 and -35 regions upstream of the 
transcription start site50. These sigma factors guide the RNA polymerase to the correct initiation site. 
On the other hand, in yeast there are three distinct RNA polymerases: RNA polymerase I for 
ribosomal RNA, RNA polymerase II for messenger RNA and some small nuclear RNA, and RNA 
polymerase III for transfer RNA and other small RNAs. RNA Polymerase II, which transcribes 
mRNA, requires multiple general transcription factors to recognize and bind to the promoter regions. 
The promoter structure in yeast is more complex than in bacteria, involving core promoter elements 
(like the TATA box and initiator sequences) and various upstream activator sequences that enhance 
transcription initiation, making the typical promoter length much bigger than in E. coli commonly 
between 400 and 1000 base pairs35. Furthermore, yeast promoters are subject to chromatin 
remodeling, which influences the accessibility of DNA to the transcription machinery35.  
Transcription regulation is simpler in E. coli than in yeast, typically involving repressor and activator 
proteins that bind to operator sequences near the promoter49. This simplicity makes the refactoring of 
a prokaryotic aTF as a biosensor in yeast advantageous, given that yeast's transcriptional regulation is 
inherently more complex35.  
In synthetic biology, orthogonalization refers to the development and use of biological systems or 
components that operate independently from the host organism's native cellular machinery51. These 
orthogonal systems do not usually interfere with the host's natural processes, allowing for more 
precise and predictable manipulation of biological functions.  
Refactoring prokaryotic transcription factors as biosensors in yeast has shown success in numerous 
applications. These include optimizing cell factories for screening cDNA libraries, protein variants, 
and combinatorial pathway designs in high throughput scenarios52,53. Additionally, aTF-based 
biosensors have been employed for environmental biosensing, monitoring hazardous methylating and 
estrogenic compounds45 (more examples are detailed in Section 1.7). 
1.6. 
Biosensor design engineering 
The functionality of aTFs as biosensors is determined by their tertiary structure, oligomerization state, 
and allosteric properties. These factors influence two main characteristics of aTF functionality: ligand 
specificity and the transfer function. 
Ligand Specificity 
Biosensors are designed to detect specific substances within complex samples. Specificity refers to a 
biosensor’s ability to accurately respond to a target analyte without interferences in the presence of 
similar molecules. This is determined by the differences in binding affinity between the target analyte 
and other molecules in the sample 45.  
Binding affinity is the strength of the interaction between the biosensor’s recognition element and the 
target analyte, influenced by thermodynamics. High-affinity interactions result in strong and stable 
binding, crucial for effective biosensor performance 54.

High specificity ensures that the biosensor responds primarily to the intended ligand rather than to 
other intracellular molecules with similar structures. This is particularly important in production strain 
environments rich in structurally similar pathway intermediates. The SELIS approach enhances 
specificity by enabling counter-selection against potentially interfering ligands 47. This method helps 
identifying biosensor variants that do not respond to non-target ligands, thereby improving specificity. 
Transfer Function 
The transfer function of a biosensor describes how the input signal (e.g., analyte concentration) is 
converted into an output signal (e.g., fluorescence emission). Understanding the transfer function 
characteristics helps evaluate the performance and efficiency of a biosensor. 
Dynamic range is the ratio of maximum output (ON state) in the presence of the ligand to the output 
in its absence (OFF state) (Figure 4b). A higher dynamic range allows for a more significant 
difference in output between close ligand concentrations, enabling a more precise signal. 
Operational range refers to the range of analyte concentrations over which the biosensor provides a 
meaningful response, from the limit of detection (LOD) to saturation (ON state) (Figure 4c). A wide 
operational range allows the sensor to function effectively across various concentrations, which is 
advantageous for applications where analyte concentrations can vary widely.  
Sensitivity (see Figure 4d) refers to the biosensor’s ability to detect small changes in analyte 
concentration and translate those changes into a measurable response. Dose-response curves for aTFs 
generally exhibit a sigmoidal response curve, often described by the Hill equation55: 
𝑂𝑢𝑡𝑝𝑢𝑡=
[𝐼𝑛𝑝𝑢𝑡]𝑛
𝐸𝐶50𝑛+ [𝐼𝑛𝑝𝑢𝑡]𝑛 
Here, Output is the analyzed response (e.g., fluorescence), and Input is the analyte concentration. The 
Hill equation involves two parameters: the Hill coefficient (n), which describes cooperativity of the 
response56, and the EC50, the analyte concentration at which the biosensor produces half of its 
maximum response 55. The Hill equation quantifies cooperativity, observed in allosteric regulation of 
ligands binding to their respective macromolecules55. Sensitivity is often quantified by the Hill 
coefficient, which can be determined by the slope of the transfer function within the operational 
range, particularly around the EC50, which also correlates with the inflection point of the dose 
response curve, indicating how quickly the response changes with concentration55.

Given the promising sensitivity, specificity and dynamic range of RamR-based biosensors in E. coli, 
the next step in adapting these biosensors for use in yeast involves designing a functional RamR-
regulated transcriptional unit with an optimized transfer function. Several factors must be considered 
==PAGE:15==

August 2024

in this process, including tuning aTF expression, codon optimization of the repressor protein, and 
engineering the reporter promoter and operator sequences.  
aTF Expression level 
The promoter driving the expression of the aTF significantly impacts the biosensor’s OFF state and 
dynamic range. A stronger promoter will promote a lower OFF state given that more aTF proteins can 
potentially bind to the operator inhibiting gene reporter expression. Additionally, a stronger promoter 
will drive the operational range towards higher concentrations. Conversely, a weaker promoter will 
drive the operational range towards lower effector concentrations by easing de-repression while also 
increasing background signal. 
Engineering the reporter promoter 
Engineering the promoter that controls the biosensor’s output is also essential. A common strategy 
involves inserting aTF operator sites into a native well-characterized yeast promoter template. A good 
reporter promoter should enable both a high level of repression in the absence of the ligand (OFF 
state), and a high dynamic range of the aTF-based biosensor in the ligand presence, i.e., upon ligand-
induced de-repression (ON state). 
The operator sequence, recognized by the DBD domain of the aTF (Figure 2c), is crucial for aTF 
binding to DNA and repressing the biosensor’s output signal, given that it impacts both the dynamic 
range and the operational range. Adding operator sequences to a native or truncated promoter intended 
to control the biosensor output generally reduces basal expression57,58. The position of the operator is 
important, and predicting its effect on the reporter gene can be challenging. Ambri et al., (2020) 
identified an 18-bp region in a yeast native strong promoter pTEF1 that supported both low OFF state 
expression and up to 8.6-fold ligand induced de-repression for a TetR-family repressor-type aTF59.  
The operator sequence itself also affects the operational range. For example, Wang et al., (2016) 
showed that swapping the operator site of XylR from Staphylococcus xylosus with the operator 
sequence from the homologous Bacillus subtilis increased the operational range 14-fold60. Multiple 
copies of the operator sequence can also increase de-repression, as shown by Hector and Martens 
(2017), where two copies of the operator sequence increased de-repression by eightfold compared to a 
single copy while maintaining the same basal expression level61. 
Genomic integration or plasmid-based expression 
When expressing heterologous proteins in S. cerevisiae, yeast expression plasmids are commonly 
used due to their ability to shuttle between E. coli and yeast cells. These plasmids must contain a 
yeast-specific origin of replication (ORI) and a means of selection in yeast cells, in addition to the 
bacterial ORI and antibiotic selection markers. There are different types of yeast ORI, of which 2µ is 
a common one ; these ORIs are similar to those present in bacterial plasmids and are considered “high 
copy”33. It is estimated that the 2µ ORI allows for 50+ copies to stably propagate per yeast cell35. 
However, plasmids can be unstable and vary in copy number depending on the metabolic state of the 
cell, leading to heterogenous expression and therefore unreliable data. Integrating one or multiple 
copies into the yeast genome offers several advantages: (1) gene copy number control, allows precise 
control over the number of biosensor and reporter gene copies, (2) ensures consistent expression 
across the yeast population and (3) lower metabolic burden in the host cells as they do not need to 
maintain high-copy plasmids. To obtain a higher protein dosage when integrating genes into the yeast 
genome, a common strategy is to integrate multiple copies of the same gene at different permissible 
locations35.  
1.7. 
Improvement of yeast cell factories with transcription-based 
biosensors 
Transcription-based biosensors have transformed the development of yeast cell factories. These 
biosensors enable high-throughput screening and identification of optimal genetic modifications by 
==PAGE:16==
Master Thesis | Cecília Castellví Domingo 
 
August 2024 
9 
 
linking metabolite levels to easily detectable signals. By employing these sensors, genetic changes 
that enhance production of valuable compounds can be easily pinpointed, leading to more efficient 
and productive industrial yeast strains. The following examples illustrate the successful application of 
transcription-based biosensors in improving various aspects of yeast metabolism. 
One such example comes from Dabirian et al., (2019), who utilized the FadR protein from E. coli to 
screen an overexpression library of genes for enhancing fatty acyl-CoA pools in yeast. Through 
fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) screening, they identified three gene targets that increased 
fatty alcohol production by 80 %53. Similarly, Li et al., (2020) employed the FapR protein from 
Bacillus subtilis to regulate the transcription of GFP in response to Malonyl-CoA. This enabled the 
screening of a genome-wide overexpression cDNA library and identification of two gene targets that 
increased production of 3-Hydroxypropionic Acid by 120 %25. 
Wang et al., (2016) developed a xylose-dependent biosensor based on S. xylosus XylR. Using directed 
evolution, they improved the xylose transportation capacity of the hexose transporter HXT14 in S. 
cerevisiae. A mutant library of the HXT14 gene was created via epPCR and introduced into a strain 
containing the xylose biosensor. After adding xylose, cells with higher fluorescence were selected by 
FACS, leading to the identification of a xylose transporter with a 6.5-fold higher transportation 
capacity 60. 
Another example was provided by Wang et al., (2020) who employed a cis,cis-muconic acid 
biosensor based on the transcriptional activator BenM from Acinetobacter sp. ADP1 and coupled to 
GFP expression. This biosensor had previously been evolved to tune its transfer function in 
production strains62. The authors used BenM variants to screen UV mutagenesis libraries of CCM 
producing yeast. Using FACS, they identified a clone with 49 % higher CCM titer, reaching a titer of 
21 g/L CCM in controlled fed-batch fermentation, representing the highest reported production of an 
aromatic compound in yeast at that point52. 
However, it is important to note that while aTF-based biosensors with fluorescent outputs are valuable 
for metabolic engineering, they require expensive instruments and often over-represent low-producing 
designs due to metabolic burden45. Alternatively, biosensors can be linked to antibiotic resistance 
genes or auxotrophy reversion to select for strains with higher productivity. For instance, Snoek et al., 
(2018) developed a system coupling the cis,cis-muconic acid-binding BenM to the expression of the 
antibiotic resistance KanMX gene, enriching high-producing variants from a random multicopy 
integration library63. Likewise, Feng et al., (2015) created a biosensor detecting progesterone, coupled 
to the HIS3 gene expression, and used it to identify 3β-HSD mutants variants producing 2-fold higher 
progesterone by selecting clones that survived in the presence of a His3-inhibitor64. 
Finally, biosensor systems can exert real-time control over biosynthetic pathways. By linking the 
sensor to a pathway-related enzyme, the enzyme's expression can be tied to the availability of the 
target metabolite. For example, David et al., (2016) used FapR, a Malonyl-CoA biosensor, coupled to 
the expression of Malonyl-CoA reductase (MCRCa) from Chloroflexus aurantiacus. This linkage 
mitigated metabolic imbalances and increased 3-HP production nearly tenfold65.

1.8. 
Aims of the thesis 
The primary aim of this Master Thesis is to functionalize the bacterial aTF RamR as a biosensor in S. 
cerevisiae. Through collaboration with Maxence Holtz and Simon d’Oelsnitz, different RamR variants 
are obtained through the SELIS workflow. Variants showing the highest sensitivity and selectivity to a 
specific MIA will be chosen. The MIAs of interest are Quinine (QUI), Mitragynine (MIT), 
Catharanthine (CAT), Rauwolscine (RAU), and Strictosidine (STR). 
The main goal is to establish a functional RamR biosensing platform in yeast and test the protein 
mutants generated in bacteria using this platform. This involves the comprehensive testing of various 
parameters including biosensor codon optimization, different reporter promoters, operator sequences, 
genome integration sites, and assay conditions. The ultimate goal is to facilitate the screening of 
pathway and enzyme variants in MIA yeast cell factories, thereby enhancing production yields and 
contributing to advancements in the sustainable and cost-effective supply of these therapeutics.

3. Results and Discussion 
This thesis aims to functionalize a bacterial aTF, RamR from Salmonella typhirium as a biosensor in 
S. cerevisiae. The biosensor system comprises two primary components: (1) the expression of RamR 
from a yeast 2µ expression plasmid, and (2) a genome integrated reporter construct featuring a 
synthetic yeast constitutive promoter with a RamR operator sequence (RamO) positioned downstream 
of the TATA box, referred to as the reporter construct hereafter. The mechanism involves RamR 
binding to the RamO operator sequence, thereby inhibiting GFP expression (Figure 7a). In the 
presence of the ligand of interest, RamR undergoes a conformational change leading to DNA 
unbinding and de-repression of GFP expression (Figure 7b).  
RamR was engineered to respond to five different MIAs: Catharanthine (CAT), Mitragynine (MIT), 
Quinine (QUI), Rauwolscine (RAU) and Strictosidine (STR) using directed evolution with the SELIS 
workflow as described above 47. This directed evolution work in Escherichia coli is not included in 
this thesis and was partly completed during a special course at DTU prior to the start of the thesis 
(Supplementary Figure 1).  
 
Figure 7: Scheme of the biosensor circuit used for screening MIA presence in yeast. RamR is expressed in a 2µ 
expression plasmid and the GFP reporter is integrated in the yeast genome downstream of a constitutive yeast promoter 
(black) with the RamO operator sequence (cyan). a) Without the MIA of interest, RamR binds to RamO inhibiting GFP 
expression. b) The MIA of interest binds to RamR, which undergoes a conformational change inhibiting DNA binding 
and therefore allowing GFP expression. 
Likewise, prior to this thesis, preliminary experiments were conducted to express RamR in S. 
cerevisiae as shown in Figure 7. In other words, synthetic promoters incorporating the RamO 
sequence downstream of the TATA box were constructed using pTDH3, a strong constitutive yeast 
promoter placed upstream of GFP. These initial tests validated the functionality of RamR in yeast, 
demonstrating its capacity to repress GFP expression via the synthetic promoter carrying the RamO 
sequence, yielding a 9.2-fold change of fluorescence intensity with and without expression of the 
RamR repressor (Supplementary Figure 2). 
The difference in fluorescence signal between strains containing RamR and those without RamR 
conditions the dynamic range of the biosensor. Maximizing this fold change is fundamental for the 
biosensor’s effective application, as it allows for the identification of real hits responding to higher 
MIA yields when performing metabolic engineering in MIA production strains. Increasing the 
dynamic range of RamR is critical as the intracellular concentration is very low and for a small input a 
detectable output would be necessary. To achieve this, several aspects of the biosensor system were 
optimized. As summarized in Figure 8, the thesis is divided into five distinct work packages (WP).

3.2. WP2- Optimization of promoter and operator sequences 
The next WP involves the optimization of the reporter construct in which a total of ten S. cerevisiae 
strains were generated, each having different integrated reporter constructs. Five strains harbor the 
pCCW12 promoter as template, while the remaining five are based on the pTDH3 promoter, both of 
which are strong constitutive yeast promoters. The pTDH3 promoter was selected due to its 
demonstrated efficacy as a template promoter for integrating operator sequences from TetR (same 
family of aTFs as RamR), showing a good dynamic range in such systems (unpublished data). 
Similarly, pCCW12 has been shown by Nasr et al., (2022) to function effectively as a template 
promoter for introducing operator for RolR, another TetR-family repressor in yeast 71,72. 
Four different operator sequences were tested (Figure 10): (1) the WT sequence found in Salmonella 
enterica (1xRamO), (2) the WT operator sequence repeated twice (2xRamO), and (3, 4) two mutated 
sequences designed for increased symmetry and improved binding to RamR, that had reduced 
background in E. coli (mutRamO_1 and mutRamO_2, respectively) (data not shown). Additionally, 
two control strains were generated for each promoter without the RamO sequence.

3.3. WP3- Generation of RamR reporter constructs targeting 
different genome integration sites 
To achieve a homogeneous sensor response in the cell population, genome integration of the reporter 
is necessary. The EasyClone Marker-Free toolkit and extension66,73 have been employed extensively 
for yeast genome engineering. This toolkit is based on vectors developed by Mikkelsen et al. (2012), 
which allow stable integration into 15 distinct genomic sites, each validated for minimal growth 
impairment and stable heterologous gene expression. Furthermore, the insertion sites on each 
chromosome are flanked by essential genetic elements to prevent the loss of inserted fragments 
through homologous recombination. Marker-free genome editing is performed using CRISPR-Cas9, 
with Cas9 counterselection facilitating this process. However, due to the limited number of integration 
sites, and the majority being occupied by MIA pathway genes in the producer strains (data not 
shown), it is necessary to assemble RamR reporter constructs targeting alternative genome integration 
sites. 
In WP1 and WP2, strains used the integration site XI-3. The newly cloned reporter constructs will 
target integration sites II-1, IV-1, VII-1, VIII-1, IX-1, XIII-1, XV-1, and XVI-1. These sites are from 
the EasyClone expansion that was published recently and are not all occupied in the MIA cell

3.4. WP4- Evaluation of RamR mutants 
The following WP consists in the evaluation of various RamR mutants engineered in E. coli sensitive 
to specific MIA ligands of interest. This process involves the construction of yeast strains 
incorporating RamR mutants, optimizing conditions for ligand uptake, and conducting dose-response 
assays to determine each biosensor's performance. 
Strain Construction 
Yeast strains were developed to integrate RamR mutants, as detailed in Section 3.1. Codon 
optimization was assessed to determine its impact on gene expression in yeast, revealing that non-
codon optimized sequences performed as well as or better than the optimized sequences. 
Consequently, 2µ yeast expression plasmids were constructed using non-codon optimized RamR 
sequences with specific mutations required for sensing the target MIA ligands (see Section 2.3 and 
Supplementary Figure 1). 
Selecting an appropriate reporter construct was critical for maximizing the biosensor's dynamic range. 
As discussed in Section 3.2, the pCCW12 promoter combined with a single RamO operator 
(1xRamO) exhibited the highest fold change in signal between RamR-repressed and non-repressed 
states. 
The reporter construct was then integrated into the yeast genome at site XI-3, selected for its high 
gene expression and integration efficiency. After successful integration, verified by colony PCR, 
various 2µ yeast expression plasmids containing RamR variants were transformed into the strain 
==PAGE:28==
Master Thesis | Cecília Castellví Domingo 
 
August 2024 
21 
 
containing the reporter construct, generating distinct strains for further evaluation (See 
Supplementary Table 3). 
pH Optimization for Ligand Uptake 
The functionality of the biosensor depends on the ligand's ability to enter the cell and bind to RamR, 
thereby inhibiting GFP expression. MIA production strains secrete MIAs when produced in vivo, 
suggesting that these compounds can cross the membrane and cell wall. However, ligand transport can 
be significantly affected by pH, with each ligand having an optimal pH for maximal intracellular 
transport63. 
A range of pH levels was tested for each ligand to identify the optimal conditions for sensor 
performance, ensuring the highest GFP signal (Supplementary Figure 6). For most ligands, pH 7 
was found to be optimal. This is likely due to the ligands' isoelectric point close to 8, where pH 7 
provides a balance allowing diffusion into the cell and cell growth. As incubation proceeds, yeast 
acidifies the media, "locking in" the ligand inside the cell, as suggested by Snoek et al., (2018). 
Ligand autofluorescence was also evaluated by inoculating a WT S. cerevisiae strain (MIA-B0) not 
containing the reporter construct with different ligands, confirming that observed fluorescence was 
due to GFP expression (Supplementary Figure 7). 
Dose-Response Assays 
After identifying the optimal pH level, dose-response curves were conducted to determine the transfer 
function of each biosensor. Cells were inoculated from an overnight culture into media with the 
specified pH, grown for two hours, and then induced with MIAs dissolved in a 10 % DMSO solution 
at various ligand concentrations. After an additional five-hour growth period, cells were analyzed 
using flow cytometry. Single yeast cells were gated (Section 2.6), and median fluorescence intensity 
values (MFI) were obtained for each condition in triplicates. 
Quinine Response 
The dose-response curves obtained against QUI can be seen in Figure 13. WT RamR (blue) exhibited 
only a small response at high concentrations of quinine, contrasting with the observed response in E. 
coli (Supplementary Figure 1b). This discrepancy suggests that a lower amount of QUI is entering 
the yeast cell compared to E. coli. Given QUI’s pI of ca. 8.5, a media pH of 7 might be too distant 
from the isoelectric point, hindering its influx. Furthermore, cells are first inoculated and grown 
without the ligand for two hours before induction, further acidifying the media away from QUI’s pI. 
To investigate this, two different tests could be conducted. First, testing more alkaline pH levels, such 
as 8 and 8.5, might align more closely with quinine’s pI. Although this may impact cell growth during 
incubation, to contrast this a longer incubation period could be tested. The second test would involve 
inducing cells with quinine during inoculation, allowing ligand entry before media acidification. This 
might result in a higher signal for all RamR variants and a more accurate, reproducible response. 
Despite the differences between yeast and E. coli, the QUI1 variant obtained after one round of SELIS 
shows improved transfer function. However, the dynamic range and the sensitivity are significantly 
reduced in yeast (orange) compared to E. coli, which presented a much higher dynamic range and 
sensitivity (Supplementary Figure 1b).  
As the QUI1 variant showed reduced background signal in E. coli and in yeast compared to the WT 
repressor, indicating stronger DNA binding capacities, we hypothesized that the protein stoichiometry 
might be too high in yeast making it difficult to derepress the sensor.

Mitragynine response 
Dose-response curves for WT RamR and two mutants thereof with varying MIT concentrations are

cell environments enhance mitragynine-RamR binding. 
Examining the transfer functions for RamR variants MIT1 and MIT2 reveals that MIT1 is more 
sensitive than MIT2. This result is unexpected given that MIT2 has had one extra round of evolution 
and has an enhanced sensitivity and dynamic range in E. coli (Supplementary Figure 1d). This could 
be potentially due to strain misidentification during the experiment, warranting a repeat assay to 
confirm. If results persist, other explanations may involve MIT2 SELIS selected mutations, which are 
found in the RamR sequence's last residues, potentially complicating folding in yeast. This could 
hinder RamR’s conformational changes, requiring more mitragynine for equivalent MIT1 output. This 
hypothesis implies that RamR folding issues are yeast-specific, given MIT2's improved sensitivity 
transfer function in E. coli. Similarly, the screening of variants in E. coli is done at 37 °C but in yeast 
the temperature is 30 °C, which could impact protein stability and function.

To summarize, the results from WP4 demonstrate the successful refactoring of RamR mutants to 
detect specific MIAs in yeast and validate the strategy of evolving proteins in E. coli for use in yeast. 
Our exploration into promoter strengths revealed that weaker promoters enhance sensor performance 
by optimizing RamR stoichiometry. Reduced expression of RamR lowers the amount of ligand 
needed to trigger a detectable response, thereby improving the biosensor's sensitivity and dynamic 
range. Although genome integration typically results in lowered expression, this approach could 
provide sufficient expression levels for more consistent and reliable sensor performance compared to 
plasmid-based systems. 
Genome integration offers a significant advantage in standardizing expression across cells, reducing 
variability associated with plasmid copy number heterogeneity and potential plasmid loss69. This 
uniformity results in a more stable and predictable biosensor response, crucial for high-throughput 
screening and industrial applications where consistency is essential.

3.5. WP5- Sensing in production strains 
The final goal of this project is to develop biosensors capable of sensing MIA production in yeast 
production strains and perform HTS for metabolic engineering. To achieve this, various production 
strains have been transformed with different biosensors (Supplementary Table 3). 
Sensing Strictosidine in Production Strains 
Strictosidine (STR), a precursor to all MIAs, is critical for enhancing the titers of other MIA 
production strains. Therefore, developing a STR-sensitive biosensor is essential. Such sensor was 
initially developed in E. coli using the SELIS workflow, leading to the selection of two RamR 
variants, B2 and C10, after one round of evolution. The transfer functions obtained in E. coli indicates 
that C10 has a greater sensitivity than B2 (Supplementary Figure 1f). However, B2 displayed greater 
selectivity, as indicated by a lower fold change in fluorescence/OD when induced with 1 mM 
tryptamine compared to C10. Additionally, the WT RamR showed a much higher response to 1 mM 
tryptamine, with a 2-fold increase compared to B2 and a 1.6-fold increase compared to C10 
(Supplementary Figure 8). 
Due to the high cost of STR and non-availability commercially, the transfer function of these RamR 
variants was not studied in yeast. Instead, the RamR variants were directly transformed into 
strictosidine production strains. Two different production strains were used: MIA-CZ1 (Holtz et al., 
2024)41 and MIA-CM3 (Zhang et al., 2022)31. MIA-CZ1 produced 25.5 mg/L (65.62 µM) of 
strictosidine, while MIA-CM3 produced 15.0 mg/L (38.6 µM). However, both strains are known to 
produce various byproducts, such as loganin, which was present at 11.4 mg/L (21.5 µM) in MIA-
CM331. 
A total of nine strains were created: each production strain, along with a WT control (MIA-B0), had 
the reporter construct integrated at site VIII-1 of the genome. Following colony PCR verification of 
proper integration, three strains were generated from each by transforming a RamR 2µ yeast 
expression plasmid containing either the WT RamR gene, the STR1 B2 or C10 variant. 
To assess STR sensor performance, strains were fed varying amounts of the immediate precursor 
secologanin. As secologanin is one enzymatic step away from strictosidine, the amount fed should 
correlate with the strictosidine produced in the cell. Figure 17 shows that WT RamR exhibits a higher 
response than either of the two STR1 variants. This could be due to STR1's higher affinity for the 
RamO operator sequence, resulting in a lower background signal compared to WT RamR and 
requiring a higher amount of strictosidine in the cell to de-repress GFP expression. 
Furthermore, all RamR variants showed an increased response with higher feeding concentrations, 
aligning with expectations of STR production. However, various side products could contribute to an 
unspecific RamR response, particularly with WT RamR, which is known for its promiscuity and 
responsiveness to multiple ligands present in the pathway. Notably, both STR1 variants exhibited a 
smaller difference between feeding concentrations, suggesting a more specific response to the low 
amount of strictosidine produced compared to potential side products, especially tryptamine, which 
has shown a significant response for WT RamR. 
As a negative control, MIA-B0 was transformed with the reporter construct at the same integration 
site as the production strain (left panel in Figure 17). The GFP signal in the WT strain was much 
lower than in the production strains. However, this does not constitute a strong negative control as the 
pCCW12 template reporter promoter used is not orthogonal and will respond to cellular conditions. 
The production strains express a high number of heterologous genes, putting the cell in a stressed 
state. Therefore, comparing biosensor responses between producer and non-producer strains is 
inaccurate. 
A more refined control would involve knocking out a pathway gene upstream of strictosidine while 
maintaining the expression of all other heterologous genes, followed by feeding both strains to 
compare them. This approach would yield more similar cellular conditions, allowing for more 
accurate conclusions. Secologanin synthase (SLS) (strictosidine biosynthetic pathway in Figure 1), an

oxidoreductase that catalyzes the reaction from loganin to secologanin, could be a suitable knockout 
candidate, impairing strictosidine production and, consequently, other MIAs. This knockout was 
performed in production strains and validated but there was not enough time to test these strains 
during this thesis. 
Determining the sensitivity, dynamic range, and operational range of STR through a dose-response 
curve would also be necessary for further advancement. As observed in WP4 (Section 3.4), this sensor 
could benefit from a lower RamR expression via a weaker promoter. Additionally, another round of 
evolution using the SELIS workflow would be beneficial, given the low sensitivity shown in E. coli 
compared to other ligands (Supplementary Figure 1), suggesting potential for increased sensitivity 
with further evolution.

Towards sensing catharanthine in production strains 
Vinblastine is a critical anti-cancer therapeutic, and recent advancements have highlighted the 
potential of microbial biosynthesis as a viable production method. Zhang et al., (2022) demonstrated 
the de novo microbial biosynthesis of vindoline and CAT using a highly engineered yeast strain, 
followed by in vitro chemical coupling to produce vinblastine. Following this breakthrough, further 
engineering has been applied to CAT production strains. Notably, MIA-IJ-A2 is currently the most 
efficient catharanthine production strain, yielding 1 mg/L when fed 50 µM geissoschizine (a precursor 
to catharanthine). 
Developing a biosensor capable of detecting CAT could significantly advance the optimization of this 
production strain. The RamR sensor, CAT2, exhibits a promising transfer function, indicating its 
potential application in production settings. However, it is crucial to assess the specificity of CAT2, 
especially concerning geissoschizine while feeding various concentrations of this precursor in 
production assays. If CAT2 proves to be sufficiently specific by not responding to 250 µM 
geissoschizine, the subsequent step would involve an assay to test different geissoschizine feeding 
concentrations. This would determine whether the RamR response is proportional to the precursor

feeding levels. Additionally, supernatants from 96-hour fermentations would be analyzed using LC-
MS to compare the biosensor’s response with actual catharanthine levels. 
To advance this research, the catharanthine-producing strain MIA-IJ-A2 was engineered with a 
reporter construct integrated at the VIII-1 locus, and cPCR verification confirmed proper integration. 
Following this, the CAT1 and CAT2 sensors were successfully introduced into the strain. 
Due to time constraints, I could not perform the full assay. However, the groundwork has been laid, as 
strains harboring the reporter construct with CAT1 and CAT2 have been generated. Additionally, the 
production strain with an SLS knockout and the reporter construct integrated is also ready for future 
experiments. 
Towards sensing mitragynine in production strains 
Holtz et al. (2024) successfully achieved the de novo production of kratom MIAs, mitragynine, and 
speciogynine in yeast by utilizing a synthetic pathway based on the fungal tryptamine 4-
monooxygenase PcPsiH. Their research, however, identified several side products resulting from the 
promiscuous activities of strictosidine synthase (STR) and dihydrocorynantheine synthase (DCS). 
These enzymes represent prime candidates for enzyme engineering to enhance specificity. The 
development of a highly sensitive mitragynine sensor would significantly facilitate this process, as it 
would enable the selection of promising enzyme candidates via FACS. 
The MIT1 sensor exhibited high specificity in the dose-response curve, indicating its suitability for 
application in mitragynine production strains. Preliminary tests with varying feeding concentrations 
should be conducted in production strains harboring the sensor to determine if the current biosensor's 
sensitivity is adequate. If not, tuning RamR expression could enhance sensitivity, and if further 
improvements are needed, another round of evolution may be necessary. 
To this end, mitragynine production strains yMHO40 and yMHO42 had the reporter construct 
integrated at site IV-1 and validated. From these, three strains were generated by transforming a 2µ 
plasmid containing the WT RamR, MIT1, and MIT2 genes. These strains would be tested comparing 
them to production strains with the SLS gene knocked out. Due to time constraints, testing of 
mitragynine sensors in the mitragynine production strains could not be performed. 
Towards sensing rauwolscine in production strains 
The transfer function for the RAU2 sensor demonstrated promising results, as previously described 
(see Section 3.4.). Similar to the approach taken with mitragynine, a preliminary test should be 
conducted to determine whether further engineering of the sensor is necessary. If the initial test yields 
no signal, enhancing the sensor's performance for rauwolscine production strains will involve 
swapping the promoter for a weaker one to increase sensitivity. 
A strain derived from ScF157 was developed with two copies of the rauwolscine synthase gene 
integrated into the genome. The reporter construct was integrated at site VIII-1, and the strain was 
transformed with RAU2. 
As with other MIAs, the negative control will consist of the production strain with the SLS gene 
knocked out.

4. Perspectives 
The next steps could involve conducting production strain tests using the SLS knockout control. This 
will determine whether the current intracellular level of MIA production in each strain is sufficient to 
be detected by the biosensors. Additionally, analyzing the supernatant content through LC-MS is 
crucial to compare the biosensor's response with the actual MIA production. This analysis will help 
determine if the biosensor's response is due to non-specific detection. If the biosensor fails to detect 
current MIA production levels or shows non-specificity, further evolution may be required to enhance 
the sensitivity or selectivity of the biosensors, depending on each sensor’s performance.  
Another aspect that could be taken into consideration is genome integration of the RamR gene, with 
different promoters tested for each production strain to optimize sensor transfer function in regards 
with production levels of metabolites of interest. 
Coupling the RamR response to fluorescence involves a costly process that requires the use of FACS. 
To reduce costs and simplify the detection of the most promising candidates, the GFP gene could be 
switched with an antibiotic resistance gene. This approach would allow a growth-coupling based 
evolution of yeast cells. For example, directed evolution of a specific enzymatic step could be 
achieved by growing strains on media with increasing concentrations of antibiotic; those expressing 
higher levels of MIAs would express a higher level of antibiotic resistance gene, allowing cell growth. 
After a certain amount of serial batch cultivations, it could be possible to isolate strains producing 
higher MIA titers. 
One of the approaches could consist in using the STR sensor to perform enzyme engineering to 
improve yield. A few enzymes in the corresponding biosynthetic pathway have been identified to have 
low activity and/or are very promiscuous, resulting in irreversible reduction of pathway intermediates 
which leads to a non-recoverable loss of carbon, significantly impacting the metabolic flux75. By 
focusing on enzyme engineering, particularly targeting enzymes with low activity or high promiscuity, 
carbon loss can be reduced, thereby increasing STR production. Billingsley et al. (2017) identified 
iridoid synthase (ISY) as a key determinant of biocatalytic selectivity that controls flux towards the 
iridoid scaffold strictosidine75. Additionally, Zhang et al. (2022) identified the cytochrome P450 
secologanin synthase (SLS) as a flux-controlling enzyme with low activity and stability31, presenting 
another opportunity for optimization. Different mutants could be obtained by different approaches and 
the best performing ones yielding a higher amount of strictosidine, will express a higher fluorescence 
that would be selected with FACS or growth-coupling. 
This RamR biosensing platform presents a versatile tool for detecting various metabolites in yeast and 
can be extended to different PNPs. The RamR variants developed by D’Oelsnitz et al. (2022) 
demonstrated responsiveness to different BIAs. These variants could be utilized in yeast to further 
advance yeast cell factories for BIA production. Additionally, D’Oelsnitz et al. (2024) developed 
another RamR-based biosensor that has proven effective in E. coli for Amaryllidaceae alkaloids, 
indicating the potential for cross-species application and further optimization in yeast systems. Being 
able to sense Amaryllidaceae alkaloids in yeast would be instrumental in developing cell factories 
capable of producing these alkaloids, such as galantamine, a compound used to treat Alzheimer's 
disease.

5. Conclusions 
 
• 
A RamR-based biosensing platform was successfully established in Saccharomyces cerevisiae. 
 
• 
Non-codon optimized RamR sequences maintained their allosteric characteristics and repression 
capabilities in yeast. 
 
• 
The biosensor demonstrated robustness over 96 hours. 
 
• 
Several RamR mutants engineered in E. coli through the SELIS workflow retained their 
properties in yeast, with potential for further enhancement. 
 
• 
The dynamic range and sensitivity of the biosensors were effectively tuned by adjusting 
promoter strength. 
 
• 
The foundation for high-throughput screening of MIA producer strains was established. 
 
 
• 
The platform is adaptable and holds potential for use with other RamR biosensors targeting 
different PNPs, such as BIAs, Amaryllidaceae alkaloids like galantamine, and other chiral 
amines.
