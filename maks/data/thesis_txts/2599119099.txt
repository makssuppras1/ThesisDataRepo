Abstract 
The objective of this thesis is to investigate the potential for grid upgrade deferral by employing an 
Energy Storage System (ESS) within a congested distribution network area to perform peak shaving 
and energy arbitrage. The suitability of a novel gravity energy storage device is considered as part of 
the study. Previous research has shown that to provide peak shaving services as part of a grid upgrade 
deferral, an energy storage system with an excellent cycle life and large energy capacity is required. 
Currently, battery energy storage cannot fulfil the long term-requirements.  
A mixed-integer linear programming model was formulated using the MatLab optimization toolbox to 
model the operation of an energy storage device connected to a District Network Operator (DNO) 
substation and participating on the UK wholesale electricity market. An initial objective function was 
created to determine the optimal shave level for increasing energy storage capacity. This ensured the 
electrical load was evenly shaved whilst utilising the entire energy capacity. A second objective 
function was created to allow the model to perform electricity arbitrage operations. This model 
maintained the maximum shave level for each ESS calculated by the previous model, making sure the 
demand could not exceed this limit, and proceeded to generate revenue when not required for peak 
shaving. 
Results demonstrated that at lower energy capacities, the revenue generated by the ESS is capable of 
paying off the system costs within the first year of operation. This is because the smaller capacity 
ESSs are reserved for peak shaving for only a short period each day and are therefore able to perform 
electricity arbitrage at other times. As more of the demand is shaved to allow for load increases, larger 
ESS capacities are required and so the system capital expenditure increases. Due to the longer 
operating periods the ESS is forced to sell the stored electricity for sub-optimal prices.  
A grid connected ESS is a feasible solution for grid upgrade deferral, and gravitational energy storage 
systems are promising in this context. This research will be of value to industry stakeholders seeking 
to optimise innovative ESS systems; as well as network operators exploring effective alternative to 
traditional grid upgrade and meet energy demands that are increasing in both magnitude and 
variability.

1 Introduction 
Energy storage is poised to become a vital element in national power systems, as there is a 
fundamental shift from centralised generation to more distributed generation systems with a high 
penetration of intermittent Renewable Energy Sources (RES). Furthermore, a predicted increase in 
residential electricity demand, combined with the uncertainty of increased and irregular demand due 
to electric vehicle (EV) charging, will cause congestion on distribution networks. Energy storage can 
therefore provide relief to networks that are predicted to encounter issues with peak demand. 
The need to modernize the electricity networks is based on the integration of more sustainable 
generation resources, the growing electricity demand, and the establishment of trans-European 
electricity markets. The growing electricity demand is due to the increasing global population, but 
more specifically increasing urbanisation. The IEA World Energy Outlook 2019 [1] suggests the 
annual global residential electricity demand is set to increase from 6.6 TWh to 8.4 TWh by 2030 and 
to 10.8 TWh by 2040. The UK government Department of Energy and Climate Change (DECC) 
estimated that total electricity demand would dip from 2014 to 2020 and increase up to the 2030s. In 
its 2015 projections, DECC forecasts electricity demand to increase from c. 300 TWh in 2014 to c. 
350 TWh in 2030; a total increase of around 16.67% over the 16 year period [2]. With increases to 
residential areas, District Network Operators (DNOs) will need to ensure security of energy supply.  
Many areas of the UK electricity grid require investment in some form of network upgrade due to 
capacity constraints [3]. These constraints may be a result of one or more factors such as a change in 
generation levels, local demand growth, or asset degradation. These capacity constraints can manifest 
into faults and cause serious harm to grid assets. The traditional method for dealing with an 
increasingly congested network node would be to upgrade the assets that are close to, or at, their 
operating limits.  
A grid connected ESSs could provide relief to networks. It would likely be of most benefit for grid 
upgrade deferral when the local electricity demand growth rate is slow and traditional upgrade costs 
are significantly high. Furthermore, energy storage technologies can provide flexibility and security at 
times of fluctuating energy generation and demand, whereas a traditional upgrade would add 
considerable overcapacity and no additional benefits. 
Pumped hydro storage (PHS) is the global leader of installed grid-scale energy storage but in the past 
ten years battery energy storage systems (BESS) have shown a rapid rate of development and decline 
in unit costs. These two technologies are set to dominate the energy storage landscape for the 
foreseeable future. However, emerging technologies such as gravitational potential energy storage 
show promise for long-term grid applications due to extensive cycle life and negligible standing 
losses. The novel energy storage solution by Gravitricity [4] will be used as a reference in this report.

1.1 Objectives & Scope of Thesis 
This report will seek to investigate the potential for an Energy Storage System (ESS) to provide 
congestion relief to a district network area that is approaching the capacity limit of the local 
substation. In addition to deferring the necessity of traditional network reinforcement, the capabilities 
and value in alternative revenue streams for grid connected Energy Storage Systems (ESS) will be 
explored using a linear programming optimisation problem. A methodology for two optimisation 
models will be formulated to represent the operation of a grid connected ESS interacting with the 
local load demand profile, whilst participating on the UK electricity wholesale market. The first of 
these models will seek to find the optimal exploitation of the ESS to provide peak shaving at a 
uniform level each day. The second model will attempt to maximise the potential revenue generated 
by constructing a charge-discharge profile to perform energy arbitrage on the UK wholesale 
electricity market. 
1.2 Gravitricity Energy Storage 
Gravitricity is a UK based company currently developing a novel gravitational energy storage 
solution. The system is designed to raise and lower large weights within a vertical subterranean shaft 
by means of steel cables connected to motor winches. To store energy, electrical power is applied to 
the winch motors and the weights are lifted up the shaft. Potential energy is stored in the suspend 
weight due to the force of gravity acting against it. To discharge energy from the system, the process 
is reversed; lowering the weight down the shaft and turning the winch motors to generate electricity.

2 Background & Literature Review 
This section explains the background theory required to understand the practical operations of energy 
storage technologies and the possible grid services they can provide. Existing research that overlaps 
with this study is also discussed. 
2.1 Current Literature 
A paper from the University of Oxford [7] specifically investigates a gravitational energy storage 
system via the sizing of weights suspended in mine shafts to maximise the energy storage capacity. It 
also verifies that the maximum ramp-rate is directly related to the power capacity of the system and so 
a variety of additional power services could be provided. A case study of the UK Midlands was 
included and estimated a potential 340 mine shafts that could be converted in that area alone, 
providing up to 804 MWh.  
A report by Sandia National Laboratories [8] detailed the findings of implementing linear 
programming optimisation problems to maximise the potential revenue for grid connected ESS. The 
report concluded the maximum potential revenue of an ESS would be generated by performing energy 
arbitrage or frequency regulation.  
An application that is not extensively discussed in current published literature is the potential for large 
scale deployment of ESSs performing peak shaving or load levelling service to affect the electricity 
market price. One study [9] conducted an economic analysis of a load levelling BESS participating in 
the Korean electricity market and assessed the long-term effects. Their results indicated that the 
overall cost of electricity increased with the size of the BESS levelling the load. This suggests that if 
load levelling is something which network operators wish to implement, intervention would be 
required in terms of policy regulation to incentivise development or a case for additional economic 
gains clearly defined. 
The deployment of battery energy storage systems for long term applications has rightfully been 
critiqued on several occasions due to their limited cycle life and potential accelerated decrease of 
lifespan due to frequent charging and discharging. One study [10] explored profit maximisation taking 
into account the impact of cycle degradation on the total profit. Results suggested that smart operating 
strategies would be required to ensure economic success of BESS in long-term deployment. 
An academic and industry analysis of emerging topics in energy storage conducted by Mejia & 
Kajikawa [11] reports that since the year 2000 the majority of new patent applications have been in 
manufacturing of secondary cells, i.e. rechargeable batteries. This is understandable since there have 
been rapid advancements in battery technology over the past two decades. The second largest subject 
area for patent applications was “Electric digital data processing”, with many of these relating to 
==PAGE:15==
5 
“Circuit arrangements for AC distribution”. These results suggest that industry (by the application of 
patents) is generally more active than academia in pushing innovation with regards to secondary 
batteries. The report goes on to state that the most similar trends between academia and industry are

2.3 Smart Grids 
The term “smart grid” began to emerge in the early 2000s as the operational landscape of grid 
networks around the world started to change towards non-traditional generation and consumption. 
Traditionally, electricity was generated, transferred along transmission lines, and distributed to 
consumers. However, with the accelerating integration of renewable energy sources and consumer 
side appliances, the transmission network is no longer a one-way system. In a smart grid the electrical 
grid operates and measures energy in real time on both the production and consumption side. 
Components/characteristics include smart meters, smart appliances, renewable energy sources, and 
efficient generation and transmission. The European Commission was the initiator of the smart-grid 
vision and the concepts [13]. Many technical and legislative challenges will need to be overcome to 
allow a full European interconnected smart grid, and energy storage will play a key role in support of 
this European strategy.

2.4.2 Pumped Hydro Storage (PHS)  
PHS technology utilises two water reservoirs at different elevations. To store energy, electrical energy 
is used to pump water from the lower reservoir to the upper reservoir. Shifting the water mass against 
the force of gravity creates potential energy within the upper reservoir. The energy stored in the upper 
reservoir is directly proportional to the head range (the height difference between the two reservoirs) 
and the volume of water being stored. The head range can vary from 100m to 1500m for different 
projects. When electrical energy is needed, the water is released from the upper to the lower reservoir 
through a turbine to produce electricity. Figure 7 shows a schematic diagram of a PHS system.

Most PHS plants around the world use geological structures, such as mountain valleys, as their 
reservoirs and are naturally fed by river tributaries. The rainfall of the area helps fill the upper 
reservoir; reducing the amount of water required to be pumped from the lower reservoir, thus 
improving the overall efficiency of the system. This is called an open-loop PHS system. In contrast, a 
closed-loop system does not have any interaction with external sources. The rated power capacity of 
PHS plants ranges from 10 MW to 3,000 MW[12], while typical power capacity ranges between 200 
– 350 MW, and energy capacity can reach up to 100 GWh. PHS plants can ramp up from 50% to full 
production capacity in about 15 seconds, from standstill to full production capacity within less than 
two minutes and from standstill to full pumping capacity within less than five minutes depending on 
the plant configuration. PHS is 70-85% efficient, with the main losses caused by friction, turbulence 
==PAGE:20==
10 
and viscous drag inside the turbine, pump and penstock. The main functions/applications of PHS are 
load following, frequency regulation, spinning reserve, voltage regulation [15]. 
Most of the favourable sites have already been developed and therefore it is increasingly difficult to 
locate new potential sites and harder again to obtain planning permission. The best locations for such 
systems are generally located in mountain or valley landscapes quite often far from urban areas where 
the electricity is needed. This means more cables need to be installed and therefore increases the 
overall cost of the project. 
2.4.3 Lithium Ion Batteries 
Lithium-Ion batteries store electricity by converting electrical energy into potential chemical energy. 
When a voltage is applied across the electrodes of a battery, the electrons transfer energy into the 
lithium-oxide anode (positive electrode) which excites the lithium ions to break free from their atomic 
bonds, flow through the electrolyte permeable membrane and attach to the graphite cathode (negative 
electrode). The ions remain there until a load is connected across the battery terminals and the ions 
move back to the anode producing energy. The electrolyte membrane allows the Li-ions to flow from 
the anode to the cathode but insulates against electrons. Figure 8 below depicts the general operation 
of a single battery cell. Connecting cells together creates a battery, and grouping several batteries 
together provides a high specific energy, as well as high energy and power densities. 
 
Figure 8: Operating Principles of a Li-Ion cell 
Various combinations of Li-ion battery chemistries produce unique performance, cost and safety 
characteristics. The choice of chemistry is usually determined by the desired operational 
characteristics. Lithium-ion batteries have power ratings ranging from 0.1MW to 100 MW, a round-
trip efficiency of up to 95%, and a cycling life of up to 1500 cycles [16]. However, this cycle life is 
==PAGE:21==
11 
dependent on operating temperature and C-rates. Their overall lifetime can be severely shortened if 
deeply discharged, so batteries would need to be oversized if a longer life expectancy was required.

2.5 Grid Services 
In this subsection, grid services that could potentially be employed by an ESS are listed and 
described. This list is not exhaustive, but the most relevant services are mentioned. 
Arbitrage is the action of buying and storing electricity when market prices are low, then selling the 
energy back to the market when prices are high. This is an effective way an ESS can generate 
revenue, and since market prices are closely linked to demand variations it can help to level the cost 
of electricity. 
Load levelling is the action of charging the ESS during periods of low demand and discharging when 
demand is high with the desired goal of flattening the overall load profile as much as possible. Load 
levelling allows for the postponement of grid upgrades or new energy generation. 
Peak shaving / valley filling is similar to load levelling but mainly for the purpose of reducing peak 
demand rather than levelling the entire load profile. Commercial and industrial customers save on 
their electricity bills due to the apparent reduction in peak demand from the generation side. 
2.5.1 Ancillary Services 
Frequency regulation seeks to rectify fluctuations in grid frequency caused by intermittent power 
generation and variable loads. The energy storage system is charged or discharged in response to a 
respective increase or decrease of grid frequency. 
Spinning reserve is when an ESS responds to a generation or transmission outage by supplying 
power to maintain network continuity while the backup generator is brought online. It enables 
generators to operate at optimum power output without the need to keep idle capacity for dedicated 
spinning reserves.  
2.5.2 Renewable Energy Integration 
Renewable energy time shift is when an ESS stores energy generated by a renewable energy source 
when either demand or electricity prices are low, then discharges when demand or prices are higher. 
Renewable Capacity firming is the action taken by an ESS to maintain a committed level of power 
output of a RES for a period of time. The ESS smooths the output and controls the ramp rate to 
eliminate rapid voltage and power swings on the electrical grid. 
2.6 UK Electricity Market Overview 
Electricity, like any other commodity, can be bought or sold on a market. However, it is unique since 
it cannot be stored in large volumes like other traded products. This means electricity demand must be 
satisfied by generation at any given instant in time. In the UK, this matching of demand with supply is 
==PAGE:23==
13 
mainly achieved via trading on the wholesale electricity market. Trading takes the form of bilateral 
contracts or exchanges and can be agreed upon months in advance or via on-the-day trading. The 
Energy System Operator (ESO) is responsible for ensuring security of supply i.e. supply meets 
demand on a second-by-second basis, and the overall balancing of the energy system. National Grid is 
the ESO for the UK electricity grid and provides the computing and communication assets required to 
monitor and control the energy system. National Grid also assumes the role of the Transmission 
System Operator (TSO) in the UK, owning the majoring of the transmission assets such as cables, 
transformers and substations. Generators produce and sell electricity, and can potentially provide 
services such as voltage control, regulation and reserve. Distribution companies own and operate 
distribution networks and are commonly referred to as Distribution Network Operators (DNOs). 
DNOs purchase electricity directly from generators on the wholesale market. Large consumers are 
also permitted to purchase on this market, and this is where grid-connected energy storage could be 
classified.  
At the wholesale level, National Grid ensures the continuous operation of the spot market and 
transmission of electricity to the district network areas. At the retail level, the system remains 
centralised since each DNO operates the network area and sells electricity to consumers and retailers. 
This model creates considerably more competition for the generating companies because the 
wholesale price is determined by the interplay of supply and demand. Generally speaking, electricity 
prices are high when demand is high. Grid-connected ESSs would do well to exploit this 
interdependent relationship between demand and price since power supplied to the grid by the ESS at 
peak times should receive higher pay-outs. The ESS in this study is, hypothetically, connected to a 
distribution network substation and receives energy from an existing transmission line.

3 Methodology 
In this chapter, a mixed integer linear programme (MILP) is presented to obtain the optimal utilisation 
of a grid connected energy storage system to perform peak shaving and energy arbitrage operations.  
Linear programming is a technique used to optimally maximise or minimise a function by means of a 
mathematical model. This is often called an optimisation problem; where an objective function is 
defined, stated whether it is to be maximised or minimised, and then constrained by various inequality 
statements that shape the operating behaviour of the model. Three scenarios are presented for 
investigation in this paper. Programming models were formulated and executed via MatLab 
optimisation toolbox. Since each system focuses around the utilisation of an ESS, the constraints 
defining the ESS behaviour will be the same for each model. The objective function for each scenario 
is the main difference in determining the desired operation for each.  
Prior to describing the operation of the models utilised in this investigation, details of the data sources 
and assumptions will be stated.

4 Results & Discussion 
The purpose of this thesis was to investigate the potential for an energy storage device to cover peak 
load demand infer grid upgrade deferral; considering the technical and economic aspects of 
gravitational potential energy storage as a long-term solution. The optimisation problem was 
formulated and solved using the MatLab optimisation toolbox. For the purposes of this project, the 
cost of storage is estimated to range between £250/kWh and £650/kWh and a comparison will be 
present of the different results these values give. The ESS system sizes were analysed between 0.5 
MW and 30 MW. 
4.1 Peak Shave & Valley Fill Results 
The first system set up was considered a based system where the optimisation sought to shave peaks 
and fill valleys of the demand profile using the full capacity of the ESS, ensuring maximum and 
minimum values were prioritised. An optimisation model was formulated via a mixed-integer linear 
programme and subjected to various constraints. The model read input data for the electricity demand 
and produced a charge/discharge power dispatch for the ESS that effectively flattened the load profile. 
The charge/discharge power was then multiplied by the corresponding electricity prices for each 
dispatch period to determine the revenue of the system. The optimisation problem used 2019 demand 
and price data and was solved for each month of the year. Even though an optimal shave level was 
determined for each size of ESS, the results indicate a shave-fill operation is not economically 
attractive. This is to be expected, since the model does not consider the price of electricity when 
producing the charge-discharge profile. However, the objective for this model was to increase the 
overall annual load as much as possible given the ESS capacity. An example of the shave-fill 
operation is shown graphically in Figure 13 below.

this phenomenon showing the first four days of February 2019. The four days span over a weekend, 
from Friday to Monday, and show the difference in demand between weekdays and weekends. The 
weekday system output shows more of a peak shave / valley fill operation (albeit with larger ESS 
capacity this still tends towards load levelling), and the operation on the weekends shows a significant 
load levelling behaviour due to the lower average demand. The corresponding electricity market 
prices have also been depicted as reference to the aforementioned “night-time” charging when prices 
begin to increase in the early morning (see charging on 04 February).

The results in Table 10 in Appendix B show that the 18.5 MWh capacity ESS can support a 9.9% 
increase in the total 2019 demand. The ESS also reduces the cost of energy transferred on the existing 
cable from £37.80/MWh to £34.39/MWh. A difference of £3.41/MWh means the savings associated 
with the new total load available of 214,471 MWh equates to £730,351 per year. However, the results 
show the annual revenue generated from this type of operation is generally low, with the best 
performing ESS capacity only generating £63,486 in 2019. Assuming a similar rate of revenue 
generation for subsequent years, the payback time (PBT) for such a gravitational energy storage 
system would be up to 76 years. This is obviously not a desirable situation and it is unlikely that an 
overall increase in electricity prices would benefit a model operating in this way since night-time 
prices would also increase.  
4.2 Peak Shave w. Arbitrage Results 
In the second method, the revenue of the system was optimised given the condition that the shave 
level was satisfied. The objective function of the optimisation programme then set out to maximise 
the revenue generated by the ESS over the course of the year. The peak shave levels for each size of 
ESS from the previous model were used as the upper boundary limit of the system. This allowed for 
the maximum possible increase in overall load and maximum revenue to be attained. To achieve the 
optimal buy and sell prices, the dispatch operation of this model exerted a more erratic charge and 
discharge behaviour. The annual revenue for each ESS capacity is shown in Figure 18 below.

5 Conclusions  
The purpose of this study was to determine the potential revenue from a grid connected energy 
storage device providing peak shaving and arbitrage, with the underlying goal of grid upgrade 
deferral. For the model performing peak shaving only, it was useful to see the variation in revenue 
with increasing ESS capacity. The lessened financial benefits after the system capacity exceeded 18.5 
MWh infers that system operators would require strong incentives to seek high levels of load 
levelling. Results from the peak shave and arbitrage model show promising prospects of long-term 
economic gain from deploying such a strategy.  
At lower energy capacities, the revenue generated is capable of paying off the system costs but for 
larger ESSs to become economically viable over longer periods of time, the cost per kWh needs to fall 
significantly below £500/kWh. 
Gravitricity’s energy storage solution shows potential for grid applications such as peak shaving and 
arbitrage due to the possibility of high number of cycles with negligible degradation. The long 
lifespan of the solution complements the longer timeframes for effective grid upgrade deferral.  
Since the demand data was a scaled down version of the entire UK electricity demand profile, it 
would be expected that the morning and evening peaks would be sharper than those presented in the 
dataset used in this study. This means that the shave level and annual load percentage would 
potentially increase since there would be less energy contain within the peaks to shave. 
The end results of the peak shave + arbitrage model indicates that after a certain capacity size of ESS 
the system becomes practically and economically beneficial for the ESS operator. The ESS is required 
to be an adequate size in order to allow a realistic increase in the local load. 
The limitations of this study include the theoretical nature of the model and economic calculations. A 
more realistic approach would be to consider the transfer of complex power, as well as the costs and 
electrical losses of other system components such as transformers and transmission cables.  
5.1 Future Work 
As the integration of renewable energy sources becomes more prevalent across the world, network 
operators will seek to reduce the variability of RES generation to a more reliable and controllable 
state. Short of curtailing the output, the obvious way of improving the consistency of output is to pair 
the system with an energy storage device. Future work could apply a similar peak shaving model to 
RES output and investigate the benefits to this shaving or time-shift the energy to peak demand 
periods.  
==PAGE:44==
34 
Specific case studies and feasibility assessment with a focus on calculating the levelised cost of 
storage and investigate the socio-economic trade-offs to significant load levelling within a distribution 
network area. Thorough economic analysis of ESS peak shave and arbitrage revenue streams that 
would include forecasted prices and demand, operational expenditure, interest, and inflation. 
For future work, an investigation into the economic dispatch of an ESS system that is providing 
frequency regulation services would be interesting. Analysis by Byrne & Silva-Monroy [8] shows that 
participation in the regulation market produces four times the revenue compared to arbitrage in the 
California Independent System Operator market using 2010 and 2011 data. 
 
A grid connected ESS therefore is a feasible solution for grid upgrade deferral, and gravitational 
energy storage systems are promising in this context. This research will be of value to industry 
stakeholders seeking to optimise innovative ESS systems; as well as network operators exploring 
effective alternative to traditional grid upgrade and meet energy demands that are increasing in both 
magnitude and variability.
