Abstract
This report presents a preliminary approach in determining the potential advantages of im-
plementing a buoyant lifting device to improve the performance of a Fly-Gen Airborne Wind
Energy System (AWES). First, a comparison of diﬀerent aerostats and means of buoyancy is
given, along with wind proﬁle modeling for AWES heights. A way of describing the average
forces on a point mass system as well as the overall tether orientation is by solving the static
average forces in the system for a given conﬁguration. An assessment of added losses due to
an aerostat is also made. In a second part, due to the intricate tether orientation, a dynamic
model accounting for the diﬀerent tether lengths and orientations of an operating Fly-Gen kite
and an aerostat. Lastly, a multiparameter comparison is done, and an overall assessment of the
dynamic losses as well as diﬀerent performance metrics are compared, such as AEP, Capacity
Factor, Rated Power, and Speciﬁc Power. By using joint power curves, consider wind shear
and speed at a reference height and joint probability distribution. Results suggest an overall
improvement in both AEP and Capacity Factor, where the lowest increase is by ∼46% while
still beneﬁting from large Speciﬁc Power values. The overall advantage is the independence
of wind proﬁle gradient on power output by virtually eliminating cosine losses associated with
elevation angle, as well as a reduction in losses associated with tether lengths, which are both
sources of losses for AWES . Joint power curves combining wind shear and wind speed show the
overall independence of power and shear exponent. The dynamic model suggests the possibility
of potential resonant frequencies associated with dynamic losses due to tether and aerostat drag,
which can be avoided through tether length control.

Introduction
The idea of tethered devices for Airborne Wind Energy Sytems (AWESs) is not new, Loyd [1]
in 1980 presented the idea of using either Ground - Generation (Ground-Gen) where a kite
reels out a tether coupled to a generator, or Flying - Generation (Fly-Gen) where the generator
is onboard the ﬂying kite. Both of these type are classiﬁed as ’Crosswind’, as they need to
ﬂy crosswind of the wind to eﬀectively exploit the wind resources. Other categories, such as
rotational or drag-based devices, have also been investigated. When compared to traditional
Horizontal Axis Wind Turbines (HAWT), the potential advantage is being able to acces strong
high-altitude wind resources while beneﬁting from lower total mass. 40 years later, with two
decades of research and experimental data [2] the technology is still in it’s early infancy, as
suggested by the latest reports [3]. Many companies like Kite power, TwingTec, Ampyx Power,
Makani Power, and Sky Sails amongst others are trying to achieve eﬀective systems delivering
more power at a smaller cost, with the latter recently being the ﬁrst to validate a power curve [4].
So far, the main limitations are due to control as well as complex take-oﬀand landing maneuvers
that make the systems unreliable. From a physical standpoint, AWES suﬀers from cosine and
drag losses.
It can and will be shown that the potential power output of an AWES device
is proportional to the cube of the cosine of an elevation and a tilt angle. The latter can be
mitigated by good kite design; the former is required to access any altitude. In order to achieve
altitude, the kite either has to have a high elevation angle, which induces cosine losses, or a long
tether, which induces drag losses.
As shown by recent studies, their are multiple ideas for using lighter-than-air devices such as
ariships or blimps for multiple applications [5] [6]. As well as multiple startup companies working
on diﬀerent designs and applications such as Flying Whales, Ocean Sky and Airlander to name
a few. Using a lighter-than-air device for airborne wind energy has been attempted multiple
times in various ways. Novel ideas such as the ones proposed by Magenn power or Altaeros
Energies present original approaches to AWESs. No research, however, implements lighter-than-
air devices with crosswind generation, at least to the author’s knowledge. The ideas presented
in this study look at two ways a lighter-than-air device can help a conventional ﬂy-gen device.
Either by utilizing the aerostat as an air-achor or as a way to mitigate the mass of the kite.
Introducing an aerostat to assist conventional airborne systems, the acronym becomes Aerostat
Assisted Airborne Wind Energy, or A3WES . The study looks at the possible emprovements
through multiple parameters; Anual Energy Production (AEP), Capacity Factor (CP), Rated
Power amongst other considerations. This is done by ﬁrst assessing a good lifter design and
making an accurate wind proﬁle model for high altitudes. In a second part, an average force
model is established and adapted to support a lifter in two new conﬁgurations, as opposed to

Aircrafts
As the ’Airborne’ in AWES suggests, diﬀerent aircraft need to be used. A multitude of designs
and sub-designs are possible; a small study and layout of the chosen aircraft and their properties
is required.
In this chapter, the kite used in the study is deﬁned, lighter-than-air aircraft are compared, and
a scalable design is selected. Moreover, a comparison on lifting gases is also established.
2.1
Kite
When it comes to AWES, the term kite is used quite loosely. Since the study assumes ﬂy-gen
systems where a ﬁxed-wing kite is used, soft kite designs do exist but won’t be covered in this
study. The goal of the study is to assess the potential improvements by mitigating the possible
losses the kite can experience. By choosing an aerodynamically eﬃcient kite, the impact of losses
is more apparent. Another advantage is allowing ﬂight at low wind speeds, which is also a part of
the study. An aerodynamically eﬃcient kite is similar to an eﬃcient glider design. Following this
approach, a design similar to the Eta self-propelled glider [7] is chosen. The kite is modeled as
a point mass with the following characteristics: Table 2.1. Note that when designing sail planes
such as the Eta glider, speciﬁc criteria are required depending on the class but also to ensure
the safety of the passenger(s) and allow for thermal soaring. In the scope of a ﬂy-gen-optimized
kite, some variations in design are allowed. The weight is stripped down since the kite doesn’t
need to support the weight of a person in a cockpit; there is no need for a canopy either. This
explains the main diﬀerence in mass. The mass also includes the weight of the turbine and the
electrical systems. The Oswald eﬃciency e is set to 1. For an eﬃcient design, it could be larger

2.2
Aerostats
The term aerostat designates any lighter-than-air aircraft with or without means of propulsion.
For this study, diﬀerent types of aerostats are compared. Lifting devices such as aerostats are
assumed to be ﬁlled with a lighter-than-air gas within an envelope. Here, the weight of nacelles,
engines, and control surfaces is omitted. The idea is to use the aerostat as a reverse anchor, in a
sense. Aerostats get their lift passively through buoyancy which puts them in the rare category
of things that are advantaged by the square cube law in terms of lift; scaling all linear sizes of
an aerostat 2 times up will make it lift 8 times more. Of course, there is an upper limit due to
ﬁnancial and structural integrity.
2.2.1
Balloons
Balloons, as their name suggests, are very simple volumes that have a mostly round spheroid
shape. For simplicity, they are modeled as a sphere with a given radius. They are ﬁlled with a
lighter than air gas, which makes them buoyant. Balloons are, due to their simple shape cheap
to manufacture, and this is why they are mostly used today for meteorological platforms or
high-altitude monitoring devices. Historically, they were the ﬁrst technology to allow manual
ﬂight by using heated air as a lifting gas. Since they are modeled as a sphere, the drag coeﬃcient
is the same from all sides and can be set to Cdsphere = 0.2 from [8].
2.2.2
Blimps
Blimps are a special, smaller kind of airship. Airships are mostly a technology of the past; today,
blimps prevail as a novelty for transportation or advertisement. Blimps are, per volume, lighter
than bigger airships because they don’t have an internal structure and are presurized with a
lighter-than-air gas (usually helium). For this study, the shape of a blimp is modeled after the
USS Los Angeles (ZR-3) built by Luftschiﬀbau Zeppelin GmbH. This airship is chosen because
it’s drag coeﬃcient as well as its overall shape are speciﬁed in [8]. The rescaling is done by
keeping the same elongation ratio l/d = 7.2 and then adjusting the length. For this study, the
longest blimps considered are 110 meters long, well within the range of modern blimps. The
shape of blimps, in general, can be modeled as a thick symmetrical airfoil proﬁle; in other words,
blimps have very low drag coeﬃcients when seen from the front, and the apparent area is also
relatively low. In a sense, blimps "cheat" by virtually being made of a series of balloons hiding
in their own wake. Based on modern startups, the blimp size is limited to 300 m, larger than the
largest airships of the last century. For ease of calculation, the volume and areas of the blimp
can be modeled as a scaled cylinder of the same elongation ratio l/d. Figure 2.1 shows the cross
section of the aerostat and an overallping cylinder of the same length and diameter, equal to
the maximum diameter of the blimp. For this speciﬁc aerostat shape, the volume, the frontal
area, and the total area can be found and then scaled accordingly to the cylinder, such that:

In general, helium and hydrogen are the two viable options used for airships. Today, virtually
only helium is used; however, hydrogen provides more lift and can be produced in an environ-
mentally friendly way, which is not the case for helium. Despite oﬀering nearly 10% more lift
for the same volume compared to helium, hydrogen’s utility is compromised by its instability
relative to its noble counterpart. Hydrogen has a habit of reacting exothermically with oxy-
gen and this troublesome tendency necessitates careful consideration. However, modern-day
technology and engineering can mitigate this risk. Another note and maybe more important
is that, due to the tiny molecules, both helium and hydrogen can’t be contained in a gaseous
state and will eventually ’seep’ through their containers. This means reﬁlls are required, which

Further assumptions

Limitations
The proposed 1DOF model’s strongest advantage is that it proposes a fully analytical solution
for small oscillations. However, it highly underestimates the overall motion of the Y-Point. This
limits the use cases of this model however it can still be used as a pre-emptive estimation of the

unfeasible by this simple model.

Model limitations
The 2DOF model is quite limited in multiple ways, due to the nature of the diﬀerential equations
the model has to be resolved through numerical integration, which leads to an overall slow
solution. When it comes to the solutions they can be mainly categorized into three groups.
−Unstable snap-through
−Stable snap-through
−Stable solution
Snap-through is the main limit of this model since the tethers are modeled as linear stiﬀrods,
in the event the two rods push against each other the system will suddenly ’snap’ resulting in
a violent acceleration. In certain cases, the acceleration spike is not enough to destabilize the
system. Due to the chaotic nature of double pendulums, a solution is deﬁned as stable when the
two angles θ1 and θ2, are bounded within a reasonable amplitude and the angular acceleration
is continuous. An example of all three cases is shown in Figure 5.8. As seen in Figure 5.8a and
Figure 5.8b, the acceleration α is not continuous. However, the latter is still stable, in that the
oscillation angles are bounded within a reasonable margin. In the true stable case in Figure 5.8c,
the acceleration α is continuous. As a reference, it takes ∼30 seconds of simulation time to
properly determine the overall nature of the solution if snap-through is present or not. It then
takes ∼2000 seconds of simulation time for the power losst to drag to converge. Since the
overall motions in the system are somewhat slow (for a good solution) a time step of dt = 0.05
is suﬃcient.
The 2DOF model is limited in multiple ways, due to modeling the tethers as stiﬀthin rods,
snap-through is bound to happen for certain cases, this means many of cases can’t be accurately
simulated. Another issue is the underestimating of dampening, the assumptions for which the
model was derived assumed small oscillations and speeds, if they get too large, the model
overestimates the motion since dampening is too small.
Further improvement would also require a proper Cl Cd table for aerostats, to more accurately
estimate the actual aerodynamic forces at play.

Results
To compare the diﬀerent possible conﬁgurations that are possible, multiple assumptions are
made as well as clear design objectives to deﬁne the scope of the analysis. The goal of the
study is to assess the possible improvements of a A3WES system. By comparing the same kite
with diﬀerent tether widths combined with diﬀerent blimp sizes, diﬀerent conﬁgurations can
be comapred. After selecting speciﬁc confugartions a more reﬁned joint power curve, including
wind shear and friction velocity, is then made. It is assumed that the tethers and elevation
angle of the kite can be adjusted ’on the ﬂy’. The power curves can then be combined with a
probability distribution for the same wind speeds.

Hypothesis
A few hypotheses for the overall behavior of the system are formulated as follows:
−A3WES allows in an overall increase for AEP and CP over AWES at similar rated power
while maintaining high speciﬁc power.
−A3WES can help improve the overall reliability of AWES .
Reliability can be measured in multiple ways. Here reliability is measured through safety in case
of a fatal crash and the ability to operate at very low wind speeds (≤5 m/s).

Equation 4.12 that the frequency also decreases with kite tether length: see Figure C.3. A
simple AWES is limited by the cone angle due to the decrease in altitude associated with a

6.2.2
Applying dynamic losses
Due to the size of the data set (over 1 million points), it’s not feasible to assess the system
under the 2DOF model. To still get a broad idea of the losses associated with the motion of
the Aerostat, a small subset of points is chosen, simply selecting every nth point to get a set of
roughly 7000 points. The available power is then deﬁned as Pavailable −Ploss. In reality, this is
not guaranteed to hold if the diﬀerence is large. The power loss would be perceived on the kite
as a tug that slows it down, and it would then reduce the agitation force and the freqeuncy on
the aerostat, converging to an equilibrium point.
By comparing the ’optimistic’ available power from the 2D-static force balance and the ’pes-
simistic’ available power, it is possible to get an idea of what aﬀects the dynamic power loss and
under which conditions a solution is good (or bad). This is shown in Figure 6.5a and the corre-
sponding statistics in Figure 6.5b. There are multiple trends shown here. First, as conﬁrmed by
1DOF, the low frequency agitates the system more, resulting in a higher power ratio. Secondly,
the negative values can be one of multiple cases; in certain cases, the dynamics of the system
would require more power than what is available for generation, and those cases are then not
possible. A negative power ratio can also indicate an unstable solution that made it through the
ﬁltering. When ﬁltering out the negative points and only looking at the ’good’ solutions, there is
then an interesting statistical distribution of the points. By cutting oﬀthe minimum frequency
to 0.1 Hz the average power ratio is ∼1.60. Under those conditions, a general estimate of the
performance can be made by assuming that the ’Real’ power output of stable solution would be
at least 62%Pavailable this assumption can act as a conservative estimate since the 2DOF model
overestimates power loss and oscillations and that the average power loss decreases with higher
frequencies. More optimistically, the power output can be set to ∼85%Pavailable. One potential
danger would be a negative feed back loop where the power loss would slow down the kite down
to a lower frequency, thus entering resonance. However, a better dynamic model is required to
assess the possibility of such an event.

6.6
Control and design strategies
For the system to operate properly, diﬀerent conﬁgurations and control strategies are possible.
As shown above, resonant frequencies can be an issue if the rotation frequency of the kite is low.
The system is also limited by the maximum tension force in the tethers.
6.6.1
Wind speed
. When operating at above-rated wind speeds, the tension forces in the tethers can’t exceed
a design limit. Since increasing tether length decreases frequency, it is best to either change
the eﬀective wind speed by changing the elevation angle of the orbit axis or modulate the drag
experienced by the kite either through control surfaces or the onboard turbines.

Multiple kite conﬁguration
The primary source of oscillations and the associated power loss are due to the unbalanced side
component of the kite tension force, If another kite were introduced, the two side forces would
mostly cancel out and drastically reduce the agitation force on the tether system. One could
even imagine a triplet conﬁguration where three kites would be placed at 120°from one-another.
The main advantage of increasing kite numbers would be the sudden increase in maximum power
output as well as an overall advantage from a material perspective.

How can A3WES be implemented
Considering a control strategy and how the tether length can be modulated to achieve diﬀerent
results can be done in a lot of ways. In general, when given a set of tethers, the system is limited
by the tension forces that the tethers can withstand. To tune the tension forces, the kite can
either modulate its glide ratio Ge by, for example, pitching of control surfaces or by modulating
the aerodynamics or the torque of the onboard wind turbine. Another way is to simply change
the elevation angle β. However, this requires knowledge of wind speeds at higher and lower
altitudes to ensure that the change in altitude and angle does not increase tension forces.
One could also imagine that instead of using lifters to elevate the altitude and achieve high wind
resources and a low orbit angle, they could simply be used for take-oﬀand landing. Takeoﬀand
landing are delicate procedures that can result in catastrophic failure. In this case, one could
imagine the kite being almost stuck to the belly of an aerostat. The aerostat is then reeled
out to a desired altitude, and from there, the kite is slowly reeled out. Since the wind resource
is already high, it can deploy safely. Once in operation, the aerostat is slowly reeled back in
and the system becomes a conventional ﬂy-gen AWES. The advantage here is not having to deal
with complex controllers and, if one assumes the blimp model used so far, a 20-meter-long blimp
would provide enough lift to allow this. There is also a question of what happens to the aerostat
on the ground. One could assume that through compressors, the aerostat can be inﬂated and
deﬂated when not in use; however, hydrogen or helium still need to be replenished since these
elements tend to seep through containers. From a more eco-friendly perspective, coupling the
entire system with an electrolyzer could allow the production of clean hydrogen on-site and
maybe open the door to energy storage. A lot of the lift force of the aerostat is used to achieve
an air-anchor eﬀect; if the lift force is large enough, it can be used as a platform for multiple
applications that can beneﬁt the economics of the system.

Disscusions
The proposed 2DOF model tries to assess the added dynamic losses and manages to give some
results; however, the model is very limited and fails to capture the broader scope of possibilities
associated with the complexity of the entire system. The overall estimated power loss seems
to be aﬀected mostly by frequency rather than agitation force. In order to further assess this,
a more reliable dynamic model is needed. A large limitation is the lack of data on aerostats;
more research computational or experimental is needed to accurately describe the aerodynamic
forces acting on the system. Other aerodynamic eﬀects were also ignored, such as ﬂow distortion
and the wake of the aerostat. A large object such as an aerostat going back and forth would
also induce a wake disturbance, which needs to be assessed. This dynamic isnt captured here,
but it could largely aﬀect the dampening. Finally, the biggest limitation is not accounting for
the ﬂexibility of the tethers. Possible resonance could also occur within the tethers. A more
accurate model would also look into the fatigue loads in the tethers, which can also help assess
the reliability of the system.
In this study, two possible placements of the lifter were looked at; for other technologies that
ﬂy-gen, the conﬁgurations might act diﬀerently. The study also doesn’t optimize the shape or
design of the aerostat; however, a diﬀerent structure with more or less side drag volume-wise
could change the behaviour of the system.
Another limitation in the study was the choice of kite. Here, a design with a very high glide
ratio was chosen to mitigate as much as possible bank angle-associated losses to have the kite
respond more to elevation angle and tether drag losses. In reality, a ﬂy-gen crosswind AWES
has very high wing loading, unlike a glider, the kite needs to support much more than its own
weight.

Conclusions
The overall study shows that there seems to be an advantage in introducing a large lifter device
to help mitigate the main limitations of conventional ﬂy-gen devices. The system can either
be tuned to have a large capacity factor and power availability or, high peak power and AEP.
The results indicate an advantage over conventional HAWT by being able to produce power
more reliably while still beneﬁting from a high speciﬁc power metric. A conservative estimate
puts the capacity factor of A3WES over 60%, where a conventional ﬂy gen device would be at
around 28% and for reference, a HAWT lies around 35%.A conservative estimate for a design
optimized for AEP can have that number be above the 14 GWh mark with a peak power of 4
MW when considering a dynamic power loss of 60%. A slightly more optimistic number would
put the AEP at 20 GWh. Though the assumption that the proposed kite can produce 4 MW
is optimistic,. The main advantage is the introduction of high reliability; capacity factors can
easily go above 50% while staying on-shore; the aerostat allows for safe take-oﬀand landing
maneuvers and protects the kite in the event it plummets towards the ground.

B.6
Added mass
In ﬂuid mechanics, added mass or virtual mass is the inertia added to a system
because an accelerating or decelerating body must move (or deﬂect) some volume of
surrounding ﬂuid as it moves through it. Added mass is a common issue because the
object and surrounding ﬂuid cannot occupy the same physical space simultaneously.
For simplicity, this can be modeled as some volume of ﬂuid moving with the object,
though in reality "all" the ﬂuid will be accelerated, to various degrees.
