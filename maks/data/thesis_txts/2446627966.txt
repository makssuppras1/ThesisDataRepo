Abstract
The increasing demand for renewable energy led to development of wind power technologies.
The wind turbines are increasing in size and power capacity and going further in deeper
waters. The last years a lot of research is ongoing in ﬂoating wind turbines which have
the capability to be employed in even deeper waters. One of the promising designs is the
Floating Power Plant which combines wind with wave energy production, with accumulative
power capacity of 7-10 MW.
Optimization of such a design is essential. Optimally controlled wave energy converters
(WECs) designed to operate at full capacity may improve the economic prospects enor-
mously. The power output from WECs may be increased by controlling the oscillation in
order to approach an optimum interaction between the WEC and the incident wave. The
present thesis is introduced to perform optimization of the FPP WECs through numerical
and analytic methods.
The optimization can be separated in several aspects. The main parts of the ﬂoating
semi-submersible platform are the 4 WECs, which consist of one absorber, one chamber and
one Power Take Oﬀ(PTO) system each. This conﬁguration utilizes one mode of oscillation
and this is the pitch motion of the absorber around its hinge. Increase in the pitch motion
leads to increase of energy production from the PTO system. The motion of the absorber
has already been shown to be directly related with the wave-induced oscillation inside
the chamber. The present study aims to provide an estimation of the resonant frequency
of the internal surface elevation through a mathematical tool. This complex problem is
simpliﬁed by considering just one individual ﬂoating ﬁxed chamber that faces the dominant
incident wave direction. The resonant frequency of the surface elevation inside the chamber
is related to its dimensional properties. The mathematical solution is based on linear wave
theory and a shallow waters approximation. An attempt has been made to solve under
less restrictive assumptions using the method of matched eigenfunctions, however without
success. The problem is described and solved mathematically in two dimensions, ﬁrstly by
omitting the y-direction and secondly the z-direction. Then a three dimensional solution is
attempted based on the two 2D solutions. However, the comparison between the analytic
and the numerical results showed low accuracy in the estimation of the resonant frequency.
Finally, a new semi-analytic solution is introduced and is calibrated according to numerical
calculations. The ﬁnal set of equations is compared for the conﬁguration of the scaled FPP
chamber with the numerical result. The model underestimated the wave amplitude of the
surface elevation and estimates very well the resonant frequency.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
About Floating Power Plant A/S
Floating Power Plant A/S is a clean-tech company in the ﬁeld of renewable energy, aiming
to develop a ﬁnancially sustainable ﬂoating platform in deep waters, that combines wind
and wave energy absorption. The ﬁrst device (model P37) to have delivered power to the
grid has already been developed and tested for several years. The next enhanced device
(model P80, with a range of 2-2.5 MW wave and 5-8 MW wind power capacity), shown in
Figure 1.1, has already been under research and development for several years, aiming to
optimize the various aspects of the hybrid platform.
Figure 1.1: The P80 wind-wave model of Floating Power Plant
1
==PAGE:16==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
An advantage of the P80 hybrid platform, compared to the other platforms in the
ﬂoating wind sector, is the integrated Wave Energy Conversion (WEC) system. It consists
of 4 chambers, absorbers and Power Take Oﬀ(PTO) systems, equal in size, as shown in
Figure 1.2. The PTO system is not illustrated.
Figure 1.2: Left: The P80 model with the 4 WECs, Right: absorber (red) and chamber
(yellow)
The mooring system and the platform are designed to allow the entire platform, and
hence the WECs, to face the dominant incident wave direction. When the incoming waves
reach the platform, they cause the absorber to oscillate around its hinge.
The kinetic
energy of the absorber is then converted to electricity via a PTO system. When the system
comes into resonance, the pitch motion of the absorber is ampliﬁed, and so does the energy
production. Previous project [6] in Floating Power Plant highlighted that the absorber’s
motion is highly dependent on both its own properties and the dimensional characteristics
of the chamber. A synopsis of [6] is described in section 3.1.
1.2
Thesis objective & scope
The system needs to resonate in diﬀerent sea states around the world. To achieve this,
insight should be gained into the physical aspects of the wave oscillation inside the chambers
and the ﬂow inside, below and around the platform.
This investigation has been carried out using numerical models, such as WAMIT [1], a
state of the art package for analyzing wave interactions with oﬀshore platforms and other
structures. Firstly, the platform is drawn in a CAD package, such as Multisurf, as shown in
Figure 1.3. This geometry is then input to WAMIT and ﬁnally the boundary-value problem

for the wave-interaction with the prescribed bodies is solved in ﬁnite or inﬁnite water depth
with the boundary integral equation method (BIEM).

To design a WEC which is resonant at a speciﬁc wave period, equal to the peak pe-

numerous cases should be designed and checked. This process can be very repetitive and

hydrodynamic quantities. It ranges from minutes to a couple of hours, even days.

The present thesis aims to accelerate the above process. The primary objective of the
project, is to provide a semi-analytic set of equations, which describes the behaviour of the
waves both inside and outside of only one chamber (see Figure 1.4), ﬁxed in all degrees of
freedom. The dimensional characteristics of the chamber are the input, while the output
is the wave amplitude inside the chamber relative to the incident wave amplitude, as a
function of wave frequency . Then, this set of equations is modelled in a MATLAB or
Mathematica script to calculate the surface elevation inside the chamber in the frequency

domain. The size and shape of the chamber can now be easily determined and stand as a
ﬁrst step in the more detailed design of the complete platform.
Brieﬂy, the scope of this thesis is:
– to develop a deep understanding of linear wave theory and its applicability
– to review the academic literature on wave interaction with simple geometries, for
application to an FPP chamber
– to extend the most relevant analytic models to treat a ﬁxed, ﬂoating chamber,
– to validate the results with available tools, i.e. Wolfram Mathematica, WAMIT and

And the objectives of the thesis are:
– the development of an analytic or semi-analytic model to describe the free surface
elevation inside an individual chamber in the frequency domain
– to provide knowledge on the relation between the dimensional characteristics on the
chamber and the resonant frequency of the internal free surface elevation
1.3
Thesis structure
The current thesis consists of 10 chapters:
Chapter 1: An introduction of the company Floating Power Plant A/S and their inno-
vative hybrid wind-wave platform, named P80, is made. The main parts of P80 are noted,
which are in need of hydrodynamic investigation. These are the WEC, the absorber and
the chamber excluding the PTO system. The objective of the thesis is described, the scope
is deﬁned and the document’s structure is brieﬂy presented.
Chapter 2: The engineering nature of the problem is transformed into a mathematical
one. The ﬂow inside, around, below and at the lee of the chamber is brieﬂy described and
the steps of the solution’s method are pointed out.
Chapter 3: The conclusions from a previous project in FPP are presented. The available
literature, from numerous analytic and numerical studies on the interaction between surface
waves with submerged structures, is presented. The various mathematical techniques are
identiﬁed and the most favorable method for the problem is selected.

Chapter 4: The linearised progressive and evanescent gravity waves are described math-
ematically. The assumptions of the linear wave theory are described in detail, potential ﬂow
and time harmonic oscillations are considered to provide the solution for the velocity po-
tential Φ.
Chapter 5: The submerged horizontal plate problem is formulated mathematically in
the two dimensional vertical plane (2DV), i.e. the y-axis is omitted, and the complete

is presented with implemented corrections factors, which are calibrated from numerical
results.
The impact of the variation of each dimensional parameter on the chamber’s
resonant frequency is analyzed. The semi-analytic model is tested for the case of the scaled
real FPP chamber with numerical result.
Snapshots of the surface elevation and wave
amplitude modulus, from both numerical and the semi-analytic model, are presented.
Chapter 9: An extensive summary of the present thesis is made, the ﬁndings are sum-
marized and an extended discussion follows on the 3D ﬂow and the resonance of the FPP
chamber. Finally, directions for future research are suggested.

Chapter 2
Problem analysis
2.1
Problem deﬁnition
From an engineering perspective, the objective of the thesis manifests high interest, since
it aims to provide a tool for a more eﬃcient dimensionalization of the chamber.
When the incoming waves reach the chamber’s entrance, see ﬁgures 2.1 and 2.2, they
are partially transmitted inside and partially below after which they travel behind the
chamber. The remaining part is reﬂected in the opposite direction. The transmitted wave
inside the chamber is reﬂected when it reaches the chamber’s back-wall, either fully or
partially depending on the geometry. Then this ﬁrstly transmitted and secondly reﬂected
wave reaches the chamber’s entrance and is again partially transmitted into the open sea
and partially reﬂected inside the chamber and so on. This process leads to a partial-standing
wave inside the chamber, known as a ”pseudo-trapped” wave, of frequency and amplitude
depending on the dimensional characteristics of the structure.
It is exactly this process, that is desired to be described mathematically. The mathe-
matical tool will enable an engineer to determine the size and shape of the chamber that
generates the ”trapped” wave according to the known wave frequency of the incoming wave.

2.2
Flow mechanism
When the wave train reaches the chamber, it is forced to adjust to the sudden change of
depth above the chamber’s bottom. The wave proﬁle is steepened as a result of the shorter
wavelength and the increase in height.
The ﬂow beneath becomes an oscillatory sloshing motion in order to satisfy the bound-
ary condition of zero normal velocity at the chamber’s bottom and the seabed according to
[11], while the mass ﬂux is essentially constant with the horizontal distance, if the wave is
longer than the chamber length.
Additionally, the outgoing waves spread in the horizontal plane from the chamber’s
entrance to the open sea.
2.3
Solution methodology
The above stated problem is challenging to solve analytically.
Over the years, simpler
geometries have been studied due to their signiﬁcance in ocean engineering and analytic
solutions have been derived using various mathematical techniques.
For more complex
geometries, numerical methods with high accuracy have been introduced.
Firstly, an extensive literature review on the hydrodynamic behaviour of submerged
and ﬂoating structures has been carried out to provide guidance to the solution of this
project’s problem, see chapter 3.
8
==PAGE:23==
CHAPTER 2. PROBLEM ANALYSIS
From this review, it was concluded that the derivation of the velocity potentials for
each of the relevant regions is complicated. Between the various mathematical methods,
the shallow waters approximation and the matched eigenfunction expansions method have
been considered the most appropriate for the problem. The problem has been analyzed
in two dimensions, by omitting ﬁrstly the y-direction and then the z-direction. Finally, a
combination of the two 2D solutions is the basis for the 3D solution.
The ﬁrst approach investigates the problem in two dimensions vertically (2DV) and
therefore does not capture the spreading of the outgoing waves in the open sea on the
horizontal plane. The second approach assumes a horizontally uniform 2D ﬂow (2DH) and
describes decently the waves in the horizontal plane. The vertical variation is omitted by
assuming same depth everywhere. To account for the eﬀect of the sudden change of the
depth, correction factors have been introduced which yield reasonable results.

Chapter 3
Literature review
The interaction between water waves and oﬀshore structures is always a fascinating subject
of study. Over the years, the water waves scattering by both ﬁxed and ﬂoating structures of
various geometries and conﬁgurations have been studied thoroughly. In ocean and coastal
engineering, linear wave theory is often applied to describe the wave kinematics and dy-
namics with high accuracy. An extensive academic literature review has been carried out
on the ”dock” problems, primarily the wave interaction with a submerged horizontal ﬁnite
plate, and on wave induced oscillations in basins and harbors. This literature has been the
base of the present thesis and is described brieﬂy in the following sections. A synopsis of
an engineering study on the FPP WEC absorber resonant condition is also included.
3.1
WEC resonant frequency
The FPP WEC has been tested numerically and experimentally several times. Through
the tests, it has been found that the resonant condition of the WEC depends on its two
main conﬁgurations, i.e. the absorber and the chamber. The incoming wave enters into
the chamber forcing the free surface to oscillate, and this oscillation, in turn, forces the
absorber to pitch around its hinge. The pitch motion of the absorber with respect to the
period of the incoming wave, i.e. Pitch Response Amplitude Operator (RAO) xi5, is shown
in Figure 3.1 from [6]. This numerical result from WAMIT corresponds to a free ﬂoating
absorber in a ﬁxed scaled FPP chamber. The ﬁrst peak , near T = 1.1 sec, is related
with the absorber’s geometry and properties, i.e. shape, moments of inertia, while the
second peak, near T = 2.1 sec, is related to the chamber’s geometry. The geometry consists
primarily of the following dimensional parameters:
– the length ℓ,
– the width 2d,

3.2
Submerged horizontal plate
There have been many theoretical and numerical studies on the hydrodynamic behaviour
of ﬂoating, submerged and bottom-ﬁxed structures. An extensive range of these studies
includes ﬂoating, submerged plate and bottom obstacle problems, either ﬁnite or semi-
inﬁnite, known as the ”dock” problems. Especially, the wave interaction with a submerged
ﬁnite horizontal plate faces some similarities with the investigated mathematical problem of
the present thesis. From a 2DV perspective, the submerged ﬁnite horizontal plate becomes
identical with the problem deﬁned in chapter 2, if a vertical wall is added at the trailing
edge of the plate and we neglect the back-wall’s angle and gap with the free surface, see
Figure 2.1. A brief review of the important literature on the submerged ﬁnite horizontal
plate problem follows.
The earliest study was by Burke [26] using the Wiener-Hopf technique. Hattori [27]
investigated the submerged plate as a possible breakwater through laboratory experiments.
Siew and Hurley [10] were among the ﬁrst to solve the problem in shallow waters by applying
the method of matched asymptotic expansions and Schwarz-Christoﬀel mapping.
This
shallow waters approximation was used by Patarapanich [8], [11] to calculate reﬂection and
transmission coeﬃcients, as well as forces and moments on the plate with ﬁnite-element
technique. A major ﬁnding is that there exists an optimal submergence h′/h and plate
length ℓof the plate that minimizes the transmission to zero and maximize the reﬂection.
Yu and Chwang [28] and Zhang and Williams [29] studied wave scattering by a submerged
circular disk and found that wave focusing is possible in the lee-side of the disk, resulting in
a higher wave height compared to the incident wave height. This also agrees with WAMIT
simulations for the investigated chamber in the present thesis. An analytic study including
evanescent modes has been made by McIver [7] and followed by Yu et. al [30] and Liu
and Iskandarani [13], [14]. A modiﬁcation to the solution has been made by Nallayarasu
et al. [5], namely including the evanescent waves only in the upstream and downstream
region and excluding them from the region over and under the plate. The boundary integral
method has been used for the same problem by Liu and Abbaspour [31] and Yu et al. [32].
Yu et al. [33] presented also the breaking condition for waves over the plate. Yu and Dong
[34] showed that vortices are developed near the edges of the plate by solving numerically
the Navier Stokes equations. While the results imply the local invalidity of the linear wave
theory, further examination is needed to quantify the extent of the invalidity. Finally, Yu
et al. [22] made an extensive review on the problem of wave interaction with a submerged

3.3
Wave-induced oscillations in harbors
The literature review in the previous section aimed to provide knowledge on the hydro-
dynamic behaviour of the chamber from the 2DV perspective by disregarding the y-axis
variation of the ﬂow, or in other words by assuming inﬁnite width in the y-direction. This
section aims to give knowledge of the ﬂow from the 2DH point of view by disregarding the
vertical variation of the ﬂow or in other words by assuming constant depth in the chamber
and the open sea. This problem is similar to the wave interaction with harbors where the
same assumption applies. Of course, the engineering interest of studying the oscillations in
harbors is to minimize them to protect ﬂoating structures such as ships. To achieve this an
engineer designs the harbor in such a way to avoid resonant conditions inside. The interest
in the present thesis is the opposite, thus to provoke resonance inside the chamber maxi-
mizing the energy yield. This section describes brieﬂy the literature on the wave-induced
oscillations in harbors.
Many analytic, numerical and experimental studies have been carried out over the
years. The calculation of the resonant condition inside a a fully open rectangular harbor of
inﬁnitely small width is well known, often quoted as a ”one quarter wave length resonator”.
In the case of a wide harbor either fully or partially open, two dimensional eﬀects on the
free surface outside the harbor’s entrance take place. Especially in the case of a partially
open harbor two dimensional oscillations happen, both longitudinally and transversely. The
problem of a narrow harbor with a small opening has been investigated by Miles and Munk
[35] by using a point source method, but lacking experimental evidence. Kravtchenco and
McNown [36] studied the oscillations inside a rectangular harbor, however he could not
predict the inﬂuence of varying width, since he did not treated the outgoing waves from the
harbor entrance to the open sea correctly. Ippen and Goda [4] pointed out the signiﬁcance
of the outgoing waves and derived a semi-analytic solution for both fully and partially open
rectangular harbors. In that analysis, they evaluated the outgoing waves using the Fourier
transform method after superimposing them with the standing wave (they assume inﬁnite
horizontal walls along the y-axis) outside the harbor and then matching the elevation and
the horizontal velocity of the superimposed waves with the waves inside the harbor.
The Ippen and Goda [4] solution is one of the cornerstones of the analysis in the present
thesis. In the case of the chamber, the standing wave outside the harbor is turned to just
an incident wave and the solution is obtained in the same manner. The diﬀraction of the
outgoing waves around the chamber is negligible and is ignored for the solution.
This
point is motivated in section 7.3.1 by an analysis based on Penny and Price [23], which
is a modiﬁed solution for the diﬀraction of water waves of the Sommerfeld [37] solution of
optical diﬀraction.

Chapter 4
Linear wave theory
In the present chapter the linear wave theory is described and the assumptions made are
discussed. The theory, also known as Airy wave theory [38] or Stokes ﬁrst order theory
[39], applies for an incompressible and inviscid ﬂuid and irrotational ﬂuid motion. The
linear theory is also, strictly speaking for non-breaking waves with small wave height H
compared the wavelength L.
Either in deep or shallow waters, where H/L < 1/7 or
H/h << 1 respectively, the non-linear surface boundary conditions can be linearized. The
linearization of the problem is considered out of the scope of the present project, thus it is
omitted.

Chapter 7
2DH chamber
In this chapter the problem is analyzed in the 2 dimensional horizontal plane (2DH). The
vertical variation of the potential is disregarded by assuming constant water depth over the
whole region under consideration, i.e. both inside and outside the chamber, as shown in
Figure 7.1 .
The problem is treated in a similar manner as Ippen & Goda [4] did for a rectangular
harbor. However, there are two aspects that diﬀer between [4] and the following analysis.
The ﬁrst is that the chamber is fully open to the incident waves, instead of a partially open
harbor. Inside the fully open chamber only longitudinal oscillations take place, instead of a
partially open where transverse oscillations are induced too. The second is that the incident
plain waves are travelling and diﬀracted around and in the lee of the chamber, in contrast
with [4] where total reﬂection of the waves is considered,by assuming inﬁnitely long walls
along the y-axis.
Figure 7.1: Deﬁnition sketch on the two dimensional horizontal (2DH) plane.

From a physical a point of view, normal plain waves are travelling from the open sea
to the chamber’s entrance. One part goes through the entrance and reaches the back wall
where is reﬂected back to the the entrance forming a standing wave. Then it is transmitted
and spread out to the open sea. The remaining part travels around, then in the lee of
the chamber and ﬁnally to inﬁnity. Mathematically, the wave motion is described by three
potentials, one for the incident wave, one for the standing wave inside the chamber and one
for the outgoing wave from the chamber’s entrance to the open sea. The outgoing wave is
the only one that varies along the y-axis, since it is diﬀracted around the chamber’s side
walls ends. It is assumed that the outgoing wave diﬀracts only in the half horizontal plane,
i.e. for x ≤0, and then dies away as it travels to negative inﬁnity. The validity of this
assumption is described in section 7.3.1.

Chapter 9
Summary, conclusions & future work
The main motivation for this study was the hydrodynamic modelling and optimization of
the Wave Energy Converters of the Floating Power Plant.
9.1
Summary & conclusions
The eﬃciency of the WECs depends on several aspects. One of the primary is the pitch
motion of the absorber around its hinge and it needs to be maximized. A previous project
in FPP has pointed out that this motion is directly related with the surface elevation inside
the chamber. Thus, to achieve maximum pitch motion and yield energy production, the
internal wave amplitude should be ampliﬁed. This can be performed by accomplishing
resonance of the internal wave. The resonance of this wave depends on the dimensional
parameters of the chamber and the frequency of the dominant wave. These parameters
are the length, the width, the draft, the inclination, the gap and the water depth. The
objectives of the present thesis have been limited to:
– providing knowledge on the relation between the dimensional characteristics of one
individual chamber with the resonant frequency of the internal free surface elevation
– the development of an analytic or semi-analytic model to describe the free surface
elevation inside an individual chamber in the frequency domain
To achieve the ﬁrst goal, an extensive academic literature review has been carried
out on wave interaction with similar geometries. This literature, based mostly on linear
wave theory, provided knowledge and deep understanding in the ﬂow mechanism around
submerged and bottom ﬁxed structures, both physically and mathematically. Speciﬁcally,
the scattering of surfaces waves from a submerged horizontal plate and the wave induced
oscillations in rectangular harbors have been the primary topics of the literature review.
The solutions for these two problems were the cornerstones of the present studies.
The second goal was intriguing and challenging. For the development of a mathematical
model describing the ﬂow in three dimensional domain, a few simpliﬁcations were needed.
63
==PAGE:78==

The shallow water approximation has been considered by assuming uniform horizontal ﬂow.
The 3D problem remains still complex, so a simpler approach has been followed. Firstly, the
3D problem has been separated in two 2D problems, from the horizontal and vertical plane.
Then, a combination of the two 2D solution was attempted to provide the 3D solution.
The geometry of the FPP chamber has similarities with both the submerged horizontal
plate and a rectangular harbor. If the submerged horizontal plate (Figure 5.1) is extended
to include a vertical wall in the trailing edge, it becomes to the FPP chamber geometry
(Figure 6.1) from the perspective of the two dimensional vertical plane. This is true if
the angle of the FPP chamber’s back-wall and the gap between the back-wall and the free
surface are zero. This approach omits any variation of the ﬂow in the y-direction.
Similarly, if any geometrical conﬁgurations next to a harbor, i.e.
fully or partially
reﬂecting boundaries, are omitted, it becomes to the FPP chamber geometry (Figure 7.1)
from the perspective of the two dimensional horizontal plane. Again this is true if the
back-wall’s inclination and the gap are disregarded. This approach omits any variation of
the ﬂow in the z-direction.
The impact of the inclination and the gap on the resonant frequency of the trapped
wave inside the chamber was implemented with correction factors in the ﬁnal semi-analytic
solution.
The FPP chamber problem has been deﬁned two dimensionally in the two planes.
However, any three dimensional eﬀects of the ﬂow will not be captured this way. Thus, a
solution is needed for the wave interaction with the 3D FPP geometry (ﬁgures 8.1 and 8.2).
The conﬁguration of the potentials for the 3D domain originate from the mathematical
description of the 2DH and 2DV problems.
Hence, this is the start of the mathematical solution of the FPP chamber. The steps
to the 3D solution are in sequence:
– the analytic solution of the 2DV submerged horizontal plate
– the analytic solution of the 2DV chamber
– the analytic solution of the 2DH chamber
– the analytic solution of the 3D chamber
– the semi-analytic solution of the 3D chamber
The ﬁrst solution has been validated with results from literature. The second solution
refers to a not common mathematical problem and no validation has been performed,
since no literature has been found on this. The third problem is again not a common one,
however the result has been compared with numerical calculations performed with WAMIT.
The accuracy was reasonable for the wave amplitude inside the chamber and good for the
64
==PAGE:79==

estimation of the resonant frequency. The comparison for the third solution, which is the
desirable one, has not provided satisfactory results. The simpliﬁcations that have been
used seem to be the reasons behind the poor accuracy. The shallow waters approximation
neglects the evanescent waves from the solution. The eﬀect of the evanescent waves with
sudden changes of the water depth is not negligible. The potential of the pressure driven
ﬂow under the chamber has been considered as the combination of a constant potential
and a potential term linearly varying in the x-direction. This mathematical description
has been suggested from literature as suﬃcient for 2D ﬂows, but remains unknown to the
author if it is valid for 3D ﬂows. Thus, this also may be a reason for inaccuracy. In the ﬁnal
semi-analytic solution, correction factors have been implemented to predict accurately the
resonant frequency. The correcting equations include the length, the width, the draft, the
inclination and the gap and were based on various numerical calculations. Variation of the
water depth is still not included in the correcting equations. Finally, the accuracy of the
semi-analytic model is tested for the scaled 3D FPP chamber. The model underestimated
the wave amplitude, however the estimation of the resonant frequency was fruitful, see
Figure 8.9.
The ﬁndings of the 3D semi-analytic solution and the thesis, in general, are summarized
below:
– The incoming wave is partially transmitted in the chamber, partially below and and
partially reﬂected back the the open sea. The transmitted wave inside the chamber
reaches the back-wall, where is again partially transmitted in the lee of the chamber
and partially reﬂected back to the entrance. When it reaches the entrance is partially
transmitted outside of the chamber’s entrance and partially reﬂected to the back-
wall. Simultaneously, more incoming waves reach the chamber and the same process
is repeated. The superposition of this inﬁnite number of waves inside the chamber is
the physical mechanism that ampliﬁes the wave amplitude,
– The ﬂow under the plate is a pressure driven ﬂow with approximately constant hori-
zontal velocity. Further investigation is needed for 3D domain,
– The outgoing waves from the chamber’s entrance to the open sea behave like radiation
waves of an oscillating system and present a circular surface pattern,
– The ampliﬁcation of the wave magnitude inside the chamber depends on several di-
mensional properties of the chamber, i.e. length, width, draft, gap, inclination and
water depth,
– The contribution of the full reﬂection from the back-wall is signiﬁcant.
The gap
between the back-wall and the free surface acts negatively in the trapping of the
waves. Even a small gap provokes high energy loss of the system,

– The length of the chamber has the highest inﬂuence on the system’s resonance. In-
crease in the length show also multiple resonant frequencies,
– The width acts negatively to the trapping of energy. The wider the chamber’s entrance
is the less the ampliﬁcation,
– The thinner the chamber, i.e ratio of length over width, the higher the ampliﬁcation.
However, the waves are going to break after a particular increase of this ratio. This
cannot be captured with linear wave theory, since inﬁnite ratio leads to inﬁnite wave
amplitude.

9.2
Future work
Some recommendations for further studies in the area touched upon in this thesis are
provided here.
Firstly, the analytic 3D model in chapter 8 should be compared with numerical calcu-
lations in shallow waters. A similar calibration could be done, as was done for the model
in section 8.4.
The semi-analytic model has been calibrated for the dimensional parameters of the
chamber apart from the water depth. The model has been tested only for one case, i.e.
the scaled FPP chamber. More validation should be carried out to provide more conﬁ-
dence. Finally, since the velocity potential now is known, moments and forces can also be
computed.
A solution based on the matched eigenfunctions method, i.e. including the evanescent
waves, will provide better results.
It has been highlighted in academic literature, that high surface disturbance happens
in the lee of similar geometries, according to linear wave theory. This should be further
investigated.
66
==PAGE:81==
CHAPTER 9. SUMMARY, CONCLUSIONS & FUTURE WORK
Multiple chambers may aﬀect the resonant condition of each others. It has been con-
cluded that the slope of the outgoing waves along the axis of the entrance converges to zero
near the chamber, however not very close to it. Mass ﬂux from the outgoing waves from
one chamber’s entrance may enter to an adjacent.
The impact of the properties of the absorber, i.e. moments of inertia and stiﬀness, on
the resonant frequency of the pitch motion should be investigated with varying properties
of the chamber’s properties.
The linear wave theory, although powerful, shows certain limits. The dimensionalization
of the chamber seems to be optimum for inﬁnitely thin conﬁguration, i.e. 2D oscillation.
However, this is not true. The dissipation of energy at the thin entrance would be high and
the wave would also break. These eﬀects cannot be captured with linear wave theory. The
use of CFD numerical models would be useful.
Finally, if particular constrains limit the magnitude of the dimensional parameters of
the chamber, either from construction-practical reasons or the limit of the linear wave
applicability (inﬁnite thin conﬁguration is not a real scenario), an optimization tool could
be developed. This tool could have as input the characteristics of the dominant wave and
the mathematical tool developed in this thesis. The output would be the ﬁnal optimum
dimensional parameters of the FPP WEC chamber.
