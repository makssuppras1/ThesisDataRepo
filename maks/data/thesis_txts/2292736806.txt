Abstract
Anti-agglomerants (AAs) belong to the category of low-dosage hydrate inhibitors that
are used in order to manage the formation and deposition of natural gas hydrates in oil
and gas production and transportation lines. AAs are surface-active agents that allow
hydrates to form but prevent them from agglomerating into larger hydrate particles. In
particular, high pressure, low temperature zones like the Gulf of Mexico have turned
out to be great application areas for anti-agglomerants, as they can operate eﬀectively
at a high subcooling. However, anti-agglomerants tend to be relatively toxic with low
biodegradability potential and are therefore strictly regulated in the North Sea. Research
is therefore ongoing in order to develop “green” alternatives including AAs of biological
origin that show suﬃcient anti-agglomerating properties that make them suitable for use
in commercial application, as well as high biodegradability potential.
The purpose of this study was to investigate the performance of two commercially-
available anti-agglomerants, the quaternary ammonium salts (QUATs) BHDP-Br and
BMA-Cl, and compare the eﬀects with a new-generation “green” biosurfactant, Surfactin.
The anti-agglomeration performance was assessed in terms of emulsion stability, anti-
agglomeration eﬀect and kinetic hydrate inhibition potential. An optical analyser was
employed to investigate the diﬀerent compounds’ emulsion stability in a water-heptane
system. In order to account for the changing salinity in real systems, the experiments
were conducted at three diﬀerent salinities (0.0, 3.5 and 7.0 wt.% NaCl). In a salt-free
environment, BMA-Cl produced the most stable emulsions of the three tested AAs, while
in the presence of salt, BHDP-Br enhanced the emulsion stability signiﬁcantly, producing
the most stable emulsions of the three AAs. In comparison to the QUATs, the emul-
sifying properties of Surfactin were rather poor and the emulsion stability times were
signiﬁcantly shorter.
The anti-agglomeration eﬀect was measured at elevated pressures and low temperatures
in an autoclave stirred tank (the Shell Cell).
The results were assessed in terms of
the recorded pressure drop during hydrate formation, and visual observations during
the experiments. BHDP-Br had the best anti-agglomerating properties and generated a
smooth homogeneous hydrate slurry. BMA-Cl also showed reasonable anti-agglomeration
properties, although some agglomeration did occur. However, BMA-Cl fully prevented
hydrates from forming in the presence of salt. It was therefore concluded that BMA-
Cl also has a kinetic hydrate inhibition potential. Surfactin showed little to no anti-
agglomeration potential, as the hydrate particles easily agglomerated into larger particles
and formed solid hydrate plugs. However, the experiments revealed that Surfactin has a
hydrate growth inhibiting potential as it reduced the amount of hydrates formed.

Part I.
Introduction
Gas hydrates are ice-like solids composed of water clathrate cage structures and enclosed
suitably-sized gas molecules. Gas hydrates occur where pressure, temperature, gas satu-
ration and local chemical conditions combine to make them stable (Dembiras 2010 pp.77;
Gao 2009), typically in subsea hydrocarbon production, transport and processing facilities
which are associated with elevated operational pressures and low temperatures.
Oﬀshore production of oil and gas faces several issues within ﬂow assurance. In partic-
ular, formation and deposition of gas hydrates have turned out to be a major challenge
in deep water operations and hydrocarbon recovery in arctic environments, as the opera-
tional conditions favour hydrate growth (Sun & Firoozabadi 2014). Uncontrolled hydrate
formation and growth have the potential to block ﬂow lines, valves and wellheads, causing
loss of production and serious safety and environmental issues (Sun & Firoozabadi 2014;
Kelland 2006).
Conventional methods of hydrate management usually refer to ﬂow line insulation and
application of thermodynamic hydrate inhibitors such as glycols and methanol.
The
thermodynamic inhibitors shift the hydrate equilibrium towards lower temperatures and
higher pressures so that the operational conditions are outside the hydrate stable re-
gion (Gao 2009; Daraboina et al. 2015b). Thermodynamic hydrate inhibition typically
requires inhibitor volumes in a range between 20 wt.% and 50 wt.% of the production
liquid’s aqueous phase. The range is relatively wide as the required amount strongly
depends upon the ﬂow’s composition in terms of salt and paraﬃns, as these components
are known to be natural hydrate inhibitors (Daraboina et al. 2015b).
Low dosage hydrate inhibitors have gained attention during the past twenty years as
they allow hydrate control at active concentrations of < 3 wt.% . This enables a wide
range of operational and capital expenditure savings that might contribute to making
new discoveries proﬁtable, in addition to extending the lifetime of existing ﬁelds. Low
dosage hydrate inhibitors are usually subdivided into two diﬀerent classes; kinetic hydrate
inhibitors and anti-agglomerants (Kelland 2006).
The kinetic hydrate inhibitors typically consist of water-soluble polymers that delay hy-
drate formation and growth for a given period of time. Despite the low concentrations,
kinetic hydrate inhibitors often suﬀer from environmental regulations due to their poor
biodegradability potential.
Recent studies have therefore been conducted on various
“green” components extracted from biological sources (Kelland 2006).
The anti-agglomerants are surface active agents that allow hydrates to form but keep
1
==PAGE:21==
the particles small and dispersed in the liquid hydrocarbon phase, so that they do not
agglomerate into hydrate plugs (Gao 2009). In comparison to kinetic hydrate inhibitors,
the anti-agglomerants are found to perform better at a higher degree of subcooling (>
10oC). Unfortunately, this group also suﬀers from strict regulations in terms of toxicity
and biodegradation. In addition, anti-agglomerants are not recommended for applications
at water cuts above 50%, as the hydrate slurry becomes too viscous to transport (Kelland
2006; Sun & Firoozabadi 2014).
The purpose of this study is to compare the eﬀect of two commercially-available, highly
toxic anti-agglomerants (BHDP-Br and BMA-Cl) with a new “green” anti-agglomerant,
Surfactin. The experiments were conducted in an autoclave stirred tank (Shell Cell) with
operational conditions close to those seen in typical in-situ oﬀshore operations. The anti-
agglomerants were tested in a model oil system at diﬀerent salinities and were assessed in
terms of the experimental results of their emulsifying properties, emulsion stability, onset
of hydrate formation, hydrate growth development and anti-agglomerating properties.

1. Fundamentals of gas hydrates
Gas hydrates occur naturally in the arctic permafrost and subsea at great depth and
extreme conditions (Demirbas 2010 pp. v-iv). They are ice-like clathrate solids that
form from water and light hydrocarbons at low temperatures and elevated pressures
(Kelland 2006; Koh 2002).
In fact, gas hydrates represent one of the world’s largest
unused reservoirs of fossil fuels, and estimates suggest that gas hydrate deposits have the
potential to supply the Earth with energy for the next 1000 years (Demirbas 2010 pp.
v-iv). In addition, gas hydrates also occur during all phases of hydrocarbon recovery,
due to harsh subsea environments and elevated pressures. With regards to production,
gas hydrates represent a huge risk in terms of pipeline and equipment blockage (Kelland
2006).
Figure 1.1: Cage structure of natural gas hydrate (Demirbas 2010 pp. 86)
The energy and petroleum industry (E&P) therefore has huge incentive to increase un-
derstanding of natural gas hydrates through research, in terms of both risk and cost
reduction. In addition, increased understanding will allow the industry to recover the
hydrate reservoirs in a sustainable way (Kelland 2006).

1.1. Composition
Gas hydrates are classiﬁed as a special type of inclusion compound. Inclusion compounds
typically consists of two molecular species. One functions as the “host” and the other as
the “guest” (Englezos 1993).
For gas hydrates, the host component is usually water,
and contributes to 85% of the structure by formation of polyhedral cavities (Englezos
1993; Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 45). Moreover, the polyhedral cavities are occupied by
the guest, typically a hydrocarbon component of low carbon number. In fact, the host
and the guest exist in a sort of chemical symbiosis, providing each other support in
terms of long-range hydrogen bonds and short-range repulsive van der Waal forces (Koh
2002; Englezos 1993). This results in a crystalline structure similar to normal ice, but
with diﬀerent thermodynamical properties, allowing a stable solid phase at temperatures
where regular ice dissociates (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 45-46).
As a rule of thumb, the majority of hydrate-forming guest molecules are hydrophobic
compounds, which fall within the ﬁrst of Jeﬀrey and McMullan’s (1967) four proposed
guest molecule groups: (1) Hydrophobic compounds (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 72). In
addition, it is also known that carbon dioxide (CO2) and hydrogen sulﬁde (H2S) are
hydrate-forming guests.
They are both water-soluble acid gases, hence belonging to
Jeﬀrey and McMullan’s (1967) second group of guest molecules, (2) Water-soluble acid
gases (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp.72). Jeﬀrey (1984) summarised a guest molecule’s required
properties in terms of two criteria, one dimensional and one functional (Jeﬀrey 1984);
1. The guest must have a dimension ensuring a “comfortable” ﬁt inside a cavity.
2. The guest cannot have either a single strong hydrogen bonding group or a number
of moderately strong hydrogen bonding groups.
In accordance with Jeﬀrey’s (1984) two criteria, it is known that diﬀerent hydrophobic
components form diﬀerent hydrate structures (Jeﬀrey 1984). In fact, this becomes very
obvious due to the ﬁrst, dimension-dependent criterion. X-ray crystallography allows
us to investigate the hydrate structures at a molecular level (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp.
349; Englezos 1993). It has been observed that the smallest hydrocarbons i.e. methane
(CH4) and ethane (C2H6) form a diﬀerent hydrate structure (SI ) than the larger propane
(C3H8) molecule (SII ) (Englezos 1993; Koh 2002). In addition, the more complex hydrate
structure H (SH ), is capable of housing even larger molecules than propane, such as
butane, various isomers of pentane and neo-hexane, at the same time as it hosts small
methane molecules. This is due the varying-sized cavities of the structure (Koh 2002;
Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 45; Ballard & Sloan 2002).
The thermodynamic conditions for hydrate equilibrium are strongly dependent on the
gas composition. Addition of medium sized gas components, even in small amounts, may

1.3. Hydrate formation and dissociation processes
This section deals with the fundamental thermodynamics and kinetics behind the stochas-
tic process of hydrate formation, from the initial stage of nucleation to the ﬁnal stage
of hydrate decomposition. Hydrates occur under conditions allowing water to dissolve
more gas than at equilibrium solubility. Such a phenomenon is called supersaturation,
and is known to be part of the initial nucleation stage, recognisable by a small pressure
drop, due to gas consumption. Subsequently, the nucleation stage is followed by a more
aggressive gas consumption in the growth period, which is where the majority of the
hydrates are formed. Finally, gas hydrates are found to dissociate at diﬀerent conditions
(higher temperatures) than normal ice, due to the presence of their “guests”, which apply
extra molecular forces to the system (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 113-181).
1.3.1. Nucleation
The conceptual process of hydrate nucleation is a microscopic, fully stochastic phe-
nomenon taking place in the liquid-vapour interface at temperatures and pressures out-
side the solution’s stable region (Jensen et al. 2008). This meta-stable state allows the
solution to absorb more gas in the aqueous phase than under normal thermodynamic
conditions (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 126-130). This phenomenon is called supersatura-
tion and typically provokes labile hydrogen bonds to cluster and grow to a critical size,
rc (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 126-130). At the critical size rc, the clusters are within an
energetically-favourable interval which results in a spontaneous growth phase, producing
stable hydrate crystals (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 126-130).

1.3.2. Growth
Labile clusters of critical size rc reach an energetically-favourable state. This condition
encourages further crystal growth on the macroscopical level, rather than dissociation
(Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 126-130). During experiments carried out in pressurised vessels,
the crystal growth stage in the process of hydrate formation may be detected as an interval
of increased gas consumption, and subsequently a major pressure drop (the B-C interval
from the ﬁgure in the previous subsection of nucleation) (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 166).
However, as the labile clusters from the nucleation stage tend to be stable in the growth
stage, experiments have shown that the key factors of nucleation (degree of subcooling,
surface area, rate of agitation, water history and gas composition) remain important,
even though mass and heat transfer become of major importance in this stage (Sloan &
Koh 2008 pp. 150).
The molecular concepts of crystal growth starts with labile clusters attracted to a crystal
surface induced by a lower Gibbs free energy at this point. During adsorption to the
crystal surface, some of the clusters’ water molecules detach and diﬀuse away.
Even
though the cluster is now attached to the crystal surface, it is still free to move in two
dimensions along the crystal. Eventually, the clusters will hit an edge perpendicular to
the initial surface, or a so-called “step”. This step restricts mobility in one additional
dimension, resulting in a cluster that is only able to move along this step. Such steps
are full of “kinks”, or crystal structural defects, that will entrap the cluster and restrict
all remaining movement. This process is repeated over and over again, every time a new
cluster attaches to the crystal surface, resulting in a growing crystal (Sloan & Koh 2008
pp. 150-151).
In order to model crystal growth, three major correlations have been developed through
empirical testing. Englezos et al. (1987) proposed a model accounting for the intrin-
sic kinetics during crystal growth. However, during recent studies this model has been
evaluated and found to be less important than Skovborg & Rasmussen’s (1993) mass
transfer theory of restrictions in gas passing through the liquid ﬁlm at the gas liquid in-
terface (Skovborg & Rasmussen 1993; Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 171). The ﬁnal correlation is
Uchida et al.’s (1999) heat transfer approach, which involves hydrates forming only at the
front of the hydrate ﬁlm, which is maintained at the three-phase (water-guest-hydrate)
equilibrium temperature. This correlation assumes that heat removed from the front is
balanced with heat generated by hydrate formation (exothermic process) (Uchida et al.
1999; Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 172).
State of the art hydrate growth theory may therefore be thought of as apparatus and
component dependent due to the fact that most empirical experiments have been carried
out on systems forming hydrate structure SI in pressurised reactors, while the practical

1.3.3. Dissociation
Several methods have been developed to prevent hydrate formation; externally heated
and/or insulated pipelines, and diﬀerent hydrate inhibitors. However, following hydrate
formation, hydrate dissociation processes are absolutely essential in order to remove hy-
drate plugs blocking pipelines (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 176). The dissociation process is
endothermic which means that external heat is required in order to transform the hy-
drate crystal lattice to vapour and liquid. In fact, the change in enthalpy, ∆H, has to
break both intermolecular hydrogen bonds between the water molecules, in addition to
van der Waals forces between water and guest molecules (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 176).
The additional van der Waals forces are the main diﬀerence between gas hydrates and
normal ice, and also explain why hydrates dissociate at higher temperatures than normal
ice (Bagherzadeh et al. 2013). Apart from external application of heat, depressurisation
has proven to be an eﬀective hydrate plug remover (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 176; Ji et al.
2001), as has injection of thermodynamic hydrate inhibitors (THI). The THIs are able to
fully prevent hydrate formation, as well as to dissociate an already-formed hydrate plug
as they are highly volatile and penetrate the hydrate lattice, changing its thermodynamic
equilibrium so that it dissociates (Kelland 2006). When testing new chemical additives
with the purpose of preventing or delaying hydrate formation, it is also important to test
the hydrate’s dissociation properties with the chemical additives in place. This is because
some of the compounds which actually work well as hydrate inhibitors tend to reinforce
the hydrate lattice when it ﬁrst forms, resulting in the hydrate plug being even more
diﬃcult to dissociate than hydrate plugs without any inhibitors (Daraboina et al. 2011,
2013, 2015).
1.4. Formation conditions
Several factors have to be in place for hydrates to form: (1) Thermodynamically-favourable
conditions, of low temperatures (2 - 12 oC) and elevated pressures (25 - 110 bar), (2)
Hydrate-forming gas dissolved in liquid water, and (3) The system has to be capable of
eﬀectively removing excess heat, due to the fact that hydrate formation is an exothermic
process (Makogon et al. 2000; Koh 2002). In addition to the three major factors men-
tioned above, a multicomponent gas, presence of a liquid hydrocarbon phase, salinity,
particle concentration, water history (memory eﬀect) and agitation also play an impor-
tant role in the process (Makogon 2000). Some of the factors function as a catalyst, while
others inhibit the formation process. It is therefore necessary to account for as many of

is commonly referred to as the “memory” eﬀect, and involves the hydrate structure being
preserved in the liquid water state (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 149). The concept of the mem-
ory eﬀect is not yet fully understood, but several conceptual theories have been proposed

The formation of gas hydrates is known to generate additional costs for a project in terms

A pipeline blockage leads to downtime

Currently, two commercial methods of gas hydrate inhibition are in use. The ﬁrst and

• Inhibition and dissociation eﬀect
• Transportation
• Storage on platform

in terms of the compounds’ level of toxicity and biodegradability (OECD 1992a). Every

Alcohols in the homologous series from methanol to butanol, tend to form short-lived
hydrogen bonds between the alcohol’s hydroxyl group and the water molecules. Sub-
sequently, these short-lived hydrogen bonds compete with the existing inter-molecular
hydrogen bonds initially binding the water molecules. The more water molecules that
are bonded with the hydroxyl group, the better the inhibiting eﬀect, as the alcohol then
prevents potential hydrate cavities forming between the water molecules. Conversely, the
alcohol’s hydrocarbon end tends to cause a clustering eﬀect amongst the water molecules,
similar to the eﬀect of hydrate formers. This means that alcohols actually have a dual
inhibiting eﬀect on hydrate formation, competing with the hydrate for both guest and
host molecules (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 231-232).
Glycols have an inhibiting eﬀect that is very similar to alcohols, since the glycols also form
short-lived hydrogen bonds with the water molecules. However, the glycols provide more
hydrogen bonding opportunities with the water as they contain an additional hydroxyl
group (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 231-232).
The reason why alcohols and glycols are called thermodynamic hydrate inhibitors is that
they actually change the hydrate-forming interval, in terms of pressure and temperature,
requiring lower temperatures and higher pressures in order for hydrates to form.

Based on the fact that one THI molecule usually only bonds with one water molecule,
large amounts of thermodynamic hydrate inhibitors are required in order to achieve the
desired inhibiting eﬀect in the ﬂowline. In fact, an amount between 10 wt.% - 60 wt.% of
the water phase is often required to obtain this inhibiting eﬀect. The required amount of
THI increases with increasing water cut, which means that the costs of hydrate inhibition
increase with increasing water cut.
The use of thermodynamic hydrate inhibitors is therefore considered extremely costly,
both in terms of the purchase of the inhibitor and associated infrastructure required in
order to maintain safe and continuous operations.
Methanol shows a slightly better inhibiting eﬀect than mono-ethylene glycol (MEG) due
to its high volatility and solubility. Despite this, MEG is currently the preferred THI,
because of methanol’s high level of solubility, toxicity and ﬂammability which makes it
more expensive to handle (Paez et al. 2001). Conversely, due to MEG0s higher molecular
weight and lower solubility the compound is easier to recover and recycle. The higher
degree of recoverability contributes to reducing the already extremely-high OPEX of
hydrate inhibition (Paez et al. 2001).

2.2. Low dosage hydrate inhibition
High operational expenditure (OPEX) and capital expenditure (CAPEX), in addition
to both safety and toxicity concerns with regards to the traditional method of thermo-
dynamic hydrate inhibition have encouraged research on alternative methods of hydrate
control (Kanu et al.
2014).
Low Dosage Hydrate Inhibitors (LDHI) have been re-
searched and developed over the past 20 years as an alternative method to control gas
hydrates (Kelland 2006). The LDHIs major advantage is that they have the same degree
of eﬃciency as the THIs at a signiﬁcantly lower concentration of active compound, for
example < 3 wt% (York & Firoozabadi 2008; Kelland 2006). A reduction in usage of
hydrate inhibitor from up to 60 wt% to less than 3 wt% covers two of Szymczak et al.’s
(2006) essential selection parameters; i.e. transportation and oﬀshore storage.
The group of low dosage hydrate inhibitors are further divided into two subgroups based
on their speciﬁc inhibition mechanism (Kelland 2006):
• Kinetic Hydrate Inhibitors (KHIs)
• Anti-Agglomerants (AAs)
Kinetic hydrate inhibitors do not change the hydrate phase equilibrium like the THIs.
Instead they aﬀect the time-dependent kinetics in the hydrate formation process (Koltveit
2014). The use of LDHIs delays the initial nucleation process and retard growth and
agglomeration of early-developed crystals. A suﬃcient delay interval will ensure the ﬂow
through critical hydrate formation zones, during shut-downs and restarts of operations
(Paez et al.
2001).
Extended ﬂuid residence time during shut-downs may cause the
temperature to drop, in addition to sudden increases in ﬂuid velocities and pressures
during start-up which can provoke hydrate formation. It is therefore desirable to plan
the shut-down properly so that hydrate inhibitors can be injected and prevent ﬂowline
blockage and equipment damage (Paez et al. 2001).
In order to express the LDHI’s eﬃciency, the term subcooling (∆Tsub) has to be intro-
duced. Subcooling is basically the diﬀerence between the mixture’s equilibrium temper-
ature (Teq) and the operational temperature (Top).

2.2.3. Composition
2.2.3.1. Quaternary ammonium salts
In the development of anti-agglomerating compounds, quaternary ammonium salts (QUAT)
have shown extremely good results (Kelland 2006). These are cationic surfactants espe-
cially tailored by The Royal Dutch Shell and consist of a hydrate-seeking headgroup
(hydrate-philic head) and a hydrophobic tail (Kelland et al. 2006; Erfani et al. 2013).
During operations, the QUATs accumulate in the oil-water interface, where hydrates ﬁrst
occur. The headgroup consists of a quaternary center which is attracted to the hydrate’s
surface. In addition, the headgroup also often contains a butyl/pentyl group that pene-
trates empty 51264 cavities (Kelland 2006). The long hydrophobic tail prevents additional
growth of the hydrate, in addition to making the surface more attractive to the hydro-
carbon phase. The surfactant’s quaternary ammonium headgroup is also known to be
attracted to pipeline walls, so the hydrophobic tail prevents contact between the pipeline
wall and the water, which turns out to reduce corrosion. Quaternary ammonium salts
have therefore turned out to be both an eﬀective AA as well as an eﬀective corrosion
inhibitor (Kelland 2006).
2.2.3.2. Zwitterions
With respect to the quaternary ammonium salts’ anti-agglomerating eﬀect, research has
been made on more environmental-friendly zwitterionic surfactants (Kelland 2006). The
idea was that these surfactants could potentially provide a similar AA eﬀect by incorpo-
rating the anion from a quaternary ammonium salt into the structure of a new surfactant,
leaving the new structure both with an anion and a cation which was believed to make it
more environmentally friendly. However, experiments revealed that only the anion part
was eﬀective, limiting the expected environmental advantages (Erfani et al. 2013).
2.2.3.3. Biosurfactants
The degree of toxicity is still considered the main bottleneck for anti-agglomerants, and
they have application approval in relatively few places around the world. However, as
the potential application areas are wide, anti-agglomerants are still considered a hot
topic of research. Currently, a new type of surfactants are being tested for application;
biosurfactants. These compounds are surface-active agents produced by microbial activity
(Borgund 2007).

Emulsion stability has turned out to be an important part of the criteria that decides
whether an anti-agglomerant is suited for application or not. York & Firoozabadi (2008)
present a method of formally testing emulsion stability, called the “bottle test”.
The bottle test is conducted by preparing samples of water, oil and anti-agglomerant in
transparent test tubes. The samples are then homogenised by shaking the test tube by
hand. Subsequently, the time it takes for 60 vol.% of the initial aqueous phase to break
out of the emulsion is recorded and the experiment repeated three times.
2.3. The impact of sodium chloride (NaCl)
Observations have proven that salt has an inhibiting eﬀect on hydrate formation (Sloan
& Koh 2008 pp. 234). Salts dissolve and ionise in solution with water. The salt ions
interact with the dipolar water molecule, creating a Coulombic bond stronger than both
the hydrogen bonds and the van der Waal forces usually present around the apolar “guest”
molecule causing the clustering eﬀect. As a rule of thumb, water is more attracted to
ions than towards a hydrate structure. Initial laboratory testing of gas hydrate inhibitors
is therefore conducted with deionised water (DI-water), in order to avoid additional
reactions between the hydrate inhibitor and the dissolved salts in ionised water.
In addition, the clustering eﬀect caused by the presence of ions also reduces the hydrate
guest molecule’s solubility in water. This additional observation is usually called the
“salting-out” eﬀect. Qi et al. (2012) report that salt may also have an impact on the
growth process, in terms of a hydration phenomenon close to the crystal surface, which
is believed to retard growth (Koltveit 2014; Qi et al. 2012).
After investigating the eﬀect of diﬀerent hydrate inhibitors in DI-water, the next step is
to test them in saline water. As mentioned above, salt has a documented inhibiting eﬀect
on hydrate formation. However, salt may also interfere with the hydrate inhibitor. It is
extremely important to examine salt’s impact on new hydrate inhibitors as production
water in subsea pipelines has a salinity ranging from normal average saline seawater
(3.5wt%) to high-saline formation porewater, with a salinity of up to 30% (Anderson
2003; Bjørlykke & Gran 1994).

3. Oil ﬁeld chemicals, ecotoxilogical testing and
chemical categorisation
In order to declare new chemicals ready for oﬀshore applications, they are required to
undergo an environmental assessment. Chemicals composed of several components must
complete an individual substance test in addition to biodegradability testing, bioaccu-
mulation testing and acute toxicity testing, in order to be declared ready for applica-
tion (Petroleum Safety Authority Norway 2015). Norway is considered to be one of the
strictest nations with respect to restrictions on oil ﬁeld chemicals.
However, in light
of recent accidents within the petroleum sector, more and more nations are following
Norway’s example, requesting a chemical assessment in terms of;
• chemical safety
• sustainability
• measurable environmental impact
• environmentally friendliness (Jordan et al. 2010; Ornelas et al. 2014).
3.1. Biodegradability testing
Biodegradability is the natural environment’s ability to decompose products into organic
parts. Depending on the product’s composition, decomposition will occur at diﬀerent
rates. Some products are fully decomposed within days, while others last for years (Atha-
lye 2012). Biodegradability should only be measured in accordance with the OECD 306
standard, which involves the “shake ﬂask” and the “closed bottle” method. Alternatively,
exceptions may be carried out through the ISO 10708 standard with authority approval
(OECD 1992a; Petroleum Safety Authority Norway 2015). The shake ﬂask and closed
bottle methods neither simulate marine environments nor return a direct measure of
biodegradability. However, tests like this provide a great indication of biodegradability
potential for the compounds of interest (OECD 1992a).
3.2. Bioaccumulation testing
Bioaccumulation is the accumulation of substances and chemicals in an organism. A
substance or chemical accumulates inside an organism when the rate of decomposition is
lower than the rate of accumulation (Bryan 1979). Bioaccumulation testing is reported
as a bioaccumulation potential LogPOW and applies to chemicals with a molecular weight
lower than 700 g/mole, and has to be tested in accordance with the OECD 117 standard,

which is a high-performance liquid chromatography test (HPLC). In addition, bioaccu-
mulation tests may also be performed in accordance with the OECD 306 shake ﬂask
method (Petroleum Safety Authority Norway 2015).
3.3. Acute toxicity
Acute toxicity refers to the occurring consequences regarding exposure to a chemical
compound (ILO 2001; Food and Cosmetics Toxicology 1978). Acute toxicity tests return
a value of the median lethal dose (LD50) and the lethal concentration (LC50), which is
a measure of required concentration in order to kill half of the test population after a
pre-deﬁned test duration (Soukupová 1999; Kelly & Lindsey 2002; OECD 1992b).
3.4. Chemical categorisation
According to the North Sea guidelines for selection of oﬀshore chemicals, an initial com-
prehensive environmental risk assessment is required in order to map the chemical’s po-
tential for environmental damage with respect to the initial properties, quantities, time
and location of discharge. In addition, the operator is obliged to always evaluate and
choose chemicals with the lowest ecotoxicological potential. All oil ﬁeld chemicals used
in the North Sea are sub-categorised into colour-coded categories - green, yellow, red and
black, which express the chemical’s level of toxicity. Chemicals within the hazardous cat-
egory red and black should only be selected if they are absolutely necessary for technical
and safety reasons (Petroleum Safety Authority Norway 2015).
3.4.1. Green category
The green category includes all chemicals that are presumed not to have a signiﬁcant
impact on the environment. All these chemicals are listed on the OSPAR PLONOR list,
and are granted permission for discharge in the ocean without any additional treatment
(Petroleum Safety Authority Norway 2015).
3.4.2. Yellow category
Chemicals which are excluded from the OSPAR PLONOR list, but are still considered not
to be a direct environmental threat, are classiﬁed as yellow. Such chemicals are therefore
granted permission to be directly discharged in the ocean (Petroleum Safety Authority
Norway 2015).

6.1. Turbiscan MA-2000 Heavy Fuel experimental method
The Turbiscan MA-2000 Heavy Fuel apparatus is an optical analyser designed to inves-

rates, in addition to particle migration and particle size variation (Mengual et al. 1999).
Turbiscan’s main advantage is that it is able to detect all the aforementioned phenomena

investigate the test sample’s development with time.
The emulsion stability testing is inspired by the reported emulsion stability testing pro-
cedure performed by York & Firoozabadi (2008). This method is based on the sedimen-
tation phenomenon, which is known to occur when the dispersed phase (water) has a
greater density than the continuous phase (n-heptane). When water breaks out of the
emulsion, the density diﬀerence causes the water to migrate downwards and accumulate
at the bottom of the test tube (Formulaction 1994). York & Firoozabadi (2008) suggest
that the time it takes for 60 vol.% of the dispersed phase to break out of the emulsion is a
proper measure of the emulsion stability. In other words, the longer it takes for 60 vol.%
of the aqueous phase to break out of the emulsion, the more stable the emulsion.
Turbiscan detects sedimentation in terms of peaks in the transmission ﬂux (T). The
time it takes for 60 vol.% of the water to break out of the emulsion is proportional to the
point where the transmission ﬂux (T) stabilises at a value close to a deﬁned reference ﬂux
and intersects the point deﬁning 60% of the aqueous phase (Formulaction 1994). The

addition to the amount of photons reﬂected from the sample (Formulaction 2009). The
initial results are printed as two proﬁles, one for the transmitted light and one for the
backscattered light in the TurbiSoft software. Subsequently, the raw data may be treated
and manipulated to determine essential sample parameters, such as sedimentation rate,
creaming rate and particle size (Formulaction 2009).
6.2.2. Operational conditions
The Turbiscan emulsion stability experiments were conducted at ambient pressure and
temperature (20oC, 1 bar). Unfortunately, it was not possible to carry out high pressure
(80 bar), low temperature (2.1 oC) experiments on the Turbiscan apparatus. However, the
experiments provide satisfactory results with regards to the anti-agglomerants’ ability to
produce emulsions, and the subsequent emulsion stability testing.
The emulsions were formed during agitation of the test tube, by ﬂipping the tube from

6.2.3. Turbiscan emulsion stability procedure
The emulsion stability testing procedure is carried out in accordance with York & Firooz-
abadi (2008). In fact, this procedure is referred to as the formal emulsion stability test.
Simply, the aim is to measure the time it takes for 60 vol.% of the aqueous phase to break
out of the emulsion. The emulsion stability testing experiments were conducted using
the following procedure;
1. Carefully submerge the prepared sample in the Turbiscan testing chamber and make
sure the test tube is in the correct position. Enter the liquid column’s height in
TurbiSoft and run one initial scan.
2. Go to TurbiSoft’s acquisition tools and insert a scanning frequency of 15 scans/hour
and a total number of scans equal to 60 scans (4 hours).
3. Release the sample from the Turbiscan machine and homogenise the sample by
tipping it on its end 12 times.
4. Quickly place the sample back in the Turbiscan chamber and start the scanning.
The initial run provided data on how much light the sample transmits before homogen-
isation. This is used as a reference value with regards to the amount of transmitted light
reported in the sedimentation product.
6.2.4. Interpretation of emulsion stability results
Turbiscan returns a numerical value for the sample’s transmission ﬂux (T) and backscat-
tering ﬂux (BS). These numbers are reported as fractions of the initial ﬂux intensity of
the light source. Both water and heptane are colourless, transparent liquids with a trans-
mission ﬂux (T) equal to ⇠93%. In the presence of an emulsiﬁer (anti-agglomerant),
water and heptane form a milky-white emulsion with a transmission ﬂux (T) equal to
⇠0%. The transmission ﬂux was found to provide better results for this application than
the backscattering ﬂux. All subsequent work is therefore conducted on the transmission
ﬂux (T) only.

7. Anti-agglomeration testing
7.1. Shell Cell experimental method
The Shell Cell is an extremely useful tool for experimenting on hydrate formation, growth
and dissociation processes. The Shell Cell is an autoclave stirred tank, especially tailored
to mimic the ﬂow conditions in an subsea pipeline. It delivers data on pressure (bar), tem-
perature (oC), stirring velocity (rpm) and the motor current (A). These four parameters
may be treated individually or combined, and provide information on essential hydrate
parameters such as induction time, growth rate, and gas consumption. Moreover, the
Shell Cell is capable of conducting both isobaric and isothermal experiments, in addition
to a ramping temperature procedure.
Despite the Shell Cell’s huge application area, it has two major limitations. Firstly, it is
only capable of running one test sample at a time. This makes the experiments extremely
time consuming as it is desirable to perform at least three runs on each test sample, in
terms of mapping out erratic data and trends. Due to this time limitation, the work in
this report is conducted isothermally only. In addition to being time consuming, the Shell
Cell is also very sensitive to temperature. The cell’s minimum operating temperature may
therefore vary with the seasons.
7.1.1. Preparation of test solution and experimental components
Methane gas of ≥99.0% purity provided from AGA Gas was used as the hydrate-forming
guest component (AGA GAS AB 2012). Methane was chosen as the hydrate guest com-
pound, in order to limit the experiments’ complexity. Sun & Firoozabadi (2014) report
that a decreasing pH value of the solution has a negative impact on several types of
anti-agglomerant’s eﬀectiveness. Natural gas contains carbon dioxide (CO2). The CO2
dissolves in the solution’s water-phase and forms carbonic acid, hence the pH of the so-
lution decreases (Sun & Firoozabadi 2014). The use of methane gas therefore ensures a
more stable solution pH (⇠7), which means that there is no need for a phosphate buﬀer
solution (Koltveit 2014). In addition, n-heptane of ≥99.0% purity from Sigma-Aldrich
was used as the liquid hydrocarbon phase of the system as it does not interact as a
guest molecule in the hydrate formation process. Methane is therefore the system’s only
hydrate-forming guest, and alone, methane is known to generate hydrates of structure
SI only.
The experiments were conducted using a 30 vol.% water-cut, at anti-agglomerant con-
centrations equal to 0.05 wt.%, 0.1 wt.% and 0.25 wt.%. The two quaternary ammonium
salts, BHDP −Br and BMA −Cl provided by Sigma-Aldrich with a purity of ≥99.0%

7.2.1. Shell Cell experimental setup
The Shell Cell is a Royal Dutch Shell autoclave stirred tank, manufactured in the Nether-
lands. The Shell Cell is constructed as a double-coated cylinder made from 316 stainless
steel. The double coated structure allows eﬀective and homogenous cooling by contin-

inner pressure chamber. The coolant is composed of a mix between glycol and water and
controlled by an external cryostat (Julabo F25, with a range from −20.0 oC to +120 oC)
in order to maintain constant temperature (Jørgensen et al. 2014). The inner pressure

equipped with a magnetic stirrer from Top Industrie. The stirrer consists of an engine
placed on the outside of the lid, and an impeller attached to a 8 mm shaft submerged in
the chamber, in order to ensure proper stirring. The impeller’s stirring velocity is adjusted
from an external control unit, also provided from Top Industrie. In addition, the lid is
equipped with a pressure and temperature transducer. The pressure transducer is capable
of operating along the cell’s pressure range, from atmospheric pressure to 150 bar, with
a maximum uncertainty of 0.075%. The temperature transducer is a platinum resistance
probe delivering temperatures with an accuracy of ±0.01oC. All data readings are carried
out through Agilent data acquisition instruments and subsequently stored and treated in
a connected computer (Jørgensen et al. 2014).

7.2.3. Shell Cell - Isothermal procedure
The isothermal experiments on the Shell Cell were conducted with 30 mL test samples.
The cell is set to maintain a constant stirring speed of 700 rpm. However, during the
cooling stage, the cell is neither pressurised nor stirred. Elevated pressures and stirring
provokes hydrate formation; it is therefore important to wait until the desired temperature
is reached in order to conduct valid isothermal testing.
Experiments carried out in this report follow the procedure below:
1. The chamber is loaded with a fresh test sample and properly sealed. The cell’s
cooling system is then set to 0.7 oC and left for 1 hour, in order to reach the
minimum operational temperature Top of 2.1 oC.
2. When the operational temperature is reached, the data acquisition is initiated and
the cell is pressurised with methane gas to approximately 95 bar.

miscibility in water and n-heptane. In order to ensure constant subcooling for all

the operational temperature is reached.

8.2. BHDP-Br
The quaternary ammonium salt tri-butyl-hexa-decyl-phosphonium-bromide (BHDP-Br)

All initial work on emulsion stability was carried out on BHDP-Br due to its proven
eﬀect as an emulsiﬁer and anti-agglomerant. In addition, BHDP-Br is also the compound
with the highest availability. The method of homogenisation (tipping the test tube from
end to end 12 times) is based on BHDP-Br emulsifying properties. Depending on the
concentration, BHDP-Br was found to generate a nice, smooth and homogeneous emulsion
in between the 3rd and 6th tip. However, 12 tips were chosen to be the correct amount
of agitation in terms of making the emulsion stable enough to be measurable, but not
too strong in terms of time-saving and eﬃciency.

Hydrocarbons are usually referred to as non-polar compounds.
However, a crude oil
is composed of a large number of diﬀerent hydrocarbons with diﬀerent properties. In
particular, asphaltenes and resins are important in terms of emulsion stability, as they
achieve an increasing polarity proportional to their number of hydroxyl groups. In fact,
it is reported that asphaltenes and resins have emulsifying properties and the presence of
such compounds will contribute to increasing the emulsion stability of the system (Wang
& Alvardo 2008; Sheu & Mullins 1993). However, the emulsions tested in this report
have been conducted with n-heptane as the system’s liquid hydrocarbon phase. Heptane
is a non-polar compound and does not contribute to stabilising the emulsions (State
University Idaho 2015).
The emulsion stability testing was conducted in a particle-free environment, which means
that the possible impact of particles (Table 8.5) does not apply. However, the subsequent
57
==PAGE:77==

anti-agglomeration testing was carried out in the presence of ﬁne-grained silica gel par-
ticles in order to shift the nucleation regime from homogenous to heterogeneous. Silica
gel particles are strongly hydrophilic and tend to accumulate in the oil-water interface
and stabilise potential oil-in-water emulsions. During live operations oﬀshore, a series
of diﬀerent particles are present, including clay particles. Due to its electronegativity
and hydrophobic properties, clay adsorbs onto the oleic phase and stabilises water-in-oil
emulsions through the principles of steric repulsion forces (Sullivan & Kilpatrick 2002;
Wang & Alvardo 2008).
Limitations in the apparatus only allowed the experiments to be conducted at ambient
pressure and temperatures. Temperature in particular has a large impact on emulsion
stability. In general, the emulsion viscosity decreases with increasing temperature, caus-
ing more inter-molecular collisions which destabilises the emulsions. A greater emulsion
stability is therefore expected at operational temperatures of ⇠2 oC. Operational pres-
sure has little to no impact on the emulsion stability. However, shear forces and internal
osmotic potential is found to impact and decrease the stability as they may result in de-
formation and merging of dispersed droplets causing the phenomenon of Ostwald ripening
(Graner & Glazier 1992). The pH of the aqueous phase has a strong inﬂuence on emul-
sion stability as the pH interacts and changes the physical properties of the interfacial
ﬁlms separating the dispersed particles and the continuous phase. However, the impact
of acidity on emulsion stability is very complex and may vary from system to system as
it strongly depends on the system’s composition in terms of the presence of organic acids
and bases, asphaltenes with ionising groups, solid particles and brine composition (SPE
International 2015). Typically, pH values found in pipelines are reported over a range
5 < pH < 7 due to the hydroxylic acids present in crudes (Sun & Firoozabadi 2014).
The pH is therefore assumed to be equal to 7 as no additional experiments have been
conducted in order to establish the system’s pH value.
Kelland (2006) does not recommend anti-agglomerant operations at water cuts exceeding
50 vol.%, due to the fact that the hydrate slurry becomes too viscous to transport. In
addition, a water-in-oil emulsion is also required so that the dispersed hydrate particles
can be transported in the continuous liquid hydrocarbon phase. In order to meet both of
these two requirements, a constant water cut equal to 30 vol.% has been experimented
upon as it is reported that the abundant phase in a water-oil-emulsiﬁer system usually
becomes the continuous phase, hence the water-in-oil emulsion (Wang & Alvarado 2008;
Janssen et al. 2001).

Daraboina et al.
(2013b) suggest that the anti-agglomerant interacts with the hydrate cages providing the
superior crystal structure an increased porosity and a substantial permeability. Subse-
quently, capillary actions expose the enclosed water to the gas phase and enhance hydrate
growth (Daraboina et al. 2013b). However, the only experiments in this report where
this same two-stage growth pattern was observed were in the methane-saturated heptane
experiments that contained salt. Furthermore, it was observed that the growth pattern
became more pronounced with increasing salinity. It is therefore concluded that this
two stage-growth pattern is provoked by the presence of salt, instead of by interactions
between the hydrate structure and the anti-agglomerant.
Sloan & Koh (2008 pp. 158) report that hydrate formation may follow diﬀerent growth
patterns at high subcooling.
It is suggested that water droplets become enclosed by
hydrate shells composed by needle-like crystals.

suggested that salt has an impact on the stability of the hydrate structure. In other words,
more salt results in less stable structures, which are more likely to collapse and cause an

Part V.
Conclusions and ﬁnal remarks
9.5. Conclusions
The purpose of this study was to investigate and compare the anti-agglomeration prop-
erties of two commercially-available quaternary ammonium salts (QUATs), BHDP-Br
and BMA-Cl, and a green biosurfactant, Surfactin. The three compounds were assessed
based on their anti-agglomerating properties, which were tested in an autoclave stirred
tank (Shell Cell), and their ability to produce a stable emulsion. The emulsion stability
was tested based on a bottle test using a light-scattering technique using a Turbiscan
apparatus. The anti-agglomerants were tested at diﬀerent concentrations as well as with
increasing concentration of salt (NaCl) in order to investigate the eﬀect of NaCl and
relate the experiments to more realistic oﬀshore applications.
The presence of an emulsion is essential for anti-agglomeration, and the emulsion stability
is an important factor when evaluating the overall suitability of the diﬀerent compounds
for hydrate management. In the absence of salt, the quaternary ammonium compound
BMA-Cl generated the most stable emulsions of the three, at all concentrations (0.05
wt.%, 0.10 wt.%, 0.25 wt.% and 0.50 wt.%). When salt was introduced (3.5 wt.% and
7.0 wt.%) to the system, the other quaternary ammonium compound, BHDP-Br, showed
improved emulsion stability and generated the most stable systems. The biosurfactant,
Surfactin, generated signiﬁcantly less stable emulsions over the whole experimental range,
both in terms of anti-agglomerant and salt concentrations.
Surprisingly, for all three tested compounds, the emulsion stability was higher when salt
was introduced to the system. This is in contrast to current literature, which ﬁnds that
emulsiﬁed systems lose stability when salt is introduced, due to lower internal electrical
stability. As suﬃcient experiments were conducted in this study to conclusively conﬁrm
that salt had a positive eﬀect on emulsion stability, it is therefore concluded that the
phenomenon is more complex than previously considered. It is recommended that further
research be conducted to investigate the eﬀect of salt on emulsion stability in the presence
of anti-agglomerants.
In the context of real-world oﬀshore applications, it is expected that the emulsion stabil-
ities at various concentrations of the tested anti-agglomerants will be even higher than
those recorded in the experiments. This is due to the presence of crudes in real systems,
which increase the acidity (pH) therefore increasing the emulsion stability. In addition,
natural emulsiﬁers such as asphaltenes and resins can be present in the crude, which have
also been observed to heighten emulsion stability. Furthermore, the lower temperature
91
==PAGE:111==
in real systems compared to the Turbiscan operational temperature is known to increase
the emulsion stability due to a higher viscosity, which causes less inter-molecular colli-
sions, which are known to destabilise the emulsion. Finally, the presence of clay particles,
among others, is known to increase stability as the particles adsorb onto one of the liquid
phases and accumulate in the oil-water interface, stabilising the emulsion.
The Shell Cell is an autoclave stirred tank that mimics real-world thermodynamic condi-
tions as far as possible, with similar operational temperatures (2.1 oC) and pressures (80
bar) to oﬀshore pipelines. Fine-grained silica-gel particles were added to the solution in
order to achieve more reproducible results as the stochastic nature of homogeneous nucle-
ation was shifted towards a more heterogeneous nucleation process. An added advantage
of adding the silica-gel particles was that they mimic the behaviour of particles present
in real-world systems, making the experiments conducted on the Shell Cell even more
applicable to real-world operations. It is therefore assumed that the emulsion stability of
the tested anti-agglomerants was slightly higher in the Shell Cell than the values reported
in the stability experiments conducted in the Turbiscan apparatus.
The initial experiments on the Shell Cell were conducted at constant temperature of 2.1
oC, at an operational pressure equal to 80 bar. The pressure was diﬃcult to reproduce
as a high fraction of the methane dissolved into solution with the liquid phase after
initiation of stirring, resulting in a subcooling in the range 8 oC < ∆Tsub < 9 oC. The
anti-agglomerating eﬀects of the diﬀerent compounds were intended to be measured in
terms of pressure, temperature, impeller velocity and motor current. However, the results
of the impeller velocity and corresponding motor current were found to be unsatisfactory,
as the parameters did not succeed in expressing the changing ﬂow properties during
hydrate formation. The anti-agglomerating properties were therefore evaluated in terms
of the system’s pressure proﬁle and visual observations through the Shell Cell’s inspection
window during experimentation.
A mentioned, liquid ﬁne-grained silica-gel particles were added to the systems in each of
the experiments. Despite the presence of silica-gel, the results of the quaternary ammo-
nium salt BHDP-Br were diﬃcult to reproduce, as each of the experiments with BHDP-Br
was characterized by a sudden random increase in the emulsion viscosity, independent
of hydrate formation. This increased viscosity caused stirring to take place only in the
center of the cell. It was therefore observed that the silica-gel particles accumulated at
the bottom of the cell, which caused the nucleation process to shift back to the more
stochastic homogeneous nucleation process.
BMA-Cl on the other hand, showed a more stable emulsion viscosity that ensured proper
stirring of the silica-gel particles, hence heterogeneous nucleation. This was conﬁrmed by
the higher reproducibility in the duration of the measured induction periods compared
92
==PAGE:112==
to those measured for BHDP-Br. The presence of both BHDP-Br and BMA-Cl extended
the induction periods signiﬁcantly in comparison to the reference system. This indicated
that both anti-agglomerants have kinetic hydrate-inhibiting eﬀects. However, for both
QUATs, no clear correlation between anti-agglomerant concentration and the induction
period was found. Similarly, Surfactin caused an extension of the induction period relative
to the reference system. However, the comparisons to be drawn between Surfactin and the
quaternary ammonium salts are limited, as the initial QUAT experiments were conducted
in a salt-free environment, while all Shell Cell experiments on Surfactin were conducted
at a salinity equal to 7.0 wt.% NaCl.
Therefore, in order to achieve comparable results between the QUATs and Surfactin, some
additional experiments were conducted on the QUATs. The buﬀer salinity was changed
to 7.0 wt.% NaCl and the QUAT concentration was kept constant at 0.05 wt.%. The
salt changed the systems’ subcooling from ∆Tsub 8 oC to 5.5 oC. In particular, BMA-
Cl responded well to the presence of salt and fully prevented hydrates from forming,
while the addition of salt to BHDP-Br caused a remarkable increase in the induction
period. It is therefore concluded that the presence of salt enhanced the kinetic hydrate
inhibitor eﬀects of the quaternary ammonium salts, in BMA-Cl more so than BHDP-
Br. As previously mentioned, Surfactin showed a minor increase in the induction period
compared to the reference system. However, when salt was present in all systems, the
increased induction period due to the presence of Surfactin was found to be more or less
insigniﬁcant when compared to those observed in the QUAT experiments.
The onset of hydrate growth was detected as a major pressure drop indicating catastrophic
hydrate growth for all the three compounds. However, the growth proﬁles developed
diﬀerently with time. BHDP-Br had almost constant catastrophic growth throughout the
whole growth period, and the growth rate was observed to increase with concentration.
BMA-Cl had a concentration-dependent shift from catastrophic to a more slow-paced
linear growth. The highest concentration (0.25 wt.%) caused the earliest shift towards
linear growth, while the lowest concentration allowed catastrophic growth to continue
almost throughout the growth period. Surfactin showed two stages of almost identical
growth. When the ﬁrst stage of catastrophic growth slowed down, the second almost
identical stage kicked in and continued until the system again stabilised at the pressure
Plim.
Considering the hydrate growth in terms of gas consumption, it became clear that neither
of the QUATs are growth inhibitors. Regardless of concentration, BHDP-Br allowed the
same amount of hydrates to form as the reference system. BMA-Cl on the other hand,
clearly slows down the formation process. However with time, BMA-Cl also allowed the
same amount of hydrates to form as the AA-free reference system. Surfactin on the other
hand, reduced the hydrate growth by approximately 5%, as seen by the amount of gas
93
==PAGE:113==
consumed, suggesting that Surfactin shows hydrate growth-inhibiting properties.
In order to mimic real-world operations that are increasingly being conducted in deeper
waters and arctic environments, some additional experiments were conducted on the low-
est concentrations of the QUATs (0.05 wt.%), using heptane pre-saturated with methane
in order to allow a higher initial subcooling (Pop = 95bar, ∆Tsub 10 oC at 0.0 wt.%
NaCl). In the absence of salt, both BHDP-Br and BMA-Cl entered the hydrate growth
phase immediately after initiation of stirring. In the presence of salt (3.5 wt.% and 7.0
wt.%), BMA-Cl did not form hydrates at all over the entire experimental period, while
BHDP-Br formed hydrates in accordance with the same two-step hydrate growth pat-
tern seen in the Surfactin experiments. Previous studies have concluded that the second
stage of catastrophic growth may result from an unstable needle-like hydrate structure
that collapses, releasing water that then becomes incorporated into a more stable hydrate
structure (Sloan & Koh 2008 pp. 158). Needle-like hydrate structures are known to occur
at high subcooling rates, however in this case, it is concluded that the salt has an addi-
tional negative impact on the stability of the hydrate structure. It is suggested that the
presence of salt promotes an earlier collapse of the structure, since it was observed that
the rate of the second catastrophic growth phase increases with increasing salt concen-
tration. Further research into salt’s impact on hydrate growth proﬁles is recommended
in order to gain understanding of this phenomenon.
9.6. Summary
This report has shown that the QUATs have excellent emulsifying properties. In a salt-
free environment, BMA-Cl produced the most stable emulsions of the three tested AAs,
while in the presence of salt, BHDP-Br enhanced the emulsion stability signiﬁcantly,
producing the most stable emulsions of the three AAs. In comparison to the QUATs, the
emulsifying properties of Surfactin were rather poor and the reported emulsion stability
were signiﬁcantly shorter.
The presence of the two QUATs in the systems caused two diﬀerent hydrate growth
proﬁles. BHDP-Br allowed a very rapid growth, while BMA-Cl slowed down the growth
process. During the formation of hydrates, BHDP-Br demonstrated extremely good anti-
agglomerating properties, in particular at the highest concentration (0.25 wt.%). BMA-
Cl had an outstanding performance in the presence of salt, and completely prevented
hydrates from forming. However, in the absence of salt, it was observed that hydrate
particles in the system containing BMA-Cl had a tendency to agglomerate into larger
particles when ﬁrst formed than in the system containing BHDP-Br. Even at the highest
concentration (0.25wt.%) of BMA-Cl, quite large hydrate particles were observed in the
system.
94
==PAGE:114==
Surfactin demonstrated poor anti-agglomeration properties as the hydrate particles were
observed to agglomerate easily and form solid hydrate plugs. However, a minor reduction
in the amount of hydrate formed was observed in the presence of salt, which implies
that Surfactin may have growth-inhibiting properties. Surfactin has clear environmental
advantages over quaternary ammonium salts, as it is biodegradable and more environ-
mentally friendly. In addition, it shows potential growth-inhibiting properties. However,
an improved performance both in terms of emulsion stability and anti-agglomeration ef-
fect, in addition to a reduction in price, is required before Surfactin is able to compete
with the performance and economic viability of the quaternary ammonium salts.
9.7. Limitations of the study
The conclusions of this research must be considered in the context of its limitations, in
particular, the limitations of the equipment used. The design of the Turbiscan apparatus
meant that the emulsion stability experiments could only be conducted at ambient pres-
sure and temperature. Impurities and chemical deposition on the test tube walls during
the process of homogenisation were found to interfere with the Turbiscan light-scattering
process, causing noise in the data, which made the results diﬃcult to interpret.
The cooling mechanism in the Shell Cell did not succeed in ensuring pure isothermal
conditions during the anti-agglomeration experiments. In addition, the motor driving the
impeller in the Shell Cell was over-dimensioned, resulting in low sensitivity to changes
in the measured motor current. Furthermore, the impellor was designed for high stirring
rates in systems with low friction, which made it unsuitable to monitor in detail the
changes in the viscosity of the system.
