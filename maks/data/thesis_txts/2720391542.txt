Abstract
This thesis is done in collaboration with the Technical University of Denmark’s au-
tonomous systems department and addresses some key visual odometry problems for a
cleaning robot designed to work in a robotics test arena with ongoing projects. The
key problems are: Extension cables, people working in the arena and handball style
netting used to separate parts of the arena, the problems are solved individually and
then later put together to form a coherent system. All modules are written in Python
and uses primarily OpenCV for computer vision and ROS for communication with the
robot. The modules are tested both individually and together, both times showing good
performance but due to high computation times measures had to be taken to reduce the
computational load on the robot.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The thesis is done in collaboration with the Technical University of Denmark’s au-
tonomous systems department using a cleaning robot built in-house from a manual
sweeping tool called a Fejemis. The Fejemis has been equipped with motors, cameras,
IMU, an Intel NUC and an assortment of different controllers, giving it the hardware
needed for autonomous driving. This thesis will only deal with the Intel Realsense d455
on the robot which is mounted on the aluminium extrusion at the top of the robot seen
in Fig. 1.1. This camera is used together with OpenCV and deep learning to detect the
people, nets and cables the locations of which are then communicated to the rest of the
robots modules using ROS (Robot operating system).
Figure 1.1: Image of the Fejemis robot.
The need for a cleaning robot arose when the company responsible for the former
solution, needed their robot back. The decision was made to produce an in-house robot
to use both for cleaning and as a test vehicle and learning opportunity, and because
manually cleaning a large space, like the ASTA (Autonomous Systems Test Arena)
facility at DTU, is very time consuming. Automating this process can have many benefits
but it also presents some unique problems.
The purpose of this thesis is to explore and evaluate the main problems the robot
will face when driving in ASTA. For each problem, methods and possible solutions will
be compared and in the end the results of these tests will be presented and discussed.

1.1
Objectives
The objective of this thesis is to run 3 different detectors at the same time on relatively
low powered hardware whilst limiting the computational load to leave room for the
navigation stack and other future potential projects on the robot. Because of this a few
performance requirements must be considered. The following are requirements both for
the overall performance of the robot and the individual accuracy of each module.
• Overall requirements:
– Must be fast enough to run at least 3-5 fps on the robots Intel NUC computer.
– Each module should output locations to the ROS network using standard
ROS message types / transforms.
– The modules should be robust enough to handle any realistic situation it could
be faced with while running in ASTA (lighting, backgrounds of different color,
large amounts of people, etc).
• Module specific requirements:
– Cable detection:
∗Must be able to detect cables regardless of the cables color, this includes
situations such as black cables on black background.
– People detection:
∗Must be able to at least estimate the current activity of people in the
frame.

2.2
Fejemis’ obstacles
While there are many different obstacles that could be looked at, this thesis will focus
on three main obstacles which are classified as either very problematic for the robot
or very desirable to detect, these choices are in part based on the experiences gathered
from the previously used robot. The three problems are: Extension cables lying on the
ground, people working in the arena and handball style nets that hang from the ceiling.
These obstacles are shown in figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Image containing the 3 obstacles which will be tackled in this thesis.
==PAGE:14==
6
2 Analysis
2.2.1
Cables
Detecting and avoiding the extension cables is in this case considered very problematic,
because in most cases the cables are too small to be perceivable by the depth sensor.
Since the depth sensor is used by the navigation stack on the robot as the primary SLAM
and localization sensor, the cables will essentially be invisible to the robot. When the
robot is driving around it will hit a cable which will cause the cables to get stuck in the
brushes of the robot and will require human intervention to untangle the cable from the
robot in order for it to continue operation. The robot needs to be able to detect and
avoid these cables. For this reason a wheel that can lift the front of the robot has been
fitted to the robot. To solve the detection problem the robot has to be able to detect the
cables and communicate their location to the navigation stack. This will be explored
further in chapter 3.
2.2.2
People
The previous cleaning robot that worked in ASTA did not account for peoples work
areas, it would of-course avoid people if they got close enough to the robot, but they
would simply be seen as obstacles and as such, it would often get very close to peoples
work areas and cause interference. This in turn caused people to press the emergency
stop on the robot so that they could keep working, causing the robot to stop and again
require human intervention. Fejemis should therefore be able to detect people at longer
distances, and attempt to figure out if they are working in an area or simply walking
around. This information can then be used by the navigation stack to avoid these areas
and it also means that robot can plan better cleaning routes around these areas, so that
these routes don’t need to be recalculated every time the robot gets close to a person.
This will be explored further in chapter 4.
2.2.3
Nets
The third and final obstacle that will be tackled is the detection of large black nets that
hang from the ceiling in ASTA, these nets are used to divide the arena into smaller parts.
Because of the way these nets are used, their positions in the arena are not fixed in place,
this again means that if the robot is planning a route to do cleaning on, it will have to
recalculate this route every time it gets near the net. The depth sensor is capable of
seeing the nets at short distances (below 2.0 to 2.5 meters), but it is desirable for the
robot to be able to spot these nets at much longer distances to improve the planning.
This will be explored further in chapter 5.

2.4
ASTA
ASTA is constructed in such a way that the arena naturally has very diffuse light all day,
and at night overhead lights can be turned on, which effectively means that the light in
the environment changes very little throughout the day, no matter if its sunny or not.
This allows computer vision parts to make some assumptions and gloss over some parts
which might be detrimental if the robot were to operate in a different environment. These
types of problems could be helped or perhaps entirely solved by histogram equalization
or similar techniques.

CHAPTER 3
Cable detection
3.1
Introduction
Cable detection is as mentioned important for the robot to accomplish safe autonomous
driving, the detection will make use of simple image analysis steps to segment and detect
the cables, this was a deliberate choice to have more control over the detection.
3.2
Analysis
To the authors knowledge detecting loose cables is not a particularly well published area
of research, nevertheless detecting power lines is a related and well documented research
field because it presents a problem for low flying planes and drones. Two papers will be
looked at, both of which try to come up with novel approaches to detecting power-lines
in real time using either a UAV (un-manned areal vehicle / drone) or a helicopter.
3.2.1
”PLineD: Vision-based Power Lines Detection For
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles”
[San+17] suggests an approach that uses image analysis techniques which do not require
training. It accomplishes this by first using a method called edge drawing which comes
from [AT11]. The edge drawing consists of four stages, 1. The image is smoothed using
a 5x5 Gaussian kernel 2. Gradient magnitudes and orientations are computed using the
Sobel operator 3. Local maximums from the gradient map are detected, they call these
anchors 4. From each anchor the direction of the edge is determined by the gradient
direction and when either the image limits are exceeded or the edge coincides with
another anchor the drawing stops. This approach shows many similarities to the Canny
edge detector.
Each edge is then looked at pixel by pixel to determine the angle of the edge at that
point. This is done so that, for example, a vertical edge belonging to a background
object can be segmented away from a horizontal cable segment.
The next step is to use ellipse fitting to each edge segment, this way the ratio between
length and width of the edge can be determined. This ratio becomes larger the longer
==PAGE:18==
10
3 Cable detection
and thinner an edge is, because intuitively this is what a cable looks like. If the ratio is
too small the edge segment is removed.
The edge segments are then grouped together based on their distance to each-other
and the angle between them.
3.2.2
”Detection and Reconstruction of Wires Using
Cameras for Aircraft Safety Systems”
[SSR19] suggests an approach which uses a deep learning architecture they call deep wire
CNN. This architecture takes advantage of the linear structure of the power-lines in the
image to put wire segments it finds in the image into clusters of wire contours. These
wire contours are then used to triangulate the position of the wire between frames.
3.2.3
Suggested solution
Since neither of the 2 papers mentioned are solving the same problem being solved here,
they are unlikely to be direct solutions. However the section 3.2.1 contains steps that
could be used or changed slightly to fit into this use-case. Because both papers assume
that the cables will be either completely straight or present only a slight curve, the
assumptions and therefore the solution must me altered. An approach which differs
slightly from section 3.2.1 is suggested, one that takes in an image and uses the canny
edge detector to generate edges which are then separated it into smaller patches, where
it is then estimated whether or not the patch contains a cable and the angle of the cable
is calculated, this angle is then compared to the angles around the patch to determine
continuation of the cable into these neighboring patches. If enough patches are believed
to contain a cable the entire cable segment is sent to the ROS network as a minimum
area rectangle fitted around the positive patches.
This approach is chosen instead of a deep learning solution because such networks
that produce bounding boxes will not work for cables, this is because of the very simple
case where the bounding box could take up the entire screen, for example if cable goes
from 1 corner of the frame to the opposite corner. And networks like U-net style networks
which would be able to predict individual pixels of cables would either be too slow for the
Intel NUC the robot uses or need training with a dataset that to the authors knowledge
doesn’t exist or just isn’t publicly available.
To find a cable in an image frame the cable is broken down into its very basic
descriptors, in this case the cable will be described as a continuous object of relatively
small diameter that has 2 parallel edges.

Pre-processing
The images taken by the robot consist of aligned RGB and depth images, these images
contain information that wont be helpful to the cable detection and will therefore need

3.4.1
Masking
The placement of the camera on the robot has the advantage of giving the robot the
possibility of seeing the sides of itself so that it can drive closer to obstacles when cleaning.
However this means that the robot is visible in the camera frame as seen in the left side
of figure 3.2, because of this a mask of the robot is produced by simply drawing the
outline of the robot and thereby removing it from the frame. The effect of this is seen
in the right side of figure 3.2. This ensures that no erroneous detections are made from
cables located on the robot itself which would cause it to constantly lift the brushes.
Figure 3.2: Left: Edge detection output, Middle: Mask of the robot Right: Edge
detection after masking.

3.4.2
Image blur
The floor of ASTA is a rough type of flooring with varying colors, which means when
doing edge detection, the image will be noisy. This could become a problem around
the extension cables if the magnitude difference between the peaks and valleys is large
enough. To help with this problem Gaussian blur is used, this smooths out the readings
in the edge detection as seen in figure 3.3.
Figure 3.3: Effect of Gaussian blur on a cross section which crosses first a black cable
then a white cable on the black background, Left: image from the robot
including the blue line which indicates the cross section, Right: Plot
showing the magnitude across the cross section.
The formula for the discrete 2D Gaussian distribution is given by:

Where x and y denote the distance to the center of the kernel and σ denotes the width
of the Gaussian distribution.
3.4.3
Image Down-scaling
Image down-scaling was done to improve performance, this often happens at the cost
of accuracy, but due to the simplicity of the feature being detected it is not seen as a
problem at the ranges where this detection is important, but it could reduce the accuracy
at longer ranges. Again this is not a problem since the cable detection will be used for
safe driving and not for planning.
3.4.4
Edge detection
The paper in section 3.2.1 suggested using edge detection to find the edges that appear
between a wire and the background, because of this a Canny edge detector will be used
to obtain this information, this type of edge detector uses a few methods to improve
the edges it generates which is why this type of edge detector is chosen. These steps
also make the edge detector more robust. The steps found in figure 3.4 contains a short
explanation of how canny works.
==PAGE:21==
3.4 Pre-processing
13
Figure 3.4: Steps performed by the canny edge detector, 1: Grey-scaled input image,
2: Gaussian blur, 3: Sobel filtered image, 4: Non maximum suppression,
5: Double threshold and hysteresis. Pictures from [Lia].
1. Input a grey-scale image.
2. Apply a Gaussian kernel as described in section 3.4.2 to the image to reduce noise.
3. Calculate gradients and intensities using an edge detection operator (such as a
sobel operator).
4. Lower cutoff suppression or non maximum suppression of edges to obtain the pixels
with the largest local magnitude (dependent on the size of this filter, though its
usually 3x3), this step is also known as edge thinning.
5. The final step is a double threshold, pixels above the upper threshold are considered
edges, pixel intensities between the upper and the lower threshold are then only
considered edges if they are 8-connected (meaning connection in any direction in
the image) to an edge. Pixel intensities below the lower threshold are discarded.
3.4.5
Patch generation
The binary image from edge detection contains too much information to process at once,
so the image is split into smaller patches. This approach produces some problems as
seen in figure 3.5 left side. This effect likely occurs because part of the cable lies on the
edge between 2 cells, which means that only 1 side of the cable is visible in those patches
which will disqualify them from being cables as both sides of the cable is not visible in
that patch.
To relieve this problem, an overlap is introduced between each patch. This way each
patch can see part of the bordering patches which eliminates the problem of having a
cable between 2 patches. The effect of this on the edge detected image can be seen in
figure 3.6. Where the left image shows a cable that would not be able to be detected
because a part of the cable lies in-between 2 patches which means it would be disqualified
as a cable candidate.
This however is not a problem in the right side of figure 3.6,
where the cable is now completely visible. This comes with an extra problem where
multiple permutations are now going to be needed when checking if the cable segment
is continuous through the frame. This will be explained further below.

3.5
Finding contours / parallel angles
While its nice to have smaller patches of the image to work on, these patches are not
very useful if there is no way of extracting the information in them. The 2 pieces of
information needed here is: Does the patch contain 2 parallel contours (one for each side
of the cable) and the angle of the possible cable in each patch. The way this is done is
relatively simple using a function from OpenCV called ”FindContours” which works by
taking a binary image and performing border following from [Sb85]. This checks each
pixel by 8 connectivity to determine the border iteratively, and when a border reaches
the original point in the sequence again, a contour is found.
This contour is then used to determine the angle / direction of the pixels in each
patch. To calculate the angle of each contour the delta between starting point and end
point is used to calculate the arc tan of these 2 deltas, which will provide us with the
angle between the points. But because contours are being used the start point and end
point of these points will be the same point meaning the 2 points in the contour which
are furthest from each-other needs to be found. This can be done relatively simply by
calculating euclidean distances between all the points in the contour. This is not the
most effective way of doing it but because the contours are relatively small (given the

3.6
Recursively checking for cable
continuation
Now that each patch has been checked for parallel contours and the general angle of
these contours in each patch has been found, the only thing missing is to check if the
cable continues through the frame. Figure 3.5 showed that the overlap is needed to
ensure proper cable detection, but the overlap comes with a downside, figure 3.6 shows
that there is no way of knowing which neighbor patch the cable will continue through.
Therefore all the possible neighbor patches in the general direction of the cable from the
current patch has to be checked. To do this the angle of the current cell is first put into
one of four categories: Horizontal, vertical, 45 degrees up and 45 degrees down. Each
of these four categories will then have to have 9 permutations to cover all the different
neighboring patches the cable can continue through. The 9 permutations for an angle
between 67.5 and 112.5 (This is horizontal since 0 is north) is seen in figure 3.7 where
the angle of each permutation is checked against the current angle to see if it is within
the limit set, this angle limit can be thought of as the maximum curvature of the cable
meaning if the limit is 90 the cable is allowed to turn 90 degrees between each patch.
Figure 3.7: Permutations for recursion, where cur = current cell and 0 = cells that
will be checked recursively.
This check will then be performed recursively in an attempt to find the patches that
indicate continuation through the frame. This means that using a recursion depth of 2
will generate the 9 permutations for the given angle, then for each of the permutations
that has a similar angle and has 2 parallel lines indicating a cable, will be put into a
list. When each permutation has been checked the patches that were put in the list will
be checked the exact same way as the original patch thus making it recursive. This will
continue until the maximum depth is reached at which point the segments will be put

the segments will be ignored and not seen as cables. This step is visualized as a flow

This all combines into an algorithm which allows the user decide how deep the
recursion should go or in other words how many cable segments need to agree that they
are cable segments in a series. The general notion here should be that the more recursion
done the more likely the candidates are to being cables. But since the robot is not a
super computer and does not have infinite amounts of processing time shallower depths
will be chosen and tested.

3.8.2
Edge-cases and faults
ASTA contains different tracks for robots to move around, some of these tracks include
white tape strips which are used to guide ”line following” robots. The problem is that
these tape strips have the same basic features as a cable. This means that the cable
detection will categorize them as cables, and they will thereby not be cleaned by the
robot. This could be problematic since the tape strips will be less effective for the line
following robots if they are dirty.
3.9
Partial conclusion
An approach to detecting curved cables of any color has been made using the assumption
that a cable is 2 parallel edges that continue through a frame, instead of assuming
that they are long lines in the image. Attempts at reducing the computational load of
this algorithm has been made with respect to upholding good detection accuracy. The
proposed detector, boasts an accuracy of 61 % but shows that using multiple frames the
odds of spotting a cable before it is hit can be increased to above 95 %. The detector
has an average run-time of 146 ms using the recommended detection settings. The tests
also showed that cables are detected regardless of their color, even when the color is very
similar to the background.

CHAPTER 4
People detection
4.1
Introduction
Unlike cable detection, people detection is less critical in that the robots path-planner
will be able to avoid people when they are close enough to the robot. But as previously
mentioned, the goal of the people detection is to avoid the areas people work in. There-
fore this detection has to work at longer ranges than the path-planner can see, in order
for the robot to plan around it. This also means that it would be desirable to keep track
of the people the robot sees, since this way the work area of each person can be mapped
individually, so that this area can be avoided.
4.2
Analysis
Due to the structural complexion of a person and the different poses a person can
be spotted in, it is deemed necessary to use a pre-trained classification model. The
model will need to output bounding boxes of each person in the image. Deep learning
was deemed necessary, since early tests showed that simpler models such as a HOG
descriptor, wasn’t going to have the desired accuracy, and showed many false positives.
The 2 problems here that need to be tackled are, choosing a detection model to
provide the bounding boxes around people in the frame. The second problem, is to then
use the information in each bounding box, to make a fingerprint of the person and save
it so that it can be used later for comparisons, in an attempt at tracking the people the
robot sees.
Detecting people is a very well researched area. Many different approaches exists,
because of their usefulness in detecting people in everything from self-driving cars to
surveillance. But the area of keeping track of people, is a less published area of research,
which could be due to the ethical problems associated with tracking people. The tracking
used in this thesis, will be based solely on the color of peoples clothes and will therefore
not be able to identify people outside of this scope. First, the selection of the people
detection model will be analysed, and after that solutions aimed at keeping track of the
people detected, will be analysed.

Detecting complex structures such as people in an image frame, comes in different forms
but almost always requires some sort of pre-trained model to have usable accuracy. This

4.2.2
Kalman filters and Simple Online and Real-time
Tracking
Kalman filters are capable of predicting the position of objects, based on their previous
positions. If this could be used to predict the movement of people in ASTA, it would
be very helpful. This is how algorithms like SORT [Bew+16] work, here Kalman filters
are used to assign id’s to detections by predicting the next location of the person using
previously locations of the person. The problem is that, the further into the future the
prediction has to be, the more inaccurate the prediction will become. This is in turn
what makes Kalman filters unusable in this scenario, because the person being tracked,
is not necessarily going to be in the frame, every frame. Human movement is very easy
to predict when people are walking down a street, since they are very likely to continue
in the direction they are currently moving. This doesn’t happen to the same degree
in ASTA, because a person might want to walk from one side of ASTA to the other,
but the person might be just as likely to walk in a circle around an object, or walk
”zig-zag” between different experiments the person is currently performing. This makes
the movement too unpredictable for a Kalman filter. The prediction of Kalman filters
is also usually on a much smaller time scale than what is needed here, usually it will be
frame to frame predictions.

4.6
K-means
As a possible replacement for the depth image segmentation, a clustering algorithm such
as K-means could be used. K-means is used to separate data into 2 different clusters,
foreground (person) and background, this approach will work regardless of distance. But
a concern exists, where if the person detected is wearing similar colors of clothing as the
background, the masking could again fail.
K-means clustering works by:
1. Randomly assign K points to be each cluster centroid.
2. Assign each data point to the closest centroid.
3. Calculate new centroids based on the mean of the points belonging to each cluster.
4. Repeat 2 and 3 until the clusters stop changing, meaning that the algorithm has
converged.

CHAPTER 5
Net detection
5.1
Introduction
Net detection is once again not one of those problems that are required for safe driving
in ASTA, because the depth camera can see the nets at short distances (below 2.0 to
2.5 m). But as it was with people detection, it is one where it could improve the driving
of the robot, allowing it to plan around the nets and make better cleaning routes. This
way the computational load on the robot can be lessened, by not having to constantly
recalculate the routes when it meets a net. The hope is that, this net detection can work
at longer distances than what the depth camera sees. It will do this using an approach
consisting of simple image analysis techniques.
5.2
Analysis
Net detection for autonomously driving robots is not directly a very researched area.
However detecting damage to nets used in marine aquaculture is a closely related field,
that often relies on autonomous underwater robots for inspection. Another field that
could be related is that of removing netting from images, this is usually seen alongside
photography and TV, where the object being pictured could be behind a net that needs
to be removed. 2 papers will be looked at in this section, 1 of which is related to marine
aquaculture and 1 related to discovering common lattices in an image. Alongside these
2 papers a theoretical approach involving Fourier analysis will also be looked at.
5.2.1
”An adaptive method of damage detection for
fishing nets based on image processing
technology”
[Zha+20] suggests 2 different approaches to detecting nets underwater, the first approach
uses a Harris corner detector in an attempt at finding the corners of the holes in the net,
these corners would then be used to detect if the net is damaged or not. If the corners
were regularly spaced the net would be assumed to be undamaged, but if the corners
detected presented irregularity in the form of a closed circle, the net would be assumed
to be damaged.
==PAGE:48==
40
5 Net detection
The second approach uses thresholding to obtain a binary image of the location of the
strands that make up the net, this image is then inverted to instead show the location
of the holes in the net, the size of these net holes is then used to find statistical outliers
which would indicate damage to the net since at-least 1 hole would be much larger than
the others.
5.2.2
”Discovering Texture Regularity as a Higher-Order
Correspondence Problem”
[Hay+06] suggests a way to find the most common lattice in an image, it does this
by performing a few steps to first generate multiple lattices which are then iteratively
improved. This approach is very promising but has 1 very big flaw which eliminates it
as a possibility, in the paper it is stated that ”For bad initializations, it takes less than
a minute to propose texels, run them through matching, and realize that there is no
meaningful lattice. A full 20 iterations can take half an hour for some textures as the
number of texels increases.” This means that when running the algorithm it could come
up with no lattice, and the time spent doing so is far outside the range required to run
real-time.
5.2.3
Fourier analysis
A different approach could be to use Fourier analysis to identify a repeating pattern in
the image, which in this case would indicate a net. Fourier analysis is most commonly
used in 1D signal analysis but can be extended to 2D where an image can be broken
down into the frequencies that describe it. This frequency image should present a spike
in a specific frequency that corresponds to the net in the image. However early tests
showed that whilst it is possible to find these frequencies, it is not very accurate and
it didn’t perform well on nets that are not perfectly stretched out. The belief is that
as the net gets more bunched up the base frequency of the net gets muddy as the net
overlaps with it self, making it significantly harder to separate it from the noise.
5.2.4
Suggested solution
Each approach described above has its advantages and disadvantages, but 2 of them
have big flaws that make them undesirable in this scenario.
The problem with the
corner detection approach is that it assumes symmetry in the net, which when looking
at figure 5.6, is definitely possible since this presents itself as a net with a consistent
pattern. However when looking at figure 5.9, it can be seen that these nets will in some
cases not present themselves as symmetric patterns. The other paper which showed
very promising results but ultimately had too big of a drawback is the lattice generation.
Because there is no obvious way to reduce the computation time without reducing the

it to be usable on an autonomously driving robot.
Since the nets do not present themselves as symmetric or uniform patterns, the final
approach of using thresholding and statistics to detect the net is the only real option
for detecting the nets left. The difference between the scenario faced in the paper and
the scenario faced here is the overlapping nets which still needs to be identified as nets,

Pre-detection

5.4
Adaptive thresholding
To find the holes in the net, a way of separating the net from whats around it is needed.
This could be done with an edge detector, such as the one used in the cable detection.
But the method used in the paper from 5.2.1 is thresholding, which works better in
this scenario than edge detection. This is mostly attributed to fact that net detection
does not have to concern itself with detecting black nets on black backgrounds, due to
ASTA providing a white background. Thresholding also proved to work better on nets

at longer distances, at which point the edge detection would not detect edges, or if the
width of the Gaussian kernel used in the edge detection, was made too small, it would
detect too many edges at closer distances, creating many unnecessary false positives for
hole candidates.
In a normal thresholding operation, a single value is used to threshold an image.
This outputs a binary mask of the image where everything above the threshold is set to
1 and everything below is set to 0. The problem with this approach is that it’s not very
’smart’. The threshold will use a single value, no matter the amount of background light
or shadows present in the image. This means that it will work on some images, but will
not work on others. Meaning it wont be consistent, and therefore cannot be trusted to
provide the needed thresholding.
The solution to this is using an approach called adaptive thresholding. In adaptive
thresholding, the thresholding is done on smaller sections of the image and the thresh-
olding value can be calculated based on, for example the mean pixel value of the section
minus some constant. This gives a smoother thresholding, which requires no tuning
between different frames, and is a more general model for thresholding an image. The
difference between normal thresholding and the 2 adaptive thresholding approaches used
in OpenCV can be seen in figure 5.2, where the normal threshold was tuned perfectly
for a different image.

6.2
Scenario

ROS concurrently and 3. To create robust and fault tolerant detection. This will be
done in three steps, 1. A comparison of the run-times seen in the test performed here,
2. Through images the outputs of each detection algorithm will be visualized alongside
the output seen in ROS and 3. A discussion of how this either fulfils or doesn’t fulfil the
requirement of being robust and fault tolerant.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Three detection models have been made and tested, they each explain their relevance
both to the robot used in the project and to research being done within the fields each
detection is related to. The detection models showed accuracies of 61%, 90% and 90% for
cable, people and net detection respectively. The computational load of each algorithm
has also been limited to make them comply with the objective of being able to run in a
real-time operation of 3-5 fps, with computation left over for other tasks the robot has
to complete, such as the running of the navigation stack.
The interaction with the detectors happens entirely through ROS, with the images
and transforms needed being taken directly from the respective ROS topics, and the
output of detections being published on their respective topics.
The robustness of the algorithms is however still doubtful due to the lack of a proper
overall systems test, which couldn’t be completed due to limitations with the robot and
its ability to drive consistently.
Based on the factors mentioned here the detectors proposed in this project are for the
most part usable, but will require more work and testing to be completely trustworthy.
