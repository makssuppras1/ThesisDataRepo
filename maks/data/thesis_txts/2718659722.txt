Abstract
Topology optimization of phase field damage evolution is quite a non-researched
field, and previous work uses approximated sensitivities to update the design. This
thesis introduces a density-based topology optimization scheme that maximizes the
total external work of a structure. Direct sensitivities are derived based on a simple
phase field model representing linear elastic fracture, and verification indicates a
correct derivation and implementation.
Uniqueness can generally not be guaranteed for the phase field solution, meaning
multiple feasible phase field solutions can exist for a design. This leads to several
objective values, which entails that the gradient of the objective function is not well-
defined for a bifurcation design.
To take several crack paths into account, the robust formulation is applied by per-
turbating the density field of minor magnitude, and the worst of the realizations
is what is improved. An apparent design stabilization effect is seen. The influence
of the number of design projections is also examined, and it is found that the per-
turbation method for the robust formulation is not suﬀicient to trigger all feasible
crack patterns.
Finally, an intuitive reference solution is made to assess the performance of the
developed topology optimization scheme. The reference design is almost as good
as the best-performing optimized designs, indicating room for improvement for the
developed topology optimization scheme.
For all the optimizations, the scheme reaches a bifurcation design with several crack
path solutions. If the objective function is non-differentiable, the sensitivities are
incorrect, making gradient-based topology optimization diﬀicult to step towards an
improved design.

Introduction
Gradient-based topology optimization is a multidisciplinary design method to ap-
proach optimal structures by updating the design on the basis of correct sensitivi-
ties. Properly most well-known is compliance minimization, where the material is
redistributed to maximize a structure’s effective stiffness. An example of topology
optimization can be seen in figure 1.1b, where it is obvious that a human-based
design iteration process would have reached a different final design.
Mechanical failure is strongly driven by crack propagation, as seen in figure 1.1a. The
usual representation is highly discontinuous as the fully damaged cracked surface is
surrounded by non-damaged material. This motivates smearing the crack-region to
a damaged zone and using the phase field approach to represent fracture.
(a) Crack growth1
(b) Topology optimization2
Figure 1.1: Examples of
The objective of this master thesis is to develop a density-based topology optimiza-
tion scheme that maximizes the total external work by redistributing two materials
- i.e., a systematic procedure to design structures that can absorb as much energy
as possible.
Previous work in the field of density-based topology optimization and crack prop-
agation modeling with the phase field approach, [1] and [2], are considering ap-
proximated sensitivities to be suﬀicient by assuming that the way how the crack
propagates is independent of the structure.
This thesis will present the fundamental steps to set up direct sensitivities based on
a simple phase field fracture model. Both the objective value and the sensitivities
will be verified. Modern filtering technics will be applied to ensure discrete physical
designs. The performance of the developed topology optimization scheme will lastly
be assessed with different numerical examples.

Phase Field Fracture Model
Mechanical structures fail due to many possible underlying physical mechanisms, of-
ten strongly governed by material cracking. Knowledge about when and how cracks
can grow is essential for preventing failure.
This chapter provides the fundamental steps for settings up a numerical fracture
model, forming the physical basis for the following optimization. The work aims
to set up a simple crack model capturing the major fracture concepts, that can be
used for topology optimization. The developed phase field fracture model will be
assumed suﬀicient.
Linear elastic fracture mechanics, where a material point is assumed to behave
linearly elastic till failure, where material separation occurs and crack surfaces are
formed, is adopted. The Griﬀith energy criterion states, that a crack can form, or
an existing crack can grow, if and only if such a process causes the total energy in
the system to decrease or to remain constant [3]. A fracture process, on an atomic
level, involves molecular bonds breaking which consumes energy. For quantifying
the energy cost associated with forming crack surfaces, the Griﬀith-Type fracture
toughness, Gc, is used when considering homogonous materials. The Griﬀith crite-
rion is a generic definition contrary to for example the stress intensity factor, which
is a pure linear elastic definition.
Linear elastic fracture mechanics defines a material point as either fully broken
(Γ) or non-damaged (Ω\Γ), as shown below in figure 2.1a for an arbitrary body,
Ω. A sharp crack representation is highly discontinuous as the cracked surface, Γ,
is surrounded by fully non-damaged material, Ω\Γ. When applying a numerical
framework to solve the crack propagation problem, a sharp crack formulation is not
preferred. First of all, because the stress field is singular and goes to infinity at the
crack tip, but secondly also the issue of how to trace the cracked surface. There ex-
ist methods to handle a discrete representation, such as XFEM formulations, where
finite elements can separate, but the stress singularity will lead to other concerns.

2.2
Bulk Energy of Deformed Body
For evolving the phase field, there must exist some potential for releasing energy,
in order to overcome the cost of forming crack surfaces, as formulated by Griﬀith.
When a specimen deforms, it stores potential energy in terms of strain energy. If
a crack propagates, the deformation state cannot be maintained near the cracked
surface, and an energy release is expected. This mechanism is often referred to as
the driving force for fracture.

failure modes [3]. Mode I fracture, where tensile stresses normal to the crack sur-

induce fracture.
The initial Griﬀith approach does not differentiate between different mechanisms
leading to crack growth when describing crack propagation as a natural effect of a
global energy balance [3].

Topology Optimization
Engineering design, no matter what kind of technological field, intends most often
to use the smallest amount of resources to meet the system requirements, without
compromising safety.
With the basis of the previously developed phase field fracture model, this chapter
will present the necessary steps to formulate a topology optimization framework,
that maximizes the total external work of a structure, by rearranging the distribu-
tion of two materials.
An arbitrary body, shown in figure 3.1a, is made of a base material, (E0, Gc0),
with inclusions of a second material, (E1, Gc1). For representing the distribution of
the two materials, each finite element is assigned with a material density, ρ. An
irregular, body-fitted, finite element discretization, shown in figure 3.1b, can map
the material distribution with a discrete density field, ρ ∈{0, 1}, where, ρ = 0,
correspond to the base material and, ρ = 1, the inclusion material.

5.5
Other Solution Strategies
Several solution strategies, for suppressing or damping the design oscillations, have
been examined, with the goal of obtaining smoother convergence and better-optimized
designs. None of them manage to treat the issue with multiple phase field solutions.
• Different material interpolation scheme:
The RAMP scheme [25],
where the initial slope is nonzero, has also been applied. Larger oscillations
were seen and the optimization converged to a design not as good as for the
SIMP method (3.1).
• Solid-void distribution:
Many topology optimization applications deal
with the distribution of one material, corresponding to ρ = 1, and where
ρ = 0 represents non-existing material. By letting, E0 ≈0 and Gc0 = Gc1, the
distribution of one material has been optimized. Very large design oscillation
was seen which tells that there also exist several solutions to the phase field
problem for one-material models.
• Increased ratio between fracture toughnesses: From the presented ex-
amples, it is evident that the optimization approaches a structure, where sev-
eral crack paths are equally occurring. For limiting the options for how the
phase field can evolve, the fracture toughness for the inclusion material, Gc1,
can be chosen large relative to the base material, in order to avoid the crack
from growing through the inclusion. This has been examined for several ra-
tios, Gc1/Gc0. For all of the optimizations, the optimizer simply converges to
a different structure, where multiple crack patterns exist.
• Use of passive elements: Passive elements define a region where the opti-
mizer is not allowed to update the element densities. In that way, sub-domains
can be enforced to either base- or inclusion material. A region of passive el-
ements made of the base material has been defined, where the initial crack
is formed. The optimization reached different-looking structures but design
oscillations were still detected.
In general, different model parameters, test specimens, load configurations, bound-
ary conditions, etc.
have been examined.
None of the studies lead to a strong
conclusion on how to handle multiple phase field solutions or to systematically per-
turb the design to trigger only one solution. This is an inherent consequence of
using direct sensitivities as the gradients define how to form a structure where two
or more crack paths are equal in terms of energy.
When imagining that the optimizer reaches a design where the phase field only
propagates around the inclusion, one may wonder whether that is very optimal. If
damage does not even evolve in the inclusion, it can indicate that too much mate-
rial has been used for the inclusion. The material distribution is therefore not that
optimal and there may exist more material-eﬀicient designs.

Maximizing Fracture Resistance
Fracture initialization, where a crack starts to propagate, is the first mechanism in
a fracture process and what leads to crack growth. For preventing structural failure,
fracture initialization is an obvious process to optimize for.
This section will study how the external work, for the part of the deformation
history dominated by purely elastic deformations, can be optimized. The timestep,
where the maximum force occurs, will act threshold for which energy increment to
include in the objective function. When denoting the peak timestep, mpeak, the
weight factors are selected such that, s(n) = 0 ∀n > mpeak, and, s(n) = 1, for the
remaining increments.

The modified sensitivities, for the optimization, are therefore not fully correct when

timestep for when the peak load occurs.

Conclusion
Based on the Griﬀith energy criterion, a simple phase field model representing linear
elastic fracture has been developed. The driving force for fracture has been modified
to suppress compression-driven damage evolution, and the staggered-approach has
been utilized to split the coupled damage-displacement problem by introducing a
history field. The phase field is not allowed to heal, which has been enforced by
introducing an irreversibility constraint for the H-field.
Sensitivities have been derived using the adjoint approach where an extra adjoint
problem has to be solved before the direct sensitivities can be determined. A finite
difference verification has shown very good agreement between sensitivity values and
finite difference approximations.
Early in the examples, it was clear that two almost identical design fields can have
different phase field solutions. This indicates that uniqueness cannot be guaranteed
in general for the phase field solution. When several objective values exist for a
design field, the objective function is non-differentiable, which makes gradient-based
optimization very diﬀicult. It has been shown that non-uniqueness is only an issue
if significant damage evolution has set in.
The robust formulation was applied to account for several crack paths by consider-
ing three design realizations, and a distinct stabilization effect was seen. Multiple
design projections have also been studied, where it was found that three design re-
alizations are not adequate to trigger all feasible phase field solutions.
A reference solution, where a rectangular inclusion is placed in front of the crack tip,
was made to evaluate how good the optimized designs are. It was found that the
reference design is as good as the best-performing optimized designs, which may in-
dicate that the developed topology optimization scheme is not very well-functioning.
6.1
Future Work
For all the examples where multiple phase field solutions have been set off, the dif-
ferent design perturbations are obtained by thresholding the grayscale differently,
resulting in a uniform design variation. It has been demonstrated that the pertur-
bation method can trigger several crack paths, but it does not find all feasible phase
field solutions.
Therefore, a more systematic perturbation method must be developed to consider
all feasible phase field solutions. Here, it is suggested to look into the damage- and
displacement equations (2.21) and (2.22) to find a way to identify when there exist
multiple solutions and how each phase field solution can be triggered individually.

A.5
Initial Project Plan (7/9-2022)
Topology optimization is a multidisciplinary design method to approach optimal1
structures, in the early days formulated for compliance minimization - in other
words: distribute the material in such a way, that the global stiffness of a structure
is maximized.
Fracture mechanics is the key to understand crack propagation and predict final-
step mechanical failure. A representation of a cracked surface, surrounded by fully
undamaged material, is highly discontinuous and may lead to numerical troubles.
This motivates for using a phase field formulation, where the sharp crack topology
is smeared to a damaged zone.
This master thesis aims for developing a density-based topology optimization scheme
that redistributes the material with the objective of maximizing the effective fracture
energy of a specimen - i.e. a systematic way to design structures that can absorb as
much energy as possible before final failure is reached.
The project is scheduled in two stages, elaborated in the following. The second stage
is not meant to involve all sub-topics, but one, depending on how stage 1 works. An
initial timeline of the project period is attached.
Stage 1
(a) One Material: By allowing each finite element to be either solid or void density,
the distribution of the material is optimized with respect to the objective.
(b) Two Materials: By letting void and solid densities from (a) correspond to
material properties for respectively material 1 and 2, the distribution of two
materials is optimized. Initially without treating interface behavior between
the two materials.

A.6
Reviewed Project Plan
Topology optimization is a multidisciplinary design method to approach optimal2
structures, in the early days formulated for compliance minimization - in other
words: distribute the material in such a way, that the global stiffness of a structure
is maximized.
Fracture mechanics is the key to understand crack propagation and predict final-
step mechanical failure. A representation of a cracked surface, surrounded by fully
undamaged material, is highly discontinuous and may lead to numerical troubles.
This motivates for using a phase field formulation, where the sharp crack topology
is smeared to a damaged zone.
This master thesis aims for developing a density-based topology optimization scheme
that redistributes the material with the objective of maximizing the effective fracture
energy of a specimen - i.e. a systematic way to design structures that can absorb as
much energy as possible before final failure is reached.
The project is scheduled in two stages, elaborated in the following. The second stage
is not meant to involve all sub-topics, but one, depending on how stage 1 works. An
initial timeline of the project period is attached.
Stage 1
(a) One Material: By allowing each finite element to be either solid or void density,
the distribution of the material is optimized with respect to the objective.
(b) Two Materials: By letting void and solid densities from (a) correspond to
material properties for respectively material 1 and 2, the distribution of two
materials is optimized. Initially without treating interface behavior between
the two materials.
(c) Filtering: To approach discrete designs, that are independent of the mesh
resolution, modern filtering techniques are to be used.
The existence of bifurcation points, which lead to that one design can have multiple
feasible phase field solutions, has been a major challenge to treat in this project. As
seen in the presented examples, the optimizer converges towards a limit state and
the objective value starts to oscillate between multiple crack solutions.
Within the time span for this work, it has not been possible to develop a fully ro-
bust formulation where all possible crack patterns are taken into account. Therefore,
none of the stage 2 topics have been explored in this project.
An updated time schedule for the project work is attached.
2Topology optimization methods do generally not guarantee global optimality. Nevertheless,
local optimums are most often improved designs and the method beats a conventional iterative
human-based design approach.
