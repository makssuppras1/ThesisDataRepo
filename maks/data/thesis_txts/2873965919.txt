Abstract
This thesis investigates the effects of precipitation hardening (PH) and subsequent low
temperature gas nitriding on the microstructure and mechanical properties of Nanoflex
precipitation­hardened martensitic stainless steel. PH was performed at 400–600 °C for
4 h after solution treatment at 1150 °C. Additional conditions included solution treatment
at 1050 °C and the as­received cold worked state, both aged at 475 °C for 4 h. For the
cold worked material, an extended PH treatment of 70 h was also studied. Nitriding was
carried out at 430 °C in 40% NH3 / 60% H2 (vol.) for 72 h. Mechanical behavior was
evaluated by tensile testing and Vickers hardness (HV1 in bulk, HV0.002 near surface).
Microstructure and phase characterization were performed using LOM, SEM­EDS, and
XRD.
The highest strength was obtained in the as received condition after PH, with ultimate
tensile strength (UTS) of 2334–2399 MPa and hardness of 640–660 HV. No overaging
was observed even after 70 h. Grain sizes after solution treatment at 1050 °C and 1150
°C were 72 µm and 157 µm, respectively.
Low temperature nitriding produced expanded austenite and a 20 µm case depth, with
near surface hardness reaching 1434 HV0.002 at 30 µm. Bulk hardness remained 640
HV1, indicating that PH occurred simultaneously with nitriding. Local microcracks contain­
ing CrN were observed, which is unexpected below 450 °C and at low nitrogen potentials.
This was attributed to the prolonged nitriding duration.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Precipitation­hardened stainless steels combine high strength with good corrosion re­
sistance [1].These properties are typically achieved through a two­step heat treatment:
solution annealing followed by ageing. During ageing, fine precipitates form within the
microstructure, strengthening the material [2].
Maraging steels, a special subclass, achieve very high strength with minimal loss of duc­
tility [3]. The term ”maraging” derives from ”martensite” and ”ageing,” as these steels are
martensitic and strengthened through ageing rather than by carbon [2].
This study focuses on Nanoflex 1RK91, a Cr–Ni–Mo–Cu–Ti precipitation­hardened stain­
less steel developed for high­performance applications such as medical devices [4]. It
offers a combination of high strength, good ductility, and corrosion resistance compara­
ble to austenitic stainless steels [5].
Understanding how heat treatment affects the microstructure of Nanoflex 1RK91 is essen­
tial to alter its properties. Solution treatment dissolves unwanted phases; ageing promotes
the formation of strengthening precipitates; and nitriding can enhance surface hardness
and wear resistance.
In this work, the effect of different ageing temperatures, and nitriding, on the microstruc­
ture and mechanical properties of Nanoflex 1RK91 is investigated. Characterization was
performed using light optical microscopy (LOM), X­ray diffraction (XRD), hardness and
tensile testing, and scanning electron microscopy (SEM). The aim is to clarify the influ­
ence of thermal treatments on phase transformations and precipitation behaviour in this
alloy.
1.2
Objectives of this study
The primary objective, driven by industrial interest, is to increase the strength of Nanoflex
1RK91 by approximately 30%. Previous studies on similar precipitation­hardened stain­
less steels, including Nanoflex 1RK91, have examined precipitate formation at 475,◦C
and other ageing temperatures [6, 3, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12], hardness evolution [13, 14],
and tensile strength development [15]. Nitriding treatments and their effect on surface
properties have also been explored [16, 17].
While the main focus here is the effect of ageing temperature on mechanical and mi­
crostructural evolution, low­temperature nitriding is also briefly assessed for its potential
as a surface hardening method.
1.3
Scope
This work experimentally investigates the mechanical and microstructural evolution of the
metastable precipitation­hardened stainless steel Nanoflex 1RK91 under different ageing
(tempering) temperatures. The goal is to identify conditions that enhance strength by at
least 30
Scope:
• Ageing treatments in the range 400–600,◦C, in 50,◦C increments, with the addition
of 475 ◦C,based on literature

Literature Review
2.1
Strengthening Mechanisms of Steels
Strengthening steel has been a focus for thousands of years, with archaeological evidence
showing the production of high­carbon steels with useful mechanical properties as early
as the Final Bronze Age (1200–800 BCE) [18]. Modern understanding of strengthening
begins with the crystal structure of metals. Atoms in metals are arranged in an ordered,
repeating lattice, defined by the smallest repeating unit, called the unit cell. Most metals
at room temperature exhibit one of three primary structures: body centered cubic (BCC),
face centered cubic (FCC), or hexagonal close­packed (HCP) (Figure 2.1).
Figure 2.1: Schematic of crystal structures, reproduced from [1].
A collection of unit cells with the same orientation forms a grain, separated from other
grains by grain boundaries. These boundaries, along with other imperfections such as
dislocations, strongly influence mechanical properties. Dislocations are linear defects
that enable plastic deformation at much lower stress than a perfect crystal would require.
Two main types are distinguished:
• Edge dislocations: caused by the termination of an extra half­plane of atoms (Fig­
ure 2.2).
• Screw dislocations: formed by a shear distortion in which atomic planes spiral
around a central line (Figure 2.3).
Figure 2.2: Edge dislocation [19].

The primary strategy to strengthen steels is to hinder dislocation motion. Various mech­
anisms achieve this [20]:
• Grain boundary strengthening
• Solid solution strengthening
• Precipitation hardening
• Dislocation strengthening .
In practice, these mechanisms are often combined to meet application specific require­
ments. In the present study, precipitation hardening, phase transformation, and nitriding
are of primary relevance.
2.2
Precipitation­Hardening Stainless Steels: Composition,
Phases, and Properties
Among the various strategies for strengthening steels, precipitation hardening stands out
as one of the most effective for achieving high strength without severely compromising
ductility or corrosion resistance. In this process, a relatively soft, ductile matrix is first
created, often martensitic or semi­austenitic, through solution treatment and quenching.
Controlled ageing then introduces a fine dispersion of precipitates that act as strong bar­
riers to dislocation motion, increasing the yield and tensile strengths [21].
Several commercial PH stainless steels illustrate the versatility of this approach:
• 17­4 PH: A martensitic stainless steel with about 17 wt.% Cr and 4 wt.% Ni. Aging
produces Cu­rich precipitates, resulting in high strength of 1400 MPa and 470 HV
[22].
• 15­5 PH: Similar to 17­4 PH but offering improved toughness and corrosion resis­
tance, making it suitable for more aggressive environments, reaching hardness of
418 HV after PH. [23].
• 13­8 Mo: A maraging stainless steel strengthened by β­NiAl precipitates, known for
its high­temperature stability, leading to UTS of 100 MPa and 590 HV [24].
• 17­7 PH: A semi­austenitic grade that hardens via transformation of metastable
austenite to martensite, followed by precipitation, leading to 424 HV [25]

• Custom 450: A low­carbon martensitic PH stainless steel that after precipitation
hardening at 565oC for 4hours has a hardness of 375 HV [26]
• Custom 455: Strengthened by Ni3Ti precipitates, and precipitation hardening after
cold rolling leads up to 1900 UTS and 553 HV [27]
Maraging stainless steels form a soft, low­carbon martensitic matrix upon quenching, with
the strength developed during ageing through the precipitation of intermetallic phases
such as Ni3Ti, β­NiAl, and Laves phase Fe2Mo [28]. This mechanism avoids the brittle­
ness often associated with carbon based hardening, enabling a combination of strength
and ductility [9].
The role of alloying elements in PH stainless steels is twofold. To stabilize desired phases
and to form specific strengthening precipitates. For example, nickel not only stabilizes
austenite but also participates in forming intermetallics like Ni3Ti or Ni3Al [11]. Copper
forms nanoscale clusters in alloys like 17­4 PH [22], and aluminum promotes Ni3Al or
β­NiAl formation. Chromium ensures corrosion resistance, while molybdenum improves
strength [7]. Table 2.1 summarizes the function of key alloying elements and the precipi­

2.3
Aging Treatments and Mechanisms of Precipitation
Hardening
Precipitation hardening (age hardening) is a heat treatment process that improves the
strength and hardness of alloys by forming fine, dispersed particles that impede disloca­
tion motion. The primary mechanisms responsible for this strengthening are dislocation
cutting (shearing) and Orowan looping. Shearing occurs when the precipitate radius (rp)
is sufficiently small, allowing dislocations to cut through them. In contrast, the Orowan
mechanism dominates when rp is larger, forcing dislocations to bypass the precipitates
by looping around them [29], as illustrated in Fig. 2.4.
Strengthening of metastable precipitation­hardening stainless steel for biomedical applications

==PAGE:14==
Figure 2.4: Illustration of dislocation being hindered by precipitates, reproduced from [20]
The process typically involves three stages:
1. Solution Treatment – Heating to 1050­1200 ◦C to dissolve alloying elements into
a single­phase solid solution.
2. Quenching – Rapid cooling to retain alloying elements in a metastable, supersatu­
rated solid solution.
3. Aging – Reheating to 400­600 ◦C to allow controlled diffusion and precipitation of
strengthening phases.
In the supersaturated state, alloying elements distort the lattice and contribute to solid
solution strengthening. Upon aging, atomic diffusion produces intermetallic precipitates
that effectively block dislocations. The precipitate type, size, and distribution determine
the mechanical performance. Excessive time or temperature leads to overaging, where
coarser precipitates reduce strength.
Precipitation behavior in Nanoflex
The precipitation sequence in 1RK91 and related maraging stainless steels has been
studied in detail:
• Early­stage precipitation — Stiller et al. [7] showed that Cu­rich clusters form within
minutes at 475 ◦C, due to its very low solobility in Fe [30]. These clusters serve as
nucleation sites for Ni­Ti­Al rich precipitates that still contain Cu, which later develop
into Ni3(Ti,Al) type phases (L phase). The Cu is rejected and forms almost pure Cu,
during the growth of Ni­rich precipitaties [10]. Mo rich phase appear after 4 hours
(R’ phase) at the surfaces of Ni­rich precipitates that hinders their growth. In [9] and
[11], they were not able to detect precipitation of Ni3Ti
• Complex phase evolution — Prolonged aging leads to the appearance of Mo­rich
phases, quasicrystalline R′­phase (icosahedral symmetry), with a composition of
48% Mo ­ 33% Fe ­ 13% Cr ­ 2% Ni ­ 4% Si [9], and Ni­rich L10­ordered phases,
with composition of 9% Fe ­ 4% Cr ­ 52% Ni ­ 15% Mo ­ 16% Ti ­ 4% Al . Liu [14]
and Hättestrand et al. [3] confirmed that the R′­phase can persist for hundreds of
hours before transforming into crystalline R­ or Laves phases, contributing to contin­
uous hardening without rapid overaging. Remarkably, even after 1000 h of aging at
475 °C, the material does not exhibit overaging due to the formation of the R′ phase­
unlike conventional maraging steels, which typically soften after only 4 h [12].

2.4
Nitriding Processes and Their Influence on Steels
Nitriding is a surface modification process in which nitrogen atoms are introduced into
steel at elevated temperatures to enhance hardness, wear resistance, and fatigue life
[33]. Among the various approaches, gaseous nitriding involves introducing nitrogen from
a nitrogen containing gas atmosphere, typically ammonia (NH3) mixed with hydrogen, into
the steel surface. When carried out at elevated temperatures (around 500 ◦C), nitrogen
diffuses into the metal, forming a hardened surface layer known as the case.
In stainless steels, however, chromium reacts with oxygen to form a thin (1­3 nm) chromium
oxide layer that not only improves corrosion resistance but also hinders nitrogen diffu­
sion.
To overcome this barrier, a process called surface activation is performed.
To
avoid chromium nitride precipitation and chromium depletion from the iron matrix, low­
temperature nitriding is often employed [34]. In such treatments, the nitriding potential,
defined as the ratio of the partial pressures of ammonia to hydrogen, plays a critical role
in determining nitrogen uptake, case thickness, and the development of residual stresses
[35].
For Nanoflex 1RK91, low­temperature gaseous nitriding at low nitriding potentials pro­
duces primarily nitrogen expanded martensite (α′N) with little or no formation of nitrogen
expanded austenite (γN) [16]. Nitriding at 420 ◦C for 20 h produced a case thickness
of approximately 20 µm with a surface hardness of about 1300 HV, while the bulk hard­
ness reached 612 HV [36]. At 425 ◦C for 16 h under an infinite nitriding potential, the
surface hardness exceeded 2000 HV to a depth of approximately 20 µm, whereas the
bulk hardness increased from the asreceived condition with 400 HV to 684 HV [35, 37].
According to [37], at higher temperatures (around 450 ◦C) CrN precipitates were ob­
served. However, the formation of CrN depends not only on temperature but also fol­
lows a characteristic C­curve, as shown in Fig. 2.6. With prolonged heat treatment, the
formation of CrN becomes possible [33]. The nitrided layer thickness increased to about
55 µm. Phase analysis at this temperature revealed the presence of expanded austenite
(γN), expanded martensite (α′
N), and ε­phase. At 425 ◦C the nitrided layer was around
20 µm thick, and at lower nitriding potentials mostly expanded martensite was formed. In
these cases, the surface hardness exceeded 2000 HV, while the bulk hardness reached
approximately 700 HV.

3.2.2
Nitriding
Gas nitriding was performed using a thermogravimetric analyzer (TGA) operating at 430,◦C
for 72 hours. The process atmosphere consisted of 40% ammonia and 60% hydrogen by
volume, a composition chosen to reduce the nitrogen potential and thereby limit exces­
sive nitrogen uptake and embrittlement. Following nitriding, one cylindrical specimen was
sectioned longitudinally and subsequently subjected to precipitation hardening at 475,◦C
for 4 hours to examine the combined effect of nitriding and post­treatment precipitation
hardening.
3.3
Cryo Treatments
To ensure the transformation of the material from austenite to martensite, three cryo­
genic treatment methods were used. Since the material has a C­curve transformation
temperature at around ­40 °C, the ideal approach would have been to place the sam­
ples in a climatic chamber and hold the temperature constant, allowing the material to
transform isothermally to martensite. Unfortunately, no such climatic chamber was avail­
able. Therefore, dry ice, at approximately ­80 °C, was selected. Although the dry ice
slowly evaporates, the temperature range during evaporation, theoretically would allow
the material to transform to martensite.
The second method involved immersing the samples directly in liquid nitrogen. The third
method consisted of first immersing the samples in liquid nitrogen, followed by transferring

for martensitic transformation.

3.4.3
X­ray Diffraction (XRD)
X­ray Diffraction (XRD) is a technique used for phase characterization and structural anal­
ysis of crystalline materials. It is based on the elastic scattering of X­rays by the periodic
arrangement of atoms in a crystal. When monochromatic X­rays strike a material, the
electrons in the atoms scatter the radiation without changing its wavelength. In a poly­
crystalline samples, certain crystallographic planes produce constructive interference, re­
sulting in detectable diffraction peaks.
The diffraction condition is described by Bragg’s Law:
nλ = 2dhkl sin θ
(3.2)
where:
n
= diffraction order,
λ
= X­ray wavelength,
dhkl = interplanar spacing,
θ
= Bragg angle.
In a typical laboratory setup, X­rays are generated by bombarding a metal anode with
high­energy electrons. The deceleration of electrons produces Bremsstrahlung radiation,
while electron transitions between atomic shells generate characteristic X­rays. In this
study, a copper (Cu) anode was used, producing Cu Kα radiation.
The X­ray beam is directed at the sample at an angle θ. Diffraction occurs from atomic
planes within the crystal, and the scattered X­rays are detected at the corresponding
angle 2θ. The diffraction pattern, intensity as a function of 2θ, contains peaks at positions
specific to the material’s lattice parameters and symmetry. By scanning over a range of
2θ, multiple sets of planes can be probed.
Phase identification is achieved by comparing the measured peak positions and intensities
with reference data from crystallographic databases.
The XRD analysis was performed using a Bruker AXS instrument. X­rays were generated
with a tube voltage of 40,kV and a current of 40 mA, using a copper target that produced
Cu Kα radiation with a wavelength of 1.5406,Å.
For the initial analysis of the cylindrical samples, an incident beam mirror was placed
in the X­ray path to convert the divergent beam into a parallel beam. The diffraction
range was set from 40° to 85° 2θ to observe the austenite and ferrite phases within the
material. This configuration minimized errors and noise caused by the curved surface of
the samples and reduced the influence of height variations on the measured diffraction
angles. A Soller slit with a divergence of 2.5◦was used to limit axial divergence, thereby
improving peak resolution and reducing background from off­axis scattering. Additionally,
a 0.2 mm incident beam slit was installed to narrow the beam, improving spatial resolution
on the sample and further reducing background intensity.

The XRD data were analyzed using EVA software for peak identification. Subsequently,
the Topas application was used to quantify the volume fraction of each phase, applying
R­values reported in the literature [39].
Following solution treatment and precipitation hardening, the range was set for 30° to 85°
to enable identification of precipitates. The flat surfaces of the samples were analyzed
using the Bragg Brentano geometry. In this arrangement, the sample lies on a focusing
beam, and the incident angle θ and detector angle 2θ are varied synchronously to satisfy
Bragg’s law. However, due to the drawing process, the material exhibited a preferred
grain orientation (texture), which limited the number of detectable diffraction peaks.
To overcome this limitation, a point focus setup was used, allowing variation of the sample
tilt angle ψ from 0◦to 70◦in 10◦increments. The resulting spectra from all ψ angles were
combined to produce a composite diffraction pattern that incorporated reflections from a
broader range of crystallographic planes.
The plots that can be seen in this report are generated using OriginPro software [40].

The Young’s modulus corresponds to the slope of the stress–strain curve in the elastic
region. At the yield strength, σy0, the material exceeds its elastic limit, beyond which
deformation becomes irreversible and deforms plastically.
By the time a material reaches its yield point, it has already experienced some plastic
deformation. Therefore, when determining the Young’s modulus, strain values are offset
by 0.2% from the linear portion, creating a parallel line. The intersection of this offset line
with the original stress–strain curve defines the yield point.
The identification of the Young’s modulus, yield point, ultimate tensile strength (UTS) and
toughness was performed using a MATLAB script, (see Appendix A.1). For tensile testing,
specimens are prepared in a “dog­bone” geometry, where the middle section is narrower
than the ends. This design ensures that fracture occurs within the gauge length, enabling
accurate strain measurement.

To obtain the most accurate Young’s modulus, the best approach is to load the sample

should closely match the slope from the loading segment. For this experiment, only one

4.5
Discussion
4.5.1
As Received Condition (AR)
The as received samples showed an extremely fine microstructure. Based on LOM (Fig. 4.1)
and SEM (Fig. 4.9), the grain size was below 2 µm. Both characterizations revealed a
difference in the 4 mm specimens, which showed a more wavy pattern, likely due to dif­
ferences in thermomechanical history. In the LOM images of the as received material
(Fig. 4.1), the ratio of austenite to martensite appeared similar. XRD measurements con­
firmed retained austenite fractions of 16%, 26%, and 24% for the 3 mm, 4 mm, and 6 mm
samples, respectively (Fig. 4.23). The 3 mm specimens contained the lowest retained
austenite fraction.
Since the material was cold drawn, reducing the initial diameter induced higher mechani­
cal stresses, which transformed austenite into martensite through strain­induced marten­
sitic transformation [36]. An increase in equivalent strain is therefore associated with an
increase in the martensite fraction.
The mechanical properties of the as received samples reflected these microstructural
differences. The UTS was 1556.7 MPa and 1459.2 MPa for the 3 mm and 4 mm speci­
mens, respectively. The average hardness was 416.7, 397.6, and 410.8 HV for the 3 mm,
4 mm, and 6 mm samples, respectively. These results are consistent with the XRD data
(Fig. 4.23), showing that hardness increases as the retained austenite fraction decreases.
4.5.2
Solution and Cryogenic treatmenns
In the attempt to do a cryo treatment of Liquid Nitrogen and Dry ice in the as received ma­
terials, as seen Fig 4.24, 4.31, the peaks of austenite did not show much difference. This
is most likely attributed to the austenite being mechanically stabilized by the surrounding
martensite and the fine microstructure of the material. Consistent with prior work [32], the
kinetics of martensitic transformation strongly depend on the microstructural state. Larger
austenite grains accelerate isothermal martensite formation, likely via a higher density of
quenched in vacancies. The same can be seen in Fig 4.40, where the hardness is almost
identical of the as received and the as received after the cryo treatment.
After solution treatment at 1150 °C, lath martensite formed upon natural cooling (Fig­
ure 4.3). The material exhibited a magnetic response upon removal from the furnace,
consistent with the presence of martensite. As noted by Holmquist et al. [32], isothermal
martensite can also form slowly at room temperature (see Figure 2.5). The 1050 °C solu­
tion treatment produced a markedly finer microstructure with grain size ≈72 µm than the
1150 °C treatment (≈157 µm). For comparison, Holmquist et al. [32] reported grain sizes
of ≈23 µm at 1050 °C and ≈128 µm at 1150 °C.
In the cryo treated conditions DI and LN DI , lath martensite is more evident across a
larger number of grains (Figure 4.3. In the XRD patterns (Figure 4.28), the LN DI se­
quence yields the most complete transformation to martensite. Immersion in liquid ni­
trogen appears to promote additional nucleation sites for the martensitic transformation.
The hardness results (Figure 4.39) show that cryogenic treatments increase hardness,
with no distinct difference between DI and LN DI. A slight shift in 2θ is also observed, Fig.
4.28. Because the grain size is relatively large, this shift does not necessarily indicate a
change in macroscopic residual stress. More likely, it reflects limited grain statistics (few
grains contributing to a given reflection) and local stress variations that alter the Bragg
angle. Overall, cryogenic treatment after solution treatment is effective in completing the
martensitic transformation, where condition LN DI seems the better approach,
The XRD analysis, Figure 4.25, also indicates a strong crystallographic texture, where

4.5.3
Precipitation hardening (PH) at 400­600 °C, 4h
The average hardness follows the expected precipitation hardening trend (Fig. 4.41).
At 400 °C, the hardness is similar to the as received condition, whereas at 500 °C it
decreases. Notably, there is an anomaly at 475 °C with a dip in hardness, which was
also reproduced in the tensile clamp pieces (Fig. 4.43). The same trend is evident in the
clamp pieces, although higher absolute hardness values were measured. This increase
may result from the tensile test (compression by the grips causing local work hardening)
or from the smaller size during precipitation hardening, where the speciments heated up
faster.
In [9], aging at 475 °C for 4 h produced a hardness of approximately 640 HV (HV1),
compared with 470 HV (HV1) obtained in this study. Note that no solution treatment was
described in that work. Their samples supplied as wire (0.9 mm diameter) after 91 %
area reduction, and no overaging was reported even after 1000 h of aging at 475 °C. The
toughness of the specimens also show some discrepancies where the 3mm shows the
least toughtness at 450 °C whereas the 4mm at 475 °C, (Fig. 4.36).
However, both the 3 and 4 mm samples showed a peak in tensile strength at 475 ° C (Fig,
4.36a), followed by a decrease at higher temperatures. This indicates that tensile strength
peaks at 475 °C, despite the hardness showed lower values at 475 ° C compared to the
neighboring temperatures.
The increase in austenite is evident in light optical microscopy (LOM) and X­ray diffraction
(XRD) with increasing precipitation hardening (PH) ageing temperature, indicating that
reverted austenite forms even at 500 °C. Revert austenite is a diffusional mechanicanism
that happens, when alloy elements like Ni that are austenite stabilizers, cluster, providing
a local thermodynamic force that enables the reversion of the marttensite to austenite.
Undentified peaks for the precipitation hardening diffractions at arround 36 ° and 42.6 ° is
estimated that are originate from the holder that was used rather than new phases, Fig.
4.32.
Althought, some discrepancies exist between the peak of the UTS and hardness, the in­
crease in following the precipitation hardening at 475 °C is clear. As discussed [7], the
formation of Cu clusters at the early stage, creates nucleation sites for Ni3(Ti,Al) phases,
along with the nucleation of Mo­rich phases after 4h hours of aging contributing to hard­
ening the material, as seen in Fig.4.36a. We cannot yet distinguish whether the softening
is driven primarily by precipitate coarsening or by the formation of reverted austenite at ≥
500 °C, as indicated by XRD (Figure 4.30).
4.5.4
Solution treatment for different temperatures with precipitation
hardening at 475°C
The lower austenitizing temperature (1050 ◦C) produced a finer grain size compared to
1150 ◦C. Both conditions were aged at 475 ◦C for 4 h. In tensile tests, the 4 mm specimens
solution treated at 1050 ◦C showed the expected increase in UTS relative to the 1150 ◦C
treatment, by approximately 14%. The 3 mm specimens did not reproduce this trend,
showing a slightly lower UTS than their 1150 ◦C counterparts, around –7% (Figure 4.37).
The most plausible explanation is specimen­to­specimen variability (e.g., defects). With
a larger number of tests, the 3 mm specimens would likely follow the same trend as the
4 mm specimens. Since solution treatment at lower temperatures produces smaller grain

4.5.5
As Receved PH at 475 °C (4 h and 70 h)
The largest increases in tensile strength and hardness were obtained in the as received
specimens after precipitation hardening, that is, without prior solution treatment, cryogenic
treatment, or additional aging steps, similar to the results in [9, 5]. The UTS reached
2334 MPa and 2399 MPa after precipitation hardening at 475 ◦C for 4 h and 70 h, re­
spectively, with no evidence of overaging at 70 h. This stability may be attributed to Mo
precipitating at the surfaces of Ni­rich particles, hindering their growth [9]. The hardness
reached 640 HV and 660 HV, consistent with previous studies [9].
Tensile strength increased by 49.9% and hardness by 56.1% compared to the as re­
ceived state due to precipitation hardening in the cold worked materials. In contrast, the
sequence of solution and cryogenic treatment resulted in lower mechanical properties,
likely due to coarser grains. The fine microstructure produced by cold working, even with
retained austenite, yielded extremely high mechanical properties.
4.5.6
Nitriding and Nitriding following PH
Hardness measurements using HV1 in the bulk of the samples (Fig. 4.45) showed that
both nitrided and nitrided + PH samples had similar hardness values to the precipita­
tion hardening as received condition (647, 643, 642 HV). This suggests that precipitation
hardening occurred during nitriding itself, as also reported in [36]. A slight decrease in
hardness was observed in the samples subjected to precipitation hardening after nitrid­
ing.
XRD patterns (Fig. 4.33) revealed that peaks after precipitation hardening (N PH) became
narrower, without expanded martensite being detected, resembling the as received condi­
tion (AR). The sample for the N PH condition was sectioned longitudinal from the nitrided
sample, after the nitriding, and the surface that was investigated was the flat sectioned
surface. Expanded austenite would not be expected in that surface, as it is indicated in
the the xrd diffraction. Formation of expanded austenite was evident on the sample after
nitriding (N). Peaks broadened and shifted to lower angles, consistent with [36, 42]. No
evidence of expanded martensite was found, unlike prior studies. Since the as received
material contained retained austenite, nitrogen most likely diffused into this phase, which
is thermodynamically favorable, instead of expanding martensite [16].
Both samples exhibited surface cracks, though they were discontinuous (Fig. 4.16). EDS
analysis (Figs. 4.17, 4.19) showed chromium and nitrogen along the cracks. At 430 ◦C,
CrN formation is not usually expected [16, 36, 42, 17] at low nitriding potential. However,
in the disc samples, a 20 µm case was clearly visible (Fig. 4.21), enriched in Cr and N
(Fig. 4.22). Oxygen detected in Fig. 4.22 was most likely due to contamination through
surface cracks. TiN was also visible in both EDS (Fig. 4.22) and LOM (Fig. 4.8).
Surface hardness tests using HV0.002 showed 872 HV for nitrided and 737 HV for nitrided
+ PH, compared to 500 HV for the as received state. Disc samples showed much higher
hardness, reaching 1434 HV0.002 even at 30 µm below the surface. Because of the
very small indents produced with 0.002 kgf load, impressions had to be measured with
LOM, which might lead to inaccurate results. For full comparison, AR + PH should also
be measured, as it is expected to give similar values to the nitrided + PH sample. In [17],
nitrided layers reached 1000–1400 HV0.01 at 400–500 ◦C. In [16], values of 1300–1500

5
Future Work
5.1
Suggestions for Further Research
Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analysis of precipitate formation is not required,
as it has already been reported in the literature. However, further investigation of pre­
cipitation hardening as a function of time, rather than only temperature, could provide
valuable insights for optimizing the heat treatment process.
Analyzing solution treatments at lower temperatures using XRD would also be beneficial
to better quantify the martensite­to­austenite ratio in finer­grained microstructures. This
could help interpret the tensile test results of precipitation­hardened specimens at 475 °C,
where the 3 mm and 4 mm samples yielded different outcomes.
Additionally, investigating the potential transformation of reverted austenite back to marten­
site would be of interest. The unidentified peaks observed in the XRD results also warrant
further investigation. To verify their origin, the base material should be scanned sepa­
rately, as well as the carbon tape used to hold the sample, while varying the ψ angle.
5.2
Improvements in Methodology
Further hardness testing of the nitrided and nitrided + PH samples using higher test loads
(kgf) could simplify the identification of surface hardness. Alternatively, performing inden­
tation measurements directly in the SEM could improve accuracy.
For tensile testing, a lower strain rate than 0.5 mm/min is recommended, as fluctuations
were observed in the initial portion of the stress–strain curve.
For the XRD analysis, it would be beneficial to section the samples to a greater length
and perform the analysis using the mirror setup on the curved surface, rather than relying
on point focus with varying ψ angles.
