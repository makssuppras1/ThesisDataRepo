Abstract
This master thesis deals with the evaluation of the current state of the art for numerically estimating
the extreme wave and current loading on an oﬀshore wind turbine installation vessel, in comparison
with standard modeling approaches. The in-line hydrodynamic load distribution along one of the
legs of the ship was calculated by using the Morison’s equation, as well as its extended version with
higher-order terms, which proved to be insigniﬁcant for this application. Two hydrodynamic models
of the truss structure were built and compared: one model considering the leg as an equivalent
cylindrical column, and a detailed model of the arrangement of the chords and bracings taken
as Morison elements. The inclusion of marine growth on the tubes showed to increase the drag
action (by at least 34%, depending on the leg model). Regarding the environmental conditions,
the most suitable model of the steady current turned out to be a power-law velocity proﬁle, which
was added up to the wave kinematics. The wave system was simulated by periodic waves, on
one hand, with linear and stream function wave models, and long-crested irregular waves, on the
other hand, consisting of ﬁrst-order irregular waves, embedded stream function, and fully-nonlinear
waves. The latter were extracted from a compilation of many simulations of DTU’s potential-ﬂow
CFD code, which was run over a wide span of non-dimensional water depths and signiﬁcant wave
heights. The highest waves induced the most onerous forces on the leg, regardless of the steepness
of the wave elevation. Nonetheless, the nonlinear waves caused the greater actions on the highest
submerged sections. The detailed leg model predicted larger loads than the equivalent leg model,
notably for the sections located in the vicinity of the free-surface when the crest passes through
the leg. Furthermore, a pressure impulse (PIMP) model was tried to quantify the slamming load
contribution in a presumably breaking wave event. Further analyses could involve tests to assess
the validity of the application of the PIMP model on a jack-up leg.

INTRODUCTION
The company Swire Blue Ocean (SBO) operates two of the largest oﬀshore wind turbine installation
ships working in the North Sea. Those self-elevating units have six legs penetrating the seabed and
lifting the hull out of the water before operations. The airgap reaches up to 25 meters, in order to
attain a heavy storm survival conﬁguration. Figure 1.1 shows a picture of one of SBO’s vessels in
a jack-up conﬁguration.
Figure 1.1: Photograph of SBO’s vessel Paciﬁc Osprey in operating conditions for installation of
a wind turbine (Source: Swire Blue Ocean’s website)
One of the most crucial components of a jack-up vessel is the leg and jacking system.
It not
only supports the weight of the whole vessel in the elevated condition, but also withstands various
external forces, of which the current, ocean wave, and wind induced forces are the most important
loading contributions. Quantifying such forces is required to assess the structural integrity and
stability of the vessels.
When analyzing the environmental conditions, a lot of factors govern the kinematics of combined
ocean waves and currents. Modeling all of them involves numerical complexities and expensive com-
putations. Approximations are generally made according to standard procedures and regulations
in order to assess the inertia and drag forces on the jack-up legs. Empirical values and formulas

As noticed in Faltinsen (1990), the ﬂuid velocity is in the wave propagation direction under the

The linear theory assumes the velocity potential and ﬂuid velocity to be constant from the mean

diﬀerence between the horizontal velocity at the wave trough and the analytical ﬁctitious horizontal
velocity at z = 0 is small compared with the velocity itself.
Irregular Airy waves
The stochastic approach consists of choosing a number of wave components from a predeﬁned
wave energy spectrum (see Section 2.1.3) and adding them up with random phases, to generate a
realization of a few hours of time series with Wheeler stretching (deﬁned later in Section 3.2.1).
The process can then be extended for a non-linear approach.
2.1.2
Higher order wave theories
The linear ﬁrst order theory represents an approximation in satisfying the free-surface kinematic
and dynamic conditions on the mean position of the free-surface. Furthermore, the ﬂuid pressure
and the velocity of ﬂuid particles on the free-surface are linearized.
A higher-order error can be noticed under the trough regarding the pressure condition at the free-
surface: the hydrostatic pressure −ρgz does not exactly cancel the dynamic pressure −ρ∂ϕ/∂t. The
error is approximately proportional to Hn where the order n ≥2. It can signiﬁcantly over-predict
the air gap between the waves and a platform deck.
This can be improved by introducing higher order terms in a Stokes expansion. Second order theory
means that we keep all terms proportional to (H/λ)2 and H/λ in the velocity potential, pressure
ﬁeld and wave loads. This model approximates more accurately the ﬂuid pressure being equal to
atmospheric pressure on the instantaneous free-surface. Moreover, it includes nonlinearities in the
velocity of ﬂuid particles on the free-surface.
The solution of the second-order problem results in a sum of a mean force and forces oscillating
with diﬀerence and sum of the frequency components used in describing the wave spectrum, in
addition to the linear solution. Mean and slowly varying wave loads (e.g. diﬀerence frequency
loads) are of importance for example for analysis of loading of moored oﬀshore structures, because
they can excite the resonance oscillations in the surge, sway and yaw motion.

As stated in Fenton (1988), a limitation to the use of Stokes and cnoidal theories is that they are
not accurate for all waves. Stokes 5th-order theory breaks down in shallow water, cnoidal theory
in deep water, and both for high waves. Stokes 5th-order method only gives reliable results in
deep water and was extensively used in the 1980s, because it was computationally very cheap. But
nowadays, with the development of more powerful computers, a stream function can be calculated
quite quickly and provides better accuracy in shallow water.
Nonlinear waves are characterized by a wave crest height larger than the wave trough height, i.e.
the distance from SWL to crest is larger than the distance from SWL to trough. Crests are steeper
and troughs are wider than for Airy waves. The phase velocity also depends on the wave height.

2.2.1
Morison equation
The conventional approach for calculating wave and current in-line forces on cylinders is the use
of Morison’s equation, which is a semi-theoretical and semi-empirical formula. It is applicable for

submerged slender bodies having small cross-sectional dimensions compared with the wave length.
In practice, λ > 5Di must be satisﬁed, with Di the reference dimension of the member. This
condition ensures that the body has no signiﬁcant eﬀect on the wave motion.
Morison’s equation is expressed as in Equation (2.31).
As mentioned in Manners and Rainey
(1992), the most basic feature of Morison’s equation is that the forces are calculated by using the
velocities and acceleration that would occur in the ﬂuid at the position of the cylinder’s centerline
if the ﬂow were not disturbed by the presence of the structure. In this theory the ﬂuid is assumed

The Reynolds number characterizes the importance of the inertia eﬀects over the viscous eﬀects in
the ﬂuid ﬂow. It is a key parameter to determine the resistance of a body to a ﬂow.
For a smooth cylinder, CD ﬁrst increases with Re increasing, and then it is decreasing tending

point to the separation point, which leads to a wide wake. After the turning point, the resistance
decreases because the boundary layer becomes turbulent earlier, in front of the stagnation point.
The presence of turbulence in the negative pressure gradient region before the separation point
causes a narrowing of the wake and a reduction of the resistance coeﬃcient toward a constant
value.
The Keulegan-Carpenter number is the ratio between the ﬂuid advection length and the charac-
teristic size of the body in an oscillating ﬂow ﬁeld; it determines which phenomenon prevails. As
stated in Sulaiman, Hofman, and Wallenburg (2017), for a vertical circular cylinder in oscillatory
ﬂow at low KC, the typical particle advection length is relatively small compared to the section
diameter. The large recirculation zones (where vortex shedding appears) encountered in the case
of a steady current condition do not have enough time to build up within one oscillation cycle.
In this case, the behavior is inertia-dominated. On the other hand, at high KC, there will be
enough time within one oscillation cycle for the vortex shedding to occur, leading to the formation
of a vortex-shedding street. In this case, the behavior is drag-dominated.

REVIEW OF THE CODES AND STANDARDS FOR
MODELLING JACK-UP VESSELS
Three main documents are referred to for the rules and standards relative to the site speciﬁc
assessment of jack-up vessels operations in the North Sea. The international standard ISO-19905-1
(2016) 19905-1 gives guidance for the site-speciﬁc assessment (SSA) of jack-up units. SNAME-
T&R5-5A (2002) completes the ISO standards and gives recommended practices for the SSA of
mobile jack-up units. Additionnally, more guidance on the environmental loads and conditions
is provided by DNV-RP-C205 (2007). Those rules are currently in use in the industry to assess
jack-up operations.
3.1
Environmental conditions
As a starting point for an SSA, three types of data shall be collected:
• Rig data
• Site data, including seabed topography. The water depth shall be referenced to the Lowest
Astronomical Tide (LAT). The mean water level (MWL) related to the seabed shall be
expressed as the mean level between highest astronomical tide (HAT) and LAT.
• Environmental data, including wave, wind and current data, as well as tides, rate of marine
growth, icing, sea and air temperature, earthquake, etc.
The directionality of the wind, waves and current may be accounted for if accurate data is available
and provided that such directionality persists for the site under consideration.
50-year return period individual extremes are recommended to be used to assess the Ultimate
Limit States (ULS) of manned jack-ups. This is in an extreme storm event assessment. Applicable
seasonal data may be utilized (e.g. summer storm), if the jack-up operation is of limited duration.
Based on SNAME-T&R5-5A (2002), the airgap is deﬁned as the distance between the underside
of the hull and the lowest astronomical tide (LAT) during operations. The minimum airgap for an
intended operation should be calculated based upon a return period storm of 50 years.
3.2
Hydrodynamic loading
Two conﬁgurations of the vessel are generally analysed: operating and storm survival. During
storm survival conﬁguration, it is mandatory that the crane is lowered down in its rest position
and not operating.
Wind, waves and current are typically considered to act simultaneously and in the same direction,
as a ﬁrst conservative approach.

3.4
Dynamic analysis of a jack-up leg
The jack-up’s legs are generally assumed to be pinned at the position of the spudcan. Choosing
this reaction point is a conservative approach for calculating the bending moment in the leg. The
most realistic (and conservative) model of the leg is to have it ﬁxed at the leg-to-hull connection.
In reality, the legs have rotational stiﬀness from the soil (so something between pinned and ﬁxed).
In dynamic eﬀects, the structure will vibrate at its natural period if excited by the forces of ocean
waves. The magnitude of this vibration is primarily inﬂuenced by the amount of wave energy at or
near the natural period of the structure and to a lesser extent by the wave energy at other periods.

of the mass and acceleration of the system. The total load on the system is the combination of static
and inertial components. The direct calculation and application of the inertial loads is preferable
to the application of a Dynamic Ampliﬁcation Factor (DAF). The dynamic response of the

particularly important for seastates having signiﬁcant energy near the natural period of the jack-up

4.1
Motivations
A lot of factors govern the kinematics of waves. Modelling all of them involves numerical complex-
ities and expensive computations.
In the standard analysis procedures, the characteristics of real oceans waves are not explicitly
represented. In the standard procedure recommended by the design and operational guidelines,
wave kinematics are deterministically computed by modelling regular and unidirectional (2D) wave
theories, such as the Stokes 5th order. However, oﬀshore structures are subjected to random waves
coming from diﬀerent directions. The conventional approach deﬁnes a kinematics reduction factor
accounting for the directional spreading eﬀects (3D). But the variation of crest height in a direction
perpendicular to the wave is neglected.
4.2
Geometry of jack-up legs
Typical jack-up vessels consist of a buoyant hull resting on four or six independent truss-work legs,
with the weight of the deck and appendages more or less equally distributed. The most popular
foundation types for jack-up units is individual footings supported type, called spudcans, because
of its easy installation.
Various types of jack-up legs have been created and especially the cylindrical and trussed types
are widely applied in the oﬀshore industry. A trussed jack-up leg is usually designed with three
cylindrical shape chords or four triangular shape chords, which are connected through bracing
members. Mainly, there are three trusswork conﬁgurations for the bracing members, namely, X-
type, reverse K-type and diamond-shaped type, as pictured on Figure 4.1.

4.3
Hydrodynamic properties of jack-up elements
For the assessment of hydrodynamic loads on jack-up platforms and vessels, SNAME-T&R5-5A
2002 and DNV-RP-C205 (2007) provide a useful empirical approach. This approach was mainly
developed in the 90s of last century, based on wind tunnel measurement data and by adding
experience-based safety factors for conservative design. Although this method is simple and easy,
drag forces calculated by the SNAME-recommended practice diﬀer notably in some reported cases
from those obtained from wind tunnel tests, as noted in Lee et al. (2009). The load results can
be conservative, leading to unnecessary deviation from the optimal design and operation of the
structure, and introducing a bias on the platform risk assessment and stability analysis. Moreover,
wind tunnel tests can only account for steady current situations and not wave oscillatory ﬂow
situations. Therefore, some uncertainty exists in wave force calculation in the SNAME approach.
Yet, for a jack up in survival condition design, the wave force will dominate the hydrodynamic forces.
The magnitude of wave loads on jack-up legs due to large wave oscillatory ﬂow can be assessed
experimentally through wave tank tests or numerically through CFD simulation techniques. For
model tests, although it used to be considered as the most reliable approach in the past, the scale
eﬀect problem is still a major uncertainty in load prediction for jack-ups prototypes.
As noticed in Mirzadeh, Kimiaei, and Cassidy 2016, jack-up platforms are drag dominated. The
response is sensitive to the kinematics at the crest, as the horizontal drag forces are maximum at
the crest.
Xie et al. 2018 have carried out tests on a model-scale truss-type jack-up leg in a water tank, in
order to ﬁnd the CD and CM coeﬃcients for this leg, under wave and current conditions. It was
seen that CD values under four diﬀerent incidence angles (0◦, 30◦, 45◦, and 90◦) were all in the
range of [0.7, 1.0]. With increasing Re in the range [1 · 104, 7 · 104], CD ﬁrstly declined and then
increased and ﬁnally declined. It was seen that CM value is located in the range [1.63, 1.99] and
CM decreases with KC increasing in the range [1, 6].
Tian et al. (2019) focused on the leg chord and performed reduced-scale model tests in a wave
tank, to experimentally investigate the eﬀects of the rack and the incident wave orientation on the
hydrodynamic coeﬃcients:
• CD value increases a lot for the model cylinder with a rack, compared to the
smooth cylinder model.
This is attributed to an enlargement of the shadow area in
the direction perpendicular to the current velocity under the same experimental conditions.
Moreover, the rack has corners and edges, which brings about water ﬂow separation and
vortex shedding occurring under any Reynolds number. The results also show that, compared
to CD, the CM value is less inﬂuenced by both the projected area and the shape of the model
perpendicular to the ﬂow direction. Therefore, the presence of a rack has little inﬂuence on
the inertia part of the force.
• The incidence angle not only aﬀects the shadow area, but also alters the drag coeﬃcient. CD
linearly decreases with incident angle increasing, and is the smallest for 90◦, for which
angle it is very similar to the smooth cylinder model without rack. For this orientation, the
drag coeﬃcient can mainly be attributed to the cylinder part. For the 0◦orientation, the
drag coeﬃcient should be similar to the one for a ﬂat plate. The incidence angle has little

4.4
Challenging the standard wave theories
In recent years, studies were performed to compute more accurately the hydrodynamic loading
and response of jack-up structures to extreme wave systems.
In Mirzadeh, Kimiaei, and Cassidy (2016), linear NewWave and linear Constrained NewWave
theories were used to investigate the eﬀects of wave directionality and the random nature of ocean
waves on the performance of an example jack-up platform. Both 2D, i.e. unidirectional wave with
kinematics reduction factor, and 3D waves, were applied numerically on the structure. A ﬁrst-
order water surface and kinematics model were used. The response of the structure was analysed
by Finite Element Method (FEM). The inclusion of 3D eﬀects in the waves showed a
26

reduction of the hydrodynamic forces when a wave crest passes through the structure.
A decrease of the response statistics - especially for the displacements at the jack-up hull and
leeward spudcan - was observed. The eﬀect of wave spreading on the response is more
pronounced at low wave periods. This is because the crest width of the 3D waves decreases
with a lower wave period. The 3D CNW model predicts diﬀerent water surface elevations on the
windward legs, which can cause more out of plane and torsional vibrations in the structure than
the model using 3D NewWave. The wave force in the perpendicular direction is not zero which is
due to the asymmetric water surface of the 3D CNW. Finally, the results reﬂect that the governing
mode of failure diﬀers as the wave direction is altered.
A jack-up leg modelled by an equivalent hydrodynamic column is not so diﬀerent from the stiﬀ
bottom-ﬁxed monopile of an oﬀshore wind turbine (OWT), with a lower equivalent diameter though.
For the design of an OWT, the standards generally recommend to calculate the extreme waves
using the stream function wave theory embedded into a linear irregular time series. The wave
period of the stream function should be the period which gives the largest load. This method is
called Embedded Stream Function (ESF).
However, Schløer, Bredmose, and Ghadirian (2017) showed that force peaks on a cylindrical
monopile depend also on the wave front slope, and are not only a function of the
wave height. The largest force on the OWT can be due to a wave with a smaller wave height and
amplitude but with a very steep wave crest. They developed a method, so-called NewForce wave
model, to predict the force time history under extreme unidirectional wave. The NewWave theory
principle is re-applied here to obtain the expected force history around a force peak from the linear
force spectrum. A second-order NewForce version was also built by add-on of the second-order
terms. They found that:
• With nonlinear waves, the expected linear behaviour of force peaks being proportional to the
wave height does not hold. The dependence to h/(gT 2
p ) cannot be ignored.
• The closer the sea states are to the wave breaking limit Hb the larger the force-peaks are.
This is because the shape of the surface elevation becomes steeper when the waves are closer
to breaking, which results in larger forces1.
• Wave nonlinearity increases with decreasing water depth. The more shallow the
more asymmetric the wave shapes become. The second-order NewForce model provides a
good estimate of the expected force shape for waves of moderate nonlinearity.
• Slamming loads could be observed for the largest force levels as a distinct hat on top of the
smoother underlying force curve. Enhanced prediction of the force shapes of more nonlinear
waves may be obtained by incorporating a wave model more accurate than second order.

SCOPE OF THE STUDY AND GENERAL
METHODOLOGY
Three areas of investigation have been identiﬁed:
• Equivalent versus detailed truss model for Morison elements: the standard approach
for calculating the hydrodynamic loading on the legs relies on an equivalent model, combining
the hydrodynamic properties of the elements section per section to obtain an equivalent
cylinder with equivalent hydrodynamic properties.
SBO is interested in challenging this
model and compare it with a detailed model where each component of the leg is modelled as
a Morison element. The purpose is to investigate whether some optimisation and operational
savings can be achieved in relation to the legs of SBO vessels.
• Inﬂuence of marine growth: biofouling increases the eﬀective diameter and roughness of
the leg elements, which results in larger hydrodynamic loads. Since the operations of SBO
vessels are of short duration, biofouling does not have time to settle on the legs. The obser-
vation of the legs showed that there is very little accumulation of biomass on the members.
While calculations generally include a standard marine growth thickness and recommend
using drag coeﬃcients for rough cylinders, it is relevant here to quantify the variation of the
hydrodynamic load when marine growth is neglected.
• Fully non-linear wave kinematics: in the standards, the sea environment is generally
modelled with partially nonlinear waves (Stokes ﬁfth-order waves or Stream Function). Such
waves are symmetrical around the peak. Recent studies at the Department of Wind Energy,
DTU, have studied the fully nonlinear wave loading and the impact of very steep waves on
OWT. This type of waves is more likely to appear in shallow water depth, and those conditions
are sometimes experienced during SBO’s vessels operations.
To the author’s knowledge,
those fully nonlinear wave models have never been tried on jack-up structures. The objective
is therefore to study the characteristics of a realistic wave loading for the ship in jack-up
condition.
The current study will ﬁrst reproduce the calculations of the equivalent leg model and then chal-
lenge this by confrontation with a detailed model of the bay.
For both models, the following
metocean conditions will be considered, in this order:
• Steady current alone
This will be a preliminary and simple model to compute the drag force on the leg, subject to
a current ﬂow solely. Three diﬀerent proﬁles will be considered to account for the variation
of the velocity with water depth:
– uniform current velocity proﬁle
– linear velocity proﬁle, suggested in SNAME-T&R5-5A (2002)
– power-law velocity proﬁle, recommended in DNV-RP-C205 (2007) and in site-speciﬁc
Dubonnet et al. (2017)
• Waves alone
It will be assumed that the leg is a ﬁxed structure, not moving. To the experience of SBO’s
engineering department, the truss structure is very stiﬀ. Including the motion of the leg

For this study, it is assumed that the vessel operates in ﬁnite water depth h = 30m. An airgap of
a = 15m is assumed between the MSL and the bottom of the hull.
7.1
Fluid parameters
The density and kinematic viscosity of salt water are chosen for a temperature of 10◦C and with
a salinity of 3.5%: ρ = 1027kg·m−3 and ν = 1.35 · 10−6 m2·s−1, based on Faltinsen (1990).
7.2
Marine growth
Marine growth - or biofouling in biological words - refers to the growth and biomass accumula-
tion of microorganisms, seaweed and sessile fauna on submerged structures. Biofouling is highly
undesirable because it adds to drag on vessels and ﬁxed structures in the marine environment, by
increasing both the roughness and diameter of the structure. According to Dubonnet et al. (2017),
for North Sea oﬀshore platforms, blue mussels will dominate the upper 20-25m provided that the
coast is within 30-50km from the structure (which is most generally the case for oﬀshore wind
farms). Below this mussel belt, non-shelled organisms will be early colonizers that subsequently
will be replaced by sea anemones after 2-3 years. Maximum thickness of mussel growth is 15-20cm
at the upper 20m of structure. Thickness of soft marine growth below the mussel belt is 5-10cm.
However those data need to be contrasted in the context of operation of WIV. The vessel is in
elevated conﬁguration above the sea level only during a couple of days generally. The legs are
only partially submerged and for short time periods. Therefore, the eﬀect of marine growth on
the hydrodynamics is not as serious as for the hull of ships and permanent oﬀshore structures.
For those reasons, in what follows and unless explicitly mentioned, a thickness of marine growth
of tm = 12.5mm is added on the outer surface of all elements of the legs (chords, bracings, span
breakers, etc.), except for the gusset plates, as it is recommended in SNAME-T&R5-5A (2002).
Moreover, the Danish Dansk Standard (1984) indicate that the hydraulic roughness of the marine
growth may be assumed to be approximately equal to its thickness but should not exceed k =
10 · 10−3m. For elements of the leg which are not aﬀected by marine growth, a surface roughness
of k = 5 · 10−6m is assumed for painted steel tubulars, from DNV-RP-C205 (2007) guidance.

Methodology
The method of computation of the current load is more complex than for the ELM; it was performed
as follows:
1. Specify the metocean conditions: the water depth h, the extreme current velocity components

3. Generate the current velocity proﬁle uC(z) (uniform, SNAME-recommended or from Hol-

In practice, it seems that, for rough elements (i.e. for a typical element in the leg with marine

compared with SNAME value of 0.65 for a smooth cylinder. Thereby, it seems clear that,
given the ﬂow conditions, there is room for optimization in the choice of the hydrodynamic
coeﬃcients of the elements of the leg, with ﬁnal aim to reduce the loads.

10.1
Methodology
1. Deﬁne the metocean conditions, as prescribed in Chapter 7.
2. Build the wave model, being it regular or irregular.
3. Extract the wave kinematics: η(x, t), u(x, z, t), w(x, z, t), ˙u(x, z, t), ∂w
∂z (x, z, t).
4. Import the hydrodynamic leg model: detailed or equivalent model. When the load is modeled
with a regular wave model, like it is the case for the Dean’s stream function method, the drag
coeﬃcient is reduced by 30% before computing the load, as recommended in Section 3.2.1,
to account for the conservatism of a deterministic analysis.
5. Deﬁne the grid points at which the load distribution must be computed. The wave kinematics
is evaluated at those points if an explicit formulation is known - which is the case for the
regular wave models (Airy or Stream Function) - or, in a stochastic analysis, the kinematics
is interpolated at the grid points from previous computations, using a cubic interpolation
scheme.
6. Calculate the loads with Morison equation, extended with Rainey terms if applicable. First,
a load distribution is computed at the grid points. Then this load is integrated numerically
over each section using a trapezoidal method. Finally, the total load on the leg is taken as
the sum of the contributions of every bays.
7. Visualize the load on the bays and the submerged part of the leg.

the left and right tangents of the curve do not coincide. Bays 5 to 8 also present those kind of
discontinuities, in addition to experiencing zero load for some portions of time. Thus, those bays
are successively fully submerged, partially submerged, and not submerged. Bay 9 is either partially
submerged or not submerged over time. Finally, the load is always zero on bay 10 and higher ones,
which are therefore never submerged.
The largest load is located on bay 1, the closest bay to the sea bottom. The reason for that is
that, on one hand, the horizontal velocities near the sea bottom are not signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from
the one at the surface, because of the ﬁnite water depth, as one can observe in Figure 10.3, and,
on the other hand, the hydrodynamic coeﬃcients of this section of the leg are very strong, due to
the presence of gusset plates there. Regarding the other bays: the velocity proﬁle is deﬁned by a
hyperbolic trigonometric function; the highest bays experience the largest velocities, which is why
they are associated with higher loads. The maximum load on bay 9 is lower than for the other
bays, because only one fourth of this bay is submerged at the crest of the wave. Please note that
these ﬁndings are related to the fact that the wave model is linear. In real life, the wave would
deﬁnitely not be linear.
The total load on the leg is computed and plotted in Figure 10.6, together with the wave elevation
on another y-axis. The two available load models (Morison equation with and without Rainey
terms) are compared. The force is the sum of several contributions: the drag force, proportional to
the square of the velocity, the inertia force, proportional to the horizontal acceleration, the Rainey
terms, if included in the load model. It looks like the addition of Rainey terms do not result in any
substantial increase of the force on the leg. The wave elevation is superimposed on the plot using
a diﬀerent right y-axis. As a reminder, the drag force is proportional to u2, described by a squared
sine, while the inertia force is proportional to ˙u, described by a cosine function. Hence those two
forces do not have their peaks at the same phase. The drag force is in phase with the wave elevation,
while the inertia force has a phase delay of π/2 with the wave elevation. The slight phase diﬀerence
between the peaks of the load and the elevation in the ﬁgure is therefore due to the existence of
an inertia term in the load. From the fact that this phase diﬀerence is very small, one can deduce
that the system is drag-dominated in these conditions. This is also conﬁrmed with the calculation
of the Keulegan-Carpenter number for one bay: KC = umT/De ≈(5m/s) · (15s)/(2m) = 37.5,
which falls into the drag-dominated regime KC > 20−30. Also, one can notice that the peak force
on bay 1 appears a few tenths of a second before the peak force for the other bays. The reason is
probably that the inertia force is larger for this bay than for the other ones. Furthermore, one can
see in Figure 10.6 that the force on the leg reaches a plateau at t = 7.5s, again due to the phase
diﬀerence between the inertia and drag forces.

The PIMP model described in Section 4.4.2 was tried on the ELM, to estimate the slamming load

The parameters of the model were essentially chosen as suggested in Pierella, Ghadirian, and
Bredmose (2019):
• The inner radius a of the wedge-shaped ﬂuid domain was taken as half of the equivalent
diameter De in the highest submerged section of the leg, as shown in Figure 10.23.
• The outer radius was chosen as b = λp/6, where λp is the peak period wavelength using
the linear dispersion relation. Actually, Pierella, Ghadirian, and Bredmose (2019) propose
to use b = λp/2, but the results for the pressure impulse presented some unexpected spatial
ﬂuctuations in the vertical direction (see Figure 10.25), and reducing b enabled to lower those
ﬂuctuations without altering signiﬁcantly the ﬁnal value of the force impulse.
• The limit azimutal angle is set to θmax = π/4, the value which matched best the CFD
simulations in Ghadirian and Bredmose (2019).
• The height of the ﬂuid domain is H = h+ηmax, where ηmax is the maximum water elevation
(or crest height) of the studied wave event.
• The height of the impact zone is taken as the crest height: µH = ηmax.
• The impact velocity is chosen as the maximum value of the ratio ηt/ηx in the vicinity of the
peak of the force, as represented in Figure 10.24. ηt = (∂η)/(∂t) is the time derivative of
η, calculated using a ﬁnite diﬀerence scheme with ﬁve-point stencil (fourth order accuracy),
and ηx = (∂η)/(∂x) the x-derivative, which is directly accessible in the DeRisk database.

Methodology
The method is based on the calculation of wave loads, which was presented in the previous Chap-

include the following:
• When a current coexists with waves, the deﬁnitions of apparent and intrinsic wave periods
apply; see Section 3.2.2. It was assumed that the peak wave period Tp used in Section 7.4
was an intrinsic period, i.e. the wave period measured by an observer following the current,
or in the absence of current. The apparent period must be calculated when the current is
superimposed; it is the period measured by a stationary observer. Equations (3.6) to (3.8)
can be used for this purpose.
• The velocity proﬁle of the steady current must be stretched-compressed to ensure that the
current velocity is constant at the free surface. Wheeler stretching is the most straightfor-
ward approach and is recommended in the ISO-19905-1 (2016) standards and DNV-RP-C205
(2007). The process is described in Section 3.2.1. Non-linear stretching is also known to give
good results, but was not applied in this study for the sake of simplicity.
• To be consistent, it was decided to apply a reduction factor on the current velocity proﬁle,
both for the equivalent and the detail leg models. This factor accounts for the eﬀects of
interference from the structure, and is calculated from Equation (3.9).
• The current proﬁle is interpolated at the grid points spread along the leg (the same points as
those at which the wave kinematics is known), deﬁned in Section 10.2.1. Linear interpolation
is used.
• The wave and current velocities must be combined before applying Morison equation. The
horizontal velocities of the current and the waves are added up at the grid points. Assuming
that current and waves come with a same heading angle yields Equation (11.1)
u = uwaves + uC

FURTHER DISCUSSIONS
12.1
About the structural analysis
The combination of two environmental loads was studied in this project, namely the drag load
induced by a steady current and the hydrodynamic load induced by waves. Those mechanical
actions were calculated using the Morison equation, which applies for slender cylinders. The result
is a force per unit length, i.e. a distribution of load over the height of the leg. This should be kept
in mind when modeling the dynamic response of the leg to this external force. Depending on the
aim and the level of complexity of the structural model of the leg, one might consider applying the
total force, i.e. the load integrated over the submerged part of the leg, or a simpliﬁcation of the
moment at the leg-to-hull connection1, instead of the varying and complicated load distribution.
However, the integrated force should not be mistaken with the reaction at the spudcan or at the
leg-to-hull connection. The emphasis is laid on the fact that all of the actions calculated in this
report were external forces; in the strict sense of the term, those forces apply to the leg in jack-up
operation as a load distribution (in N/m), and not as a point force nor as a moment at the top or
the bottom of the leg.
The ELM built in this thesis is a hydrodynamic model, which should only be used to calculate
hydrodynamic loads. That means for instance that using the equivalent diameter De as a struc-
tural feature for the mechanical behaviour of the leg would be a wrong approach. The structural
analysis and strength of the leg are out of the scope of this study, even though such a study will
require a knowledge of the environmental load distribution along the leg, which was calculated in
this thesis. A discussion has been opened in June 2020 with the classiﬁcation society DNV-GL,
owning the global structural model of SBO’s ships, to compute the response of the structure to
the hydrodynamic loads calculated in this thesis. This is currently under study.
1. A simple way of structurally modeling the leg is to consider a beam ﬁxed at the top (leg-to-hull connection)
and pinned at the bottom (soil). It is also the most conservative approach for the leg loads. The ﬁxed support
inhibits theoretically all movement at the top, including vertical or horizontal displacements as well as rotations. On
the other hand, the pinned support inhibits any displacement in the soil but permits - in theory - rotations to occur
freely (note that in real life, the legs actually have rotational stiﬀness from the soil). In that scenario, the beam has
more supports than required to be statically eﬀective. Indeed the pinned support could be removed, turning the
structure into a cantilever beam, which is still an eﬀective load-bearing structure. That makes the system redundant
in terms of supports, a so-called "indeterminate structure". In practice for the mechanical calculations, this means
that the moment at the ﬁxed support can not be calculated directly from a basic balance of the force and moments.
It will remain unknown after writing the balance. It can however be determined applying Castigliano’s theorem of
least work, which states that "the redundant reaction components of a statically indeterminate structure are such
that they make the internal work (strain energy) a minimum". The computation of the strain energy for this system
is out of the scope of the thesis, because it would require an equivalent structural model of the leg, with equivalent
moment of inertia. That explains why the computation of the reaction moment at the top is not straightforward
and not part of this study.

When it comes to the DeRisk database method, the introduction of short-crested waves is
currently in the planning stage.
• The only force computed in the project was the inline (horizontal) force, because it is the
main hydrodynamic force acting on the leg of a jack-up vessel. Even though a complete
study of the hydrodynamic forces on a submerged structure would normally also include
the analysis of the lift (vertical) force, this step is generally missing from the standards for
the operation of jack-up vessels, such as ISO-19905-1 (2016) and SNAME-T&R5-5A (2002).
However, it is necessary when designing this kind of vessel to check that the vibration modes
of the structure do not match with the range of frequency of a possible external excitation.
What is more, the system can be subject to signiﬁcant fatigue damage due to cross-ﬂow
and in-line vibrations, i.e. elastic motion cycles. In DNV-RP-C205 (2007), section 6.10.1,
it is mentioned that a non-zero lift force is observed on a cylinder when it is positioned
downstream of another cylinder. Indeed, the wake generated by the upstream cylinder is
not uniform, and the wake ﬁeld presents velocity gradients, at the origin of Wake Induced
Oscillations (WIO). The oscillating lift force depend on the relative distance between the
cylinders. Moreover, a lift force oscillations can also be measured on a single cylinder if
vortex-shedding appears. In that case, the Vortex Induced Vibrations (VIV) happen
at the Strouhal frequency f = Stu/D, where St is the Strouhal number (around 0.2 in the
sub-critical regime). The VIV may increase the mean drag coeﬃcient of a member, with
eﬀects on the global analysis and possible interference with other members. Speciﬁc for the
calculation of the VIV is given in DNV-RP-C205 (2007), chapter 9.
Computing the total lift force oscillation on the entire leg from the DLM might be diﬃcult,
because each element is likely to be associated with oscillating loads due to vortex shedding,
and this with diﬀerent phase angles. The calculation of the lift force would be easier for
ELM. Alternatively, it is possible to estimate the oscillating force on a few elements within
one bay.
• The slamming wave model developed at DTU and was tried for the ﬁrst time on a truss
structure, for this application. The model can only be applied to the hydrodynamic ELM, i.e.
on the equivalent column. It would in fact be overly complicated to try to compute an impact
contribution on every single element. It was assumed that the equivalent hydrodynamic cross-
section area is the one to be used for the pressure impulse model. This assumption has not
been validated yet. Further studies could involve wave tank tests to measure the impact
wave load on a model-scale truss structure and compare to the numerical model.

legs of SBO’s vessels. All of the load results presented in this report were extracted for leg

CONCLUSIONS
In this master’s thesis, the hydrodynamic loading on a state-of-the-art jack-up vessel was evaluated
in the elevated conﬁguration. The methodology followed to calculate the extreme environmental
actions of the current and the waves on the submerged portion of one leg typically involves three
main steps: ﬁrst, calculate the water elevation and the kinematics (velocities, gradients, and accel-
erations) of the ﬂow at the center position of the leg, secondly, perform hydrodynamic modeling
of the truss structure of the leg, and ﬁnally, compute the load distribution along the underwater
sections.
A combination of sea state parameters - the water depth, the signiﬁcant wave height, and the
peak wave period - was speciﬁed for the whole project, intended to match an example operation
of the oﬀshore-wind installation ship. The most realistic current-induced loads were obtained by
simulating a steady ﬂow with a power-law velocity proﬁle. A comparative study was carried out for
the wave-induced loading. Diﬀerent kinds of models were tried to obtain the wave kinematics. As
a ﬁrst simple model, a regular wave was generated from Airy’s linear wave theory, then compared
with the stream function theory. Additionally, the wave loading was computed in a random sea,
using the highest wave event extracted from a realization of ﬁfteen hours, composed of long-crested
ﬁrst-order irregular waves. This was compared with two other irregular wave models: on one hand,
a high-order stream function wave, embedded in the realization and replacing the most extreme
event, and on the other hand, a three-hours-long realization of fully nonlinear waves, as an output
from the OceanWave3D potential-ﬂow solver. The highest waves induced the highest total forces
on the leg, regardless of the steepness of the wave elevation.
Nonetheless, the more nonlinear
the wave was, the greater the load was on the highest submerged sections, at the instant of the
crest passing through the leg. The standard rules were applied to combine the wave and current
kinematics. The eﬀect of the direction of the waves/current relative to the leg was studied. As
expected, there was a 60◦-periodicity in the hydrodynamic load for a changing angle of attack of
the waves/current, although this load variation was very limited.
Two hydrodynamic models of the leg were developed in this study for SBO’s vessels: an equivalent
model, ﬁrstly, in which the hydrodynamic properties were averaged section per section, yielding an
equivalent cylindrical column, and a detailed model, secondly, where each element of the leg was
considered as a Morison element for which the drag and inertia coeﬃcients were ﬁnely chosen to
suit the experimented ﬂow regime. Even though both leg models give quite similar results in steady
current, the detailed leg model proved to be more conservative than the equivalent one as soon as
the waves were included in the environment. The eﬀect of marine growth on the hydrodynamic
properties of the leg was investigated and it turned out that it was noteworthy. The drag load was
substantially reduced in the absence of biofouling on the leg extension.
The load was computed utilizing the Morison’s equation, which is typically used in the industry
for the hydrodynamic load computation on ﬁxed slender cylindrical structures. A higher-order
extension of the equation was as well implemented but did not add any signiﬁcant surplus to
the load estimates for all of the wave models. Finally, the pressure impulse model developed at
DTU within the DeRisk project was applied to the equivalent leg model and the slamming load
contribution was estimated and analyzed, relative to the inline force impulse.
Further work could be carried out to improve the model, focusing perhaps on the current blockage
phenomenon in coexisting wave and current conditions, on the three-dimensional wave models and
