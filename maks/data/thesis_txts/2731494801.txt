Abstract
This project further optimized the previous Danok testing program, focusing on two main
aspects: algorithmic improvements and comparative hearing tests for different groups of
test subjects.
On the algorithmic front: First, to encourage greater engagement and interest among chil­
dren during testing and reduce fatigue, we incorporated visual cues such as images into
the program. Second, for the convenience of test administrators, the user interface and
operational logic of the Danok testing program were optimized. Test administrators only
need to click a single button to initiate tests in different scenarios, significantly reducing
the user’s operational costs and making it user­friendly, even for those with limited com­
puter expertise and without the need for additional specialized training. Lastly, a crucial
addition to the original Danok program was the integration of reverberation algorithms.
Leveraging the Copenhagen hearing and balance centre(CHBC) Spatial Acoustics Lab­
oratory, realistic reverberation environments in real­life situations can now be simulated,
enabling Danok to conduct hearing­related tests and research from new perspectives.
In terms of hearing tests: To better understand testing differences among individuals with
different native languages, four major categories were established within the recruited
population. These included Danish­speaking adults, non­Danish­speaking adults, non­
Danish­speaking children, and Danish­speaking children. Testing was conducted under
various background noise and reverberation conditions, allowing for comprehensive re­
search into the differences between these language groups.

1
Introduction
1.1
Research Background
The original version of the DANOK corpus testing program was designed for native Danish­
speaking adults and was not suitable for application in a pediatric testing platform for clin­
ical practice. In light of this, a series of optimizations were implemented based on the
original DANOK corpus testing program, resulting in the development of a child­friendly
test utilizing the DANOK speech corpus that is applicable to clinical practice. Addition­
ally, to assess the language independence of the optimized DANOK test and the use of
meaningless words, a comparison was conducted between native Danish­speaking adults
with normal hearing and non­native adults with normal hearing. Finally, an evaluation of
the optimized DANOK testing program was conducted in a group of children with nor­
mal hearing who were non­native but spoke Danish. This assessment aimed to examine
the relationship between the scores of children and adults, verifying the suitability of the
optimized DANOK speech corpus for child­friendly testing in clinical practice. Currently,
there is an ongoing recruitment of native Danish­speaking children for this project, but the
results of these children are not included in the scope of this study.
1.2
General Introduction
This research is primarily focused on the relevant study previously published by Torsten “A
Danish nonsense word corpus for phoneme recognition measurements”(Dau and Nielsen,
2019)[1], while also drawing inspiration from the two articles(Dau et al,2022) [2] and(Van
Eeckhoutte et al,2020) [3].
Speech clarity is a major concern for most hearing aid users. Hearing loss can result in
significant difficulties in understanding speech, particularly in noisy environments. Over
the years, extensive research has been conducted on the perception of speech in complex
real­life auditory scenes. In noisy backgrounds with low signal­to­noise ratios (SNR), indi­
viduals with hearing impairment are more susceptible to a decrease in speech clarity and
an increase in the impact of hearing loss compared to those with normal hearing [4](Gygi
and Shafiro, 2013; Holube, Fredelake, and Hansen, 2007)[5]. Enhancing language clar­
ity becomes crucial, and common signal processing algorithms go beyond simple speech
amplification. Typical noise reduction algorithms achieve this through two independent
processes: directional beamforming and digital noise reduction.
For instance, directional beamformers aim to improve SNR based on spatial differences
between speech and noise. Subsequently, digital noise reduction is applied to the out­
put signal of the beamformer to further reduce residual noise. This situation occurs in
environments where noise is dispersed or in the same direction as the target speaker.
Directional beamformers seek to leverage spatial separation between speech and noise,
while digital noise reduction algorithms aim to exploit temporal and spectral differences
between speech and noise.
However, despite such strategies, the effectiveness of signal processing may vary due to
differences in the speech signal and background noise, determined by different spectral
and temporal/spatial characteristics. The clarity of some speech segments may increase,
while others may show no effect or even worsen. If only a single outcome measure,
such as the Speech Recognition Threshold (SRT) clarity test or the Hearing in Noise Test
2

(HINT), is used, these subtle segmental effects cannot be isolated, resulting in an aver­
age outcome that masks potential minor differences.
Dividing whole sentences into individual words and further segmenting each word into dif­
ferent phonemes allows for a more detailed assessment of the impact of processing these
subtle differences on speech clarity. Phonemes represent the shortest units of speech that
can change the meaning of words in a language. If a phoneme is misidentified, listeners
may misunderstand a word and consequently a sentence. Therefore, studying and test­
ing the recognition scores of individual phonemes may be highly suitable for evaluating
whether this goal is achieved.
Through investigations(Daneman et al,1995)[6], it has been found that older listeners ben­
efit more from context than younger listeners, likely due to their greater experience in
using context to recover lost speech information. Therefore, in phoneme­based tests in­
volving meaningful speech stimuli (such as everyday words), listener recognition scores
for each phoneme may reflect information associated with available contextual associa­
tions rather than ”acoustic” information. A closer correlation between available acoustic
information and recognition scores can be achieved by reducing context­related infor­
mation in the speech signal. A simple approach is using meaningless words, as in the
danok study conducted in this research. However, in tests based on meaningless stimuli,
listeners should report the phonemes they perceive without leaning towards meaningful
words. In addition to the speech corpus, a test program based on scoring individual tar­
get phonemes was developed. The corpus and the test program were comprehensively
evaluated. The aim was to investigate whether this test could be considered a reliable
tool for assessing phoneme perception. The speech corpus itself can be used for various
types of experiments and experiments that do not depend on the test program introduced
here. The speech corpus was named DANOK (DAnsk NonsensOrdsKorpus).

In the following report, abbreviations will be used to represent the three groups as follows:
DNA for Danish Native Adult; NDA for Non­Danish Native Adult; BC for Bilingual Children.

2.4
Stimuli
2.4.1
Ling sound stimuli
Ling sounds are currently used in clinical practice during Auditory Verbal Therapy. These
are realistic utterances of speech and consist of the following phonemes: /m/, /u/, /a/,
/i/, /sh/, and /s/. The Ling sounds represent various different speech sounds from low to
high pitch (frequency). They help to test child’s hearing and check they have access to
the full range of speech sounds necessary for learning language. The Ling Sound Test
can provide quick and easy verification of auditory abilities, can establish continuity and
preparedness for training and development of additional listening skills, can serve as a
guide for setting auditory training goals.
2.4.2
DANOK stimuli
The Danok test comprises 24 words, each consisting of an onset consonant (/l/), a vowel
(/a/), and one of 12 medial consonants (/p/, /t/, /k/, /b/, /d/, /g/, /m/, /n/, /l/, /f/, /v/, /s/). The
test is conducted with two speakers, one male and one female. The primary focus is on

evaluating the accuracy of identifying the medial consonants, with a particular emphasis
on assessing the more challenging recognition of medial consonants compared to onset
consonants(Dau and Nielsen, 2019)[1].These nonsense words were created based on a
common scheme :
CV C + /i/
(2.1)
where C represents a consonant and V represents a vowel. The fixed ending imparts
meaningless characteristics to these words, as /i/ is rarely the final phoneme in disyllabic
Danish words.
Figure 2.1: Speech corpus
The test involves four conditions: speech at 55dB with noise at 0dB, speech at 55dB
with noise at 50dB, testing in a normal (non­reverberant) environment, and testing in a
reverberant environment (with a 1­second reverberation time). The noise used is speech­
shaped noise (SSN) with a shape similar to that of human speech. Throughout the entire
testing process, the noise is continuously played without interruption. In each test session,
the order of the test words is randomized.
2.5
Apparatus and procedure
2.5.1
Ling sound procedure
The utilization of Ling sounds is grounded in the notion that they encompass the entire
spectrum of speech, essential for language acquisition and speech production. Given
the practicality for clinical applications due to the limited set of six sounds, Ling sounds
are employed in conjunction with the Affinity software and speakers within the CHBC
Sound Booth room for threshold detection. Subsequently, participants are presented with
”soft speech” at 55 dB SPL, and they are asked to repeat the words heard, enabling the
assessment of their auditory capabilities. The use of 55 dB SPL for ”soft speech” playback
is particularly pertinent as clinicians are particularly interested in the audibility of speech
at low intensity levels.
Simultaneously, hearing aid fittings are validated at 55, 65 and 75 dB SPL, reflecting
soft, moderate (normal), and loud speech feedback standards. However, when using
Ling sounds, the assessment is solely focused on speech detection.
It remains un­
known whether participants respond to /s/ or /f/, and whether they can discern differences
between /s/ and /f/ sounds, especially when utilizing frequency­lowering hearing aids.

Despite the concept that Ling sounds cover the entire frequency range, natural speech
sounds like Ling sound phonemes have energy across wide, overlapping frequency re­
gions, which might not exhibit the expected frequency specificity. It is plausible that listen­
ers perceive /s/ sounds but, in reality, hear /f/ sounds due to the broad frequency content
inherent in natural speech. To account for potential speech misperceptions, DANOK tests
are deemed necessary in the evaluation process.

the normal (non­reverberant) environment testing protocol, five specific speakers were
utilized. These speakers were positioned as follows: directly in front, on the left and right
sides of the front, and on the left and right sides behind the listener. The specific locations

As shown in the fig. 2.4 , this is the GUI interface of the DANOK testing program, fea­
turing a total of 6 programs: ”random,” ”training,” two ”normal environment” programs,
and two”reverberant environment” programs. Firstly, the ”random” program arranges the

select and initiate the test based on the randomly assigned number. Before the actual test
begins, a brief training session is conducted with 12 words (using ”maXi”), and the actual

testing procedure.

accuracy is not compromised due to fatigue. The final recorded testing protocols con­

DANOK calibration :
To calibrate the DANOK program, switch to the calibration mode. Choose one speaker for
playback and adjust the sound level meter to A­weighting. Place it at the listener’s position
in the CHBC room. In real­time, receive the sound from the DANOK calibration mode and
record the readings on the sound level meter. Input these values into the calibration field
of the DANOK interface. Repeat the same procedure for different speakers.
Afterward, exit the calibration mode and play 50dB SPL noise in normal mode. Check if
the sound level meter reads 50dB SPL. If not, recalibrate until the readings on the sound
level meter align with the actual playback sound intensity. The calibration procedure for
reverberant and normal (non­reverberant) modes is identical.

Discussion
In this section of the discussion, a more detailed comparative analysis of the average test
results for different test groups will be conducted. From the previous comparisons of the
three groups, it is evident that there are significant differences in the data between the
reverberant and normal (non­reverberant) environments, particularly in the data from the
children’s group. However, the earlier discussion primarily provided comparisons within
each group, examining the correct recognition rates within different environments. The
study aims to delve deeper and understand the extent of differences in correct recognition
rates among different groups within the same environment. Whether there is correlation
in the relevant data is a key aspect of this report.
Therefore, the next step involves a focused investigation into the specific differences be­
tween the Danish native­speaking adult group and the non­Danish native­speaking adult
group in both the reverberant and normal (non­reverberant) environments. Additionally,
the test data from the non­Danish native­speaking children’s group will be incorporated.
This analysis aims to unravel the nuances of correct recognition rate disparities among
these groups in similar environments and explore potential correlations. This task is a
primary objective of the report.

4.6
Limitations
The current quantity of test data is somewhat limited for all three groups: Danish native­
speaking adults, non­Danish native­speaking adults, and non­Danish native­speaking
children, with each group comprising only 7 and 9 listener. Relatively speaking, the data
pool is not extensive. Subsequent efforts can be directed towards expanding the dataset
using the same methodology. This expansion is anticipated to provide more substantial
feedback, particularly in terms of fine­grained details and data precision.

5
Conclusion
5.1
Summarize the Study
Through this project, we can understand that, in a normal (non­reverberant) environment,
adults whose native language is Danish exhibit an overall higher accuracy in low signal­
to­noise ratios compared to adults with non­Danish native languages and non­Danish­
speaking children. This suggests that the speaker’s native language influences the test
subjects’ recognition accuracy. Looking at the testing results for the children’s group, non­
Danish­speaking children perform the poorest in low signal­to­noise environments among
the three groups. As the signal­to­noise ratio increases, their accuracy gradually reaches
the level of the adult group, indicating that adults can better capture useful information in
noisy environments, possibly due to experiential influences.
Analyzing the data in reverberant and reverberant plus noisy environments, the accuracy
of non­Danish­speaking adults and Danish­speaking adults is essentially the same. How­
ever, the data for the children’s group becomes lower, suggesting that children are more
susceptible to confusion in target speech in environments with both noise and reverbera­
tion.
Throughout the entire project, from recruiting testers, checking their hearing, conducting
the tests, to later data analysis and report editing, has left a profound impression on me.
I hope everyone takes good care of their hearing and shows kindness to those around
them with hearing issues.
5.2
Future works
Future work and research will primarily focus on the following aspects:
• Conducting tests on Danish­speaking children with normal hearing and comparing
the results with non­Danish­speaking children.
• Conducting tests on Danish­speaking children with hearing impairment.
• Further optimizing the algorithm to enable selection through sound, eliminating the
need for manual answer selection.
• Conducting differentiated tests based on different reverberation times.
• Exploring the combination of auditory and visual stimuli.
• Incorporating an adaptive program into the algorithm to automatically adjust different
SNR values based on accuracy.
• Conducting tests in various real­life scenarios, such as windy environments, outdoor
settings, and more.
These future endeavors aim to enhance the comprehensiveness and effectiveness of the
DANOK testing program.
