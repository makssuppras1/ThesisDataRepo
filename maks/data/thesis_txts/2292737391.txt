1. Introduction 
 
1.1 Problem description 
 
 
Marines diesel engines used for low-speed vessels, particularly two-stroke machines, can run 
on heavy fuel oils, which are the most economic and the lowest quality fuels. They are quite known to have 
big amounts of sulfur and it can’t be removed from fuel oils before entering the engine. During combustion 
the sulfur is oxidized to SO2 and a small fraction of it is further oxidized to SO3. These compounds (SOx) can 
and must be treated afterwards, due to  environmental problems. But inside the engine, they can enhance 
problems of corrosión, where SO3 can combine with water and form  sulfuric acid (H2SO4), maximum 
responsible of corrosion. This should be avoided in terms of efficiency and lifetime of the engine. There are 
measures to mitigate this effect like lubrication in the cylinder line and additives for the fuel, but they 
increase the costs. So in order to improve the efficiency of the engines and future development of them, a 
study of SO3 formation is required. For the moment, it is known that the oxidation of SO2 to SO3 takes place 
through a sequence of gas-phase reactions which are comparatively well established. However, fuel oils 
contain trace amounts of vanadium, that can´t be removed from the oil, and vanadium oxide (V2O5) is 
known to catalyze the SO2 oxidation. Due to its variability in geometry and electronic structure vanadium is 
used in multiple industrial processes as catalyst . For this reason, it is expected to contribute in the pathway 
to SO3 ,and the aim of the project is to contribute elucidating some light if the catalytic oxidation of SO2. 
 
1.2 Objectives 
 
The aim of this project is through experiments and kinetic modeling to evaluate the relative 
importance of homogeneous and heterogeneous (catalyzed by vanadium oxides) oxidation of SO2 into SO3. 
By evaluations of the rate of the catalytic oxidation and of the potential amounts of catalytically active 
vanadium oxide present in the system, it will be determined if heterogeneous oxidation routes contribute 
significantly compared to the homogenous oxidation that is taking place. 
The kinetics of SO2 oxidation over vanadium oxides will be studied in a fixed bed reactor. The 
vanadium aerosols will be produced in a flame spray pyrolysis setup that enables the formation of oxide 
nanoparticles by spraying a combustible solution of organometallic salts into a flame.  With this setup, 
catalytically active oxides can be produced by a mechanism similar to the one leading to formation of the 
catalytic species within the engine.  The experimental results will be used to establish rate equations for the 
catalytic reaction. The rate of SO3 formation by heterogeneous routes will then be compared to the rate of 
SO3 formation by homogeneous oxidation to evaluate whether or not the catalytic species have a 
significant impact on the speciation of the sulfur oxides.

2. Literature study 
 
2.1Two-stroke marine diesel engines 
2.1.1 Operation 
 
Internal combustion engines are used for land and marine transports and power generation. 
According to the application they can be divided into three main classes: light-duty, heavy-duty, and large-
scale engines. Light-duty engines are mainly used in automobiles and heavy-duty engines in buses and 
trucks. Large-scale, medium or low speed engines have a different application range, covering marine 
applications, such as ships and tankers, and land-based power production [1]. 
 
One of the main differences between medium and low speed is that the last ones work with 
two-stroke diesel engines, which means they can produce more power but at lower rpm, as seen in Figure 
2.1. The two-stroke cycle is so called because it takes two strokes of the piston to complete the processes 
needed to convert the energy in the fuel into work. Because the engine is reciprocating, this means that the 
piston must move up and down the cylinder, and therefore the crankshaft must revolve once [2], a 
schematic diagram is shown in Figure 2.2. 
 
 
Figure 2.1: Diesel engines for power generation from MAN Diesel [3]. 
 
The slow speed ones, has two benefits to an owner of a ship. The first, probably the most 
important, allows the engine to burn heavy fuel oil (HFO) efficiently. Although, the black, tar like substance 
can be used as fuel in both the four stroke and two stroke engine. This fuel is much cheaper, currently 
around $300USD/ton, compared to the "ordinary" refined diesel fuel oil (MDO), with the cost being around 
$600USD/ton. On the other hand, the two stroke engines have a less complicated design, namely no intake 
valves, this reduce the possibility of things failing. The most prominent builders of large slow speed two-
stroke engine are Sulzer of Switzerland, producer of the first big two stroke design, and Burmeister & Wain 
(B&W) of Denmark, the pioneers of the engine’s application to ship design. [4]

Low speed, two-stroke diesel engines operate with an air excess ratio of 2.5-3. Diesel 
combustion at high air/fuel ratios generally means hydrocarbons, soot and carbon monoxide are oxidised 
more completely to water and carbon dioxide in the cylinder. More than half of the air is available for the 
combustion process, while the remaining part is scavenged through the cylinder [5]. Later, fresh air is used 
to scavenging the exhaust gases. 
 
 
 
Figure 2.2: steps and schematic diagram of a two-stroke diesel engine cycle. [4] 
 
 
Fuel 
 
Light-duty engines can be operated using petrol or diesel, but heavy-duty and especially large-
scale engines are exclusively fuelled by diesel and heavy fuel oil (HFO), respectively.HFO is a general term, 
and many other names exist to describe this product range: residual fuel oil, bunker fuel, bunker C, fuel no. 
6, industrial fuel oil, marine fuel oil, and black oil. Generally, the prefixes heavy, medium or light, when 
applied to a fuel oil, indicate the viscosity and density of the product [2]. 
 
Chemically, HFOs resemble asphalt, and therefore they are considered to be suspensions of 
asphaltenes in an oily medium. Residual oils also contain organo-metallic compounds derived from the 
original crude oils, the most important trace being vanadium (V). For example, Caribbean (Venezuela) and 
Mexican crude oils contain high amounts of V. Other important metallic elements present in HFOs are Ni, 
Fe, K, Na, Al and Si [1]. 
 
 There are fifteen grades of residual fuels that are distinguished by three letters and two 
numbers. The first two letters are common to all residual grades, “RM” denotes “Residual Marine” and the 
third letter refers to the characteristics of the fuel. The two numbers are the viscosity of the fuel at 100 °C 
[6]. Limits for vanadium and sulfur content in fuels are shown in Table 2.1

Lubricant 
 
Due to the extreme conditions inside the combustion chamber, the liner is manufactured from 
a cast iron alloyed with chromium, vanadium and molybdenum. Cast iron contains graphite, a lubricant. 
The alloying elements help resist corrosion and improve the wear resistance at high temperatures [8]. 
 
Helping this purpose exists a cylinder lubricant, it is injected into each cylinder to provide a 
uniform film of lubricant on the liner wall. The main purposes of the lubricant are to ensure the basic 
functions of lubricating the piston and liner, and sealing the gap between piston ring, piston and liner wall. 
The additive package in the cylinder lubricant maintains the stability of the lubricant, neutralizes acid 
condensation on the liner wall and scavenges fuel and lubricant debris [9]. 
 
 
 
Load 
 
 
 
The engine load is how much demand is placed on the engine for power. An engine will run 
economically if the engine is well maintained and is run where the Specific Fuel Oil Consumption is the least 
(SFOC) (g/kWh). An engine is said to be performing well or well-maintained if it can be safely run at the 
RPM required at an efficient load. A higher power generated at a lower RPM indicates an over loaded main 
engine [10]. 
 
The simplest way to reduce operating costs is to reduce the fuel bill. Most vessels are propelled by a slow 
speed two stroke engine directly coupled to a fixed pitch propeller. If the speed of the engine is reduced, 
then although the vessel will slow down, the amount of fuel required to travel each nautical mile is 
reduced. This is because the correlation between speed and shaft power is not linear, but cubic [11].

2.1.2 Emissions (PM, SOx, NOx) 
 
Combusting marine oils and residuals (MDO, HFO) all have disadvantages in terms of 
nitrogen oxide (NOx), particulate matter (PM) and sulphur oxide (SOx) emissions. As seen in Figure 2.5 with 
an example of a two-stroke marine diesel engine from MAN DIESEL  which run with a 3% S fuel [5].  
 
Figure 2.5: schematic figure of a two-stroke marine diesel engine run with HFO [5]. 
 
PM (particulate matter) 
 
Particles emitted from ship diesel engines are mainly composed of organic carbon, sulfate 
and ash. Nickel and vanadium are the major components of ash and are formed by burning low-grade fuel 
oils [1].Much of the variation in the particulate composition comes from the type of fuel used, and because 
heavy fuel oil (HFO) represents over 80% of the fuel consumed by the world fleet, emissions from HFO 
clearly needed to be characterized, and that is what Murphy et al. did [12].Recently, they performed 
simultaneous on-board and air borne measurements of particles emitted from a modern container ship 
(with two-stroke marine diesel engine). They reported particles in two size ranges: 5–8 and 30–100 nm. The 
5–8 nm nanoparticles dominated the total number concentration. Transmission electron microscope (TEM) 
data showed that these nanoparticles were composed of V, Ni and S. The 30–100 nm particle range also 
contained vanadium, sulfur and nickel components but showed a greater presence of organic carbon.  
Zheng et al. [13] tried to synthesize 5–8 nm ash with similar physical and chemical 
characteristics, as the particles reported by Murphy et al. [12]by the spray flame pyrolysis method [14]. 
They used heavy fuel oil (HFO) as the liquid phase particle precursor and apply spray flame pyrolysis with a 
lab-scale oxy-hydrogen flame burner to generate ash particles. They said that that size and composition of 
the laboratory-generated particles is close to that of real ship emitted ash particles. The absence of 
elemental carbon in laboratory-generated particles is comparable to the nano-mode (e.g. 5-8 nm) ship 
emitted particles [13].

Vanadium, like many other metals in the fuel matrix, enters the gas stream in combustion 
systems by vaporization of volatile organic vanadium compounds. Nucleation of vaporized metal compound 
results in nano-size particles in the fine mode. Eventually they can grow to the submicron regime by 
coagulation, and condensation. However, their typical concentration, and short residence time in a 
combustion system do not allow these fine particles to grow to the supermicron regime [15]. As the 
temperature decreases once the gas stream exits the combustion zone, various aerosol dynamics proceed 
resulting in the transformation of vanadium into particulate phase. A schematic picture is seen in Figure 
2.6: 
 
 
 
Figure 2.6: Aerosol dynamic process of vanadium in a combustion system [16]. 
 
 
Other study [17], measured the particle characteristics in medium-speed diesel engines with 
HA-HFO (high ash content >0.1%) (V: 400 ppm; Na: 50-60 ppm; S: 2.5 wt. %). They divided the particles in 
two main classes: fine mode ones 40-100 nm in diameter, and coarse ones. The fine mode was originated 
by the nucleation of the volatilised fuel oil ash species (V, Ni, and Na). Examination of the detailed structure 
of these particles revealed that the primary particles that were seen as spherical in the SEM had even finer 
structures. The inner part of these particles consists of the nucleated gaseous metal oxide species 
(vaporised in the combustion) and the following layers are formed by the condensation of other fuel ash 
species and tar, soot, etc. The primary particles are adhered and partly sintered together to create cluster 
aggregates. 
They also found that there was not enough oxygen in the particles to form vanadium 
pentoxide. This can be caused by many different factors, such as short residence times or carbon containing 
particles acting as surface toxicants by blocking the active surface. However, the estimated amount of 
sulphuric acid in the particles was high, being approx. 27 wt. %. This, on the other hand, requires the 
formation of vanadium pentoxide to catalyse the oxidation of SO2 to SO3 to form sulphuric acid. Which is 
interesting, because that is what we are researching. The average elemental composition of the particles in

SOx 
 
The formation of SOx is a direct result of the sulphur content of the fuel. During combustion, 
fuel bound sulphur will rapidly oxidise to SO2. Some 3-5% of the SO2 may be oxidised further to SO3. The 
actual amounts depends on excess air, pressure, temperature and fuel sulphur content [6]. The average 
sulphur content in HFO worldwide is around 2.5% (of mass), but HFO with a sulphur content of up to 4.5% 
is possible [18].Due to environmental issues, the international requirements on emissions of SOx (sulphur 
oxides) and PM (particulate matter) are determined by the MARPOL convention (International Convention 
for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships), which specifies a global limit and a local (SOx ECA (emission 
control area)) limit on the sulphur content in marine fuel [19]. 
 
                           Figure 2.8: Fuel sulfur content according to MARPOL [19] 
==PAGE:13==

Today there are no economically viable methods, either at the oil refineries or on board ship, to 
separate the sulphur from marine diesel fuels. This means that the sulphur cannot be prevented from 
entering the engine’s fuel injection system and combustion space. Although the SOx requirements can be 
met by using a low-sulphur fuel, the regulation allows alternative methods to reduce the emissions of SOx  
to an equivalent level, like scrubbers after the engine [20]. 
 
NOx 
 
The NOx emissions are also regulated by the MARPOL convention. The level Tier II 
regulations came into force after 1 January 2011,and a clear development and research targets for fulfilling 
IMO (International Maritime Organization) Tier III (valid from 1 January 2016 in ECA areas), which are more 
estrict regulations,have been initiatedby the two-stroke marine engines builders [19]. 
 
MAN Diesel & Turbo offers two alternative methods to meet the Tier III NOx requirement on 
two-stroke engines. The first method, exhaust gas recirculation (EGR), is an internal engine process to 
prevent the formation of NOx by controlling the combustion process. The second method, selective 
catalytic reduction(SCR), is an after-treatment method using an additive to reduce the NOx generated in 
the combustion process [19].

2.1.3 Operational problems 
 
 
There are some aspects that must be taken into account if a two-stroke marine diesel engine 
want to be operated correctly, for example: lubricating oils, which contain calcium, zinc and phosphorous. 
Burning them contribute to deposits and emissions in the exhaust system. Or hot corrosion, which is a 
result of sticky ash compounds of vanadium, sodium, zinc and nickel that adhere to hot metal surfaces. 
Sulphur will contribute to make the ash deposits more fluid and easier for oxygen to penetrate the melted 
deposit. When ash deposits stick to a metal surface, they eat away the outer protective oxide layer, which 
makes the metal more vulnerable to further degradation [6]. 
 
 
But the engines in many of today’s vessels, both new and old, have started to suffer more 
and more from cold corrosion. Cold corrosion is directly related with sulfuric acid, which may potentially 
cause corrosion either by condensation, if the liner temperature drops below the dew point of the 
combustion gas [21] or by direct transport of acidic gas phase species into the cylinder liner lube oil film 
[22]. The acidic species may diffuse through the cylinder lube oil and reach the metal surface eventually 
leading to corrosional wear. This can be avoided through neutralisation of sulfuric acid with alkaline lube oil 
additives. Further it has been proposed that SO2 is absorbed in the lube oil film in which further reaction 
with water leads to acid formation [23]. 
 
 
This problem has recently increased due to the Tier 2 requirements that try to reduce NOx 
emissions, and the 2013 Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) requirement to focus on fuel consumption. 
Two-stroke engines have changed parameters in the engine design, some of them shown in Figure 2.9 
 
 
Figure 2.9: design changes over a 2-stroke Mk8.1model from MAN DIESEL. [26] 
 
Engines have met these requirements with these kinds of changes, one of them increasing 
their stroke in new engines, while propeller size has grown 20%. As explained in [25]: the new engines burn 
almost the same amount of fuel as before but are running at 20 percent lower revs, so fuel per stroke has 
increased, firing pressures have increased significantly, and combustion temperatures have lowered. 
 
==PAGE:15==
 
11 
 
The increased combustion pressure leads to increased dew point. For such reason, cylinder 
liner temperature is lower than dew point and this leads to increase water and acid condensation on the 
cylinder walls, which lead to cold corrosion. Especially when an engine operates as low load, this cold 
corrosion occurs much more, probably due to the longer time the scavenging air, which is at low 
temperature, is in contact with the combustion gases. And this problem makes life time of cylinder liner 
rapidly reduced [26]. 
 
 
Besides this, market conditions are also exacerbating the problem, particularly for container 
ships where slow steaming has become the norm. Every new engine has a problem to fulfill the new 
regulations, but also those older vessels that are slow steaming are being modified to make them more fuel 
efficient [25]. The need to cut fuel costs has led to ‘slow steaming’: the trend towards running engines at 
part, or even very low loads, often well below the loads for which the engines have been designed [27]. 
 
Improving the understanding in this field will be helpful in evaluating potential in-cylinder 
corrosional wear both for engines in service, especially considering choice of lube oil type and lube oil 
consumption, but certainly also for engines in the design phase.

2.2 Formation of SO3 and H2SO4 
 
2.2.1 Homogeneous formation of SO3 
 
 
Theoretical investigations of SO3 formation in diesel engines is absent in contrast to gas 
turbines where considerable attention has been found throughout the years. However, there are some 
studies that indicate a low reaction rate for the homogeneous oxidation of SO2by oxygen molecules at 
temperatures below 1173K [28,29,30], or a report where the distribution of sulfur compounds in a large 
two-stroke diesel engine is modelled [31]. For this purpose they used for the sulphur mechanism a well 
established set of reactions by Glarborg et al. [32], which is a result of a continuous effort in literature data 
review, experimental laboratory reactor measurements as well as theoretical predictions [33,34,35,36,37]. 
In order to describe the formation of sulfuric acid the kinetic parameters were taken from [38]. 
The results in this modeling were: 3−5% of the injected sulfur is oxidized to SO3 that is 
formed primarily in the temperature range from 2000 to 1300 K during cylinder expansion. Due to 
thermodynamic restrictions SO3 is weakly present at the highest temperatures close to the diesel flame. 
However SO3 is increasingly favored as the gas temperature reduces during cylinder expansion. Basically all 
of the SO3 is formed locally in the temperature range from about 2000 K and down to 1300 K. Moreover 
down to about 1650 K SO3 is well described by general chemical equilibrium.  
 
If water is present, it may react with the SO3 to form H2SO4 in a reversible reaction. It is ruled 
by equilibrium as seen in Figure 2.10. The dissociation reaction in which H2SO4 is broken down to H2O and 
SO3, is favored at higher temperatures. At low temperatures, below 200°C, all of the SO3 is present as 
H2SO4. Above 500°C, it is almost entirely free SO3. The equilibrium point also depends on the concentration 
of water vapor at a given temperature. More of the initial SO3 will be present as H2SO4 when the water 
concentration is high [18]. 
 
 
Figure 2.11: concentration of SO2, SO3 and H2SO4 as a function of temperature at a pressure of 10 bar (upper) and 100 
bar (lower). Initial concs. XO2=0.15, XH2O=0.05, XS=0.01, no other components than argon present [18].

In a study of the homogeneous oxidation of SO2 by Glarborg et al. [32] some experiments 
were carried out in a temperature range of 1200-1500 K. The calculations indicate that SO3 is formed 
mainly by the sequence, 
 
SO2+  OH(+ M) →  HOSO2(+ M)  
HOSO2+  O2 →  SO3 +  HO2 
 
While the direct reaction between sulfur dioxide and oxygen is too slow to be important due to a large 
barrier [32]. 
 
SO2+  O2 →  SO3 +  O  
 
Below 1200 K the reaction systems are considered frozen, i.e. the amount of the species 
already formed will be constant, and the maximum conversions of SO2 are around 5% [21,22]. 
 
Under moist conditions the oxidation of SO2 is enhanced, since presence of water vapor 
gives rise to an O/H radical pool that promotes the oxidation process [39].

2.2.2 Catalyzed formation of SO3 
 
Vanadium is one of the main components of ash in heavy fuel oils, together with: nickel, 
sodium, silicon and aluminium. Typical values of ash in residual fuels are 0,03-0,07 % (m/m) [6], with limits 
as seen in Table 2.1. Vanadium is the most abundant among them, and as said before, a study where the 
composition of the exhaust gas ash particles of a mediums speed engine was characterized [1], vanadium 
was found in big quantities, together with sulphur, and mostly in the fine mode (40-100 nm). 
 
 
There is no literature with information about SO3 formation by heterogeneous ways inside 
the engines. But there are papers where vanadium pentoxide is expected to catalyze the oxidation of SO2 
inside the combustion chamber [6,17]. This is due to the presence of vanadium in a lot of commercial 
applications where it is catalyzing the reaction, but the most important is the sulfuric acid production 
process, which involves the catalytic oxidation of sulfur dioxide to sulfur trioxide over a supported 
vanadium catalyst. Although the catalyst used is not only vanadium, alkali elements are added to reduce 
the work temperature (K, Na, Cs)(usually in the form of sulfates or oxides) [40-43]. Under reaction 
conditions (400-600 °C), the active vanadia component of the catalyst exists as a molten salt forming a very 
thin liquid layer on the surface of the silica support. During the activation process, the catalyst takes up 
large amounts of SO3 , whereby molten alkali sulfates and pyrosulfates are formed, which dissolve the 
vanadium salts, and thus, it has been recognized that the active phase of the working catalyst is a molten 
salt  mixture[40,41].Topsøe and Nielsen [44] studied the influence of K, Na, Rb and Cs and considered that 
sodium was the worst catalyst activator since they are able to take up less SO3 than corresponds to normal 
pyrosulphate. Which is not beneficial for the reduction in the melting point and viscosity of the molten salts 
formed.

3. Experimental work 
 
 
In order to determinate experimentally the catalytic activity of vanadium for the oxidation of 
SO2 to SO3 is necessary:first of all, catalytic particles, some solid material with vanadium that simulates in 
the best way the ones in the engine. Then, a reactor where place them and measure some variables that 
help to study the kinetics. For the vanadium particles was chosen a flame spray pyrolysis (FSP) method, as 
described below we can achieve similar nanoparticles to the ones formed during combustion. The reactor is 
a quartz fixed bed reactor, it operates as a plug flow reactor, is expected to be inert [39] and we can have 
isothermal conditions. The concentration of SO2 in the outlet will be measured. Both setups are described 
below. 
 
3.1 Flame Spray Pyrolysis 
Liquid fed flame spray pyrolysis (FSP) is a novel one-step synthesis method for preparation of 
nano-sized particles [56,57]. Typically, organic metal compounds are dissolved in an organic solvent and the 
precursor solution is sprayed as micrometer sized droplets with high velocity oxygen and ignited with a 
small premixed methane-oxygen flame (Figure 3.1) [58]. The solvent and metal compounds quickly 
evaporate and combust to form atomically dispersed vapors of metal oxides, which nucleate to form 
clusters when reaching cooler parts of the flame. The formed clusters grow by surface growth and 
coalescence, accompanied by sintering [59]. Theseresults in non-porous nanoparticles, which coagulate and 
sinter to form agglomerates and aggregates with high inter particle porosity [56]. Most of the combustion 
enthalpy for the spray flame comes from the solvent [60]. It is important, that the combustion enthalpy is 
sufficiently high and the flame is sufficiently hot for rapid vaporization of the liquid droplets of the 
precursor solution, so that nucleation and particle growth occur from the gas phase; otherwise, micrometer 
sized hollow or shell like particles are formed [56,60].                                            
Materials produced by FSP are very interesting candidates for heterogeneous catalysts 
because of their high surface area. Furthermore, the use of precursor solutions containing several metal 
compounds allows one-step preparation of multi-component catalyst materials. Conventional catalyst 
preparation would include precipitation, washing, drying and calcination of the support material followed 
by impregnation, drying and calcination of the active phase [61]. On lab-scale this might take two to four 
days depending on the number of steps, but with FSP the calcined catalyst is obtained within a few hours 
work.  
Besides heterogeneous catalysis, FSP has been applied in several other materials research 
fields, e.g. synthesis of nano-phosphors for bio-labeling [62], gas sensors [63], battery materials [64], fuel 
cells [65], solar cell materials [66] and bio-active nanoparticles [67]. Research and development for pilot 
scale FSP production with production rates around 100 to 500 g/h is performed [68-71].

Figure 3.1: Schematic diagram of the FSP setup at DTU-Chemical Engineering. The precursor solution is injected with a 
syringe pump in the central capillary and is sprayed with high velocity oxygen around the capillary at a pressure drop 
of at least 1.5 bar. The capillary holder can be moved up and down to adjust the pressure drop (PI). The precursor 
droplets are ignited with a premixed methane oxygen flame and the particles collected on a filter above the flame with 
the aid of a vacuum pump. All gas flows are controlled by calibrated mass flow controllers (MFC). Cluster formation, 
particle growth and agglomeration are schematically shown. 
The nozzle (Figure 3.2) and filter holder were designed and constructed at ETH Zürich. The 
capillary has a 0,413 mm inner diameter (Hamilton Gauge 22), and is placed in the capillary holder which 
can move vertically to adjust the dispersion gas pressure drop across the nozzle, typically 1.5 to 3 bar. If the 
pressure drop is lower than 1.5 bar, large droplets are formed in the spray, while the droplet size 
asymptotically reaches about 5 μm with increasing the pressure drop [58].  
The precursor is loaded in a 100 ml gas tight syringe (Hamilton), which is placed in a syringe 
pump (World Precision Instruments ALADDIN), that is calibrated to the inner diameter of the syringe, 
allowing flows between 0.1 and 10 mL/min, but typically 3 to 7 mL/min is used (3 in our case). Generally, 
higher flows result in larger primary particles due to the larger flame and longer residence time at high 
temperature [70,72-74]. Gas flows for the dispersion gas and pilot flame are controlled by calibrated mass 
flow controllers (Brooks) allowing up to 10 Nl/min for the oxygen dispersion gas and 5 Nl/min for the pilot 
flame oxygen and methane, but typically 3 to 7 Nl/min for the dispersion gas is used (5 in our case) 
Generally, the higher the flow of dispersion gas the smaller the primary particle size, due to a smaller flame 
and shorter residence time with increasing oxidant flow [70,72,73].

The pilot flame was operated at 3 Nl/min oxygen and 1 Nl/min methane, which is 50 % excess oxygen in

the end of the FSP synthesis.  
Typically, 50 mL of a precursor solution containing 0,125 mol/L metal was burned and collected on a

spatula and sieving the material through a 600 μm sieve, to remove large pieces of filter material that may 
come loose during scraping. However, small amounts of fine filter material pieces are unavoidable in the 
product and n could slightly affect the surface area measurements and the catalytic activity measurements,

Analyzer 
The analyzer used was a NGA2000 MLT4(Rosemount Analytical), which counts with an UV 
analysis. Molecules as SO2 containing 𝜋𝜋-electrons or non-bonding electrons can absorb the energy in the

glow-discharge lamp is used as radiation source. The analyzer has a dual-section analysis cell, the difference 
between the radiation intensities coming from the measuring and reference side of the analysis cell is a 
reliable measure of the concentration of the absorbing gas.

3.3 Parameters 
 
The parameters used in the experiments were chosen according to the literature, the limited 
time of the project and the previous experience of the supervisors with this topic and similar setups. 
 
First of all, it was decided that the experiments would be run with different compositions of 
catalyst, constant flux rates of the gases and varying the temperature to obtain data of SO2 conversion. The 
residence time was expected not to be important and was confirmed later. According to this and assuming 
a kinetic model, the results obtained were related with the kinetic parameters that fit this data the best in a 
non-linear regression, as will be shown later. 
 
Catalyst composition  
 
As seen before vanadium becomes more active with alkali elements such as: potassium, 
sodium or cesium. For these reasons it was decided to study if one of these elements has relevance in the 
catalytic activity. 
Only sodium was found to have an important concentration in marine fuels in comparison 
with vanadium [1]. It was taken the composition of the particulates in the exhaust gases of a medium-
speed marine diesel engine that run on heavy fuel oil  (HFO), and a ratio V/Na (weight) of 7,9/1 was found 
in accordance with (Figure 2.7). 
Some tests with this composition and 100% vanadium were decided to do at the beginning, 
finding that the vanadium doped with sodium had more reactivity. For this reason, more compositions of 
V/Na were tested. In accordance with the eutectic vanadium-sodium mixtures and its melting point as 
shown in Figure 3.12, a lower ratio and a higher ratio in weight was chosen (4 and 15). 
Other elements like Nickel is also expected to be catalytically active, although, with less 
presence than vanadium in the fuels and the limited time for the experiments it was decided to be studied 
in future works. 
Summing up, three compositions of vanadium-sodium (wt.) were chosen: 4-1, 7,9-1 and 15-
1. Besides, a catalyst composed for only vanadium. The amount of vanadium was fixed and the amount of 
sodium was increased in the preparation. In Appendix A is further explained which was the method to 
prepare the dissolutions and the amounts that were introduced in the flame spray pyrolysis. 
The catalytic particles were mixed with the same amount of silica (150-200 µm), in order to: 
have a good heat dispersion in the bed, a place where the vanadium-sodium compounds can melt (melting 
points around 550-700 °C) and avoid a big pressure drop due to the small size of the vanadium particles. 
The amount of catalyst in the reactor was decided according to similar experiments reported 
in papers [55], in a range of 0,1-1 grams. It was chosen 0,25 grams of catalyst and another 0,25 grams of 
silica.

5. Comparison 
 
 
Determining the kinetic parameters for the oxidation of SO2 to SO3 with vanadium was the 
objective in order to compare with the homogeneous way (gas phase oxidation reactions). These reactions 
are well-stablished by Glarborg set of reaction [32], nevertheless, the comparison between both ways is not 
so obvious. 
 
In order to provide a reliable conclusion and say if the oxidation with vanadium is similarly 
fast to the oxidation in gas phase, some assumptions must be done to compare both cases, one certain 
situation is going to be studied: 
- 
Pressure will be fixed in 50 bar. At about 50−60 crank angles past TDC (Top Dead Centre) the 
SO3/H2SO4 concentration peaks in accordance with practical experience that shows the highest 
rates of surface corrosion in the upper part of the cylinder liner [21], in accordance with Figure 2.4 
there is 50 bar of pressure when the piston passes this points.  
 
- 
Temperature range (500-1500 K). The temperature in the engine is constantly changing and varies 
inside the engine, as seen in Figure 2.3. We thought this range cover the temperatures where the 
oxidation can happen significantly, in accordance with the literature. 
 
- 
600 ppm of SO2. In accordance to the Figure 2.5 from MAN DIESEL & TURBO of a typical MC type 
two stroke diesel engine running with fuel of 3% S content. 
 
- 
Time of reaction of 0,1 seconds. The 4T50MX test engine located at the research facility at the 
headquarters of MAN Diesel & Turbo A/S in Copenhagen has a nominal performance of 7500 kW at 
123 rpm. This correspond to 0,24 seconds per stroke.  Our time seems reasonable.

6. Conclusions and future work 
 
In order to satisfy the environmental regulations that are coming and run safely the two-stroke 
marine engines, problems with corrosion and especially cold corrosion must be studied. The aim of this 
project has been helping to the understanding of this problem, particularly in the formation of SO3, which 
can reacts with water and form sulfuric acid. All the sulfur passes to sulfur dioxide and the oxidation of SO2 
to SO3 that takes place in gas-phase has reactions which are comparatively well established. However, the 
fuel oil contains trace amounts of vanadium and vanadium oxide (V2O5) is known to catalyze SO2 oxidation, 
meanly for the contact process in the sulfuric acid production.  The importance of the catalyzed pathway to 
SO3 is still unknown but in this project some experiments and kinetic modeling have been done to evaluate 
the relative importance of homogeneous and heterogeneous (catalyzed by vanadium oxides) oxidation of 
SO2 into SO3. 
The experimental method followed with the flame spray pyrolysis (FSP) has pretended to 
emulate the formation of vanadium particles in the engine. Besides, the composition of these particles is 
not only vanadium, more elements are present in the engines and sodium seems to be the most important, 
it was expected to make the catalyst more reactive and the results confirmed it, the more vanadium the 
more reactive the catalyst becomes.  The particles synthesized with the flame spray pyrolysis method have 
shown reactivity, although the exact composition and stechiometry is unknown. At least, the 
vanadium/sodium ratio is expected to be the same as in the initial dissolution before burning it, and they 
are expected to be representative of the particles ratios inside the engine also.  Some XRD or atomic 
absorption spectroscopy (AAS) analysis should be done to know more about the particles. The surface 
chemistry in this kind of particles could be compared then with the supported vanadium catalysts in the 
solid state and liquid state (like the sulfuric acid process), based on the literature. 
The oxidation was studied in a fixed bed reactor at atmospheric pressure, these are the first 
results for the oxidation of SO2 with this kind of particles (as far as we know) and the effect with pressure 
should be studied in the future. 
The results obtained for the concentration of SO2 depended a lot on the waited time for the 
analyzer signal to get stabilized. More experiments of reproducibility should be done. 
The results of the experiments at different temperatures have been studied to determinate the 
kinetic parameters. It has been used a simple kinetic model with a non-linear regression where the 
parameters that fit better the experimental data were chosen. Only the catalyst with the highest content in 
sodium shows a similar trend between model and experimental data. This could be because of different 
chemistries are involved, one at low temperatures (solid state) and other at high temperatures, when the 
catalyst melts. The catalyst with more sodium would be mainly represented by the chemistry in the liquid 
state, due to the higher concentration of shulphates that reduce the melting point, and that is why fits 
better. Others kinetic models should be studied or more experiments should be done to determinate the 
kinetic parameters (different concentrations and weights of catalyst).  
 
==PAGE:59==
 
55 
 
The experiment with a lower concentration of SO2 showed higher conversion above 350 °C and 
close the equilibrium the same. This could be because the reaction needs two active sites or the SO3 that is 
formed is blocking the sites, so we have more conversion with less concentration of SO2. Although in the 
literature this behavior was found below 400 °C for a vanadium catalyst supported in silica. The trend 
followed by the rest of catalysts is quite similar, but with this one is quite different. 
The residence time was found not to be important in the range of temperatures studied. 
The homogeneous way dominates the oxidation of SO2 in our comparison. Above 600 °C the 
homogeneous way is governing and above 700 °C the difference is around 3 orders of magnitude. The 
heterogeneous way is more important below 600 °C, but the conversions are significantly low.  
Some kinetic parameters for the heterogeneous way in the comparison were tried. In spite of 
these assumptions, the conversions below 700 °C are still low (max 0,001), at higher temperatures the 
differences are around 1-2 orders of magnitude.   
The experiments with lower temperatures after melting the catalyst showed higher 
conversions, a kind of hysteresis. Probably the sodium-vanadium compound melted in the silica surface 
(with higher surface area), spreads out across the silica surface, and the catalytically active surface area 
may increase. The experimental data was not enough, so the kinetic parameters obtained and then 
compared with the gas-phase could be not very reliable. More experiments should be done and an analysis 
of the catalyst surface could be interesting. 
The concentration of vanadium per mol of SO2 is supposed homogeneous in the engine in our 
simulation. One possible situation is the accumulation of the particles in the lube oil of the piston walls, 
where the temperatures are lower due to refrigeration. Some analysis in real or test engines should be 
done to the lube oil. 
The vanadia particles were saturated with SO2 during our experiments cause we needed a 
stable signal. The vanadia surface in the engine is unknown but we think this is the best way to determinate 
the kinetic parameters of the reaction. 
The comparison between gas-phase and vanadium particles has been studied in the stroke of 
power where the pressure is high and the temperatures too, in the stroke of compression could be 
different, with lower temperatures due to the scavenging air, and lower pressures. 
There are equipments for the vessels to satisfy the NOx regulations that are coming, for 
example: exhaust gas recirculation (EGR). In this system, around 40% of the exhaust gases are recirculated 
to the engine as scavenging air, they are treated with NaOH and almost all the sulhpur is removed but not 
all the particles, the fine ones are entering again the engine dopped with sodium and sulphur. As studied in 
this project, this should be taken into account when studying corrosional problems in the engine. 
Trying to elucidate if vanadium is important in the formation of SO3 has been the goal of this 
project, although more work should be done. For the moment, the oxidation of SO2 with vanadium 
catalysts is not as fast as the gas-phase.

temperature was running during a longer time. We couldn’t afford really long times for each temperatures

experiments was limited. 
When the measurement is taken the temperature is changed to next one or the experiment with 
the catalyst is finished, in this last case the oven is switched off, the flow rates are set to 0 and the gas 
valves are closed, except the nitrogen that cleans the system.

Appendix B: mass flow controllers calibration 
 
 
The mass flow controllers (MFCs) are the devices to control the flow rates of the gases. The 
MFC is given an input signal by the operator (that it compares to the value from the mass flow sensor and 
adjusts the proportional valve accordingly to achieve the required flow. The flow rate is specified as a 
percentage of its calibrated full scale flow and is supplied to the MFC as a voltage signal. 
 
These MFCs (Bronkhorst) are calibrated with N2 at normal conditions (0 °C, 1 atm), for this 
reason, in order to send the flow rate desired to the reactor, they were calibrated withan air flow calibrator 
(Gilibrator 2). The volumetric flow rate is determinate with a sensor which calculates the time that a soap 
bubble needs to do a determinate distance. The calibration was done checking the temperature and 
pressure in the room, then the values were transformed to standard conditions. It was represented the 
flow rate [smL/min] vs the valve’s percentage and using a trend line the MFCs could be used correctly.
