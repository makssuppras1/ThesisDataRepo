Abstract
Isolated populations are shaped by social behaviors and geographic constraints,
but the relative importance of these factors in driving genetic diversity remains
unclear.
This thesis investigates how population size, geographic proximity, and in-
breeding avoidance strategies influence patterns of ancestry, relatedness, and
genetic variation. Using a forward-in-time simulation framework based on the
Wright-Fisher model, populations are modeled over multiple generations with
mating partners chosen based on geographic distance—implemented via the von
Mises distribution—and mating rules that exclude close-relative pairings.
Simulations were run across a range of population sizes and spatial parame-
ters, from uniform (panmictic) mating to strong geographic clustering (increasing
𝜅). Inbreeding avoidance was examined progressively by first excluding sibling
matings, then additionally excluding first cousins. By tracking both genealogical
lineages and genetic segments—including identity-by-descent (IBD) and runs of
homozygosity (ROH)—the effects of these parameters on genetic diversity were
evaluated. Geographic proximity not only increased ROH, but also prolonged
the divergence between genealogical and genetic ancestors, as spatial constraints
limited the spread of genetic material across the population. In contrast, avoiding
cousin-level inbreeding significantly reduced ROH.
This work provides a flexible framework for exploring how social and spatial
constraints shape genetic structure, and offers insights applicable to conserva-
tion biology, anthropology, and the evolutionary dynamics of isolated human and
natural populations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Isolated human populations—bounded by oceans, mountains, or cultural norms—serve as
natural experiments for studying evolution. These groups, termed insular populations, experience
restricted gene flow, small effective population sizes, and unique demographic histories that amplify
genetic drift, founder effects, and inbreeding [1]. When individuals in such groups inherit the
same segment of DNA from a common ancestor, the segment is said to be identical by descent
(IBD), which can lead to runs of homozygosity (ROH) if both copies of the same genomic region
match [2] [3]. Over many generations, repeated inheritance within a small, isolated population
causes autozygosity to rise significantly, increasing the occurrence of recessive genetic disorders
[4]. From remote islands to culturally bounded communities, these populations highlight how
isolation reshapes the genetic landscape through increased homozygosity and a greater prevalence
of recessive disorders [2]
One illustrative case is the population of the Faroe Islands, an isolated human group with a
history traced back to Viking settlers around the 9th century[5]. The population remained small
for centuries, with estimates suggesting a population of about 4000 in the late 1300s, increasing
to 9000 by the 1900s and rising to approximately 50000 its modern population. Migration to and
from the island has been minimal. The demographic history has also been shaped by challenges
such as epidemics- including a smallpox outbreak of 1709 and periods of famine, which have
reduced the population drastically[6]. These demographic shocks have contributed to a unique
genetic pool, characterized by limited genetic disorders. For example, the incidence of Primary
Carnitine Deficiency, a rare metabolic disorder is estimated to occur in about 1 in 300, due to
founder effects and prolonged isolation[5]. Genomic analyses reveal elevated autozygosity, with
some individuals showing up to 25% of their genome in runs of homozygosity, consistent with the
effects of inbreeding and drift in a small, closed population[5]. Such extreme autozygosity aligns
with predictions from forward-in-time simulations, showing that small populations can accumulate
ROH covering 40% of the genome within 50 generations, even without consanguinity [7].
While island populations offer clear examples of geographic isolation, insularity can also arise
through cultural or social boundaries that restrict gene flow. Over 10% of the global population de-
scends from unions between individuals who are second cousins or closer, primarily due to cultural
and religious norms[4]. For instance, The Old Order Amish of North America descended from just
200 Swiss-German founders in the 18th century, have maintained strict religious endogamy and
large family sizes. This cultural insularity has elevated frequencies of autosomal recessive disor-
1
==PAGE:9==
ders like Ellis-van Creveld syndrome (1 in 5000 Amish births vs. 1 in 100,000 globally), a legacy
of limited gene flow and consanguinity [8]. Similarly, in parts of South America—particularly
among Indigenous Andean and Amazonian communities—geographic isolation and kin-based so-
cial structures have shaped distinct genetic profiles [9, 10]. These populations often exhibit founder
effects and reduced genetic diversity, contributing to a higher frequency of certain rare disorders,
such as congenital adrenal hyperplasia, hereditary deafness, and G6PD deficiency [11, 12]. Such
patterns reflect how cultural and environmental isolation can interact to influence both genetic
variation and disease risk.
Aims and Objectives
By formalizing these interactions into a computational framework, this thesis extends a
forward-in-time Wright-Fisher simulation model [7] to investigate how geographic structure and
social practices such as inbreeding avoidance shape genetic diversity and ancestry patterns in
isolated populations.
The primary research questions addressed are:
• How does inbreeding avoidance affect genetic diversity and genealogical convergence in
structured populations?
• How does geographic proximity in mate selection influence genetic relatedness, homozygos-
ity patterns, and genealogical convergence?
• How does population size affect genetic diversity and genealogical convergence in structured
populations?
Based on previous studies and initial observations, the thesis tests the following hypotheses:
• Geographic clustering in mate choice will increase both the frequency and length of ROH
segments and lead to earlier TMRCA and IAP due to faster genealogical convergence within
spatially restricted populations.
• Implementing mating restrictions against siblings or first cousins will reduce immediate
inbreeding events, leading to lower overall ROH levels, reduced IBD sharing, and later
TMRCA and IAP due to slower convergence of genealogical lineages.
• The impact of population size on genetic diversity and genealogical convergence will align
with previous findings, predicting that smaller populations exhibit higher ROH levels and
earlier TMRCA and IAP due to stronger genetic drift.

2.1

2.1.1
The Human Genome
Life as we understand it is defined by the genetic blueprints of the myriad organisms with which
we coexist. Each organism carries a genome that encodes the biological instructions necessary
to develop and sustain a living example of its species. The human genome is organized into two
distinct components : the nuclear and the mitochondrial. The nuclear genome consists of roughly
3.2 billion nucleotides of DNA distributed across 23 pairs of linear chromosomes, ranging in size
from approximately 50 million to 250 million nucleotides. These chromosomes include 22 pairs
of autosomes and two sex chromosomes (X and Y). The mitochondrial genome, by contrast, is
a circular DNA molecule of 16569 nucleotides located in mitochondria, is inherited exclusively
from the mother, and provides a unique avenue for tracing maternal lineage across generations [13].
This dual genomic structure -nuclear and mitochondrial- reflects the evolutionary and functional
divergence of these genetic systems.

2.1.2
Sexual Reproduction
Sexual reproduction plays a significant role in this genomic architecture by facilitating the
mixing and inheritance of the nuclear genome. It relies on meiosis, a specialized cell division
process exclusive to reproductive cells. Meiosis involves two nuclear divisions, one after the other,
Meiosis I and Meiosis II. This process not only halves the chromosome number, preparing for the
fusion of gametes, but also facilitates the extensive mixing and inheritance of the nuclear genome
essential for genetic diversity.
Meiosis I:Synapsis, Recombination and Independent Assortment
In Meiosis I, homologous chromosomes pair up and align to form bivalent structures containing
four chromatids. This pairing, called synapsis, enables crossing over, where chromatids within the
bivalent physically break and exchange DNA segments. This recombination shuffles the genetic
material between homologs, ensuring that the daughter cells contain a unique mix of maternal and
paternal DNA.

Following recombination, as the cell progresses to metaphase I, another crucial mechanism
called independent assortment comes into play. This process involves the random orientation of
each tetrad at the metaphase plate, which determines how the pairs of homologous chromosomes
are separated into two daughter cells. Independent assortment allows for a mix of maternal and
paternal chromosomes to be distributed randomly to each gamete. For example, in a human cell with
23 chromosome pairs, this results in 223 or over 8 million possible combinations of chromosomes,
vastly increasing the potential genetic diversity [13].
Meiosis II: Separation of Sister Chromatids
In Meiosis II, sister chromatids separate,moving to opposite poles to form haploid cells. This
stage amplifies the diversity created in the first meiotic division, ensuring that the four resulting
gametes are genetically distinct. The events of Meiosis are illustrated in Figure 2.3 below.

2.1.3
Mutations
Another key molecular factor that plays a significant role in genetic diversity is mutation.
Mutations are permanent changes in the DNA sequence that introduce novel genetic variation.
These mutations can occur as a result of errors during the DNA replication process in meiosis,
despite the presence of repair mechanisms, or due to environmental factors. The types of mutations
include substitutions, insertions, deletions, or chromosomal rearrangements.
While mutations
in non-coding regions ( 98% of the human genome) generally have no functional impact, those
in coding regions may change protein structure or truncate proteins entirely.
Most mutations
are neutral or deleterious, but rare beneficial ones can enhance survival or reproduction, driving
adaptation over generations. Over generations, mutations are passed on, lost because of genetic
drift, get fixed by chance or with the help of selective pressures, new mutations are constantly being
produced, and the pairing of particular alleles is being shuffled via recombination [14].

2.1.4
Population Genetic Forces
The genetic diversity of a population resides in its gene pool, which is the sum of all alleles
across its members’ genomes[16]. In isolated systems, such as island communities or socially con-
strained groups, restricted gene flow and small effective population sizes reduce genetic diversity,
causing allele frequencies to drift stochastically over generations [17]. This erosion of the gene
pool amplifies autozygosity (e.g., ROH) and shared IBD segments, phenomena that are central to
this study’s focus on the tracking of ancestral lineages. Populations change over time due to three
fundamental forces: natural selection, genetic drift, and gene flow.
Natural selection is the process by which heritable traits that enhance survival or reproductive
success become more prevalent in a population over generations. This mechanism, first articulated
by Charles Darwin, operates when individuals with advantageous traits—such as camouflage in
prey species or disease resistance—outcompete others, leaving more offspring. For example, in
snow-covered environments, rabbits with white fur evade predators more effectively than their
brown-furred counterparts, leading to a gradual dominance of white fur alleles in the population.
This principle extends beyond morphology: antibiotic resistance in bacteria arises through selection
favoring mutations that confer survival advantages in drug-exposed environments [18]. Similarly,

Gene flow, the transfer of alleles through migration, counteracts genetic divergence by ho-
mogenizing allele frequencies across populations. When individuals move and reproduce in new
groups, they introduce novel genetic variants, mitigating diversity loss caused by drift. For ex-
ample, mainland birds migrating to an island may introduce alleles for larger beak size, enabling
island populations to exploit new food sources. Conversely, restricted gene flow—as observed in
geographically isolated populations like Darwin’s finches—allows genetic differences to accumu-
late, driving the evolution of distinct traits such as beak morphology[20]. In human populations,
historical migration events, such as the movement of Neolithic farmers into Europe, reshaped
regional genetic diversity [21]. However, when gene flow ceases, populations diverge, creating
unique genetic signatures that reflect their isolation.

Genetic drift, the random fluctuation of allele frequencies, is particularly influential in small or
isolated populations. For example, in island communities with limited migration, rare alleles may
disappear purely by chance, even if they confer no disadvantage. This stochasticity is quantified by
the effective population size (Ne) , which reflects the number of individuals contributing genetically
to future generations. Unlike the census size (N) , which counts all individuals, Ne accounts for
factors like unequal reproductive success, overlapping generations, and historical bottlenecks [17].
For instance, a population of 1,000 individuals may have an Ne of just 200 if reproductive
success is highly skewed (e.g., a few dominant males sire most offspring).
This smaller Ne
accelerates genetic drift, acting as if the population were far smaller than its census size. In isolated
systems like the Faroe Islands, repeated bottlenecks and endogamous mating practices reduce Ne,
reflecting the erosion of genetic diversity and stochastic loss of genealogical ancestry over time.

2.3
The Divergence Between Genealogical and Genetic Ances-
try
Genetic ancestry reflects a subset of genealogical ancestry due to processes like recombination
and genetic drift. Even individuals separated by significant geographic distances share numerous
genealogical ancestors over relatively short time periods, with Ralph and Coop (2013)[23] noting
that pairs of individuals from opposite ends of Europe share hundreds of genetic common ancestors
over the last 1,000 years. Despite this shared ancestry, striking regional differences exist, driven by
factors such as geographic proximity, migration patterns, and population history.
While genealogical ancestry traces all ancestors within a pedigree, genetic ancestry reflects
only those who contribute detectable DNA due to recombination and drift. As Thompson (2013)[3]
explains, genetic ancestry at any given locus is shaped by the random segregation of DNA across
generations, which can result in certain genealogical ancestors failing to contribute any detectable
genetic material.
Over time, recombination breaks apart segments of inherited DNA, leading
to a high variance in the distribution of shared genetic material across the genome (Thompson,
p. 303). This process explains why genetic ancestry provides only a partial view of genealogical
relationships, with distant ancestors often being genetically unrepresented despite their genealogical
ties.
This divergence becomes even more pronounced in small, isolated populations, where limited
genetic diversity and higher rates of inbreeding amplify the effects of genetic drift. In such popula-
tions, a smaller pool of ancestors contributes genetic material, leading to an increased probability
that individuals inherit identical genetic segments from shared ancestors. [17]; [Franklin1977]).
Thompson [3] further emphasizes that in these populations, the high variance of recombination
across the genome leads to some ancestral genetic contributions being entirely lost, resulting in
extended stretches of homozygous DNA. These processes have significant implications for the
study of genetic ancestry, as they highlight how the size and structure of a population influence the
retention or loss of genealogical relationships at the genetic level.
Pedigrees provide a structured framework to trace genealogical relationships and the inheri-
tance of genetic material across generations. Donnelly [24] introduced the concept of chromosome
pedigrees, emphasizing how genetic material flows through lineages, which he modeled as a
continuous-time random walk. This approach links genealogical ancestry to genetic ancestry by
highlighting the conditions under which segments of the genome are shared identical by descent
(IBD). Within this framework , the variance in recombination across the genome can result in the

2.4
Metrics for Measuring Genetic Diversity
2.4.1
Time to Most Recent Common Ancestor (TMRCA)
TMRCA represents the number of generations required to trace a group of individuals back
to their most recent shared ancestor. It is widely used in coalescent theory, which models how
gene lineages merge over time[25]. The expected TMRCA depends on various factors, including
population size, mutation rates and mating structures.In a population with constant size and random
mating, coalesce theory predicts that TMRCA follows an exponential distribution, with larger
populations leading to longer coalescence time [26]. However, in real world populations where
there is non-random mating, population bottlenecks and geographic structure, the distribution of
TMRCA changes.
2.4.2
Identical Ancestral Point (IAP)
The Identical Ancestral Point (IAP) refers to the time at which all individuals in a population
share the same set of genealogical ancestors, even though many of these ancestors may not have
contributed detectable genetic material. This concept is distinct from the time to the Most Recent
Common Ancestor (TMRCA), as the IAP considers all ancestral lineages, rather than just the single
lineage leading to the most recent shared ancestor. In large human populations under random mating
models, the IAP has been shown to occur within the last 3,000 years, indicating a surprisingly
rapid convergence of genealogical ancestry despite geographic dispersion and demographic scale.
However, due to genetic drift and recombination, many of these ancestors’ genetic contributions
are lost over time, meaning that genealogical ancestry extends much further back than detectable
genetic ancestry [27]. Supporting this, Derrida, Manrubia, and Zanette [28] analyzed the statistical
properties of genealogical trees and found that ancestral lineages rapidly coalesce, leading to a
scenario where all individuals in a population trace back to the same set of genealogical ancestors
in a surprisingly short time frame. Their results demonstrated that, even when accounting for
realistic population structures, the time to IAP remains logarithmic in population size, further
reinforcing the rapid collapse of genealogical lineages into a common ancestral set.

of a pedigree or to specific points in a gene’s coalescent history within a population. DNA that
is an exact replica from an ancestor is considered identical by descent, which is always measured
relative to a chosen ancestral reference population. This ancestral reference can be the founders of
a pedigree or a particular individual from a specific historical time[3].

2.4.4
Runs of Homozygosity ROH
Runs of homozygosity (ROH) are long stretches of consecutive homozygous genotypes that
arise when identical haplotypes are inherited from both parents. First recognized by [29], ROH
reflects autozygosity and has significant implications for gene mapping and human health. [30]
highlighted the importance of analyzing the lengths, numbers, and distribution of ROH, demon-
strating their presence even in populations without known close inbreeding. ROH patterns provide
valuable insights into demographic history, as their length and frequency reflect past population
dynamics. Short ROH segments indicate background relatedness from distant common ancestors,
whereas long ROH arises from recent parental relatednesses, such as in populations that have un-
dergone bottlenecks or recent isolation [31]. [4] further showed that small or isolated populations
tend to have more and longer ROH due to increased genetic drift and reduced effective population
size, while large populations exhibit fewer ROH.
Beyond demographic inference, ROH also plays a critical role in studying the genetic basis of
complex diseases, as increased homozygosity can increase the risk of recessive disorders. These
findings underscore the broad utility of ROH in understanding human genetic diversity and disease
susceptibility. In figure ??, the female ancestor’s chromosomes (red and pink) represent those

2.5
Inbreeding and inbreeding avoidance
2.5.1
Inbreeding
Inbreeding, a well-studied phenomenon in biology, occurs when offspring are produced
through the mating of genetically close individuals or organisms [32]. This process increases
homozygosity and exposes deleterious recessive alleles, reducing biological fitness and leading to
a condition known as inbreeding depression [33] . A high homozygosity rate is problematic for
a population because it can expose harmful recessive genes caused by mutations and reduce the
benefits of genetic diversity [34]. Small, isolated human populations, such as those found on islands
or within religious isolates, are especially vulnerable to inbreeding due to limited mate choice and
endogamous marriage practices [35]. Studies have documented elevated rates of genetic disorders
Page 14 of 63
==PAGE:22==
2.6. Geographic Proximity
in such populations, including increased frequencies of congenital diseases, metabolic disorders,
and neurodevelopmental conditions [4]. The Faroe Islands exemplify this trend: their small and
genetically homogenous populations have documented a history of genetic drift and founder effects
[36][37].
Due to geographic and social isolation, the population exhibits high levels of IBD sharing
which leads to increasing the prevalence of ROHs [36]. Studies have documented elevated rates
of genetic disorders in the Faroese population.
For example, this population exhibits higher
frequencies of carnitine transporter deficiency (CTD) and glycogen storage disease type IIA, both
of which have been linked to the small effective population size and historical bottlenecks.[38].
While some genetic variation has been introduced through external migration, its impact has been
minor, and endogamy continues to play a significant role in shaping genetic diversity [37], [36].
The adverse effects of inbreeding were first systematically documented by Charles Darwin,
who noted reduced offspring survival rates and compromised health among inbred offspring[39]
Subsequent studies have shown that inbreeding is associated with decreased fertility, increased
risk of stillbirth and infant death, premature delivery, low birth weight, and stunned growth[40]
[41]. Surviving inbred children are more prone to suffer from physical abnormalities and inherited
genetic conditions.
Research has established links between inbreeding and a wide variety of
disorders, including blindness, hearing loss, neonatal diabetes, limb malformations, disorders of
sex development, and schizophrenia[42] [43] .
2.5.2
Inbreeding Avoidance
Inbreeding avoidance has thus emerged as a significant evolutionary strategy for maintaining
genetic diversity and preventing the adverse consequences effects of inbreeding depression [44].
Natural mechanisms for inbreeding avoidance include kin recognition, where individuals are able to
identify and avoid mating with close relatives, and dispersal behaviors, where they move away from
their birth group to reduce the probability of mating with a relative. These strategies are vital for
population health and longevity, ensuring a diverse genetic pool is maintained over generations[45]
.
By simulating inbreeding avoidance mechanisms in computational models—such as prioritiz-
ing genetic diversity or penalizing matings between closely related agents—algorithms can emulate
evolutionary strategies that maintain population health and adaptability. This approach not only
mirrors biological systems but also provides insights into optimizing genetic diversity in artificial
populations, a critical factor in evolutionary computation and population management studies

between individuals increases [1]. In order to investigate genetic diversity in a population within

Chapter 3
Methodology
This chapter outlines the computational framework developed to investigate how geographic
constraints on mate selection and inbreeding avoidance rules shape genetic diversity and ancestral
lineage patterns in structured populations. It covers the fundamental principles of the model, how
populations are initialized, and how genetic information is inherited across gnerations. It also
explains how mating behaviors are simulated and how key genetic and genealogical metrics are
used to analyze the results.Finally, it outlines how different parameters can be adjusted to explore
various evolutionary scenarios.
The original framework, implemented in Golang,designed to track how genes are spread, how
they recombine, and how genetic material is inherited over several generations. It relies on the
standard assumptions of the Wright-Fisher model.
• Discrete-time generations
• Constant population size
• Uniform probability of recombination across the genome
• Autosomal inheritance (excluding sex chromosomes and mitochondrial DNA)
• Random mating (gender-neutral)
• No mutations
3.1

To expand its analytical capabilities, the model was ported to Python to leverage its scientific
computing ecosystem (NumPy, SciPy) and enhance analytical flexibility. The translation process
retained core Wright-Fisher assumptions: non-overlapping generations, constant population size
N, autosomal inheritance, and mutation-free evolution. Key algorithmic components—including
recombination mechanics and generational transitions—were reimplemented using vectorized op-
erations for computational efficiency.

3.2
Population Architecture Across Generations
The model starts to set up the population through the simulation of all individuals, generation
by generation.
Initialization (Generation 0)
The initial population is generated with N diploid individuals assigned random angular
positions(𝜃) uniformly ∈[0, 2𝜋), simulating geographic preferences that influences mate selec-
tion.
The population is evenly split, with half of the individuals being female and the other half
male. Chromosomes are initialized as continuous segments, with the total length equal to the sum
of the chromosomes lengths. The chromosomes are initialized with no recombination breakpoints
since generation 0 has no ancestors.
Generational Transition
Each generation is completely replaced by the next, with no overlapping individuals between
generations. Parental genetic material is passed to offspring via recombination breakpoints. From
the beginning, each individual is assigned.Unique identifiers (IDs) , one for mother and one for the
father, even though generation 0 starts without prior lineage. These IDs become crucial as they are
used in subsequent generations to track familial relations and prevent inbreeding.
Recombination Mechanism
Recombination is modeled as a stochastic process guided by a user-defined recombination
rate, which determines the likelihood of crossover events occurring along the chromosomes. Each
parent’s chromosomes undergo recombination independently: breakpoints are generated along
the chromosome lengths using a Poisson process, where the number of breaks is proportional to
the recombination rate and the total chromosome length. These breakpoints partition parental
chromosomes into alternating segments, which are inherited by the offspring.
For example, a maternal chromosome might be split into segments at positions 0, 100, and
200, with the offspring inheriting segments alternately from the maternal and paternal haplotypes.

==PAGE:28==

The breakpoints are generated, sorted, and assigned to maternal or paternal haplotypes. This
approach mirrors biological recombination, where genetic material is shuffled between homologous
chromosomes.
Importantly, the recombination mechanism remains consistent with the initial
framework—no modifications have been introduced to the core logic.
3.2.1
Mating System
In each generation 𝑡+1, the process of creating an offspring, starts with the selection of a female
from generation 𝑡who will become the mother. Once a female is selected, potential male partners
are identified from the same generation. These males are those who remain available,meaning they
have not yet been paired with another individual and marked as "singles".Once a female is paired
with a partner and becomes a mother in an earlier instance, she stays with the same partner, and
they continue to have additional children together.
Inbreeding Avoidance
The next step is to filter out any potential partners who are closely related to the female,
specifically siblings and first cousins. This is done by filtering out males who share at least one
parent (mother or father) with the female by comparing parental lineage IDs. For cousin avoidance,
males sharing at least one grandparent with the female are also excluded.
Geographically Structured Mating
After establishing the list of eligible males based on ibreeding avoidance, the selection of a
male partner is spatially structured using the von Mises distribution. This distribution is centered
on the mother’s geographic location 𝜇prioritizing males who are geographically proximate to her.
This spatial bias is modulated by the concentration parameter 𝜅which determines the strength
of clustering around the mother’s location. A higher 𝜅value ( e.g. 𝜅= 20 or 𝜅=100) restricts
mate selection to males very close to the mother, simulating strong philopatry or limited dispersal.
Conversely, lower 𝜅value ( 𝜅=0, 𝜅=1 ) relax this constraint, allowing mating to occur over broader
spatial ranges, akin to panmixia.This distribution is applied to simulate the continuity of spatial mate
choices, akin to populations spread along geographical features like archipelagos. The strength of
geographic preference is modulated by the kappa parameter of the von Mises distribution, where
higher values indicate a stronger preference.
Once a male is selected, the pair is marked as partnered and removed from the pool of available
mates, enforcing strict monogamy.
The model explores how spatial clustering of mates influences genetic diversity and related-
ness. By adjusting the parameter 𝜅, the simulation tests scenarios where individuals mate either
within tight geographic ranges (high 𝜅,) or across broader areas (low 𝜅,). Simultaneously, it eval-
uates three tiers of inbreeding avoidance—no restrictions, excluding siblings, or excluding both
siblings and cousins—to study how these rules interact with spatial behavior.

3.2.2
Children’s location placement
After the mating pair is selected, the offspring’s geographic location should be determined.
Throughout the development of the model, we tested three different ways to determine where each
child should be placed on the circle. The goal was to find a method that would reflect realistic
patterns of dispersal while preserving the spatial structure across generations.
The first approach was to place each child somewhere between their parents. More specifi-
cally,this was done by selecting a location between the parents’ angles on the circle.At first, this
method seemed a reasonable and simple approach. However, after several generations,individuals
started clustering in a narrow region of the circle.As a result, spatial diversity and balance were
lost.
To address the clustering issue, a second method was tested in which each child’s position
was sampled uniformly across the circle, independent of parental locations. Although this resolved
the clustering problem, it also erased all spatial continuity between generations.This approach
resembled panmixia and undermined the goal of modeling structural spatial populations.
To strike a balance between these two extremes, we implemented a third strategy using the
von Mises distribution. In this method, the distribution centered on the midpoint between the
mother’s and father’s angular position . The concentration parameter 𝜅dictates how tightly the
offspring’s location clusters around this midpoint.This mechanism ensures that offspring tend to
stay in locations that closely resemble where their parents are found, which mirrors what we often
see in the natural world.
Therefore, this method was adopted for the simulations, as it successfully preserved spatial
continuity, maintained realistic child placement, and avoided the problems encountered with the
previous approaches.Additionally, the model allows the user to adjust the 𝜅parameter as an input,
providing flexibility to explore different levels of spatial dispersal.

3.3. Genetic and Genealogical Metrics
3.3
Genetic and Genealogical Metrics
3.3.1
Genealogical Ancestors and Descendants
To calculate genealogical ancestors, the model traces lineage backward through generations
by iteratively collecting the parents of each individual. Starting from the present-day population,
a breadth-first search algorithm expands the set of ancestors for each individual by adding their
parents’ unique identifiers (IDs) at each preceding generation.For example, if an individual in
generation 10 has parents labeled "ID 45" and "ID 82" from generation 9, those IDs become their
immediate genealogical ancestors. The time to the most recent common ancestor (TMRCA) is
determined as the earliest generation where at least one ancestor is shared by every individual in
the modern population. Beyond the TMRCA, the identical ancestors point (IAP) is identified as
the generation where all individuals either contribute to the entire modern population or have no
surviving descendants.
Genealogical descendants, conversely, are calculated forward in time.The model aggregates
all direct offspring, grandchildren, and subsequent generations connected through parent-child
relationships. For instance, an individual in generation 3 might have 5 children, 12 grandchildren,
and 20 great-grandchildren, resulting in 37 genealogical descendants by generation 6. The average
number of descendants per individual quantifies reproductive success, reflecting how effectively
lineages proliferate across generations.
3.3.2
Genetic Ancestors and Descendants
For genetic ancestry, the model tracks inherited chromosomal segments labeled with numer-
ical identifiers (IDs) corresponding to their original ancestors. In the founding generation, each
individual’s chromosomes are assigned unique IDs based on their population index. During re-
production, recombination splits parental chromosomes into segments that retain these ancestral
IDs. To quantify genetic ancestors, the model propagates these IDs backward through generations,
reconstructing chromosomes based on parental breakpoints. For each individual, the number of
unique ancestral IDs in their genome is counted, and the mean across the population is computed.
This approach captures the stochastic nature of DNA inheritance, where recombination ensures
genetic ancestors are a subset of genealogical ancestors.
Genetic descendants are defined as individuals who inherit at least one chromosomal segment
with a specific ancestral ID. Unlike genealogical descendants, which include all familial offspring,
genetic descendants reflect the stochastic survival of DNA through recombination.
3.3.3
Calculation of IBD
To compute the IBD, every unique pair of individuals within a generation is compared. For each
pair, four haplotype combinations are evaluated: maternal-maternal, maternal-parental, paternal-
maternal, paternal-paternal,ensuring that shared segments from either haplotype contribute to
IBD detection. The algorithm detects overlapping segments with matching ancestral IDs, merges
adjacent regions and computes total shared genomic length.
Page 27 of 63
==PAGE:35==
3.3. Genetic and Genealogical Metrics
To provide a comprehensive measure of relatedness, the IBD proportion is calculated by
dividing the total IBD length which is the sum of all these overlapping regions across all pairs by
the theoretical maximum possible shared length, which accounts for the number of unique pairwise
comparisons and the total genome length.
3.3.4
Calculation of ROH
To calculate the ROH in a generation, the focus shifts to individual’s genome.This process
begins by merging recombination breakpoints from maternal and paternal haplotypes to define
contiguous genomic blocks. Each block is analyzed to determine if the ancestral segment identifiers
(IDs) from both parental haplotypes match across its entire length, ensuring precise detection of
homozygous regions. For instance, if a maternal segment spanning positions 0–100 and a paternal
segment spanning the same interval share the same ancestral ID, the entire block is classified as
ROH. In cases where blocks overlap multiple segments, ROH is only confirmed if all overlapping
segments in both haplotypes consistently share identical IDs.
Metrics such as total ROH length, count of distinct ROH regions (ROH frequency), and the
proportion of the genome covered by ROH are calculated to quantify homozygosity.

Chapter 5
Discussion
In this section, we highlight the key findings from our analysis of genetic and genealogical
ancestry, patterns of descent, genetic material transmission, and runs of homozygosity in simulated
populations. These results provide insight into the dynamics of inheritance shaped by population
size, spatial structure, and social mating rules. Through forward-in-time simulations that incorpo-
rate meiosis, recombination, and genetic drift, we were able to observe how these processes interact
to influence coalescence, genetic redundancy, and homozygosity across generations.
5.1
Effect of Population Size
The simulations robustly demonstrate that population size is a primary determinent of ancestral
covergence. As we trace lineages backward in time reveals a clear interplay between population
size and genetic heritage. Initially, the number of ancestors grows exponentially—reflecting the
doubling effect of each generation—but this rapid increase eventually transitions into a logistic
pattern due to the finite size of the population. In larger populations, the exponential phase is
prolonged, whereas in smaller populations, genetic diversity is rapidly exhausted, leading to a swift
convergence toward a common set of ancestors. In contrast, the number of genetic ancestors, which
is affected by recombination and independent assortment, continues to grow over many generations
and eventually stabilizes at roughly 80% of the population size around the time of the most recent
common ancestor (TMRCA). The observed fluctuations near this 80% level are attributed to the
inherent randomness in reproductive success. These results are consistent with the outcomes from
the initial simple model presented by [7], confirming that population size significantly influences
genetic inheritance and that our expanded framework successfully captures these fundamental
dynamics. Moreover, the proportion of IBD segments in our simulations highlights how population
size ROH segments increase as the population size decreases, consistent with empirical studies in
small populations such as the Faroe Islands. [5]

5.2. Effect of Geographic Proximity
5.2
Effect of Geographic Proximity
Introducing spatial structure through the 𝜅parameter had a notable influence on how quickly
genetic lineages merged within the population. In the panmictic case (𝜅= 0), individuals were
free to choose mates from anywhere in the population, resulting in rapid mixing of lineages. This
led to a relatively early coalescence, with the time to the most recent common ancestor (TMRCA)
occurring by generation 11, and the identical ancestors point (IAP) reached by generation 21. Such
quick convergence highlights how unrestricted mating accelerates the merging of genealogical
histories.
As 𝜅increased, mating became increasingly localized, with individuals more likely to select
nearby partners. This geographic constraint slowed the diffusion of lineages across the population.
Under strong spatial clustering (𝜅= 20), the TMRCA was delayed until generation 33, and the IAP
did not occur until generation 67. This pattern illustrates how spatial structure prolongs the time it
takes for all lineages to merge, as geographically isolated subgroups maintain separate genealogies
for longer.
Spatial structure also affected the extent to which individuals in the present population shared
common ancestors. In panmictic populations, individuals could trace their ancestry back to a larger
portion of the population, reaching over 700 unique genetic ancestors. In contrast, strong geographic
clustering limited the spread of genetic material, and the number of genetic ancestors leveled off at
around 400. This indicates that under geographic constraints, lineages remain more localized and
are less likely to contribute genetic material across the entire population. The slower mixing and
reduced coverage are direct consequences of mating within spatially restricted neighborhoods.
Furthermore, spatial structure had a pronounced effect on homozygosity. As 𝜅increased, so
did the accumulation of runs of homozygosity (ROH). By generation 100, the proportion of the
genome in ROH segments nearly doubled from about 9% in the panmictic population to over 17%
when 𝜅= 20. This increase can be explained by the reduced effective population size within local
mating groups and the higher likelihood of mating among distant relatives as it can been seen od.
Since recombination shuffles genetic material across generations, smaller and more isolated gene
pools reduce the diversity of recombined segments, allowing longer homozygous stretches to persist.
These patterns may also help explain empirical observations from real-world isolated populations.
For example, Gíslason et al. [5] studied individuals from the Faroe Islands, an archipelago with
small, geographically distant villages separated by challenging terrain and limited transportation.
Their genetic analysis revealed extensive runs of homozygosity (ROH), high relatedness between
individuals assumed to be unrelated, and evidence of historical bottlenecks. These features were
interpreted as signatures of long-term isolation and restricted gene flow. Our simulation results
under strong spatial constraints (𝜅= 20), where mating is localized to nearby individuals, mirror
these patterns. In these scenarios, we observe a rapid accumulation of ROH segments, reduced
mixing of lineages, and increased genetic clustering—all of which could arise naturally in a setting
like the Faroe Islands. Thus, our model provides a plausible explanation for how the physical
separation of villages could lead to the genetic patterns observed by Gíslason, illustrating how even
simple spatial mating rules can give rise to pronounced genetic structure over time.
Interestingly, while ROH patterns were sensitive to geographic constraints, the average pro-
portion of identity by descent (IBD) segments showed only minor differences between spatial

structures. All scenarios converged to similar IBD proportions by generation 100. This lack of
a strong effect might be due to the balancing forces of genetic drift and mutation. While drift
increases IBD by reducing genetic variation, mutation introduces new differences. Over long peri-
ods, these opposing forces can offset each other, making IBD proportions relatively stable across

a substantial effect on population-level genetic diversity.
Overall, these results confirm the hypothesis that geographic structure influences the rate of
ancestral convergence and the accumulation of homozygosity. The observed patterns are consistent
with predictions from population genetics theory and match known empirical observations in
structured human populations.
5.3
Effect of Inbreeding Avoidance
The results demonstrate that inbreeding avoidance significantly alters genetic dynamics in
a simulated population of 1,000 individuals, mirroring patterns observed in human populations.
When mating between close relatives (e.g., siblings or first cousins) is unrestricted, coalescence
events—such as the time to the most recent common ancestor (TMRCA) and the identity ances-
tor point (IAP)—occur earlier compared to scenarios where such matings are restricted. This
acceleration reflects the rapid merging of genetic lineages under inbreeding, as repeated pairings
among close kin concentrate ancestry into fewer, overlapping lineages. Conversely, avoiding sib-
ling or cousin matings delays coalescence by distributing genetic contributions across a broader
set of ancestors, slowing lineage convergence. These findings align with studies of human popula-
tions, where consanguineous mating practices (e.g., cousin marriage) are associated with reduced
effective population sizes and accelerated coalescence due to increased background relatedness
[35].
Notably, restricting first-cousin matings specifically reduced the proportion of the genome
in runs of homozygosity (ROH), underscoring the genetic benefits of this strategy. Long ROH
segments, typically indicative of recent inbreeding, were less prevalent when cousin pairings
were avoided. This parallels empirical observations in human populations, where consanguinity
increases ROH segments and elevates risks for recessive disorders [31].

Limitations
Several limitations should be considered when interpreting these results. First, the model
assumes non-overlapping generations, which differs from real populations where overlapping age
groups influence ancestry patterns over time. This simplification may underestimate the complexity
of lineage merging and the persistence of genetic contributions across generations.
Second, the simulations are computationally intensive, especially for large population sizes or
broader parameter sweeps. As population size increases, so does the time and memory required to
track ancestry and genetic information across generations, which can limit the feasibility of running
extensive or highly detailed experiments.
Another important consideration is the relatively low frequency of sibling matings in the
baseline scenario, even without explicit inbreeding avoidance. This natural rarity complicates
efforts to isolate and quantify the impact of avoidance mechanisms, as the differences between
restricted and unrestricted mating scenarios may be subtle.

Conclusion
This thesis explored how spatial structure and social constraints shape genetic and genealogical
ancestry in isolated populations, using a forward-in-time Wright-Fisher simulation. Geographic
proximity was found to promote homozygosity and concentrate genetic ancestry, while inbreed-
ing avoidance—especially the restriction of sibling and cousin matings—helped maintain genetic
diversity by broadening the pool of genetic ancestors and descendants.
The findings also highlight the importance of accounting for both drift and mutation in
interpreting long-term identity-by-descent (IBD). While drift increases shared segments over gen-
erations, accumulating mutations can obscure ancestral relationships, complicating the definition
of IBD over time.
Overall, the results support the central hypotheses: geographic proximity tends to increase
homozygosity, while inbreeding avoidance reduces it.
Although genealogical patterns remain
relatively consistent, genetic ancestry is strongly influenced by the interplay between spatial and
social factors. These insights underscore the value of incorporating both biological processes and
social behaviors when modeling ancestry in structured or isolated populations.
Future Work
This model offers numerous opportunities for further research and exploration.
Mathematical formalization: Future studies should aim to mathematically formalize our
simulation results, providing a deeper analytical foundation for understanding observed genetic
dynamics.
Incorporating mutations: Introducing mutation processes would greatly enhance biological
realism, enabling more comprehensive examinations of genetic diversity and evolutionary patterns
over time.
Modeling alternative mating systems: Incorporating alternative mating systems, such as
polygamy and inbreeding preference, could better reflect the diversity of human reproductive
behaviors across cultures and time periods.
Tracing IBD segment age: Another promising research direction involves explicitly tracking
the generational origins of identity-by-descent (IBD) segments, allowing for quantification of
the temporal depth of genetic relationships—such as distinguishing between segments inherited
recently or those tracing back many generations.

Scaling up population sizes: Expanding simulations to larger populations will test the
scalability of the model and improve its relevance to broader population genetic questions.
Estimating founding population sizes: Investigating the minimum founding population
sizes required to achieve the observed genetic diversity in isolated populations—like the Faroe
Islands—would provide valuable insights into historical colonization and demographic events.
