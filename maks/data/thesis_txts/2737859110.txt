Abstract
Due to climate change, extreme heat events are becoming more frequent. To mitigate this
impact on the indoor environment, cooling of buildings must become a primary focus to
enhance resilience. This study adresses two matters related to the application of phase
change materials (PCMs) in buildings – the influence of the different active cooling sys­
tems on the performance of PCM enhanced ceiling panels, and the resiliency behaviour
of such panels in three future climate scenarios containing heatwaves.
An experimental analysis was conducted in a test room at the Technical University of Den­
mark using water circulation and displacement air ventilation cooling systems to assess
PCM performance and indoor thermal comfort. The analysis of operative temperatures
across four experimental scenarios showed that the upper limit of 26°C in Category II,
according to standard DS/EN 16798­1, could not be maintained when active cooling op­
erated only during non­occupancy hours (outside 08:00­18:00). However, the continuous
operation of the cooling system throughout the wwhole day kept the operative temperature
within Category II limits. The experimental results showed that the air ventilation system
provided better thermal comfort, achieving good conditions for 63.61% of the occupancy
time, compared to 51.42% with the water circulation system. On the other hand, the wa­
ter circulation system was more effective in discharging the PCM in the ceiling panels
compared to the air ventilation system.
The resilience of a validated PCM model with water circulation as the active cooling sys­
tem was also tested against extreme heat events in the IDA ICE 5.0 simulation software.
The study used 3 midterm future heatwave weather files representing the most severe,
most intense, and longest heatwaves to investigate the room’s performance under ex­
treme heat events due to climate change. Simulations were conducted for the warmest
week of each year, assessing the operative temperatures. The results indicated that the
PCM structure performed best under the most intense heatwave, achieving 35.83% of
good thermal comfort, followed by the longest heatwave at 30.72%, and the most severe
at 30%. A comparison of operative temperatures in heatwave scenarios with and without
PCM was also conducted, showing that PCM models had lower operative temperatures,
demonstrating the potential of PCMs to enhance building resilience.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
As the human population increases, the energy demands also rise, and the world strug­
gles with the escalating climate crisis, which leads to global temperatures and extreme
heat phenomena. One of the main weather extreme hazard are heatwaves, which are
the number one weather­related killer phenomenon, with more than 8,000 cases of heat­
related mortality in the first decade of the 20th century [1]. Because of the higher popula­
tion density and the urban heat island phenomena, the extremely high temperatures chal­
lenge cities more than the rural areas and may cause heatstrokes, heat exhaustion and
other heat­related diseases [2]. The frequency and intensity of extreme climate events
are expected to increase, and mortality during heatwaves is estimated to increase from
7.6% to 33.6%, posing significant risks to urban populations [3].
Today, with an increasing emphasis on sustainability and energy efficiency, buildings are
expected to offer comfort to residents while minimizing energy consumption [4]. As hu­
mans spend most of their lives indoors [5], it becomes crucial to ensure that the indoor
thermal conditions are safe and fall within acceptable limits, particularly to prevent ad­
verse health effects during extreme weather events. A historical example is the 2003
heatwave in Europe, during which 74% of mortality cases occurred indoors [6]. During
heatwaves, buildings often experience power outages, due to the strain on the electricity
grid in such conditions. The increased demand for electricity during heat stresses can re­
sult in blackouts and grid failures, affecting the functionality of mechanical cooling systems
and potentially leading to indoor spaces overheating.
In the face of preventing such hazards, the concept of resilience in buildings takes on
new meaning, as a key concept to preserve thermal comfort in buildings. Following the
IPCC’s (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change) definition, resilience is a system’s
capacity to anticipate, absorb, adapt, and recover from hazardous events effectively [7].
The IEA’s (International Energy Agency) Annex 80: ’Resilient Cooling of Buildings’ project
emphasizes the critical nature of developing, assessing, and communicating strategies for
resilient cooling and overheating protection in response to climate change and associated
hazards [8].
This thesis aims to contribute to the field of resilient building design, investigating the in­
tegration of Phase Change Materials (PCMs) in radiant ceiling panels. PCMs, known for
their high latent heat storage capacity, have the potential to maintain indoor thermal com­
fort during extreme events and contribute to a building’s thermal mass, thereby enhancing
its resilience. Furthermore, by mitigating temperature fluctuations and integrating renew­
able energy sources, PCMs stand out as a potential significant contributor in the transition
to nearly­zero emission buildings (nZEBs).
Key resilience criteria for resilience will be discussed, like absorptive, adaptive, restorative
capacities, and recovery speed, alongside thermal resiliency indicators such as passive
survivability, thermal autonomy, and recovery capacity. The findings will provide valuable
insights for the construction and energy sectors, especially in regions prone to tempera­
ture extremes and unreliable power infrastructure.

1.2
Objectives and Contributions
This study aims to assess the performance of PCM ceiling panels in maintaining indoor
thermal comfort and efficiency utilizing varying active cooling strategies, including simula­
tions mimicking heatwave conditions. In addition, the study examines how incorporating
PCM layers into the thermal mass of buildings can enhance their resilience.
The work is divided into two main parts: the experimental phase and the simulation phase.
The experimental segment took place in one of DTU’s (Technical University of Denmark)
experimental chambers as full scale tests, aiming to assess the PCM’s performance, as
well as the indoor thermal environment. This involved alternating water circulation and air
ventilation as active cooling mechanisms, along with adjusting parameters such as flow
rates and supply temperatures during both occupied and unoccupied hours.
The simulation phase was conducted using the software IDA ICE 5.0, with the model being
based on Hansen’s previous work [9]. This model accurately translated the experimental
setup into the software simulation and was used to validate the experiments and conduct
different calculations with heatwave weather files.

2
Indoor Environment in Buildings
It has been scientifically proven that a healthy and comfortable indoor environment greatly
influences an occupant’s performance of regular tasks [10]. Consequently, indoor envi­
ronment has gained increasingly more focus when designing buildings and rooms nowa­
days.
Four primary factors may be used to categorize the internal environment of buildings [11]:
• lighting
• acoustics
• indoor air quality (IAQ)
• thermal environment.
The thermal environment and indoor air quality will be the primary topics of this thesis,
although it should be highlighted that all indoor environment factors are crucial to consider
when constructing a structure. The comfort of an indoor environment is a multi factor
procedure which leans on all the four aforementioned factors.
The criteria for the thermal environment and indoor air quality in this thesis are based
on the indoor environment guidelines found in the European Standards DS/EN 16798­
1:2019 [12] and DS/EN 16798­2:2019 [13]. The precise specifications are described by
other standards as ISO 17772:2019 [14] and DS/EN ISO 7730:2006 [15]. Following the
published standard DS/EN 167981:2019, the indoor environment of a building is divided
into four indoor environmental quality (IEQ) categories, as depicted in Table 2.1.

Theoretical Background
3.1
Thermal Energy Storage
Energy storage is defined by storing a certain form of energy, which can be later used
when necessary. Some methods of storing energy include mechanical, chemical, elec­
trical, and thermal energy storage (TES). Thermal energy storage, specifically, entails
heating storage media over a charging period and releasing the stored heat when it is
needed [18]. This process not only enhances the energy efficiency of systems but also
helps alleviating differences between energy supply and demand [19]. TES technologies
allοw for the storage of surplus thermal energy during times that demand is reduced, and
then enabling its retrieval and utilization during peak demand periods or when renew­
able energy sources are not accessible, a process referred to as load­shifting. This helps
achieve energy conservation and sustainability goals, enhancing the efficiency of energy
systems. Applications for thermal energy storage include solar heating, waste heat uti­
lization, or buildings, where TES can be used to optimize Heating, Ventilation, and Air
Conditioning (HVAC) systems, by lowering energy consumption, temperature fluctuations
and peak loads.
Thermal energy storage includes three storage methods: sensible heat storage (SHS),
latent heat storage (LHS), and thermochemical storage (TCS), as seen in Figure 3.1. The
first two are based on physical processes, while the last one involves chemical processes
[20]. Each method presents distinct benefits and applicability depending on factors like
energy density, temperature needs, and duration of storage. Sensible heat storage em­
ploys materials with high specific heat capacities that absorb and release heat, while not
being subjected to a phase change or a chemical reaction due to temperature variations.
Latent heat storage, on the other hand, utilizes phase change materials to store and re­
lease thermal energy, which occurs during phase change of the materials to different
forms. Finally, thermochemical storage involves the storage media undergoing reversible
endothermic/exothermic chemical reactions for energy storage and release [21]. For sen­
sible heat storage, shown in part (a) of Figure 3.1, the graph depicts a linear relationship
between temperature and stored heat, represented by the blue shaded area, which means
that the heat stored depends on the specific heat capacity of the material and the temper­
ature change. In contrast, part (b) illustrates latent heat storage, where the graph depicts
the purple areas for sensible heat storage and the middle blue area for latent heat storage
during a phase change.

3.1.1
Sensible Heat Storage
Sensible heat storage is the most popular method for heat storage [23]. This process
involves the accumulation of thermal energy by increasing the temperature of a solid or
liquid without inducing a phase change, effectively storing the thermal energy within the
material [24], [25]. Sensible heat storage materials don’t change phase within the tem­
perature range needed for the storage application [18].
The two main benefits of sensible heat storage are the low cost and the lower risk associ­
ated with toxic materials, reducing potential health and safety concerns [22], [24]. While
this method is widely common, it presents some disadvantages. For instance, when sen­
sible heat storage is used in the building sector, large volumes of material are needed to
achieve sufficient cooling storage.
The most common material for sensible heat storage is water. Extensive research has an­
alyzed sensible liquid heat materials, which are utilized in solar thermal plant applications.

Latent heat storage is another method for heat storage, during which the materials un­
dergo phase change from one physical state to another at a desired temperature required

one state to another, with the most common being the solid­liquid transformation [26].
Solid­gas and liquid­gas transformations are typically not used for thermal energy storage
in building structures due to the significant volume changes associated with these phase

Thermochemical Heat Storage
Thermo­chemical storage makes use of the absorbed or released energy in the form of
heat during a reversible chemical reaction, through the formation or the breaking of chem­

originally absorbed and stored in the chemical bonds during the endothermic reaction.

3.2
PCM description
The latent heat storage mediums are known as phase change materials. These materials
are substances with a high enthalpy of fusion and are capable of absorbing, storing, and
releasing significant energy during the phase transition process, maintaining a constant
temperature when encountering a rise or fall in temperature [29]. The latent heat storage
based on PCMs can store a large amount of heat in smaller weights and volumes of

[30]. The heat storage capacity of PCM­based latent heat storage is influenced by [27]:

• the weight of the heat storage medium.

and present good chemical properties that allow them to undergo numerous phase change
cycles with negligible loss in performance [31].
3.2.1
PCM operating principle
PCMs play a pivotal role in thermal management due to their ability to store and re­
lease thermal energy during phase transitions, thereby stabilizing temperature fluctua­
tions within a defined range. PCMs have the possibility of changing their physical state
through four distinct phase transitions:
• Solid to Liquid
• Liquid to Gas
• Solid to Gas
• Solid to Solid
Among these, the solid to liquid transition is the most common one, because during
this transition PCMs can absorb significant amounts of latent heat without undergoing
a change in volume.

3.2.2
Classification of PCMs
PCMs are divided in categories based on their melting temperature and material com­
position. Regarding their melting temperature, they can be divided in two main groups:
materials that melt at low temperatures, below 200°C, and those that melt at high tem­
peratures, above 200°C [21]. Based on their chemical composition they can be classified
into organic, inorganic, and eutectic PCMs, as illustrated in Figure 3.3.
Figure 3.3: Phase Change Material classification [21].
Organic PCMs are divided into paraffin and non­paraffin compounds. The former may in­
clude paraffin wax, while the latter include fatty acids, esthers, alcohols and glycols [33].
Organic materials have the ability to freeze and melt repeatedly in cycles without phase
segregation, typically presenting non­corrosive behavior [21]. Paraffins are used exten­
sively in TES, because they offer a wide range of phase change temperatures, exhibit a
high heat of fusion, experience minimal supercooling, and maintain low vapor pressure
when melting, while having a stable performance [34]. While the use of both paraffin and
non­paraffin compound in low and moderate temperatures is limited, they are recognized
for their ability to absorb a significant amount of heat during their phase change from solid
to liquid, though they have low thermal conductivity (around 0.2 W/m·K) [24], [33], low vol­
umetric latent heat storage capacity and may become unstable at higher temperatures.
Studies conducted by Zalba et al. [35], Kuravi et al. [36], Jegadheeswaran et al. [37],
among others, have delved into these properties.
Inorganic PCMs are divided into two sub­categories: salt hydrates and metallics. Salt
hydrates are inorganic salts mixed with water that form a typical crystalline solid [21]. At
the melting point, the hydrate crystals separate into anhydrous salt and water, or a lower
hydrate and water. Inorganic phase change materials are preferred for their high latent

3.2.3
PCMs in Building Applications
Building construction activities account for a significant part of the total energy needs,
considering that the building sector in Europe accounting for over 40% of total primary
energy use [42]. It is thus imperative to explore sustainable cooling methods to enhance
their resilience and efficiency without compromising thermal comfort of the indoor envi­
ronment. Emphasizing the importance of both active and passive cooling methods, the
focus of this paper centers on passive cooling strategies.
One approach to achieve this goal involves leveraging thermal mass, a property that en­
ables buildings to absorb, store, and release heat energy over time. Thermal mass plays
a crucial role in stabilizing indoor temperatures, mitigating fluctuations, and reducing the
need for energy­intensive heating and cooling systems. Greater thermal mass allows for
more heat energy storage during off­peak times, which can then be partially reclaimed
during peak periods [43]. However, modern building design often faces challenges bal­
ancing the benefits of thermal mass with practical considerations such as aesthetics and
cost constraints, leading to a preference for lightweight construction. This is where the sig­
nificance of phase change materials lies, as they offer a sustainable solution to enhance
thermal mass without increasing structural weight [44]. By delaying and reducing tem­
perature peaks during fluctuations, PCMs act as effective thermal regulators and when
they are integrated into building materials, they enhance thermal comfort for humans and
promote energy savings [45]. Kalnæs et al. [33] outlined the key characteristics of phase
change materials for building applications, as shown in Table 3.3.

ing. This keeps the indoor temperature comfortable throughout the day and reduces the
energy demand on cooling systems. At night, as temperatures fall, the PCM releases

PCM building incorporation techniques
The incorporation of PCM into building materials can be achieved through various tech­
nologies, such as direct incorporation, impregnation, encapsulation, and stabilization [47],
[48].
• The direct incorporation method involves mixing PCMs directly with building mate­
rials like cement, mortar, and gypsum during manufacturing [49]. It is the simplest
and most cost­effective option. However, it presents certain drawbacks such as di­
minishing the bonding strength between aggregate and binder paste, impacting the
hydration process, and reducing mechanical properties and durability [47].
• In the impregnation technique, building construction materials or products are im­
mersed in liquid PCM, which is absorbed through capillary action [50]. Typically
suitable for lightweight buildings, this technique requires a covering layer to seal
the impregnated material and prevent leakage [51]. However, ambient temperature
increases can cause PCM leakage from the porous material, affecting system per­
formance. The impregnation technique is suitable for achieving high energy storage
densities and can be used for building materials where direct incorporation of PCMs
is not feasible, however it is a more costly method [52].
• Encapsulation involves coating PCMs with a shell to form capsules. It requires en­
capsulating PCM before incorporating it into construction elements. Encapsulations
are categorized based on size into macro, micro, and nanoencapsulation. Macro
encapsulation is the most popular PCM containment type and involves the intro­
duction of the PCM into a container with a size larger than 1 mm, such as tubes,
spheres, and panels, which are then used in construction elements. On the other

3.3
Resilient Cooling of Buildings
According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), resilience is ”the
ability of a system and its components parts to anticipate, absorb, accommodate, or re­
cover from the effects of a hazardous event in a timely and efficient manner, including
through ensuring the preservation, restoration, or improvement of its essential basic struc­
tures and functions” [53]. Resilient cooling of buildings, specifically, is a concept increas­
ingly gaining traction as the world faces more extreme weather events and a heightened
focus on sustainability. It refers to the cooling systems and strategies in buildings that en­
able structures to withstand and recover from extreme weather phenomena, while main­
taining indoor thermal comfort. Sometimes, strategies that are energy­efficient might not
be resilient to heat as well, and often there is a trade­off between the two goals. In their
paper, Zhang et al. proposed that like energy efficiency, sustainability and low­cost de­

Energy in Buildings and Communities Programme (EBC), a specific initiative within the
International Energy Agency (IEA) dedicated to improving energy efficiency in buildings,
has highlighted this through IEA EBC Annex 80 ­ Resilient Cooling of Buildings, formed

and low­carbon resilient cooling solutions against climate change, in order to prevent haz­

Zhang et al. [3] suggest summarizing the resilient characteristics of cooling strategies from
a technical viewpoint using four criteria: absorptive capacity, adaptive capacity, restorative
capacity and recovery speed.
• Absorptive capacity refers to a system’s ability to absorb the impacts of disruptive
events and minimize their consequences with minimal effort.
• Adaptive capacity is the system’s ability to adjust to undesirable situations by un­
dergoing changes.
This involves learning from events, evaluating performance,
modifying configurations, and becoming more flexible to future disruptions.
• Restorative capacity is the system’s ability to return to normal or improved opera­
tion.
• Recovery speed measures how quickly the recovery process occurs. This speed
can be enhanced if absorptive activities are well­implemented and the system can
rapidly mobilize and effectively utilize all available resources.

Literature Review
The literature review section of this paper explores various studies and experiments re­
lated to the use of phase change materials in building resilience and energy efficiency,
particularly in the context of extreme weather events such as heatwaves and power out­
ages. The discussion provides a comprehensive understanding of how modern building
practices can enhance thermal comfort and energy efficiency while ensuring resilience
against extreme weather conditions and system failures.
The current thesis builds on the foundational work of Bogatu et al. as presented in their
paper titled ”An Experimental Study of the Active Cooling Performance of a Novel Radiant
Ceiling Panel Containing Phase Change Material (PCM)” [54]. The study investigates the
performance of a new macro­encapsulated PCM panel (MEP) with embedded pipes as an
active ceiling cooling system, comparing it to existing radiant cooling technologies. The
PCM panel showed an average specific cooling power of 11.3 W/m², closely matching the
performance of radiant ceiling panels. The MEP provided effective thermal regulation,
maintaining the indoor environment within comfortable limits for 83% of the occupied time.
Additionally, the MEP demonstrated a significant capability to shift cooling loads to off­
peak hours, thus reducing peak energy demand. The study concludes that the MEP
panel presents a promising solution for enhancing building energy efficiency and comfort,
particularly in refurbishment projects where increasing thermal mass is beneficial.
In a previous study, Bogatu et al. presented an experimental comparison between radiant
ceiling panels (RCPs) and ceiling panels containing microencapsulated phase change
material to assess their ability to maintain a comfortable indoor thermal environment [55].
The experiments evaluated seven scenarios, 5 with active cooling where the water flow
rates and solar heat gains where varied, and 2 without. The results indicated that RCPs
outperformed PCM panels in terms of thermal comfort based on standard EN 15251:2007.
Both types of panels exhibited optimal performance within Category III of the standard.
Specifically, radiant cooling panels maintained an operative temperature within the optimal
range for Category III, 22°C to 27°C, for 91% of the time and for Category II (23°C to 26°C)
for 75% of the time, with a water flow rate of 140 kg/h. In contrast, PCM panels achieved
Category III of the standard for only 48% and Category II for 30% of the time, even at
a higher water flow rate of 220 kg/h. Despite their heat storage capability, PCM panels
presented low storage capacity, improper pipe positioning, and poor thermal conductivity.
The study highlighted the potential of PCM panels to enhance thermal mass but noted
the need for improved design and integration to fully leverage their benefits.

5.1
Methodology
The experiments conducted in DTU’s Chamber 6 had two primary goals: first, to analyze
the performance of the radiant ceiling panel containing PCM, and second, to evaluate the
indoor thermal environment.
The first goal was achieved by examining the cooling power of the panels, which involved
measuring the heat flux from the room to the panel using heat flux sensors, with results
provided in W/m².
The second goal was accomplished by assessing the indoor thermal environment ac­
cording to standard DS/EN 16798­1:2019 [12] and DS/EN 16798­2:2019 [13]. This as­
sessment was made by analyzing the operative temperature from the sensors placed on
the two separate stands, as previously mentioned. For simplicity, the average operative
temperature from these sensors was calculated.
The examination involving the operative temperature (Top), heat extracted by water cir­
culation (Qextract,w), heat extracted by the ventilation system (Qextract,a), and heat loads
(QTHG), relates these parameters to the active ceiling area (Apanels), which measures 17
m², as previously mentioned.
In order to verify the heat flux from the experiments, the calculation of heat flux, Qcalc,PT ,

conditions in real­life tests for assessing PCM resilience. The model was created using
the simulation software IDA ICE 5.0, which is a tool created by EQUA Simulation AB. It
used for dynamic, multi­zone simulations over desired periods of time, helping to analyze
the indoor thermal conditions and the energy consumption of a building [70]. The model
used in this work was created by Hansen [9], and several changes were made to adapt
to the current requirements. In this chapter, a description of the model will be presented,
followed by the analysis of the simulation results.

Discussion
The aim of this thesis was to establish an experimental setup and a simulation model to
evaluate the performance of Rubitherm RT24 as a PCM in macro­encapsulated ceiling
panels, validate the simulation with experimental data, and assess how the PCM en­
hances the resilience of buildings. The high thermal capacity of PCM created a self­
regulatory effect, stabilizing indoor temperatures and improving thermal comfort, along
with the active cooling mechanisms used during the experiments.
The experiments demonstrated that better indoor thermal comfort was generally achieved
with a ventilation system compared to a water circulation system. This was attributed to
the ventilation system’s direct cooling effect, as displacement ventilation supplied low­
temperature air directly into the room. Conversely, the water system used pipes embed­
ded in the ceiling panels, which did not directly lower the operative temperature. Instead,
it first discharged the PCM and then, in a second step, lowered the indoor temperature.
Thus, the discharging of PCM panels proved to be more effective with the water circulation
system, as the surface temperatures were lower during the water­cooling experiments, in­
dicating that the water system made better use of the PCM’s characteristics.
The experiments were limited by issues in Experiments 3 and 4, due to the inability to con­
trol the lower cooling setpoint in Chamber 6’s control software when having two different
setups during the day. If it was feasible to control the lower setpoint and Experiments 3
and 4 had worked properly, it would have been possible to determine how long each active
cooling system would operate, potentially finding the optimal balance between their active
hours. This could help identify the best operation to discharge the PCM while maintaining
the lowest possible energy consumption.
The simulation was verified with the water circulation system, chosen because the PCM
was more effectively discharged using the water circulation system. It would also be in­
teresting to validate the model with the air ventilation system to evaluate the performance
of the structure under different cooling mechanisms.
In the analysis, s expected, the HW files produced higher outdoor and operative temper­
atures compared to the Typical Meteorological Year (TMY) files. These HW files were
chosen because they were available from the Annex 80, but analyzing more heatwave
files could enhance the resiliency analysis.
The resiliency analysis showed that the PCM structure performed best under the most
intense heatwave scenario, indicating that the PCM structure maintained Category II good
thermal comfort for short periods of high temperatures rather than prolonged heat periods.
This suggests that while PCM can significantly improve thermal comfort during intense but
brief heatwaves, its effectiveness diminishes with extended heat exposure.

Conclusion
This thesis explored the integration and performance of Phase Change Materials (PCMs)
in radiant ceiling panels to enhance building resilience against extreme heat events. By
conducting experimental setups and simulation models, the study aimed to investigate
the performance of PCMs under various conditions and assess their impact on indoor
thermal comfort and energy efficiency.
The experimental results indicated that the displacement air ventilation system generally
provided better overall thermal comfort compared to the water circulation system. The
operative temperatures with the air setups remained within Category II limits of the DS/EN
17798­1 standard for 63.61% of the occupancy hours, compared to 51.42% for the water­
based cooling setups.
On the other hand, the water circulation system proved to be more effective in discharging
the PCM, optimizing its thermal storage capabilities. This was indicated by the heat ex­
traction graphs, showing much higher values for the water heat extraction compared to the
air. The water­based experiments managed to discharge almost all the PCM­containing
panels completely, which was proven by the surface temperature measurements indi­
cating temperatures equal or lower than the PCM’s solidification point of 21°C. During
the air­cooling experiments the PCM was not fully melted, because the panels’ surface
temperature never reached 21°C. In addition, it was observed that the lowest temper­
atures were reached earlier in the water experiments compared to the air experiments.
Despite some limitations in the experimental setups, the findings underscored the poten­
tial of PCMs to create a self­regulatory thermal effect, stabilizing indoor temperatures and
improving comfort.
Simulations validated against experimental data confirmed that PCMs are particularly ef­
fective during short, intense heatwaves but less so during prolonged heat periods. The
PCM structure performed best during the most intense heatwave of 2055, achieving
35.83% of good thermal comfort, followed by the longest heatwave of 2045 at 30.72%,
and the most severe heatwave of 2054 at 30%. The analysis of different heatwave scenar­
ios showed that PCM­enhanced structures maintained better thermal comfort and lower
operative temperatures compared to non­PCM models, highlighting the resilience bene­
fits of PCM integration. However, in both scenarios, the operative temperature exceeded
good thermal comfort limits (Category II) during the day, and in some cases of the non­
PCM models, Category IV was also exceeded during heatwave conditions, leading to
unacceptable indoor thermal conditions.
Overall, the study demonstrates that PCMs can enhance indoor thermal comfort and re­
silience in buildings during extreme heat events. However, further research is needed
to optimize control systems, analyze various cooling periods, and explore different types
and quanitities of PCM to fully realize the potential of PCMs as a sustainable cooling so­
lution. This research contributes valuable insights into the application of PCMs in building
design, promoting energy­efficient and resilient buildings in the face of climate change.
