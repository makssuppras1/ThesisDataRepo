Abstract
The rise of digital communities on social media platforms has facilitated the growth and
interconnectivity of hateful networks, allowing ideologically aligned groups to engage and
organize more efficiently. In response to the continuous efforts by social media companies
to curtail hateful activity, these groups have adapted by forming intricate, cross-platform
networks that allow them to preserve their communities.
This thesis seeks to address this challenge by developing Reikan, an automated scraping
framework to map and analyze hateful groups on multiple different social media platforms,
focusing on analyzing the links within and across these platforms. Additionally, it aims
to identify user overlaps between groups, providing a cross-sectional view of individual
participation across hateful spaces. By analyzing these networks’ structure and user base
intersections, this research will contribute to a deeper understanding of hateful group
interconnectivity and offer a foundation for potential intervention strategies.
The data found via the proposed framework helped shed light on what domains, such as
news sites etc. the different hate clusters link to. Furthermore, we also analyzed 100
cross-platform connections and their context for being created, finding that roughly 60%
of the connections were made to share information, while around 40% were created for
recruitment purposes. We also discovered 182 users with the same username taking an
active part in hateful clusters across multiple platforms.
The speed and ease of which the framework gathered all this data in a relatively small
time frame supports the conclusion that this work is useful in relation to future studies
within the realm of cross-platform analysis of hateful groups.

Introduction
1.1
The problem
Social media has transformed the way people communicate, with much of our daily inter-
actions now occurring on digital platforms. Most of the interactions happening on these
platforms are benign, however hateful groups have also adapted to this communication
form. As such, these platforms have become fertile ground for such groups to spread pro-
paganda, misinformation and hate speech. The groups also use the platforms to facilitate
the radicalization and recruitment of new members [1]. The platforms used in this manner
spans mainstream platforms such as Youtube and Reddit [2], as well as ”Alt-Tech” plat-
forms like Gab [3] and Truth Social [4], which have gained popularity among right leaning
individuals as a response to bans and perceived censorship on more traditional social
media platforms.
In addition to mainstream and alt-tech platforms, hateful groups leverage platforms such
as Telegram and Discord, where the ecosystem is much more closed-off. Here users are
usually required to obtain a group-specific invitation before gaining access. These private
groups can be especially concerning, as their private nature means there is virtually no
oversight or moderation [5, 6], leading to teenagers as young as 15 [7] being radicalized
and often introduced to a larger cross-platform network.
The cross-platform nature of these networks presents significant challenges when it comes
to monitoring and shutting down hateful activity. If a group is shutdown, the network can
often regroup and regenerate from other leftover active groups [8]. Furthermore, as a
network grows it becomes almost impossible to traverse manually. This has given rise to
a demand for more automated solutions for discovering new groups and classifying them
as hateful or not. The concept of a web crawler can be repurposed in this situation to help
provide a viable solution. Traditionally, web crawlers provide a systematic way of map-
ping the internet. Web crawlers initially visit certain websites, defined by a seed list, and
as they visit they will almost inevitably find hyperlinks to other websites. These websites
will then be added to a list of websites to crawl next, and the process will continue. This
process enables large-scale data collection, and is the basis of modern search engines,
which use web crawlers to rank and organize websites based on how interconnected they
are among other factors.
Repurposing this concept for network analysis on social media offers a strong foundation.
A framework based on web crawlers could systematically explore social media platforms,
starting from an initial set of known hateful groups. As long as the relation between the
group in which a link was found and the group the link points to is stored, the crawler
would slowly expand the dataset of groups, building a graph of connected communities.
Previous studies have explored methods to map relationships between cross-platform
communities, however gaps remain. Many studies do not address the classification of
these networks as hateful, or rely on manual classification. Furthermore, there is a lack
of detailed descriptions of data collection methods, as well as a notable lack of research
incorporating Discord into cross-platform analyses, despite the platform’s perceived role
in these ecosystems.

1.2
Contributions
In this thesis we aim to address the gaps in current research by developing an automated
framework to aid in the mapping of interconnected hateful networks across social media
platforms. Specifically, the contributions of this thesis are as follows:
• The design and implementation of Reikan (faroese for ”wandering”), an open-source,
automated, flexible and extensible framework to better facilitate data collection across
different social media platforms. Reikan allows the easy integration of new platforms
into the network mapping process, which in turn enables scalability. The design and
implementation is discussed in the design and implementation sections.
• An extension of existing network mapping efforts by including Discord communities,
as well as an expansion of the dataset by also incorporating user data, message
logs and other metadata. This provides a more solid foundation for analysis, in
turn providing a more comprehensive view of interconnectivity across social media
platforms.
• Finally, to demonstrate the usability of the data gathered via Reikan, we will answer
the following research questions:
– RQ1: To what degree are these scraped platforms interconnected?
– RQ2: Are there any users shared between platforms?
– RQ3: What types of domains are primarily being linked to?
– RQ4: What is the likely motivation for linking to another cluster?
As a result of these contributions, the thesis may further the understanding of intercon-
nected hateful communities online, by allowing studies on interconnected networks on
social media to be performed more easily and faster than before. This, in turn, allows
respective platform owners and legislators to better mitigate the formation of these com-
munities.
1.3
Thesis structure
The thesis is structured a follows:
• Section 2 discusses the work related to this thesis. This includes previous scraper
frameworks, previous studies on interconnected groups across social media, and
studies on how to scrape information from different social media platforms.
• Section 3 goes into details about the design of the proposed framework and each
of its components.
• Section 4 describes the implementation of Reikan in detail, and gives an in-depth
description of the code and logic used to implement the design.
• Section 5 describes how to use the Reikan.
It specifies how to design a basic
scraper, how to customize the classifier, how to configure the Reikan and lastly
how to run it all together.
• Section 6 discusses the data gathered by the Reikan.
• Finally, section 7 presents possible future work, and concludes the thesis.

As the project hinges on collecting large amounts of pseudonymized data, we have con-
sidered what data is necessary to store, as well as how to do so in compliance with current

The primary legal basis for the data collection and processing contained within this thesis
is legitimate interest as outlined in GDPR Article 6(1)(e)1. The purpose of this research is

1.4.1
Data collected
We collect data for the following entities:
• Clusters (Servers, groups).
• Channels (when applicable).
• Users.
• Comments.
For each entity we store the following data:
• Clusters:
– ID.
– Name.
– Platform.
• Channels:
– Channel ID.
– Name (when applicable).
• Users:
– ID (platform specific).
– Usernames.
– Platform.
• Messages:
– Message ID.
– Server ID.
– Channel ID.
– Platform.
– Textual content.
– Time made.
– Author (user) ID.
Data related to servers and channels is not classified as personal data, and as such is not
subject to GDPR. User IDs and usernames that we collect are classified as pseudony-
mous personal data. We only store data that is essential to achieving research goals.
Usernames and global names are used for weighting the resulting network graph based
on shared users. User IDs are used for ensuring that we do not log the same user multiple
times (if they should change username), or accidentally log different users as the same

1.4.2
Export control under EU legislation
The European Union has controls on items and technologies that fall under certain criteria.
For the most part, these criteria are, according to Erhvervsstyrelsen [9]:
• Is the product or technology on the EU control list of dual-use products?
• Can the product be used in the development or production of WMD and/or military
application, even if the product is not on the control list?
• Can the product be used in connection with cyber surveillance?
• Providing technical assistance, i.e. transfer of critical know-how or technology, may
also be subject to a license requirement.
These measures are primarily intended to stop technology that can be used for internal
suppression to fall into the wrong hands, such as hostile governments or even terror-
ists, even if the technology was not intended for this use-case. It can be argued that
a framework such as ours, which gathers information from information systems such as
user messages and the like can be used in connection with cyber surveillance, and there-
fore falls under these dual-use export control laws used by the EU. We have attempted
to reach out to certain individuals at DTU who we were told could guide us through this,
mostly in regards to what parts of the code we could publish and which we could not,
however we have not received any reply whatsoever so far.
This has left us to our own devices when it comes to the interpretation of EU export control
legislation, which is a tricky task for us, as we have no experience at all in the legal
department.
Based on our own (non-legal expert) interpretation of regulation (EU) 2021/821 of the
European Parliament and of the Council of 20 May 2021 [10], under category 5, section
5A001.j, export control applies to telecommunications systems, equipment, components
and accessories including software designed for network communications surveillance
systems. For the measure to apply, the system must have all of the following:
• Performing all of the following on a carrier class Internet Protocol (IP) network (e.g.,
national grade IP backbone):

– Analysis at the application layer (e.g., Layer 7 of Open Systems Interconnection
(OSI) model (ISO/IEC 7498-1));
– Extraction of selected metadata and application content (e.g., voice, video,
messages, attachments); and
– Indexing of extracted data; and

The above items are taken directly from the relevant section of the legislation.
It is arguable that the Reikan actually implement all of these points. Specifically, Reikan
operates on the application layer of the OSI model, extracts application content and in-
dexes it. It is unclear what exactly indexing means, but we take it to mean organizing and
saving it in a database. However, while Reikan is specially designed to map the relational
network of a group of people, it is NOT specially designed to execute searches on the ba-
sis of ”hard selectors”. We take hard selectors to be defined as in other EU legislation. An
example of such a definition is [11]: ”’Hard selectors’ (5) means data or set of data, related
to an individual (e.g., family name, given name, e-mail, street address, phone number or
group affiliations).”
While the system is not specially designed for the execution of searches based on ”hard
selectors”, it would be possible for a user of the system to seed Reikan with only a single
cluster, thus fetching data related to an individual, i.e. messages and group affiliation,
in this case membership of the seeded group. While it is not possible to target individual
users in a network this way with Reikan (such targeting would require prior knowledge that
the individual was a member of the group), this point might still apply to the framework,
even if it is not specially designed for these searches. That being said, while the system
stores relationships between clusters, as well as the active users of these clusters in order
to later calculate the degree of interconnectivity between groups, it is not intended to be
used as a tool which can be used to gather all user information from a specific community.
As such, it is still a little unclear if the EU export control regulation, in its current state,
applies to our system. Due to this, we do not give the code in full during the report, as
certain sections have been redacted, either for the sake of relevance or simply to save
space. This also has the benefit of not disclosing the entire system, which should mean
that we remain compliant with export control. Furthermore, we have chosen not to publish
the project code publicly for now until we get the go-ahead from DTU.
If indeed it is decided that the system falls under export control, then we have two main
ideas of how to fix this. The first is to further pseudonymize all user data, such that
user data cannot be tracked to specific user account belonging to natural persons on the
platform affected by the system. The other option is to simply not store user data at all,
instead calculating interconnectivity based on how many times one cluster links to another
for example.

Related Work
Social media has changed how individuals interact with each other, but also how they pro-
duce and consume content. This includes political content, and online echo chambers has
lead to an increase in polarization and radicalization online [12]. This has lead to research
regarding how such political content is spread in a network, the different kinds of language
used across these groups, as well as how different networks interact. In this section we
will go over work related to studies of multiple interconnected platforms, before moving
on to scraping frameworks in general. We will then discuss work focused on scraping
specific platforms, before finally discussing work related to automatically detecting hate
via artificial intelligence.
2.1
Mapping online communities
The following papers where found based on a key-word search on Google scholar and
DTU Findit for the following terms:
• extremist interconnectivity across social network platforms.
• extremist groups social media interconnection.
• extremist group networks multiple social media platforms.
• radical group communication (multiple OR different) social media.
• extremist groups social media.
• The above queries where ”extremist” were replaced with ”hate”.
This lead to two papers regarding connections from extremist groups to Rusian disinfor-
mation networks, as well as the spread of Covid-19 content across multiple platforms by
similar groups. While reading the references of these papers a third work related to the
adaptive dynamics of cross-platform networks was found, which was then included in our
corpus.
Leahy et al. [13] covers the inter-connectivity of so called hate clusters, which is an overall
term for communities, groups, servers and so on that actively participate in extremist
rhetoric and hate speech. This terminology avoids confusion when it comes to terminology
regarding specific platforms, and as such we have also adopted it for this thesis from this
point on. The study focuses on how, if at all, these groups are connected to Russian
funded media, and primarily focuses on groups on 4chan, Telegram, Gab, Facebook and
VKontakte. Data collection is handled by software which connects to the APIs of these
platforms, and scans comments for URLs via the Application Programming Interface (API)
of the platform. When a new cluster is found, the twenty most recent posts are analyzed
manually, and if it is found that the content of at least two of these posts is ”encouraging,
condoning, justifying, or supporting the commission of a violent act to achieve political,
ideological, religious, social, or economic goals”, then the cluster is included in the overall
network. To the best of our knowledge however, the source code for the data gathering
process is not available, and the study does not directly focus on the interconnectivity
between these clusters.
Johnson et al. [8] covers the self-healing properties of online extremist networks. It is
found that if a cluster is banned on one platform, then clusters reorganize or spawn new

sub-clusters to maintain the larger network’s integrity. The study mostly focuses on Face-
book and VKontakte. The study also places clusters as nodes in a graph, and uses the
ForceAtlas2 algorithm to alter the graph such that the further two nodes are, the less inter-
connected they are. To the best of our knowledge, this study also doesn’t include source
code for their data gathering, nor do they specify exactly how they classify a cluster as
hateful. Furthermore, the study focuses primarily on Facebook and VKontakte, and while
we do not consider the self-healing properties of the network, our work aims to expand on
the idea of interconnectivity presented, by measuring the shared-user overlap between
clusters across additional social networks.
Velásquez et al. [14] constructs a cross-platform network of clusters involving Telegram,
Gab, 4chan, Facebook, VKontakte and Instagram. The authors focus on how Covid-19
misinformation and related hateful content propagate across different social networks.
Like Leahy et al. the authors manually review the twenty latest posts or artifacts in the
cluster, and if at least two contain hate speech, the cluster is added to the network. This
study also utilizes ForceAtlas2 to visualize interconnectivity between different clusters,
however does not provide source code for the data gathering process. Once again, we
intend to expand this work by automating the classification process, as well as adding a
new social media platform to the data pool.
Studies that focus on communities spread across a single platform, such as X and Tele-
gram have also been created. These studies offer a more granular and closeup view of
online communities on specific platforms [15, 16]. Some studies focus entirely on so-
cial networks within a single group, such as Richard et al. [17], which focuses on social
networks within a Discord server, and how to find influential users within this community.

Web-scraping is itself language agnostic, with relevant tools and libraries written in sev-
eral languages [24]. Scraping consists of three primary tasks [22, 24]. These tasks are
fetching, extraction, and transformation. First, the scraper needs to fetch an online re-
source which includes relevant data. This exchange between the scraper and the remote
server happens via the HyperText Transfer Protocol (HTTP), similarly to how a browser
would fetch a website. The scraper will then need to extract the relevant data. How this
is done is dependent on the structure of the fetched resource. If the data is structured as
HyperText Markup Language (HTML) or Extensible Markup Language (XML), parsing li-
braries such as lxml1 and BeautifulSoup42 are quite accessible and easy to use, provided
one knows a little Python [25]. If the data is structured using JavaScript Object Notation
(JSON) or plain-text, then it is possible to simply use a JSON-parsing library, or regular
expressions [26]. The last step is to transform and standardize the data so that it is easy
to process for analysis.
When it comes to fetching online resources, there are two primary ways to do it. One
is to use a HTTP library to make HTTP requests to some endpoint. However, in some
cases, this endpoint will return a HTML document, which might rely on JavaScript code to
render certain resources. It is uncommon for HTTP libraries to support running JavaScript
during a request, and as such the page you get when using a library might be very different
compared to the same page displayed in a browser. Browser automation tools, such as
Selenium, can be used to get around this issue [22, 27]. These tools can be used to
script certain actions a user might take in a browser, such as scrolling, clicking on certain
elements and so on. Running a browser is not cheap however, and introduces a large
overhead. This overhead can be reduced by running the automated browser in headless
mode, meaning that the browser does not spend resources rendering the fetched HTML,
CSS and JavaScript contents. While this does reduce overhead, the overhead is still
larger than making a HTTP request for a single resource, and as such using HTTP libraries
is preferred when possible to reduce overhead.

The works cited in the following section were found by searching for the following key-

• hate detection.
• hate detection survey.

The anonymity social media platforms provides often reduces accountability for users
actions on said platforms. As a result, moderation has become essential in addressing
issues such as inappropriate content, general toxicity, and harmful behaviors like hate
speech. However, manually moderating hate speech is impractical due to the vast amount
of content being generated. To address this challenge, researchers have increasingly
focused on developing more effective methods for detecting hate speech.
Hate speech is notoriously difficult to detect, since communication is complex and contin-
uously evolving. Using simple keyword-based detection means people quickly learn how
to circumvent the detection system. This was seen in early communities, where the chats
had an inbuilt hate speech detection system that would censor specific words and ban
repeat offenders. People quickly adapted, by using slight different spellings of the words,
inputting or removing characters in the middle of the words (as seen in leet speech or
disemvowling) or by simply inventing new words.
By utilizing large, annotated datasets, machine learning algorithms can be trained to iden-
tify common hate speech patterns. These can then be applied to detect and remove such
harmful content from online platforms such as chat services or social media platforms.
Such algorithms can be further fine-tuned to recognize specific types of hate speech, or
tailored for a specific platform.
In a study [64] by Gandhi et al. the authors present a survey of the latest research and
state-of-the-art machine learning models for hate speech detection. According to their
survey, most of these models can achieve accuracy rates ranging from 75% to 85%.
Few models exceed 95% accuracy, though these are fine-tuned for specific contexts,
which limits their generalizability and introduces significant drawbacks when applied out-
side their intended use case. An example of such a fine-tuning is the models made in [65]
by Bilal et al., where the introduced model can only detect racism, and not other forms of
hate speech. The biggest limitation of these models is the quality and size of their train-
ing data. As emphasized by the authors, both the training and test datasets significantly
influence the performance of the models. The datasets can be biased towards certain
groups, thereby impacting how the models behave in practice. Moreover, many of the
14
Reikan: Mapping Hate Networks
==PAGE:23==
used data sets lack multilingual data, with a disproportionate representation of English-
language content. This makes most models, unless specifically trained on multilingual,
or non-English datasets, perform poorly when identifying hate speech in languages other
than English. Languages differ widely in terms of nuances, complexities and cultural con-
text. Features such as irony, sarcasm, dark humor and language specific linguistic quirks,
all play a crucial role in the classification of hate speech. And these datasets often fail to
capture the full range of these complexities.
This is where Large Language Machines (LLMs) comes into play. LLMs are trained on a
vast swath of data, enabling them to generate accurate responses across a wide range of
contexts, as the scope of their training data encompasses a vast array of diverse subject
matter, such as books, academic papers, news articles, blogs, social media and other
publicly available textual content. This constitutes a large amount of linguistic patterns, on
which they are then trained. The large majority of the data LLMs are trained on is English,
but even though only a small portion of the data is multilingual, the sheer amount of data
used makes these models able to handle many languages. LLMs were first introduced
to the general public, via OpenAI’s ChatGPT, which launched in late 2022. Due to the
LLMs impressive ability to handle a variety of tasks (although not without the occasional
inaccuracies, or hallucinations), researchers quickly began using the models to tackle
many different complex challenges, including the detection of hate speech.
Only looking at research from the last year, the general consensus among researchers
is that LLMs are, without any modification, generally good at detecting hate [66, 67, 68,
69, 70, 71, 72] But they are not perfect. As seen in other use cases for LLMs, they
can hallucinate and return what is contextually gibberish [73]. Furthermore LLMs, like
many other machine learning models (and humans) are generally bad at understanding
sarcasm and irony in text [74]. The consensus seems to be that smaller LLMs, with
8B to 13B parameters, are generally on par or slightly worse than other state-of-the-art
hate speech detection methods. It is believed that more complex models (such as 70B
or 135B parameters) are better at detecting hate, though the research on these larger
models is limited, likely because of the large amount of resources needed to run such
models [72, 69, 75]. These results are made in a zero-shot scenario, where no changes or
modifications are made to the models, before their utilization. Like other machine learning
models, it is possible to fine-tune LLMs. All papers, who engage in fine-tuning LLMs,
agree that fine-tuning makes the model considerably better at detecting hate speech,
so much so that even a fine-tuned TinyLLM (a very small LLM with parameters in the
high millions, instead of billions) can be just as good, and oftentimes better, than a larger
non-modified model. Though none of the papers tested how well the model could detect
multilingual hate after it was fine-tuned [76, 77, 78, 79].

Design
In this chapter, we will dive into the design of the different components which make up
the Reikan framework. The chapter first presents an overview of the system as a whole,
as well as an overview of the execution flow. The chapter then discusses each individual
core component, starting with the orchestration component. Finally, the chapter rounds
of with a brief discussion on key design decisions and trade-offs.
3.1
Design overview
The goal of Reikan is to automatically discover, map, and classify hateful groups across
multiple platforms. To achieve this goal, the framework must be extensible and flexible,
such that it is simple to add a new platform when needed. Furthermore, the framework
must orchestrate each scraping module in a robust manner, meaning that if a scraper
encounters an error, it does not affect other scrapers or the data currently in the process of
being collected. It also needs to ensure that the gathered data is shared across scrapers
when necessary, such as when a scraper finds a link to an external cluster on another
platform.
A diagram of the high-level architecture of Reikan can be seen in figure 3.1.
The architecture ensures error isolation via the runner, which acts as an orchestration
layer. As such, the runner is responsible for starting scrapers when needed while passing
on links or cluster identifiers to the right platform scraper. It is also responsible for ensuring
that scrapers run asynchronously, and that errors do not propagate between different
scraper modules. Data then flows back from the scrapers to the orchestrator, which then
ensures that content is parsed if necessary and placed in the database. The classification
module will then pull messages from the database and classify them, before applying a
classification to the cluster the messages was pulled from as a whole. We believe that
this architecture enables scraper modularity as well as efficient data flow.
The overall data flow of the framework, from seeding initial groups to preparing data for
visualization is as follows:
1. The initial groups are seeded manually. Clusters are manually chosen based on the
frequency of hateful content in the latest messages. If 10% or more of the messages
contain hateful content, the clusters are added to the initial seedlist.
2. In the scraping task, the runner fetches the IDs of the clusters that are scheduled
to be scraped. These IDs are then passed on to a scraper instance for the same
platform, which begins fetching content. If the cluster has not been classified yet, the
scraper fetches messages up to a predefined limit. Otherwise it fetches messages
that are newer than the last scrape, although no older than some configurable limit.
3. Each scraper returns all relevant information such as users, messages, channels,
when the scrape was completed and so on.
4. This information is written to the database, except for messages, which are added
to a parsing queue.
5. The scraping flow returns to item 2.
6. In another task, the content of each message is parsed to discover if it contains a
link to a different cluster. If a link is discovered, it is written to the database.

possible for a scraper to forego the HTTP engine if necessary, instead implementing their
own HTTP logic, or utilizing existing libraries if needed. As such, implementing scrapers
is flexible when it comes to implementing the logic that fetches data via HTTP.
The Runner also maintains a message queue, containing messages which have been
scraped. This is intended to isolate all but the most basic parsing tasks from the scrap-
ing modules. The idea is that, if a scraper finds a large volume of messages with large
amounts of text, the scraper might spend more time on parsing content than fetching it,
compared to simply having message parsing take place outside of the scraper modules’
context.
Finally, the Runner also orchestrates the classifier, which runs in the background at all
times. Whenever a new, unclassified, cluster is scraped the runner collects the messages
from the given cluster, and passes them to the classifier one at a time. When all messages
are classified, or the amount of messages classified as hateful passes a threshold, the

3.2.1.1
Concurrency and asynchronous design
Much of the work that Reikan enables is I/O-bound, meaning that significant time is spent
waiting for external systems to respond. These systems include HTTP APIs, databases
or disk I/O. One example is scrapers in the framework, using the HTTP engine, must
wait to receive, and send, HTTP requests. Additionally, the framework also encounters a
potential delay when it needs to write the fetched data to the database on disk. If these
operations were handled synchronously, the entire program flow would be blocked during
each operation, severely limiting scalability and performance. Instead, the framework
is designed to perform I/O-bound tasks asynchronously when possible. This allows the
framework to perform other tasks while waiting to receive the response of a HTTP request,
or while waiting for data to be written or read from the database.
This allows the Reikan to scale much easier, both in terms of the amount of different
scrapers added to the framework, but also when it comes to the amount of clusters in the
network, and therefore the amount of traffic that the framework must be able to handle,
to effectively stay up to date on recent activity in all different clusters in the network. This
is achieved by leveraging asynchronous programming concepts such as the event loop
combined with multi-threading to safely and efficiently decouple individual processes from
each other.
Reikan utilizes coroutines, this being functions that can be suspended and resumed at
specific points during execution, unlike regular functions which simply return a value and
exit. To handle the suspension and resuming of coroutines, the framework employs an
event loop. Coroutines get registered with the event loop, which continuously checks
if any there are any coroutines ready to run. When a ready coroutine is found, it gets
executed by the event loop up until a predefined point in the code by which the event loop
knows to suspend execution. At this point the event loop then resumes other coroutines
that are ready to continue. This ensures that the program executes asynchronously and
concurrently, however it does not mean that code is executed in parallel. This is caused be
the event loop being single-threaded, and so no coroutines are being executed in parallel.
This simplifies the implementation of the design, as there are no shared resources which
can be accessed at the same time. This means that there is no need for implementing
synchronization via for example mutexes or semaphores to guard critical regions.
Reikan also employs multi-threading in certain cases however. In CPU bound tasks, a
new thread is spawned to handle the workload, such as to not overload the event queue.
These tasks include parsing multiple messages for links, as well as performing hate de-
tection via the classification module. The design ensures that input is batched, and that
only one thread is running per task. This means the framework is never using more than
one thread to parse messages for example, which again means that developers do not
need to worry about shared resources.
These concurrency mechanism are used to handle the concurrent execution of both scraper
logic, as well as parsing and classification. This lowers the amount of runtime needed to
gather relatively large datasets. That being said, the actual orchestration logic built on top
of these concepts is rather straight-forward. The Runner has 4 loops which continuously
check for work to be done, either by the parser, scraper, invite checking or classification
module. For example, the Runner will check for each scraper module that it currently has
scraping jobs in its job queue. If they do then it waits for a certain amount of time and
tries again later. Otherwise, it pulls clusters due for a scrape from the database, and adds
these as jobs in the scraping queue for that scraper. Similar logic follows for the other
20
Reikan: Mapping Hate Networks
==PAGE:29==
modules. I.e. the Runner checks that the message queue has reached a certain size, or
that it has been a while since the last message was received before starting any parsing
work, while for classification it simply checks for unclassified clusters, spawning a thread
and classifying them if they exist before handing of control to the event loop once more.
3.2.2
Data models
To ensure each scraper only gathers the necessary information required to fulfill the
project goals, Reikan introduces five separate data models. These are used to model
the data hierarchy of the data received. Figure 3.4 illustrates these models and their
relationships.
Figure 3.4: Diagram of models used by Reikan
The cluster model encapsulates all collected data from a cluster, thereby simplifying the
later hand-off to the database by providing a structured and hierarchical way to store
information. Each Cluster object encapsulates the following:
1. Zero or more User objects, which represent individual users and their associated
and relevant information. This includes platform specific identifiers and usernames.
2. One or more Channel objects. These represent communication channels found
within the clusters.

3. Zero or more Message objects. These are stored within the related Channel ob-
jects where the message was originally posted. Each message includes essential
information such as the content, timestamp, and a reference to the author, which is
linked to a User object.
4. Zero or more Cluster Connection objects. These objects are critical for mapping
the inter-cluster relationships. Each object contains identifiers for both the origin
and destination clusters, meta-data about the platforms of these clusters and finally
the link upon which the connection is based. This link would usually be an invite link
or a direct reference, such as a forwarded message.
This structure of models is important for the construction of the network graph, as it helps
represent nodes via the Cluster object and edges via the Cluster Connection object.
This not only simplifies database operations, but also the later transformation of collected
data into a graph-based format.
3.2.3
BaseScraper
To ensure that each scraper follows a uniform design pattern, Reikan imposes an ab-
stract base scraper class, aptly named BaseScraper. A listing showing the definition of
the BaseScraper class can be found in the appendix as Listing A.2. The abstract class
defines three functions, one named Run, which is the method that the runner will use to
run the scraper. This function takes three arguments. The first argument is the cluster
ID, the second argument is a timestamp and the third is an integer. The timestamp is
necessary to determine up to which point the scraper should gather messages. The inte-
ger, if bigger than zero, tells the scraper how many messages should be gathered in total
from a specific cluster. This is used when a cluster is scraped for the first time, as it helps
restrict the volume of messages that needs to be classified in order to assess whether the
cluster is hateful. The function returns either a Cluster object or Null, if the scrape was
unsuccessful.
The second function it defines is named check_if_cluster. This function takes a cluster
identifier, and checks if the scraper has access to the cluster. If successful it returns the
cluster ID, otherwise it returns Null. The Runner uses this function to check the validity of
found links. For example, if the link points towards an open cluster, then the function will
check if the link is still valid. If it points towards a ”closed” cluster, then the function will
verify that the scraper is able to join the cluster. This function is necessary as the scrapers
sometimes encounters dead or expired links, and other times clusters are banned or oth-
erwise made inaccessible, thus creating the need for a function which makes it possible
to efficiently locate and prune dead nodes from the overall network.
The third function is named prepare. All this function does is perform extra setup outside
of the initialization function of the scraper object, if the scraper requires it.
Lastly, the BaseScraper class defines a constructor which ensures that all needed argu-
ments to the scraper, as defined in the configuration file, is passed to the scraper object
which implements the abstract class.
The combination of these functions provides a robust foundation for scraper modules,
enabling them to focus on their primary task of data collection, while adhering to a uniform
interface.
3.2.3.1
Web Scrapers
Each scraper is designed in accordance with the platform it is intended to scrape. As such,
while basic functionality follows the interface outlined by the BaseScraper abstract class
as defined above, the actual design of individual scrapers is hard to describe, as every
22
Reikan: Mapping Hate Networks
==PAGE:31==
scraper module built during the thesis has been different. That being said, in general they
follow the following logic:
• General cluster information is fetched, based on the provided cluster identifier.
• The scraper then proceeds to crawl the timeline of the cluster, until either the speci-
fied post limit is reached, or it finds a message that is older than the given timestamp.
• Each message is then parsed into a Message object, as defined in the Data Models
subsection. Messages often include the authors’ user information, which is parsed
and placed into a User object.
• A Cluster object is then created, which is given a dictionary of Channel objects,
each of which in turn contain an array of Message objects found in that channel.
What constitutes as a Channel is different from each social media platform. The
Discord scraper treats every text channel as its own Channel object for example,
whereas other scrapers might treat every post on a timeline as its own Channel,
due to each post effectively being its own ”thread”.
• User objects are also placed in the Cluster as a dictionary.
• The Cluster object is returned to the Runner.
The framework utilizes dictionaries while storing cluster information in-memory, to avoid
the duplication of data. By storing users in a dictionary, where the key is that users ID while
the value is the object containing the users information for example, we avoid accidentally
storing the same user twice. the same logic follows for the storage of channels.
3.2.4
Message Parsing
As previously mentioned the Runner contains a message queue, where scraped mes-
sages are queued up to be parsed for invite links. The reason for creating a parser, is to
ensure that scrapers spend as little time as possible on anything not specifically needed
for scraping purposes. As such a parsing task should run alongside the scraping task.
Once enough messages are queued, or a time limit is reached, the messages should be
dequeued and parsed.
As parsing text is a CPU heavy task, the parsing of the dequeued messages should hap-
pen in a thread different from the main Runner thread. In this new thread, the parser uses
regex to parse all messages given to it, by matching invite link patterns for each imple-
mented platform. If such a pattern is found, the connection between the cluster which the
message comes from, and the cluster that it links to, is saved. These connections can
then be returned to the Runner where they will be logged into the database together with
the parsed messages.
3.2.5
HTTP Engine
Currently the HTTP Engine centralizes logic such as retries and rate-limits, while there is
room for further features in the future. The engine consists of a queue, which scrapers
can place HTTP requests and arguments such as HTTP headers, cookies and so on into.
The engine will then make the request on behalf of the scraper and handle any errors as
required before handing of the response to the scraper.
The engine is defined on a platform basis, meaning that all scraper instances of a platform
will share one engine instance. I.e. all instances of the Discord scraper will share a single
instance of the HTTP engine, while Gab scrapers will share a different instance. This
makes it possible to define the rate-limit and max-retries taken by the engine on a platform
basis.

The variables rate_limit and max_retries are used by the engine to determine the initial
time waited when encountering a 429 HTTP status (status code for ”Too Many Requests”).
If it encounters such a status, it will start by waiting the defined amount of seconds, before
applying an exponential back-off. This means that it might start waiting for 2 seconds, but
if it encounters the same status code for the same request, it will then wait 4 seconds and
so on. The max_retries variable simply determines how many times it will attempt to
perform a request before returning Null. This only occurs when it runs into a 429 status
code, or unexpected status codes too many times in a row. If it instead receives a known
error code such as 404, it returns an exception instead.
3.2.6
Database
As mentioned previously, Reikan utilizes structured models to ensure consistency when
it comes to the unstructured data collected by the scrapers. The models represent the
data in-memory, representing the data in a hierarchical manner while scraping is ongoing.
The framework then maps the data stored using these models to a relational database
schema. This mapping ensures consistency between what data is gathered and what is
stored. Figure 3.5 shows the database schema.
Figure 3.5: Reikan’s database schema
The mapping from object to database performed by the framework is almost direct, how-
ever there are a few differences. Most of these are timestamps or other metadata. This
is used internally by the framework to keep track of which clusters to put into the scrap-
ing queue, as well as for debugging purposes. Most of the tables’ purpose mirror that

of their related object, however there are two tables which are not directly represented
by an object in the framework. These are the user_connection and links tables. The
user_connection table’s intended purpose is to keep track of connections between users
and clusters, i.e which cluster a specific user is a member of. By counting shared users
between two clusters, the framework can assign weights to edges in the final network
graph. These weight can then be utilized to reflect the degree of interconnection between
two clusters. The links table is used to store links separately in order to easily identify
and quantify the different domains found during scrapes. This makes it easy to identify
current trends in scraped spaces, as well as any new platforms that might gain popularity,
making it easy for scraper developers prioritize new platforms for integration.
To maintain data integrity, tables such as messages, cluster_connections and channels
etc. have composite primary keys, as well as foreign key constraints, which are some-
times bound to the partial primary key. This key structure ensures that the data being
stored exists, and that the data being gathered by the scrapers is correct. This prevents
dangling references in the database, as well as duplicate entries. It also makes it easy to
remove clusters from the dataset if needed via cascading deletions. By enforcing these
relations, all associated data such as channels, messages etc. is automatically deleted,
in turn preventing orphaned or dangling records. This design lays a strong foundation for
subsequent analysis, classification and visualization of hateful networks.
3.2.7
Classification
The classification module handles the classification of scraped clusters.
The module
fetches messages from the database from clusters that have yet to be classified and clas-
sifies these messages individually as either hateful or non-hateful. The precise definition
of hateful will be left to the implementation of the classifier. The number of messages first
fetched from a cluster can vary between platforms and clusters. Therefore, a percentage
based approach is used to classify clusters, where at least 10% of the initially collected
messages should be hateful, before the cluster as a whole is classified as hateful.
The classification module is separated from the scrapers, fetching the needed messages
from the database. The module sleeps for a configurable amount of time, before waking
up to check for any new clusters to classify. If such a cluster exists, then it will fetch
the initially scraped messages of the cluster. If there is only 100 or less initially scraped
messages, the module makes the classifier classify all messages. If there are more than
100 initially scraped messages, only max 20 messages from every channel, are classified.
If the number of hateful messages reaches the threshold of 10% of the total amount of
messages to be classified, the module stops classifying, and sets the cluster’s is_hateful
flag in the database to True. If all messages are classified without reaching the threshold,
it set the flag to False. The module then repeats this process for all clusters without
classification, before going back to sleep, for the set amount of time.
For the classifier itself, a BaseClassifier interface have been created to facilitate im-
plementation, though the interface is very simple. It can be found in listing A.3 in the
appendix. The interface consists of two functions. These are the constructor and the
classify_message function. The constructor takes no arguments, as there is only ever
need of a single classifier, meaning any needed arguments can be hard-coded in the clas-
sifier class. To make it as easy as possible to implement a new classifier, the classifiers
primary job is to classify clusters a single message at a time. To do this it also keeps
track of how many messages for a specific cluster has been marked as hateful, as well
as update the classification status for the cluster in the database.

3.3
Key design decisions and trade-offs
There are two major design decisions that impact the framework the most. First of all, we
chose to use asynchronous instead of parallel execution. Secondly, we chose to use a
relational database management system (DBMS) for the framework instead of a graph
based DBMS. When it comes to the choice between a relational DBMS and a graph
DBMS, it mostly comes down to a lack of experience with graph DBMS’s, and a lack
of experience with query languages such as Cypher, which is used in for example Neo4j,
one of the more widely adopted graph DBMS’s according to DB-engines [80].
While using a graph DBMS would likely make it easier to export and visualize the gathered
data, seeing as Neo4j have plug-ins such as APOC (Awesome Procedures On Cypher)
[81] which support the export of Neo4j data to a format understood by graph visualization
tools [82], it does not, to the best of our knowledge, offer commonly used features from
SQL based relational DBMS’s, such as cascading deletes [83]. This can of course be
side-stepped via user built queries and the like, however it adds to the learning curve of
graph DBMS’s, and it adds to the time needed to build a working product, as well as the
complexity of the final product.
Furthermore, Stefan Armbruster from the Neo4j team highlights some of the pitfalls of
onboarding Neo4j in a project, which includes [84]:
• Testing Neo4j for the first time on a mission-critical project.
• Working with team members who don’t have any graph database experience.
• Working entirely independently and learning everything yourself.
• Applying relational database logic and thinking.
The first three of these pitfalls would be unavoidable for our project, and realistically we
would probably fall into the 4th pitfall occasionally, as we are used to only think ”relation-
ally” when it comes to database logic. We reached the conclusion that most likely these
pitfalls apply to other graph DBMS’s also, and as such chose to heed the advice of the
Neo4j team, instead going with a well structured relational DBMS.
In relation to asynchronous versus parallel execution, it comes down to ease of implemen-
tation and complexity, as well as the needed speed of the scrapers, i.e. how fast does the
framework gather clusters and so on. As noted in the concurrency design section (section
3.2.1.1) we chose a mix of parallel (multi-threaded) execution and asynchronous (single
threaded) execution. We believe that this gives the project the best of both worlds. In our
current design, we benefit of the relatively low complexity of asynchronous programming,
in that we do not have to worry about mutexes, critical regions and shared memory to
the same extent as we do in parallel programming, thus lowering the final complexity of
the framework, making it easier to maintain and develop in the future. However we do
still get the benefits of lower cumulative execution times for I/O-bound processes such as
waiting for network responses and database operations. At the same time CPU-bound
operations, such as parsing messages for links and classifying content is handled on a
separate thread in batches.
We believe that this mix of both concepts creates the best value when you consider both
complexity and runtime, especially when you consider that users will most likely be running
the framework for weeks if not months at a time. Furthermore, when it comes to scraping
there is such a thing as too fast. While we do currently handle HTTP status codes such
as 429 - too many requests, we fear that increasing the amount of traffic sent to a single

platform, tied to a single account, while originating from a single IP address could result
in accounts used for scraping being banned. We are lucky in the sense that we are using

4.1
Tools and technologies
First and foremost, we chose to implement Reikan in the Python programming language.
We chose this language as it offers a relatively simple syntax, as well as being a language
we are both familiar with. The Python ecosystem is also a factor as it offers a large amount
of libraries, many of which are useful for the project. Python is not usually classified
as a high-performing language [85], however no matter what language is used for the
implementation, the slowest part of the framework would most likely always be that related
to the fetching of data via HTTP. Based on this we chose access to the high-level APIs that
Python offers over a negligible speed increase in certain areas of the framework, such as
message parsing.
As mentioned, the project utilizes multiple libraries made available via the Python ecosys-
tem. Key libraries include:
• llama-cpp - offers Python bindings for LLM models that have been translated into
the GGUF format. Most notibly the LLaMa models which was chosen for this project12.
• asyncpg - An asynchronous database interface library designed specifically for Post-
gresql and Python.
• asyncio - Part of the Python standard library. Is used to write concurrent code.
• asyncio.Queue - Used for the message queue in the runner module. Also used for
queueing HTTP requests.
• aiohttp - Asynchronous client/server framework. Built on top of asyncio
• logging - For logging error messages etc. Part of the Python standard library.
We chose Postgresql as the underlying DBMS for the framework. This decision was made
based on integrated ACID (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation and Durability) compliance.
We will briefly go through each of these terms and what they mean in relation to databases.
Atomicity in this context means that the entire query is treated as a single transaction,
which either succeeds or fails [86]. This means that the database remains unchanged in
the face of an unexpected error, such as power outages and the like. This helps ensure
data integrity, as partial changes to data can cause problems in the future.
Consistency implies that the database is brought from one valid state to another, always
preserving database constraints [86]. For examples, this ensures that database transac-
tions that violate foreign key constraints will fail.

4.2.4.2
Discord
Discord uses the term guild to refer to an isolated collection of channels, which can be
both text and voice based. Users must be members of the guild to have access to the
channels, although some channels can be restricted, such that a user only has access to
a subset of channels. Figure 4.2 shows an overview of the flow of the Discord scraper.
Figure 4.2: The flow of the Discord modules run function.
To summarize the figure, the Discord module has a run function, as defined in the abstract
class BaseScraper. This function takes the ID of a specific guild. The module then fetches
all guilds that it is a member of, and checks if the provided guild ID is in the list of accessible
guilds. If it is not, then the module returns None. It then goes through each text channel
in the specified guild. If it is the first scrape of the guild that the module performs, then it
simply scrapes 50 messages from each channel it has access to. Otherwise it scrapes all
messages up until the point of the latest scraped message. The Discord API calls used all
return JSON objects, and each message object contains a partial user object, describing
the user that sent the message. This object is parsed by the module and turned into a
User object, as previously defined in section 3. To make sure that the module fetches
Reikan: Mapping Hate Networks
41
==PAGE:50==
as many users as possible from the guild, not just those who are actively commenting,
the module uses a websocket connection to access the Discord websocket API. Using
an undocumented API call, the module then fetches a limited set of users from the guild.
These users are parsed into User objects, and a Cluster object is created and returned
to the runner.
Unlike other scraper modules, the Discord module does not implement the prepare and
check_if_cluster functions as defined in the BaseScraper. Due to the difficulty of pro-
gramatically joining Discord guilds, we need to manually join guilds instead. Because
of this, we can be sure that the guild exists before we scrape it. Furthermore it doesn’t
require any extra initialization.
Technical description:
The Discord module’s attributes, as specified in BaseScraper are as follows. First, the
module’s name, which is set to ”Discord”. Two required arguments are also defined,
these being ”token” and ”engine”. The token must be defined in the configuration file,
and is used for authenticating against the Discord API, while the engine requirement
means that the runner needs to inject the HTTPEngine into the Discord module. Lastly,
check_invite_required is set to false, as we manually verify and join each guild our-
selves.

The Telegram scraper is implemented with the use of the python library Telethon. This
library provides bindings for the Telegram API, and is very well documented6. Conse-
quently, this scraper does not utilize the framework’s HTTPEngine, as Telethon provides
its own engine for HTTP communication.
There are three different types of clusters on Telegram, that we scrape. These are Broad-
cast Channels without replies, Broadcast Channels with replies and Group Chats. For
simplicities sake, these will be called Channels with/without replies and Groups.

As all the other scrapers, Telegram has two different functionalities. There is the scraping

For a channel without replies, the _scrape_broadcast_no_replies function is invoked.
As there is only one stream of data in this type of cluster, there is no need to create multiple
Channel objects. Instead a single base channel is created where all posts are stored.
Then for each post the relevant data is logged into the different models, as required by
the framework. The scraper then returns the data to the Runner when the post scraping
limit is reached, or if it has scraped all new content.
For a channel with replies, the _scrape_broadcast_with_replies function is invoked. As
every post can be replied to, they are each seen as separate streams of data, meaning a
Channel object is created for each post. As the posts themselves also can contain data,
they are also logged as the first message in the corresponding Channel object. Then all
replies to the posts is scraped and logged under said Channel object. This continues until
the scrape date or number limit is reached.
For a group, the _scrape_group function is invoked. The structure of a group is different
from that of a channel, being more akin to that of a Discord server.Different topics can be
created, and users can then write within these topics. Therefore every topic is seen as
its own stream of data, and are as such created as separate Channel objects. Then for
every message scraped, a check is made to make sure which topic it is written to, before
it is logged under the corresponding Channel object. As with the other two functions, the
scraping continues until either the date limit or the message limit is reached. A figure
describing the flow of the scraper can be found in figure 4.5

message is written in a topic. It works this way because technically all messages in a
topic is a reply to the MessageService message that created the topic. Therefore the
reply_to attribute contains another attribute called forum_topic, which is true when the
message was sent in a topic and not simply a reply to someone else’s message. If a
message is simply sent in a topic, the reply_to.reply_to_msg_id attribute is returned.

reply_to.reply_to_top_id is returned instead.

the ID equal to that of a given Telethon message object.
4.2.4.4
Gab
Like the Discord scraper, the Gab scraper interacts directly with the Gab API, via the
HTTPEngine (see section 4.2.5). It has not been possible to login to the platform pro-
gramatically, therefore this scraper can only access information that can be found without
having a authenticated session. While this does limit the information that can be gathered,
it still exposes enough information to be worth including. The Gab API is undocumented,
and as such required a significant amount of reverse engineering to identify the available
and necessary endpoints.
Overview:
The Gab scraper module retrieves resources by invoking a _fetch_url function, which
queues a request with the HTTPEngine and awaits and returns the response. Responses
from the Gab API are delivered in a JSON format. If the requested resource is not available
the function returns None. This event is handled separately in each function that fetches
resources in this scraper.

The connection checking functionality is only a single and relatively simple function in this
scraper. It is handled by the check_if_cluster function, which queries the API to get
information about the group. If the API returns a valid response, the group exists, and
the ID and name are returned. If the API endpoint returns the HTTP status code 404 not
found, then the scraper returns None.
The scraping functionality begins by querying the API for information about the given
group, through the _get_group_info function. This works the same as the
check_if_cluster function, except this function uses the information from the API to con-
struct and return a Cluster object. Then the _scrape_cluster function is called, which is
the main scraping function that will call all other necessary scraping functions. This func-
tion queries the API for all posts posted to a group and for every post, it will register the
required information and invoke the next function _fetch_further_post_information,
which fetches further information about the post. It then invokes the _fetch_comments
function. The _fetch_comments function invokes the _parse_comments on the first com-
ments given by the callee. Then, if there are more comments to fetch, it will fetch these
also, and again, invoke _parse_comments on these comments. It does this until there
are no more comments, or until the limit is reached. The _parse_comments will construct

Message and User objects from each comment given to it. Furthermore it will also fetch
and parse comments made to the comments, as these secondary comments will not show
up on the previously made API queries. We will discuss this in more detail in the technical
description.
While the API query in _scrape_cluster returns information about the content and the
author of the posts on the cluster, it does not contain information about where it was for-
warded from (if applicable), or about any of its comments. This is where the next API
endpoint comes into play, as this endpoint provides information about where the post
was originally posted and information about the first 40 comments. If the post was for-
warded from somewhere, the connection is logged. Afterwards the _fetch_comments
function is invoked. Since the scraper already has information about the first 40 com-
ments, _fetch_comments immediately invokes _parse_comments on these first 40 com-
ments. If there are more than 40 comments, then after the first 40 are parsed, the func-
tion queries another endpoint to fetch more comments, and _parse_comments are then
invoked on these as well. It will keep fetching more comments until there are none left, or
the limit given to the scraper is reached.
After all the posts are scraped and the relevant information gathered, the run function
calls a final function before returning the Cluster object to the Runner. This is the func-
tion named _fetch_user_information, which simply ensures that the needed informa-
tion about all users scraped, are correctly collected. If there is a user on which some
information is missing, this function will fetch that information. After all scraped User ob-
jects are ensured to have the needed information, the run function returns all information

These variables are then formatted using the necessary values before being used to
query the API. These variables are created in another file within the same directory called

The VKontakte (VK) scraper also interacts directly with the API for the service it scrapes
via the framework’s HTTPEngine. The VK API is well documented7, though the English
translations are not always great and sometimes entirely lacking.
Overview:
This scraping module has a dedicated function to query the API endpoints. It takes some
Figure 4.7: The flow of the VKontakte module’s run function.
data and the name of the desired endpoint, and queues a request with the HTTPEngine.
Responses from the VK API comes in the form of JSON. If the API receives something
unexpected or something else goes wrong, it will always return a JSON containing an
”error” object. This makes it easy to detect when something goes wrong.
The connection checking functionality queries the VK API for information about the given
group and returns the needed information back to the runner. If the received JSON con-
tains an error or if the cluster is otherwise inaccessible, the functions returns a correspond-
ing exception. If the cluster exists and is accessible, the function returns the necessary
data back to the Runner.

Similarly to other scraping modules, the scraping functionality begins by querying the API
for information about the cluster to ensure that it is still accessible. Once the cluster is
confirmed accessible, the scraping can begin. If it is the first time the cluster is scraped, a
counter is initialized. This counter will be passed to and returned by subsequent functions,
to ensure the correct amount of messages are scraped from the cluster. Every time a
post or comment is handled, the counter is incremented. Then when the limit is reached,
the scraper returns the scraped data to the Runner. To begin the scraping, the scraper
queries the API for posts uploaded to the cluster. The posts are fetched in bulks of 100,
which is the maximum amount of posts that can be fetched at a time. For every post,
the date of posting is checked by the scraper, which then logs relevant information about
the post before fetching comments as well. If the post is older than the limit specified by
the Runner the scraper returns the data scraped so far back to the Runner. If the limit
is set the specified amount of messages is collected, irrespective of the date limit given.
For every comment, the required information about the comment and the author of the
comment is logged. The API only returns limited information about the authors of posts
and comments while specifically querying for posts and comments. Therefore once all
the wanted posts and comments have been collected, a separate API call is queried to
get the needed information about the authors. Once either the date limit of message limit
are reached, the scraper returns the collected data back to the Runner.
Technical description:
As with comments on Gab, comments on VK can be split into direct comments, being
comments made directly to a post and secondary comments, which are comments made
to other comments. The API works by sending HTTP POST requests to a specific end-
point, and supplying the needed data through the HTTP request body, such as the access
token and the API version queried, these two arguments are always needed, and as such
will not be mentioned below. A list of the API endpoints and the needed data can be found
below.
• groups.getById: Used to fetch information about group.
Data needed in request body:
– group_id: The ID of the cluster.
– group_ids: Multiple cluster IDs.
Either of the two parameters can be used, but not both.
• wall.get: Used to fetch posts from a specific cluster.
Data needed in request body:
– owner_id: The ID of the cluster.
– count: The number of posts to fetch.
– offset: The number of posts to ignore before fetching count posts.
• wall.getComments: Used to fetch information about comments.
Data needed in request body:
– owner_id: The ID of the cluster.
– post_id: The ID of the post.
– count: The number of comments to fetch.
– extended: Whether or not to retrieve extended information.

The queue_request function is used by scraper modules to place HTTP requests into the
queue of the engine. The engine then checks the queue in _process_queue while it has
not received a stop signal, or the queue has at least one item. The stop signal would be
sent to the queue if the framework detects that a user is attempting to stop execution by
entering CTRL+C in the console running the process. The engine gives control back to the
asyncio event loop after each request by calling asyncio.sleep(). The sleep duration is
defined in the configuration file and used as a parameter during initialization of the class.
Essentially, the scraper awaits a Future object, representing the eventual HTTP response
when making a request via the HTTPEngine. By doing this the scraper suspends its own
execution and transfers control back to the event loop. This means that other coroutines
can continue working, either processing or making requests of their own. If the engine
encounters an error, then it binds that exception to the Future. The exception is then
propagated to the context of the scraper queuing the original request, which then handles
it gracefully. The abstraction provided by this approach enables each scraper to become
modular, in that it doesn’t need to handle network requests or more sophisticated parsing
of content, instead it simply needs to focus on the correct structuring of data. Furthermore,
by employing Futures and coroutines, multiple scrapers can queue requests in parallel
while the engine’s worker coroutine handles everything under the hood. This helps ensure
that the framework remains responsive and can scale to many scrapers simultaneously,
supporting the architecture goal of asynchronous execution.
This approach not only streamlines scraper development but also adheres to the design
principles of robustness and flexibility, which underpin the overall goal of a scalable and
reliable group discovery framework. To illustrate how a scraper might utilize this engine,
we refer to the scraper implementation sections above. Alternatively, listing A.7 in the
appendix demonstrates how the Discord scraper gathers data using the HTTPEngine.

Listing 4.41: Function for processing the message queue
The function enters an infinite loop which uses the asynchronous function asyncio.wait_for
to wait for messages. Each ”message” consists of a 4-tuple, which includes relevant in-
formation pertaining to the scraped message. This includes the actual message, but also
metadata such as the ID of the cluster it was found in, along with channel and platform in-
formation. The framework utilizes batching by waiting for at least ten thousand messages
before processing. Alternatively, if the queue has waited for six hundred seconds without
receiving a new message, it starts processing the current messages it has received, even
if it has received less than the required batching amount of ten thousand. This batching
leads to less overhead when it comes to allocating resources for parsing the messages,
as this is done on a separate thread. More specifically, we reduce the amount of times
the framework creates and destroys threads, which leads to a more efficient use of re-
sources. The function also utilizes the asyncio.sleep() function to yield control back
to the event loop. This ensures that the queue-handling task does not monopolize the
runtime, allowing other coroutines to run concurrently.
4.2.6.2
Message Parsing
Once a batch of messages is ready for parsing, the framework invokes the parse_messages
function. This function takes the entire batch of 4-tuples, and then creates a thread which
is used to match the content of each message with a pre-defined regular expression.
Listing A.8, in the appendix, contains the relevant code for this functionality.
By calling the asyncio.to_thread function, the CPU-bound parsing occurs in a separate
thread, preventing the event loop from being blocked. This means that the event-loop,
which mostly handles I/O-bound tasks is isolated from CPU-bound work, which aligns with
the asynchronous design outlined in section 3. The parser then inspects each message’s
content via the find_links_in_message function. Based on platform specific patterns,
specified via regular expressions, if a link is determined to ”point” to another platform,
then a
ClusterConnection object is created as a means to store these cross-platform refer-
ences. The framework also records any standard URLs found in a simple list. This is
used for manually checking if any new platforms start getting traction in the network, or if
the regex used catches all expected tokens.
Discord links are handled separately from other found links, in a function aptly named
handle_discord_links. Discord does not expose any API methods for programatically
joining new clusters, and the platform’s robust anti-botting measures means that it is non
trivial to join clusters automatically, in contrast with other platforms included in this thesis.
Instead, the framework utilizes a Discord webhook to create a notification when a new
Discord guild has been discovered, at which point we join it manually.

Deciding how to classify the identified clusters presented a challenging task, as several
requirements needed to be met by the Classifier. Given the automated nature of the
framework, manual classification was not feasible, necessitating the use of an automated
process. Based on the current literature, machine learning models is the most effective
state-of-the-art approach for such a task. Given the short time-frame from which as much
data as possible needed to be fetched, we did not want to limit the data collected be-
cause of language barriers. As such a model that is capable of understanding a multitude
of languages was preferred. However, the field of machine learning is predominantly
dominated by English-language models, with only a very limited number of models being
capable of handling multiple languages. Even multilingual models often fall short as most
are only capable of handling 2 languages [64]. Moreover, the data collected may contain
sensitive information, making it essential that the data be processed locally, without being
transmitted to a third party. As the project timeline didn’t allow for the training of our own
model, it became necessary to use a pre-trained model available for download, that can
be used in a out-of-the-box manner. Several models were considered, but only a few met
all the criteria. The models that fulfilled these requirements include:
1. Detoxify8.
2. Various large LLMs.
Detoxify, while effective at detecting toxicity in multiple languages, does not specifically
identity hate speech, which unnecessarily complicates the Classifier. And while it is
capable of detecting toxicity in multiple languages, some languages that the framework
can easily stumble upon are not represented, such as German.
As shown in the related work section, LLMs have been shown to be capable at detect-
ing hateful content. Since our local server were able to run larger LLM variants, there
was no need to risk using a smaller fine-tuned LLM which might have lost its ability to
detect hate in many languages during fine-tuning. The largest LLM models the server
8https://github.com/unitaryai/detoxify

could run, while being able to classify quickly enough, were the 70B parameters vari-
ants. Since earlier LlaMa models have demonstrated strong capabilities in detecting hate
speech in a zero-shot environment, meaning they can identify hate speech without requir-
ing further training or fine-tuning [66, 69], and they are open source, the newer LlaMa-3
model was chosen. Although LlaMa models are primarily advertised as supporting seven
languages9, research has shown that they are also capable of understanding text in ad-
ditional languages, such as Russian10 [87, 88].
Taking all these factors into consideration, the LlaMa-3.3-70B LLM was selected for the
task of classification. One of the additional advantages of this model is its availability in
the GGUF format, which facilitates easy setup and use via the llama-cpp Python library.
The implementation itself is straightforward. The Classifier is a class with two functions,
the constructor __init__ and the classifying function classify_message, which returns
True or False, indicating whether a message is hateful. It uses llama_cpp to instantiate
an LLM instance, using the parameters given. These parameters are as follows:
• system: Tells the LLM model how it should behave (only given when prompting the
created model).
• n_ctx: Tells the model how many tokens it is allowed to process (both input and
output tokens).
• n_gpu_layers: Tells the model how many processing layers should be offloaded to
the GPU.
• use_mmap: Tells the model whether or not it is allowed to use RAM to speed up
processing.
The constructor starts by checking if a local model of the model has already been down-
loaded. If so, it loads the models from the cached model otherwise it download and caches
the model for future use. This is done to limit the need for downloading an already cached
model. The previously mentioned system parameter is defined as follows:
”You are a model designed to identify whether some given text is hateful, you can only
answer YES or NO”.
The only other function classify_message takes a given message as input. As, for the
sake of speed, the classifier is limited to only 8192 tokens, the input message also has
to be limited. While very few messages on social media is longer than 8000 words, they
do exist, and if the classifier tries to process these, it will crash. Therefore we limit the
amount of words given to the classifier to 8000. After this is done the classifier creates the
user prompt, that together with the system prompt will define what the model outputs. The
classifier extracts the output from the model and returns True if the message is classified
as hateful and False is not. However, as is common with LLMs, the model’s responses
are not flawless, and it may occasionally output something other than ”YES” and ”NO”,
particularly when the messages being classified is nonsensical or only consists of a link
(which the model cannot access due to its lack of internet connectivity). In these cases,
the classifier simply returns False.

prompt itself. However, this approach led the model to refuse to respond when encoun-
tering certain words deemed too hateful to comment on. While this was a small anecdotal

4.2.8
Database Manager
The Database Manager contains all logic required for interacting safely with the under-
lying PostgreSQL database. We chosePostgreSQL as the underlying database for mul-
tiple reasons. Firstly, it was the option that provided the least complicated setup. For
example, PostgreSQL is fully ACID compliant in all configurations, and also supports con-
current read and write operations out of the box. This is in contrast of DBMS’s such as
MySQL, which requires specific storage engines to provide the same features[89]. Further-
more, PostgreSQL seems to have higher write performance compared to DBMS’s such
as MySQL[89], which in turn has higher read performance. As the project mostly performs
write operations, the write performance of PostgreSQL plays an important role in why we
chose it as well.
One might wonder why the Reikan framework doesn’t store gathered data directly in a
graph DBMS like Neo4j instead of PostgreSQL. There are a couple of reasons for this.
First of, We are not familiar with Cypher and prefer to work with SQL based DBMS. Fur-
thermore, we would like the framework to be used by as many people as possible, and
as SQL is a more established query language, making Cypher a core part of the project
adds another barrier of entry. Secondly, using an SQL based DBMS also enables others
to use the framework with the SQL based DBMS of their choice, simply by changing the
database driver in the DatabaseManager, whereas using a Neo4j locks all queries behind
the Cypher language. After loading all clusters and connections into Gephi, the program
will produce graphs such as those seen in section 6.
The Database Manager utilizes the Python package asyncpg to asynchronously interact
with the database, underscoring the requirement of asynchronous execution in the frame-
work. When the framework is started, the Database Manager establishes a connection
pool for the database. Connection pools allows the Database Manager to create many
connections at once, and then lend them out when needed, which reduces the overhead
required for each database commit compared to creating a connection for each commit.

4.3
Technical considerations
We believe that through the choice made in the design and the implementation of the
framework, we have created a solution that is modular, extensible, robust and scalable.
The question then is how scalable is it? The framework as is uses multiple home-brewed
asynchronous task queues, which simply query the database every once in a while to
check if work is available, exemplified by the scraper task and classification task executed
by the runner. Alternatively, a Asyncio.Queue is checked in the message parsing task.
These tasks all have in common that they are rather simple, and that they work, however
we as developers don’t necessarily have a lot of say in when specific tasks are executed,
instead leaving this up to the event loop of asyncio. So far this has been fine, however
it is possible that as the network grows, or as more scraping modules are added, it might
be beneficial for the efficiency of the framework for the developers to have greater control
of when certain tasks are done.

==PAGE:100==
While developing the framework we came across task-queue implementations for Python,
which were made for use cases such as scheduling periodic batch processes and spread-
ing out database inserts[91]. These task-queues offer a greater control of when work is
done and by whom. However, this was discovered quite late in the implementation pro-
cess, and we deemed that a rewrite would be too risky compared to a simpler solution
that was working well enough already. It also adds complexity to the final product, and
another moving part that might break, so we ultimately decided on the simpler approach.
A similar question of performance arises concerning asynchronous versus parallel pro-
gramming. Currently, we use a mix of these concepts where appropriate, but all scraping
modules currently share the event loop. While this works for now, the question is what
happens if there are 20 scraping modules all sharing the loop, and what impact that might
have on performance when you take the current design into account. A potential fix for
this issue would be to batch scraping jobs similarly to how we currently do it for the mes-
saging task. We could for example batch scraping jobs in batches of 15 or so, and split
this batch into threads of 5 scrapers each. That said, we do not currently know if this would
be beneficial to the scraping performance, as the current solutions works well enough for
the current size of the network we’ve gathered, and so we haven’t identified any bottle-
necks so far. To support this claim that the current implementation works well enough
for the current size of the network gathered, we’ve performed some benchmarks of the
framework. These benchmarks were performed on a machine with the following specs:
• Graphical Processing Unit (GPU):
– NVIDIA RTX 3090, 24GB VRAM
• Central Processing Unit (CPU):

– 32GB DDR4 3200MHz
During testing the biggest contribution to wait time was the rate limiting of the HTTPEngine.
This can be optimized based on each platform, but was set to send 4 HTTP requests per
second for all platforms. Using the Python library time, we measured the total time the
framework ran, the time the CPU spent on the process and the time the CPU spent on the
orchestration thread. To do this we used the functions time.monotonic, time.process_time
and time.thread_time. Running the framework through the weekend, we ended up with
the following benchmark:
The program ran for a total of 259.200 seconds.
During this time, the CPU spent a total of 2.080 seconds on the whole process.
The CPU spent a total of 1.803 seconds on the orchestration thread (not time spent on
other threads, such as created parsing threads). From this, we can see that only 0.8% of
the time are spent actually processing data and orchestration, while the rest of the time

The script initializes the Neo4j database schema while ensuring that each cluster node
has a unique id property. Neo4j queries are written in Cypher, and executed by the script
via a Neo4j Python driver. The script then proceeds to load all clusters saved in the Post-
greSQL database into a pandas data frame, before adding the clusters to the graph as

set to shared_users + 1. We add 1 to the weight to avoid zero-weight edges, as this can
cause an edge to be ignored by Gephi. The graph is then exported to the GraphML format
via calling APOC trough a Cypher query.

6.3.1
RQ1: The Complete Network
We created a network graph of the complete network by exporting the gathered data to
Gephi. Following this the ForceAtlas2 algorithm was applied on the data. This algorithm
places nodes in the graph with respect to the weights of their edges, i.e. nodes with high
weight edges will be placed closer together than nodes with low-weight edges. Every
cluster scraped is a node, and a connection from one cluster to another cluster is an edge
between those two nodes. Because of the nature of connections between clusters, these
edges are directed. The edges are weighted with the amount of shared users between the
clusters the edge connects. Currently, shared users are determined to be present if users
with identical usernames are present in both clusters. The graph has these properties as
we wanted to distinctly show closely related groupings of clusters in the final graph.

Finding shared users between platforms is interesting, as it can be used to find how people
generally move around cross platform, and how they move around in hateful circles. That
said it is no easy task, since users can easily change usernames or use variations of
their username on different platforms. These variations can be anything from changing
or adding a single character, to changing the entire username, therefore it would take a

As this is out of scope for this thesis, we have chosen to make the simple assumption

will be called username.
It is important to remember that only the Discord scraper has the ability to fetch most
users from a cluster. The other scrapers do not have access to such an API endpoint, and
therefore only users that have actively participated in the clusters by posting messages
have been scraped.
Based on the gathered usernames, we have the following data:
• We have scraped a total of 638.432 users across the platforms.
• 182 of these users have usernames found on multiple platforms.
• 213 different clusters have at least 1 shared user with a cluster on another platform.
For all but one of these 182 users with usernames found on multiple platforms, it applies
that they were only found on 2 different platforms. For most of these users, it also holds
that they are only part of one cluster on one of the two platforms, and all other clusters
that they are a part of on, where found on the second platform. For example a user is

these 38 users are part of multiple clusters across two platforms. From this we can see
that users are generally not active on multiple of these platforms, and when they are, it
is in a limited setting, where they have a primary platform that they use. Another equally
likely conclusion is that users have a tendency to change their username when moving
from one platform to another.

The information/knowledge sharing category is very broad, as almost any discussion will

disinformation. Recruiting is when the connection is trying to get users from one group to

those where the context around the connections is about getting users to join another
group. For example, if a message contains a news report about something that has hap-
pened, and it has a ”Follow us at X to learn more” or ”Subscribe for updates”, we do not
consider it recruiting. Lastly is raiding, which is when one group links to another in order
to get users to harass or disrupt this other group. Analyzing these 113 connections we
find that roughly 80% of these connections is about sharing information, and 20% is about
getting users to join another cluster. We did not find any connections made for the pur-

different platforms, this ratio is a bit different, with roughly 60% of the connections being
information sharing and almost 40% being for recruitment purposes.

clusters share users, as well as the motivation for each connection being made.

7
Conclusion
This thesis set out to address the gaps in current research regarding cross-platform stud-
ies of interconnected groups. In the preceding chapters we discussed the design and
implementation, as well as the usage, of an extensible, automated and flexible frame-
work for data collection. The framework, Reikan, is introduced with the aim that it will
make research in this area faster and easier to perform in the future. Furthermore, we
demonstrated the capabilities of the framework by presenting the resulting graph built on
data collected by it over a period of 1½ months.
A major contribution of this thesis is the publication of the methodology required to effec-
tively gather clusters from a network spanning multiple platforms, as well as the automa-
tion of the classification of these clusters. Furthermore, we added Discord to the list of
platforms scraped, although platform limitations mean that it is hard to draw any concrete
conclusion based on the data scraped, as it is rather sparse. By applying the framework,
we were able to answer basic research questions, such as what domains clusters link
to, if there exist any shared users between clusters, as well as the implied reasons for
sending links to other clusters.
Despite these achievements, there are still some limitations which open up for future
research. For example, the reliance on identical usernames is rather naive when it comes
to cross-platform networks. Secondly, the usage of AI for classification opens the doors for
a more granular classification compared to the YES / NO system we use in the framework
currently.
7.1
Future Work
While we have touched upon some of the questions that the data collected from this frame-
work can help answer, there are more which could be interesting to look at. By running
the framework for a longer period of time, questions about how the networks evolve could
be answered, and how specific real life events affect the online hate network. Previous
research have been done on ”gateway” clusters. For example in Hickey et al. [92], which
finds that users having joined a single hateful Reddit cluster (subreddit) are more likely
to join another. Furthermore, it claims that the rate of which hate clusters grow increases
with the size of other related hate clusters. Applying this research in a cross-platform set-
ting could be interesting, simply to see if the same applies when users have to jump from
one platform to another. Having a better method of detecting users between platforms
other than simply their usernames, could hopefully also lead to research about how users
are radicalized by being linked from a moderated platform to another, less moderated
platform, for example by how many VK clusters link to a corresponding Telegram group.
While these research questions could be interesting to answer, there are also extensions
to the framework that could be interesting to add. The obvious is to add more platforms,
as the hate network is likely much larger than the currently scraped platforms. Another
interesting addition would be to add a language classification, to see if hate in different
languages evolve in the same way. This would also make it easier to see if hate networks
from different linguistic backgrounds interact, and if so in what way? Lastly, it could be
interesting to add an anonymisation module to the framework, making the data collected
more easily publishable, ensuring that other researchers have access to more scraped
data.
