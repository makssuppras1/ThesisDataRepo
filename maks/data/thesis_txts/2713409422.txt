Abstract
This project investigates the hydrogen evolution properties, as a result of corrosion, of
magnesium alloys with zirconium and neodymium for potential use as orthopedic implant
materials.
The project has been performed in collaboration with Lund University. The investigations
are motivated by neutron imaging data performed at Lund University which indicates a
hydrogen layer as a part of the known corrosion layer consisting of MgO and Mg(OH)2.
This indicated a significant difference in the concentration of hydrogen within 100µm of the
specimen surface and at a distance of more than 100µm from the specimen surface. These
results motivated FIB-SEM analysis due to the high spatial resolution of the technique
compared to neutron imaging. To initiate corrosion the specimens were subjected to a
NaCl solution (Lund).
SEM, including fractured surface analysis, FIB-SEM, and EDS analysis were carried out
on specimens of Mg-0.2Zr alloys while EDS and fractured surface analysis were carried
out on specimens of Mg-0.8Nd alloys. Initially, SEM analysis was performed on cross-
sectional areas of both alloys. No structures as a result of corrosion were observed. SEM
analysis was performed on fractured surfaces of both alloys. Intergranular fracture was
observed on Mg-0.2Zr alloys and no intergranular fracture was observed on Mg-0.8Nd
alloys.
The intergranular fracture observed on Mg-0.2Zr alloys was suggested to be a
result of decohesion as porous structures were observed on the grain boundaries.
EDS analysis included averaging over 14 line measurements to determine the consistency
and chemical content of the corrosion layer. The line measurements were performed on
epoxy-embedded specimens. The line measurements were obtained starting within the
epoxy at a distance of 10µm from the specimen surface and going 105µm into the spec-
imen.
The averaged EDS data showed an increase of magnesium content from 0% to
approximately 100% within a distance of 17µm from the specimen surface. Furthermore
oxygen content peaked at around 2µm from the specimen surface at 15-20%. The stan-
dard deviation of the EDS analysis indicated a consistent and homogeneous corrosion
layer, which is desirable regarding implant materials as localized corrosion may lead to
failure of the implant.
A total of 11 FIB-SEM data sets were collected with SE2 and InLens detectors. The slices,
making up each volume, were aligned and separated into SE2 and InLens detectors. Then
each slice was illumination corrected locally, in each slice, and globally, across all slices. Fi-
nally, the porosity was segmented using edge-detection filtering followed by morphological
procedures. This resulted in 11 3D volumes. 6 out of 11 volumes contained porosity with
four being volumes collected from specimen surfaces and 2 being collected at a distance
250µm from the specimen surface (in the material). The 6 volumes containing porosity
were assessed in terms of number of porous structures and objects in 2 and 3 dimensions
(2D, 3D), total porosity, calculated as the pixels labeled as porosity divided by the total
number of pixels in each volume, the average size of structures and objects in 2 and 3D,
and the average size of the 10% largest and smallest objects. No significant difference,
corresponding with the hypothesis, was observed in the assessment of the 6 volumes. It is
suggested that this might be a result of the grain diameter being dimensionally equal to
the width of the data collected with FIB-SEM, hence the spatial resolution of FIB-SEM
being too high. Therefore, the hypothesis could not be accepted. The hypothesis was not
rejected as a result of statistical uncertainty.

Resumé
Dette projekt undersøger hydrogenudviklings egenskaberne, som et resultat af korrosion,
af magnesium med zirconium og neodymium som legeringsmaterialer til potentiel brug
som ortopædiske implantatmaterialer.
Projektet er udført i samarbejde med Lund Universitet. Undersøgelserne er motiveret af
neutron imaging data udført på Lunds Universitet, som indikerer et brintlag som en del
af det kendte korrosionslag bestående af MgO og Mg(OH)2. Dette giver anledning en
signifikant forskel i koncentrationen af brint inden for 100µm fra prøvens overflade og i
en afstand på mere end 100µm fra prøvens overflade. Disse resultater motiverede FIB-
SEM-analyse på grund af teknikkens høje rumlige opløsning sammenlignet med neutron
imaging data. For at påbegynde korrosion blev prøverne nedsænket i en NaCl-opløsning
på Lund Universitet.
SEM, inklusiv brudfladeanalyse, FIB-SEM og EDS-analyse blev udført på prøver af Mg-
0.2Zr-legeringer, mens EDS- og brudfladeanalyse blev udført på prøver af Mg-0.8Nd-
legeringer. Indledningsvis blev SEM-analyse udført på tværsnitsarealer af begge legeringer.
Der blev ikke observeret strukturer som følge af korrosion. SEM-analyse blev udført på
brækkede overflader af begge legeringer. Intergranulær fraktur blev observeret på Mg-
0.2Zr-legeringer, og ingen intergranulær fraktur blev observeret på Mg-0.8Nd-legeringer.
Det intergranulære brud observeret på Mg-0,2Zr-legeringer blev foreslået til at være et
resultat af hydrogenudvikling på korngrænserne, da porøse strukturer blev observeret på
korngrænserne.
EDS-analyse omfattede et gennemsnit af 14 linjemålinger for at bestemme korrosionslagets
konsistens og kemiske indhold. Linjemålingerne blev udført på epoxyindlejrede prøver.
Linjemålingerne blev opnået ved at starte inde i epoxyen i en afstand på 10µm fra prøvens
overflade og 105µm ind i prøven. De gennemsnitlige EDS-data viste en stigning i magne-
siumindholdet fra 0% til ca. 100% inden for en afstand på 17µm fra prøvens overflade.
Ydermere toppede iltindholdet ved omkring 2µm fra prøvens overflade (inde i prøven) ved
15-20%. Standardafvigelsen af EDS-analysen indikerede et konsistent og homogent korro-
sionslag, hvilket er ønskeligt med hensyn til implantatmaterialer, da lokal korrosion kan
føre til brud og fraktur af implantatet.
I alt blev 11 FIB-SEM datasæt indsamlet med SE2 og InLens detektorer.
Billederne,
der udgør hvert volumen, blev registreret med henhold til overfladen og adskilt i SE2-
og InLens-detektorer. Derefter blev hver skive intensitetskorrigeret lokalt, i hver enkelt
billede og globalt på tværs af alle billeder. Til sidst blev porøsiteten segmenteret ved hjælp
af kantdetektionsfiltrering efterfulgt af morfologiske procedurer. Dette resulterede i 11 3D-
volumener. 6 ud af 11 volumener viste sig at indeholde porøsitet, hvor fire er volumener
opsamlet omkring prøveoverflader og 2 er opsamlet i en afstand 250µm fra prøveoverfladen
(i materialet). De 6 volumener indeholdende porøsitet blev vurderet med hensyn til antal
porøse strukturer og objekter i 2 og 3 dimensioner (2D, 3D), total porøsitet, beregnet som
de pixels mærket som porøsitet divideret med det samlede antal pixels i hvert volumen,
gennemsnitlig størrelse af strukturer og objekter i 2 og 3D, og den gennemsnitlige størrelse
af de 10% største og mindste objekter. Der blev ikke observeret nogen signifikant forskel,
svarende til hypotesen, i vurderingen af de 6 volumener. Det foreslås, at dette kan være
et resultat af, at korndiameteren er dimensionsmæssigt sammenlignelig med bredden af de
data, der er indsamlet med FIB-SEM, hvorfor den rumlige opløsning af FIB-SEM er en
begrænsende faktor i og med dataopsamlingen er begrænset af Galium-elementet i FIB-
SEM mikroskopet. Derfor kunne hypotesen ikke accepteres. Hypotesen blev ikke forkastet
som følge af statistisk usikkerhed.

Introduction
Millions of people suffer from bone fractures every year and this number is only expected
to increase due to age-related diseases with increasing life expectancy [1].
The place
of injury, reduced ability, and complexity of bone fractures depends on several factors.
Some fractures are too complex for external surgery due to the glassy behavior of bones.
Other types of fractures are best cured via internal bone implants [2] [3]. Traditional
metal implants, made from cobalt-chromium or titanium, lie too far from the mechanical
behavior of human bones. Young’s modulus of cobalt-chromium alloys ranges between 100
and 200 GPa, while the corresponding Young’s modulus of cortical human bone (cortical
refers to the outermost layer of bone) is around 60 GPa [4] [5] . Too large of a difference
in the mechanical properties of implant materials compared to bone tissue, may lead to
a phenomenon known as stress-shielding of bone tissue, which may deprive the bone of
normal stress and may impair the healing process of the bone [6]. Another disadvantage
to traditional implant materials is the potential need for removal. There exist different
opinions on routine removal, which is advocated by some and opposed by others, but
since the implant isn’t dissolved, some patients may require removal of the hardware due
to various implant-related problems [7] [8].
Biodegradable implants dissolve in the human body and seem as an acceptable solution
to some of the presented constraints [9]. Multiple biodegradable polymeric materials have
clinically been used during the last decades, although polymers generally exhibit insuﬀi-
cient mechanical properties compared to bones. Magnesium alloys offer great potential as
absorbable implant materials. They are known to degrade within a certain period after
surgery and are therefore great for accomplishing medical functions. The mechanical prop-
erties of magnesium alloys are superior to that of synthetic polymers and traditional metal
implants. For comparison, the density of magnesium is 1.73g/cm3 [10], while the density
of titanium is 4.51g/cm3 [11] and the density of surgical steel is around 8g/cm3 [12]. The
density of human femoral cortical bone is around 2g/cm3 [13], closest to magnesium. The
Young’s modulus of magnesium is 45GPa, dimensionally more comparable to human bone
than traditional implant materials. Effects of stress-shielding are expected to be avoided
due to the mechanical properties of magnesium being closer to human bone.
Even though the compatibility of magnesium to bone tissue has been proven, the degra-
dation rate of magnesium is too high compared to the healing process of bone. Ideally,
the degradation rate and the bone healing rate should be equal in magnitude. This al-
lows for the bone to heal fully before the implant is dissolved. One particular concern is
high amount of hydrogen gas evolution, which can form gas cavities in the surrounding
implantation tissue and the material bulk (kilde).
Studies suggest the use of alloying elements to control the corrosion rate of magnesium
[14]. In this project, two magnesium alloys are studied to better understand the corrosion
behavior of magnesium and the impact of alloying elements on this. The focus has specifi-
cally been on the hydrogen evolution and how these differ between two magnesium alloys.
The alloys of choice are Mg0.8Nd and Mg0.2Zr which are expected to differ significantly
in degradation properties.

Motivation
Magnesium has interesting properties in terms of mechanical properties and biocompatible
properties but the degradation properties of the material are still rather unknown today
[15] [16]. When magnesium is exposed to water, corrosion begins with hydrogen evolution
known as the primary cathodic reaction, although hydrogen evolution inside the material
bulk is in an unknown process [17]. The goal of this project has been to gain insight into
the hydrogen evolution properties of magnesium alloys. This study is done in collaboration
with Lund University in Sweden. The project is a part of an in-depth research project
at Lund University, where multiple magnesium alloys have been and are currently being
studied with a focus on their corrosion properties.
Although an unanswered question
remains: does hydrogen evolution take place inside the material bulk of magnesium alloys
and if so how can this be quantified by SEM and FIB-SEM?
Neutron imaging has been performed at Lund University, which has given rise to these
questions.
Neutron imaging data has indicated the formation of three layers, on the
surface of magnesium, after being subjected to a corrosion medium.
The inner layer
consists of hydrogen. The middle layer consists of oxidized pure magnesium (MgO) and
the outer layer consists of magnesium hydroxide (Mg(OH)2). In order to accept or reject
this hypothesis, whether there exists a hydrogen concentration gradient inside the material
bulk FIB-SEM analysis has been carried out. Traditional SEM has been performed in
combination with EDS to understand the chemical composition of the outer corrosion
layers. FIB-SEM has been used as the primary tool to try to understand the hydrogen
evolution inside magnesium alloys.
Neutron imaging data indicated a hydrogen layer
combined with MgO and Mg(OH)2 with a total width of 100mu from the surface. This
gave motivation for 3D volume collection, using FIB-SEM, from within 100mu and over
100mu from the specimen surface.
During the project, following questions have tried to be answered: What impacts the
degradation and how does the degradation affect the biocompatibility of magnesium and
its alloys? Are SEM, FIB-SEM, and EDS appropriate techniques to determine hydrogen
evolution in magnesium and its alloys? How does hydrogen travel inside the material bulk
and how is hydrogen development interpreted trough SEM, FIB-SEM and EDS.

Background
The discovery of the formation of hydroxide film as magnesium is exposed to moist air dates
back to 1831 [18]. Later, the influence of the aqueous medium on corrosion of magnesium
was analyzed and reported by [19]. This was the first study to propose the hydrogen
gas evolution with the dissolution of magnesium.
The combined study of magnesium
corrosion and the biodegradable properties of the element wasn’t studied until recently
by [20]. These early developments have been crucial in designing new magnesium alloys,
specifically for biodegradable implant materials.
The following Chapter encounters background knowledge and covers areas including im-
plants in medical applications, biodegradable implants, degradation of pure magnesium,
and the alloying effect of zirconium and neodymium. Furthermore, the Chapter includes
descriptions of the microstructure of magnesium alloys, specifically the phases of the alloys
used in this study. The results and discussion section presupposes general knowledge in
the mentioned areas.
3.1
Magnesium alloys in medical applications
Magnesium alloys have several applications in the biomedical field, specifically in ortho-
pedic implants where hip joints, screws, bone plates, and stents are widely used. Certain
conditions should be met for implant materials to be suitable for the human body [21].
First of all, the human body should coexist with the implanted material, meaning that
the implanted material should not result in a significant change to the surrounding tissue
or the implant itself. Another condition is that the implant material should not induce
inflammation in the surrounding tissue. In orthopedic implants, the surrounding tissue
is bone tissue. The majority of orthopedic implants today include cobalt-chromium, tita-
nium, and surgical steel. Traditional implant metals have significant drawbacks related to
the use of these materials. The mechanical properties of titanium and surgical steel are
significantly different compared to the cortical human bone. Table 3.1 compares mechan-

tables, as each metal widely varies in characteristics based on alloy type, heat treatment

Large differences in mechanical properties can introduce stress shielding. Stress shielding is
a phenomenon, where the bone is deprived of natural stress and as a result of that regrows
based on the new stress factors. This is also known as Wolf’s law [22]. Specifically, the
law states that natural healthy bones will adapt, and change to adapt to the stress that it
is subjected to. Stress shielding is avoidable and can be disabled by adapting the stiffness
of the implant material [23]. The result of stress shielding can be that the surrounding
bone tissue becomes porous or the implant material fails [23].
In most cases, a second surgery is needed to remove the implants. In some older patients
(+70 years) second surgery is not wanted due to increased risk of infection and other
complications [24].
According to Table 3.1 magnesium have more comparable mechanical properties to human
cortical bone than traditional implant materials. Furthermore, by using magnesium the
need for a second surgery is eliminated due to its degradation properties. Magnesium

be practical in some cases but unwanted in other cases, where the bone healing rate is
slower than the degradation of the implant material. Too high of a corrosion rate will
potentially result in the implant material dissolving before the bone is fully healed.
3.2
Biodegradable implants
Corrosion and degradation are often used interchangeably in the field of biodegradable
materials [25]. However, there is a clear difference between these two words. Corrosion is
a general term in material science that describes the process of a metal converting into a
chemically more stable oxide. Degradation involves the deterioration of the material when
being exposed to a certain environment resulting in loss of the material [26] (from this
point corrosion is used when describing the chemical process, while degradation is used
when viewing materials in the light of biodegradable materials).
The main advantage of biodegradable implants is that they degrade after a given amount
of time. Ideally, the implant is fully degraded after it has fulfilled its purpose, whether this
is to induce a specific cellular behavior or to support structures such as bone or muscle.
This makes a second surgery for removing metal implants unnecessary, as the implant is
dissolved when the healing process of the bone is complete. The healing process of bone is
stimulated due to the constant degradation of the implant. Specifically, this corresponds
with the successive loss of the mechanical properties of the implant, during degradation,
which implies increasing load on the healing tissue [27].
Biodegradable implants are often described based on their interaction with the surround-
ing tissue. The type of interactions with the surrounding tissue is either biotoxic if the
surrounding tissue dies [9]. If the biomaterial doesn’t initiate a response or interact, when
introduced to the biological tissue it is known as a bioinert-nontoxic and biologically in-
active implant [28]. If the material provokes some sort of response from the living system
without being a toxic response the implant is known as bioactive nontoxic and biologically
active [29]. An implant is called bioresorbable / biodegradable-nontoxic if the implant
material is nontoxic and dissolves in the body with the surrounding tissue replacing it
[30].
Depending on the size of the implant and the location inside the body, the type of inter-
action between the implant and the surrounding tissue must be defined. It should also
be noted that there are both advantages and disadvantages to each type of interaction.
Bio-toxic implants can in some cases be acceptable if the implant material is not in direct
contact with certain types of biological tissues, meaning that the toxicity is tissue specific.
If this type of interaction between the implant and surrounding tissue is acceptable, other
parameters like cost of implant and durability are assessed.
Traditional orthopedic implants may be bioinert- or bioactive nontoxic but as magnesium is
naturally biodegradable the appropriate interaction category for magnesium alloy implants
is biodegradable-nontoxic. Toxicity is a common parameter to asses in implant materials.
Toxicity is mostly based on the toxicity of corrosion products, for instance, toxic metal ions,
but is also related to the corrosion rate [31]. Comparing titanium and magnesium from
Table 3.1 the corrosion rates differ widely and are not comparable dimensionally which
naturally impacts the influence of toxic products on biological tissue and is therefore taken
into account when determining the toxicity [14].

3.3
Degradation of magnesium
Magnesium possesses multiple interesting biological and mechanical properties, although
its use in practical clinical applications has been limited due to its poor corrosion resistance
[32].
Unlike aluminum alloys and steel, which possess passive oxide films that offer strong corro-
sion resistance, corrosion products on the surface of magnesium offer little in passivization
of the material bulk [32].
Magnesium and its alloys possess two constraints related to corrosion behavior. First, due
to the high degradation rate, hydrogen gas evolution must not result in the formation of
subcutaneous bubbles in the surrounding tissue. Second, the corrosion behavior should
be homogeneous, so localized corrosion does not occur in such a way that this leads to
premature failure of the implant [32].
Additionally, hydrogen gas evolution can pose a threat to living cells in biomedical appli-
cations [33]. Therefore, pure magnesium needs to be altered in a way that the corrosion
rate, and thereby the hydrogen gas evolution, can be controlled. Multiple studies inves-
tigate the impact of heat solid treatment and several chemical treatments of these alloys
as one way to change the degradation rate of magnesium. Other studies suggest alloying
elements to control the degradation rate and thereby the hydrogen gas evolution [34] [?].
[34] investigates the influence of alloying elements together with heat treatment and corro-
sion medium treatment of three different alloys. The study shows that corrosion resistance
is generally improved by material heat treatment when super-saturated solid solution film
is formed. Corrosion medium also promotes the passivization of a protective film from
degradation products.
[?] investigates the corrosion behavior of three alloys using isothermal calorimetry and
pressure measurement techniques. The project indicates that the binary alloy Mg-0.2Zr
slows the corrosion down relative to other alloy samples. In opposition, other experiments
show that Mg-0.8Nd has the highest corrosion rate.
3.3.1
Metabolic processes for implanted magnesium
Magnesium is an essential co-factor in many human metabolic enzymes. Magnesium plays
a part in cell membranes in both transportation mechanisms and stabilization of the
cell membrane. Furthermore, it influences neuromuscular conduction and serves several
functions in the central nervous system [35].
Magnesium is known to be the most reactive element of all structural materials due to its
negative equilibrium potential E0(Mg/Mg2+) of -2.36 V at room temperature [36]. The
high corrosion behavior of pure magnesium can be explained by magnesium’s high specific
dissolution behavior (formation of Mg2+ ions) which might be a result of a poor pacifying
corrosion layer.
The application of electrolytic cells where thin layers of metal are deposited onto an
electrically conductive surface is known as electroplating. A cell consists of two conductors
of metal while these are immersed in an electrolyte. Both silver and gold plating of jewelry
is performed to improve the appearance while zinc and tin may be applied to confer
corrosion resistance.
The principle of micro-electroplating is the same but it all is done in micrometer scale.
Micro-electroplating is known to accelerate the corrosion of magnesium in the presence of
cathodically acting local impurities and secondary phases [37].
6

During exposure in a normal atmosphere, reaction with oxygen results in a homogeneous
oxide layer on the surface (10 nm of thickness after only 10 s of exposure). Upon reacting
with water, either directly or in a humid environment, this layer becomes magnesium
hydroxide (Mg(OH)2). This film is poorly soluble in water and the film maintains notably
high stability in environments with pH above 10 [38].
During the corrosion of magnesium, hydrogen tends to develop by electrochemical pro-
cesses and is released. The cathodic partial reaction, due to which hydrogen gas develops,
is shown in equation 3.1. This occurs as a balancing reaction to the anodic dissolution of
magnesium metal seen in equation 3.2 [38].
2H+ + 2e →H2
(3.1)
2Mg →2Mg+ + 2e
(3.2)
Due to the strong reactivity of magnesium in environments with pH<10, equation 3.3
takes place simultaneously.
2Mg+ + 2H2O →2Mg2+ + 2OH−+ H2
(3.3)
During corrosion of magnesium, multiple chemical processes take place in both areas cov-
ered and uncovered by oxide films (MgO and Mg(OH)2) [38]. Since multiple processes take
place at once in magnesium corrosion, this is contrary to the principles of electrochemical
measurements that assume one anodic or one cathodic reaction at a time. This results
in a subsequently lower amount of calculated release of hydrogen gas, compared to the
actual amount [38].

3.4
Alloying effects of Zr and Nd
The most common alloying components in magnesium alloys are aluminum, calcium, zirco-
nium, and zinc. When these alloying elements react with magnesium intermetallic phases
can form. Contrary to alloys, intermetallic phases show a lattice structure that differs
from those of component metals. The lattice bond of various atomic species is a mixture
of a predominantly metallic bond and lower fractions of other kinds of bonds, which may
include covalent and ionic bonds, resulting in special physical and mechanical properties
of this phase [23].
3.4.1
Effect of Zr on the corrosion behavior of Mg
Elements including zirconium, have been found to have different effects on corrosion re-
sistance depending on their content. Generally, when the content of zirconium is high,
corrosion resistance suffers, while when the content is low, the corrosion rate slows down
[39] [40].
7
==PAGE:20==
Zirconium is added to magnesium due to its unique ability to reduce the grain size of
magnesium alloys [41].
The reduction in grain size significantly improves the casting
abilities and mechanical properties of magnesium alloys [42] [43].
Most recent developments have seen magnesium alloys containing zirconium being tested
for use in biomedical applications [44].
There is a substantial growing interest in the
fundamental understanding of the influence of zirconium on the corrosion of magnesium.
Some studies have suggested that the addition of zirconium to magnesium improves the
corrosion resistance of magnesium. However, the focus of these studies has been on singular
commercial magnesium alloys, where Zr is a minor addition to other mixture elements,
as opposed to magnesium-zirconium binary systems. Furthermore, the zirconium content
hasn’t been altered in these studies. Alloying elements in magnesium-zirconium binary
systems are known to form intermetallic phases with zirconium [45].
3.4.2
Effect of neodymium on the corrosion behavior of magnesium
Neodymium is a widely used alloying element in magnesium-zinc-zirconium-based alloys
together with magnesium-aluminum-based alloys to improve corrosion resistance and me-
chanical properties. The second phases of Mg12Nd and Mg41Nd5 (figure 3.2) are thought
to have formed and isolated the magnesium matrix resulting in a reduction of grain size
resulting in increased tensile strength of magnesium alloys. It has been reported that the
addition of 1% to 6% of neodymium to magnesium alloys reduces grain size and thereby
improves tensile and corrosion properties [45].
3.5
Environmental effects on Mg corrosion
Magnesium does not demonstrate corrosion resistance in all environments. Magnesium
shows strong corrosion resistance in some environments, like basic solutions, while it dis-
solves rapidly in neutral and acidic environments. If the corrosion rate and the corrosion
products of magnesium alloys can be controlled, a dimensionally high corrosion rate is
compared to traditional implant materials (Table 3.1). This is desirable if corrosion prod-
ucts are tested to be non-toxic and are either used in biological processes inside the body
or excreted as waste [38].
3.6
Hydrogen evolution
Hydrogen evolution (HE) is categorized into three groups [46].
1. Environmental HE- occurs when the material is subjected to an atmosphere con-
taining hydrogen, e.g. in storage tanks
2. Internal Reversible HE- occurs when hydrogen gas enters the metal during its pro-
cessing
3. Hydrogen reaction embrittlement- occurs at higher temperatures when hydrogen
chemically with a constituent of the metal to form a new microstructural element or
phase, such as a hydride or to generate gas bubbles, also known as blistering

Corrosion Medium
The corrosion of metals and alloys under solid NaCl deposits in an aqueous solution is seen
to cause corrosion and has received considerable attention in recent years. NaCl aqueous
solutions are also known to be used alongside simulated body fluids for corrosion behavior
analysis [30].
Furthermore, a study indicates that the electrochemical behavior of magnesium alloys
changes depending on the chloride ion concentration and its surface film. Furthermore,
results show enhanced localized corrosion resulting in rapid initiation and quick growth of
active spots, facilitation of breakdown and high hydration of surface oxide films [48].

sponds with hydrogen gas evolution. Opposed to optic microscopes, SEM uses electrons
and since the wavelength of electrons is smaller, around 3.88pm, than visible light, around
200nm, this allows for higher resolution, using electron microscopy, specifically down to
nm scale [49] [50].
An SEM produces images of a specimen by scanning the surface of the specimen with
a fine beam of electrons. The components of a scanning electron microscope is shown
15
==PAGE:28==
in figure 4.5. The electron beam is synchronised with the spot of the display cathode
ray tube (CRT). A detector measures the intensity of a chosen second signal (for example
secondary electrons (SE)) and the brightness of the CRT spot is controlled by an amplified
signal. If the intensity of the emitted secondary signal changes across the specimen, then
contrast will be present in the image on the CRT [49] [50].
Figure 4.5: Components of a scanning electron microscope
SEM produces images, which can be interpreted the same way as everyday optic images due
to the way light and shade is perceived. Similar to everyday images, in images produced
by SEM holes, holes appear dark and hills have a bright and dark side. Although optic
images and images produced by SEM contain significant similarities, it is important to
understand the ways they differ. One difference is the no ”ray path” between the sample
and image screen, which emphasises the importance of electronics in manipulating and
displaying the image before it is displayed on a CRT. Furthermore, opposed to the human
eye, which is sensitive to all points in an image simultaneously, an SEM image is collected
point by point. This can be compared to a person standing in a completely dark room with
a torch scanning the wall for objects. By scanning the torch systematically side-to-side
the person slowly start to build up an image in their memory of the objects on the wall.
The SEM works in a similar fashion and this systematically side-to-side scan is known as
a raster pattern. Another important consideration is the fact that an image formed by an
SEM is not necessarily of the surface of the sample. In fact, it is possible, by choosing the
electron energy, to control the depth of penetration of the electrons and the type of signal
16
==PAGE:29==
emitted used to construct the image [49] [50].
In figure 4.6 the information contained within the interaction volume of primary electrons
and the sample, is shown. When electrons hits a sample they interact with atoms in the
sample. Some of the possible outcomes is depicted in figure 4.6. As shown in figure 4.6,
some electrons bounce back and out of the sample. These are denoted back scattered
electrons. Others may bump into atoms and displace electrons, which knock out of the
atom and out of the sample. These are denoted secondary electrons. Further information
can result from interactions between the electrons and the sample, including X-rays, light
and heat. The depth and diameter of the interaction volume depends on the kV combined
with the density of the specimen. As shown in figure 4.6 secondary electrons results from
the top of the interaction volume, which corresponds to approximately the top 15nm
of specimen. Back scattered electrons on the other hand result from deeper within the
sample, which corresponds to approximately the top 40% of the specimen. X-ray can be
collected from the entire volume [49] [50].
Electron-specimen interactions are divided into two groups- elastic and inelastic scattering
[49] [50].
Elastic scattering The trajectory of the electron changes, but the kinetic energy and ve-
locity of the electronic remains practically constant. The result is back scattered electrons
[49] [50].
Inelastic scattering The trajectory of the electron, is opposed to elastic scattering, only
slightly influenced, but there is loss of energy trough the transfer of energy to the specimen.
This results in:
1. Heat (phonon excitation)
2. Visible light (cathodoluminescence)
3. Bremsstrahlung (continuum radiation)
4. Characteristic X-ray radiation
5. Secondary electrons
6. Auger electrons (when outer shell electrons are ejected)
Since secondary electrons result from the surface layer of the sample, SEM images based
on secondary electrons reveal the surface features of the sample. A result of this is also
edges, peaks and finer structures like crests become bright, in particular because secondary
electrons can escape these features easily, although a variety of other factors can influence
the brightness and contrast of an SE image [50].

==PAGE:30==

Back scattered electrons have much higher energy, approximately around 50eV and 5000eV.
Since back scattered electrons have higher energy levels, these tend to naturally emerge
from deeper locations within the sample. This introduces a constrain that the resolution
of BSE images is less than those of SE images. BSE, although, has some advantages,
which makes this technique interesting in analytical SEM. The intensity of the BSE signal
strongly correlates with the atomic number of a particular area in a sample [49] [50].
The Merlin and Cross-Beam microscope are equipped with two detectors for secondary
electrons, an InLens detector and a conventional SE detector (SE2). The InLens detector
is placed inside the electron column of the microscope. Due to a magnetic field at the
pole piece, secondary electrons are collected eﬀiciently. Specifically, at low voltages and
small working distances is high quality images achievable with InLens. Images obtain,
in particular, contain information of lateral resolution while conventional SE2 detector
images shows images with topographic information most prominently [51].
4.4
Focused Ion Beam
Focused Ion Beam (FIB) systems use a focused beam of ions, in most cases gallium ions.
At high currents, FIB systems can be used to sputter or mill on a specimen surface that
is exposed to the beam. Figure 4.9 shows the process for milling on a specimen surface.
In FIB milling the ion beam hits the specimen surface and sputters a small amount of
material away.
This leaves the surface of either secondary ions as a result of the ion
beam interaction, or neutral atoms. At high currents material can be removed from the
specimen surface allowing for precision milling of the specimen. The material removal rate
is determined by the material and other parameters including ion energy and the angle
of ion beam. Simultaneously, the specimen can be imaged by a dual-beam by scanning
18
==PAGE:31==
electron microscope. This has made FIB milling a widely used tool for producing 3D
micro-devices and high-precision microstructures made of bulk materials [52] [53] [51].
A FIB setup resembles an SEM, although, while the SEM setup uses a focused beam of
electrons to image a sample in the chamber, a FIB setup uses a focused beam of ions.
A FIB-SEM setup incorporates both electron and ion beam columns, which allows for
features to be investigated with the use of both beams respectively.
The FIB is also
used to remove material in specimens or for making specific structures in a sample, which
is especially used in electronic devices.
If material is removed, combined with images
captured with SEM, this results in a stack of images. These can later be reconstructed
into 3D volumes and analysed [53] [51].
Most instruments use liquid metal ion sources (LMIS), especially gallium ion sources. In
gallium LMIS, the metal piece is placed in contact with a tungsten needle. The heated
gallium wets the tungsten and flows to the tip of the needle. From here the surface tension
and electric field form the gallium into a cusp shaped tip known as a Taylor cone. The
great electric field at the tip causes ionization and emission of ions [52].
A disadvantage of FIB milling is the slow processing speed. FIB milling can be combined
with chemical- or gas-assisted ion etching to increase the material removal [52].
Another potential disadvantage of FIB milling is known as curtaining or waterfall effect.
Milled faces show line as a result of more, and less material is removed from the face,
where milling is performed. This is an irregular non flat surface, which can influence the
quantitative image analysis and obscure features. Curtaining is a result of spatial variation
of the sputter rate of the sample and the modulation of the current density by forward
scattering of ions. This may result in materials with porosity, rough surfaces, height steps
and composites of hard and soft materials [52] [53].
4.5
Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy
Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS) uses the X-ray spectrum to obtain a localized
chemical content. Location of the X-ray signals in the interaction volume is indicated in
figure 4.6. In principle all elements from atomic number 4 (beryllium) to 92 (uranium)
are detectable, while some instruments are not equipped for lighter elements with atomic
number under 10. Both qualitative and quantitative analysis can be made. Qualitative
analysis involves the identification of elements in a straight line, while quantitative analysis
includes the determination of the concentration of each element across a line [54] [51].
EDS analysis is performed at high voltage to allow a solid sample to be bombarded with
a beam of electrons and interact with these to produce a X-ray signal. The scanning of
the beam is performed in the same raster pattern as SEM analysis. EDS allows for X-ray
measurements of both points, lines and maps. Images produced by electrons collected from
the surface can also give indications about the surface topography or mean atomic number
differences depending on the mode [54] [51]. Since X-ray intensities are measured by the
number of photons, the measurements are limited by a statistical error. The precision,
denoted by 2σ, in most elements can be as good as ±1% but as a rule of thumb the average
analytical accuracy is said to be 2% [54] [51].
Due to the bombarding of electrons, a continuous X-ray spectrum is produced, alongside
the characteristic X-ray signals. This limits detection of smaller peaks due to a background
of noise. This is known as Brehmstrahlung [54] [51].
When focusing on small details at a solid specimen the spatial resolution of EDS should
be kept in mind. This is dependent on the penetration and spreading of electrons inside
19
==PAGE:32==
the specimen. The resolution is a function of the density of the material. In silicates
(3gcm3) the typical resolution is close to 2µm. For better spatial resolution one may refer
to transmission electron microscope with X-ray spectrometer and use ultra-thin specimens
(around 100nm) [54] [51] [55].
Due to the shallow depth of the electron probe analysis, specimen should be polished, so
roughness in the surfaces doesn’t affect the result. Since the EDS column sits in a 54◦
position height differences can result in the EDS signal being measured from a hill blocking
the actual location of measurements, resulting in a different signal [55].
As for SEM analysis non-conducting surfaces must be coated with carbon coating for
incident (instance?) electrons to flow to ground. Carbon coating is preferable in EDS due
to its low atomic number (6) compared to gold (79), commonly used on SEM specimens,
which has more significant influence on X-ray intensities [51] [55].

4.9
Data processing
4.9.1
3D porous volumes
The data collection in this project consists of the combination of three techniques on sam-
ples prepared the way they are described in section 4.2. Scanning electron microscopy
has been used for direct analysis of specimen surfaces. SEM has also been used in combi-
nation with FIB for collection of a series of data for later to be stacked and analysed as
a 3D volume. Finally, EDS has been used in combination with SEM to understand the
alloys’ phase structures and the degradation layer of the alloys. In this section the data
processing for the 3D volume will be explained.
The raw image data from the FIB-SEM session consisted of data from two different de-
tectors. The first step was to separate the data into the two different detectors and to
align the data within each detector. For later analysis it was important for the data to be
separated and aligned properly. Registration within the field of image analysis describes
the alignment of multiple images in relation to some structures, which can be found across
images. Although a constrain is introduced when images are taken continuously, while
removing material. This is an issue due to the constant change in features. There are
several ways to do this manually, but this would be time consuming across multiple data
sets. To optimize the registration, an algorithm developed by Peter Stanley Jørgensen,
was implemented in order to automatically register features across slices and align all im-
ages. In order for the algorithm to function properly, approximately 30% of the height
of the image collection was the surface of the specimen. The algorithm finds a defined
number of features and aligns the images according to these.
The second step was illumination correction. There exists a few reasons for why the images
may have uneven illumination. First of all is the specimen tilted when performing FIB-
SEM which may be an factor. Second of all may height differences within the specimen
result in illumination differences. Illumination correction is an important step before the
final segmentation. If there are large illumination differences in and across slices, this may
introduce bias in the segmentation and alter the results. The illumination correction is
also handled trough an algorithm developed by Peter Stanley Jørgensen. The first step
of illumination correction consist of manual inspection of the image data. This step is to
inspect and to crop the image slices to get rid of any unwanted areas. These areas includes
the layer between the specimens and the epoxy, which includes the outer degraded layer
(Mg(OH)2) of the specimen. Other unwanted areas are areas with either very high or
very low contrast that aren’t corrected by the illumination correction step. In this step,
unwanted image slices can be removed, which are introduced due to some artefacts or
noise. As there exists multiple methods for illumination correction details on this is not
provided here.
26
==PAGE:39==
The last step is the segmentation of the porosity observed in the volumes.
This was
achieved by using an edge detection filter. Specifically, the approxcanny method is used
in MATLAB. The approxcanny method is an approximated version of the popular Canny
method. Both methods use two thresholds. All edges below the threshold are disregarded
and keep all edges with edge strength above the higher threshold. The thresholds varied
across the five data. After edge detection with approxcanny morphological operations were
applied in order to fill empty structures in 2D. As the final step statistics in 2D and 3D
were obtained. The statistics in 2D is understood as the summation or average across all
slides. The statistics includes number of structures (2D) and objects (3D), total porosity,
calculated as the total number of pixels labeled as porosity divided by the total number
of pixels in each data set, average size of structures and objects, and the average size of
the 10% largest and smallest structures and objects.

5.4.1
Considerations of surface fracture analysis
Rough estimation of grain size of the Mg-0.2Zr was achievable from surface fracture anal-
ysis. The estimation is later used in data FIB-SEM data collection and analysis of 3D
spatial distribution of pores.
The analysis furthermore showed intergranular fracture in Mg-0.2Zr and no intergran-
ular fracture in Mg-0.8Nd. Since intergranular fracture is usually a result of weakened
grain boundaries this might indicate weakened grain boundaries in Mg-0.2Z. Furthermore
porosity was seen on visible grain boundaries in Mg-0.2Z. A theory known as decohesion
theory is stated by Oriani and Joesphic . This theory proposes that the cohesive force
between atoms in the metal lattice is reduced in proportion to the hydrogen concentration.
Furthermore it takes into account the increase of hydrogen solubility in the strained region
at the tip of the crack. An expansion of the theory includes other presence of impurities
such as liquid metals.
According to the hypothesis stated in Chapter 2 the highest concentration of hydrogen
is found within 100µm from the specimen surface. Area 1 and 3 indicated in figure 5.9
is located within 100-200µm from the surface and area 2 is located within 200-300µm. A
general tendency for no visible grains towards the middle of the specimen, approximately
500µm from each edge of the specimen, is seen in Mg-0.2Zr.
Due to the amount of
samples present, in total, one specimen was fracture surface analysed. It is therefore hard
to conclude based on one analysis definitely, but the analysis gives a slight indication of a
gradient in hydrogen concentration insight the material bulk.
If the intergranular fracture in Mg-0.2Zr is a result of hydrogenous gas development in
grain boundaries, this suggests that the overall hydrogen development in Mg-0.2Zr is higher
than in Mg-0.8Nd since there is no intergranular fracture in Mg-0.8Nd. There might be a
time factor associated with this difference and potentially other sources of error. The time
factor might influence the result in multiple ways. First of all the solution treatment, in
corrosion medium, of the two specimens must have occurred for exactly an equal amount
of time. Furthermore how the hydrogen development continues inside the material bulk
may differ in the two materials.
Studies show a difference in the degradation rate of the two alloys, hence, a difference
in the hydrogen development rate, which might explain the significant difference in the
fractured surfaces of the two alloys.
Geometric and size distribution was done on features seen on grain boundaries. Porous
structures seen on grain boundaries were estimated in the sizes of 1 to 4µm. Small black
areas were also seen on the grain boundaries. These were located randomly and in the
size range of 200 to 300nm. It was concluded that these structures were a result of the
manufacturing of the alloy after confirmation from Dmytro who had observed similar
structures on other specimens.

Considerations about 3D spatial distributions of pores
Recall the hypothesis- there should be a difference in the spatial distribution of pores in 3D
between volumes collected from the interface and from volumes collected from a distance
of 250µm from specimen surface. According to the hypothesis hydrogen evolution should
decrease in concentration or structures/objects as a result of hydrogen evolution should
decrease in size when increasing the distance from the specimen surface. The porosity
observed in the porous volumes is assumed to either correlate with hydrogen evolution or
be a direct result hereof.
The fractured surface analysis showed porous structures on multiple grain boundaries.
Furthermore, intergranular fracture was observed on grain boundaries with porous struc-
tures indicating that they might be a result of hydrogen evolution. The size distribution
of structures at grain boundaries in the fractured surface analysis and porous structures
obtained from FIB-SEM analysis was done in section ?? in order to confirm whether these
might be a result of the same process. The comparison between porous structures of the
fractured surface analysis and from the porous volumes indicated that these structures
is a result from the same process taking into consideration that the geometry, size and
location of the porous structures were similar. Even though decohesion between contigu-
ous grains might be a possible explanation of the intergranular fracture observed in the
Mg-0.2Zr specimen, this would require further similar experiments on specimens with the
same alloy. This would also require similar experiments performed on other magnesium
alloys with known corrosion and hydrogen evolution properties. Fractured surface analysis
might be a quantifiable technique for hydrogen evolution properties in magnesium alloys
due to intergranular fracture observed in Mg-0.2Zr specimens, possibly resulting from de-
cohesion but in order to confirm this it would require further studies with specimens both
with and without initiated corrosion.
The largest amount of number of structures across all slices was in 2D found in data set 7
and 8 while the largest amount of objects in 3D was found in data set 1 and 8. As described
in section 4.9.1 due to the method of segmentation variations between each porous volume
may be significant. Figure 5.20 to 5.24 gives an idea about the spatial distribution of pores

[56] defines intergranular corrosion as localised corrosion at and

although the spatial resolution of FIB-SEM come short. Since the diameter of the grains,

experiments would be needed in order to significantly conclude a correlation between
corrosion and grain boundaries.

6
Conclusion
In this project magnesium alloys with zirconium and neodymium was examined with
potential use in orthopaedic implants.
Specifically, the hydrogen evolution properties
inside the material bulk has been studied using scanning electron microscopy, focused ion
beam-scanning electron microscopy, and Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy.
The aim of the project was to determine whether SEM and FIB-SEM were appropriate
techniques to investigate hydrogen evolution properties in magnesium alloys.
SEM and EDS analysis have been performed on three specimen (Mg-0.2Zr) interfaces
between the surounding epoxy and the specimen. The EDS analysis consisted of taking
the average of 14 line measurements including the chemical content of magnesium, oxygen
and carbon. The line measurements started 10µm in the epoxy going 105µm into the
specimen. The averaged EDS data showed a consistent and homogeneous outer corrosion
layer consisting of magnesium and oxygen.
It was concluded that the corrosion layer
consisted of MgO and Mg(OH)2.
One condition of the magnesium alloys to be used
in biomedical applications was even and homogeneous corrosion without any signs of
localised corrosion. The EDS analysis did not show signs of localised corrosion although
due to the limited statistics further experiments should follow if the material is intended
for biomedical applications. In extension to that experiments determining the toxicity of
the alloying element in combination with magnesium should be made.
Fractured surface analysis has been performed on two alloys, Mg-0.2Zr and Mg-0.8Nd.
The results showed intergranular fracture on Mg-0.2Zr and non on Mg-0.8Nd. The inter-
granular fracture on Mg-0.2Zr was suggested to be a result of hydrogen concentration at
the grain boundaries resulting in reduced cohesive force.
FIB-SEM analysis has been carried out in order to quantify porosity, assumed to be a
result of hydrogen evolution, in 3D. Number of structures and objects in 2D and 3D, total
porosity, average size, including average size of the 10% largest and smallest structures and
objects was carried out. No systematic significant difference was observed when comparing
volumes collected from the surface specimen and from a distance of 250µm from the
specimen surface (inside the material). With these result the hypothesis that hydrogen
concentration, within magnesium alloys, reduces as the distance to the specimen surface
is increased, was not able to be accepted. The hypothesis could not rejected due to the
limited statistics. The limited statics is merely a result of the amount of data collected but
indeed a result of the resolution of FIB-SEM. With FIB-SEM analysis porous volumes with
high resolution could be produced, although the size of the data collection was indicated
to be similar to the grain size resulting in quite randomized number of grains and grain
boundaries, hence porosity, in each volume. If 200 volumes are collected this might not
be an issue but considering the time put into this project this might not be possible in

A.1
Introduction and motivation
Magnesium and its alloys have attracted significant attention as temporary bio-degradable
implants (bio-implants) over the past decade. Magnesium is non-toxic and over time it is
dissolved and absorbed, meaning that contrary to today’s steel or titan implants, magne-
sium alloy implants do not need to be removed from the body after the healing process
has completed.
As surgery allways carries risk of infection and further complications,
biodegradable implants are preferable. Although a great barrier to overcome is the high
chemical reactivity of magnesium.
Gaining control of the degradation rate and struc-
tural stability in aqueous media is therefore important for future research in the field.
Fundamental understanding of physical processes around the alloy surface is still limited.
Successfully filling these knowledge gaps on the underlying process will enable eﬀicient
design of new biodegradable magnesium alloys [?] [?].
Multiple studies show that magnesium exposed to aqueous media at ambient temperature
generates large amounts of hydrogen gas (H2). Furthermore alloys of magnesium with
either zirconium or neodymium show significant amount of hydrogen that penetrates into
the metal and remains trapped in the bulk. Data of said magnesium based alloys us-
ing neutron imaging analysis already exists and is acquired from Lund University. This
project is made in collaboration with Lund University. The neutron imaging data gives
quantitative analysis of hydrogenation and degradation of the respective alloys, although
with neutron imaging the individual nano- to micrometer scale porous structures that de-
velops from the surface and inward in said alloys, are not accessible due to the low spatial
resolution of this technique. Understanding the morphology of the 3D porous structures
is crucial for understanding magnesium alloy degradation [?] [?].
To fully understand the 3D porous structures of these magnesium alloys, this project will
apply 3D reconstruction of the porous microstructure by 3D Focused Ion Beam (FIB)
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM). This data of the structures will be compared to
the data gained from the neutron imaging with the purpose to link the microstructural
information with the OH-phases distribution.
The project is divided into two sub-categories with the first (described in the sections
above) being collection of experimental data on the FIB-SEM instrument, data processing,
including 3D reconstruction and finally analysing the microstructure and how it relates to
material degradation. The second sub-category is a holistic literature research where the
alloys are compared to the literature with focus on biological applications and later a more
general approach in regards to what goes into making these alloys suitable as bio-implants.
Some considerations on this matter are:
Magnesium is a lightweight material and is known to be the lightest among structural met-
als. Furthermore magnesium is relatively cheap due to it being the eight most abundant
element in the crust of the Earth. These properties has led to high interest in magnesium
for biomaterial purposes [?] [?].
Magnesium and its alloys are candidates for a new generation of bio-implants. Magnesium
is known to fully dissolve in body fluid. The degradation take place during the healing
process and if the corrosion rate can be controlled this will leave no debris after end healing.
Magnesium and its alloys has a particular interest in the field of orthopedics. The present
materials used for orthopedics as screws and plates are titanium or steel, which have a
stiffness which is relatively high compared to natural bone. The high stiffness can lead to a
process known as ”stress shielding” where the surrounding tissue is highly compromised. In
contrast to magnesium, these metals also release toxic substances, specifically metal ions,
65
==PAGE:78==
during the degradation process. In comparison the biological and mechanical properties
of magnesium is closer to that of bones. Magnesium alloys have a Young’s modulus of 40
- 45 GPa, whereas the Young’s modulus of bone is 10 - 27 GPa. If this is compared to
the Young’s modulus of titanium and 316L stainless steel: 110 and 193, the motivation
for using magnesium in bio-implants, becomes more pronounced [?] [?].
