Abstract
This thesis covers 3D embedded bioprinting of tissueôÙladen constructs for urogenôÙ
ital tissue engineering. The project aims to combine rapid mechanical dissociation
of primary bladder tissue and rapid fabrication via 3D embedded bioprinting of imôÙ
plantable, tubular constructs.
In this project, a general procedure for rapid grinding of bladder tissue was esôÙ
tablished with a focus on optimizing grinding time, grinding unit cell spacing and
postôÙgrinding centrifugation time. InôÙvitro culture experiments were conducted to
compare cellular viability and outgrowth of different conditions of the above variôÙ
ables. Two grinding time conditions were investigated and compared: 60 seconds
and 120 seconds. From the results of this study it was found that the grinding duôÙ
ration of 60 seconds constituted a more efficient protocol. Two grinding unit cell
spacing conditions: 100ö¥m and 180ö¥m were examined. InôÙvitro culture results
demonstrated that the grinding unit cell spacing of 100ö¥m was the most optimal
of the two. Five centrifugation time conditions were compared: five, four, three,
two and one minute. The findings of this study suggested that the five minutes
centrifugation time generally displayed a larger and more consistent outcome in
terms of cellular survival and proliferation for inôÙvitro culture of mechanically disôÙ
sociated primary bladder tissue.
The optimized rapid grinding procedure was then used to formulate and develop
a soft gelatinôÙbased bioink. Various compositions of the soft bioink were produced
and cultured. However, samples were improperly embedded and it was not possiôÙ
ble to analyze and compare the various conditions. One sample was sectioned in
the wrong orientation, and the histological investigation suggested viable cells in
the bioink after a culture period of two weeks. In addition, a tough gelatinôÙbased
ink was formulated and mechanically tested in a suture retention strength testôÙ
ing. The findings of this study showed that the addition of cNFC fibers to the ink
improved suture retention strength of the printed sample.
Lastly, a simple twoôÙlayered 3D embedded printed construct was developed and
optimized. The optimized print was then utilized for 3D embedded bioprinting of
tissueôÙladen twoôÙlayered constructs. The samples are under histological processôÙ
ing.

Introduction
Hypospadias is one of the most prevalent congenital urogenital conditions, afôÙ
fecting more than 18 per 10,000 born male infants in Europe. Complex cases of
hypospadias can pose severe and longôÙterm consequences if not treated properly
in the early years of infancy [1] .
Existing therapeutic approaches for surgical reconstructions of complex proxiôÙ
mal hypospadias are not without constraints [1]. Conventional tissue engineering
strategies have been applied in urogenital tissue engineeringëƒ preclinical and clinôÙ
ical studies have been conducted on both acellular and cellôÙseeded scaffolds. It
has been found that cellular scaffolds generally produce better outcomes for the
patientëƒ acellular scaffolds were associated to a larger extent with postôÙoperative
complications such as urethral collapse and strictures [2, 3, 4].
However, conventional tissue engineering strategies for fabricating cellôÙseeded
scaffolds are associated with heavy processing and extensive inôÙvitro culturing of
autologous cells [5, 6]. Advances made in the field of tissue engineering employ
microôÙgrafts of autologous tissue in the fabrication of tubular constructs used in
urogenital surgical reconstruction [7]. However, no rapid automated procedure
exists to engineer urogenital constructs comprised of autologous tissue.
This thesis addresses some of the current challenges in urogenital tissue engiôÙ
neering, aiming to: increase automation, decrease protocol duration and improve
functional outcome of the tissueôÙengineered conduit. This is done through comôÙ
bining rapid mechanical dissociation of primary bladder tissue with 3D embedded
bioprinting. 3D embedded bioprinting is a highôÙspeed technique that enables fabôÙ
rication of three dimensional structures through extrusionôÙbased printing of autolôÙ
ogous cellôÙcontaining bioinks [8]. This technique holds promise for fast onôÙsight
fabrication of patientôÙspecific conduits in the future.
This thesis focuses on the following three main areas: 1) Establishing a general
procedure for rapid grinding of primary bladder tissue and assess viability and celôÙ
lular outgrowth inôÙvitro as function of protocol. 2) Formulating bioinks compatible
with suturing and evaluate cellular survival and outgrowth using said bioinks and
3) Evaluating cellular survival and outgrowth from bioprinted constructs laden with
microôÙsectioned tissue.

2.1
The Urogenital System
The upper urinary tract comprise the two kidneys and ureters. The main functions
of the kidney include filtration of blood, elimination of waste products and reguôÙ
lation of ionôÙtransport, blood buffers and blood pressure. The ureters serve as
tubular conduits for conveying urine from the kidneys to the bladder by peristalôÙ
sis. The lower urinary tract comprise the urinary bladder and urethra. The urinary
bladder is a hollow organ that serves as a reservoir for the continuously produced
urineëƒ enabling storage of urine and controlled micturition through muscular conôÙ
traction of the detrusor muscle and relaxation of the external urethral sphincter.
The urethra is a tubular conduit that allows for urine flow from the bladder and out
[9].

2) Muscularis and 3) Adventitia/Serosa. The mucosal layer comprise a lamina

lamina propria comprise mainly extracellular matrix with fibroblast, sensory nerve
endings and fine vasculature. The muscularis propria includes three distinct layôÙ
ersëƒ an inner longitudinal layer, a middle circular layer and an outer longitudinal
layer. The adventitia/serosa includes extracellular matrix, connective tissue with

2.1.2
Urothelium Histology and Cell Morphology
The bladder urothelium can be divided into three layers based on the type of cells
present: 1) Apical cell layer 2) Intermediate cell layer and 3) Basal cell layer. The
apical cell layer comprise Umbrella cellsëƒ large hexagonôÙshaped transitional epôÙ
ithelial cells with diameters ranging between 25ôÙ250 ö¥m depending on the degree
of distension of the urothelium. Umbrella cells are interconnected by tight juncôÙ
tions, through which the impermeable barrier function of the urothelium is estabôÙ
lished [9]. Umbrella cells can be mononucleate or binucleate, with large rounded
nuclei of diameter sizes 9ôÙ12 ö¥m. The basal cell layer comprise smaller, cuboidal
cells of diameter sizes around 10ö¥m with small condensed nuclei. Intermediate
cells display sizes that range between that of umbrella and basal cells. IntermeôÙ
diate and basal cells are mononucleate [10]. The urothelium cell morphology of
the urethra is slightly different from that of the urinary bladderëƒ here the urothelium
exists as stratified columnar epithelium [9].
2.1.3
Congenital Urogenital Conditions and Current
Therapeutic Approaches
With a prevalence of more than 18 per 10,000 births in Europe, hypospadias is one
of the most frequent congenital urogenital conditions in newborn males. HypospaôÙ
dias manifests as a displaced urethral opening on the ventral part of the penis, due

various degrees of severityëƒ complex cases are associated with urethral openings

2.3
Rapid Mechanical Dissociation of Primary
Bladder Tissue
Tissue dissociation is an important part of tissueôÙengineering. In addition to manôÙ
ual dissection, more automated processes exists such as gentle enzymatic disôÙ
aggregation and rapid mechanical dissociation. Previous studies comparing enôÙ
zymatic disaggregation and mechanical dissociation, have favored mechanical
dissociation in regards to cellular preservation, orientation and function as well
as processing time [13, 14]. In 2022, M. Montanari et al. [13] conducted a study
on tissue dissociation of various organ sources from ratsëƒ comparing enzymatic
disaggregation by trypsin solution to rapid mechanical dissociation though auôÙ

flow cytometrym to evaluate cell functionalities post tissue dissociation. Here it
was found, that the rapid mechanical cutting system demonstrated improved cell
preservation of key cellular organelles such as mitochondria and lysosomes [13].
In addition to better preservation of cell functionalities, mechanical dissociation
can bring down processing time significantlyëƒ from days to minutes scale [14].
Thus, rapid mechanical dissociation methods combines automatization with genôÙ
tle tissue processing and high throughput.

Alginate Microparticle Support Matrix
Special requirements must be met by a supportive material matrix in 3D embedôÙ

Confluency % Analysis
The Incucyte AIôÙbased image analysis system includes cellular confluency (%)
analysis. Confluency % is defined as the percent of area, of the bottom of a well,
that is covered by tissue fragments and adherent cells. Average well confluency
% is a quantitative measure of the mean confluency inside a wellëƒ it is calculated
in pixels from 49 microscopy images inside each well. This type of analysis is an
efficient tool for estimating the degree of cell proliferation and expansion in tissue
culture over time. The confluency % analysis was calibrated manually through
application of customized filters. On day 0ôÙ4 filters were implemented to exclude
red blood cells from confluency % measurements. These included a min. area
of 160 ôçm2 and a eccentricity between 0.3 to 1. From day 4 and beyond, filtering
was implemented by a min. area of 315 ôçm2 to include growing urothelial cells
and exclude smaller dead tissue fragments. Confluency % growth curves and
statistical plots and tests were made in GraphPad Prism 10 software.

General Discussion
The first aim of the thesis focused on establishing a general procedure for rapid
grinding of primary bladder tissue. Three major inôÙvitro cell culture studies were
conducted: 1) Grinding time study 2) Grinding unit cell spacing study and 3) CenôÙ
trifugation time study. Two key metrics were investigated and compared for all
conditions: % confluency and tissue fragment size distributions. These variables
were investigated to determine which conditions should be used to achieve optiôÙ
mal cellular survival and expansion post mechanical dissociation.
In the grinding time study two conditions were compared: 60 seconds and 120
seconds (for three technical replicates). The statistical unpaired tôÙtest of confluôÙ
ency % found no significant difference in the ability of the two grinding times to
achieve high confluency after one week of cell culture. However, confluency meaôÙ
surements from a two week period showed that the 60 seconds grinding condition
generally displayed higher confluency % when compared to 120 seconds grindôÙ
ing. The tissue fragment analysis found that both grinding conditions resulted in
quite homogeneous size distributions in the range of 14ôÙ113 ö¥m cell diameters.
However, the 120 seconds grinding protocol appeared slightly more consistent in
the production of evenôÙsized fragments. These findings suggest that both grindôÙ
ing time conditions are appropriate to use in mechanical dissociation of primary
bladder tissue. In the context of selecting the optimal grinding time, it is relevant to
choose the most efficient grinding timeëƒ by using the 60 seconds grinding time, the
total duration of the mechanical dissociation procedure can be shortened. Time
optimization is especially important in a clinical setting, where the patient is lying
on the operating table.
In the grinding unit cell spacing study two conditions were compared: 100ö¥m and
180ö¥m grinding unit cell spacing (for three technical replicates). The statistical
unpaired tôÙtest of confluency % found that the 100ö¥m cell spacing condition disôÙ
played significantly higher confluency than the 180 ö¥m cell spacing condition after
one week of cell culture. Furthermore, the 180 ö¥m cell spacing condition displayed
pronounced variability in confluency % between replicates from day 7 to 14 of cell
culture. These findings suggest that the 180ö¥m cell spacing condition is suboptiôÙ
mal in achieving cellular viability and proliferation inôÙvitro when compared to the
100ö¥m cell spacing condition. This did not align with our initial expectation that
large cell aggregates would display better survival than small ones. However, it
had still not been confirmed that the 180ö¥m cell spacing condition indeed did proôÙ
duce larger tissue fragments. A tissue fragment size analysis was carried out to
investigate if the 180ö¥m cell spacing condition resulted in the production of larger
tissue fragments. The result of the analysis suggests that the 180ö¥m grinding
unit cell spacing does not produce larger tissue fragments, when compared to
the 100ö¥m grinding unit cell spacing. This finding did not conform to our initial
hypothesis that increased grinding unit cell spacing produces larger tissue fragôÙ
ments. The tissue fragment size analysis was performed from microscopy data
obtained within a few hours after mechanical dissociation (Day 0). The images
Bioprint of TissueôÙladen Tubular Constructs
47
==PAGE:55==
and image analysis were acquired before media change, so it is not possible that
large tissue fragments have been washed away with the media at this point. One
could speculate if smaller cell aggregates were more likely to adhere to the botôÙ
tom of the well already. In that case, any disturbance of the well plate during
handling before microscopy imaging could likely lead to swirling of detached large
tissue fragments from the bottom of the well where imaging takes place. One
could also speculate that the presence of small clumps of red blood cells could
affect the tissue fragment size analysis at this early point in timeëƒ it is possible that
centrifugation of the sample could lead to clumping of red blood cells that might
compromise the size distribution of a sample with large amounts of blood cells.
However, it is not expected that erythrocytes stay clumped together for several
hours after centrifugation. Overall these findings suggest that a 100ö¥m grinding
unit cell spacing is optimal for achieving cellular survival and expansion post meôÙ
chanical dissociation.
In the centrifugation time study, a 5 minute control was compared to four centrifuôÙ
gation time conditions: 4, 3, 2 and 1 minute. The ordinary oneôÙway ANOVA staôÙ
tistical test of confluency % found no significant difference between the 5 minute
control and the four other centrifugation time conditions after one week of cell culôÙ
ture. However, confluency measurements from a two week period showed that
the 5 minute control condition generally displayed higher and more consistent conôÙ
fluency % when compared to the four other time conditions. The tissue fragment
analysis showed a clear pattern of a more homogeneous tissue fragment size disôÙ
tribution as centrifugation time increased from 1 to 5 minutes. Altogether, these
findings indicate that a 5ôÙminute centrifugation time is optimal to achieve cellular
viability and proliferation post mechanical dissociation.
General improvements can be applied to all three cell culture studies. For a more
accurate comparison of confluency % between conditions, the source of the bladôÙ
der tissue should be kept constant across conditionsëƒ by eliminating bladder speôÙ
cific causes of variations between conditions, a more exact examination of grindôÙ
ing times and their effect on cellular expansion can be conducted. It is important
that the mechanical dissociation protocol can be applied widely to all bladdersëƒ
not just biologically different pig bladders, but also human bladders in a clinical
setting. Progress can be made in terms of investigating if the grinding protocols
produce consistent cellular proliferation for biologically different bladder tissues.
It is relevant to include more biological replicates of both grinding time conditions.
Advances were made during this master thesis project to make more replicates,
however, on multiple occasions the produced samples were subjected to infecôÙ
tions. Another important source of error present in all three cell culture studies is
the unknown size of the extracted cell pellet that is resuspended. Even though
all samples were resuspended to create a 90% seeding density, the cell pellet of
each sample was not weighed and could potentially be of different sizes. This
was taken into consideration in later experimentsëƒ cell pellet weighing was impleôÙ
mented and accurate cell suspension concentration was incorporated as a part of
the standard mechanical dissociation procedure.

Conclusion
This thesis revolved around various aspects of 3D embedded bioprinting of tissueôÙ
laden constructs in tissue engineering.
First, a general procedure for rapid grinding of primary bladder tissue was esôÙ
tablished and optimized. In this regard, three comprehensive inôÙvitro cell culture
studies were conducted. From the grinding time study it was found that grindôÙ
ing durations of 60 seconds and 120 seconds achieved similar levels of cellular
survival and expansion in culture post mechanical dissociation. Thus, it was conôÙ
cluded that the grinding duration of 60 seconds was the most efficient protocol of
the two. From grinding unit cell spacing study it was found that a grinding unit cell
spacing of 100ö¥m was superior to 180ö¥m in achieving high cellular viability and
proliferation of inôÙvitro culture of primary bladder cells. Furthermore, our findings
suggest that the application of a 180ö¥m grinding unit cell spacing does not produce
larger tissue fragment sizes from rapid grinding of primary bladder tissue. From
the centrifugation time study it was found that the 5ôÙminute centrifugation duraôÙ
tion achieved greater and more consistent cell survival and expansion in inôÙvitro
culture of mechanically dissociated primary bladder tissue. Future work should
focus on including more technical replicates to increase the statistical power of
the mechanical dissociation studies.
This thesis also investigated the development of bioinks for 3D embedded bioprintôÙ
ing compatible with suturing and cellular survival. Various gelatinôÙbased bioink
compositions were formulated and cultured over a two week period. UnfortuôÙ
nately, except for one sample, it was not possible to complete histological proôÙ
cessing of the samples in time for this report. The one completed sample was
investigated histologically and the findings suggested viable cells in the bioink afôÙ
ter two weeks of culture. This finding shows promise that some of the produced
bioinks are compatible with cellular survival. Future work should prioritize the
completion of sample processing to allow for histological investigation and evalôÙ
uation of cellular viability when encapsulated in various bioinks. Tough gelatinôÙ
based bioinks were investigated to evaluate if the inclusion of cNFC fibers could
improve suturability of the bioinks. Here, it was found that the addition of cNFC
fibers improved the suturability of the inks. More replicates are to be conducted
in the future to confirm these findings. It is also suggested to continue to explore
the mechanical properties of other fiber concentrations.
A simple square twoôÙlayered construct was developed and the print fidelity opôÙ
timized. Finally, succesful 3D embedded bioprinting of tissueôÙladen twoôÙlayered
constructs was carried outëƒ the tissueôÙengineered constructs were cultured over a
twoôÙweek period. The samples are being processed for histological investigation
to be compared and evaluated in the near future.
