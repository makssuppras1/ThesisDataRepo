Abstract  
In pavement management a Falling Weight Deflectometer (FWD) is one of the preferable tools in 
determining the current state of a pavement. An FWD drops a weight on a spring-buffer-system, which 
transforms the kinetic force into a dynamic load on the pavement. The deflection is measured at different 
radial distances, which can be used in the analysis of the pavement conditions.  
The deflections are utilized in backcalculation of the E-moduli of the different layers in the pavement. The 
backcalculation is based on a set of simplification, which makes the process manageable and still produces 
realistic results. Neglecting some simplifications e.g. nonlinearity of the subgrade, can prove unfavorable 
for the backcalculated moduli.  
During this thesis two sites have been investigated, where one site was observed to behave nonlinear for 
the subgrade, while the other was observed to behave linearly elastic. These two sites were tested over 
three times at different weather conditions. Both of these sites were constructed with embedded sensors. 
The presence of embedded sensors allows for either a validation with the embedded sensors or inclusion in 
the backcalculation process for increased reliability.  
In the investigation of these sites, a code has been developed, which is able to perform a linear 
backcalculation on the deflections measured. This code is validated and analyzed on both synthetic and real 
problems as to ensure its reliability. The backcalculated E-moduli was compared with the commercial 
program ELMOD and validated with the embedded sensors.  
The sensors were also used as inclusion in the backcalculation process, where it was concluded that only a 
limited increased reliability was observed for the pavement behaving linearly.  
The nonlinearity of both sites was tested, and the material constants from the nonlinear models of Witczak 
& Uzan, Witczak-MEPDG and PRIMAX-MEPDG were found. The material constants were not used in the 
implementation of nonlinear treatment of the subgrade as an unfound error is expected to have occurred 
in the regression of these material coefficients.  
As a nonlinear model was not developed, a description explaining how this was to be done in possible was 
included instead. This description takes into consideration the backcalculation process needed to 
successfully implement nonlinearity in a model, and in takes into consideration how the nonlinearity is to 
be calculated based on the assumptions of the linear model developed.  
To follow up on this, more investigations on the inclusion on embedded sensors need to be performed. A 
deeper investigation on the effect of nonlinearity is suggested, as it was not properly solved within this 
thesis.

1 Introduction 
1.1 General background 
One of the earliest examples of roads is from the Roman Empire. These roads were primarily used by the 
military for an increased effect of movement for troops. When not used by the Roman army, common 
people could use these roads as a way of transportation of goods and people between cities. Today, many 
years later, the prime user of the road is the common people instead of the military, making the objective 
of the roads to connect specific activities such as residential areas, industrial areas, retail and public areas. 
The connection of all these activities is called infrastructure.  
With a growing traffic, the demands of the infrastructure are ever increasing. Therefor pavement is 
considered a great asset for mobility. Lack of maintenance has consequences for the functionality of the 
road. These result in a reduction of the value of the road network, as the non-maintained pavement loses 
value in national economy. Value can be lost in terms of reduced effectiveness (e.g. reduced traffic speed) 
of the road hence the user wastes time and loss of capital value. To minimize this loss of value, a structural 
survey is crucial.  
Throughout time a lot of roads have been constructed to create the infrastructure known today. As the 
roads within the network are in operation, they will unavoidably deteriorate due to exposure of traffic. At 
some point in the life of the pavement, a choice must be made, whether to maintain the pavement, 
demolish and rebuild the pavement or simply leave it as it is. Before making this decision, the state of the 
pavement must be determined, to back up the decision. The current state of the pavement is determined 
through structural survey. The structural survey determines the state of the road network or part of the 
network, making engineers able to decide on what sections a limited budget should be prioritized.  
Within structural survey, non-destructive testing (NDT) is a highly used means of determining pavement 
properties by measuring either the deflection basin or wave propagation, without damaging the pavement 
structure. An alternative to NDT could be destructive testing, where pavement properties would be 
determined by for instance drilling out cores of the asphalt pavement, and testing them at a lab. Though, 
the NDT is though more preferable, due to its non-destructive abilities for obvious reasons. Furthermore, 
NDT has the advantage of the entire testing being performed in situ.  
Within NDT, several measurement devices have been developed, such as the Benkelman beam (BB), falling 
weight deflectometer (FWD), lacroix deflectograph (LD), portable pavement analyser (PSPA) etc. 
These measurement tools work in a similar way, despite their different methods (Goel & Das, 2008). They 
stimulate the asphalt pavement, by applying a load, to get a response. This loading is modeled with 
assumed pavement properties to create a forward solution and thereby archive a modeled response. If the 
difference between modeled and measured response is low, this is an indication of the right properties 
being assumed. It should be noted that the accuracy of the forward solution is based on the assumptions 
within the model. A simple model would include assumptions such as linear elastic, isotropic and 
homogeneous materials. The model can then be improved by dropping these simplifications to get a more 
accurate response corresponding better to the actual response.  
The future of NDT may include embedded sensors in all pavements, hence enabling more accurate testing 
of structural conditions, and the possibility to monitor conditions over time. By including embedded 
sensors in the roads, knowing the layer thicknesses, boundary conditions and the loading applied upon the 
sensors, the inverse problem can be solved and a forward solution created, to make the structural survey 
==PAGE:15==
  
 
15/105 
more effective. The load could either be an NDT device or perhaps a slowly moving vehicle with a 
predetermined weight. Currently this is not possible hence the NDT devices are used instead. One of the 
most commonly NDT devices used is the falling weigh deflectometer.  
A FWD is the most common device type used within NDT of pavements. The FWD exists in several different 
shapes and sizes, but the basics are the same. A general principle of the FWD is sketched below on Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1 – sketch of FWD, showing load cell, geophones, falling mass and deflection basin (Goel & Das, 2008). 
A FWD applies an impulse load to the pavement, while also recording the deflection of the pavement in 
different radial distances from the applied impulse load. The impulse load is generated by dropping a 
weight from a height onto a spring-buffer system that transfers the kinetic load to the pavement as a pulse 
load. The recordings are performed with geophones attached to the device also dropping the load. The 
timespan of which the readings are recorded is approximately 30 𝑚𝑠. Within this time period, a single (and 
the first) deflection basin is recorded. By connecting all components (sensors and load application) to a 
steel rod, constant distances are ensured. A load cell attached to the transfer plate records the magnitude 
of the load drop. 
The FWD was initially introduced in 1960’s France. The FWD has gained acceptance through time and is 
now widely used due to its effectiveness, in terms of both speed and accuracy of measurements (Brown, 
1962). The load simulated by a FWD is somewhat proportional to the loading of a moving vehicle. The 
deflections recorded are used through calculations to estimate the elastic moduli of the pavement layers, if 
the height of each layer is well-defined (Karadelis, 2000). 
Within the category of FWD’s there are more than one type of equipment besides the standard FWD. The 
Light FWD (LFWD) is a handheld device with a smaller impact load, and only able to measure at the point of 
impact. The LFWD is suitable for testing subgrade and base. Another type is the Heavy weight 
deflectometer (HWD) that works by the same principles as the standard FWD, but has the ability to 
simulate high load vehicles such as airplanes.  
==PAGE:16==
  
 
16/105 
The major drawback of the FWD is the stop-and-go procedure, where the equipment has to be set up at all 
measuring points. This procedure might lead to another disadvantage, which is the closing of lanes during 
survey. Alternatively, moving load devices can be used, if a fast structural survey is preferred. On the 
positive side, the FWD is known to produce results of high repeatability and reproducibility (Zaghloul, 
Ahmed, Swan, Jumikis, & Vitillo, 2005). These abilities make the FWD highly usable to provide data for 
determination of pavement properties.   
1.2 Research Aim 
This M.Sc. thesis involves analysis of test data developed through non-destructive testing. The testing will 
be performed in collaboration with Sweco Danmark A/S on two predetermined sites augmented by 
embedded sensors with FWD. The analysis of FWD measurements allows for inference of in situ properties. 
Backcalculation with data from embedded sensors should yield increased reliability, and hence enable a 
more accurate forward solution.  
The objective of this thesis is to determine the integrated properties of a material not yet tested in local 
conditions. The material TECHNOpor® is used at one of the two predetermined sites, with the only 
difference being the usage of this material against a regular subgrade. The general purpose of the thesis 
will be to create a finite element or linear elastic code solving the backcalculation problem. The finite 
element or linear elastic code will be developed in such a way, that it will be usable in future commercial or 
research projects.  
Furthermore the effect of non-linearity at different radial distances is investigated, based on the theories of 
Witczak-Uzan and Witczak-MEPDG.  
1.3 Methodology  
In order to meet the requirements of the research objective, a stepwise schedule has been derived for the 
project. The plan covers the project from Literature review to the hand-in of the project. In general the plan 
concerns the rest of the thesis from this point. The schedule is divided into the following steps: 
1 
Literature review of non-destructive testing for the determination of in situ properties and 
specifically the usage of falling weight deflectometer for determination of these properties. 
2 
Literature review of backcalculation of in situ properties including non-linearity. 
3 
Literature review concerning usage of embedded sensors. 
4 
Review all information concerning the test site and test equipment, including layer thickness, 
sensor location, pre-built-in material properties, FWD-equipment and sensor condition.  
5 
Complete non-destructive testing of pavement with falling weight deflectometer at two

2.1 Pavement structure 
A pavement is a structure which primary purpose is to distribute the applied traffic load down to the 
subgrade, though this is not the only purpose of the construction. The pavement must also protect the 
underlying soil from frost, as frost can have devastating effects on a pavement structure. The pavement 
consists of several layers, of which some are asphalt wearing layer, asphalt bound base layer, unbound 
base, subbase and subgrade, listed from top to bottom of the structure. Each layer has a function, which 
combined make a full pavement. A general build of a pavement is seen in Figure 2 below.  
 
Figure 2 – Principal sketch of pavement construction 
The subgrade is considered the foundation of the pavement, and consists of the natural soil on the site, 
which has been stripped. If the soil is not considered suitable as a foundation it can be replaced with a soil 
with higher strength or can be treated with a mineral to increase its strength.  
The subbase is the layer placed directly above the subgrade and it is used to ensure that water can be 
drained, and it works as a capillary breaking layer, hence it does not reduce the bearing part of the 
pavement.  
The base layer is an unbound layer that primarily consists of mainly gravel or crushed rock depending on 
availability. In Denmark gravel is a common material and is therefore often used. The main function of this 
layer is to distribute the load from the bound materials above to the subbase and subgrade below. If this 
layer is thicker than 200-250 𝑚𝑚 it is laid out over several times to ensure full compaction of the depth of 
the layer. This is also indicated on Figure 2 with the dashed lines within the base layer. However, in most 
cases however, the layer will be no thicker than 250 𝑚𝑚.  
==PAGE:20==
  
 
20/105 
The asphalt base layer is a bound base layer, which main function, as the base layer, is to distribute the 
load, and prevent permanent deformations at the pavement surface. If needed, an extra layer is put on top 
of the asphalt base layer, called binding layer, which ensures a better connection between the bound 
layers. Furthermore high shear stress is often found at the depth of the binding layer. 
The wearing layer is the top layer in the pavement structure. This layer can have various abilities such as 
noise reduction, reduction of light reflection or draining effect. The prime objective of the wearing layer is 
to protect the rest of the pavement from the weather, distribute the loading from vehicles, provide 
sufficient skid resistance and provide a smooth and functional surface. The wearing layer is a bound layer, 
hence it contains bitumen, which increases the stiffness of the material. This layer is typically no more than 
40 𝑚𝑚 in thickness.  
The pavement is expected to transfer the loads down to the subgrade without it inflicting permanent 
deformations to either the pavement or the subgrade. Each layer is subjected to critical stresses and strains 
at different points, and these points are often used to determine the maximum allowed impact on the 
pavement. The critical reaction in the asphalt layer is the horizontal strain in the bottom of that layer. The 
critical reaction in unbound layers below the asphalt layers is the vertical stress at the top of the layer. 
These critical reactions are schematically shown in Figure 3. The critical reactions are especially interesting 
in terms of fatigue, because over time the actual response will approach the critical reactions, which hence 
determines the life of the structure.  
 
Figure 3 – Critical responses in terms of stresses and strains in the top layers of a pavement build, where the critical reaction are 
vertical stresses on all layers and horizontal strain in the bottom of the bound layer 
These reactions are affected by a number of parameters, such as the magnitude of loading, layer thickness, 
material properties, temperature conditions, degree of saturation, pavement condition etc. All these 
parameters affect how the pavement behaves, but does not necessarily impact the behavior in equal 
magnitude.

2.2 Usage of Embedded sensors 
As computer technology has progressed, microelectronic measurement equipment has been developed to 
be used as embedded sensors. The usage of embedded sensors have enabled pavement engineers to 
monitor the stresses, pressure, temperature, moist content, velocity and deflection at the point of the 
sensor. Combining monitored measurements within the soil with non-destructive testing or destructive 
testing enables a fair prediction of the pavement performance. The usage of sensors can help confirm or 
determine if the pavement performance is in accordance with design, if taken into use at an early stage.  
Embedded sensors are built in the pavement at different strategic locations depending on what the 
engineers wish to monitor. When monitoring strains, the places of interest for the sensors to be placed 
==PAGE:22==
  
 
22/105 
could be at the bottom of the asphalt concrete structure, where the horizontal tensile strain is expected to 
be highest, as shown in Figure 3. Horizontal strain is measured using with asphalt strain gauge, which 
measures the electrical resistance of wires as these wires expand due to loading. Below this point, vertical 
stresses are considered important to monitor. Earth pressure cells can measure vertical stress. At these 
positions it is also relevant to measure the deflection of the soil at that specific point. Geophones are highly 
usable in terms of measuring deflection. The points of measurement are mostly relevant below the wheel 
path, where the actual loading occurs.  
A geophone is a cheap, simple and robust measurement device that monitors movement. The geophone is 
known for its high performance in terms of reduced electrical noise, linearity, and dynamic range in 
comparison to accelerometers. A geophone is a motion transducer and does not require power supply to 
work. The problem with geophones in relation to accelerometers is however that the geophones measure 
velocity instead of acceleration as the accelerometers do. This though can be an advantage considering that 
the velocity signal only has to be integrated once to get deflection while acceleration has to be integrated 
twice, which reduces uncertainties in analysis of deflections (Brincker, Bolton, Brandt, Allé, & M, 2011; 
Velarde et al., 2017). 
However, there are a few drawbacks to the usage of embedded sensors. First of all they can be difficult to 
install during construction, and they are not guaranteed to work properly after construction of the 
pavement, even with this aspect taken into consideration.  
Another disadvantage is that a sensor embedded in a pavement replaces the original material and can 
hence disturb the stress distribution within this layer as the sensors stress-deformation abilities differ 
significantly from those of the soil in which it is installed. Only a sensor with the exact same in situ stress-
deformation properties over time as the soil will nullify this drawback (Peattie & Sparrow, 1958). That 
scenario is however highly unlikely. Peattie & Sparrow concluded that the diversity of results due to a 
sensor was less than 3 percent with the type and size of the sensor used. The research was performed with 
an earth pressure cell. This indicates that embedded sensors still provide reasonable results despite the 
change in material properties.  
Data synchronization of embedded sensors can prove difficult, if the sensors are not linked to the 
equipment forcing the response. Data synchronization is not a problem when working with peak values, 
but with the usage of time series, time synchronization is considered more important. During a research on 
the used of embedded sensors to evaluate the performance of deflection measurement devices by Velarde 
et al, the synchronization was performed with an arbitrary systems GPS clock with all sensors, ensuring that 
the timestamp would correlate with each other (Velarde et al., 2017).  
In terms of modeling, embedded sensors can be included in the backcalculation process. Doing so will 
increase the accuracy of the backcalculated moduli. By inclusion of the embedded sensors the number of 
variables in the backcalculation is increased, which theoretically should yield a higher accuracy of the 
backcalculated E-moduli.   
An alternative is to exclude the embedded sensors from the backcalculation and instead used them as a 
validation of the backcalculation. A validation cannot be performed with data that has been used in the 
backcalculation, because data used in a validation has to be independent of results to be validated. By 
using the embedded sensors in a validation, the overall accuracy of the backcalculated E-moduli is lowered, 
but the correctness of the moduli is validated.  
==PAGE:23==
  
 
23/105 
Both of these two ways of using data from embedded sensors have their pros and con, though it is up to 
the individual what is preferred of the two options. 
2.3 Falling weight deflectometer 
The FWD equipment can be mounted on either a trailer, be truck mounted or be enclosed in a van. The 
FWD as described earlier applies an impulse load that can correspond to a vehicle moving at approximately 
50 𝑘𝑚/ℎ. The impulse load is applied through the spring-buffer system on a loading plate with a standard 
radius of 150 𝑚𝑚, though other sizes can be used under special conditions. The plate is in contact with the 
pavement through a rubber layer in between. A seeding drop before start of measurement ensures full 
contact. The load plate is made of steel with a rubber material around it, to fully optimize the contact area 
and load distribution, and it is furthermore designed to allow for deflection measurement at the center of 
it.  
 
Figure 5 - Deflection basin in relation to induced load and FWD sensors 
The impulse load affects the pavement and creates a deflection basin as schematically shown in Figure 5. 
Figure 4 shows the deflection basin introduced by a load, and where the individual sensors measure the 
deflection. It should be noted that the pavement build and size of the deflection basin are not in equal 
scale. If it were to be in scale, the deflection basin would not be visible in this drawing. Deflections are 
usually measured in 𝜇𝑚.  
The impulse load is applied by a weight drop mechanism, which drops a mass. The mass is lifted up to the 
drop point by a hydraulic or mechanic system along a guiding rod, which ensures the positioning of the 
mass according to the drop point. The mass is dropped on cone or half-sphere-shaped rubber buffers. 
These rubber buffers are connected to the transfer plate below it. The buffers have several functions. First 
of all they help the distribution of the force applied by the mass and protects the transfer plate below, but 
the buffers are also there to determine the pulse time inflicted.  
The pavement deflection is typically measured by geophones (or other motion transducers) at different 
radial distances typically varying from 0 𝑚𝑚 to 2400 𝑚𝑚 from the center of plate. The geophone with a 
radial distance of 0 𝑚𝑚 is connected to the load plate. The number of geophones varies with each 
equipment, but is typically within the rage of 9 to 15 geophones, including the geophone at a radial 
distance of 0 𝑚𝑚. Not all of the geophones are necessarily placed in the same side of the loading plate. A 
few geophones can be placed opposite the others, making the FWD able to test the load transferring effect 
of a joint. This is mostly relevant when testing concrete pavements. The geophones are attached to a steel 
==PAGE:24==
  
 
24/105 
rod, which is lowered down to the pavement at the same time as the load plate, as the measurements are 
to be recorded. The operation of the beam and the dropping of the weight is done automatically through a 
computer in the vehicle, hence enabling a test without the operator having to get out of the vehicle.  
A geophone sensor is a motion sensor, which translates a movement into an electrical signal. The 
magnitude of the electrical signal determines the magnitude of the movement, which is equal to the 
deformation. To ensure the correct conversion to deflection, all geophones are calibrated regularly.   
Pavement deflection is not the only recordings made by the FWD. The data-file created during 
measurement also collects force of the drop, pressure from load plate and the recorded pulse time. The 
force of the drop can be adjusted by changing the drop height and the weight that is dropped. The typical 
load range is 7 𝑘𝑁 to 300 𝑘𝑁, depending on the FWD model. Some FWD models are restricted to a 
maximum load lower than the here indicated. The higher loading models can be used to simulate airplanes 
as well as normal traffic. An example of one of these models is the PRIMAX 3500 - HWD, which is able to 
provide these higher loadings and thus simulate a load from aircrafts. A load cell is responsible for 
recording the magnitude of the drop.  
Both air temperature and pavement surface temperature are automatically recorded at the time of impact 
in the data-file. During the FWD measurements, the onsite engineer records the temperature in the asphalt 
concrete physically. The temperature is recorded by drilling a small hole in the pavement, in which a liquid 
is poured. The temperature of the liquid is measured after some time. The asphalt concrete temperature is 
used to correct the recordings into standard conditions, which are 25 oC. A GPS tracking system records the 
coordinates of the point of impact, both longitudinal, latitudinal and altitude coordinates are included in 
the GPS tracking, but this tracking system is not very precise. The tracking is usable down to a few meters, 
hence making it usable for commercial usage where each measurement point can be 100 𝑚 from the 
previous. For research purposes, the distance between measurement positions can be very low in 
comparison, making the GPS system unusable.  
Previously mentioned was the FWD’s well known abilities to produce results of high accuracy, repeatability 
and reproducibility (Zaghloul et al., 2005), but the FWD is not useable under all conditions. If the asphalt 
concrete layer is very thin (say less than 75 mm), the FWD measurements are not to be fully relied upon 
due to the geometry of the loading and measurement system. In general it can be said that the moduli of 
thin layers are difficult to determine by FWD measurements (Per Ullidtz & Coetzee, 1995).  
The FWD is known for its repeatability and reproducibility using a calibrated FWD at certain conditions at a 
certain pavement section, but the measured deflection basin may not be the same if a different calibrated 
FWD is used, even from the same producer. One of the problems with using different FWD’s is that the 
buffer-system may not be equal. The change in the buffer-system alone can give a variation of up to 10% 
depending on the pavement structure. The repeatability and reproducibility is generally significantly higher 
if the equipment used is calibrated, but it is recommended not to use different equipment on the same 
site, and if there is, a correlation analysis between the equipment is highly recommended (Zaghloul et al., 
2005).  
The usage of FWD provides good data for determining the pavement properties. The properties are not 
necessarily completely true based on the FWD alone. By including another NDT evaluation technique in the 
analysis, the accuracy of the determined properties may be enhanced. Furthermore, by using a NDT 
method that work at a different magnitude wavelength than FWD, a more accurate and complete 
pavement profile can be found (Goel & Das, 2008).

2.4 Backcalculation  
Obtaining data through NDT is only the first step towards determining the pavement properties. The data is 
used in make a rehabilitation design, where the different properties of the pavement layers are taken into 
consideration in the backcalculation process. The backcalculation determines the elastic properties of the 
individual layers. In earlier days a mechanistic-empirical design procedure was used in the rehabilitation 
design, which based on the input (deflection at deferent predetermined spots, the pavement structure and 
expected traffic) will provide the additional overlay pavement thickness. The additional thickness is needed 
to gain a predetermined strength that can withstand the traffic. This procedure can still be used, but does 
not take into account the current properties of the individual layers as the backcalculation does. This 
procedure is analyzing the pavement structure as a whole in terms of allowable deflection. The procedure if 
often fast compared to backcalculation and can possibly be done using charts. The analysis using 
backcalculation is more resource consuming in terms of both time and computational power, but yields an 
understanding of the state of the individual layers. In more recent years computational power is no longer 
such big a factor as previously and can hence be neglected.  
There are different ways to perform the Backcalculation to solve the inverse problem depending on the 
assumptions on which the Backcalculation is performed, and the data on which it is performed on i.e. time 
dependent or static (Uzan, 1994). It is not uncommon to only use the peak load and peak deflection in the 
analysis to determine the properties, by utilizing the theory of linear elasticity (Matsui, Nishizawa, & Kikuta, 
n.d.). There has also been investigations of the comparison between static and dynamic Backcalculation 
procedures, where is was indicated that a dynamic backcalculation yields results of a slightly higher 
accuracy, but is also more resource consuming than a static analysis (Maina, Steyn, van Wyk, & le Roux, 
2013). 
Many software programs have been created able to handle backcalculation, such as ELMOD, MODCOMP, 
RoSy, MODULUS, EVERCALC, PADAL, ANSYS, ABACUS etc. Each of these software programs includes 
different assumptions and different calculation methods, and they are applicable under different 
circumstances, without necessarily deviating from other software programs very much. As computational 
power has increased, the yielded results from software programs have increased in fit, though not 
necessarily in the representation of the pavement response, but the models themselves have been 
improved as to fit the preset limitations and assumptions. These programs can roughly be divided into 
three categories in terms of calculation form. The theories behind these calculation forms are listed below 
(Per Ullidtz & Coetzee, 1995). 
- 
Layered elastic theory (LET) 
- 
Boussinesq & Odemark (BO) 
- 
Finite element method (FEM) 
A problem using backcalculation to derive modulus for the different layers is that not all layers have a 
specific modulus. The moduli of pavement layers are situational and change according to the current 
conditions e.g. temperature or compression, hence it is not fully possible to compare a backcalculated 
moduli to moduli achieved by other means. These situational moduli are primarily affected by the stress 
conditions. The stress condition however can be affected by loading time, temperature, moisture, bitumen 
content and more (Per Ullidtz & Coetzee, 1995). 
Different theories have been developed upon the determination of response of both elastic (Burmister, 
1945; Singh, 1986) and viscoelastic layers in a pavement (Ding & Shen, 1991),but the initial theory of 
isotropic LET was developed by Burmister with his general theory of stresses and displacements in layered 
==PAGE:26==
  
 
26/105 
systems (Burmister, 1945). In the theory, the stresses and displacement are determined through a series of 
differential equations based on the work of Love (Love, 1892).  
2.4.1 
Boussinesq & Odemark 
Boussinesq originally developed his theories back in 1885. These theories were only applicable for a one-
layered system, which is why Odemark developed a transformation formula in 1949, which is able to 
transform a multi-layered system into an equivalent one-layered system (Per Ullidtz, 1998).

Pavement that is modeled through Boussinesq is in reality anisotropic and inhomogeneous but the 
assumptions concerning these two points can still be considered reasonable, as the materials used are 
produced from controlled sources, and are adapted during construction, which contributes in the imitation 
of these assumptions. The only problem might be the subgrade, as it is highly affected by moisture in 
comparison to other layers, whether the subgrade material is native soil or replaced fill. The moduli of the 
subgrade may also be affected by depth or varying overburden pressure.  
A pavement material is considered isotropic if the material properties are the same in all directions in the 
material. In a 3-dimensional system if a material has similar properties along two directions, but different 
properties along the third, it is considered cross-anisotropic. This effect is normal within pavements, where 
the two directions in the horizontal plane behave somewhat similarly, while the vertical direction has 
different properties generally due to vertical compaction during construction and during usage. There have 
been investigations on the impact of cross-anisotropic in relation to FWD deflections that show a decreased 
surface deflection the higher ratio between vertical and horizontal modulus (Ahmed, Tarefder, & Islam, 
2013).  
2.4.1.2 
Odemark’s method of equivalent thickness 
Another of the limitations in terms of modeling with Boussinesq was the assumption of homogeneousness, 
which limited the theory to a single semi-infinite layer. To account for this problem, the method of 
equivalent thickness (MET) was developed by Odemark in 1949. Odemark’s MET enables the conversion of 
several layers into one equivalent homogenous layer. The alternation takes into account the stiffness, 
height and poisons ratio of the materials. Odemark’s MET basically transform the stiffness of the upper 
layer to an equivalent height with the properties of the layer lying below, hence fulfilling Boussinesq’s 
requirement of a homogeneous material. The principals of the transformation are shown below on Figure 
7.

The theory of Boussinesq and Odemark enables estimation of the E-modulus of the different layers in the 
pavement through solving the inverse problem. Solving the inverse problem with Boussinesq and Odemark 
is an iterative process as the result is known, but part of the cause unknown, where the result being the 
deflection and part of the cause being the layer properties. Through the iterative process, the difference in 
responses is minimized. As the differences are minimized, a model that is able to solve forward problem 
can be established (Uhl, 2007). 
In determination of the deflection in the top layer however, the properties of that layer would be used in 
Boussinesq’s equation. If the response in the second layer is needed, the whole structure is calculated as if 
having those properties. This principle goes for all lower layers.

2.4.3 
Non-linearity of subgrade 
Materials within the pavement are assumed to behave linear elastic according to Boussinesq, but in reality, 
these materials can behave visco-elastically or non-linearly elastic. Backcalculation of FWD data can result 
in unrealistic moduli of especially the subgrade. If the modulus of a layer varies with the distance from the 
load, then using a linear elastic backcalculation may yield incorrect moduli. The typical result would be an 
overestimation of the top layer and the subgrade, while an intermediate layer is underestimated (Per 
Ullidtz, 1998). Theoretical pavement response models are often simplified to linear elastic, but research has 
shown that modeling the subgrade non-linear can yield more realistic moduli (P Ullidtz, 2000).

(Horak, 1987), just as in the Jacksboro case.  
2.5 Optimization 
In order to optimize the calculated results, and to compare the accuracy of the modeled results, a common 
measuring unit must be used. The residual is defined as the difference between observed and predicted 
value, and is displayed in (Eq. 35). The residual is found for each individual measurement, and can be 
summarized in different ways according to what it is to be used for. In term of comparing deflection basins, 
RMS is usually the go-to comparison. The RMS displays the average difference between measured 
(observed) and calculated (predicted) results. Displaying the difference as the sum of squares residual (SSR) 
will result in the same optimization, but the SSR is not as comparable to the observed results as RMS is.  
The coefficient of determination is a tool used to determine the goodness of a fit. If this value is equal to 1, 
a perfect fit between the computed and observed values is seen. The coefficient of determination is 
especially useable in terms of regression, where most tested models are evaluated in this parameter.

PA, GAB, SG and BL are all Danish abbreviations for specific materials or material compositions. The two 
upper material compositions contain bitumen as a binder, while the subgrade material is unbound.  
PA is short for powder asphalt and is used as a wearing layer on sidewalks, bicycle lanes or small roads with 
limited and light traffic. PA includes very soft bitumen, which makes the load distributing properties 
limited, but is more resistant to cracks due to its flexibility. Soft bitumen also increases the asphalts self-
healing effect. As this layer does not properly distribute the pressure, the underlying layers must take the 
increased local pressure into consideration of this. A special ability of this wearing layer is its ability to 
compact from traffic load after the initial compaction and cooling.  
GAB I is an asphalt base layer that consists of stone material of a generally larger size in comparison to the 
wearing layer. The nominal max size of GAB I go up to 22 mm. The maximum stone size determines the 
height of the layer, which also goes the other way around. The bitumen in GAB I is usually hard. Hard 
bitumen has a high stiffness and a low penetration. The purpose of the base layer is to reduce and 
distribute the pressure from traffic loadings, as to protect the unbound material below, and is usually 
placed directly below the wearing layer.  
SG II is short for gravel type 2, and is an unbound base course, which means that there is no bitumen 
content in the layer. The thickness of the base layer is usually not higher than 200-220 mm, as it is 
necessary to construct it in two layers if it is higher. The nominal max size of SG II is usually up to 32 mm. 
Typically there is a requirement to the gravel of an E-modulus of 250-300 MPa. SG II and SG I consists of the 
same range of grain sizes, but SG I has a higher requirements for the size distribution for the finer grains 
and for the quality of the sand (Berg, 2004). 
BL is directly translatable to Subbase layer. BL consists of sand and gravel, mainly in the range 0,063 to 63 
mm, but no larger than 90 mm (Berg, 2004).

experiment as the deformation properties of TECHNOpor are determinable by measuring deflection.

3.6.1 
Noise in data  
Noise is unwanted and/or unexplained variation in a sample. The different disturbances within recordings 
can be placed in three different categories of noise for this particular. The first type of noise is a product of 
the variance in pavement construction. The second type of noise is variation measurement, for instance 
load magnitude, which is seen in Figure 15. The third and final noise contributor is environmental noise, 
which can be vehicles passing by at the time of recording.  
For the first noise type, which is regarding pavement construction, it is seen in Table 2, that there is a 
natural variation in layer thickness during construction caused by unevenness. Furthermore the difference 
in aggregate size may cause some local areas to achieve higher or lower compact ability in comparison to 
the average. It is well known that the compaction has an impact on the stiffness of the individual layers. 
During construction tests on both compaction and construction tolerances are performed.  
For the second noise type, which is regarding measurement variation, it is seen in Figure 15, that this 
variation in limited, but can still contribute to the variation in deflection to some degree. In the 
backcalculation process the difference in load magnitude is however countered, as the load magnitude in 
included as a variable in the equations. Hence, the variation in load magnitude can be seen as a variation in 
magnitude of the deflection basin, but not the shape. The varying magnitude of the load is but one of 
problems which might occur during measurement. Partial contact between loading plate and pavement 
might cause some deviation in measured deflection, even though this should be countered with the usage 
of initial drops. For the embedded geophones there is no guarantee that these are providing correct results 
in comparison to the surface geophones, despite the surface geophones being calibrated according to the 
embedded before usage. Regarding the embedded sensors, they might be affected by the change is soil 
properties around the geophones. If there is noise based on this, the noise will however be seen on all 
recordings, and will therefore result in different backcalculated moduli, but no further deviation in the 
measured deflection.  
The third noise type, the environmental noise, includes all the parameters that are uncontrollable, such as 
vehicles driving by during measurement, and causing an additional deflection. This deflection is estimated 
to be rather small in comparison to the FWD generated deflections, as the vehicles are bound to be a 
certain distance from the geophones, and the measured deflection contribution will therefor correspond to 
a FWD generated deflection on one of the mid to outer geophones, which is not a high contribution. 
Furthermore, the geophones are motion transducers, so the vehicle would have to pass the FWD setup at 
the exact time of measurement for the contribution to be included as the measuring time is approximately 
30 𝑚𝑠. This would also mean that it is unlikely for several consecutive measurements to be affected by 
this. In Figure 16 it is seen that the same variation occur during all three loadings, which makes traffic 
contribution highly unrealistic.  
Based on the above-mentioned suggestions of noise contributors, it is estimated that the majority of noise 
contribution origins from the first category, variation in pavement construction. The variation in load 
magnitude is not to be neglected, but it is not estimated that this is the main noise contributor based on 
the differences in magnitude between the three loading situations in Figure 16.

4 Validation of backcalculation theory 
This section describes which theories will be used in the model and what simplifications are made.  
To ensure the correctness of the theories they will be validated through comparison to a LET program. This 
comparison will be made on a fictive pavement construction with no relation to the pavement described in 
section 3. The theories compared with LET will mainly be BO, BOB and CFS.  
BO will be tested at all radial distances. BOB will also be tested at radial distances, as to se how it performs 
at 𝑟≠0, though it is not expected to have better alignment with ELLEA1 than BO. CFS will only be tested at 
𝑟= 0.  
As the theories are validated through the comparison with LET, the theories to be used in the model are 
described along with the limitations in the end of this section. It should be noted that all references to ‘the 
model’ refers to the models LBM and NBM, which will be developed in section 64

5 Creation and validation of linear model 
This section describes the creation of the linear backcalculation model (LBM). LBM is created based on the 
theories used described in section 4.1.3. The model will be able to determine the stiffness of the pavement 
layers based on FWD provided pavement response. 
As the model is created theory wise, it will be tested for what optimization algorithm is to be implemented.  
After implementation of the algorithm, the model will go through two stages of validation with synthetic 
data. First stage will be with plain synthetic data, while the second stage will include noise in the synthetic 
data. The noise used will be normal distributed.  
As the model has been validated on a fictive pavement with and without noise, the model will be compared 
to the ELMOD program in section 5.3. In this comparison the data achieved from the test sites, which is 
described in section 3.5, will be used.  
This section will end with a validation of the model using the embedded sensors, where the measured 
deflection will be compared to the deflection provided by the backcalculated E-moduli under the recorded 
loading.  
5.1 Creation of initial model 
LBM which will be created to describe the pavement response under a loading, and based of FWD provided 
data, with the purpose of determining the physical properties of the pavement structure, is based on the 
theories of Boussinesq and Odemark as described in section 2.4 with the inclusion of the closed form 
solution in the centerline. The models limitations are based on the limitations of the theory of Boussinesq, 
though slightly different as to include other theories included in the model such as CFS. Furthermore the 
non-linearity of the subgrade is to be implemented, though this is not implemented in this section. The 
limitations of the linear model are listed below, with limitations of the nonlinear is placed in brackets.

5.2.1 
Optimization of backcalculation 
In the backcalculation, the total deflection is found by summation of the compression of the individual 
layers. The compression of the individual layer is calculated by subtracting the deflection at the bottom of 
the layer from the deflection at the top of the layer. For the infinite layer, the deflection at the bottom, 
𝑧= ∞, is assumed to be equal to 0, and has therefor no need of being included because the deflection of 
the top of the layer is equal to the compression of that layer. The individual layers, which compression in 
summarized is displayed below in Figure 36.   
 
Figure 36 - Pavement with transformed properties displaying what properties are used in calculation of total deflection 
The total deflection is found at each radial distance, according to the radial distances of the surface 
geophones. The total deflections are compared to the measured deflection, and all of these comparisons 
are summarized, yielding SST. The summarized compared deflection can be minimized, and hence 
optimized, in several ways. The most common way is to minimize the residual, which is the difference 
between, predicted and actual values, or in this case, the calculated and measured deflections. The 
Residual is given in (Eq. 35). The difference is determined in units of thickness, but can also be determined 
in units of percentage difference.  
The danger in using the percentage difference is especially found at the outer points, where a small 
difference in deflection can cause a large difference in percentage in comparison to absolute deflection. 
Minimizing through absolute difference will cause the opposite, where the inner geophones are prioritized. 
It is however chosen to optimize the residual based on the absolute difference, as the investigated field is 
rather wide and the compared values furthest away from the impact load are small.  
Another possibility would be to use both percentage and absolute difference, either combined or using 
percentage at inner geophones and absolute at the outer geophones. This would however put high 
==PAGE:67==
  
 
67/105 
requirements on the optimization algorithm and would require a larger analysis as to how it is best 
implemented to ensure best possible results.  
The backcalculated E-moduli can be compared in different ways, but the RMS value is too be used in the 
final model for two reasons. The main reason being ELMOD being able to displays its optimization as RMS. 
The second being RMS is a value describing the average difference between measured and calculated 
deflection at each geophone. Other options could be to display the optimization through the coefficient of 
determination, 𝑅!, or 𝑆𝑆𝑅.  
5.2.2 
Optimization of coding  
For the best possible residuals to be found at all times, different types of coding is tested to se which of the 
functions return the lowest residuals, and still yield reasonable results. Furthermore the computation time 
is also taken into account even though it is not as important for non-commercial usage. 
Three possibilities are tested in terms of coding, the matlab function lsqnonlin, the matlab function 
patternsearch and a self developed function. The three different functions are shortly described in this 
section, and will be compared in section 5.2.3 as part of the validation.  
All of these functions are functions used to optimize the output of another function. The optimized 
function is handling the comparison of the backcalculated and measured deflection as described in section 
5.2.1. 
5.2.2.1 
Lsqnonlin function 
Lsqnonlin is a nonlinear optimization algorithm using the residual to find the optimal solution through least 
square. The function considers an initial guess, a function to be optimized and ranges of which the solution 
must be within. The function then finds the best fit to a range of data within the given boundaries. The 
function evaluates all residual at the initial guess and determines a slope at that point, which it uses to 
determine a step size and the next point. Lsqnonlin requires all residual as the output from the evaluated 
function. 
The function terminates based on different scenarios. The optimal termination is when lsqnonlin has 
converged to a solution, but the function can also be terminated if the change in the solution is to small, or 
if the change in summation of the square of the residual becomes to small. These three are the optimal 
terminations, but a termination also occurs if to many steps have been taken or to many functions are 
evaluated.  
5.2.2.2 
Patternsearch function 
Patternsearch is working in a similar way as lsqnonlin, but patternsearch has the ability to start with a 
costume initial step size. Patternsearch, just as lsqnonlin considers an initial guess, from which it works. The 
better the initial guess, the fewer steps are taken before a solution is found. The function finds the 
minimum value from the output of another function, based on a set of constraints or limitations. 
Patternsearch requires an output of a single value, which is to be optimized. The function uses the initial 
step size to determine the size of an initial mesh from which starts. 
Patternseach calculates the output of the function at distances of the initial mesh size in all directions 
(changing the input parameters both positive and negative of the step size in all combinations) from a 
guess and compares is to the output in the previous guess. The initial mesh size is set to 1 by default, but 
can be changed according to what is needed, though only the initial mesh size can be affected. The output 
at distances are compared to the output at the reference point, and If a smaller value is successfully found 
==PAGE:68==
  
 
68/105 
at a distance, the mesh size is doubled, while if the smallest value is at the reference point, the mesh size is 
halved. The function is terminated in the same manner as lsqnonlin, which is when either the mesh size 
becomes small enough, the change in the input (layer stiffness) is considered too small, the change in the 
output (residual) is considered too small or when a maximum iteration or function evaluations are reached. 
All of these termination criteria can be costume set. (Mathworks, 2016) 
5.2.2.3 
Self developed function (SDF) 
The final function, which was originally developed to display the residual at any given E-moduli 
combination, works in a slightly different manner in comparison to lsqnonlin and patternsearch. The 
function was created as an alternative to the two functions lsqnonlin and patternsearch. It was also 
developed because the full spectrum of residuals was deemed interesting to inspect.  
Instead of taking an initial guess into account, this function is inspecting a range of data; hence the initial 
guess is instead a guess of a maximum and minimum value for the stiffness of each layer. The function finds 
the residual values for all points in the grid based on the boundaries with the size of 𝑋!, where 𝑋 an odd 
number which is determined based on how fast and precise the function is needed to run. 𝑋 is required to 
be odd as to ensure a ‘center’ value at each iteration. If an even number is used, the algorithm can get 
stuck switching between two solutions.  
SSR is determined in all of the 𝑋! points, where the smallest of the determined SSR are set as a new center. 
The function is repeated in the new center, with a slightly smaller limitation unless the previous boundaries 
are part of the new center. If the new center is within all boundaries, the range is multiplied by 0.47 of the 
previous. The multiplication of 0.47 is used due to the same spots not being located twice within the first 
couple of iterations, with exception of the center point. The procedure is repeated 𝑁 number of times, 
where the limitation ranges are slowly decreasing, down to an acceptable accuracy. 𝑁 is set as a maximum 
value of the number of iterations, but the function can also be terminated before the 𝑁th iteration is 
reached, if the change in SSRS is significantly low.  
The main problem using this function is the time it takes to find all the residuals, making this function many 
times slower than the lsqnonlin and patternsearch functions, it does not however have the same problems 
of finding the global minimum, as the full spectrum is investigated, as long as the limitations are correctly 
defined. This function also enables the ability to generate plots showing the change in residual based on 
one or more of the parameters, which was what the function originally was intended for. The average time 
per iteration over 20 iterations is displayed below in Table 11 for different values of 𝑋. In the table it is seen 
that the time increases per iteration is increasing exponentially with 𝑋. The higher values of 𝑋 are not 
feasible in terms of modeling time, especially not for commercial usage.

as expected due to the temperature conditions under which the FWD readings were performed. Series 3 
was executed in low temperatures in comparison to series 1 and 2, which were executed in the summer 
months. As the temperature decreases, the stiffness of a pavement will increase and vice versa, which 
explains the differences in E-moduli between the different series.  
There is a lot higher variation in the backcalculated data for site 2 than for site 1, not only in magnitude but 
also in percentage. The obvious cause of this increased variation could be nonlinear behavior of the 
subgrades, but other factors may also influence the results. The variation within the individual series might 
also be caused by the variations in the pavement under construction, as no pavement is homogeneous, 
despite it being assumed in all calculations. 
If two points are considered in a pavement close to one another, the difference between the two can be 
quite large. If the construction tolerances in one point yield the thickest possible bound layer, while the 
other results in the thinnest possible layer, there can be quite a difference in the backcalculated results. 
Furthermore the program does not take cross-anisotropic and inhomogeneous materials into 
consideration, which also have an affect on the results (Ahmed et al., 2013).

Providing E-moduli over an area may be useable in the future, if a continuous FWD device is developed. The 
idea of such a device would be to be able to perform FWD measurements with a fixed frequency while 
moving. Having FWD measurements say every half-meter continuously throughout the length of the road, 
would make use of a combined E-moduli to display a realistic continuous development of the E-moduli over

6.1 Determination of nonlinearity from surface modulus 
In terms of determining the nonlinearity of the subgrade, the first inspection goes on the surface modulus. 
According to the subgrade benchmarking system (Horak, 2007) as displayed in Table 1 the surface modulus 
will change with depth differently according to how the subgrade behaves. The visual change of surface 
modulus for different subgrade behaviors are seen in Figure 8, where a linear and two nonlinear subgrades 
are displayed. These two nonlinear subgrades displayed in the figure are stress softening and stress 
stiffening. In general it is said that granular materials can be classified as stress stiffening, and fine-grained 
as stress softening, under compaction and traffic, which have been found through various studies and 
researches of different materials (De Bruin & Visser, 2000). 
In determination of the surface modulus, at least the same number of deflections are needed as the 
number of layers investigated, where each of these measurements are performed at distances 
corresponding to the upper layer intersections under the assumed load distribution, so that the outermost 
deflection is caused by the compression of the lowest layer alone. The compression of the two lower layers 
will then cause the second furthest deflection measurement. The maximum deflection measured at the 
center load is then a result of the compression, and hence moduli, of all layers (Horak, 1987). 
Using the benchmarking system developed by Horak, different subgrade moduli have been achieved 
depending on the loading and on the location of the FWD. A summation of the results is presented in Table 
29, where it is seen that the results for each Series and site correspond well with one another, with the 
mayor difference being the scaling of depending on the load. By visually comparing the two sites in Figure

6.2 Angle of optimal stress 
In section 2.4.3 it is described how the different stresses used in the nonlinear models are determined. 
These three stresses are determined based on vertical and horizontal stresses at any given point. These 
stresses are determined using the equations from Boussinesq and in general with the theories 
implemented in the LBM. 
These stresses are dependent on both the depth and radius of where they are determined, or dependent 
on the distance and angle. Figure 57 presents the different stresses, both normal stresses in all directions 
and octahedral stresses. The stresses are presented as a factor to be multiplied on 𝑃/(2𝜋) assuming 𝑅=
1. For 𝑅≠1, the percentile difference between the stresses will stay unchanged, but the magnitude will 
scale with the increase of 𝑅 inversely, as these are inversely proportional.  
Upon choosing the angle, which these stresses are based on for the nonlinear models, it is logically to use 
the angle causing the highest stresses. Using an angle that causes two low stresses in the backcalculation 
will enable the nonlinear models to predict an overestimated modulus. The overestimation will happen as 
the stresses used do determine the material parameters for the subgrade are lower than those calculated 
in the subgrade at other spots.  
As the Witzak & Uzan model (Eq. 32) is only dependent on the deviator stress as the dynamic contributor, 
the highest stresses will occur at an angle of 39.3°. The Witzak-MEPDG (Eq. 33) and PRIMAX-MEPDG (Eq. 
34) models are dependent on the octahedral tangential stress as dynamic contributor, the angle used 
would optimally be 57.3°. The PRIMAX-MEPDG model is also dependent on the dynamic bulk stress, which 
is observed to peak at 54.6°.

For the non-linearity to be implemented, a regression is need at to determine the material constants given 
in (Eq. 32), (Eq. 33) and (Eq. 34). These constants can be found through two ways. The first is to include all 
of the constants in a single regression. The other way would be to perform a two-step regression where 
two material constants are found at first, where one is 𝑘!, and the other is a constant, which for the 
purpose is called 𝑘!", including both the 𝑘! and 𝑘! contributions. After 𝑘! has been determined, a second 
regression is performed, where only 𝑘! and 𝑘! are unknown. The regressions are then two regressions with 
two parameters, instead of one regression with three parameters.  
These two regressions have to be linked for this to work optimally, as 𝑘!" is not variable, but the 
contribution from 𝑘! is varying with different radial distances, because the dynamic contribution is varying 
according to angle and distance from load impact. Therefor an optimization of 𝑘!" and 𝑘! will not 
necessarily yield the optimal solution. The two regression forms would have to be tested with each other to 
se is the same constants are found. If equal results are found, the two-step regression is preferable is it 
takes less computational power and would therefor be faster.  
Both types of regression are tested as to find if they produce similar results. To find how the two different 
regression types work according to each other, a set of data is needed to work as a basis for it. The two 
regression types are tested on series D32, though only for the first FWD location, as to find is they perform

6.3.3 
Implementation of nonlinearity in NBM 
As proper material constant were not found, and hence an implementation of these in NBM cannot occur, 
it is instead briefly explained, in this section how the implementation was planned to happen.  
The modulus of the subgrade is theoretically varying with depth, which is troublesome to implement in 
Odemark’s MET. Instead the subgrade will be divided into 𝑛 equally thick sub layers, which will have an E-
modulus corresponding to the modulus at the middle of the sub layer. The variable 𝑛 is determined by the 
deflection calculated at the top of the corresponding sub-layer. If the deflection is below 1 𝜇𝑚, the next 
layer will be considered the semi-infinite layer, with a modulus of half a thickness of a sub layer in the semi-
infinite layer. The thickness of the sub layers is a preset thickness determined based on the accuracy. A 
thickness of 100 𝑚𝑚 should prove sufficient, though this is not confirmed in any manor.

7 Conclusion and recommendations 
7.1 Conclusion 
The general objective of the thesis was to develop a linear elastic code solving the backcalculation problem. 
The linear elastic code (LBM) was successfully developed, analyzed and validated with embedded 
geophones. The LBM was developed to generally follow the limitations of Boussinesq and Odemark with 
exception of the assumption of a point load. The final limitations of the LBM are as listed: 
- 
Linear elastic behavior of all layers 
- 
Homogeneous and isotropic materials 
- 
Up to three layers resting of a semi-infinite layer 
- 
Evenly distributed load over a circular area  
The LBM was tested on two predetermined sites, where one of the sites were found to have a nonlinear 
subgrade, while the other observed as to behave linear. The testing and validation on the linear site proved 
to work for LBM and the backcalculated results were within reasonable limits considering the conditions 
under which the pavement was tested e.g. temperature conditions.  
A hypothesis was made upon the usage of embedded sensors in the backcalculation process would yield 
increased reliability for the backcalculation. This hypothesis could based on the findings not be confirmed, 
as the inclusion of the embedded sensors did not provide more realistic results.  
The general backcalculation process was experienced to be associated with high uncertainty in the 
determination of E-moduli for a 4-layered system.  
For the nonlinear analysis, the backcalculation model to handle this aspect was unsuccessfully developed, 
as the material constant were ill defined through the tested regressions. If the material constants were 
unsuccessfully found, as the regression to find these constant were associated with errors.  
The importance of a nonlinear treatment of the subgrade behaving nonlinear was clearly observed 
throughout the project, but was not implemented due the material constants being associated with errors.  
If the material constants were to be found in the future, a brief guide of the implementation and the 
general backcalculation process have been developed, as to help future implementation of nonlinearity of 
the subgrade.

7.2 Recommendations 
It would be interesting to make a slightly different backcalculation after the combined E-moduli has been 
determined. This backcalculation should optimize the height of the different layers; as to find how the 
height would deviate if all materials are considered homogeneous and isotropic. Depending on the results, 
the affect of construction variation could be somewhat determined.  
As the residual sensitivity for the inner geophones most unexpected provided results opposite of the 
expected, a further analysis on different pavements with and without synthetic data is recommended, as 
the results are seen to be untrustworthy for that specific analysis.  
As a 4-layered system is associated with high uncertainties in the backcalculation process, it would be 
interesting to find if the same uncertainties would be present for a 3-layer system. As the native subgrade 
was deemed outside the area of backcalculation (Christian Busch, 2015), this could technically be left out of 
the analysis, and thereby the results would be likely to change accordingly.  
Regarding the nonlinear backcalculation processes, it could be interesting to find if a the assumption of 
variation of the subgrade with depth and with radial distance from the loading is considered unreasonable, 
and there instead should be assumed a variation with depth and no variation with radial distance. There 
has not been time to test this in this thesis, as other points were deemed more important.  
The most important future recommendation of this thesis would be the determination of the material 
constants, as these material constants are critical for the implementation of nonlinear treatment of the 
subgrade.
