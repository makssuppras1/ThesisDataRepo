Abstract
In today’s healthcare environment, rising operational costs and the demand for high-
quality patient care highlight the need for improved efficiency in hospital logistics. The
adoption of technologies like Automated Guided Vehicles (AGVs) and Autonomous Mobile
Robots (AMRs) is growing in response to these challenges.
This thesis explores the potential of simulation and optimization algorithms, particularly
linear programming, to enhance logistics systems in complex healthcare environments.
The under-construction New North Zealand Hospital (NHN) serves as a case study. The
primary aim is to assess the effectiveness of various dispatching logics for AGVs and AMRs
based on several Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) related to operational efficiency and
service quality. Discrete Event Simulation (DES) is used to evaluate these dispatching
logics.
Results reveal that a dynamic dispatching logic, complemented by linear programming,
significantly improves key metrics related to task completion and system efficiency. These
findings highlight the effectiveness of dynamic dispatching logic, providing a strong case
for its consideration in AGV and AMR systems within hospital logistics.
This research serves as an in-depth exploration of AGV and AMR task dispatching logics,
demonstrating the performance benefits of dynamic dispatching methods through compre-
hensive simulation studies. While the results indicate potential improvements in hospital
logistics, they also highlight the importance of context, as the value of these enhancements
must be assessed against costs and available resources. Ultimately, this thesis contributes
to a more nuanced understanding of autonomous transport systems within the healthcare
sector, aiding decision-making processes in complex logistical environments like the NHN.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In the modern healthcare landscape, hospitals face several challenges, including rising
costs due to the increasing incidence of chronic diseases, the aging of the population,
and new treatment options (OECD, 2015). Within this context, the logistics department
becomes essential for hospital operations, managing critical activities such as inventory
management, information systems, transportation, and catering services. Notably, logis-
tics expenses account for around 30% of the total costs incurred by hospitals (Poulin,
2003; Dobson et al., 2015), emphasizing the importance of optimizing logistics operations
to improve efficiency and overall patient care standards.
Coordinating and distributing medical resources, including supplies, equipment, and staff
is a critical role played by hospital logistics. This function ensures the constant availability
of these resources, which ultimately contributes to the quality of patient care (Boˇzi´c et al.,
2022). Effective logistics management ensures the availability of essential resources, re-
duces treatment delays, and creates a better working environment for healthcare providers.
However, hospital logistics management poses numerous challenges, primarily due to the
intricate nature of healthcare institutions(Boˇzi´c et al., 2022). These facilities are tasked
with coordinating operations across various departments, managing a diverse array of med-
ical supplies and equipment, and adapting to the constantly shifting demands of patients
and healthcare providers (Vries and Huijsman, 2011). One paramount challenge hospitals
face is achieving equilibrium between cost and quality, as they must deliver exceptional
care while maintaining stringent budgets and reducing operational expenditures.
Hospitals are adopting Automated Guided Vehicles (AGVs) and Autonomous Mobile
Robots (AMRs) to address these logistics challenges. By implementing these automated
systems, hospitals can streamline their logistics processes, resulting in improved overall
efficiency and reduced operational costs (Bergmann et al., 2021). Moreover, these sophis-
ticated technologies aid in optimizing resource allocation, minimizing human errors, and

ing the need for staff oversight in logistics operations (Pedan et al., 2017). Programmable
to follow designated routes and adapt to hospital environment changes, this technology
offers versatile and efficient logistics solutions (Fragapane et al., 2020).
Furthermore, the use of simulation and optimization algorithms in conjunction with AGVs
and AMRs offers considerable potential for enhancing logistics systems in hospitals, such as
the New North Zealand Hospital (NHN). This approach enables the optimization of AGV
systems to meet both current and future needs. Simulation tests at various parameters
help gauge AGV demand, pinpoint process bottlenecks, and propose system modifications
(Dobrzanska and Dobrzanski, 2021; Morinaga et al., 2019). Integrating these advanced
technologies also enables hospitals to develop more effective contingency plans, ensuring
smooth operations even in the face of unexpected events. Moreover, collecting and an-
alyzing data generated by AGVs and AMRs fosters continuous improvement of logistics
processes, leading to increased efficiency and substantial cost savings.
In summary, the incorporation of simulation and optimization algorithms, coupled with
the employment of AGVs and AMRs, offers considerable potential for enhancing logistics
systems in hospitals, such as the NHN. Addressing hospital logistics challenges through au-
tomation bolsters overall performance, curtails expenses, and enhances patient care. The
potential impact of these technologies on hospital logistics is immense and could revolution-
ize healthcare operations and patient care. By adopting automation and harnessing the
power of simulation and optimization algorithms, hospitals like the NHN can substantially
upgrade their logistics systems, ensuring a more efficient, effective, and patient-centric ap-
proach to healthcare delivery.
1.1
Problem Definition
This study began as dialogues with professionals in healthcare logistics, initially involving
representatives from NIRAS, a leading industry consulting firm. The conversation later
expanded to include specialists from the healthcare sector, resulting in a partnership with
the NHN. Determining the project, comprehending practical needs, and correlating it with
academic interests constituted an iterative process that took place during the initial period
of the thesis.
A connection between NHN’s and NIRAS’ interest was observed. Both sides want to un-
derstand AGV systems’ operational capabilities and how it impacts logistics’ performance
in healthcare. NHN plans to future-proof their hospital logistics by understanding the
impact of different AGV/AMR systems’ features. Meanwhile, NIRAS intends to leverage

1.1. PROBLEM DEFINITION
this knowledge for their consultancy in AGV/AMR system simulations.
This study aims to investigate the potential of simulation and optimization algorithms in
evaluating the logistics systems of hospitals, being the NHN system the case study for the
project. As it has been described in the introduction, the integration of AGVs and/or
AMRs into hospital logistics operations has shown promising results in terms of improved
efficiency and reduced costs. However, the successful implementation of these systems in
complex healthcare environments requires a deeper understanding of the challenges and
opportunities involved.
The NHN serves as an ideal case study to explore the practical implications of integrating
AGVs and AMRs in a hospital setting, as it will represent a modern healthcare facility
grappling with the pressures of delivering high-quality patient care while managing costs.
By leveraging advanced simulation and optimization techniques, this project will examine
the hospital’s logistics processes and identify opportunities for improvement.
The primary objective is defined by the research question:
How can simulation be employed to evaluate different AGV task dispatching
logics?
To be more detailed, some of the aspects that this project also intends to analyze are

1. What is the impact of different AGV task dispatching logics on the performance of

3. What factors contribute to bottlenecks in the AGV system, and how can simulation
be used to identify and address these to improve operational performance?
This study aims to explore the logistics associated with the AGV system within a hospital
setting, focusing on its efficiency and performance. The following delimitations have been

The integration of AGVs in hospital logistics presents numerous benefits. These include:
• Improved efficiency: AGVs can operate 24/7, reducing delays and ensuring the
timely delivery of resources throughout the hospital.

the burden on hospital staff, allowing them to focus on more critical tasks such as
patient care.
• Enhanced safety: AGVs are designed to follow specific routes and detect obsta-
cles, minimizing the risk of accidents and improving overall safety in the hospital
environment.
• Increased accuracy: Automated systems can reduce the likelihood of errors in ma-
terial handling, ensuring that the right supplies are delivered to the appropriate
locations.

3.1.1
Hospital Logistics
This section will discuss the various challenges faced by hospitals in managing their logis-
tics. These challenges range from the involvement of clinical staff in supply chain activities
to addressing nursing shortages and ensuring the availability of necessary supplies. Ex-
amining these issues has the objective of providing a comprehensive understanding of the
complexities involved in hospital logistics management.
Hospitals are complex organizations that require a diverse array of supplies to function
effectively. These supplies, ranging from medical equipment and pharmaceutical products
to linens, meals, stationery, and cleaning products, are essential for providing quality care
to patients. Ensuring that these supplies are readily available poses significant logistical
challenges for hospitals (Landry and Beaulieu, 2013).
One of the primary issues in hospital logistics is the involvement of various clinical staff in
supply chain activities. Due to the multitude of information and material flows within a
hospital, almost every staff member has a role in the supply chain, often without realizing it
(Landry and Beaulieu, 2001). However, clinical staff members typically lack the expertise
and resources to manage logistics activities efficiently.
Furthermore, many industrialized countries are facing nursing shortages, making it cru-
cial to allocate clinical staff’s efforts toward patient care.
In light of this, a substan-
tial proportion of hospital employees are dedicating a significant portion of their time to
logistics-related tasks (Ferenc, 2010).
Addressing these challenges requires a more efficient and streamlined approach to hospital
logistics management. This includes ensuring the availability of necessary supplies and
providing proper training to staff members involved in logistics tasks.

AGV System Applications in Healthcare
This section presents studies on AGV and AMR systems’ use and application in healthcare.
The paper by Fragapane et al. (2020) mentions that the current policy in healthcare is
to create larger hospitals and close smaller ones with the purpose of grouping knowledge
and expertise for better treatments while reducing some costs in aspects like the logistics
and supply chain of a hospital facility. The authors state that commonly in a hospital,
25-30% of the budget is employed for medical supplies and their handling. As phrased
by Fragapane et al. (2020), ”adding more resources to supply the demand for goods shows
short-term positive results. However, for long-term positive results, the internal logistics
has to be adapted.” Given these considerations and the ongoing trend towards wider, flatter
hospital layouts, technologies like AGVs and AMRs are increasingly being employed to
enhance internal logistics. This approach aims to provide a future-proof solution. The
study highlights the use of discrete-event simulation for designing and validating AGV
systems in hospitals. The study emphasizes that the simulation and application of AGVs
must be supported by a transportation schedule and comprehensive system understanding.
Therefore, in this context, an assessment of material and information flows has been
conducted, highlighting the interconnections between different hospital departments. The
interaction between Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) and AGV Task Management
System shows potential system improvements in order to perform logistical tasks in a
more efficient way and ”break the ’silo’ culture in hospitals” (Fragapane et al., 2020).
Moreover, the development of models for more complex AGV systems is necessary to
manage multiple material flows and address challenges in hospitals.
Fragapane et al. (2020) study the relevance of AMRs within the context of a hospital
facility. It is mentioned from a survey that ”nursing’s non-value-adding and non-nursing
activities consume around 40% of nurses’ time.” (Fragapane et al., 2020). The use of
automation in hospital logistics effectively relieves nurses from transportation tasks, which
often require these employees to cover long distances on foot. Initially, AGVs were the
only choice, but with the arrival of AMRs, flexibility has been improved thanks to path re-
calculation and more agile robots that make easier the co-existence of people and robots.
In particular, a case of sterile instrument transportation is discussed, demonstrating how
the implementation of AMRs for transporting wagons improved the performance of that
operation, as well as freeing up one full-time employee who can spend his or her time on
more valuable tasks.

3.1.4
Discrete Event Simulation in Healthcare
Lal et al. (2015) stated ”Due to the complexity associated with healthcare processes, discrete
event simulation is the most popularly used decision support tool in assessing trade-offs
between multiple objectives of healthcare systems”. Moreover, Lebcir et al. (2017) con-
cluded: ”As the complexity of health systems increases in an environment of constrained
resources, so too does the need for evidence-based decision making”.
Discrete event simulation (DES)’s key advantage is its ability to capture the dynamic,
stochastic nature of systems in healthcare, including patient flow, resource allocation, and
decision-making processes. DES is a powerful tool increasingly being applied in healthcare
(Karnon et al., 2012). DES allows stakeholders to model complex, dynamic systems, pro-
viding a deeper understanding of various elements within the healthcare delivery process
(Fone et al., 2003).
In the context of hospital logistics, DES serves as a potent tool for optimization. It has
demonstrated its effectiveness in the allocation of resources, reduction of patient waiting
times, and improvement of service quality (Komashie and Mousavi, 2005; Jahangirian
et al., 2010). Given the complex nature of hospital operations, managing interactions and
dependencies among various elements of the hospital supply chain can be challenging (Fone
et al., 2003). Discrete-event simulation holds significant value in facilitating well-informed
decisions aimed at enhancing system performance (Rohleder et al., 2007).
Numerous case studies highlight the successful implementation of DES in healthcare set-
tings.
For instance, Komashie and Mousavi (2005) utilizes DES to model emergency

Optimization in Simulation
Optimization through simulation has proven highly valuable in automation science and
engineering disciplines, such as optimizing manufacturing processes and supply chain man-
agement. Its particular strengths include the capacity to manage system complexity, un-
certainty, and non-linearity (Goodwin et al., 2021; Fu, 2002).
A comprehensive review by Fu (2002) details the extensive use of optimization in combi-
nation with simulation across a variety of industries and domains. This review showcases
the versatility and power of simulation optimization, highlighting its application in areas
such as manufacturing, transportation, and service industries.
Aqlan and Ashour (2020) demonstrated the use of a hybrid simulation-optimization frame-
work in managing cross-docking assignments within an electronics manufacturing com-
pany. They minimized total costs in a cross-docking setup by intelligently assigning trucks
and orders to docks, utilizing a discrete-event simulation model to accommodate system
variability. They provided real-time scheduling and dynamic resource allocation by iter-
atively adjusting the model based on the simulation results. This case underscores the
potential of merging simulation with optimization to solve complex assignment problems
while adapting to dynamic conditions. Likewise, Lal et al. (2015) highlights the utility of
DES in healthcare, particularly for optimizing patient experience and controlling costs.
However, Goodwin et al. (2021) notes that the use of simulation optimization in real-
time decision-making situations can be computationally inefficient due to the limited time

3.2
Simulation
Simulation is a representation of a real-world system that aims to mimic its behaviors and
qualities.
Simulation offers numerous advantages in various fields, as it allows professionals to test
and analyze different scenarios and outcomes without the costs and risks associated with
real-world experiments. For instance, it can be used to optimize production processes in
manufacturing or improve patient flow in healthcare settings (Law, 2014). A key advantage
of simulation is its ability to examine intricate, real-world systems containing stochastic
elements, which are difficult to accurately represent through mathematical models that
can be assessed analytically. Simulation estimates the performance of an existing system
under projected operating conditions and helps compare alternative system designs or
operational policies to determine the best solution for specific requirements (Law, 2014).
Moreover, simulation offers enhanced control over experimental conditions, allowing the
study of systems with extended time frames in a compressed time scale or, conversely,
exploring the intricate workings of a system in an expanded time.
This adaptability
renders simulation a potent instrument for comprehending and optimizing diverse systems
and processes (Law, 2014).
Simulation, while offering numerous benefits, also has its drawbacks and limitations. Sim-
ulation models provide a simplified portrayal of real-world systems, potentially omitting
some of the nuances and complexities. As a result, when assessing the output results, it
is crucial to account for the possibility of reduced accuracy (Law, 2014). Additionally,
each execution of a stochastic simulation model yields only approximations of a model’s
genuine traits for a particular set of input parameters (Ching, 2003). Achieving precise
outcomes may require several runs. This can make simulation models less suitable for
optimization than analytical models. Several replications might be necessary for precise
outcomes, rendering simulation models less appropriate for optimization than analytical
models. If a credible analytical model is accessible or readily created, it could be favored
over a simulation model (Law, 2014). Furthermore, developing simulation models can be
costly and require significant time investment. If a model does not accurately represent
the system being examined, the simulation outcomes, no matter how remarkable they
seem, may not offer valuable insights into the real system (Law, 2014).
In the world of system analysis, there are three main types of simulation techniques that
are extensively utilized: Discrete-Event Simulation, Agent-Based Simulation, and Sys-
tem Dynamics (Sumari et al., 2013). These simulation models provide powerful tools for

analyzing complex systems, each with its own unique approach and benefits, allowing
researchers to choose the one that best fits their specific scenario and objectives.
Discrete-Event Simulation (DES) focuses on modeling systems as sequences of distinct
events unfolding at specific points in time (Banks, 2010; Majid et al., 2010). DES excels
at depicting process flows, using a top-down approach and stochastic methods (Sumari
et al., 2013). This type of simulation uses activity diagrams to portray the interactions
of entities, which can vary from customers to products, in specific events (Sumari et al.,
2013).
It also showcases a series of activities and the waiting time from one state to
another, facilitating the prediction of subsequent events (Brailsford and Hilton, 2001).
Moreover, DES heavily depends on numerical data for accuracy and uses graphics and
animations for better model representation (Sumari et al., 2013). Despite its strengths,
it’s worth noting that DES may not accurately reflect the impact of true variability and
is less suited for analyzing models related to human behavior (Majid et al., 2010).
Agent-Based Simulation (ABS) captures system information through object-oriented mod-
eling, effectively representing complex systems composed of interacting, autonomous agents
with diverse attributes and behaviors (Macal and North, 2010). Individual agents, often
depicted via scripts or graphical editors, facilitate the observation of emergent system be-
havior (Macal and North, 2010). Notably, ABS is a bottom-up modeling approach that
focuses on the characteristics, interactions, and behaviors of individual agents (Stainsby
et al., 2009). Furthermore, ABS can model social systems. It allows patterns and struc-
tures to emerge that weren’t explicitly programmed into the model (Macal and North,
2010). One of the challenges in implementing ABS can be the significant amount of inter-
action required among the agents. This interaction necessitates frequent communication,
which may be costly, particularly in large systems, thereby making ABS a less selected
method due to these high communication expenses (Sumari et al., 2013).
System dynamics is an interdisciplinary method, using both qualitative and quantitative
approaches to understand complex systems (Sterman, 2000; Sumari et al., 2013). It focuses
on analyzing feedback loops to identify causes and effects and relies heavily on computer
simulation to visualize real-world scenarios (Sumari et al., 2013). Primarily, it uses causal
loops for qualitative understanding and stock-and-flow diagrams for quantitative analysis
(Sumari et al., 2013). This approach enables a deep understanding of systems’ behaviors
over time, and the ability to analyze the effects of different interactions (Escudero-Marin
and Pidd, 2011). However, if the problem is not clearly defined, the application of system
dynamics can prove challenging and time-consuming, especially in large systems with
complex scenarios (Sumari et al., 2013).

Simulation methodology
In this section, the general simulation methodology described by Rossetti (2015) is pre-
sented as it constitutes the principle structure of this project. The guideline is known as
the DEGREE methodology and it consists of a series of steps to follow when building a
simulation model. The steps are listed as follows:
1. Define the problem.
2. Establish measures of performance for evaluation.
3. Generate alternative solutions.
4. Rank alternative solutions.
5. Evaluate and iterate during the process.
6. Execute and evaluate the solution.
The DEGREE problem-solving methodology provides a structured approach for effectively
and efficiently performing simulation modeling tasks. From the first to the last step, it
helps to ensure that the right problem has been considered; the used metrics are coherent
with the problem; as well as to make sure that multiple solutions have been analyzed and
evaluated. Nonetheless, simulation includes specific tasks that must be performed in the
problem-solving process. When using the DEGREE approach for a problem that requires
simulation, it is important to adjust the procedure to best fit the needs of the project. As
a result, Rossetti refined the methodology to better accommodate the interaction between
simulation and the overall problem-solving process.

Process Flow and the ’communicating-through-list’ concept
The Process Flow constitutes the 2D simulation environment in FlexSim. It is a token-
driven flow where one or multiple tokens are created and as they go through the pro-
cess flow, many activities and commands are executed. FlexSim has a library containing
already-built activities that have specific functionalities, while it also gives the option
to code your own activity if needed.
The programming language in FlexSim is called
FlexScript and it is an object-oriented language.
A token is an entity represented by a circle in the process flow and it can be associated
with various objects or aspects of a model. For example, a token can represent a flow item
or a task executer in the process flow, as well as it can be representative of something
more abstract such as a looping logic. In that case, there could be one token going on a
loop through activities and triggering specific actions on other tokens or objects in the 3D
model.
One of the main features of the process flow which is also highly relevant for this project
is the concept Lists. A List is a shared asset that can represent a list of tokens, flow
items, task executers, and much more (FlexSim, 2023b). A unique characteristic of lists
is their ability to facilitate communication and interaction between two separate process
flows. This is made possible by two key process flow activities: ’Push to List’ and ’Pull
from List’. When triggered by a token, these activities ’push’ and ’pull’ a defined value to
or from the list, respectively. The ’pushed’ value then becomes available for reuse by the
same or a different token later in the process. This allows two disconnected process flows
to be synchronized in the performance of a task if needed. Especially, by default, a token
entering a ’push to list’ activity waits there until another token pulls the value it entered
on the list. Consequently, the original token can continue to flow, executing subsequent
activities. This feature enables the synchronization of activities and management of the
logic execution order. In Figure 3.3, an example of the utilization of a list for connecting
two flows is presented. The process flow on the left represents the creation of an item
that has to be transported from one area to another (see also Figure 3.2 for a visual

Optimization Model
This section explores the Assignment Problem, which has been identified as the preferred
optimization model for computing the optimal solution in this study’s context.
The Assignment Problem, fundamental in Mathematics and Operations Research, aims to
optimally match tasks with resources. Each task is assigned to a singular resource, which
in turn can be assigned only one task. The primary aim is to optimize a given criterion -
be it cost, time, or efficiency (S. Singh, 2012).
The Assignment Problem can usually be represented as a square matrix, where the rows
represent the tasks and the columns represent the resources.
Each cell in the matrix
contains a cost or benefit associated with assigning a particular task to a specific resource.
The goal is to find the assignment that minimizes the total cost or maximizes the total
benefit.

Regarding the AGVs
1. AGVs operate within an ideal operational environment, meaning they can move

5.2
Baseline Model
In this section, a description of the Baseline model is presented.
The Baseline model
consists of a model that features all the behaviors that have been evaluated as basic and
will serve as the foundation for the application of further developed logic. Therefore, the
Baseline model includes all the characteristics that the more advanced models also have,
apart from the logic for dispatching a task to an AGV, which in fact constitutes the core
of the study.
The Baseline model is based on the First Come, First Served (FCFS) dispatching method.
The FCFS approach is a fundamental task dispatching logic that serves as the backbone
for various operational strategies in diverse fields. The central system of the FCFS logic is
simple and intuitive: tasks are processed in the order they arrive with the task that arrives
earliest being served first. This mechanism ensures a fair and transparent distribution of
tasks. However, while its simplicity is a strength, it also opens up avenues for further
optimization.
While the FCFS approach governs task dispatch order, additional considerations include
AGV selection and charging rules after task completion. In the case of AGV selection,
two alternatives are considered: the choice of the first available AGV or the selection of
the nearest AGV. Regarding the charging behavior, the option of assigning a ’home node’
to each AGV is compared with directing AGVs to charge at the nearest station. Through
discussions with the hospital representative and AGV system vendors, the Baseline model
incorporates the selection of the nearest AGV and the rule to drive to the closest charging
station. This model minimizes the time and distance required for an AGV to reach the
assigned task location. The decision was primarily driven by common fleet management
practices, which favor assigning tasks to the closest AGV. The option to use the nearest
charging station was deemed more realistic and favorable for maximizing AGV uptime
and availability.
Further, renouncing dedicated home charging stations for each AGV
circumvents infrastructure limitations. This allows for flexibility, for example, if additional
AGVs are required due to an increase in trolley transport demand.
Moreover, initial
simulations of the four possible combinations of AGV selection and charging station rules

Arrivals Creation
The process flow for creating arrivals operates a mechanism that generates trolley tokens
in the simulation, based on the input data. Within this flow, a token is produced every new
model day, undergoing a procedure to create and dispatch a trolley token to the designated
trolley flow. Once the command for creating the trolley token has been executed, the
token continues the flow by checking if there are more trolleys to be created according to
an Arrivals table. At the start of every simulation day, a fresh Arrivals table is created.
It factors in a daily transport variability of +/-5% and adds a degree of randomness to
the assignment of specific locations and arrival times, within predefined hours, for each

The ’Arrivals’ table, detailing daily trolley transportation tasks, is computed based on the
preceding calculation of the ’Transport Plan’, which is a table that is calculated using the
input data tables described in Section 5.1. The algorithms for both these calculations are
derived from a conceptual framework previously developed by NIRAS. The full algorithms
can be found in Appendix D while a description of the logic behind them is provided below.
The scope of these two pieces of code is to calculate the ’Arrivals’ table. This table contains
information about how many trolleys will have to be transported in a day together with
detailed information for every transportation, from the type of flow to the arrival time in
the system. According to how the input data has been collected, an intermediate step is
needed for calculating the aforementioned table. In fact, the calculation of the ’Transport
Plan’ precedes, and is used for the ’Arrivals’ table’s creation.
1. The ’Transport Plan’ table’s computation relies on two specific input data tables: the
’Locations’ table, which details the association between locations and departments,
and the ’Location Transports’ table, providing the number of transports required per
department. The ’Locations’ table, including information on what location is related
to what department, is crossed with the ’Location Transports’ table which contains
data about the number of transports required per department. The process consists
of reading the number of transports of a specific flow corresponding to a specific
department and evenly distributing this amount among all the locations that are
related to that department. Nested for loops are used in this calculation to read the
two tables. The results are then reported into the ’Transport Plan’ table. This table
presents all locations in the rows and all types of flows in the columns. The code uses
a triangular distribution to account for variability in transport quantity that varies
with a +/-5% around the input numbers. The ’Transport Plan’ creation is crucial

because, while the hospital has the number for how many trolleys each department
needs, the simulation model requires detailed information on the number and type
of trolleys that every specific location has to interact with.
2. Following this, the ’Transport Plan’ table is used as input for the ’Arrivals’ calcu-
lation. In this step, each cell’s value in the ’Transport Plan’ table is read and the
same number of rows is added to the ’Arrivals’ table with all the details related to
that transport. The only data added after this process is the arrival time, which
is calculated based on the ’Hourly Distribution’ table. This table shows what the
distribution of trolleys should look like in 24 hours per every flow type. Therefore,

’Arrivals’ table is ordered by arrival time, enabling the simulation to read the table
in sequential order.
Trolley’s Flow
This process flow outlines the ’journey’ of a trolley through the system. At first glance,
this flow might resemble the flow chart diagram from the conceptual model, but in re-

comprehensive view of the entire system, the ’trolley’s flow’ concentrates specifically on
the pathways and operations of the trolleys, disregarding other system logic. This process
flow can be simplified as follows.
1. Trolley arrives.
2. Trolley reserves origin and destination slots.
3. Trolley requests an AGV for transportation and waits for it to arrive.
4. Trolley is picked up by the AGV and is transported to its destination.
5. Trolley is dropped off at the destination slot.
While the trolley’s process might appear simple, it’s important to highlight a few key
aspects that ensure the simulation model operates correctly, in line with established as-
sumptions and desired behaviors. The first aspect concerns the assumption that the order

of the transport. Figure 5.5 illustrates how the flow differentiates between trolleys coming

6.3.3
Dynamic Task Dispatching
Dynamic Task Dispatching is an approach that allows for continuous reassessment and
redistribution of tasks based on real-time updates and priorities. As presented in Section
2.3.2, an AGV system can feature the option of re-assigning tasks to improve the perfor-
mance of the system. Therefore, Dynamic Task Dispatching, which consistently evaluates
system conditions, can potentially lead to higher performance.
The idea behind the Dynamic Task Dispatching logic is that at every event, such as the
arrival or completion of a task, the system recalculates which available AGV is best suited
for each uncompleted task. The system recalculates by minimizing an objective function,
accounting for factors like the total distance traveled by AGVs, and the priority level
of each task. This implies that the system is continuously looking for the most optimal
assignment of tasks to AGVs, and can reassign tasks if a more optimal assignment is
found.
In order to further discuss this logic, new definitions of ’available task’ and ’available
AGV’ are provided. Henceforth, a task is considered available and thus, part of the AGV
Tasks list until the trolley associated with that task has been loaded onto an AGV. On
64
==PAGE:77==
6.3. AGV DISPATCHING LOGICS
the other hand, an AGV is deemed available until it has loaded a trolley. However, there
is a specific case where these definitions are not applicable. The exception is related to
the AGV being on an elevator while the dynamic system is evaluating the best AGV for
every job. The Appendix F provides a detailed explanation of the process flow used to
control and handle situations when AGVs are inside an elevator. However, there are two
instances where an AGV, when inside an elevator, cannot be regarded as available for the
dynamic dispatching algorithm calculation. One of these instances is the exception to the
available AGV definition mentioned earlier.
• AGV carrying a trolley on an elevator. In this case, no exception occurs as
according to the definition of available AGV, an AGV loaded with a trolley cannot
be considered for other tasks until it has completed the current task.
• AGV traveling empty on an elevator. In this case, an exception has to be
considered based on the idea that if an AGV is on an elevator already traveling
towards a task that has been assigned to it, it cannot change its travel direction
until it reaches the designated level. Thus, when the AGV reaches the level of the
previously assigned task, it is logical that the AGV collects the corresponding trolley.
In line with this concept, an empty AGV on an elevator, traveling toward a task in
one of the AGV rooms connected to that elevator cannot be considered an available
AGV. Consequently, the dispatching logic must ensure that the task assigned to that
particular AGV is not classified as an available task.
Mathematical Model
As presented earlier in Section 3.3, the Assignment Problem model is employed to optimize
the process of task dispatching. The objective of the model is to minimize the total distance
traveled by the AGVs considering the priority of the dispatched tasks. Thus, each time
the algorithm implements the model, it considers the distance between each task and
each AGV and the tasks’ priority, as costs to be minimized in the cumulative formula.
However, the model applies two distinct methods for considering priority: one that solely
uses the task-associated priority and another that combines the task priority with the
time remaining before a task is deemed delayed (urgency). The two different approaches
are now discussed, being aware that while in the mathematical model an array called
’priority’ is used and multiplied by the cost in the objective function, that same array can
be representative of either the priority or the newly calculated urgency factor.
As previously mentioned, the priority is represented by a number, with a lower value
representing a higher priority. Due to how the mathematical optimizer works, it has been
evaluated that the priority will have to be provided reversely. For example, if a task has
65
==PAGE:78==
CHAPTER 6. EXPERIMENTAL STUDY
a priority 1, it must have a value of 5 in the priority array; or if it has a priority equal to
2 then it will have to be 4 in the array, and so on. Thus, considering the priority being a
number between 1 to 5, the calculation 6 −Priority is done when populating the priority
array. By doing this, the optimizer that tries to minimize the objective function will give
more weight to the tasks with an actual higher priority.
A similar approach is employed when calculating the urgency factor that is to be input
into the model. In this case, the idea is to multiply the priority with the time left. The
’time left’ for a task is calculated by considering when a task was introduced into the
system and the stipulated completion window for the task. The highest value obtainable
by multiplying a priority by the time left is 450, which is the result of priority 5 multiplied
by the time left equal to 90 minutes. Thus, the urgency factor calculations are centered
around this maximum value of 450. Furthermore, two separate cases are taken into account
to handle scenarios where the ’time left’ is either positive (task not yet delayed) or negative
(task is delayed). If the time left is negative it means that the task is already delayed,
therefore the algorithm should give higher priority to it. The two different calculations
are presented below.
If time left >= 0:

As for the task dispatching logic process flow for the Baseline model described in Chapter
5, this process flow is also characterized by having one token looping through the flow every
time a new event triggers the logic. There are two events that can trigger the dynamic
tasks dispatching logic: a new task enters the system, or an AGV becomes available.
Following this, the system verifies whether there are tasks awaiting dispatch. If not, then
it means that the system has been triggered by an AGV becoming available again. In such
a scenario, the newly available AGV is directed to the closest charging station, following
the token’s circulation through the left path of the process flow. On the other hand, if
there are tasks available for dispatching, then the token follows the right side of the flow
executing additional logic and algorithms, which are described below in order of execution.
1. Are there AGVs available? This decision point controls whether or not there
are available AGVs for receiving a new task, in accordance with the definition of
’available AGV’ presented and considering the status of AGVs on an elevator. Should

==PAGE:81==

AGVs be available, the dispatching logic advances. If no AGVs are available, the
algorithm is bypassed and the token moves to the flow’s ’reset’ section.
2. Are there tasks outside elevators? By the given definition of ’available task’, a
task remains on the list until the related trolley is loaded on an AGV. Therefore, the
system has to verify if there are tasks available that are really not taken, considering
the elevators’ interaction logic. The algorithm halts if the task list contains only
tasks that have been assigned to AGVs currently on an elevator.
3. Selecting of AGVs and Tasks. This phase involves the accurate selection of AGVs
and tasks for executing the algorithm, in addition to filling the priority array for
the optimization model. The first step is to make sure that the AGVs not available
are not considered, particularly those on an elevator that constitute an exception,
are not considered. Afterwards, the tasks are selected making sure that the ones
related to AGV on the elevator are not being considered. Lastly, the priority array

Bottlenecks
Besides the number of AGVs operating, performance limitations arise mainly from the
availability of slots within the AGV rooms.
The speed at which an operator handles a trolley directly impacts system efficiency. De-
creased handling time leads to more frequent slot availability, potentially reducing wait
times.
Additionally, the logic that determines the start of a transport also plays a significant
role. In the current model, a transport begins only after a destination slot for the trolley
is reserved. However, this approach may not apply to all situations. Alternative systems
may initiate transport irrespective of destination slot availability, continuously evaluating
availability during the transport process. This evaluation may include temporary pauses
in predetermined areas until a slot becomes available.

7.1
Choice of Method
Selecting a suitable research method is important to achieve the objectives of this study.
The study aims to evaluate various AGV task dispatching logics, focusing specifically on
their performance within the New North Zealand Hospital (NHN). Given this research ob-
jective, multiple methodologies were considered. Following a thorough evaluation, simula-
tion, particularly Discrete Event Simulation (DES), was selected as the main methodology
for the study.
7.1.1
The Decision for Simulation
Simulation is particularly fitting for this study, as it excels at representing the complexities
of a hospital logistics system, modeling the evolution of this system over time, and allows
for the comparison of alternative AGV dispatching strategies under projected conditions
82

7.1. CHOICE OF METHOD
(Law, 2014). Reinforced by Cheng (1992), who stated: ”the emphasis of a simulation
model tends to be one of ’what if’ rather than ’what’s best’ as emphasized by other opti-
mizing operations research techniques such as mathematical programming”.
In other words, simulation offers a platform for risk-free experimentation and testing of
scenarios with different parameters to identify potential strategies. In a complex environ-
ment such as a hospital, where changes could have significant impacts on patient care, this
’try before you implement’ ability bears particular value.
As seen in Section 6.3.3, while simulation is the main method in this study, it is comple-
mented by linear programming, a type of mathematical modeling, used to enhance the
dynamic task dispatching logic simulation.
Additionally, simulation often produces visual outputs, aiding in understanding the studied
system. Given the team’s experience in this method, the development of an advanced
model within the project’s timeline is streamlined.
Another potential method is Real-life Testing. However, while it offers highly accurate
insights into system behavior, it can be costly, time-consuming, and potentially disruptive
to regular operations. Furthermore, ethical considerations may restrict the extent of real-
life experimentation in healthcare settings. Ultimately, real-life testing is not feasible for
this study as the New North Zealand Hospital is not yet operational.
7.1.2
The decision for Discrete Event Simulation
The selection of a simulation technique significantly impacts the study’s outcomes, in-
fluencing the model’s structure and level of detail.
Discrete Event Simulation (DES),
Agent-Based Simulation (ABS), and System Dynamics (SD) are among the commonly
employed simulation methodologies (Jahangirian et al., 2010).
Section 3.2 provides a detailed analysis of these techniques, from which DES emerged as
the chosen method for this study
DES presents a distinct advantage for this study due to its strong ability to model complex
systems as sequences of distinct events (Banks, 2010). DES’s focus on numerical data and
visual outputs is especially valuable for representing the process flows and interactions
within a hospital setting (Sumari et al., 2013).
While ABS is a potent technique for modeling systems with interacting, autonomous
agents, its bottom-up approach primarily emphasizes the behavior of these agents (Law,
2014). Given that all AGVs in this study exhibit the same behavior and characteristics,
this aspect is not a concern.
System Dynamics (SD) isn’t optimal for this study due to its focus on broad, continuous

The relevance of this project extends across multiple sectors, notably within the healthcare
sector on one side and companies that support hospitals on the other.
The results and discussions of this project may apply to other hospitals. In fact, there

notable: the layout of the facility and the operational needs inherent to hospitals.
It is usual for hospitals to have a zone like the described Service Village where goods
arrive and depart from the hospital. This area is usually located in a separate building
connected to the main hospital by a tunnel or a corridor (Jensen, 2023). Consequently, the
decision between utilizing AGVs or human staff for distant goods delivery heavily relies on
the hospital’s architectural layout. In addition, hospitals are stable and consistent service
businesses with similar types of operations and the need for similar types of goods and
resources (K¨uhl, 2023). Thus, the problem encountered with the transportation of food
in the studied case may be an issue that many other hospitals face.
Furthermore, a key responsibility of hospital management is to constantly strive for sys-
tem improvements and greater efficiency. For hospital logistics, particularly intralogistics,

7.3
Future Work
In this section, an investigation of possible future work for improving the presented study
is provided. The discussion begins by considering additional features of the case-study
model. It then continues by introducing potential improvements applicable to broader
simulations of AGV systems in a hospital context.
As previously mentioned in Chapter 6, the intermediate storage has not been considered
and this could lead to not very representative results for the New North Zealand Hospital.
Therefore, adding all the flows to and from the intermediate storage could improve the
accuracy of the system, resulting in a more useful model to estimate the required num-
ber of AGVs. While the addition of these flows may not necessarily improve or worsen
performance, it will certainly yield a more accurate representation of the studied system.
Moreover, during one of the final conversations with the Hillerød hospital management
representative, new ideas about the prioritization rules arose. Specifically, some tasks, like
food and sterile instrument deliveries, must not be delayed. These flows should not be
delayed regardless of circumstances. Consequently, this feedback prompted the considera-
tion of a prioritization rule combining both priority and urgency approaches. A suggestion
for implementing such improvement is to add more weight to tasks with priority 1, so the
model will always prefer these.
This will require revisiting the logic presented in the
Dynamic Urgency model.
In the current dynamic task dispatching simulation model, an AGV may be allocated a
task and start navigating toward the task location. However, if another AGV, closer to
the task, becomes operational shortly after, the scenario changes. In such instances, the
initial AGV is disassociated from the task and must return to a charging station, leading
to unnecessary travel. Adding an additional complication to the dispatching logic could
improve this behavior, so the system will also consider where the AGVs are driving and
take that into account when assigning a task. In this proposed new system, AGVs could
be assigned secondary tasks, to be completed once the current task is done.
Another interesting point of improvement is the evaluation of the busiest areas of the
hospital in relation to the rules for sending AGVs to charge. The efficiency of the system
could be enhanced by having the AGVs already close to where most tasks start. This
approach considers situations where multiple transports originate from a specific hospital
area during a particular period. Thus, strategically positioning AGVs in those areas could
prove advantageous. For example, there could be a situation in which the majority of

more starting from the hospital’s main building in the afternoon. Therefore, such patterns
could guide the system in dispatching AGVs to their starting locations. This system logic

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This final chapter provides a conclusion for the thesis, addressing the research questions
and highlighting the most important findings.
This thesis used Discrete Event Simulation (DES) to evaluate various AGV task dispatch-
ing logics from the basic to dynamic logics. In fact, the risk-free environment of the virtual
model enables extensive scenario testing without potential negative impacts on the real
system.
As discussed in Chapter 6, more complex dispatching logic significantly enhanced the
performance of the AGV system model. With the defined settings, the system improved its
on-time delivery level from 90.98% with FCFS logic to 99.65% with a dynamic assignment
of tasks. This, coupled with increased AGV Efficiency, demonstrates how various task
dispatching logics influence the performance of the AGV system. This study reveals that
more advanced dispatching logics can yield improved results.
In non-dynamic logic, the dispatch sequence of tasks is primarily influenced by their prior-
ity. Instead, in a dynamic model, it is observed how optimizing task dispatching benefited
the system. The Assignment Problem-based mathematical model, introduced in Section
3.3, proved to be a useful tool. It was particularly beneficial to optimize the allocation
of tasks (jobs) to resources (AGVs) in the study. However, a more complex objective
function, incorporating task priority and time allowances, more accurately represents the
intricacies of a hospital system, considering multiple factors. The dynamic dispatching
approach showed superior results due to the system’s continuous, dynamic recalculations
and the optimization of the mathematical model. This approach offers the potential for
improved performance, even with the same number of AGVs.
Moreover, the simulation enabled the identification of potential system bottlenecks. Upon
88
==PAGE:101==
testing various model scenarios, it became clear that the number of AGVs in use is a criti-
cal factor for successful outcomes. Increasing the AGV count from 22 to 24 in the dynamic
model, improved on-time delivery from 99.65% to 99.89%, indicating a significant reduc-
tion in the number of delays from 26.25 to 7.92. More significantly, employing additional
AGVs decreased the number of delays from 26.25 to 7.92. Hence, using different logics
and running multiple scenarios in simulation assists in pinpointing system bottlenecks. In
fact, while running the same dispatching logic model with a different number of AGVs
showed how this parameter can affect the performances, running different dispatching log-
ics showed other aspects. Despite enhancements to the task dispatching algorithm, the
delay count for priority 1 tasks never reached zero. This indicates that the issue might
not lie within the AGV system’s performance, but could relate to other factors, such as
arrival distribution or slot availability.
To conclude, the project demonstrated how simulation can be applied to evaluate an
AGV system and its logic. Hospital management can leverage this information to assess
system performance under different settings, aiding decisions about whether to upgrade or
install AGV systems. Consulting firms, like NIRAS, could also benefit from this research,
gaining insights into AGV system operations and their evaluation through simulation.
Particularly, the task dispatching logic plays a crucial role in system evaluation, although
it is among several factors to consider.

Appendix
B
Comparison between different rules for FCFS
In this appendix section, we present possible rule combinations for selecting the AGV and
determining its charging location after task completion. We provide a brief description of
each option, followed by the results of key performance indicators (KPIs) from a single
run using the same input data. This initial evaluating approach served as a backup for the
decision of the features to include in the baseline model. Below, the KPI results show a
quantitative preference for the combination of the nearest AGV and the nearest charging
station. This option also presents a more realistic scenario.
B.1
FCFC - First available AGV - Home Node
In this policy, the principle of task dispatching is based on the order of task arrival.
Simply put, the tasks are assigned to AGVs in the sequence they appear, with the first
available AGV taking up the task. This approach offers a straightforward method of task
management. When an AGV is idle, it automatically navigates to its designated home
node to recharge. For this purpose, each AGV is assigned a home node, which ensures an
even distribution of AGVs across the operational field.
B.2
FCFS - First available AGV - Nearest Charging Station
This policy operates similarly to the previous option in terms of task dispatching: tasks
are allocated in the order of their arrival, and the first available AGV is assigned the task.
However, the key difference lies in the approach of recharging. Instead of having each
AGV return to its own home node, an idle AGV seeks out the closes available charging
station. This optimization reduces the total distance traveled by the AGVs, potentially
increasing operational efficiency and battery life.
B.3
FCFC - Nearest available AGV - Home Node
This policy retains the task dispatching rule based on the arrival order but introduces a
change when it comes to task assignment. Instead of the first available AGV taking up the
task, the nearest AGV to the task locations is assigned. This optimization aims to reduce
the total distance traveled by the AGVs for task completion. When an AGV becomes
idle, it reverts to its home node for recharging, maintaining the home node concept from
the first described policy.

B.4
FCFS - Nearest available AGV - Nearest Charging Station
This policy combines the advantages of the previous two optimizations. Task dispatching
is based on the order of arrival, but the assignment is given to the nearest available AGV,
not simply the first one that is free. This optimization works in tandem with the charging
optimization, where an idle AGV proceeds to the nearest charging station, rather than
a fixed home node. This combined optimization approach aims to maximize operational
efficiency by minimizing both task execution and recharging distances.
