Opportunities in Developing a New Prescription System for Veterinary Care
by Anders Wulff KRINGELBACH
The current prescription system in the veterinary area uses paper, faxes, phone calls, and en-
crypted emails. The veterinary prescription system is years behind the human medical prescrip-
tion system. This project explores why the old prescription system is still the same, and if there
are any opportunities in developing a new prescription system for veterinary care.
The research included various types of actor meetings and desk research to diverge the under-
standing of the veterinary area, and its elements.
The research was analysed, and different perspectives of the veterinary area was discovered
and reﬂected on.
The network of elements was synthesised leading to an overarching prediction of which direc-
tions the veterinary area will evolve and what needs a new prescription system might accommo-
date and for which elements.
The project concludes that there are especially two emerging elements in the network that
will inﬂuence the development of a new prescription system for veterinary care. One is a new
prescription system which will only accommodate the needs of veterinary personnel by reducing
the difﬁculties connected to prescribing medicine. The other element will further reduce the needs
for a new prescription system for veterinary personnel, by promoting the sale of medicine directly
from the veterinary clinic.

1.1
Introduction
"The prescription system [in the
veterinary area] has bothered me
for 10 years" (Mette [A])
"I am afraid that nothing will ever
happen if no one just sits down
and ﬁgures it out" (Mette [A])
This was what an informant told me when
she suggested me to look into the prescription
system as a possible project for a thesis. Look-
ing into the prescription system for veterinary
care, it is evident that this prescription system
has been subject to less innovation than in the
human medical area.
Vets can prescribe medicine by: physical
prescriptions, fax prescriptions, call-in pre-
scriptions, and the rarely used option: en-
crypted email prescriptions.
In the human medical area, the doctors
mainly use a state owned prescription system
called the FMK. Doctors can send electronic
prescriptions through the FMK to a prescrip-
tions server that the pharmacies have access
to.
Today the vets are still explicitly excluded
from using the FMK. The vets have been
given permission to send prescriptions by
email in a PDF-format. However it is spec-
iﬁed only to allow encrypted emails, which
means that only few vets make use of it.
The project has explored the reasons why
the the veterinary area is still using this old
prescription system, and how emerging ele-
ments might change that.
1.1.1
Project framing
The project is framed around the veteri-
nary area of Denmark.
More speciﬁcally,
the project revolves mainly around the pets,
health care, and medicine. The pet veterinary
area of Denmark (from now referred to as vet-
erinary area) has some central actors which
help to explain the framing.
These are de-
ﬁned and elaborated in 6.3 (p. 30). The pets
(companion animals, hobby animals) are cen-
tral to the others actors in this area. The pet
owners are the caretakers of the animal, and
customers of the vets. The vets are the ani-
mal doctors that treat the pet when they are
injured or ill, and check their health regu-
larly.
The pet owners are the clients of the
vets, and the the pets are the patients. Phar-
macy personnel is the providers of prescrip-
tion medicine and non-prescription medicine.
Prescriptions are used by the pet owner to
acquire prescription medicine from the phar-
macy. Medicine is a treatment for the pet.
Other actors have been used to frame the area:
e.g. associations, state agencies, administra-
tions, dog registry, different projects.
Some actors and elements have been explic-
itly excluded, but from which information
and inspiration has been drawn: e.g. farmers,
human medicine.

Project backgrounds
The project started with the prescription sys-
tem in veterinary care. Initially, I hoped that
the project could develop into entrepreneur-
ship. The veterinary area was explored to ﬁnd
out the elements and the movements of the

Chapter 2
Literature Review
In the recent years scientists and citizens are
increasingly aware and concerned about an-
timicrobial resistance (AMR) in humans and
production animals.
Awareness has now
been raised towards the pet animal veterinary
area. A study on the spread of pet-associated
zoonotic infections mentions that:
"Patient surveys and epidemio-
logic studies on the topic sug-
gest that the occurrence of pet-
associated disease is low overall.
Owing to a relative absence of re-
portable pathogens and compli-
cating factors (e.g.
non-pet ex-
posure pathways, frequent sub-
clinical shedding by pets),
the
proportion of human disease at-
tributable to pets is unknown, and
any reported frequency of such in-
fections is likely underestimated."
(Stull, Brophy, and Weese 2015)
The extent of which human diseases are de-
rived from pets is unknown, meaning that
current incidents could be both rare or fre-
quent just as well as contained or wide-
spread. What can be derived from this is, that
there is a risk that the problem might be very
severe. If antimicrobial resistant zoonotic dis-
eases become frequent and wide-spread, it
might increase the spread and infect more hu-
mans. The problem might not be severe yet,
and it is still a «What if, then what?»-scenario.
Right now scientists are ﬁguring out how to
avoid the worst case scenario, and one answer
is antimicrobial stewardship (AMS). The pur-
pose of antimicrobial stewardship is to "[...]
preserve important antibiotics for urgent clin-
ical need" (Currie et al. 2018).
Antimicrobial stewardship programs are dif-
ferent initiatives that are put in place to reach
this purpose. The initiatives can be: Guide-
lines, legislation, education, local policies,
standards, structural reasoning, and redis-
tributing prescription privileges (ibid., Hard-
efeldt et al. 2018).
This literature review will discuss the dif-
ferent drivers identiﬁed interactions between
vets and pet owners. Then suggestions to im-
proving this interaction will be presented.
The literature reviewed comes from differ-
ent countries (United Kingdom, Australia,
Canada, and Denmark). In all countries vets
are independent.

The complexity of these problems has some

prove the symptoms. Deﬁning the problem
as «overuse and inappropriate prescription of an-
timicrobials», could apply the solution of inter-
actions leading to appropriate prescriptions.
But even after re-framing the problem, it is
still a complex problem.

3.1
Approaches to Design
Designers map how they understand the con-
text they work with. They should not let oth-
ers dictate how this context should be per-
ceived and socio-technical network should be
mapped (elaborated in section 3.4).
The design process will be iterative and
probably uncertain, but when the process is
over and done, it will be possible to describe
the process that was undergone. The socio-
technical network in which the design pro-
cess has taken place will start at a point from
which the understanding of the context will
be developed (elaborated in section 3.3).
Finally, in order to understand the con-
text and develop the mapping of the socio-
technical network a set of methods will be
used. These methods can be prescriptive or ad
hoc (Cross 2000, p. 47. Most often prescriptive
methods will be adapted to work in the spe-
ciﬁc context as the designers understand it.
The methods are used in order to help the de-
signer doing at least two things: Helping the
designer to think about the network and con-
text in a useful way, and helping the designer
to visualise and share their understanding of
the system and their process at a ﬁxed time or
in a dynamic development (chapter 4, p. 19).
3.2
Performance of everyday ac-
tivities
"The goal for everyday activities is to reach
as rapidly as possible a satisfactory level that
is stable and ’autonomous’" (Ericsson 2008).
These everyday activities cover especially the
secondary activities that actors do at their
work.
The ones that they have to do, but
would prefer not to do. Some people would
maybe perceive reading and writing emails
as something other than their core activities.
Some of these people might prefer not to do
these secondary activities. Typing is one of
the skills that are used to answer an email.
An article states that "[t]he amount of daily
typing by college students is essentially un-
related to current typing speed during a test"
(ibid.).
The skills that are automatised are
helpful for the user in order to spend less ef-
fort to do the task. When skills have been au-
tomated, there is a risk that they will not be
further improved.
"[...A]n individual’s performance
is adapted to the typical situa-
tional demands and is increas-
ingly automated, and they lose
conscious control over aspects of
their behavior and are no longer
able to make speciﬁc intentional
adjustments" (ibid.)
The last part of the statement is termed in
absolution, to which I disagree.
People are
able to consciously consider their automatic
behaviours, but they have to deliberately try
to do this. Increasing autonomy saves effort,
but it decreases or stops development to the
point where the user becomes satisﬁed with
their skills.
Ericsson (2008) suggests that the solution to
learning skills for secondary activities beyond
the automation level is by doing what is called
deliberate practice.
This is in short a way
==PAGE:26==
12

of practising short-comings of a skill deliber-
ately, by putting concious effort on the aspects
of the skill that need improvement. This the-
ory will be used to explain some observations
made during the project.
3.3
Design & Problem-solving
process
Design and problem-solving approaches are
based on lots of different factors. Every per-
son will approach every design-context dif-
ferently.
When teams undergo design pro-
cesses, things are slightly different. Each team
member will individually assess the context,
and will have an idea of a number of ap-
proaches that can be used during the process.
The idea or plan of a process will then likely
look somewhat different to the actual process
when looking back on the ﬁnished project.
Three different reasons are:
(1) Negotiation (Beyer and Holtzblatt 1998,
p. 126-127)
(2) (Understanding of) Context changes
(Cross 2000, p. 7)
(3) Iterative learnings (ibid., p. 7)
Negotiation is central, as the team will have
different understandings of the context at
probably every phase of the projects, and the
dynamic nature of the projects will create it-
erative learnings changing the understanding
of the context for every member once again.
Viewed as a development arena the design
team meetings are "[...]
where negotiation,
conﬂicts, and ideas are exchanged and devel-
oped" (Jørgensen and Sørensen 1999).
The design models and methods used by the
team are then used for at least two reasons:
(a) Communication within the team (Beyer
and Holtzblatt 1998, p. 126-127) and to
actors (ibid., p.211-212)
(b) Thinking,
understanding
and
(re)framing (Cross 2000, p. 7) (Beyer and
Holtzblatt 1998, p. 211-212)
In a design context the two points—(a) and
(b)—are very related. The communication be-
tween the team-members contributes to new
understandings for every team member. And
exactly this is what the negotiation element
of the designing process is about. A design
team will have a degree of alignment—that
is how well the team as a whole understands
the project context. The use of the models and
methods can be approached differently, as the
team can either use them in collaboration or a
subgroup of the team can use the method and
come to an agreement within the subgroup
but for the team.
If the process of using a
model or method is the negotiation, the ﬁnal
«document», «drawing», or «mapping» will be
the agreement.
If the team decides to delegate work of us-
ing a method to a subgroup of the team, there
can be beneﬁts such as being able to spend
more time on other tasks. The team-members
outside the subgroup can spend time doing
other tasks, but the time spent on applying
the method will probably be shorter, as the
number of team-members who have to ne-
gotiate and align understandings is smaller.
However, delegating the use of methods will
not align the understanding of the project for
all team-members, and the collective under-
standing of the project will be less coherent
and therefore more «fragmented».
In some design-contexts the team is actually
a single individual, as it is in this project. In
projects like these the approaches to design-
ing and problem-solving will look somewhat
different. The reason is that the negotiation el-
ement is not incorporated in the same way. A
person can have an internal negotiation as it
happens when a person tries to form an opin-
ion on a subject. It is also feasible that a person
can form opinions quickly from observations
due to conﬁrmation bias.
This internal negotiation will then not ap-
pear «naturally», compared to when a team
engages in discussions and negotiations. This
will result in an alignment that everyone can
accept, or at the very least a compromise that
all team-members can tolerate.
The nego-
tiation between team-members—and maybe
even some co-creating actors—is what en-
sures that the understanding is well-balanced
and elements are not taken for granted.

3.3.1
Wicked and ill-deﬁned problems
The wicked problems (Rittel and Webber
1973) and ill-deﬁned problems (Cross 2000,
p. 14-15) are two slightly different deﬁni-
tions.
Both deﬁnitions describe complex,
open-ended problems with no single true so-
lution (ibid., p. 14-15).
"[...I]t becomes morally objection-
able for the planner to treat a
wicked problem as though it were
a tame one, or to tame a wicked
problem prematurely, or to refuse
to recognize the inherent wicked-
ness of social problems" (Rittel
and Webber 1973)
Wicked problems are large scale problems
in open-ended networks, and "There is no
deﬁnitive formulation of a wicked problem"
(ibid.).
When a solution has been found,
one formulation of the problem has also been
found.
Solutions therefore help to deﬁne
problem spaces.
One of 10 properties of
wicked problems is: "[e]very wicked prob-
lem can be considered to be a symptom of an-
other problem" which indicates that there are
no wrong solutions. Only "good and bad" so-
lutions that change the society (ibid.). Solving
higher level problems can be seen as prefer-
able to curing symptoms.
But higher-level
problems are difﬁcult to deﬁne, and therefore
more difﬁcult to resolve (ibid.). A quote from
Rittel and Webber (1973) questions the beneﬁt
of incremental solutions in a complex network
(ibid.):
"If, however, the problem is at-
tacked on too low a level (an in-
crement), then success of resolu-
tion may result in making things
worse, because it may become
more difﬁcult to deal with the
higher problems." (ibid.)
Incremental solutions to speciﬁc aspects of a
wicked problem can end up hurting the so-
ciety, because it hurts the possibility of solv-
ing other aspects of the problem. This might
be because the incremental solution restricts
the solution space. The incremental solutions
might stop social momentum that is required
make change, as the needs are slightly met
with the incremental solution. A lot of money
could be spend on an incremental solution.
This could make another solution worth less,
just because a lot of money has already be
spend. Incidentally, the incremental solution
also improved the problem for some elements
in the network, making a solution yet again
less valuable. Incremental solutions such as
these might be caused by the designers view-
ing the wicked problem as a tame problem.
The problem with this is explained by another
property of wicked problems (ibid.):
"Every solution to a wicked prob-
lem is a ’one-shot operation’; be-
cause there is no opportunity to
learn by trial-and-error, every at-
tempt counts signiﬁcantly" (ibid.)
As soon as the elements change or emerge
in the socio-technical network, it will make
changes that may lead to the changes in the
wicked problem. This means that when big
changes emerge in a socio-technical network,
the desirability and prioritisation of solutions
might also change.

3.4.2
Enter the Arena
Jørgensen and Sørensen (1999) asks the ques-
tion "[... H]ow should we map the ﬁeld, how
can change be enabled, and how can a re-
structuring of relations be made possible?".
An answer to the question could be develop-
ment arena. Jørgensen and Sørensen (1999)
deﬁnes the development arena as follows:
"A cognitive space that holds to-
gether the settings and relations
that comprise the context for prod-
uct or process development that
includes:
• a number of elements such
as
actors,
artefacts,
and
standards that populate the
arena,
• a variety of locations for ac-
tion, knowledge and visions
that deﬁne the changes of this
space, and
• a set of translations that has
shaped and played out the
stabilization and destabiliza-
tion of relations and arte-
facts."
(ibid.)
In this short deﬁnition of the development
arena theory "product [and] process develop-
ment" (ibid.) is emphasised, however "[...] not
all changes really achieve something new and
not all processes of change are creative and
shape new things." (ibid.).
The term arena is also used in political sci-
ence as political arena, and I would argue that
some features of the development arena the-
ory can be applied to many different social sit-
uations.
A situation could be a person trying to change
a destructive habit, such as alcoholism. The
person decides to map the different elements,
such as people and things that contribute to
the person’s drinking habit. They may try to
relocate themselves in the arena in relation to
these elements in order to help themselves to
stop drinking. The person might also do this
to get closer to an element that can help them
get out of the drinking habit.
Another situation could be a person who has
been in prison. The person will be subject to
change as they will be moved from their nor-
mal situation—the prison arena—to the soci-
ety.
The person might be aware that some
ex-convicts get into criminality again because
they have challenges ﬁnding their way back
into the society. They might want to map the
new arena in order to understand how they
can ﬁt into the arena in a way that they can
avoid getting into criminal activities again.
While the persons in these two situations

Interviewing the actors
During the project unstructured interviews

One example of unstructured interviews are
the "in-depth interviews" (ibid., p. 107). The

the interviewee explain their views on the

This can assist the designers as a
"breath-ﬁrst [...] strategy" (Cross 2000, p. 26)
that diverges the understanding of the context
and network.
The designers "[s]ignal where [they] want
the discussion to go, but give the speaker the
opportunity to take it somewhere else" (Bate
and Robert 2007, p. 85), and the designers
should "recognise that dialogue is discovery
and [that] only partial knowledge will ever
be attained" (ibid., p. 85). The interviewees
can also take on the role as a form of infor-

The focused interviews can be used to "cor-
roborate" (ibid., p. 107) certain facts or under-
standings that the designers have of the con-
text and network. The purpose can also be for
designers to answer («burning») questions, or
simply to get expert opinions or statements
on a subject. These types of interviews can
be important to the designer, as they are often
working within ﬁelds that are new to them.
4.1.2
Roll what Snowball?
The method normally referred to as Roll the
Snowball as described in Lindegaard (2006)
and Jørgensen (2009, p. 49) is a ﬁeld work
approach used to get around a lot of actors.
First, the designers would interview the ac-
tors they know they want to talk to, and the
idea of the method is then to get recommen-
dations of actors to interview next. This can
be any kind of interview. Initially the design-
ers might know some actors that they know
they want to talk to. Then the metaphor could
be that the designers start out with a small
snowball. Then the snowball is rolled until
it is large and the garden has been emptied
of snow—this means that actors have begun
referring to actors that have been previously
interviewed.
An interesting detail in Lindegaard (2006) is
that the method is referred to as Roll a Snow-
ball which could imply a scepticism towards
there being only one possible snowball to
roll—The Snowball.
This relates to the "ill-
deﬁned problems" by Cross (2000). Solutions
to ill-deﬁned problems are only "good or bad,
appropriate or inappropriate and [not] true-
or-false" (ibid.).
In practice this means that there could be
many paths that actors could lead a team of
designers, and it is also the designers’ jobs to
decide the boundaries of the project, so it ﬁts
within the time-frame. Some metaphors here
could be that the designers are recommended
to roll the snowball into the neighbours’ gar-
den. The actors could lead the designers to
unexpected paths that might mean that other
paths can not be taken. Or maybe the snow-
ball will grow until it can not be rolled any

more, even if there is snow left in the garden.
This means that the designers have gathered
so much data, that might make them neglect
some data.
4.1.3
Follow the actors
Lindegaard (2006) and Jørgensen (2009, p. 49)
describe a method called Follow the actors.
This method as a way to approach interviews
that assist the designers in stepping away
from their own framing of the situation, as the
designers can get insights into how the actors
do activities in a real or staged context. This
can free the designers from self-biases.
Lindegaard (2006) describes observation
and participation observation. Participation ob-
servation is a form of observation where the
designers ask questions while they are ob-
serving. There are examples of why this can
be very valuable, as the actors can explain
what they are doing and how they under-
stand it. This will provide the designer with
more perspectives.
The other possibility is
observing without interfering, which will be
elaborated in section 4.1.4.
In Beyer and Holtzblatt (1998, p. 41) the
approach called Contextual Inquiry is pre-
sented.
Contextual Inquiry uses something
called master/apprentice model which em-
phasises that a partnership between the de-
signer and the actor is valuable to get a lot
out of the interaction, as the apprentice (the
designer) can follow the master (actor) while
they are doing their activities in real context
(ibid., p. 42-46). The real context allows the
designer to see what users are actually doing,
as "[...the actors] are not aware of everything
they do or why they do it; they become aware
in the doing." (ibid., p. 43).
This can also be interpreted as that actors
will not be able to truly summarise how they
do an activity in a normal interview. Addi-
tionally, actors will also not be able to show
the designer how they approach an activity in
context if they are not in real context. The rea-
son to this is that when the context is staged,
some of the contextual details are removed
or changed. For instance time-pressure might
not be a factor for actors in a staged contexts.
To summarise, follow the actor can be in staged
or context situations, and the designers can be
either participating or not. Additionally, con-
textual inquiry introduces that the relation-
ship between the designer and the actor also
plays a role in what data can be derived from
the interactions.
4.1.4
Fly on the wall
Fly on the wall describes a type of observation
where the designer is not allowed to interfere
(St. Matthew-Daniel and Kamper 2001). This
might sometimes have a slightly more in situ
effect than a participatory approach, but in
most cases people who know they are being
observed will be affected, where the Fly on the
wall metaphor is lost. However, in an exper-
iment called ’A ﬂy on the wall’ presented by
Mulenburg (2003) they tried something that
deals with this challenge by hiding the ob-
server. The author was placed in a project-
review within their company in order to ob-
serve the dynamics of the meeting. However,
the other people at the table did not know
that the author was there to observe, as they
thought he was just a participant in the meet-
ing.
"Many of the people in the room
never spoke. Some of them could
have been observers like me, but I
don’t know that. I may have been
the only one to observe, although
I hope not." (ibid.)
At the project-review there were many people
who did not know each other, and not every-
one was formally introduced which made it
easier to blend in for the observer. I believe
that hidden observer type of observation can
have some strengths.
However, it does re-
quire speciﬁc types of situations for the ob-
served actors to be unaware that they are be-
ing observed.

some other interesting nuances that have not

4.3.1
Sequence models for users
"Sequences capture the most ba-
sic information about work prac-
tice. Not only do they tell you how
work is really done, they show
how it is structured and the intents
people care about" (ibid., p. 101)
Sequence models provide the designer with
an understanding of activities: activity, inten-
tion, and steps (ibid., p. 96-97). Understand-
ing of sequences can be captured by contex-
tual inquiry, staged observations, detailed in-
terviews, user guides, or a mix. Guides to per-
forming an activity are almost never detailed
enough or an actual representation of how ac-
tivities are performed. However, guides can
reveal the intention of the activity but also the
purpose of the activity. Details of the actual
activities can be captured in contextual obser-
vations, staged observations, and interviews.
Here the details of the actual activities can be
compared to the prescripted sequences from
the guide.
In the end, applying sequence models can
reveal what is essential for the activity and
provide value for the persons doing the activ-
ity. This can then reveal how activities can be
re-framed. Some steps might be possible to
improve, reduce, remove, automate or com-
bine (ibid., p. 259).
Other «inefﬁcient» steps
might be valuable for the persons performing
the task. Removing enjoyable steps or tasks
might be negative for the person.
Getting an understanding of several different
activities might also give an understanding of
repetitions across activities.
These different
activities can then be viewed together to give

6.3.1
The pet owners
This actor is actually a hybrid actor (Jørgensen
and Sørensen 1999) that can have different
roles in the different arenas, and in relation to
the different actors. The content of the term
pet owner, holds the idea that this actor is the
owner of the pet. The pet owner can be thought
of in at least two ways: The formal pet owner
and the practical pet owner.
The formal pet owner is a title of the regis-
tered owner of a dog (mandatory registration)
or another registered pet (elaborated in sec-
tion 6.4.5, p. 58).
The practical pet owner can then be thought
of as the human members of the household
in which the pet lives. Even temporary care-
takers of the pet, if the right social situation
arises, can take on the role as the pet owner
(«Whose pet is this?», to which the caretaker an-
swers: «Mine!»).
In relation to the pet the actor can be the
pet caretaker.
The formal pet owner might
be the primary caretaker, and the rest of the
household can then be secondary caretakers.
In the veterinary arena the actor is a client
or customer and the pet is the patient. Client
is here being deﬁned as a receiver of profes-
sional advice and customised solutions—the
consultation (Fisher 2018). Customer is then
deﬁned as when the actor is only acquiring
products and services (ibid.)—buying pet food
at the veterinary clinic. The actor will mainly be
referred to as client in the veterinary arena.
In the pharmacy arena the actor is a client
or a customer. The actor will mainly be re-
ferred to as a client, as the actor will receive
professional advice.
This actor that lives in the same household
and takes care of the pet in daily life will be
termed pet owner in most cases. This is use-
ful in the context of pet registration, pet well-
being, and expenses of the pet (which will
usually be paid for by the pet owner, but not
necessarily). The pet owner will be referred to
as the client in the contexts where it is espe-
cially ﬁtting in relation to veterinary personnel
and pharmacy personnel.
6.3.2
The veterinary personnel
The veterinary personnel is generally thought
of as 3 professions that plays different roles
in a veterinary clinic: The vet, the veterinary
nurse, and the veterinary clinic assistant. Not
all roles have to be occupied by a clearly de-
ﬁned actor.
In a veterinary clinic, an edu-
cated vet is a «mandatory» profession, in the
sense that a vet can take the role of the veteri-
nary nurse and veterinary clinic assistant, but
the other way is not legally possible (Danske
Love 2018, §6). For instance, only vets are al-
lowed to write prescriptions and operate on
animals (ibid., §2). It may be more ﬁnancially
practical to have the vets doing the assign-
ments that only they can handle, as the vets
earn money through consultations with their
clients (pet owners).
The veterinary nurse assists the vet in con-
sultations, surgery, births, laboratory works,

rated in section 6.4.4, p. 51).
In general, the vets will be speciﬁed, and
the veterinary personnel will refer to veteri-
nary nurses and veterinary clinic assistants.
6.3.3
The pharmacy personnel
The actors working in the pharmacy will
mostly be referred to as pharmacy person-
nel (a sort of punctualisation). The reason is,
that the personnel I met in the pharmacy I
visited had a kind of ﬂuent roles, except for
one task (which was to send data of what
medicine was sold during the day to Lægemid-
delstyrelsen—The Danish Medicine Agency.
So while there is a lack of empirical data
to support a meaningful division into roles,
it does not provide any advantages due to
the relationships with the other actors. When
pet owners go to the pharmacy to get their
prescription medicine they would probably
not be able to distinguish between pharma-
cists and pharmaconomists, at least based on
the observations I have made at the pharma-
cies I have been to. The fact that the Phar-
macy Union only distinguishes between fe-
male and male clothing in their design guide,
could indicate that this observation might be
general, but it is difﬁcult to say (Apoteker-
foreningen.dk 2018).
In normal situations when veterinary clin-
ics call pharmacies to order medicine, the
vets will be indifferent whether they speak to
pharmacists or pharmoconomists. This actor
will therefore be called pharmacy personnel.
6.3.4
The prescriptions
The prescriptions can be viewed as a phe-
nomenon:
the vet has granted the pet
owner/client the permission to collect the
medicine prescribed by the vet.
The phar-
macy personnel is given permission to hand
out the prescription medicine when provided
with a prescription and the identiﬁcation
needed (often just a name).
By framing the prescription as a phe-
nomenon, the prescription can be thought to
even exist if vets just hand out the medicine
in the veterinary clinic, without an actual pre-
scription. The vets consult, diagnose, permit
the use of medicine, and hand out medicine.
The prescription part is the permission to buy
prescription medicine and it is «hidden» for
the pet owner, and as a phenomenon the pre-
scription exists.
The prescription can also be viewed as non-
human actors, or artefact. There are different
artefacts: The physical prescriptions, the fax
prescriptions, the calling-in prescriptions, and
the encrypted email prescription.
The different types of prescriptions have
some different properties (elaborated in sec-
tion 6.4.6, p. 61). One of the different proper-
ties is that the physical prescription is given
to the pet owner, for them to take care of,
and they have to bring the prescription to
the pharmacy to get the medicine. The other
types of prescriptions (fax, call-in, encrypted
email) are send directly from vet to pharmacy.
Pet owners can only get in touch with the
prescriptions through the pharmacy. The pet
owner can access the contents of the prescrip-
tion by calling the pharmacy, for instance to
make sure that the prescription is there, and to
check if the prescribed medicine is in storage.
For the pet owners, the prescription might
some times be thought of as the product of
a consultation, which then effects the vets to
view it the same way (This is explained in the
literature review, chapter 2, p. 5).

The Ballad of Sally the Dog
Returning to the case of Sally the dog that
was mistakenly diagnosed with terminal
and untreatable cancer. Afterwards she was
re-diagnosed with some other conditions
that could be terminal, if left untreated.
In the speciﬁc case Sally was diagnosed
with untreatable cancer the ﬁrst day, due
to the symptom that she coughed up blood.
When they scanned her, it looked as if she
had lumps in her lungs and liver. The doc-
tor could not understand that she was feel-
ing quite well except for the coughing of
blood.
Nonetheless she was given about
a month to live. Ib, one of Sally’s owners,
then brought her home.
The next day, the vet called Anne Lise and
Ib again and suggested them to come for a
new scanning by a heart specialist just to be
sure, as the vet was a puzzled about Sally
feeling quite well. Ib found this behaviour
a bit repulsive, as he felt that the vet was
trying to pump more money out of him.
He was convinced, that Sally had been di-
agnosed with terminal cancer, and that she
was untreatable, and that more treatment
and scanning was useless.
His initial re-
sponse was to decline the proposal from the
vet. However Anne Lise trumped Ib’s sug-
gestion and decided to try it out. It turned
out that Sally did in fact not have cancer but
water in the lungs, enlarged liver and heart,
and a heart valve issue, seen mainly with
smaller dogs. This puts forward a number
of reﬂections:
• What was the cancer diagnosis built upon? If Sally had died without the follow-up
scanning, would this episode wrongly have been archived as a cancer case? Later sim-
ilar cases would then refer to and build on this case as cancer, and then again it might
wrongly diagnose another episode. It can be suspected that wrong diagnoses can make
it more difﬁcult to diagnose future cases.
• Can a disease registry help to prove or disprove that conditions such as the heart valve
issues actually is seen mainly with smaller dogs.
• Ib’s reaction to the vet’s call the day after the diagnosis might be at bit controversial.
However he attributes this point of view to the fact the veterinary area is a liberal pro-
fession, so the more money he spends, the more they earn. Ib also mentioned, that his
friends’ ﬁrst reaction as he told them Sally had been to the vet was: "How expensive
was it?". This shows that it can be very difﬁcult to be a vet in a veterinary clinic. On
one hand there are a ﬁxed costs connected to having a veterinary clinic, and dynamic
costs following with every consultation. These are all expenses that can be seen in the
pricing of getting a consultation with a veterinary.
———————————————————
Analysis and reﬂection:
Limits of the current data: The current data are
quite limited. It is difﬁcult to asses the scale of
different issues such as inbred breeds and the
spread of antimicrobial resistance. The vets
also had trouble ﬁnding out what was wrong
with Sally, but fortunately a heart specialist
found out what was actually wrong. The well-
being of pets, the spread of antimicrobial re-
sistance, and plain expensive misdiagnoses of
pets are several good reasons why better data
is needed. The solution could of course be the
disease registry. Data from a new prescription
system could also assist in medicine related
research.

The deliberate practice theory explains, to
some degree, why people will stop becoming
better at using an IT-system they work with
every day. The idea is that in order to get bet-
ter to a skill that you are satisﬁed with, you
have to decide to practice deliberately. The
core activity of Mette [D] and her colleagues
is to provide great service to the pet and pet
owner, and not to work with the IT-system.
But the IT-system is nonetheless an integrated
part of her daily life.
So the veterinary personnel will become
better at using their IT-system until a cer-
tain point when they will stop progressing.
The reason that they stop progressing is most
likely because working with the IT-system is
only one of several secondary activities they
have to work with every day. It can be dif-
ﬁcult to improve a secondary activity when
the schedule in veterinary clinic is busy. This
and the deliberate practice theory (ibid.) can
provide an explanation to why not everyone
becomes experts in the IT-system they work
with every day.
———————————————————
Analysis and reﬂection:
Why some people become better at secondary ac-
tivities: I have a hypothesis on why some peo-
ple end up being experts at secondary activ-
ities.
Even if these activities are relatively
complicated, these people are practising these
secondary activities deliberately. The reason
that they are practising this deliberately is
interesting.
I hypothesise that people who
are obsessed with doing tasks more efﬁciently
are more likely to become better at using IT-
systems. There are also people who tinker a
lot with different things, such as a Veterinary
IT-system.
However, in order to let workers and users
focus on core activities in their job and the
secondary activities that they enjoy doing, we
can design in order to reduce time needed
for un-enjoyable secondary activities. The re-
sources can be released by automating a lot of
sequences in working with IT-systems, as we
expect that most workers are uninterested in
improving their proﬁciency in skills used in
secondary activities, if they are satisﬁed with
the level of their skill.
Another possibility could be doing gam-
iﬁcation (Wikipedia 2018), as it might in-
crease the interest in doing secondary activi-
ties faster.

Dog-chips are chips inserted in necks of

in order to ﬁnd the owners of lost dogs. The
chips are scanned using special chip-scanning
devices (Virum Dyreklinik 2018). In Denmark
this chipping of dogs is the effect of a regula-

Dogs (Hundeloven) dictates that all dogs must
be marked (chip or ear tattoo) and registered
within 8 weeks after being born (Hundeloven
2018).

• Integration to Other Systems

Crossing systems & Registries
There are several other animal registries that

(appendix 5).
There is a Europe-wide co-

Dog registries in Europe in the hope that ev-

roPetNet 2018). Similarly, there are other Dan-

registries: Dyreregisterportalen (2018) (it is
currently off-line, 02/07-2018) and Chipregis-

Reasons for the current unchanged prescrip-
tion system
To understand why the prescription system
is as it is, the reasons for implementing FMK
and the prescription server in the human area
must be understood. The FMK and the pre-
scription server was originally implemented
because some some pharmacists were falsi-
fying the physical prescriptions in order to
get money from the state health insurance
(Trekroner Apotek [F]). So the state had a di-
rect economic incentive to create the FMK,
which was to save money on prescriptions.
Compared to the veterinary area, the state has
no direct economic reason to spend money
on animal medicine in general, so therefore
they are not interested in a vet prescription
server. Therefore, either there has to be pre-
sented some indirect incentives and/or wel-
fare reasons for the state to allocate resources
to this area.
———————————————————
Analysis and reﬂection:
Public healthcare?
Anne Lise [B] suggested
that pets health should be supported simi-
larly to humans.
However, allocating a lot
of resources to create a prescription system
is a difﬁcult subject. On one hand, pets are
something that only some of us have, and that
most of us have for enjoyment. So it would
be controversial if the state would spend too
much money on a new prescription system.
This controversy is probably one of the rea-
sons why there is no public health care sup-
port for pets. On the other side, there is a se-
rious risk that pets are going to contribute to
antimicrobial resistance. So even though pet-
sourced antimicrobial resistance is not every-
body’s fault, it will deﬁnitely be everybody’s
problem.
Possible rebound effects: On one side we do not
know how bad the pet-associated antimicro-
bial resistance problem is, but that in itself is
also a problem. The data is insufﬁcient to con-
clude anything. On the other side, we have
to keep on doing antimicrobial stewardship
programs to make sure the issue is not getting
worse.
This is one of the reasons why this prob-
lem is wicked. There are probably many ways
to improve how vets interact with prescrip-
tions. The question is: If it is easier to pre-
scribe medicine, will more medicine be used?
Alternatively, what might happen is that the
vets will prescribe more medicine, but use
less from their own clinic. For instance, right
now there are no data supporting if vets are
more likely to give broad-spectrum antimicro-
bials from their clinic, if the alternative is to
prescribe small-spectrum antimicrobials and
then send the pet owner to the pharmacy.
But if this is the case, a new prescription sys-
tem might increase the probability of vets us-
ing prescriptions rather than ordering to the
clinic.
These are only questions, but they are some of
the questions that makes it difﬁcult to suggest
a feasible solution that takes all the perspec-
tives into consideration.

7.1
Why it should be created
This section presents some of the argument
why a new prescription system should be cre-
ated. There are several of these elements that
have reasons for and against, and the reasons
against will be presented in section 7.2 (p. 85).
The elements that represent a reason why a
new prescription system should be created.
• Users require change
• Package sizes and availability seeks
data
• Antimicrobial stewardship needs re-
search and society deserves control
7.1.1
Users require change
The project began with an informant that had
been annoyed with the prescription system
in veterinary care for 10 years.
The needs
for a new prescription system have been dis-
covered through observations, statements, in-
terviews, desk research, and a lot of careful
thinking. The current prescription system still
uses very old technologies, so the sequences
are comprehensive.
Because of this, vets are avoiding the use of
prescriptions by ordering medicine for their
veterinary clinic. When the vet does have to
prescribe medicine, they and the pharmacy
personnel will have to spend too much time
administering prescriptions. The pet owners
are also quite bothered with it, being either re-
stricted to a speciﬁc pharmacy or to a physical
prescription.
There might be enough reasons here to try
and pursue a solution to this problem, sim-
ply from the users’ interactions.
However,
it turns out that veterinary personnel, phar-
macies, and pet owners have challenges that
are more pressuring, such as IT-systems,
medicine availability, and package sizes. But
just because other issues are more important
to some actors it does not mean that the pre-
scription system is not also an opportunity
worth chasing.
Why a new prescription system would mat-
ter
A prescription system that is more mod-
ern, and probably developed to work with
internet technologies could reduce the time
wasted on creating prescriptions.
The pet owners need a prescription sys-
tem where are restricted to neither a speciﬁc
pharmacy nor a physical prescription.
But
the medicine availability at the pharmacies is
low, so there is a trade-off. However, the pet
owner could call the speciﬁc pharmacies and
ask them to order the medicine, as the phar-
macy personnel might be able to see the pre-
scription through a system. So dealing with
pet owners’ needs still require some careful
thought.
The veterinary personnel needs a pre-
scription system where it takes shorter time to
get the prescription to the pet owner or phar-
macy personnel. However, they are also in-
terested in that pet owners have good experi-
ences with this prescription system.
The pharmacy personnel is interested in a
system where it is easy to get the information
on the prescription into the pharmacy system.
They are also interested in a system where the
pet owner has to call them less and have the
medicine available when the pet owners come
to the pharmacy.

The
reasons why medicine packages are too big
can be discussed, but more data on the needs
might help.
It bothers pet owners when the medicine is
not available at the pharmacy.
They might
have to go twice or wait until the next day
to get the medicine they need.

There is a need for more research on the
spread of antimicrobial resistance. A new pre-

of for instance a disease registry, which could
help to be the basis for research. The society
could be very negatively affected if antimicro-
bial resistance becomes very severe.
Why a new prescription system would mat-

A new prescription system based on us-

It might even develop a better

data to a registry. A prescription system saves
vets time and they will upload data to a reg-

Data from both a prescription system and
a disease registry could contribute to a better
understanding of how antimicrobials are used
and the scale of antimicrobial resistance. Bet-
ter data and research could contribute to leg-
islations or initiatives that improve antimicro-
bial stewardship.
7.2
Why it cannot and should not
be created
This section presents some of the reasons why
speciﬁc solutions to creating a new prescrip-
tion system can and should not be created.
The section will also evaluate some of the his-
toric reasons why some of the possible solu-
tions have not been created. The elements that
represent a reason that a new prescription sys-
tem can and should not be made:
• Veterinary habits
• Other pressing issues
• Money, money, money
• Legislation for politics’ sake
7.2.1
Veterinary habits
Whatever the reason is, it can be seen that vets
have medicine in their clinics. Vets apparently
do not use prescriptions that much. So when
someone asks: «The prescription system in the
veterinary is so old, can they get a new one?», an
answer could be: «Well they are barely using
it». This is an inﬁnite loop for the vets. The
prescription system is bad, so the vets will
not use it.
So the prescription system does
not get improved, and the vets will not use it.
Some vets also see the act of giving pet own-
ers medicine during the consultation as an im-
portant tradition.
Why a new prescription system will not be
prioritised
Some vets will keep ordering medicine
from the pharmacies as long as it is easier.
This is a reason, even if it is just a symptom,
for the state or developers not to invest in a
new prescription system, as too few are using
it now. A better system might be used more.

7.2.2
Other pressing issues
From the vets’ perspectives there are several
challenges in the veterinary area.
For in-
stance: Interactions with a bad IT-system (al-
though they might be improving now), acqui-
sition of pet owners, providing better service
to pet owners, reducing time spent on non-
consultation tasks, and acquiring new scan-
ners and other equipment. These challenges
are all priorities as well. The current prescrip-
tion system might even be more annoying for
the veterinary clinic assistants and nurses if
they are the ones preparing the prescription,
rather than the vets.
The vets are the ones
that earn money from consultations, so it can
be imagined that reducing the wasted time of
vets would be a priority.
Why a new prescription system will not be
prioritised
The vets will invest in other priorities be-
fore they invest a lot in a new prescription sys-
tem. A solution would have to be quite cheap
for them to use and to implement.
7.2.3
Money, money, money
For the state, there is probably not a lot of
money so save from creating a prescription
system.
There are no well-fare reasons (ex-
cept for pet owners, veterinary personnel, and
pharmacies) or indirect expenses that a new
prescription system would improve. It can be
seen as a bit problematic to support such a
seemingly «nice-to-have» and niche issue that
only covers pet owners, vets, and pharmacies.
Because the prescriptions are not being
used much by the vets, there might not be
many of reasons for companies to develop a
new prescription system. There might also be
worries that the state will have to monopolise
the prescription server if new needs arrive in
the future (such as a very strict control with
antimicrobials).
Why a new prescription system will not be
prioritised
A new prescription system paid for by
the state would be not be prioritised for eco-
nomic reasons. The state has almost no direct
economic interest in the veterinary area com-
pared to the human medical area. The reasons
why a new prescription system should be
paid for by the state would then likely be be-
cause of well-fare improvements or to reduce
indirect expenses that for instance antimicro-
bial resistance could cause.
It is likely that
the price of developing and operating a state-
ﬁnanced prescription system would have to
be quite low to be feasible.
A new prescription system paid for and
operated by a company would be done only
if there is something to earn.
This might
be a possibility if the socio-technical network
changes.
7.2.4
Legislation for politics’ sake
Right now the legislations say that vets have
to use physical, fax, call-in, or encrypted
emails for prescriptions. Pets and vets are ex-
plicitly omitted from using the FMK. How-
ever, vets have not been explicitly omitted
from using the prescription server. The ques-
tion is if there are any political reasons why
the vets are only allowed to use the 4 types of
prescriptions mentioned above.
Why a new prescription system will not be
prioritised
These legislations make it difﬁcult for
companies to prioritise developing a prescrip-
tion system that are technically outside the
legislation, as there is a risk that it will not be
approved by the legislators. However, there
may be some workarounds that comply with
current legislations (see section 7.3.1, p.
87
and 7.6.3, p. 89).

Why it can be created

why a new prescription system can be cre-
ated. The elements that represent a possibility
that a new prescription system can be created:
• The encrypted email prescription
• Legislations for technicalities’ sake

7.3.1
The encrypted email prescription
According to the interviewees, the encrypted
emails are not a widely used method of pre-

to implement it. The encrypted part was added
recently to increase security of the informa-

However, it is possible to set up en-

Why a new prescription system is possible
The encrypted emails might be difﬁcult for
veterinary clinics to set up. However, there
are possibilities for developers to create possi-

be solutions on their way (see section 7.6.3, p.

7.3.2
Legislations
for

The current legislations might be in place
only to accommodate the current possibilities
(physical, fax, call-in, and encrypted email
prescriptions) that are viewed as safe. If leg-
islations are easy to change, for instance by
proving that a new solution would be good
and safe, companies might try to develop a
new prescription system. The challenge here
is to make companies develop something,
even if they are not sure it will be possible to
sell the prescription system to vets and phar-
macies.
Why a new prescription system is possible
If the legislations are «only» there to make
sure that prescriptions are handled in a re-
sponsible way, there might be a possibility for
a company to make partnerships with Dan-
ish Veterinary Association and Pharmacy As-
sociation for the purpose of making continual
negotiations on requirements and restrictions
while developing a possible new prescription
registry.

• Disease registry
• Feral Cat Invasion
• Fear of antimicrobial resistance
7.4.1
Disease registry
The disease registry project has been on-going
for a few years now, and the last updates I
have seen are from 2016. It seems that the de-
velopment is moving on slowly, but some of
the primary criteria was to get vets to accept
the disease registry.
How a new prescription system could help
If it is possible to combine disease registry
with a new prescription system, the weak-
nesses of the disease registry could be re-
duced. Depending on how everything works
out vets might be willing to spend some
money on a prescription system, even though
some of the other arguments I have presented
propose that this would be difﬁcult.
How-
ever, if the vets would spend money on a
prescription system and disease registry, this
could also help with some of the funding chal-
lenges that the disease registry project is fac-
ing (Meyer and Sandøe 2015, p. 22).
7.4.2
Feral Cat Invasion
A new prescriptions system will have an in-
direct effect on this issue through the disease
registry and a cat registry. The number feral
cats is about 650.000 now, and it is estimated
to be rise (Det danske katteregister 2018c).
The issues are that feral cats multiply, and
therefore more cats needs to be neutralised.
The cat registry will be used for keeping check
on the animals, and enabling authorities to
handle the feral cats.
How a new prescription system could help
Since neutralising cats is performed by
vets and costs money, not many people will
take responsibility for doing this to feral cats.

The idea is that data on neutralising cats can

7.4.3
Fear of antimicrobial resistance
If pet-associated antimicrobial resistance be-

A new prescription that can gather data

7.5.1
Veterinary settlement
The veterinary settlement might be more
problematic than it seems. There are different
possible versions, as the negotiating parties
are still negotiating the speciﬁcs of the veteri-

only the vets will be allowed to divide pack-
ages of medicine for sale, and the pharmacies
will not be allowed to. This could have quite a
big inﬂuence on how vets are going to interact
with prescriptions.
Why this will make change difﬁcult
If vets are allowed to divide packages of
medicine while the pharmacy personnel is
not it could make some changes.
The pet
owners will prefer to pay the right amount
of money for their medicine. The examples
showed some of Sally’s medicine with more
than half of the package left. This means that
pet owners might be able to pay haft price for
the medicine if they buy it from the vet (ei-

medicine for them). This also effects vets, as
it will be good service to help pet owners to
save money on medicine. This could have big
effects on the creation of a new prescription
system as the vets might order medicine from
the pharmacy rather than sending prescrip-
tions to pet owners. This might reduce the use
of prescriptions even further, and make a new
prescription system even less attractive.
How will veterinary medicine producers re-
act if this veterinary settlement is going to re-
duce their revenue?
It can be discussed if
medicine producers will raise the prices of
the medicine as a reaction to medicine pack-
ages being divided. There are relatively few
producers of veterinary medicine (Apoteker-
foreningen [H]), so the veterinary medicine
producers might have some freedom in decid-
ing the prices. Under all circumstances it will
be interesting to see what consequences this
veterinary settlement will have.

8.2.2
Incremental solutions to wicked
problems in other areas
The ﬁndings from this projects among other
have been, that the incrementation of solu-
tions on a small scale might end up damaging
the efforts on the large scale. This quote was
also used in the theory:
"If, however, the problem is at-
tacked on too low a level (an in-
crement), then success of resolu-
tion may result in making things
worse, because it may become
more difﬁcult to deal with the
higher problems." (Rittel and Web-
ber 1973)
During the project I realised that many ele-
ments in the network could have potential re-
bound effects on the development of a new
prescriptions system.
Meanwhile, the way
development of a new prescription system
would be solved would also have rebound ef-
fects on other elements. These rebounds ef-
fects are some of the trade-offs that design-
ers will have to choose between. However,
sometimes only the incremental solutions are
economically, politically, or technically feasi-
ble. In this case the good solution would have
been one that takes possibilities of antimicro-
bial resistance issues in the future into consid-
eration.
For many years some sustainability innova-
tions in the car industry have been incremen-
tal.
The cars became smaller, more fuel ef-
ﬁcient, and therefore less damaging for the
environment. The implications of this might
very well be that more people can effort to
buy a car and that it is cheaper to drive.
This is still happening after electric cars have
been introduced. Hybrid cars and small super

petroleum efﬁcient cars that are actually less
damaging for the environment than the cur-
rent electronic cars are still being introduced
and produced.
Looking at the medium large scale, the hy-

postponing the transitioning to electric cars.
But in the large scale, development of elec-

8.2.3
Antimicrobial resistance in the
farming area
The livestock farming area of Denmark is in
the media from time to time because of an-
timicrobial resistance problems. There was an
article as late as the 26th of July 2018 (Erik-
sen and Himmelstrup 2018) about increas-
ing use of an antimicrobial called Neomycin
in pig production. Neomycin is in a group
of antimicrobials that recently changed sta-
tus to being a "critically important group of
anti[microbials] for the treatment of humans".
The extensive use of antimicrobials can cause
development of antimicrobial resistance.
According to Michael Pedersen (chairman
of Danish Society for Clinical Microbiology),
the risks are that people will die because the
hospitals usually use an antimicrobial close to
Neomycin to start treating humans who are in
lethal danger until the doctors can diagnose
which speciﬁc infection is making the person
ill. And because the infection might be resis-
tant to Neomycin, the emergency treatment
will not work either.
Torben Hauskov (chairman of Danish Pig
Producers) points out that prohibiting the use
of Neomycin can cause animal abuse, as the
pig producers will be unable to treat ill ani-
mals.
The question is if design thinking and net-
work analysis mentioned in the literature re-
view can help to support antimicrobial stew-
ardship programmes even further.
One of the reasons that this might be quite
essential is that even though there are guide-
lines for the use of antimicrobials in this live-
stock farming area, there are still systemic
problems (Veterinærforlig 2017).
For exam-
ple it is quite questionable that there is a rise
in the use of Neomycin in the pig produc-
tion, while similar antimicrobials are also be-
ing used in human medicine as a critical an-
timicrobial. The question is if the current level
of communication between the legislators of
the human medical area and the veterinary
area is sufﬁcient.
8.2.4
Implementing IT-systems in the
human medical area
The implementation of the Sundhedsplatform
(The Health Platform) was recently investigated
by Rigsrevisionen (Rigsrevisionen 2018). Two
conclusions were that the users were not con-
sulted during the testing of the system, and
that user learning from health personnel was
neglected when estimating the winnings of
implementing the system.
There is a discrepancy between the devel-
opment of the IT-system and the understand-
ing of the users.
For instance, the doctors
are now expected to write everything into the
journal instead of using the health secretaries.
This has reduced the number of patients that
the doctors can treat during a day. There is
also a big discrepancy between how develop-
ers understand the Danish system, an exam-
ple is that there is a billing module (Landex
2017).
The implementation of Sundhedsplatformen
has failed to incorporate that there has to be
a coherence between the SOFT-dimensions of
a workspace: elements/actors, space, organ-
isation, ﬁnance, and technology. The imple-
mentation was a sudden and radical shift. In-
stead of easing the users into learning the sys-
tem and adapt their workpractices slowly to
the changes. A sudden implementation also
removes the possibility of learning from feed-
back.
The interesting difference between the hu-
man medical area and the veterinary area is

8.4
Conclusions
The project concludes that there are especially
two emerging elements in the network that
will inﬂuence how the development of a new
prescription system for veterinary care will
develop:
(1) The coming veterinary settlement which will
allow vets, but pharmacy personnel, to divide
packages of medicine in order to sell parts of
a package to pet owners.
This will reduce
the use of the current prescription system and
limit the need for a new system further
(2) The developers of veterinary IT-systems are de-
veloping an encrypted email feature for their pre-
scription modules which will satisfy the needs
of veterinary personnel, but it will be unlikely
that any other actor in the veterinary area will
have their needs satisﬁed.
Simultaneously,
momentum for changing the prescription sys-
tem will be dimmed as a result of veterinary
personnel having their needs satisﬁed.
The veterinary personnel will likely be sat-
isﬁed with their prescription system when
these two emerging elements has been desta-
bilised and later reconﬁgured the network.
The integration of encrypted email features will
probably be much better than the current
ways of prescribing medicine, which will re-
duce the pains associated with prescribing
medicine for veterinary personnel.
The
veterinary
settlement
will
make
changes that reduce the amount of prescrip-
tions in veterinary care, which will further
reduce the needs for a new prescription sys-
tem.
The needs of other actors/elements in the vet-
erinary area might keep pushing for devel-
opment of a prescription system to get their
needs satisﬁed. The need for a Disease reg-
istry to monitor pet welfare and antimicro-
bial resistance might not have to be combined
with the new prescription system, but the dis-
ease registry might still be developed as a par-
allel system.
