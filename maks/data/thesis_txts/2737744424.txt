Abstract
With the increasing number of UGVs operating in urban areas worldwide, the need for
accurate GNSS positioning also increases. This thesis describes this growing need and
presents an image-based sky classification algorithm applied to real world test data using
an UGV and a car on the campus of the Technical University of Denmark and the cities
of Lyngby and Copenhagen.
An algorithm was created using machine learning and image analysis techniques to cre-
ate a binary sky segmentation mask. It combines the Gaussian Mixture Model with im-
age pre-processing (blur, color spaces) and post-processing (removal of smaller blobs,
morphological operations). The algorithm has been test with an UGV and in urban en-
vironments during a variety of weather and lighting conditions, thus proving its viability.
The processing requirements for the presented algorithm are likely feasible for real time
automation in future work.

1.1
Motivation
What does the city of the future look like? Starting in the late 19th century with writers
like Edward Bellamy, Karel Čapek, and Thea von Harbou, people have never stopped
dreaming of futuristic cities. What all these visions have in common is the existence of
robots. Today, we are already there. Robots are not only working in their fixed position in
a factory anymore; they are actively navigating our streets. First autonomously operating
Unmanned Grounded Vehicles (UGVs) deliver food on campuses in the US and in first
cities in Europe [2]. Other robots have been developed to collect gum or cigarette butts
on the street [3].
Figure 1.1: Capra Hircus UGV. Picture Source: [3].
In September 2023, the European Space agency (ESA) launched a project to create digi-
tal twins of cities with the help of UGVs. The resulting autonomous robot for laser scanning
and inspections (ARLI) will create accurate 3D measurements of its surroundings using a
light detection and ranging (LiDAR) sensor. The project is run by the Department of Space
Research and Technology of the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The scope of
this research project is to build an autonomous UGV based on the Capra Hircus (see Fig-
ure 1.1) that creates and maintains a digital twin of the city of Aarhus, Denmark. The digital
twin is supposed to optimize city management functions like the detection of potholes or
trash and the monitoring of vegetation. The autonomous UGV will regularly navigate the
city to continuously update the digital twin. This will allow differences between the existing
and the updated version to be identified. [4]
But even though the first UGVs are on our streets, there are still some obstacles in au-
tonomous unmanned ground navigation that need to be overcome. Autonomous vehicles

rely on multiple navigation possibilities such as Global navigation satellite system (GNSS)
signals, odometers, or LiDAR sensors. The accuracy of positioning can degrade when
updates from these systems, particularly GNSS, are compromised. This degradation can
occur due to obstructions like tall buildings and trees, multipath effects where signals re-
flect off surfaces, and atmospheric conditions that affect signal propagation. [5]
At the moment, small positioning errors may still be manageable since other road users
can easily adapt to autonomous UGVs because there are not many of them on the road.
However, the more autonomous vehicles on the roads in our cities, the more precise their
positioning needs to be. Inaccurate positioning can also significantly impact the UGV’s
task of creating a digital twin. To maintain the digital twin the collected LiDAR data must
be consistently comparable after each data acquisition run. Positioning inaccuracies can
lead to misalignment in LiDAR mapping, resulting in inaccurate digital twins and flawed
city management functions. This leads directly to the problem statement.
1.2
Problem Statement and Objective
To get a accurate position using GNSS signals a receiver needs at least four satellites
in direct line of sight (LOS) [6]. This is easy to achieve in rural areas, but in urban en-
vironments there are many obstacles blocking the direct signal path of a satellite: high
buildings, street lights, trees, et cetera.
Figure 1.2: GNSS signal reception in an urban environment. (Author created figure in-
spired by [7]).
The GNSS signals that reach a receiver, especially in an urban environment, can be
divided in four categories (see Figure 1.2). There are direct LOS signals (green) and
blocked signals of not-line of sight (NLOS) satellites (red). There is also a phenomena
called multipath, where a signal reaches the antenna via two or more paths [6]. In the
above image that would refer to the signal of the green and yellow line, where the signal
from the same satellite reaches the receiver as a direct signal, but also as a signal reflected
by a building’s surface (yellow). There is also the possibility of the signal being diffracted
by an obstacle, like a building or a tree (blue). Reflected or diffracted signals can also
originate from an NLOS satellite and thus lead to inaccurate positioning. This problem
needs to be addressed in order to create reliable and precise positioning of UGVs in
urban environments.

A relatively simple and cost-effective solution is to improve the GNSS signal with an sky-
pointing camera positioned next to the antenna. These images of the sky from the re-
ceiver’s point of view can be used to segment the captured images of the surroundings
into sky and non-sky areas. The GNSS signals can then be linked to the image to elimi-
nate satellite signals that are not in a direct LOS. A flowchart describing this process can

be seen in Figure 1.3. The images used to describe this process were created as part of
this work and will be explained in more detail later on.
The objective of this thesis to develop an algorithm to create sky segmentation masks to
eliminate diffracted, reflected and NLOS GNSS satellite signals.
The algorithm should work in different weather and lighting situations. This is particularly
important for its application in Denmark, which lies in the North Temperate Zone at a lati-
tude of approximately 56°North. Denmark’s climate is characterized by frequent changes
in weather, ranging from sunny days to overcast, rainy, and even snowy periods. Ad-
ditionally, Denmark’s location results in significant seasonal variations in daylight, with
long summer and short winter days. Testing the algorithm under these varying conditions
ensures its robustness and reliability. The goal is to create an algorithm capable of main-
taining accurate GNSS positioning throughout the year, regardless of weather or time of
day.
In addition, it should be designed efficiently so that it only requires a small amount of com-
puting capacity to allow real time processing. Real-time processing ensures that the UGV
quickly and accurately identifies and responds to its surroundings, maintaining reliable
GNSS positioning even in dynamic and challenging urban environments. By minimizing
the computational load, the algorithm can run on the UGV’s onboard systems without
extensive hardware upgrades. This not only enhances the UGV’s performance but also
makes the solution more cost-effective and scalable, allowing for widespread deployment
across various urban settings.
1.3
Outline
The thesis is structured in the following way:
Chapter 2 discusses the current state-of-art of the research regarding sky segmentation
using machine learning and non-machine learning algorithms.
Chapter 3 gives a brief overview over the most important technological concepts this work
is based on. It covers the theory behind the image pre-processing methods, the concept
of GNSS and classification with machine learning.
Chapter 4 provides an insight into the methodology of the work, including the hardware

Literature Review
Reflection, diffraction or even complete blockage - the urban environment presents many
obstacles to the reception and accuracy of GNSS signals. That’s why this issue has been
the subject of numerous research papers. The literature review presented in this chapter
aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the current state-of-the-art research and
methodologies designed to enhance GNSS signal accuracy in urban settings, along with
their respective advantages and limitations.
Over the past 15 years, researchers have explored approaches to supplement GNSS sig-
nal data with additional sources of information to improve the accuracy and reliability of
positioning. While some, like Li et al. [7] or Changhui et al.[8], explore the coupling GNSS
data with additional satellite information like Doppler measurement, carrier-to-noise den-
sity ratio, elevation angle, NLOS visibility ratio, azimuth angle or correlator-level measure-
ments, the majority of papers examine the coupling of GNSS data with imaging.
A large part of the literature examines the coupling of GNSS data with imaging techniques.
This coupling is approached in two ways: with or without the use of machine learning algo-
rithms. While this thesis focuses on the development of a machine learning classification
algorithm for sky segmentation, it also discusses non-machine learning methods. These
traditional methods are foundational for sky segmentation and image pre-processing, and
they provide essential context for understanding the advancements made through ma-
chine learning.

direction of this thesis. They are described in greater detail in the following section.

Morphological Opening and Closing
Two techniques used in this work to process the sky segmentation mask are the morpho-
logical opening and closing of the images. Both operations are a combination of the basic
morphological operations erosion and dilation.
Erosion reduces the size of on object in an image. That’s why it is often used to reduce
noise in images. Dilation on the other hand adds pixels to the boundaries of an image
and therefor enlarges the object. This operation can be used to fill in gaps within the
object. [22]

When using machine learning for sky segmentation, there are two general approaches:
supervised learning and unsupervised learning.

Supervised image classification methods are for example: KNN, SVM, or Neural Net-
works.
Supervised algorithms need training with a labeled training data set. A larger training data
set generally leads to better predictions by the algorithm. At the beginning of this thesis,
there was not a sufficiently large training data set available. Therefore, unsupervised
algorithms were considered first. In unsupervised algorithms it is only necessary to define
the number of classes to which the data should be classified. Since the results were very
promising, this thesis is looking into unsupervised algorithms only.
Unsupervised algorithms that have been explored in the process of this thesis are Princi-
pal Component Analysis (PCA), K-means, and Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM).
PCA is a method of dimension reduction. Its goal is ”to discover a lower-dimensional
representation of the high-dimensional data set where it is assumed the lower-dimensional
representations are linear. [28]”. This is especially useful in improving the efficiency of
machine learning algorithms.
Looking at the literature, K-means has proven to be a reliable unsupervised algorithm to
identify sky regions in an image. It partitions the observations of an input dataset into K
groups based on the similarity of their features. The observations are assigned to the
closest cluster. The K-means algorithm is an iterative process. While the center of the K
clusters are chosen randomly in the beginning, they update to be the mean of the assigned
observations. Now the observations will again be assigned to the closest cluster. This
process will repeat until the center of the K clusters are not changing anymore. [28]
Starting out with testing K-means this thesis settled on using GMM. The process of picking
this machine learning algorithm is described in subsection 4.4.1.

GNSS
In 1998, the UN defined GNSS as ”a space-based radio positioning system that includes
one or more satellite constellations, augmented as necessary to support the intended op-
eration, and that provides 24-hour three-dimensional position, velocity and time informa-
tion to suitably equipped users anywhere on, or near, the surface of Earth [...]. There are
two core elements of satellite navigation systems: the Global Positioning System (GPS)
(operated by the United States of America) and the Global Orbiting Navigation Satellite
System (GLONASS) (operated by the Russian Federation)” [30]. In addition to these two
systems, the European GNSS Galileo went live in 2016 and the Chinese GNSS Beidou-
3/Compass launched its last satellite in 2020 [31][32].
The framework that specifies the position of a GNSS receiver on Earth is provided by
the World Geodetic System 1984 (WGS 84). Within that framework, the position of the
GNSS satellites and the receiver are represented in an earth-centered, earth-fixed (ECEF)
Cartesian coordinates. It includes an ellipsoid as the geometric model for the Earth’s
shape, Earth’s geoid and its gravity field. [6] A visualization of the difference between the
ellipsoid and the geoid can be seen in Figure 3.8.

and the geoid (green). Picture Source: [33].

Research Design Proposal
First of all, it is important to briefly explain the inspiration for the research design. The
selection of hardware design is straightforward. In order to obtain the data points using
the algorithm, a setup was required, which collects the images from a sky pointing fish-eye
camera and a GNSS antenna along with a receiver to gather the positioning data. Even
though some test runs were made in the early beginning with a GoPro Camera taking
rectangular images of the sky, it was quickly decided to use fish-eye images only. This
decision is simply based on the fact that even though the algorithm is supposed to operate
in an urban environment there are certain scenarios where there might be available GNSS
satellite signals in a low LOS close to the horizon which might be missed in a rectangular
image. The fish-eye camera ensures to include the horizon. In addition, basic hardware
equipment for the implementation of a system for recording the required image and GNSS
data was already available at the technology lab of the department. A schematic overview
of the hardware structure can be seen in Figure 4.1. More details about the final structure
are given in section 4.2.

2. the adaptability of the algorithm to process data and deliver the created masks in

was decided to investigate unsupervised algorithms in more detail, as the time frame was
limited for building up a new database for training an algorithm. However, none of the un-
supervised machine learning algorithms seems to be able to fulfill all of the above criteria
alone. This is the reason why a form of image pre-processing is used in all the research
work examined. subsection 4.4.1 presents the selected machine learning algorithms and
image pre-processing in more detail.
In conclusion, the research design proposal for building a proof of concept imaged based
sky classification algorithm to improve GNSS positioning in a (Danish) urban environment
looks the following: A simple test setup with a fish-eye camera in combination with a
GNSS antenna and receiver that can be easily installed on different vehicles. The image
data will then be pre-processed and used to find a well-performing unsupervised machine
learning algorithm. After that the created sky mask will be combined with the satellite data
gathered by the GNSS receiver. This data is then reviewed manually to verify the result
of the algorithm.

5.1.1
The Pre-Processing
It became evident from a thorough review of the existing literature that pre-processing the
images was a crucial step for achieving optimal segmentation results. Based on prelim-
inary investigations, several pre-processing techniques were considered to improve the
performance of the GMM algorithm. The pre-processing methods under consideration
included morphological operations, blurring techniques, and the application of different
color spaces. The application of color spaces will be discussed separately in the follow-
ing subsection 5.1.2.
Morphological operations, which involve the transformation of the structure of the image
using operations like opening and closing, were evaluated for their potential to remove
noise and small artifacts. Blurring techniques, aimed at smoothing the image and reducing
noise, were also considered for their ability to enhance the segmentation process.
Other potential pre-processing methods, such as the use of the energy function, were
initially assessed. However, these methods were not pursued further due to their limited
impact observed during preliminary tests.
As described in subsubsection 4.4.1, hand-annotated masks were created to serve as a
ground truth for comparison. These masks allowed for the calculation of the IoU percent-
age, providing a quantitative measure of the accuracy of the segmentation for each image
and procedure.
To examine the effectiveness of the two different pre-processing strategies (morphological
operations and blur), the GMM was applied to the data set concatenating all color spaces
of each picture. The resulting density plot (Figure 5.1) shows the distribution of IoU values
for the three methods: GMM alone, Blur combined with GMM, and the morphological
operations (Open and Close) combined with GMM.
The statistical tests were conducted using a kernel size of (15,15) for the blur and the
morphological operations. This specific kernel size was selected based on the results of
preliminary tests with a subset of images.

almost all of the examined cases, they were classified as sky. Since GMM is used in this
work, there is no possibility to train the algorithm by annotations to classify windows as
buildings and not skies. Therefore, a specific post-processing of the masks created by
the GMM algorithm is required.

between the sky and other objects. A rough outline is often sufficient for this purpose,
ensuring that the algorithm remains focused on the primary task of accurate sky classifi-
cation.

effects of morphological operations during pre-processing, these techniques might prove
beneficial at the post-processing stage, where the goal is to enhance the quality of the

5.2
Performance of the Selected Algorithm
Having designed the sky classification algorithm based on the above statistics, the next
step is to discuss its effectiveness under different conditions, such as different sceneries,
lighting situations or weather conditions - and also its efficiency.
5.2.1
Performance Depending on the Training Image
The performance of a sky segmentation algorithm is influenced by the choice of the train-
ing image. This is particularly the case with the GMM algorithm, as only one training
image can be used to predict the subsequent sky segmentation masks.
Starting this thesis, the existing archive data and therefor its recording structure was used
to create the sky classification algorithm. This data consisted of short video sequences
recorded by the fish-eye camera ranging in length from 1 to 10 seconds. The exported
frames from those video sequences were stored in individual folders. The GMM model
was trained on the first frame of every short video sequence to predict the remaining
frames. This method formed the basis for all the statistical data and initial findings in the
study.
As the research progressed, further experiments revealed a significant improvement in
results when the algorithm was trained on an image taken in a location with open sky
view. This could not be achieved if the algorithm was always trained on the first frame
of each video sequence. If the test run started in a suitable location this starting image
can be used to train the GMM model. By processing all subsequent frames based on this
single training image, the algorithm demonstrated enhanced accuracy and consistency
in sky classification. This refined approach, while not backed by quantitative metrics, is
supported by visual evidence.

Vegetation: Trees with and without Leaves
The presence of vegetation, particularly trees, introduces another layer of complexity.
Trees with dense foliage can create complex patterns that may confuse the algorithm,
leading to inaccuracies in sky segmentation. Conversely, trees without leaves present a
different challenge, where the algorithm must correctly identify the bare branches. Trees
can cause diffraction and scatter of a satellites GNSS signal when a GNSS receiver is
shadowed by vegetation [34]. Given the amount of vegetation in Danish cities, this topic
needs to be addressed.
Trees with full foliage add 35% attenuation to a GNSS signal compared to trees without.
This leads to the conclusion that the majority of attenuation is cause by the wood, not the
leaves.[34] Nevertheless, its is better to exclude trees and leaves as good as possible from
the sky segmentation mask. To achieve that the blur with a kernel size (35,35) was added
to the algorithm (see section 5.1). The algorithm can distinguishing between trees, leaves,
branches and sky. Nevertheless, it should be noted that the algorithm may encounter
limitations in certain circumstances, which will be discussed by analyzing some examples.
The algorithm recognizes the outline of trees, with (Figure 5.10) and without foliage (Fig-
ure 5.5c). The more dense the shape of the tree the better the outline. Figure 5.8a, for

With the six different data collection runs described in section 4.3, it was possible to gather

5.2.5
Limitations
While the integration of the sky segmentation algorithm with GNSS signals has shown
promising results, several limitations have been identified that need to be addressed in
future work:
Complex image situations: During the analysis of the algorithm’s results, several chal-
lenging scenarios were identified that may cause misclassifications in the predicted sky
mask.
These include bright building facades that can be mistaken for clouds, small
branches and scattered foliage, and the sun itself, which can cause glare. All of that
can interfere with the sky outline.
Weather Conditions: The current dataset lacks diversity in weather conditions. The algo-
rithm has not been tested in rainy conditions, which can affect both the sky segmentation
performance and GNSS signal quality. Rain can obscure the sky, create reflections, and
introduce noise that complicates the segmentation process.
Time of Day Variations: The algorithm has not been thoroughly evaluated during dawn
and dusk when lighting conditions change rapidly. These periods can pose challenges for
the sky segmentation process due to varying light intensity and shadow patterns. For the
sake of completeness, it is necessary to mention that this algorithm is meant to operate
during day light only.
Single Training Image Dependency: The GMM used for sky segmentation relies on a
single training image. This dependency can limit the algorithm’s adaptability to different
lighting and weather conditions present in various test scenarios.

The integration of the created sky masks into the GNSS positioning process involves the
following steps:
(1) Image Capture: The UGV captures images of its surroundings.
(2) Sky Segmentation: The algorithm processes these images to generate a sky mask,
identifying regions of the sky visible to the UGV.
(3) GNSS Signal Processing: The GNSS receiver captures available satellite signals.
(4) LOS Detection: Using the sky mask, it can be determined which satellites are in the
LOS.
(5) GNSS Positioning: An updated position of the UGV can then be determined using
LOS satellite signals only.
The previous chapters have investigated step (1) and (2), the development and perfor-
mance of a sky classification algorithm. Having established the effectiveness of this algo-
rithm, the focus shifts now to steps (3) to (5) the integration of the GNSS signals. This
chapter examines if and how the combination of the sky segmentation mask and GNSS
can enhance positioning reliability of an UGV. The effectiveness of this combined ap-
proach is evaluated in individual selected scenarios. Both the test drives with the car
and with the UGV are examined. The analysis of the UGV test runs as it resembles the
final use case. However, the test drives performed by car are also of interest since they
showcase the algorithm’s performance in an urban environment.

6.3

Various Scenarios
The integration of sky segmentation masks with GNSS positioning aimed to enhance the
accuracy and reliability of positioning for UGVs in urban environments. The analysis of
test runs for both test scenarios, the UGV and the car in an urban environment, provides
first results regarding the performance and potential of this combined approach. It should
be noted that these results represent individual cases only and therefore provide only
an indication of the effectiveness of the approach. Further evaluation of the data and
additional test runs are necessary to statistically confirm the effectiveness of the sky seg-
mentation. Nevertheless, the test scenarios just analyzed provide valuable information,
which is explained and discussed below.

Urban Environment
The dynamic nature of the urban environments, with constantly changing conditions and
obstructions, poses a significant challenge for GNSS-based positioning. Despite these
challenges, the results from Copenhagen and Lyngby test drives demonstrated that the

the UGV test drive. The higher speed of the vehicle without the possibility to make stops
for measurements made it difficult to analyze the collected data consistently. That is why,
despite a large amount of data, only selected individual points were chosen for an analysis.
While overall the sky segmentation mask demonstrates its effectiveness by improving
positional accuracy at singular points, the remaining positional errors and sharp turns in
the yellow tracks indicate areas for further improvement in the algorithm. The goal would
be to enhance this with real-time processing.
Another issue that needs to be investigated is the case where satellites are in LOS but
located exactly at the edge of the generated sky mask. Those satellite signals are likely
to be diffracted. Excluding those signals from the positioning calculation in the test cases
led to more accurate results. This indicates that implementing an additional ”buffer zone”
of several pixels around the created sky mask could improve positioning accuracy and
should therefore be addressed in future work. However, it is important to consider that
adding a buffer zone might also reduce the number of LOS satellite signals available,
potentially limiting the effectiveness of GNSS positioning. Balancing the size of the buffer
zone to optimize accuracy while ensuring sufficient LOS satellite signals remains a critical
area for future research.

Conclusion and Future Work
7.1
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to enhance GNSS positioning accuracy for UGVs in urban environ-
ments by integrating machine learning and image analysis techniques to generate sky
segmentation masks. It sought to overcome the challenges posed by multipath errors,
signal diffraction and signal blockages, which are common in urban settings.
The thesis began with the development and validation of a sky segmentation algorithm
designed to identify sky regions in images captured by a sky-pointing fish-eye camera.
This algorithm proved to effectively distinguish between sky and non-sky elements in the
majority of the test cases. The created segmentation masks were then used to identify
LOS and NLOS satellites. This process was crucial for manually filtering out NLOS signals,
which are more likely to be affected by diffraction and multipath errors.
The findings from the test runs indicate that using sky segmentation masks to filter out
NLOS satellites can improve the GNSS positioning accuracy. Specifically, the results
demonstrated reduced positional jumps and smoother trajectories, highlighting the poten-
tial of this approach for urban navigation.
Despite these promising results, several limitations were revealed. The sky classification
algorithm shows highly effective in most of the examined cases. However, some complex
image situations like bright building facades on cloudy days or sun flares are still lowering
with the accuracy. Moreover, the combination of the sky segmentation algorithm with
GNSS positioning showed certain limitations. The algorithm’s effectiveness was reduced
in areas with insufficient LOS satellites, and the lack of automation in the UGV tests slows
down the analysis, underscoring the need for real-time processing capabilities.
In summary, this research contributes to the ongoing efforts to improve GNSS position-
ing in challenging urban environments. The integration of sky segmentation masks and
advanced image analysis improves urban navigation. While the approach has shown con-
siderable promise, addressing its current limitations and refining the methodology will be
crucial for its practical application. That’s why recommendations for future work on this
thesis are presented below.
7.2
Future Work
A crucial first step is to expand the current test database.
Even though the current
database already contains thousands of images from six different test runs, it lacks di-
versity in certain key aspects. Additional images from situations such as dusk and dawn
are needed to test and ensure the functionality of the algorithm at any daylight situation.
This is particularly important in the northern latitudes, where the incidence of light varies
greatly throughout the year. Adjustments to the sky segmentation process may be neces-
sary to handle these challenging periods effectively. Moreover, constructing a waterproof
prototype would allow testing of the UGV and the algorithm’s performance under rainy
conditions, which has not yet been explored.
Another focus of future work could be the improvement the algorithm beyond its current
performance. While all of the used methods have been proven statistically to improve the
sky segmentation, the optimization of the chosen processing steps was not the subject of

image situations, such as those involving scattered foliage.
A final point of future improvement on the classification algorithm is the choice of the
training image. It has been proven throughout the work of this thesis that the choice of
the training image is crucial to the algorithm’s performance. Conducting further research,
supported by statistical analysis, could provide deeper insights into how different training
