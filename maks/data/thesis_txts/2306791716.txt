Abstract
A multivariate linear regression considering 4327 10-min averages of solar wind/IMF parameters, geo-
magnetic indices, and Iridium R
⃝radial current density data has been used to derive an empirical model,
which maps the currents above the high-latitude ionosphere during geomagnetic storms. The response
time of total current intensity to changes in the solar wind is studied in detail and found to be much
shorter for dayside than for nightside (20 min versus 60 min). The plots of FACs produced with this
model depict the typical R1 and R2 systems, although the peak current densities inside the regions are
between 0.38 and 0.61 the observed values. Predictions are also computed from Weimer model and global
BATS-R-US model coupled with RCM, but none of them yield FAC density predictions larger than 65% of
the observations. The position in MLAT of the currents calculated with the empirical model is predicted
more accurately than using the other two models, but the location in MLT is not.
The most noticeable pattern of FACs during geomagnetic storms is its equatorward movement. This
process, described by a colatitude expansion factor, occurs both in the empirical model predictions
(∼1◦−2◦/100 nT) and observations (∼2◦−5◦/100 nT). Signiﬁcant and prolonged diﬀerences between the
observed and predicted position of R1 FACs are mainly observed around substorm onset. In some cases
the predictions of the empirical model do not seem to agree with the traditional paradigm of expansion
and contraction of the polar cap with opening and closing of magnetic ﬂux. It is concluded that the
empirical model fails to predict the behaviour of FACs in response to substorms.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Field-aligned (or Birkeland) currents play a major role in the electric ﬁeld, momentum, and mass transfer
between the solar wind, the magnetosphere, and the ionosphere, and therefore are a key element to study
for the space physicists. Knowledge of the FAC structure and dynamics is crucial to understanding how
solar wind energy is transferred from the magnetosphere to the ionosphere.
Birkeland [1908] introduced the concept of electric currents ﬂowing vertically into the polar ionosphere,
during his discussion of observations of aurorae and associated magnetic perturbations made along his
expeditions. At the dawn of space age, using data from the polar-orbiting satellite 1963 38C, Zmuda et al.
[1966] reported transverse magnetic disturbances in the auroral regions and pointed out that they were
likely due to currents ﬂowing along magnetic ﬁeld lines, the so-called FACs. However, it was Iijima and
Potemra [1976] who, using more than one year’s magnetic ﬁeld data from the TRIAD satellite, translated
these magnetic disturbances into vertical electric currents and presented the global average structure of
FACs that produce the observed magnetic perturbations.
Since then, these current systems have been extensively investigated based on the observations from
LEO satellites (e.g. Ørsted, CHAMP, MAGSAT, and Iridium R
⃝constellation), as well as inferred from
SuperDARN radars and ground-based magnetometer network observations. Signiﬁcant progress has been
achieved in understanding the distribution, source regions, and evolution of current systems in response to
solar wind and IMF conditions. It has been found that the FACs distribution is mainly determined by the
IMF orientation, and that the intensity of these currents are a complex function of many parameters, such
as MLT, reconnection rate and solar wind electric ﬁeld. Nonetheless, there are still many open questions
in need of investigation.
Geomagnetic storms are the periods of greatest energy transfer from the solar wind to the magnetosphere-
ionosphere system, and since FACs are coupling agents between these regions, it is of particular interest to
understand these current systems during storms. While statistical patterns of FACs are fairly well known
[e.g. Iijima and Potemra, 1984; Weimer, 2001; Papitashvili et al., 2002], little has been investigated about
their storm-time characteristics, in part because storm-time currents do not repeat in the same sequence
from storm to storm [Anderson et al., 2005].
The time response and conﬁguration of FACs after rapid and dramatic changes such as IMF reversals

MHD models (e.g. University of Michigan’s BATS-R-US) have improved our understanding of the
evolution and dynamics of the FAC systems in response to external changes. Due to their prominent
role in the magnetosphere-ionosphere coupled system, it is of major importance to understand how FACs

1.2
Aim of the study
The major goal of this project was to develop an empirical model to predict the distribution and intensity
of the large-scale FAC systems for both hemispheres, based on radial current density data, solar wind
and IMF parameters, and geomagnetic indices, during storm-time conditions. In particular, the following
goals were achieved:
1. Identify which are the solar wind and IMF drivers, both individually and in combination with others
as coupling functions, that correlate best with FAC intensity.
2. Investigate the delay response of FACs to solar wind and IMF variability during geomagnetic storms.
3. Develop an algorithm for automatic identiﬁcation of the FAC regions in maps of observations and
predictions of radial current density.
4. Investigate the equatorward expansion of FACs during storm-time conditions.
5. Validate the model by calculating its prediction metrics and comparing them with those from the
empirical Weimer model and the global MHD BATS-R-US model coupled with RCM.
1.3
Data and methodology
The ﬁrst step was to identify the geomagnetic storms in the period January 2011 −November 2015, and
to delimit their main phases. Then, ﬁles of FAC density, solar wind parameters and geomagnetic indices
were acquired for each main phase. The former were available as 10-min measurements in a 24 (MLT
bins) × 50 (MLAT bins) grid for both hemispheres. Solar wind ﬁles were provided in 1-min cadence and
geomagnetic indices in either 1-min or 1-h resolution. All 1-min measurements were averaged in intervals
of 10-min. After analysing the completeness of the data, the total number of 10-min intervals was 4327.
The correlation among all 10-min averages of solar wind/IMF parameters, coupling functions, and
total current intensity was calculated. The variable which best predicted total current intensity and the
delay which maximized the correlation were identiﬁed. As a result, the empirical model was written as a
linear regression ﬁt between the best predictor and the FAC density in each bin, yielding 1200 individual
regression ﬁts constituting the model.
The model was used to predict FAC density and total current intensity, and the results were compared
with the observed data by the Iridium R
⃝constellation and predictions of Weimer model and BATS-R-US
model coupled with RCM. The large-scale FAC regions were identiﬁed in each grid, both of observations
and predictions, and several parameters were calculated from them. The performance of the models
was then assessed statistically with prediction metrics. Finally, the equatorward movement of FACs in

Chapter 2
The near-Earth space environment
Most of the visible matter in the universe exists as a ﬂuid composed of electrically charged particles, called
plasma, rather than a gas made of neutral atoms or molecules. Plasmas are found in the solar atmosphere,
the interplanetary medium, planetary magnetospheres, and planetary ionospheres. Since the beginning of
the space age in the late 1950s, space probes have visited planets and comets in the Solar System, and
performed in situ measurements of plasmas. The space environment surrounding the Earth has also been
extensively studied by instruments on board rockets and satellites, and in the case of the ionosphere, by
means of radio wave reﬂection or radar techniques.
The solar-terrestrial environment, sometimes called geospace, refers to the regions of the Earth’s
atmosphere, ionosphere, and magnetosphere, inﬂuenced by the physical conditions in the solar interior,
solar atmosphere, and solar wind. Starting some 50−70 km above the Earth’s surface and extending up to
distances measured in tens of Earth’s radii (RE = 6371 km), the solar-terrestrial environment is a region
of interactions and boundaries: interactions between terrestrial matter and solar radiation, between solar
and terrestrial magnetic ﬁelds, between magnetic ﬁeld and charged particles; and boundaries between
solar and terrestrial matter, and between regions dominated by diﬀerent patterns of ﬂow [Hargreaves,
1995].
2.1
Interaction of the solar wind and the geomagnetic ﬁeld
The solar wind is a highly conducting plasma emitted by the Sun at speeds of about 500 km/s as a result
of the expansion of the solar corona. This plasma consists mainly of electrons and protons, and a 5% of
helium ions. Because of its high conductivity, the solar magnetic ﬁeld is frozen in the plasma and drawn
outward by the expanding solar wind. The IMF is of the order of 5 nT and the typical electron density of
the solar wind near Earth is approximately 5/cm3. When the solar wind impinges on the geomagnetic
ﬁeld, it cannot penetrate it but rather is slowed down, and to a large extent, deﬂected around it, as
illustrated in Figure 2.1.
Through its interaction with the magnetized solar wind, the geomagnetic ﬁeld is intimately involved in
transferring energy and momentum from the Sun into our space environment. The magnetic ﬁeld region
surrounding the Earth is called the magnetosphere. Given the existence of the geomagnetic ﬁeld, the
form and structure of the magnetosphere are determined largely by emissions from the Sun. Since solar
5
==PAGE:24==
2.2. STRUCTURE OF THE INNER MAGNETOSPHERE
activity is continuously changing, the magnetosphere also changes from hour to hour, and from day to
day, responding readily and rapidly to variations in the solar emissions [Moldwin, 2008].
The Earth’s magnetosphere is, to a zeroth-order approximation, a giant magnetic cavity that shields
the Earth from the ﬂowing solar wind. Within the magnetosphere the dynamics of charged particles
are determined by the conﬁguration of the geomagnetic ﬁeld, which resembles less and less a dipole
farther from Earth because of its interaction with the solar wind. The magnetosphere is a benign host for
communication systems and satellites, but it can be energized greatly by the solar wind, and change into
an inhospitable environment, aﬀecting not only systems in space, but also those in the lower atmosphere
and on the Earth’s surface. Fortunately, much has been learned about the magnetosphere and its control
by the Sun. If we know, or can accurately predict, the plasma conditions of the solar wind ahead of the
Earth, we can predict at least the statistical behaviour of the magnetosphere [Rusell, 2007].
The magnetopause is the boundary of the magnetosphere. Its position depends on the strength of the
solar wind dynamic pressure due to its density and velocity. As the solar wind dynamic pressure increases,
the magnetopause moves earthward, and its location can be deduced from the pressure balance across
the boundary between the solar wind dynamic pressure and the magnetic pressure of the magnetosphere.
Typical distances from Earth to the subsolar magnetopause range from 6 to 12 RE.
Because the solar wind is supersonic, a shock wave is formed on the dayside of the magnetosphere.
This wave, called the bow shock, slows the solar wind and diverts it around the magnetosphere. As shown
in Figure 2.1, the shock is formed 2 or 3 RE upstream of the magnetopause. The region between the bow
shock and the magnetosphere is known as the magnetosheath and contains turbulent plasma. In crossing
the shock, the solar wind is slowed down to about 250 km/s, and the loss of kinetic energy is dissipated as
thermal energy, increasing the temperature to 5 × 106 K [Hargreaves, 1995; Baumjohann and Treumann,
1997].
In the antisunward direction the magnetosphere is extended into a long magnetotail. It has been found
that on the nightside the geomagnetic ﬁeld beyond about 10 RE tends to run in the Sun-Earth direction,
and there is a central plane where the ﬁeld reverses direction, called the neutral sheet. The magnetotail
consists of two magnetic lobes, one tied to the north polar cap and the other to the south polar cap. In
the northern lobe the ﬁeld points toward the Earth, and in the southern, away from it, with a typical
value of the magnetic ﬁeld strength around 30 nT. Both lobes are separated by the plasma sheet, a region
with higher density than the lobes. This region carries the cross tail neutral sheet [Moldwin, 2008].
2.2
Structure of the inner magnetosphere
As noted in Figure 2.1, the magnetic ﬁeld resembles a dipole close to Earth. The dipole region of the
Earth’s magnetosphere is called the inner magnetosphere. The interaction of the geomagnetic ﬁeld with
the solar wind is responsible for the distortion of its dipole ﬁeld. The non-dipolar regions are called the
outer magnetosphere. The plasma in the magnetosphere consists mainly of electrons and protons. The
sources of these particles are the solar wind and the terrestrial ionosphere. In addition there are small
fractions of He+ and O+ ions of ionospheric origin and He++ ions originating from the solar wind.

2.2.1
Principal particle populations
The geomagnetic ﬁeld holds within it several populations of charged particles. Immediately surrounding
the Earth, deep within the magnetosphere, is a region of cold dense plasma that corotates with the Earth.
This region is called the plasmasphere and consists mostly of hydrogen and helium, but also an appreciable
amount of oxygen. There is a very sharp boundary to the dense plasmasphere called the plasmapause.
Often overlapping with the plasmasphere and trapped on closed ﬁeld lines are the Van Allen radiation
belts. Apart from cosmic rays and solar protons, the Van Allen particles are the most energetic in the
magnetosphere and contribute to the ionization of the upper atmosphere when precipitated out of the
trapping region. The radiation belts consist of two distinct regions of energetic particles. The outer belt,
composed mostly of energetic electrons has its inner edge around 3 RE and its variable outer edge usually
just beyond geosynchronous orbit (6.6 RE). The inner belt consists of energetic electrons and protons
and extends out to about 2.5 RE [Moldwin, 2008].
The plasma sheet is associated with the magnetotail, essentially with the central plane where the
magnetic ﬁeld reverses direction. Plasma sheet particles are energized within the magnetotail, and they
are important in auroral activity and the behaviour of the high-latitude ionosphere. The sheet is about
10RE thick and extends across the tail between the dawn and dusk sides [Hargreaves, 1995].
2.2.2
Current systems
The combination of plasma and electric ﬁeld in the magnetosphere allows electric currents to ﬂow
(Figure 2.2). Several current systems can be identiﬁed and there are connections, some intermittent,
7

between them. These currents are very important for the dynamics of the near-Earth environment. They
transport charge, mass, momentum, and energy. In addition, the currents generate magnetic ﬁelds, which
may severely alter or distort any pre-existing ﬁelds [Baumjohann and Treumann, 1997].
The compression of the geomagnetic ﬁeld on the dayside is associated with current ﬂowing across the
magnetopause surface, the magnetopause current. In the nightside magnetosphere, the tail-like ﬁeld is
accompanied by the tail current ﬂowing on the tail surface and the neutral sheet current in the central
plasma sheet. Both currents are connected and form a Θ-like current system, when seen from along the
Sun-Earth line. This system carries a total current of 108 A.
Because of the shape and strength of the Earth’s dipolar ﬁeld, ions ﬂow from midnight to the dusk
side, whereas electrons move in the opposite direction. This diﬀerence in ﬂow directions of positively
charged ions and negatively charged electrons gives rise to the ring current, which encircles the Earth
from east to west. The ring current in turn produces a magnetic ﬁeld that points in the opposite direction
to the dipolar ﬁeld at Earth’s surface. When the ring current increases suddenly, as during a geomagnetic
storm, a rapid decrease in the geomagnetic ﬁeld strength is observed. A depression of 30 nT at the surface,
which would correspond to a moderate storm, would be due to a 106 A current at a distance of 4.5 RE
[Moldwin, 2008].
There are other currents, called ﬁeld-aligned currents, that connect the ring current and plasma sheet
to the ionosphere. They were predicted by Birkeland in 1908, but solid evidence for their existence was
found until the early 1970s. When ISIS-2 and TRIAD satellites crossed the auroral zone, they registered
magnetic signatures such as those in Figure 2.3, which shows a westward magnetic ﬁeld extending over
2◦−3◦of latitude. To explain this it was necessary to suppose that the satellite had passed through a

2.3
Ionosphere
The solar ultraviolet radiation that impinges on the Earth’s atmosphere ionizes a fraction of the neutral
atmosphere. Above 80 km collisions between particles are infrequent and a permanent ionized population,
the ionosphere, is formed. It is the transition region from the neutral atmosphere to the fully ionized
magnetospheric plasma. Typical electron densities in the mid-latitude ionosphere are 105/cm3 and the
magnetic ﬁeld strength is of the order of 104 nT [Baumjohann and Treumann, 1997].
The ionosphere extends to rather high latitudes, and at low and mid-latitudes, gradually merges into
the plasmasphere. At high latitudes, electrons from the plasma sheet can precipitate along magnetic
ﬁeld lines down to ionospheric altitudes, where they collide with and ionize neutral atmosphere particles.
Photons emitted by this process create the aurorae, typically observed inside the auroral oval, which
contains the footprints of those ﬁeld lines that form the plasma sheet. The polar cap lies inside of the
auroral oval, and it is formed by ﬁeld lines connected to the tail lobes.
A number of current systems ﬂow in the conducting layers of the Earth’s ionosphere, at altitudes
between 100 and 150 km. Among others, these are: the auroral electrojets inside the auroral oval, the Sq
currents in the dayside mid-latitude ionosphere, and the equatorial electrojet near the magnetic equator.
The lower ionosphere below a height of about 90 km is very weakly ionized and is called the D-region.
The upper ionosphere is the region above this height and consists of two well-separated layers, the E-region
which has its ionization peak around 110 km, and the F-region about 300 km altitude. Figure 2.4 shows
height proﬁles of the ionospheric electron density during day and night hours in mid-latitudes. The
F-regions splits into two further layers, the F1 around 200 km, and the F2 around 300 km height. With
electron densities up to 106/cm3, the latter contains the densest plasma in the near-Earth’s environment
[Baumjohann and Treumann, 1997].

2.4
Magnetic reconnection
Magnetic reconnection occurs when a magnetized plasma parcel (a ﬂux tube) of one polarity is brought
up against a magnetized plasma parcel of opposite polarity. When reconnection occurs, ﬁeld lines connect
and change their topologies. In the interaction of the solar wind with the Earth’s magnetosphere there
are three possible magnetic topologies between the IMF and the Earth. A magnetic ﬁeld line might not
intersect the Earth at all and we refer to them as interplanetary. A ﬁeld line might intersect the Earth’s
surface twice, for example by leaving the south polar region and crossing the surface in the north polar
region. Such ﬁeld lines are called "closed". Field lines leaving the Earth could also link to the magnetic
ﬁeld lines in the solar wind, and these are called "open". Plasma can become trapped on closed ﬁeld lines,
and densities can build up. The plasmasphere and the radiation belts are found on closed ﬁeld lines. On
the contrary, open ﬁeld lines have much less plasma since it can stream along the magnetic ﬁeld away
from Earth [Rusell, 2007].
Figure 2.5 shows how ﬁeld topology changes on the dayside magnetosphere. Assume that the IMF is
directed predominantly southward (line 1’) and is brought up against the northward-directed geomagnetic
ﬁeld (line 1). These are two distinct ﬁeld lines, one that has both ends in the solar wind and one connected
to both poles on Earth (interplanetary and closed, respectively). When they come together, they can
reconnect and in addition to converting some of the magnetic energy into particle kinetic energy, the two
original topologies are converted into two ﬁeld lines of a new type. Field lines 2 and 2’ have one end
attached to the Earth and the other stretching out into interplanetary space. The solar wind ﬂow pulls
the solar wind portion of both lines antisunward. As the solar wind end of these open lines moves away
from the Sun, the ﬁeld lines get swept back behind Earth (lines 3, 4 and 5) into the magnetotail.
The solar wind imparts an electric ﬁeld across the magnetosphere directed from dawn to dusk in the
plasma sheet, with the same direction as the neutral sheet current in Figure 2.2. This electric ﬁeld causes
the plasma sheet ﬂux tubes to move earthward in a motion called convection. After a solar wind ﬂux tube
(line 1’) reconnects on the dayside magnetosphere and is swept back over the polar cap (lines 2, 3 and 4),

2.5
High-latitude electrodynamics
The circulation of ﬂux tubes within the magnetosphere aﬀects the footpoints of the ﬁeld lines in the high-
latitude ionosphere. Since this is a conducting medium, the electric ﬁeld associated with the ionospheric
convection will drive ionospheric currents.
2.5.1
Ionospheric convection and conductivity
The motion of the ﬂux tubes across the polar cap due to magnetic reconnection shown in Figure 2.5
also moves the ionospheric footpoint of the ﬂux tubes and the plasma attached to them across the polar
cap to the nightside. Similarly, the sunward convection of magnetospheric ﬂux tubes leads to a sunward
convection of the foot points of these ﬂux tubes in the dawn and duskside high-latitude ionosphere, inside
the auroral oval.

In general, the plasma conductivity can be treated as a scalar, e.g. in the solar wind and the fully

must be taken into account: the lower part of the ionosphere. Collisions between the ionized and the
neutral part of the upper atmosphere in the presence of a strong magnetic ﬁeld lead to a ﬁnite anisotropic
conductivity tensor formed by three elements. The ﬁrst is the Pedersen conductivity, which governs
the Pedersen current in the direction transverse to the magnetic ﬁeld. The second element is the Hall
conductivity, which determines the Hall current in the direction perpendicular to both the electric and
magnetic ﬁeld. The third element is the parallel conductivity and governs the magnetic FAC, driven by
the parallel electric ﬁeld component [Baumjohann and Treumann, 1997].
Since the ionospheric conductivity is a tensor with three diﬀerent components, the convection electric
ﬁeld generates three types of currents. The ﬁrst type are the FACs ﬂowing parallel to the magnetic ﬁeld
into and out of the ionosphere. Secondly, the Pedersen currents ﬂow perpendicular to the magnetic ﬁeld
lines and parallel to the ionospheric convection ﬁeld. Finally, Hall currents ﬂow perpendicular to both the
magnetic and the electric ﬁeld.
2.5.2
Auroral electrojets
Particles from the plasma sheet that precipitate into the auroral oval generate signiﬁcant ionization. As
a result, the conductivity in the auroral oval is much higher than that of the polar cap. Therefore, the
high-latitude current ﬂow is concentrated inside the auroral oval, where it forms the auroral electrojets.
These currents are the most prominent features at auroral latitudes and carry a total current of about 106
A [Baumjohann and Treumann, 1997].
Figure 2.6 summarizes the knowledge about the conductivity structure, the electric ﬁeld distribution
and the current ﬂow related with the auroral electrojet system. Inside the auroral oval, the ionospheric
conductivity is enhanced above the solar ultraviolet-induced level due to ionization of neutral particles
by precipitating electrons and ions. The large-scale pattern of magnetospheric convection is reﬂected in
the auroral oval electric ﬁeld. As shown in the inset of Figure 2.5, the transport of ﬂux tubes results in
a two-cell convection pattern. However, in reality the convection pattern is slightly more complicated.
The auroral zone electric ﬁeld associated with the two-cell system of plasma transport has typical values

points equatorward in the postmidnight and morning sector.

2.6
Dynamics of the magnetosphere
Reconnection and convection in Earth’s magnetosphere are not steady processes. Strong southward IMF
can bring enhanced reconnection at the dayside and lead to an increase in the magnetic ﬂux transferred
to the nightside. Increased magnetic pressure in the magnetotail leads to thinning of the current sheet,
which enables magnetic reconnection. Since IMF polarity points north and south irregularly, the amount
of reconnection and energy input to the magnetosphere is highly variable [Kivelson and Rusell, 1995].
2.6.1
Substorms
The dayside reconnection rate depends on the number of southward oriented IMF lines that get into
contact with the Earth’s magnetopause during a given time interval. In particular, the magnitude of the
southward IMF component is rather variable. Hence, there are intervals when the magnetosphere is very
quiet and convection is prevented, whereas in other periods, a lot of ﬂux is reconnected on the dayside
and the magnetosphere is very active.
Eventually, all magnetic ﬂux transported to the tail must be reconnected and convected back to
the dayside magnetosphere. However, only part of the ﬂux transported into the tail is reconnected
instantaneously and convected back to the frontside magnetosphere. The remaining ﬁeld lines are added
to the tail lobes, enhancing the magnetic ﬂux density. After approximately an hour these stored ﬁeld lines
are suddenly reconnected in the tail, and their magnetic energy is explosively released.

The reconnection of previously stored ﬂux tubes in the tail lobes has dramatic eﬀects on the mag-
netospheric plasma and related phenomena like aurorae and magnetospheric and ionospheric currents.
These eﬀects, with and approximate duration of one or two hours, are summarized under the term
magnetospheric substorm [Baumjohann and Treumann, 1997].
The enhanced aurora that is observed during a substorm is associated with increased auroral ionospheric
currents measured by the magnetic index called the Auroral Electrojet (AE) index. AE measures the
diﬀerence between the strength of the westward and the eastward electrojets, measured by the AL and
AU indices, respectively. Both electrojets, but particularly the westward near midnight, are intensiﬁed by
the onset of a substorm [Moldwin, 2008].
2.6.2
Storms
Occasionally, the amount of energy transferred into the magnetosphere from the Sun can increase rapidly,
in association with the impact of an IMF directed southward from a CME with the Earth. These plasma
structures apply a coherent stress to the magnetosphere over many hours, and this can increase the
ﬂow of energy and momentum into the magnetosphere and the rate of convection. The response of the
magnetosphere is quite diﬀerent under such conditions than during a substorm. Enhancement of the ring
current is observed in association with this increased energy input, which is related to a rapid decrease in
the Dst index.
A geomagnetic storm is characterized by three phases (Figure 2.7). First, there is the SSC characterized
by a compression of the magnetosphere and a brief rise of Dst due to an increase in and motion earthward
of the magnetopause current. The SSC generally lasts for tens of minutes to hours. The magnetic ﬁeld
then decreases rapidly in what is called the main phase, signaled by a rapid drop of Dst. During the main
phase, which lasts for days, charged particles in the near-Earth plasma sheet are energized and injected
deeper into the inner magnetosphere, producing the storm-time ring current. This phase is characterized
by the occurrence of multiple intense substorms and their associated auroral eﬀects. When the IMF turns
northward again, the rate of plasma energization and inward transport slows, and the ring current can
begin to restore to its pre-storm state, in what is known as the recovery phase [Russell, 2007].

2.7
Magnetosphere-ionosphere coupling
The Earth’s magnetosphere is bounded by the magnetopause and the solar wind on the outer side and
by the ionosphere on the inner side. These boundaries are not stationary but dynamic regions. Since
the magnetopause, the magnetosphere and the ionosphere are threaded by the same magnetic ﬁeld lines,
momentum and energy are transferred among them. Dynamic processes occurring in each of these regions
have to interact and adjust to each other. The magnetosphere-ionosphere (M-I) coupling is the interaction
of diﬀerent physical processes taking place in either of these two regions [Kamide and Baumjohann, 1993].
The key electrodynamic element in the M-I coupling is the current that ﬂows along ﬁeld lines between
these two regions, the FACs. This interaction is crucial for many phenomena such as plasma convection in
both the magnetosphere and the polar ionosphere, magnetospheric substorms, and generation of aurorae.
All magnetospheric processes are in some way controlled by the ionospheric electrodynamics, and all
processes in the ionosphere are connected to the state of the magnetosphere.
Baumjohann and Treumann [1997] depicted a simpliﬁed version of the logic governing the entire M-I
coupling system as shown in the block diagram in Figure 2.8. A change in one of the boxes will imply
changes in all other boxes, so the physical mechanisms can only be fully understood by examining the
coupled system in its entirety. For instance, a variation in magnetospheric convection will modify the
electric ﬁeld mapped into the ionosphere, which in turn will change the ionospheric current ﬂow related to
the electric ﬁeld through Ohm’s law. By the requirement of current continuity, this will change the FACs
and the distribution of currents in the magnetosphere. The coupled set of equations governing the logic
diagram of Figure 2.8 has not been solved until recently. One example is the RCM which can reproduce
the large scale features of the M-I system in near-equilibrium situations.

The terrestrial ﬁeld-aligned current system
Field-aligned currents play a central role in the transport of energy and momentum to the ionosphere by
conveying stress between the magnetosphere and the ionosphere. Aurorae are the result of an enormous
global electric circuit that carries current along lines of magnetic force to the ionosphere. In addition to
coupling stresses, FACs also drive polar ionospheric dynamics. Although the basic conﬁguration of these
currents is understood, little is known about their variability.
From LEO the FAC signatures are typically hundreds of nT, so they are relatively easy to detect. In
fact, the average conﬁguration of the FACs was ﬁrst determined by magnetic ﬁeld measurements from
TRIAD satellite [Iijima and Potemra, 1976]. As mentioned in the previous chapter, the large-scale currents
are composed of two systems: the equatorward region 2 (R2) system and the region 1 (R1) immediately
poleward of it. Typically FACs are located at MLATs between 65◦and 75◦. During very quiet times
the currents contract as far poleward as 75◦, and during geomagnetic storms can expand equatorward
to 45◦[Anderson et al., 2000]. The currents are present in both the North and South Hemispheres
with systematic diﬀerences depending on the season [Papitashvili et al., 2002]. The dependence of the
dayside currents on the IMF has been explored statistically [e.g. Anderson et al., 2008; Korth et al., 2009]
indicating in some cases current patterns poleward of the Region 1/2 system, called cusp or Region 0

The NBZ current system

thoroughly previously. This included a new FAC system denoted as NBZ (northward IMF Bz), which

3.3
Response of ﬁeld-aligned currents to solar wind variability
FACs play a fundamental role in conveying stresses in the coupled M-I system, therefore it is crucial to
determine the parameters controlling them, in order to understand the transport of energy and momentum.
Since the 1980s, when the ﬁrst empirical models of FACs were derived, the importance of the solar
wind speed and density and the IMF in governing these currents has been known. Comprehensive
observations from various single satellites in addition with upstream solar wind and IMF conditions
provided quantitative, two-dimensional distributions of the FACs. Statistical studies of magnetic ﬁeld
observations by DE 2 [e.g. Weimer 2001, 2005] and Ørsted [e.g. Papitashvili et al., 2002] revealed the
IMF direction to be the fundamental quantity in determining the global-scale coupling process.
Korth et al. [2010] performed a study to determine the inﬂuence of the intensity of the solar wind electric
ﬁeld and dynamic pressure on the current intensities and distributions. Their study used magnetometer
data from the Iridium R
⃝constellation between February 1999 and December 2007, and FACs were derived
by a spherical harmonic ﬁtting and applying Ampere’s law to the ﬁt. One of their key ﬁndings was that,
regarding the solar wind electric ﬁeld dependence (Eyz), the total FAC increased with Eyz for all clock
angles (α = arctan(By/Bz)). The slope of the linear ﬁt between the total current, Itot, and Eyz was
smallest for northward IMF (α = 0◦), and the largest, for southward IMF (α = 180◦) (Figure 2 in Korth
et al., 2010). It was also found that the currents expanded equatorward with increasing Eyz with the
largest colatitude expansion per unit electric ﬁeld for southward IMF (Figure 4 in Korth et al., 2010). The
strength of the electric ﬁeld was also found to be the dominant factor controlling Itot for purely southward
IMF.
In addition to modulating the FACs intensity, the solar wind electric ﬁeld also modiﬁes the current
patterns. As Eyz increases the currents expand equatorward. The expansion of the FACs to lower latitudes
implies expansion of the polar cap and an increase in the open magnetic ﬂux. The amount of open ﬂux is
controlled by the reconnection rate at the dayside magnetopause and the magnetotail. When the solar
wind electric ﬁeld increases, so does the reconnection rate and the polar cap responds to the increase in
open magnetic ﬂux by expanding equatorward. Since R1 FACs are intimately related to the location of
the boundary between open and closed magnetic ﬂux, the currents must also follow the expansion to lower
latitudes [Korth et al., 2010].
The solar wind dynamic pressure (Pdyn) is known to aﬀect the size of the magnetosphere and the
density of the plasma inside it, both of which are expected to inﬂuence the FACs. The linear trend lines
in Korth et al. [2010] showed that the total current intensiﬁed with increasing Pdyn, independent of the
IMF orientation. The maximum increase of the total current per unit Pdyn was observed for southward
IMF (Figure 8 in Korth et al., 2010). However, the equatorward expansions due to increases in Pdyn were
markedly smaller than for Eyz.

⃝magnetic

Korth et al. [2011]. Using the global MHD BATS-R-US code coupled to the RCM, it was found that
the global model reproduced the Iridium R
⃝statistical patterns well, with a two-dimensional correlation

3.4
Models of high-latitude electric ﬁelds and potentials
It is well known that signiﬁcant electric ﬁelds in the high-latitude ionosphere are produced due to the
interaction of the solar wind ﬂowing past the geomagnetic ﬁeld. These electric ﬁelds cause the plasma to
drift or convect in cells. The basic conﬁguration of the convection pattern is formed by two cells, with a
region of positive electric potential in the dawn side and a region of negative electrical potential on the
dusk. If the IMF is pointing southward the plasma moves antisunward over the polar cap and sunward at
lower latitudes. As the IMF rotates from southward to northward the cells become distorted and there
has been evidence that four cells are formed with northward IMF [Weimer, 1995]. Various empirical and
physics-based models have been developed to describe these convection patterns, in both pictorial and
numerical form.
3.4.1
Empirical models
One of the ﬁrst empirical models was derived by Heppner and Maynard [1987]. It showed several patterns
derived from many measurements of the electric ﬁeld for various orientations of the IMF and the Kp index.
In each group, lines of electric equipotential were hand-drawn to obtain the best match with the electric
ﬁeld obtained at various MLTs. In a later version these pictorial results were transformed numerically by
a calculation of spherical harmonics.
Weimer [1995] derived an empirical model of the high-latitude electric potential using data from the
vector electric ﬁeld instrument on DE 2 satellite between August 1981 and March 1983. By integrating
the component of the electric ﬁeld along the path of the satellite, the electric potential was calculated.
To obtain convection patterns for diﬀerent orientations of the IMF, data from 2879 passes were divided

into 32 groups according to the angle of the IMF vector in the Y Z plane and its magnitude. Since any
arbitrary function on the surface of a sphere can be expanded in an inﬁnite series of spherical harmonics,
the electric potential was described with a series of spherical harmonics. Given the large number of

were the ﬁrst results to show how the electric potential evolves from normal two cells into four cells in
a logical and consistent manner as the IMF rotates from southward to northward, with the dawn and
dusk convection cells both developing two separate peaks that move in diﬀerent directions and, ultimately,
form four cells [Weimer, 1995].
However, the utility of the maps presented in Weimer [1995] was limited by the fact that they were
derived just for a few discrete combinations of IMF magnitude, angle and season. Weimer [1996] developed
a method to derive the ionospheric electric potential patterns for any arbitrary combination of the IMF,
dipole tilt angle, and solar wind velocity. This improvement was based on an interpolation of the harmonic
coeﬃcients from the derived patterns.
Simultaneously, studies on the subject of FAC patterns were also performed. A similar conﬁguration
to the four-cell convection pattern of the electric potential for positive Bz was also found for the electric
current under northward Bz, with a pair of reversed polarity FACs poleward of the R1, the NBZ current
system. Weimer [2000] introduced a new technique for the analysis of satellite magnetometer data to
obtain maps of the FACs for various IMF, tilt angle, and solar wind conditions. The models of the
currents were derived from maps of the magnetic Euler potential, obtained from a cross product of the
magnetic ﬁeld perturbations with the geomagnetic ﬁeld unit vector, and integrating the result along the
satellite path. The magnetic Euler potentials were calculated using similar harmonic ﬁtting techniques to
those used previously for developing electric potential patterns. Finally, taking the Laplacian of the Euler
potential yielded the FAC density.
Further improvements of the Weimer models were achieved in 2001, when the electric potential model
was revised in order to include details of the nightside processes, since it has been known that plasma
convection and potentials near midnight are inﬂuenced by substorm activity in the magnetotail. The
substorm factor was thus included in Weimer [2001a], where the eﬀect of substorms on the electric
potential patterns was reproduced by using the AL index as an optional parameter. Likewise, Weimer
[2001b] presented maps of the FACs incorporating these substorm eﬀects, controlled by the AL index.
In its present version [Weimer, 2005], the Weimer ionosphere model combines two models, one for
high-latitude electric potentials and the other representing FACs. Both are based on measurements from
the DE 2 satellite between August 1981 and March 1983 in polar orbits at altitudes from 300 to 1000
km (as in the ﬁrst edition). Simultaneous IMF and solar wind measurements are from IMP 8 and ISEE
3 satellites. The processed database comprises 2608 valid polar cap passes with electric potential and
2403 with magnetic potential. This version is based on spherical cap harmonic analysis, in which each
coeﬃcient varies according to the IMF and the tilt angle. Among others, it yields as an output the FAC
density mapped to an altitude of 110 km. An example for a given set of conditions of the solar wind and

3.4.2
BATS-R-US model
BATS-R-US is a model developed by the Computational MHD Group at the University of Michigan.
In short, this code solves 3D MHD equations in ﬁnite volume form via numerical methods. The model
uses an adaptive grid composed of rectangular blocks arranged in varying degrees of spatial levels.
The magnetospheric MHD part is attached to an ionospheric potential solver that provides electric
potentials and conductances in the ionosphere from magnetospheric FACs [Tóth et al., 2012]. A run
of the BATS-R-US model can include the RCM in the inner magnetosphere, providing coupling to the
ionosphere.

4.3
Data preparation
As a previous step to deriving the model, further computations were performed with the data. Current
intensity was calculated from the FAC measurements, as well as two coupling functions (i.e. combination
of basic parameters) from solar wind data: the half-wave rectiﬁer and the Newell coupling function.
4.3.1
Computation of total current intensity
Given the FAC density in a 1 h MLT by 1◦MLAT grid, the area in each bin was calculated using a
sphere centred at the Earth and with a radius of 6481 km: 1 RE plus 110 km height over the Earth’s
surface. This value was chosen considering that the electrically conducting ionospheric E-region is located,
on average, at this altitude. In the E-region, collisions between neutrals and ions are frequent, thus the
electric conductivity is anisotropic, generating Pedersen, Hall and FACs. This procedure is in agreement
with Weimer [2005], in which the current is also mapped at this altitude. The current intensity was
calculated in each bin and summed over all bins to compute the total current intensity (Itot) for every
10-min interval in each hemisphere.

4.5
Model construction
In order to identify the best predictors for the model –among solar wind/IMF measurements and coupling
functions– the Pearson product-moment correlation coeﬃcient3 (r) among them and total current intensity
(in each hemisphere) was calculated for all events. This procedure was performed in three steps:
• First, the correlation was computed for measurements taken at the same time, i.e. among solar
wind/IMF parameters, coupling functions, and total current intensity at time t.
• Then, delays in the interval δt = 0, 10, 20, ..., 120 min were introduced among solar wind/IMF
parameters, coupling functions, and total current intensity. The case δt = 0 min was the solution
from step (1).
• Finally, the computation of the correlation coeﬃcient was performed among solar wind/IMF
parameters, coupling functions, and total current intensity in each MLT sector, also considering
delay ranging from δt = 0 to δt = 120 min.

Chapter 5
Results and discussion
The ﬁrst part of this chapter deals the analysis of the correlation among solar wind and IMF parameters
with total current intensity, which enabled the selection of the best predictor. The empirical model is
presented, and maps of predictions of FAC density, displaying graphically both R1 and R2, are shown in
the second part. The Weimer and BATS-R-US model predictions, as well as the AMPERE data, are also
illustrated with some examples, and comparisons among predicted and observed total current intensity
are shown in the next two sections. A statistical analysis of several parameters derived from the models
is performed by means of prediction metrics in the ﬁfth part of the chapter, showing their strengths
and weaknesses. The last section discusses the equatorward expansion of FACs in storm-time conditions
showing latitudinal proﬁles of FACs along the dawn-dusk meridian and examples of timeseries of the
location of R1 currents for selected storms.
5.1
Correlation among solar wind/IMF data and total current intensity
5.1.1
Correlation without delay
For both hemispheres, the strength of the association among the variables, as described by the Pearson
product-moment correlation coeﬃcient (r), is shown in Table 5.1. The correlation of By and n with Itot
was very weak (r < 0.2), and weak in the case of v (r ∼0.25). For Bz the correlation increased, and it
was moderate (r > 0.5) for vBs and dΦ/dt. The relationship among v and Bz, and Itot, increased when
using them as terms in the coupling functions (product in both cases, raised to a certain power in the
Newell parameter). Since the half-wave rectiﬁer is related to the merging electric ﬁeld when Bz < 0, it was
reasonable that it had a larger correlation with total current intensity. In addition, the Newell coupling
function, representing the rate at which magnetic ﬂux is opened at the magnetosphere, had already shown
its high predictive power in Newell et al. [2007], and the same pattern was observed for Itot.
In the case of the North Hemisphere (Figure 5.1), as the solar wind speed and the Newell parameter
(being a function of the former) increased, the total current also did. The opposite pattern was observed
for Bz and vBs where their smallest values were associated with the largest current intensities. In the
case of By and n, a positive association between them and Itot was also identiﬁed, though to a less extent.

5.1.3
Correlation with delay δt and MLT dependence
As mentioned in Chapter 3, some studies (e.g. He et al. [2014]) have investigated the current intensity
day-night response to solar wind change, and have found a noteworthy diﬀerence between both sectors.
The same approach was considered in this case, and the procedure from the previous subsection was
expanded to separate MLT sectors. The correlation coeﬃcient among the three parameters that performed
best (Bz, vBs and dΦ/dt) and the total current intensity in each MLT, is depicted in Figure 5.4. Similar
patterns were observed for both hemispheres and for the three variables. The values of r peaked (with
opposite signs before and after noon) on the dayside (0600 to 1800 MLT), on average at 10 −30 min,
while on the nightside (1800 to 0600 MLT), at 40 −100 min. He et al. [2014] reported comparable values,
where r maximized at 15 −20 min on the dayside (r ∼0.59) and at 55 −100 on the nightside (r ∼0.49;
Figures 1c and 1d in He et al., 2014), also using 10 years of CHAMP data.
Table 5.3 summarizes the peak values of r in four sectors, two in the dayside (from dawn to noon,
0600 −1200 MLT; and from noon to dusk, 1200 −1800 MLT) and two in the nightside (from dusk to
midnight, 1800 −0000 MLT; and from midnight to dawn, 0000 −0600 MLT). In general, the values of
r decreased slightly when considering the MLT dependence (e.g. none of the correlation coeﬃcients in
Table 5.3 exceeded |0.6|, while in Table 5.2, four coeﬃcients did). It is worth noting that the correlations
were highest in the dayside, in particular between 0600 and 1200 MLT, and lowest from 1800 to 0000
MLT. Another remark was that at 1200 MLT, the correlation was observed at delays much larger than
before and after noon. For instance, in the North Hemisphere and for vBs, r peaked at 1200 MLT with
δt = 60 min, while from 1000 to 1100 MLT such lag was δt = 20 min, and between 1300 and 1700 MLT, it
was δt = 10 min.

5.6
Equatorward expansion of FACs during geomagnetic storms
As mentioned in Chapter 2, one of the most prominent processes that take place during geomagnetic
storms is the equatorward expansion of the auroral oval, due to enhanced solar wind input. In the case
of extremely intense storms, the equatorial boundary of the nightside auroral oval has been observed
to expand below 50◦[Wang et al., 2006]. Iijima and Potemra [1978] found that morning and afternoon
FACs shift their centers 2◦−3◦towards the equator during substorms, in comparison to quiet conditions.
Korth et al. [2014] found that FACs expand equatorward with increasing Eyz, with a modest contribution
from Pdyn. Coxon et al. [2014] also observed that FACs move equatorward with enhanced dayside
reconnection (increasingly southward IMF) later moving poleward after substorm onset, in accordance
with the Expanding/Contracting Polar Cap Paradigm (ECPC).
The ECPC is explained in terms of the Dungey cycle, i.e. the circulation of plasma and magnetic
ﬁeld in the magnetosphere, driven by its coupling with the solar wind. Broadly speaking, as dayside
reconnection occurs, the amount of open magnetic ﬂux inside the magnetosphere increases, and later,
nightside reconnection in the tail reduces the amount of ﬂux. It is the amount of open magnetic ﬂux in
the magnetosphere which governs the location of the polar cap, thus when there is more open magnetic
ﬂux, the size of the polar cap is increased [Coxon et al., 2014].
Substorms are an essential ingredient of the ECPC. The ﬁrst phase, known as the growth phase, when

Chapter 6
Conclusions and future recommendations
On the basis of 4327 10-min averages of AMPERE radial current density measurements, ACE solar wind
and IMF data, and AL indices, and considering 58 main phases in the period 2010 −2015, an empirical
model that produces maps of FACs during geomagnetic storms has been developed. The model was written
in the form of a linear regression ﬁt and three variables were used as predictors, each representing physical
storm-time processes. The ﬁrst one, the Newell coupling function, correlated the best with total current
intensity (r ∼0.6), and measured the rate at which magnetic ﬂux was opened at the magnetopause during
dayside reconnection. The second predictor, the AL electrojet index, was in part related to substorm
activity, and represented nightside processes occurring in the magnetotail. The last variable, the By
component of the IMF, introduced asymmetry in the FAC patterns and convection cells. The response of
the magnetosphere to solar wind and IMF variability was found to depend on MLT, with a 20-min delay
in the dayside and 60-min in the nightside. These results can be explained in terms of the two main solar
wind-magnetosphere coupling modes, i.e. directly driven processes responsible for the dayside dynamics
and unloading processes that act primarily in the nightside.
The empirical model produced maps that resembled the observations, and the large-scale R1 and R2
systems were plainly visible. However, it failed to reproduce the NBZ currents as well as the ﬁne-structure
that was actually measured. Moreover, the ratio of predicted to observed total current intensity was 0.56,
and similar values between 0.38 and 61 were found for the ratio of radial current density. Moreover, none
of the other two models used in this study, namely Weimer and BATS-R-US coupled with RCM, succeeded
in yielding current intensity and radial current density predictions comparable to the observations. By
means of prediction metrics, it was found that the empirical model predicted the positions in MLAT
better than the other two models (SS > 0), as well as current enclosed in the regions, although the latter
depended on the criterion chosen to deﬁne their boundaries.
From a physical point of view, the equatorward expansion and poleward contraction of FACs associated
to the occurrence of substorms –as explained by the ECPC paradigm– was also studied. Average latitudinal
proﬁles of radial current density along the dawn-dusk meridian showed that FACs exhibited more variability
for disturbed geomagnetic conditions, both in predictions and observations (σ ∼1.5 times higher than
for quiescent intervals). Furthermore, the proﬁles based on AMPERE data exhibited more dispersion in
MLAT than the empirical and Weimer model predictions. The equatorward expansion was identiﬁed in
all the regions, though more obvious in the observed than in the predicted. The slopes of the linear ﬁts of
77
==PAGE:96==
Dst to MLAT yielded FACs moving equatorward between 1◦and 5◦per each 100 nT decrease in Dst.
Finally, three case studies of the latitudinal position of the R1 FACs centres as a function of substorm
occurrence (measured by AL) and solar wind/IMF conditions were conducted. The empirical model
reproduced in some of the cases the equatorward and poleward movement of the currents simultaneously
with the observations. However, in some other periods, the movement of the centre did not correspond to
the IMF (mainly Bz) variations, in opposition to the physical explanation of the ECPC paradigm. It was
concluded that the cause of the largest diﬀerences (both in magnitude and duration) between the empirical
model predictions and the observations of the position of R1 FACs was the substorm occurrence, as most
of the intervals with large discrepancy took place close to substorm onset. In addition, BATS-R-US
predictions of the polar cap and R1 currents location supported the role of the ECPC paradigm in
describing the dynamics of FACs during the substorm cycle.
Possible improvements to the model are the following:
• The time interval for the selection of geomagnetic storms can be widened, as AMPERE data are
readily available in the experiment website. A larger variety of storms, especially intense and
super-intense storms, would be desirable, since these were the intervals in which the predictions of
equatorward expansion of currents diﬀered the most from the observations.
• Two factors that drive ionospheric conductivity should be investigated as possible predictors for the
model: the solar radio ﬂux at 10.7 cm (F10.7) and the seasonal dependence. The distribution of
storms in the present study (8 in winter vs 28 in summer, in the North Hemisphere) prevented such
analysis to be performed. Other studies (e.g. Papitashvili et al., [2002]; Wang et al., [2006]; He et
al., [2014]) have indeed studied FACs according to season and found substantial diﬀerences among
summer, winter and equinox, revealing interhemispheric asymmetries dependent on MLT.
• As Newell et al. [2007] suggested, magnetospheric phenomena respond to solar wind driving on
various timescales and decay with diﬀerent hysteresis. Their procedure consisted on varying the
number of hours of solar wind data integrated over and the weights used in the integration. Their
conclusion suggested a "memory eﬀect", in which the previous n hours of data contribute to the
current state of the magnetosphere. It is reasonable to suggest a similar analysis for FAC intensity,
since in this study only the delay, but not the integration time or possible weighting, was considered.
• Including AL in the model did not seem to successfully account for substorm occurrence, since
it measures both the westward convection electrojet and the substorm electrojet. Separating the
contribution from the two is not straightforward, so other variables related to this phenomenon may
be included in the model instead. One of them, the distribution of aurorae, is known to be coincident
with the substorm onset and hence, with the behaviour of FACs. Measurements such as the location
and brightness of the auroral oval from the Imager for Magnetopause-to-Aurora Global Exploration
(IMAGE) cameras may be used as a proxy of substorm occurrence and of equatorward/poleward
movement of FACs.
