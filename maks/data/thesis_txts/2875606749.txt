Abstract 
 
This study evaluated the effects of bacteriophage immersion treatments on 
Flavobacterium psychrophilum infection, called Ranbow Trout Fry Syndrome (RTFS), 
on survival and physiological condition of rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) fry. 
Four treatment groups were tested: Bath Before, Bath After, SM Buffer, and No 
Treatment controls. For each group, triplicates were used: two aquaria were dedicated 
to mortality and one to carry out weekly samplings of fish. Control aquaria without the 
F. psychrophilum infection were included. Fish growth, bacteriological re-isolation, fish 
survival, and phage diffusion and density were analysed over a 7-week experimental 
period. Mean weight and length increased consistently across all treatments, with no 
significant differences in majority of condition factor (K) (p > 0.1), indicating that phage 
exposure did not influence growth performance. Bacteriological re-isolation rates were 
moderate to high in dead fish samples (40–75%), with kidneys and gills showing the 
highest infection prevalence. However, statistical analyses (p > 0.3) confirmed that re-
isolation frequencies did not differ significantly among treatments. Regarding sampled 
fish, kidney was identified as major re-isolation organ, but there was no significant 
difference between treatments. At termination, re-isolation was generally low (<20%) 
and limited primarily to the brain. Kaplan–Meier survival analysis revealed overlapping 
survival curves between treatments, with final survival ranging from 76–85% and no 
significant treatment effect (p = 0.34). Phage assays did not confirm the presence of 
active phages in tissues, and phage resistance test produced invalid results and will 
have to be repeated. Overall, the results suggest that under the tested conditions, 
phage immersion did not have a significant effect on infection dynamics or improved 
fish survival, though further research into methods of phage delivery may be required 
to achieve therapeutic efficacy. Tested conditions showed no increase in mortality and 
comparable growth indicators, suggesting safety of the treatments used.

1. Introduction  
1.1 Rainbow trout aquaculture in Denmark 
Rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) is one of the main fish species farmed in 
Denmark. It is a Salmonid fish species native to North Pacific Coast of North America 
and Asia. Rainbow trout can either spend its whole life in freshwater streams or migrate 
to seawater (FishBase.org, 2025). Those that migrate to seawater are called 
steelheads (FWS, 2025). They all prefer colder waters of around 1.3-10 °C 
(FishBase.org, 2025). They are commonly farmed freshwater species with very good 
Feed Conversion Ratio (FCR) of around 0.9, depending on system and feed. They were 
one of first species that had their life cycle harnessed and has had over a century of 
selective breeding that has yielded favourable traits for farming. Including both 
freshwater and marine farms total production for the year 2024 was about 35000 tones 
(DTU Aqua Report 474, 2024). World rainbow trout production, which is around 
940000 tonnes (D’Agaro et al., 2022), accounts for roughly 1% of the global 
aquaculture production, which is around 94 million tonnes (FAO, 2024). Trout are 
mainly farmed in raceways, ponds, and recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS). 
Ponds and raceways depend on a constant supply of clean freshwater and are subject 
to strict regulation to prevent nutrient runoff in the discharge. In contrast, RAS facilities 
recycle water, giving farmers greater control over temperature, biosecurity, and waste 
discharge (Jokumsen & Svendsen, 2010). But they are usually much more expensive 
to run and require more advanced technology, making them less suitable for small 
scale farming (Badiola et al., 2018). Aquaculture is now one of the fastest growing food 
sectors in the world. The total production of farmed aquatic animals reached 94.4 
million tonnes in 2022. This marked the first time that production had overtaken wild-
capture fisheries (FAO, 2024). Investing in more intensive production also brings new 
issues. Increased stocking densities and environmental stress can make fish more 
susceptible to disease (Boyd, 2020). Some of fish diseases include: Rainbow Trout Fry 
Syndrome (RTFS) caused by Flavobacterium psychrophilum (Madetoja et al., 2002), 
Infectious Salmon Anaemia (ISA) caused by Infectious Salmon Anaemia Virus (ISAV) 
which has caused billions in losses in Chile (Assefa and Abunna, 2018), Viral 
Haemorrhagic Septicaemia (VHS), which was eradicated in Denmark (Danish 
veterinary and Food Administration, 2023) that is caused by Viral Haemorrhagic 
Septicaemia Virus (VHSV). In Denmark, issues are caused since 1997 by Bacterial 
Kidney Disease (BKD) caused by Renibacterium salmoninarum (Lorenzen et al., 1997), 
and monitoring is also done at national level for Infectious Pancreatic Necrosis (IPN) 
caused by Infectious Pancreatic Necrosis Virus (IPNV) (Håstein et al., 2001).Recently, 
in 2021,  Infectious Hematopoietic Necrosis (IHN) caused by Infectious Hematopoietic 
Necrosis Virus was detected, and therefore  the IHN-free status held until then by 
Denmark has been revoked (Danish veterinary and Food Administration, 2023). There 
==PAGE:5==

are many other diseases caused by either viral, bacterial, fungal or protozoic 
pathogens (Alfred et al., 2020)  
Environmental and health issues in aquaculture are closely connected. When water 
quality declines, fish experience stress. Longer periods of constant stress can damage 
their immune systems beyond recovery and lead to more frequent disease outbreaks 
(Noga, 2010). Danish farms operate under tight nitrogen and phosphorus discharge 
limits (HEELCOM, 2023) and this is pushing farmers to invest in cleaner and more 
efficient technologies to reduce their output. Feed formulation is another area where 
innovations are happening. Dependence on fishmeal is considered environmentally 
unsustainable, as it removes wild fish from ecosystems. Using unprocessed fish 
byproducts may also pose biosecurity risks (Henry et al., 2015). So new ingredients 
such as insects, plants, and microbial proteins are being researched (Henry et al., 
2015). Additionally, climate change is altering water temperatures and rainfall patterns 
which may cause changes to fish diseases known characteristics (Okon et al., 2024). 
To reduce losses from diseases, as they can be substantial, and increase profits 
margins there is research into biosecurity technologies as well. New approaches and 
technologies can make disease outbreaks less common and less severe. But they 
need to be integrated with other developments to make sure they function properly.

Intensive fish farming favours the spread of pathogens. High stocking density, handling, 
and temperature changes cause stress and make fish more susceptible to disease.  
Bacterial, viral, and parasitic infections cause major economic losses through mortality, 
slow growth, and treatment costs (Austin & Austin, 2016).  
F. psychrophilum is one of the problematic bacterial pathogens in salmonid farming. It 
is a Gram-negative rod bacterium. It is a pathogen responsible for the RTFS in fry and 
bacterial cold-water disease (BCWD) in more mature fish (Madetoja et al., 2002). 
Symptoms include fin erosion, skin lesions, and systemic infection. Mortality in F. 
psychrophilum outbreaks can exceed 50% in general (Nematollahi et al., 2003), but 
there are different values per size of infected fish. Fry can reach 30-90% of mortality, 
fingerlings 10-15% and bigger fish in general develop lifelong issues instead (Lorenzen 
et al., 1996). The disease is found in temperate regions across Europe, North and 
South America, and Asia (Starliper, 2011). F. psychrophilum survives well in farm 
settings. It forms biofilms on tanks and eggs, helping it persists outside hosts (Sundell 
& Wiklund, 2011). It spreads through water and from broodstock to offspring (Brown 
et al., 1997). These traits make hatcheries especially vulnerable and full eradication 
==PAGE:6==
 
 
3 
 
difficult. Vaccination is not yet practical for small fry due to their fragility and size as 
well as not having a fully developed immune system yet (Marana et al., 2022).  Injection 
is labour-intensive and immersion vaccines offer limited protection in early life stages 
(Gómez et al., 2014). As a result, antibiotics are commonly used during outbreaks and 
there is no commercially available vaccine for RTFS (Marana et al., 2022). This practice 
of treating infection with antibiotics is becoming less effective and less acceptable in 
recent years. Resistant F. psychrophilum strains have been documented per example 
in Chilean isolates, showing reduced susceptibility to several antimicrobials (Miranda 
et al., 2016). There are also reports of isolates resistant to antibiotics in Danish farms 
(Donati & Madsen, 2023). Treatment options for fry become extremely limited once 
resistance emerges (Bondad-Reantaso et al., 2023).  
The recent spread of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a serious threat for public 
health. The World Health Organization (WHO) has listed AMR among the greatest 
threats to global health in 2023 (WHO, 2023). Overuse and improper dosage of 
antibiotics in aquaculture contributes to this problem, leading to greater regulatory 
pressure to adopt safer alternatives. (Lim et al., 2025). Approaches include selective 
breeding for resistance, improved hygiene, and novel therapies like probiotics, 
prebiotics or bacteriophages. Among these, phage therapy is a promising option for 
controlling F. psychrophilum infection in fry where antibiotics and vaccines are limited 
(Landor et al., 2025). 
 
1.3 Phages as an alternative to antibiotics 
Bacteriophages or simply phages are viruses that infect and destroy bacterial cells. 
Each phage recognizes specific receptors on a bacterial cell, it attaches and then 
injects its genetic material in the host. Then it uses the host’s cell machinery to produce 
new phage particles and when replication is complete the bacterial cell bursts. This 
mechanism releases new phages into the environment (Hyman & Abedon, 2010). 
Phages have two main life cycles called lytic and lysogenic. In the lytic cycle, the phage 
immediately takes over the bacterial cell by overtaking its protein synthesis machinery 
allowing it to replicate itself. It replicates rapidly and causes cell lysis which kills the 
host. This is the cycle used for phage therapy, as it quickly removes harmful bacteria. 
In the lysogenic cycle, the phage integrates its DNA into the bacterial genome. The 
integrated phage DNA is called prophage: it remains dormant and replicates along with 
the bacterial cell. Under stress it can switch to the lytic cycle which then leads to cell 
lysis (Weinbauer, 2004). Lytic phages are preferred for disease control because they 
actively destroy bacteria without potentially transferring genetic material between 
strains (Hyman & Abedon, 2010). Phages do not infect animals or humans; they target 
only specific bacterial hosts. This narrow host range helps protect beneficial microbes 
==PAGE:7==
 
 
4 
 
in fish and surrounding water (Hyman & Abedon, 2010). Phages are also natural and 
biodegradable. They multiply only when their bacterial hosts are present, which makes 
them unable to accumulate and environmentally safe for aquaculture (Silva et al., 
2014).  
Phage therapy is not a novel concept, it was first explored in the early 1900s after the 
discovery of bacteriophages by Twort and d’Herelle (Sulakvelidze et al., 2001). Early 
studies showed that phages could treat bacterial infections in animals and humans. 
Their use declined after antibiotics became widely available in the western world in the 
1940s following the second world war (Summers, 2012). Interest in phages has grown 
again in recent decades in the west due to the spread of antibiotic resistance, which is 
causing phages to be re-examined as an alternative disease control method. (Kortright 
et al., 2019). Advances in genomics and microbiology have allowed better 
understanding of phage–host interactions. Scientists can now identify suitable phages 
for specific pathogen causing a disease, allowing them to create phage mixtures 
known as cocktails (Chan et al., 2013). Phages have already been tested against 
several bacterial pathogens in aquaculture. These include F. psychrophilum, 
Aeromonas salmonicida, and Vibrio spp. (Silva et al., 2014; Kim et al., 2015; Donati et 
al., 2021). Experimental studies have shown that phage treatment can lower bacterial 
loads and increase fish survival rate (Fiedler et al., 2023). Researchers have explored 
different delivery routes: I. Immersion baths expose fish directly to phages in water; II. 
Oral feeding by incorporating phages into pellets; III. Intraperitoneal (IP) injection 
where phages are injected directly in the fish, bypassing the immune system of the 
fish (Donati et al., 2021). For F. psychrophilum, phage therapy is especially relevant. 
Fry cannot be vaccinated effectively, and antibiotic options are limited (Gomez et al., 
2014). These results suggest that phage-based technology could help existing 
biosecurity measures in hatcheries.  
Challenges still remain. Bacteria can develop resistance to phages similarly to 
antibiotics, but this can be reduced by combining multiple phages with different 
infection mechanisms (Chan et al., 2013). When bacteria develop resistance to phages, 
they can also change their virulence and resistance to antibiotics (Chan et al., 2016). 
F. psychrophilum showed ability to reverse the mutations that influence gliding motility 
and virulence, allowing it to switch between more virulent and less virulent states 
(Jørgensen et al., 2022). This has implications for phage therapy in general. As such 
ability can mean that F. psychrophilum might reduce virulence to survive phage 
treatment and then when phage numbers are reduced return to virulent state and 
cause disease. Phages are also very diverse across regions. This means that local 
isolates must often be matched to local pathogens. Regulatory guidelines for 
approving phage-based products are still emerging (Cisek et al., 2017). Phage therapy 
is a promising, targeted, and environmentally friendly alternative to antibiotics (Silva et 
al., 2014). It supports global efforts to combat AMR (Kortright et al., 2019) and may 
==PAGE:8==
 
 
5 
 
also help maintain a balanced microbiome in aquaculture systems and improve fish 
health (Çagatay, 2023).  
 
1.4 Aim of the Master Thesis Project 
The experiment reported and discussed in this thesis is part of the AquaPhage project, 
which investigates phage therapy and phage products as a sustainable alternative 
approach to managing RTFS. The current study focuses on bath immersion treatments, 
where fry are exposed to water containing specific phages. This allows  phages to get 
in contact with the gills, skin, and gut surface, where infections begin, and incorporate 
itself in the organs. Previous experiments showed that phages administered through 
intraperitoneal injection (IP) could reduce infection prevalence (re-isolation of the 
pathogen from fish organ samples) (Donati et al., 2021). In the same study, other 
delivery methods were used - oral delivery and immersion treatment - but significant 
differences between control and treated group were not found in these delivery 
methods, only IP showed statistical significance between control (No treatment) and 
treated fish mortality (Donati et al., 2021). The current study examines immersion 
methods as well, because they are practical and realistic to use in hatcheries. By 
testing immersion therapy, the project seeks to develop a realistic and scalable method 
to control RTFS. The findings will help reassess whether phage bath treatments can 
be integrated into commercial farming. This work supports goals of reducing antibiotic 
use and advancing environmentally responsible aquaculture.  
The main goal is to compare phage treatments methods both between infected and 
uninfected fish and  to assess if they are safe to use for the fish and if they have a 
significant effect on disease. The main questions were: 1) if the phage solution and the 
procedure used for the bath treatment could harm the fish; 2) if delivered phages have 
an effect on the RTFS development in fish and the presence of F. psychrophilum in 
fish organs. Additionally, the ability of F. psychrophilum to develop resistance to phage 
treatment was also assessed.

2. Materials and methods  
2.1 Experimental Design of Fish Trial  
An animal experiment was conducted to assess the efficacy of bacteriophage bath 
treatment against RTFS. The aquaria were divided into two main groups: infected and 
uninfected.  Each group was further subdivided into four treatments groups: no 
addition (control), SM buffer addition, phage bath, and phage bath combined with pre-
bath exposure. Infected groups were maintained in triplicate, while uninfected groups 
were maintained in duplicate. One aquarium was designated as a sampling aquarium 
in each subgroup. Other aquaria in subgroup were designated as mortality aquaria. 
From mortality aquaria dead and moribund fish were collected for survival analysis, 
while no sampling was done to ensure more realistic disease spread. Fish were 
periodically collected in sampling aquaria for size measurements, health assessments, 
bacteriological analyses, and organ sampling. The setup used can be seen on the 
Figure 1 below.

Blue uninfected group. All grouped aquaria are one subgroup. Borders represent treatment. Blue: No 
treatment; Pink: SM Buffer; Red: Bath After; Green: Bath Before. White aquaria were not used.  
Rainbow trout fry used for the experiment were obtained from the university’s 
pathogen-free nursery. Fish had an average weight of 1–2 g at the beginning of the 
experiment. A total of approximately 1,200 fry were randomly distributed into 20 
aquaria, with each containing approximately 60 fish. The aquaria had a functional water 
volume of 8 L and were supplied continuously with freshwater at a flow rate of 2-3 L/h. 
Water temperature was maintained at 12 °C.  Dissolved oxygen levels were maintained 
near saturation by means of air stones installed in each aquarium. Fish were fed twice

Bacterial isolate used was FPS-D51, which was selected among a panel isolated from 
a RAS system rainbow trout farm. It was chosen after carrying out an experimental 
infection through bath challenge where different isolates were tested to assess which 
one would have shown a good progression of the infection (survival rate around 50%). 
This isolate was chosen because of its results in the experiment and the phage 
susceptibility.  
The bacteriophage that was chosen is FPSV-D45. It was chosen after preliminary 
studies where multiple phages were tested among the established collection of F. 
psychrophilum isolates (host range). It was the most reproducible phage with good 
traits for phage treatment i.e. for its lytic characteristics. Its genome was not yet 
available at the time of the experiment. There were phages found during the study that 
had better phage treatments traits but were much harder to produce in proper 
concentrations. As a result, this phage was chosen. It was produced by a partner 
institute in the project, Danish Technological Institute (DTI), and was tested by another 
partner university, Copenhagen University (UCPH). For the first 3 weeks phage 
produced by using SANI (SANI Membranes A/S. Membrane filtration: Vibro® 
technology) membrane were used, while for the weeks 4,5and 6 the phages produced 
by Downstream Processing (DSP) method were used. Phage concentration was 
1,8x1011 Plaque Forming Units (PFU)/ml and 3x1010 PFU/ml respectively. Both phage 
preparations were stored in SM Buffer at the temperature of 4-6°C before usage.   
 
2.2 Phage Pre-bath Treatment  
On Day -1 (Tuesday), aquaria assigned to the phage bath + pre-bath subgroup 
received a phage pre-treatment before bacterial challenge. For this procedure, aquaria 
were drained and refilled with 1.8 L of freshwater and 0.2 L of phage suspension. The 
phage suspension was prepared by diluting concentrated phage stock in 
demineralized (MilliQ) water to achieve 1:100 dilution. Afterwards, the 200 ml of diluted 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
8 
 
phage suspension was additionally diluted 1:10 by mixing it with 1800ml of freshwater 
to achieve final 1:1000 dilution when it was administered to the fish in the aquaria. Fish 
were exposed to the phage suspension for 5 hours under constant aeration and no 
water flow to keep phages in aquaria. The aquaria were refilled to their standard 
volume of 8 L using the freshwater system after the period. This pre-bath procedure 
was designed to have phages already in the rearing water and in the fish themselves 
prior to infection, as some studies have suggested that it can enhance treatment 
efficacy and reduce mortality in infection. (Nikapitiya et al., 2020; Schulz et al., 2019).  
 
2.3 Bacterial Bath Challenge  
On Day 0 (Wednesday), fish were infected with the selected F. psychrophilum isolate 
by bath exposure. Bacteria were prepared as follows. First, the selected F. 
psychrophilum stored at -80°C in Tryptone Yeast Extract Salt Broth (TYES-B) medium 
supplemented with 15–20% glycerol was inoculated into 5 mL of TYES-B and 
incubated for 72 hours at 15°C at 110 RPM. Second, 0.5 mL of the pre-cultivated 
culture was transferred into 100 mL of fresh TYES-B and incubated for 48 hours under 
the same conditions. This was done in two 2L flask for total of a 2400 ml (200x12) of 
highly concentrated bacterial suspension (109 CFU/mL). For the bath challenge, the 
bacterial suspension was adjusted to a final concentration of approximately 10⁸ 
CFU/mL in aquaria, by diluting 200 mL of 10⁹ CFU/mL culture into 1800 mL of 
freshwater. This yielded a total bath volume of 2 L. Control fish were exposed to 200 
mL sterile TYES-B medium diluted in 1800 mL of water. Prior to infection, aquaria 
water was removed, and fish were exposed to air for 30 seconds to induce mild stress, 
as this increases susceptibility to infection (Donati et al., 2025) After the air exposure 
the fish had their allocated bath.  After the exposure period of 6 hours the normal water 
flow of 2-3 L/h was restored. This allowed aquaria to refill to their usual volume. TYES 
is one of the standard mediums for F. psychrophilum research (Holt et al., 1989). 
 
2.4 Phage Bath treatment  
On Day 1 (Thursday), all aquaria received their respective treatments according to 
group allocation. The 2,1 L of phage suspension was prepared by diluting 21 mL of 
concentrated phage stock in 2079 mL demineralized (MilliQ) water, to perform a 1:100 
dilution of the phage stock. Aquaria assigned to phage treatments were supplied with 
1800 mL of freshwater containing 200 mL of prepared diluted phage suspension. 
Control aquaria were treated with 2 L of freshwater only, while SM buffer-treated 
aquaria received 1.8 L of freshwater mixed with 0.2 L of prepared SM buffer. SM buffer 
is the standard buffer used for diluting and storing phages (Larsen, 2021). One 
thousand and one mL of SM buffer was prepared by diluting 10X SM buffer, by mixing

1,1 mL of SM Buffer 10X and diluting it 1:1000 in 1098,9 mL of MilliQ water. All bath 
treatments were conducted for a duration of 5 hours under constant aeration with no 
water flow, after which aquaria were refilled to a total volume of 8 L with the standard 
freshwater supply.  
Immediately following treatment and after 24 hours, water samples were collected to 
monitor phage persistence and environmental distribution. Small-volume samples (3 
mL) were taken from all aquaria to quantify phage concentration in the rearing water. 
Fifty µL of chloroform was added and samples were stored at 4-6°C.   
 
2.5 Experimental Duration and Sampling  
The treatment procedures described for day 1 (Thursday) were repeated once weekly, 
every Thursday, over a period of six consecutive weeks. The first treatment day 
(Thursday) was designated as Day 1. Subsequent samplings were conducted every 
Tuesday. On each sampling day (Tuesday), fish were collected from the sampling 
aquaria and were euthanized using overdose of anaesthetic (3-aminobenzoic acid 
ethyl ester, MS-222, 150–200 mg/L in freshwater, not buffered) (Donati et al. 2025) A 
total of 20 fish from the uninfected groups and 20 fish from the infected groups were 
collected. Uninfected groups were sampled in the morning (approximately at 10:00), 
while infected groups were sampled in the afternoon (approximately at 13:00). They 
were examined for biometric measurements (length and weight), bacteriological status, 
and for organ collection. Each fish was first weighed in grams and then placed on 
sterile dissection paper for measurement of length in centimetres. Weight and length 
measurements were used to calculate Condition Factor (K) = (

Bacteriological examination was performed prior to organ collection following 
measurements. While each Thursday was bath day, where the same exact procedure 
from day 1 were repeated with similar concentrations, midway through the experiment 
the phage stock solution was changed as the first one was depleted. Second one had 
lower concentration of active phages and was made using slightly different technology. 
The animal experiment was terminated after 7 weeks on 17th, 18th, and 19th of June,

from infected mortality aquaria (n = 20 per treatment) were also sampled to assess the 
disease status at the end of experiment and to check if F. psychrophilum can still be 
reisolated. This was done to check if any pathogen survived in the fish.

2.6 Bacteriological Examination  
Bacteriological analyses were performed using sterile inoculation loops. Methods used 
were the standard ones for fish bacteriological examination (Elliot, 2014). For each fish, 
material was collected from selected tissues and plated onto one of two TYES-A plates, 
with each plate shared by two fish. One plate was divided in four sections and streaked 
with the material from gills and mucus (external tissue), while the second plate was 
divided into six sections and streaked with samples from the brain, spleen, and kidney 
(internal tissue). This approach allowed efficient use of agar plates while making 
sampling faster. To avoid drying of fish and to obtain high-quality mucus samples, all 
mucus and gills samples collection was performed first. Then the sampling of internal 
organs was done. Plates were then placed in Incubator at 15 C for 120 hours to allow 
F. psychrophilum colonies to form. Plates were split into parts for each organ and if 
inside the area for that organ there was a F. psychrophilum colony it was considered 
positive. In dubious cases, MALDI-TOF [matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionisation 
(MALDI) time of flight (TOF) mass spectrometry (MS)] (Bruker) was used (Jansson 
et al. 2020).  
 
2.7 Organ Collection and Preservation  
During bacteriological examination, fish were also dissected to collect organs for 
further analyses. All dissections were carried out using sterile dissection kits, which 
were disinfected with 70% ethanol between fish and between organs to prevent cross-
contamination. The organs collected included mucus, gills, spleen, intestine, kidney, 
and brain. During the organ collection, before placing organs in tubes, bacteriological 
samples of internal organ were also taken. Samples were placed into prelabelled 
sterile 2 mL PCR-safe Eppendorf tubes prepared in advance. Tubes containing 300 µL 
of SM buffer were used for phage assays, pre-weighed to allow measurement of tissue 
mass. Tubes containing 500 of µL RNAlater® (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) were 
used to collect kidney and gills. These samples were collected to be further used for 
molecular analyses such as PCR and qPCR, which were performed for the purpose of 
the PhD and were not done in this study. Additional sterile tubes without preservatives 
were used for microbiome analyses. Samples stored in SM buffer were kept at 4–6°C, 
while those preserved in RNAlater or designated for microbiome studies were stored 
at −80°C. Five µL of chloroform was added to SM buffer tubes to halt microbial activity 
and preserve samples.  
For each fish, organs collection was performed as follows. At first, skin mucus was 
obtained by cutting two rectangular sections of skin from the dorsal lateral surface 
using a sterile scalpel, thereby minimizing contamination from underlying tissues. One 
sample was placed in SM buffer while the other one was placed in the empty tube for 
microbiome analysis. Gill arches were excised using scissors and processed

2.8 Mortalities Sampling  
Dead or moribund fish recovered from the mortality aquaria were sampled using the 
same procedures applied during scheduled sampling. Each dead/moribund fish was 
recorded and labelled accordingly. Bacteriological examinations of internal organs 
were performed on all dead/moribund fish, typically using one plate per fish instead of 
the shared-plate approach used for fish collected from sampling aquaria.   
 
2.9 Phage Assay  
Phage detection was carried out using organ samples collected during experiment and 
preserved in SM buffer and to perform phage count in stock solutions. Prior to the 
assay, F. psychrophilum (FPS-D51) was cultured from laboratory stock. The bacterium 
was initially grown in TYES-B broth and subsequently streaked onto TYES –A plates. 
A single colony was then transferred into fresh broth medium and incubated to obtain 
an actively growing culture suitable for use in phage overlay assays. Method used was 
based on the phage assay described by Adams in 1959.  
For plaque formation assays, the bacterial culture was mixed with molten soft TYES-A 
(0.4% agar) maintained at 43°C and poured over solidified TYES-A (1% Agar) plates 
to produce a uniform bacterial lawn. Once the overlay had solidified, 5 µL drops of SM 
buffer containing the organ sample or phage solution was spotted onto the surface. To 
release phages from the organ tissue before plating, SM buffer tubes were vortexed 
for 15 seconds and then centrifuged. Vortexing promoted the release of phages from 
tissues, while centrifugation ensured that tissue debris and chloroform remained at the 
bottom of the tube. This method left a clear supernatant suitable for spotting. For the 
stock solution, which was positive control, only vortexing was done to ensure that 
solution was well mixed. Plates were incubated under conditions optimal for F. 
psychrophilum growth, which are 15 °C. After, 120 hours the spots were checked for 
plaques that have formed. Number of plaques was then used to calculate PFU/mg of 
tissue.

2.10 Phage Resistance Assay  
To assess the potential emergence of phage-resistant bacterial variants, isolates 
recovered during the final sampling and during the experiment from fish organs were 
tested alongside F. psychrophilum stock strains against the standard phage 
suspension. Selected bacterial colonies were first inoculated into TYES-B broth and 
incubated until visible growth was achieved. The 100 µL of the resulting cultures were 
then combined with the 5 µL of phage suspension and mixed with 4mL of molten soft 
agar (0.4% agar) maintained at 43-45°C. This mixture was vortexed to ensure good 
mixing and then poured over TYES-A plates (1% Agar) to create bacterial lawns, which 
were subsequently incubated under standard growth conditions for F. psychrophilum 
as described before. Following incubation, plates were examined for bacterial growth 
patterns as indicators of resistance or susceptibility to the phage treatment. Complete 
inhibition of bacterial growth indicated full susceptibility and effective phage lysis. The 
appearance of discrete bacterial colonies rather than a continuous lawn could indicate 
the development of resistant bacterial clones. While continuous lawn indicated 
resistance to phages in bacteria.  
 
2.11 Statistical Analysis   
All statistical analyses were conducted in Rstudio (R Core Team, 2025). Data were 
checked for normality using Shapiro–Wilk tests and quantile–quantile (QQ) plots, and 
for homogeneity of variances using Levene’s and Fligner–Killeen tests. Pairwise 
treatment comparisons were conducted using Holm-adjusted post-hoc tests. The 
condition factor (K) did not meet normality assumptions even after transformation; 
therefore, non-parametric analyses were performed. Differences among treatments 
were assessed using the Kruskal–Wallis test, followed by Dunn’s post-hoc pairwise 
comparisons with Holm correction for multiple testing. Survival data were analysed 
using Kaplan–Meier survival curves and compared among treatments with the log-rank 
test. Additionally, a Cox proportional hazards model was fitted to estimate hazard ratios 
and test treatment effects on mortality risk. Statistical significance was set at p < 0.05 
for all analyses.

4. Discussion 
This study was done to assess the safety and efficacy of phage immersion treatment 
to reduce mortality and usage of antibiotics in RTFS outbreaks in rainbow trout farming. 
To do so, a fish experimental trial was set up. Fish were split into 2 groups, one infected 
with the bacterial pathogen (F. psychrophilum) and one not infected. Four different 
treatments were administered to the fish: No treatment, SM Buffer, Bath After and Bath 
before. The phage utilized was selected based on its ability to lyse the selected strain 
for infection. All treatments had no negative effect on the fish survival or condition 
factor meaning that they are safe for the fish. Infection was not significantly influenced 
by the treatments. Instead, re-isolation differed among organs and between weeks but 
not treatments. Phage assay done on samples were not usable as there was toxic 
substance in samples inhibiting F. psychrophilum growth.  
 
4.1 Growth and condition factor   
Fish weight, length, and condition factor (K) were measured throughout the experiment 
to evaluate whether phage exposure or handling affected fish performance. During 
both the mortality phase and at termination, no treatment-related differences were 
observed.  Majority of the fish analysed showed normal growth across aquaria and K 
values remained similar among treatments. The dead fish and showed no statistically 
significant differences regarding K among treatments. Fish from termination had 
statistically significant difference in variance but their average value was not significant, 
suggesting that there was more variance in certain treatment groups than other, but 
that there was no statistically significant difference between treatments. Additionally, 
tests showed a significant difference in in K between certain sampling aquaria. As 
infected aquaria had bigger K it is possible that mortality in sampling aquaria resulted 
in more feed pellets being given per fish in those aquaria than in uninfected aquaria 
resulting in slightly bigger K for fish in infected group, as feed given was not altered to 
account for the dead fish.  The lack of significant effects on fish growth between most 
of the aquaria and treatments potentially shows that phage immersion method, SM 
Buffer and TYES-B had not impacted fish condition negatively. This finding is important 
for trout aquaculture, because it shows that the methods used are safe for the fish. 
Similar results were reported by Donati et al. (2021). In the article it is reported that 
bath phage treatments did not increase mortality compared to untreated 
controls. These results are suggesting that immersion-based phage exposure is well 
tolerated by trouts. Donati et al. (2021) reported Standard Growth Ratio (SGR) values 
higher than this experiment. Possible reason could be feeding schedule that was used 
in this experiment. Especially, the fish were fed half their usual allocated feed on days 
before sampling and treating and during the days that these procedures were carried 
out. Additionally, feed amount was increased midway the experiment, so it is possible 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
28 
 
that fish have been given less than optimal amount of food for some days causing 
lower SGR.  On the other hand, their survival results align with the present study in 
showing no adverse effects on fish health or performance following phage treatment. 
Together, these findings support that phage immersion, SM buffer, and associated 
handling at the concentrations and frequencies used in this experiment are safe to use 
for rainbow trout.  
 
4.2 Survival dynamics  
Average mortality in the infected groups remained below 30% across all treatments. 
Overall, this mortality level was relatively low, and it was difficult to draw strong 
veterinary or epidemiological conclusions. The mortality rate was below average 
reported for RTFS outbreak in fry which is between 30-90% but was higher than 
mortality rate reported for fingerlings of 10-15% (Lorenzen et al., 1994). This is 
expected as exact severity of outbreak is hard to simulate in experimental setting. 
Virulence is linked to fish lines, as some fish are more resistant to certain diseases than 
others. As such it is possible that the fish used were more resistant than average. 
Disease progression among treatments followed a pattern, with a lag phase at 
beginning, an exponential phase in the middle of experiment, and a gradual decline 
toward the end of the experiment. The highest mortality was recorded in the Bath 
After group.

not cause any mortality of their own. However, Donati et al. (2022) reported that 
phage treatment can alter the gut microbiome of healthy fish even if targeted pathogen 
is not present. This could imply that there is a secondary effect. Because microbiome 
was not assessed in this thesis, potential secondary effects of the treatments remain 
unknown. Microbiome is important part of any organism, many metabolic and immune 
related interactions have been reported to be influenced by microbiome in teleost 
(Kelly and Salinas, 2017). As a result, any changes to microbiome can have an effect 
on fish health.  
The lack of mortality reduction in this study is similar to findings reported by Donati et 
al. (2021), The paper reported no significant survival difference between bath-treated 
and control groups. In their study, the overall mortality was higher across treatments 
(42–45%) than in the this experiment (20–28%). They used phage concentrations of 
approximately 10⁶ PFU/mL for the first two baths and 10⁵ PFU/mL for the third, while 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
29 
 
this experiment used higher concentrations (1.8 × 1011 and 3 × 1010 PFU/mL before 
dilution), resulting in final bath concentrations of 1.8 × 10⁸ and 3 × 10⁷ PFU/mL (1:1000 
dilution). Despite these higher doses and more frequent baths, survival did not improve 
compared with Donati et al. (2021) results. The difference in mortality between the two 
studies may be partly explained by differences in the F. psychrophilum strains, 
infection routes, and fish lines used. The present experiment used isolate FPS-D51, 
whereas Donati et al. (2021) used isolate FPS-950106-1/1, which may have differed in 
virulence. The lower mortality in this study could indicate that FPS-D51 is a less virulent 
strain. In addition, the infection method differed substantially: Donati et al. used 
intraperitoneal (IP) injection to introduce the pathogen directly into the fish, while the 
present experiment used a bacterial bath challenge to better simulate natural exposure 
under farm conditions. Bath infection more closely mimics how fish encounter F. 
psychrophilum in the environment through skin, gills, and mucosal surfaces. These 
tissues require the pathogen to overcome external immune barriers before 
establishing infection. In contrast, IP injection bypasses these defenses, which typically 
leads to higher infection rates and mortality (Donati et al., 2021; Madsen et al., 2013). 
The lower mortality observed here is therefore consistent with the less invasive 
infection route and may reflect the more natural host–pathogen dynamics expected in 
aquaculture settings, but it is still below usual reported value. Also, it has been 
described that fish lines have different susceptibility to the bacterial infection, so this 
could be also a factor influencing the infection model. 
 
4.3 Bacteriology: sampling phase (temporal and organ effects)   
Bacteriological analysis was done on samples collected weekly to monitor disease 
progression across organs, weeks, and treatments. Some organs had higher re-
isolation percentage than others, especially kidneys, which had the highest percentage, 
while skin and spleen had the least. The brain and gills had an intermediate level of re-
isolation. Similar organ dependence has been reported in previous studies, where the 
kidney was identified as a major target for F. psychrophilum infection and persistence 
(Donati et al., 2021). Additionally, skin samples were analysed for the first time in this 
kind of experiment and as such there was no certainty of how the skin samples results 
would be. The low recovery rate from skin may partly reflect methodological 
challenges, as skin drying prior to plating could have reduced bacterial detection, 
additionally, many TYES-A plates that had skin samples inoculated on them had 
overgrowth of other bacteria forming trout external microbiome. However, even when 
this is considered, the skin does not appear to serve as a major site for re-isolation for 
F. psychrophilum. The Kruskal-Wallis statistical test done showed no significance 
between treatments (p=0.221), but significant variation was detected between organs 
(p = 0.007). These results confirm that the infection pattern was organ-dependent, 
similar to findings by Donati et al. (2021), who also observed higher recovery from 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
30 
 
kidney tissue compared to other internal organs. Infection dynamics followed a trend. 
External organs, such as the skin and gills, showed higher level of detection of the 
pathogen during the initial weeks with a noticeable drop after week 3, while internal 
organs, had similar big rise in beginning but less severe decline which also began at 
week 3. This pattern likely reflects the role of external tissues as the initial barrier and 
entry point for F. psychrophilum (Jarau et al., 2018). Once the pathogen breached 
these barriers it established systemic infections in internal organs. Internal organs are 
known to support longer persistence and more severe disease (Deng et al., 2022; 
Madsen et al., 2013). The decline in re-isolation rates during weeks 4–7 indicates 
effective bacterial clearance and resolution of the outbreak. This is suggesting that the 
surviving fish immune system successfully controlled and eliminated the pathogen by 
the end of the trial.  
 
4.4 Bacteriology: mortalities and termination  
Bacteriological analyses were also carried out on samples collected from dead fish. As 
expected, these individuals showed higher re-isolation levels of F. psychrophilum than 
those sampled during the trial, which supports the assumption that mortality is a result 
of the infection. Similar patterns of elevated re-isolation in moribund or dead fish have 
been reported previously (Jarau et al., 2018; Donati et al., 2021). Re-isolation was not 
100%, most likely caused by growth of the other bacteria on TYSE-A plates, which 
made F. psychrophilum colonies undetectable. Among the examined organs the 
kidney again showed the highest average re-isolation rate. This is similar to already 
mentioned study and results from sampling aquaria that identify the kidney as a 
primary site for F. psychrophilum persistence (Donati et al., 2021). Statistical analysis 
showed no significant differences between treatments (p = 0.3). This result suggests 
that treatment type did not influence clearance or re-isoaltion of bacteria in the fish. At 
the end of the experiment, surviving fish from the infected aquaria were also sampled. 
Most re-isolations at this point were negative. This probably meant that the disease 
had largely resolved by the termination of the trial. Positive re-isolations were rare, 
generally below 10% of total samples. The brain was the organ with the most positive 
re-isolations at termination at around 13%. This finding likely reflects that the brain 
is one of the last tissues to be infected by F. psychrophilum (Donati et al., 2021; Deng 
et al., 2022), meaning that bacterial load in other organs may already have been 
reduced by the immune system of the fish. Among treatments, brain re-isolation was 
highest in the no-treatment group (20%) and lowest in the SM buffer group, both 
phage-free controls. While treatment had no significant effect (p = 0.8), organ type had 
a significant influence on re-isolation (p = 0.01), primarily due to the elevated 
prevalence observed in brain samples. This organ-specific pattern supports the 
concept that F. psychrophilum infection dynamics are tissue-dependent. Internal 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
31 
 
organs, especially kidney and brain, are last place of bacterial activity once other 
tissues have been cleared of the pathogen (Donati et al., 2021).  
 
4.5 Virology and intestinal inhibition  
Phage assays were performed to quantify phage concentrations in tissue samples, with 
the initial aim of calculating concentration per tissue mass (PFU / mg). However, the 
assays did not yield measurable plaque-forming units (PFU), preventing such 
calculations in this study. Instead, attention was directed toward a recurring issue 
observed during the assays. Which was the growth inhibition in the intestine samples. 
This inhibitory effect appeared in both phage-treated and untreated samples during 
bacterial lawn development, indicating that the phenomenon was not caused by phage 
activity but rather by a compound originating from the intestinal microbiome. Similar 
interactions between microbial metabolites and phage detection have been reported 
in studies investigating gut–phage–microbiome dynamics (Fujiki et al., 2023; Albarella 
et al., 2025). At higher dilutions no plaques were observed, and the inhibitory effect 
disappeared completely. This result is suggesting that dilution reduced the 
concentration of the inhibitory substance below its minimum inhibitory concentration 
(MIC). This pattern supports the hypothesis that the substance was antimicrobial in 
nature rather than viral. Consequently, no PFU values are reported in this thesis 
beyond those established for the initial phage concentrates. It is therefore plausible 
that a compound produced by the fish’s normal intestinal microbiome interfered with 
bacterial growth on the assay plates.  
 
4.6 Phage resistance check  
Phage resistance testing was done to assess the ability of used F. psychrophilum 
isolate to develop resistance to the applied phage during the trial. The test was done 
once using bacterial isolates collected during the last 2 weeks only. All of the assays 
were positive. Even those that were not supposed to have any growth (control plate) 
showed growth. This means that there was some issue with methods and procedures 
done during the resistance test. As such no conclusion was done on phage resistance 
in this experimental setup. If there was more time the experiment would be redone, 
and the cause of failure would need to be found.  
4.7 Why immersion phage therapy did not alter outcomes  
The lack of measurable therapeutic effect from a phage immersion can be attributed 
to several factors. First, phage presence on fish mucosal surfaces was 
insufficient to provide 
protection. 
Similar 
studies 
have 
shown 
that 
phage 
concentrations at infection sites decline rapidly after immersion, limiting the time 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
32 
 
window for bacterial suppression (Christiansen et al., 2014). In this study, the baths 
may have resulted in phage contact with the skin or gill mucus, but it was insufficient 
to establish a protective population. Additionally, mucosal and immune barriers could 
have reduced phage viability. Phages can be cleared by the host before reaching 
bacterial cells (Kortright et al., 2019).  
Another consideration is the specificity and matching between phage and bacterial 
strain.  Phages are typically strain-specific, and a single isolate may not target all 
bacterial variants within an outbreak (Hyman & Abedon, 2010). As a single isolate was 
used and it was tested with phage before this is most likely not the case here. However, 
suboptimal host–phage pairing can limit efficacy. Additionally, systemic spread of F. 
psychrophilum may have outpaced phage access and spreading through the organism. 
Immersion delivery primarily affects external surfaces. This means that once infection 
spreads internally phages cannot easily reach the bacteria (Donati et al., 2021). Bath 
treatments are generally the least effective route for rapidly progressing systemic 
diseases, as they depend on sustained mucosal contact (Christiansen et al., 2014). 
Together, these factors help explain why bath phage therapy did not significantly alter 
outcomes in this experiment.  
 
4.8 Limitations  
Several limitations should be acknowledged. Infection assessment relied on binary re-
isolation (presence/absence) rather than quantitative bacterial load, preventing 
evaluation of treatment influence on infection intensity. The design also lacked 
repeated within-fish sampling, which would have allowed tracking of infection 
dynamics over time in individual. But using such methods would be very technically 
challenging and require specialized protocols and techniques. Technical limitations in 
phage quantification were another constraint: growth inhibition from intestinal and 
tissue components prevented plaque enumeration, a problem also noted in other in 
vivo phage therapy studies (Donati et al., 2021). Consequently, in-vivo 
phage concentration remains unknown, limiting interpretation of efficacy. It is possible 
that there were phages in samples that were tested during the experiment, but they 
were below detection limit for methods used and were therefore not detected and 
recorded. Increasing drop volume from 10µL as was used in this experiment to 100µL 
might be a way to decrease detection limit. One another possible reason for lack of 
phage detection could be sampling schedule as well. Organs were sampled on 
Tuesdays, while bath treatment was administered on Thursdays. This means that there 
are 5 days between treatment and sampling. As such, it is possible that phage 
concentration in organs was higher on days after treating and then declined over time.  
Lastly, results are context dependent. They reflect one bacterial isolate (FPS-D51), 
one phage, and an immersion route. Other combinations of different phages, more 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
33 
 
repeated exposures, or alternative delivery routes might yield different outcomes. 
These limitations mean the findings cannot be generalized beyond this experimental 
framework.  
 
4.9 Implications for aquaculture and future prospects 
From an aquaculture perspective, the absence of adverse effects on fish survival or 
growth demonstrates that phage treatment was safe under the tested conditions. This 
agrees with other work showing that lytic bacteriophages are tolerated in aquaculture 
species and do not impair growth or water quality (Donati et al., 2021; Kortright et al., 
2019). However, the immersion failed to reduce mortality or bacterial re-isolation, 
which is consistent with other studies where baths provided no lasting protection 
(Donati et al., 2021). As a result, while safe this approach was ineffective against F. 
psychrophilum under the used dosage and method.  
Future studies could test higher phage concentration, more repeated exposures, or 
phage cocktails (Castillo et al., 2012; Jørgensen et al., 2022). Higher number and more 
frequent exposure could help phages establish themselves on the fish surfaces and 
potentially inside. Phage cocktails have been proven to be more effective and are used 
for human health as well. Extended mucosal contact could also be explored through 
adhesive or slow-release formulations that prolong phage presence. Donati et al. 
(2021) explored different delivery methods and concluded that feed-based method 
might be a better idea. Especially when the logistics of performing a bath in true 
aquaculture tanks (>1000L) is considered. As such fixing phages on feed and finding 
effective concentrations of phages in feed should be explored. A common issue in this 
experiment was overgrowth by other bacteria on TYES-A plates. A study into a way to 
inhibit this growth might also be a good avenue of research that could be useful for 
many studies regarding F. psychrophilum 
 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
34 
 
5. Conclusion 
All in all, there is no significant difference between the treatments. There is a significant 
difference in K only among sampling aquaria, while other K values were insignificant. 
Re-isolation and survival was insignificant among all treatments and in all groups. 
These results suggest that immersion therapy treatments methods and concentrations 
that were used for RTFS were not effective at reducing fish mortality. Similar K 
between infected and uninfected group suggests that the fish was not negatively 
affected by methods used, meaning that the treatments could be considered safe. The 
gut microbiome was not analysed, meaning that there could have been some 
unidentified negative aspect that would only show its effect later, when the fish reached 
more mature size. Phage assays had issues with antimicrobial substance in intestine 
samples. A similar issue was already noted before in another study.  A modified assay 
method that deactivates the substance could be useful for a future similar experiment. 
Phage resistance testing was not successful in this thesis so no insight into resistance 
was made. In conclusion, bath immersion frequency and concentration used in this 
experiment was not optimal as a therapeutic measure against RTFS. Other papers 
have suggested that feed-based technology, with phages fixated on feed pellets, could 
serve as a better delivery method of phages. A possible issue within the current 
experiment was the lack of phages in internal organs of the fish. Feed pellets could 
have delivered the phages to the intestine from where they would have easier time 
propagating trough the organism. Future studies in immersion therapy should focus 
on finding what barriers stop the immersion method from being effective, especially 
what blocks phage entrance, so that these issues may be overcome.
