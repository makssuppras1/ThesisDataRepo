Abstract
The consumption of meat stored in modiﬁed atmosphere packaging (MAP) has increased
during the last years. The composition used for fresh meat products today is usually
70-80% oxygen and 20-30% carbon dioxide. It is, however, well known that both protein
and lipid oxidation are negative side eﬀects of the high oxygen concentration in MAP.
Despite that muscle physiological characteristics have been shown to impact both lipid
and protein oxidation, only little attention has been paid to the subject.
The objective of this study was, in the light of previous work, to investigate lipid
and protein oxidation in glycolytic, oxidative and fat tissue. For this purpose pieces of
pork belly were evaluated after 2 or 7 days of refrigerated storage in anoxic (0% O2) or
high-oxygen (80% O2) MAP. The tissue types were characterised based on fatty acid
composition, pigment content and for muscle tissue using ﬁbre-typing. Primary and
secondary lipid oxidation products were evaluated from peroxide values and thiobarbituric
acid reactive substances. The oxidative status of the proteins was qualitatively evaluated
from western blot against myosin heavy chain and using oxy-blots.
Muscle physiology was found to have an impact on the oxidation processes taking place
during storage in modiﬁed atmosphere. The protein oxidation was more intense in the
glycolytic muscle tissue, where the lipid oxidation was dominating in the oxidative muscle
tissue. The fat tissue was found less oxidised in regard to lipid oxidation.

Introduction
The consumption of meat stored in modiﬁed atmosphere packaging (MAP) has increased
during the last couple of years (dr.dk, 2014). The usual composition used for fresh meat
products today is 70-80% oxygen and 20-30% carbon dioxide but there are both pros and
cons with this composition of atmosphere. The high concentration of oxygen helps to
maintain the red colour (Grill, 1996) and the carbon dioxide inhibits the microbiological
growth and hereby extend the shelf life (Belcher, 2006). It is favourable for the industry to
be able to produce a product with a longer shelf life. At the same time the maintaining of
the red colour appeals to the consumers view of a fresh meat product. It is, however, well
known that both protein and lipid oxidation are negative side eﬀects of the high oxygen
concentration in MAP.
Oxidation of both lipids and proteins have been reported and both found to cause
quality deterioration (Delles et al., 2011; Lund et al., 2007). Rancidity of the meat can
appear due to lipid oxidation (W¸asowicz et al., 2004) where oxidation of proteins can lead
to cross-linking causing textural changes (Lund et al., 2007). The tenderness has been
suggested to be linked to the formation of cross-linking (Bao and Ertbjerg, 2015; Lund
et al., 2007). These are all quality parameters which have impact on the consumer appeal.
The oxidation level depends on a range of both endogeneous and exogenous factors
(Min et al., 2008). The content of the prooxidant iron varies greatly not only between
mammalians but also between muscle types (Min et al., 2008). However, the impact of
muscle physiological characteristics is not taken into to account in MAP or well docu-
mented in literature. A range of previous studies have been carried out to investigate
MAPs inﬂuence on a single mammalian muscle (Bao and Ertbjerg, 2015; Lund et al., 2007;

Zakrys-Waliwander et al., 2012). A study including diﬀerent muscles has been carried out
as well, although the muscles were subjected to diﬀerent treatments prior to packaging in
MAP (Spanos et al., Submitted).
The aim of this project is in the light of previous work to investigate how the gas
composition aﬀects lipid and protein oxidation in the diﬀerent muscle and fat tissue of
pork belly when packed in MAP. A piece of pork belly consist of light muscle tissue from
one muscle and dark muscle tissue from one or more muscles, Figure 1.1. Pork belly was
found as a suitable cut for this project as the diﬀerent muscle tissues have been through
the exact same treatments. Variance from processing and environmental condition can
therefore be eliminated.
Figure 1.1: A piece of pork belly illustrating the light muscle tissue at the upper part of
the piece and the many groups of dark muscle tissue at the lower part of the piece.
The pork belly was packed in MAP with 5 diﬀerent gas compositions and stored for
2, 5 and 7 days. The storage time 5 day will not be evaluated in this study. Mainly two
diﬀerent gas compositions will be evaluated for storage time 2 and 7, namely anoxic MAP
containing 0% oxygen and high-oxygen MAP containing 80% oxygen. For some analysis
more or less of the gas compositions will be evaluated.
The diﬀerent samples will be characterised and the level of fat, meat pigments, ﬁbre-type
and natural content of the antioxidant α-tocopherol will be quantiﬁed. The level of
lipid oxidation will be examined from the level of thiobarbituric acid reactive substances
(TBARS) and the peroxide value (PV). Diﬀerent methods will be evaluated for the
determination of PV. Sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-

Lipid Oxidation
The following chapter will focus on lipid oxidation in pork meat. Unsaturated lipids
occurring in food systems are very susceptible to lipid oxidation which can lead to
rancidity and compromise the food quality (Gabri¨els et al., 2000; W¸asowicz et al., 2004).
Furthermore some products of lipid oxidation are suspected to be hazardous to health
(Min et al., 2008).
Lipid oxidation is aﬀected by the food matrix. It can be introduced as early as in
the ﬁrst manufacturing steps and may continue through distribution, storage and ﬁnal
consumption. There is a large range of factors which may inﬂuence lipid oxidation, some of
which is introduced pre slaughtering (amount and type of unsaturated lipids1, metals and
enzyme activity) but also a range of factors which may be introduced post slaughtering
(light, temperature and oxygen) (Gabri¨els et al., 2000; Lametsch, 2007; Min et al., 2008).
Further the naturally presence of prooxidants and antioxidants in meat have an impact on
the level of lipid oxidation (W¸asowicz et al., 2004). The natural balance of these substances
is destroyed as the muscle is converted into meat after slaughtering (Min et al., 2008).
Lipid oxidation can occur in diﬀerent ways, namely autoxidation and photo oxidation
(W¸asowicz et al., 2004). This chapter will discuss these two types of oxidation with respect
to initiation, mechanisms and the chemical markers they leave in food.
1The lipid composition is known to be aﬀected by the diet of the pig (Danish Pig Research Centre by
H. Maribo, 2011) as well is the content of intramuscular fat known to vary between muscles.

2.1
Initiation and Mechanisms of Lipid Oxidation
Oxidation of lipids in pork is initiated as soon as oxygen is available. As previously
mentioned the atmosphere used in MAP often consists of 70-80% oxygen to which oxygen
is available for the meat from the ﬁrst processing steps until consumption. The mechanism
of autoxidation and photo oxidation along with their initiation will be described in this
section.
2.1.1
Autoxidation
Autoxidation of lipids, also referred to as lipid peroxidation, is found to be one of the
primary causes of quality deterioration in meat (Baron and Andersen, 2002; Min et al.,
2008). It is a spontaneous reaction initiated by formation of a free radical which persist the
mechanism. The essential stages of autoxidation can be divided in three steps; initiation,
propagation and termination (Coultate, 2009; Gabri¨els et al., 2000; W¸asowicz et al., 2004).
The initiation step occurs in the presence of an initiator (In) which abstract the hydrogen
from the unsaturated lipid (LH) resulting in the formation of a lipid radical (L•)(W¸asowicz
et al., 2004). The In can either be a free radical present in the food, such as hydrogen
peroxide radical (HOO•), hydroxide radical (HO•), lipid peroxide radical (LOO•), lipid
alcoxyl radical (LO•) and L•, or it can be an alkyl formed by thermal or photochemical
homolytic cleavage of an R–H bond (Kolakowska, 2003). Singlet oxygen (1O2), a very
reactive form of oxygen, gives rise to the formation of the initiation radical (Baron and
Andersen, 2002; Coultate, 2009).

Equation 2.1.1 (Coultate, 2009; Frankel, 1980; W¸asowicz et al., 2004).

Metals can have an inﬂuence on the initiation of lipid autoxidation. Traces of transition
metals such as iron (Fe) and Copper (Cu) can through a one-electron redox reaction
decompose LOOH (W¸asowicz et al., 2004). The initiating reactions are described by
Equation 2.1.8-2.1.9. Mn is the metal in oxidation state n. Mn + 1 is the metal in

Mn + LOOH →LO• + OH−+ Mn+1
(2.1.8)
Mn+1 + LOOH →LOO• + H+ + Mn
(2.1.9)
In Equation 2.1.8 the metal acts as an electron doner. Hereby the the metal is oxidised
and a LO• is formed. In Equation 2.1.9 the metal is reduced and a LOO• is formed. The
radicals can then react in the propagation step of autoxidation and the reduced metal can
react again in Equation 2.1.8 (Honikel, 2009; W¸asowicz et al., 2004). Iron is recognised as
the crucial transition metal in regard to lipid oxidation and can exist in a great range of
diﬀerent forms in biological systems (Baron and Andersen, 2002; Min et al., 2008). Iron
can react with hydrogen peroxide similar to the reaction with LOOH. This is known as
the Fenton reaction and is also known to initiate lipid oxidation (Bartosz, 2014).

Myoglobin (Mb) and other heme-compounds can initiate lipid autoxidation (Baron and
Andersen, 2002). The prooxidative activity of Mb is coupled to the colour cycles of meat,
as illustrated in Figure 2.1.
Ferrylmyoglobin (MbFe(IV)=O) has been recognised as one of the species which has
the greatest impact on lipid oxidation (Min et al., 2008). It is able to form a lipid
peroxide radical by reaction with a lipid peroxide. Metmyoglobin (MbFe(III)) is in the
presence of hydrogen peroxide able to form perferrylmyoglobin (•MbFe(IV)=O) which is
rapidly reduced into MbFe(IV)=O. The reduction can either occur by auto reduction or
by reaction with a lipid resulting in the formation of a lipid radical (Baron and Andersen,

Enzymatic Initiation of Autoxidation
Oxidation of lipids in meat can be initiated by activity of the enzymes, e.g. Lipoxygenase.
Lipoxygenase is an iron-containing enzyme capable of directly oxidising an unsaturated
lipid forming a LOOH (Min et al., 2008). To activate the enzyme, iron is oxidised from
Fe(II) to Fe(III) by an already present LOOH or H2O2 (Honikel, 2009; W¸asowicz et al.,
2004). The Fe(III) of the active enzyme is reduced to Fe(II) while oxidising the unsaturated
lipid. The oxidation of the lipid releases hydrogen from a methylene group (Honikel, 2009).
This forms a conjugated diene system, which by reaction with oxygen, generates ﬁrst a
lipid peroxide radical and ﬁnally a LOOH (Honikel, 2009; W¸asowicz et al., 2004). As
described hydrogen peroxide can react to form free radicals, Equation 2.1.4. Thereby,
lipoxygenase is able to initiate autoxidation (Honikel, 2009; Min et al., 2008).

Photo Oxidation
Photo oxidation can proceed through two diﬀerent mechanisms namely type I and type
II. Type I is the main photo oxidation mechanism taking place in protein rich tissue. In
tissue with a high content of lipids type II will be the dominating mechanism (Grippa
et al., 2014). Photo oxidation requires a sensitiser and UV-light to initiate (W¸asowicz
et al., 2004). In pork the known photo sensitisers Riboﬂavin (Vitamin B2) is present which
could make photo oxidation possible in combination with UV light, unsaturated lipids and

Primary Oxidation Products
The primary oxidation products are the LOOHs formed during the propagation step where
a LOO• absorb a hydrogen from a lipid, Equation 2.1.3 (Ayala et al., 2014; Hamilton
et al., 1997). The LOOHs are non-volatile, odorless and relatively unstable compounds.
Their ability to break down to two free radicals makes them very destructive and essential
for lipid autoxidation, Equation 2.1.4 (Hamilton et al., 1997).
The content of LOOHs can be measured through the peroxide value (PV). By the
addition of iron(II) in the presence of peroxides, iron(II) will be oxidised to iron(III).
Iron(III) can reacts with thiocyanate to form a red ferri-thiocyanate-complex which can
be determined spectrophotometric. The reactions of the complex formation are described

The PV can however only be used as an indicator because of the instability of peroxides

Secondary Oxidation Products
The secondary oxidation products are, on the contrary to the primary, stable. They
are volatile aromatic compounds perceived as oﬀ-ﬂavors and a warning that the food is
no longer edible. The secondary products can be aldehydes, ketones, acids and alkanes
formed at the termination step, Equation 2.1.5-2.1.7. The secondary oxidation product is
dependent on the fatty acid oxidised. Malondialdehyde (MDA) is often used as a secondary
product marker for oxidative stress (Ayala et al., 2014). It is one of the end products of
lipid oxidation of many fatty acids. It is mainly formed from cleavage of a diene peroxyl
(Coultate, 2009), which is formed during the oxidation of polyunsaturated fatty acids
(PUFA) containing more than two methylene-interrupted double bonds (Esterbauer et al.,
1991).

of its ability to decompose hydrogen peroxide. Hydrogen peroxide is known to initiate
lipid autoxidation by reaction with metmyoglobin (Baron and Andersen, 2002; Min et al.,

Protein Oxidation
The following chapter will focus on protein oxidation in pork meat. A large range of
diﬀerent proteins are present in meat. Likewise lipids, proteins are able to undergo changes
during storage. Protein oxidation is highly correlated to lipid oxidation although the
formation of protein oxidation products have been reported to occur slower than for lipid
oxidation (Lametsch, 2007). Protein oxidation can lead to a range of diﬀerent consequences
for the meat product. It can aﬀect the water-holding capacity of the meat, which is linked
to the amount of drip loss occurring during storage (Delles and Xiong, 2014; Xiong, 2000).
Further protein oxidation has been linked to textural changes of meat due to the possibility
of cross-linking between proteins (Lund et al., 2007).
Protein oxidation will be discussed in relation to initiation and mechanism, along with
the diﬀerent consequences protein oxidation leads to in pork meat. Analytical methods
for determination of protein oxidation will be discussed brieﬂy. Further protein oxidations
impact on respectively meat texture and lipid oxidation will be addressed.
3.1
Initiation and Mechanisms of Protein Oxidation
The mechanisms and impact of protein oxidation in fresh meat are highly unknown (Zhang
et al., 2013) and is a major study area in the ﬁeld of food chemistry currently. Protein
oxidation is initiated under the same conditions as lipid oxidation, an elevated level oxygen
activated by light or occurrence of reactive species such as radicals, peroxides and metal
ions (Lametsch, 2007).
Likewise lipid oxidation, protein oxidation is believed to proceed through a free radical

3.2
Markers of Protein Oxidation
The formation of carbonyl groups is often used as a general marker for protein oxidation.
If searching for the oxidation of a speciﬁc amino acid another marker should be used. It
is, however, not all oxidation products which are stable enough to function as markers
(Davies et al., 1999).
The carbonyls are relatively stable compounds but the precision of carbonyls as a marker
for protein oxidation can be discussed. The carbonyls α-aminoadipic and γ-glutamic
semialdehydes (AAS and GGS, respectively) formed from the side chains of lysine and
arginine respectively account for approximately 70% of the total protein carbonyls formed
in meat (Lund et al., 2011). However, they are able to undergo a further reaction which
leads to the formation of acids. Since acids are not able to undergo reaction with DNPH a
further reaction will lead to an underestimation of the protein oxidation level. Furthermore
carbonyls can be formed through non-oxidative pathways (Adams et al., 2001; Lametsch,
2007; Xiong, 2000) which would lead to an overestimation of the content.
3.3
Interaction between Protein and Lipid Oxidation
The interaction between lipid and protein oxidation is dependent on several factors.
ROS has diﬀerent reaction rates with diﬀerent proteins and fatty acids. For instant the
radical HO• will react faster with the proteins albumin (k=8 · 1010dm3mol−1s−1) and
collagen (k=4 · 1011dm3mol−1s−1) than with the unsaturated lipid linoleic acid (k=9 ·
109dm3mol−1s−1)(Bartosz, 2014).

As described the oxidation of both lipids and proteins results in the formation of radicals.
The radical formed through lipid oxidation might initiate the oxidation of proteins but this
could also occur the opposite way (Bartosz, 2014; Lund et al., 2008). Previous studies have
reported both, but diﬀerences in the initiation as well as food matrix makes them diﬃcult

occur faster than protein oxidation in quadriceps femoris (Lund et al., 2008), which is
believed to be an oxidative muscle.

4.1
Muscle Physiology
Pork belly consists of striated muscle tissue for which the muscle ﬁbres consist mainly
of My and actin ﬁlaments. The muscle ﬁbres can be separated into two categories, type
I and type II ﬁbres. Type II can be divided into further three subcatogories, namely
a, x and b (McArdle et al., 2009). Type I ﬁbres are predominating in oxidative muscle
ﬁbres which are the majority in muscles having a constant activity. They have a high
content of Mb compared to the muscle predominated by type II ﬁbres (glycolytic muscle
ﬁbres). Since Mb is a red pigment, muscles with a high content of oxidative ﬁbres are often
referred to as red muscles, where muscles with a high content of glycolytic muscle ﬁbres
are referred to as white muscles (Warberg, 2009). Besides Mb red muscle ﬁbres are rich
in mitochondrias, capillaries and lipids, where white muscle ﬁbres are rich in glycolytic
enzymes (e.g. Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide - hydrogen (NADH)) (Ess´en-Gustavsson
et al., 1994; Ma et al., 2015; McArdle et al., 2009). The enzyme lipolysis which is able to
break down triglycerides to free fatty acids has also been found to appear at a higher level

in oxidative muscle tissue of rats (Alasnier et al., 2000).
The MyHC composition of the ﬁbres is also diﬀerent between muscle types. Red oxidative
muscle ﬁbres have a high content of MyHC7 where white glycolytic muscle ﬁbres, Type II a,
x and b have a high content of respectively MyHC2, MyHC1 and MyHC4 (McArdle et al.,
2009). It should, however, be mentioned that another classiﬁcation of ﬁbre composition
has been suggested (Ma et al., 2015).

Lipid Oxidation
The degree of lipid oxidation was evaluated based on both the presence of primary and
secondary lipid oxidation products.
6.2.1
Primary Oxidation Products
The primary oxidation products, PV, was evaluated using two diﬀerent extraction methods
as the conclusion of the previous PV results described in Section 5.3.5. Both methods gave
PVs in the same range. Method (1) using the extraction technique described by Bligh
and Dyer, 1959, was found to give higher standard deviations and more irregular results
than method (2). The results of method (1) are found in Appendix F. Method (2) using
the extraction technique by Hara and Radin, 1978, and suggested by Shanta and Decker,
1994, to be used for meat samples was found to give the best results. The results from
Method (2) are found in Table 6.3 for both per kg oil and per kg tissue. By expressing
the results per kg of tissue not much information was revealed. A tendency to increase in
PV level from dark to light and then fat was observed. However, the increase was only
found to be signiﬁcant between dark and fat tissue, except in anoxic MAP at 2 days, and
between dark and light in high-oxygen MAP at 7 days. For the short storage time (2 days)
the gas composition did not have an impact on the PV. At the long storage time (7 days)
the increased level of oxygen in the MAP resulted in an increase of the PV. The storage
time was only found to have signiﬁcant impact on dark tissue in high-oxygen MAP. In the
anoxic MAP all tissue types seemed to be unaﬀected by the storage time whereas in the
high-oxygen MAP a tendency of increased storage time was observed.
For the PV per kg of oil no changes in the fat tissue were observed for storage time 2
days. The PV per kg oil are illustrated in Figure 6.6. For the fat tissue with in storage
time of 7 days the increase in oxygen resulted in a signiﬁcant increase in the PV. For both
light and dark tissue the PV per kg oil was found to increase with the increase of oxygen
in the MAP. For the light tissue the PV per kg oil was also found to increase with longer
storage time. For the dark tissue an opposite tendency was found, although it was not
signiﬁcant. A signiﬁcant diﬀerence between tissue types was found for all combination
of storage time and gas composition with dark tissue having the highest PV and fat the
lowest.

Protein Oxidation
The protein oxidation of the samples was evaluated from oxiblot. Furthermore the presence
of cystein cross-linking oxidation products were evaluated trough comparison of western
blotting of the samples under both reduced and non-reduced conditions.
6.3.1
Identiﬁcation of Carbonyls
The identiﬁcation of carbonyl groups were attempted through oxiblotting. The blotting
was performed using two diﬀerent methods as described in Section 5.4.4. By the use of
method (1) no useful oxyblot was obtained due to unspeciﬁc binding. The oxyblots can be
found in Appendix G. The results presented here are obtained by the use of method (2).
Two oxyblots were performed for the muscle tissue, both on non-reduced samples. The
ﬁrst blot was done to compare the impact of both storage time and oxygen concentration
in the MAP. The blot is shown in Figure 6.8. In general the light muscle tissue revealed
more intense bands as well as more bands compared to the dark muscle tissue. Increases
in both oxygen concentration and storage time seamed to increase the intensity of the
bands for the light muscle tissue. For the dark muscle tissue the strongest signal was
observed for the sample stored in anoxic MAP for 7 days. At short storage time (2 days)
the intensity of the bands increased with increase in the oxygen concentration. Carbonyl
formation has previously been reported to increase with increased storage time (Lund
et al., 2007; Yang et al., 2013).
The bands just below 238 kDa are believed to be carbonyl formed in MyHC. For the
light tissue the bands observed above MyHC are cross-linked MyHC. A single band above
MyHC was also present for the dark tissue stored in anoxic MAP in 7 days. For the light
tissue the cross-linking products seemed to be aﬀected by storage time. Additionally the

6.4
Summative Discussion
For both muscle types the oxidation was aﬀected by the storage time and oxygen concen-
tration in the packaging. Based on previous results reported by others this was expected
(Lund et al., 2007). Furthermore the oxygen concentration in the packaging has been
reported to eﬀect the penetration of oxygen into the meat (Chaix et al., 2014; Zaritzky and
Bevilacqua, 1988). The lipid oxidation of the fat tissue was also aﬀected by both storage
time and oxygen concentration but for the protein oxidation no patterns was observed.
54
==PAGE:75==
Comments on Failed Experiments
This could be due to the in general low content of protein in the fat, but also the fact that
the gel did not exhibit suﬃcient strength to get through the blotting procedure.
The muscle tissue revealed contrary oxidation levels between protein and lipid oxidation
with the dark tissue exhibiting the most lipid oxidation and the light tissue the most
protein oxidation (based on carbonyls). These results supports that the rate of lipid and
protein oxidation is highly correlated to the characteristics of the lipid and protein fraction.
Myoglobin has been suggested to catalyse both lipid and protein oxidation. The results
from this work support the theory of myoglobin catalysing lipid oxidation. Although it
also supports the theory of myoglobin resulting in the formation of myosin cross-linking as
reported by others (Frederiksen et al., 2008). The cross-linked products found in the dark
muscle tissue were not carbonylated as they did not appear on the oxyblot. Carbonylated
cross-linking products were observed for the light tissue in the oxyblot. If additional non
carbonylated MyHC cross-linked product were formed is unknown and should be examined
further. The content of non-heme iron has been linked to the carbylation of amino acid
side chains and could be a factor leading to the higher degree of protein oxidation in the
light muscle tissue. However, to make any conclusion on this it is necessary to carry out
further analysis on the muscle tissues iron content. The amino acid composition could also
have an impact since some amino acids are known to have a greater impact on protein
oxidation. Any conclusion on this will although also require some further work. Further it
is known that some carbonyls are able to undergo further reaction, which results in the
formation of acids. These acids will not appear on the oxyblot.
The presence of spoilage bacteria has previously been found to be higher at long storage
time when packed in high-oxygen MAP (Tørngren et al., 2013). Whether the presence
of spoilage bacteria’s could have an impact on the oxidation rate could be considered.
However, the study was only preformed on pork belly as one piece.

Further Work
The results obtained in this work are not possible to transfer directly to an industrial use.
Further work is necessary for putting it in to such a perspective.
It was in this study proven that the physiological properties of the muscle tissue have
an impact on the oxidation when stored in high-oxygen MAP. However, it will newer be
relevant to split the muscle of pork belly as done in this study. Pork belly has previously
been found to obtain the best quality when packed in a high carbon dioxide modiﬁed
atmosphere (Tørngren et al., 2013). However, the impact of the muscle physiology makes it
relevant to consider diﬀerentiating the gas composition for diﬀerent meat cuts and animals.
Textural changes along with shelf life and appearance should also be included in further
analyses as they are highly relevant for the consumer appeal and thereby interesting for
the industry to optimise.
From a more food chemistry angle the indistinctness of the degree of oxidation and
mechanisms still exists and not all analyses were performed with success. The failed
analyses should be repeated but since time was a limiting factor it was not possible to do
it in this study. Along with repetition of the failed analyses additional analyses could also
help to clear the mechanisms going on in the diﬀerent tissue.
All protein analyses preformed in this work were qualitative. To be able to compare the
results with other studies quantitative methods should be applied. Some suggestion could
be determination of heme- and non-heme bound iron as well as determination of free thiol
groups.
Additionally the analyses of the protein in the fat tissue should be optimised further
and some work should be done to characterise the protein proﬁle as well.

==PAGE:78==
Further Work
A more thorough product characterisation should be carried out including the amino
acid composition of the muscle tissue. Since it is known that some amino acids are more
susceptible to oxidation than other it could clear information about the diﬀerence in
oxidation between glycolytic and oxidative muscle tissues. The characterisation of the
lipid fraction should be carried out for the samples in high-oxygen MAP as well as it might
reveal information of which lipids that undergo oxidation.
The inﬂuence of carbon dioxide in MAP was proven to have an impact on the oxidation
proﬁle of the meat proteins. Furthermore it has previously been linked to a crispier texture
of the cooked product. In spite of these ﬁndings it could be interesting to evaluate carbon
dioxides impact further.

8
Conclusion
The results in this study clearly show that muscle physiology has an impact on the
oxidation processes taking place during storage in modiﬁed atmosphere. Great diﬀerences
were revealed between the two muscle types examined. In regard to carbonyl formation
the protein oxidation was more intense in the glycolytic muscle tissue. Both primary and
secondary lipid oxidation products were dominating in the oxidative muscle tissue. The
gas composition along with the storage time had an impact on the lipid oxidation. In the
glycolytic muscle tissue formation of carbonyls was found to be depended on both storage
time and oxygen concentration as well.
Cross-linking was found in both muscle tissue types and did for both appear to be
depended on both storage time and oxygen concentration in the MAP. However, the
cross-linking observed in the oxidative muscle tissue was not found to be carbonylated. In
the oxidative muscle tissue the cross-linking occurred between with myosin heavy chain
Type I. For the glycolytic it is unknown. A comparison of the degree of cross-linking in
glycolytic and oxidative muscle tissue respectively, was not possible.
The exact impact of the presence of the prooxidative myoglobin was not fully established.
Further work should be done to examine the characterisation of the muscles thorough.
The fat tissue appeared less oxidised than the muscle tissue in regard to lipid oxidation.
No comparison was possible for the protein oxidation as muscle and fat tissue were
examined on diﬀerent blots. Only the proteins of high-molecular weight was oxidised in
the fat tissue. Cross-linking was not evaluated for the fat tissue. Storage time and oxygen
concentration in the MAP was found to have an impact on the level of lipid oxidation in
the fat tissue.
