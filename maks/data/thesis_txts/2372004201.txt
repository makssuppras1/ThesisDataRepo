Abstract
Capacitative Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducers (CMUTs) have been studied world-wide since
1994, and are almost ready for use in commercial ultrasonic scanners for medical imaging. Be-
fore the project, the disadvantages of CMUTs compared to piezoelectric transducers, which are
currently used in standard ultrasonic transducers, were occasional instabilities, and a low emitted
pressure. The goal of this thesis was to make CMUTs able to emit pressure, comparable to that
of the piezoelectric transducers, by optimizing the geometry of the CMUTs.
Two new geometries, bossed CMUTs and tent CMUTs, and an increase in the plate thickness
of current circular CMUTs were proposed as possible solutions to increase the emitted pressure
from CMUTs. To gain a thorough understanding of the new geometries, analytical models were
developed for idealised CMUTs. These were later used for validation of ﬁnite element models de-
veloped for this project. The models can be used to describe the complete CMUT system, and were
used to determine the parameters for the CMUTs so that they have speciﬁc resonance frequencies
and pull-in potentials.
The fabrication of CMUTs for the plate thickness investigation, and CMUTs with the two new
geometries were carried out in the DTU Danchip class 100 cleanroom, and observations about the
yields of the CMUTs are presented.
Impedance measurements performed in air are presented, which validates the ﬁnite element
models, and demonstrates that the tent CMUTs and most of the CMUTs from the plate thickness
investigation are stable in time, and can thus be used for acoustic measurements.
Acoustic measurements for the tent CMUTs and for four plate thicknesses are presented, where
it is demonstrated that the generated pressure can be increased, compared to the current stan-
dard for CMUTs, by increasing the plate thickness or using the tent geometry. From the acoustic
measurements it was also found that the sensitivity of the CMUTs could be increased signiﬁcantly
from both optimizing the geometry and increasing the plate thickness.
This thesis concludes that with the new geometries and increased plate thickness CMUTs have
become competitive with commercial piezoelectric transducers, and that if they are operated under
similar conditions, the CMUTs have the potential to generate a pressure several times that of a
piezoelectric transducer.
It was furthermore concluded that the depth resolution potentially can be doubled as a larger
pulse-echo signal can be generated with the tent CMUT using one pulse, compared to the signal
obtained using two pulses to drive the CMUT, which is the current standard.
The ﬁndings in this project has contributed to the design and fabrication of an ultrasonic
scanning probe based on CMUTs, which can generate as much pressure as a piezoelectric based
scanning probe.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Capacitative Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducers (CMUTs) are an emerging transducer tech-
nology showing great promise for medical ultrasonic imaging. They oﬀer several advantages over
conventional piezoelectric transducers, which are the current standard in ultrasonic imaging [1].
Aside from medical imaging, capacitative micromachined transducers also show great promise in
other topics like chemical and gas sensing due to their high sensitivity, and easy functionalization
[2].
In this chapter general concepts relating to ultrasound and transducers will be covered. First an
introduction to ultrasonic medical imaging will be presented, where the best obtainable resolution
of ultrasound imaging, and the pressure propagation in a medium will be covered. Second, piezo-
electric transducers and CMUTs will be covered, where some of the advantages and disadvantages
of each technology are discussed, followed by a short description of the previous CMUT work done
at DTU and other research groups. Finally the goals for this project will be covered, together with
an outline of the thesis.
1.1
Ultrasound
Ultrasound can be deﬁned as cyclical pressure waves with frequencies of 20 kHz and above, which is
at the limit of tones the human ear can detect [3]. Ultrasonic waves are well described as mechanical
pressure waves, which can propagate through an acoustic medium by vibration of the molecules
in the medium. These pressure waves can be split into two categories, longitudinal pressure waves
and transverse pressure waves depending on the vibrational direction of the molecules. For medical
imaging the longitudinal waves are of interest, since these can travel through soft tissue [3].
When an ultrasonic wave propagates through a medium, it will be partially reﬂected, when it
passes an area where the density of the medium changes. This reﬂected pressure wave can then be
detected by a transducer. Thus an ultrasound image can be created by generating an ultrasonic
pressure wave and registering when the reﬂected waves are received, which corresponds to a speciﬁc
depth, dependent on the propagation speed in the medium [3]. The detection limit for when two
density changes can be detected separately, is equal to the wavelength of the ultrasonic wave, times
the number of waves, also called cycles. The wavelength can be described as [4],

1.2
Transducers
Both the standard piezoelectric transducer, and the CMUT are interfaced in the same way. They
are mounted in a scanner probe, which then connects to controlling electronics, an example of a
linear array probe can be seen on ﬁgure 1.1.
The probe is used to apply speciﬁc electrical potentials, and electrical pulses to the transducer
part of the probe, which then generates ultrasonic pressure waves. The transducer is also used
to register reﬂected ultrasonic pulses, which can then be converted into electrical signals used to
generate ultrasound images. The ability to both generate and measure the acoustic signals is one
of the reasons why electromechanical transducers have become very popular for ultrasonic imaging
[3].

1.2.1
Piezoelectric Transducers
Piezoelectric materials were discovered in 1880 when French physicists Jacques and Pierre Curie
discovered that applying a force onto quartz generated electrical charges. The process also works
in reverse, so that a change in an applied electric potential across a piezoelectric crystal will make
it contract or expand [6].
By applying an AC potential across a piezoelectric material it can be used to emit ultrasonic
pressure waves at a desired frequency. Commercial piezoelectric transducers are currently based
on ceramics [6], which have a high acoustic impedance compared to human tissue, and therefore
requires a matching layer which has a thickness of λ
4 [7]. The piezoelectric transducer is driven by
a periodic potential, where the ﬁrst half period of a pulse will be unmatched, and that a signal is
generated for half a period after the pulsing has stopped, distorting the ideal signal.
Due to the very high stiﬀness of the ceramic material it will continue to vibrate after the pulsing
of the material has ended, which will continue to generate decreasing pressure waves. This gives a
low depth resolution, and low bandwidth due to the long signal [7].
A piezoelectric transducer has been reported to generate a surface pressure of 1.68 MPa when
it is operated at 3 MHz [8]. For the pressure measurements performed during this project, this
surface pressure corresponds to a pressure of 870 kPa, due to a one centimetre distance between
the hydrophone and the transducer 1.
1.2.2
CMUTs
A typical CMUT consists of two electrodes - a plate and a substrate, with a vacuum gap and insu-
lation layer between, as seen on ﬁgure 1.2, where electrical contact to the two plates are obtained
through wire-bonds.
The system is a capacitor, where the plate is bendable, as it is only clamped at the edges. The
substrate is very thick compared to the top plate, so it is assumed to never bend.
By changing the electric potential across the plates, the electrostatic attraction between the two
plates will change, pulling the top plate closer to the substrate when the potential is increased,
or going towards a ﬂat state when the potential is reduced. By changing the applied potential in
the MHz range, the plate can be used to generate ultrasonic pulses at a speciﬁc frequency, and
reﬂected pulses can be detected by measuring changes in the capacitance of the plates [9].

The advantages of using CMUTs as transducers include larger bandwidths [10] allowing for use
of a probe for multiple purposes, reducing the need for specialised probes, and smaller dimensions
[9], allowing scanning in previously unaccessible areas, such as scanning from inside the body [11].
Other advantages are cleanroom compatibility of the fabrication processes, potentially allowing for
integration of the controlling electronics, reducing the footprint of the scanner systems, acoustic
impedance near impedance of human tissue [10], making matching layers unnecessary, better depth
resolution [9], allowing for higher quality images making proper diagnosing of blood clots easier,
and ﬁnally row-column adressed arrays for 3D imaging [9].
When circular CMUTs are operated, they are usually operated at the ﬁrst resonance frequency,
where the entire plate moves up and down at the same time. The second mode of operation oc-
curs at roughly four times the frequency of the ﬁrst mode for circular ﬁrmly clamped plates [12].
In this mode the center of the plate and the outer part of the plate ﬂex in opposite directions,
which reduces the displaced volume of the plate. If a CMUT transducer has a too large fractional
bandwidth, the two modes will have a signiﬁcant overlap, and energy can be lost due to excitation
of the second mode if the CMUT is not driven with a square pulse [13]. When using the square
pulses to avoid the excitation of the second mode, the bandwidth should be as large as possible.
The two main issues of the CMUT, which needs to solved for commercialization, are that insta-
bilities occur for some transducers [14], and that piezoelectric transducers generate more pressure
and have a higher pulse echo response [10], which allows for deeper tissue imaging of ex the liver.
Current CMUTs fabricated at DTU with a plate thickness of 1.85 µm and a resonance frequency
of 3 MHz can generate a surface pressure of 550 kPa when they are operated at 83 percent of the
pull-in potential and with an AC bias of 150 V peak to peak with four sinusodial pulses [8]. For the
pressure measurements performed during this project, this corresponds to a pressure of 285 kPa.
1.2.3
Previous Work, State Of The Art, and Work at DTU
CMUTs have been fabricated since 1994 [15] in several research groups. Several geometries of
CMUTs have since been fabricated [16] in attempts to optimize the bandwidth of the structures
and the emitted pressure and pulse-echo response.
One of the ways proposed to increase the pressure from CMUTs is to make the center of the
CMUT more rigid by adding a mass to the center of the CMUT, making the CMUT bend more
near the edges and behave more as a piston [17][18][19][20]. This kind of CMUT is called a bossed
CMUT, and can be seen on ﬁgure 1.3.
The idea of adding a mass to the center of a CMUT was ﬁrst proposed in 2007 by Wong et al. [19]
where the center masses were made of gold on silicon. The purpose of the design was to create
high pressure ultrasonic transducers, which could be used to destroy cancer tumours by exposing
them to high ultrasonic pressures. A 3.5 cm by 3.5 cm array of CMUTs was created, however no
dimensions for the CMUTs have been given.
In 2008 Wang et al. [17] reported to have fabricated bossed CMUTs with a sacriﬁcial release
process.
In 2009 Huang et al. [20] demonstrated an increase in sensitivity of 95 percent and a pressure gain
of 81 percent by adding a boss to a square CMUT. The obtained sensitivity at 85 percent of pull-in
was 8.25 mV/kPa, for an array with an area of 5500 µm by 500 µm at a distance of 86.1 mm. The
obtainable pressure was 5.75 kPa/VAC at 85 percent of pull-in, and a distance of 13.7 mm from the
CMUT.

Another way, proposed to increase the pressure, is using a type of CMUT called tent CMUTs.
They consist of a large plate, ﬁrmly clamped at the edges, and resting on an array of pillars which
support it. Tent CMUTs were, to the knowlegde of the author, ﬁrst fabricated in 1999 at Stanford
[21], and have since been described in several papers [22][20][23][24][16]. A sketch of a tent CMUT
can be observed on ﬁgure 1.4.
In 2004 it was demonstrated that compared to a square CMUT with a ﬁll factor of 0.89, tent
CMUTs could give an increase in pressure and sensitivity of 46 percent and 44 percent respectively
[16]. Fill factor is deﬁned as the part of the total area consisting of bendable plates.
In 2008 it was found that by increasing the aspect ratio of the pitch between the pillars supporting
the plate, the ﬁrst and second resonance frequencies could be moved further apart than what was
done using a square cell for the tent structure [24].
Measured pressures from tent CMUTs have been given in two papers, [16] and [11], however
essential information is missing from both papers, making comparisons diﬃcult.
In paper [16] it is reported that with a plate thickness of 1 µm a pressure of 41.6 kPa was obtained
at a distance of 13 mm from the CMUT array, which had a size of 540 µm by 540 µm, and was
operated at 2.5 MHz, and at a bias ”just below collapse” using 30 sinus pulses with a 10 V AC
peak to peak. No data is given for the pulse-echo signal.
In paper [11] a pressure of 1.5 kPa is obtained at a distance of 10 mm at 5 MHz using 10 sinus
pulses, with an element size of 80 µm by 560 µm, an unreported plate thickness, and a DC bias of
40 V is applied to the CMUT, which is equal to the reported pull-in potential of 40 V which the
authors claim they did not operate the CMUTs at.
Bossed tent CMUTs were also reported to have been fabricated, but only the bandwidth of the
structures is reported.
In a paper by Savoia et al. [10], another comparison has been made between the pressure and
bandwidth of CMUTs compared to piezoelectric transducers. The reported pressure generated
by the piezoelectric transducer is 60 percent larger than that of a circular CMUT with a plate
thickness of 1.5 µm at 12 MHz. At 8.5 MHz the reported pressure gain from using a piezoelectric
transducer is 100 percent compared to the circular CMUT.
It is however also reported that the CMUT is 46 percent and 62 percent more sensitive at 8.5 MHz
and 12 MHz respectively.
The absolute pressures generated peak to peak is 160 kPa for the CMUT and 255 kPa for the
piezoelectric transducer. This measurement is performed at a distance of 15 mm, with an array
size of 200 µm by 3000 µm, 4 pulses of AC bias of 120 V peak to peak, and at 70 percent of the
pull-in potential.
CMUTs have been an active area of research at DTU since 2006, where Thomas Pedersen
proposed a capacitative structure for pressure sensing [25]. Since then many PhD and student
projects have been dedicated to improving the technology.
The current focus of research is a collaboration between institutes at DTU and several external
partners, in part funded by Innovation Fund Denmark, and with Erik V. Thomsen as the principal
organizer for DTU Nanotech. The project began in 2012 and will end in 2017, with the purpose
of developing CMUTs for commercial ultrasonic scanners for medical imaging.
During the past several years signiﬁcant work on the theoretical understanding of and how to
fabricate CMUTs has been performed by Thomas Lehrmann Christiansen and Mette Funding La
Cour as evident by their respective PhD Thesis’ [9] and [26]. Large contributions have also in
the recent years been made by Søren Elmin Diederichsen and Mathias Engholm, by continuing to
optimize the way the CMUTs are fabricated at DTU, and increasing the understanding of CMUT

Project Goals and Thesis Outline
The purpose of this project is to remove one of the two ﬁnal barriers to commercialization of
CMUTs for ultrasonic scanning, by increasing both the emitted pressure from the CMUTs, and
their pulse-echo responses compared to the CMUTs currently fabricated at DTU, which are 5 MHz
circular CMUTs with a top plate thickness of 2 µm, which are also fabricated during this project
for comparability.
By increasing the plate thickness it is hypothesised that the maximum emitted pressure can be
increased, while some sensitivity and bandwidth might be lost. To test if the pressure and sensi-
tivity can be increased by modifying the geometry of the CMUT, both bossed and tent CMUTs
will be investigated during the project.
The thesis will consist of six more chapters, as listed below, with a brief description of their
contents.
Theory Derivation of equations describing the behaviour of the CMUTs under varying conditions,
and some general considerations in relation to operation of the transducers are presented.
CMUT Parameter Determination Based on the equations derived in the previous chapter,
and on COMSOL models, the parameters for the CMUTs are chosen.
Photolithographic Masks Based on the parameters found in the previous chapter photolitho-
graphic masks are created, and described in this chapter.
Fabrication The fabrication processes of the CMUTs are presented.
Measurements and Characterization The properties of the CMUTs are investigated including
the resonance frequencies in air and water, the pull-in potential, and the pulse and pulse-echo
responses.
Discussion and Conclusion The work done during the project is summarised, and the results
are evaluated. Some considerations in relation to the fabrication and the pulse and pulse-echo
results are discussed.

Chapter 2
Theory
The CMUT can be used as a transducer from the electrical domain to the mechanical domain, and
from the mechanical domain to the acoustic domain. To design and fabricate CMUT transducers a
deep understanding of the underlying physics of the CMUT is important. In this chapter CMUTs
are described analytically to investigate how the eigenfrequency and pull-in voltages of the CMUTs
are inﬂuenced by several parameters, most importantly plate thickness and size of the CMUT cells.
The calculations for the well described circular CMUT will ﬁrst be presented, where an introduc-
tion to the isotropic plate equation, the calculation of the pull-in potential, and the calculation
of the eigenfrequency will be given. This will be followed by the same calculations for two less
described CMUT designs, the bossed plate CMUT, and the tent CMUT.
Finally the eﬀective spring constant and the lumped element model for the CMUT will be covered.
The results from this chapter will be used in chapter 3 to aide with the design of the CMUT
transducers, where the eﬀective areas of the CMUTs are increased.

2.1.4
Eigenfrequency
It is important to ﬁnd an expression for the resonance frequency of a CMUT as the resonance
frequency will inﬂuence the maximum obtainable resolution, the penetration depth of the ultrasonic
pressure waves [4], and is the frequency the CMUT is operated at. When designing CMUTs it is
important to distinguish between the resonance frequency and the eigenfrequency. The resonance
frequency accounts for damping of the vibrating plate, while the eigenfrequency does not. The
eigenfrequency can be found using the Rayleigh-Ritz quotient, which compares the maximum
kinetic and strain energy of the vibrating plate.
As a rule of thumb the expected resonance
frequency of a plate in water will be the eigenfrequency of the plate divided by roughly 2-2.5 [31]
[32]. The exact calculation is described by Kwak in his paper [33], where it is demonstrated that
the damping is dependent on, the aspect ratio between the thickness and radius of the plate, and
on the mass density relation between the plate and the liquid.
The Rayleigh-Ritz quotient is found by using that the maximum strain energy and maximum
kinetic energy of an oscillating plate are equal at the eigenfrequency,

Chapter 3
CMUT Parameter Determination
In the previous chapter the expected behaviour of CMUTs was described, and models for the eigen-
frequencies and pull-in potentials for the circular, bossed and tent plate CMUTs were created.
For this project it is required that the CMUTs have a resonance frequency of 5 MHz in water
during operations, corresponding to between 10 and 12.5 MHz in air [33], and a pull-in potential
of 225 V. The CMUTs are going to be operated at 80 % of their pull-in potential, which means
that spring softening signiﬁcantly inﬂuences the resonance frequency of the CMUTs. Due to the
uncertainties introduced during the fabrication of the devices and the complexity of accounting for
the spring softening and a metal layer on top of the plates, COMSOL Multiphysics models have
been used to ﬁnd the desired radii and gaps of the CMUTs. This type of model has previously
been successful at predicting eigenfrequencies and pull-in potentials, and will be tested with zero
applied potential against the analytical models.
This chapter will ﬁrst present the parameters used for the plate thickness study. Then a de-
scription of the COMSOL model used for the parameter selection of the bossed plates is described,
followed by argumentation for why diﬀerent parameters are chosen. Then the COMSOL model for
the tent plates will be covered, and ﬁnally the parameters chosen for the tent plate CMUTs and
why they were chosen.
The plate thickness study has been designed so that the CMUTs are fabricated with bumps in
the centres, to reduce the parasitic capacitance, making them better for pulse-echo measurements.
The bossed CMUTs and the tent CMUTs would also have beneﬁted from this, however the pro-
cessing time is signiﬁcantly increased when creating the bumps, so due to the time constraints of
the project, it was decided not to spend time on fabricating the bumps.

3.3
Tent CMUT Parameters
Before designing the tent CMUTs, measurements had been performed for the plate thickness study
on all ﬁve thicknesses. It was possible to obtain results for the 2, 10, and 15 µm plate thicknesses,
where 9.3 µm showed the highest pulse, and pulse echo response. Results on the 5 and 20 µm thick
plates could not be obtained due to instabilities of the CMUTs during measurements, and it was
therefore of large interest to get data for the emitted pressure and pulse-echo signal of a 5 µm thick
plate as it was close to the 9.3 µm plate thickness. To be able to perform this measurement it was
chosen to use a 5 µm plate thickness for the tent design, where one of the elements will consist of
circular CMUTs for reference measurements, which can also be used to perform the measurements
from the plate thickness study.
The tent CMUT is a three dimensional system, but unlike the bossed CMUT it is not possible
to simulate it using a 2D axialsymmetric model. It is however possible to only simulate a quarter
of the CMUT due to symmetries. The geometry used for simulation can be seen on ﬁgure 3.5,
where symmetry axes are placed on all sides of the model, creating the inﬁnite array assumption
also used for calculation of the analytical expression. However, unlike the analytical expression,
the pillars are included with a ﬁnite size in the model, which results in a larger eigenfrequency for
a unit cell with the same size, as the bending part of the plate is decreased.
The same physics package as for the bossed CMUT is used in this model.
The model for the tent CMUT consists of the pillar, the two plates, the metal contact, and the
gap between the two plates. The reason for not adding the insulation layers is that this would add
to the computational time, without adding information that is not easily calculated analytically.
From ﬁgure 3.6 it can be observed that only the bottom substrate and the pillar have clamped
boundary conditions, and the top plate does not, since it is the closest representation of the real
system. The potential is applied between the two plates by placing a ground node on the top of
the substrate and a terminal adding a potential on the bottom of the top plate.

3.4
Summary of parameter determination
In this chapter the parameters for the plate thickness study are listed without a description of
the COMSOL model used to ﬁnd the parameters, as this determination is not carried out by the
author. The rest of the chapter covers the COMSOL models used to determine the parameters for
the bossed plate CMUTs, and tent plate CMUTs. These COMSOL models have been thoroughly
tested with mesh convergence tests, and by comparing them to analytical expressions obtained in
the previous chapter.
For the bossed CMUTs parameters for two chips has been chosen, where several values for α are
chosen. On one of the chips, the eigenfrequency has been kept constant, and for the other the
pull-in potential has been kept constant.
For the tent CMUTs ﬁve diﬀerent parameter sets has been chosen, which allows for investigation
of the minimum achievable pillar size, and investigation of how the aspect ratio of the unit cells
inﬂuence the location of the second resonance mode.
At the beginning of the chapter it was mentioned that the resonance frequency target in air would
be 10-12.5 MHz due to the damping of the CMUTs [33], however during the parameter estimation
this range has been narrowed to 10.5-11 MHz as the plates tend to be thicker than ordered, which
increases the resonance frequency.

A chip layout includes twelve elements organised as seen on ﬁgure 4.2, where the overall layout

The primary purpose of the chips is to investigate the emitted pressure and sensitivity in water,

Aside from the twelve elements each chip also has four saw marks, and a substrate electrode.
As seen on ﬁgure 4.2 each element consists of a small rectangular area, which is 250 µm by 275 µm,
used either for wire-bonding or contacting with a probe. The other large rectangular part of an

the element is to make it comparable to other chips fabricated in the group, while the width is to
allow for at least ﬁve hexagonal CMUTs from the 20 µm thick plate thickness study to be placed
next to each other.
The substrate electrode is designed to be 150 µm from the contact pads below each element, due
to a 200 µm ﬁxed spacing between the two probes used for impedance measurements.
The spacing between each element is chosen to be 50 µm to ensure that the elements does not
short circuit if a particle lands at the element edges.
The structures at the corners of the chips are created to make alignment during dicing of the wafers
easier.
The ﬁnal critical dimension is the minimum bonding distance between the CMUTs, which is
5 µm for the circular, square, and hexagonal CMUTs, as indicated on ﬁgure 4.1.
The size of a chip is 13.15 mm by 8.3 mm, which makes it possible to include at least 52 chips

cavities will no be covered by the element, and a high risk of shorting occurs.
The exposure dose test structure can show if the photo resist has been over or underexposed during
the lithography step, by investigating which blocks are in contact. The corners of the blocks at
the arrow, and all blocks below should touch, while all above should not make contact. If some
structures touch, but should not, or some that should touch does not, it means that a wrong
exposure time or development time has been used. Over and underexposure depends on whether
the process is a positive or negative lithography process.

5.1
Local Oxidation Of Silicon
The LOCal Oxidation of Silicon (LOCOS) process utilises that both O2 and H2O molecules have
diﬃculty diﬀusing through SiN, which makes it possible to selectively oxidise a silicon wafer. When
a layer of SiO2 is grown on a wafer it is from H2O or O2 molecules reacting with the wafer surface
to grow the oxide. This process consumes part of the exposed wafer, as seen on ﬁgures 5.1 and 5.2,
and only 56 % of the grown oxide is above the original wafer surface. The structure at the nitride
edge is also called a birds beak structure.
During the fabrication of the CMUTs this technique is used extensively, since it can be used
to create silicon bumps, and oxide cavities while maintaining a low surface roughness in the areas
of the wafer which must be used for bonding. The LOCOS process also allows for very precise
control of the height of the bump, and gap height, since the oxide thickness can be controlled with
nanometre precision.
5.2

is a regular highly doped single-side polished wafer.
The same type of bottom wafers was used for all fabrications. They are single-side polished,
with a thickness of 525±25 µm, and are highly doped with antimony to make them n type with a
resistivity of <0.0025 Ωcm. These wafers are available through the Danchip wafer stock with the
ID ON405.
The top wafer is a silicon on oxide wafer (SOI), where the device layer is highly doped. A repre-
sentation of the SOI wafer can be seen on ﬁgure 5.3.

5.3.3
Bonding and handle removal
The fusion bonding process consists of bringing two wafers together in contact, causing weak bonds
to be created between the OH groups on the wafer surfaces creating Si-O-Si+H2O. This is followed
by an annealing of the wafers at 1100 ◦C for one hour and ten minutes, which allows diﬀusion
of the H2O to the silicon surface where the O reacts to grow more oxide, while the H2 diﬀuses
through the silicon lattice. This leaves very strong bonds of 2 J/m2 [37]. This process requires a
very low surface roughness on the order of 5 ˚ARMS [38] and extremely clean wafers, as a single
particle can prevent bonding in a radius of 500 µm from the particle [36]. Just before the fusion
bonding process, both the substrate wafer and the SOI are RCA cleaned, and then transported
to the fusion bonding machine in clean wafer transport boxes. The bonding is then performed in
vacuum with the device layer of the SOI facing towards the front side of the substrate wafer, after
which the wafers are annealed, see step H.

Electrode creation and plate structuring
The next step is to create the substrate electrode opening, which is done using UV lithography

Wafer dicing
The wafer must now be diced into chips to make it possible to perform acoustic measurements.

the cleanroom at Danchip. The resist layer can then easily be removed afterwards by rinsing the

Single LOCOS for tent CMUTs
The fabrication process for the tent CMUTs is very similar to that of the double LOCOS process,
however the step to create the bump is skipped, and only one LOCOS step is performed. An
overview of the most important steps can be seen on ﬁgure 5.5, and a detailed process ﬂow can be
seen in appendix A.3.
The ﬁrst step of the fabrication process is to grow a 400 nm dry oxide at 1100 ◦C for nine
hours, followed by deposition of 50 nm stoichiometric nitride and 100 nm poly silicon with the
same parameters as for the plate thickness study. It is important that the oxide is grown using a
dry process as it will be the primary insulating layer, and must for this design have a breakdown
potential of at least 225 V in total or 0.55 V/nm. It is also important that the nitride is 50 nm or
less, otherwise it will prevent fusion bonding, since the edges of the nitride are lifted higher than
the post oxide is grown, an example where 100 nm nitride was deposited instead of 50 nm can be
seen on ﬁgure 5.6.
The next step is to structure the nitride on the frontside of the wafers, and remove it from the
backside. This is done by spinning a layer of photoresist on the frontside of the wafer, and then
performing a standard wet poly silicon etch for four minutes to remove the poly silicon from the
backside of the wafers. The uniform resist layer is stripped using a plasma asher, and a new resist
process is performed on the front side using the cavity mask.
A dry etch is performed to remove the exposed poly silicon, and then the resist is stripped, followed
by a nitride wet etch, which removes the nitride on the backside of the wafers, and structures it
on the frontside. Finally the remaining poly silicon is stripped using a dry etch, see step B.
The wafers are now oxidized using a wet process to create a gap of 166 nm, the actual gap mea-
sured afterwards was 162 nm, see step C. An image of the wafer at this step can be seen on ﬁgure 5.7.
The fusion bonding is performed after a RCA clean of both the substrate wafers and SOI wafers,
where only one 15 second HF dip is performed to preserve the post oxide, see step D.
The SOI wafers used are Ultrasil #4-9443, which has a 2 µm thick BOX.
The handle layer of the SOI is removed using dry etching, as the thick BOX layer is deemed
suﬃcient to protect the edges of the wafers from the non-uniformity of the etch.
After the handle layer etch is performed, the BOX and backside oxide are removed using BHF, see
step E.

The large particles on the wafer meant that it was not possible to operate in contact mode, but
with the size of the substrate electrodes this was not a problem.
The etch of the device layer to form the boss structures can be performed in two ways, using
dry etch, and KOH.
To use KOH it is required to deposit a nitride or grow an oxide on the wafer, and then structure
this using uv lithography and dry etch before the KOH etch can be performed. Furthermore the
KOH etch available is very dependent on the crystal planes of the silicon, etching the (110) plane
at twice the speed of the (100) plane, while almost not etching in the (111) [39]. This would give
the bosses eight sloping sidewalls, at 45 ◦angles to each other. The selectivity between the silicon
and the nitride plate layer is extremely good, so it is expected that the plate will be intact after
the etch.
The advantage of using the dry etch is that it only requires a single lithography step before the etch
to form the bosses, it is however not nearly as selective as the KOH etch. A new low power, and
low frequency dry etch was developed by a process specialist at Danchip, where it was estimated
that the selectivity between the silicon and the nitride should be on the order of ten to one, with
an etch rate of 210 nm/cycle.
On the basis of a potential 20 nm reduction in the plate thickness, compared to the extra processing
and sloped side walls from the KOH etch, it was decided to use the dry etch.
The lithography process to deﬁne the boss structures also had to be performed using proximity
exposure since the mask could not make contact with the wafer. This reduced the radius of the
boss structures, which is critical for the eigenfrequency of the plates with α near 1. It was not
possible to overcome this problem by varying the exposure dose nor the development time.
The dry etch proved to be less selective than previous tests had indicated, resulting in a plate
thickness of 90 nm, compared to the target of 240 nm.
The metal deposition, top electrode deﬁnition, element separation, and dicing were performed
in the same way as the double LOCOS fabrication and the tent fabrication.

This chapter will cover the measurements performed on the three types of chips described and
fabricated in the previous chapters.
First a short section about the two types of measurements is given, where the expected signals will
be presented, and the printed circuit board (PCB) mounting for acoustic measurements described.
This is followed by measurements performed on the bossed plate chips, then the measurements
on the tent plate chips are described, and last the measurements from the plate thickness study
are presented. At the end of the chapter a short overview of the obtainable pressures and pulse
echo-responses will be given.
The ﬁrst type of measurements are impedance measurements performed in air, where the
contact to the CMUT is obtained through two probes directly contacting the substrate electrode,
and the top electrode contact pad. These measurements serve several purposes. First they are
used to ﬁnd the resonance frequency of the CMUT in air, which is close to the eigenfrequency of
the CMUT, which can be used to validate the COMSOL models and analytical expressions for
future projects.
Second, they allow for pull-in tests on chips which are not mounted on PCBs. This is extremely
useful since it reduces the risk of accidentally operating the mounted chips in pull-in mode, if the
pull-in is signiﬁcantly lower than expected.
Third, they can give information about the phase angle of the impedance at diﬀerent frequencies,
which indicates the mechanical-electrical coupling, where a phase angle near 90◦indicates the best
achievable coupling between the electric and mechanical domain.
Finally and most importantly the measurements can be used to test if the devices are stable or
unstable in time, when a DC bias is applied across the plates.

6.4
Chapter Summary
In this chapter the measurements performed on the bossed CMUTs, tent CMUTs, and the plate
thickness study circular CMUTs have been described. It was found that resonance frequency in
air, and the pull-in potential for the bossed CMUTs were much lower than designed, which was
expected due to the fabrication problems described in the previous chapter.
The pull-in and resonance measurements in air performed on the tent CMUTs were all close
to the desired values, and it is thus found that the COMSOL model created in the parameter
determination chapter is suitable for use in future projects for design of new structures. When the
tent CMUTs were operated in water it was found that their resonance frequency only dropped by
around 30 percent, which was very unexpected as a drop of 50 to 60 percent was predicted. This
can be due to the radically diﬀerent geometry of the plate, with a much larger ﬁll factor than the
circular CMUTs. The strange phenomenon in this relation is that the circular reference CMUTs
also experienced a drop of roughly 30 percent in the resonance frequency.
It was observed that the resonance of the substrate was 8 MHz, which would also be the center
frequency for the tent CMUTs, and as a results the tent CMUTs were not operated at optimal
frequencies. Despite the not optimal operation, the generated pressure could be increased by up
to 28 percent, and the sensitivity by 30 percent for a pulse-echo increase of 67 percent. With the
large increase in the pulse-echo response from the A5 tent CMUT it is possible to operate it using
one dual square pulse, instead of two dual square pulses, and thus obtain a larger response.
The measurements from the plate thickness study indicated that one has to be careful with
not violating the Thomsen rule, which the 5 µm circular CMUTs was on the edge of violating, and
was violated for the 9.3 µm, 15 µm, and 20 µm circular CMUTs. For the CMUTs with thicknesses
of 9.3 µm and above two resonance peaks were detected in air, which can be a result of crosstalk
between the CMUTs in the same element, since the bonding distance is insuﬃcient to completely
damp the vibrations in the plate.
The largest increase in both pressure and pulse-echo came from going from a 2 µm plate to a
9.3 µm plate, where the emitted pressure increased by 87 percent, the sensitivity increased by 90
percent, and the pulse echo response increased by 257 percent.
If the gains from optimizing the geometry by going from circular to tent CMUTs produces
the same relative gain for CMUTs with a 9.3 µm thick plate, it is hypothesized that the potential
pressure gain is 139 percent, the gain in sensitivity is 147 percent, and the gain in the pulse-echo
response is 496 percent.
If a tent CMUT with a 9.3 µm thick plate was operated at 90 percent of its pull-in potential it
could potentially generate a pressure of 824 kPa, which is very close to the 870 kPa generated by
the piezoelectric transducer.

Chapter 7
Discussion, Conclusion & Outlook
This chapter will ﬁrst discuss some of the choices made during the project and bring some obser-
vations in relation to observed phenomena. This will then be followed by the conclusion and the
future outlook for CMUTs for medical ultrasonic imaging.
7.1
Discussion
For the fabrication of the bossed CMUTs it was decided to use silicon nitride as the plate of the
CMUT, and a large silicon boss. This choice was made with an assumption that the dry etch pro-
cess developed by DTU Danchip would not signiﬁcantly etch the silicon nitride as it was primarily
a chemical etch due to the low power and low bias applied during the etch. Had it been known
beforehand that the etch would not be as selective the boss or plate would have been created from
another material which allowed for isotropic wet etch without etching the plate.
A substitute material could be silicon dioxide as the plate material, where silicon could still be
used as the material for the boss, as the selectivity between silicon and silicon dioxide is on the
order of 1:100 when etching useing highly anisotropic dry etching [42].
For the fabrication of the tent CMUTs and regular circular CMUTs the alignment step with
the smallest critical dimension is that of the top electrode to the cavities, where the misalignment
becomes critical at 5 µm for the trimmed electrode design for the plate thickness study. For the
bossed CMUTs the alignment with the smallest critical misalignment distance is the alignment of
the bosses, where misalignment of just 3.5 µm would result in the boss being outside of the cavity
for both α = 0.8 and α = 0.9. Even smaller misalignment can potentially change the resonance
frequency and pull-in potential of the CMUTs as the bending is uneven across the plate as seen
on ﬁgure 7.1. This eﬀect makes the bossed CMUTs particularly sensitive to misalignment, since
it can signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the resonance frequency and pull-in potential of the CMUTs.
For the plate thickness study it was noted that the center frequency of the circular CMUTs
with plate thicknesses of 9.3 µm and 15 µm was slightly higher than expected. When the expected
resonance shift between water and air measurements for circular plates is calculated by Kwak [33],
it is under the assumption of thin plates, however this is not the case as the aspect ratio for the
two are 1:6.5 and 1:5 for the 9.3 µm and 15 µm CMUTs respectively, which are not above the 1:20
requirement for a thin plate [30]. The tent plate CMUTs never ﬁt into the model developed in
[33] as it requires circular plates, so it is not a large surprise that the damping of the plate shifts

7.2
Conclusion & Outlook
The aim of the project was fulﬁlled by designing, fabricating, and characterizing several types of
CMUTs, which proved to emit more pressure and generate a higher pulse-echo response than was
currently obtainable at DTU. The increased sensitivity and pressure from the CMUTs demon-
strated in this project proves that CMUTs potentially can have larger pulse-echo responses than
traditional piezoelectric transducers, removing one of the two barriers to commercialization of
CMUTs for ultrasonic imaging.

7.2. CONCLUSION & OUTLOOK
95
An introduction to the most relevant acoustic theory was given together with a comparison
of CMUTs and piezoelectric transducers for ultrasonic medical imaging, where it was established
that the only two things holding back commercialization of CMUTs was a low pulse-echo response
and occasional instability of the CMUTs.
The behaviour of the regular circular CMUT was described, followed by derivations of expressions
for the eigenfrequency and pull-in of the bossed plate CMUT, and the eigenfrequency, pull-in
distance, and pull-in potential for the tent CMUT were derived. The derivation of the pull-in
potential for the bossed CMUTs, and any expressions related to the tent CMUT except for the
plate bending proﬁle has not the the authors knowledge previously been presented in the literature.
These analytical expressions were used for validation of ﬁnite element models to determine the
dimensions of the CMUTs, which would have pull-in potentials of 225 V, and resonance frequencies
of 10.7 MHz in air, and 5 MHz in water due to the strong damping from the liquid.
With knowledge of the desired CMUT parameters a chip design was created, which allowed for easy
impedance and acoustic testing. This was followed by creation of the three sets of photolithographic
masks required to fabricate the diﬀerent types of CMUTs.
The three fabrications were carried out at the class 100 cleanroom facilities at DTU Danchip,
where a total of eleven wafers were fabricated, eight of which were for the plate thickness study,
two for the tent CMUTs, and one wafer for the bossed CMUTs. For the plate thickness study,
yields varied between 30 percent and 100 percent depending on the plate thickness. From the
tent CMUT fabrication three chips and scattered elements were usable across the two wafers for
a yield of only three percent. For the wafer with bossed CMUTs only the reference elements had
the desired dimensions, while all other CMUTs had too thin plates and smaller than designed boss
radii, however the amount of usable chips were 21 for a yield of 40 percent.
Impedance measurements were performed on chips from all three of the studies, and it was
found that the pull-in potentials and resonance frequencies were in agreement with the COMSOL
model for the circular and tent CMUTs. The resonance frequencies and pull-in potentials for the
bossed CMUTs were as expected lower than target due to the dimensions being diﬀerent from
what was designed.
Acoustic measurements were also performed, where it was observed that it was possible to increase
the emitted pressure and sensitivity of circular CMUTs by 87 percent and 90 percent respectively
by changing the plate thickness from 2 µm to 9.3 µm. These two increases together give an increase
in pulse-echo response of 257 percent, however the fractional bandwidth of the CMUT is lowered
to 70 percent, which is comparable with that of a piezoelectric transducer.
During the acoustic measurements it was also found that a large reduction in emitted pressure
occurs near the resonance frequency of the substrate, which forced the tent CMUTs to be oper-
ated at a frequency 2 MHz from their center frequency. In spite of the not optimal conditions
for the tent CMUTs, the pressure and sensitivity of CMUTs with a plate thickness of 5 µm could
be increased by up to 27 percent and 30 percent respectively by having a tent structure instead
of the regular circular CMUT. This increased the pulse-echo response by 67 percent, but with a
potential loss of bandwidth. It was also found the a tent CMUT driven by a single dual square
pulse could generate a pulse-echo response which was 11 percent larger than the pulse-echo signal
from a circular CMUT driven by two dual square pulses, potentially increasing the depth resolution
of the CMUT by a factor of two.
From the acoustic measurements it was furthermore found that tent CMUTs are less damped than
circular CMUTs, and the resonance shift corresponded to a factor of 0.66.
As future work it is recommended to fabricate new tent CMUTs, where the resonance frequency
of the CMUTs in water is not the same as the resonance frequency of the substrate to quantify
exactly how large a pressure and sensitivity gains the geometry can oﬀer, as gains of up to 46

Output Pressure and Pulse-Echo Characteristics of CMUTs
as Function of Plate Thickness
Søren Elmin Diederichsen, Jesper Mark Fly Hansen, Mathias Engholm,
Jørgen Arendt Jensen, and Erik Vilain Thomsen
Technical University of Denmark, 2800 Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark
seldi@nanotech.dtu.dk
The energy transduction of a Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducer (CMUT) de-
pends on the movement of a ﬂexible plate, which has a low mechanical impedance compared
to its bulky piezoelectric counterpart. Consequently, the output pressure of a CMUT is gene-
rally lower. This limits the penetration depth, and thus the visualization of deeper structures in
the body, as well as non-linear imaging such as tissue harmonic imaging. The objective of this
work is to investigate how the output pressure and pulse-echo signal of a CMUT scales with
the (silicon) plate thickness.
The CMUTs are fabricated using two consecutive local oxidations of silicon, followed by
direct wafer fusion bonding to a silicon-on-insulator (SOI) wafer [1]. The plate thickness is
thereby determined by the SOI wafer device layer thickness. CMUTs with plate thicknesses of
2 µm, 9.3 µm and 15 µm are realized. The cell radii and gap height resulting in an immersion
frequency of 5 MHz and pull-in voltage of 200 V are designed using ﬁnite element analysis. The
fabricated CMUTs are coated with PDMS and mounted in a water tank, where a hydrophone
and a plane reﬂector, respectively, are placed in a distance of 1 cm from the CMUT surface.
The two-way fractional bandwidths are measured by pulsing with a 16-period square wave at
discrete frequencies using a Tektronix AGF3102C arbitrary function generator. The peak-to-
peak output pressures are measured with a hydrophone by pulsing with two-period square
pulse. The hydrophone and plane reﬂector measurements are carried out with an AC voltage
of ± 10 V and a DC voltage corresponding to 80% of the pull-in voltage.
The table shows the measured pull-in voltages, center frequencies, peak-to-peak output
pressures, maximum peak-to-peak pulse-echo signals and -6 dB fractional bandwidths for the
fabricated CMUTs. An optimum in both peak-to-peak output pressure and pulse-echo signal is
seen for the 9.3 µm plate, which still has a moderate bandwidth of 60%. In fact, the 9.3 µm plate
results in a 1.9 times higher peak-to-peak output pressure and a 3.6 times higher pulse-echo
signal compared to the 2 µm plate. On the other hand, the highest bandwidth is observed for the
2 µm plate. This study shows that a higher output pressure and pulse-echo signal (evaluated
at the center frequency) can be achieved by customizing the plate thickness.
Reference
[1] K. K. Park, H. Lee, M. Kupnik, and B. T. Khuri-Yakub, "Fabrication of Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic
Transducers via Local Oxidation and Direct Wafer Bonding", Journal of Microelectromechanical Systems, vol. 20,
no. 1, 2011.
