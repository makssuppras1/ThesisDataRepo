Abstract 
Bioprinting proposes an audacious solution to solve the current organ shortage and trafficking 
situation: printing organs and tissues from patients stem cells. At the core of the technology lies the 
bioink, a matrix supporting the cells while they communicate, differentiate and organize into an organ 
or a tissue. The present study aimed at developing a new kind of protein-based bioink using Genetic 
Code Expansion to benefit from new properties. Elastin-like-Proteins (ELPs) were chosen as a scaffold 
for the incorporation of non-canonical amino acids. To enhance the adhesive properties of ELPs, the 
incorporation of L-Dopa, an analogue of tyrosine responsible for mussels’ adhesion on rocks, was 
attempted. However, the proteome-wide reassignment of Tyrosine codons to L-Dopa by Selective 
Pressure Incorporation didn’t yield substituted ELPs. Part of the data seems to hint at the formation of 
Dopa-metal complexes preventing its incorporation into proteins, while not being the only obstacle. As 
a substitute, Meta-FluoroTyrosine incorporation was performed, as it could display similar adhesive 
properties as Dopa. The incorporation of Meta-Fluorotyrosine in ELPs scaffold by Selective Pressure 
Incorporation was a success and the simultaneous incorporation of 4R-Fluoroproline greatly increased 
the yield of ELPs according to results to be confirmed by Mass Spectrometry. As a mean of performing 
site-specific incorporation of these non-canonical amino acids as well as others further from the initial 
Bioprinting objectives, a computational model based on PyRosetta was developed and guided the 
design of Pyrrolysyl-Synthetase (PylRS) mutants. Two of the predicted mutants could be tested by Stop 
Codon Suppression experiments. One PylRS mutant, bearing the single mutation C348F did incorporate 
its target amino acid, an alien looking compound labelled as Bicyclo[1.1.1]pentane alanine. These 
results together showcase the potential offered by Genetic Code Expansion to Bioprinting and open 
the door for the development of novel adhesive biomaterials and bioinks.

1 Introduction 
The project resides at the crossroads of various disciplines within regenerative science and synthetic 
biology.  Its objective is to engineer cell factories to generate innovative bio-adhesive materials, which 
will function as tissue linkers or bioinks for Bioprinters.  This section outlines the multiple fields related 
to the project and provides the foundational knowledge required to understand its context. 
1.1 Bioprinting  
In 2019, Noor et al1 reported in Advanced Science that they had bioprinted small, functional, 
cellularized hearts from a patient's stem cells. Although only a few centimetres in size, these hearts 
exhibited mechanical properties similar to decellularized rat hearts, offering hope to thousands of 
individuals worldwide awaiting heart transplants.  
Since the first kidney transplant in 1954, such surgical procedures have saved countless lives and 
improved the life expectancy of millions. However, the increasing demand for organ transplants has 
resulted in a severe shortage of available organs. Lengthy waiting lists are becoming  dangerously long, 
forcing people to  turn to “transplantation tourism” or organ trafficking2. 
Bioprinting presents a promising solution to this crisis by developing techniques to use stem cells to 
grow and print functional tissues and organs suitable for transplant.  
1.1.1 Start of the art 
Bioprinting can be defined as: ‘the use of computer-aided transfer processes for patterning and 
assembly of living and non-living materials with a prescribed 2D or 3D organization to produce bio-
engineered structures’’3. Each bioprinting workflow consists of three main steps4: 
1. Create a printing design* 
2. Select the correct bioinks 
3. Print 
Various bioprinting technologies have emerged over the years. The most used technology today is 
extrusion bioprinting, in which filaments are printed from bioinks that bond together to form a 
tissue/fabric. Alternative methods include Lithography bioprinting, which uses light to locally solidify 
layers of hydrogel resins and then assemble them into a 3D structure, and spheroid bioprinting, which 
exploits the tendency of cells to self-organize into spheroid structures and agglomerates into tissues.  
The field of Bioprinting has significantly evolved since its first international workshop in 20045,  Recent 
achievements and the continuous evolution of technologies have made bioprinting relevant in  various 
fields, including disease modelling, developmental biology and morphogen studies.  
This project aligns with these developments by creating a new class of adhesive biopolymers that will 
serve as bioinks. 
1.1.2 Bioinks 
The backbone of bioprinting technologies is their bioink. Far from being homogeneous, bioinks are 
generally composed of cells and their scaffolds. These tissue scaffolds† can  include various materials 
such as proteins (Silk, Elastin, collagen), polysaccharides or synthetic polymers6.

Tissue scaffolds not only serve an architectural function but also facilitate the flow of nutrients, air and 
water within the printed tissues, and provide elasticity when needed. To  achieve this, bioinks are 
designed and used  based on core properties tailored the specific needs of the cells4.*

1.2 Smart hydrogels 
Amongst tissue scaffolds, hydrogels possess numerous attractive features. They exhibit good 
biocompatibility, are generally biodegradable, and most contain cell binding sites that facilitate cell-cell 
attachment, spreading, growth and differentiation. 
Hydrogels are 3D networks with high water content, known for their impressive swelling capabilities 
and notable biological properties, such as biodegradability. These scaffolds have gained  significant 
traction in recent years due to the  advancement in the design  of “smart hydrogels” -  hydrogels with 
responsive capacities like temperature, pH and magnetic sensitivity8. For instance, chemical-responsive 
hydrogels can be particularly useful in wound healing, as the pH of the wounded tissue changes during 
the healing process. Interestingly, some temperature-responsive hydrogels are referred as ‘4D-
hydrogels” or “bio-origami”, because their structure can periodically change within the organism,   
responding to physiological temperatures range9.  
Although “smart hydrogels” are gaining popularity for their high tunability, they still share the flaws of 
other hydrogels, such as their spatial inhomogeneity (inhomogeneous crosslink density distribution) 
and non-adherence, so they need to be secured with secondary dressing and they also display quite 
low mechanical strength. They are also difficult to isolate from the tissue they support8.  
These shortcomings, in otherwise highly useful materials, highlight the need for new generations of 
adhesive materials with enhanced adherence, mechanical strength and biocompatibility. 
1.3 Adhesive biomaterials  
For bio-inks, the polymer used as an extracellular matrix is crucial for the tissue’s ability to coordinate 
and shape into the desired organ. It must be strong enough to support the cells while allowing them to

move, communicate and differentiate. Currently, many types of adhesive biomaterials exist, based on 
bio-molecules such as polysaccharides and protein-based adhesives10.  
However, these adhesive biomaterials often have their adhesive properties disrupted by water, raising 
issues for many applications, especially in vivo11.  Solution to this problem may lie in taking inspiration 
from marine organisms that achieve strong adhesion under stressful marine conditions. The mussels 
of the genus Mytilus are capable of such adherence, and the protein contained in their foot (organ used 
for adhesion), called Mussel Foot Protein (Mfp), has been extensively studied to understand their  role 
in the adhesion process12. 
1.3.1 Mussel foot proteins 
Mussels live on the seafloor or stuck on rocks; they are not benthic organisms therefore they need to 
be strongly attached to surfaces to thrive. This adhesion is the job of the byssus’s, a non-living structure, 
consisting of a root, a stem, and threads. The byssus can exert a hold equivalent to the gravitational 
force of 40 kg, despite the mussel weighting only about 150g. This strong hold is essential for resisting 
the harsh conditions of the wave-swept environment in which mussels dwell.  Mussel foot proteins 
(Mfps) are expressed in the plaque of the byssus thread. Six different types have been identified and 
labelled Mfp1 to Mfp612. 
 
Figure 2 – Byssus thread diagram from Budisa and Schneider12. Mfp1 is a large, coating protein of 108 to 130 KDa which 
displays a repeated amino acid sequence. Mfp2 is the most abundant protein of the byssus, it has a foam-like structure and 
is likely responsible for the structural integrity and stability of the plaque. Mfp3 is a tiny (6 KDa) protein with a high degree of 
polymorphism and no amino acid repeating sequence. It’s believed to help plaque cohesion by its inherent hydrophobicity 
and to act as a drying agent, enhancing surface adherence. Mfp4 is a large (93 KDa) protein acting as the bridge between the 
thread and the plaque. Mfp5 is the protein responsible for the surface adhesion of the plaque thanks to a high proportion of 
Dopa and phosphoserine, it displays a conserved sequence. Mfp6 is believed to protect Dopa residues from oxidation in Mfp5. 
Byssus plaques are remarkable microenvironment managers whose adhesive properties are linked to 
very specific properties of the microenvironment in which their proteins reside. The exceptional 
adhesive power of mussels is linked to catechol chemistry via a post-translationally modified amino 
acid: 3,4-dihydroxyphenylalanine also known as Dopa. The adhesion properties of this amino acid are

only available in a narrow redox and pH frame, forcing  the plaque to maintain a  tightly controlled 
microenvironment13. 
This process, as well as adsorption, is facilitated by two other amino acids: Lysine and phosphoserine 
(a serine analogue). Their polarity allows them to undergo charge-dependent complexation which is 
needed for coacervation*15. This footwork allows for the non-canonical amino acid L-DOPA to display 
its adhesive properties†. 
1.3.1.1 
L-Dopa, Catechols and quinones chemistry  
L-DOPA is a non-canonical amino acid (ncAA) analogue of Tyrosine that displays a catechol moiety 
instead of Tyrosine’s phenol moiety.  
Catechols and quinones are very simple structures that can be obtained from irradiation of deep-space-
like ice or interstellar ice. It is assumed that they were already present at the origin of life on Earth. 
Today, they can be found in many of Nature’s molecules, from plant secondary metabolites to bacterial 
siderophores. Catechol moieties are far better siderophores and have better surface-adhesive 
properties than quinone. Siderophores containing catechols  have a binding constant of up to Kb=1052 
m-1.17 
The two neighbouring hydroxy groups in catechol act as nucleophilic sites, forming a bidentate complex 
with a wide range of metals, from iron to ruthenium, with varying stoichiometry. However, complex 
formation is heavily pH dependent, as low pH generally impairs the binding capacity of catechol12.  
One drawback of catechol’s properties is its high redox potential of +795 mV VS NHE, making it prone 
to oxidation to quinone.  Quinone displays poorer quality in terms of metal coordination and surface 
adhesion. 
This  finding led to the functionalization of the catechol harbouring L-DOPA with protective groups to 
avoid oxidation18. In a controlled environment such as the mussel foot, the redox state of catechol is 
controlled by several factors, including protein-protein interactions, and sequence-context-dependent 
interactions, so that its adherence proprieties come into play.  
Although Dopa containing Mfps appears to be a promising technology, there are still some challenges. 
Firstly, purification of Mfps often yields poor results, therefore, the industrial production of Mfp is not  
practical12. Secondly, Mfps are proteins from mussels that could trigger an immune response in the 
human body. A solution to both problems would be to use L-DOPA and the favourable amino acid 
sequence of Mfp, which favors its adhesive properties in a more familiar type of protein derived from 
human Tropoelastin: Elastin-like polypeptides (ELP).

1.3.2 Elastin-like proteins 
Elastin is the protein of the extracellular matrix that is responsible for the extensibility and elasticity of 
arteries, lung parenchyma and other tissues. As the protein is very insoluble, it is first synthesized as 
Tropoelastin, its 70 kDa precursor.  
The sequence of Tropoelastin consists of an alternate of hydrophobic and crosslinking domains. In 
order for Tropoelastin to assemble into an elastin matrix, the lysine residues in the crosslinking regions 
are oxidatively deaminated by an enzyme named lysyl-oxidase (EC 1.4.3.13) . These deaminated lysines 
then form crosslinking boundaries leading to the formation of (iso)desmosine and the elastin matrix.  
Further steps are required for assembly into an elastin matrix, but these can be described as “self-
aggregation” of Tropoelastin due to their non-enzymatic nature. This matrix-forming process is also  
referred as Coacervation, just like in the case of the mussel foot.14 
As already mentioned, the Tropoelastin sequence consists of a sequence of crosslinking domains and 
hydrophobic domains. The Elastin-like polypeptide sequence is based on a repeat of the hydrophobic 
domain of Tropoelastin. The consensus sequence for ELPs is [Val-Pro-Gly-X-Gly]x, where X is the “guest 
residue” which can be any amino acids15. These polypeptides have been shown to have similar 
properties to natural elastin and are capable of coacervation due to the hydrophobic effect* and 
potential cross-linking induced by the guest residue. This phase transition enables their purification by 
a method known as inverse transition cycling (ITC) (cf 2.4 Inverse Transition Cycling) which is 
considered cost-effective. Nowadays, ELPs have many different applications ranging from tissue 
engineering (cartilage repair using ELP hydrogels) to Drug delivery (assembly as nanoparticles  
combined with conjugation properties).20 
One flaw that ELPs have inherited from their protein origin is the limited repertoire of building blocks 
available to them. Like all other peptides and proteins, ELPs are limited to the 20 canonical amino acids 
(cAAs), which, unlike synthetic polymers that can technically use any type of building block, only cover 
a limited range of chemistries. To overcome this difficulty, non-canonical amino acids (ncAAs) can be 
translationally inserted into the ELPs sequence to create new biochemistries that are better suited to 
the needs of modern medicine and bioindustry.

1.4 Xenobiology – Genetic code expansion 
Naturally, non-canonical amino acids (ncAAs) such as Dopa are the result of post-translational 
modifications (PTMs) in eukaryotic organisms or genetic peculiarities in prokaryotes. The  issue with  
PTMs is that while some fungal  cell factories  can perform specific PTMs, most  other PTMs are not 
transferable from one organism to another and  often yields quantities that are not industrially 
relevant12.  
To address the challenges posed by elusive PTMs and to expand the repertoire of cellular amino acids, 
multiple methods have emerged. These methods fall under the field of Chemical Synthetic Biology, also 
known as Xenobiology. 
While Synthetic Biology aims at engineering existing life forms and astrobiology searches for other life 
forms throughout the universe, Xenobiology endeavors to create alien organisms that utilize 
biochemistries foreign to Earth. The objectives of Xenobiology are beyond mere scientific curiosity. 
Creating alien life forms helps us better understand the mechanisms of life and their origins, allowing 
us to justify choices made by living organisms billions of years ago and bringing us closer to uncovering 
the fundamental principles of the origins of life. 
On an industrial note, Xenobiology enables the production of molecules that have neither seen nor 
produced before.  By focusing on the central dogma of molecular biology, Xenobiology creates synthetic 
cells with genetic mechanisms completely orthogonal to those of natural organisms, ensuring 
biocontainment*. A significant amount of research is currently happening in the field, ranging from 
Xeno-DNA (XNA) to Genetic Code Expansion. This report will mainly discuss the latter: Genetic Code 
Expansion (GCE), more information about other areas of research in Xenobiology can be found in the 
review by Gerecht et al22 and illustrated in Fig 5. The objective of Genetic Code Expansion is to extend 
the chemical repertoire of cells by enabling an orthogonal translation system to incorporate non-
canonical amino acids (ncAAs) into proteins. 
1.4.1 History of Genetic Code Expansion 
It has been long established that certain aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases are capable of integrating analogs 
of their corresponding amino acids into proteins uniformly across the proteome, predominantly in 
microbial auxotrophic strains27. One of the earliest example of genetic code expansion dates back to 
1958 when Cowie and Cohen28 produced an active substituted β-Galactosidase enzyme where 
methionine residues where replaced by selenomethionines. Before their study, only linear growth and 
protein synthesis had been observed for auxotrophic E. coli cultured with ncAA29. However, they were 
the first to achieve exponential growth in a culture grown on an ncAA: selenomethionine. Later, the 
first example of site-specific ncAA incorporation into a protein was reported in 1989 when Noren et al 
introduced synthetic amino acids into beta-lactamase30. By using a chemically modified tRNA, ncAAs 
were incorporated on amber stop codons (UAG) in vitro, making it the first Stop codon suppression 
(SCS) experiment. Due to the tedious chemical steps required to obtain this result, the study was 
regarded only as a “proof-of-concept”. In the '90s to 2000’s, scientist reviewed early studies on ncAAs 
 
* Biocontainment aims at preventing any “escape” of engineered genetic information through unintended vertical gene transfer 
(reproduction) or horizontal gene transfer26.  
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
insertion by using auxotrophic strains and in combination with recombinant DNA technology and 
metabolic engineering: the Selective pressure incorporation (SPI) method was established31,32. 
Auxotrophic strains for phenylalanine were forced to use phenylalanine analogues para-chloro-
phenylalanine (p-Cl-F) and para-bromo-phenylalanine (p-Br-F), leading to the production of proteins 
containing these ncAAs33,34. To circumvent the structural constraints imposed by SPI, in 2001 the 
Tyrosine tRNA synthetase from the archaea Methanosarcina jannaschii was used to introduce O-
methyl-l-tyrosine in response to amber codons35. This system was the first engineering of an 
orthogonal tRNAs and aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases system (aaRS), orthogonality due to the 
inefficient cross-species aminoacylation and the synthetase’s lack of an anticodon binding. Although 
this orthogonality* is true in bacteria, it is not in Eukaryotes, therefore limiting the range of action of 
this system. To address this problem, in 2004 Blight et al used the aaRS corresponding to the 
Pyrrolysine, considered to be 22nd amino acid, from Methanosarcina barkeri to turn the amber codon 
into a sense codon in E. coli36. This enzyme  has the advantage of being already paired with a tRNA that 
contains recognizes the amber codon, and  because Pyrrolysine is structurally very different from other 
amino acids, Pyrrolysine synthetase is unlikely to incorporate endogenous amino acids37.  
1.4.2 Applications of Genetic code expansion 
Creating alternative biochemistries has applications in various domains, ranging from protein structure 
studies to cancer therapeutics or Biocatalysis. 
1.4.2.1 
Genetic code expansion in Medicine and Academia 
The expanded chemical repertoire offered by ncAA incorporates allows for reactions never seen in 
Nature to take place in vivo. Many treatments benefit from these incorporations:  
• 
Antibody-drug conjugates are a new class of drugs with enhanced efficiency against cancer, 
ncAAs incorporation not only showed significant effects in in vivo disease models but also 
reduced side effects by improving drug specificity38. 
• 
Bispecific antibody synthesis was made easier by the incorporation of pAcF, allowing for 
copper-free click chemistry39.  
• 
When translated into the murine tumour necrosis factor-α, p-nitro phenylalanine increased 
the immunogenic response without the need for strong adjuvant40.  
Academic studies also benefit from genetic code expansion, as ncAAs can act as infrared probes or 
inhibit protein cleavage making them very useful for protein structure as well as enzyme substrate 
specificity studies41.  
1.4.2.2 
Genetic code expansion in Biocatalysis and Industry 
Biocatalysis and cell-factories are becoming increasingly popular in the Industry for multiple reasons, 
some of which include their efficiency, cost-effectiveness and lower impact on the environment 
compared to chemical synthesis42. Biocatalysis has already benefited from GCE, and part of the Xeno-
enzymes engineered to this day show promising results for the industry: 
• 
A substrate-promiscuous cytochrome P450 enzyme variant has displayed the Engineered P450 
catalyst’s highest total turnover (34,650) number reported to date (2014) on a complex 
molecule when residues within its active site where substituted to p-amino-F43.

• 
A bacterial phosphotriesterase cloned from an Agrobacterium radiobacter strain, has seen its 
activity increased by 8-11 fold when the tyrosine residue at position 309 was substituted with

• 
An (R)-amine transaminase’s activity was increased 15-fold and 8-fold for 1-phenylpropan-1-

• 
CalB N74D, a Lipase B from Candida antarctica fluorinated in vivo in Pichia pastoris by replacing 
its tryptophan, tyrosine and phenylalanine residue by fluorinated analogues* led to a 
prolonged shelf life and better storage45. 
1.4.3 Methods for non-canonical amino acids incorporation  
Two main methods are used to incorporate non-canonical amino acids into proteins, each with their 
constraints and specificity: Selective Pressure Incorporation and Stop Codon Suppression. 
1.4.3.1 
Selective Pressure Incorporation (SPI) 
The Selective Pressure Incorporation (SPI) method is based on the similar structure of certain ncAAs 
with cognate amino acids and therefore the possibility of these amino acids’ cognate tRNA synthetase 
to load analogues as they would canonical amino acids (cAAs)24. Hundreds of cAAs analogues can be 
incorporated with this method, it is even hypothesized that up to 70% of sense codons could be forced 
to code for ncAAs resembling specific cAAs by SPI46. 
This incorporation method is based on a combination of genetic engineering and media tuning. First, 
the cells to perform incorporations must be auxotrophic for the cAAs that are to be replaced, they 
should not be able to biosynthesis these amino acids. They are then incubated in a media (e.g. minimal 
media) that contains limiting concentrations of the cAAs to be replaced. Once all the cAAs have been 
consumed, visible by a growth arrest / slow down, analogues are added in the media later 
complemented with an inducer (e.g. IPTG) to start the overexpression of the target protein. Methods 
in yeasts and mammalian cells may vary but follow the same logic: Growth with cAAs until depletion 
and replacement by ncAAs analogues24. 
1.4.3.2 
Stop codon suppression (SCS) 
Stop Codon Suppression, or SCS method is the real mean of genetic code expansion. This method aims 
at developing new aaRS for ncAA based on different systems such as the Pyrrolysyl-tRNA synthetase, 
which will be the focus of part of this project. The name of the methods comes from the target codon 
of the designed tRNA: UAG, the amber stop codon. These aaRS systems should be orthogonal to the 
system they are used in (e.g. PheRS:tRNA from S. cerevisiae used in E. coli47) and engineered to favour 
the incorporation of a desired ncAA. Multiple methods like mutant libraries48, directed evolution49 or 
computational modelling50 can guide the design of such aaRS, the latter being the method of choice for 
this project. Once the aaRS exhibits specificity towards the ncAA (not always achieved) and good 
incorporation efficiency, it can be used to perform site-specific incorporation of ncAAs in response to 
an in-frame amber stop codon24.  
1.4.3.3 
Comparison of Selective Pressure Incorporation and Stop codon suppression 
Selective Pressure Incorporation (SPI) is a straightforward method, often used to test the tolerance of 
a specific host to a given ncAA. Achieving high titter of substituted and multi-substituted proteins is 
easier then with SCS. Whereas SCS is more tedious in steps preceding protein expression, such as 
 
* Respectively 5-fluoro-L-tryptophan, meta-fluoro-(DL)-tyrosine, or para-fluoro-L-phenylalanine.

enzyme design and testing, it allows for ncAA structurally very different from any cAAs to be 
incorporated, which is impossible with SPI. Even if SCS is more labour intensive, it is the true means of 
genetic code expansion as it adds ncAAs to the cell’s repertoire without replacing cAAs but rare stop 
codons (even non-existent in edited strains). The orthogonality of aaRS designed by SCS experiments 
also allows for fermentation conditions to be less specific, which in industrial settings would surely save 
expenses while bypassing the cAAs contamination problem of SPI. In conclusion, SPI is a 
“straightforward” method for the expression and study of substituted proteins, while SCS is a more 
tedious method which aims at expanding the genetic code more permanently24.

2 Technical introduction to the project 
To help with the development of new bioinks suitable for Human application, this project hypothesized 
that ELP scaffolds containing L-Dopa or Meta-Fluorotyrosine, either on specific sites or as a substitute 
to Tyrosine residues in the ELP’s sequence should display adhesive properties suitable for bioprinting 
and wound healing purposes. Secondly, it was hypothesized that the yield of the bioink could be 
increased by substituting Proline residues by Fluoroprolines. To test those hypothesis, two methods 
were used: SPI and SCS. SPI required the design of a new auxotrophic strain while SCS was helped by 
computational modeling. The computational enzyme design workflow was applied to L-Dopa, Meta-
Fluorotyrosine, Fluoroprolines and to two amino acids, γ-(S)-Trifluoromethyl proline and 
Bicyclo[1.1.1]pentane alanine, that have no direct connection to the projects goals if not to assess the 
effectiveness of the computational model. In this section, the technical aspects of the project will be 
explained in more depth. The variety and complexity of the different technical workflows will be 
explained to qualify the results presented hereafter. 
2.1 Pyrrolysyl-tRNA Synthetase 
Methanogenic archaea are notorious for reducing carbon dioxide to methane. However, the 
Methanosarcinales order of these methanogens has developed the ability to reduce another substrate, 
methylamine, to methane. This additional reducing capacity  is due to an extra amino acid: 
Pyrrolysine51. Considered to be the 22nd amino acid of life,  pyrrolysine's pyrrolidine ring is used in the 
catalytic site of methyltransferase to position the methylamine substrate appropriately for the transfer 
of a methyl group to a corrinoid factor52. Unlike selenocysteine, the 21st amino acid, evidence suggests 
that Pyrrolysine is co-translationally incorporated. That means it is not the result of a post-translational 
modification of Lysine, its closest cAAs equivalent, but directly incorporated like any other cAAs. The 
enzyme  responsible for this incorporation is the Pyrrolysyl-tRNA synthetase (PylRS)  which displays 
three main characteristics that make it an outstanding genetic code expansion tool51: 
• 
High substrate side chain promiscuity: The enzyme possesses a deep hydrophobic pocket to 
accommodate Pyrrolysine (cf Fig 6). The hallmark of the enzyme’s active site in complex with 
Pyrrolysine as a substrate, includes two key hydrogen bonds: one involving the side chain 
amide nitrogen of N346 and the Pyrrolysine side chain amide oxygen and the other involving 
the pyrroline nitrogen and the phenolic oxygen of Y384. However, MmPylRS binds Pyrrolysine 
in a relatively non-specific way, it for instance doesn’t perform hydrophobic interactions. 
Coupled with the absence of an editing domain, this allows for many Pyrrolysine and Lysine 
analogues to be recognized by the native MmPylRS enzyme. 
• 
Low selectivity toward α-amine: The α-amine of Pyrrolysine only forms a single hydrogen bond 
with Y384.  This residue, located in a flexible loop region, often has a random position and 
stabilizes only after Pyrrolysine binding. Multiple mutants with higher affinity for their 
respective substrate have shown preference for phenylalanine53,54 (Y384F) over the native 
Tyrosine,  highlighting the relatively low importance of Y384 in substrate binding efficiency. 
• 
Low selectivity toward the tRNA anticodon: In contrast to many other cognate aaRS, MmPylRS 
does not exhibit high selectivity towards its unique tRNA anticodon. Mutating the anticodon 
does not lead to the arrest of aminoacylation activity55. This feature has been exploited to 
change MmPylRS specificity to new anticodon and even to base quadruplets. 
 
While the native MmPylRS enzyme has already been shown to be able to incorporate different lysine 
analogues engineered variants can incorporate phenylalanine analogues and alpha-hydroxyl acids51. 
This project aimed at designing new MmPylRS systems for the incorporation of Dopa and Meta-

Fluorotyrosine, two tyrosine analogues that should benefit from the research done on phenylalanine 
and tyrosine analogues incorporations. Additionally, a MmPylRS mutant for an exotic phenylalanine 
analogue, Bicyclo[1.1.1]pentane-alanine*, was designed. Finally, aaRS for two proline analogues, 
fluoroproline and γ-(S)-Trifluoromethylproline, were also designed, marking a first in the field.

2.2 Computational modelling 
As previously cited, multiple methods exist to design efficient aaRS systems for new ncAAs. However, 
due to the relatively short timeframe of the project and a desire to learn more about computational-
aided enzyme design, the directed evolution and mutant libraries methods were discarded in favour of 
a computational modelling approach using PyRosetta56.  
PyRosetta is the Python library associated with a suite of modelling software: Rosetta. Rosetta has 
been extensively used for protein folding prediction or ligand folding and offers a wide range of objects 
very useful for the molecular modelling of biomolecules. The philosophy of Rosetta regarding modelling 
relies on the thermodynamic principle stating that the configuration of a biomolecular system at 
equilibrium tends toward that which is the lowest in free energy. Rosetta approximates these free 
energy terms using mathematical models that accounts for various interactions occurring in 
biomolecular systems (hydrogen bonding, Wan der Waals interactions, solvation, etc.). Because the 
configuration space of a biomolecule (i.e. the number of configurations it can adopt) is often too large 
to explore fully, Rosetta performs many short Monte Carlo trajectories† starting from random 
configurations, ultimately returning the configuration with the lowest free energy. Since the lowest free

great interest in such or similar compounds, as they are “orthogonal” to known biochemistry of life. 
† Monte Carlo trajectories are a succession of pseudo-random events computed according to a set of rules defined as a model (inputs, outputs) and probability

2.2.1 Docking 
To approximate biological reality as closely as possible, only protein structures resolved through X-Ray 
crystallographic methods will be used. The coordinates of the ncAA are translated and rotated to align 
the ncAA’s backbone and orientation as closely as possible to the ligand in the reference 
crystallographic structure* (cf Fig. 7). This method is referred to as “Guided docking”. If no analogues 
of the ncAA possess a complex with MmPylRS resolved cryptographically, the native enzyme bound by 
Pyrrolysine will be used (PDB 2ZCE).  Using a standard PyRosetta ligand refinement script, the ncAA 
position is further refined to minimize any clash with the enzyme structure. The hypothesis is that since 
PyRosetta relies on Monte Carlo simulations to refine the ligand position, defining the starting point of 
the simulation as an already referenced position of a close analogue increases the chance of a realistic 
docked complex.

2.2.2 Design  
Here, “Design” refers to running multiple Monte Carlo trajectories in PyRosetta to generate a list of 
amino acids with their respective configurations (rotamers) at certain positions that would lower the 
free energy, and therefore increase the stability, of the complex. Another option is to only “repack” 
certain residues, which signifies exploring configurations of residues without changing their chemical 
nature (not mutating).  
In our algorithm, we sequentially fed PyRosetta with all the possible position combinations of the 14 
residues of MmPylRS’s binding pocket (cf Fig 6) and asked it to design the most stable [PylRS, ncAA] 
complex out of each position combination while allowing for the repacking of neighbouring residues 
and ligands positions (e.g. finding the best amino acid sequence by only mutating residues 276 and 300 
and repacking neighbouring residues and the ligand).

complex, binding affinity to the target ncAA, and to an analogue used for comparison to assess enzyme 
specificity.

2.3 Incorporation assessment system  
Assessing the incorporation efficiency in an SPI experiment is relatively straightforward. Multiple 
strategies can be used but the most common is to use fluorescent proteins containing the cAAs to be 
replaced and measure sample fluorescence to quantify the incorporation of your analogue(s). It is also 
possible, and recommended, to purify and analyse the protein with mass spectrometry (MS) to verify 
your protein’s molecular weight (cAAs and their ncAAs counterparts often have a significant difference 
in their molecular weight*) to test for cAAs contamination. Also, the difference in intensity between 
peaks of the MS spectrum can allow you to have an estimate of cognate efficiency for specific ncAAs 
incorporation and/or reveal the presence of cAAs contamination. 
On the other hand, the assessment of ncAAs incorporation in an SCS experiment is not as plain. The 
success of an SCS experiment depends on multiple factors. The incorporation efficiency can vary 
depending on the number of amber codons in the target gene, the location of these amber codons, 
the space between these amber codons and the sequence context around these incorporation / 
suppression sites. Another aspect is specificity, when designing a new orthogonal aaRS, the goal is to 
have an orthogonal pair that specifically incorporates one ncAA and not multiples, similar or not, amino 
acids. It is necessary to consider all these factors when assessing the incorporation efficiency of a 
designed aaRS, therefore SCS requires multiplexing. A workflow to optimize the discovery of functional 
orthogonal pairs is to grow cells in 96 well plates with a reporter fluorescent protein with various 
sequences (e.g. different numbers of suppressor sites, different locations, etc) and/or in different ncAAs 
conditions (e.g. no ncAAs, analogue to ncAA, etc) to compare the fluorescence of the cultures. With a 
microplate reader, associating a fluorescence value with each aaRS variant and conditions allows for a 
good assessment of the incorporation efficiency and its requirements in terms of the target gene 
sequence.  
Here, plasmids PM312 and PM313 were used to assess the incorporation efficiency of designed aaRS 
for Dopa, Fluorotyrosine, Fluoroproline, γ-(S)-Trifluoromethyl proline and Bicyclo[1.1.1]pentane-
alanine. Plasmid PM312 harbours a sumo-sfGFP fusion protein containing one amber codon at residue 
n°2 (R2amber) of the sfGFP and plasmid PM313 contains the same protein fusion with 3 amber codons 
at residues n°2, n°39 and n°101 of the sfGFP (R2amber, N39amber and K101amber). Both reporter 
proteins have been tested with well-established aaRS18 and displayed significant fluorescence when 
expressed in cells harbouring efficient aaRS.

2.4 Inverse Transition Cycling  
Inverse Transition cycling (ITC) is a purification method based on the coacervation properties of ELPs. 
When exposed to warm temperatures (30-40°C) and/or high salt concentration (e.g. NaCl), ELPs 
coacervate into aggregates that can be centrifuged down. The first step of ITC is referred to as the “hot 
spin” where coacervation is induced to centrifuge down the ELPs and discard the protein that remained 
soluble in the supernatant. The second step aims at removing potential proteins that aggregated with 
the ELPs, to do so the ELPs solution is cooled down at 4°C, which re-solubilizes the ELPs* but not 
necessarily the contaminants. The sample is then centrifuged at cold temperatures and the 
supernatant containing a pure ELP fraction can be extracted, this is the “cold spin”. To enhance the 
purity of the sample, the cycle is usually repeated 3 to 5 times. This method is considered inexpensive, 
less labour-intensive than other purification methods and industrially applicable on a gram-to-kilogram 
scale.58

2.6 Non-canonical amino acids of the projects 
Apart from Dopa, this project aimed at studying the properties granted to ELPs by other ncAAs, mainly 
fluorinated analogues of canonical amino acids. 
2.6.1 Fluoroprolines (4R-FPro and 4S-FPro) 
Two protein secondary structures ruled unchallenged on how proteins fold: alpha-helix and beta-
sheets. Yet, one amino acid disrupts these structures and questions the very nature of protein 
backbones: Proline. 
Because of its pyrrolidine ring, proline is so rigid that the cis/trans isomerisation of its peptidyl-proline 
bound is a rate-limiting step of protein folding. Usually, a peptide bond exhibits a strong preference for 
the trans-amide (backbone) conformation. However, due to its unique cyclic chemical structure, proline 
can adopt two characteristic conformations in the peptide backbone: trans (~93% of proline residues 
in proteins) and cis (~7% of proline residues in proteins). Chemical modifications can alter the intrinsic 
conformational preferences of proline residues in the protein backbone, thereby affecting the protein's 
ability to fold. Therefore, proline analogs are particularly attractive candidates for proline substitution 
in proteins because they allow for the manipulation and study of folding properties. For instance, 4S-
Fluoroproline (4S-FP) promotes peptide bond formation in cis conformations, leading to ribosome 
stalling and a reduction in protein yield. In contrast, 4R-Fluoroproline (4R-FP) favors trans 
conformations, accelerating translation and consequently increasing the expression of the target 
protein60,61.  
As ELPs are unstructured proteins with repetitive sequences containing many proline residues in trans 
conformation that regularly appear in the sequence, it is essential to exploit the effect proline presence 
by increasing both translation speed, protein synthesis yields60. Therefore, our working hypothesis, and 
hope for industrially relevant bioinks production, is that 4R-FPro, by favoring trans conformations, will 
facilitate recombinant expression. Experimental substituting prolines with 4R-FP will be performed 
using SPI. 
On the other hand, SCS experiments, which are complementary to other ncAA insertions in orthogonal 
translation, are not possible with proline analogs due to the lack of suitable orthogonal pairs. 
Therefore, one of the aims of this study is to explore possibilities for creating orthogonal enzymes 
capable of recognizing proline analogs. 
2.6.2 Meta-Fluorotyrosine (3-FY) 
Expressing Dopa-substituted proteins has proven challenging over the years, with controversial reports 
and varied experiences from different projects in the lab. One major issue with the chemistry of L-Dopa 
and catechols, in general, especially in physiological milieus, is their well-documented redox instability. 
Replacing one vicinal OH group in the catechol moiety with a stable fluorine atom results in a more 
stable substituted aromatic compound. At the amino acid molecular scaffold level, this corresponds to 
the non-canonical amino acid 3-Fluorotyrosine / Meta-Fluorotyrosine (3-FY), a tyrosine analogue well 
known as a substitute for tyrosine residues in SPI experiments. Our expectation is that this analogue 
could potentially share Dopa’s adhesive properties while allowing for better protein yields. 
Compared to Dopa, 3-FY possesses a fluorine atom instead of the second hydroxyl moiety, with a similar 
van der Waals radius as oxygen and hydrogen (1.47, 1.2, and 1.57 Å, respectively) and its isosteric 
relationship with oxygen62. Although fluorine is a poorer hydrogen bond acceptor than oxygen, it has 
been established that fluorine is tolerated within certain enzymatic catalytic sites and even preferred 
over hydroxyl moieties as a substrate63. 3-FY has already proven its value in increasing the stability of 
fluorescent proteins and modifying certain of their biophysical properties64. The purpose of using 3-FY 
==PAGE:23==

instead of Dopa is to bypass the difficulties experienced with Dopa substitution and to study the yields 
and adhesive properties of 3-FY-substituted ELPs.

2.6.5 About Catechol and Fluorinated amino acids toxicity 
As shown in the previous part, multiple fluorinated ncAA have been experimented with in this project, 
with the highlight of Meta-Fluorotyrosine being seen as a potential replacement for Dopa. However, 
both catechol and fluorine-bearing compounds can display toxicity. 
Catechol toxicity can be divided into two aspects, the reactive oxygen species (ROS) product of catechol 
oxidation to quinones leading to oxidative stress and quinones toxicity. Quinones cytotoxicity is 
attributed to the alkylation of essential protein thiol or amine groups, the production of ROS through 
their reduction to semiquinones and, in fast-growing cells, to DNA modification67. In the case of 
catechol hydrogels for cell encapsulation, it has been shown that cytotoxicity is following quinone 
concentrations rather than catechol or hydrogen peroxide concentrations68. 
Even if the carbon-fluorine bound in fluorinated compounds is quite strong, fluorine can function as a 
leaving group which, in Humans exposed to more than 10mg a day, can lead to skeletal fluorosis* and 
gastrointestinal side effects. Also, reactive fluorinated compounds can leak into the cell's metabolism. 
Namely, fluororoamines and fluoroethers have been shown to yield fluoroacteic acid that disrupts the 
Krebs cycle.70  
However, most of the potential harmful effects of catechols and fluorinated compounds are 
concentration dependent. As mentioned previously, catechols are present throughout life and Dopa 
 
* Skeletal fluorosis is a condition caused by the ingestion of large quantities of fluoride. Its symptoms are weaker bones with stiffness and 
pain in the joints.69

cent of pharmaceuticals are now fluoro-pharmaceuticals71 and fluorine is commonly found in 
toothpaste. As such, for localized medical applications such as bioprinting and wound healing the use 
of either type of compound should not be problematic. Although as an additional safety layer, the 
present study aimed at lowering the half-time of the ncAAs in vivo by designing PylRS mutants that 
could efficiently channel them to the cell’s translational machinery.

3.2 KCM competent cell preparation 
Cells of the desired culture were cultured overnight in LB media. The next day, 0.5 mL of the overnight 
culture is inoculated into 50 mL of LB media at 37°C and shaking at 250 rpm. When OD600 reaches 
between 0.3-0.5 the culture is spined down at 2000 g and 4°C for 10 min. Cells pellets were 
resuspended in TSB media from the fridge and kept on ice for 30 min. After 30 min, aliquots of the TSB 
culture were made and snap-froze with liquid nitrogen. Competent cells were kept in a -80°C fridge.  
3.3 KCM competent E. coli cells transformation with plasmids 
25 µL of KCM competent cells were mixed with 5.0 µL of KCM 5X solution (0.5 M KCl, 0.15 M CaCl2, 0.25 
M MgCl2), 1-5 µL of plasmid and sterile water up to 25 µL. The mix is chilled on ice for 30 min then 
placed in a 42°C water bath for 30s for expression or 45s for cloning vector and immediately placed 
back on ice. The mix was inoculated in 0.5 mL of LB (37°C, 250 rpm) for 1 hour. Finally, 150 µL of the 
culture were plated on LB agar with the appropriate antibiotic, culture can be spined down and 
resuspended in minute amount of LB to increase transformation efficiency in case of double 
transformation. The next day, one colony was picked for overnight culture to obtain desired 
transformed cells for expression, plasmid purification or storage at -80°C.  
3.4 Selective pressure incorporation - SPI 
Cells from an overnight culture were washed twice (spun down for 2 min at 17.900 g and resuspended 
in NMM buffer) and inoculated in NMM media containing a limiting amount of cAAs to be replaced. 
From the 7h to 8h timepoints, OD600 was checked to ensure the growth arrest and therefore cAAs 
depletion. Once growth arrest was reached, 0.5-2.0 Mm of the analogue was added to the media 
==PAGE:27==
27 
 
(complemented by 1 mg/mL of ascorbic acid in the case of Dopa). After 30 minutes, 0.2 mM of IPTG is 
added to the culture to induce target protein expression. The culture was left to grow overnight. 
3.5 Soluble fraction preparation 
Cultures from the expression experiments were spun down at 7000 g and 4°C for 10 min then 
resuspended in 1/10 or 1/20 of the culture volume in the appropriate purification buffers (1 mM of 
PMSF was added to increase purification yields). Resuspensions were sonicated and centrifuged at 
17500 g at 4°C for 25 min. The supernatant was recovered and labelled as the sample's soluble fraction 
and later purified for the target protein. 
3.6 Protein purification through Ni-Agarose resin columns  
Protein purification was done using HisTrapTM HP column from Cytiva. To prepare the column, it was 
first washed with 20% ethanol, water, elution buffer (20 mM Tris, 200 mM NaCl and 300 mM imidazole) 
and finally binding buffer (20 mM Tris and 200 mM NaCl). Samples were loaded and left to incubate for 
15 min. Washing of proteins weakly bound to the column was performed by increasing the elution 
buffer concentration to 10%. After the elution of weakly bound proteins was over, the elution buffer 
concentration was switched to 100% and proteins were eluted in microcentrifuge tubes.  
3.7 Protein purification through inverse Transition cycling 
After sonication of resuspended cell pellets, 0.5% of polyethyleneimine (PEI) solution was added to 
remove negatively charged residual DNA. Cell lysate was centrifuged at 17500 g at 4°C for 25 min and 
the supernatant was recovered. 0.3M of sodium citrate was added to the soluble fraction to lower ELPs 
transition temperature. Hot spin: samples were placed in a 37°C shaker for 20 min and then centrifuged 
at 16000g for 20 min at 37°C. The supernatant was discarded, and the pellets were resuspended in a 
cold PBS buffer. Cold spin: the samples were placed on ice and occasionally vortexed. Samples were 
then centrifuged at 16000g at 4°C for 10 min. Supernant was recovered and 3 total cycles of hot spin 
and cold spins were performed to increase the purity of the protein sample. 
3.8 Polymerase Chain Reaction  
In PCR tubes, 1 µL of each primer from 10 µM stock solution, 1 µL of dNTPs from 10 mM stock solution, 
10 µL of enzyme buffer, 0.1 µL (0.2 U) of Q5 DNA polymerase, 0.2 µL (10 ng) of DNA template and water 
up to 50 µL were added. The PCR temperature protocol was as follows: 94°C to fully separate DNA for 
2 min then 30 to 35 cycles of 1) denaturing: 94°C for 30 seconds 2) annealing: 5°C below primers Tm 
for 30s 3) extension: 72°C for 30s / kb of the amplified gene length. After the cycles, 72°C for 5 min as 
the final extension step. PCR product lengths were assessed with 1% agarose gel electrophoresis. 
3.9 Gibson assembly  
PCR products were purified and then ligated into functional plasmids by using the GeneArt™ Gibson 
Assembly HiFi Master Mix from Thermo Fisher Scientific. 0.04 pmol of each fragment was mixed with 
equal volume of HiFi Master mix and incubated at 50 oC for 15-30 min to complete isothermal DNA 
fragment assembly; then 1 µL of the reaction was transformed into DH5α chemically competent cells 
to clone the recombinant constructs.

4.1 Dopa incorporation by Selective Pressure Incorporation  
The incorporation of Dopa into proteins is a known challenge within the field of Genetic Code 
Expansion. Matthias Hauf successfully achieved statistical incorporation of Dopa into Mfp5 with low 
yields during his PhD. However, since then, the ChemSynBio group has not been able to replicate Dopa 
incorporation by SPI method. The first test was to attempt to repeat Matthias Hauf’s SPI experiment 
using a protein that is easier to express than Mfp5, such as the ELP-sfGFP fusion protein from plasmid 
PM551. Multiple attempts were made, but none proved successful. Interestingly, it was noticed that 
the culture turned to a copper-like color after a night of growth in the presence of Dopa. 
The first hypothesis for the absence of incorporation was that Dopa couldn’t enter E. coli’s cytoplasm. 
Many factors could explain this absence of transmembrane transport, so the choice was made to 
investigate potential Dopa transporters. There were two main hypotheses regarding potential Dopa 
transporters:  (1) Dopa is transported across the membrane by an aromatic amino acid transporter, 
either the tyrosine transporter TyrP or the general aromatic amino acid transporter AroP, (2) Dopa is 
transported by a catecholate siderophore transporter such as CirA and Fiu both iron-catecholate outer 
membrane transporters or FepA the ferric enterobactin outer membrane transporter74. The focus was 
put on aromatic amino acid transporters. 
While investigating the regulatory networks of the transporters AroP and TyrP, it was discovered that 
specific increases in amino acid concentration in the media should enhance the activity of these 
transporters. Margaret et al  demonstrated that the activity of TyrP transporters  significantly improved 
in media supplemented with Phenylalanine75.  For AroP, Glutarate was identified as an indirect 
enhancer, acting as a de-repressor  of the transporter74. Further investigation using the KEGG 
database76,  revealed that Glutarate is a product of Lysine degradation.  To test the hypothesis that 
increased transporter activity would enhance Dopa incorporation, two experiments based on media 
composition were performed: F100 (TyrP): NMM media containing 100x the normal phenylalanine 
concentration to study how TyrP activity affects Dopa incorporation; K100 (AroP): NMM media 
containing 100x the normal lysine concentration to study how AroP activity affects Dopa incorporation. 
However, both SPI experiments using the F100 and K100 enriched media did not result in Dopa 
incorporation into proteins.  
Soon after, an article from Gao et al77 provided some answers to the transporter hypotheses by using 
E. coli as a whole-cell catalyst to transform Dopa into Dopamine.  The study explicitly shown that AroP 
is the preferred transporter for Dopa over TyrP and PheP. However, the low yield of Dopamine obtained 
from their experiment could indicate a low transporter activity. They did not test for the catecholate 
siderophores as Dopa transporters, leaving the hypothesis to be explored in future studies. The 
absence of Dopa incorporation in the presence of increased Lysine concentrations in the K100 
experiment, which was supposed to induce AroP over-expression, was not deemed as a significant 
result since AroP expression was not directly investigated.  Consequently, the idea of transporters being 
the direct cause for the absence of Dopa incorporation was discarded.  At this point, Meta-
Fluorotyrosine, or 3-Fluorotyrosine (3-FY), was considered as an alternative to Dopa. 
Two new hypotheses emerged to explain the Dopa issues: (1) Dopa is degraded before it can reach 
the translational machinery, (2) Dopa does not enter the cells because it coordinates metals 
extracellularly*. To test the second hypothesis, a solution of Dopa was mixed with a concentrated 
aliquot of the TeknovaTM trace element solution used in NMM media. The solution immediately turned

4.2 Fluorinated amino acids incorporation by Selective Pressure Incorporation 
As mentioned previously, incorporating Dopa proved so troublesome that an alternative was chosen: 
Meta-Fluorotyrosine. This fluorinated analogue is hypothesised in the ChemSynBio lab to have the 
potential to display similar adhesive properties as the catechol-bearing ncAA (cf 2.6.2 meta-
Fluorotyrosine (3-FY)). Incorporation of 3-FY was performed by SPI and it proved much more successful 
than Dopa incorporation, as [3-FY]ELPs were produced and purified from the expression plasmid 
PM488.  
Although the yield allowed for purification, it remained quite low compared to wt-ELPs expressed in 
NMM (cf Fig 14). This could be attributed to NMM being an obstacle to good protein expression or to 
3-FY not being a good substrate for the protein translation machinery.  Additionally, purifying ELPs using 
Nickel columns at room temperature proved inefficient due to the tendency of ELPs to coacervate at 
room temperature, thereby clogging the column. Consequently, inverse transition cycling, a method 
specific to ELP purification, was adopted as the new standard to increase purification yields (cf 2.4 
Inverse Transition Cycling).

5 Discussion  
5.1 Dopa incorporation and metabolism in vivo 
The Mass Spectrums of Dopa&Lysate and Dopa&Trace were obtained when using a soft-ionisation 
method (Electrospray ionisation) while the individual Dopa and trace element were obtained using an 
ionisation method too harsh to preserve coordination bonds, as it fragmented Dopa itself. A simple 
experiment to identify Dopa-metal complexes seen in Dopa&Lysate would be to run the individual 
Dopa and trace element solutions using a soft ionisation method. Still, further studies on Dopa 
transport across the membrane and half-time in E. coli’s cytoplasm would be needed to overcome the 
present situation. The hypothesis that Dopa is degraded before incorporation is supported by the 
presence of the 3-carboxyethylcatechol 2,3-dioxygenase (EC 1.13.11.2) enzyme, encoded by the gene 
mhP in E. coli. This catechol dioxygenase can cleave the Dopa’s catechol ring, preventing the recognition 
by Tyrosine synthetase. Additionally, catechol dioxygenases perform the first step of the meta-cleavage 
pathway, leading to the degradation of catechols into Krebs cycle intermediates79,80  This might explain 
why cells, faced with Dopa cytotoxicity, prefer to degrade it into more useful compounds. A first step 
towards understanding the obstacles to Dopa incorporation should be to design an assay allowing for 
Dopa intracellular concentration measurements. Once this assay is established, the transport of Dopa 
across the membrane under different conditions should be investigated. The mhP gene in E. coli could 
be knocked out and Dopa quantified intracellularly to test for its degradation. Also, the biosynthesis 
route could be explored, to ensure the presence of Dopa intracellularly. Several biosynthetic pathways 
could be explored such as feeding the Tyrosine phenol-lyase enzyme with catechol (EC 4.1.99.2 ), 
forcing it to produce Dopa instead of Tyrosine81. Another option would be to implement the cascade 
from Fordjour et al82, starting from glucose and yielding Dopa. Also, if the production switches form a 
bacterial cell-factory to a eukaryotic setup, using the Tyrosine hydroxylase enzyme (EC 1.14.16.2)83 
transforming tyrosine into Dopa could be envisioned if an aaRS is developed for Dopa. Finally, 
alternatives for Dopa adhesive properties could be found, in the present project we tried with Meta-
Fluorotyrosine but other ncAA could be better suited for bioinks, such as Tropolon-alanine* which is 
similar to Dopa but possesses a 6-carbon ring which might allow it to circumvent catechol’s degradation 
by natural enzymatic pathways while conserving adhesive properties. 
5.2 Fluorinated ELPs yields and biophysical properties 
The incorporation of fluorinated amino acids proved easier to perform than Dopa incorporation. The 
[4R-FP]/[3-FY]ELPs and [3-FY]ELPs were produced via a standard SPI workflow and the substitution of 
proline by 4R-FPro does seem to increase the yield of ELPs production, although it needs to be 
confirmed both by concentration assessment and by MS in regards to potential cAAs contamination. 
Although easy to express, the [3-FY]ELPs† and [4R-FP]/[3-FY]ELPs displayed a strange coacervation 
pattern. It seems both at room temperature and on ice, the ELPs stayed coacervated. The only way

that the produced ELPs, containing ten to thirty percent of fluorinated amino acids would not be fitted 
for our goals because of their high tendency to aggregate. On the other hand, the influence of the same 
fluorinated ncAAs could be investigated in proteins containing less codons to be substituted, as tyrosine 
codons are within the guest residue position of the ELPs motif, some guest residue positions could be 
switch to other amino acids codons, decreasing the concentration of 3-FY in the final protein. As of 
prolines, the repeating motif of ELPs sequence imposes 20% of proline in the final protein, to decrease 
the amount of fluoroproline would probably mean changing the bioink scaffold to a different protein 
or involving site-specific incorporation of 4R-FP. 
5.3 Biases of the current computational design of PylRS and future updates and 
prospects 
Testing only FTyrRS3 and BpaRS2 is not statistically relevant for evaluating the reliability of the PyRosetta 
wrapper developed as part of this project. Many biases were introduced in the logic of the wrapper 
which could occasionally provide functional enzymes such as BpaRS2 but also results in non-functional 
or non-specific enzymes like FTyrRS3. During the docking procedure, the reference structure used will 
heavily influence the results and analogues of a target ncAA, even if structurally close, might behave 
very differently in vivo. Also, the ncAA is first docked to a Wild Type MmPylRS structure and the docked 
complex was then mutated while allowing some degrees of freedom to the ncAA (repacking), this 
approach doesn’t allow for the de novo creation of a binding pocket within the enzyme because of the 
coordinate constraints imposed on the ncAA. It could also be argued that the number of mutations is 
of the essence, as the functional mutant is only one mutation away from MmPylRS (Y384F). Workflows 
using computational enzyme design have already been used in the past for aaRS design, however rather 
than yielding mutants to be tested by rational engineering, the computational steps gave positions to 
mutate in libraires, for example by site-saturation mutagenesis50,85 . The PyRosetta wrapper could be 
adapted to output positions following statistical tests rather than a mutant lists. Interestingly Beyer et 
al85 identified the Rosetta metrics that were the most reliable to computationally evaluate new aaRS 
mutants by using reference aaRS (MjTyrRS) docked with various ncAA as test data, an idea that could 
help refine the present PyRosetta Wrapper. Alternatively, a method based on deep-learning such as the 
“family-wide hallucination approach” used to design de novo luciferase by Yeh et al86 could yield potent 
aaRS, if the amount of data available characterizing the given aaRS is sufficient. If future experiments 
confirm the ability of the PyRosetta wrapper to yield even slightly functional and specific enzymes, they 
could be a starting point for directed evolution experiments, which should greatly increase their 
catalytic activity and specificity. Multiple workflows exist for directed evolution of enzymes with the 
current favorite for screening large quantities of variants efficiently being the Dead-and-alive assays87, 
where the positive and negative selection are based off cells survival in given conditions. Yet, in the

6 Conclusions and Outlooks 
The basic motivation of this project is to utilize Xenobiology and Synthetic biology of protein translation 
to generate biomaterials that can serve as wet bioadhesives and inks for bioprinters useful in general 
tissue regeneration experiments. The key ncAA to provide these valuable materials with wet adhesion 
capabilities is L-Dopa. The substitution of Tyrosine residues by L-Dopa in the ELPs scaffolds makes them 
interesting for the formulation of bioinks and their potential use in bioprinting of organs and tissues, 
benefiting the growing number of people awaiting transplantation or improving the healing process, 
as well as for many other purposes, from dentistry to cosmetics. Now, by performing SPI experiments 
with Dopa, it was clear something was obstructing its incorporation into proteins. It was first though to 
be a lack of transporters, but after reading through literature it was clear that Dopa could be 
transported through the membrane by the AroP transporter77. Now, the efficiency of this 
transmembrane transporter for Dopa should be investigated further. Another hypothesis for the 
absence of Dopa incorporation was that it was coordinating metal extracellularly, which forbade the 
ncAA from crossing the membrane using AroP. Dopa SPI experiments were performed in media not 
containing metals but to no avail as Dopa was still not incorporated in our ELPs scaffolds. To identify 
the potential complexes Dopa was forming with metals, multiple mass spectrometry measurements 
were run, which allowed us to identify multiple peaks of a Dopa and cell lysates mix as potential Dopa-
metal complexes. However, mass spectrum of Dopa mixed with single metal salt solutions all displayed 
the same peak pattern, not allowing us to identify the Dopa-metals complexes. This absence of 
complexes is hypothesized to come from a harder ionisation method used in the latest mass 
spectrometry measurements compared to the previous soft ionisation method used, for instance, to 
obtain the Dopa in cell lysate mass spectrum. Even if Dopa coordinating metals extracellularly could be 
an obstacle to incorporation, the degradation of Dopa by enzymes of the meta-cleavage pathway, such 
as the 3-carboxyethylcatechol 2,3-dioxygenase encoded by the mhP gene in E. coli, seems like a more 
promising hypothesis80 for a predominant obstacle. More knowledge of Dopa interaction with the 
membrane and bacterial metabolism is needed, identifying the Dopa-metal complexes (or on the 
contrary proving the absence of metal coordination) as well as knocking out the mhP gene could be a 
starting point for a new project leading to Dopa efficient incorporation in vivo. To bypass the difficulties 
faced when trying to incorporate Dopa, a fluorinated analogue of tyrosine, Meta-Fluorotyrosine (3-FY) 
was proposed as an alternative. Additionally, to increase ELP yield, it was decided to complement 3-FY 
with 4R-Fluoroproline (4R-FP) incorporation. During a first set of experiments, [3-FY]ELPs were 
obtained and purify using an affinity column, which yielded only a fraction of the produced [3-FY]ELPs, 
most likely because the extraction was performed at room temperature and coacervated ELPs clogged 
the column. As a result, during later sets of experiment expressing [3-FY]ELPs and [4R-FP]/[3-FY]ELPs, 
it was decided to use the ITC method to increase yields. From an analysis performed on the SPI cultures’ 
soluble fractions it seems the complementary incorporation of 4R-FP to3-FY’s increased the proteins 
yields compared to 3-FY alone, although it would need to be confirmed by MS and concentration 
measurements. Later, the first cycle of the ITC purification methods did yield proteins, most probably 
the substituted ELP scaffolds, however after overnight storing at -20°C both [3-FY]ELPs and [4R-FP]/[3-
FY]ELPs coacervated, even when put on ice. The only reliable method found to resolubilized the turbid 
solutions was to switch the pH of their storage buffer to pH 12, which prevented further analysis. 
Altogether, these early results concerning fluorinated ELPs seem to indicate they are very prone to 
coacervate and might have a low transition temperature. Even so, ELPs coacervation properties can be 
affected by many factors, such as their concentration, the presence of salt or the hydrophobicity of 
==PAGE:40==
40 
 
their guest residue58 which might be unrelated to fluorination. Yet, if further experiments proved that 
fluorinated ELPs such as [3-FY]ELPs or [4R-FP]/[3-FY]ELPs display a new kind of phase transition 
behaviour, it could be explained by the hydrophobicity of the ten and thirty fluor atoms present in the 
proteins respectively. If 3-FY substituted ELPs display adherence properties relevant to bioprinting and 
wound healing, an approach to control their new phase transition behaviour could be to perform site-
specific incorporation of 3-FY and 4R-FP instead of proteome-wide codon reassignment. To achieve 
site-specific incorporation of fluorinated ncAA or Dopa and to design enzymes capable of incorporating 
exotic-looking amino acids, several Pyrrolysine Synthetase (PylRS) mutants were designed based on 
computational models, two were tested. One mutant, supposed to incorporate 3-FY didn’t yield any 
results. However, the computational approach also yielded a Synthetase capable of incorporating the 
alien amino acid [1,1,1]Bpa in response to an amber-stop codons. While not directly linked with the 
goal of this project, the extreme hydrophobicity of the bicyclo[1.1.1]pentane moiety of [1,1,1]Bpa 
makes it a largely applicable amino acid in biology and medicine, especially in anti-microbial peptides 
and proteins. Altogether, the results of this study are encouraging future research on the potential of 
ncAAs substituted ELPs as bioinks, Dopa metabolism should be investigated, the fluorinated ELPs 
tendency to coacervate could be an hint of strong adhesion properties which could be desirable for 
bioprinting purposes and finally the computational model ,updated and refined, could yield functional 
PylRS enzyme for site-specific incorporation of fluorinated ncAAs and exotic amino acids such as 
tropolon-alanine. As well, the present PyRosetta wrapper or another computational approach could be 
used to add extra layers of functionalities to the ELPs scaffold though the incorporation of ncAAs 
modifying the pH response of the proteins89, allowing it to adapt to the wound healing process. Overall, 
the projects showcased the use of an expanded genetic code granting alien biochemistries in vivo for 
regenerative medicine and bioprinting purposes.
