Abstract
This thesis presents a comprehensive analysis and design of a composting system tailored to
efficiently manage the organic waste generated at the Universidad Nacional de Rio Cuarto (Rio
Cuarto National University) (UNRC) campus. The study involves a detailed technical feasibil­
ity assessment of conventional aerobic composting and vermicomposting processes and an
evaluation of their respective efficiencies and environmental impacts.
Key objectives include analysing the composting process to suit the specific characteristics
and quantities of the UNRC waste. Then compare the benefits and drawbacks between con­
ventional composting and vermicomposting. Additionally, the project aims to understand the
current challenges of organic waste management at UNRC, determine necessary laboratory
measurements, and explore potential improvements for future applications.
Traditional composting includes some advantages such as simplicity, the ability to handle a
wide range of organic waste, and the production of high­quality compost. However, it can be
slow, may require turning to aerate, and can produce odors if not managed properly. On the
other hand, vermicomposting uses earthworms to accelerate the decomposition process. This
method is faster and typically richer in nutrients and beneficial microbes, enhancing soil health
and plant growth. The process is less labor­intensive.
While both methods are valuable and feasible for UNRC organic waste, vermicomposting is of­
ten considered superior due to its faster processing time, higher quality compost, enhanced nu­
trient content, improved soil health benefits, pathogen reduction, and lower labor requirements.
Despite the need for careful management of the worms, the overall benefits make vermicom­
posting a more efficient and effective solution.
Composting organic residues instead of disposing of them in landfills has a number of ad­
vantages, such as a notable drop in carbon dioxide emissions, especially nitrous oxide and
methane, and a decrease in odours. Moreover, organic compost produces nutrients, fixes car­
bon, improves soil fertility, and helps sustainably farmed land.

Introduction
Global situation
Currently, the negative impact that human activities can have on the environment has become
evident, manifesting in various ways around the globe such as global warming, desertification,
pollution, among others. Within this context, one of the most complex problematic situations that
mankind must face is the production and management of waste. Inefficient and/or inadequate
waste management can have repercussions on safety, health, and life, both environmentally
and socially [1].
Organic waste dropped in landfills represents a global challenge due to its ability to generate foul
odors, attract vectors, and contribute to the greenhouse effect by decomposing and producing
methane under anaerobic conditions. Additionally, its high moisture promotes the formation
of leachates, which can contaminate groundwater. This problem affects both governmental
authorities responsible for waste management in cities and private entities that generate these
wastes as part of their primary or secondary activity.
The Sustainable Development Goals (SDG), approved by all United Nations (UN) member
states, represent a comprehensive framework addressing a range of global challenges, includ­
ing organic waste management. Among the 17 SDG are key commitments related to environ­
mental management and sustainability. In this context, organic waste recycling emerges as
an essential part of sustainable development strategies, as it contributes not only to residues
reduction but also to the generation of renewable resources and the promotion of more envi­
ronmentally responsible practices. Promoting the recycling of organic waste, therefore, aligns
with the principles and objectives of sustainable development, offering a concrete path to ad­
vance towards a more sustainable and equitable future for all. The growing interest and concern
about the environment and climate change are mainly attributed to the environmental commit­
ments made by countries in the UN framework agreements on climate change [2]. Out of the
17 objectives, the ones most related with the following thesis are:
• SDG 3: Good Health and Well­being
• SDG 6: Clean Water and Sanitation
• SDG 11: Sustainable Cities and Communities
• SDG 12: Responsible Consumption and Production
• SDG 13: Climate Action
• SDG 15: Life on Land
Effluents
The exponential growth of the population worldwide has resulted in a proportional increase in
residues generation. This phenomenon, combined with rapid industrialization and uncontrolled
urbanization, has led to the saturation of nature’s capacity to absorb these wastes, resulting in
many cases in irreversible ecological disturbances. It is crucial to recognize that pollution is a
challenge that affects all inhabitants of any community, regardless of size, and represents one
of the main challenges facing humanity today [3].
In various parts of the world, including Argentina, it has become common to liberate urban efflu­
ents directly into water bodies, arguing that riverbeds act as natural filtration devices. However,

this practice overlooks the negative consequences of the pollution it is generating. A fundamen­

The use of non­conventional technologies emerges as a suitable solution to manage urban

In 2005, the UNRC established an interdisciplinary experimental plant for the treatment of urban

solar radiation and aquatic macrophytes, which help purify the water. These plants will also be
harvested for compost production once they have completed their growth cycle.
In contemporary times, effluents are seen as a valuable resource rather than mere waste. This
shift in perspective reflects a deeper understanding of water management and the appreciation
of water resources. Treated properly, effluents may contain nutrients and other elements that
can be reclaimed and reused in various processes, thus promoting circular economy and sus­
tainability [5]. The intention is to shift from a traditionally linear approach to a cyclic one, which
involves the reuse of domestic wastewater [6].
Awareness of the importance of sustainable waste management has led to the adoption of
specific international and national regulations designed to protect human health and environ­
mental quality. They also promote prosperity through an innovative circular economy, where
resources are managed sustainably and nothing is wasted. Moreover, biological treatment of
organic waste through biological composting aligns with these objectives by contributing to ef­
ficient waste management. The thematic strategy on soil protection recommends the use of
natural compost as it is one of the best sources of stable organic matter, essential for the re­
constitution of humus in degraded soils. This approach not only supports environmental health
but also enhances soil quality and fertility, fostering a more sustainable ecosystem [7]. Please
note that throughout this thesis project, terms such as ”organic compost,” ”biological compost,”
or ”natural compost” are used interchangeably to refer to both conventional compost and ver­
micompost.
Canteen food waste
The UNRC campus has a university cafeteria with a capacity of approximately 400 students.
This generates a large amount of organic waste per day, which is currently not utilized and
ends up in municipal landfills. It will be a study task to analyze the amount generated per day,
characteristics, as well as the logistical issue related to transportation from the cafeteria to the
composting plant. When managed appropriately, food waste may enhance soil and promote
sustainable agriculture practices since it contains a range of important nutrients and valuable
organic compounds.
Yard residues

ratio and a rich organic content, making it extremely valuable. When composted, this material
adds vital nutrients and strengthens the soil’s structure, encouraging strong plant development
and increasing the fertility of the garden as a whole. Unfortunately nowadays UNRC is not

Thesis purpose
The purpose of this thesis is to plan and design a composting system for three types of organic
waste: aquatic macrophytes, food waste and yard residues. This involves conducting a techni­
cal feasibility study to efficiently manage the organic waste generated, considering the specific
characteristics, quantities and available resources of the institution. Therefore, it was desig­
nated by UNRC to locate the plant near the wastewater treatment lakes. The main objective
is to use this composting process as an integral strategy to sustainably manage the generated
waste.
This project aligns with environmental sustainability and, in turn, with the concept of a circular
economy, as it transforms waste into a valuable resource. The composting plant converts or­
ganic waste into high­quality compost, closing the material’s life cycle and contributing to a more
sustainable system. The reuse of these wastes promotes resource regeneration and reduces
dependence on new raw materials.
The dumping of organic waste constitutes a topic that need to be solved at some point in UNRC
campus. These wastes are not adequately treated, resulting in a negative environmental im­
pact. Additionally, a valuable resource (compost) is wasted, which could be utilized or sold in
the market. Therefore, the central problem addressed by this thesis is the need to design a
composting system on the university to sustainably manage the generated organic waste.
Biogas
Before diving into the details of the composting process, it is important to consider alternative
waste management options, such as biogas, and understand why composting is a more suit­
able solution for this project. Biogas production from organic waste offers several advantages
and disadvantages. On the positive side, it is a renewable energy source that reduces organic
waste in landfills and lowers Greenhouse Gases (GHG) emissions by capturing methane. It en­
hances energy independence and provides nutrient­rich digestate that can improve soil health.
Economically, biogas plants can create jobs and stimulate local economies, while the product
is versatile for electricity generation, heating, and as a fuel. Properly managed systems also
help reduce odors from waste decomposition.
However, there are notable challenges, such as the high initial investment required for setting
up a biogas plant and the need for technical expertise to operate it. The production can be
variable, and maintenance and operational costs are high. There are potential concerns about
methane leaks, odor, and air quality if the plant is not properly managed. Ensuring a consistent
supply of organic waste is essential for continuous production, and, considering that sometimes
the canteen stop cooking activities in holidays, could be a problem. Furthermore there is a
minimum quantity of organic waste to generate biogas and make it economically feasible. On
top of that, managing the digestate requires additional infrastructure and knowledge. Finally,
from the legal perspective, navigating regulatory requirements and obtaining necessary permits
can be a complex process and expensive.
Given these considerations, composting emerges as a more suitable option for the context of
this thesis. This approach, designated by UNRC, aims to locate the plant near the wastewa­
ter treatment lakes, offering a sustainable strategy to manage generated waste. Composting,
compared to biogas production, requires a significant lower (or null) initial investment, is less
technically complex (no specialized workforce needed), do not requires a minimum waste quan­

Waste management in Argentina
The legislation number 25.916 regulates the management of waste and sets minimum budgets
for environmental protection for the overall management of residential waste. Argentina’s waste
management system still has a lot of obstacles to overcome. Inadequate waste management
practices are prevalent in every city throughout the nation. Each resident produces 1.15 kg of
waste per day on average, which adds up to about 49,300 tonnes per day. The waste is made
up of 50.03% dry waste and 44.49% organic waste (food and garden waste).
These numbers lead to the existence of approximately 5,000 open­air waste disposal sites
(landfills), which continue to be many towns’ preferred method of disposing of waste. One
third are made by the government and called ”formal waste disposal”, and are controlled and
regulated. However, two­thirds are ”informal and uncontrolled” landfills. The landfill disposal
rate for the whole country is 64.7% [8].

Literature review
5.1
Organic waste
These encompass all discarded materials originating from living organisms. The main cate­
gories of organic residues include: domestic waste, agricultural byproducts, animal and human
excreta, municipal bio­solids, and industrial byproducts. Organic refuse typically constitutes
secondary products derived from agricultural, industrial, or municipal operations, often termed
”waste” due to their secondary status in these processes.
Waste management entails the process of gathering, temporary storage, transportation, recy­
cling, valorization, or disposal of waste materials resulting from human activity. This is done in
an endeavor to mitigate their impact on human health and the environment [9].
In refuse disposal, the waste hierarchy or the 3 Rs (Reduce, Reuse, Recycle) categorizes waste
management strategies based on their desirability in minimizing residues. The foundation of
most waste management strategies is built upon these principles. The primary objective is to
extract the maximum practical benefits from products and generate the least amount of trash
in the process. The waste management pyramid hierarchy prioritizes actions such as reducing
waste generation, reusing products, and recycling materials wherever possible, then recovery,
and finally the last preferable: landfill (see Figure 5.1). This framework guides decision­making
in waste management, aiming to promote sustainability and resource efficiency.
Figure 5.1: Waste hierarchy pyramid. Source: ISM Waste & Recycling [10]
5.2
Biological treatment of organic waste
Today, the use of biological treatment methods such as composting and fermentation, which
convert organic waste into valuable resources, is expanding. As landfill space becomes increas­
ingly scarce and costly, and the environmental impact of these sites is common knowledge,
biological treatment methods are growing in popularity. These methods become important in
the quest to reduce waste overall and divert it away from landfills and incinerators.

Composting is a natural biological decay process under controlled aerobic conditions that occurs
on diverse solid organic substrates, resulting in a stable product, known as compost. In this
process, various microorganisms, including bacteria, fungi, and actinomycetes, break down
organic matter into simpler substances more suitable for soil application and conditioning. The
effectiveness of the composting process relies on the environmental conditions prevailing in the
composting system, such as oxygen, temperature, moisture, pH, Carbon Nitrogen ratio (C/N)
of the materials to be composted, organic matter, the size, and activity of microbial populations.
These environmental conditions are regulated to optimize the composting process [11].
Compost is the outcome of the composting process and is regarded as an organic, natural, and
nutrient­rich fertilizer used to enhance soil quality. Compost is particularly abundant in organic
substances and nitrogenous compounds; additionally, this product contains notable amounts of
calcium, potassium, phosphorus, and other mineral elements (depending on the organic waste
from which it originates), as well as a wide array of enzymes that play a crucial role in soil fertility,
alongside phyto­regulatory elements that positively influence plant growth. All of this renders
compost the foremost organic fertilizer, distinguished by its substantial bacterial and enzymatic
content [11].
5.3
Landfill
Landfill is the term used for a site where waste is buried below ground level in isolation. It is one
of the most commonly used methods for final waste disposal. Due to its simplicity and low cost,
it is the preferred destination for waste in developing countries. The landfill is the endpoint for
municipal solid waste that cannot be reduced, recycled, composted, incinerated, or otherwise
processed.
Landfills can be classified into two types: open­air and buried. Open­air landfills present more
social and environmental issues compared to buried landfills. Firstly, individuals with limited
economic resources often turn to open­air landfills to scavenge for food and other things, which
poses serious health risks. Exposure to untreated waste can lead to significant health problems
and diseases. Secondly, this waste treatment cause greater environmental pollution due to the
dispersal of contaminated particles by the wind. This spread of pollutants can negatively impact
nearby communities and crops, compromising air quality and the health of residents and the
adjacent agricultural environment.
An efficiently designed landfill should provide tools for leachate management and potential col­
lection of landfill gases for potential use as an energy source. Forward planning should also
anticipate the productive utilization of landfill properties in the future, when its closure is defined
[12].
There is limited knowledge regarding the types of substances present in landfills and how these
substances can affect water, soil, vegetation, and their interactions with other compounds both
inside and outside living organisms. A wide range of heavy metals and anthropogenic sub­
stances are concealed in landfills.
Due to the indiscriminate presence of waste, different chemicals mix with organic waste, causing
microorganisms to mutate into undesirable forms. Therefore, it should be mandatory to assess
how the combination of different wastes will influence the environment in the long term [13].
Landfill leachate is a liquid of latent contamination that, unless carefully controlled when re­
turned to the environment, can cause very harmful effects on groundwater and surface water
surrounding a landfill. Therefore, leachate monitoring is necessary to ensure that landfill oper­
ations do not cause harm to human health or the environment [14].

5.3.1
The issue of organic waste in landfills
Space: In many countries, landfill areas fill up quickly due to the volume of waste generated.
Furthermore, lack of planning means that lands dedicated to waste disposal cannot be used for
other purposes in the future due to the inherent contamination. Organic Waste (OW) accounts
for a significant percentage of the garbage ending up in landfills. Utilizing OW for other purposes
helps extend the lifespan of landfills and reduces waste disposal costs.
Water pollution: The organic waste component present in a landfill is decomposed by mi­
croorganisms using aerobic and anaerobic respiration processes. The result is the formation of
leachate containing bacteria, decaying material, and any chemical contaminants present in the
landfill. This leachate poses a significant risk as it accumulates at the bottom of the landfill and
can seep into groundwater, contaminating it.
Greenhouse gas emissions: It is the organic waste that, when decomposed in anaerobic
environments, produces methane (CH4). Methane is one of the gases that has the most impact
on the greenhouse effect, producing much more impact than carbon dioxide concerning this
effect. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) confirm that ”CH4 emissions
from landfills represent about 3.5%–7.0% of global CH4 emissions” (IPCC, 2001, IPCC, 2013,
Bogner et al., 2008, Rachor et al., 2013, Capellin et al., 2014, Olivier et al., 2017).
Global trends regarding preferences for the disposal of OW and the underutilization of nutrients
and energy present in them motivate the analysis of their treatment through more sustainable
methods. The resulting effects or risks of sending the organic fraction of waste to landfills
(associated with economic, social, and environmental costs) further support transforming OW
into beneficial products.
5.4
Composting process
During composting, microorganisms consume oxygen (O2) as they break down organic matter,
generating heat, carbon dioxide (CO2), and water vapor. Colomer Mendoza [15] emphasizes the
importance of guiding biodegradation through efficient metabolic pathways to achieve optimal
end products in a shorter time frame. Accelerated microbial growth results from maintaining
conducive conditions.
5.4.1
Phases
Preparation
Initial steps involve conditioning and blending raw materials to regulate their moisture content,
particle size distribution, removal of extraneous elements, and optimization of nutrient levels to
achieve an appropriate Carbon/Nitrogen (C/N) ratio conducive to composting [16].
1. Mesophilic Decomposition (25 ­ 40°C)
This primary phase, also termed the inception phase, witnesses the enzymatic breakdown of
easily degradable compounds such as sugars and proteins by fungi, actinomycetes, and bacte­
ria (primary decomposers). This enzymatic activity induces a temperature surge, transitioning
swiftly from ambient conditions to 40°C, concurrently giving rise to organic acids (i.e., the aci­
dogenic phase), thereby lowering the pH to approximately 5 or 5.5. The pH reduction facilitates
fungal proliferation and the enzymatic breakdown of lignin and cellulose. Temperature elevation
stems from microbial metabolic heat production, augmented by insulation against heat dissipa­
tion provided by the compost mass. However, unless a critical mass is achieved, heat loss
occurs commensurately with generation, maintaining material temperature at ambient levels
[16].

2. Thermophilic Decomposition (35 ­ 65°C)
The phase is characterized by the gradual replacement of mesophilic flora by thermophilic mi­
croorganisms adapted to elevated temperatures. During this stage, recalcitrant compounds
such as cellulose, hemicellulose, and carbohydrates undergo transformation, with a substantial
fraction of organic material metabolized to CO2 coupled with volumetric reduction due to mois­
ture evaporation. Decomposition rates remain brisk, peaking near 62°C. Thermophilic fungi
exhibit maximal growth within the range of 35 to 55°C, with higher temperatures inhibiting their
proliferation. While most microorganisms are incapacitated beyond 65°C, temperatures may
escalate further, occasionally exceeding 80°C. This thermophilic phase is pivotal for compost
sterilization, eradicating human and plant pathogens, insect larvae, and weed seeds. Ster­
ilization necessitates not only elevated temperatures but also the presence of a specialized
microbial community, dominated by actinomycetes responsible for antibiotic synthesis. Tem­
peratures surpassing 70°C may impede mesophilic flora recovery post­peak temperature, re­
mediable through judicious recolonization strategies. Notably, the conversion of nitrogenous
compounds by thermophilic microorganisms yields ammonia (NH3), inducing a rise in pH to
alkaline levels (between 8 and 9) due to organic acid consumption [16].
3. Mesophilic Cooling Decomposition (<40°C)
Following depletion of substrates and waning thermophilic activity, temperatures gradually abate,
fostering the recolonization of mesophilic organisms from surviving spores or through external
inoculation. While the initial mesophilic phase is marked by the degradation of sugars, oligosac­
charides, and proteins, the subsequent phase entails the breakdown of recalcitrant compounds
like cellulose, lignin, and starch by a diverse microbial consortium comprising bacteria and fungi.
Despite a pH decline, a slight alkaline milieu persists [17].
4. Maturation/Stabilization
Predominantly characterized by humification processes and the gradual decomposition of re­
calcitrant compounds, this phase witnesses a surge in fungal populations juxtaposed with a
decline in bacterial numbers. Concurrently, pile temperature diminishes, oxygen consumption
subsides, and the compost attains pH levels ranging between 7 and 8 [18].
Refinement
This concluding phase entails particle size refinement, moisture regulation, elimination of unre­
acted material, laboratory analyses, and quality assurance protocols. In industrial contexts, or
for commercial applications, packaging and labeling procedures are instituted [19].
5.5
Vermicomposting process
This new biotechnology recycles organic waste into earthworm castings, a highly effective fer­
tiliser, by using Californian red worms. Similar to coffee grounds, these odourless castings
have five times the amount of nutrients as urea, which makes them ideal for enriching soil and
promoting crop growth.
Californian red worms (Eisenia fetida), through their digestive tubes and with the combined
action of microorganisms, transform organic matter into a product called vermicompost, a high­
quality fertilizer with great economic value. Despite popular belief, earthworms are good for the
health of the soil because they don’t damage plant roots because they eat microorganisms.
Waste management is becoming a crucial concern in modern industrial cultures. Earthworms
have recently gained a lot of attention due to their amazing capacity to eat decaying organic
waste. This has led to conjecture on the potential use of earthworms as a viable strategy to
deal with urban trash.

Vermicomposting is not an exothermic process; there is no noticeable increase in the substrate

5.6
Factors
Considering composting as a biological process carried out by microorganisms, it is essential
to take into account the factors that affect its course of action. These factors collectively de­
termine the speed and extent of waste decomposition. The closer they are, in conjunction with
ideal values, the faster and more complete the composting will be. The factors of interest in
composting include:
1. Substrates
In composting, the substrate is the waste intended for composting, and similar to any other bio­
logical process, the physical and chemical characteristics of the substrate are critical for process
viability. Essentially, nutrient availability, concentration, and balance dictate the feasibility of the
composting process. Some of the most important physical characteristics of the substrate are
primarily related to particle size and moisture content. For successful composting, the utilized
waste materials must have all the necessary nutrients [19].
2. Types and Sources of Nutrients
The macronutrients for microbes are Carbon (C), Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), and Potas­
sium (K). Among the micronutrients, we have Cobalt (Co), Manganese (Mn), Magnesium (Mg),
Copper (Cu), and a number of other elements. Calcium (Ca) falls between macro and micronu­
trients. While a substrate may contain sufficient nutrients, they are only available to microbes if
these nutrients are in an assimilable form for them.
3. C/N Ratio
One of the most important aspects of the overall nutrient balance is the ratio between organic
carbon and total nitrogen, known as the Carbon­to­Nitrogen ratio (C/N). A starting material with a
C/N ratio between 25­30 is perfect for composting. Living microorganisms, in their metabolism,
use around 30 parts of carbon for every part of nitrogen [22].
If the amount of carbon significantly exceeds that of nitrogen (high C/N ratio), biological activity
decreases, and in a composting operation, this would lengthen the composting process. The
desirable level of C/N is, to some extent, a function of the nature of the residues, especially
carbonaceous components. If carbon is found in compounds that are difficult to decompose by
biological attacks, this carbon becomes available very slowly to microbes. Compounds of this
type are mostly lignin, some aromatics, and certain physical forms of cellulose [23].
Regarding active nutritional needs in composting, the C/N ratio of the material to be composted
is the factor that requires the most attention and care. Experience shows that, almost without
exception, with a perfect C/N ratio, all other nutrients are present at adequate levels. If a com­
post with a high C/N ratio is used, the applied compost may deprive the soil of the necessary
nitrogen to support plant growth. If the compost has a low C/N ratio, the released ammonia can
have a toxic effect on plant roots [24].
Generally, materials that are green and moist tend to have a high nitrogen content, while those
that are brown and dry are rich in carbon. Materials with a nitrogen concentration include grass,
plant trimmings, and fruit waste. Here is where macrophytes are located as well. If the C/N
ratio of a residue is too high, it can be lowered by adding nitrogenous residues or by removing
some cellulose content (i.e., carbon). Conversely, if the C/N ratio is too low, it can be raised by
adding carbonaceous waste.
A C/N ratio in the range of 25:1 to 40:1 generally results in an efficient process. A proper mix
of carbon and nitrogen helps ensure that composting temperatures are high enough for the
process to work efficiently. Although an efficient mixture is necessary, achieving an exact blend

==PAGE:21==
that yields the desired C/N ratio can be challenging.
4. Particle Size
Particle size is crucial in composting, influencing reaction rates and completion degree. Shred­
ding material aids microbial attack, speeding up the process. However, excessively small par­
ticles can hinder oxygen diffusion and lead to microbial collapse. Ideal sizes vary but generally
range from 1 to 5 cm. As composting progresses, particle size decreases and density increases,
affecting aeration and moisture retention.
5. Temperature
Composting is a microbial bio­oxidative degradation process of an organic matter mixture. This
exothermic process generates a relatively large amount of energy. Microorganisms can only
use 40 to 50% of this energy to synthesize Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP), with the rest being
lost as heat. This heat causes an increase in temperature in the compost mass, which can
reach temperatures in the range of 70­90°C. Finstein [25] refers to this process as ”microbial
suicide,” as high temperatures inhibit microbial growth, delaying the biodegradation of organic
matter. Only a few species of thermophilic bacteria show metabolic activity above 70°C. To
achieve a high rate of biodegradation and maximum microbial diversity, the temperature should
be between 30 and 40°C.
Despite the importance of the thermophilic phase in pathogen reduction, it should not be com­
pletely eliminated during composting. In fact, the thermophilic phase should be maintained at
the beginning of the composting process, when the availability of molecules ready for degrada­
tion allows temperatures to reach 70°C. It is preferable for the temperature not to decrease too
quickly, as higher temperatures and longer times lead to faster decomposition and sanitization.
Temperature control is essential because low temperatures result in slow waste transformation,
prolonging retention times, while high temperatures are necessary for the destruction of most
microorganisms.
6. pH
Organic matter can generally be composted within a wide pH range (from 3 to 11) [11]. How­
ever, the desirable range is between 5.5 and 8.0. In practice, adjusting the pH of a compost
mass is not easily accomplished. Typically, the pH begins to decline at the beginning of the
process (reaching 5.0) due to the formation of organic acids during the acidogenic phase. As
the acidification phase ends, the pH tends to increase, coinciding with the peak of thermophilic
activity. By the end of this process, the pH is close to values of 8.0­8.5. Ultimately, during the
maturation phase, the pH decreases to values between 7 and 8. Acidic pH values in finished
compost indicate a lack of maturity, usually due to a composting time that is too short or the
occurrence of anaerobic processes in the mass.
7. Aeration (Ventilation)
The provision of oxygen is one of the most influential factors in composting, around which vari­
ous composting systems have been designed. The air content in the interstices of the compost
mass varies in composition as the composting process evolves. Generally, during composting,
the carbon dioxide content gradually increases while the oxygen level decreases. The average
CO2 plus O2 content in the mass is around 20%, with oxygen concentrations ranging from 15 to
20% and CO2 levels from 0.5 to 5% [26]. During composting, CO2 is released due to microbial
respiration, and therefore, its concentration varies with microbial activity and the raw material
used as a substrate.
When the oxygen level falls below these levels, anaerobic microorganisms begin to outnumber
aerobic ones, leading to fermentation and anaerobic respiration processes taking control. It
Technical analysis of composting process with organic waste generated in UNRC campus (Argentina)
16
==PAGE:22==
is therefore crucial for microorganisms to have a constant supply of oxygen to maintain their
metabolic activities uninterrupted. After several hours of composting, oxygen levels drop sig­
nificantly and need to be replenished through ventilation. In addition to providing oxygen to the
mass, ventilation serves other functions, such as temperature and humidity control. The venti­
lation process must be managed to provide sufficient oxygen for aerobic respiration (which, in
turn, promotes heat generation) while preventing excessive temperatures within the composted
mass.
There is a considerable variability in the results of studies calculating oxygen requirements dur­
ing composting, reflecting the significant variability in the composition of the solid waste used in
composting. Despite this variability, a conclusion can be drawn from all studies: oxygen con­
sumption reflects the intensity of microbial activity and is also an indicator of compost stability.
Achieving good oxygen distribution throughout the mass requires the addition of a supporting
material (chipped pruning or wood) to provide structure and porosity. Also, this will provide
Carbon content to the compost. Aeration must be maintained at appropriate levels, considering
that the oxygen requirement varies throughout the process, being low in the mesophilic phase,
peaking in the thermophilic phase, and decreasing towards the end of the composting process
[26].
8. Humidity
Water is essential for microbial activity throughout the composting cycle. The ideal moisture
content varies depending on factors like particle size and composting method, but generally, a
starting material with around 60% moisture is satisfactory. Insufficient moisture (<8­12%) can
halt microbial activity, resulting in physically stable but biologically unstable compost. It’s ad­
visable to maintain moisture levels above 40% to sustain microbial activity. Excessive moisture
promotes anaerobic processes, leading to slower decomposition and lower­quality compost.
Adequate moisture ensures proper gas exchange and prevents leachate formation. Towards
the end of composting, moisture content should be reduced to around 30% to prevent further
biological activity in the stabilized material. The maximum allowable moisture content depends
on the structural strength of the composting material; materials with greater structural strength
can tolerate higher moisture levels.
While composting relies on the above factors to optimize the biological decomposition process,
not all of these factors are equally crucial for vermicomposting. For this process the most im­
portant to follow are temperature, moisture, and pH levels.

In vermicompost

the right conditions, vermicomposting can produce usable compost in a shorter period, typically

This may include maintaining an appropriate temperature (usually between 15°C and 25°C),
ensuring optimal humidity (around 60­80%), and providing a suitable C/N relation in the feed
materials [21].
Overall, vermicompost demonstrates superiority over conventional compost across various as­
pects. Specifically, it excels as an inoculant for compost production compared to the majority
of other compost types [21].

A. Carbon Footprint
The total amount of greenhouse gases released either directly or indirectly by human activities,
such as consuming electricity, driving cars, and disposing of waste, is measured by a carbon
footprint. It is commonly stated as equivalent kilograms of atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO2)
released.
The carbon footprint of landfills is a critical aspect to consider in urban waste management due
to its significant environmental impact. It is measured in kilograms of CO2 equivalent per ton
of food waste disposed. According to a study conducted by the consultancy firm G­advisory
[36], based in Spain and Andorra, in 2021, it is estimated that the disposal of urban waste in
landfills generates approximately 780 kg of CO2­equivalent per ton of waste (kg CO2 eq/tn).
Similarly, Yasmin et al.[37] confirm that the amount of methane emitted to the atmosphere from
landfills reaches a value of 734 kg CO2­equivalent per ton of wet waste processed.
From the urban waste ending in landfills, just a portion is coming from food waste. Studies car­
ried out between Brasil and Argentina [38] show that the fraction of organic waste in Argentina
is around 50%. Therefore, considering that half of landfills waste in Argentina is coming from
food waste, the final emissions are approximately 390 kg of CO2­equivalent per ton of waste,

The IPCC confirms that ”the most important anthropogenic GHG are CH4 and CO2, and methane
Global Warming Potential (GWP) is 20–28 times of the carbon dioxide’s one over a 100­year

B. Nutrients
Organic waste in landfills does not generate useful nutrients for the soil. Instead of decom­

process does not effectively recycle nutrients or improve soil quality, thereby losing the benefi­

7.7
Positive aspects
The following are some advantages of composting over disposing of organic waste in a landfill:
1. Reduce GHG emissions: When organic waste breaks down anaerobically in landfills, methane
and another contaminant gases are produced. This is avoided when organic waste is com­
posted.
2. Improves soil health and biodiversity: Compost contains critical elements that improve fertility,
water retention, and soil structure. It promotes healthy plant growth and increases microbial
activity. It also contributes to the maintenance of healthy soil ecosystems, which support a
range of species, including beneficial insects and microbes, by providing a habitat and food for
them.
3. Avoid waste from landfills: By composting, organic waste is kept out of landfills, which lowers
their capacity and prolongs the life of already­existing landfill sites.
4. Reduces odors and pest problems: Well­managed composting systems improve the environ­
ment by reducing odour and pest problems related to organic waste decomposition in landfills.
5. Conserves resources: By adding organic matter back to the soil, composting closes the
nutrient cycle and lessens the demand for artificial fertilisers.
6. Minimises transportation emissions: By reducing the need to transport organic waste across
long distances to landfills, on­site or local composting lowers the transportation­related GHG
emissions and air pollution.

Scalability at campus:

posting process, affecting temperature and humidity, which are crucial factors for the efficient
decomposition of waste. In colder regions, it may be necessary to use technologies that main­
tain the optimal temperature for microbial and worm activity. Finally, it is essential to consider
the composition and amount of organic waste generated in each region, which can vary ac­
cording to local agricultural practices and eating habits. This may require adjustments in the

3. Animals
The composting process can be affected by the presence of various animals that interfere with
the decomposition of organic waste. Among the most common animals that can cause problems
are rodents, such as rats and mice, which are attracted to food scraps and can burrow into the
compost pile, disrupting the process and potentially spreading diseases. Another animal to
consider are birds, especially in vermicomposting since they can kill earthworms.
Insects like flies and beetles can also be problematic. Flies, especially house flies and fruit
flies, are attracted to decomposing organic materials and may lay eggs, resulting in a larval
infestation. This can produce unpleasant odors and accelerate decomposition in an undesirable
way.
To minimize interference from these animals, an anti­hail mesh (see Figure 7.4) will be installed
around the compost pile. This mesh will allow air to pass through but will prevent larger animals
from gaining access, ensuring that composting proceeds without interruptions.
Ants can pose several challenges in composting, such as disrupting the decomposition process
by altering aeration and moisture levels, attracting predators, and competing with beneficial
composting organisms for resources. Their presence can also lead to the spread of ants to
gardens or farms where compost is used, and certain ant species may pose health risks through
bites or stings. Effective management strategies include maintaining optimal moisture levels
(around 80%), regularly turning the compost, and ensuring the pile reaches high temperatures
to deter ant colonization.

4. Odors
In composting, controlling odors is essential to ensure a pleasant environment and prevent
potential nuisances. Several strategies can help minimize or eliminate odors:
• Proper balance of ingredients
• Aeration
• Moisture control
• Balanced composting: Avoiding certain materials
• Additives: Some additives, such as biochar or activated carbon, can help absorb odors

The growth of organic farming necessitates larger amounts of organic fertilizer, which includes
semi­fermented manure, compost from various organic materials, or green fertilizers. How­
ever, sourcing these materials in the desired quantities is becoming increasingly challenging.
Therefore, it is crucial to first sort waste properly and then apply composting techniques. Conse­
quently, developing new composts from organic materials that have yet to be effectively recycled
remains a critical objective for our era.
It is important to remember that insufficient aeration impedes adequate water evaporation, lead­
ing to excess moisture and an anaerobic environment. This condition results in foul odors and
increased acidity.

These projects have great scalability potential. When replicated in other locations, more jobs

6. Drying variations
Drying macrophytes using natural methods, such as exposure to the sun, can be significantly
influenced by weather conditions and the season. During sunny and dry periods, the process is
more efficient, allowing for quicker moisture evaporation and reduced drying times. However,
during rainy or humid weather is slower, potentially leading to longer drying times and the risk of
incomplete drying. Additionally, seasonal variations affect the intensity and duration of sunlight.
Therefore, when planning to dry macrophytes using solar methods, it is essential to consider
these environmental factors to ensure effective and consistent results.

Conclusion
Numerous topics about the waste management at the UNRC campus have been discussed
throughout this investigation. This will enable the ultimate results about the study question
to be withdrawn. Implementing the concept of ”think globally, act locally” could play a crucial
role in the initial efforts to integrate a circular economy into waste management infrastructure.
It is a good idea to start this approach on university campuses, where many measurements
and research can be developed, and then expand to larger projects, such as municipal waste
management.
The proposed solution for organic waste management offers a viable and efficient approach
that can be implemented throughout Argentina. This solution not only addresses the problem
of organic waste accumulation in landfills, ground and wastewater treatment lakes, but also
contributes to the production of high­quality natural fertilizers that can be used in agriculture.
The implementation of organic waste recycling initiatives has the potential to create numerous
employment opportunities across various sectors. From waste collection and sorting to com­
posting facility operation and maintenance, there is a demand for skilled and non skilled labor
at every stage of the recycling process. Additionally, the expansion of compost markets can
stimulate job growth in agricultural settings, where the use of organic fertilizers becomes more
prevalent.
Conventional composting and vermicomposting are both effective methods for managing or­
ganic waste and producing nutrient­rich soil amendments. Traditional composting include some
advantages such as simplicity, the ability to handle a wide range of organic waste, and the pro­
duction of high­quality compost. However, it can be slow, may require turning to aerate, and
can produce odors if not managed properly.
Vermicomposting method is faster and is typically richer in nutrients and beneficial microbes,
enhancing soil health and plant growth. The process produces earthworm castings, which are
highly beneficial for soil structure and fertility. At the same time it reduces pathogens and harmful
bacteria in the waste, contributing to a safer end product. The process is less labor­intensive,
and it can be done on a smaller scale, making it suitable for urban and small­scale applications.
However, earthworms are sensitive creatures that require specific conditions to thrive. Factors
such as temperature, moisture, and pH levels must be carefully monitored and maintained.
Proper care and management are essential to prevent stress or mortality among the worm
population.
In conclusion, while both methods are valuable and feasible for UNRC organic waste, vermi­
composting is often considered superior due to its faster processing time, higher quality com­
post, enhanced nutrient content, improved soil health benefits, pathogen reduction, and lower
labor requirements. Despite the need for careful management of the worms, the overall benefits
make vermicomposting a more efficient and effective solution.
Organic composting significantly reduces carbon dioxide emissions compared to landfilling prac­
tices. This ones emit methane and nitrous oxide, potent greenhouse gases with high GWP, and
produce unpleasant odors. Composting, while emitting some CH4 and N2O, minimizes these
gases through proper management, reducing emissions to approximately 36 kg CO2 equiva­
lent per ton of waste, compared to 390 kg from landfills. Additionally, natural compost enriches
soil health and fertility, sequestering carbon, generating nutrients and supporting sustainable
agriculture.
