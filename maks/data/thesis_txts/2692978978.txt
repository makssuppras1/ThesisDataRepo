Abstract 
An adequate thyroid hormone status during development is critical for normal brain development. Even 
mild maternal thyroid hormone insufficiency during pregnancy has been linked to adverse outcomes in 
the brain of the offspring. Tremendous attention within the area of early brain development has been 
brought to parvalbumin-expressing interneurons, as abnormal level of expression and/or function of 
these neurons have been linked to a variety of neuropsychiatric disorders. This report integrates 
theoretical knowledge of developmental TH insufficiency and brain development with in-house 
experimental work on postnatal day 16 rats exposed to propylthiouracil, octyl-methoxycinnamate, DE-71, 
methimazole, or amitrole during gestation and lactation. The experimental work measures two thyroid 
hormone-responsive endpoints: cortical gene expression and parvalbumin immunoreactivity in the cortex 
and the hippocampus, examined through RT-qPCR and immunohistochemistry, respectively. Results 
presented in this project revealed dose-dependent reductions in the relative gene expression of ten 
thyroid hormone-responsive genes in consequence of developmental thyroid hormone deficiency. 
Further, reductions in Pvalb expression correlated with reductions in parvalbumin immunoreactivity in 
the cortex following exposure to TPO-inhibitors. Finally, axons derived from GABAergic interneurons 
located both in the cortex and the hippocampus were subjects to altered parvalbumin expression in 
response to thyroid hormone deficiency.

1. Introduction 
Thyroid hormones (THs) are critical regulators of fetal and early postnatal brain development both in 
humans and vertebrates. In fact, emerging evidence suggests that TH action is prevalent in fetal tissues 
prior to the onset of fetal thyroid gland function, as both THs and their associated receptors are detected 
in the fetus before the fetus itself is able to produce the hormones (Patel et al. 2011). Severe TH 
deprivation during development has been associated with severe mental retardation, however, even mild 
maternal TH deficiency (e.g. hypothyroxinemia) may lead to an increased risk of abnormal brain 
development in the offspring. The lack of dietary iodine is one of the most prevalent causes, worldwide, 
of TH insufficiency during gestation (Patel et al. 2011), but continuous, undesirable, and somewhat 
unavoidable, exposure to thyroid disrupting substances indeed contribute to the problem.  
The brain activity depends on interconnections of neurons and neurotransmission, the process by which 
the neurotransmitters released by one neuron crosses a synapsis to transduce an electrical signal in 
another neuron (Gilbert, O’Shaughnessy, and Axelstad 2020). In short, the central nervous system is a 
complex network of neurons (primary signaling cells) and glia cells that support neurons in various ways. 
Neurons consist of a cell body, called the soma, from which finger-like projections, axons and dendrites, 
arise. The axons carry electrical signals (action potentials) away from the soma toward other neurons, 
whereas dendrites receive incoming signals (Masland 2004).  
Two major classes of neurons exist: excitatory projection (or principal) neurons using the 
neurotransmitter glutamate and inhibitory intrinsic neurons using γ-aminobutyric acid (GABA) as their 
primary neurotransmitter. In rodents, neocortical projection neurons are called pyramidal cells and 
constitute approximately 80% of all neurons in the cortex, whereas the remaining 20% of cortical neurons, 
the intrinsic neurons, are called interneurons (Masland, 2004; Liodis et al., 2007). In rodents, the majority 
of interneurons in the somatosensory (S1) subregion of the cortex can be grouped into three 
nonoverlapping subpopulations based on expression of either the serotonin receptor 5HT3aR (~30%), the 
calcium binding protein parvalbumin (PV; ~40%), or the neuropeptide somatostatin (~30%) (Rudy et al. 
2011). Particularly, PV-expressing interneurons have caught tremendous attention within the area of early 
brain development, because abnormal level of expression and/or function of these neurons have been 
linked to a variety of neuropsychiatric disorders, including epilepsy, autism, schizophrenia, anxiety 
disorders, Alzheimer's disease, and attention deficit disorder, among others (Kann 2016). 
==PAGE:8==
 7 
When studying effects of (low-level) TH deficiency on brain development, it is essential to understand 
both the interaction of a certain potential disrupting chemical with the TH system and the relationship 
between the proportion of TH alteration and the development of the brain. For instance, a chemical may 
not by itself be neurotoxic (i.e., enter the developing brain to produce an adverse effect on the structure 
or the function), but rather interfere with the TH system thereby causing neurological impairments 
(Gilbert et al. 2012). Additionally, the fact that TH insufficiency during vulnerable periods of brain 
development can induce effects that are not seen immediately but only become evident at later life stages 
further complicates the research.  
The present report aims to integrate theoretical knowledge of developmental TH insufficiency and brain 
development with in-house experimental work on postnatal day (PND) 16 rats in order to supplement the 
current knowledge of PV expression and TH deprivation during development. First, I will present the main 
features of the TH system and how certain thyroid disrupting chemicals (TDCs) might adversely affect this 
system. Then, I will shortly introduce the early human brain development and how THs, and the lack of 
proper levels, influence this complex process. As proper PV expression in a subset of interneurons has 
shown to be extremely important for normal brain function, an introduction of this protein and 
subsequently the impact of developmental TH insufficiency will follow. Lastly, several cortical genes are 
suggested to be regulated by THs, thus I will present a small segment of current knowledge about the 
influence of developmental TH deprivation on cortical gene expression. The theoretically part of this 
report will be followed by an introduction to the experimental work that tries to elucidate associations 
between developmental exposure to MMI or amitrole and potential effects on cortical gene expression, 
in addition to associations between cortical Pvalb mRNA expression and the actual expression of PV at the 
protein level in rats exposed to propylthiouracil (PTU), octyl-methoxycinnamate (OMC), DE-71 (mixture of 
polybrominated diphenyl ethers), methimazole (MMI), or amitrole during gestation and lactation. 
1.1. 
The Thyroid Hormone System 
In short, THs (thyroxine [T4] and triiodothyronine [T3]) are tyrosine-based hormones produced in the 
thyroid gland in response to brain hormones from the hypothalamus (i.e., TRH) and the pituitary gland 
(i.e., TSH) and secreted into the circulation when needed. Figure 1 shows a schematic representation of 
the TH system (A) and the TH synthesis (B). Tissue-specific concentrations of THs, especially T3, is 
determined by circulating transport, uptake across target tissue membranes, and local deiodinase activity. 
Within the circulation the majority of THs are bound to carrier proteins, whereas less than 1% are present 
in an unbound (free) form (Moog et al. 2017). When needed, bound hormones are released and 
==PAGE:9==
 8 
subsequently actively transported into target tissues via a number of transmembrane TH-transporters 
such as monocarboxylate transporter 8 and organic anion transporter 1c1 (Gilbert et al. 2020; Patel et al. 
2011).  
At the cellular level, THs mediate their function via nuclear TH receptor alpha and beta (TRα and TRβ), 
which are encoded by two distinct genes (THRA and THRB), both expressed within the brain (Gilbert et al. 
2020). Upon post-transcriptional splicing different isotypes are generated. TRα1 and TRα2 are widely 
expressed in the fetal neocortical plate, whereas TRβ is expressed in more restricted zones of the brain 
(Gilbert et al. 2012). TRs are ligand-dependent transcription factors that regulate gene transcription both 
in the presence and absence of the ligand, primarily T3. Upon activation, hence in the binding of T3, the 
TR homo- or heterodimerizes and then translocates from the cytosol to the nucleus, where it controls 
gene expression by initiating the release of corepressors and recruitment of coactivators to permit gene 
transcription (Gilbert et al. 2020; Moog et al. 2017). However, in the absence of T3, the unliganded 
receptor (aporeceptor) recruits corepressors, thus preventing transcription initiation (Hadjab-Lallemend 
et al. 2010). The aporeceptor will often suppress target genes that are activated by THs, whereas genes 
downregulated by THs will be activated by the aporeceptor. Thus, inappropriate low TH during 
development can cause insufficient brain development as the aporeceptor undesirably regulates gene 
expression.

1.2. 
Thyroid Disrupting Chemicals 
Thyroid disrupting chemicals (TDCs) are broadly defined, as xenobiotics that alter circulating or tissue 
concentrations of THs, the function of the thyroid gland, or regulatory enzymes associated with TH 
homeostasis (Crofton 2008). TDCs include pesticides, polychlorinated biphenyls, polybrominated flame 
retardants, and phthalates, among other chemical compounds. These compounds are very persistent and 
even though many of them have been banned decades ago, they still remain in the environment due to 
their long half-lives, with the consequence of an ongoing human exposure. As TDCs include several classes 
of compounds with distinct chemical structures, they do not impact the TH system in a uniformly way, 
rather act through a variety of mechanisms. Brain development is believed to be influenced by TDCs 
through the capacity to interfere with intracellular TH availability, thereby impacting transcription of 
target genes (Crofton 2008). The interference of TH synthesis and availability can occur at multiple levels: 
blocking of iodine uptake, inhibition of thyroid peroxidase (TPO) activity, displacement of THs from plasma 
carrier proteins, interference of TH membrane transporters, inhibition of deiodinase activity, and/or 
induction of hepatic metabolism (Crofton 2008). Either way, the disruption may result in modification of 
the amount of T3 available to interact with TRs, in turn, impacting transcriptional activity either indirectly 
or directly. Altered gene expression might be restricted to transient local readouts of disrupted TH action, 
but it is possible that these effects also become apparent at protein level, thereby potentially leading to 
abnormal development and/or function of the affected organ.  
The present report use data from animals that have been exposed to PTU, MMI, amitrole, OMC, or DE-71 
during development. PTU and MMI are both anti-thyroid drugs used to treat hyperthyroidism (overactive 
thyroid gland) in humans. In research, they are used as model compounds to study adverse effects of 
developmental hypothyroidism, due to their well characterized mode of action, i.e. inhibition of TH 
synthesis by interfering with the enzyme TPO that convert iodide into iodine and incorporate the iodine 
molecule into amino acid tyrosine on thyroglobulin (Yoshihara et al. 2019). Amitrole is a pesticide that 
disrupt the TH system through inhibition of TPO, but possibly also by interfering with the secretion of 
thyroglobulin in addition to iodide uptake from the blood stream into the thyroid gland (Hongmei et al. 
2011). OMC is an UV-filter suggested to disrupt the TH system through inhibition of deiodinase activity 
(Klammer et al. 2007). DE-71 is a commercial polybrominated diphenyl ether mixture that is used as a 
flame retardant in several everyday products. It disrupts the TH system through upregulation of hepatic 
conjugating enzymes and/or through displacement of T4 from transthyretin (Szabo et al. 2009).

1.3. 
Early Human Brain Development  
The development of the human brain is a complex and dynamic process that begins in the early embryonic 
state and extends at least through late adolescence (Stiles and Jernigan 2010). Both proper gene 
expression and environmental inputs are required for normal brain development, and fundamental 
alterations may result from disruptions of either leading to neurodevelopmental disabilities of the 
organism.  
The first step in brain development involves differentiation of neural progenitor cells. This process is 
initiated within the first month after conception. To begin with, the pool of progenitor cells expand via 
symmetric proliferation division, followed by a shift toward an asymmetric division after a couple of 
weeks, with the concurrent production of new neural progenitor cells and neurons (Moog et al. 2017). As 
the neurons are produced they start migrating toward different brain areas, including the cerebral cortex, 
where they establish neural networks by creating connections with one another (Stiles and Jernigan 2010). 
The migration of post-mitotic neurons into the developing cortex results in the formation of a 6-layered 
structure, which is visible by the end of the second trimester, whereas the final form is not reached until 
gestational week 35 (Moog et al. 2017). These six layers are formed in an ‘inside-out’ manner with earlier 
migrating neurons located in the deepest layer of the cortex and later migrating neurons forming more 
superficial layers (Stiles and Jernigan 2010). The cortex is divided into several subregions, for instance the 
S1 region, and is involved in brain functions such as sensory perception, consciousness, and language 
(Moog et al. 2017). Another important brain region is the hippocampus, which plays an important role in 
learning and memory (Gilbert et al. 2012). The hippocampus also consists of several substructures, 
including cornu ammonis (CA)1-3 and the dentate gyrus (DG). By 10 weeks of gestation, the human 
hippocampus is first evident, however, it is not until the 14th week of gestation that distinct substructures 
of the hippocampus become evident, though still not fully developed (Gilbert et al. 2012).  
Synaptogenesis, the process by which neurons create the important synaptic connections to one another 
by extending axons and dendrites, is initiated when neurons reach their final destination within the 
developing brain (Moog et al. 2017). These connections of neurons form large, complex networks across 
the brain by which neurons can communicate with one another by the means of excitatory and inhibitory 
transmission (Gilbert et al. 2020). Neurotransmitters, signaling molecules, released from one neuron are 
transduced into an electrical signal in another neuron by traversing the synapse between the two neurons. 
In the mature human brain GABA is the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter, which regulates the 
excitatory activity of glutamate, the primary excitatory neurotransmitter (Gilbert et al. 2020). However, 
==PAGE:13==
 12 
during fetal development GABA acts as an excitatory neurotransmitter involved in processes like cell 
proliferation, migration, and synaptogenesis (Moog et al. 2017). Appropriate connectivity among neurons 
within the circuit is essential for adequate information processing and is, among other factors, achieved 
by proper positioning of spatiotemporal migrating neurons. For even more efficient cell-to-cell 
communication the process of myelination of the axons begins around mid-gestation (Moog et al. 2017). 
All these processes are critical in the functional maturation of developing neural circuits, thus the function 
of the brain, and extend well into the postnatal period.   
1.4. 
Thyroid Hormones and Early Brain Development 
Normal brain development is dependent on THs (Patel et al. 2011). For instance, THs have been implicated 
in the development and maturation of brain structures, including the neocortex and hippocampus, as well 
as neurological processes such as neurogenesis, neuronal migration, myelination, and synaptogenesis 
(Mohan et al. 2012). Thereby, even modest alterations in maternal TH status during pregnancy have been 
associated with adverse outcomes in the progeny, such as altered brain structure and function in the child, 
psychomotor deficits, lower intelligent quotient scores, reduced cognitive function, and abnormal 
hippocampal activity (Gilbert et al. 2020). The human fetus completely relies on sufficient maternal supply 
of THs during the first half of pregnancy to ensure proper neurological development, as the thyroid gland 
is not formed until the second trimester and only fully matured by the end of gestation (Gilbert et al. 2012; 
Moog et al. 2017). Maternal T4 easily crosses the placenta and is subsequently transported to peripheral 
organ in the fetus, including the brain, where it is converted into T3 via deiodinase activity to regulate 
gene expression (Patel et al. 2011).  
Knowledge of THs and brain development is primarily derived from rodent experiments. As in humans, 
proper neurological development in rodents depends on THs, and both TH receptors (TRs) and deiodinase 
enzymes have been detected in the brain before the onset of the fetal thyroid gland (Patel et al. 2011). In 
rodents, the thyroid gland is functional from approximately embryonic day 17 (E17), a few days before 
birth (Gilbert et al. 2012), but THs have been detected in the rat embryo as early as 3 days after 
implantation (Bernal 2015). Thus, as seen in humans, before the onset of fetal thyroid gland function, the 
fetus completely rely on maternally derived hormones. The proportion of THs of fetal origin increases 
during development, however, at term maternal contribution of T4 in the fetal pool still accounts for 
approximately 17.5% (Bernal 2015).  
A so-called critical window for TH action in brain development has been suggested to extend from E18 to 
PND21-25 in rats, depending on the specific developmental endpoint (Patel et al. 2011). During this period 
==PAGE:14==
 13 
appropriate levels of THs may be particularly important as crucial structures of the brain develop and 
mature. However, even though the early postnatal period seems to be most critical in rodents, 
appropriate supply of THs during fetal development cannot be neglected. The brain development in 
rodents during the first two weeks of life resembles human brain development in third trimester (Gilbert 
et al. 2012).  
Thus, TH insufficiency during gestation and early postnatal life can result in detrimental brain 
development, as appropriate TH signaling is required for normal neural development, migration and 
function. The following subparagraphs will shortly introduce experimental evidence for this postulate.  
1.4.1. Neurogenesis  
Fetal neurogenesis, the process by which neurons are produced from neural progenitor cells, has been 
suggested to be controlled by THs during development. Neurogenesis involves both symmetric (expansion 
of the progenitor pool) and asymmetric (direct generation of neurons) division, as mentioned above 
(Mohan et al. 2012). Developmental hypothyroidism, maternally induced via MMI (0.025%), has been 
shown to prolong the symmetric division subsequently with remained low asymmetric division, thereby 
potentially depressing fetal neurogenesis (Mohan et al. 2012). 
1.4.2. Neural Migration  
Defective migration of neurons can result in the formation of ectopic clusters of neurons, called 
heterotopias (Moog et al. 2017). In rats, moderate developmental TH deficiency can induce subcortical 
heterotopias in offspring (Gilbert et al. 2014), possibly because migration of neurons into the cortex and 
subsequent arrangement of the cortical layers are highly dependent on appropriate levels of THs (Pathak 
et al. 2011). This relationship may, in part, be explained by the downregulation of the extracellular matrix 
protein RELN in neurons in TH deficient rats, as neuronal migration and establishment of cortical layers 
requires proper RELN levels (Pathak et al. 2011).  
1.4.3. Myelination  
Myelination is the process by which myelin, produced by specialized oligodendrocytes, forms a sheath 
around axons to propagate fast and efficient electrical signals throughout the nervous system (Pagnin et 
al. 2021). TH insufficiency during development has been linked to decreased expression of several 
important myelin genes causing delayed and poor deposition (the result of a single axon having up to 100 
myelin layers) of myelin (Pagnin et al. 2021). Myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein (MOG) is a myelin 
protein exclusively expressed at the surface of myelin sheaths and oligodendrocyte processes. Moderate 
==PAGE:15==
 14 
doses of PTU during gestation and early postnatal life have been demonstrated to significantly reduce the 
expression of Mog up to 71% in offspring, thereby potentially impairing the communication between 
neurons (O’Shaughnessy et al. 2018; Royland, Parker, and Gilbert 2008).  
1.4.4. GABAergic interneurons  
THs have been implicated in several aspects of GABAergic interneuron development and maturation as 
well as neural circuit formation. A subpopulation of GABAergic interneurons, which in particular seems to 
be adversely affected following developmental TH insufficiency, are those expressing PV. These cells are 
derived from progenitors within the medial ganglionic eminence (MGE) and reach their final destination 
in the cortex and hippocampus through tangential migration (Cuevas et al. 2005). Early maternal 
hypothyroxinemia (reduced serum T4 with normal T3 and TSH) has been suggested to interfere with 
normal migration of MGE-derived interneurons in the offspring, because specific signals involved in 
tangential migration were altered in fetuses, possibly as a consequence of mild TH deprivation during 
early pregnancy (Cuevas et al. 2005). Furthermore, several studies have observed that inappropriate low 
levels of TH during development result in a reduction in PV immunoreactivity, in addition to impaired 
development of axonal and dendritic arborization, in the cortex and hippocampus (Berbel et al. 1996; 
Gilbert et al. 2007; Uchida et al. 2014). This improper reduction may affect the firing properties of these 
cells thereby reducing the ability to counterbalance the action of glutamatergic neurons, with the 
consequence of a malfunctioning brain in the offspring.  
1.5. 
Parvalbumin-Expressing Interneurons 
Parvalbumin (PV) is a calcium binding protein, suggested to play an important role in buffering cytosolic 
calcium in a subset of inhibitory interneurons, which have GABA as their neurotransmitter (Gilbert et al. 
2007). In those cells, the presence of PV has been linked to high electrophysiological activities and firing 
patterns, by which this subgroup of GABAergic interneurons can be distinguished from other cortical 
neurons, in addition to their morphology (Fitzgerald et al. 2020). In the cortex, PV-expressing (PV+) 
interneurons are represented by two types of cells: large basket cells that establish synapses onto the 
soma of postsynaptic neurons and chandelier cells (or axo-axonic cells) that innervate the axon initial 
segment of pyramidal cells (Ferguson and Gao 2018). Additionally, in the hippocampus, a few amount of 
PV-expressing interneurons are also represented by bistratified cells. 
In rats, whether a GABAergic interneuron becomes PV+ is determined during early fetal development, 
while the actual ability to produce the protein is not acquired until the late postnatal period (Gilbert et al. 
2020). In rats, Pvalb (the gene encoding PV) expression starts around PND8-10 before reaching adult-like 
==PAGE:16==
 15 
levels and distribution around PND18-21, and peaks around PND14 (Lecea, del Río, and Soriano 1995). By 
the means of in situ hybridization, PV-positive neurons are first detected in the deep cortical layer V 
followed by adjacent layers, with the exception of layer I, before reaching mature levels of expression 
simultaneously in layers II-III and VI (Lecea et al. 1995). Even though several factors have been reported 
to be important for the development and maturation, the precise mechanism(s) involved in the 
differentiation of PV+ interneurons from progenitor cells to mature cells have not been fully elucidated, 
though several models have been suggested.  
At maturity, PV+ interneurons have the ability to regulate pyramidal neuron activity, as a single cell makes 
connective synapses to almost every surrounding pyramidal neuron (Ferguson and Gao 2018). The 
regulation mainly consists of two principles: (1) feedforward inhibition via strong hyperpolarization, 
thereby limiting the window in which incoming excitation can generate an action potential and (2) 
controlling the rate at which the firing of a neuron increases in response to increasing excitatory input 
(Ferguson and Gao 2018). Hence, PV-expressing interneurons appear to be very important for an 
appropriate excitatory/inhibitory balance within the brain, a balance shown to be crucial for proper brain 
development and function. Additionally, PV+ interneurons have been implicated in the generation of 
gamma oscillations (30-80 Hz), thus being linked to cognition and information processing (Ferguson and 
Gao 2018). Indeed, post-mortem studies have directly linked decreases in Pvalb mRNA expression, in 
addition to PV+ interneuron activity, to schizophrenia (Enwright et al. 2016), Parkinson's disease (Lanoue, 
Blatt, and Soghomonian 2014), and autism (Filice et al. 2020), among other neurodevelopmental 
disorders.  
1.6. 
PV Immunostaining and Thyroid Hormone Deficiency 
In rats, changes in PV immunoreactivity are not dependent on neither severe nor continuous TH 
deficiency, however, hormone insufficiency during development seems to be required. Moderate doses 
of PTU (3 ppm) during development have been found to dramatically reduce PV immunostaining in the 
hypothyroid neocortex and hippocampus (Gilbert et al. 2007). The pattern of reductions observed in 
weaned offspring remained into adulthood, despite fully recovered TH status. In contrast, TH insufficiency 
initiated in adult animals did not seem to affect the density of PV immunostaining in the cortex nor the 
hippocampus (Gilbert et al. 2007). The observed reduction of PV immunoreactivity induced by 
developmental hypothyroidism seems to correlate with significant downregulation of the tissue-specific 
expression of Pvalb (Shiraki et al. 2012).  
==PAGE:17==
 16 
Phenotypic expression of PV is most likely TH dependent, as T4-replacement (PND8-14) significantly 
restored the loss of PV-immunoreactive cells in the cortex and hippocampus seen in developmentally 
hypothyroid rats (Gilbert et al. 2007). In mice, reductions in PV expression in the cortex, and to a lesser 
extent in the hippocampus, could also be restored by early postnatal (PND0-20) administration of 
exogenous T3 (Uchida et al. 2014). Thus, the critical window (the period after which supplement/ 
administration of THs do not support recovery) in TH-mediated development of PV-expressing 
interneuron seems to be restricted to the first two-three postnatal weeks in rodents, where the cortical 
network formation is characterized by depolarizing actions of GABAergic signaling (Uchida et al. 2014, 
Westerholz et al. 2013). 
Reduced PV immunostaining could be a result of fewer PV-expressing interneurons, delayed maturation, 
retarded migration, or simply just a low expressing of PV. Evidence suggests that in mice, altered PV 
immunoreactivity in consequence of receptor-mediated hypothyroidism is the result of impaired 
migration and/or maturation of PV-expressing interneurons rather than the result of altered regulation of 
PV expression (Wallis et al. 2008). By contrast, experiments in rats suggest that in the absence of THs 
these specific GABAergic interneurons lose their ability to express PV rather than disappear, as TH-
replacement during the critical window effectively inverted the decrease in PV immunoreactivity (Gilbert 
et al. 2007). Thus, convincing evidence explaining the observed reductions in PV immunoreactivity in the 
cortex and/or hippocampus following developmental TH insufficiency is still lacking.  
1.7. 
Cortical Gene Expression and Thyroid Hormones Deficiency  
As mentioned in paragraph 1.3, correct temporal gene expression is required for normal brain 
development. Thus, TH deficiency has been associated with adverse effects on the mRNA expression of 
several important genes within the developing brain.  
It is necessary to identify TH-regulated genes to understand the molecular impact of TH action in the 
developing brain and to unravel the specific roles of THs at different developmental stages in the brain. 
However, it seems extremely challenging, as it can be difficult to determine whether changes in the 
transcription of a target gene are a direct or indirect result of TH action. Often, genes directly regulated 
by THs contain a functional TH responsive element in the promoter region, yet exceptions exist (Bernal 
2015). Further, genes may be involved in multiple pathways within the brain, hence being more wide-
acting, or involved in more specific and limited developmental processes (Bernal 2017).  
==PAGE:18==
 17 
The single most important result of a gene is the production of a protein molecule, which act as active 
agents in biological processes such as the development of the brain. Hence, proper gene expression and 
subsequent gene product formation and function are essential for normal development of the brain. 
Before the protein can be formed, information in the genetic sequence must be extracted (transcribed) 
and translated. Nonetheless, altered gene transcription can possibly lead to altered protein expression.  
In rats, developmental exposure to modest doses of PTU (1-3 ppm), adversely affected TH levels both in 
serum and the brain, where it was predicted that a 40% decrease in serum T4 would correspond to brain 
T4 levels 70% below control levels (O’Shaughnessy et al. 2018). As THs exert their function mainly through 
TR-mediated transcription activation (Patel et al. 2011), it is plausible to expect that reduced brain levels 
of THs would result in differential expression of TH-dependent genes in the brain, which might be reflected 
in adverse or inadequate brain development/function. Several genes within the cortex have been 
identified as TH responsive, though only a few are identified as direct targets of THs. One of these genes 
directly controlled by THs is Krüppel-like factor 9 (Klf9), which plays a role in neurite extension and 
branching (Denver et al. 1999). Another well-defined TH-regulated gene is Hairless (Hr), which encodes a 
protein that facilitate transcriptional repression of unliganded TRs (Potter et al. 2002). 
A large study, evaluating the effect of PTU on 30 cortical TH-responsive genes  in developmentally exposed 
rats, identified seven target genes, showing significantly reduced expression at all doses tested (1, 2, 3, 
and 10 ppm) concurrently with significant reductions in brain T4 (O’Shaughnessy et al. 2018). Among these 
genes, Pvalb showed the highest degree of changes with 97% reduction in response to the highest dose. 
This gene encodes the calcium-binding protein PV, which is selectively expressed in a subset of GABAergic 
interneurons (see paragraph 1.5). This class of genes (associated with decreased brain T4)  also included 
Coll11a2, Itih3, and Agt, which are implicated in cell and tissue structure, neuronal signaling and 
migration, respectively (O’Shaughnessy et al. 2018). Five additional genes, differentially expressed at 
moderate to high PTU doses coinciding with significant decreases of T3 within the brain, were also 
identified (O’Shaughnessy et al. 2018). Among these genes Hr demonstrated the largest magnitude of 
change with 90% reduced expression in high dose exposed rats. Mog, encoding a myelin specific 
glycoprotein, were also among genes related to altered brain T3 levels. These six mentioned genes have 
been suggested as potential biomarkers for minor disruptions of TH homeostasis, due to the plausible 
relationship between their expressions and the presence of THs within the brain (O’Shaughnessy et al. 
2018).

Aims of the Study 
- 
To investigate the expression of a battery of 10 TH-responsive genes in male PND16 rats after 
exposure to MMI and amitrole to clarify whether potential effects on cortical gene expression are 
mode of action dependent or more compound specific. 
- 
To investigate the immunoreactivity of parvalbumin-expressing interneurons in the cortex of male

2. Methods 
Data from two in-house developmental toxicity studies were included in this project. The first study 
evaluated effects of PTU (TPO-inhibiting drug), OMC (UV-filter), and DE-71 (flame-retardant), whereas the 
second study evaluated effects of MMI (TPO-inhibiting drug) and amitrole (pesticide).   
All laboratory work (immunohistochemistry, RNA extraction, cDNA synthesis, and RT-qPCR) have been 
performed by laboratory technicians (Mette Voigt Jessen and Heidi Broksø Letting) due to pregnancy, 
whereas all data handling, including counting of PV+ cells, and statistical analysis have been accomplished 
by the author. 
2.1. 
Animals 
Study 1: Time-mated Sprague-Dawley (SD) rats (n = 56) were orally administrated with either control 
vehicle (corn oil), PTU (1 or 2.5 mg/kg bw/day), OMC (375 or 500 mg/kg bw/day), or DE-71 (20 or 40 mg/kg 
bw/day) by gavage from GD7 to PND22, with the exception of the day of delivery. On PND16 pups were 
humanely killed and brains were excised and stored at -80°C until staining. Prior to immunostaining, brains 
were cut in slabs of 10 µm.  
Study 2: Time-mated SD rats (n = 58) were orally administrated with either control vehicle (corn oil), MMI 
(8 or 16 mg/kg bw/day) or amitrol (25 or 50 mg/kg bw/day) by gavage from GD7 to PND22, with the 
exception of the day of delivery. On PND16 pups were humanely killed and brains were excised and stored 
at -80°C until RNA extraction and/or trimming and processing for immunostaining.  
2.2. 
Immunochemistry 
The 10 µm brain slabs from male PND16 rats were stained by standard immunohistochemistry 
procedures. Initially, brain slabs were deparaffined by the means of increasing ethanol and xylen. To 
expose PV (target protein) in the tissue, heat-induced antigen retrieval was performed in Tris/EDTA buffer 
(pH 8.9 - 9.05). Unspecific background staining was blocked by 1% BSA treatment followed by incubation 
in primary polyclonal antibody (raised in rabbit) diluted in 1% BSA (1:8000) over night at 4 °C. On the 
second day, the sections were washed sequentially in buffer (sodium chloride, BSA, and Triton) and 
incubated in 3% hydrogen peroxide to block endogenous peroxidase activity. Sections were incubated 
with EnVision secondary anti-PV antibody (anti-rabbit) for 30 minutes at room temperature. DAB was 
used to visualize PV-expressing interneurons. Sections were counterstained with hematoxylin to visualize 
nuclei followed by dehydration and lastly prepped for mounting.

2.4. 
RNA Extraction, cDNA Synthesis, and Quantitative RT-PCR 
Approximately 20-30 mg rat cortex tissue from male PND16 pups was homogenized in 350 μl RLT buffer 
using Qiagen Tissue Lyser II (30 osc/sec for 2 minutes). Total RNA was extracted using RNase-Mini kit 
(QIAGEN, Cat. No. 74106) according to manufacturer’s instructions with minor modifications: to ensure 
that any potential present DNA was degraded, an additional DNaseI treatment was performed, and RNA 
extracts were eluted in 50 μl RNase free water to obtain adequate concentrations at 500 ng extracted 
RNA. RNA quantity was measured on a nano-drop spectrophotometer. Samples were diluted (1:10) in 
RNase free water and normalized prior to cDNA construction. cDNA was synthesized from 500 ng total 
RNA using Omniscript reverse transcription kit (QIAGEN, Cat. No. 205113), random primer mix (2 μl per 
sample, Nordic Biolab S1330S), and SUPERase-In RNase inhibitor (20U/ μl per sample, Invitrogen, Cat no. 
AM2694) according to manufacturer’s instructions. RT-qPCR reactions were made up by TaqMan Fast 
Universal PCR Master Mix (2X), TaqMan Gene Expression Assay (20X) (Table 1) and 3 μl diluted cDNA 
(1:20) according to manufacturer’s instructions. A total volume of 11 μl RT-qPCR reactions for each sample 
were placed on a 384-well plate and analyzed on a QuantStudio 7 Flex Fast Real-Time PCR System using 
the following RT-qPCR program: (1) an initial cycle of 20 sec of 95°C, (2) 1 sec of 95°C, and (3) 20 sec of 
60°C. Step (2) and (3) was repeated for 45 cycles. Samples were run in technical duplicates. Threshold 
cycle (Ct) values were obtained and analyzed by the comparative Ct method (Schmittgen and Livak 2008), 
obtaining the relative gene expression for each gene of interest compared to the expression of geometric 
means of the two house-keeping genes (reference genes) Rps18 and Ubc, previously shown to be stable 
expressed in rat brain (Ramhøj, Axelstad, and Svingen 2019). Only samples with duplicate-differences in 
Ct-values < 1 were included in the analysis, resulting in the exclusion of 5 samples. Table 1 summarizes 
the battery of genes currently investigated, in addition to their functions and expected outcomes. These 
genes were selected based on previous studies (Axelstad et al. 2019; O’Shaughnessy et al. 2018).

2.5. 
Statistical Analysis 
Pearson’s correlation analysis was performed in GraphPad Prism 9 to verify analysis of PV-IR cell count in 
duplicate (including right and left hemisphere) for both the cortex and the two distinct subregions of the 
hippocampus (CA1-3 and DG, respectively). The analysis was performed in respect to all stained PV-IR cells 
and individually for dark, medium and light stained PV-IR cells. The Pearson’s correlation calculates the 
statistical evidence for a linear relationship between two variables in a population and their mutual 
association. All statistical analyses showed positive correlations between right and left hemispheres with 
respect to the first study, except for overall cell count in the cortex and DG in addition to light stained PV-
IR cells within the DG (data not showed). Relatively large differences in PV-IR counts in the CA1-3, and to 
some extent in the DG, were observed in the right and left hemisphere of brains from the second study. 
This might be explained by the fact that a large number of brains from this study were cut obliquely 
resulting in one hippocampus being smaller than the other. These large differences in PV-IR cell counts 
within the hippocampus resulted in a questionable correlation analysis for this part of the brain (only 
significant correlation in respect to dark stained PV-IR cells) compared to the first study (data not shown). 
However, because only two brains had destroyed or missing parts of the hippocampus, this effect is 
negligible. Thus, in general subsequent statistical analysis of PV-IR cell count was performed as means of 
duplicates. In cases where one hemisphere was destroyed or immunostaining was missing (more than 
20% of the area of interest), the statistical analysis was carried out as a single determination, which is 
considered acceptable due to significant correlation analyses.   
Statistical analysis of PV-IR cell count data were performed by one-way ANOVA (analysis of variance) and 
Dunnett’s multiple comparison test in GraphPad Prism 9. Data were individually analyzed in each area of 
interest (cortex, CA1-3, and DG, respectively) both as total cell count and as distinct subcategories of 
staining intensity (dark, medium, and light PV-IR cells). Residual and QQ plots determined the reliability 
of the ANOVA test. If insufficient, data were log transformed. Statistical significance was evaluated by 
multiplicity adjusted P values obtained from Dunnett’s multiple comparison test.  
Specific diffuse PV-staining was statistically analyzed by two-sided Fisher’s Exact Test, a statistical method 
used to determine whether categorical data are correlated to one another. Data were examined by 
analyzing 2x4 contingency tables for each treatment against controls in VassarStats online Fishers Exact 
Test calculator.

Statistical analysis of gene expression data were performed on relative gene expression also by one-way

In general, statistical significance is denoted *p < 0.05, **p < 0.01, ***p < 0.001, and ****p < 0.0001. The

3.2. 
Differential Effects on Specific Diffuse Cortex Staining  
Cortices from PND16 male rat pups were assigned to four distinct levels of specific diffuse PV-staining 
(Figure 5), believed to represent axonal PV expression. The majority of controls cortices from the first 
study (~80%) had a rich diffuse staining throughout the area of interest with bands of dark brown staining 
(Figure 10A). None of the exposed groups reached this level of “normal” diffuse staining. The statistical 
analysis revealed that the distribution of the four distinct scores among high dose PTU-exposed rats was 
significantly different from controls (p = 0.0005). Half of the rats in this treatment group (five of ten 
animals) had almost no diffuse PV-staining, whereas only a single rat had rich diffuse staining within the 
counted S1 subregion (Figure 10A). Additionally, OMC-exposed rats, regardless of dose, had significantly 
different distribution patterns compared to controls (p = 0.0025 and p = 0.0062 for low and high dose 
groups, respectively). Interestingly, no rats exposed to low dose OMC experienced rich diffuse PV-staining, 
but 50% had a moderate staining pattern (Figure 10A). By contrast, no cortices were assigned to the fourth 
category (i.e., minimal diffuse PV staining) among the high dose OMC group, whereas 75% showed 
==PAGE:33==
 32 
moderate diffuse PV-staining throughout the S1 subregion. Less pronounced results, though still 
statistically significant, existed for rats exposed to low dose DE-71 (p = 0.0436). The majority of these rats 
had high levels of dense diffuse PV-staining (~45%), whereas the remaining cortices showed moderate 
levels of diffuse staining (~33%) and light diffuse PV-staining (~22%) (Figure 10A). Thus, based on the 
distribution pattern of diffuse PV-staining observed in control rats, apparently a high level of dense diffuse 
PV-staining could be considered a baseline, whereas no diffuse PV-staining were associated with marked 
disturbances in TH status, which existed following high PTU exposure (Axelstad et al. 2019).  
Generally, more unspecific (background) staining appeared on brains from the second study (Figure 5E-
H), which could be a result of unstable immunostaining when preparing the slides. It is also possible that 
the concentration of antibodies used to stain cortices from the second study was not optimized for a 2-
day protocol, thus giving a too high concentration, though this is most likely not the case. Due to this 
difference, classifying cortices from the second study into the four categories was not as straight forward 
as with cortices from the first study. The characteristic bands that appeared in varying degrees on cortices 
from the first study (Figure 5A-D) were none existing in the majority of cortices from the second study 
(Figure 5E-H). However, this lack of appearance might again be explained by the high unspecific 
background staining rather than an actual absence. Despite the obvious difficulty in comparing the specific 
diffuse staining between cortices from the two studies due to the difference in unspecific background 
staining, the classification system was still applied to cortices from the second study, but the discrepancy 
in the unspecific background staining should be kept in mind and direct comparison of diffuse staining 
between the two studies will not be performed.  
The specific diffuse PV staining in controls from the second study all fell into category A (i.e., rich and 
dense bright brown staining throughout the entire area) (Figure 10B). Generally, low dose exposure (both 
MMI and amitrole) only mildly reduced the staining in a few animals, whereas high dose MMI and amitrole 
severely reduced the appearance of diffuse staining. The most severely affected group appeared to be 
high dose MMI, where four of six of these animals had minimal diffuse staining, followed by the high dose 
amitrole group, where pups were equally distributed among the moderate, light, and minimal diffuse 
staining category (Figure 10B). The observed alterations were supported statistically (MMI p = 0.002; 
amitrole p = 0.002).

4. Discussion 
The experimental work conducted in this project demonstrated a likely association between reduced 
Pvalb mRNA expression in the cortex and subsequent reductions in the PV protein expression in a subset 
of cortical interneurons following developmental TH deprivation in PND16 male rats. This indicates that 
changes in gene expression, in respect to PV, is not just a transient readout of TH insufficiency, but rather 
a step in an unfortunate direction toward impaired brain function, as PV-expressing interneurons control 
the output of local networks by suppressing pyramidal cell activity (Kann 2016). Developmental exposure 
to MMI and amitrole, in addition to PTU (Axelstad et al. 2019), significantly reduced the mRNA expression 
of several TH-responsive genes in the cortex at PND16 in a similar manner. This suggests that potential 
effects on cortical gene expression are related to the mode of action by which a certain TDC interfere with 
the TH system. In respect to PV expression, or more precisely PV immunoreactivity, in the cortex and 
hippocampus, effects were also more or less associated with specific mode of actions rather than globally 
related to hypothyroidism. In general, at PND16 PV immunoreactivity was most severely affected in the 
cortex compared to the hippocampus, suggesting that in rats at this time point PV expression in the cortex 
is more sensitive to developmental exposure of TPO-inhibitors than PV expression in the hippocampus.  
4.1. 
TPO-Induced Hypothyroidism and Reduced PV Immunoreactivity in the Brain 
Mean total PV+ cell count in the cortex of PND16 male rats was significantly reduced in response to 
developmental exposure of high dose PTU, MMI, and amitrole (Figure 6A and Figure 7A). In contrast, mean 
total PV+ cell count within the hippocampus was not significantly affected in neither of the two subregions 
(CA1-3 and DG) by any treatment (Figure 6B,C and Figure 7B,C). Thus, at PND16 PV+ interneurons located 
in the cortex seem more sensitive to developmental TH deficiency than PV+ interneurons in the 
hippocampus. These observations support previous findings in hypothyroid mice, where the order of 
sensitivity in respect to reductions in cortical and hippocampal PV+ cells were cortex > DG > CA (Uchida et 
al. 2014). By contrast, in rats at PND23 similar reductions in PV immunoreactivity were detected both in 
the cortex and the DG subregion following developmental PTU exposure (3 ppm) (Gilbert et al. 2007). 
These results were associated with serum T4 levels reduced by ~70% compared to control levels. 
Furthermore, PND21 rat pups developmentally exposed to MMI (50 ppm) also exhibited significantly 
reduced number of PV+ cells in the DG subregion, correlated with even smaller serum T4 reductions 
(approximately 29%) compared to controls (Shiraki et al. 2012).  
==PAGE:39==
 38 
Theoretically, differences in the severity of TH deprivation could partially explain discrepancies between 
the present and previous results, where it is expected that larger reductions in serum TH levels correspond 
to larger reductions in PV immunoreactivity. Pups analyzed in this report exhibited reductions in serum 
T4 levels by ~35% relative to controls following low dose PTU (1 mg/kg bw) exposure (Axelstad et al. 2019), 
a reduction that did not correlate with reduced number of PV+ cells in the cortex nor the hippocampus. 
High dose (2.5 mg/kg bw) PTU, on the other hand, which significantly reduced PV immunoreactivity in the 
cortex, caused serum T4 concentrations to fall below the level of quantification (Axelstad et al. 2019). 
Combined with reported reductions following developmental exposure to 3 ppm PTU (Gilbert et al. 2007), 
this indicates that a certain amount of serum T4 reduction should be in place before a reduction in cortical 
PV immunoreactivity is seen. However, differences in the severity of TH deprivation cannot explain why 
effects in the hippocampus are absent in the current study. Based on results reported by Gilbert et al. 
(2007), reductions in serum T4 concentrations in animals exposed to 2.5 mg PTU/kg bw should be 
sufficient to cause effects in the hippocampus. The animals of the present study were assessed one week 
before animals in the previous study (PND16 vs PND23), thus, it is possible that the specific assessment 
point may affect whether reductions in the hippocampus are evident or not.  
Currently, developmental MMI exposure resulted in reduced serum T4 by approximately 38 and 94% in 
low and high dose group animals, respectively (Ramhøj et al. 2021, unpublished data). Only high dose 
exposure correlated with significant effects in mean total PV+ cell count in the cortex, and the effect was 
missing in the hippocampus. According to results reported by Shiraki et al. (2012), this level of hormone 
reduction should be sufficient to significantly alter PV+ cell count in the DG. Again, the specific assessment 
time is more likely to explain the current lack of significant results rather than the severity of the TH status.  
Western blot analysis of PV protein expression in the hippocampus revealed increased PV levels as a 
function of postnatal age (Gilbert et al. 2007). However, the relative dose-dependent reduction of PV 
protein observed at PND14 following developmental TH deficiency did not seem to be different from that 
observed at PND21. Thus, whether PV+ cell counts are investigated at PND16 or at PND21-23 should not 
yield different results. Accordingly, it would be interesting to include more assessment points in the future 
to elucidate whether specific early time points are more sensitive than others, whether different brain 
regions might be most sensitive at different time points, and possibly whether potential effects are 
restricted to specific assessment points.

4.2. 
OMC and Effects on Parvalbumin 
Surprisingly,  mean total PV+ count in the cortex appeared more or less unaffected in both OMC groups 
(Figure 6A), despite previously reported significant dose-dependent reductions of Pvalb (Axelstad et al. 
2019). Thus, no correlation was found between Pvalb mRNA expression and PV protein expression in the 
cortex of OMC-exposed animals. However, as Pvalb expression was downregulated in the cortex of OMC-
exposed pups at PND16, adverse effects on cortical TH-responsive gene expression does not seem to be 
restricted to TPO-inhibitors, but can also be induced by compounds that do not significantly reduce serum 
TH levels, such as OMC. In such cases, effects are possibly due to disruptions on a more general level 
rather than affected TH status. This indicates that OMC might act more as a neurotoxic chemical instead 
of a TDC.  
The correlation between expression levels of protein and mRNA is dependent on regulatory processes 
such as post-transcriptional and translational regulation. By this mean, the lack of effect on PV protein 
level, observed in response to OMC exposure, may be explained by an induction of a buffering mechanism, 
for instance increased post-transcriptional activation. 
4.3. 
Mode of Action Dependent Effects on PV-IR in the Brain 
PV immunoreactivity in the cortex and hippocampus was also evaluated by the staining intensity, i.e. 
differentiating between dark, medium, and light stained cells. This differentiation revealed that even 
though no significant effect was observed on mean total cell count, the number of cells divided by their 
staining intensity might be significantly different compared to controls. High dose PTU significantly 
reduced the number of medium stained PV-expressing interneurons in the CA subregion, even though the 
total number of PV+ interneurons seemed unaffected (Figure 6B). Additionally, high dose MMI or amitrole 
significantly reduced the number of dark and light stained PV+ interneurons in the DG, despite unaffected 
total cell count (Figure 7C). As all these TDCs induce TH deficiency (Axelstad et al. 2019, Ramhøj et al. 
2021, unpublished data), the current results indicate that the ability of GABAergic interneurons, destined 
to become PV+ interneurons, to produce PV is somewhat reduced in response to developmental TH 
insufficiency. However, DE-71 significantly reduce serum T4 (Axelstad et al. 2019) without affecting the 
number of PV-IR cells, neither total mean nor divided by staining intensity, or the Pvalb expression in the 
cortex (Figure 6A and (Axelstad et al. 2019)). Further, developmental exposure to 375 mg OMC/kg bw 
resulted in significantly reduced PV+ count of dark stained cells in all three investigated areas of the brain 
(Figure 6A-C) despite minor alterations in serum TH levels (Axelstad et al. 2019). By this means, TH 
deprivation during development does not always result in alterations in PV expression in the rat brain, 
and it is possible that the specific mode of action by which a chemical interfere with the TH system may 
==PAGE:41==
 40 
be important in whether certain endpoints within the brain are affected or not. For instance, the mode of 
action by which DE-71 interferes with the TH system may induce some sort of compensatory mechanism 
that protects the developing brain, perhaps by maintaining the brain TH levels, that is not seen following 
TPO-induced disruptions.  
4.4. 
Developmental TDC Exposure and Effects on Axonal PV Expression 
Diffuse staining within the cortex, believed to represent staining of PV-expressing axons, was divided into 
four distinct categories. As the majority (80%) of control animals from the first study and all controls in 
the second study had a dense diffuse PV-staining (Figure 10), this was considered a baseline. No treatment 
group reached this percentage of ‘normal’ diffuse staining. The most severely affected groups appeared 
to be high dose PTU followed by high dose MMI and amitrole in addition to both doses of OMC (Figure 
10). Interestingly, no animals developmentally exposed to low dose OMC or high doses of either MMI or 
amitrole seemed to have a high degree of PV-stained processes. Nevertheless, clear differences in staining 
of PV-positive axons appeared between exposed and control rats. A decrease in the overall PV-staining of 
processes might represent either an impairment of axonal development and/or a decrease in PV-
expressing axons in the cortex in consequence of TH system disturbances. Either way this might be 
reflected in altered firing properties of these GABAergic interneurons, ultimately leading to impaired 
inhibition of excitatory neurons. The current findings are consistent with previously results reported by 
Berbel et al. (1996). They observed less intense and restricted immunostaining of PV-terminals in adult 
rats rendering TH insufficient from late gestation and throughout life. In addition, Gilbert et al. (2007) 
observed a significant dose-dependent reduction in process staining of PV-IR neurons in the DG of PTU-
exposed rats at weanling. Taken together, these results suggest that axons derived from GABAergic 
interneurons located both in the cortex and the hippocampus are subjects to altered PV expression in 
response to TH deficiency. 
4.5. 
TPO-Induced Hypothyroidism and Dose-Dependent Reductions in Cortical 
Gene Expression 
A battery of ten TH-responsive genes were investigated to elucidate whether cortical gene expression is 
affected in a mode of action dependent manner or more compound specific in PND16 male rats following 
developmental TH insufficiency. In general, similar effects were observed after developmental MMI and 
amitrole exposure. The fact that all ten target genes were dose-dependently reduced, though a few not 
statistically significant, in addition to previous results from developmental PTU exposure (Axelstad et al. 
2019), indicate that, at least for TPO-inhibitors, the mode of action predicts the effects on gene expression 
in the cortex. Affected gene expression indicates TH insufficiency in the brain itself. Previously, both in 
==PAGE:42==
 41 
fetuses and pups, strong correlations between serum and brain levels of T4 have been reported 
(O’Shaughnessy et al. 2018). Thus, as both MMI and amitrole reduce serum TH levels (Ramhøj et al. 2021, 
unpublished data), the effects on cortical gene expression are possibly a consequence of local TH 
insufficiency.  
The current results on cortical gene expression are generally in line with previous findings in rats 
maternally exposed to PTU, and to some extend OMC, during gestation and lactation (Axelstad et al. 2019; 
O’Shaughnessy et al. 2018). Surprisingly, reductions in Klf9 mRNA expression (~44% in high dose MMI and 
amitrole groups) did not reach statistical significance (Figure 11G), despite the fact that this gene is directly 
regulated by T3 in the developing rat brain (Denver et al. 1999). Low power in addition to a large deviation 
might account, at least in part, for the insignificant results reported in the present report. Another 
possibility could be maintained brain TH levels despite reduced serum levels, however, this does not seem 
likely, as both MMI and amitrole significantly reduced other TH-responsive genes in the cortex in addition 
to other TH-dependent endpoints in the brain, such as PV immumnoreactivity.  
4.6. 
Reductions in Pvalb mRNA Expression Correlated with Reductions in PV Protein 
Expression in the Cortex Following TPO-induced TH Insufficiency 
Most pronounced reductions in gene expression, both in term of MMI and amitrole exposure, were 
observed in respect to Pvalb expression (Figure 11). PV, encoded by the Pvalb gene, is, as previously 
mentioned, essential for the fast-spiking properties of those interneurons expressing the protein 
(Ferguson and Gao 2018). Moreover, both MMI and amitrole reduced the PV immunoreactivity in the 
cortex, thereby suggesting an association between reductions in mRNA and consequently reductions at 
protein level. As similar results were observed following developmental PTU exposure, at least in the high 
dose group (Axelstad et al. 2019), it may be concluded that TPO-induced TH deficiency during 
development adversely affect the brain function at later stages by decreasing the expression of Pvalb and 
subsequently the protein expression of PV in the cortex.

5. Future Perspectives 
Despite emerging evidence of reduced Pvalb gene expression in addition to PV immunoreactivity in the 
cortex and hippocampus in consequence of developmental hypothyroidism there are still many questions 
that remain unsolved. One of the most critical is, how does developmental TH deficiency result in reduced 
PV immunoreactivity? Is it a result of PV+ interneuron loss, failed migration, or impaired ability to express 
PV? To clarify how even mild TH insufficiency during critical periods of early brain development might 
result in reduced PV immunoreactivity in the brain, it is necessary to identify potential molecular targets 
of THs involved in the proliferation, differentiation and/or migration of this specific subset of GABAergic 
interneurons expressing PV at maturity.  
The first step in identifying potential molecular targets is to identify genes involved in proliferation, 
differentiation and/or migration of PV+ interneurons, which expressions are altered in response to 
developmental hypothyroidism. Ideally the expression should be altered in response to mild 
hypothyroidism as this resembles human conditions more than severe TH deprivation. The literature has 
shown several promising suggestions of genes that could be interesting to look further into such as Agt 
and Itih3, which are involved in migration and neuronal signaling, respectively. However, an investigation 
of novel genes might be needed as well. This can be achieve by microarray analysis, as this can determine 
the expression level across a larger number of genes. Subsequently the protein level of affected genes 
should be investigated, for instance by Western blotting or maybe ELISA, to determine whether reductions 
in gene expression is propagated at protein level. This is important knowledge, as proteins are functionally 
involved in biological processes compared to mRNA. Lastly, to ensure that THs are actually involved in the 
expression of certain genes/proteins, a TH-replacement experiment should be conducted. This 
experiment will determine whether control levels are restored in hypothyroid animals following TH 
administration.   
If potential targets can be identified, it may be possible to clarify what causes the reduced PV 
immunoreactivity observed following developmental TH insufficiency. Due to the association between 
reduced PV expression and several neurological diseases, clarification of how TH deprivation during 
development affect the PV-expressing neurons, might prevent, or at least reduce, the risk of developing 
disorders like schizophrenia and autism.

6. Conclusion 
This project aimed to investigate the association between potential changes in cortical Pvalb gene 
expression and parvalbumin protein expression following developmental TH deprivation and whether 
potential effects at protein level was globally related to hypothyroidism or more compound specific. 
Additionally, whether potential effects on cortical gene expression were mode of action dependent or 
more compound specific was investigated through in vivo experiments on hypothyroid male PND16 rats 
developmentally exposed to MMI and amitrole.  
Results presented in this project revealed that alterations in PV immunoreactivity is more pronounced in 
the cortex than in the hippocampus following developmental exposure to PTU, MMI, and amitrole at 
PND16, and that the observed effects on PV immunoreactivity in the cortex correlated with reductions in 
cortical Pvalb gene expression. All tested TPO-inhibitors (PTU, MMI, and amitrole) reduced the PV+ cell 
count in the same manner both in the cortex and the hippocampus (Figure 6 and Figure 7). High dose PTU 
(2.5 mg/kg bw), MMI (16 mg/kg bw) and amitrole (50 mg/kg bw) significantly reduced total mean cell 
count in the cortex, whereas in the hippocampus a tendency was only observed in the DG subregion. 
Because Pvalb mRNA expression was markedly reduced in these animals (Figure 11 and (Axelstad et al. 
2019)), the current results at protein expression were expected.  
DE-71 had no effect on either PV immunoreactivity, regardless of investigated subregion, or Pvalb mRNA 
expression (Axelstad et al. 2019). Even though, no effects were seen on cortical endpoints, despite 
significant reductions in serum T4 levels in DE-71-exposed pups at PND16 (Axelstad et al. 2019), effects 
on gene expression and subsequent protein expression in the cortex are consistent with each other.  
The only investigated compound, by which the association between reduced Pvalb expression and 
subsequent reduced PV immunoreactivity does not seem to be completely consistent, is OMC. Low dose 
OMC (375 mg/kg bw) significantly reduced the number of heavily labelled cells in all three areas without 
altering the total mean number of PV-IR cells. These results were seen concurrently with significant, 
though relatively small, decreases in Pvalb expression and only small alterations in serum T4 levels 
(Axelstad et al. 2019). Therefore, it seems that OMC is able to induce adverse effects in the brain by other 
means than through disrupted TH homeostasis.   
In terms of cortical gene expression of ten TH-responsive genes, generally, dose-dependent decreases 
was observed following developmental exposure to MMI and amitrole (Figure 11). Combined with 
==PAGE:45==
 44 
previous results from developmental PTU exposure (Axelstad et al. 2019), this indicates that effects in 
cortical gene expression is mode of action dependent rather than compound specific. In respect to MMI 
or amitrole exposure, it was surprising that reductions in Klf9 mRNA expression, which is directly regulated 
by THs, did not reach statistical significance, as serum T4 levels were dramatically reduced at PND16 
(Ramhøj et al. 2021, unpublished data). However, low statistical power in addition to large variations 
within groups might partially explain this results.  
In summary, the current data indicates that reductions in Pvalb mRNA expression in the cortex correlate 
with effects on protein level. Furthermore, the effects on protein level, in addition to cortical gene 
expression in general, seem to be related to the mode of action by which a specific compound interfere 
with the TH system.
