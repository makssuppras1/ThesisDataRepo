Abstract
This thesis demonstrates the optical parametric process Four Wave Mixing (FWM)
through experimental research and nonlinear signal processing. The project’s motiva-
tion is to investigate how third-order nonlinear effects influence FWM characteristics.
Theoretical descriptions of each phenomenon’s origin are explored as well. Experi-
mental procedures to achieve the best possible conversion eﬀiciency are outlined and
supported by comparing the results with analytical expressions from the scientific
literature.
Explorations of fiber bandwidth are visualized through measurements by detun-
ing the signal from the zero-dispersion wavelength. Furthermore, an on-off Raman
gain has been investigated for both co, and counter-propagating direction, where the
results extracted in the co-propagation case agreed with expectations raised from
the theory.
For the counter-propagation method, the results were not as desired,
which has led to several discussions. Essential for the overall system performance is
Stimulated Brillouin Scattering (SBS), which limits the maximum achievable power
through the Fiber Under Test (FUT). Hence, this quantity is inspected, focusing on
its threshold value and how to suppress it in optical communication systems.
Finally, suggestions for modifications of the experimental setups are outlined.
These suggestions include how to perform optimized measurement sets in the labora-
tory and how to develop a numerical model applying the Split Step Fourier Method
(SSFM) to solve the nonlinear propagation equations for degenerate FWM.

Resumé
Denne afhandling demonstrerer den optisk parametriske proces Firebølge blanding
gennem eksperimentelle undersøgelser og ikke-lineær signal behandling. Motivationen
for projektet er at udforske tredje-ordens ikke-lineære effekter, der påvirker Firebølge
blandings karakteristik. Teoretiske beskrivelser af fænomenernes oprindelse er også
beskrevet.
Eksperimentelle fremgangsmetoder brugt til at opnå den bedst mulige
omdannelseseffektivitet er beskrevet og understøttet ved at sammenligne resultaterne
med analytiske udtryk fra videnskabelig litteratur.
Undersøgelser af fiber båndbredde er visualiseret gennem målinger, hvor signalet
justeres væk fra nul-dispersions bølgelængden. Ydermere, undersøges gevinsten fra en
Raman forstærker i både fremad- og bagudrettet udbredelsesretning, hvor resultaterne
udtrukket fra den fremadrettet opsætning levede op til forventningerne defineret af
teorien. Essentielt for den samlede systemydeevne er stimuleret Brillouin spredning,
der begrænser den maksimalt opnåelige lysstyrke i fiberen under test. Derfor er denne
kvantitet udforsket med fokus på dens grænseværdi, samt hvordan den kan nedsættes
i optiske kommunikationssystemer.
Til sidst fremvises forslag til hvad der kunne modificeres i de eksperimentelle
opsætninger. Disse forslag inkluderer, hvordan udførelsen af forsøgssæt kan optimeres,
samt hvordan en numerisk model, der udnytter split-step Fourier metoden til at løse
de ikke-lineære udbredelsesligninger for degenereret Firebølge blanding, kan blive
udviklet.

Introduction
Ever since the first internet connections were established in the late 1960s by the
Advanced Research Projects Agency Networks (ARPANET), the interest in revo-
lutionizing telecommunication networks has expanded rapidly [1]. ARPANET was
founded by the US military to communicate with other base stations across America
during the Cold War, and was subsequently used for transmission over the Atlantic
Ocean. From this initial point of network history, the amount of internet subscribers
has grown over the last half-century. This development has created a demand for
higher bit rates, and more capacity [2].
The networking company Cisco has esti-
mated this demand to grow by approximately 26 % every year until 2022, which
leads to a particular need for innovation [3]. Figure 1.1 shows a forecast on IP traﬀic

glass wires are of interest since it has made high-speed transmission with minimal
losses over several kilometers possible. However, limitations are also met in this field,
where one is the high powers obtainable in optical laser sources, i.e., nonlinear effects.
Nonlinearities in optics have been investigated since Sir C.V. Raman experimen-

the 1920s. However, the development of the first lasers in the early 1960s increased
the exploration of nonlinear phenomena and has led to many applicable methods
such as optical switching, wavelength conversion, and signal amplification [5]. Hence,
the natural origin of nonlinear light interaction comes with enhancing and limiting
factors to reach new heights in data throughput and bandwidth.
This thesis will investigate the nonlinear effects and mainly focus on wavelength
conversion, where Four Wave Mixing is the essential process to research. In addi-
tion, nonlinear scattering interactions will be of interest since they greatly affect the
light behavior during propagation through the fiber. Raman scattering will be en-
abled as an amplification method to visualize such pump lasers’ impact, and Brillouin
scattering will limit the maximum power achievable for the Four Wave Mixing pump.
1.1
Thesis structure
After introducing this thesis, Chapter 2 describes the many aspects of nonlinear light
interactions and from where they originate. This includes a general description of the
optical Kerr effects related to the nonlinear refractive index and the inelastic scatter-
ing processes. Since Four Wave Mixing is the key light interaction to investigate for
wavelength converters, this will be further detailed together with Stimulated Raman
Scattering and Stimulated Brillouin Scattering.
Chapter 3 is presented with a motivation for the project’s experimental part. This
leads to descriptions of the optical equipment used for laboratory setups and how data
is collected through instrument control toolboxes in Matlab. Results gathered from
these experiments are analyzed at the end of Chapter 3.
Chapter 4 compares the theoretical expectations explained in Chapter 2 with
graphs and numerical calculations seen in Chapter 3. This chapter also considers
further investigations if the project’s duration was extended.
Finally, Chapter 5 concludes on the entire project and the results obtained. This
is followed by appendices with content important for the project.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
To conduct results gathered from an experimental setup, it is necessary to understand
the physical origins and processes occurring along the transmission line. As the the-
sis title indicates, nonlinear optical signal processing will describe the application of
wavelength conversion. This chapter focuses on the relevant theoretical background
to give some expectations and considerations when exploring numerical and experi-
mental investigations in Chapter 3. To increase the nonlinear effects through the fiber,
the Fiber Under Test (FUT) is a Highly Nonlinear Fiber (HNLF). Hence, several
nonlinear phenomena must be described, where some of them will get more attention
than others.
First, the different types of nonlinearities will be outlined in Section 2.1, after
which Section 2.2, 2.3, and 2.4 will present Four Wave Mixing (FWM), Stimulated
Raman Scattering (SRS), and Stimulated Brillouin Scattering (SBS), respectively.
These specific effects will have a great impact on the experimental analysis given in
Chapter 3.
2.1
Fundamentals of Nonlinear Optics
In general, when explaining nonlinear optical effects, it is related to the light in-
teraction with itself or with other materials.
It is possible to describe the foun-
dation of electromagnetic field propagation through matter by deriving Maxwell’s
equations. However, since several textbooks already did this, the following chapter
focuses on the third-order nonlinear effects that make the refractive index intensity-
dependent [2], [5], [6]. For each third-order nonlinear effect, the induced polarization
of the electromagnetic field depends on the third-order material response function in
the frequency domain, i.e., the χ(3) susceptibility tensor. This will be further reviewed
when analyzing the FWM process in Subsection 2.2.1.
In Figure 2.1, some relevant third-order nonlinear effects in optical fibers are
categorized concerning the intensity-dependent refractive index (Kerr effect) and the
inelastic scattering processes. As seen, three phenomena are written in bold since
these will be further investigated in Sections 2.2-2.4.
A short description of Self-
Phase Modulation (SPM) and Cross-Phase Modulation (XPM) are included in the
following subsection because of their importance in the description of FWM.

2.2
Wavelength Conversion
One of the thesis’s main goals is to describe the application of wavelength converters
and their characteristics through experimental setups. To set some expectations for
results gathered in the laboratory, the physical origin of FWM must be clarified.
2.2.1
Physical concepts of Four-Wave Mixing
Four Wave Mixing is a optical parametric process, where four different waves for
pump ωp, signal ωs, and idler ωi frequencies are interacting. The process can be
defined both as degenerate and non-degenerate. To clarify the two scenarios, the

2.3.1
Origin of Raman scattering processes
Since the experiments outlined in this thesis focuses on Raman amplified FWM pro-
cesses inside a HNLF fiber, the physical aspects of this pumping method is relevant.
Looking at the origin of the process, Raman scattering can be spontaneous or stimu-
lated. Considering a solid-state quantum mechanical point of view, optical photons
are inelastically scattered by molecular vibrations called phonons. In this process,
photon energy (Ep = hνp) is either lost or gained, which shifts the frequency of light.
If the scattered light shifts to a lower frequency, the components are called Stokes lines
(Es = hνs). Vice versa, the light shifted to higher frequencies are called anti-Stokes
lines (Eas = hνas) [11]. The frequency shift occurring in these processes is equal to

As described above, Raman scattering can also appear stimulated by the signal

pump photon is excited from ground level to an upper energy state when considering
quantum mechanics.
The pump photon is converted into a second signal photon
identical to the first one, excited from the higher energy level to the ground state.
The remaining energy from the nonresonant process produces an optical phonon,
which implies that the initial signal photon has been amplified.
2.3.2
Raman impact in optical communication systems
When designing Raman amplifiers, several things should be under consideration due
to the gain obtained from the SRS process. First of all, the Raman gain spectrum
depends on the separation of signal and pump frequencies. The energy conservation
requires that the frequency separation must be equal to the generated phonon fre-
quency. Secondly, the gain does not depend on the direction of propagation for pump
and signal. Since the phonon diagram branch is quite flat, momentum conservation is
ensured regardless of the relative direction selected for the pump and signal. Further-
more, due to SRS, the long upper-state lifetimes buffering pump fluctuations are not
present as for EDFAs. With this in mind, the signal light has the possibility to prop-
agate through these pump fluctuations if the Raman pump is counter-propagating.
Finally, the gain from SRS is polarization-dependent as the coupling becomes stronger
when signal and pump waves are co-polarized [5]. Figure 2.6 shows an optical system,

2.4
Stimulated Brillouin Scattering
When applying a high power pump source to an optical transmission system, nonlin-
earities automatically limit the maximum obtainable output power. In general, when
discussing limitations in bandwidth and capacity for telecommunication networks, the
Nonlinear Shannon limit is often used to describe the boundaries met [12]. In this
project, SBS is within this limitation and plays a significant role in the experimental
results since very high EDFA and Raman powers are investigated. Hence, a short in-
troduction to SBS will be given, followed by how limitations are seen in transmission
systems.
2.4.1
Physical origin
As for SRS, Brillouin scattering is an interaction between the incident electromagnetic
fields and sound waves/phonons. However, the phonons are characterized as acoustic
instead of optical [13]. To describe how the phenomena occurs along the fiber core,
Figure 2.7 shows a very general perspective of an input pump wave (blue) in the
z-direction that interacts with a backward propagating Stokes wave (green).
The
interaction between these creates an interference pattern defined as a beat signal (red)
traveling in the positive z-direction like the input pump. When the incident pump
power is increased to a certain value, the Brillouin scattering becomes stimulated
instead of spontaneous, i.e. population inversion is realized since more atoms are in
the final state than in the ground state (see Figure 2.5). A phonon/acoustic wave is
generated and propagates through the fiber due to electrostriction. This wave act as
a moving grating, which causes diffraction of the input pump, and thus, some of the
transmitted light will be reflected and transfer the energy to the counter-propagating
Stokes wave.

passing the sensitive pre-amplifier. The pump wave is directly transmitted through
an Erbium Doped Fiber Amplifier (EDFA), which amplifies the light drastically to
improve the optical parametric process in the HNLF. Since the pump wavelength
is fixed, a Band-pass Filter (BPF) is added after the EDFA to reduce noise around
the pump peak. By applying a 90/10 coupler, the signal and pump light are mixed,
where only the 90% power output is observed. The combined lightwave is transmitted
through 498 m HNLF generating the idler, after which it gets attenuated by a 20 dB
coupler before reaching the ANDO Optical Spectrum Analyzer (OSA). This coupler
act as a 20 dB attenuator protecting the OSA from being damaged by the high-
intensity light.

3.1.2
Implementing co-propagating Raman amplifier
Modifying the setup illustrated in Figure 3.1, a Raman pump is now applied to the
system. This experiment focuses on the impact of co-propagating Raman amplifica-
tion through the fiber together with the coupled signal-pump lightwave. Different
from the bandwidth measurement, the signal wavelength is now fixed to λs = 1551
nm inspired by similar academic research [18].
Another BPF has been added to
optimize the signal peak power, and hence also the idler peak power. As seen in Fig-
ure 3.2, the combined signal and pump light is sent through a WDM coupler, where
the light is co-propagating with the other input source: the Raman pump operating
at λR ≈1452 nm. To minimize power losses along the transmission line, a specific
WDM coupler was selected, where one input fits with wavelengths in the 1540 nm -
1560 nm area and the other input guides light most eﬀiciently around 1430 nm - 1460
nm. The idler is generated through the HNLF-SPINE fiber, after which the Raman
assisted FWM light is attenuated by 20 dB to protect the OSA. Since this was the
first established setup, extra 10 dB attenuation was implemented for safety reasons.
In regard to data acquisition, the measurements generated in this part of the
experiment required varying the EDFA power applied to the pump line. The am-
plifier’s output power was manually adjusted in the interval from 24.0 dBm - 32.0
dBm, with 0.1 dBm spacing for each measurement point resulting in 81 data points.
Measurements were collected both with and without the Raman pump to visualize
the amplification impact. Hence, this design of the experiments allows for comparing
the gathered results in a final analysis.
3.1.3
Implementing counter-propagating Raman amplifier
The setup for a counter-propagating Raman amplifier is very similar to the one seen
in Figure 3.2. From the two light sources and up until the 90/10 coupler, nothing

Raman pump is done after the HNLF as illustrated in Figure 3.3. Thus, a back-
scattering process in the fiber is emphasized. Instead of using a WDM coupler like
previously, a circulator was appropriate for the system design. The light from the
Raman pump at entrance 1 only transmits back through the HNLF, while the Raman
scattered FWM light at entrance 2 only passes to the OSA line at 3. This protects

From a telecommunication perspective, the limitations SBS brings to high power
optics are challenging to suppress. Considering the wavelength converter investigated
in this thesis, if the generated idler frequency was to be used for data transmission, the
maximum obtainable OSNR and CE would be crucial. Especially, the signal-to-noise
ratio is important for several applications, since it directly relates to the transmission
performance through the Bit Error Rate (BER) and the Q-factor [22]. The main
purpose of these terms is to determine how many transmitted bits are received without
any errors. If the BER equals to 10−9, then only 1 bit error occurs for every billion
bits transmitted. The related Q-factor is often extracted from oscilloscopes and is
determined by the intensity between modulated bits divided by the difference in
variance in the two intensity levels [23]. Hence, the bigger difference between idler
peak power and ASE noise level, the smaller probability of bit errors occurring in the
receiver.
With this in mind, the need for suppressing SBS in optical communication systems
are highly demanded. There are several ways to manipulate Brillouin scattering as
the power threshold in Eq. 2.22 includes several parameters. Obviously, increasing
the physical length of the transmission medium results in a decrease of SBS threshold,
which is also pronounced in Figure 4.6 taken from [13].
Furthermore, the threshold can be controlled by the design of fiber index profile
and hence the effective modal area Aeff. In the case of standard step-index fibers, an
increase in effective area will lead to a higher SBS threshold. However, this is a bit
different for other types of index profiles due to significant variations of acousto-optic
areas Aao guiding the acoustic phonon generated by SBS. In step index fibers, the
Aao can be approximated to be equal to Aeff, but since the HNLF-SPINE fiber used
in the experiments have a different index profile, the effective optical area should be
replaced with the acousto-optic area for variations in SBS threshold [24] [13].
Changing the material of the waveguide can also lead to changes in SBS. Instead
of doping the silica core with GeO2, it has been suggested to use aluminium oxide

4.2.2

numerical model describing the light propagating in both linear and nonlinear regime.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This thesis has researched the possibilities of utilizing two laser sources to generate a
new frequency when propagating light through a nonlinear medium. The frequency
added to the spectrum was denoted as the idler signal and rose from an optical
parametric process occurring in third-order nonlinear effect called Four Wave Mix-
ing (FWM). The thesis primarily focused on the degenerate case, where only three
frequencies, signal, pump, and idler, were interacting. Covering theoretical phenom-
ena on several nonlinear effects related to the Kerr refractive index and inelastic
scattering processes, considerations on how to demonstrate a wavelength converter
through FWM was established. Furthermore, expectations on how much on-off gain
could be experienced, if Stimulated Raman Scattering (SRS) was assisting the three
frequencies along the Fiber Under Test (FUT).
Before instantiating the experimental work, setups were designed to build an
optical parametric amplifier using a Highly Nonlinear Fiber (HNLF)-Stable Phase-
matching for Improved Nonlinear Eﬀiciency (SPINE), measuring the bandwidth of
the fiber, and applying a Raman pump for both forward and backward direction rel-
ative to the FWM light. To automate the process of retrieving data from an Optical
Spectrum Analyzer (OSA), several Matlab scripts have been developed to save the
spectra and process the data to CSV files. Additional scripts were created for nonlin-
ear post-processing generating the figures seen in the thesis.
From an experimental analysis of the three frequency interaction process, a band-
width measurement of the FUT demonstrated the gain in terms of Conversion Eﬀi-
ciency (CE), where the obtained pattern around the zero-dispersion wavelength was
in good agreement with other scientific papers. Keeping the pump at 1545.4 nm and
tuning the signal wavelength away on both sides of the pump resulted in an expected
form characterized by two humps, where the signal wavelength should be placed for
optimal wavelength conversion. However, the full bandwidth wasn’t visible in the
plots and hence, another measurement set with a larger wavelength span would be
appropriate for future work.
In the last two experimental setups, an Erbium Doped Fiber Amplifier (EDFA)
connected to the pump wavelength was manually tuned from 24 dBm to 32 dBm.
Applying the Raman pump as a co-propagating light source together with the FWM
pump, measurements showed a positive on-off gain for lower input EDFA powers. But
due to the inelastic scattering process Stimulated Brillouin Scattering (SBS), satura-
tion of the measured output power was detected for EDFA power above 27 dBm. The

5 Conclusion
Optical Signal-to-Noise Ratio (OSNR) and CE for signal and idler together with the
total measured output power, determined the overall performance of the wavelength
converter. The evolution of these entities were in good agreement with expectations
for the forward Raman setup. However, when modifying the Raman pump to prop-
agate in the opposite direction, the results showed different behaviours, where the
on-off gain was expressed beyond the SBS threshold. The general conclusions drawn
from the experimental data was that more measurement sets should be conducted in
order to accept any hypothesis concerning the counter-propagating Raman amplifica-
tion.
In the discussion, a comparison between experimental extracted data and analyt-
ical expressions were conducted. The theoretical on-off Raman gain was evaluated
to be 1.48 dB, while the experimentally measured was 0.43 dBm. The conclusion to
this significant difference was that the power contribution from the Raman amplifier
might be depleted by the high power FWM light. Since the theoretical equation only
assumed the small-signal limit, the distant values were suﬀiciently viable. Further-
more, the actual SBS threshold measured in the laboratory was 23.5 dBm due to
attenuation between EDFA and fiber input. Using the FUT product specification,
an analytical SBS threshold was computed to be 18.2 dBm. Initially, it seemed very
promising with such a high threshold compared to the manufacturer itself, but after
further investigations, the variance made good sense. The fiber can be fabricated in
different lengths from 50 m to 2000 m, which they didn’t account for in the calcula-
tions of the effective length.
Finally, the thesis reviews future investigations if more resources were allowed.
First of all, some modifications in the setup should be made before redoing the mea-
surement sets. Further automation of the experimental procedure related to remote
controlling the EDFA from the computer, would also be prioritized to streamline the
measurement procedures. Another interesting approach is to test different types of
fibers with similar nonlinear parameters. For this, a comparison in every entity re-
viewed in the thesis could be interpreted. At last, the possibility to model the FWM
process numerically using the Split Step Fourier Method (SSFM) was described. An
example solving the propagation equation for a soliton system was demonstrated.

APPENDIX B
Experimental
Procedure
To understand the procedure followed to obtain the experimental measurements, this
appendix gives an overview of the most important steps to perform before starting.
Common for all three experiments are the following points, which optimizes the total
measured output on the OSA.
1. Make sure every optical equipment is placed in the order depicted in either
Figure 3.1, 3.2, or 3.3.
2. Inspect every fiber-end with a fiber scope, and clean them carefully if necessary.
3. Before launching light into the pre-amplifier, check the output from the Variable
Optical Attenuator (VOA) using a power meter.
Set the output below the
intensity tolerance of the pre-amplifier.
4. Turn on one laser at a time, and note the attenuation through each optical
equipment.
If unacceptable losses are met, the equipment should either be
repaired or replaced.
5. Adjust the Band-pass Filter (BPF)s to suppress noise around the peak frequen-
cies.
By inspecting the FWM spectrum on the OSA while tuning the filter
wavelengths, this process is straight-forward.
6. Finally, the two CoBrite lasers emitting the initial light are optimized in state
of polarization by using the Polarization Controller (PC)s. A smart technique
was utilized by setting the OSA spectrum to observe the idler peak at a high
resolution. Since the idler frequency is generated by combining the signal and
the pump, optimizing the two polarization controllers around this wavelength
were favorable.
With these steps completed, the results illustrated in this thesis can be retrieved
by using the Matlab functions described in Appendix C.
