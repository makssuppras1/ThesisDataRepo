Summary 
Optimization of well production can be achieved through a successful completion, which in turn, is 
determined by the ability of the well cementing to provide a successful zonal isolation. However, 
despite the technological advances of the cementing industry, numerous wells are currently at risk due 
to an unsuccessful zonal isolation. In addition to the traditional methods, several newly developed 
technologies have been introduced to address the issue of the annular barrier loss. Also, thanks to its 
deformation and extremely low permeability, shale has already been used as a permanent plug and 
abandonment barrier. In this project, an experimental study was realised to evaluate whether the 
swelling properties of formations like shale can be used to render them an efficient annulus filling 
material, able to provide complete zonal isolation during well production.  
To test this hypothesis, swell pressure and free swell tests on 10 Farsund formation shale specimens 
from MAERSK’s appraisal well Jude-1, in the southwestern Danish North Sea, were conducted with 
the use of three different pore fluids, namely: deionized water (DW), brine simulating the North Sea in 
situ conditions (NSS) and a highly saline fluid, with 72g/L NaCl (72NaCl). Additionally, considering 
that clay swelling is highly affected by the material’s consistency and mineralogy, Atterberg limits, 
mineral density, salinity, BET, and XRD measurements were also realised. 
More specifically, the specific surface of the test material was found to range between 35.97 m2/g and 
37.29 m2/g, the liquid limit was estimated to be 45.0 %, whilst the plasticity index was found to be 
22.2 %. In addition, the mineral density measured was 2.667 g/cm3. Combining these results with the 
available geological information on the sampling area, it was concluded that the test material was a 
mixed clay consisting primarily of Illite. During the Swell Pressure tests, a slight swell was observed, 
even though the load frame was set to a current position. To eliminate this effect and return the 
specimens to their initial height, an adjustment to the loading frame position was made. Therefore, two 
different swell pressures were recorded. For both pressures, the highest value was exhibited when the 
72g/L NaCl brine was used, with the swell pressure prior adjustment reaching the 9.9 MPa and 14.0 
MPa after the adjustment. A similar effect was observed for the swell potential measurements, 
reaching 3.9 % when the 72g/L NaCl brine was used. 
 
Throughout this study, a simple, effective and repeatable test procedure for future research was 
established. Overall, this study shows that swelling formations can be a promising solution for annulus 
filling around the casing as well as for filling cracks and cavings of the cement sheath. Nevertheless, 
considering the sensitivity of the swell potential to both the physical parameters as well as the pore 
fluid composition of the formation, more study is required on soils above the reservoir to establish the 
proper fluid that will activate and maximise the swelling effect.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background   
Until recently swelling clays and shale formations were known in the Oil and Gas Industry only for 
the tremendous problems they have been causing during the exploration and production of 
hydrocarbons (Anderson et al., 2010; Wilson and Wilson, 2014; Zhou, 1996; Krueger, 1986). More 
specifically, as stated in Anderson et al. (2010) their presence is often followed by stuck pipe, tight 
hole and cavings to the formation, which result in high operational costs and huge financial losses in 
the oilfield industry. To control their negative effects, several chemical techniques have been 
developed that inhibit their excessive swelling (Anderson et al., 2010; Mohamed, 2013; Cheshomi et 
al., 2017).  
However, the last ten years the Petroleum Industry exhibits an increasing interest towards the positive 
effects stemming from the presence of swelling clayey formations. More specifically, thanks to their 
accessibility and particularly low permeability, which ensures complete zonal isolation, the clayey 
shale formations are now being used as effective barriers for the Permanent Plug and Abandonment of 
wells (Williams et al., 2009; McDaniel, 1980; Torsæter et al., 2014; Kristiansen, 2015). Moreover, as 
noted by Nelson and Guillot (2006), even though cementing is considered necessary for a successful 
zonal isolation, it is a costly procedure that requires delicate handling to form an efficient cement 
sheath. In addition, a low quality cementing operation, including a bad cement mixture or poor 
placement often leads to the formation of cavings or cracks, which require expensive remedial 
operations and can result in minor environmental issues or huge catastrophes (e.g. Macondo oil spill) 
if left untreated.  
1.2 Motivation 
Recently, the scientific community working in collaboration with the oil industry is considering ways 
to use shales with high swell potential, and particularly those containing clay minerals, such as 
Montmorillonite, Bentonite, and mixed-layer Illites, as an asset rather than a hazard during 
engineering operations. In addition, shales are a natural, low-cost solution that could provide both an 
environmentally friendly barrier and a long-term solution. Hence, the motivation of this project is to 
investigate whether, in addition to their use as a permanent abandonment barrier, shales found in the 
drilling section of a well, could also serve as an efficient annulus filling material, able to provide 
sufficient zonal isolation in hydrocarbon operations. 
1.3 Aim & Objectives 
The aim of this project is to study the swelling properties of the Farsund shale formation from North 
Sea, with respect to the pore water salinity. For this purpose, swelling pressure and free swell tests 
were conducted using an oedometer apparatus and three different pore fluids, namely: deionized water

2. Problem Formulation 
This chapter provides some general information on the cementing operations taking place prior the 
completion of an oil well and presents the available remedial techniques used to repair the cement 
sheath. Also, it introduces shale as an annular barrier and presents previous studies on this topic.   
2.1 Well Cementing  
In 2006, Nelson and Guillot published a book that contains the state of the art techniques used during 
well cementing. In this book it is stated that the first step to the optimization of well production is a 
successful completion, which in turn, is determined by the ability of the well cementing to provide a 
successful zonal isolation. As seen in Figure 2. 1 during cementing the cement slurry is pumped to the 
annular space between the formation and casing or between two consecutive casing strings through the 
drill string.  
 
Figure 2. 1. Schematic illustration of well cementing (Drilling Course, 2015).  
By principle, well cementing is divided in primary and remedial cementing. According to Nelson and 
Guillot (2006) the term ''primary cementing'', as illustrated in Figure 2. 2, is used to describe the 
process of placing cement in the area between the casing and the formations, which is called annulus. 
The main objective of primary cementing is to provide zonal isolation in the well and prevent any 
fluid movement from one well zone to another. This is achieved by creating a hydraulic seal between 
a) the casing and the cement and b) the cement and the formation, while preventing the formation of 
fluid channels in the cement body (Figure 2. 3). Other functions of the cement sheath include the

Newman et al. (2001) note that the failure of a primary cementing can result in huge economic losses. 
Characteristically, they report that approximately 15% of the primary cement applications fail, which 
results in huge losses for the Petroleum Industry reaching 470 M. USD per year. They also argue that 
close to one-third of these problems can be attributed to fluid migration during the placement and 
curing of the cement.  
Regarding the remedial cementing, Nelson and Guillot (2006) note that it is performed: a) to repair 
faulty primary cementings, b) close casing holes, c) alter the characteristics of the formation, and d) 
achieve permanent well abandonment. It has two types of application: the squeeze cementing and the 
plug cementing. During squeeze cementing the cement slurry is placed into the wellbore with 
hydraulic pressure which contributes to leaving a competent cement, able to seal all voids. When plug 
cementing is applied, a limited volume of cement is placed at a specified location in the wellbore to 
establish a solid seal or plug. 
According to Nelson and Guillot (2006) in 1999 3,810 wells with casing vent flows, and 814 wells 
with gas migration issues were recorded in Alberta (Canada). They also point out that up until 
recently, it was considered that the efficiency of the cement sheath was determined only by its 
compressive strength. However, today it is recognized that the compressive strength alone cannot be 
used to prove whether a cement can successfully provide zonal isolation throughout the well life and 
following the permanent well abandonment. The quality of the cement sheath is determined by its 
ability to withstand several failure mechanisms, which are presented in more detail in Section 2.2. 
2.2 Loss of Zonal Isolation  
Despite the technological advances of the cementing industry, numerous wells are currently at risk due 
to zonal isolation implications. Nelson and Guillot (2006) state that an unsuccessful zonal isolation 
can result in over-pressured casing strings, while production fluids and gas may migrate and 
contaminate other subsurface zones, such as aquifers of fresh water causing environmental and 
operational hazards. Also, the production stream of the well can be interrupted, reduced, or cease all 
together due to the loss of lift efficiency, inefficient stimulation, the excessive production of water or 
gas, and the potential loss of the well in case of casing collapse. Hence, the operator should closely 
monitor the quality of the cement sheath and bond to ensure a safe and successful well production.  
However, there are various mechanisms that can affect the integrity of the cement sheath throughout 
the well life. Cracking is the most common mechanism and as stated by Nelson and Guillot (2006) it 
can be caused by thermal or pressure changes in the well (see Figure 2. 4 e). The second most 
common mechanism is debonding which takes place when the interface bond either between the 
cement and rock or between the pipe and cement fails. Figure 2. 4 a, shows how the poor bonding 
between casing and cement can lead to leak paths (microannulus). Figure 2. 4 b shows the leaks due to 
poor bonding between the cement plug and casing, while Figure 2. 4 f illustrates the leaks developed 
as a result of the poor bonding between the cement and formation. Debonding can be caused by the 
gradual pressure decrease during the well production, the cement shrinkage over time, the casing 
movement as subsidence occurs, temperature and pressure variations, and stimulation practices. The

2.3 Remedial Operations for Annular Barriers 
As noted by Nelson and Guillot (2006) remedial operations are used to a) repair defective primary 
cementing, b) allow further drilling operations, c) provide adequate zonal isolation for a smooth well 
production, d) seal annular leaks before the well abandonment, and e) ensure permanent isolation in 
the case of permanent well abandonment.  
There are numerous solutions available to ensure an effective well barrier. It is widely recognised that 
remedial cementing is one of the most traditional solutions to mitigate the lack of annular seal. 
During this process, cement is used to seal off faulty areas, as described in Section 2.1. Nevertheless, 
since this method has a low possibility of success, the most widely applied method is the section 
milling, which according to Skjerve (2013) is usually applied in 4 steps: a) section milling at the 
desired interval b) cleaning of the created hole, c) underream, and d) placing of balanced plug.

Another solution often used to achieve adequate zonal isolation is the cut and pull method. Based on 
Sandven (2010) this method is used in cases where there is no cement in the annulus. During this 
procedure, a free point (not cemented) is spotted, usually by performing a stretch test, and then the 
casing above it is cut and pulled out of the hole. However, in cases where the casing needs to be 
removed in more than one step, the procedure can be very time-consuming.  
In addition to the traditional methods just described, several newly developed technologies have been 
introduced in order to address the issue of the annular barrier loss. More specifically, according to 
Scanlon et al. (2011) the performance of section milling is significantly improved by using a 
combination of state-of-the-art cutters with a downhole optimization sub (tool that gathers 
downhole data). As stated in their study, the long-lasting cutters and the real-time data used for 
operational improvements, greatly reduced the rig time (reducing the average performance from 65 
days to 46 days for each well). Skjerve (2013), notes that another efficient solution is the new 
Hydrawash method developed by Hydrawell Intervention, which is designed based on the newly 
introduced Perforate, Wash and Cement (PWC) system that is acknowledged by NORSOK as an 
alternative for creating a permanent well barrier. The biggest asset of this system is that it can be used 
to plug wells that do not have sufficient annular barriers without having to remove the casing. 
Furthermore, considering that many of the isolation issues are related to the nature of the cement, 
which is used as sealing material, lately, new materials are being studied to serve as alternatives. The 
numerous revolutionary cement systems (self healing-, elastic-, foam- cements, etc) and entirely new 
sealing materials (geopolymers, compressed Sodium Bentonite) developed have opened a new path for 
the quest to a robust and at the same time cost-effective permanent barrier. 
Although there are several existing remedial technologies available, future promising systems are also 
considered. According to Skjerve (2013), two of them are the Hydrawash run on coiled tubing, that 
is designed to operate in very small vessels, and the SwarfPak by Westgroup, which is an innovative 
milling tool aiming to reduce the environmental footprint and rig time. 
2.4 Shale as an Annular Barrier 
2.4.1 
Background Information 
In the previous sections, the importance of a successful zonal isolation was emphasized. New 
materials as well as traditional and new technologies were presented to address potential implications. 
In addition to these techniques, recently, a new approach is being studied to mitigate the issues caused 
by an unsuccessful cement operation or a failed cement sheath. As stated by Williams et al. (2009) it 
has long been acknowledged that during and after the drilling operations certain formations tend to 
move inward and close off the well. Even though until recently, this mechanism was thought to be 
undesirable due to the problems it caused during drilling and casing running, Williams et al. (2009) 
note that it could also be used for the formation of an annular barrier around the casing. 
Characteristically, based on their concluding remarks, the deformation of a shale can secure a 
sufficient contact between the casing and the formation, while it also aligns with the NORSOK 
standard plug and abandonment (P&A) requirements.

Based on the concluding remarks of McDaniel (1980), Torsæter et al. (2014), and Kristiansen (2015), 
an additional advantage of shale is that it exhibits a significantly lower permeability than cement. 
Also, according to Kristiansen (2015), it may exhibit better self-healing properties than cement. More 
specifically, during the drilling process, small cracks tend to develop around the borehole, forming a 
cracked zone that is being referred to as the excavation damage zone (EDZ), while after its setting, 
cement may also develop cracks due to thermal and pressure loads. However, self-healing is much 
more likely for a ductile, weak, and soft material with a lower Young’s Modulus, such as shale. To 
support this argument, Kristiansen (2015), pointed out that self-healing of cracks has been observed in 
host rocks of nuclear waste storage at a depth of a few hundred meters. Simultaneously, taking into 
account that cement tends to deteriorate over time shale seems to constitute a promising alternative. 
2.4.2 
Barrier Philosophy according to the Norwegian Standards 
Even though the activities in the Petroleum Industry are usually planned according to international 
standards, the Norwegian climate conditions and safety framework dictate certain amendments to 
these standards. To accommodate these needs, the Norwegian Petroleum Industry developed the 
NORSOK standards, which presents the requirements for well integrity throughout the drilling and 
well operations.  
As stated in Chapter 9 of NORSOK D-010 (2013) a formation may be considered a permanent well 
barrier in case it a) includes all annuli, b) provides a uniform seal all around the annulus (vertically 
and horizontally as seen in Figure 2. 5) and c) extend across the entire well cross section which 
according to Williams et al. (2009) can be verified by wireline ultrasonic logging. The NORSOK D-
010 (2013) standard also refers to the necessary properties of a permanent well barrier. Specifically, 
the barrier shall: 
• 
Be impermeable 
• 
Provide long term integrity 
• 
Be non-shrinking 
• 
Be ductile (non-brittle) so as to withstand mechanical loads or impact 
• 
Have resistance to different chemicals and substances, such as H2S, CO2, and hydrocarbons 
• 
Provide bonding to steel 
• 
Be harmless to the steel tubulars so as to ensure integrity

3. Theoretical Background

study such as information on the clay mineralogy, on the factors that affect the swell potential of a

3.1 Clay Mineralogy  
The properties and behaviour of clayey soils are primarily affected by clay’s mineralogical structure. 
In more detail, a clay platelet is formed by several unit layers that are stacked together. Karpiński and 
Szkodo (2015) note that each unit layer contains 2 or 3 parallel sheets which consist of different types 
of components. There are two basic components in the clay mineral: 1) the silica tetrahedron (Si4O10)4- 
and 2) the gibbsite (Al2(OH)6) or brucite (Mg3(OH)6) octahedron. As seen in Figure 3. 1 a, the silica 
tetrahedron, which is the smallest structural unit of the clay mineral, usually consists of four oxygens 
and one silicon ion, while in some cases the silicon ion can be replaced by an aluminum or magnesium 
ion (isomorphous substitution). Figure 3. 1 b presents a silica sheet unit cell, while Figure 3. 1 c shows 
a silica sheet that formed from several silica sheet unit cells put together. 
 
Figure 3. 1. (a) Silica (Si4O10)4- ) tetrahedron, (b) silica sheet unit cell and (c) silica sheet formed from 
several silica sheet unit cells put together. 
The main clay mineral groups are: 
• 
Smectite group (i.e. Montmorillonite and Nontronite)  
• 
Illite group  
• 
Chlorite group 
• 
Kaolin group (i.e. Kaolinite, Dickite, Halloysite, and Nacrite) 
• 
Mixed layer clay variations  
The most frequently encountered clay minerals are Kaolinite, Illite, and Montmorillonite. As stated by 
Karpiński and Szkodo (2015) Kaolinite is a 1:1 mineral, meaning that its layers are comprised by one 
tetrahedral and one octahedral sheet, while Illite, and Montmorillonite, are 2:1 minerals with layers 
consisting of two octahedral sheets on top and bottom and one tetrahedral sheet in the middle.  
As previously described, the present study investigates the water-induced swelling of shale. Based on 
Foster (1955), swelling is dominated by clay hydration which in turn differentiates based on the

Sheets, and layers, are connected through different types of bonds that according to Mitchell and Soga

•

The inter-sheet bonds are primary, which in principle are very difficult to break, while the inter-layer 
bonds are secondary, meaning that they can break more easily. When the interlayer bonding is strong, 
more layers are kept together, and thus larger particles are formed with a lower specific surface area 
(m2/g). Therefore, as stated in Foster (1955), kaolinitic clays are not highly prone to swelling upon 
hydration, as opposed to sodium Montmorillonite clays that swell extensively, while calcium and 
magnesium Montmorillonites and the Illites (or Hydrous Micas) are considered to have an 
intermediate swell potential. 
3.2 Interaction of Clay and Water 
The mechanisms that predominately affect the interaction between the clay and water according to 
Mitchell and Soga (2005) are:  
➔ Osmosis and osmotic pressure. As shown in Figure 3. 2 a, negatively charged surfaces have a 
higher concentration of cations when they are being approached. Therefore, water molecules 
tend to diffuse on the surface to make the concentrations equal. 
➔ Hydrogen (dipole) attraction. As shown in Figure 3. 2 b the positive poles of the water 
dipoles direct toward the negative surfaces, while the orientation degree decreases with the 
distance from the surface. 
➔ Hydrogen bonding. As shown in Figure 3. 2 c, the surfaces of soil minerals are comprised by 
a layer of oxygens or hydroxyls. Hydrogen bonds are formed due to the oxygens that attract the 
positively charged parts of the water molecules, and the hydrogens that attract the negative 
ones. 
➔ Ion hydration (in the case of isomorphous substitution). As shown in Figure 3. 2 d the 
hydration water is attracted to clay surfaces that are negatively charged.

3.3 Factors that govern the soil swelling behaviour  
Sivapullaiah et al. (1996) note that there are three distinct swelling phases for a soil; the intervoid, the 
primary and the secondary swelling. In more detail, the Intervoid Swelling happens in the voids that 
are formed by coarse non-swelling particles and is taking place due to the swelling of fine clay 
particles, while it does not cause any volume change. Next, during the Primary Swelling a significant 
increase in swelling is observed that constitutes about 80% of the total swelling.  Finally, follows the 
Secondary Swelling, which is a slow process that exhibits a linear relationship with the logarithm of 
time. 
As previously mentioned smectitic clays have a significantly higher swell potential than Illite and 
Kaolinite. Mitchell and Soga (2015), state that this is a direct result of their extremely high specific 
surface and weak interlayer bonds as well as its intense isomorphous substitutions that make it more 
prone to interaction with water. Hence, the amount of Smectite in a soil specimen affects significantly 
its total swell potential. However, according to Sivapullaiah et al. (1996) the size and amount of non-
swelling fractions can also affect the swell potential, given that in the case of a constant percentage of 
swelling clay the overall swell diminishes when the amount and size of the non-swelling particles 
increases. Characteristically, they stated that soils containing small sized non-swelling particles exhibit 
instant primary swelling, while soils with a significant amount of large-sized non-swelling particles, 
exhibit primary swelling in later stages of the swelling process. In addition to the aforementioned 
factors, various other parameters such as the sample disturbance and natural (or initial) water content, 
aging and loading history, pH, salinity, and cementation must also be taken into account when 
studying the swell potential of a soil.  
3.3.1 
 Sample disturbance and natural water content 
Based on Erguler and Ulusay (2003) undisturbed samples have lower swell potentials. AlZubaidi et al. 
(2016) made the same observation, stating that remolded samples were accompanied by higher swell 
potentials. Also, Erguler and Ulusay (2003) as well as AlZubaidi et al. (2016) mention that the higher 
the natural water content, the lower is the swelling and the swelling pressure of a soil specimen. This 
is only logical since there is only so much water a soil can absorb during swelling. Hence, from the 
above, it can be concluded that the swell potential is also related to the initial dry (or alternatively 
bulk) density. According to AlZubaidi et al. (2016) the greater the initial dry density the greater is the 
swell potential and the swelling pressure, as shown in Figure 3. 4 a and Figure 3. 4 b respectively. 
Consequently, a high degree of sample disturbance (i.e. alteration of the natural water content, dry 
density, and particle structure) can lead to an error during the estimation of the swell potential. Based 
on Yilmaz and Marschalko (2014) even though acquiring undisturbed samples is not always possible 
they are preferred, given that they lead to more representative results.

similar hysteresis phenomenon in the swell curves that is eradicated after a few cycles.

3.3.4 
The effect of calcite and cementation 
Calcite is often found in clays and can affect significantly the overall behaviour of the soil. Mitchell & 
Soga (2005) mention that calcite is a carbonate mineral that spawns from the shells of aquatic 
organisms and it can be found in the form of shells, bulky particles, precipitates, or in solution. In 
cases of high compaction calcite and quartz dissolved in water tend to re-crystallize and act as glue for 
the sediment particles. This process is called natural cementation and occurs when the amount of water 
remaining after the compaction is not sufficient to dissolve the minerals, which leads to their 
precipitation. Interparticle cementation also takes place with the addition of stabilizers like lime and 
cement to the soil. Cementation tends to counteract the soil tendency to swelling. For this reason, 
intact soils that might be cemented, tend to swell less. 
3.4 Clay Testing  
Swelling can be estimated by two test types, namely swell pressure and free swell test, which are used 
to estimate the swelling potential of a soil. 
3.4.1 
Purpose and Principle of Swell Pressure test  
The Swell pressure test is a standardised experimental procedure described among others in ASTM 
D4546 (2014). There, the term swell pressure refers to the minimum vertical stress needed to prevent 
swelling and can be measured using the ASTM Test Method B or single-point wetting-after-
loading test.  
In 2001 Di Maio described the swelling pressure as ‘The pressure required to keep a soil element at 
constant volume, when boundary conditions are such as to induce a tendency to volume increase’. 
According to Di Maio’s study, swelling can be affected by numerous parameters such as the unloading 
profile and nature of liquids, while based on Mitchell and Soga (2005) the specific surface area, also 
plays a huge role in the swelling pressure measurements. Of course, if one considers the later 
statement it can be concluded that mineralogy and the size of the particles affect the swelling pressure 
as well.

3.4.2 
Purpose and Principle of Free Swell test  
The Free Swell test is a standardised experimental procedure that is used to estimate the swell 
potential of a soil. According to ASTM D4546 (2014), the one-dimensional swell deformation, which 
is induced by the wetting of intact specimens, can be measured using the Test Method B or single-
point wetting-after-loading test.  
Based on ASTM D4546 (2014), during the Test Method B, initially, a load corresponding to the in situ 
conditions is applied until equilibrium is reached. Then, test water is added and the one-dimensional 
swell under approximately zero vertical load induced by wetting is recorded. It should also be noted 
that the test method, wetting procedures, and loading must resemble the field conditions as much as 
reasonably possible since small variations of these parameters can noticeably affect the results.  
However, the standardised method presented in ASTM D4546 (2014) has certain limitations. More 
specifically, given that this is a one-dimensional test, it can only simulate the swell in one direction. 
Moreover, the laboratory inundation represents an extreme wetting case. Hence, the field swell might 
be smaller than the laboratory one. In addition, the results are significantly affected by the disturbance 
and compositional variability of the intact specimens, especially in the case of cemented soils of low 
plasticity. Also, as stated in ASTM D4546 (2014) laboratory swell rates may differ from the field rates 
due to non-uniform properties of the soil, the presence of fissures, different content of coarse particles, 
and varied wetting. In order to acquire reliable test results, the wetting profile, the stress sequence and 
the chemical content of the testing water should be as close as possible to the field conditions.

4.2 Classification Tests 
The varied structure, composition and morphology of a clay mineral can significantly affect its 
geotechnical properties, such as swelling pressure and swell potential. Therefore, to be able to conduct 
an in-depth study and evaluation of the results acquired from the oedometer tests, first it was necessary 
to classify the testing material. To conduct this classification four different methods were applied: a)

4.2.2 
Atterberg limits 
4.2.2.1 
Purpose and Principle  
Holtz and Kovacs (1981) stated that the engineering properties of a fine-grained soil are significantly 
affected by the water which is contained in its voids. Hence, one needs to be able a) to determine the 
water content in a soil sample and b) to predict to what extent it affects the engineering behaviour of 
the soil. The Atterberg limits were developed to answer both questions. Holtz and Kovacs (1981) also 
note that the empirical Atterberg limits are measured in thoroughly remoulded soils and that they 
exhibit an extremely satisfactory correlation with the engineering behaviour of the soil. The main 
Atterberg limits are the liquid limit (LL), plastic limit (PL), and plasticity Index (PI). 
==PAGE:39==

Liquid limit 
ISO (2004) describes the liquid limit as the water content at which the soil passes to the liquid state. Its 
value can be determined using the Casagrande method or the Fall Cone method. In this study the Fall 
Cone method was used. The Fall Cone method is a fast, and easily repeatable test that does not 
exhibit significant deviations for different operators as opposed to the Casagrande method which 
highly depends on the user’s skills and experience. As described by ISO (2004) after placing the soil 
sample into the cup, its surface is made planar and subsequently, the cone is brought in contact with 
the surface of the sample (see Figure 4. 2). Then, the cone makes a free fall for 5s and the penetration 
depth is recorded. This procedure is repeated for various water contents in order to draw a semi-
logarithmic diagram of cone penetration depth versus water content. According to this method the LL 
is the water content which corresponds to 10 mm penetration. 
 
Figure 4. 2. Example of standardised fall cone equipment. 1) adjustable stand arm 2) plexiglass with 
graded scale 3) fall cone 4) specimen 5) mixing cup 6) index line (reproduction from ISO, 2004). 
 
Plastic limit 
The plastic limit of a plastic soil is the water content which is the boundary between plastic and semi-
solid state. According to ASTM D4318 (2017) the method used to determine the PL is by pressing and 
rolling a soil portion into a 3.2 mm diameter thread until it crumbles as seen in Figure 4. 3 and Figure 
4. 4. Holtz and Kovacs (1981) mention that if the soil crumbles in a bigger diameter than 3.2 mm then 
the water content is below the plastic limit, while for a smaller diameter than 3.2 mm the plastic limit 
is exceeded. The final PL value for a soil sample is found after the measurement has been repeated 
several times.

determine the volume of the particles. After this, the mass of the particles is measured, and the density 
of the solid particles, ρs is calculated by dividing the dry mass of the particles with their volume.

the average calibrated mass. To achieve this, the empty and clean pycnometer was weighed with its 
stopper (Mpe). Then, the prepared slurry was carefully poured into the pycnometer using the funnel 
until it was half full.  
 
Next, the pycnometer and the bottle with the deionized water were put in a Salvis vacuum chamber 
(see Figure 4. 14) to remove the air for one hour. After removing it from the vacuum, the pycnometer 
was fully filled with de-aired, deionized water ensuring that its outside and neck were clean of any 
liquid that was spilled during transfer. Subsequently, the stopper was inserted slowly into the 
pycnometer (stoppers and pycnometers have corresponding serial numbers on their surfaces to ensure 
perfect sealing). The slow positioning of the stopper caused water to exit from the capillary tube 
piercing lengthwise the stopper.  
 
After having ensured that the capillary tube of the stopper was full of water, the exterior surface of the 
flask was carefully cleaned (avoiding drawing water from the capillary tube) and the weight of the 
pycnometer with its stopper and contents was weighed (Mps). After weighing, the capillary stopper 
was removed and the temperature (Ts) of the slurry was measured using a thermometer. 
 
Subsequently, a glass cylindrical container (beaker) was used to pour in the slurry. After having 
measured its empty weight (Megc) all the content of the pycnometer was carefully put in the beaker 
ensuring that no soil was lost during the transfer. The cleanness of the pycnometer was ensured by 
washing away the soil with deionized water. Finally, the glass container with the slurry was put in the 
oven at 105 oC until the specimen was completely dried and then the mass of the oven dried specimen 
with the container was measured (Mfgc).  
 
The last step was to fill the clean pycnometer with de-aired and deionized water, at the same 
temperature, and weigh it with its stopper after cleaning its exterior (Mp). The temperature of the water 
was also recorded.

4.2.4 
Specific surface area testing - The BET method 
4.2.4.1 
Purpose and Principle of BET testing  
Mitchell and Soga (2005) note that Montmorillonite's ability to absorb substantial quantities of water 
is related to its extremely high specific surface area (SSA). Therefore, knowing the specific surface 
area of a soil is necessary in order to understand its engineering behaviour. According to Sridharan 
and Rao (1972) the specific surface area (m2/g) is also associated with other physical properties of 
soils, such as the Atterberg limits, the base exchange capacity, clay’s mineralogy and the soils 
structure. Additionally, they state that the SSA can provide a convenient basis for the specification of 
the soils' texture.  
Sridharan and Rao (1972) note that the most widely used equation in the adsorption field is the BET  
equation. The fundamental principle of the BET theory, according to Sridharan and Rao (1972), is that 
when a gas comes in contact with a solid at its condensation temperature, it is physically absorbed on 
the surface of the soil particles. De Boer (1970) notes that in practice, this method is usually applied 
by using nitrogen as the adsorbate. If the solid's surface is uniform, it will be covered entirely by a 
layer of gas molecules, which is called a monolayer or unimolecular layer. On 1918 Langmuir 
expressed the adsorption behaviour of a monolayer with an isotherm equation, while on 1938, 
Brunauer et al. introduced a multilayer model based on Langmuir's equation by making the following 
assumptions: 
• 
On a free surface the number of adsorbed layers can approach infinity at the saturation 
pressure of the adsorbate gas (Nitrogen) po, at the adsorption temperature. 
• 
The Capillaries of an adsorbent have a similar diameter, resulting in a uniform 
adsorption. 
• 
The heat of adsorption in the second and higher adsorbed layers equals the molar heat 
of liquefaction. 
• 
For the second and higher adsorbed layers, the adsorption tendency of a molecule does 
not depend on the number of layers that underlie its adsorption site. 
According to Sridharan and Rao (1972) the already defined SSA of a soil may be calculated by 
knowing the amount of the vapour adsorbed at various partial pressures. In order to perform the SSA 
calculation: 1) the adsorbed monolayer volume is evaluated, and 2) this quantity is converted to SSA 
using the molecular area, am. The Brunauer, Emmett and Teller (B.E.T) equation for multilayer 
adsorption, as stated in De Boer (1970) is:

4.2.5 
X-ray diffraction  
4.2.5.1 
Purpose and Principle of X-ray diffraction  
Since some minerals swell more than others as already mentioned (i.e. Montmorillonite is more prone 
to swelling than Kaolinite and Illite), and the presence of non-swelling particles can affect the swell 
potential of the soil, it is crucial to be able to identify the mineralogical composition of soil. This 
information can be extracted from X-Ray diffraction (XRD) tests. 
X-rays are electromagnetic radiations that occur between the gamma ray spectrum and the ultraviolet 
spectrum. They were discovered in 1895 and their wavelength is approximately 1 Å (10-10 m). A few 
years after their discovery scientists started to use them to study the crystalline structure at an atomic 
level. In 1912 Max von Laue introduced a theory stating that, when X-rays contact a crystal, its atoms 
act as scattering centres causing the diffraction of X-Rays as illustrated in Figure 4. 16. As shown in 
this figure, an X-ray diffractometer consists of three main elements: an X-ray source, a specimen 
holder, and an X-ray detector. The X-rays are produced by a source and accelerated (on slit) prior 
being directed towards the specimen. When X-Ray electrons of sufficient energy hit the target material 
they can dislodge its inner shell electrons and hence characteristic X-ray spectra, which consist of 
several components, are generated. In order to generate monochromatic X-rays that are needed for 
diffraction, the rays are filtered using crystal monochrometers. As the samples rotates several 
intensities are generated that finally result in a diffraction pattern.  
The diffraction pattern produced from the scattering of the X-rays, provides information about the 
arrangement of the atoms inside the crystal. According to Speakman (2016), for each material there is 
==PAGE:54==

                    54 
 
a different crystal structure which results in a unique diffraction pattern that can describe the atomic 
arrangement of the material and be used to identify it.

To calculate the diffraction angle, which is considered to be equal to the incidence angle of the 
incoming X-rays as seen in Figure 4. 17, Bragg’s law (see Equation 4.15) is used. Cullity and Stock 
(2014) state that Bragg’s law is a simplistic model which is used to describe the essential conditions 
necessary in order for diffraction to occur. Speakman (2016) mentioned that for a fixed X-ray 
wavelength, λ, a family of planes will form a unique diffraction peak at a certain angle 2θ. In order to 
derive Bragg’s Law it is taken into account that the incident X-rays are always a) parallel up to the 
point z and b) in phase (see Figure 4. 17 a). As seen in Figure 4. 17 a, the lower beam continues to the 
second layer where atom B scatters it. Also, to continue traveling parallel and adjacent to the top 
beam, the lower beam has to travel an extra distance equal to AB + BC.  
Considering that the phases of the two beams are equal, the extra distance must be equal to the product 
of an integral, n and the wavelength of the incoming X-rays, λ as seen in Equation 4.12: 
nλ = AB +BC   
(eq 4.12)

exceed unity and is equal to the wavelengths number in the path difference of rays scattered by 
adjacent planes. The spacing of the atom planes, d is also the hypotenuse of the triangle ABz (Figure 
4. 17 b), so using trigonometry θ, which is the diffraction angle, can be related to d by Equation 4.13: 
AB = d sinθ

4.3.4 
Standard testing procedures 
 
Swell Pressure 
The swell pressure tests were conducted based on the ASTM D4546 (2014) - method B. More 
specifically, the intact specimen was installed in the oedometer using dry filters and loaded to the in 
situ conditions (~9.5 MPa) with a rate of 0.5 MPa/hr to ensure sufficient number of data points during 
loading. For the first reading, the deformation measuring device was set to zero. After applying the in 
situ vertical stress, the specimen was left to stabilise until an equilibrium condition was reached. Next, 
the specimen was unloaded until ~0.5 MPa with a rate of 0.5 MPa/hr and was left again until 
equilibrium was reached. Subsequently, the axial actuator was fixed in position and the specimen was 
inundated filling the cell with the fluid selected. Due to relaxation of the load frame the transducers 
recorded some minor displacement. Finally, the load was adjusted so that the specimen would return 
to its initial height. Throughout the whole process the effective stresses and strains were recorded in 
real time. The test plan for the Free Swell is summarised in Table 4. 9.

These tests were conducted following the Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) method, whilst four

of Halloysite. However, this is in disagreement with the geology of the sampling area presented in 
Section 4.1, the Atterberg limits, as well as the mineral density estimated above. Hence, it is possible 
that the samples consist of a mixed clay containing Illite and Kaolinite. In order to confirm the 
results, XRD experiments were deemed necessary.

5.3 Free Swell Tests 
 
Prior discussing the results, it should be noted that due to the limited time available for the Free Swell 
tests not all specimens were inundated for the same amount of time. More specifically, the inundation 
time varied from 1464 min to 5357 min. Therefore, to extract comparable results, it was decided that a 
common time frame was necessary, so the results presented below correspond to the first 1400 min 
after the start of inundation (slightly less than 24 hours).  
 
Also, it should be stated that sample 1.1_FS_NSS exhibited a differentiated swell behaviour compared 
to the other specimens that was attributed mainly to the high temperature to which it was subjected 
during its installation in the cell. More specifically, prior testing, the cell of the sample 1.1 was put 
into the oven to dry. However, while the cell was still very hot (>60oC), the sample was inserted into it 
and remained there for 1 hour prior starting the experiment, since the possible implications were not 
properly considered at the time. It is possible that during this time the moisture content decreased due 
to evaporation resulting in significantly higher values of swell potential than those expected. Even 
though this sample is not directly comparable with the others, it could serve as a important example of 
how high temperatures and moisture content changes could affect the swell potential of a formation. 
This is an interesting observation as it indicates that any difference to the test procedure could affect 
significantly the test results. In addition, this could be extremely valuable for the Petroleum Industry 
given that in hydrocarbon operations a typical reservoir stimulation technique is gas injection, which 
can result in changes to the formation water and hence to the swell potential of the formation subjected 
to this process.

In Section 3.2.5, a previous study of the Author was presented showing correlations that could be used 
for the estimation of the swell potential of a soil using only its plasticity index. To evaluate if the

6. Conclusions & Future Research 
In this project, an experimental study was conducted to evaluate whether the swelling properties of 
swelling formations, like shale, can be used to render them an efficient annulus filling material, able to 
ensure complete zonal isolation. To achieve this, the swelling pressure (MPa) and swell potential (%) 
of 10 Farsund shale specimens were measured using three different pore fluids, namely: deionized 
water (DW), brine simulating the North Sea in - situ conditions (NSS) and a highly saline fluid, with 
72g/L NaCl (72NaCl). Additionally, in order to investigate the relation of the physical characteristics 
of the samples with their swelling properties, classification tests such as Atterberg limits tests, mineral 
density measurements, and mineralogical analysis were also conducted. Overall, throughout this study 
a simple, effective and repeatable test procedure was established. 
Given that most of the drilling is conducted on cap rocks (i.e. rocks of extremely low permeability that 
form an impermeable barrier above and around the reservoir) it could be argued that, ideally, the 
material tested in the present study should have been acquired from a depth interval above the 
hydrocarbon reservoir. However, the cores tested were acquired from the Jurassic Farsund Shale of the 
Mesozoic Era, found at a depth of 9,282΄ 0΄΄ (or ~2,829.2 m), which is below the reservoir. 
Nevertheless, this formation exhibits extremely low permeability, close to the one of a cap rock, whilst 
its composition and saturation resemble those of the Upper Lark. Additionally, both formations exhibit 
similar mechanical behaviour, albeit Farsund is slightly more plastic. Considering all the above, as 
well as the advantages of testing a ‘real’ core from the North Sea, it was decided that the Farsund shale 
would serve as an excellent testing material able to provide realistic results. Finally, the fact that, the 
interval of upper Jurassic – lower Cretaceous in the North Sea Central Graben has not been 
extensively cored since, as stated by Pegrum & Spencer (1990), it was not considered a promising 
area, adds additional value to the results of this study. 
Using the Atterberg limits of the formation presented in Table 6. 1, in combination with Casagrande’s 
mineralogy chart it was concluded that the Farsund shale is a mix containing both Montmorillonite 
and Illite, albeit Illite seemed to prevail. This argument was also supported by the measured mineral 
density, ρs =2.667 g/cm3, which according to the bibliography shows that the material could be a mix 
of Illite (2.6 - 2.9 g/cm3) and Smectite (2.0 - 2.6 g/cm3). However, by comparing the specific surface 
of the samples (see Table 6. 1) with bibliographic values, it was concluded that this is a relatively 
small specific surface area and that it could correspond to a mixed clay containing Illite and Kaolinite. 
To assess the above conclusions and determine the exact composition of the test material, XRD tests 
were deemed necessary. The XRD tests results agreed with the BET results and the geology of the 
sampling area and showed that the test material is a mixed clay consisting mainly from Illite and 
containing a smaller amount of Kaolinite as well as a slight amount of Chlorite (see Table 6. 1).

During the Swell Pressure tests, a slight swell was observed (strain between 0.2 and 0.6 %) even 
though the load frame was set to a fixed position. This led to making an adjustment to the loading 
frame position to return the specimen to its initial height. Therefore, two different swell pressures were 
recorded for the Farsund shale. The swell pressure before the adjustment Pswell1 ranged between 4.8 
MPa for the case of the deionised water and 9.9 MPa for the 72 g/L NaCl brine, whilst the North Sea 
water exhibited an intermediate value of 6.9 MPa. Concerning the swell pressure after the adjustment, 
Pswell2, the highest value (14.0 MPa) was measured when the 72 g/L NaCl brine was used, whereas 
the lowest value (10.1 MPa) was observed for the North Sea Seawater. Overall though, both 
measured pressures exhibited reasonably high swell pressure values (see Figure 6. 1) which combined 
with the extremely low permeability of shale, especially after swelling, could render the Farsund shale 
a competent annulus barrier. 
 
The swell potential measured ranged from 1.7 % to 3.9 %, with the highest value achieved when the 
72 g/L NaCl brine was used, whilst the lowest value was given for the North Sea Seawater. 
However, at this point it should be noted that the North Sea Seawater and the deionized water 
exhibited very similar results, with the deionised water resulting to a 2% swell potential (see Figure 6. 
2). Furthermore, it was observed that approximately 80 % of the swelling occurred during the first 100 
minutes after the start of inundation.

The aforementioned results demonstrate that with a proper fluid composition there is a possibility of 
inducing a higher swelling than 3.9 % which is extremely promising with respect to the initial 
hypothesis that swelling clay formations could be used as an annulus filling material around casing. 
However, more research is advised in order to establish a brine composition that would enable the 
formation to reach the maximum possible swelling pressure. Also, the fact that an illitic shale could 
provide such values of swell potential indicates that a montmorillonitic formation could exhibit a 
significantly higher swelling resulting in most favourable conditions. Also, taking into account the 
numerous disadvantages involved in the traditional remedial procedures (see Table 6. 2) it could be 
concluded that the use of swelling formations as filling material for cracks and cavings would be an 
extremely beneficial alternative for the oil and gas industry.

An additional observation, based on limited testing (1.1_FS_NSS) indicates that high temperatures and 
partial saturation, affects the swell potential of a formation. This observation could be valuable for the 
Petroleum Industry given that in hydrocarbon operations the reservoir stimulation using gas injection 
may result to changes in the formation water and hence to the swell potential of the formation. 
Another useful observation is that a higher bulk density can lead to lower swell potential values 
considering all other properties being equal. Hence, the initial bulk density is a crucial parameter that 
should be taken into account before any operation. These finding agree with Mitchel and Soga’s 
(2005) observations who argued that the free swell and swell potential are affected a) from physical 
properties and composition, and b) from environmental factors, like the density, water content, 
confining pressure, water availability, temperature, and fabric of the soil. 
 
Additionally, an estimation of the swell potential was conducted based on the Author’s previous study. 
However, the estimated swell potentials exhibited an extremely high deviation (> 110 %) with respect 
to the measured value of specimens that could be attributed to the fact that the formulas were derived 
using reconstituted soil sample. Therefore, additional testing of intact specimens is recommended in 
order to develop a chart as that obtained for reconstituted soil. As a secondary conclusion it should be 
noted that the preconsolidation to in situ stress had an extremely positive effect as it contributed in 
achieving a faster response by ensuring the specimens’ complete contact with the oedometer cell.

A few recommendations for future research on this topic include:   
• 
Any compliance of the loading machine as well as the precision of the machine should be 
taken into account in order to evaluate the uncertainties of the tests. To achieve this and 
extract reliable results, a minimum of two tests is advised for each case. 
• 
Preconsolidation is highly recommended considering that a bad connection of the sample with 
the cell results in higher testing times, since water needs to be absorbed deeper to give the 
same result.  
• 
An equal inundation time for all the free swell experiments is highly advised given that the 
final saturation degree of the specimens could vary depending on the inundation time. 
• 
To be able to use swelling formations as a filling material for cavings a significant amount of 
swelling would be required. Therefore, more research needs to be conducted in order to 
determine the time frame within which this swelling could occur and if this would be viable 
for hydrocarbon operations. 
• 
More research needs to be done to establish a pore fluid composition that will enable the 
formations with swelling properties to reach their full swelling potential. 
• 
The material used was found to have medium plasticity and comprise of a mix of Illite which 
is not considered to be very prone to swelling. Hence, to evaluate whether swelling clay can 
be used efficiently as a filling material, more tests need to be realised using intact samples of 
higher plasticity. 
• 
More experiments need to be conducted using specimens of various dimensions and with a 
wide range of different Do/Ho ratios to evaluate how the different scaling could affect the 
results and simulate more accurately the in situ conditions.
