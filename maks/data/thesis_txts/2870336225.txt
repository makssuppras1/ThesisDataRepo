Abstract
This master thesis presents research work on electrochemically deposited ironôÙcarbon
coatings focused on the effect of pulse current deposition compared to direct current deôÙ
position. Coatings were tested primarily through tribological means both dry and under
lubrication. The pulse current depositions showed an overall better resistance to wear
than the direct current depositions. For the testing of specimens under lubrication, a new
holder for pinôÙonôÙdisc testing was developed.
Cracking of the coatings was observed after tribological testing. This lead to an investiôÙ
gation of the development of the macro stress in the plating. The stress was observed to
change from compressive to tensile over a period of 5 to 6 days, before then stabilizing
into tensile stress after approximately 20 days. Time sensitive hardness tests were conôÙ
ducted showing that the hardness and ductility of the coatings worsened over a period of
14 days.

Introduction
Interest in new technical coatings was rekindled around 2016 when the environmental
impact of chrome plating was scrutinized. The use of hardôÙchrome plating utilizing hexôÙ
avalent chrome, Cr(VI), was questioned. Not because of the danger of the finished plating,
which is almost completely chemically inert. It is because of the hexavalent chrome in the
process of making technical chrome surfaces. Hexavalent chrome is highly carcinogenic,
making the fumes from plating baths dangerous. It is used in aqueous solutions, making
discharge to the environment a danger. Because of these points, the EU commissions
REACH regulation banned any new uses of Cr(VI), which has left the market without any
good alternatives.
Among the technical surfaces that has received a resurgence in interest is the electroôÙ
chemical deposition of iron with varying coôÙdeposits. The primary variation of this coating
that has been investigated is ironôÙcarbon. The deposition method has its roots in research
done during the mid 90s in Japan, where the addition of carbon to iron plating were inôÙ
vestigated.
The research into the ironôÙcarbon coating was continued at DTU with a PHD thesis by
Jacob Obitsû¡ Nielsen, who optimized the electrolyte content and deposition parameters
to get the highest hardness and plating efficiency.
The development of the ironôÙcarbon coating was continued by several associate profesôÙ
sors and students at DTUãs Department of Civil and Mechanical Engineering. Most reôÙ
cently by Bastian Munch Kjû¡ller, who among other things, worked to optimize the techniôÙ
cal properties of the coating by changing the applied current mode. Kjû¡ller expanded the
plating parameters by adding on and off time to the current, to increase the hardness and
wear resistance of the coating.
This thesis then continues the work of investigating the wear resistance and stability of
the coating. Tribological wear tests will be conducted both dry and with added lubricant to
assess the coatingãs wear resistance under conditions closer to those it would experience
industrially.

2.1
Electrochemical deposition
Electrochemical or electrolytic deposition is a process of additively coating a substrate
surface and can be used for many purposes. Electrochemical deposition may be used
for adding mostly decorative coatings such as with silver or gold on medals, for adding a
conductive surface in microelectronics with copper, or for adding a hard surface as has
been the case with chrome four parts, the electrolyte, the two electrodes, the cathode,
and the anode, and the electricity source. The electrolyte is a mostly waterôÙbased solution
with added salts, which provide electrical conductivity by adding ions to the solution. The
electrolyte can be either acidic or alkaline, depending on the salt and other additives
added. The cathode is the substrate, the surface upon which the coating is added through
a reduction reaction, while two different types of anode exist: the inert anode, which is
used only as part of the electric circuit to allow current to run through the electrolyte, and
the sacrificial anode, which is made of the same material as the coating and provides
metal ions to the solution keeping the concentration in the solution mostly constant. The
mass and or thickness of a coating can be mostly predicted using Faradayãs first law. The
law uses the current I, the time t, the molar weight M, the number of electrons transferred
z, the charge transferred or the Faraday number F, the cathode area A, and the density
p, to calculate the thickness of the coating d.

zFAp

position process, with the primary one of those being hydrolysis, the breakdown of H2O
molecules into their component hydrogen and oxygen gas at the cathode and anode,
respectively. These secondary reactions may also include the formation of hydroxides
and oxidation or reduction reactions of additives, which may lead to the codeposition of

There are three overall current categories, DC current also referred to as galvanostatic

2.2.3
Wear
Wear is at itãs most basic definition the loss of material from a solid, caused by mechanical
interaction with a counter body. This counter body can be a solid, but it can also be a fluid
or gaseous. When a solid and a counterôÙbody is in contact and in relative motion the
interaction will cause wear to one or both bodies. This report focuses on wear between
solids, where the primary material and counter material are both solids.
Figure 2.4: An exmaple of a typical wear curve. With (I) runôÙin wear stage, (II) steady
state wear stage and (III) runôÙout wear stage. Figure from [20]
Wear occurs in three phases. RunôÙin, steady state and runôÙout, presented in figure 2.4.
As the wear occurs a progressive amount of material is lost. This can be measured and
quantified as a weight or a volume depending of the measurement method vs the wear
length or time.
RunôÙin wear
At the runôÙin stage, the different phenomenon have not yet taken full effect. This phase is
generally a ramp up into the steady state phase. A large reason for this that the surface
roughness reduces the asperity of the contact. The roughness of the surface makes the
spikes of the material sticking out the first contact with another body. In these spikes,
the forces and mechanism that cause wear are concentrated and a very high wear rate
is observed. As the spikes wear down and the asperity increases, the wear reduce as
the contact force is spread out. When the asperity stops changing the runôÙin phase is
concluded and steady state wear commences [20].
Steady state wear
While the stage after runôÙin has occurred often settles into a steady state of constant wear,
this is not all ways the case. Sometime there is a period of no wear, where after a certain
incubation time progressive wear occurs. In lubricated systems with additives, ãnegativeã
wear can occour, where a film builds up on the surface. But generally a linear regime is
observed [18, 19].

RunôÙout
As the body or counter body starts to break down, the final stage is reached. This is usually
indicated by a friction coefficient no longer being in a steady state, jumping rapidly up and
down. The runôÙout usually happens when a layer is worn though, and is a phenomenon
observed with most surface coatings.[21].
Types of wear
Several types of wear exists, arising from different phenomenons. The phenomenons
relevant to this thesis are surface fatigue, abrasion and adhesion.
Surface fatigue arises from cyclical loading. Cyclical loading creates fatigue cracks that
slowly propagates, causing the material to spall.
Abrasive wear happens when the counter body is much harder, the counter body plunges
into the main body, and the hard asperities of the counter creates microôÙcuttings in the
body.
Adhesive wear happens when when the two surface layers form micro welds (Atomic
bonds) in the plastically deformed asperities. If the bond is stronger than the strength of
one of the bodies the material will transfer onto the other. Generally this happens from
the softer to the harder body. This material can remain in on the counter, be rejected or
even transfer back to the original surface [19].

ãÂ A beaker is filled with 80% of the target volume of DI water, the temperature probe

ãÂ The chemical products are precisely weighed, and citric acid is added to the solution.

ãÂ The iron anode is immersed in the electrolyte to prevent the solutionãs oxidation.

when the microscope cannot properly register the surface. There is also a lot of noise that

Figure 3.21: PreôÙtreatment procedure of surface capture before further analysis. ConôÙ

Remove outliers
Specifically laser and optical confocal microscopes needs an initial outlier rejection step
as glare from the surface can cause the microscope to index a point as much higher or
lower than the surroundings. For this procedure Mountain maps has a outlier rejection
filter that is not well documented but is standard to use. It is used on its ãmediumã setting
with outliers around the edges and single standalone outliers dealt with. All outliers are
replaced with nonôÙmeasured (NM) points. NM points have no height value attributed to
them.
SôÙFilter
An SôÙfilter is a type of lowôÙpass filter. It attenuates short wavelengths, for this case it is
used to removes micro roughness, as roughness on the order that we are looking at is not
relevant. The roughness on a very small order is also at the point of being indistinguishôÙ
able from noise, and the filtering will give us as better filtered surface. A cutoff of 0.25ôçm
is used, this is also know as a Nis cutoff.
Leveling
The next step is a leveling operation also known as an FôÙoperation. Here the procedure
from is different for the different specimen types. The operation for the square specimens
(SQ) is a simple plane operation. A 2nd degree polynomial plane is found from the surface
and retracted from the surface to remove the from. This way, any slope from the plating
operation is removed, and the normal to the surface will be parallel for the entire surface.
This ensure that any height difference between points on the surface is from the roughness
and not the shape of the surface.
Fill nonôÙmeasured points
Lastly before the surface becomes the primary surface, all the points removed in the
outlier rejection needs to be filled in again. This is especially important for the wear track
analysis, as the analysis tool cannot handle nonôÙmeasured (NM) points. The wear track
analysis will treat NM points in the wear groove as non existent and not contributing to
the area, as such the area of the wear groove is not estimated correctly. The operation
is carried out on the 3D surface, the fillôÙin points calculated such that there is no sudden
jumps in height. This is not just a linear interpolation, as entire holes are filled in which
are not necessarily square.

Roughness
For surface roughness, two different approaches are made. Firstly a more traditional
2D approach, replicating a stylus instrument, and a more modern 3D or areal approach.
The advantages of the 3D approach is that the analysis is not a dependent on hard to set
evaluation length parameters. When a roughness from a specification needs confirmation,
evaluation length is easy to set as the evaluation calls for a specific length to confirm or
deny it. However when a roughness just needs to be reported the evaluation length needs
to be carefully considered as different evaluation lengths will majorly affect the results
attained.
For the 2D roughness parameters a line profile is needed. Five line profiles is extracted
from the 3D surface, parallel to each other at different areas. The surface is ?isotropic?
and as such the parallel line profiles should not bias the measurements. Usually the
roughness is measured perpendicular to machining marks such that the roughness meaôÙ
surement becomes as large as possible, in this case the only thing to avoid is the wear
groove. These five line profiles are combined into a series and the roughness analysis is
then performed on the series. The output is Ra, Rz and Rsk. Ra is the classical roughness
parameter and is used everywhere. It is arithmetic mean height. So the average height
of the surface from the mean, both above and below the mean line. Rz is the maximum
height of the profile. From the lowest valley to the highest peak. The last parameter is
Rsk, skewness, which describes whether the material is mainly in valleys or peaks. PosôÙ
itive Rsk is mainly peaks, negative mainly valleys. All these parameters are presented in
figure 2.6. Finally to capture the full surface rougness a length paramert of 0.8 mm was
chosen.
For the 3D roughness, or areal, parameters the area of the wear track is exluded. From
this an SKôÙcurve, or material ratio curve is extracted. This material ratio curve also gives
us all the relevant roughness parameters. Sk is the core height, found from the material
ratio curve. It tells about the distribution of the majority of the surface. Spk and Svk
are the reduced peak and valley (dale) height. This describes the height of peaks and
Tribological characterization of electroplated FeôÙCôÙbased coatings
37
==PAGE:50==
valleys. These are closely related to Smr1 and smr2 which describes the ratio of material
associated with Spk and Svk respectively.
For the line profile method, the direction of measurement was parallel to the wear groove.
This is not the correct way in order to measure the roughness for the purposes of specôÙ
ification verification, but it is more appropriate for the purpose of the wear tests. The
measurement shall be done across the machining grooves, which for the case of the
workshop specimens measurement should be coming radially from the center. The wear
test does not engage the machining roughness in this way and would not tell us anything
related to the wear tests.
Wear groove
In ASTMôÙG99 it is described how the wear groove can be easily measured if only single
body wear is experienced. The width of the wear groove on the test piece or radius of the
wear scar on the wear pin can be measured and the volume loss determined exclusively
from this. However as soon as two body wear is experienced, the assumption that the
wear volume can be derived from the width of wear scar cannot be used. The wear
groove will not be perfectly round or the wear scar will not be perfectly flat. For out tests
it is observed that two body wear occurs As such wear groove analysis will have to be

Continuing with the groove types from the previous section, microscopic analysis of the
surfaces and wear grooves offers explanations for the visual difference between the difôÙ

The reflective groove type under LOM analysis proved to be discontinuous as shown
in figure 4.25a.
The wear grooves also donãt show any signs of having significantly
breached the surface of the specimens, instead consisting of smaller areas of surface
level scratches. The visible groove type is best described as an evolution of the reflective
groove. The wear mark consists of a continuous area of slightly curved scratch lines and
lighter color compared to the surrounding surface. The edges of the wear area are at
times not fully parallel, but may be wavy. Examples of the wear area is shown in figures
4.25b and 4.25c.

edges, layered lines of similar color, and thin wavelike crack formations. The sharply
distinct edges are a result of the difference in reflectivity between the grooves and the
rest of the surface. The lines within the groove are due to the morphology of the groove,
and is shown in figures 4.25d and 4.25e and highlighted using CôÙDIC in figure 4.25f. The
crack formations are visible at different magnifications and shows a repeating pattern that

Deposit stress analysis
From the observation of cracks in the bottom of the wear grooves it was theorized that the
material would have some amount of internal strain. Furthermore, it was also expected
that this would change over time, as the electroplating process incorporates hydrogen
into the surface. As such, the internal stress of the specimens was thought to be time
dependent.
4.9.1
Depositions
In total 5 different DSA specimens were produced using the three preôÙdeposition methôÙ
ods described in the methodology chapter. When DSA01 was prepared using anodic

was affected, with several large areas being removed by the gas bubbles. This not only

received a layer of deposit, but may also have contaminated the electrolyte, thus being
potentially responsible for SQ08ãs divergent thickness and weight of deposition.
When the no attempt at removing potential grease on the specimens were attempted, as
with DSA02 and DSA03, there were also problem with the deposit. Both specimens ended
up with bare areas on the deposited side of the legs, thus potentially potentially invalidating
the stress equation calculations. Both specimens were still hung on the analyzer, as it was
assumed that they may still give some insight into how a proper test would be executed.
The last two specimens DSA04 and DSA05 were prepared using soapy water, as per the
ASTM standard. The resulting deposits were without any visible fault and were such used

Stress Evolution effects
The stress evolution tests, meaning the DSA and the hardness change over time, were
done based on what seemed to be a inconsistency between the material characteristics
of the of coating and the results of different tests. It was reported by Nielsen that the
coating contained internal compressive stresses[2], which should at least partially prevent
crack growth. The compressive stress of the coating did not show the expected effect
on crack growth, as shown in figures 4.30a, 4.27b, and 4.26c. The cracks within the
dry wear tracks, were thin and constricted to the wear track, but still plentiful, which is
odd considering the high compressive stress. The cracking experienced by SQ06B and
SQ09C during their second cutting was not limited to specific areas, but is found within
the bulk of the coating. The cracks went deep into the coating and as seen in figure 4.30b
could be quite thick. The most severe cracking was shown by specimen SQ09D after
tribological testing, the cracks in the wear track of SQ09D did not show any patterns, but
were instead completely chaotic.
The difference between the expected none to minor cracking, and the at points, quite seôÙ
vere cracking observed led to the extended deposit stress analysis. The stress analysis
showed that under room temperature conditions, with no direct sunlight, the compressive
stress of the coating would decrease and then slowly change to tensile stress. The inôÙ
ternal stress evolution observed then prompted the time conscious hardness test series,
that showed the effect of time on the hardness and ductility of the coating as previously
discussed.
It was not within the scope of this thesis to investigate the driving forces behind the stress
evolution, but based on the known content of the coating[8] and some background knowlôÙ
edge abut iron, it is possible to make some hypothesizes about the driving forces.
As it stands there seems to be three possible driving forces behind the stress evolution,
the diffusion of coôÙdeposited hydrogen, the oxidation of iron, and some physical change
in the coating. The diffusion of hydrogen should be possible at room temperature and
as such it should be possible for interstitial hydrogen to leave the coating, removing their
contribution to the compressive stress.
As no precautions to prevent oxidation were taken is almost guaranteed that some form of
oxidation occurred. But for the oxidation to contribute to the stress evolution observed is
questionable as iron oxides are generally larger than the source iron, and as such would
more likely be expected to contribute to compressive stresses.

Past and Future Changes in Methodology
5.4.1
Deposition
The primary change in the deposition methodology compared to the latest methods used
by Kjû¡ller is the change of anodic degreasing to cathodic degreasing. Some specimens
prepared using anodic degreasing experienced decoupling of parts of the coating during
tribological tests, so to prevent this from repeating, the degreasing method was changed
to increase the attachment between the coating and the substrate. The change to cathodic
degreasing necessitated other changes, the most impactfull being the switch copper wires
to steel wires as copper is not allowed in the cathodic degreasing solution, ãas it may
contaminate itã. Steel wire has other strengths and weaknesses compared to copper wire,
such as greater stiffness and the ability to be workôÙhardened. This affected the deposition
of the square and discôÙshaped specimens differently and to varying extents. The different
material characteristics of the steel wire meant that the bindings securing the wire to the
square specimens were not as secure as tight as with copper wire. It fortunately does not
seem to have affected the coatings significantly, but steel wire should probably be avoided
if lighter specimens are used. The increased stiffness of the steel wire also had a positive
effect, as the steel wire did not bend as easily and it was therefore, easier to ensure
that the substrate ended up directly parallel to the anode, compared to the copper wire.
The steel wire is more problematic when it comes to the disc specimens, as previously,
copper wire could be coiled around the disc and tied together, but due to the stiffness and
workôÙhardenability of the steel, it can not be replicated.
5.4.2
Tribological Tests
Previous tribilogical tests of ironôÙcarbon deposits were done using aluminium oxide ceôÙ

and the MTMãs results 100Cr6 alloy balls were used. The lower hardness of the 100Cr6
balls meant that according to tribological standards, the width of the wear groove could
not be used to assess the wear of the coating.
For future investigations of the ironôÙcarbon coating, it should not be necessary to restrict

the shallowness of the wear tracks.

During testing of with the CSM tribolometer, it was observed that small remnants of oil

should be conducted in a close timeframe, it would be best to either only use the specimen

Conclusion
At the onset of this thesis, the focus was on the effect of lubrication on the tribological
properties of the ironôÙcarbon electroôÙdeposited coating. This was originally expected to
be done primarily using the MTM2 ballôÙonôÙdisc tribometer, but due to breakdowns of the
machine, it was out of service for a large part of this investigation. To compensate for
these breakdowns, an alternative test was devised. By designing and manufacturing a
specimen holder for a CSM pinôÙonôÙdisc tribometer, tests under oil could be run on this
machine as well. The MTM2 tribometer was eventually fixed after the modification of the
CSM tribometer had been made. As such, tests were run on both.
From the machines, three test levels were made. MTM2 ballôÙonôÙdisc test, which were alôÙ
ways under oil, CSM pinôÙonôÙdisc test with and without lubrication. It was initially planned
to run two different kinds of lubricants: a gear oil and an oilôÙwater emulsion commonly
employed in cold rolling. However, it was quickly determined that none of the test enviôÙ
ronments were good for oilôÙwater emulsion, as the water phase would evaporate during
testing. The oilôÙwater emulsion is designed to be continually supplied and refreshed at
the interface to combat this evaporation problem. It was impossible to do this, and testing
continued with gear oil exclusively. From the two tests with oil, it was also concluded that
these could not be compared. The difference in the tribological interface was too great.
The two machines had different contact geometries and slide/roll behavior. The ultimate
conclusion is that tribological tests cannot compare to each other. It is very difficult to
change only one thing in the system such that the influence of other parameters are not
affected.
The next test levels came from the variation in the current mode during plating. SpeciôÙ
mens were prepared using pulsed current and direct current, which had previously been
investigated to improve the mechanical properties of the surface. It is shown that the hardôÙ
ness of the surface improves with pulsed current vs direct current. The roughness of the
surface is not affected. The tribological properties are improved. For partial sliding wear
under oil and dry sliding wear, the pulsed current plating outperforms the direct current
plating. For the test with lubricated sliding, the results are inconclusive.
During the investigation of the wear track after dry sliding, it was observed that cracking
had occurred in the bottom of the wear track. This led to an investigation of the internal
stress of the plating types. It had also previously been observed that the coating changes
over time. An analysis of the internal stress over time was made. Here, it was experienced
that both pulsed current plating and direct current plating changed drastically over time.
The specimens started with compressive stresses, amd it was observed that after 5.5
days, the stress changed to tensile. The initial compressive stress was measured at
200 GPa, and after over 800 hours, it arrived at a final tensile stress of 130 GPa. This is
the conclusion for direct current plating, as only one experiment was completed without
problems.
The change in internal stress over time was also shown to have an effect on the hardness
of the plated surface. The hardness increased over the course of two week from and
average of 683HV to 931HV. There was not found any statistically significant difference
between the hardness of pulsed current and direct current plated samples.

Outlook
Looking back at the work done during the creation of this thesis there are some things
that could have been done better, and if the thesis should be redone, would be.
Relatively few hardness tests were conducted during the course of the thesis. This was
primarily because the Struers Durascan 70 G5 was out of commission for several months
and once it was fixed it was reserved for the better part of a month for teaching. As such if
forced to redo thesis more hardness tests would be conducted. These would either take
the form of more surface tests, as with the time conscious hardness tests or they would
take the form of more cross sectional hardness tests. Either case would allow for a more
time dependent hardness resulting in a more extensive data set of the effect of time on
the hardness of the ironôÙcarbon coating.
When it comes to CSM tribological tests, then there are two large regrets, one for the dry
tests, and one for the oiled tests. For the dry tests, it is that more was not done to prevent
oil contamination of the dry tests. Thus if the thesis should be redone all dry and oil tests
should be conducted with separate specimen holders and test balls, so as maintain the
purity of the dry tests. When it comes to the lubricated tests, the largest regret is that no
conclusion concerning the effect of the current mode on the wear rate could be reached
based on the results. As such, if the thesis should be redone a larger data set with longer
tests should be gathered.
The SEM investigations were started too late in the process and was interrupted by techôÙ
nical problems with the SEM, and as such, there were several specimens and surface
features that were not examined. If the thesis should be redone, then smaller SEM invesôÙ
tigations should be done continuously during the course of the thesis.

ture study into the wear rate of ironôÙcarbon coatings under lubrication could be conducted
using the longest test lengths employed in this thesis as the shortest tests. This could
either prove difficult as the longer test length, would necessitate longer time in between
tests, which could affect the results as seen with the hardness. This could be somewhat
counteracted by employing two tribological test machines simultaneously, such as by suôÙ
ing both CSM tribological testers available at DTUãs Department of Civil and Mechanical
Engineering, allowing for two square subôÙspecimens to be comparatively tested, as it has
proved difficult to compare across sampels. The focus of the study could also be on the
MTM2 machine, as more force and speed can be employed generating the wear tracks
faster. This would unfortunately also necessitate a new specimen for each test, adding
some potential uncertainty based on the repeatability of the depositions. A study could
also be designed around the effect of time since deposition on the coatings wear resisôÙ
tance, by repeating specific tribological tests at set time intervals after deposition.
However, the larges improvement for future tribological investigations involving oil, is to
know more about tribology fromt the begning. This project was severly hindered by the fact
that both authors had no knowledge of tribology before hand, especially tribology under

Hardness Evolution
The change in hardness observed in section 4.4.1 raised many questions about the staôÙ
bility of the coating that may be answered in future work. A future study that is underway
is continuing the hardness tests of SQ11 and SQ12, to observe what further changes to
the hardness, if any will happen. Another possible study would be to change the storage
medium of specimens and repeat the hardness over time tests, to see if storage in a desôÙ
iccator or under oil affects the change in hardness and brittleness. Such a study could
also aid in narrowing down what causes the observed hardness evolution, by limiting the
reacting atmosphere.
Deposit stress analysis
There are a lot of unanswered questions left regarding the internal stresses of the ironôÙ
carbon coatings. The effect of DCP and PCP on stress evolution is still unknown as specôÙ
imen DSA05 broke before any large changes were observed. The effect of the substrate
on the stress evolution is also unknown, therefore a study comparing different substrate
materials and their evolutions may be prudent. Another possible study to help identify the
underlying mechanics of the stress evolution would be to store the specimen in vacuum,
as changes in the time line of the stress evolution could help identify the extent to which
hydrogen diffusion and oxidation affect stress evolution.
