Abstract
This study explored 3D extrusion bioprinting as an alternative to traditional gel casting for
creating in vitro skeletal muscle and neuromuscular junctions (NMJ) models. We aimed to
gain automation, scalability, and greater design freedom while investigating the potential
for NMJ formation between primary human skeletal muscle cells (HSkM) and a motor
neuron-like cell line (NSC34).
A co-culture medium was developed to support the differentiation of both NSC34 and
HSkM cells. A retinoic acid concentration of 5 μM induced the longest neurite exten-
sions, reaching approximately 40 μm. Further supplementation with serum-free supple-
ments (N2 and B27) and neurotrophic factors (BDNF, GDNF, and agrin) increased neurite
length to approx. 60 μm but did not inhibit NSC34 proliferation. Immunostaining con-
firmed successful differentiation of HSkM cells, but NSC34 differentiation was attenuated
in co-culture, preventing full NMJ formation. This could be due to species incompati-
bility, misexpression of myogenic differentiation factors, or excess serum in the muscle
differentiation medium.
The embedded bioprinting process was optimized, resulting in a bioink with reduced xan-
than gum concentration (0.1% (v/v)) and an optimal flow rate of 500 nL/mm for robust
”dog bone” shaped bundle formation. Increasing the printing temperature did not signifi-
cantly affect viscosity or induce apoptosis.
Bioprinting was tested on two ribbon platforms (150 μm and 200 μm), where both proved
too stiff for active contraction of the bundles. HSkM muscle bundles exhibited more sub-
stantial compaction and contraction force compared to co-cultured bundles, potentially
due to NSC34 proliferation hindering myotube formation. The 200 μm ribbon platform,
being stiffer, allows for greater contractile forces of the bundles and, thus, stronger tissues.
These findings reveal that 3D bioprinting offers a more scalable, flexible, and automated
approach to creating customizable skeletal muscle and NMJ models. However, challenges
remain in achieving full NMJ functionality when co-culturing HsKM and NSC34 cells.
Optimizing culture conditions, controlling cell proliferation, and selecting bioprinting pa-
rameters are crucial to creating physiologically relevant in vitro models. Further research
is needed to refine this model and overcome its limitations to create successful NMJ for-
mation and functional integration. This study contributes valuable insights to the field of
muscle tissue engineering. It highlights the potential of 3D bioprinting for creating more
sophisticated and physiologically relevant in vitro models for studying neuromuscular de-
velopment and disease.

Introduction
The skeletal muscle is the largest organ in the human body by mass and is essential for
respiration, locomotion, posture, and whole-body energy homeostasis. For skeletal mus-
cle contraction to occur, motor neurons innervate and send signals through the NMJ [1].
The skeletal muscle NMJ is a highly organized synapse between the motor neuron axon
and skeletal muscle fibers. It is designed to transmit efferent signals from motor neurons
to muscle fibers, facilitating muscle fiber contraction. Acetylcholine receptors (AChR)
on the NMJ’s postsynaptic muscle fiber membrane mediate this signal by binding acetyl-
choline (ACh) neurotransmitters, specialized signaling molecules, released from vesicles
residing in the pre-synaptic motor neuron axon terminal to regulate synapse formation and
function [2].
Traditional two-dimensional (2D) models for studying skeletal muscle responses to drugs
or injury have been widely utilized for their simplicity and ease of use. However, these
models cannot replicate the three-dimensional (3D) architecture and mechanical properties
of native human tissue, as they are typically grown on flat surfaces. This limitation restricts
cell-extracellular matrix (ECM) and cell-cell interactions. The lack of spatial organization
leads to altered cell behavior and gene expression, both of which are essential for muscle
function and development [3]. As a result, research has shifted toward 3D-engineered
muscle tissues (EMTs) to create more sophisticated models. These models enable the
study of muscle physiology, drug testing, and potential therapeutic applications. Designed
to mimic the native 3D architecture of skeletal muscle, these tissues are manufactured
using bioprinting and hydrogel scaffolding techniques. One model for studying skeletal
muscle tissue is the commercial Curi Bio Mantarray two-post system, which represents
significant advancements in skeletal muscle engineering. One of the primary advantages
of the Curi Bio two-post system is its ability to replicate natural muscle’s physiological
and biochemical properties, including the alignment of muscle fibers, which is essential
for effective muscle contraction and force generation. Additionally, it utilizes magnetic
sensing to measure the contractility of the tissue over time [4]. However, this system
relies on gel casting, which limits the freedom of tissue design and the automation of
tissue manufacturing. Casted tissues are often thick, further restricting oxygen and nutrient
supply - both essential for cell survival [5].
These limitations could be addressed using 3D extrusion bioprinting, a technique capable
of producing complex 3D structures that closely mimic the architecture of skeletal muscle
tissues. Extrusion bioprinting offers precise spatial control over the deposition of bioinks,

allowing the creation of complex, multilayered structures that closely replicate the native
architecture of human skeletal muscle [6]. The flexibility of this technique is further en-
hanced by the ability to incorporate various bioinks, enabling the use of different ECMs
and cell types. Extrusion bioprinting is highly automated, making it a viable option for
scalable and reproducible large-scale tissue engineering applications. This level of au-
tomation addresses the challenges associated with gel casting, which is labor-intensive
and poorly suited for high-throughput production [7] [8].
The interaction between muscle fibers and the NMJ is crucial for proper muscle function,
as it facilitates communication between motor neurons and muscle fibers. However, the
complexity of this interaction is often poorly represented in in vitro skeletal muscle mod-
els. Engineering NMJ models can significantly advance current skeletal muscle models,
enabling more effective investigation of muscle function in vitro. The synaptic transmis-
sion of ACh is essential for proper muscle function. Disruption of cholinergic signaling
can lead to several neuromuscular diseases, such as myasthenia gravis, congenital myas-
thenic syndrome, and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) [9][10]. ALS, a progressive
neurodegenerative disease involving the loss of motor neurons and muscle atrophy, cur-
rently has no effective treatment despite significant research efforts. To address this, a
study by Osaki et al. developed an ALS-on-a-chip model to investigate potential drug can-
didates and the pathogenesis of ALS. However, on-a-chip systems are expensive to manu-
facture, unsuitable for high-throughput applications, and therefore not ideal for large-scale
production [10]. Another study by Afshar Bakooshli et al. developed an innervated skele-
tal muscle model to study NMJs and congenital myasthenic syndrome by co-culturing
muscle cells with neuronal spheroids [2]. However, both these models rely on casting
techniques for manufacturing, which are labor-intensive and inflexible in design.
To overcome these shortcomings, this project aims to investigate using embedded extru-
sion bioprinting to create a 3D neuromuscular junction in vitro model. The model will
incorporate primary human skeletal muscle (HSkM) cells and a murine hybrid cell line
(NSC34) derived from the fusion of neuroblastoma and motor neuron-enriched spinal cord
cells. While NSC34 cells are not identical to human motor neurons, they offer several ad-
vantages, including their ability to differentiate neuron-like cells, form functional NMJs
with muscle cells, and respond to electrical stimulation[11][12]. These components will
be printed within a sacrificial alginate support matrix, which, upon removal, will yield
neuromuscular tissue.
The aims of this project are:
1. Develop a co-culture medium optimized to support the differentiation of both
muscle and neuronal cell types. This involves identifying a media formulation

Background
This section provides a background for understanding this project by introducing the native
biological functions of skeletal muscle and neuromuscular junctions. It also highlights
state-of-the-art skeletal muscle and NMJ models while offering essential information on
the materials used in this project.
2.1
The Musculoskeletal system
The musculoskeletal system is one of the body’s major tissue systems and consists of three
major muscle subgroups: skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscles. The skeletal muscle is
attached to the bone through tendons and is the subgroup that is responsible for produc-
ing all body movement and comprises approximately 40% of the human body’s weight
[13]. The skeletal muscle fibers are multi-nucleated, range from 10 to 100 μm in diame-
ter, and can be several centimeters long. The nucleus of the cells is in the periphery and is
adjacent to a sarcolemma, which are tubular sheets that form a barrier between the extra-
cellular and intracellular compartments. The sarcolemma comprises a plasma membrane
and a polysaccharide layer that merges with collagen fibers. It forms inward folds called
transverse tubules (T-Tubulus), key sites for ion exchange [13].
Each skeletal muscle comprises thousands of muscle fibers wrapped together by connec-
tive tissue. This makes a pattern of red and white lines, giving the muscle a striated ap-
pearance, this striated pattern of muscle fibers forms sarcomeres, the contractile units of
the skeletal muscle. The contractions of the skeletal muscles lead to the movement of the
bone that it is attached to and thus the movement of the body [1].
2.1.1
Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ)
The skeletal muscle contractions respond to stimulus from the neurons that innerve the
muscle. The innervation of a skeletal muscle typically comprises motor neurons with
myelinated axons and the NMJ [1]. The NMJ is a highly specialized synapse between a
motor neuron nerve terminal and the muscle fibers sarcolemma, which is responsible for
converting electrical impulses into electrical activity in the muscle [14] (Figure 2.1).
The structure of an NMJ can be divided into three parts: the pre-synaptic nerve termi-
nal, the synaptic cleft, and the post-synaptic motor end plate. The nerve terminal consists
of a myelinated neuron, which is unbranched until it reaches the target muscle, where
it is divided into smaller branches to innerve multiple muscle fibers. These endings are
known as the nerve terminals or terminal boutons. The nerve terminal membrane has ar-
eas of thickening known as the active zones. The active zones have SNAP (synataxins

neurotransmitter ACh ready for vesicle exocytosis. The nerve terminal has a complex
machinery, allowing synthesis, exocytosis, and recycling of the synaptic vesicles. Sur-
rounding the nerve terminal are non-myelinating Schwann cells, covering the NMJ and
contributing to the synaptic formation, maintenance, and repair.
The synaptic cleft is the gap between the pre-synaptic terminal and the post-synaptic mus-
cle membrane. The muscle membrane is filled with a specialized extracellular matrix
protein called the basal synaptic lamina. This is crucial for NMJs’ alignment, structural
integrity, and organization. The basal lamina consists of laminins and different types of
collagen, which interact with cellular receptors to regulate synapse development and func-
tion [14]. The enzyme acetylcholinesterase is attached to the basal lamina; this enzyme is
essential for muscle contraction. If ACh remained in the synaptic cleft, it would continu-
ously bind to acetylcholine receptors, causing continuous muscle contraction. However,
due to this enzyme, ACh is broken down into acetyl and choline, neither of which can
activate the acetylcholine receptors [15].
Across the synaptic from the synaptic end bulb is the sarcolemma, the motor end plate, or
postsynaptic membrane in the context of NMJs. There is one NMJ for each muscle fiber,
typically located near the center of the fiber. There are two functions of the motor end
Development of a 3D Bioprinted Neuromuscular platform: Engineering Functional Human Skeletal
Muscle with Integrated Motor Neurons
5
==PAGE:16==
plate. First is the presence of junctional folds. These junctional folds are deep grooves
of the sarcolemma, which provide a large surface area for the ACh and synaptic bulb to
interact. The second function is that, with the junctional folds, there are 30-40 million
acetylcholine receptors. These receptors are transmembrane proteins that function as ion
channels once they are activated [15].
The binding of ACh to the acetylcholine receptors opens the ion channels, which allow
for the influx of sodium and trigger an action potential that travels from the end plate
throughout the muscle. This causes muscle shortening and thus a contraction [15].
2.2
The Extracellular Matrix
The Extracellular Matrix (ECM) is a complex network of macromolecules that provides
structural and biochemical support to cells and tissues. In skeletal muscle, the ECM com-
prises various components, including proteoglycans, glycoproteins collagen, and elastin
[16]. This complex assembly supports cells mechanically, sustains biochemical signal-
ing, and is key for skeletal muscle regeneration. The components of the ECM further
interact with cell receptors that regulate cell migration, proliferation, and differentiation
[17]. The collagen of the ECM forms the intramuscular connective tissue (IMCT) and,
thus, the central fibrous components. The IMCT is organized into three layers: (i) the en-
domysium, being the innermost layer, that encloses the muscle fibers individually; (ii) The
perimysium bundling groups of muscle fibers; and (iii) the epimysium, which envelopes
the entire muscle. The IMCT contains various forms of collagens where types I and III
are the most abundant. The endomysium interacts with the sarcolemma, which consists
of most of the laminin and collagen type IV [16].
2.2.1
ECM-mimetic hydrogels for in vitro tissue engineering
Most engineered tissues are made with 3D hydrogels derived from natural ECM proteins
such as collagen or fibrin. Which is due to hydrogels capability of providing a high area of
cell adhesion, providing mechanical support, maximizing diffusion, and degradation once
cells can take over and produce their own ECM [18].
2.2.1.1
Fibrin for Muscle tissue engineering
Fibrin is the main structural component of blood clots. It is also a natural component of
the ECM, which plays a critical role in the body by initially preventing bleeding and then
undergoing remodeling, resorption, and replacement over time. These dynamic properties
make fibrin an excellent candidate for skeletal muscle tissue engineering [18]. Fibrino-
gen can be gelled by adding thrombin, a serine protein that converts fibrinogen into a
fibrin hydrogel [19]. However, a notable disadvantage is its rapid degradability. This
limitation can be addressed by regulating fibrinolysis through the use of cross-linkers and
anti-fibrinolytic agents such as 6-Aminocaproic acid (ACA)[18].

2.2.1.2
Geltrex for neural tissue engineering
In neural tissue engineering, extracellular ECM proteins such as laminin have been widely
demonstrated to significantly enhance cellular behaviors, including migration, adhesion,
and differentiation, and mimicking the native ECM of the CNS. Laminin has further
proved efficient in increasing ACh receptors when combined with agrin in vitro [20].
Despite its proven efficacy, laminin is expensive and, therefore, not suitable for large-
scale or long-term applications. To address this limitation, basement membrane extracts
such as Geltrex, rich in laminin, collagen IV, and growth factors, are commonly used as
cost-effective alternatives. This hydrogel closely mimic the natural ECM environment of
neural tissue, creating a supportive 3D matrix that promotes NSC expansion, differentia-
tion, and survival in vitro. Geltrex also enhances the structural integrity of neuronal cul-
tures, ensuring that cells maintain proper morphology and establish functional networks.
Furthermore, their gel-like properties facilitate cell embedding, particularly useful in 3D
culture systems that mimic the in vivo environment [21].

Skeletal Muscle
Development and maintenance of adult muscle tissue require functional innervation of
the muscle [18]. Spontaneous contractions of in vitro skeletal muscle tissue are charac-
terized by the ability of muscle tissue to contract without external stimulation. Differenti-
ated skeletal muscle tissues with myotubes can exhibit spontaneous contraction activation.
This is primarily due to the excitation-contraction coupling mechanism, which involves
membrane depolarization and, thereby, intracellular calcium spikes [27]. The role of a
neural input can be studied in a 3D environment using electrical stimulation by provid-
ing external electrical inputs, typically received from motor neurons. However, long-term
electrical field stimulation can induce electrochemical damage and cell death in the tis-
sue. While it can mimic a neural input, it can not completely mimic the physiological and
chemical interaction between muscle and nerve [18].

3D printing for Tissue engineering

2.4.1
Extrusion bioprinting
Extrusion bioprinting is an emerging technique to apply or deposit bioinks or biomaterial
options to make 3D constructs that can recapitulate the biological and mechanical proper-
ties of human tissue. Extrusion bioprinting is, therefore, a suitable option for constructing
in vitro tissue models for testing or validating newly developed drugs.
Extrusion bioprinting systems are configured to deposit bioinks layer-by-layer to create
3D constructs. Bioprinters usually resemble the schematic shown in Fig. 2.7. The Printing
systems typically consist of a printing head, a three-axis positioning system, and a printing
stage, all connected to a computer.

2.4.1.1
Embedded bioprinting
Extrusion bioprinting is a powerful technique to fabricate living tissue constructs. How-
ever, it is challenging to print weak hydrogels precisely layer-by-layer. Thus, embedded
bioprinting using extrusion bioprinting is an emerging strategy to fabricate complex struc-
tures derived from soft materials within a reservoir with a supporting matrix. In this way,
hydrogels, such as fibrin, are put into a stable print. During the printing procedure, the
printing head deposits the bioink inside the supporting matrix, which facilitates the for-
mation and stability of the temporary architectures. Once printing is complete, two post-
printing strategies can applied. The printed bioink is removed, or the support matrix is
[29]. For this project, strategy 2 is applied, where the bioink is cross-linked inside the
support gel, which is then removed by enzymatic degradation.

Alginate Support
The microjammed alginate support was made by suspending 1% (w/v) alginate (80-120
cP, Wako) in 50 mL MilliQ water in a 100 mL glass jar. The suspension was stirred for 2
hours on a magnetic stirrer in an 80 ◦C preheated water bath. Equal volumes of sterile 2
mg/mL CaCO3 and the alginate solution were mixed and stirred at room temperature on
a magnetic stirrer for one hour. After 1 hour, acetic acid (99. 99%) was added in a 1:500
ratio and vigorously stirred at 650 rpm at room temperature for at least four hours.
The gelled solution was then mechanically fragmented by homogenization at 15.000 rpm
for 10 minutes using an Ultra-Turrax homogenizer (IKA) with a T25-S25N-10G dispens-
ing tool. The solution was then divided into 50 mL falcon tubes, and 0.5% (v/v) chloroform
was added for sterilization. The solution was centrifuged at 18.500 G for 20 minutes, and
the supernatant was discarded. The particles were resuspended in F10 medium (Ham’s

then centrifuged at 18.500 G for 20 minutes. The leftover media was discarded, leaving a
pellet of F10 media + 1% (v/v) P/S immersed in microjammed alginate particles.
Meanwhile, the freeze-dried carboxymethylated nanofibrillated cellulose (cNFC20) fiber

Contractile Force Calculations
The contractile force was calculated from stiffness values provided by K. Pedersen, who
performed stiffness measurements of both thicknesses of the ribbons. This yielded the two
2nd degree polynomials in Supplementary Fig. A.1. She then determined that deflections
up to 400 μm per side fit a linear polynomial, which gave the values 1.68 ± 0.12 μN/μm
for the 150 μm ribbon and 2.75 ± 0.13 μN/μm for the 200 μm ribbon (Supplementary table
A.1). These values were then multiplied by the compaction, which gave the contractile
force.

standard deviation (STD), compaction percentage, and contractile force were calculated.

Prism v.10, where statistical analysis using a two-way ANOVA test was performed. This
data was then visualized using a bar graph, where significant data was included.

Temperature trial

yield comparable results to those observed in the initial bioink trial. Additionally, we
decided to print on 200 μm ribbon platforms to evaluate whether the increased ribbon
thickness would produce different outcomes. The room-temperature plate was also paced
to assess if this impacted the results.

5
Discussion
This chapter is divided into sections covering media composition, differentiation condi-
tions, and functional analysis of NSC34 and HSkM co-cultures. The first part focuses on
the effects of retinoic acid (RA) and neurotrophic factors on NSC34 differentiation and the
impact of serum-free conditions. The second part discusses co-culture outcomes by as-
sessing neuronal and neuromuscular junction marker expressions. The third part discusses
3D bioprinting for NMJ formation, focusing on bioink optimization and mechanical prop-
erties. Finally, contractile properties of mono- and co-cultures are discussed to evaluate
tissue functionality.
5.1
Formulation of co-culture medium
When formulating a co-culture medium, we started by optimizing the differentiation of
NSC34 in the sense of optimizing the medium for neurite elongation. After this optimiza-
tion, we moved on to co-culture HSkM and NSC34 to determine if the both cell types were
supported using CoC differentiation medium.
5.1.1
Retinloic Acid supplementation
When developing a Co-culture medium to support neurite outgrowth and NSC34 differ-
entiation, we initially focused on retinoic acid (RA), a crucial metabolite of vitamin A
known to play an essential role in nervous system development. Specifically, all-trans
retinoic acid has been shown to increase neurite outgrowth from several types of neuronal
cells [34]. Our initial approach involved formulating a simple co-culture differentiation
medium, with a 50:50 medium for each cell type and a trial on different concentrations
(0 μM, 1 μM, 5 μM, and 10 μM) of all-trans RA was added to the formulation. In single
NSC34 cultures it showed the longest neurite lengths to be ≈40 μm, with a concentration
of 5 μM; thus, a different formulation had to be formulated to increase the neurite length.
5.1.2
Serum-free and Neurotropic Factor supplementation
A study by Maier et al. reported that serum deprivation in combination with RA showed
that cells were matured and expressed cholinergic markers [11]. Therefore, muscle basal
media (MB-2222) was exchanged with muscle differentiation medium (MD-5555), sup-
plemented with N2 and B27 instead of muscle supplement (MS-3333). Where N2 is a
serum-free supplement recommended for the growth and expression of neuroblastomas
as well as post-mitotic neurons in primary cultures, and B27 is an optimized serum-free
supplement used to support the low- or high-density growth and short- or long-term via-

bility of several neuronal cell types [35] which for a co-culture model with C2C12 murine
muscle cells and NSC34 proved to provide the best condition for co-differentiation [36].
In addition to supporting neuronal survival and activation of choline acetyltransferase,
glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor (GNDF) was added to the formulation, as well as
brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), which has been proven to promote the survival
and/or differentiation of motor neurons in vitro [37]. With this combination, the longest
average neurites extensions for the co-culture medium formulation were measured to be
58.19±8.42 μm for NSC34 utilizing skeletal muscle growth medium (CGM) as a growth
medium and 67.20±23.55 μm for NSC34 utilizing neuronal growth medium (NGM) as
a growth medium. However, with both types of the co-culture medium, even with the
serum-free switch, the proliferation of the NSC34 was observed, thus potentially inhibit-
ing the motor neurons’ maturation process [36]. The ability of RA to induce post-mitotic
neural phenotypes in various stem cells in vitro provided early evidence of its role in
regulating the switch between proliferation and differentiation [38]. By day 10 of differ-
entiation in the RA trials, cultures treated with 10 µM RA exhibited reduced proliferation
compared to those exposed to lower RA concentrations (Fig. A.4). This observation could
be due to RA’s ability to reduce proliferation in neuronal cells. Thus, increasing the RA
concentration to 10 µM may effectively reduce proliferation [38]. Other studies, such as
one by Samara et al., suggest that motor neuron cells first need to undergo serum starvation
and RA treatment, followed by exposure to BDNF [35].
5.2
Co-culturing
When culturing NSC34, it was observed that the longest neurites were extended using
NGM as a growth medium. Although, when co-culturing NSC34 and HSkM, utilizing
NGM yielded inefficient myotube formation and a low α-actinin response and, thereby,
maturation of HSkM. MB-2222 is specifically formulated for the growth and maintenance
of HSkM and is commercially formulated, meaning that it is unknown which growth fac-
tors are included and could be excluded from NGM. However, from the differentiation
experiments of NSC34, it was proven that neurite extensions were formed using CGM
(Fig. A.5 and 4.2), although not as extended as the ones utilizing NGM. CGM did how-
ever , support growth and differentiation of both cell types, thereby proving to be optimal
for co-culturing.
5.2.1
Internexin and α-Bungarotoxin response
For single NSC34 cultures, an internexin response was observed, thereby, a mature dif-
ferentiation of NSC34. This was, however, not the case when co-culturing as no signal
of either internexin or α-bungarotoxin was returned. Where internexin presence indicates
neuronal maturation, α-bungarotoxin shows nicotinic acetylcholine receptors at the neu-

differentiation of NSC34 and that no neuromuscular junctions between the two cell types
were present. A study by Delfini et al. found that the misexpression of mouse Ectopic

cell–motor neuron interactions [10]. This effect is further present in the 3D dome cultures
shown in Fig. 4.5, where neurite extensions, though slightly blurred, appear elongated,
and immunostaining revealed a strong internexin response. This presents a potential so-

to both 2D and 3D cultures.
5.2.2
Co-culturing of 3D tissue
To assess whether 3D culturing promotes sufficient differentiation of both HSkM and
NSC34 cells, as well as NMJ formation and innervation, a bioink formula (Bioink 3)
was developed. This formula was identical to Bioink 2, except for adding a 10:1 HSkM:
NSC34 ratio, as used in the 2D co-culture experiments. When co-culturing the 3D bio-
printing tissue, two different medium formulations were utilized: the one formulated
through 2D culture experiments (CoC1, Table 3.2) and the commercial Curi Bio differ-
ential medium optimized for 3D culturing, with the same supplements added, making the
main difference the lack of neurobasal medium in the commercial medium and CoC1 not
being optimized for 3D conditions. There are, however, studies where both Neurobasal
and MD-5555 mediums have been utilized for 3D culturing, deeming them potentially
suitable for 3D tissues [40][41].
When co-culturing NSC34 cells with HSkM cells, the 3D co-cultures appeared thicker and
more uneven compared to single HSkM cultures (Fig. 4.11). This unevenness in tissue
structure may explain the significant differences in compaction and contractile force ob-
served between co-cultures and mono-cultures (Table 4.2; Figs. 4.16 and 4.13). It further

5.3
Contractions of tissue
For both single and co-cultures, it was noticed that no spontaneous or passive contrac-
tions were observed, except for one of the single HSkM on day 14 of differentiation and
7 of pacing. For the 2D simultaneous and sequential cultures, this is most likely due to
incomplete NSC34 differentiation, leading to lacking NMJ formation and, thus, inner-
vation. It could, however, further be due to NSC34 axons being demyelinated in vitro,
compared to human axons, which in vivo are myelinated. This could potentially hinder
nerve signal propagation, which is critical for initiating a muscle contraction. Thereby,
myelination of NSC34 could potentially lead to signal propagation and contractions in the
co-cultures. A study by Takaku et al. successfully myelinated NSC34 by incorporating
adult rat Schwann cells (IFRS1) [42]. Therefore, co-culturing Schwann cells with NSC34
could lead to myelination and, thus, ACh signaling from NSC34 to HSkM, leading to
spontaneous contractions of co-cultures.
Spontaneous contractions were observed at 4x magnification under a bright field micro-
scope, along with high contractile forces in mono-cultures, after seven days of pacing.
This indicates that electrical stimulation strengthens the tissue compared to non-paced
counterparts and facilitates passive and spontaneous tissue contractions. During pacing,
the tissue was imaged using a customized camera; however, no active contractions were
detected within 10 minutes of observation. This does not necessarily mean active con-
tractions were absent, as the tissue’s spontaneous contractions were clearly visible at 4x
magnification in the bright field microscope after pacing. The inability to detect active
contractions on the camera could be attributed to factors such as poor camera resolution
and motion blur, the stiffness of the ribbons, or insufficient electrical stimulation, result-
ing in small active contractions that were too subtle to be captured visually through the

No passive nor active contractions were observed in co-culture tissues; this could be due
to insufficient innervation, as well as the low compaction and, thereby, contractile forces,
compared to the mono-culture counterparts (Fig. 4.13 and 4.15). However, it can be
observed that just as the mono-cultures, paced co-cultures generally compacted more,
making it evident that applying electrical stimulants exercises the tissues, making them
stronger overall than their non-paced counterparts. In Bursac et al., this was likewise ev-
ident when comparing their paced tissues to their non-paced control counterpart, which
showed that pacing increased the contractile force by a factor of three [31].
5.3.1
Ribbon thickness and stiffness
The mechanical properties of engineered 3D muscle tissues are strongly influenced by
the characteristics of their anchor points [43]. As tissues were cultured, differences in
compaction were observed under varying conditions. After 14 days of culture, tissue
compaction was manually measured using ImageJ. These measurements revealed that the
highest compaction occurred in the paced mono-cultures with 150 μm ribbons, while the
lowest compaction was observed in the co-cultured tissues in the CoC1 medium. While
these observations could indicate that these tissues were weaker, the compaction alone
does not necessarily correlate with tissue strength. The different ribbon thicknesses (150
μm vs. 200 μm) significantly impact the force required for tissue deflection, as thicker
ribbons exhibit greater stiffness.
The contractile force exerted by cells within a tissue and passive tension can be quantified
by measuring the displacement of elastic substrates or microwires in response to mus-
cle contraction. The stiffness values of the two platforms, as measured by K. Pedersen
(the producer of the devices), demonstrated that the 200 μm ribbons were substantially
stiffer than the 150 μm ribbons (Table 4.2). Specifically, the force required to deflect a
single 150 μm ribbon by 1 μm was 1.68 ± 0.12 μN, whereas the same deflection for a
200 μm ribbon required 2.75 ± 0.13 μN. This indicates that the 200 μm ribbons required
61.09% more force for the same degree of deflection compared to the 150 μm ribbons.
Consequently, while the 150 μm platforms exhibited greater compaction, the increased
stiffness of the 200 μm ribbons necessitated more force for tissue compaction (Fig. 4.15).
This is further supported by Vandenburg et al., who states that greater compaction does
not necessarily indicate stronger tissues; instead, it reflects a structural response to en-
vironmental constraints such as differences in anchor point stiffness [44]. This finding
indicates that the strongest tissues formed were those cultured on the 200 μm ribbons, as
the increased stiffness of the anchor points provided greater mechanical resistance. This
is consistent with observations by a previous study by Engler et al. who demonstrated
that stiffer anchor points enhance tissue maturation and contractile strength by improving

mechanotransduction [45]. A study by Kang et al. further demonstrated that appropriate
anchoring can enhance cell alignment and promote myotube fusion and differentiation,
essential for effective contractions [46].
The high stiffness of the anchor points/ribbons leads to no observation of active contrac-
tions of the tissue. It further illustrated when compared to the biowire system of Zhao et
al. Using their force/displacement formula, a 1 μm displacement in their biowire system
required 0.37 μN of force. This indicates that the 150 μm ribbons are approximately 5
times stiffer, and the 200 μm ribbons are approximately 7 times stiffer than their system.
Although this platform was created for cardiac tissue, thereby, not tested with skeletal
muscle tissue. It does however, indicate that the ribbons in this project are too stiff com-
pared to the strength of the tissue for active deflection to be observed.
It was further observed that as the tissue became stronger by applying electrical stim-
ulance, the more likely they were to de-attach from the ribbons or rupture (Table 4.2),
thereby leading to higher failure. Stiff anchoring points can lead to tissue breakage when
subjected to pacing. Several studies have reported that if the anchoring points for skeletal
muscles are excessively stiff, the muscle fibers cannot deform actively, leading to local-
ized stress. This can lead to micro-tears or complete ruptures of the tissue, specifically
during repetitive contractions [47] [48][49]. Another study by Wang et al. reported that
appropriate anchoring, whether too stiff or too compliant, can hinder the mechanical per-
formance and stability of tissues. It further states that the mechanical properties of the
environment, such as the anchoring points, strongly influence cellular tension, leading to
necking of the tissue; if the system has an overly stiff anchoring point, the midpoint of
the tissue will become high stress zone, which will eventually lead to mechanical failure
[43]. Thus, the stronger the tissue gets on the stiff ribbons, the more likely the tissue will
be to tear. Therefore, reducing the ribbon stiffness would lead to lower failure and higher
tissue survival.
5.4
Bioink conditions
The reason for testing new bioink conditions was that we wanted to lower the cell con-
centration compared to the cell concentration of 10-20 mio cells/mL in GBH and TRW
bioprints, both to lower the costs of a bioprint and to match the Curi Bio cell-laden hydro-
gel conditions. When reducing the cell numbers, we suspected that xanthan gum could
potentially cause issues, as it could hinder cell-cell interactions, causing weaker tissues
and higher failure rates. Xanthan gum is a high-viscosity gel-like material that can im-
pede the mobility of muscle cells. This can hinder the ability of the cells to migrate and
interact with each other, which is vital for the formation of functional muscle tissue. Re-
search suggests that bioinks getting too viscous can restrict cell penetration and reduce the

Muscle with Integrated Motor Neurons

==PAGE:63==
effective surface area available for cell-cell interaction [50]. Thereby reducing the xan-
than gum from 0.25% to 0.1% produced stronger tissues with lower failure rates, as seen
in Fig. 4.9, where Bioink 2 with 0.1% compared to Bioink 1 with 0.25 % is superior in
anchor attachment, compaction, and failure rate.
To determine the optimal bioink deposition, three different flow rates (250 nL/mm, 500
nL/mm, and 1000 nL/mm) were tested (Table 4.1). A comparison of these tissues on day
10 of differentiation revealed that the 500 nL/mm bundles exhibited the classic dog bone
shape (Fig. 4.9), which is desired as it promotes the alignment of multi-nucleated muscle
fibers and supports the native development of muscle tissue [2]. For this reason, a flow
rate of 500 nL/mm, resulting in a bundle volume of 22.80 μL, was selected for further
prints. This volume closely matches that of Bursac et al., whose bundles exhibited active
contractions during pacing [31]. However, unlike their results, active contractions were
not observed in our bundles during pacing. Active contractions were also observed in the
study GBH/TRW large bundles, with a deposited ink volume of 33.4 μL. It is important
to note that the cell densities used in the studies by Bursac (14.7 million cells/mL), GB-
H/TRW (20 million cells/mL), and Zhao (55 million cells/mL) were significantly higher
than the density used in our system (approximately 6 million cells/mL, Table 2.1), suggest-
ing that a higher cell concentration may increase tissue strength. This is further supported
by Neufurth et al. , who states that sufficient cell density is crucial for proper nutrient
supply and tissue integrity, indicating that higher cell concentrations can support better
tissue formation and mechanical strength [51]. Additionally, it should be noted that Curi
Bio’s system, which uses the same cell density as ours, has a significantly larger bundle
volume than the other tissues. This suggests that the cell density or bundle volume should
be increased to achieve stronger tissues capable of active contractions on our ribbon plat-
form.
The bioink was previously printed at 5 ◦C and not room temperature since Geltrex remains
liquid at this temperature. Increasing the printing temperature could lead to premature
gelation and potentially increase the ink viscosity. While printing, the increased ink vis-
cosity could increase resistance during extrusion, resulting in clogging of the nozzle [52].
Another issue that could potentially arise is the effect that high-viscosity bioinks can have
on cells, as shear and stress can cause cell viability to decrease [53]. However, in the case
of printing at 5◦C vs. room temperature, no significant differences were seen in either
ease of printing or bundle failure (Fig. 4.10). Showing that the increase in gelation and
viscosity did not cause any significant stress leading to apoptosis.

Conclusion
In alignment with the first aim of this project–developing a co-culture medium optimized
for the differentiation of both HSkM and NSC34–initial trials focused on enhancing NSC34
differentiation through RA supplementation. A concentration of 5 μM was identified as
the most effective for promoting neurite elongation. However, since the average neurite
length only reached approximately 40 μm, additional modifications, including serum-free
conditions and neurotrophic factor supplementation, were introduced to enhance differ-
entiation while minimizing NSC34 proliferation (CoC1; Neurobasal (48%), MD-5555
(48%), RA (5 μM), GDNF (10 ng/mL), BDNF (10 ng/mL)), N2 (1X), B27 (10X) and
agrin (1 ng/mL). These adjustments increased the average neurite length to approximately
60 μm. Despite these optimizations, co-culturing HSkM and NSC34 did not result in
neuromuscular junction formation, as seen by the absence of spontaneous contractions,
internexin, and α-bungarotoxin. This failure could be attributed to species incompati-
bility, misexpression of myogenic differentiation factors, or excess serum in the muscle
differentiation medium. Furthermore, the serum-free conditions did not attenuate the pro-
liferation.
In alignment with the second aim, bioink conditions were optimized. Bioink 2 ( Fibrinogen
(5 mg/mL), XG (0.1%), HSkM (6 mio/mL), F10+ACA (≈60 %), and Geltrex (20 %))
demonstrated the lowest failure rate, well-defined dog bone-like bundle structures, and
printability at room temperature, making it suitable for skeletal muscle bioprinting.
Transitioning to 3D cultures presented structural challenges in co-cultures compared to
mono-cultures, including increased thickness and heterogeneity, likely due to excessive
NSC34 proliferation. This proliferation may have hindered myotube fusion, impairing
passive tissue contraction. While neither active nor passive contractions were observed in
3D mono- or co-cultures, paced mono-cultures exhibited greater compaction and contrac-
tile forces compared to their non-paced counterparts, confirming that electrical stimulation
enhances tissue strength. However, co-cultures displayed lower mechanical integrity, re-
ducing compaction and contractile force. Additionally, thicker ribbons (200 μm) required
61.09% more force to compact than thinner ribbons (150 μm), indicating stronger tissue
formation. However, the stiffness of both anchoring points and ribbons likely contributed
to the absence of observable active contractions during pacing and increased failure rates
due to necking under mechanical stress.
For future studies, the co-culture medium should be optimized by increasing the RA con-
centration and serum-depriving NSC34 cells before adding supplements such as BDNF.

Future Directions
To successfully engineer a neuromuscular junction (NMJ) on this specific platform, sev-
eral modifications and strategies could be implemented. A sequential co-culture approach
appears to yield the best outcomes for these cell types, as the differentiation medium for
HSkM contains a higher serum concentration than is optimal for differentiating NSC34
cells into motor neurons. Therefore, independently differentiating both cell types before
co-culture is likely the most effective strategy.
For example, HSkM tissue could be cultured for two weeks simultaneously but separately
by differentiating NSC34 cells into spheroids. Following the method by Afshar Bakooshli
et al., these spheroids could then be manually embedded in a Geltrex matrix and positioned
on the platform ribbons near the mature HSkM muscle tissue [2]. A co-culture medium,
similar to that used in Fig. 4.11 for co-cultures with Curi Bio commercial differentiation
medium with supplements (N2, B27, GDNF, BDNF, and RA) where the RA concentration
could increase to 10 μM, could then support the continued differentiation of both cell types,
while potentially suppressing the proliferation of NSC34. This would encourage neurite
outgrowth and eventual interaction with the muscle tissue, facilitating the formation of
NMJs. However, this sequential approach reduces automation, making it less desirable
than a simultaneous seeding strategy. If simultaneous seeding on the platforms were to
happen, the cells should be printed independently, where the NSC34 could be printed
on the ribbons, near the HSkM tissue. However, a low serum co-culture medium that still
supports HSkM differentiation should be developed for this approach. A solution to lower
the stiffness of the ribbons should further be addressed to decrease the overall failure rate
as the tissues get stronger and to allow for active deflection of the ribbons.
To reduce the stiffness of the ribbons, several methods could be applied. In this study 6
mm in length ribbons were utilized, though from stiffness testing it can be seen by increas-
ing the length of the ribbons for both 150 μm and 200 μm ribbons the stiffness decreases
(Fig. A.1). Thereby increasing the length of the ribbons, could be a potential solution
for the device. Otherwise post-processing could be investigated, in Zhao et al., it can be
seen that when post-processing at 70 ◦C, the stiffness of the wires increase the longer they
are exposed to high temperatures [22]. Thereby lowering the post-processing tempera-
ture or the exposure time could decrease the stiffness of the ribbons. If these parameters
were investigated, active and spontaneous contractions of the tissue could potentially be
observed, and device failure could be significantly reduced.
Another strategy for successful simultaneous seeding is to modify the device to compart-

Protocol: Hand-cast Gelatin substrates
Author Name: Asli Aybike Dogan
Date: January 4, 2024
Place: DTU Health Tech
Equipment:
●
Fume hood
●
50 mL Falcon tubes
●
Micropipette
●
Pipette tips
●
Vortex mixer
●
Water bath set to 37°C
●
Syringe filter with 0.45 μm pore size
●
Refrigerator set to 4°C
Materials:
●
PBS (phosphate-buffered saline)
●
Low bloom gelatin (48723 Sigma-Aldrich)
●
Chloroform (for sterilization)
●
mTG (ACTIVA TI microbial Transglutaminase—1002–1 K, 100 U/g)
Procedure:
Gelatin Solution Preparation
1.
Under the fume hood, prepare 50 mL of PBS including 0.5% chloroform. Don't
remove any of the PBS - just add 250 ul chloroform.
2.
Divide the 50 mL PBS+chloroform solution into 5 different 50 mL Falcon tubes.
Note: A 10 mL aliquot of the PBS+chloroform solution is sufficient for each gelatinized
coating solution.
3.
In each Falcon tube, add low bloom gelatin (Sigma) to obtain an 8% w/v gelatin
solution (0.8 grams of gelatin per falcon tube with the 10 ml in).
4.
Gently shake the Falcon tube up and down to mix the gelatin thoroughly.
5.
Avoid vigorous shaking, as this can create bubbles and prevent complete gelatin
dissolution.
6.
Place the Falcon tubes in a water bath set to 37°C.
7.
After 30 minutes, check if the gelatin has completely dissolved.
==PAGE:75==
8.
If not, gently shake the Falcon tubes upside down to facilitate complete
dissolution.
9.
Continue incubation for 30 minutes more. Do not incubate the gelatin solution
for longer than 1 hour, as prolonged exposure to high temperature may cause
gelatin degradation.
mTG Solution Preparation
1.
Weigh 1.2 g mTG powder and dissolve it in 50 mL of PBS.
2.
Gently mix the solution upside down to ensure thorough dispersion of the mTG
powder.
3.
Filter the mTG solution through a 0.45 μm syringe filter to sterilize it. Filter it into
a falcon tube. Use a 50 mL syringe. You can clean and reuse the syringe for next
time (only for mTG)
Crosslinking of Gelatin with mTG
1.
Remove the gelatin solution from the bath and carefully mix it with the activated
mTG solution in a 1:1 ratio under the laminar flow hood.
2.
Immediately cast the gelatin-mTG mixture onto the desired substrate, ensuring a
thickness of approximately 1 mm (130 μL/cm²). For differentiation purposes, the
number of cells is 30.000/cm^2, remember this to make sure choosing the right
culture dish.
3.
Incubate the coated substrate at 4°C for 15 mins and 37°C for 4 hours to allow
crosslinking to occur.
Washing Steps
1.
After crosslinking, keep cast gels in the fridge at 4C or start washing steps by
adding 1 mL PBS into substrate wells.
2.
Incubate the substrate in PBS for 30 minutes under the fume hood or incubator
to remove unbound gelatin and other impurities.
3.
Gently rinse the substrate with PBS to remove any remaining debris.
4.
Equilibrate the substrate with growth media for at least 30 minutes and remove
the equilibration media before seeding cells.
Additional Notes:
●
The plates should be tightly covered with Parafilm and placed in plastic bags under the
LAF bench before incubation steps to ensure the humidity in place and avoid
contamination risk.
●
The washing steps are crucial for removing unbound gelatin and ensuring optimal cell
adhesion.
●
The washing steps can be carried out during the trypsinization period, as the
==PAGE:76==
incubation time is relatively short.
●
The gelatin-coated substrate can be stored without PBS at 4°C up to 1 month and in
PBS at 4°C for up to 1-2 weeks before use.
