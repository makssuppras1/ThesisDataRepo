Abstract
The present thesis develops a novel method to perform an N-1 contingency assessment using
quantum computation. The contingency analysis is based on the direct-current power ﬂow
(DC-PF) and the line outage distribution factors (LODFs). To encode the problem into
a quantum circuit, the power transfer distribution factors (PTDF) matrix and the LODF
matrix are factorized into unitary matrices via the singular value decomposition (SVD)
and the linear combination of unitaries (LCU) method. A case study is then solved to
benchmark the results obtained through classical and quantum approaches. The quantum
circuit is run via state vector simulation. To conclude, there is a discussion surrounding
when quantum computation for operational security applications in power systems could
provide an advantage over classical computation methods.
Index Terms – Quantum Computing, LODF, PTDF, N-1 Contingency Assessment

Introduction
Power systems are complex infrastructures, so their real-time operation is exceptionally
challenging. Ensuring a secure and reliable electricity supply is paramount. Therefore,
numerous and constant calculations and checks, necessary to steer the system to its opti-
mal operation without violating any system constraints, are being performed at any time.
Among such analyses, the present project focuses on power ﬂow calculations and contin-
gency assessment, more speciﬁcally, N-1 contingency assessment.
The electric power system is shifting from a centralized (natural gas, coal, nuclear, etc.) to a
distributed generation (solar, wind, etc.) due to transitioning towards a more environmen-
tally conscious electricity generation to tackle climate change and reduce CO2 emissions.
Conventional generation is dispatchable and deterministic. However, distributed renew-
able energy sources (RES) are stochastic regarding resource availability. This increasingly
high integration of RES and power electronics into the power system presents signiﬁcant
challenges, such as capacity forecasting and system stability, since it is harder to schedule
electric power generation and its impact on the operational security of the grid.
Power ﬂow calculations are essential for analyzing the steady-state operation of a power
system. Nonetheless, these calculations assume normal operating conditions, neglecting
any potential impact from component failures or outages. It is essential to determine if
a power system would still operate within its limits if a failure occurs. Consequently, the
N-1 contingency analysis investigates, one at a time, whether a generation or transmission
component failure would result in an insecure power system operation. For this reason,
by evaluating the system’s response to such contingencies, operators can identify vulner-
abilities, develop mitigation strategies, and maintain the system’s reliability [1]. Under
this high RES penetration scenario, such analyses are laborious, and the time taken to do
them on a classical computer escalates the larger the system exponentially. Therefore, new
methods to tackle these diﬃculties should be developed.
The advent of quantum computing has unveiled a new computation paradigm that could
revolutionize many ﬁelds, including power systems engineering. Quantum computing har-
nesses the principles of quantum mechanics to process and solve problems in ways that,
in some cases, surpass the capabilities of classical computers. It has already been proven
in other ﬁelds that, for some applications, these calculations could be done quadratically
or exponentially faster. For instance, Shor’s Algorithm [2] to factorize large integers or
Grover’s Algorithm [3] for unstructured search.
This exceptional computational power generates expectations around its potential to ad-
dress the complex calculations, such as optimization and simulations, inherent in power
system analysis. For example, the work in [4] demonstrates that quantum amplitude es-
timation (QAE) can be used to estimate risk measures with a quadratic speed-up over
classical Monte Carlo simulation. Thus, this technique could better model stochastic RES
to extract critical ﬁgures such as the value at risk (VaR) or the conditional value at risk
(CVaR). Furthermore, others have implemented quantum algorithms for power ﬂow appli-
cations utilizing the HHL algorithm [5], which could yield exponential speed-up.
On the other hand, as regards to operational security of the power system, quantum com-
puting could enable N-1 contingency assessments on large-scale power systems considering
multiple failure scenarios simultaneously within a reasonable time [6]. This could lead to

more robust contingency plans, improved grid resilience, and reliable and secure power
system operation [7].
Although there is literature about how to solve a power ﬂow using quantum computation
methods, the author has identiﬁed that there is no literature regarding how to perform
an N-1 contingency analysis via the same approach. That is why, to widen the portfolio
of available tools for power system analysis, this research investigates the application of
quantum computing techniques to conduct an N-1 contingency assessment. This project
seeks to address the computational challenges associated with analyzing multiple failure
scenarios simultaneously by leveraging the unique properties of quantum computing.
The main objectives of this project are brieﬂy stated below and will be discussed next:
• Use quantum computations to perform power ﬂow calculations
• Perform power system contingency assessment using quantum computations
• Identify where quantum computations for contingency assessment can provide an
advantage over classical methods
First of all, the classical power ﬂow problem must be encoded as a quantum circuit. Once
this is accomplished, the base steady-state power ﬂows necessary for the subsequent con-
tingency analysis can be determined. Second is to design the innovative method to encode
and execute the N-1 contingency assessment as a quantum circuit. Lastly, in a qualitative
approach, the third goal seeks to provide insight and leads to recognising the potential of
the developed methodology over classical methods. In general terms, The objective is to
create the methodology to design and implement a quantum-based algorithm to do an N-1
contingency assessment and benchmark it with the classical method.
The classical power ﬂow problem is simpliﬁed to its direct current equivalent. This will
outline the steady-state power ﬂows of the system. As per the contingency assessment, the
work focuses on transmission component outages, in particular, line outages. The study is
perform using the well-known line outage distribution factors.
As this project is state-of-the-art research, the limited existing literature, knowledge and
expertise may compromise reaching any signiﬁcant ﬁndings in the time scope of the project.
Moreover, the ongoing development of quantum computing software may also present some
challenges as it is constantly evolving.
In summary, this thesis aims to pave the way for the future development of quantum
algorithms for N-1 contingency assessment based on the direct current power ﬂow and
line outage distribution factors. The potential impact of near-term ﬁndings could revolu-
tionize the power system industry by signiﬁcantly enhancing and accelerating computing
capabilities, empowering grid operators to make more robust and informed decisions.
Undertaking a thesis in quantum computing for power systems is a profoundly motivat-
ing endeavour for me. As an engineer, and someone who is inspired by knowledge and
innovation, the opportunity to explore the intersection of quantum computing and power
systems engineering is thrilling. This work has given me a window to combine my passion
for power systems with cutting-edge technology, such as quantum computing, with hopes
to make a small but meaningful contribution to advance the understanding and application
of quantum computing in power systems.

2.1
Introduction to Quantum Computing
Although the reader is assumed to be familiar with the topic, this section reviews the
essential literature of interest regarding quantum computing and quantum information.
Following next in section 3.3, an introduction to quantum computing and its use in the
present work is provided.
The best way to start this quantum journey is by reading the book Quantum Computing
and Quantum Information by Michael Nielsen and Isaac Chuang [8]. It has been appointed
as an excellent source textbook that provides a comprehensive introduction to quantum
computing and quantum information theory. The authors present complex concepts in a
plain and compact form. It covers a wide range of topics, including but not limited to
quantum gates, algorithms, cryptography and error correction techniques. Furthermore,
practical applications of quantum computing are also explained. The book reﬂects some
of the most prominent advancements in the ﬁeld up until its publication to ensure that it
is relevant for both industry and academia.
Two quantum algorithms have revolutionized the ﬁeld of quantum computing. These are
Shor’s and Grover’s algorithm. In 1994, Peter W. Shor published a seminal paper titled
Algorithms for quantum computation: discrete logarithms and factoring [2], which intro-
duced a groundbreaking quantum algorithm capable of eﬃciently factorize large integers
and solve discrete logarithms in polynomial time. These were traditionally very challenging
problems. Its direct impact is on the ﬁeld of cryptography since some public-key cryptog-
raphy systems, such as RSA, are based on the complexity to factorize large numbers using
classical computers.
Shor’s algorithm is cited as a compelling application of quantum
computing which has prompted the research into post-quantum cryptographic algorithms.
On the other hand, in 1996, Lov K. Grover introduced another unprecedented algorithm
known as Grover’s algorithm, which proved that it is possible to signiﬁcantly speed up un-
structured database search using quantum computing principles [3]. The paper discusses

2.2
Quantum Algorithms for Linear Systems of Equations
The DC-PF problem is expressed as a system of linear equations. To solve the former via
quantum computations, a suitable method is required. This is the Harrow, Hassidim and
Lloyd (HHL) algorithm.
In 2009, Harrow, Hassidim and Lloyd published a paper titled Quantum Algorithm for
Linear Systems of Equations. They proposed a novel quantum algorithm for solving a
linear system of equations (A⃗x = ⃗b), where A is a large and sparse Hermitian matrix, ⃗x is
the unknown solution vector, and⃗b is the input vector. The algorithm is designed to extract
an approximation of the expectation value of some operator associated with the solution
vector ⃗x rather than solving the system to obtain the complete solution vector itself [11]. It
involves quantum state preparation, phase estimation, eigenstate transformation, and ﬁnal
classical post-processing. If appropriately used, this algorithm has an exponential speed-
up over the best classical algorithm (conjugate gradient method). Despite its limitations
[12], it represents a signiﬁcant breakthrough in the quantum computation and quantum
information ﬁeld and an enabler for future development.
Understanding such a critical quantum computing algorithm can be challenging. There-
fore, the step-by-step guide in [13] has been found better to comprehend the underlying
principles of such an algorithm. The paper explains the HHL algorithm analytically fol-
lowed by a numerical example in bra-ket notation of a 4-qubit system. The Qiskit code
that builds the quantum circuit of the previous example is provided so it can be simulated
on IBM Quantum cloud platform. The Appendix contains a review of basic concepts that
supports the narrative. Last of all, a tutorial that also introduces the HHL algorithm
can be found on Qiskit’s website [14]. It guides the reader through diﬀerent stages which
encompass deriving a circuit, implementing it using Qiskit, and running it via a simulator
or a real backend.

tions are worth mentioning for studying the classical load ﬂow problem.
The book Power system stability and control by Prabha S. Kundur is an extensive and
vital reference for stability and control studies of power systems [1]. In this project, it is

primarily used to study the classical alternating-current power ﬂow (AC-PF) problem, the
fast decoupled load ﬂow (AC-FLDF) [15] and the direct current power ﬂow (DC-PF).
Due to the nature of the system of equations drawn by the AC-FDLF and the DC-PF
problems, numerous authors have developed quantum power ﬂow algorithms based on the
HHL algorithm. Although each application varies or has diﬀerent features, they all have
similar hurdles.
Several papers that represent the state-of-the-art regarding quantum power ﬂow are brieﬂy
outlined next. If deemed appropriate, the reader can omit these reviews and move forward
to the last paragraph in this section.
Eskandarpour et al. "Experimental Quantum Computing to Solve Network DC Power Flow
Problem"
The paper investigates how the DC-PF problem can be solved using quantum computing.
The HHL algorithm is used for such a purpose. The suggested implementation uses Qiskit
and simulates via the IBM Quantum cloud platform. It is successfully tested on a small-
scale system. However, the HHL-based approach may present scalability issues for larger
power systems. Furthermore, it is concluded that the current NISQ hardware diﬃcult the
comparison against theoretical limits [16].
Fang Gao et al. "Solving DC power ﬂow problems using quantum hybrid algorithms"
This publication elaborates on the performance considering limitations of the current Noisy
Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) hardware. Since the phase estimation stage of the
HHL algorithm is imperfect, it proposes a quantum-classical hybrid approach that is ulti-
mately proven to achieve comparable precision with fewer qubits than the standard HHL
realization. The performance is analyzed for diﬀerent accuracy and qubit size [17].

As previously stated, the author has not found any literature about N-1 contingency anal-

Two key concepts are investigated, the power transfer distribution factors (PTDFs) and

This paper introduces a novel method of calculating PTDFs based on cycle ﬂows, which is

procedure to compute PTDFs and LODFs. It is stated that the cycle ﬂow description
provides a conceptual advantage since it deals directly with power ﬂows and does not

Song et al. "Implementation of PTDFs and LODFs for Power System Security"
Since cascading faults triggered by line overloading detriment power system reliability, this

shift required to adjust the power ﬂow of overloaded lines back to secure limits in the event
of a single or multi-line outage [22].
Güler et al. "Generalized line outage distribution factors"
Distribution factors are widely used for many security assessment applications. The in-

PTDFs and LODFs, are derived. The sever impact of cascading outages in recent blackouts
has driven research towards alternative manners to evaluate LODFs under multiple-line

under multiple-line outages without the need to reevaluate post-outage system parameters.

work, [24] proves that the LODFs for multiple-line outages can be obtained in terms of the
PTDFs of the pre-contingency network.

3.3
Quantum Computing
Quantum computing is an emerging technology and ﬁeld of research set to revolutionize
various aspects of computation and information technology. It leverages the principles of
quantum mechanics to develop powerful computational systems. Quantum computers use
quantum bits or qubits, which constitute its basic unit of information. Qubits exploit two
phenomena in quantum mechanics, superposition and entanglement. These unique char-
acteristics enable quantum computers to handle certain calculations exponentially faster
than their classical counterparts [8].
Superposition refers to the ability of a qubit to exist in multiple states simultaneously. For
instance, a qubit in superposition can simultaneously represent 0 and 1 with certain prob-
abilities so that, upon measurement, it will yield 0 and 1 according to the corresponding
probabilities [29]. On the other hand, entanglement is a phenomenon where two or more
qubits are correlated in a way that the state of each qubit cannot be described indepen-
dently of the others. This correlation holds even if the entangled qubits are physically
separated. Both phenomena play a crucial role on increasing computation capabilities and
enabling parallel computation.
The so-called state vector representation is used to characterize the state of a quantum
system (one or multiple qubits). The entries of a state vector are complex numbers named
amplitudes. Each is related to the probability of measuring that particular state. State
vectors are useful to manipulate quantum states.
The operators used to evolve a quantum system are denominated unitary operators. As
any physical operation on a state involves modifying the Hamiltonian and utilizing it to
evolve the state, any operation applied to a quantum state must be unitary [30]. Unitary
operators are reversible, preserve the probabilities associated with quantum states, and
form the basis for quantum gates. These are the equivalent to classical logic gates. Some
well-known quantum gates include the Hadamard gate, Pauli gates (X, Y, Z) and the
CNOT gate.
Quantum computing is a paradigm shift in information technology and has the potential
to transform industries. Its potential applications expand across domains such as opti-
mization, cryptography (see Shor’s Algorithm [2]), drug discovery, machine learning and
ﬁnance. Understanding the concepts of qubits, state vectors, and unitary operators is cru-
cial to design and develop quantum algorithms capable of surpassing classical computation.

The process of encoding an arbitrary rectangular matrix to a quantum circuit comprises

For the second step, two mathematical tools are used: the singular value decomposition
(SVD) and the linear combination of unitaries (LCU).
The SVD is a powerful mathematical tool used to decompose a rectangular matrix into
the product of three matrices: two unitary matrices (U and V H) and a diagonal matrix
(Σ) [34]. This decomposition is widely used in ﬁelds such as mathematics, engineering
and data analysis, as it provides valuable insights into the structure and properties of
the original matrix, for instance, signal and image processing, data compression, matrix
approximation, etc.

into a quantum circuit as a unitary operator. However, the diagonal matrix Σ is usually
not unitary. Therefore, it must be decomposed as a combination of unitary matrices. The
second mathematical instrument is named LCU, which is used to break down the diagonal
matrix Σ into a sum of unitary matrices.

4.4
Discussion
The classical implementation has proven an eﬀective method to perform an N-1 contin-
gency analysis based on DC-PF and LODF. As per the quantum implementation, let it be
re-stated that it is possible to perform an N-1 contingency analysis using quantum compu-
tations. Besides, the results drawn via simulation match those obtained classically. Most
of the discussion hereafter is centred around the quantum method.
The ﬁrst remark is about the scalability of the system. The current application relies on
three operations, the PTDF, LODF and PFC gates. Each encodes a sparse rectangular
(PTDF) or square (LODF and PFC) matrix that is not unitary. The SVD and the LCU
have been used to factorize such matrices into unitary operations. Nonetheless, each time
the LCU is used, another auxiliary qubit is added to the system, increasing the width of
the quantum circuit. This has a negative impact and makes the proposed solution not
optimal. Reducing the width of a quantum circuit is critical to avoid unnecessary noise
in current NISQ hardware. Therefore, ﬁnding alternatives to the current LCU approach
would help to decrease the circuit’s width.
Regarding the state vectors shown during the process, it has been proven that amplitude
encoding is practical for tracking how the system evolves after each operation. Another
drawback of the current LCU method is that the Hadamard gates add junk information
to the top half of the state vector. Since the amplitude of these undesired states is more
signiﬁcant than those under monitoring, extracting the results by circuit measurement
is diﬃcult as the probability of measuring the monitored states is lower.
Given these
circumstances, a method to amplify the amplitudes of the ﬁrst half of the state vector
would be useful.
As per the scaling used to extract the results, the compound scaling factor which yields
from multiplying eq. (4.12), eq. (4.13), eq. (4.14) and eq. (4.15) has a value of 36 units.
Although it may seem reasonable, it also hinders obtaining the results from the quasi-
probabilities because the amplitudes have a smaller absolute value after all the gates.
It has been demonstrated that the quasi-probabilities obtained through simulation match
the amplitudes of the state vector, and vice versa. The objective of the current implemen-
tation is to detect if a line is overloaded. For this reason, the fact that the sign of the
power ﬂow is lost upon measurement does not impact the solution. The only information
lost is the direction of the power ﬂow, which could be helpful, however, it is unnecessary
in this case.
Last of all, as a curiosity, the quantum circuit has also been run using an actual backend.
However, due to the current NISQ hardware, the results were not accurate enough to be
considered in the present work. Nevertheless, the rapid development of quantum computing
hardware and better error correction techniques could make executing the current quantum
implementation on a real quantum computer feasible.

5
Conclusion
The DC-PF is a suitable method to rapidly and continuously calculate a power system’s
active power line ﬂows. Although it is a linearization of the classical AC-PF problem, it
suﬃces to estimate the AC line power ﬂows, plus convergence is assured. Since knowing the
value of the voltage angles was not necessary for this project, the PTDF matrix has been
built and used to compute the line power ﬂows given the net active power bus injections.
A N-1 contingency analysis has been performed under DC-PF conditions via the well-
known LODFs.
These factors are derived from the notion of a power transaction and
measure how the power ﬂow of an interrupted line is redistributed to the remaining lines
in the system. The post-contingency power ﬂows for all N-1 contingencies are calculated
using the LODFs. Any scenario leading to line overloading is not considered to be secure.
As per the quantum approach, the SVD combined with a LCU can be used to encode
an arbitrary rectangular matrix (such as the PTDF, LODF and PFC) into a quantum
circuit. However, the present approach regarding LCU challenges system scalability. The
auxiliary qubit used on each matrix block (see Figure 4.17) unnecessarily increases the
circuit’s width. Besides, the Hadamard gates on the latter qubit make it diﬃcult to read
the results from the quasi-probabilities obtained through circuit measurement since they
amplify the amplitudes of undesired states.
It has been veriﬁed that the results obtained via state vector simulation and circuit mea-
surement match. The only information lost upon measurement is the power ﬂow direction,
which is not needed to conduct the N-1 contingency assessment in this project. Last of
all, at the time of writing, running the current implementation on a real backend yields
extremely noisy results, thus not being suited for a real case application yet.
It has been conﬁrmed that quantum computations can be used to perform DC-PF and
N-1 contingency analysis. The proposed methodology is appropriate for constructing a
quantum circuit that simultaneously computes the active power line ﬂows for the base case
and all N-1 scenarios. The quantum circuit has been conceived in a modular fashion to
facilitate the implementation of new features and debugging.
The proposed solution can be optimized and improved. More research is needed to tailor
an implementation that could outperform the current methods using classical computing.
That being said, this project paves the way for the future development of quantum algo-
rithms for N-1 contingency assessment based on DC-PF and LODFs. Near-term ﬁndings
have the potential to revolutionize the power system industry by greatly improving com-
puting capabilities. This would empower grid operators to make more informed decisions,
leading to a more robust and eﬃcient power grid.

Future Work
Several work items have been identiﬁed to enhance the current implementation. The two
that would have an immediate impact on the proposed method are described in further
detail below:
• Introduce an observable to extract a state vector’s maximum value (or inﬁnity norm)
or to determine its location. During the project, this has been one line of investiga-
tion which is still open. Two approaches have been considered: using Grover’s search
algorithm to detect the location of the biggest amplitude; and developing an observ-
able that would be applied to the solution vector of the HHL algorithm, which would
yield the maximum value of the state vector. The latter could lead to exponential
speed-up.
• Reduce the qubit width of the quantum circuit for the N-1 contingency assessment.
First, this could be done by discarding the cases where the post-contingency line
power ﬂow is known to be zero beforehand. These cases correspond to the diagonal
elements of P C
line. On the other hand, other LCU methods which do not require an
ancillary qubit could be adopted [38].
Following the ﬁrst bullet point, if such an observable could have been implemented and
tested, the HHL algorithm would have had more prominence in the present work. The
author thinks combining the latter observable with the HHL algorithm could present an
opportunity to design and develop an algorithm with a clear advantage over classical com-
putation methods.
Finally, other tasks that could also contribute to this project are enumerated next:
• Test other quantum algorithms for systems of linear equations [39] based on the HHL
algorithm
• Investigate other LODF implementations based on graph theory [40]
• Substitute the deterministic generation with stochastic RES to apply Quantum Monte
Carlo methods as in [4], which has proven to provide quadratic speed-up (QAE)
• Expand the research to the classical AC-PF and the AC-FDLF problem formulation
