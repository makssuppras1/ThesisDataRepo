Abstract
The field of EEGôÙbased authentication has witnessed significant advancements in recent
years, driven by the increasing demand for secure and efficient access control systems.
However, traditional models face challenges in capturing the complex spatial and temporal
dependencies present in EEG signals, hindering their ability to achieve high accuracy.
This research explores the application of the Transformer model, originally designed for
natural language processing, in the domain of EEGôÙbased authentication to address these
limitations.
The study begins with an extensive literature review, highlighting the potential advantages
of the Transformer model in modeling sequential data and longôÙrange dependencies. Two
EEG datasets, the PhysioNet EEG Motor Movement/Imagery Dataset and the BCI ComôÙ
petition IV Dataset 2a, are thoroughly described, along with essential preprocessing techôÙ
niques to ensure data quality.
The Transformer model is studied, emphasizing its attention mechanism, selfôÙattention,
and multiôÙheaded attention capabilities. A modified Vision Transformer model is adjusted
to handle the specific characteristics of EEG data, such as spatial and temporal depenôÙ
dencies The model is adapted for EEG data classification, utilizing a channel attention
mechanism to weight different feature channels within EEG data, enhancing the modelãs
perception capabilities and reducing interference between channels.
Through a series of experiments, the performance of the modified ViT model is comôÙ
pared with traditional models, including CNNs and LSTMs. The results demonstrate the
Transformerãs ability to effectively capture longôÙrange dependencies and outperform conôÙ
ventional models in EEGôÙbased authentication tasks.

Introduction
Authentication systems are designed to verify the identity of users and grant them access
to protected resources, such as online accounts, databases, or digital services, which
play a critical role in ensuring the security and integrity of our personal and sensitive inforôÙ
mation. Traditionally, authentication methods have relied on something the user knows,
such as passwords or PINs, as well as something they possess, like physical tokens or
smart cards. While these methods have been widely adopted, they have inherent limiôÙ
tations. Moreover, as technology advances, the need for more robust and userôÙfriendly
authentication methods becomes increasingly evident. One such approach is EEGôÙbased
authentication, which leverages brainwave patterns recorded through ElectroencephalogôÙ
raphy.
1.1
EEGôÙBased Authentication
The human brain, which contains approximately 86 billion neurons, communicates primarôÙ
ily through electrical signals. Electroencephalography (EEG) is a nonôÙinvasive technique
for measuring the electrical activity of the brain through the placement of electrodes on
the scalp. It detects and records the collective firing of neurons, which results in distinct
brainwave patterns. A sketch showing an example EEG setup is depicted in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Sketch of how to record an Electroencephalogram.[1]
These patterns are distinctive to each individual, making them possible biometric identiôÙ
fiers for authentication purposes.
Due to the damping effect of the skull, the measured electric potentials have small amôÙ
plitudes spanning from 10 V to 100 V, requiring signal amplification. The amplitudes of
subdural grid electrodes are up to 103 times stronger[2]. To ensure that results are deôÙ
tectable and comparable across individuals, standardized electrode positions have been
established. Jasperãs 10ôÙ20 system[3], which was introduced in 1958, and Chatrian, LetôÙ
tich, and Nelsonãs 10ôÙ10 system[4], which was proposed in 1985, are two of the most
common electrode implantation systems.

EEGôÙbased biometric classification authentication, as shown in Figure 1.3 yellow part, inôÙ

The Transformer Model
The Transformer model was a sequenceôÙtoôÙsequence model when it was first introduced,
and the key feature that sets it apart is the use of selfôÙattention. To process a sequence,
the most common thing that comes to mind is to use the Recurrent Neural Network (RNN).
Its input is a sequence of vectors, the output is another sequence of vectors, and it can
only compute sequentially from left to right or from right to left.
The modelãs ability to parallelize is constrained by this process, which makes the calculaôÙ
tion at each time step dependent on the computation results of earlier time steps.
And also, the gradient disappearance problem will always exist with RNNs due to their
inherent flaws. As you can see from this simple RNN equation 1.1:

domain of EEGôÙbased authentication. By leveraging the inherent selfôÙattention mechaôÙ

EEGôÙbased authentication refers to the verification and identification of individuals using
electroencephalogram (EEG) signals. Due to its potential to provide secure and userôÙ

2.1
EEGôÙBased Authentication
EEGôÙbased authentication involves capturing and analyzing the electrical activity of the
brain using electrodes placed on the scalp. Several studies have explored the potential
of EEG signals for authentication purposes. These studies employ various techniques,
including eventôÙrelated potentials (ERPs), frequencyôÙdomain analysis, and timeôÙdomain
analysis, to extract meaningful features from EEG signals.
2.1.1
Datasets
EEG datasets consist of recordings obtained from multiple electrodes placed on the scalp,
capturing brain signals in realôÙtime. These datasets contain a wealth of information about
various cognitive and neural processes, such as motor control, sensory perception, atôÙ
tention, memory, and emotion.
Researchers and clinicians leverage EEG datasets to study brain responses under differôÙ
ent conditions, identify neurological disorders, and investigate brain activity during specific
tasks or stimuli. These datasets are instrumental in understanding brain dynamics, mapôÙ
ping functional connectivity between brain regions, and uncovering abnormalities that may
indicate the presence of neurological conditions or cognitive impairments.
Public EEG datasets, in particular, have significantly contributed to the progress of the
field, allowing researchers worldwide to access standardized data and collaborate on diôÙ
verse research projects. There are some famous public datasets shown in appendix B.
These datasets are typically accompanied by detailed annotations, behavioral tasks, and
demographic information about the participants, enabling researchers to contextualize the
recorded brain activity and draw meaningful conclusions.
The usage of EEG datasets spans a wide range of applications, including brainôÙcomputer
interface development, brainôÙmachine interaction, sleep analysis, emotion recognition,
seizure detection, and cognitive workload assessment. Moreover, EEG datasets serve
as benchmarks for evaluating the performance of novel algorithms and machine learning
techniques in various brainôÙrelated tasks.
In this era of big data and advanced analytics, EEG datasets continue to be a valuable
resource, fueling advancements in neuroscience and facilitating groundbreaking discoverôÙ
ies. As technology evolves, the collection, sharing, and analysis of EEG datasets promise
to unravel the mysteries of the human brain and inspire innovative applications to improve
human health and wellôÙbeing.

2.1.2
Related Research
In recent years, there has been significant research focused on developing robust EEGôÙ
based authentication methods. Studies have investigated different approaches for feature
extraction, classification algorithms, and authentication protocols. These approaches aim
to accurately identify individuals based on their unique brain activity patterns recorded in
EEG signals.
Traditional machine learning approaches have been used for MIôÙEEG signal classificaôÙ
tion, involving preprocessing, feature extraction, and classification steps. Preprocessing
includes channel selection, signal filtering, signal normalization, and artifact removal. FeaôÙ
ture extraction techniques encompass temporal, spectral, and spatial features, with comôÙ
mon spatial patterns (CSP) being widely used. Classification is performed using various
algorithms such as naive Bayesian classifier, linear discriminant analysis (LDA), support
vector machine (SVM), and extreme learning machine (ELM).
However, traditional methods face challenges such as noise interference, low signalôÙtoôÙ
noise ratios (SNRs), subject dependency, and the need for manual feature extraction.
These limitations have motivated the adoption of deep learning (DL) algorithms for MIôÙ
EEG classification. DL can automatically learn complex features from raw data, eliminatôÙ
ing the need for extensive preprocessing and manual feature extraction. DL models have
shown remarkable performance in various fields and have been applied to EEGôÙbased
brain signal classification.
Researchers have explored the use of advanced machine learning techniques, such as
deep learning models, to improve the performance of EEGôÙbased authentication. Deep
learning architectures have shown promise in capturing complex patterns within EEG sigôÙ
nals and achieving higher authentication accuracy. Table 2.1 shows the taxonomy of the
deep learning models applied to EEG classification.
Discriminative Models, such as CNNs, were a popular DL model using EEG data. They
excel in extracting local and spatial patterns from EEG data. CNN architectures consist
of convolutional layers for feature extraction, pooling layers for dimensionality reduction,
and fully connected (FC) layers for classification. The study in [7] proposed a light CNN
architecture with few parameters to classify different EEG tasks using raw signals and
achieved remarkable performance. Contrarily, in [8], the authors presented a CNN model
for multiple MI classification with features extracted from MI data using the FBCSP apôÙ
proach. In [9], a temporalôÙfrequency image representation was proposed using a WT and
coupled with a CNN model to classify MI signals, achieving an accuracy of 85.59% in a
dataset with four MI classes [10].
RNNs, particularly Long ShortôÙTerm Memory (LSTM) and Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU)
networks, were also utilized for temporal feature extraction from sequential EEG data.
LSTM models are capable of learning longôÙterm relationships and overcoming vanishing
gradient issues, while GRU is a simplified version of LSTM with a lighter architecture.
Kumar et al. [7] used an LSTM model combined with CSP and SVM for MI classification.
The study proposed an adaptive method for frequency band selection using a genetic
algorithm, achieving an average accuracy of 69.59% using a twoôÙclass dataset.
Representative DL models refer to DL architectures that specialize in feature extraction
in an unsupervised manner, which can be used for various tasks, such as clustering and
classification. Representative DL models include deep AEs (DôÙAEs), deep RBMs (DôÙ
RBMs), and DBN. Hassanpour et al. [11]proposed a stacked sparse AE model, defined

3.2
Data Preprocessing
Data preprocessing is an essential step in working with EEG data to improve the quality
and reliability of the signals. In the context of EEG data, preprocessing involves converting
raw data into a format that is more suitable for analysis and interpretation.
3.2.1
The requirement for preprocessing
Preprocessing EEG data is important for a number of reasons. First of all, because the
spatial information is lost, the signals detected on the scalp may not accurately reflect the
signals coming from the brain. Second, there is often a lot of noise in EEG data, which
might mask weaker EEG signals. Blinking or muscle movement artifacts can taint the data
and skew the image. The goal of this step is to distinguish the important brain signals from
the random neural activity that is detected during EEG recordings.
3.2.2
Filter
In EEG data processing, filtering refers to the application of digital filters to the raw EEG
signal in order to remove unwanted noise or extract specific frequency bands of interest.
Filters are used to shape the frequency content of the EEG signal and improve the signalôÙ
toôÙnoise ratio.
There are different types of filters commonly used in EEG data processing, including highôÙ
pass filters, lowôÙpass filters, bandôÙpass filters, and notch filters:
ãÂ HighôÙpass filter: A highôÙpass filter attenuates or removes lowôÙfrequency components
from the EEG signal, allowing only highôÙfrequency components to pass through.
This filter is useful for removing drift, baseline wander, and other lowôÙfrequency arôÙ
tifacts.
ãÂ LowôÙpass filter: A lowôÙpass filter attenuates or removes highôÙfrequency components
from the EEG signal, allowing only lowôÙfrequency components to pass through. This
filter is used to remove highôÙfrequency noise, such as electromagnetic interference.
18
EEGôÙbased Authentication With Transformer
==PAGE:26==
ãÂ BandôÙpass filter: A bandôÙpass filter allows a specific frequency range, known as the
passband, to pass through while attenuating frequencies outside this range. It is
useful for isolating specific frequency bands of interest, such as alpha (8ôÙ12 Hz) or
beta (12ôÙ30 Hz) rhythms.
ãÂ Notch filter: A notch filter is designed to attenuate a narrow range of frequencies,
typically centered around the powerline frequency (e.g., 50 Hz or 60 Hz) and its
harmonics. It is used to remove powerline noise and related interference.
To implement filtering in EEG data processing, digital filter designs are typically used,
such as Butterworth, Chebyshev, or elliptic filters. These filters can be implemented using
various algorithms, such as finite impulse response (FIR) or infinite impulse response (IIR)
filters. The filter parameters, such as the cutoff frequency or the width of the passband or
stopband, can be adjusted based on the specific requirements of the EEG analysis.

The split_data function aims to partition the dataset into distinct subsets for training, valôÙ

and unbiased subsets. The procedure can be summarized as follows:
Input: dataset, val_split, test_split, channel_index_start, channel_index_end
Output: (x_train, y_train, x_val, y_val, x_test, y_test)
1. Initialize empty arrays: x_train, y_train, x_val, y_val, x_test, y_test
2. Calculate the number of distinct classes in the dataset: no_of_classes
3. For each class in the dataset, do the following:
ãÂ Retrieve the indices of the samples belonging to the current class.
ãÂ Calculate the number of samples for validation and testing using the provided split
ratios (val_split and test_split).
ãÂ Randomly select a set of indices for validation samples (val_idx) without replaceôÙ
ment from the available indices.
ãÂ Remove the selected validation indices from the original indices to prevent their
inclusion in the training or testing sets.
ãÂ Randomly select a set of indices for testing samples (test_idx) without replacement
from the remaining indices.
ãÂ Remove the selected testing indices from the remaining indices, leaving only the
samples intended for training.
ãÂ Append the corresponding samples and labels to the appropriate arrays: x_val,
y_val for validationëƒ x_test, y_test for testingëƒ x_train, y_train for training.
4. Shuffle the training, validation, and testing data arrays to introduce randomness and
minimize bias during subsequent operations.
5. Return the shuffled training data (x_train, y_train), validation data (x_val, y_val), and
testing data (x_test, y_test) as tuples.
This data split strategy ensures that each class contributes to the training, validation, and
testing subsets in proportion to their representation in the original dataset. By randomly
selecting samples for validation and testing without replacement, the function guarantees

The Vision Transformer has demonstrated impressive results, outperforming traditional

Each layer in the provided table has a specific function in the overall architecture of the
model:
LayerNorm: Layer normalization is applied to normalize the input tensor across the specôÙ
ified dimensions. It helps in improving the stability and convergence of the model during
training.
Linear: The linear layer performs a linear transformation on the input tensor, where each
element of the output tensor is a weighted sum of the input elements. It introduces nonôÙ
linearity to the model.
Dropout: Dropout is a regularization technique that randomly sets a fraction of input units
to zero during training. It helps in preventing overfitting and improves the modelãs generôÙ
alization ability.
AvgPool2d: Average pooling is applied to reduce the spatial dimensions of the input tenôÙ
sor. It computes the average value of each nonôÙoverlapping patch in the input tensor,
resulting in a downscaled tensor.
Conv2d: Convolutional layers apply filters to the input tensor, performing a convolution
operation. They are used to extract features from the input data.
BatchNorm2d: Batch normalization is applied to normalize the input tensor by subtracting
the batch mean and dividing by the batch standard deviation. It helps in reducing the
internal covariate shift and accelerating the training process.
LeakyReLU: Leaky ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) is an activation function that introduces
a small negative slope for negative input values, allowing a small gradient to flow during
backpropagation and mitigating the dying ReLU problem.
Rearrange: Rearranges the dimensions of the input tensor according to the provided
pattern, enabling reshaping operations.
channel_attention: This custom module implements the channel attention mechanism,
which is used to highlight important channels in the input tensor. It performs linear transôÙ
formations, pooling operations, and attention calculations to produce an output tensor that
emphasizes relevant channel information.
MultiHeadAttention: The multiôÙhead attention layer implements the selfôÙattention mechaôÙ
nism. It computes attention scores between different positions in the input tensor, allowing
the model to focus on different parts of the input sequence. It uses linear transformations
to project the input tensor into query, key, and value representations, and then calcuôÙ
lates the attention scores using matrix multiplication. The attended values are obtained
by combining the attention scores with the values and are projected back to the original
dimension.
ResidualAdd: This module performs a residual connection by adding the input tensor to
the output tensor of the inner function. It helps in preserving and propagating the gradients
and allows the model to learn more effectively.
FeedForwardBlock: The feedôÙforward block consists of linear transformations, activation
functions (GELU), and dropout regularization. It introduces nonôÙlinearity and complex
mappings between the input and output tensors.

nonôÙlinearity to the model. It is used to model the relationships between the input and
