Abstract 
Consumption of non-alcoholic and low-alcoholic beverages is increasing with popularity not 
only amongst people with health or moral issues but amongst everyone with conscious mindset. 
Moreover, mead as an alcoholic beverage is trending because of the natural and more 
sustainable ingredients. Production of low-alcoholic mead is desirable because wine industries 
prefer low-alcoholic versions over non-alcoholic because of impacts on flavours. Optimisation 
of production process for low-alcoholic mead is dependent on fermentation conditions and 
parameters viz, temperature, honey concentration, typed of yeast, yeast dosage and other 
related process like pasteurization. Decrease in the amount of honey added and replaced by 
malt categorises the mashing process in beer-mead hybrid. Addition of malt can eventually 
decrease the generation of ethanol during the fermentation process by using the yeast strains  
from WhiteLabs® which are negative towards maltose and longer chains. Decreasing the 
fermentation temperature inactivates yeast performance partially and decreases the rate of 
sugar conversion. However, fermentation temperature is also dependent on yeast strains and 
their optimal temperatures. Preliminary sensory analysis helps to select more favourable yeast 
strains viz., Lalvin® EC1118 and WhiteLabs® WLP603. Further analysis of yeast doses added 
suggest the chemical reaction during fermentation process is limited by sugar content, as 
intended. HPLC analysis of ethanol and sugar content for all the samples suggest that lower 
ethanol content at 10 0C and 3 – 4 weeks of fermentation time. Pasteurization at 60 0C for 15 
mins does not affect sugar or ethanol content. However, enhances overall flavour and aromas. 
Futurtistic studies can incorporate studies related to efficiency and economics for the 
appropriate choice for the yeast strains and fermentation conditions.

1. Introduction 
Mead or Honey wine, is an alcoholic drink based around fermentation of the mixture of honey and 
water. It is not supposed to be super alcoholic and therefore, the usual alcohol content ranges from 7 - 
22 % (ABV) 2,3. However, the alcohol content and the taste of mead is majorly dependent on the 
quantity, quality and type of honey and time of fermentation 4. Mead or Tej is the national drink of 
Ethiopia5. Currently, the main ingredients of the mead are: Honey, Water, Yeast 6. 
 
Out of the many styles of the modern fermented drinks, mead is relatively easy to produce because of 
its limited requirements in terms of special equipment or ingredients. As per some headlines, 
consumption of mead has observed a steep increase of 42 %, partly credited to multiple movies and TV 
shows set in historic medieval periods displaying authenticity 6. Mead is accounted as the most popular 
reintroduced alcoholic beverage that disappeared post industrialization. According to the reports from 
2013, Poland is the largest producer of mead at 1.2 million litres per year 7. 
 
1.1. History 
Mead (Miodh in Old Irish) is one of the oldest fermented drinks in the world and woven in global 
history. Few discoveries around the world, especially in the ancient cultures of China, India, Greek or 
Egypt, have dated mead or honey wine back to pre-historic period i.e., as early as 6500 BC. Whereas, 
it has been discovered in Europe, especially in Scotland, between 2800 - 1800 BC. It is also mentioned 
as ‘Nectar of Gods’ in numerous mythologies viz., Greek, Celtic, Old Norse 5,6,8.   
 
Anglo-Saxon cultural history and ancient linguistic evidence refer to bees and honey and also about 
honey and fermented grains. Archaeological research alongside folklore around Nordic Scandinavian 
brewing traditions about mead range from Viking Bronze Age to Neolithic times. Viking history and 
myths dictate that mead (or Mjød) used to be consumed for taste to cover up for incorporated with herbs 
and/or mushrooms. And the induced psychotropic properties and hallucinations helped the Viking 
people before battles. It was also considered being medicinal with associated health benefits and the 
properties and nutrients of the added herbs 4. 
 
1.2. Traditions 
Invention of mead could have possibly been accidental as honey contains many microbes and could 
have fermented naturally when stored in open air. Mead was produced traditionally, with dilution of 
honey with water in clay or wooden vessels and stored in air and therefore, fermented by airborne 
yeasts. Mead was then matured and rested for more than 12 months to increase the alcohol content up 
to 16 % and develop flavours, before consumption 4,6. 
 
Since commercialization of sugar in the 16 – 17th  Century, production of mead was superseded by 
cheaper options like beer, wine or ales with faster maturation and resting period. Even though prices of 
honey have increased at the cost of deforestation, the importance of natural ingredients have restored 
the value of the mead in terms of the repopularised tasty drink. Few of the new-age yet culturally apt 
producers use traditional methods of production of mead and follow the old recipe and methods with 
added alcohol to limit the period of maturation 6. 
 
1.3. Types 
Etymology of the word ‘Mead’ suggests often references to an ale or a boozy substance and therefore, 
mead could be categorized as a collective with varieties with combination of ingredients, tastes and 
techniques. Since sugar is fermented into alcohol, types of sugars fermented classifies the type of 
alcohol: Grapes/Fruits with added sugar is used in wine, Malted grains are fermented into beers while 
in commercial meads, honey is the major source of fermentable sugars 4.

1.4. Method 
The common recipe includes 2 kg of Honey added to 3.5 L of Water to produce 5 L of Mead. Lemons 
and/or tea is added occasionally, to darken the colour of the mead. Maturation period of 12 months and 
fermentation period of 4 – 6 weeks are the most time consuming steps 6,7. Fermentation step is dependent 
on the type of honey, pH of mead wort, yeast and yeast nutrients 3. Saccharomyces cerevisiae is the 
most common species of the fresh yeast used for production of mead 7. 
 
2. No-Alcohol and Low-Alcohol Trends 
Consumption of alcohol or alcoholic drinks is still considered as social lubricant at formal or informal 
events worldwide and until recently, consumption of alcohol was associated with being ‘cool and hip’. 
However, with increasing studies about side-effects of consumption of alcohol has changed the scenario 
and fetched in a natural consciousness in people across the globe.  
 
Consumption of alcohol directly or indirectly causes ~ 3 million deaths every year worldwide because 
of the genotoxic and carcinogenic nature of ethanol and intoxication induced accidents. Some studies 
suggest that ethanol consumption of ~ 350 g per week reduces average life span by 4 – 5 years by 
increasing the likelihood of serious health issues (viz, liver, stroke, cancer, etc.), mental disorders and 
accidents, homicides and injuries 10,11. Therefore, an increase in promotion and potential health benefits 
of low- or non-alcoholic drinks works as a better strategy than conventional methods like: restrictions 
on availability and drinking and price hikes 10.

3. Research Strategy 
Low alcoholic version of mead can be brewed with either physical or biological methods. While 
physical methods like thermal de-alcoholization and membrane separation tend to be expensive, 
biological methods with modifications in the mashing and fermentation regimes pose to be viable 
option. These include changes in mashing temperatures, choice of selective strains of yeast, etc. 
 
At the first step, alteration in mashing temperature and time reduce the overall content of fermentable 
sugars and therefore, limits the alcohol generation. Secondly, the modifications of fermentation 
conditions can result in partial inactivation yeast. Also, use of different strains of yeasts with varying 
selectivity of sugar conversion can result in reduced % ABV 18,19. 
 
3.1. Parameters 
3.1.a. Substrate Concentration 
Since fructose is the main simple sugar along with glucose and sucrose, the first and most effective way 
of reduction in the amount of fermentable sugar in wort is by limiting the amount of added honey. 
Moreover, addition of malt produces a beer-mead hybrid and therefore, need for the sugars is partially 
fulfilled by maltose and other non-fermentable sugars for the taste and mouthfeel.  
 
3.1.b. Mashing Conditions 
Secondly, the extent of the fermentable sugar extraction into the wort is controlled by temperature, time 
and pH of the mashing process. A single step cold extraction at 50 0C for 2 h can limit the extraction of 
% fermentable sugars in total sugars. Extraction at higher temperatures in the range of 75 – 80 0C can 
convert polysaccharides into maltose. However, the taste is impacted. Therefore, a combination of 
different enzyme activities and associated temperature regimes can yield a suitable mixture of simple 
and complex sugars for low-alcoholic version with sweet taste and perfect mouthfeel 18–20. 
 
3.1.c. Types of Yeast Strains 
Different species of yeasts with varying favourable conditions of temperature and alcohol tolerance directly 
impact the fermentation process. Saccharomyces cerevisiae is the most common strain of yeast with 
medium alcohol tolerance and wide range of fermentation temperature. However, some strains like 
Metschnikowvia pulcherimma have a low alcohol tolerance and lower optimal temperature and are 
usually used in sequential fermentation with S. cerevisiae for interesting sensory qualities. Moreover, 
the non-conventional brewing strains from WhiteLabs® are selectivity refrain from fermenting maltose 
or other larger sugars. These wild strains are isolated from fruit trees in Denmark and produce esters 
resulting in fruity flavours 21–26. 
 
3.1.d. Yeast Dosage 
Recommended doses of the yeasts suggested by the manufacturers are based on optimal temperatures, 
normal fermentation times and certain alcohol tolerance levels. Concentration of the yeast added during 
the inoculation will directly affect the fermentation process, taste and the alcohol content. High 
concentration can lead to a faster fermentation or internal competition for the available sugars. While 
low concentrations might slow down the fermentation process or might not initiate at all. 
 
3.1.e. Temperature 
Extensive studies have shown that the optimal temperature for fermentation of regular mead using S. 
cerevisiae is ~ 24 0C with range of fermentation temperature 15 – 25 0C 9. Higher temperature ( > 25 
0C) fermentations promote faster fermentation and increased sugar consumption by other nutrients. On 
the contrary, fermentation at lower temperatures of < 15 0C are associated with sluggish fermentation 
and decreased performance for some strains while steady fermentation for some strains. Low

3.1.f. Fermentation Time 
Regular mead takes 3 – 6 weeks of time for fermentation where as it can be reduced to up to 4 weeks 
on addition of pH influencer to accelerate reactions 2,9. However, partial inactivation and limitation of 
fermentation time controls the extent and performance of fermentation. 
 
3.1.g. pH 
Optimal pH plays a major role in catalysis of enzymatic reactions. In a normal brewing process, acidic 
additives like citric acid reduces the pH of the wort and accelerates sugar extraction process. During the 
infusion mashing process, optimal pH range for highest extraction yield is 5.2 – 5.4, highest fermentable 
sugar content is 5.3 – 5.4 and fastest conversion is 5.3 – 5.4. Overall, optimal pH range for mashing 
process is 5.2 – 5.7. Higher pH levels of the mash can result in inefficient starch conversion and protein 
hydrolysis which produces less fermentable sugars like dextrin 29,30. 
 
3.1.h. Effects of Post-fermentation processes 
Yeast can be active, be it in limited amounts, during refrigerated storage because of ineffective filtration 
or separation. And it can bring in more alcohol content, undesirable flavours and off odours because of 
over fermentation. Ethanol and sugar content in the fermented samples can be impacted by the type of 
process (e.g. Centrifugation, Pasteurization, Filtration) depending on operational parameters like PU 
units, speed and time of centrifugation and membrane size.

4. Method and Materials 
As discussed above, overall content of the fermentable sugars is controlled by reduction of quantity of 
honey and replacement by addition of malt and the extracted sugars. Therefore, the process incorporates 
combination of steps of mashing and boiling with honey. The simple production process is represented 
in a flow diagram as below: 
 
 
Figure1: Schematic description of production process 
 
In order to limit the development of the alcohol content in the final product, it is important to limit the 
extent of extraction of sugars into the wort by controlling the activities of 𝛼-amylase and 𝛽-amylase 
with pH of the wort. Unlike regular beer brewing process, addition of citric or tartaric acid or any 
additives to reduce pH is skipped in order to avoid decrease in pH 2,27. 
 
Another propose of lower pH (~ 4.4) is to bring microbiological stability. However, pH is lowered by 
addition of honey, with an average pH of honey is 3.9 with a range of 3.4 – 6.1 3,27. Also, pH is further 
reduced by boiling of the honey-must and wort mixture with lemon balm with rosmarinic acid content 
and microbiological spoilage is contained by high temperatures 30,31.

4.1.c. Pilsner Malt 
Pilsner malt (Château Pilsen 2RP, product from Castle 
Maltuing®, Belgium) is a type of Belgian malt with 
lightest color with EBC of 3 – 3.5. It has a sweet malt 
flavor with sufficient enzymatic strength and hence, can 
be used as a base malt for light/pale beers or lower 
percentage of the mixture for other beers. It has a 𝛽-glucan 
content of 220 mg/L with a total protein content of 11.5 % and a pH range of 5.6 – 6.0 39,40. 
 
4.2. Preparation of Honey-must  
Enzyme catalysed sugar extraction progresses along the following steps 41,42: 
1. Acid Rest (35 – 45 0C): Phytic acid reduces pH, formed by break down of phytin by phytase and 
manages glucans to avoid gumming of the mash 
 
2. Protein Rest (45 – 59 0C): Proteinase converts long chain polysaccharides responsible for haze 
and instability into medium chains between 45 – 53 0C. Furthermore, peptidase breaks moderate 
chains important for body and foam retention into shorter chains between 55 – 58 0C. Optimal rest 
of 15 – 30 min at 54 0C does not affect the body but decrease haze 43.  
==PAGE:12==
 
 
8
3. Starch Conversion 1 (54 – 65 0C): 𝛽-amylase converts long sugar chains into single maltose units 
fast and efficiently for 1 – 2 hr rest for a drier and thin body brew 43. 
 
4. Starch Conversion 1 (68 – 75 0C) 𝛼-amylase trims off simple sugars from the end of long chains 
and develops less fermentable mixture along with dextrin and polysaccharides responsible for 
sweeter taste and full body. 20 min rest at 72 0C results in a thick body 41,43,44. 
 
5. Enzyme Deactivation (76 – 77 0C):  5 min rest at 78 0C to deactivate amylase 43,45. 
 
A strategic combination of rest times for 𝛼- and 𝛽-amylase for limited fermentable sugar content with 
moderate to full body. The wort is boiled with honey and lemon balm to develop a mixture of honey-
must and wort to be termed here after as “honey-wort” during the overall mashing process.

4.3.a. Strains 
A. Flavia® MP346: Metschnikowvia pulcherimma has low alcohol tolerance and suggested to be 
used in sequential inoculation, usually followed by S. cerevisiae, within a fermentation 
temperature range of 15 – 22 0C. Since the recommended dose of ~ 0.25 g/L is for a sequential

lot of esters to give fruity aroma and flavours. It is a maltose negative strain in the fermentation

recommended pitching rate of 2000000 cells/mL corresponds to 1.32 OD600. Therefore, a normal 
dose of R = 1.5 OD600 of the revived strain is used for inoculation 21,23,24.

4.9. Sensory Parameters 
The aim of the study is optimize the parameters for the production of low-alcoholic mead and therefore, 
sensory analysis conducted is preliminary without any special panels. Evaluation are purely based on 
factors and perception of aroma, flavour, taste, mouthfeel and appearance only, without any scientific 
or statistical analysis of any individual compounds responsible for organoleptic characteristics.  
 
However, previous studies suggest that ethanol has highest viscosity at 10% (v/v) in a drink and hence, 
is associated with body and fullness. Moreover, ethanol occupies headspace and indirectly affects aroma 
and flavour perception. Also, ethanol impacts the sweetness because of the conversion of sugars. 
Glycerol, naturally being viscous, is also determines mouthfeel 52. 
 
Volatile compounds like esters, phenols, acids, etc. are responsible for aroma and mouthfeel of the 
mead. These volatile compounds are results of chemical reaction during fermentation and are also 
associated with the honey, lemon balm and the yeast strains 53. 
 
Sensory analysis is conducted in two stages during the study: First analysis is used to select two most 
favourable yeast strains based on the taste and organoleptic characteristics of their respective fermented 
samples. Second analysis is conducted to corelate fermentation temperature of the two strains with taste 
and overall flavour of their respective fermented samples. Also, it is used to rate and conduct a 
rudimentary sensory analysis based on the following factors: Aroma, Sweetness, Sourness, Bitterness, 
Alcoholic taste, Mouthfeel, After Taste and comparison to a closet drink.

6. Discussion 
Maltose is most consumed during fermentation of samples with 15% substrate concentration. Therefore, 
in samples with 20 % substrate concertation, alcohol generation is more likely to be limited by the yeast 
concentration. While almost all the simple sugars are consumed in the samples with 10 % substrate 
concentration which suggest alcohol generation is more likely to be limited by sugar content instead.  
For production of low-alcoholic version of mead, 10 % substrate is desirable honey-wort concentration.  
 
From Trial 1, it is evident that rate of alcohol generation is higher at 20 0C as compared to 10 0C as 
depicted in figure 8. 1st Biological replicate of 10 % substrate has small content of ethanol generated 
suggesting self-fermentation even when refrigerated. Therefore, mashing process is slightly modified 
in terms of boiling at higher temperature for microbiological safety. 2nd biological replicate if 10 % 
substrate does not show considerable ethanol content.  
 
From previous studies for Snoremark®, Denmark, headspace above the fermentation samples does not 
affect levels of alcohol in the samples. Therefore, it is not considered as a parameter to be evaluated54.  
 
Flavia® MP346 and WhiteLabs® WLP618 have optimal temperatures ~ 15 0C. Therefore, these strains 
are less favorable at low temperature fermentation because of the risk of over fermentation as the main 
strategy for low alcoholic mead is partial inactivation of yeast strains. Also, growth curves suggest 
Lalvin® EC1118 and WhiteLabs® WLP603 as more convenient strains for low alcohol at both the 
temperatures of 10 and 20 0C. 
 
Moreover, drastic change in ethanol content from 10 to 20 0C for Flavia® MP346 and WhiteLabs® 
WLP618, suggest less favorable strains because of risk of over fermentation and off-flavors. While 
WhiteLabs® WLP603 has almost consistent level of ethanol content for both 10 and 20 0C which 
suggests thermal stability. Overall, alcohol generated by Lalvin® EC1118 has more alcohol content 
than that with WhiteLabs® WLP603, with all parametric changes.  
==PAGE:34==
 
 
30
 
Increased cell count at 20 0C as compared to that at 30 0C suggest that WhiteLabs ® strains have optimal 
around 20 0C. While for Lalvin® EC1118 cell growth decreases as temperature decreases.  
 
7. Conclusion 
 
10 % substrate produces least levels of alcohol because sugar content is the limiting factor for the 
chemical reactions. Ethanol content for all the samples with 10 % substrate concentration lies between 
2 – 4% (v/v) regardless of the type of yeast uses, fermentation temperature and dose of yeast.  
 
On an average, 10 0C is most favourable fermentation temperature for all the strains of yeasts. Lalvin® 
EC1118 and WhiteLabs® WLP603 are more favourable yeast strains based on sensory analysis, alcohol 
content and fermentation stability.  
 
Futuristic selection of the fermentation conditions and yeast strain can be determined by economics and 
detailed study of compounds associated with sensory analysis 
 
Low alcoholic mead is favourable because sustainability agenda is propagated further with the 
possibility of utilisation of spent grains from beer industries as its contains ~ 40 – 50 % polysaccharides.
