Summary
This thesis concerns an examination of the ﬁeld of damage detection (or structural
health monitoring) using scanner laser Doppler vibrometry (SLDV). The application
of SLDV for damage detection seems to have a great potential since no sensors are
mounted to the test structure. No requirement for sensor attachment entails a high
level of ﬂexibility, and the structural dynamic response remains unaﬀected. However,
previous studies have shown that the current damage detectability is inadequate.
A literature survey has been conducted in order to evaluate the vast research
area, and the curvature shape based methods are found to be relevant for further in-
vestigations. The methods are tested numerically using ﬁnite element simulations, and
subsequently the most promising methods are tested in the laboratory using SLDV.
Laboratory tests are used as basis for investigations on how diﬀerent issues,
e.g. the properties of the scanned surface and the applied spatial sampling, aﬀect the
quality of the SLDV measurements, and hence, the ability to detect for damages. Fur-
thermore, the diﬀerence in using modal and operating data in connection with damage
detection is investigated.
Finally, resonant as well as non-resonant data is applied in combination with the
found curvature shape based methods to detect for damages in beam-like steel struc-
tures.
Keywords: Damage detection, structural health monitoring (SHM), damage index,
scanner laser Doppler vibrometry (SLDV), curvature shapes, data smoothing, spa-
tial sampling, resonant data, non-resonant data, ﬁnite element analysis, laboratory
experiments.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Structural damage detection (SDD), also referred to in literature as structural damage
identiﬁcation (SDI) and structural health monitoring (SHM), has a signiﬁcant impor-
tance in today’s civil, mechanical, and aerospace engineering. Structures are exposed
to greater complexity, higher operational load and longer life time periods, which makes
it increasingly important to monitor their health [12]. The application of health mon-
itoring techniques for machinery are already well established [30], but have not been
adopted to large and complex structures such as bridges, buildings and wind turbines.
Instead, the SHM for such structures relies on old fashioned oﬀ-line non-destructive
methods, like X-ray and visual inspection, which are very costly and time consuming
[30].
Many research activities regarding SDD have been going on for more than thirty
years, which is due to a couple of diﬀerent factors. Basically, the factors can be cat-
egorised as serious failures resulting in loss of human life that have received media
attention, economic issues, and technical prosperity [12]. The Aloha Airlines ﬂight
that lost its exterior skin, back in 1988, is a good example [12]. The huge media cover-
age intensiﬁed the public’s attention on testing and monitoring aeroplanes, in order to
ensure that no such accident ever could happen again. The public’s concerns were after
a while replaced by political action and, hence, regulatory agencies and industry was
forced to ﬁnd the necessary resources in order to develop new technologies, to avoid
such a situation happening again. Furthermore, technological advancements have a
great inﬂuence on the ongoing research, due to the increase in cost-eﬀective computer
memory and more accurate and contact-less sensors.
There is a substantial interest among scientist and engineering companies to
invent new on-line non-destructive methods to detect structural damage. Diﬀerent
techniques can be used for detection, such as smart materials, image processing, opti-
cal ﬁbers, mechanical vibrations and statistical pattern recognition. The aim of this
project is to apply scanning laser Doppler vibrometry (SLDV) for damage detection,

which seems to be suitable in combination with vibration-based methods. Well written
survies within this ﬁeld are presented in the thesis by J. Holt (2012) [23] and in the
journal article by Fan et al. [15]. As mentioned, many other promising techniques
exist and are brieﬂy discussed in [9, 29].
In general SDD can be split into two categories; global-damage detection and
local-damage detection [70]. Local-damage detection is classiﬁed as a non-destructive
technique (i.e. CT scanning and ultrasonic), but is only useful for smaller structures
and individual components [71]. To be able to detect for damage on a complete struc-
ture, one needs to apply global-damage detection. One appropriate choice of technique
is to use vibration-based damage detection. This technique can brieﬂy be described
as follows. Any structure can be modelled, as a dynamic system containing mass,
stiﬀness and damping. Once the structure is exposed to any damage, the structural
parameters will change, which leads to a change in the frequency response function,
and the modal parameters of the structural system. Thus, the change in the modal
parameters can be interpreted, as a damage indicator, which can be tracked from an
early stage [6, 4].
A comprehensive study has been going on for many years to ﬁgure out which
modal parameters are the best to monitor, in order to detect for damages. The shift in
natural frequency and mode shape (MS) are found to be insuﬃcient, since the sensi-
tivity towards cracks is inadequate for in-situ measurements [12]. Furthermore, other
studies have shown that a more reliable damage indicator can be found by inspecting
the mode shape curvature (MSC) [15]. The MSC can be used as an indicator, since a
reduction in stiﬀness will result in increasing curvature.
The aim of the work in this thesis is to investigate how it is possible to detect
structural damages by applying already existing vibration-based detection algorithms
in combination with SLDV. In literature only few attempts concerning the use of
SLDV within the ﬁeld of vibrations-based damage detection have been found. With
the SLDV technique one has the possibility to measure the vibration modes at high
spatial resolution, which to some extent seems to be necessary in order to suﬃciently
detect damages [50]. However, the invention of the SLDV techniques see to entail
new issues, such as the increased noise content on the measured response, and how to
determine the optimal spatial sampling resolution [50].
The motivation for this project is established due to aspirations to develop an
eﬃcient, contact-less and reliable damage detection method. Think about the oppor-
tunities of being able to scan an aeroplane for damages, while people are boarding or
even better if one was able to continuously monitor the health condition. If these ideas
could be realised, the time an aeroplane is out of service can be reduced signiﬁcantly,
which can save the airline companies a considerable amount of money. Furthermore,
the safety of their daily ﬂights can be improved. The SLDV could be one of the key
features to apply if such a technique is to be realised.
This project will consider vibration-based damage detection since it is a global
method, which seems to be well suited and used in combination with the SLDV [57].
Scientists have in the laboratory measured the displacement MS of steel, aluminium
and composite beam-like and plate-like structures applying SLDV [40, 45]. By using
the MSC-based methods it seems possible to detect saw-cut damages with ≈50% re-
==PAGE:25==
3
duction in stiﬀness [45], which is far from being good enough if such method has to be
applied in real applications.
The limitations behind the current technique motivates the project to investigate
how this technique can be improved. Numerical and laboratory experiments can be
used to investigate e.g. the eﬀect of the spatial sampling resolution, ﬁltering the mea-
sured response, deciding which modes to consider and changing the surface condition
of the scanned structure. The investigations will be used to setup some guidelines for
how to apply the SLDV technique to detect for damages. Using the found guidelines
the aim is to be able to detect smaller cracks than previously reported. The test speci-
mens applied in the project are slender beam structures, which actually in many cases
seems to fairly represent parts of more complex structures.
The ﬁrst part of this thesis is a background chapter containing a literature sur-
vey, within the ﬁeld of vibration-based damage detection, which is carried out in order
to identify the most promising detection methods. Furthermore, it is identiﬁed how
other scientists have applied the SLDV technique for damage detection. The following
chapter considers a theoretical analysis of the topic, where the principles behind the
curvature based methods are studied. Furthermore, a FE-analysis is carried out for
the chosen test specimens to ﬁnd the most promising damage detection method. The
theoretical analysis will be followed by an experimental analysis, where the eﬀect of;
changing the spatial sampling resolution, ﬁltering the measured response, selectively
picking out which modes to consider and changing the surface condition of the scanned
structure, will be investigated. The experiences from the experiments are used to op-
timize the test setup and the considered damage detection methods in order to decide
whether the SLDV technique can be used for detecting damages better than previously
presented by other scientists. Finally, the experiences with using the SLDV for damage
detection will be discussed, with the propose of outlining the main issues behind this
technique, and how to develop this technique prospectively.

2.1
Structural Health Monitoring
After a certain period of service, small damages will occur to any structure, due to e.g.
fatigue (caused by dynamic loading). If no one discovers the almost invisible damages
they will just continuously accumulate and develop, which can cause catastrophic
failure, when a certain critical limit is exceeded. To avoid such failure, one needs to
monitor the structural health, so the right maintenance of the structure can be chosen.
When the SHM is implemented during the in-service period of a structure, the main
task is to test for any structural change, which is caused by cracks or other types of
damages. Due to practical reasons, it is not suﬃcient to only know the presence of the
damage. Instead an appropriate solution has to be able to indicate the location, type
and severity of the structural damage [69]. On this basis, a proper SHM algorithm
is capable of giving a reasonable evaluation for the health status of the in-service
==PAGE:28==
6
Background
structure and judging the remaining service life. Therefore, Yam et al. [69] divided
the SHM into ﬁve levels:
1. Identiﬁcation of damage presence in a structure
2. Localization of damage
3. Identiﬁcation of the damage type
4. Quantiﬁcation of damage severity
5. Prediction of the remaining service life of the structure
A complete SHM algorithm has to cover all ﬁve levels, but there seems to be no such
method existing yet. The aim of this thesis is only to consider the two ﬁrst levels, but
it is expected that the technology advancements will do the remaining levels solvable
in the future.
It is important that the techniques applied for structural damage detection have
a minimal eﬀect on the structural response or ideally are contact-less. The aim of this
thesis is to apply one of the more advanced and recently developed sensors; the SLDV.
The SLDV is a contact-less sensor, which ensures that the measured vibration-response
is a true presentation of the real structure. Furthermore, an excessive advantage is that
the sensor resolution can be very high, and this ensures a detailed measure of the MS.
Therefore, the SLDV is particularly suitable for methods using the MS for detecting
damage. However, a drawback is that the technology is not yet able to capture large
and complex structures.
Using the SLDV, one can measure the velocity, v(t), of the scanned surface, which
can be used to determine modal parameters such as natural frequencies and MS. Fur-
thermore, the measured velocities can be used for strain measurements, which will not
be further elucidated in this project. However, application of the SLDV seems to be
relevant within the ﬁeld of vibration-based damage detection, which will be discussed
further in this chapter.
2.2
Vibration-based Damage Detection
Currently local methods such as ultrasonic methods and X-ray methods are used to
investigate structural damages, but these methods require that one knows the vicinity
of the damage, which is not guaranteed for most real existing large civil and mechan-
ical engineering structures [15]. Hence, to overcome these issues, one has to apply a
global detection method, like the vibration-based detection methods.
The fundamental idea behind vibration-based damage detection is that any dam-
age on a structure will change its physical properties, such as the mass, stiﬀness and
damping. Since the properties change, the damage will be traceable from the modal
parameters, such as the natural frequency, modal damping and MSs. Simply, one can
imagine that a crack will reduce the area moment of inertia of a beam, which locally
reduces the stiﬀness, and thereby eﬀects the vibration features of the beam (e.g. the
==PAGE:29==
2.2 Vibration-based Damage Detection
7
natural frequencies).
In vibration tests, the response and the excitations are measured and recorded
as a time history, from which it can be hard to identify structural damage. There-
fore, a more popular method is to examine the modal domain data through modal
analysis techniques, where the time domain data is transformed into the frequency
domain, from which it is possible to extract the modal parameters. Even though this
technique seems to be the most promising, a lot of research has been going within all
three domains (i.e. time, frequency and modal), which seems to continue, since no
single existing method can solve all types of damage detection problems [15]. How-
ever, the modal methods seem to gain their popularity due to the easily interpreted
modal parameters, which seem to be much easier to understand than the more abstract
mathematical features extracted from the time and frequency domain.
An extensive research has been going on for more than thirty years within the
ﬁeld of vibration-based damage detection. During the years, a broad range of methods,
algorithms and techniques were developed to be able to solve various problems faced
in basic and complex structures. In 1998, Doebling et al. [12] presented an extensive
review of vibration based damage detection methods up to 1996. This review was
accomplished with an updated version in 2002 by Sohn et al. [56], which covered the
research going on from 1996-2001. More recently reviews were created by Yan et al.
[70] and Fan et al. [15] and cover research going on up to 2006 and 2011 respectively.
Furthermore, the paper from Fan et al. includes a comparative study of the various
methods.
In general, damage detection methods can be classiﬁed as either response-based
methods or model-based methods. Both approaches have the same goal, namely to iden-
tify any damage, but to obtain this, diﬀerent techniques are applied. The response-
based method only depends on the response of the investigated structure and no fur-
ther modelling is needed. While the model-based method relies on a detailed numerical
model (i.e. FE-model), which is adjusted according to the measured response. In this
thesis the aim is to apply response-based methods in order to on-line detect for dam-
ages. By applying this type of method the computational eﬀort is decreased, which
seems to be important if one wants to detect for damages on-line [15].
2.2.1
Damage Detection Methods
The objective of this section is to orientate the reader on which vibration-based de-
tection methods exist, and what are the advantages and drawbacks for each method.
With inspiration from Fan et al. [15] the methods are divided into three diﬀerent
groups: Natural Frequency-based Methods, Mode Shape-based Methods and Mode Shape
Curvature-based Methods. Each group is split into two subgroups; one considering the
traditional methods and another one considering the modern signal processing meth-
ods.

2.2.1.1
Natural Frequency-based Methods
The natural frequency-based methods are some of the oldest ones, but they are practi-
cal to use and easy to understand in principle. Think about the sound of two glasses
rattling against each other. Then think about how the sound will change, if one of the
glasses has a crack. It is easy to imagine, that the sound will change, or in other words,
that the frequency content will change signiﬁcantly, which is a direct indication that
a crack has appeared. The choice of using the change in natural frequency to indicate
damages is appealing, since the natural frequency can be conveniently measured from
only using few sensors.
The use of natural frequency-based methods for damage detection has been exten-
sively studied in the past three decades. However, the methods suﬀer from that almost
all of them are model-based. To model complex structures and damages in general is
a challenging task, which in some cases can introduce signiﬁcant modelling errors [15].
Even for simple beam structures, which can be modelled by Euler-Bernoulli beam the-
ory, it is well known that the natural frequencies are over-predicted for short beams
and high frequency bending modes. Therefore, the application of many frequency-
based methods are limited to only a small selection of simple structures.
Another limitation of these methods is that frequency change caused due to the
presence of a damage is very small for minor damages, and may be almost invisible
compared to the damages caused due to change in environmental or operational con-
ditions. For in-situ measurements, the frequency variation due to ambient vibration
and environmental eﬀects is estimated to be as high as 5-10 %, which implies that the
measured frequency change has to be at least about 5 % in order to detect the damage
with conﬁdence [46]. Ni et al. [34] proposed a statistical damage detection model,
which used pattern recognition techniques to distinguish between damage and envi-
ronmental change in frequency. The model was used for long-term structural health
monitoring of the Ting Kau Bridge.
Fan et al. [15] concludes that the frequency change-based damage detection methods
can only be applied to simple structures (e.g. beam-like structures), where a precise
and realistic model is obtainable. However, these methods do not have any practical
application for real complex structures, due to their limited ability in localising dam-
ages and especially in cases with multiple damages. None of the natural frequency
based methods will be applied in this project.
However, the change in frequency
caused due to the presence of the damage will brieﬂy be discussed from a numerical
and experimental point of view in Chapter 3. and 4., respectively.
2.2.1.2
Mode Shape-based Methods
Compared to using the natural frequencies, the advantages of using the corresponding
MSs is that they contain local information, which makes them more sensitive towards
damages, and enables the possibility of localising both single and multiple damages
[15]. Furthermore, the MSs are found to be less eﬀected by environmental changes,

MSs are more prone to noise contamination than natural frequencies [15].
During the last three decades many methods considering MS data have been
developed. Fan and Qio [15] categorize the developed methods into two groups. The
ﬁrst group is called traditional mode shape methods, which try to estimate the damage

detection, relative new methods have been designed, and will be discussed further in
this and the upcoming section.

A new group of methods have been developed, since in most in-situ cases, one does
not have any experimental data on the undamaged structure. In this case, the model-

vibration-based damage detection.

2.3
Damage Detection Utilising Scanning Laser Doppler
Vibrometry
This section will brieﬂy describe the SLDV system (the SLDV system is described
further in Section 2.4) and afterwards a literature survey of the application of SLDV
within damage detection is carried out.
The SLDV is non-contact vibration measurement technique, evaluating velocities at
scan-points on a structural surface, over a wide bandwidth. The technology is based
on the Doppler eﬀect, where the velocity of a vibrating object is estimated from the
frequency shift of the backscattered laser beam light. The applied Polytech PSV-500
vibrometer system (which has been used in this project) is a 1D system that can
measure out-of-plane motions of a complete surface by automatically probing a set of
pre-deﬁned scan-points.
With the advent of the SLDV system, a range of new opportunities are estab-
lished, since it now is possible to quickly have non-contact vibration measurements and
at the same time obtain a high density of scan-points (high spatial sampling resolu-
tion). The research that has been going on within the ﬁeld of using SLDV for damage
detection can be split into three categories; frequency dependent deﬂection response,
MSC and ultrasonic wave propagation.
2.3.1
Deﬂection Response
Waldron et al. [66] did in 2002 succeed in detecting the presence of a fatigue grown
crack in a clamped-clamped aluminium beam-structure, by inspecting the ODSs at
resonant frequencies, measured by the SLDV. In 2003 Ghoshal et al. [18] took the in-
vestigations a step further and measured the ODSs of a clamped-free composite beam-
plate structure, by utilising the properties of the SLDV. By inspecting the ODSs of the
test specimens (with and without delamination damages), the presence of the damages
was recognised. The experiments showed that it is considerably easier to detect smaller
delaminations by using high frequency resonant ODSs.
In 2000 Pai et al.[37] developed the technique Boundary Eﬀect Evaluation (BEE)
for detecting damages in beam and plate like structures. The idea behind the method is
that the presence of a damage will introduce new boundaries, which generates spatially
localised eﬀects to the high frequency dynamic response of the structure. It turned out
that damages can be localised by inspecting the boundary eﬀects of ODSs measured
with a 1D SLDV. Basically the damages were localised by comparing a sliding-window
least-square ﬁtting of the generalised solution of a harmonically vibrating beam to a
harmonically excited ODS. Hereby, it was possible to localise saw-cut damages without
having any measurements of the undamaged beam. Pai et al. (2004) [36] succeeded
with using this method for detecting damages in an aeroplane wing panel (slots in the
panel stiﬀener). However, the exact damage extent does not appear from the paper.

Curvature Shape
As discussed in Section 2.2.1.3, Pandey et al. (1991) [38] proposed to utilise the MSC
for damage detection, since numerical results showed that the MSC of a beam struc-
ture turned out to be highly sensitive towards damage. Research regarding the MSC
method has been going on for many years, and with the opportunities incorporated in
the SLDV technique new attempts have been carried out.
Qioa et al. (2007) [40] investigated the use of 1D SLDV to evaluate MSC algo-
rithms, such as the Gapped-Smoothing Method (GSM) (proposed by Ratcliﬀe & Bagaria
in 2000 [42]), the Generalized Fractal Dimension (GFD) (Hadjileontiadis et al. in [19],
2005) and the Strain Energy Method (SEM) (Cornwell et al. [10], 19991), for detection
of delamination and saw-cut damages in composite beam and plate structures. They
observed that the damage detection algorithms were able to detect saw-cut damages
with a stiﬀness reduction of ≈50%. Furthermore, the investigated methods turned out
to be more sensitive towards delamination damages, when compared to the saw-cut
damages.
In 2010 Rucevskis [45] proposed to use the magnitude of the squared MSC to
detect damage location and extent (as discussed in Section 2.2.1.3). Compared to al-
ready existing MSC-based methods, the advantage of the proposed method is that it
requires MS measurements only from the damaged state of the structure. Rucevskis
suggested to use the average sum of the squared MSC of all the measured modes
in order to reduce the inﬂuence from measurement noise. The proposed ideas were
demonstrated experimentally by a free-free and clamped-clamped aluminium beam
with saw-cut damages. The deﬂection shape of the test specimens were measured with
SLDV from which it was possible to localise the saw-cut damages in combination with
the proposed MSCSMI. However, in the article it seems to be unclear whether modal
or operating data has been applied.
2.3.3
Guided Wave Propagation
To detect for damages in plate-like structures, guided wave inspection techniques has
been used in combination with a 3D vibrometer to measure the vibration pattern
created by Lamb waves. This method was proposed by Professor Staszewski at the
university of Sheﬃeld in 2004 [31, 58]. Staszewski observed a signiﬁcant noise level
in the measured Lamb waves from measurements carried out with a 1D vibrometer
for an aluminium plate-like structure. To handle this issue, he proposed to apply a
Savitzky-Golay ﬁlter or the Kaiser-Bessel ﬁlter. Furthermore, it was found that the
change in velocity amplitude of the Lamb waves in the vicinity of a crack was sensitive
towards changes.
In 2007 Staszewski et al. [59] presented the use of a 3D vibrometer to measure the
out-of-plane and in-plane Lamb waves generated in a steel plate, with the objective of
detecting fatigue crack damages. The RMS velocity amplitude of pulsed Lamb waves
was measured with grid-points distributed over the surface of the plate, from which it

Only a few years later Staszewski et al. [60] utilized the possibilities of the 3D SLDV

2.3.4
Current Research
In-plane strain measurements and damage detection techniques based on the governing
diﬀerential equation of displacement are being developed.
With the advent of 3D
SLDV this is now possible and at the same time a high spatial resolution in 3D can
be obtained. In 2012 Wildey presented in his PhD thesis [68] a new damage detection
technique based on the governing diﬀerential equations of displacement in beam and
plate structures. He developed and tested two algorithms to determine a violation
in the governing diﬀerential equations (GDE) caused by either a delamination in a
composite beam with out-of-plane vibrations, or by a crack in a plate with in-plane
vibrations. The proposed algorithms numerically diﬀerentiate measured quasi-static
displacement in order to determine the residual term of the GDEs of displacement.
Basically, a non-zero residual term will indicate a violation of the GDE, which can be
interpreted as the presence of a damage.
2.4
Scanner Laser Doppler Vibrometer
The aim of the experimental work is to accurately, and with high resolution of mea-
surements, determine the MS of a beam-like structure. To achieve a high resolution of
measurement points, a SLDV is applied. The Department of Mechanical Engineering
at DTU has a Polytec SLDV (PSV-500H), which has been applied in this project. The
general PSV system consists of a PSV-I-500 Scanning Head, a PSV-F-500-H5 Front-
End and a PSV-W-500-H Data Management System.
The PSV system utilises a helium-neon laser to measure the vibratory velocity at a
single point on the surface of an object in the direction of the laser beam through laser
heterodyne interferometry. The interferometer signal is deciphered by a velocity de-
coder inside the scanning head control box, and an analogue voltage signal proportional
to the velocity is generated [39].

Optical Conﬁguration
The scanning head consists of a laser, beam splitter, mirrors, lens, Bragg cell and
optical detector, and is sketched in Figure 2.2. The helium-neon laser is utilised to

object beam, from which a proportional electric signal is produced. The electric signal
is converted into velocity through a digital velocity decoder.

2.5
Mode Shapes vs. Operating Deﬂection Shapes
In this section the diﬀerence between MS and ODS will brieﬂy be discussed. Each of
them have their advantages in general, but which one is most suitable within the ﬁeld
of damage detection?
2.5.1
Mode Shapes
Under proper conditions, most structures can be made to resonate, which causes the
structure to vibrate with excessive, sustained motion. Examples of that would be:
Exciting a bell with a hammer, will cause it to resonate, while exciting a sandbag
will not cause it to resonate. These resonant vibrations are caused by an interaction
between the inertial and elastic properties of the materials within a structure, and are
==PAGE:41==
2.6 Data Smoothing
19
deﬁned as structural modes [52]. The modes are used to better understand structural
vibrations, by characterising the resonances. Each mode can be deﬁned by a modal
frequency, modal damping, and a MS.
To estimate the MS, modal data is required, which implies that the input force
needs to be measured, otherwise the frequency response function (FRF) can not be
determined. This is not always possible for in-situ measurements, where the excitation
force could be environmental eﬀects e.g. when a truck passes an bridge or if a wind
gust hits a wind turbine. From the measured FRF, the modal parameters, such as the
natural frequency, ω, MS, φ, and damping, ξ, are obtained by ﬁtting single or multiple
d.o.f. systems to the response. More details about how it is done in the presented
experimental work is given in Section 4.4.1.2.
2.5.2
Operating Deﬂection Shapes
What is measured in the laboratory, is the ODS of the investigated structure. The
structure is forced to vibrate due to a force f(t), which cause a response depending on
the magnitude and location of the excitation force. Generally, the ODS can be deﬁned
as any forced motion of two or more points on a structure. The motion of the points
is speciﬁed with a vector, which means that it has a location and direction associated
with it.
To determine the ODSs of a structure, the input force is not accounted for and one
only needs to measure the dynamic structural response [53]. Instead of measuring the
input force, a single data point from the measured response is chosen as a reference, and
used for normalisation of the measured response. Since there is no need for measuring
the input force, the ODS is used for in-situ measurements, where the input force is
unmeasurable. However, the ODS at resonance is assumed to be almost identical with
the MS, if the considered system is lightly damped [53].
Having the normalised structural response, operational modal analysis (OMA)
can be used to identify the modal parameters, which relies on a curve-ﬁtting procedure
as it is done for the modal analysis. However, the ODS can be directly evaluated from
the complex measured response by combining the modulus and argument, and one
avoids the curve-ﬁtting procedure, which seems to be hard to completely automate,
i.e. within a damage detection algorithm. More detailed information about the applied
curve-ﬁtting method can be found in Section 4.4.2.2.

2.8
Summary
The development within the ﬁeld of structural health monitoring and damage detection
has been going on for many years, almost four decades. Even though an enormous
amount of research within this ﬁeld can be found in the literature, there is no single
method which seems to fulﬁl all ﬁve criteria listed in the beginning of this chapter.
2.8.1
Vibration-based Methods
The scope of the project was to apply SLDV to detect for structural damages, and with
this in mind, the literature survey was concentrated around vibration-based damage
detection. From the literature, diﬀerent damage detection methods considering modal
parameters, such as natural frequency and MS, are found. The methods considering
==PAGE:44==
22
Background
the change in natural frequency seem to be insuﬃcient since the sensitivity towards
damage is low and inherently the methods are ill-suited at localising multiple damages.
Using the change in MS to detect for damages seems to be a more accurate method.
The MSs contain local information about the structural behaviour, which increase
their sensitivity towards detecting damages, and at the same time multiple damages
is detectable. Pandey et al. [38] suggests to use the MSC for damage detection, which
is determined as the second derivative of the displacement MS. The MSC method
seems to be much more sensitive and its ability to detect small damages is found to be
reasonable. However, to determine the MSC, one has to numerically diﬀerentiate the
displacement MS twice, which has a major drawback since noise and other irregularities
in the measured displacement MS are magniﬁed.
Sazonov et al.
[50] carried out
experiments showing that both undersampling and oversampling of the MS may have
adverse eﬀects on the damage detection.
Ratcliﬀe [41] succeeded with developing the response-based GSM, where the damage
is localised by piecewise ﬁtting a cubic polynomial curve to the MSC and calculating
the squared diﬀerence between the cubic ﬁt and the MSC. Only a few years later, the
method was expanded to broadband analysis, where not only the resonant data was
considered. However, this extension seems to have a promising eﬀect on the damage
detection.
2.8.2
Application of SLDV
Various types of methods have been developed for damage detecting, and some of them
can be applied in cooperation with the SLDV technique. These methods are in general
based on mechanical principles, such as the ODS and OCS. Furthermore, Lamb waves
have been utilized for plate-like structures, where the propagation ability seems to be
the key feature.
The methods concerning ODS are basically utilising a sliding window least-square
ﬁt of the general solution for the deﬂection of the undamaged structure, which is
compared with the ODS of the damaged structure, measured with 1D vibrometer.
Structural damages turned out to introduce sharp discontinuities when comparing the
least-square ﬁt and the measures ODS.
A few attempts have been made to apply methods based on the OCS, when
measuring the ODS of structure with a 1D vibrometer. Not all found research seems
to clearly indicate whether they apply modal or operating data together with the
proposed methods, and no arguments for choosing among them are given.
Guided Lamb waves seem to be good at propagating over long distances within a
structure. This property of the Lamb waves has been utilised and methods have been
developed based on the reﬂection of these waves caused by damages.

2.8.3
Motivation for Choice of Research
The motivation for this project is established due to the aspirations about developing
an eﬃcient, contact-less and reliable damage detection method. Think about the op-
portunities of being able to scan an aeroplane for damages while people are boarding,
or even better if one was able to continuously monitor the health condition. If these
thoughts could be realised, the time an aeroplane is out of service can be reduced
signiﬁcantly, which can save the airline companies a considerable amount of money.
Furthermore, the safety of the daily ﬂights can be improved. The SLDV could be one
of the key features to apply if such a technique is to be realised.
This project will consider vibration-based damage detection since it is a global
method, which seems to be well suited and used in combination with SLDV. Scientists
have in the laboratory, measured the displacement MS of steel, aluminium and com-
posite beam-like and plate-like structures applying SLDV. By using the MSC-based
methods, it seems possible to detect saw-cut damages with ≈50% reduction in stiﬀ-

applications.
The limitations behind the current technique motivates the project to investigate
how this technique can be improved. Numerical and laboratory experiments can be
used to investigate e.g. the eﬀect of; the spatial sampling resolution, ﬁltering the mea-

of the scanned structure. The investigations will be used to setup some guidelines for
how to apply the SLDV technique to detect for damages. Furthermore, Ratcliﬀe et al.

eﬀect on the ability to detect for damages. The broadband method will be applied in
combination with SLDV measurements and the accompanying guidelines. The aim is
to test whether one is able to detect smaller cracks than previously reported.

3.3
Finite Element Analysis
In this section vibration-based damage detection will be discussed from a numerical
point of view. A FE-analysis is carried out to provide insight and understanding of the
structural dynamic behaviour of a damaged structure, and to validate the suggested
damage detection methods. The aim is to use the experiences from the FE-analysis to
guide the experimental work and to compare diﬀerent detection methods initially.
Generally FE-analysis has been extensively used within the ﬁeld of damage de-
tection and the literature abounds with papers considering this topic. This project
aims to experimentally test for structural damages using SLDV, which is closer re-
lated to real applications. However, the FE-analysis is also included in this project
since it is a valuable tool for parameter studies, which can be used to attain a better
understanding of the fundamental principles behind the suggested damage detection
methods.
The test specimens used for the FE-analysis are basically the same as those used
for the experimental analysis in Chapter 4, but some of the considered damage cases
diﬀers. To model the considered test specimens, the CAD-software SolidWorks has
been applied and the analysis of the structural dynamic characteristics is performed
in the commercial FE-software ANSYS Workbench.
3.3.1
Test Specimens
The test specimens used in the experimental setup have to be chosen according to the
available laboratory and equipment, since it may somehow restrict the geometric and
material parameters one can apply.
Initially a clamped-free beam was considered since this conﬁguration would ensure
that the structure would be restricted, and not be able to move in space (the same
could have been obtained using a clamped-clamped or clamped-pinned conﬁguration).
The space restriction is useful if one experimentally wants to directly compare dif-
ferent test specimens (damaged and undamaged state), by determining the change in
shape. However, preliminary tests made it clear that the clamped boundary was hard
to reproduce in the laboratory. Even small boundary imperfections were detectable in
the measured response, which made it hard to distinguish between imperfections and
the presence of a damage. In the literature [25, 37] the noise content in the measured
response seems to be essential for the opportunities of successfully detecting for dam-
ages, so it was decided to change the conﬁguration.
The free boundary seems to be best reproducible in the laboratory, and it was
decided to change the test conﬁguration according to that. By changing the type of
conﬁguration to a free-free beam, one does obtain better measurements with fewer un-
wanted irregularities, but the drawback is that one is not able to directly compare the
measured responses (the healthy and damaged state). However, this would also rarely
be the case for real applications. Mostly, one does simply not have any reference (the
response of the healthy structure) to compare with, and the applied damaged detection

3.3.2
FE-Model Setup
To setup the FE-analysis, the commercial FE-software ANSYS Workbench has been
applied. The idea behind the FE-analysis is to simulate the intended experiments and
obtain numerical results free from noise and physical imperfections. Hence, it is pos-
sible to distinguish between limitations caused by the applied detection methods and
the restriction due to the general noise level.
The test specimens are modelled in SolidWorks as CAD ﬁles, with the previ-
ously declared dimensions in Section 3.3.1.1. The CAD ﬁles are imported to ANSYS
Workbench and modelled with SOLID186 elements, and no eﬀort is given to optimise
the FE-analysis computational-wise, since the investigated structures are geometrically
simple and relatively small. The aim has been to obtain reliable simulations, which
can be used to test the considered damage detection methods. If BEAM-elements were
applied, the model would be easier to solve, but the drawback would be that it adds
some limitations on how to geometrically model the damages.
It is important to emphasise that, at this stage, it is assumed that the vibra-
tion amplitudes are so small, that no contact between the crack-walls will occur (no
breathing cracks [54]).
A mapped mesh is generated with an element size of 1 mm and the boundaries
are considered to be free-free, since it has been experienced that other types of bound-
aries are hard to realise in the laboratory (discussed in Section 3.3.1). Due to the
ﬁne mesh which might be required in order to detect small damages, the mesh conver-
gence study has been omitted, since a ﬁne mesh has been applied. A modal analysis is
performed using the Mechanical APDL solver in ANSYS to obtain the modal parame-
ters, i.e. natural frequency and MS. The modal quantities are exported to Matlab for
subsequent data processing.

3.3.7
Damage Indices
As described in Section 2.2.1, there exists several diﬀerent MS indices; some of them
use the healthy structure as a reference, while other indices do not need a reference.
In Figure 3.8 ﬁve diﬀerent MS based damage detection indices are plotted for the entire
beam span, considering Damage Case A. The indices presented are a weighted sum of
the ﬁrst eight identiﬁed transverse out-of-plane vibration modes.
The ﬁrst index, MSI, expresses the diﬀerence between the reference and the
damaged structure. Even though the extent of the introduced damage is relatively
signiﬁcant and the data is almost noise free, one can rarely identify the damage from
the MSI. The conﬁdence level is unacceptable and the normed residual (NR) seems
to be considerable at the boundaries, which is undesirable, since no damage exists in
these areas.
Diﬀerentiating the MSs once with respect to the spatial coordinate x, one ob-
tains the mode shape slopes (MSSs), which can be used to estimate the MSSI. From
Figure 3.8, the MSSI and MSI index are comparable, except at the boundaries, where
no outliers exist for the MSSI. But still the general level of the NR is quite high, and
no distinct spikes are identiﬁed.
As discussed earlier, the MSCs seems to be more sensitive to structural damage
than the MSs, which also appears clearly in Figure 3.8 (c), where the MSCI is plotted.
A distinct spike exists at the damage location, and the magnitude of the spike makes
the index signiﬁcant, and predicts the damage location with more than 95 % of conﬁ-
dence.
If the MSCs are individually squared, one obtains the MSCSI, which in Figure
3.8 (d) seems to have a slightly better performance. By squaring the MSC, any ir-
regularities in the response will be ampliﬁed, which can be useful when detecting for
damages. Even though the MSCI and MSCSI are almost equally good in the considered
damage case, one expects an improved performance for the MSCSI, when the damage
becomes considerably smaller. The MSCSI seems to amplify any small irregularities in
the response, which can be observed in the MSCSI plot when inspecting the damage
index next to the damage location (a lot of non-signiﬁcant spikes appears).
The MSCSMI in Figure 3.8 (e) and the MSCSI seems to be almost equally good
at detecting the considered damage. However, the MSCSM-index has a great advan-
tage, since no reference structure is needed. Due to the independence of the reference
structure, the MSCSM-index is judged to be more useful for real applications.
It has to be emphasised how valuable it is to have a damage index, which is
independent on any reference. Primarily, the reference can either be obtained from a
detailed model of the considered structure or having measured the structural response
at an undamaged state. However, to have a detailed model of a realistic structure is
rarely the case, either due to the complexity of the structure or because such a reﬁned
model is almost unaﬀordable for the client. For newly built structures, one has the
opportunity to measure the structural response at a probably undamaged state. How-
ever, what is expected to be an undamaged state might actually include minor defects,
which have been introduced under the construction. Finally, environmental eﬀects
(e.g. temperature variations) and ambient loading (e.g. wind gusts) might make it

Theoretical Analysis
3.4
Summary
In this section the theoretical analysis will be summarised before the experimental
analysis is continued in the following chapter.
3.4.1
Mode Shape Curvature Principles
The principles behind the MSC based method were analysed, in order to understand
how the method is capable of detecting structural damages and what to look for when
testing in the laboratory. From asnalysing a diﬀerential beam element, it was demon-
strated that the moment variation M(x, t) is continuous along the beam span and no
’jumps’ will exist, which conﬁrms that any sudden change in curvature can be related
to the change in ﬂexural stiﬀness EI.
The undamaged (healthy) beam was modelled analytically to be able to estimate
the dynamic properties of a potential test specimen. During an iterative process, the
geometrical parameters were determined such that the dynamic properties were rea-
sonable in connection to the applied laboratory equipment.
3.4.2
Finite Element Analysis
A FE-analysis was carried out in ANSYS Workbench to validate the suggested tra-
ditional MS and MSC methods. The analysis found that the MSCSMI proposed by
Rucevskis et al. [45], was almost equally good at detecting damages when compared to
the MSCSI. However, the MSCSMI only requires data from the damaged state, which
makes this method preferable in many in-situ cases. In the experimental analysis only
methods which are independent on the reference state will be considered, since the
FE-analysis showed that even the traditional MSCSMI is suﬃcient in detecting dam-
ages under such conditions.
The FE-results were compared to what other researchers have found, and a good
agreement between the results was established. Furthermore, the results obtained by
Cadellino [5] regarding noise simulation and ﬁlter processes were investigated in or-
der to ﬁnd a method to handle noise and other irregularities in the MS data. The
investigations found that the SG-ﬁlter is suitable for smoothing the MS data, before
diﬀerentiating twice with respect to the spatial coordinate to obtain the MSC.

Experimental Setup
To measure the structural response, a Polytec Scanning Vibrometer - 500 (PSV-500)

Appendix C). Diﬀerent types of connections such as a nylon stinger and beeswax at-
tachment have been tested. However, the steel stinger seems to be a proper choice due
to the small displacements, which ensure that the local ﬂexural stiﬀness at the stinger
attachment is not aﬀected signiﬁcantly. The use of a steel stinger and the screwed
attachment results in a stiﬀconnection (in the longitudinal direction), which ensures
a good energy transfer from the shaker to the specimen. If the shaker connection is
situated close to a nodal point, the corresponding mode will not be excited. This
situation has to be avoided for the modes of interest, and has already been discussed
in Section 3.2.1. From the analytical predictions, it turned out to be suitable to mount
the shaker 100 mm from the left free beam end.
The force transferred from the shaker to the test specimen is measured with
an Endevco Piezoelectric Force Sensor (type 2312) and the signal is ampliﬁed with
a B&K Vibration Meter (type 2511), before it is sent to the PSV Front-End. The
excitation signal is set in the PSV software and is generated in the Front-End module,
and ampliﬁed by a B&K Power Ampliﬁer (type 2706) before entering the shaker.
The PSV-500 scanning head is placed in front of the test specimen, such that
the shaker connection and possible damages are pointing away from it, and a complete
uninterrupted surface can be applied for scanning. To ensure comparability among
diﬀerent measurement sets, the distance from the scanning head to the test specimen
is kept constant. For best signal-to-noise ratio, the distance to the object should be
approximately an integer number of the lasers resonator length, which is 20.4 cm for
the applied Helium-neon laser [39]. Setting the distance to ≈100 cm, one will expect
a good signal-to-noise ratio.
The SLDV measures the surface velocity parallel to the laser beam. To obtain
the velocity perpendicular to the object, each measured velocity has to be corrected
according to the respective scan point. Using trigonometric functions, one is able to
determine the velocity component perpendicular to object, which is implemented as
an option in the PSV-software.
By not choosing the bandwidth as unnecessarily wide, measurement time can be
saved. Setting the bandwidth to 6.25 kHz, the eight lowest modes will be detectable
from the response (see Table 4.2). Setting the number of applied FFT-lines to 6400,
one obtains a frequency resolution ∆f = 0.977 Hz. To increase the signal-to-noise
ratio, an average of ﬁve samples is determined for each scan point.
The test specimens are scanned in 1D, with a grid-line located at one beam sur-
face, parallel to the longitudinal beam axis. The spatial resolution ∆s can easily be
varied in the PSV-software, and as it will appear later, it turns out that the applied
sampling resolution ∆s is extremely important in relation to the performance of dam-
age detection algorithms.

4.4.1
Modal Data Processing
In this section, the applied modal data processing will be presented. The MSs will be
determined by curve-ﬁtting a set of estimated FRFs, which subsequently are used to
determine the MSCs.
The essentials by using experimental modal analysis (EMA) is to describe the
natural characteristics (modal parameters), such as frequency (ω), damping (ξ) and
MS (φ) from the measured vibratory behaviour. The modal parameters are used to
validate the FE-model. Furthermore, the MSs are used to detect for local structural
changes, which can be interpreted as a damage indicator.
4.4.1.1
Frequency Response Function
To experimentally determine the modal parameters of a structure, FRFs are required.
Fundamentally, a FRF is a mathematical representation of the relationship between
the input and output of a dynamic system. However, in many applications it is not
possible to measure the input forces.
To overcome this problem, methods such as
operational modal analysis (OMA) have been developed, which determines the modal
parameters without having the input forces [28]. In the presented modal analysis, the
input force is measured and traditional EMA is applied.
The FRFs used in the presented EMA are determined by dividing the cross spec-
trum between the reference (force) response and the roving (displacement) response,
Gxy, by the auto spectrum of the reference response Gxx:

4.4.1.2
Modal Parameters
The modal parameters are determined from curve ﬁtting the FRRs determined in Sec-
tion 4.4.1.1. Curve ﬁtting, or parameter estimation, is a numerical process which is
used to represent a set of experimentally measured data points with an assumed an-
alytical model. From the curve ﬁtting process, one obtains the parameters that are
used in deﬁning the analytical model (natural frequency ω, damping ξ and residue φ).
A global curve ﬁtting technique has been applied, which implies a two step pro-
cess. Firstly, the frequency and damping parameters are estimated, and subsequently
the MSs. Since the damping is known before the MSs are estimated, they are in general
more accurately estimated during the second step [44]. Furthermore, the advantage is
that more accurate frequency and damping estimates can be obtained by processing all
the measurements simultaneously (global technique), when compared to curve ﬁtting
single measurements (local technique). Or one could say, that the applied technique
enhances the global features and attenuates the local, which may only be found in few
of the measurements.

Operating Data Processing
In this section, the operating data will be considered, which implies that the analysis
will not take into account the excitation (input) force.
Instead of estimating the
modal parameters by curve-ﬁtting mathematical models to the measured FRFs, the
measured responses will be used to directly determine the operating conditions. In the
operating analysis, the resonant frequencies ω and the corresponding ODS ψ(ω) will
be determined and are compared to the modal parameters, since only a small amount
of structural damping is present (see Table 4.4).
The operating quantities are distinguished from the modal parameters by being deﬁned
in the complete frequency band and not only for speciﬁc frequencies.
4.4.2.1

at each d.o.f., along with the correct relative phase information between multiple roving

4.4.3
Faulty Damage Detection - Rubber Bands
To simulate a free-free beam in the laboratory, rubber bands have been mounted to
the test specimens, which seems to aﬀect the back scattered laser light signiﬁcantly.
In Figure 4.9 (a) the 2nd ODS is plotted for damage Case A, where the presence of
the rubber bands clearly can be identiﬁed (marked with red circles). Inherently, these
irregularities are magniﬁed signiﬁcantly when estimating the OCS (Figure 4.9(b)), due
to the applied second order numerical diﬀerentiation approximation.
The found irregularities in the OCSs are extremely dominant and will inﬂuence
the considered damage indices, and the searched damage will become almost invisible.
To avoid faulty damage detection, due to the presence of the rubber bands, it is decided
to average out the eﬀect by using the two neighbouring points. However, this could
also have been solved by physically mounting the rubber bands without shading the
scanned surface.
For all the presented modal and operating data, the eﬀect from the rubber bands
are averaged out, in order to have a better representation of the measurements.

4.5.2
Surface Treatment
Theoretically, the reﬂective properties of a scanned surface are signiﬁcant in connection
to the performance of the SLDV system. Highly reﬂective surfaces obey the law that
the angle of incidence equals the angle of reﬂection. To acquire measurements from
such surface requires that the optics of the SLDV have to be aligned such that the
laser beam is reﬂected back within the aperture of the optics. Conversely, diﬀusivly
surfaces scatter the incident light over a large angular area with an intensity per unit
solid angle following the Lambert’s cosine law [2].
In the laboratory it has been found that the quality of the SLDV measurements
are highly dependent on the surface condition of the scanned object. The SLDV equip-
ment has a great advantage since it measures the response without any contact with
scanned object, which can eﬀect measured response. However, the technique has a
drawback, since it requires good reﬂective (diﬀusively) properties of the scanned sur-
face.
To obtain an optimal scan surface in the laboratory one can use reﬂective tape or
paint. However, this might not allays be practically possible for in-situ measurements,
where the scanned object e.g. is a bridge or wind turbine.
In Figure 4.12, the FRF Hxy(ω) and the coherence γ(ω)2 are plotted for a ran-
domly chosen but representative scan point (situated approximately one quarter from
the left beam end), with and without attached reﬂective tape on the scan surface,
respectively. From the FRF and coherence, one can clearly see how the measured
response becomes noisy if the scanned surface is untreated steel. Looking at the FRF
for the untreated-steel-surface, the noise ﬂoor is situated at approximately −120 dB
which is ≈60 dB below the maximum response peak. Considering the FRF for the
reﬂective-tape-surface, the noise ﬂoor is undetectable, since it is situated below the
minimum measured response, which indicates that the noise ﬂoor is at least 160 dB
below the maximum response peak. Furthermore, inspections of the input Fx(ω) and
output Fy(ω) spectrum separately indicate that the observed noise ﬂoor in the FRF
emanates from the output spectrum (the plots are omitted in the report).
The presented experiment demonstrates very well the importance of the surface
condition of a scanned object, when measuring the vibratory response using SLDV
technology. The characteristics of the applied reﬂective tape are developed with the

4.5.4
Mode Sensitivity
From the FE-analysis in Section 3.3, it is observed (from the change in natural fre-
quency) that the sensitivity towards structural damages varies among the considered
modes depending on the damage location. Inherently, this will have a signiﬁcant eﬀect
on the performance of the suggested damage indices, and it has to be accounted for
in the implementation of the damage detection algorithm. In this section, the mode
sensitivity eﬀect will be investigated from an experimental point of view, in connection
to the suggested damage indices.
A great advantage in using low-frequency modes is that they generally are global
compared to the high-frequency modes which are better at capturing the local be-
haviour. This reasoning is directly linked to the spatial wavelength of the considered
mode, which is decreased when the mode number is increased. Using high-frequency
modes might be better at accurately locating structural damages, but high-frequency
modes require a high resolution of sensors. However, using the SLDV system to mea-
sure the structural response Fy(ω), a high spatial resolution is easily obtained.
It is observed from the FE-analysis in Section 3.3 that the greatest change in
frequency is experienced when considering the lowest modes, which is consistent with
the argument that the natural frequency is a global parameter. However, the same
might not be true for the corresponding MSs or the experimental obtained ODSs.
In general, the following is observed in connection to the MSCs and OCSs; for
high frequency modes the ﬂexural bending moment M(x) (and thereby also the MSC
φ′′(x) and the OCS ψ′′(x)) at the anti-nodes is greater when compared to the low-
frequency modes. The greater ﬂexural bending moment seems to trigger a more sig-
niﬁcant irregularity in the OCS, as it is observed in Figure 3.7 and 4.8. However,
this observation does not unconditionally conclude that the damage detectability is in-
creased for high-frequency modes. Increasing the frequency level will equally increase
the number of anti-nodes in the MS and ODS, but inherently it will also happen to
the number of nodes (where M(x) = 0). In other words, the chance of making the
damage untraceable is increased with the frequency level, since the chance of a node
and damage to coincide with each other is increased.

4.5.6
Improvements for the Damage Detection Setup
In the previous sections, diﬀerent issues observed in the laboratory in conjunction with
the application of the SLDV for vibration based damage detection have been discussed.
This section will brieﬂy summarise the considerations and address what is suggested
for the ﬁnal damage detection setup.
The spatial sampling resolution is discussed in section 4.5.1 and the appearance of
the obtained OCSs seem to be highly dependent on the spatial sampling resolution ∆s
used for acquisition. If the structural response is sampled at only few data points, the
measurements might not capture the true behaviour. However, too many data points
seem to make the obtained curvature shapes erratic. The SG-ﬁlter is found to be
eﬃcient at suppressing the irregularities introduced when using a high number of data
points, but the smoothing eﬀect from the ﬁlter does also inﬂuence the irregularities
introduced due to presence of the damage. Experiments showed that the ability of
detecting for damages using the OCSSMI seems to be unaﬀected by the number of
data points (i.e. 35, 130, 200 and 340 data points), at least when damage Case A is
considered. However, it is expected that the number of data points does inﬂuence the
performance of the damage indices, when the detected damage becomes considerably
small, thus it is decided to apply 200 data points.
Experiments have shown that the surface condition of the test object has a great
inﬂuence on the response measured with the SLDV. The reﬂective properties of the
scanned surface are crucial for the possibilities of obtaining usable data sets.
For
the damage detection carried out in the upcoming section, reﬂective tape has been
attached to the surface of the test specimens.
Both single harmonic and pseudo-random excitation have been tested to ﬁgure
out if one is more suitable for the purpose of detecting for damages. The presented
experiment might not be suﬃcient enough to make any generalisation. However, the
experiment shows that the single harmonic excitations seems not too preferable for
the considered test setup, as it is proclaimed in [40, 21, 45]. Since pseudo-random
excitation is more eﬃcient and enables the opportunity for broadband analysis, it is

decided to use this excitation type when detecting for damages.
The considered damage detection indices are established by a summation of the
identiﬁed modes. However, the sensitivity towards the searched damage seems to be
diﬀerent from each mode, due to the change in location of the nodes and anti-nodes. To
ensure the best performance of the presented damage indices, each mode is individually
analysed, and only relevant modes are included in the summation.
4.6
Damage Detection Results
In the previous sections, it has been investigated how to test for damages on beam-like
structures utilising the SLDV technique, and diﬀerent issues regarding the damage
detection in general have been elucidated. To ﬁnd the limitations of using the SLDV
in cooperation with MSC-based methods to detect for damages, tests have been car-
ried out on beam-like structures. This section will present the results obtained in the
laboratory, which somehow quantiﬁes how well damages are detected, when applying a
currently existing 1D SLDV system to obtain OCSs. The experiments are carried out
in a closed environment (in a laboratory at DTU Campus, Lyngby), and the results
can certainly not be transferred directly to in-situ applications.
To establish whether modal or operating data is most suitable for damage de-
tecting in connection to the curvature based methods, the results from both types of
dataset will be presented. Furthermore, the presented detection methods (i.e. OCSSM
and GSM) will be expanded to consider the entire measured frequency band, such that
not only the resonant data is applied. It is proclaimed by Ratcliﬀe et al. (2000) [42]
that using non-resonant data will improve the damage detection ability.

data to clarify which type of data is most suitable to apply when one wants to detect
for structural damages. In this section, the result for each type of data is presented
and discussed.

4.7.2
Controlled Excitation Testing
The aim of the project was to use SLDV measurements to detect for damages, since a
high spatial resolution of non-contact measurements is expected to be valuable. The
SLDV frequency response testing was carried out using controlled excitation, which
was obtained using a shaker with a controlled input signal. Preliminarily, the SLDV
measurements were compared with the results obtained from the hammer impulse tests
in order to verify the application of the SLDV system and to quantify the structural
impact from the shaker attachment.
Only minor deviations, ±4%, were identiﬁed between the two types of measure-
ments, which mainly is expected to be caused by the presence of the shaker. The
ﬂexural stiﬀness of the steel stinger contributes to the local stiﬀness of the attached
test beam, which eﬀects the dynamic response. However, it is found that the modes
are unevenly aﬀected by the presence of the shaker, since the distribution of nodes
and anti-nodes will aﬀect the displacement and rotation that occurs close the shaker
attachment.
4.7.3
Data Post-Processing
The data post-processing was carried out in Matlab, except the modal parameter es-
timation which was obtained by curve-ﬁtting FRFs in ME’scope. In general, the data
processing was separated into two parts; one considering the modal data and another
one dealing with the operating data. The distinction between the two data types was
made tight in order to ﬁgure out which type of data was most suitable for damage
detection. From a theoretical point of view, the two types of data should perform
equally. However, there was an expectation about that the curve-ﬁtting procedure
might hide some valuable information about the vibratory behaviour, when ﬁtting ide-
alised mathematical models.
The estimated natural frequencies were compared with the resonant frequency
identiﬁed directly from the measured response and only minor deviations (±3%) were
observed, as it was expected. More dramatic was the diﬀerences observed between the
estimated MSs and ODSs, or more speciﬁcally the corresponding MSCs and OCSs.
The investigations showed that the MSs and ODSs appear smooth and comparable.
However, the numerical diﬀerentiation procedure used to obtain the MSCs and OCSs
ampliﬁes any irregularity in the displacement shapes which reduces the ability to de-
tect for damages. A Savitzky–Golay ﬁlter was used to smoothen the MSs and ODSs.

4.7.4
Issues Regarding Damage Detection
In the laboratory, diﬀerent issues regarding damage detection utilising the SLDV were
recognised and the observations and experiences were addressed. A general issue re-
garding SLDV measurements was the unavoidable optical phenomenon of speckle noise,
where the signal was spoiled at a few points and some kind of data smoothing was
necessary. The application of the Savitzky–Golay ﬁlter turned out to be a good way
to introduce a controlled smoothing eﬀect to the measurements.
The sensitivity towards irregularities in the measurements was found to depend
highly on the spatial sampling resolution, and a trade-oﬀbetween the sensitivity to-
wards damage and non-damage related irregularities was recognised. To detect for
minor damages (low reduction in stiﬀness), a high spatial sampling resolution was
needed. However, the small sampling intervals increased the sensitivity towards non-
damage related irregularities in the measurements and the identiﬁed damage related
spike was found to be non-signiﬁcant.
Theoretically, the reﬂective properties of a scanned surface are important in con-
nection to the performance of the SLDV system, and laboratory tests stressed the
practical consequences. By applying reﬂective tape (developed for this propose), the
general noise ﬂoor in the SLDV measurements was reduced signiﬁcantly, which inher-
ently had great eﬀect on the ability to detect for damages.
It was addressed in a review by Fan et al. [12] that investigations indicated that
the type of applied excitation should eﬀect the performance of the damage indices.
It was tested whether the suggested damage indices perform diﬀerently depending
on if single harmonic or pseudo-random excitation was applied. The laboratory tests
showed that for the considered test setup the choice of excitation method did not really
matter. However, the pseudo-random excitation seemed to be much more convenient
to apply, and furthermore, this type of excitation can be applied for broadband analy-
sis.
From the FE-analysis in Section 3.3, it was observed (from the change in natural
frequency) that the sensitivity towards structural damages varied among the consid-
ered modes depending on the crack location. Inherently, this had a signiﬁcant eﬀect
on the performance of the suggested damage indices, depending on which modes were
considered. To ensure the ability to detect for minor damages, it was found that each
mode should be individually analysed and subsequently only the relevant ones had to

4.7.5
Damage Detection Results
It was investigated how to test for damages on beam-like structures utilising the SLDV
technique, and diﬀerent issues regarding the damage detection in general were elu-
cidated.
To ﬁnd the limitations of using the SLDV technique in cooperation with
curvature based methods to detect for damages, tests were carried out on beam-like
structures. To establish whether modal or operating data was most suitable for damage
detecting based on MSC and OCS data, the results from both types of dataset were
presented. Furthermore, the presented detection methods (i.e. OCSSM and GSM)
were expanded to consider the entire measured frequency band such that not only the
resonant data was applied.
The laboratory experiments showed that the ability for damage detection was
increased when using operating data rather than modal data. Furthermore, it was
found that the traditional method (i.e. the OCSSMI) and the modern method (e.i. the
GSMI) were performing equally well, and were able to detect saw-cut damages in steel
beams with ≈43% in stiﬀness reduction, which was slightly better than previously
reported [45, 40, 25].
Finally, the broadband analysis showed that non-resonant data can be useful
when detecting for damages, since it was found that for some arbitrary chosen dam-
age locations, the damage related spikes in the indices appeared signiﬁcantly in non-
resonant frequency ranges. Furthermore, the GSMBI tended to be slightly more sen-
sitive, when compared to the broadband OCSSMI, but due to non-damage related
irregularities in the SLDV measurement, the identiﬁed damage related spikes were
non-signiﬁcant, and the presence of a damage with ≈21% in stiﬀness reduction was
only weakly indicated.

Discussion
The theoretical and experimental results obtained from the analysis have already been
discussed in the respective chapters. Furthermore, the experiences and observations
from the laboratory are discussed in Section 4.5. In this ﬁnal discussion, the ability of
using the SLDV technique for damage detection is considered and a set of guidelines
are established in order to clearly communicate the gained experiences. Finally, it
is discussed how to develop the SLDV technique in connection to damage detection
applications.
5.1
Application of the SLDV Technique
The application of SLDV for damage detection has great potential since no sensors are
mounted to the test structure. No requirement for sensor attachment entails a high
level of ﬂexibility, such that the same SLDV system can be applied for diﬀerent types
of structures, without spending a considerable amount of time on sensor reattachment.
Furthermore, the contactless sensors ensure that test applications are unaﬀected by
the presence of the sensors, which might enable the ability to detect for damages in
in-service applications. However, this project demonstrates how far away from reality
the application of SLDV for damage detection is. Laboratory tests showed that for
beam-like structures, one is able to detect saw-cut damages with ≈43% ﬂexural stiﬀ-
ness reduction. If the damage is reduced to ≈21% reduction in stiﬀness, the damage
detection algorithm only determines the location with a low level of conﬁdence. Nev-
ertheless, 20 % stiﬀness reduction is too much to consider for real applications, since
such a signiﬁcant damage might have catastrophic consequences for the functionality
of the application. Especially within the air-plane industry where every component is
==PAGE:106==

dimensioned according to a low safety factor.
Research regarding damage detection (or structural health monitoring) has been
going for more than thirty years, and still no reliable and global damage detection
method seems to exist.
In most real applications, damage detection relies on old-
fashioned local methods such as X-ray and visual inspections. This demonstrates very
well the complexity of this topic and the many challenges to face in order to obtain
applicable damage detection methods.
The limitations behind the application of the SLDV technique in connection to
damages detection have been investigated in this project. Numerical and laboratory
experiments have been used to investigate, e.g. the eﬀect of the spatial sampling resolu-
tion, ﬁltering the measured response, deciding which modes to consider and changing
the surface condition of the scanned structure.
The test specimens applied in the
project are slender beam structures, which actually in many cases seem to fairly rep-
resent parts of more complex structures.
The literature survey in Section 2.3 indicates diﬀerent applications (i.e. deﬂec-
tion response, curvature shapes, guided Lamb waves and strain ﬁelds) of the SLDV
measurements for damage detection. However, all the presented research has one thing
in common; none of the application methods are able to detect for minor damages (real
case problems). Ratcliﬀe et al. [42] demonstrates the potential of using broadband
data in combination with curvature shapes and accelerometer measurements. Since
this broadband analysis method seems to be undiscovered in connection to SLDV
measurements, this has been attempted in this thesis. Laboratory tests showed that
broadband data can be useful in connection to damage detection, since some damages
seem to be detectable from a broad range of frequencies. Even for some locations the
damage seems to be better represented by non-resonant data. However, the damage
detection ability seems not to be improved considerably, since the general level of mea-
surement errors seem to be too high.
In general, the application of the SLDV technique to detect for damage seems to be
limited by a number factors. Some of them are directly linked to the SLDV technique
and others are more related to the applied curvature based damage detection method.
One of the main issues seem to be the speckle noise in the SLDV measurements, which
introduce a greater amount of measurement errors to the sampled data, when com-
pared to other sensor types, e.g. accelerometers. Inherently, the error varies from
each scan point, which causes small irregularities in the measured response. In this
case, non-damage related irregularities are present in the ODS and MS data and are
ampliﬁed considerably, when diﬀerentiating the data numerically to obtain the OCS
and MSC, respectively. However, laboratory tests demonstrated how the quality of the
measured data can be increased signiﬁcantly, if the reﬂective (diﬀusively) properties of
the scanned surface are optimised. For some in-situ applications, it might be rather
demanding or inconvenient to attach tape or paint the to scanned surface, but when
considering scanning an aeroplane, this might not be inconceivable. Why not just
change the reﬂective properties of the painted surfaces?
A more general issue is the ampliﬁcation of experimental or numerical errors
in sampled data sets, when diﬀerentiating twice with respect to the spatial index.
Depending on the chosen spatial sampling resolution, the sensitivity towards irregu-

not related to the speciﬁc use of SLDV:
• Scan surface: The reﬂective (diﬀusively) properties of the scanned surface have
great inﬂuence on the quality of the measured data. Under optimal conditions

• Spatial sampling: The spatial resolution of ODS samples has a great impor-

to distinguish between damage and non-damage related irregularities. On the
contrary, using a low sampling resolution might result in an insensitive damage
index.
• Types of excitation: According to Qiao and Rucevskis [40, 45], the ability to
detect for damage is improved if single harmonic excitation is applied. However,
laboratory tests (Section 4.5.5) demonstrate that for the considered test setup,
no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in performance is observed if pseudo-random excitation
is applied.
• Data smoothing: A Savitzky–Golay ﬁlter has demonstrated great smoothing
ability (Section 4.5.3) to the acquired ODS data. However, if the non-damage
related irregularities in the acquired data are comparable in size with the damage
related irregularity, the ﬁlter does not have any chance to distinguish them.
• Mode sensitivity: The distribution of nodes and anti-nodes in the ODS data
depends on the considered frequency.
However, this variation inﬂuences the
damage sensitivity, since a damage is undetectable if it coincides with a nodal
point, while the detectability is increased if the damage location is close to an

from a broad range of frequencies. Even for some locations, the damage seems to

5.2
Further Research
Damage detection (or structural health monitoring) in general has received a great
amount of attention among scientists, industries and political agencies. The popu-
larity is an advantage to this ﬁeld, ensuring a continuous development. The project
demonstrates well how far away the application of SLDV for in-situ damage detection
is from reality, and how a signiﬁcantly amount of research is required, considering both
the SLDV system and data post-processing, in order for this to succeed.
Considerations about issues eﬀecting the application of SLDV for damage detec-
tion are listed, and can be used as inspiration for further research:
• The SLDV system: If the application of SLDV for damage detection is to
be used in real applications, advancements in the SLDV technique are required.
The current existing technique suﬀers from issues regarding speckle noise, where
the SLDV signal is spoiled at a few points by "speckle drop-out", and some form
of data smoothing seems to be needed to give a true picture of the MS. However,
depending on the level of applied smoothing eﬀect, there is a risk that damage
related irregularities become invisible (undetectable). The SLDV technique has
to become more robust, especially if it is to be applied for in-situ measurements.
• Numerical diﬀerentiation: In this project, a 4th order central diﬀerence ap-
proximation has been applied to numerically diﬀerentiate displacement shape
data with respect to space, which seems to be a method that more scientists
agree on [15, 40, 45, 41]. However, in general, there exists a couple of numerical
diﬀerentiation methods, which are better in handling noisy data. Nevertheless,
doubts exist if any of those methods are able to suppress the irregularities due
to measurement errors, without eﬀecting the damage related irregularities signif-
icantly.
Hensman et al. have proposed a new method for detecting discontinuities in the
smoothness of a function, without directly calculating the curvature, i.e. without
diﬀerentiating the MSs. The proposed method uses a Gaussian approach and is
tested on FE-simulations [22]. It seems to be relevant to test this method further
in connection to SLDV measurements.
• Modern signal processing: New signal processing methods are continuously
developed and have to be tested in connection to damage detection. An example
of a newly tested methods is pattern recognition. The pattern recognition tech-
niques utilise artiﬁcial neural networks and genetic algorithm to match pattern
features in e.g. FRFs [17].
==PAGE:109==
5.2 Further Research

• Analytical model: To better understand how to utilise modal parameters such
as MSs for damage detection, it seems valuable to have a simple analytical model
determining the MS and MSC of the damaged beam. The damaged beam can be
considered as a stepped beam, which currently can be solved semi- analytically
by using e.g. the Adomian decomposition method [32].
• Mode sensitivity: The laboratory test clearly indicated how the damage sensi-
tivity changed depending on the considered frequency (mode), see Section 4.5.4.
The experiments considered the ﬁrst eight identiﬁed resonant frequencies, and
nothing indicated that the damage detectability was either increased or decreased
for the high frequency modes. However, a diversity among the considered modes
was found due to the variation in the distribution of nodal points
It is suggested that a damage detection algorithm is capable of selecting only the
most relevant modes, which have to be considered in the searched damage index.
It is demonstrated (see Section 4.5.4) that a wise selection of modes to consider
improves the damage detectability of already existing methods. However, how
does the damage detection algorithm wisely chose among the considered modes?

Conclusion
Damage detection (or structural health monitoring) is a research ﬁeld of great interest
among many scientists. The great deal of attention conﬁrms the importance of the
ability to monitor or eﬃciently and eﬀectively detect an operating structure for dam-
ages, e.g. aeroplanes, wind turbines and bridges. Due to the variety in application
capability, a great amount of diﬀerent industries are interested in the development
of diverse and reliable methods (and techniques) for real applications. Furthermore,
political and regulatory agencies have a great interest in this ﬁeld since they are com-
mitted to ensure public safety, i.e. in connection to complex engineering structures.
The amount of literature within the ﬁeld of damage detection is almost over-
whelming and the diﬀerent suggested methods are countless. However, the methods
seem to be insuﬃcient when implementing them in real applications, and currently
in-situ damage detection depends on old-fashioned local methods, e.g. X-rays and
visual inspections. In this project, the vibration based methods or more speciﬁcally
the curvature based methods are considered, since the high spatial resolution of sam-
pling points obtained with the SLDV can be utilised. The curvature based methods
are found to be suitable in connection to the SLDV technique, since these methods
produce high sensitivity to structural damages. Especially if the displacement shape
is acquired with a high resolution of sampling points. However, due to speckle noise in
the SLDV measurements, non-damage related irregularities in the displacement shape
are observed. Even though the found irregularities are so small that they are invisible
to the eye, they become signiﬁcant when diﬀerentiating twice with respect to space (to
obtain the curvature shape).
Initially, the principles behind the curvature based methods were considered,
and the fundamentals were sketched. The theoretical considerations clearly indicated
the potential of these methods, but at the same time some limitations were observed.
Inherently, the damage extent would inﬂuence the triggered irregularity in the cur-
vature shape, which could eﬀect the damage detectability. Furthermore, the damage
==PAGE:112==
90
Conclusion
sensitivity was found to be highly eﬀected by the damage location and the considered
displacement shape.
A FE- analysis was carried out in order to initially test diﬀerent damage in-
dices. The analysis indicated that the MSC based methods were signiﬁcantly better
at detecting saw-cut damages in steel beams, when compared to MS based methods.
Furthermore, the FE- analysis made it clear that indices only using MSC data from a
damaged conﬁguration (i.e. the MSCSMI) did perform almost as well as the indices
including the response from the non-damaged conﬁguration (i.e. the MSCSI).
A comprehensive experimental analysis was carried out in order to investigate
the damage detection ability when using SLDV measurements in combination with the
curvature based methods. A lot of time was spent on the considered test setup, to
ensure a high quality of measurements, such that unknown eﬀects did not aﬀect the
considered damage indices. Impulse frequency response testing by using an accelerom-
eter was carried out, and compared with the results from the SLDV test setup. A
comparison of the results showed only minor deviations between the two test setups,
which conﬁrmed a proper application of the SLDV system, and that the structure is
hardly aﬀected by the shaker attachment. The experimental result was compared to
the FE-results and a good agreement was found.
The experimental work indicated diﬀerent issues regarding damage detection and
laboratory experiments were used to indicate their importance in connection to dam-
age detectability. The SLDV measurements showed a great sensitivity to the reﬂective
properties of the scanned surface. If the untreated steel surfaces of test specimens
were applied, the measured response was highly aﬀected by speckled noise, and the
measurements were useless if minor damages had to be detected. If reﬂective tape was
attached to the scan surface, a great improvement was observed. Furthermore, it was
observed that the spatial sampling resolution had to be chosen wisely depending on
the noise content and the general spatial variation in the response. However, a need for
data smoothing was observed and the Savitzky–Golay ﬁlter was found to be suitable
for this purpose. Finally, it was observed that the damage detection sensitivity had
a great variation depending on the frequency content. For some speciﬁc frequencies
the damages became undetectable since the damage locations coincided with a nodal
point.
The experimental analysis involved ﬁve diﬀerent damage cases where the damage
extent and location was varied. Initially, the analysis was separated into two parts;
one considering the modal properties and another considering the operating properties.
The analysis indicated that the obtained OCSs appeared more smooth when compared
to the MSCs, which was an important observed diﬀerence in connection to the damage
detectability. The observed diﬀerence was explained by small curve-ﬁtting errors for
the MSs, which were ampliﬁed when the MSCs were determined. However, the damage
detection results demonstrated a more signiﬁcant, and with higher conﬁdence, damage
indication if operating data was applied.
The OCSSMI and GSMI were compared to investigate if the damage detectabil-
ity could be improved by piecewise ﬁtting a cubic polynomial curve to the OCS, and
calculating the squared diﬀerence between the cubic ﬁt and the OCS. The results indi-
cated that both indices were not able to detect damages introducing less than ≈40%
==PAGE:113==
91
stiﬀness reduction.
The OCSSMI and GSMI were expanded and applied for broadband analysis,
and the potential of using non-resonant data for damage detection was demonstrated.
It was observed that for certain damage locations, non-resonant data had a better
indication of the damage location. However, the results did not really indicate any im-
provements in the damage sensitivity, and still, it was not possible to detect damage
introducing less than ≈40% stiﬀness reduction.
In general, this project demonstrates how challenging it is to apply the SLDV
technique to detect for structural damages in in-situ applications (or even in the lab-
oratory). The future prospects seem to be dependent on the possibilities of reduc-
ing non-damage related irregularities in the SLDV measurements.
Future research
will hopefully improve the current SLDV technique and invent new signal processing
methods, resulting in more robust and eﬀective damage detection. However, the op-
portunities oﬀered by the application of the SLDV technique for damage detection
seem to have a great potential and future research is the key to reach the goal.
