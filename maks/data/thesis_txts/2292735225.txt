Chapter 1
Introduction
Airborne Wind Energy (AWE) is a design concept which uses bridled aeroplanes, blimps,
and kites connected to a ground station to harvest wind energy at altitudes of 300m and
higher. The aim is to tap wind resources at higher altitudes where wind speeds are more
constant and the renewable wind power density is abundant for human need (Archer &
Jacobson, 2005).
Horizontal axis wind turbines generate most of their energy output
from the outer section of the blades due to the high tangential velocity the blade tips are
experiencing. This means that the tower and most of the blades used in a conventional
wind turbine are support structures, but take most of the investment cost. With ﬂying
crafts not only higher altitudes can be reached to tap wind energy but also most of the
support structure used in conventional wind turbines can be omitted.
There are two energy generation methods, on-board and ground-based. The on-board
generation uses propellers attached to the aeroplane or blimp which are driven by the
wind and the generated electricity is conducted through the cable (tether) to the ground-
station. For kites a ground-based generation is used and the kite pulls on the tether
which is connected to a drum to drive the ground-based generator in the reel-out phase.
As soon as the kite fully extended the tether from the drum the kite’s angle of attack
is reduced and reeled-in by the ground-station which consumes energy, where the net
energy output is positive. Both methods have their advantages and disadvantages, but
the energy output is approximately the same (Ahrens, Diehl, & Schmehl, 2013).
The technology still proves diﬃcult to implement due to challenges in controlling, material
selection, and safety measures for commercial use.
Nevertheless, AWE experienced a
growing interest in research and industry, and companies such as Makani Power (Google),
Ampyx Power, EnerKite, and Swiss Kite Power currently develop models for commercial
use. To depict this potential (Loyd, 1980) computed the theoretical energy output of
a 576m2 kite based on a C-4a wing with a lift-to-drag ratio of 20. At 400m altitude
with an average wind speed of 10m/s the power output is approximated to be 6.7MW.
Comparatively, the biggest horizontal axis wind turbine has an energy output of 7MW.
In this thesis the rib structure’s topology of a parafoil kite used in AWE and paragliding
is optimised. To ensure an optimal performance of the kite it has to maintain its shape

To avoid deﬂation in

the aerodynamic eﬃciency.
To optimise the number and position of these holes topology optimisation in combination
with a geometrically non-linear Finite Element Method (FEM) model for membranes is
applied. Topology optimisation (from Greek, topo = ”place”, logy = ”study”; the way
parts of an object are connected to each other) introduced by (Bendsøe & Kikuchi, 1988)
is a structural optimisation method which ﬁnds an optimum material distribution in a
design domain with boundary conditions for a given objective function (e.g. stiﬀness of
the structure) and constraints. In this thesis the Solid Isotropic Material with Penalisation
(SIMP) method is adopted for the optimisation which uses a ﬁctitious density variable for
each ﬁnite element ranging between 0 and 1. Voids (low density) with reduced element
stiﬀness are introduced by the optimiser at regions where the element strain energy is low,
and material is added (high density) where the element strain energy is high (Bendsøe,
1989). The ﬁnal redistribution of material density results in an optimised structure. The
membrane FE model incorporated in the optimiser was developed by (de Rooij & Abdalla,
2015) and is based on the tension ﬁeld theory which models out of plane wrinkling in the
membrane as an in-plane contraction to avoid the use of highly reﬁned 3D shell element
meshes. The membrane FE model was originally based on displacements and was re-
written to force-based analysis in order to apply aerodynamic loads.
The research question to be answered becomes: Is the combination of SIMP with
the ﬁnite element interior-point implementation of tension ﬁeld theory a ro-
bust and eﬃcient structural optimisation method for membranes, and can
this method be applied on ribs of parafoil kites? In order to answer this question
the implementation of the optimiser is tested on several simple veriﬁcation cases to en-
sure correctness of the program and whether its robustness and computational eﬃciency
are guaranteed. The rib model itself is considered in two ﬂight conditions, high and in-
termediate angle of attack, to ensure the structure is optimised for the most important
conditions in the ﬂight envelop.
The thesis is structured as follows. Chapter 2 gives an overview of the state of the art in
membrane mechanics and topology optimisation relevant for this thesis. In chapter 3 the
principle of implementing the tension ﬁeld theory in ﬁnite deformation is explained and
occurring stability issues are discussed and a solution is proposed. Chapter 4 includes
the derivation of the objective function and its sensitivity, ﬁlters, and the optimisation
algorithm used. The veriﬁcation is done on three simple structures, a sheared membrane
strip, a rotated annular ring with rectangular cut-out, and a rectangular membrane in
tension, which are presented in Chapter 5.
The aerodynamic load computation and
resulting forces acting on the rib structure are shown in Chapter 6 and the ﬁnal rib
model optimisation is presented in Chapter 7. Finally in Chapter 8, the thesis results are
concluded and recommendations are given on further work.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
In this chapter the tension ﬁeld theory and an outline of the state of the art in mem-
brane mechanics and topology optimisation is presented. First, the tension ﬁeld theory is
introduced which models wrinkles in the membrane as in-plane contraction assuming no
bending resistance. In the past, analytical and numerical methods have been developed
and the most important ones will be listed here. The section about topology optimi-
sation describes common ﬁlter techniques used in SIMP, and optimisation strategies for
geometrically non-linear structures.
2.1
Tension Field Theory
Membranes are very thin plates which have a negligible bending resistance and thus tend
to wrinkle under the slightest compression. One diﬀerentiates three membrane states de-
pending on the direction of the principle stresses. The membrane is taut if both principle
stresses are positive, wrinkled if one of the principle stresses is zero and the other posi-
tive, and slack if both principle stresses are zero, see Table 2.1. In Figure 2.1 the three
membrane states depending on the stress direction are depicted for clariﬁcation. It can
be seen that wrinkling occurs when the stress is acting only in one direction, causing the
membrane to deform out of plane.

2.1.1
Analytical Methods
(Wagner, 1929) was the ﬁrst to propose the tension ﬁeld theory to simplify the post-
buckling analysis of ﬂexible shear panels used in aircraft construction. He realised that
the wrinkle direction in a panel which is loaded in shear corresponds to one of the two
principle stress axes, whereas the other principle stress was approximately zero. Wagner
therefore developed the governing equations to describe the load carrying capabilities of
thin panels by assuming a two-dimensional, no-compression continuum with negligible
bending stiﬀness. When a negative stress on one of the two principle directions appears
the membrane will wrinkle, i.e. the compressive stress vanishes and is ”replaced” by an
in-plane contraction which causes zero strain energy, see Figure 2.3. The resultant stress
ﬁeld in that state is called tension ﬁeld which is directed perpendicular to the wrinkle
direction.

The theory has been further developed in (Mansﬁeld, 1970) and (Mansﬁeld, 1977) which
included an analytical solution for membranes with anisotropic and non-linear elastic
properties. The derived method considers tension rays in a wrinkled membrane which
change their orientation depending on the applied load and position in the membrane.
By incorporating the tension rays into a potential function the strain energy density
could be obtained. Two concrete examples were solved with his methods and validated
with experiments which showed good agreement with his theory. The ﬁrst example was
a sheared membrane clamped at one edge (Figure 2.2a), and the second was a rotated
annular membrane with a clamped cut-out in the middle. The problem with his method
was that it is only applicable to few boundary conditions and simple shapes and the
mathematics involved included solving an indeﬁnite surface integral. On the other hand
the two examples serve well as veriﬁcation of numerical membrane solvers because they
were experimentally validated.
A diﬀerent approach was taken in (Pipkin, 1986) and (Pipkin, 1994) where a relaxed strain
energy function was developed for isotropic and geometrically non-linear membranes,
respectively. The relaxed strain energy is a non-decreasing and convex function allowing
no negative stresses in the membrane which satisﬁes the basic assumption of the tension
ﬁeld theory. The convex property allows a stable and unique solution of the displacement
ﬁeld.
2.1.2
Numerical Methods
With the development of FE software the tension ﬁeld theory was also implemented nu-
merically. (Roddeman et al., 1987) proposed a method for describing the stress situation
in a membrane by augmenting the deformation gradient. The augmentation includes two
wrinkling parameters, wrinkling direction and measure of wrinkliness, which in combina-
tion avoid negative stress in the membrane. the method is applicable for geometrically
non-linear analysis of anisotropic membranes. The results show good agreement with
numerical and analytical solutions, but complete slack regions lead to a singular stiﬀness
matrix.
Alternatively to augmenting the deformation gradient the constitutive relation can be
changed which is done in (Akita et al., 2007). Here, the membrane deformation is split into
strain carrying and zero-strain carrying parts. The latter is projected out of the stiﬀness
matrix by modifying the constitutive law. The projection method was further developed

In (de Rooij & Abdalla, 2015) the relaxed strain energy function developed by Pipkin is

2.2
Topology Optimisation
Since its introduction in (Bendsøe & Kikuchi, 1988) topology optimisation experienced
a huge development. To reduce the complexity of the homogenisation method (Bendsøe,
1989) introduced the Solid Isotropic Material with Penalisation (SIMP) model. It makes
use of a penalisation exponent, which is assigned to every element density to improve
convergence speed and penalise intermediate densities to converge to a 0-1 solution. This
method proved to be simple to implement and the sensitivity of the objective function can
be derived with little eﬀort. On the other hand the mesh-dependence for a continuous den-
sity variable causes the optimiser to converge to diﬀerent solutions with mesh reﬁnement,
and consequently no mathematical solution exists. To counteract this issue, restrictions
on the density variables can be applied such as sensitivity ﬁltering, which in combination
with the volume constraint enforces a length scale on the topology.
(Sigmund, 1997)
introduced a method which adjusts the sensitivity of each element depending on their
neighbour elements as a weighted average commonly used in image processing to avoid
blurring. The weighted average is depending on a ﬁxed radius, which deﬁnes the range of
elements taken into account by measuring the distance between their corresponding cell
centres. With the sensitivity ﬁlter the chequerboard phenomenon is circumvented as well,
which is a caused by an overestimation of the stiﬀness of void and material elements in
chequerboard composition. The interface between material and void has a grey transition
which persist even with sensitivity ﬁltering.
Additionally to the sensitivity ﬁlter a density ﬁlter was adopted in (Bourdin, 2001) and
(Bruns & Tortorelli, 2001). Consequently, the density and therefore the stiﬀness is inﬂu-
enced by its neighbouring elements and the optimisation converges to one solution with
mesh reﬁnement. To reduce the grey transition dilemma a smoothed Heavyside projection
was introduced in (Guest et al., 2004) to further penalise intermediate densities at the
transition between material and void. The projection ﬁlter increases its steepness each
time after a number of iterations to completely penalise grey elements, approaching a real
Heavyside function. The ﬁlters are described in more detail in Chapter 4.4.

2.2.1
Geometrically Non-linear Topology Optimisation
So far most of the papers about topology optimisation consider linear material and ge-
ometry behaviour, and not many sources about geometrically non-linear topology opti-
misation exist For large displacements in a structure the ﬁnite deformation theory loses
its validity, which ignores higher order terms in the strain expression. The remedy is
geometrically non-linear FE which uses the non-linear Green-Lagrange strain expression
to better estimate large displacements. Due to its non-linearity the structure’s stiﬀness
depends on the displacement and therefore requires the displacement ﬁeld to be solved
iteratively.
The most common approach is the Newton-Raphson scheme which has a
good convergence rate as it ﬁnds the displacement at each load increment by reusing the
previous displacement solution until the residual of internal and external energy in the
structure approaches the convergence criteria.
Due to the lack of a stiﬀness matrix which describes the whole displacement path in the
structure the usual compliance expression as objective function for topology optimisation
is invalid. (Buhl et al., 2000) suggested the use of end-compliance which corresponds to
the compliance in the deﬂected conﬁguration as objective function but reported several
problems, as the design might become degenerated due to structural collapse. Alterna-
tively, the complementary energy expression can be used which results from integrating
the load over the structure’s displacement. It is shown that the topologies resulting from
the complementary energy are stable for the design load and all previous load steps, which
is not true for the end-compliance. Because of the promising outcome of minimising the
complementary energy it is used in this thesis as will be explained later.
One important issue with geometrically non-linear topology optimisation is the numerical
instability which can occur due to low density elements. As a result the tangent stiﬀness
matrix can become indeﬁnite thus cannot be inverted and causes non-convergence of the
equilibrium iterations. For that reason various techniques were introduced to circumvent
non-convergence. (Bruns & Tortorelli, 1998) suggested hyper elastic material to increase
the stiﬀness of highly stretched elements with low density. Later (Bruns & Tortorelli,
2003) proposed to exclude and reintroduce low stiﬀness elements from the FE analysis.
As reported in (Buhl et al., 2000) both method still cause problems which makes them
unappealing. Buhl also observed convergence problems in the FE solver when the dis-
placement of nodes surrounded by void started to oscillate. He suggested a method to
simply ignore low density elements in the convergence criterion of the FE solver since they
do not have a structural importance. With this method snap-through eﬀects could be
simulated which occur for suﬃciently large applied loads. In (Wang et al., 2014) a diﬀer-
ent approach is taken which is based on an energy interpolation scheme. Here, low density
elements are modelled with the small deformation theory and high density elements with
large deformation theory. The interpolation is done using a Heavyside function.
Despite all numerical issues reported in the past being global (indeﬁnite stiﬀness matrix)
the occurring problems for the membrane solver experienced during the thesis work were
of local origin, caused by indeﬁnite wrinkling strain tensors. Inverting matrices which are
close to singular introduces a round-oﬀerror and convergence problems were observed.
Chapter 3.5 reports about the exact reasons and how to circumvent them.
The only paper about structural optimisation in combination with the tension ﬁeld the-
ory is (Akita & Natori, 2008). They used his developed projection technique for wrinkled
==PAGE:26==

3.5
Numerical Stability
As described in the previous section for the sheared membrane example the eﬀective stiﬀ-
ness depending on wrinkling strain and stress has an inﬂuence on accuracy. Additionally
to that, stability issues of the FE solver were experienced during the thesis work, which
caused the solver to either give completely inaccurate results (due to insuﬃcient tension
in the membrane), or no convergence at all. Listing the two problematic membrane states,
slack and wrinkling, the reasons for the numerical stability issues can be explained.
• Slack: Low stresses cause the eﬀective element stiﬀness tensor approach zero re-
sulting in singular stress-strain relations. Also, the slack variable approaches the
barrier of stress positiveness which causes solution to become inaccurate since the
penalty factor is not exactly zero.
• Wrinkling: Highly wrinkled elements arise from the wrinkling strain and stress
tensors being close to rank-one. Inverting these tensor, as required for the eﬀective
stiﬀness tensor determination, introduce round-oﬀerrors which lead to inaccuracies
or non-convergence of the FE solver.
In order to resolve the slack issue the applied forces on the membrane have to be suf-
ﬁciently high.
This gives more tension on the membrane, increasing the stresses and
==PAGE:39==

shifting the slack strain away from the barrier. In case of wrinkling it becomes more
problematic, as this issue is problem and load case dependent.
For the rib structure
optimised in Chapter 7, elements neighbouring the bottom support suﬀered from severe
wrinkling which cause the FE solver to compute inaccurate wrinkling strain and stress
updates, thus slowing or even inhibit convergence. The remedy is to ﬁx more nodes at

3.5.1
Numerical Stability for Topology Optimisation
The stability issues became apparent during the topology optimisation as well when the
density in certain areas was reduced. A drastic change in density for some elements at
the interface, the region between material and void, caused the wrinkling strain tensor to
become close to singular. The use of the Heavyside projection ﬁlter to reduce the interface
thickness therefore often introduced stability problems. For slack regions the inaccurate
strain energy computation caused the optimiser to add some material to slack elements
since the sensitivity is proportional to the strain energy in each element, as can be seen
in Chapter 4.
An interpolation scheme to model low density elements diﬀerently (like in (Wang et al.,
2014) where low density elements are model as geometrically linear) was omitted because
stability issues occurred independent of load magnitude and minimum Young’s modulus
Emin. Several attempts were made to make the FE solver more stable, without success.
This included using a relatively high Emin, up to 10% of the material Young’s modulus
E0, which did not guarantee the optimiser to converge. Another attempted approach was
to shift the wrinkling stresses in the elements which were close to singular in the positive
deﬁnite region, similar as when the wrinkling strain is initialised (see Section 3.4). This
method prevented the penalty factor µ to reduce to the required stopping criterion, and
was therefore unsuccessful as well. Because no overall solution was found the optimisation
was highly depending on load case, load magnitude, and ﬁlter type. Nevertheless, in the
result section all examples shown were obtained by applying suﬃciently high loads.
Another point worth mentioning is the used incompressible Neo-Hookean material law
for the topology optimisation, which caused the FE solver to require more iterations to
converge or result in no convergence at all. In case of no convergence low density elements
caused the local deformation to become too high and the Neo-Hookean material law,
which increases its stiﬀness for high stretches, caused numerical instabilities. Therefore
most problems, unless speciﬁcally stated, were run with the linear elastic Saint-Vernant
material law to avoid instabilities.

3.5.3
Centring Parameters
In each predictor and corrector step the penalty factor µ is reduced with factors called
centring parameters, i.e. µpred = β1µ and µcorr = βcµ. In the predictor the centring
parameter is a constant value given as β1 = 0, but in the corrector step βc is chosen as the
maximum of β2 = 0.1 or the normalised ratio between predicted and corrected wrinkling
strain energy. Numerical stability issues were observed when the penalty parameter was
already reduced to the required value but the error in strain energy was not yet satisﬁed.
Consequentially, µ was further reduced until the strain energy was suﬃciently stable,
which could lead to ill-conditioned eﬀective stiﬀness tensors. To remedy this issue an
if -condition was introduced which changes the centring parameters β1 and β2 to become
1 if µ was suﬃciently small (below 10−6 of the starting value) to avoid further decrement.

Membrane Topology Optimisation
In topology optimisation the given structure is optimised with respect to a predeﬁned
objective function which describes the structure’s performance. For geometrically lin-
ear FE analysis usually the compliance (product of force and displacement vector f T u)
is minimised to decrease the total displacement, and therefore increase the structure’s
stiﬀness. In case of geometrical non-linearity no linear stiﬀness matrix exists which de-
scribes the displacement path for an applied load. Therefore, the complementary energy
expression is minimised which corresponds to the area above the stress-strain curve and
can be determined with the tangent stiﬀness matrix and load vector integrated over all
load increments. In case of the membrane ﬁnite element model the integration can be
circumvented and a diﬀerent expression is utilised as will be shown in this chapter. First,
the standard topology optimisation method for linear structures is shown which includes
the sensitivity analysis. Next, the complementary energy derivation and its sensitivity
analysis is presented, and ﬁnally the MMA optimiser, ﬁlter options, and a ﬂow chart of
the optimisation problem are shown.

4.5
Numerical Implementation
Similar to the membrane FE solver in topology optimisation the minimum of the objective
function has to be found iteratively. Figure 4.3 shows the basic structure of the algorithm,
starting with design variable initialisation as well as the FE model. For each given design
vector consisting of all element density values the FE solver is run and the complementary
energy and sensitivity is determined.
Afterwards, the sensitivities and in case of the
Heavyside projection ﬁlter the design vector are ﬁltered to enforce mesh independence,
avoid chequerboarding, and reduce the grey transition between material and void. In
order to ﬁnd the next set of design variables the MMA optimiser is used. After updating
the new design variables a convergence check is performed, comparing the relative change
in design variable of current and previous iteration. If the stopping criterion is satisﬁed
(relative change in densities of current and previous iteration has to be below 1%) the
optimiser stops and the ﬁnal design is displayed. Otherwise, the FE solver is run for the
updated design variables and the described procedure is repeated until convergence.

An operating cross-wind kite usually ﬂies in shape eight trajectories which supplies the
highest pulling force on the tether. During ﬂight the wind speed and angle of attack
experienced by the kite change constantly. For the topology optimisation only the two

only valid at the mid sections of the kite, where the span-wise lift distribution reaches
its maximum and its slope is at its lowest. Resultantly, only ribs at the mid sections
are considered here, which have bridle attachments to counteract the aerodynamic forces.

7.1
Numerical stability
With the FE solver being sensitive to low tension and highly wrinkled elements several
attempts were made to run the rib model with point supports (single supported nodes)
and the aerodynamic load. The elements next to the point supports suﬀered from severe
wrinkling, and the tension in the leading edge was insuﬃcient. Thus the FE solver failed
to even run a normal analysis with a volume fraction of 1. For that reason some changes
were made to the FE model:
• The point supports were exchanged with several ﬁxed nodes, such that no stress
concentration in the neighbouring elements was observed.
• The leading edge was vertically cut oﬀat the front support and an individual linear
FE analysis with the front part was performed, where the cut was fully clamped.
Then the rest of the rib was analysed with the membrane FE solver. The loads
at the cut were given by the reaction forces from the leading edge part. Figure

Chapter 8
Conclusion and Recommendations
8.1
Conclusion
With the increasing demand in larger kites, especially for AWE to produce a suﬃcient
amount of energy, the used membrane structures need an optimal strength to weight
ratio. Simultaneously, the structural deformation has to be as low as possible in order
to preserve the kite’s aerodynamic shape. The rib structures in a ram-air kite require
holes to guarantee a constant ram pressure inside each cell to avoid deﬂation. The size
and position of these holes has to comply with the above mentioned requirements. For
this matter, topology optimisation in combination with a membrane FE solver based on
the tension ﬁeld theory was applied on a rib structure to ﬁnd the optimal hole position.
The FE solver, developed by (de Rooij & Abdalla, 2015), is based on the convex relaxed
strain energy function which guarantees positive stress in the membrane. The FE solver
allows using 2D elements on a coarser mesh and is still able to recognise wrinkled and
slacked areas in the membrane, whereas the actual wrinkle shape is lost. Compared to a
3D shell element solution this method improves computational speed, which is crucial in
topology optimisation where the FE model is used in every iteration.
The relaxed strain energy in combination with ﬁnite deformation allows to determine the
stress states in the membrane: taut, wrinkled, and slack. Its derivation is given in Chapter
3, where curvilinear coordinates were introduced to express the strain in the membrane.
The relaxed strain energy is based on the introduction of wrinkling strain which has
to be positive for all displacements. From that constraint optimisation formulation the
governing equations were obtained. Due to the convexity properties of the relaxed strain
energy the minimisation of potential energy led to a unique solution.
With topology optimisation experiencing an immense growth over the last decades its ap-
plication for geometrically non-linear structures still involves numerical challenges. Since
no linear geometrical relation between load and displacement holds an alternative com-
pliance expression had to be found. In this thesis the complementary energy was used
to optimise the given membrane structure. In Chapter 4 the formulation of complemen-
tary energy was derived which is based on energetically conjugate stress-strain relations.

The membrane topology optimisation scheme was tested on three diﬀerent problems to
demonstrate its functionality. The ﬁrst problem consisted of a sheared membrane strip,
where an analytical solution for the membrane state was given in (Mansﬁeld, 1970). The
optimiser successfully removed material in the slack regions which do not add any strength
to the structure, as can be seen in Section 5.1. The convergence of the optimiser initially
showed a steep slope and converged to a stable local minimum. The membrane topology
in comparison with a geometrically linear case proved to be signiﬁcantly diﬀerent due to
the lack in bending resistance.
The use of Heavyside projection ﬁltering introduced some numerical stability issues for
the membrane FE solver, but in most cases a smooth convergence was achieved. The in-
stabilities originated from two types of stress states: slack and wrinkling. Highly slacked
elements, such that the solution approached the barrier of stress positiveness and became
inaccurate. These instabilities could be seen in the topology as well, because the sensi-
tivity is directly proportional to the strain energy. Highly wrinkled elements also caused
the FE solver to experience convergence problems, as wrinkling implies a rank-one wrin-
kling strain tensor. Numerical errors were introduced when inverting the wrinkling strain
tensor, resulting in non-convergence.
The second problem consisted of a membrane which was stretched from three sides and
ﬁxed at the forth, resulting in a purely taut state, see 5.2. The optimiser introduced holes
close to the support, which changed position depending on load magnitude. The linear
FE solver did not succeed in giving a distinct topology due to a strong local minimum
which occurred when the density was initially equally distributed. On the other hand,
the membrane optimiser clearly introduced a distinct topology and successfully reduced
the complementary energy.
A rotated ring with supports at a rectangular cut-out in the centre was the last test
example, found in Section 5.3.
The optimiser reduced density at locations where the
membrane was initially wrinkled. This problem suﬀered severely from instability problems
from the membrane FE solver, if the loads where not high enough or the membrane was
rotated too much. Also, the use of a Heavyside projection ﬁlter caused instabilities and
prohibited the topology optimiser to converge to a 0-1 solution.
Before the rib structure was optimised a 2D aerodynamic analysis was performed to apply
realistic loads on the rib structure. Compressibility eﬀects were neglected due to the low
wind speed of kites, and the panel code program Xfoil for a steady inviscid analysis was
used to determine the pressure distribution over the rib. In Chapter 6 the expression for
superimposing internal and external pressure on the rib is stated, which resulted in a pure
tensile load. The span-wise pressure was assumed to be constant between two cells. This
allowed a simple load expression, by integrating the acting pressure over two cell halves
and the top and bottom length of the rib.
The aerodynamic loads were applied on the rip structure to perform a topology optimisa-
tion. For that matter, the two load cases were linearly combined for the ﬁnal design. An
analysis on the best performing structure including the volume fraction was performed,
which showed the trend that higher volume fraction improve the structure’s performance

Recommendations
To improve the topology optimisation method for membranes several recommendations
are given. First, as describe in the result section the FE solver restricted the optimiser
in case of elements which were highly slacked or wrinkled. This resulted in cumbersome
ﬁne tuning of the boundary conditions and ﬁlter used in each individual problem. There-
