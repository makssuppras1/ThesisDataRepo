Abstract
The rise of critical IoT applications within different essential industries like the
health care and energy industry as led to a greater focus on Quality of Ser-
vice(QoS) as factors like latency, packet loss, jitter and throughput are important
for these applications. Traditional networks typically have limited QoS implemen-
tations due to the complexity involved. Therefore, this project aims to leverage the
new networking paradigm of Software-Defined Networking (SDN) to explore the
creation of a secure and efficient QoS management system.
A review of different IoT messaging protocols has been conducted to gain a bet-
ter understanding of how IoT applications transfer data at a higher layer and to
choose the most suitable IoT messaging protocol to fulfill the QoS requirements
of critical IoT applications. The selected protocol should be used in combina-
tion with QoS tools implemented using the ONOS SDN controller and the Mininet
emulator, which utilizes OVS switches as its networking devices.
In the final implementation, the metering functionality of ONOS was utilized to
mark and limit traffic based on classification performed using Deep Packet In-
spection. The results demonstrated that meters are useful for limiting latency and
jitter in the network, but this comes at the expense of an increase in dropped
packets. Thereby, the utilization of queues with Weighted Random Early Drop
(WRED) and meters of the type REMARK should be considered for future imple-
mentations to mitigate packet drops.

Introduction
Quality of Service (QoS) has become even more essential in the communica-
tion industry as many sectors are transitioning towards greater automation and
connectivity, thereby increasing their reliance on QoS in networks. The grow-
ing adoption of Internet of Things (IoT) solutions in various industries, such as
healthcare, introduces new critical applications that require a high level of QoS.
For these applications, even a single lost or delayed packet can lead to huge
problems.
The problem at the moment is that there still doesn’t exist an uncomplicated so-
lution that ensures the QoS requirements. As a result, most network providers
opt to over-provision the network, despite it being a costly and inefficient solution.
This is why extensive research has been conducted over the years to find an effi-
cient model that can guarantee the QoS requirements, which has lead to the two
models of Integrated Service (IntServ) and Differentiated Service (DiffServ). The
IntServ model has been deemed too complicated by most network providers due
to its per-flow state management and maintenance that also adds extra signalling
overhead in the network.
DiffServ is less complex model as it aggregates traffic flows into a set of classes
based on the Differentiated Services Code Points (DSCP) marking, which allows
it to prioritize some of the aggregated traffic flows. However, DiffServ cannot pro-
vide strict QoS guarantees as it doesn’t use resource reservation like IntServ, but
instead aggregates a number of flows into a class that treats each flow identically
[1]. This is were Software-Defined Networking (SDN) comes into the picture as its
centralized nature provides a better overview of the network which can be used
to make more strict and less complicated adaptations of DiffServ.
SDN is an approach that centralize the control plane of the network through the
use of a centralized software-based controller. This controller utilizes application
programming interfaces (APIs) and protocols such as OpenFlow to respectively
communicate with the underlying network elements and the business/user appli-
cations running on top of it. The SDN controller provides centralized control and
an unified view of the network, which facilitates easier management and coordi-
nation of the DiffServ policies across the whole network. This ensures that the
QoS application is consistent across all the devices on the network, which wasn’t
always possible with the traditional dedicated hardware switches as different ven-
dors could have different QoS implementations[2].
The programmability of the SDN controller also makes it possible to dynamically
adjust QoS requirements in network, so that it adapts to the changing traffic pat-
terns and network conditions. This adaptability can be automated through the
implementation of scripts that dynamically adjust QoS parameters in response to
predefined policies or monitored network events[3]. The adaptability that SDN
provides is essential for many new applications, where IoT applications like smart
security cameras are among the ones that can benefit from it. The smart secu-
rity cameras can use the adaptability to have a lower QoS during periods where

The abbreviation IoT refers to an interconnected physical devices embedded with
sensors, software, and connectivity capabilities that enable them to collect, ex-
change, and act upon data to automate and optimize processes. The wide variety
of IoT applications has led to not one, but many standard protocols for IoT. The
development of all these protocols is mainly due to the fact that IoT devices fre-
quently operate under battery constraints. The IoT protocols include both lower-
layer protocols like Zigbee and Bluetooth Low Energy(BLE), as well as application
layer protocols such as MQTT and CoAP[5].
Project objective:
The purpose of this master project is to use the properties of the Internet of Things
(IoT) and Software-Defined Networking (SDN) to improve the IoT applications’
Quality of service (QoS). As critical services are very reliant on QoS to ensure that
they have the sufficient requirements needed to function, so creating an efficient
and secure Quality of Service management system is an important task for future
networks.
This is a relevant topic as more and more critical services are being deployed on
the internet and these critical devices have requirements to latency, reliability, and
throughput as a dropped or delayed packet in some cases could cause a disaster.
In this project a design and implementation of an SDN based infrastructure and
controller is being developed to distribute and schedule the network traffic, so
that the necessary requirements to latency, reliability, and throughput for the crit-
ical IoT application is best ensured. This means a research of IoT applications
and their needed QoS requirements must be done, so that a QoS model can be
developed that support the different IoT applications.
The SDN implementation will be based on the Mininet network emulator, Open
vSwitch multi-layer virtual switch, OpenFlow protocol and ONOS SDN controller
platform. In addition, P4Runtime may be investigated to further improve the QoS
for the IoT applications.
The goal for the IoT part of the project is to investigate different IoT messag-
ing protocols like MQTT, AMQP (Rabbit MQ), STOMP, CoAP, XMPP, DSS, Web-
Socket etc. and see how the different protocols integrate with the developed SDN
QoS management system. This means looking into what the different protocols
can provide in terms of efficient and secure communication like improvements in
throughput, latency, reliability, and security features.
Different kind of test will be made to test the implemented QoS management
system. Some of the tests conducted will be the following:
• QoS policy validation: Test that the set QoS policies are being followed by
sending different kind of traffic through the system and see if they are being
treated according to the defined policies.

Software-Defined Networking
Software-Defined Networking (SDN) springs from the idea of decoupling the con-
trol plane from the data plane and then centralizing the control plane in a SDN
controller.
This makes it easier for the network administrators to manage the
network by giving them a better and more uniform view of the whole network.
The invention of SDN lead to the standardization of a new protocol called Open-
Flow which is the protocol that enable most of the communication between the
SDN controller and the network elements through the South Bound Interface
(SBI). The Figure 1 illustrates the overall SDN architecture, where the SBI can
be seen between the control layer and the infrastructure layer. There is also an-
other interface between the business/user applications at the top and the control
layer in the middle, which is called the North Bound Interface (NBI). The NBI cre-
ates a link between the SDN controller and the application through the use of
Application Programming Interface (API)s [6].
Figure 1: An overview of the SDN architecture from [6].
The three network layers seen in Figure 1 have the following functionalities in the
overall architecture:
• Application layer: These are the applications often written by the network
administrator that set the requirements and desired behavior of the network
traffic. For instance, what kind of Quality of Service (QoS) is needed for a
specific kind of traffic.
• Control layer: The SDN controller is the one providing the link between the
applications and the data plane, so it can provide information from the data
plane to the application layer and translate decisions from the application
layer into instructions on the data plane.
• Infrastructure layer/data plane: This consists of the networking devices that
forward packets based on the instructions given by the SDN controller. For
instance, marking incoming IoT packets with a DSCP value allows for the
implementation of QoS policies for these packets.

OpenFlow
OpenFlow is the first standard protocol that facilitates the communication between
OpenFlow switches and the SDN controller. For example, figure 2 shows the
overall components in an OpenFlow switch. The individual design of an Open-
Flow switch is more complicated than seen in the figure, so the description of the
OpenFlow switch used in this project can be seen in the section about the Open
vSwitch (OVS).
Figure 2: The overview of an OpenFlow switch from [7]
Figure 3 shows a more detailed description of how the packets are processed by
the OpenFlow switch. It can be seen that the packet comes in through the ingress
port. At this point, the OpenFlow switch will not have defined any actions to be
performed on the packet like forwarding it to a certain port or altering its header.
The set of actions that will be executed is determined by the ingress processing
which consist of a collection of flow tables that each have a set of flow entries.
OVS always start by looking at flow table 0 and see if there are any entries that
match with the incoming packet. In case multiple flow entries match with the
packet, then the flow entry with the highest priority is chosen.
The switch will then update the counters and execute the instructions of the flow
entry.
These instructions may include updating the action set, modifying the
packet header, and adjusting other metadata. The instructions can also be ac-
tions such as "Goto Table" and "Group" action, which respectively directs the
switch to proceed to another table for ongoing packet processing or process a
Page 5 of 63
==PAGE:13==
February 22, 2024
group table once the ingress processing has concluded. This all depends on the
pipeline processing, which enables the network administrators to define advance
forwarding logic[7]. If the packet has not been dropped in the ingress processing
due to table miss or other specified actions, then egress processing will be done
in a similar way to the ingress processing. The only major difference is that egress
processing may have no tables. In such cases, it will be skipped, and the packet
will be sent out on the output port specified in the ingress processing or group
table.
The flow tables often should support a table-miss entry, which can be either send-
ing the packet to the controller, sending it to a different flow table, or dropping the
packet. The table-miss entry always has the lowest priority and matches with all
fields omitted as it should only be chosen in case there aren’t any other table en-
tries that match with the packet. A table-miss entry does not by default exist in an
OpenFlow switch but must be created by the SDN controller.
Figure 3: The table matching procedure in the OpenFlow switch from [8].
Figure 4 shows a table entry in OpenFlow version 1.0-1.2, which has the three
fields [8]:
• Match fields: It contains the possible fields that can be matched to the in-
coming packets. This can be from the Ethernet protocol, Virtual Local Area
Network (VLAN) tags, Internet Protocol (IP), Transmission Control Protocol
(TCP)/User Datagram Protocol (UDP) protocol and more.
• Counters: It is used to get some statistics about the network, which can be
based on four different types of counters: per table, per flow, per port, or per
queue.

==PAGE:14==
February 22, 2024
• Instructions: It is used to modify packets, add metadata, or add actions like
forwarding packets on the chosen port, dropping the packet, etc.
Figure 4: The overview of a table entry in OpenFlow 1.0-1.2 created with inspira-
tion from [9] and [6].
One of the latest versions of the OpenFlow protocol added a few extra fields to
the flow entries in an OpenFlow switch as seen in Figure 5. The new fields are
the following:
• The priority field is used to set different priorities on the entries so that if
two entries is a match for a packet, then the one with the highest priority is
chosen.
• The Timeouts field defines the time before the entry expires from the flow
table.
• The Cookie field works similarly to cookies in a web browser as it can store
additional information about an entry that can be used by the controller.
Page 7 of 63
==PAGE:15==
February 22, 2024
• The Flags field can change the way the entry is managed. For example,
it might be that OFPFF_NO_PKT_COUNTS tells the switch not to count
packets from that flow.
Figure 5: An example of a table entry in a typical OpenFlow table from [7].
The overall structure of an OpenFlow group table can be seen in Figure 6, which
includes an ID, counters (a type), and some buckets that have parameters and
actions. The ID is used by the controller to update the group table and by the
other flow tables to send packets to the group table. Counters are utilized for
statistics, and the type indicates the type of a group table. It can be one of four,
where only indirect and all are required to be supported by an OpenFlow switch:
• Indirect: The indirect group is often used when multiple different flow entries
need to have the same set of actions executed as the indirect group only has
one bucket with a set of actions.
• All: The all group executes all the buckets and their actions and therefore it
is often used for multicasting or broadcasting of packets.
• Select: The select group is often used for load balancing as each bucket
has a certain weight that can be used together with a selection algorithm to
divert the traffic onto different ports.
• Fast failover: The fast failover group has multiple buckets, but only one
will be used at a time. Each bucket has a watch port/group that checks
the liveliness of the port/group. Therefore, the fast failover group monitors
the liveliness of the currently used bucket’s watch group/port, allowing it to
switch to the next live bucket in case of a failure in the primary bucket’s
watch port.

SDN controllers on the market
The SDN controller is responsible for managing the control plane in an SDN net-
work and is often called the ’brain’ of the network, as it creates the routing and
policy decisions. Therefore, the design of the SDN controller is very important for
the system to function properly. This has led to a lot of research in the design and
architecture of the SDN controller. The article [11] discusses it through the as-
pects of state consistency, scalability, flexibility, security, availability, latency, and
placement. These design aspects are being described in greater detail in this
subsection to get a better understanding of the design requirements of an SDN
controller:
State consistency
between the SDN controllers and the SDN switches is im-
portant as state inconsistency can cause many problems like the switches not
having the expected forwarding policy. State consistency is especially important
when multiple controllers are active in the system, which is possible for distributed
controllers like Open Network Operating System (ONOS). State consistency has
been implemented in many ways, where an example is the network information
base used to keep the state consistency on the ONOS controller. Some advan-
tages of distributed controllers are better scalability and load balancing as a new
controller can be added when the network grows and the load of the network can
thereby be distributed between all the controllers.
Scalability
is important for SDN implementations as the controller being a cen-
tralized control plane will get a lot of traffic, which makes it a potential bottleneck.
This has lead to the research of solutions to make the controller able to better
scale with a growing underlying network, where the main solution has been to use
a distributed controller architecture. Other solutions, such as implementing flow
handling strategies and proactive flow installation, have also been proposed. Flow
handling strategies aim to reduce the load on the controller by managing minor
flows directly on the switches. Proactive flow installation involves pre-installing
flows on switches before the flow installation has been initiated by a packet-in
message.
This could involve installing flows on all switches between the two
hosts before the first packet in a flow reaches the first switch in the network.
Flexibility and modularity
has become an important aspect when developing
software-based systems. In general, the structure of the network is constantly
changing.
Thereby, there is a need for a flexible system that can change to
accommodate new types of traffic. Flexibility of a system often depends on its
modularity, since breaking the system into self-contained and interchangeable
modules/components makes it easier to adapt to changes. This modularity can
be seen in the ONOS controller implementation as the controller compromises
many services and subsystems.

Availability and low latency
are two important aspects in networks today. This
is particularly due to an increasing number of critical real-time applications that
rely on a robust networks, demanding both high availability and low latency. High
availability is accomplished by the network discipline called network survivability
which expands the principles of fault tolerance. Fault tolerance is using redun-
dancy and diversity to ensure respectively that there is no single point of failure
in the network and that the redundant components aren’t in a shared risk group.
Thereby, the network can function even through failures as it has a network com-
ponent to fall back on (like a second ONOS controller). The latency in a network
depends on factors like processing delay and propagation delay. This means the
placement and processing capabilities of the controller are important to inves-
tigate as these can contribute negatively to the latency of the network. A dis-
tributed controller like ONOS is a good solution to this problem as it can distribute
the processing load between multiple controllers.
Security
is an important aspect of the design of an SDN controller as its cen-
tralized nature gives it some advantages and disadvantages when it comes to
security. The importance of a secure controller design is highlighted in the arti-
cle [12], which delves into potential threats to various components of the system.
The findings in [12] reveal that the controller is particularly vulnerable to security
breaches, since it is a possible target in 11 out of the 14 surveyed attacks. The
security of the controller and SDN in general will be reviewed in greater detail in
the next subsection.
Security
SDN brings numerous benefits to enhance network management, such as pro-
viding a unified and real-time view of the network. However, these improvements
are not without drawbacks. The threats against SDN networks are often more se-
vere than those of the traditional network as the controller has such a prominent
role in the network. In traditional networks, individual network devices are re-
configured independently, while the controller in SDN networks can re-configure
all the network devices [13].
The introduction of the SBI in SDN also creates new threats to the network as con-
trol and configuration messages are sent between the controllers and the network
devices. The NBI also generates a new threat landscape as external apps can get
access to the controller through this interface. And when it comes to distributed
SDN networks, controller-to-controller communication serves as an additional en-
try point that can become the target of Man in the Middle (MITM) attacks. This
highlights the importance of incorporating controller-to-controller communication
into the design of a secure SDN system.
Thereby, the introduction of the centralized architecture of SDN networks has led
to the emergence of a new threat landscape, emphasizing the importance of con-
ducting a comprehensive security review when designing an SDN network. Some
Page 10 of 63
==PAGE:18==
February 22, 2024
of the new possible attacks and their countermeasures are being investigated in
the rest of this subsection [12]:
• The Denial of Service (DoS) and Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS)
attacks can be used to flood the controller with traffic disrupting the whole
network so that new flow requests cannot be processed by the controller.
The DoS attacks against the controller can be done through SBI using a
compromised switch that floods the controller with Packet-in message, so
that the controller is too busy processing these packets.A compromised
switch can also flood the controller with new fabricated flow requests, caus-
ing the controller to send packet_out messages to other switches with new
flow installations. As a result, the other switches in the system will fill their
flow tables. These attacks are only some of many DoS and DDoS attacks
that can be implemented in SDN networks, so the mitigation of these at-
tacks is very important. The attacks can be mitigated by having strong au-
thentication and authorization of controllers and network devices. This can
be combined with rate limiting, traffic filtering (firewalls and intrusion detec-
tion/prevention systems), and anomaly detection to make a response plan
that can further secure the system if the system gets breached [12].
• Man in the Middle (MITM) attacks in SDN networks are mainly targeting the
SBI as intercepting and modifying packets between the controller and net-
work devices can enable the attacker to manipulate flow requests and modi-
fications. An example could be intercepting a ’packet-out’ from the controller
and changing it from ’forward’ to ’drop’. The controller-to-controller commu-
nication could also be a target for MITM, since a successful MITM attack
here could lead to disruption of the network. Mitigating MITM attacks can
be achieved by implementing the TLS/SSL protocol. This protocol encrypts,
authenticates, and ensures the integrity of messages on the SBI, so that
they cannot be intercepted or modified [12].
• Abuse of permissions and authority poses threats mainly to the NBI as it
can connect the controller to external illegitimate applications or third-party
applications that have been downloaded from the SDN App Store. These
applications can come with significant security risks as they can read and
write to the network state. Given full permissions, the applications can im-
pact or crash the network, and in some cases, create a remote channel to
gain access to shell commands on the controller. Therefore, trusting third-
party applications completely is a bad idea.
This is why the article [13]
presents an overview of different solutions for the problem, where the main
mitigation method among the solutions is to only give the third-party appli-
cations the permissions needed for operation.

IoT messaging protocols
The messaging protocols described in this section all have been used for IoT
applications and operate on the application layer. It means that they are respon-
sible for the format of the data exchange between applications on the source and
destination devices. The protocols described in this section is Message Queu-
ing Telemetry Transport (MQTT), Advance Message Queuing Protocol (AMQP),
Constrained Application Protocol (CoAP), Extensible Messaging and Presence
Protocol (XMPP), Data Distribution Service (DSS) and Simple Text Oriented Mes-
saging Protocol (STOMP).
MQTT
MQTT is one of the oldest Machine To Machine (M2M) protocols, first introduced
in 1999. It has been further developed and standardized by the standardization
organization OASIS, since then many brokers have been implemented by com-
panies such as HiveMQ and Eclipse. The overall goal of the MQTT protocol is
to be a lightweight and efficient messaging protocol that still retains some relia-
bility through the use of TCP as the transport protocol. TCP provides reliability
by its guaranteed resending of lost packets and that the packets arrive in the cor-
rect order. Encryption is not an in-built feature in MQTT protocol itself but can be
added by using TLS/SSL. MQTT offers three different QoS levels, implemented to
ensure reliability in case of unstable TCP connections. This is crucial to prevent
message loss in transit, which could be catastrophic if it involves critical packets
[14]. The difference between the 3 QoS levels is as follows:
• QoS 0 - At most once - The message is only published once by the client
and the broker will not send back an acknowledgement. The broker will only
deliver the message if the subscriber is currently connected as it doesn’t
store the message for future delivery.
• QoS 1 - At least once - This ensures that the subscribers get the message
at least once by using acknowledgements (PUBACK packets) to confirm
that the message has been received. In case the sender doesn’t receive
the PUBACK packet within a specified time period, then it will resend the
packet, which could lead to duplicate packets at the receiver.
• QoS 2 - Exactly once - This ensures that the message will only be received
exactly once by using a four-part handshake between the publisher and the
broker.
MQTT has the publish/subscribe architecture as seen in Figure 7, which means
that MQTT clients can either subscribe or publish to certain topics contained in
the MQTT broker. The MQTT topics are UTF-8 encoded strings, which can have
a max length of 65535 bytes.
The publisher sends data to a specified topic,
which none, one, or multiple subscribers can subscribe to. This means the broker
will forward the packets it receives that have already been subscribed to, while
it can retain messages for topics yet to be subscribed to. The publish-subscribe
architecture of the MQTT protocol provides characteristics like [5]:
Page 12 of 63
==PAGE:20==
February 22, 2024
• Efficiency due to the small minimum header size of only two bytes, which
makes it lightweight and therefore a good protocol for constrained devices.
• Scaleable as new connections can easily be created through the use of new
topics.
• Data agnosticity since the protocol doesn’t rely on a specified data format,
but can send all kinds of formats like JSON, XML, or a custom formatting.
• Bi-directional connections are possible as an MQTT client can both sub-
scribe and publish data at the same time, which means a topic can be cre-
ated for each direction.

Advance Message Queuing Protocol (AMQP) and Rabbit Mes-
sage Queuing (MQ)
AMQP is another lightweight protocol that has been standardized by OASIS. It
was initially introduced in 2003 and was fully standardized in 2011. AMQP has a
larger fixed header, spanning 8 bytes, in contrast to MQTT’s 2-byte fixed header.
Additionally, AMQP has the option to include a payload and an extension of the
header. It also uses TCP as its transport protocol and TLS/SSL for security like
MQTT, while it doesn’t have some predefined QoS levels like MQTT. It does use
something called a delivery tag (unique identifier) to track the delivery of the mes-
sage. This tag helps in distinguishing messages that can be considered settled
(requiring no acknowledgment) from those that are unsettled (requiring acknowl-
edgment). The AMQP messages can be set to be settled or unsettled before
being sent, respectively resembling the ’at most once’ and ’at least once’ QoS
levels in MQTT [15]. It is also possible to implement the ’exactly once’ QoS level
in AMQP, but it is not commonly used.
RabbitMQ is a message broker that originally was based only on AMQP, but it
has since been developed to include more protocols like MQTT, STOMP, and
XMPP. The architecture of RabbitMQ consists of producers, exchanges, queues,
and consumers, where an example can be seen in the Figure 8. The producer is
Page 13 of 63
==PAGE:21==
February 22, 2024
similar to the publisher in MQTT as it sends messages to the exchange who then
forwards them to the correct queue. The consumers are similar to the subscribers
in MQTT, since they can subscribe to the queues that they want to consume mes-
sages from. The exchange is similar to the broker as it routes the messages from
the producers to the queues through bindings. Four different types of exchanges
defines the routing behavior between the producer and the consumer. The four
exchange types can be seen below [16]:
• Direct - This exchange type compares the routing key of the message to
the binding keys of the queues and only forwards the message if there is an
exact match between the two.
• Topic - Topic exchanges route messages based on a routing pattern, a list
of words separated by periods, which may include wildcards like * and #.
These wildcards respectively match all words in a specific location of the
routing key or zero or more words before or after a given location in the
routing key. For instance, a binding key like ‘home.#’ routes all messages
with ’home’ as the first part of the routing key to binding queue, such as
’home.livingRoom’ or ’home.bedRoom.lights’.
• Fanout - The fanout exchange forwards the incoming packets to all queues
that are bound to the exchange. Thereby, the routing keys would simply be
ignored by the fanout exchange.
• Headers - The header exchange is similar to the topic exchange, but it for-
wards messages based on the header instead of looking at the routing key.
Thereby, the message is routed to a queue if the specified header values
in the message are equal to those of the queue’s binding key. The x-match
argument indicates if "all" or just "any" of the header values should be a
match to forward the packet to a queue.

Constrained Application Protocol (CoAP)
CoAP is a lightweight application protocol based on the Representational State
Transfer(REST) architecture and it was standardized by the IETF CoRE (Con-
strained RESTful Environments) Working Group in 2014 [14]. It was originally built
to be an HTTP-like protocol for constrained devices as it has a small fixed header
of only 4 bytes and it uses UDP instead of TCP. UDP is a connection-less proto-
col that uses less bandwidth compared to TCP, since it doesn’t use bandwidth to
establish a connection between sender and receiver as well as it doesn’t have re-
transmission in case of dropped packets. The lack of retransmission makes CoAP
more unreliable compared to other constrained device messaging protocols like
MQTT and AMQP that uses TCP, which is why it has implemented QoS in the
form of confirmable (at least once) and non-confimable (at most once) [17].
The CoAP header can be seen in Figure 9, which includes the fixed header of
4 bytes as well as the possible additional fields of Token, Options and Payload.
The version (Ver) field is a 2-bit field that describes the version of CoAP, while
the field type (T) defines the type of message (Confirmable, Non-confirmable, Ac-
knowledgement and Reset). The Token length (TKL) field indicates the length of
the token that is used in the request messages to avoid spoofing as it contains
a non-trivial and randomized string. The Code field defines the message class,
which can be GET, PUT, POST and DELETE for the request message and even
more options for the response messages. The Message ID field is used to iden-
tify and handle duplicate messages, but is also used to match responses with
requests [5].
Figure 9: The header of the CoAP protocol.
The Figure 10 illustrates the CoAP architecture that is based on REST, which
means it can easily be converted to HTTP through a proxy.
Thereby, CoAP
supports communication between devices in the constrained environment and
servers on the internet that support either CoAP or HTTP. It can also be seen in
Figure 10 that the devices in the constrained environment can communicate with
each other using CoAP.
CoAP is like HTTP built upon the request-response model, which means the client
makes a request and the server responds, but this is not the most ideal model for
constrained devices that sometimes sleep, since they wouldn’t be able to respond
when sleeping. Another disadvantage is the possible increase in requests that a
sensor can experience if many devices use its measurements.

through a method called Observe, which is similar to subscribing. It uses the Uni-

contains the updated value of the observed resource, the token provided during
registration, and an incremented observe value. The inclusion of the token and
the observe value helps the server and client to keep track of the observation [18].

Extensible Messaging and Presence Protocol (XMPP)
XMPP is an open messaging protocol that has been designed with instant mes-
saging in mind. It was standardized by the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF)
in 2004 and it uses TCP as its transport protocol. The main strength of the XMPP
protocol is its security features as it has in-built authentication and encryption
through the use of TLS and Simple Authentication and Security Layer (SASL) [5].
XMPP is using the Extensible Markup Language(XML) stanza-based streams
to connect clients to the XMPP servers as it is based on the request-response
model, but the publish-subscribe model has also been made possible to use by an
extension of the protocol. The XMPP servers give all clients connected to the sys-
tem a unique address based on the following format "user@domain.com/resources",
which is called a jabber ID. The overall structure of an XML stanza can be
seen in Figure 11, which has 3 components called presence, message, and IQ
(info/query) [17]:
• Presence contains information about the sender and receiver of the mes-

availability and status. It can also contain a type, which describes if the user
wants to subscribe or unsubscribe to a resource among other things.
• Message is used to convey information when using instant messages be-
tween XMPP entities. It contains the Jabber IDs of the sender and receiver,
the message itself, and the message type, such as "chat" or "groupchat".
• Info/query is used for exchanging information and performing queries

queries. It can contain the Jabbar ID of the receiver and sender, the type

Data Distribution Service (DDS)
DDS is a publish-subscribe protocol communication standard that has been de-
veloped by the Object Management Group (OMG). DDS is a middleware stan-
dard designed for real-time and mission-critical systems that require efficient and
reliable data communication. DDS implementations can support multiple trans-
port protocols, including TCP and UDP. The choice of transport protocol depends
on factors such as the desired level of reliability, latency requirements, and the
characteristics of the network environment. The DDS protocol doesn’t rely on a
broker like MQTT and AMQP. It is a decentralized system based on peer-to-peer
communication. Thereby, unicasting and multicasting are used to deliver the in-
formation between the subscribers and publisher through the data-centric DDS
domain. DSS supports 23 different QoS parameters, which span from setting the
reliability of the packets (best effort or reliable), to adjusting the history parameter
which decides the number of samples/data that need to be stored by a topic.
The DSS architecture consists of two layers, which are the Data-Centric Publish-
Subscribe (DCPS) and Data-Local Reconstruction Layer (DLRL). The DLRL layer
is optional and mostly used for better integration between the applications and
the DSS. It can be seen in the Figure 13, where it is located between the applica-
tions and the DCPS layer [20]. The DCPS layer is responsible for the delivery of
information between the publisher and the subscribers using specified topics.
Five entities are essential for communication in DDS, which are the Publisher,
DataWriter, Subscriber, DataReader and Topic. The publisher uses the DataWriter
to publish the samples to the subscriber, where a sample is a single piece of data
that conforms to the data type specified in the topic. The subscriber receives the
published data through the DataReader and delivers it to the application that re-
quested the sample. The topic, defined by a data type and its name, establishes
connections between DataWriter and DataReader, facilitating communication
between Publisher and Subscriber within the DDS domain, as illustrated in Fig-
ure 13.

WebSocket and Simple Text Oriented Messaging Protocol
(STOMP)
WebSocket has been developed to support real-time web applications between
clients and servers. Thereby, it is not developed directly for constrained devices as
it is based only on the client-server model, and uses TCP as its transport protocol
and TLS for security. It uses HTTP to establish the connection by sending an
HTTP GET request with an upgrade attribute set to WebSocket, which is the first
step in setting up a connection as seen in Figure 15. Hereafter, the server sends
back an HTTP response to indicate either a successful upgrade to WebSocket or
that it does not support the WebSocket protocol [17].
The handshake, as it is called, makes the websocket protocol compatible with
already existing infrastructure developed for HTTP like firewalls and proxies as it
uses HTTP to establish the connection. The next step after the initial handshake
is to set up a full-duplex communication channel over port 80. This connection
provides the possibility for both the server and the client to send messages to
each other at any time, and even simultaneously. In the end, the client or the
server can terminate the connection by sending a specific close frame, which is
indicated by setting the opcode to the value 8 in the websocket header [21]
The complete websocket header can be seen in Figure 14, which contains a fixed
header of 2 bytes and an optional header that can be up to 12 bytes. The first
field, called FIN, indicates whether the packet is the final fragment of the message
when it is set to 1 and not the final fragment when set to 0. It is always followed
by 3 zeroes in the header. The next field is the Opcode, which indicates the type
of the frame. The values for Opcode can include text (1), binary (2), closing frame
(8), and so on. The Mask field indicates if a packet is masked to protect against
attacks like Cross-Site Scripting (XSS). If the packet is masked, then the packet
will also include a masking key. The length field indicates the size of the payload.
If a packet exceeds 125 bits, it is denoted by either the value 126 or 127 in the
length field. If set to 126, then the length field has been extended by 2 bytes,
while if set to 127, it indicates that the length field has been extended by 8 bytes.

Summary
This section presented six different messaging protocols that have been used to
implement IoT applications. However, some of the presented messaging proto-
cols are more widely used by IoT applications, while others were originally de-
signed with other applications in mind. AMQP was initially designed by the finan-
cial sector to create a reliable, secure, standardized, and interoperable protocol
that could facilitate communication between different financial systems. There-
fore, the main focus was not on making it lightweight for IoT applications, which
are often constrained by battery limitations.
XMPP and STOMP were designed for instant messaging, meaning they lack the
lightweight nature required by many IoT applications. The same can be said for
DDS, as it has been implemented for real-time applications like aerospace and
defense systems. While DDS offers a robust set of QoS features, it is not ideal for
constrained IoT applications.
CoAP and MQTT are the only two protocols discussed in this project that have
been designed directly for IoT applications and their requirements. Therefore,
they are both good choices when choosing a messaging protocol for an IoT appli-
cation. In the end, MQTT has been chosen for testing as it aligns better with the
project’s goal of creating secure and efficient QoS management for IoT applica-
tions. MQTT has more support for QoS as it has 3 predefined QoS levels, while it
uses the more reliable TCP protocol instead of less reliable UDP protocol.

Quality of Service
In most IP-based networks, the default service of network traffic is an approach
called best effort that is based on the method called First In First Out (FIFO). As
the name suggests, the system starts by serving the first packet in the queue,
then the next packet in the queue, and so on. The method is often considered to
be fine as long as the system has enough bandwidth to throughput the network
traffic.
This is usually not the case though, since implementing a system with that amount
of bandwidth is expensive and the utilization of the system is often very low.
Thereby, systems will often lack bandwidth in the busy times, which leads to la-
tency, packet drop, and jitter. The packet drop, latency, and jitter are especially
harmful to video and voice traffic. However, they don’t have a large impact on
non-real-time data traffic, such as downloading a file. Thereby, it would be an ad-
vantage to prioritize the real-time traffic by using QoS. This is why different QoS
methods have been investigated and researched for many years, which has led
to the two major QoS methodologies that are used to design and implement QoS
in IP networks [25]:
• Integrated services (IntServ) is an end-to-end architecture model for QoS
that uses the resource reservation protocol (RSVP) to reserve resources
and enforce admission control for the establishment and management of the
dedicated flows. It is often referred to as hard QoS as it directly allocates the
necessary bandwidth to the different flows in the network. This is not a very
scalable solution as the system has a finite amount of bandwidth that can
be reserved (often nearly not enough), while the solution adds significant
overhead to the traffic due to the maintenance of reserved traffic flows.
• Differentiated Services (DiffServ) is a QoS methodology designed to be a
scalable and flexible solution. It implements QoS by having a set of traffic
classes with different priorities, which can be based on the requirements of
the company that deploys and manages the network. The packets are first
marked with the assigned traffic class and hereafter QoS tools are used to
provide the QoS requirements related to their traffic class.
The DiffServ methodology has been chosen for this project as it is more scalable
and could benefit from the control and flexibility that SDN provides. The proce-
dures and tools used to provide the QoS are described in detail in the next couple
of subsections.
Classification and marking
The classification of the traffic is the first step that must be done in order to provide
QoS in the network by providing higher priority to critical packets. Classification
means that we identify the packets based on certain properties like the type of
application, IP addresses, Type of Service (ToS) value, and so on. When the
Page 22 of 63
==PAGE:30==
February 22, 2024
packet has been identified then we can mark it with the appropriate priority, so
that packets like voice or video packets will get a higher priority in the network.
There exist many methods to classify the traffic, which include Access Control List
(ACL), port-based classification, Deep Packet Inspection (DPI), statistical classi-
fication, and protocol-based classification. Port-based and protocol-based classi-
fication are the simplest of the examples as they respectively look on the port or
the protocol when classifying the traffic [26].
The traffic that has come from outside of the network’s trusted boundaries will
always get reclassified and marked again since markings from the outside cannot
be trusted. This is done to ensure that devices don’t simply mark the packets
themselves as being high-priority packets, which may not be true.
There are
some devices from outside the network like some of Cisco’s VoIP phones that
also can be trusted to make their own markings.
The marking of the packets can be done in both layer 2 and layer 3 of the packets,
but the layer 3 marking approach is the one that is often used. The layer 2 marking
is done in the Ethernet 802.1Q/P Frame that contains a 3-bit field called user
priority field (PRI), which means that up to 8 different priority classes can be
implemented.
The layer 3 marking is done by using the 8-bit Type of Service (ToS) field in the
IP header, which means the markings in this field will remain unchanged on its
path through the network unless it is reclassified and marked again by a switch
or router. The marking in layer 3 is done using one of two approaches called IP
precedence and Differentiated Service Code Point (DSCP), where DSCP is the
more modern and expanded approach of the two [27]:
• IP precedence is using the 3 leftmost bits in the ToS byte to mark the prece-
dence of the packets. This means that it can only implement up to 8 different
classes. They later added the ability to use the next three bits in the ToS
byte for specifying requirements for the delay, throughput, and reliability of
the packets, while the last two bits were reserved for future use. Thereby,
the following ToS value 10010100 would mean priority class 4 (100XXXXX)
with low delay (XXX1XXXX), normal throughput (XXXX0XXX) and high reli-
ability (XXXXX1XX).
• DSCP is using the 6 leftmost bits in the ToS byte to mark the priority of the
packets, which means that there can be up to 64 different priority classes if
needed. The normal approach is to use the first 3 bits for the priority classes
like in IP precedence, while the other 2 bits are used for drop precedence,
and the last bit is always set to 0. The drop precedence is used by the
Weighted Random Early Detection (WRED) to set the minimum threshold
for when to start dropping the packets to avoid congestion. The Figure 17
shows the Assured forwarding (AF) implementation which is an example of
how QoS can be implemented using the DSCP approach.
• The final two bits of the ToS byte are reserved for TCP specifications and
Page 23 of 63
==PAGE:31==
February 22, 2024
controls, such as Explicit Congestion Notification (ECN) [28].

Policing and shaping
Policing and shaping are two methods used for managing traffic that exceeds the
specified rate called the Committed Information Rate (CIR), which is planned and
set by the network administrators.
Policing
is mostly used by the network operators as they often lease some of
their network capacity to other providers or customers through a Service Level
Agreement (SLA) and they don’t want the customer to exceed the negotiated CIR.
This approach is often used in the telecommunication industry as there are often
few operators that own the physical infrastructure in a country, while many of the
cellphone providers are leasing a part of their infrastructure through a SLA. The
Internet Service Providers (ISPs) also use policing to block all exceeding traffic
of their customers, so that you cannot send with a higher bitrate than what was
specified in your subscription.
Policing works by using an algorithm like the token bucket algorithm, which in-
volves adding a specific quantity of 1-byte tokens to the bucket each time a new
packet arrives. This process follows the given formula:
NT = (PAT –PPAT)·PR

(1)
Where NT is the number of tokens, PAT is the packet arrival time, PPAT is the
previous packet arrival time, and PR is the policing rate. Thus, the policing rate
(PR) determines the number of tokens allocated per time interval between packet
arrival times.

The next step is to check if the packet size in bytes exceeds the number of tokens
in the bucket or not. If the packet is smaller or equal to the number of tokens, then
it is a conforming packet and it will be sent normally. But if the packet is larger
than the current number of tokens, then the packet would be an exceeding packet
and will likely be dropped. The bucket can contain a maximum number of tokens
that is called the Committed Burst Size (CBS), which means that the bucket can
overflow. The overflowing tokens will be discarded in the classic version of the
token bucket algorithm.
Another version of the algorithm exists as seen in Figure 18, where the overflow-
ing tokens of the first bucket will be put into a second bucket with a maximum
number of tokens called the Excess Burst Size (EBS). When the EBS has been
reached, then the tokens will start to overflow, and the overflowing tokens will be
discarded. The tokens from the second bucket can be used in case of a burst,
which means some of the exceeding traffic called Excess Information Rate (EIR)
could be sent even though it exceeds the CIR. In case there aren’t enough tokens
in both buckets to send the packet, then the packet is called a violating packet
and will be dropped.

Shaping
is similar to policing as it limits the exceeded traffic, but it buffers the
exceeded traffic in queues instead of dropping them. This is why it is often used
by the customers of an SLA as they sometimes prefer to buffer packets instead
of having them dropped by the operator. The benefits of shaping depend a lot
on the customer’s traffic composition as real-time traffic cannot be buffered for
long due to its real-time nature. Therefore, shaping is particularly useful in cases
where traffic is not highly dependent on latency or in situations where it is crucial
to prevent packet drop during transit.
Cisco Internetworking Operating System (IOS) has multiple different versions of
traffic shaping that can be implemented, where two of the most commonly used
shaping methods are Generic Traffic Shaping (GTS) and Class-Based Shaping
(CBS) that respectively shapes on a per-interface basis and a per-class basis.

Queuing and Congestion Avoidance
Queues serve the purpose of buffering data in situations where the routing de-
vice’s CPU is occupied processing another packet, or the output port is engaged
in transmitting another packet when a new packet arrives for that same port. The
reason buffering occurs is often because the routing device has a larger capacity
or input rate on the ingress interfaces compared to its egress interfaces. Buffer-
ing can also happen due to an insufficient CPU on the routing device, so it is not
fast enough to process all the incoming packets. Queues are normally based on
the FIFO principle, which means the first packet that arrives is the first to be pro-
cessed. When the queues’ maximum storage capacity is reached, packets are
dropped due to limited storage space. This results in the router discarding any
incoming packets, including higher-priority ones, which is a condition called tail
drop.
This is where QoS becomes crucial, as a single hardware queue based on FIFO
often cannot guarantee the necessary latency for real-time packets during con-
gestion. Therefore, QoS will have to be implemented either in the form of extra
hardware queues called strict priority queues or software queues that decide what
packets should be placed first in the hardware queue. There are many different
techniques of software queuing that ensure priority packets will be sent first on the
hardware interface, where some of the most common ones are the following [31]:
• Priority Queuing (PQ) divides the packets into four different priorities each
with its own queue, which is high, medium, normal, and low. The high-
priority packets are always served first, then the medium-priority packets,
and so on. The advantage of this queuing technique is that the high-priority
packets always will be processed first, while the disadvantage is that this
can lead to no service at all for the low-priority packets when the network is
congested. This condition is called starvation of the low-priority queue.
• Round Robin (RR) and Weighted Round Robin (WRR) are commonly used
scheduling algorithms in networking.
In RR, traffic is evenly distributed
among all queues, treating each queue equally, whether in terms of pack-
ets or bytes. On the other hand, WRR enables the assignment of specific
weights to individual queues, allowing for more granular control over traffic
distribution. RR and WRR work by cycling through the queues and check-
ing if the queues have anything to send, if so then they send packets equal
to the specified weight. This ensures that none of the queues will starve,
but the implementation of RR will not give any priority to the high-priority
packets because of the equal weights.
• Custom Queuing (CQ) is very similar to WRR as it uses RR to cycle through
the queues and it sets a certain byte or packet limits for each queue that en-
sures more bandwidth for the high-priority queues than low-priority queues.
When it is a queue’s time to send, then it can send several packets equal to
its defined limit. There are a maximum of 17 queues in CQ, where the first
Page 26 of 63
==PAGE:34==
February 22, 2024
queue 0 is used for network control traffic and thereby cannot be configured.
• Fair Queuing (FQ), Weighted Fair Queuing (WFQ) and Class-Based Weighted
Fair Queuing (CB-WFQ) are three queuing algorithms based on the prin-
ciple of RR. The main goal of FQ is to make the scheduling of packets
more fair as it takes the number of bytes into account instead of the number
of packets. This means that a queue sending many small packets will be
able to send more packets overall. WFQ is similar to FQ, but it allows the
administrator to set weights on each queue instead of having equal weights.
This makes it possible to use QoS as the higher priority queues can get a
bigger weight and thereby their packets will be sent more often. CB-WFQ is
an extension of WFQ, which is based on user-defined traffic classes. These
traffic classes each have a queue and the characteristics of bandwidth,
weight, and maximum packet limit assigned to them.
• Low-Latency Queuing (LLQ) is similar to CB-WFQ, but LLQ also has one
or more strict priority queues that each have a certain amount of bandwidth
allocated to it. Thereby, the packets from the priority queues are sent first
until either the priority queues are empty or they have reached the band-
width allocated to them.
Another way to ensure the QoS requirements is to try preventing congestion by
using a congestion avoidance technique like Random Early Detection (RED) or
WRED that randomly starts dropping packets before the congestion happens.
Dropping packets before congestion occurs can trigger TCP slow start for TCP
connections whose packets were dropped. This mechanism limits the number
of packets these connections can send for a period after the dropped packets
are detected. Thereby, the overall traffic will decrease, so the congestion can be
prevented.

The Figure 19 is a graph of RED drop ranges with the probability of discarding
the packet on the y-axis and the average queue depth on the x-axis. RED has the
following three important parameters that impact when and how often a packet
is dropped, which are the minimum threshold, the maximum threshold, and the
MPD (Mark Probability Denominator).
The minimum threshold is the point where the queue starts dropping incom-
ing packets with a certain probability p. Hereafter, p will increase slowly up to
p = 1/MPD just before the maximum threshold is reached. When the maximum
threshold has been reached, then it starts dropping all of the incoming packets
like a normal tail drop. An example of RED can be seen in Figure 19 which has
a minimum threshold of 25, a maximum threshold of 45 and an MPD of 5. This
means incoming packets will start getting dropped at an average queue length of
25, then p will increase slowly up to p = 1/MPD = 1/5 = 20% just before it reaches
the maximum threshold of 45, where the drop percent will be 100% [32].

classes, then WRED can have one for each traffic class or queue. As a result,

high-priority UDP-based packets when the need arises to drop packets.

proves beneficial because prematurely dropping UDP-based voice or video pack-

Assured forwarding is one method used to implement differentiated services,

the 3 first bits define the traffic class, while the next two bits define the drop rate
and the last bit is always zero. The drop rate can be either low, medium, and

it starts randomly dropping packets earlier than the other two drop rates.
Explicit Congestion Notification (ECN) is another congestion avoidance technique
that has the objective to avoid dropping packets by informing hosts when early
indications of congestion are detected. This notification is made to prompt them
to reduce their transmission speed so that network congestion can be prevented.
This works by using the two last bits in the ToS field called the ECN-Capable
Transport (ECT) bit and the Congestion Experienced (CE) bit. The ECT bit is set
by the source host to indicate if it is capable of using ECN or not, while the CE bit
is set by the router in case of congestion.
Thereby, the routers rely on the destination host to notify the source host of the
congestion by setting the two ECN bits to 11. The destination host also has the
option of using one of the optional TCP flags [33]:
• ECN-Echo (ECE) communicates to the source host that it has received a
packet with the CE bit set.
• Congestion Window Reduced (CWR) communicates that the destination
hosts have reduced their congestion window due to the registered conges-
tion.
Routing
Routing policies are also an important tool when it comes to ensuring the QoS
requirements in a network. These policies can enhance QoS by either routing
traffic based on QoS parameters or implementing multipath routing for improved
resilience and network utilization.
QoS-aware routing
takes the QoS requirements into account when choosing
the path through the network. This means higher priority packets that need low
latency are often sent through the shortest path, while lower priority packets can
Page 29 of 63
==PAGE:37==
February 22, 2024
then be sent through the longer path as they don’t have as high latency require-
ments. The article [34] proposes an algorithm that first checks if both/all QoS
flows can be sent through the shortest path without creating congestion. If that
isn’t possible, then it reroutes one of them to a feasible path that can provide the
necessary QoS if one exists.
Multipath routing
is another tool that can be used to enhance QoS in a network
by distributing traffic across multiple paths. This makes multipath routing more
resilient, as it already has alternative working paths available in case of failures.
In contrast, a single-path routing scheme needs to recalculate the route upon
path failure. Multipath routing can also offer the benefit of improved real-time
load balancing, and thereby a better utilization of the network. This advantage is
particularly true when combined with an SDN controller, as it can monitor network
conditions in real-time and determine the optimal paths based on factors such as
link utilization, latency, and available bandwidth.
The article [35] discusses a solution that combines DiffServ in the form of two
traffic classes and multipath routing to provide good QoS. It can be seen that Diff-
Serv ensures better QoS for the high-priority class, while the multipath element
ensures that both traffic classes receive better QoS by utilizing the network more
effectively. Additionally, multipath routing provides instant recovery in case of a
failure as it continuously sends data through multiple paths. However, this can
lead to temporary congestion on the still-working path until a new path has been
found.

Open Network Operating System (ONOS) controller
Open Network Operating System (ONOS) is an SDN controller that manages the
control plane of an SDN network, which makes it easier to control and manage
a network in real-time as the controller has an overview of the entire network.
The ONOS controller is a distributed and modular controller, which means the
controller can be distributed across multiple instances. A distributed controller
improves the robustness of the network as it can provide both diversity and redun-
dancy in the case that the controller is located on multiple servers with different
geographic locations.
The Figure 23 shows the modularity of the ONOS controller as it shows the 6 dif-

in the ONOS framework, where a service is a cohesive functional unit consisting

The brown group is the different interfaces that can be used by the network ad-

administrators or developers, which are installed on the northbound interface of

QoS in SDN networks
The QoS model chosen for this project is DiffServ, so this section will look into
what tools can be used to implement it in SDN networks. The main contributor to
QoS in SDN networks is the OpenFlow protocol as it lays the foundation for the
communication between the controllers and the network devices. The evolution of
the OpenFlow protocol began by enabling queues with minimum rates in OF1.0.
Later, in OF1.2 and later versions, queues with both minimum and maximum
rates were introduced. Finally, in OF1.3, meters were introduced for ingress rate
monitoring. The controller and OVS also come with tools that can be used for
implementing and managing QoS. For example, the ONOS controller can create
and manage meters, while the OVS switch comes with the ovs-vsctl command-
line tool that can be used to create and manage queues [37].
Queues
The OpenFlow protocol doesn’t directly handle the creation and management of
queues, but it can be used to communicate with the OVS switch for queue deletion
and creation, as well as to gather statistics and information about the queues.
Additionally, it supports the ’set_queue’ action, which the controller can utilize
when determining traffic treatment for different flows in the network. The OVS
switch utilizes the ovs-vsctl command-line tool to create and delete QoS configu-
rations and queues on the switch. ovs-vsctl communicates with the ovsdb-server
for the creation of new QoS configurations and queues. The ovsdb-server is a
daemon that are in charge of managing the Open vSwitch Database (OVSDB)
that stores information about the configurations of the switch. This information is
then utilized by ovs-vswitchd to dynamically configure the switch in real-time [37].
The Open vSwitch Database (OVSDB) is a structured database, which is built
around having tables for each component that are linked together. The QoS table
can only be connected to at most one port table, so there can only be one QoS
table per port. The QoS table can have multiple queue tables connected to it each
represents a separate queue. As OVS is based on Linux, it utilizes the Linux Ker-
nel’s Traffic Control (TC) program to create queues and manage the scheduling
of traffic from the different queues on the port. In addition to scheduling, TC can
also implement other QoS methods like traffic shaping and policing.
TC offers a variety of scheduling algorithms to choose from. Examples include
FIFO (First In First Out), HFSC (Hierarchical Fair Service Curve), SFQ (Stochastic
Fair Queuing), CODEL (Controlled Delay), FQCODEL (Fair Queuing Controlled
Delay), and HTB (Hierarchical Token Bucket). Among these, HFSC and HTB
are Classful scheduling algorithms, while the others are Classless scheduling
algorithms. Classful scheduling involves the organization of traffic into classes
based on Type of Service (TOS), which allows for differentiated treatment based
on class-specific rules. Classless scheduling treats all traffic uniformly without
explicit categorization.

Meters
Metering was first introduced in the OpenFlow 1.3 specification [7] and comes in
the form of meter tables that have one or more meter entries, enabling the defini-
tion of per-flow metering or aggregated metering depending on the traffic selector
in the flow table that utilizes the meter. The meter tables are used to monitor the
ingress traffic and react when the traffic doesn’t conform to the specified rate. The
per-flow meter tables can be combined with the per-port queue to make more ad-
vanced QoS like DiffServ, so that the requirements of different kinds of traffic can
be met.
The form of a meter entry can be seen in figure 24 and it includes a unique ID,
meter bands, and counters. The controller uses the meter ID when it chooses
to use a meter as part of the flow’s traffic treatment. The counters in both the
meter entry and in the meter bands are updated whenever a packet has been
processed by them and it is used for monitoring and statistics. The meter entry
can have multiple bands that have their own band type, rate, burst, counters, and
type-specific arguments.
The band types available in OpenFlow at the moment are ’drop’ and ’DSCP re-
mark’, which respectively drop the traffic that exceeds specified rate limits and
set the DSCP remark to a new value. The rate specified in the meter band is
used by the meter to determine which band to apply. The selection process is
implementation-specific, but it often involves choosing the band with the highest
rate below the measured rate. The burst field allows for temporarily exceeding
traffic when bursts happens so that short-lived spikes in traffic can occur without
rate limiting the traffic. The measured rate is often calculated by using either a
token bucket or sliding window, where the token bucket approach was described
in the QoS section [7].

QoS requirements for critical IoT applications
The creation of a good QoS model for critical IoT applications depends on the
requirements necessary for the applications to function properly. Therefore, the
QoS parameters of latency, bandwidth, and packet loss have been reviewed to
develop the QoS model. The article [4] has established a QoS profile for sensors,
actuators, audio, and video by evaluating various sources. The QoS profiles in-
dicate that high-priority video traffic has the highest demand for bandwidth and
latency, while high-priority sensor and actuator traffic have the highest demand
for low packet loss.
The QoS model developed in this project should accommodate the requirements
of both high-priority video traffic and sensor traffic. By considering the highest re-
quirements among the different profiles mentioned in the article [4], we determine
that packet loss should be close to 0% and latency should be less than 100 ms to
be at an acceptable level for VoIP [44]. Achieving packet loss close to 0% implies
that the high-priority queues should either not have Weighted Random Early Drop
(WRED) installed or have WRED with large minimum and maximum thresholds
to minimize packet drops. The high-priority queues should be allocated adequate
bandwidth and priority to meet the latency and bandwidth requirements. How-
ever, it’s crucial to strike a balance to ensure that other queues are not starved of
resources.

Hierarchical Token Bucket (HTB)
is used as a traffic shaping tool to ensure
that the QoS requirements of critical IoT applications are satisfied as packets
travel through the network. It works by having a hierarchy of token buckets that
each are based on the structure seen in Figure 28. The black part of the figure
outlines the overall logic behind selecting the current token rate in HTB, which
depends on the minimum rate, maximum rate, and traffic burst/spike rate. If the
minimum rate is larger than the maximum rate, then the minimum rate is used
at all times. If the maximum rate is larger, then it is chosen as the token rate,
but the maximum allowed rate depends on the total amount of token that can
be borrowed from the parent element. The amount of allowed borrowed tokens
depends on the priority of the queue and the minimum rates of other queues
attached to the parent element. The traffic burst rate can be added to the token
rate if bursts are allowed and configured on the OVS switch.

queue size and queue type, which are respectively set to unlimited (only limited

The token bucket algorithm consists of a link to the parent queue/QoS element’s

bucket. The size of the queue’s token bucket also determines how many tokens
can be stored for later use in case of a burst.

itself. Queue 0 and Queue 1 belong to the leaf class, which means they perform

their minimum rates, while the remaining tokens from the parent will be distributed
based on priority, maximum rate and traffic demand.

ONOS App
The two main approaches to forwarding in an SDN network is reactive and proac-
tive forwarding, while other types like predictive forwarding and adaptive forward-
ing is less common. The proactive and reactive forwarding can also be combined
to create a hybrid forwarding scheme, which could involve installing flow rules on
all the subsequent switches on the path between two hosts when the controller is
handling the flow request from the first switch on the path between them. Reactive
forwarding has been chosen for this project because it is more scalable, flexible,
and automated compared to proactive forwarding. The main reason proactive for-
warding isn’t as scalable is because it requires prior knowledge of traffic patterns.
This is because flow rules need to be pre-installed on all switches [46].
The Figure 30 shows the exchanges that occur between the OVS switches and
the controller when the first packet in a flow traverses the network between H2 and
H5. Each exchange with the controller starts with the switch sending a packet-in
message with a copy of the packet (or its header) to the controller because it does
not know how to process the incoming packet. When the controller receives the
packet, it processes the packet based on the logic specified in the reactive for-
warding application. Hereafter, it sends a packet-out message to the switch with
an action specifying what to do with the packet. This message is often followed
by a flow_mod message instructing the switch to install a new flow rule, allowing
it to handle similar packets in the (near) future, depending on the timeouts value
set [46].

and H5.

The attached reactive forwarding code is based on the reactive forwarding code
provided by ONOS. It represents an application developed for the ONOS (Open
Networking Operating System) platform, which leverages services provided by
the ONOS project. One essential service is the ’topologyService,’ which plays a
crucial role in the reactive forwarding application. By interfacing with the ’topology-
Service,’ the reactive forwarding app gains access to real-time information about
the network’s topology, including device connectivity and link statuses. This in-
formation is crucial for making informed packet forwarding decisions. Specifically,
the application dynamically selects the output port for packets based on the cur-
rent network topology and the computed paths between the source and destina-
tion hosts using the ’topologyService’. This enables the reactive forwarding app
to adapt efficiently and responsively to changes in the network topology.
The ’packetService’ in ONOS is another essential service as it facilitates packet
processing based on selectors and treatments. Packets are selected for treatment
based on parameters such as source or destination addresses. The packet pro-
cessor in this project is based on reactive forwarding, which means the controller
needs to configure a ’requestPackets’ operation using a selector that targets all
IPv4 packets. This indicates its intent to process all such packets in case the OVS
Switch lacks a flow entry for them.
Hereafter, packets undergo inspection to determine their content. The controller
first ensures that the incoming packets are not control packets like Link Layer
Discovery Protocol (LLDP) packets, which should not be processed. Following
this verification, the controller checks if it has knowledge of the destination host
and the availability of a path to it. If not, the packet is flooded, and processing
is halted. However, if a path exists, further processing occurs. This may involve
installing a meter and defining a flow rule. Finally, a ’packet_out’ message and
a ’flow_mod’ message are sent to the OVS switch. These messages instruct the
switch on how to handle the processed packet and install a flow rule based on a
traffic selector and treatment, ensuring proper handling of similar packets by the
switch.

Discussion
The preceding section outlined the outcomes derived from the final implemen-
tation that employed an ONOS application with reactive forwarding to establish
meters at the initial switch of flows upon initialization. The Within the test envi-
ronment, two flow types were generated using Mininet for emulation, with OVS
serving as the virtual switch.The results were largely consistent with our expecta-
tions based on the QoS theory presented earlier in the report. This is especially
true regarding packet queuing due to busy interfaces and the metering of packets
using an expanded version of the token bucket algorithm. These results demon-
strate the utility of meters as effective tools for policing flows based on QoS re-
quirements, since it could partly mitigating congestion and its associated latency
and jitter by dropping exceeding packets.
However, the dropping of exceeding packets is also a disadvantage of meters,
as it results in packet loss. Packet loss may not always align with the QoS re-
quirements of critical IoT applications, since some applications demand near-
zero packet loss. An example of such an critical IoT application is a device that
remotely monitors the heart condition of a patient and sends status updates every
minute. If a packet is lost from this application, then the person could be in risk of
dying of a heart attack before the ambulance arrives.
This is why the initial plan was to avoid dropping packets that exceeded the high-
priority threshold by instead adjusting their DSCP marking to best effort. Unfor-
tunately, this approach was not feasible due to interoperability issues between
the ONOS controller and the OVS switch. Challenges like this underscore why
SDN implementation remains limited, as it continues to undergo research and
development. One challenge in the development of SDN frameworks like ONOS
is the independent nature of many existing projects, such as Ryu, OpenDaylight,
and Floodlight, which can lead to slower progress overall. At the same time, the
ONOS controller is quite extensive, with many services and subsystem, so the
focus is on many aspect other than the QoS in the ONOS controller.
Consequently, it can be seen that other university projects have encountered sim-
ilar challenges to those described at the beginning of the results section. This can
be observed in both the project [37] and the project [49], where both encountered
issues with dependencies, interoperability, and varying levels of support for Open-
Flow features between the ONOS controller and the OVS switch. These projects
were developed in the mid-2017, and it can be seen that some of the problems
have since been resolved. An example of an issue that has been resolved is that
OVS switches now support the use of meters, which wasn’t the case in early 2017
before the release of OVS version 2.8.
This further highlights that the development of a stable, out-of-the-box SDN con-
troller has not been achieved yet. This complexity arises from the multitude of
subsystems within the SDN controller that require seamless integration, coupled
with the necessity for compatibility with OpenFlow switches like OVS, mandat-
ing the establishment of a communication framework utilizing OpenFlow among
Page 52 of 63
==PAGE:60==
February 22, 2024
other protocols. Consequently, internet service providers (ISPs) have hesitated to
widely adopt the SDN framework, despite its promising features, due to the signif-
icant costs associated with replacing all existing equipment with new OpenFlow-
enabled switches. Thus, SDN frameworks must mature further before ISPs are
willing to commit to such substantial investments.
The implementation of an SDN network is also complex and would require some
employees to undergo education on SDN to properly set up and maintain the
network. Therefore, many ISPs are still not ready to take the risk of migrating
their network to an SDN network. This was discussed in the article [50], which
states that full mitigation would be nearly technically impossible, which is why they
propose a hybrid solution between the traditional network and the SDN network.
This would also make it easier to test the SDN part of the network without the risk
of the whole network shutting down due to unforeseen errors.
Despite the challenges associated with SDN deployment, it remains a promising
networking paradigm that could address emerging issues as more critical and
non-critical devices and applications become interconnected. This potential in-
cludes the implementation of QoS on a broader scale within ISP networks, a
practice often overlooked due to the complexity of managing QoS on a per-switch
basis. Currently, the primary solution for ensuring a good Quality of Experience
(QoE) for network users has been network overprovisioning.
The implementation of SDN and QoS management could be a big step towards a
more efficient and automated network that uses SDN network’s centralized mon-
itoring and control to make real-time adjustments. This could involve rerouting
best-effort traffic to less-used paths when the shortest path is just about to get
congested, and thereby achieving better network utilization. It could also involve
real-time management of QoS by adjusting QoS settings in switches whenever
the network state affects the QoS requirements of high-priority flows.
In conclusion, while SDN deployment poses challenges, it offers promising solu-
tions to current and future networking needs, particularly in managing QoS and
adapting to the evolving internet landscape. The concept of IoT is among the
technologies that will require an evolution of the current network, so that it can
accommodate the substantial amount of data originating from interconnected de-
vices and the increasing QoS demands of emerging critical applications.

Conclusion
The primary objective of this thesis was to explore strategies for addressing the
Quality of Service (QoS) needs of emerging critical IoT applications through the
utilization of a robustly supported IoT messaging protocol in combination with an
SDN-based network infrastructure. Unlike traditional networks, which often face
complexity in implementing QoS due to their decentralized nature, SDN networks
benefit from centralized controllers that can use its uniform view of the network to
control and manage the QoS settings.
Thereby, a review of different IoT protocol was done to investigate which protocol
would be the best to use to make a secure and efficient QoS management sys-
tem. MQTT was selected as the preferred IoT protocol due to its comprehensive
QoS features, which include support for three QoS levels (At most once, At least
once and Exactly once). The second and third QoS level enable the retransmis-
sion of messages for high-priority packets in case of an unstable TCP connection.
This combined with the default retransmission mechanism of the TCP transport
protocol further enhances reliability and significantly reducing the likelihood of
packet loss. The MQTT protocol can also be secured by using the TLS protocol
that ensures the confidentiality, integrity, authentication, and forward secrecy of
the packets, which significantly increases the security level of the MQTT packets.
Additionally, MQTT’s lightweight design, with a fixed 2-byte header, makes it ideal
for resource-constrained IoT devices, enabling energy conservation and efficient
operation. Its publish-subscribe architecture allows devices to enter sleep mode
during idle periods, further enhancing energy efficiency and extending battery life.
Overall, MQTT’s robust QoS support, lightweight structure, and publish-subscribe
model render it an optimal choice for facilitating QoS in critical IoT applications
within an SDN framework.
The master thesis should also include an implementation of the SDN network
that would enable the QoS management as a proof of concept. Here, the ONOS
controller was chosen due to its good performance, high modularity and scala-
bility. The implementation of the SDN-based QoS management ran into many
dependencies and interoperability issues that limited the final implementation to
a meter based QoS management system, which could limit the traffic based on
its priority (DSCP value). This could make the network able to avoid congestion
with the disadvantages of dropped packets, which was part of the original QoS
implementation design.
Implementing QoS in SDN is more cost-effective than in traditional networks, of-
fering potential for complex QoS techniques like DiffServ and Intserv. So despite
the the current dependencies and interoperability issues that can occur in the
ONOS, then many projects prioritize the development of SDN-based QoS solu-
tions, which will only increase as ONOS gets more mature and stable. The SDN’s
programmability and flexibility can lead to innovative QoS approaches that may
soon surpass traditional techniques, promising an era of unprecedented network
innovation.

Future works
The current project investigates some IoT messaging protocols alongside an
ONOS-based SDN network to explore potential QoS and security features they
could provide. However, the implementation remained incomplete due to various
issues, which should be addressed in future development efforts aimed at es-
tablishing an efficient and secure QoS management platform for IoT. Therefore,
future work will focus on resolving the ONOS compatibility error that obstructs the
use of the QueueConfigBehaviour function to retrieve queue IDs from the OVS
switches in the network. Additionally, efforts will be made to address the installa-
tion failure of the meter band type REMARK on OVS switches that happens due
to the occurrence of a Bad_band error.
Currently, the functionality of detecting the first switch on the flow path is hard-
coded, which is not ideal.
This can be fixed by using the topologyService to
retrieve the flow’s path and determining whether it is the first switch on the path or
not. Additionally, the meters installed on the first switch should be configured for
deletion after some time following the timeout of the associated flow rules, ensur-
ing that the switches’ memory is not unnecessarily occupied by unused meters.
The integration between MQTT and the SDN network should be investigated in
further detail, as there were some issues encountered with the Mosquitto broker
in the newest version of Mininet compared to the older version used at the be-
ginning of the project. Additionally, it would have been interesting to compare the
performance of various IoT messaging protocols within an SDN network.
The reliability of a network is closely linked to its Quality of Service (QoS), as a
network failure can severely impact the Quality of Experience (QoE) for users.
Therefore, implementing reliability measures such as backup paths could be ben-
eficial. This can be achieved through mechanisms like fast-failover group tables
or leveraging the topologyService for rapid recovery and rerouting in case of fail-
ure. Additionally, multipath routing offers another option for faster recovery, as it
already utilizes multiple paths and can immediately switch to a non-failing path in
case of a failure, without needing to compute a new route.
