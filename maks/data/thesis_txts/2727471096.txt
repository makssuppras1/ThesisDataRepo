Abstract 
The demand for sustainable products is at an all-time high, making it clear that we must 
transition from a linear economy to a circular economy (CE) and prioritize initiatives with the 
best actual improvement. Transitioning to a sustainable society requires a comprehensive 
understanding of CE principles and their relevance. This master's thesis aims to break down 
the concept of CE, explore the principles of Dual Circularity (DC) within CE, and investigate 
the potential impacts of Rebound Effects (RE) on initiatives using a DC approach.  
 
The thesis provides an in-depth analysis of CE, elucidating its significance in transitioning to 
a sustainable society. Subsequently, the concept of DC is explored, emphasizing its relevance 
and role within the broader CE framework. The thesis delves into the complexities of REs, 
examining how triggers, drivers, and mechanisms play a part in undermining the intended 
outcomes of DC initiatives.  
 
Together with this theoretical foundation, the project conducts case studies in three companies 
to gather practical knowledge on identifying RE, resulting in the developing of a new tool to 
identify possible REs due to initiatives using a DC approach. This tool addresses the need to 
identify possible REs effectively and facilitates extracting, analyzing, and organizing 
information from different initiatives to identify and understand potential REs. 
 
The tool brings a unique perspective and enables a new branch of research in RE by focusing 
on initiatives with DC principles. The tool is designed to be used on already implemented 
initiatives and initiatives under development. By facilitating the process of identifying and 
understanding RE, the tool helps maximize the actual sustainability impact of products for 
companies and provides a standardized classification and identification for researchers. 
 
The tool consists of an interactive and reactive questionnaire that examines the existence of 
triggers, drivers, and mechanisms in the analyzed initiative. The tool provides the user with 
developed guidance and training during the interaction, which means that the result of using 
the tool beyond identified RE is increased knowledge about how and why RE occurs.

Glossary of terms  
 
Circular Economy (CE) 
A systems solution framework that tackles global challenges like climate change, biodiversity loss, waste, 
and pollution. It is based on three principles, driven by design: eliminate waste and pollution, circulate 
products and materials (at their highest value), and regenerate nature. 
 
Dual Circularity 
CE initiative, which fully considers the flow of materials/products within and between biological and 
technological cycles, targeted at the enhanced overall sustainability performance of the system. 
 
Initiative 
An act, strategy, or product intended to solve a difficulty or improve a situation; a new approach to 
something. 
 
Rebound Effects (RE) 
Lower-than-expected environmental benefits due to the implementation of DC initiatives.  
 
Product 
A product is a tangible or intangible item or service created or offered by a company or individual for 
sale or consumption.

1. Introduction 
Sustainability is a crucial aspect of the modern world, encompassing social, economic, and 
environmental dimensions. It emerges from concerns about the impacts of human activities on the 
planet, such as climate change, resource depletion, and ecosystem degradation (Bhamra & Hernandez, 
2021; UN, 2015). In response, there has been a growing movement towards sustainability, aimed at 
promoting "development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future 
generations to meet their own needs" (WCED, 1987)  
 
The concept of sustainability emerged after concerns about the environmental risks of unrestricted 
economic and population growth (Donella Meadows et al., 1972; George, 1879; London, 1998; 
Schumacher, 1973).As the visible effects of pollution became more apparent, environmental awareness 
increased, prompting reactive approaches and end-of-pipe solutions(McAloone & Pigosso, 2017). When 
it was evident these measures were insufficient, preventive strategies and eco-design emerged to improve 
resource efficiency (Ahmed, 2012; Swart et al., 2004). However, sustainability was still seen as an 
expense, and the take-make-waste approach of the linear economy prevailed until the late 2010s 
(McAloone & Pigosso, 2017; Rose et al., 2002). After 2010, many companies began formulating 
measurable sustainability goals, shifting focus to effective tool implementation (Bovea & Pérez-Belis, 
2012; McAloone & Pigosso, 2017; D. Pigosso et al., 2011). 
 
Sustainability is commonly linked to 17 sustainable development goals (SDGs). The United Nations 
General Assembly established the goals in 2015 as a part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable 
Development, replacing the Millennium Development Goals (UN, 2015). The SDGs were a global 
initiative urging collective action to end poverty, protect the planet, and ensure everyone can enjoy peace 
and prosperity. They embody an interconnected framework that addresses sustainable development's 
economic, social, and environmental dimensions.  
 
Circular Economy (CE) has emerged as a powerful approach to achieving sustainability and many 
developed SDGs. By reimagining economic systems, embracing sustainable practices, and promoting 
circularity, numerous opportunities for sustainable development, economic prosperity, and 
environmental stewardship can be fulfilled (Schroeder et al., 2019). 
 
CE is recognized as a practical approach to sustainable development and is seen as a promising 
alternative to the traditional linear take-make-dispose system (EEA, 2019). CE aims to establish a closed-
loop system where waste and pollution are minimized and resources are used for as long as possible 
(EMF, 2019). While CE is often used as an umbrella definition for seeking sustainability through 
circularity, there is a need for a more comprehensive understanding of how to maximize the impact of 
CE initiatives (EMF, 2018, 2019).  
 
One area of CE which has received some criticism is that the visualization of CE is a notion adopted 
from the Cradle-to-Cradle concept resulting in an unnecessary separation of the two cycles (McDonough 
et al., 2003; Metic et al., 2022). The separation of the biological and technological cycles, visible in the 
butterfly diagram, overlooks that many bio-based products and materials also enter the technical cycle. 
This separation limits the potential achievements of CE and potentially neglects the benefits of using 
and recovering the value of renewable materials (EMF, 2019; Metic et al., 2022). This calls for a more 
holistic approach to CE combining technical and biological circularity to achieve a sustainable and 
regenerative economy. 
 
In response to this criticism, the concept of Dual Circularity (DC) has emerged as an approach within 
the broader framework of CE (Metic et al., 2022). While CE aims to establish a closed-loop system where 
waste and pollution are minimized, DC takes the concept further by encouraging having both biological 
and technological cycles in mind when deciding how products and systems are designed for sustainability 
(Metic et al., 2021).

By applying a DC approach, initiatives can be designed to enhance the overall sustainability 
performance by maximizing the circulation of materials and products, minimizing waste and resource 
depletion, and fostering regenerative practices (Metic et al., 2021). By emphasizing the integration of 
biological and technological cycles and recognizing the interconnectedness of natural ecosystems and 
industrial processes (Metic et al., 2022), the DC approach sets a higher standard for CE initiatives. It 
encourages the development of innovative solutions considering the entire life cycle of materials and 
products, from sourcing and production to use and end-of-life management. This comprehensive 
perspective helps to ensure that initiatives contribute to long-term environmental, social, and economic 
sustainability (Metic et al., 2022). 
 
Despite the adopted perspective of DC, achieving the desired sustainability outcomes of new CE 
solutions can still be challenging. To ensure the effectiveness of CE and DC in promoting sustainability, 
comprehensive approaches are required to avoid burden shifting and indicate how the benefits are 
distributed within the system (Corona et al., 2019; EEA, 2019). When initiatives are evaluated after 
implementation, the planned sustainability improvement often does not match the actual impact (Zink 
& Geyer, 2017). Part of this difference has been explained by identified Rebound Effects (RE) (Berkhout 
et al., 2000). 
 
RE is an often-overlooked area that plays a crucial role in understanding the true impact of intended 
sustainable initiatives. RE directly influences the outcomes of new initiatives and their effects on 
surrounding systems (Castro et al., 2022a). The phenomena of RE have the origin and leading research 
in an energy economics perspective (e.g., improvements in energy efficiency, yield reductions in the 
consumption of energy and/or material resources that are lower than anticipated). However, the 
knowledge and understanding of RE within other fields is growing. One of these areas is RE due to CE 
initiatives (Metic et al., 2021).  
 
This project is grounded in a comprehensive review by Metic & Pigosso (2022) titled: "Research avenues 
for uncovering the REs of the circular economy: A systematic literature review." Their study provides a 
valuable overview of existing literature on RE and presents the RE conceptual framework, which offers 
insights into the triggers, drivers, and various mechanisms associated with RE across different actors, 
levels, and directions. The RE framework developed by Metic and Pigosso (2022) will be a fundament 
for this project, guiding the exploration and analysis of RE. 
 
This growing area of research on RE in a CE will contribute to a better understanding and ultimately 
more sustainable initiatives but also this research will inherit the identified problems with the existing 
definitions and visualizations of CE. Therefore, this project will focus on combining two research areas, 
something that has not been done before, i.e. RE due to initiatives using a DC approach. 
 
To conclude, the reality of achieving sustainable impact through CE is more complex than what is 
depicted by the CE butterfly diagram. Embracing a holistic approach to sustainable development 
requires new tools to facilitate the complicated process. The biological and technological cycle 
interconnection needs to be more frequently addressed in academia and practice, as it holds the potential 
for promoting more sustainable initiatives. RE has been recognized in CE research, but we still need to 
enhance our understanding of identifying and assessing these effects efficiently and effectively due to DC  
solutions. To contribute to the work towards understanding these identified gaps in research, this thesis 
will work towards the stated goal of: 
 
To develop a tool for identifying possible RE due to initiatives using a DC approach. 
 
To fulfill this objective, the project has conducted two cycles.  (1) identify companies actively working 
with initiatives using a DC approach. I.e., initiatives utilize principles from both cycles using bio-based 
materials and strategies such as reuse, repair, remanufacture, recycling, etc. Develop a way to gather 
and analyze these company initiatives to identify possible RE. Collect lessons on the identification

2. Project Methodology 
The Methodology for the project was developed on the principles of a DRM framework. This framework 
outlines an iterative research process consisting of four phases. (i) The first phase is research Clarification 
(RC), which entails understanding and defining the research problem. In this step, the focus is on 
reviewing existing literature, identifying gaps in knowledge, and formulating clear research objectives 
and hypotheses. The RC phase helps establish the goals, scope, and context of the research project 
(Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009) 
 
Following RC, the next step is (ii) Descriptive Study I (DS1). This step focuses on conducting systematic 
observations and data collection to comprehensively describe phenomena or situations related to the 
research problem. The focus is to answer questions such as "What is happening?" or "What are the

characteristics of a particular phenomenon?" DS1 aims to establish a baseline understanding, uncover 
patterns, and inform subsequent research activities (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). 
 
Once DSI is complete, the (iii) Prescriptive Study (PS) begins. This step focuses on generating and 
evaluating potential solutions or interventions to address the identified research problem. The aim is to 
develop and refine design concepts, prototypes, or models to propose prescriptive solutions. The 
emphasis is on exploring and evaluating different design options to determine the most effective and 
feasible approach to solving the problem (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). 
 
The final step in the DRM framework is (iv) Descriptive Study 2 (DS2), which is a follow-up to DS1. 
This step focuses on conducting further observation and data collection to evaluate the effectiveness and 
impact of the implemented solution from the PS. The aim is to assess whether the prescribed solution 
achieved the desired outcomes and identify unforeseen consequences or issues. The findings from DS2 
help refine the solution and generate valuable insights for future design projects (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 
2009). 
 
These four steps (i-iv) form a continuous chain of loops within the DRM framework. Each step builds 
upon the previous ones, simplifying the iterative nature of design research. This cyclic process fosters a 
deeper understanding of the problem, generates innovative solutions, and evaluates their effectiveness, 
ultimately leading to more impactful and successful design outcomes (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009).

fulfilled. Cycle 1 followed a qualitative analysis of possible RE due to two various DC initiatives in two

3.1 General Introduction to Circular Ekonomi 
CE is an alternative economic model that aims to replace the traditional, take-make-use-dispose pattern 
of the linear economy (EMF, 2013). The concept of CE dates back to the late 1970s but gained significant 
traction in society later (Schöggl et al., 2020). Due to the broad scope of the concept, many different 
definitions have been created to explain and define CE; however, a common denominator is the strive 
to establish a closed-loop system that focuses on retaining value (Metic et al., 2022; Nasr et al., 2018).  
 
Some researchers define CE as a system that promotes regeneration by reducing resource input and 
minimizing waste, emissions, and energy leakage. This is achieved by slowing, closing, and narrowing 
material and energy loops through durable design, maintenance, repair, reuse, remanufacturing, 
refurbishing, and recycling (Geissdoerfer et al., 2017). Another frequently used definition of CE is to 
keep resources valuable for as long as possible by recycling them back into the product cycle when they 
are no longer needed to minimize waste (EC, 2015). The problem with these definitions in the context 
of this project is their failure to include or specify the place of biological and natural materials and 
methods in the CE. More definitions need to be examined to find a definition that includes this essential 
part of the sustainable transformation of societies. 
 
CE has also been defined as a way for an economy not to affect the environment around it. Instead, it 
should focus on restoring any impact on resource attainment while ensuring that little waste is produced 
through the products' production process and life cycle (A. Murray et al., 2017). This definition 
highlights that the environment in which CE operates needs to be restored after the resources are 
extracted. This highlights the importance of using natural regenerative materials in transitioning towards 
more sustainable approaches. However, the definition is still too vague in standardized ways to 
implement this. Therefore, this project has chosen to use the EMF (2021) definition of CE:

Their description of CE involves two cycles - biological and technical (EMF, 2019). The cycles aim to 
optimize resource utilization and minimize waste generation. They both emphasize the significance of 
closing material loops to maintain resource value. However, they differ in their primary focuses and 
strategies (EMF, 2013).  
 
The biological cycle on the left side of Figure 2 concentrates on biological materials and processes, 
including agriculture, forestry, and biomimicry, promoting regenerative practices to foster natural loops 
and ecosystem health (EMF, 2018). After consumption, the cycle uses natural loops to reintroduce 
biologically-based materials into the system through composting and anaerobic digestion (EMF, 2019). 
However, ideally, materials are reused multiple times before they are used in higher natural loops or are 
reintroduced as nutrients or used for energy (EMF, 2013). 
 
The technical cycle, depicted on the right side of Figure 2, prioritizes design for disassembly, material 
substitution, and technological innovation to enable resource recovery and reuse (EMF, 2014). The cycle 
focuses on materials that contaminate the biological nutrient flow and do not quickly degrade (EMF, 
2019). The technological cycle employs strategies such as sharing, repairing, reusing, 
remanufacturing/refurbishing, and, as a last resort, recycling to recover and restore products, 
components, and embedded energy before reaching their lowest value (EMF, 2014).  
 
While the technical and biological cycles have distinct focuses, they are interconnected and can mutually 
reinforce each other within a CE. The technical cycle can benefit from utilizing bio-based materials and 
biomimetic design from the biological cycle. In contrast, the biological cycle can benefit from 
technological advancements such as precision agriculture and smart farming (Metic et al., 2021). 
However, these synergies are often overlooked due to the separation, different approaches, and domains 
associated with each cycle (Metic et al., 2022). The technical cycle tends to align with industrial and 
engineering disciplines, while the biological cycle is closely related to ecological and biological sciences

3.2 General Introduction to Dual Circularity 
One effort to counteract the identified problem by separating the technical and biological cycles is the 
concept of DC (EMF, 2019; Metic et al., 2022). The idea encourages considering biological and 
technological cycles when deciding how products and systems are designed for a sustainability-driven 
shift to CE. Metic et al. (2022) argue for the need for the concept because CE tools are designed with a 
flaw from their origin. The visualization of CE is a notion adopted from the Cradle-to-Cradle concept 
resulting in an unnecessary separation of the two spheres (McDonough et al., 2003).  
 
Moreover, Harris et al. (2018) argue that the distinction could be more explicit in practice, leading to 
overlooking potential contributions, such as improving circularity and sustainable use of resources. To 
counteract this, Metic et al. (2022) have defended the concept of DC: 
 
”A holistic approach to support decision making when designing a CE initiative which fully considers the flow 
of materials/products within and between biological and technological cycles targeted at the enhanced overall 
sustainability performance of the system.’’  
(Metic et al., 2022, P.1055) 
 
Although the concept of DC is relatively new, many CE initiatives have used these principles 
unknowingly. Initiatives that have used DC approaches have demonstrated the ability to create products 
that can utilize the full potential of a CE and thus maximize its intended sustainability impact (Metic et 
al., 2022). Since initiatives that use DC approaches have an increased chance of creating a more 
sustainable end goal, these initiatives have been chosen as the focus of this project. Based on this and 
Metic et al. 2022 definition of DC, a general definition has been created for the initiatives that are the 
focus of this project. 
 
” CE initiative which fully considers the flow of materials/products within and between biological and technological cycles 
targeted at the enhanced overall sustainability performance of the system.’’ 
 
This concept and approach of DC have been applied in this project, focusing on CE initiatives operating 
in both cycles, see Figure 3. By actively counteracting the otherwise classical separation in the analysis 
of new initiatives, a more holistic analysis can be performed, and finally, Tools can be designed with a 
more holistic approach that helps to counteract this separation in the future.

3.3 General Introduction to Bio-based Materials and Products 
Knowledge about bio-based materials and products is essential to comprehend the often-overlooked 
biological cycle (Brunklaus & Riise, 2018). Bio-based materials and products have gained significant 
attention as sustainable alternatives to conventional materials in various industries (Corrado & Sala, 
2018). Derived from renewable biological resources such as plants, biomass, microorganisms, and other 
biological feedstocks, these materials offer a range of potential benefits, including reduced environmental 
impacts, improved resource efficiency, and sometimes enhanced product performance. These resources

undergo various processes, such as extraction, fermentation, or chemical synthesis, to create materials 
for different applications (Ratner & Bryant, 2004). While bio-based materials have a historical presence, 
recent advancements in biotechnology, chemical engineering, and sustainable manufacturing have 
greatly expanded their range and application in the construction, textiles, packaging, automotive, and 
electronics sectors(EEA, 2018). 
 
The use of bio-based materials and products offers several advantages. Firstly, they are derived from 
renewable sources, reducing reliance on fossil fuels and mitigating environmental impact (Ratner & 
Bryant, 2004). Additionally, bio-based materials possess favorable biodegradability properties, enabling 
them to break down and minimize waste generation naturally (Corrado & Sala, 2018). This 
characteristic is particularly beneficial in applications where end-of-life disposal is a concern, such as 
packaging and single-use products (Bröring et al., 2020). 
 
Using bio-based materials and products contributes to sustainable development by promoting the 
transition to a CE. By incorporating renewable resources and reducing reliance on non-renewable 
inputs, bio-based materials help minimize the depletion of natural resources (Gómez-Castro & 
Gutiérrez-Antonio, 2022).  
 
The future of bio-based materials and products is promising. Research and development will advance 
feedstocks, production processes, and material properties (Ratner & Bryant, 2004). Additionally, the 
growing awareness of environmental sustainability and the increasing demand for bio-based alternatives 
will likely drive the adoption of bio-based materials across various industries further (Morone et al., 
2021). 
 
However, bio-based materials and products face specific challenges despite their advantages. One key 
concern is the potential land and resource competition between food and bio-based material production. 
As bio-based materials rely on agricultural feedstocks, there is a risk of diverting arable land away from 
food crops, potentially exacerbating food security issues (Weiss et al., 2012). However, the problems do 
not stop there. This substitution between food and product production is only the first step in the change. 
There can be system or behavioral changes, called RE, due to the use of bio-materials. These REs are 
often difficult to predict and can compromise the planned effects of the initiative (Streicher & Kirchner, 
2019). 
 
It is crucial to incorporate a comprehensive understanding of the system and how new initiatives affect 
it to fully harness the potential of bio-based materials and products using DC approaches (Metic & 
Pigosso, 2022). One way to work towards this is by broadening the knowledge of RE resulting from 
initiatives using bio-materials.

3.5 General Introduction on How to Identify Potential RE 
Different ways of defining and measuring RE have been proposed in different industries and with 
different objectives. Metic and Pigosso (2022) have evaluated and compiled most of these, focusing on 
RE in CE. In their research, they obtained many lessons and gaps, for example, that most ways of 
identifying RE had no comprehensive understanding of causality leading up to RE. They also identified 
an absence of a compilation of types and mechanisms acting in RE. They have proposed a RE 
conceptual framework to counteract this, see Figure 5. The framework enables a comprehensive 
assessment and understanding of RE due to CE initiative and DC concepts. The framework aggregates 
all previously identified elements and different REs identified in previous research. 
 
The RE conceptual framework consists of 3 main parts: (i) Triggers, (ii) Drivers, and (iii) RE 
Mechanisms. (i) A trigger for a RE can be seen as a cause for the emergence of the RE (Metic & Pigosso, 
2022).  (ii) A driver for a RE can be seen as a factor that develops the RE further (Font Vivanco et al., 
2016). (iii) A mechanism can be seen as a term to describe why and how RE occurs (Metic & Pigosso, 
2022). Besides the names of all triggers, drivers, and mechanisms, the conceptual framework contains 
descriptions and examples. The mechanisms are also sorted in a canvas to indicate the level, type, and 
actor it affects (Metic & Pigosso, 2022). Type indicates if the mechanism has a direct or indirect effect. 
Direct effect means enhanced consumption of the CE initiative and/or the same resource used by the 
CE initiative. Indirect effect means enhanced consumption of other resources or/and products due to 
the CE initiative. The actor indicates whether the mechanism works with the producer (P) or consumer

3.5.1 
Potential triggers for RE 
A new initiative is often created for a combination of different reasons. These reasons are why the 
initiative is started and time and resources are spent developing and implementing it. These reasons are 
usually referred to as triggers. Examples of triggers that might encourage change are the emerging 
concern for global warming or the depletion of existing resources (Upadhyay et al., 2021).  
 
Many of the triggers that lead to innovation and steer products to use CE or DC principles are positive, 
but some have an inherent risk of leading to RE (Metic & Pigosso, 2022). These specific triggers are 
difficult to identify and separate as many initiatives are created for different reasons. However, with the 
help of studying CE initiatives where RE is identified, researchers have derived them backward to 
identify triggers with an inherent risk of leading to RE (Metic & Pigosso, 2022).  
 
In Metic & Pigosso's (2022) study, triggers focusing on CE approaches have been listed with a proven 
risk of leading to RE. These have then, in this project, been adapted for DC initiatives. The translation 
between CE and DC has been done through reasoning and discussion. A fundamental principle to make 
this possible has been that DC approaches do not introduce any new complexity but use only existing 
concepts in CE. This means that triggers identified for CE are most likely the same for DC. The only 
difference is how they are formulated. The formulation can be changed due to its generic nature. Exactly 
how a trigger works will always be unique to each case, but describing it in general terms can include as 
many cases as possible. For this project, the focus has been on RE due to initiatives using DC approaches.

3.5.2

triggers, many different factors can act as drivers for an initiative. However, even here, some drivers 
have been identified with an inherent risk of leading to or amplifying the impact of RE (Metic & Pigosso,

3.5.3 Potential RE Mechanisms 
Some triggers start the journey towards potential RE, and some drivers can worsen the situation, but 
what do they lead to? To understand and describe RE, the concept of mechanisms has been created.

combinations of triggers and drivers. The mechanisms are used to understand RE systematically by

standardized approach can be created. In order to simplify the understanding, mechanisms can be 
described as what is causing the quantitative RE (Lange et al., 2021). These mechanisms have been 
identified by breaking up previously identified RE into different parts, which have then been mapped

acting (Metic & Pigosso, 2022). Table 3 and Table 4  contain a description and examples of mechanisms

4. DS1 Discussion  
During DS1, four concepts were investigated and chosen as the project's focus. CE, DC, Bio-based 
materials/products, and RE. The common thread of maximizing sustainability ties together these four 
concepts 
 
CE was investigated first because of its recognized qualities to transform our economy sustainably. Due 
to the concept's scope, many definitions have been created, but this project has chosen to use EMF's 
definition and visualization. The visualization of CE in the butterfly diagram is constantly recurring in 
research and academia. The diagram shows strategies and approaches to promote sustainability 
separated into two parts, biological and technological. The technological one often gets more focus 
because these strategies are easier to implement in the design. However, more and more initiatives are 
==PAGE:34==
Lukas Olofsson  
 
 
 
 
   
S213713 
 
 
 
 
                       Master Thesis 
 
 
22 
combining the two different cycles by, for example, combining biological materials with technological 
strategies. This is particularly evident when new bio-based materials and products are investigated. 
These are created from natural components but should be reused or shared instead of recycled directly. 
By creating bio-based products that can be created in a sufficiently permeable state to operate in the 
technological cycle and eventually transition to the biological cycle, this new way of combining the two 
can be a beneficial alternative to create more sustainable initiatives. 
 
The need to encourage approaches that combine the two cycles has also emerged in the academic and 
research world. In response, the concept of DC has been created. DC is created as a way of facilitating 
products to exploit the full potential of CE. As much evidence suggests that this approach is preferable, 
it has also been used as a constraint in selecting initiatives that this project focuses on. Focusing on the 
most promising option for future development increases the chance of the most significant impact. 
However, there are still barriers not sufficiently explored that can work against this goal.  
 
RE is one of them. By studying RE and contributing to the collective understanding of how they arise 
function, and can ultimately be identified, the chances of planned impacts matching the real effects of 
different initiatives are increased. As REs are complex and their effects are expressed in often overlooked 
ways, disaggregation and unification are required to create a concrete understanding. This has been 
done by applying and adapting a conceptual framework to build a common way of analyzing them in a 
structured way. This framework is derived from and created by previous research and represents only a 
start to work towards understanding RE. During DS1, many attempts were made to expand the content 
of this framework, as the author believes this is not the final version. However, this was easier said than 
done. Analyzing initiatives with a DC focus to find new unique triggers, drivers, or mechanisms is 
extensive work that requires many initiatives for consistent conclusions to be experienced. Instead, a 
safer path was chosen by not introducing parts that could not be verified. This work is instead moved 
on to future researchers who can use the results from this project to expand the database of which REs 
can arise from initiatives using the DC approach. Ensuring that only verified data is used reduces the 
margins of error, which was deemed more important due to the ambiguity of RE.

drivers, and mechanisms have been investigated and adapted to fit this focus. Each component has been 
given a new description and example adapted for DC based on previous research with a CE focus on

3.1 Define a Method to Gather Data 
In qualitative research, selecting the most appropriate method for data collection is crucial to ensure 
reliable and insightful findings. Identifying the most effective approach to gathering rich and 
comprehensive data is often challenging (Smith, 2023). Various methods exist for gathering qualitative 
data, including participant observation, document analysis, focus groups, and interviews (Guest et al., 
2013). Each method offers unique advantages and considerations, and their suitability depends on the 
research objectives, context, and available resources.

For this project and the scope of the data gathering, it became evident that semi-structured interviews 
were the most suitable method for collecting qualitative data from case companies. Interviews balance 
flexibility and structure, allowing the author to guide the conversation while enabling participants to 
express their thoughts and experiences in-depth (Bryman, 2015). Formulating pre-determined questions

participants' viewpoints, ensuring a comprehensive exploration of the research topic (Guest et al., 2013). 
In conclusion, the best method for data collection is interviews, and therefore will, the following steps of 
PS1 aimed to develop questions fit for that format.

3.3 Tested Interview Questions  
The result of testing the interview in an external workshop gave much feedback. One significant finding 
was that participants found it challenging to understand how to respond on the established 4-point scale. 
This feedback prompted reevaluating the interview approach, shifting towards a more open discussion 
format where the questions served as a foundation rather than strictly adhering to the predefined scale. 
 
Another valuable lesson learned from the workshop was that the number of questions in the interview 
was well-balanced, considering the available time and allowing for meaningful discussion. This insight 
reinforced the effectiveness of the interview structure and ensured that participants had sufficient 
opportunity to provide comprehensive responses. 
 
3.4 Developed Interview Templet 
Based on the insights gained from the workshop in step (iii), it was determined that further refinement 
of the 4-point response scale for the mechanism questions was necessary. The original purpose of this 
scale was to enable comparability across different mechanisms and simplify the responses of interview 
subjects. The 4- point scale was chosen after exploring various analysis methods. LCA (Thiesen et al., 
2008), Material Flow Analysis (Horvath et al., 2019), System Dynamics (Dace et al., 2014), input-output 
analysis (Geels et al., 2017), Social Life Cycle Assessment (Moltesen et al., 2018), Scenario Analysis 
(Swart et al., 2004), etc. 
 
However, when it was discovered that the 4-point scale was problematic for the interviewee, it was 
decided that two different strategies would be implemented to counteract this.  
 
(i) During the interviews, the questions would be used as a basis for discussion, and the four-point scale 
would not have to be answered alone by the interviewee but in consultation with an RE expert present 
during the interview. This approach was judged to be the best because the scale remained crucial for 
the results to be comparable.  
 
(ii) A more project-specific approach, utilizing the 4-point scale, needed to be developed. The outcome 
is an additional template inspired by the principles of framework analysis. Framework analysis is a 
network of interconnected concepts that collectively provides a comprehensive understanding of a 
phenomenon (Jabareen, 2009).

4. PS1 Discussion  
During PS1, the lessons learned and the results created in DS1 were used to create a way to collect useful 
data from companies and then compare it with the theoretical data from DS1. Since there is no 
invariable way to identify RE in initiatives such as DC approaches, this work started without limitations. 
The only fixed point was the knowledge of how RE worked through the adapted conceptual framework 
containing triggers, drivers, and mechanisms. Starting with how RE works, it was assessed that they 
could be identified similarly. Therefore, an approach was chosen to create a way to identify the 
individual components in order to be able to map how different RE existed due to the initiative.  
 
It was not easy to create a systematic way to look at an initiative and compare it with a long list of 
triggers, drivers, and mechanisms that theoretically can lead to RE. Many problems and difficulties were 
identified. For example, the list of components is described with a general focus, and the initiative to be

analyzed is very focused. This bridge to make it possible to fit the focus into the general required a 
balancing act of not excluding or including too much. At the beginning of the process, it was judged that 
this balance could be built into the questions in a satisfactory way that the interviewees could manage to 
do this themselves, but as the questions were created and tested, it was judged that more and more 
context was needed for this. This shift can be seen in the results of the development of questions. For 
example, the mechanism questions are initially created with a scale in mind to facilitate answering the 
questions, but at the end of the process, it was assessed as a function to facilitate the author's analysis of 
the answers. The shift in the primary focus of the scale was particularly evident after the external testing 
of the questions. An attempt was made to change back the function of the scale by creating a customized 
template. However, this work was not completed before the interviews were conducted, which meant 
the template was also primarily used in the author's analysis of the collected data in DS2. 
 
There were also problems with the amount of information to be collected. A compromise in the planned 
set-up allowed for a realistic interview timeframe. This was to focus only on mechanisms and not on 
triggers and drivers. This choice was made with the motivation that the mechanisms are the primary 
part of RE, and theoretically, it should be enough only to identify these to understand identified RE. 
The downside is that this also peels away a tiny amount of the context of what is being investigated. 
However, since the author and an RE expert would participate in the interviews, it was judged that this 
context could be added as needed.  
 
This choice of planning to contribute context and knowledge during the interviews was made to enable 
as much information as possible to be collected during the subsequent interviews. As there are no 
standard ways of identifying RE, there is the knowledge that the created questions and upcoming 
interviews will fulfill two purposes of the project. As part of cycle one, they should identify RE in 
companies that use DC approaches in the analyzed initiatives, but as part of the information collection 
for cycle two, it should also generate as much knowledge as possible about how this process works in 
practice and finally how it can be standardized by creating a new tool with the specific purpose of 
identifying RE. Thus, even if the interviewees could not answer the questions without the help of the 
interviewers, it was considered that this upcoming experience could generate lessons about the type of 
context needed in the upcoming tool. The same applies to the created temple. Even if it was not 
completed by the time the interviews were held, it could be tested in the upcoming analysis process. 
 
5. PS1 Conclusion 
The most important conclusions from PS2 are that to identify RE in different initiatives, knowledge of 
triggers, drivers, and mechanisms has to be used. Based on these components and their adaptation 
during DS1, questions and helping context have been developed. By identifying these components 
separately, an understanding of how RE works can be obtained and thus adapt generic descriptions of 
mechanisms to initiative-specific descriptions.

1. Introduction 
In DS1, a project-adapted version of Metic & Pigasso's (2022) conceptual framework was created. Using 
this framework, a set of questions was created in PS1 to identify components of this framework in 
different companies. These questions have been used in DS2 first to identify components and then merge 
these components to identify and understand RE due to different initiatives utilizing DC approaches. 
The reason why companies are interested in identifying potential RE is to, among other things, 
maximize actual sustainability impact, minimize the risk of unconscious greenwashing, and better 
understand future changes to cost management and resource efficiency (C. K. Murray, 2013). By 
identifying companies' different REs, the first step in understanding and counteracting RE is initiated 
by adapting existing products and enabling a knowledge base to create new products with minimal RE 
(Matraeva et al., 2022). DS2 aimed to help grow the broad knowledge base on practically identifying 
RE using the methodology developed in PS1 to analyze two companies implementing initiatives using 
DC principles. This was the final part of cycle one, and the aim was to gather enough information to 
answer RQ1 satisfactorily. DS2 followed a 3-step process: 
 
(i) 
Identify Case Companies 
(ii) 
Data collection 
(iii) 
Data Analysis.  
 
2. Methodology 
DS2 followed the process of a qualitative case study. Identified case companies to work with and then 
used a qualitative approach to gain in-depth knowledge about them through interviews. Since the 
developed interview questions directly link to the existing literature on RE mechanisms, a seamless link 
between practical data collection and the theoretical, conceptual framework could be created. This 
connection was utilized during the interview to provide context, especially in the subsequent phase of 
methodically analyzing the collected data. The collected data were analyzed with the help of the created 
template and 4-point scale.  
 
Understanding and confidently concluding the analyzed cases required several iterations and reviews of 
the collected data. This was to ensure an unbiased assessment. Identifying the different components, 
triggers, drivers, and mechanisms separately and then adding them together allows for a more 
straightforward analysis but also makes it more challenging to draw preconceived conclusions. This was 
important because there was an imbalance in the level of knowledge between the interviewee and the 
interviewer/analyst due to the risk of projecting conclusions on the collected data. To counteract this 
further, there was a requirement to find concrete evidence and citations for each choice made in 
identifying separate components. The results were evaluated and further processed during internal 
workshops with RE Experts. 
 
The following sections will describe the different steps in more detail.

2.2 Data collection 
The second step of the process was to formulate a hypothesis about what score the different mechanisms 
would get on the 4-point scale. This was based on the result in DS1, the template created, and collected 
company knowledge via their websites and introduction meeting. The meaning of this was to use it as a 
fail-safe for the credibility of the later on collected data.  
 
The project then used semi-structured video interviews for data collection. The semi-structured 
interviews were chosen to collect data, allowing for a more flexible approach while addressing crucial 
research questions. Moreover, semi-structured interviewing is preferred if the researcher needs to keep 
an open mind about the information the interviewee needs to gather. This structure typically starts with 
a set of questions that guide the conversation, and the interviewer can also ask follow-up questions and 
probe for additional information as needed (Bryman, 2015).  This format of semi-structured interviews 
required no adaptation of the generated results from PS1. The interview questions developed there were 
used as a starting point, and then follow-up questions were adapted based on responses from the 
interviewees. 
 
The interviews were conducted as described by Bryman., (2015). In addition, the questions were sent 
out in advance to allow for preparation by the interviewee. This step was deemed necessary as the topic 
under investigation is complex and specific questions required information to be obtained in advance. 
Twenty-three previously developed questions to establish boundaries and identify mechanisms were sent 
in advance with some accompanying descriptions, see Appendix B. The interviews were conducted 
online, recorded, and transcribed with the participants' permission. This allowed the data to be analyzed 
multiple times. 
 
Once the data was collected, the information obtained was used to identify different mechanisms, 
drivers, and triggers. The mechanisms were identified on a scale of 1-4 and compared with the 
hypotheses. The different triggers and drivers were only identified with yes and no based on gathered 
data about the companies and the discussion about their mechanisms. The results were then processed 
in a workshop with experts in the area of RE due to CE initiatives using DC approaches to ensure their 
accuracy.

translation between companies' specific initiatives and the general descriptions of triggers, drivers, and 
mechanisms could be done. This work was highly experimental and relied heavily on testing and trial

patterns or behaviors, the project could assess the immediate and direct consequences of implementing 
sustainability strategies (Metic & Pigosso, 2022).  
 
Following the analysis of direct effects, the indirect effects mechanisms were analyzed. These 
mechanisms consider the broader economic context and the spillover effects of the initiative. By 
exploring how the change may stimulate, for example, economic growth, the author can understand the 
potential indirect REs (Freire-González, 2017).  
 
Their relation was explored after analyzing the identified direct and indirect mechanisms separately. 
The relationship between direct and indirect RE mechanisms may vary depending on the context. 
However, research suggests that when direct RE mechanisms are estimated to be low, the range of values 
for indirect RE mechanisms tends to be more comprehensive. In other words, when sustainability 
measures result in relatively minor direct effects, the potential for indirect effects becomes more 
uncertain and can vary across different scenarios. Conversely, when direct effects are estimated to be 
high, the potential variability for indirect effects decreases (Freire-González, 2017).  
 
By comprehensively identifying and understanding both direct and indirect effects, the project can 
conduct an in-depth analysis to assess the overall impact of the CE initiatives in companies A and B. To 
expand on this analysis, the possible drivers and triggers were connected for both direct and indirect 
mechanisms. Ultimately, the findings were combined to describe a RE consisting of identified direct and 
indirect mechanisms, drivers, and triggers. The data analysis involved multiple iterations, revisits, and 
reviews of the collected material to ensure accuracy and reliability. 
 
3. Result 
The results obtained from DS2 were utilized in two ways. First, lessons learned will be applied in cycle 
two and a concrete handover to the analyzed companies. 
 
The handover aimed to support and guide the analyzed companies' business endeavors. This 
communication aimed to inform them about the potential RE that could arise from their initiatives. 
However, it is essential to note that despite the existence of REs, the belief remains that their initiatives 
are still superior to conventional approaches. This work only aims to maximize the impact of the 
initiatives. 
 
The information regarding possible RE serves as a means of supporting and guiding the companies in 
their future endeavors. By being aware of their REs, the companies can make informed decisions and 
take appropriate measures to mitigate negative consequences. The knowledge of potential RE allows the 
companies to anticipate and address challenges that may arise during the implementation and expansion 
of their initiatives, ensuring a more effective and sustainable approach. 
 
Additionally, the information on RE can guide the case companies in optimizing their initiatives. Case 
companies can fine-tune their strategies and adapt business practices by understanding RE mechanisms, 
drivers, and triggers. This guidance helps them navigate the complexities of sustainable business models, 
optimize their initiatives' outcomes, and enhance their long-term success. It gives them a comprehensive 
understanding of their initiatives' potential challenges and opportunities, enabling them to proactively 
address RE and make informed choices that align with their sustainability goals.

3.4.1.1 
Triggers 
In the case of Co. A, several factors were identified as triggers in the initiative that could lead to a RE 
with the effect of counteracting the desired improvement in sustainability compared to existing 
initiatives. Globalization (T4), one of the triggers, had the potential to result in decreased resource 
savings due to the shift from national to international resource extraction, thereby reducing the positive 
social and environmental impacts (Laurenti et al., 2016; Ruzzenenti & Basosi, 2008). This risk was 
relevant for Co. A, which sourced materials from different suppliers in various parts of Europe and 
produced specific components in East European countries. The disruptions caused by the pandemic also 
prompted the company to develop relationships with multiple suppliers and part manufacturers as 
contingency measures, leading to a more global nature of the initiative's value chain. 
 
Another trigger identified was Economic growth and affluence (T5). Economic growth and increased 
affluence often led to further investment in sustainable technologies rather than resource decoupling 
(Nørgård & Xue, 2016; van den Bergh, 2011). For Co. A, its CE initiative assumed that a growing 
economy would necessitate the construction of more houses. Despite not being the most affordable 
option on the market, the company targeted buyers who are a part of the expanding economy. 
 
Innovation and investment (T6) were identified as triggers that could exert additional pressure on 
planned resource savings. Technological and organizational advancements and financial support often 
lead to economic growth and new markets (Warmington-Lundström & Laurenti, 2020). For Co. A, its 
endeavor to develop a product suitable for the niche market of reusing building materials exemplified 
the influence of innovation and investment as triggers. 
 
Population growth (T8) was another trigger that could lead to a potential RE. As the population grew 
and life expectancy increased due to improved well-being, healthcare systems, and quality of life, life 
expectancies increased (van den Bergh, 2011). This trigger was crucial for Co. A, as its success relied on 
the continued growth of the population and the subsequent demand for housing. 
 
Lastly, whether enforced or voluntary, resource conservation (T9) was identified as a trigger that could 
release a different set of behaviors causing direct and/or indirect RE (Nørgård & Xue, 2016; van den 
Bergh, 2011). Co. As initiative aimed to minimize waste in manufacturing and disrupt existing markets 
through modularity and reuse. 
 
3.4.1.2 
Drivers 
Three drivers have been identified that possibly play a significant role in driving the RE in Co. A's 
initiative: D1, D2C, and D3. These drivers have the potential to drive the identified triggers further and 
influence the success of the DC initiative and the reduction of an environmental impact compared to 
existing alternatives. 
 
Economic factors (D1), including price, cost, and wealth, influence the RE considerably (Sorrell, 2007, 
2009, 2012). These factors are influenced by the identified triggers, such as globalization (T4) and 
affluence (T5) and can subsequently impact the price of Co. A's prefabricated building block system. If 
economic factors decrease the system's price, it may stimulate greater demand and consumption, 
countering resource-saving benefits. Moreover, if economic growth results in increased wealth and 
disposable income, consumers may have a greater capacity to consume resources, including housing 
materials, at higher rates. Thus, economic factors can significantly impact consumer behavior and 
consumption patterns, potentially contributing to the RE. 
 
Consumer behavior (D2C) refers to the choices and preferences of individuals or households concerning 
resource consumption (Azevedo, 2014). If consumers perceive Co. A's prefabricated building block 
system as a cost-effective and sustainable solution, they may increase their demand for the product. This 
increased demand, in turn, can offset the resource-saving goals of the initiative. Additionally, consumer 
behavior is influenced by social norms, cultural values, and the level of awareness regarding sustainability

3.4.1.3 
Direct mechanism 
The direct mechanism identified in Co. A's RE was M4.DeC: Motivational and M5.DeC: Time. These 
mechanisms influence changes in consumer preferences, perceptions, and behavior resulting from 
increased product efficiency and the reduction of time in use for builders.  
 
Motivational (M4.DeC) refers to the motivational effect on consumer behavior due to the perceived 
benefits and attractiveness of the prefabricated building block system. Co. A's product offers 
sustainability features, ease of use, and design flexibility, positively shifting consumers' perceptions and 
preferences ((Girod & De Haan, 2009; Santarius & Soland, 2018). Consumers are motivated to choose 
the system over conventional construction methods, driven by its perceived environmental friendliness 
and other advantages, and because of this, they may change their behavior and use the product in a less 
evidently friendly way.  
 
Time (M5.DeC) plays a role in Co. As RE as well. Time influences consumer behavior by affecting their 
decision-making processes and usage patterns. The time saved in the construction process using the 
prefabricated building block system can increase overall consumption. With reduced construction time, 
consumers may be inclined to build more houses or expand existing ones, increasing resource 
consumption. The time factor interacts with the motivational effect to shape consumer choices and the 
extent of resource savings achieved. 
 
3.4.1.4 
Indirect mechanisms 
Additionally, it is essential to consider the indirect mechanisms involved in Co. A's RE. The two 
identified mechanisms are M11.IeC and M11.IeP: Consumption Accumulation These mechanisms 
come into play when the demand for more efficient and sustainable housing solutions supplements, 
rather than replaces, conventional construction materials. Co. A's prefabricated building block system is 
designed to be used in addition to traditional materials, rather than as a complete substitution. As a 
result, consumption accumulates, where consumers acquire the system alongside conventional 
construction materials (Farrant et al., 2010). This accumulation offsets some of the resource-saving 
benefits of the DC initiative. While the system offers sustainability advantages, the simultaneous use of 
conventional materials can limit the overall impact on resource efficiency and sustainability.

4. DS2 Discussion 
During DS2, the developed methodology was tested to identify RE in actual initiatives implemented by 
companies. This was the last part of cycle one and involved much work to piece together different parts. 
First, relevant parts of the interview responses would be linked to what was required by the interview 
questions, i.e., characteristics of specific mechanisms.  Then these answers would be transformed into a 
numbered scale to compare them with each other. Then triggers and drivers would be identified based 
on the information gathered during the interview. Once all these theoretical pieces were identified, they 
would be transformed from the generic descriptions in the conceptual framework to a focused 
perspective based on the two companies initiatives. Once this was done, relationships between the parts 
were examined to understand the whole. Through this, a visualization of how RE worked could then be 
made. The process was extensive, and the choice of direction was not always obvious. This approach 
generated a result specific to the analyzed companies but also an insight that this was wrong.  
 
If RE identification is to become a standardized process that can be incorporated to a greater extent, 
the process needs to be significantly simplified. There must be a more precise direction and a more 
universal categorization system. Only through this can the process be simplified and repeated effectively. 
This is something that needs to be explored further in cycle two.  
 
The results and process also showed the analysis's importance in identifying RE. Although the 
mechanisms are theoretically sufficient to describe the inner workings of RE, triggers, and drivers 
provide a necessary context for how the mechanism takes shape. If this context is removed, it is 
sometimes difficult to understand precisely why the mechanism exists or operates. The exact realization 
between these parts is sometimes difficult to identify, but using multiple parts makes the whole easier to 
understand.  
 
The translation between general descriptions of triggers, drivers, and mechanisms and the specific forms 
they take in different initiatives is essential to understanding RE. General identifications are necessary 
to create a standardized approach, but the individual interpretation of the result is equally important. 
Without this translation, it is challenging to clarify RE as identified.  
 
After completing DS2, the experience is mixed. The reality of the complexity and uncertainty of the 
nature of RE becomes clear, but the need to understand them becomes apparent. It becomes evident 
that many elements are essential for a comprehensive analysis, but a more precise focus must be 
established to enable an affordable analysis. A methodology must be created, and a tool to facilitate the 
process is needed.

1. Introduction 
During DS1, the theoretical background was created. During PS1, based on this background, a method 
to collect data to identify RE was created. During DS2, this method was tested by interviewing two 
companies and then analyzing the results to identify their possible RE. This process was complicated 
and highly specialized for the two cases. Results were then communicated to the companies, and lessons 
learned from the process were processed. Based on these observations and lessons learned, the PS2 stage 
began. During PS2, the objective was to utilize the results from cycle one to develop a tool to identify 
potential RE due to initiatives using DC approaches. The tool had to meet the requirements of (i) being 
used for initiatives that have already been implemented and (ii) for initiatives under development. This 
was because the work on identifying RE has historically been almost non-existent in DC initiatives. 
Current and future initiatives must be addressed to identify potential RE due to DC to achieve maximum 
effectiveness. 
 
Tools are essential in various fields as they offer evidence-based recommendations, best practices, and 
standards to guide professionals, organizations, and individuals in making informed decisions. They can 
help ensure consistency, quality, and safety (Yazan et al., 2022). Developing Tools is a meticulous and 
collaborative process that involves gathering, synthesizing, and critically appraising existing evidence 
and expert opinions to provide reliable guidance to practitioners and stakeholders (Shekelle et al., 1999). 
 
Tools can bridge the gap between research evidence and its application in real-world settings. Tool 
development systematically reviews available evidence to provide practitioners with a comprehensive 
and up-to-date overview of the best information and strategies for addressing specific issues or achieving 
particular goals (Bovea & Pérez-Belis, 2018). Tools are often living things requiring periodic updates to 
reflect new evidence, technological advancements, and evolving best practices. Therefore, ongoing 
monitoring, evaluation, and revision processes are essential. Tools remain relevant, accurate, and 
valuable over time (Bovea & Pérez-Belis, 2018).

3.1 Motivation for a new tool 
From the case studies with the two companies, it has been realized that identifying RE can be a complex 
and demanding process. During the literature review, no standardized way of implementing this process 
was determined. This adds complexity and reduces the possibility of collecting similar lessons between 
initiatives, companies, and products. By creating a standardized tool to identify possible RE, the process 
can be simplified, and the understanding of RE can be increased. 
 
3.2 Scope and requirements  
From the very beginning, the scope of the tool was to guide in identifying RE in new CE initiatives. 
However, because of the nature of the selected case companies, the collaboration with a Ph.D. project, 
and the time limitation of the project, the scope was reduced to focus on RE due to initiatives that use 
DC approaches. Adapting and concentrating on this area allows for easier development and evaluation 
of the tool. This focus also aligns with the primary goal of maximizing the sustainability of new and 
existing initiatives as products using DC approaches promote material resilience and extend possibilities 
of closing, narrowing, and slowing the loops (Metic et al., 2022).  
 
During the interviews with Co. A & B, it became clear that one of their goals with their new products 
was to help create a more sustainable alternative using DC principles, but they had less understanding 
of how RE could affect these planned sustainability effects. It also became apparent that they found this 
concept fascinating and necessary, but their knowledge in this area was limited. So, these companies did 
not primarily demand a tool to identify RE due to a lack of knowledge. Still, during the cooperation and 
their increased understanding, the demand grew. These two companies cannot individually represent 
the entire market of sustainable initiatives using a DC approach, but they can demonstrate a need for 
increased knowledge and understanding. This can be achieved in many ways; in academia and the 
research community, this work begins by reviewing previous lessons learned. However, this route is often 
too costly and slow for companies who need to demonstrate the relative benefits of all actions. So, instead 
of creating a teaching tool on RE, creating a tool for identifying RE, increased knowledge can be 
experienced in combination with the relative benefits of identifying personal RE. 
 
So, this is the requirement that the tool should fulfill. By helping companies to identify RE, knowledge 
can be created and shared within the industry. For companies, this can lead to better understanding and 
better products. For society, it can lead to initiatives planned and predict environmental effects better 
match the actual effects. This reduces the possibility of greenwashing and enables a better understanding 
of which initiatives are preferable and what is required to achieve the world's sustainability goals. For 
the tool to contribute to this, it must meet requirements, see table 12.

again to understand how various parts can affect each other.

3.3.3 Interactive tool creation 
Many lessons from the whole project process were implemented when designing the tool. Many decisions 
were made, and the process involved many variations before the final design was chosen. However, it 
has been broken down into a few separate components to understand the process better. 
 
3.3.3.1 
Program selection 
To turn the format (questions and information about triggers, drivers, and mechanisms and their 
correlations) into an interactive tool, a program or system had to be selected to enable its use. Various 
systems, such as Figma, Python, Excel, Google Forms, and other standardized questionnaire programs, 
were analyzed. The objective was to identify a system that strikes the right balance between simplicity 
for the development process, accessibility for the user's experience, and enough technical features to 
facilitate the tool's inner workings. 
 
This proved harder to find than expected. The programs designed to create forms (Google Forms, etc.) 
could not handle the complex branching logic needed to keep the questions from repeating themselves. 
Figma and Python had too few built-in functions to develop the tool time-efficiently. As a result, MS 
Excel was considered the best option. 
 
Due to several key factors, Excel offered the possibility of developing an interactive tool. First, its familiar 
and user-friendly interface caters to both development and useability. The development process can 
efficiently create the questionnaire framework, while users find it intuitive to answer questions and 
navigate the form and program. Excel's built-in features, such as conditional formatting, formulas, and 
data validation, prove instrumental in implementing complex branching logic without resorting to 
question repetition.

During PS2, all the lessons learned from earlier parts of the project were used. This process required 
much information to be put together in a manageable format. Using the developed material from DS1 
and PS1, it was possible to incorporate the lessons learned during DS2. This approach was constructive 
for the creative work as identifying RE without a developed tool could be tested practically and not only 
theoretically. Through this work, it is assessed that the final tool was developed primarily through 
identified practical needs during PS1. Despite this focus, the identified theoretical gaps could also be met 
by developing components used in the final design based on existing research presented in DS1.  
 
The final tool is deemed to fulfill the design requirements acceptably and can help companies and future 
researchers create more sustainable initiatives. However, it is essential to note that the tool has been 
designed to identify RE. This focus has been chosen as it is the first step in working with RE. Many 
different reasonable ways to go after identification are also necessary for this work, but no primary focus 
has been placed on these. For example, natural steps after identification are to measure the effect, 
mitigate the effect, or compare the effect between different initiatives. Identifying RE effects does not 
directly affect the balance between planned and actual sustainability improvements.  
 
It has sometimes been challenging to incorporate functions for these different tasks during the process, 
but a choice has been made to proceed slowly. By focusing on a specific task, the tool has been created 
with a general focus and offers a solid foundation for future tools. The tool's ability to identify only offers 
a greater possibility of applying it in more situations and with more specific purposes.  
 
Although the focus has been on identification, a secondary function has proved necessary to achieve that 
goal. To identify something, a certain level of understanding must be present. As the tool provides 
detailed descriptions and examples of triggers, drivers, and mechanisms, the user of the tool is educated 
in the field of RE for initiatives that use DC approaches while identifying their RE. By creating an 
understanding of the inner components of the existence of RE, the tool also contributes to future steps 
such as the mitigation and measurement of RE. There is also a belief by the author that this 
understanding can contribute to indirectly reducing possible RE in future initiatives created by a user of 
the tool. It is challenging to solve a problem that is not identified. However, if the problem is identified 
and some understanding exists, it is more difficult to ignore. 
 
The final tool created is comprehensive in the amount of information presented. This choice has been 
made because of the lessons learned from DS2 about the importance of context and the generally limited 
knowledge of RE among users. This scope has been deemed necessary, but despite this, there are risks 
that users with previous knowledge of RE may be disturbed by the extensive process. To counteract this, 
possible iterations of the tool have been discussed. One such variant would have been to create a variant 
that did not include triggers and drivers but only mechanisms. This second approach would be 
interesting to test further in the future. However, the author believes there are several problems with 
excluding components too early. To make this scaled-down version of the tool, the author would like to 
test the developed tool on several initiatives first.  
 
Since the tool is built on previously identified triggers, drivers, and mechanisms, there is a risk that this 
basis does not include all possible REs or that some components are redundant. By testing many 
initiatives in the tool, these patterns can be identified and analyzed, and the content can be further 
developed based on this result. 
 
5. PS2 Conclusion 
Under PS2, the main objectives of the project have been achieved. To develop a tool for identifying RE 
due to initiatives that use a DC approach. This process concludes that a tool that will facilitate a user 
through an RE identification process must teach as much as produce results. Without users 
understanding what they are answering, obtaining a correct result is impossible. But by providing 
context, the tool can guide the user to answer the questions in the correct way.

11. Introduction 
The project has conducted two cycles and identified different possible RE in three various CE initiatives 
that all use DC principles to maximize the sustainability of their developed products. The analyses have 
shown that these initiatives, despite and because of the choices made to maximize sustainability, there is 
an unknown risk of RE that makes the planned savings in comparison with ordinary products not equal 
to the actual sustainability savings. This assumption is based on the theoretical principle that an 
identified RE has this effect. The process of identifying REs has been carried out in two ways: (i) 
systematically analyzing collected data and (ii) using a newly created tool. The developed tool has 
enabled a more straightforward, faster, and standardized process of identification which will contribute 
to an improved situation for companies and researchers working on identifying RE in initiatives using a 
DC approach. In this discussion chapter, project outcomes and learnings will be critically discussed. This 
chapter differs from previous discussions at the end of each chapter in that the focus here is on the project 
as a whole. 
 
12. Summary of Findings 
The project has explored a unique area of how initiatives using a DC approach can potentially lead to 
RE. This work has been based on established theory and lessons learned from working with three 
companies during the project. During the first part of the project, DS1, the theoretical background was 
established by exploring areas such as CE, DC, Bio-based Materials and products, and RE. By studying 
and analyzing CE, DC, Bio-based Materials, and products, a basic understanding of the strategies the 
three company initiatives implemented to create products with better sustainability properties compared 
to previous products was created. By understanding how initiatives are designed with these principles, 
the project's main focus could then be investigated, RE. During DS1, existing literature on RE due to 
CE initiatives were analyzed and then adapted to the project's focus on DC approaches.  
 
12.2 Cycle one 
The results of cycle one were used in two ways: (i) a handover to the two companies analyzed; (ii) a set 
of developed components to gather relevant data and lessons on identifying RE in initiatives using a DC 
approach. Result (ii) was the most important for the project, while result (i) helped the two companies in 
their future sustainability work.   
 
Result (i) is not attached in this report as the handover to the companies is done in connection with Julija 
Metic's Ph.D. project, which has a longer time frame than this project. 
 
The main lessons in result (ii) were that identification cannot be made without some understanding, 
context is important, focus one initiative is required during RE identification, and there is a reason why 
RE often remains unidentified. These lessons were learned during the data collection with the companies 
and the author's data analysis. These lessons were then applied in cycle two. An example of how this 
process took place is that during the interview with Co. B, the interviewee, on several occasions, deviated 
from the analyzed product by answering a question with another product in focus. So, questions 1-5 
were responded to with product x in mind, and then question 6 was answered with product y in mind. 
This meant that the analysis was complicated as all questions were intended to be answered with the 
same product in mind to compile a total RE for the initiative. To counteract this stage in an interaction 
with the tool developed during cycle two, a section was incorporated into the tool where the user had to 
actively specify and reflect on their initiative to guide the user to focus on only one initiative at a time. 
 
12.3 Cycle two 
During cycle two, two main activities were performed: (i) the development of a tool and (ii) the testing of 
this tool. Between these two parts, small iterations took place, but no significant changes were 
implemented in the tool after testing it. Cycle two utilized large amounts of the developed material from 
cycle one, especially in activity (i). The process in these two cycles was not wholly chronological, but 
small iterations and changes overlapped between the cycles. This can be seen in, for example, the

from the two cycles. The result was an admirable improvement where the tool enabled the process to be 
shortened from two weeks to just over an hour. This significant difference in workload is believed to 
stem mainly from the fact that during the process in cycle one, the data was analyzed in an unstructured 
way due to the lack of standardized directions. Both the first process and the process of identifying with 
the tool utilized the conceptual framework, but during cycle one, many attempts were made to identify 
relationships between the various components. This work was highly incremental and time-consuming 
without resulting in significant additional knowledge about RE. Therefore, it was chosen to focus on the 
part of the identification that generated higher knowledge for the user. This was to translate the general 
descriptions and examples of identified triggers, drivers, and mechanisms to the specific initiative. To 
facilitate this, a general guideline was created as the last step in interacting with the tool. This part of the 
process was found to work during the test with Co. C but can probably, in some initiatives, be somewhat 
more complicated and require some training to master. 
 
There are several reasons why the leap from general to initiate specific learning was considered more 
accessible when the tool was used. The author believes this was mainly due to the structure offered by 
the tool. Data is primarily sorted by answering the questions more specifically in the tool than during 
the interview discussions. The following reason was that the tool offered a more manageable format for 
the results. This was also done directly with the answers given, meaning understanding the results was 
quicker. Because data collection and analysis took place in parallel, it was easier for users to see the links 
to their initiative, and thus the translation from generic to focused was already started during data 
collection. 
 
13. Comparison with Existing Literature 
The results from cycles one and two are primarily derived from the previous work by Metic and Pigosso 
(2022) studying RE due to CE. This study builds on and uses large parts of their results for the analysis 
in cycle one and the tool's design. The choice to use their conceptual framework as a common thread 
throughout the project was not evident from the beginning. For example, several alternatives for 
presenting the results in the tool were evaluated before it was finally chosen to use the conceptual 
framework. The previous options have never managed to visualize how triggers and drivers can activate 
mechanisms that describe how and why RE exists equally well. Thanks to this direct use of the 
conceptual framework, the final tool can be seen as an aid in using their results. This brings both 
opportunities and risks. Since the tool is so heavily based on their results, the risks in the performance of 
their study are inherited. However, this risk should be significantly reduced by conducting the three case 
studies where their results have been tested.  
 
The advantage of this origin is that only a small change is required to adapt the tool for all CE initiatives 
if requested. The focus in this project has meant that information has been adapted for initiatives with a 
DC approach, but as the same number of triggers, drivers, and mechanisms have been used, the same 
format can be adapted to test more initiatives.

14. Discussion of Methodology  
The project has generated a usable and reliable result by applying a customized version of a DRM 
process. The process has been constructive in moving the work forward in the right direction. A good 
balance between theory and creativity has been achieved by dividing the project into chapters that 
alternate between investigating existing phenomena and developing new concepts. After the end of the 
project, some parts of the process could have been altered to prioritize the project's resources better. A 
large part of the work during DS2 was spent on identifying RE in the two companies initiatives. This 
work was sometimes demanding and messy, and in retrospect, some of it ended up outside the project's 
set goal, "To develop a tool for identifying possible RE due to initiatives using a DC approach." 
Identifying these REs before the tool was developed was instructive for the federation and the companies, 
but a large part of it was not included in the project because it did not match the stated goal. It was 
instead a trade-off with the companies to motivate them to contribute their time to the project. If the 
work had been done differently, a greater focus would have been placed on only identifying problems in 
the process instead of making such an in-depth analysis of RE in their initiatives. This could have 
provided more data to create the new tool. 
 
However, it must be pointed out that the possibility of comparing an entire process of identifying RE in 
initiatives using a DC approach created an excellent opportunity to quantify the effect of the new tool. 
So even if it was a resource-intensive work, it was a necessary part of the overall project. 
 
15. Interpretation of Results 
At the start of the project, a research goal was set. Based on this goal, a process was planned to reach 
that goal. This goal provided the direction of the project in all subsequent decisions. Literature studies 
were made, companies were interviewed, and concepts were created to reach this goal. The question 
now is whether the result has achieved that goal. 
 
Research goal: 
 
To develop a tool for identifying possible RE due to initiatives using a DC approach. 
 
The project has created a tool to identify RE initiatives using a DC approach. So, on the surface, this 
goal is at least achieved. The developed tool enables identification by helping users analyze their 
initiative in a way that rarely happens naturally. This guidance lists possible risks regarding specific 
triggers and then drivers. These can ultimately result in different mechanisms, and again the tool helps 
the user to go through identified variants systematically. Through this systematic approach, the user 
starts with all possible risks and then excludes those not actionable on their initiative. The components 
that remain after this process are the basis on which the user transforms into an initiative-specific 
description of possible REs. In this way, REs that exist because of their product are identified and 
understood.  
 
This approach allows for all options to be explored in identifying RE. However, the tool only works if 
the user interacts with it correctly. The tool provides the user with information and guidance, but the 
user has to give something back to get a result. This means that there is a risk that the tool will not be 
able to fulfill its purpose if the user does not use it actively. The tool cannot just magically identify RE 
by a user describing their initiative with a DC approach. It only works if the user is willing to give energy, 
effort, and accurate information. This is not unique to this particular tool. A hammer does not nail the 
nail for the user. It only facilitates the process.  
==PAGE:81==
Lukas Olofsson  
 
 
 
 
   
S213713 
 
 
 
 
                       Master Thesis

However, even if a user correctly uses the tool, there are more risks with the final result. The tool is based 
on sometimes subjective and hypothetical assumptions from the user about their initiative. This means 
that interpretations, misunderstandings, or limited information can influence the result in the wrong 
direction. Even this is not unique to this design. Qualitative methods have this inherent risk, which 
cannot be designed away in a format like this.  
 
To summarize, the result in the developed RE identification tool cannot on its own identify RE, but it 
can facilitate the process considerably. A user can identify RE without the tool, but it will be easier if it 
is used, as proven during the test in DS3. 
 
16. Addressing Research Questions and Hypotheses 
In addition to the project's stated objective, two research questions and a hypothesis were developed. 
Through the process, the project has generated sufficient information to answer the questions and test 
the hypothesis. 
 
16.1 Research Questions 
 
RQ3. How to identify potential RE resulting from initiatives using a DC approach? 
 
Direct and indirect mechanisms must be mapped and identified to identify possible RE due to initiatives 
that follow a DC approach. By excluding all mechanisms unrelated to the initiative, a result can be 
obtained with those actions. It can be beneficial to examine triggers and drivers to facilitate this process. 
These elements help to create context and facilitate the process of identifying mechanisms. Once the 
mechanisms are identified, they must be adapted to the specific initiative to obtain a correct 
understanding. 
 
RQ4. How can a tool be developed to identify potential RE resulting from initiatives using a DC 
approach? 
 
A tool can be developed by applying the same approach described under RQ1, but the content must be 
packaged more structured. A more accurate result can be obtained by creating a tool that guides 
identifying triggers, drivers, and mechanisms. The tool needs to be created with a specific focus and 
includes enough descriptions for a user to handle it without the help of an expert in the field. 
 
16.2 Hypothesis 
 
Adopting DC solutions may result in possible RE that could partially offset the intended benefits, and utilizing a new tool 
can facilitate the identification process and understanding of those REs. 
 
Various elements necessary to test the hypothesis have been developed throughout the process. 
However, during DS3, the hypothesis was tested in its entirety. A DC initiative had been implemented, 
a new bio-based material. This initiative was analyzed using a sophisticated tool, and a previously 
unidentified RE was identified that counteracted the planned sustainability effect. The RE identified 
that the users of the material chose to use the initiative in a way that counteracted the planned lifetime 
of the material. This was due to their desire to show they were using sustainable materials.

assumptions made need further testing and development. In particular, this applies to investigating

This project offers the basis for different future directions of research.

to find more triggers, drivers, and mechanisms specific to initiatives using DC initiatives. By investigating 
more DC initiatives, a better understanding of RE can be obtained due to this approach. Similarities, 
connections, and standards in how RE arises from these initiatives can be explored.   
 
(ii) The tool is only developed to identify RE. A new area to explore is how functions of measuring and 
mitigating these effects can be implemented and standardized using the results of the developed tool. 
 
(iii) The tool is designed for initiatives that use a DC approach, and it would be interesting to see if it is 
possible to broaden this focus to other initiatives. If this can be done in the same tool or if additional 
tools need to be developed. 
 
19. Project Conclusion 
After the completion of the project, many conclusions have been learned. The process of developing the 
tool has been long and reflective. Creating a standardized way of identifying RE based on initiatives 
using a DC approach has created a perception that RE is more accessible and understandable. A process 
that took weeks before the tool's existence can now be done in hours. This makes it possible to examine 
and compare many initiatives without much effort.  
 
During DS3, when the tool was tested, this accessibility and proximity to an otherwise difficult-to-
understand concept experienced by the company representative for Co. C. He started the process with 
a complete lack of knowledge about the concept and finished the process with a clear understanding of 
the identified RE. This process, which previously required both study and learning, could be carried out 
in the context of a necessary task, identifying the RE.  
 
The ability to create this tool would not have been possible had it not been for the DSM process. Iterating 
between investigating existing concepts and developing new ones created a creative process grounded 
in reality without inhibiting innovation.  
 
The project's focus on initiatives using a DC approach has created an exciting space. All three companies 
interviewed have been very knowledgeable about using bio-materials and take great pride that they have 
actively chosen to use natural materials. This sense of doing something good for the environment is 
reflected in their product development. They are very interested in developing them further to maximize 
their sustainability. For this reason, this tool also enables a kind of equalizer between companies that use 
sustainability as a marketing strategy and companies that genuinely want to change the way they leave 
the world behind. This imbalance can disappear by enabling easy identification of actual impacts 
compared to planned or marketed ones. The developed tool is not quite there yet, as it only identifies 
and does not measure impacts yet. However, by helping to create a greater understanding, it is only a 
matter of time before this becomes a reality.  
 
In conclusion, developing a tool to identify RE due to initiatives using a DC approach is possible. This 
tool provides a real improvement in the efficiency of the process compared to previous approaches. 
Switching between identifying and understanding RE improves the process incrementally each time a 
user uses the tool. This is made possible by the previously developed conceptual framework and the 
general focus on the described triggers, drivers, and mechanisms.
