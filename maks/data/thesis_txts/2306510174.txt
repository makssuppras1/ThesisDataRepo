PREFACE 
The context of this thesis is about studying the effects of the large solar penetration in 
power systems’ frequency. The experimental work consists of modelling in RTDS (Real 
Time Digital Simulator), a software program that specializes in simulation of power sys-
tems of different scale and nature. The operation principle of RTDS demands the use of 
particular hardware (RACKS), therefore most of the work was accomplished in the de-
partment’s facilities. Half of the project period was roughly spent on the modelling part 
and the rest of it for writing the thesis. I would like to express my deep gratitude to my 
co-supervisors Guangya Yang and Ha Thi Nguyen for their constant support and help 
during the project.  
 
I would also like to show my deep appreciation to the RTDS support team for their con-
stant correspondence and technical support in crucial parts of the project, without any 
hesitation or delay.

ABSTRACT 
In this thesis work, the frequency stability is investigated, in the case of large-scale solar 
PV penetration. The initial power system that was chosen is Nordic 32 test system and 
the studies include different and realistic scenarios such as generation tripping or load 
increases.  
 
The study begins with a detailed analysis of Nordic 32 system, which is a representation 
of the Swedish transmission system. Itself, it is divided into sub-subsystems, where dif-
ferent generation systems prevail in each one, such as hydropower plants and thermal 
power plants. The modelling is done through the RTDS RSCAD software, which is ideal 
to capture short-time events in power systems. The first part of the modelling was on the 
construction of the grid without any solar PV generation units, in order to examine the 
frequency quality of the initial system in steady state operation and in different power 
imbalance scenarios. The whole procedure was done gradually and in a way, that facili-
tates the implementation of the second stage of the study. 
 
The study continued with the modelling of the photovoltaic (PV) units with 3 certain 
penetration levels and their connection with the main grid. For the sake of the power 
stability, the initial grid had to be re-configured, so that it could operate with the new 
power sources. Due to the solar units’ nature of operation, this stage played an important 
role for understanding the necessary limits of renewable power integration in the system. 
This part of the study included similar actions as the first part, meaning that the same fault 
scenarios were simulated through RTDS in order to compare the two different versions 
of Nordic 32. The inertia-less PV system establishes some difficulties for the system to 
restore its default values during power imbalances. 
 
Lastly, the sum of the results was always examined as a comparison with the frequency 
ranges that the ENTSOE Standard has established for the Nordic Grid System.

INTRODUCTION 
This chapter introduces the subject of the thesis and the problem that is derived. The tasks 
and general methodology are also described. Except for these, a thesis layout will give 
the ability to readers to navigate through the report easily and efficiently. 
  
1.1 Background 
 
The global trend of renewable energy sources is still vivid and always growing. Govern-
ments around the world are investing in renewable energies in all their forms, in order 
either to reach certain levels of penetration or to reduce the operational cost of conven-
tional means of power generation.  Especially solar power is becoming increasingly pop-
ular, also after the new technological advances in the photovoltaic (PV) panels that take 
place. These advances relate to higher efficiency and lower operational cost. This leads 
many countries to adopt new policies in favor of higher installed PV capacity. 
 
On the other hand, large-scale solar PV penetration tends to create some known issues to 
the grid. The problem lies to the nature of operating principle of the PV systems [1]. 
Instead of using a rotational shaft and traditional electromagnetic principles to generate 
electricity, the PV panels use certain components such as power electronic devices, nec-
essary to convert the DC output of the panel to AC input for the utility grid. Another 
important power electronic component that is used is the maximum power tracking sys-
tem that enables the PV system to remain in maximum power generation mode through-
out a sunny or cloudy day. Investigation and research have shown that these power elec-
tronic means are the cause of many technical experts claiming that a large- scale solar PV 
penetration is very hard and dangerous to be implemented for the power system stability. 
 
1 
==PAGE:19==
 
Introduction 
The major element that shows the general health of a power system is its frequency, or 
system frequency. It is important for the power stability and the safety of electrical equip-
ment of both ends of the utility grid. When a frequency event occurs, the system is de-
signed in that way that will try to compensate for this deviation using inertia, or system 
inertia, a form of kinetic energy stored naturally by the induction generation units. The 
synchronous machines will inject this energy into the grid to counteract the frequency 
deviation. As the solar PV systems are inertia-less, their integration with the grid, makes 
the latter inertia-less as well. The lower this system inertia, the more inefficiently the grid 
frequency reacts on abrupt changes in generation and load imbalances.  
  
In order to investigate the conditions, in which the system operates efficiently or fails, a 
software-modelling program will be utilized, called RSCAD by RTDS. RSCAD is user-
friendly software, intended to create a working environment familiar to the power system 
engineer. It allows the user to perform all of the necessary steps to prepare and run simu-
lations, and to analyze simulations results. It is also capable of performing dynamic sim-
ulation studies of stability issues of large-scale grids without any restriction; therefore, it 
will be the main simulation software in this thesis work. 
 
1.2 Methodology and tasks 
 
The thesis study will be carried out by modelling of a power system. The software that 
will be used is the RTDS RSCAD. As for the system, the Nordic 32 Test System will be 
used. This system represents Sweden’s power grid and was developed by Svenska Kraft-
nat for simulation purposes [2]. The system consists of 32 high voltage buses and 9 low 
voltage buses. Initially, the system does not include any PV generation units. That is why 
modifications have to be made in order to fulfill the study requirements. For realistic 
stability issues, the modelling part will also include the power control modelling along 
with the inertia modeling. In the first stage, the test system will be examined without the 
PV plants in its steady state operation, in order to ensure that the grid frequency operates 
within the limits. These limits are given by the ENTSOE standard [3], regulating the fre-
quency range of a system, like the Nordic 32 system. This standard will act as a guideline 
throughout the whole study. Then, the same frequency characteristics are to be examined

1.3 Literature review 
 
Much research has already been done in the field, concerning power stability in grids with 
integrated renewable energy sources. Most of these investigations though study only volt-
age stability issues and with minimum or moderate solar PV or wind energy penetration. 
In addition, the most commonly examined parameter is the voltage stability of the system 
and not the frequency. As for the system, Nordic 32 has been thoroughly examined in 
many cases and scenarios, so plenty information can be extracted by these studies. 
 
Particularly, former Master students Baheej Alghamdi and Mohammed Alamri from 
Chalmers University have extensively studied the impacts of large-scale integration of 
solar photovoltaics and load characteristics on voltage stability. Although their results are 
not to be used in this thesis, their methodology and conclusions were quite important and 
many methods were imitated for this work. They used the same system - Nordic 32 - and 
as a part of their project, they modified the system to simulate the solar PV penetration 
into the system. Consequently, concerning the effects of large-scale integration of solar 
PVs, the following has been concluded: 
 
3

Introduction 
• The tripping of a major conventional generation unit established the maximum 
PV penetration to be 30%, based of course on the frequency deviation that was 
examined during the studies. It was also observed that the higher the PV penetra-
tion, the higher the frequency deviation during a critical fault. Whenever PV ca-
pacity was installed in the system, a conventional generation unit of equal size 
was deactivated, so the system was becoming more and more inertialess. 
• After comparison between different levels of PV penetration, it was proved that 
the voltage recovery time was significantly worsened after a major fault in the 
system. 
• Another important function of the PV system was also proved. When the PV sys-
tems cannot inject active power to the system, probably due to irradiance changes, 
they can instead inject reactive power and thus support the system generation units 
in sever disturbances. 
 
S. Achilles, S. Schramm and J. Bebic for the U.S. Department of Energy wrote another 
interesting report. Although they studied a different test system based on IEEE standards, 
their observations are quite thought-intriguing and are as following: 
 
• System inertia and frequency regulation are reduced, as conventional generation 
is de-committed. This produces more severe power imbalances creating larger fre-
quency deviations. 
• Thermal units are shown to operate at less efficient load levels, in case of not de-
commission of conventional generation. 
• Higher PV penetration and moderate load conditions results in lower reactive 
power support, especially after the de-commission of conventional generation 
units. In turn, this results in longer recovery rimes after faults and more frequent 
voltage collapses. 
• Load shedding, an important safety scheme of a power system is shown to be 
affected. While severe frequency changes take place, the system is designed to 
implement load shedding to stabilize the frequency. This method is based on fre-
quency protective relays that disconnect complete feeders. Significant PV gener-
ation seems to disturb their operation, resulting in lower load capacity eventually 
disconnected.

GRID INERTIA AND FREQUENCY 
This chapter contains the theoretical and fundamental elements on what exactly inertia 
is and its role in electricity generation. The ENTSOE standard is also shown, along with 
its chapters, related to frequency range. 
 
2.1 Grid Inertia 
 
Inertia as a mechanical concept is one of the physics basics. Essentially, things that are 
moving will keep moving unless an external force makes them stop. Similarly, things that 
are idle will stay idle unless again an external force initiates a moving process. The same 
idea is valid for rotating objects. 
 
In the case of power plants, inertia is also present. In a common coal-fired power plant, 
coal is brunt and steam is produced. The generated steam spins a turbine, which generates 
electricity, supplied to the connected grid. The spinning process works with heavy rotat-
ing masses that naturally exhibit inertia. For example, if coal stops being added to the 
burning chamber, the rotating turbine will keep spinning, even for a while. 
 
The same concept exists in other power plants, containing rotating parts. For example, a 
hydroelectric power plant is also commonly used. By utilizing a dam, the force of water 
manages to spin a turbine, producing electricity. If somehow the water flow is discon-
nected from the whole process, the turbine will keep spinning, again for a little while. 
 
The majority of generators, used in the grid system are rotating machines [4]. These ma-
chines store inertia during normal operation. The kinetic energy stored in a single gener-
ator is also called inertia constant Hgen and is given by the following equation:

ventional generators, thus removing a certain percentage of the total system inertia. If a 
large generator is suddenly cut off, the entire system, meaning the generators will slow 
down and start operating in a lower frequency. At that moment, the generators will try to 
compensate by releasing their stored energy, called inertia. The inertia is used to resist 
this decline in speed and frequency.  
 
Operating in lower frequency is not acceptable for machinery such as steam turbines and 
thermal generators. Therefore, the generators start tripping off, in order to protect their 
systems. This though can lead to a general system blackout. To avoid situations like these, 
the system consists of safety control systems that disconnect some of the loads to reduce 
the stress in the generators and halt the steep decline of frequency.

means of generation has resulted in an inertia decline. Specifically, solar generators use 
inverters to provide power to the grid. This power is inertia-less. Thus, in an event of 
large generator tripping, the system frequency will decline much faster than in the con-
servative generators case. This also increases the chance of the other generators tripping.

2.3 Frequency Droop and Control 
 
In a typical electrical grid, the synchronous generators implement a type of frequency-
speed control, also called droop speed control [6]. In droop speed control, the prime 
mover of the generator’s turbine is called governor, which uses the dynamic pressure 
generated by expanding or minimizing the steam to turn the blades of the turbine. The 
governor in a hydroelectric power plant regulates the water flow to regulate the turbine 
speed. The primary objective of the governor in the steam turbine operation is also to 
maintain a constant speed of rotation irrespective of the varying load. This type of control 
allows the parallelization of synchronous generators, so that loads are shared evenly 
among generators according to their power rating.  
 
How the frequency is related to the speed control can be explained through equation (2.6). 
The synchronous generator frequency (in Hz) is proportional to the number of poles of 
the generator P and its speed N (in RPM). When the generators are connected to the grid, 
the grid because of the large difference between individual power generation and total 
load sets their operating frequency. Although they run at the same frequency, they tend 
to run at different speed, due to their varied number of poles.

2.4 ENTSO-E Network Code on Load – Frequency Control and 
Reserves 
 
As the system frequency is a common physical parameter for a grid operating in a syn-
chronous area, its variations affect all the installed components of the system [7].  Vice 
versa, all installed components affect in their own severity the frequency quality. Every 
transmission system operator (TSO) has its own responsibilities for each system, as far 
as normal operation and maintenance are concerned. However, the synchronization of 
many areas has established the need for standardization of the common methods that 
TSOs have to use, in order to maintain frequency quality. The Network Code on Load-
Frequency Control and Reserves (NC LFCR), developed by ENTSO-E (European Net-
work of Transmission System Operators for Electricity), sets the boundaries, regulations 
and necessary conditions that have to be met inside the European Union (EU). 
 
12

The target principles of the NC LFCR are:  
 
 To define some common Load-Frequency Control processes and control methods, 
 To set the conditions for maintaining the frequency quality in all EU synchronous 
areas, 
 To determine common requirements for Reserve Providing Units for the provision 
of Active Power Reserves to the Reserve Connecting TSO. 
 
The key objective of NC LFCR is to achieve a solid and widely acceptable set of technical 
rules, which include the implementation of all necessary processes, while taking into con-
sideration the rise of the renewable energy sources, especially due to their unique charac-
teristics [8]. The NC LFCR is not a genuine code, but more of a combination of all pre-
vious codes, which regulated different synchronous areas in Europe. It is the result of all 
past experiences, measurements and studies in the electricity grids.

must be monitored real-time and maintained properly in the safety range. For the design 
of the control processes, the monitored values refer to the frequency quality defining pa-

ance incident. The final target is to prevent the so-called under-frequency load shedding, 
in case of a large and uncontrollable imbalance. In such a scenario, the system protective

3.3 Photovoltaic Conversion  
 
When a semiconductor, ex. a diode, receives solar irradiance, every photon of larger en-
ergy than the energy gap, has the ability to be absorbed into a chemical bond and to pro-
duce an electron. While the solar irradiance lasts, a voltage is produced across the termi-
nals of the diode. This is called photovoltaic phenomenon. The configuration, which is a 
current source, can be called PV cell. Lastly, the output current is called photocurrent Iph. 
The photocurrent is proportional to the capacity of the solar irradiance Pph and the diode 
surface. 
 
It is a fact that it is impossible to convert the sum of the solar irradiance, received by the 
PV cell, into electrical energy. A part of the irradiance is reflected by the PV cell surface 
and released towards the atmosphere. Some of the irradiance that enters the PV cell can-
not be absorbed, due to some photons’ lack of energy. Even some of the photons with the 
necessary energy do not take part into the conversion, because they tend to transform into 
heat. Concluding, only the photons that have energy E = Eg are used for the production 
of electrical energy.

3.7 Control Functions of PV plants 
 
As PV generation grows to the point of significantly having an impact on the grid, it has 
been needed from PV plants to support grid stability and reliability. A key feature of such 
a system is a plant-level controller, which controls a large number of individual inverters, 
which affects plant output at the grid connection point, or point of common coupling 
(PCC) [18]. 
 
There are many strategies and recommendations for the development of a controller sys-
tem, but most of them concentrate on satisfying grid requirements such as voltage control 
and regulation, voltage and frequency fault durability, reactive and active power control 
and frequency response criteria.

3.7.1 Automatic Generation Control 
 
In an electric power system, automatic generation control (AGC) is a system for adjusting 
the power output of multiple generators at different power plants, in response to changes 
in the load. Since a power grid requires that generation and load closely balance moment 
by moment, frequent adjustments to the output of generators are necessary. The balance 
can be judged by measuring the system frequency; if it is increasing, more power is being 
generated than used, and all the machines in the system are accelerating. If the system 
frequency is decreasing, more loads are on the system than the instantaneous generation 
can provide, and all generators are slowing down [19]. 
 
In a PV system, the plant controller implements plant-level logic and closed-loop control 
methods with real-time commands to the inverters to achieve reliable regulation. Typi-
cally, there is one controller per plant that is controlling the output at a single HV bus, 
also called PCC. The controller monitors system measurements, mainly frequency and 
determines the desired operating conditions of various plant devices to meet the specified 
targets. It could manage capacitor and/or reactor banks. In addition, it could manage all 
the inverters in the plant ensuring that they are producing the necessary active and reactive 
power in order to satisfy the expected values at the PCC. 
 
When the plant operator sends an active power curtailment command, the controller cal-
culates and distributes active power curtailment to individual inverters. In general, the 
inverters can be throttled back only to a certain specified level of active power and not 
any lower without causing the DC voltage to rise beyond its operating range. Therefore, 
the plant controller dynamically stops and starts inverters as needed to manage the spec-
ified active power output limit. It also uses the active power management function to 
ensure that the plant output does not exceed the desired ramp rates, to the extent possible. 
It cannot, however, always accommodate rapid reduction in irradiance due to cloud cover 
[20].

3.7.2 Frequency Support 
 
Without special controls, the renewable energy sources do not contribute to the system 
inertia. Although the wind turbines use some of the kinetic energy that is stored in their 
shaft, the solar panels cannot store any kinetic energy. The exemption is when the limited 
energy stored in the DC capacitor, in combination with a battery unit is used to support 
the frequency control. There are four options on frequency support by solar or wind 
sources. 
 
1. Energy Storage System ESS: 
Batteries or capacitors are added together with the PV unit or wind farm. By co-
ordinating control between the two systems, it is possible to optimize the power 
output by the renewables and consequently the frequency control [21]. 
2. Another option would be a de-loading, partial, or full curtailing of the solar or 
wind unit. This creates a power reserve that can be used to support frequency in a 
longer period. However, this option is highly costly, due to extra production sup-
port mechanisms. 
3. A third option (only available to wind turbines) is the interchanging of the kinetic 
energy with the grid [22]. In a frequency dip, the wind turbine would raise its 
power output to deliver the necessary frequency output. The rotor speed will there-
fore decrease, so in order to raise it again, the power output will decrease. This 
could serve as a short-time measure and it could affect the frequency deviation, 
as second frequency dip can occur because of the decrease the in power output 
afterwards. 
4. Demand side management (DSM): 
Finally, through this procedure, interventions can be made at the load side. For 
instance, a load that is voltage-independent can be used to support the frequency 
[23].

RTDS RSCAD MODELLING 
This chapter introduces the RTDS platform. It gives an insight to all simulation and mod-
elling tools, as well as how it is possible to successfully a model a power system of large 
scale, with the implementation of all its components and functions. 
 
4.1 RSCAD Modules 
 
The Real Time Digital Simulator (RTDS) implements a sophisticated, yet easy-to-use 
graphical interface, called RSCAD. It consists of several modules to enable the user to 
design every kind of simulation and analyze thoroughly the simulation results. Every 
power system should recognize the interface features. The modules of RSCAD include 
FileManager, Draft, RunTime, TLine, Cable, MultiPlot and CBuilder [24].

Through Draft, the main process of modelling takes place. The right side of the Draft 
screen consists of all detailed libraries of power system, control and protection systems 
and automations components. The schematics are created by using components from 
these libraries. When the designing part is over, the user can run compile execution code, 
in order to check any errors in the design, before actually run the simulation. Lastly, there 
are some advanced functions such as hierarchy components, single – line diagram mode 
and load flow initialization.

This module allows the user to interact with the real time simulation. The canvas includes

graphical environment controls the entire simulation.  
 
There is also the possibility for all the above components to be grouped and minimized, 
mainly for practical reasons. RunTime automatically triggers plot updates when the user 
interacts with the simulation. Calculation functions are available to condition plots. As 
well, text, annotation and time stamps can be added to document simulation results. The 
plot data can be saved or printed directly from this module.

4.1.4 TLine 
 
A very important parameter of a power system, the data of which can change the com-
pletely the behavior of the system is the transmission line. For the precise modelling of a 
transmission line, a specific module is used, called TLine. Physical AC and DC data is 
entered into the TLine module and it returns to the user a representative module. Travel-
ling wave models, like Bergeron, Frequency dependent model, can be calculated and used 
in this model. According to the amount of data possessed, the user can even select the use 
of Pi model, especially if certain parameters fail to participate in the calculation process.

4.2.2 Transmission Line Models 
 
Either using travelling wave models or PI models can model the transmission lines. PI 
models are normally used when there is not enough data available for the examined sys-
tem or the length of the lines are short enough not to affect the travelling wave time. This 
could affect the accuracy of the simulation results. Of course, RSCAD has a special option 
for the user to opt for an alternative model solution, if this time value is affecting the 
simulation process. Its long lines connecting every subsystem of it characterize Nordic 
32. Therefore, only the travelling wave model is preferred and presented in this session. 
 
The TLine module is used to lay out and define the geometry and parameters of the trav-
elling wave transmission line. Generally, transmission lines consist of several mutually 
coupled phases or conductors. The extent of coupling depends on the geometry of the line 
and the proximity of the individual phase conductors to one another and to ground. The 
TLine module is used to define the line, its geometrical configuration and other parameter 
data for travelling wave line models. Both the Frequency Dependent and Bergeron lines 
are defined in this manner. An example of a transmission line model, where the TLine is 
used, is shown Figure 4-17.  
 
In the RTDS, travelling wave transmission line models are solved in the modal domain. 
That is, a transformation matrix is used to convert the line admittance and impedance 
matrices from the phase domain into the modal domain. Phase domain voltages and cur-
rents are converted into the modal domain and back into the phase domain at each end of 
the line, as required. In theory, the transformation matrix is frequency dependent. Due to 
various difficulties however, it is solved at only one frequency. For the Bergeron line 
model, this limitation is not a problem, as the model is meant to be accurate at only one 
frequency in any case.  
 
For the Frequency−dependent model, the 3-phase line model is affected very little over a 
wide frequency range. However, in the six-conductor line, mutual coupling at various 
frequencies must be carefully checked. More conductors than six are not supported.

4.2.3 Dynamic Load Models 
 
In order for a system to be stable, it needs to have power demand, or loads. For stability 
purposes, the loads are modelled as dynamic loads. That way, the load is dynamically 
adjusted to maintain the P and Q set points, managed by the user. RSCAD accomplishes 
that by using a variable conductance X. An example of load modelling is shown in Figure 
4-20. 
 
 
Figure 4-20: Dynamic Load model 
 
The library icon is shown at the left part, whereas the right part shows the configuration 
window. Flexibility offers plenty to the user. First, there are three types of loads: the RL 
for a resistive-inductive load, the RC for a resistive-capacitive load and the RX. If RX is 
selected, the load type will change based on the value of Q. If Q is negative, the load is 
modelled as an RC type, whereas if Q is positive, the load is modelled as an RL type load. 
The connection between the resistance R and conductance X can also be determined, by 
setting the btype option to R//X for parallel connection or R-X for a series connection. 
The P and Q settings are maintained throughout the simulation, but in case there is need 
for changes in load values during the simulation, the P and Q values can be changed 
through the Slider option. In RunTime, a slider with the same name can be used for setting 
new values in the respective loads. The initial P and Q values are inserted in the window

4.2.4 Transformer Models 
 
In RTDS, various types of transformers can be represented. Three phase transformers 
models have each winding connected in either Y−grounded, Y−ungrounded or delta. The 
user is free to choose the configuration through appropriate data entry during the Draft 
session. A single-phase two winding transformer and three phase two and three winding 
autotransformers models are available. In this system, the majority of the transformers 
windings are Y-grounded and such a transformer is shown in Figure 4-22.  
 
 
Figure 4-22: Transformer model 
 
When both sides of the transformer are Y connected, the line to neutral voltages on the 
secondary will be in phase with the corresponding line-to-neutral voltages on the primary. 
44 
==PAGE:62==
 
RTDS RSCAD Modelling 
Branch currents and current injection history terms of corresponding primary and second-
ary windings will then also be in phase. 
 
Ideal transformers can be defined and simulated by choosing the Ideal option in the trans-
former’s configuration menu, in the type menu item. An ideal transformer will have no 
magnetizing inductance and will therefore be represented by the specified leakage reac-
tance only. Non−ideal transformers will involve a magnetizing branch along with the 
specified leakage reactance value. If core saturation effects are not modelled; the magnet-
izing reactance will be linear. If, however the user chooses to represent non−linear satu-
ration effects, the magnetizing branch representation changes from that of a simple in-
ductance to a more complicated combination of inductance in parallel with a non−linear 
current source. All transformers in this project are considered ideal though. The ideal 
transformer option results in the simplified representation depicted in Figure 4-23. Here 
the transformer is modelled as a simple series reactance together with a ratio changer. No 
magnetizing path is provided. 
 
The configuration window can be seen in Figure 4-24. The user can select the windings 
connection, whether the Delta-connected winding will lag or lead the Y-connected wind-
ing, the transformer type. Moreover, the fundamental parameters can be inserted, such as 
the rated MVA of the transformer, the base frequency and the leakage inductance. Of 
course, these values are given by the manufacturer and can be found in the Appendix 
session, especially for the Nordic 32 system.

6.1 Generation Tripping 
 
One of the most common fault incidents during a power system operation, is when a 
generator unit disconnects from the rest of the network. Either this is caused by a failure 
at the transmission line connecting the generator and the nearest bus or the generator itself 
stops producing electrical energy, usually due to faults in the electrical machines of the 
generator unit. As a result, the system has to face a power imbalance, the severity of which 
depends on two factors. Firstly, the amount of the power output of the specific generator. 
Secondly, the integrity of the safety control system of the power system, which is initially 
designed to maintain the preset values of frequency and voltage. 
 
6.1.1 Time-domain simulation 
 
Figure 6-1 shows the first case of studies. Frequency measurement is taken in bus 4011 
and the generator, chosen for tripping, is the generator G20. The proximity of the gener-
ator to the measurement location has been intentional, as the severity of the fault would 
not be visible to the graphs otherwise. Moreover, this generator produces 2000 MW, 
therefore it will be a substantial loss of power for Nordic 32 system. As it can be seen, 
the frequency reaches at 49.56 Hz at the moment of generation tripping. This exact mo-
ment, a power imbalance is created in the system and the frequency deviates according to 
equation (2.2.4). The rest of the generators start slowing down, in order to compensate 
for the power loss. This means they are releasing the inertia, which they were storing just

control systems of the network intervene, as shown in the same figure. Before the system 
succeeds in stabilizing its frequency, a frequency oscillation is exhibited, which lasts for

Results and Conclusions 
6.4 Future Studies 
 
As this thesis includes studies that are only a small part of integrated power systems stud-
ies, it would be beneficial to suggest some additional approaches to investigate more top-
ics. 
 
• As Nordic32 represents only one kind of power systems, it would be interesting 
to examine systems, such as the Danish one, which is based on many interconnec-
tions with neighboring networks. Alternatively, the study of isolated systems, 
such as in islands, will contribute to the solutions of problems that many island-
nations face in their power distribution systems. 
• The solar PV penetration is considered high enough in this study. However, the 
general target of many countries, including Denmark, is to increase the penetra-
tion level of renewable energy sources even more. Therefore, instead of trying to 
set a maximum level of penetration for existing system, it would be wise to re-
search ways to upgrade these systems, in order for them to accept higher levels of 
RES power. 
• The results showed impact of various fault disturbances on the power system, dur-
ing the injection of PV power. A different approach could show the impact on the 
PV systems during the same fault scenarios. 
• A much more realistic model for the PV system should be developed. The model 
used in this study has been modified to suit the needs of the simulation, but it does 
not incorporate any type of controller that could provide reactive and active power 
support to the rest of the grid. This would facilitate more in-depth analysis in 
power systems.
