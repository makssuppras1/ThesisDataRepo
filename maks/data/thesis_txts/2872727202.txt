Kinetic Study of Clay Calcination
Abstract
Concrete is one of the best materials in construction, however, 5-7% of anthropogenic
CO2 emissions comes from the cement industry.
Supplementary cementitious materials
(SCMs) are a promising strategy to reduce CO2 emissions, by replacing a portion of clinker
in cement production with materials produced at lower temperatures such as calcined clay.
This thesis studies the thermal decomposition of three clay minerals; kaolinite, montmoril-
lonite, and muscovite, along with two industrial samples: B2 and Ireland clay using thermo-
gravimetric analysis (TGA) at various heating rates (5-100 K/min). The aim is to better
understand and describe the kinetic behaviour of clay minerals.
Kaolinite was found to follow a third-order reaction model (F3) with an activation
energy of Ea = 187kJ/mol and a pre-exponential factor of k = 2.91×1010s−1, corresponding
well with some results in literature.
Montmorillonite was found to exhibit a three-step
mechanism, with each step well described using a third-order reaction model (F3). The
activation energies for montmorillonite were found as: Ea1 = 84.3kJ/mol, Ea2 = 500kJ/mol,
and Ea3 = 226kJ/mol and pre-exponential factors: k1 = 4.57 × 106s−1, k2 = 2.28 × 106s−1,
and k3 = 3.45 × 108s−1. Muscovite showed behaviour similar to a three-step mechanism
like montmorillonite. However, due to time limitation, muscovite is only treated as a one-
step mechanism, resulting in an activation energy of Ea = 606kJ/mol and pre-exponential
factor of k = 2.21 × 101s−1. The method of constituents was found to accurately predict
the behaviour of artificial mixtures of phyllosilicates. It was less accurate for industrial clay
samples due to compositional uncertainties.
This thesis confirms previous findings for kaolinite, and provides new kinetic parameters
for muscovite and montmorillonite. The findings of this project contribute to improvement
in understand and kinetic modelling of clay minerals.

Resumé
Beton er et af de bedste materialer i byggeindustrien, men 5-7 % af verdens men-
neskeskabte CO2-udledninger stammer fra cementindustrien. Supplerende cementbaserede
materialer (SCM’er) er en lovende strategi til at reducere CO2-udledningen ved at erstatte
en del af klinkerne i cementproduktionen med materialer der er lavet ved lavere tempera-
turer, såsom kalcineret ler. Denne afhandling undersøger den termiske nedbrydning af tre
lermineraler: kaolinit, montmorillonit og muskovit, samt to industrielle prøver: B2 og Ireland
clay, ved hjælp af termogravimetrisk analyse (TGA) ved forskellige opvarmningshastigheder
(5-100 K/min). Målet er at opnå en bedre forståelse og beskrivelse af den kinetiske opførsel
ved lermineraler.
Kaolinit viste sig at følge en reaktionsmodel af tredje orden (F3) med en aktiveringsen-
ergi på Ea = 187kJ/mol og en præeksponentiel faktor på k = 2.91 × 1010s−1, hvilket stem-
mer godt overens med visse resultater i litteraturen.
Montmorillonit udviste en tretrins
mekanisme, hvor hvert trin kunne beskrives med en tredjeordens reaktionsmodel (F3). Ak-
tiveringsenergierne for montmorillonit blev fundet til: Ea1 = 84.3kJ/mol, Ea2 = 500kJ/mol,
and Ea3 = 226kJ/mol, med præeksponentielle faktorer: k1 = 4.57 × 106s−1, k2 = 2.28 ×
106s−1, and k3 = 3.45×108s−1. Muskovit viste en adfærd svarende til en tretrins mekanisme
som montmorillonit, men på grund af tidsbegrænsninger blev muskovit dog kun behandlet
som en ét-trins mekanisme, hvilket resulterede i en aktiveringsenergi på Ea = 606kJ/mol
og en præeksponentiel faktor på k = 2.21 × 101s−1. Method of constituents viste sig at
kunne forudsige adfærden af blandinger af phyllosilikater nøjagtigt, men var mindre præcis
for industrielle lerprøver på grund af usikkerheder i sammensætningen.
Denne afhandling bekræfter tidligere resultater for kaolinit og bidrager med nye kinetiske
parametre for muskovit og montmorillonit. Resultaterne fra dette projekt bidrager til en
forbedret forståelse og kinetisk modellering af lermineraler.

1
Introduction
Cement is essential for the infrastructure of the modern world as we know it. The ce-
ment industry has only increased their production in the last few decades and is currently
responsible for 5-7% of the global anthropogenic CO2 release [1, 2]. A major problem in
cement production is the amount of CO2 released, due to the thermal decomposition of
limestone into lime, consequently worsening the state of climate change. With a high de-
mand for cement, it is necessary to find a suitable method for producing it with lower CO2
emissions. Supplementary cementious materials have shown a great potential to reduce CO2
emissions, especially calcined clay [3]. To activate the clay, it needs to be heated causing
dehydroxylation to happen, and a reactive amorphous phase with pozzalanic properties is
formed. In the industry, flash calciners are often used with a small particle size and low res-
idence time (few seconds). Therefore its important to have precise and well-defined kinetics
to describe the calcination of clays. However, kinetics are dependent on many factors such
as heating rate, presence of impurities, crystalline structure and chemical composition of the
material. The difference in heating rate between laboratory equipment and industrial flash
calciner has not been studied in detail [4].
1.1
Aim of this project
The aim of this project is to model clay calcination process in industrial equipment,
using models that combine heat transfer, kinetics of clay calcination, transport processes
and fluid dynamics.
The aim of this work is to develop methods and provide accurate
kinetics for thermal dehydroxylation of clays.
1.2
Approach
In this project, thermogravimetric analysis (TG) was performed at different heating
rates (5, 10, 20, 50 and 100 K/min) and composition to study the effect on the kinetics
of clay minerals. This was done using Netzsch simultaneous thermometric analysis (STA)
equipment, with small sample sizes to minimise the impact of heat transfer. The kinetics de-
rived was compared with data in the literature and extrapolated to high heating rates similar
to industrial flash calciners. Additionally, the behaviour of industrial clay mixtures were em-
ulated with the method of constituents to study synergistic effects between its components.
Thus, the aim of this study was to analyse the possibility of modelling clay minerals.

Cements material and production
Cement is one of the most fundamental construction materials used in our society, it is
robust, versatile, safe, and quite resistant to corrosion [5], making it excellent for long-lasting
construction. Having construction materials with low climate impact is progressively more
important due to climate changes.
2.1
Cement production
Cement production can be divided into five general steps. The raw material used in
production of cement is chalk from chalk graves and sand [6]. The raw material from the
excavation sites is initially crushed by a cement crusher, then a stacker and reclaimer ho-
mogenise them in preparation for milling [7]. After homogenisation, it is milled to the desired
size, typically in the size range of 0.95-3.81 cm [3]. In modern cement production, a precal-
ciner is used to disperse and suspend the milled feed into the air, providing high-efficiency
solid-gas heat exchange [8]. The precalciner works in a low temperature flameless combustion
state that occurs at 820 to 900 °C, a certain amount of heat is used from the exhaustion of
the rotary kiln outlet [8]. The precalciner is used to make cement production more efficient
and initiate the decomposition of the raw feed, Reaction {1} is not completely finished in
the precalciner [8]. After the precalciner, the partially calcined meal is processed in the ro-
tary kiln that is equipped with a burner. The raw materials are exposed to flame radiation,
hot gas convection, and brick conduction from the kiln that increase the chemical reactions,
leaving the rotary kiln as clinker [9]. After the clinker has cooled down, it is crushed and
separated into desired sizes. Lastly, the cement is packed in bulk or in bags for delivery, the
process is also summarised in figure 1 [7].

2.3
Environmental impact
One of the largest disadvantages of cement is the CO2 emissions during production.
Cement production is responsible for 5-7% of the global anthropogenic CO2 released [1, 2].
Calcination is the dominant CO2 emitter in cement production, responsible for roughly 50%,
while 40% comes from thermal energy which goes to grinding and burning of the cement,
and 10 % comes from transportation and storage, combined [1].
The demand for cement has only increased since 1970, when roughly 0.94 billion tons
were produced [2], in 1995 that increased to 1.39 billion tons [1]. The emitted CO2 is in the
range of 0.5-0.9 tons of CO2 pr. ton of cement produced, resulting in a lot of CO2 produced.
The International energy agency (IEA) in 2006 projected that cement production will have
increased by 45% in the year 2050 to 5.2 billion tons [13], while other sources such as the
world cement association (WCA) estimate a fall or levels similar to the levels of 2020 [14].
From figure 2 it can be seen that cement production appears to have stagnated over the
years.

Supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs)
2.4
Supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs)
Numerous projects have tried to reduce the emitted CO2, and reductions in CO2 emis-
sions are still being investigated. Numerous waste products have been added as supplemen-
tary cementitious materials (SCMs), ranging from steel slag to rice husk [15]. One of the
concerns is also the abundance / availability at the location of cement production, which is
one of the reasons for also investigating many different SCMs. Clay minerals have also been
used as SCMs replacing some of the clinker in traditional cement, maintaining its compres-
sive strength and in some cases providing higher strengths [5]. However, clay will need to be
activated, which can be done by heating the clay, which creates an amorphous phase that is
highly reactive [16]. A typical cement replacement is limestone calcined clay cement (LC3),
that consist of ordinary portland cement (PC) and calcined clay (LC2), LC2 is also called
metakaolinite [5]. An estimate from Sharma et al. [5] states that addition of calcined clay
can reduce CO2 emissions by 30% and the thermal energy required is 60% lower compared
to clinker production [5]. In figure 3, a comparison can be made between some LC3 and
OPC. LC3 requires 20% less thermal energy compared to the same amount of clinker [5].
Both gypsum and limestone are two minerals found naturally, therefore, when added, they
do not emit CO2, hence why there are no CO2 contributions from gypsum and limestone
[17, 18].

4.2
Differential scanning calorimetry
Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) measures the change in thermal characteristics,
such as heat capacity, based on changes in heat flow as a function of time or temperature
[24]. Measurement of heat flow to or from the sample allows the method to detect phase
transitions such as melts, glass transition, phase changes, and curing [24]. Another thermal
analysis method is differential thermal analysis (DTA), which measures the temperature
difference between reference material and sample material, but DTA cannot quantitatively
measure the heat flow like DSC [27].

4.3
Simultaneous thermometric analysis
A common modern technique is simultaneous thermometric analysis (STA); it is com-
posed of TGA combined with DTA or DSC, hence why it is also called TG-DSC or TG-DTA.
STA is often used since one method reinforces the data from the other analysis method or pro-
vides a different perspective on the data [28]. An STA from Netzsch can be seen figure 4, the
heating elements are placed around a protective tube, and the interior of the protective tube
is heated where the sample carrier is placed, the thermocouple then measures the temperature
at the top of the protective tube. The balance is highly protected at the bottom of the STA,
its protected against radiation (radiation shield), a protective flow (stabilising the weighing
environment) and even has an evacuating system [26]. In this research field, the temperature
range is generally from room temperature to ∼1100◦C [29, 22, 30, 30, 31, 32, 33, 33]. The
heating rate varies from 1-40K/min [29, 22, 34, 30, 35, 31, 32, 33, 36, 37], for the results
from the literature, a few of the Netzsch STAs can go up to 1000K/min [26].
4.4
Compositional and structural analysis
Many kinetic investigations also use analysis methods such as X-ray diffraction (XRD),
X-ray flouresence (XRF), and Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) to investigate
the structure of clay minerals and also the influence of temperature on the structure [38].
XRD is where a monochromatic X-ray beam is elastically scattered due to periodic arrange-
ments of atoms, the scattered X-rays are then collected and compared with a database;
every crystalline material has a unique signal [24]. XRD is used to gather information about
atomic structure of crystalline solids [39]. In XRF a sample is irradiated with high-energy
X-rays, an inner electron is then hit and dislodged. An electron from a shell farther out,
than the shell the dislodged electron came from, drops to the shell with a missing electron,
in that process it releases fluorescent light [40]. XRF can be used to determine the element
(peak energy) and concentration (intensity of peak) [40]. FTIR records the relative amount
of energy as a function of wavelength when it passes through a sample [23, 24]. XRD and
FTIR could be used as an additional confirmation method for the structure of the propagated
products [23].
4.5
Thermal shocking
Thermal shocking is a method quite similar to TGA where a sample is heated by an
energy-releasing element; unlike TGA the is exposed to the surrounding atmosphere. It is
widely used to test ceramics at high heating rates, such as in aviation conditions [41].
4.6
Thermal soaking
Thermal soak is a experimental method that consists of three phases; phase 0 is the
heating, phase 1 is the steady state where the equipment / sample is under constant high
thermal load, and phase 2 is the soak phase, where the heating is stopped and the temper-
ature rise is observed due to natural convection [42]. Using this method with regard to the

Kaolinite experiments
This chapter will summarise relevant experimental data on kaolinite, and one of the
most common methods to fit the data to the reaction kinetics is the Kissinger method
[22, 29]. Ondro and Trník [29] study a 90 wt.% kaolinite from Sedlec Ia, initially they ensure
that the composition is as pure as stated by the manufacturer, and then crush and sieve
them so the particles are less than 100 µm in diameter [29]. Ondro and Trník test in the
temperature range of 30 to 1020 ° C using TG-DTA with heating rates below 10 °C min−1
under static air atmosphere [29]. The results Ondro and Trník present are similar to the
results they document in the literature, which can be seen in table 7 finding an Avrami
exponent corresponding to diffusion controlled growth with a decreasing nucleation rate for
dehydroxylation and grain edge nucleation after saturation for crystallisation of the Al–Si
spinel phase from metakaolinite, according to Avrami theory (see table 6) [29]. Ondro et al.

carried out in literature. Ptáček et al. used 30 mg [22], so When using ∼500mg it becomes
important to consider the effect of heat transfer. A similar experiment to that of Ondro and

6.3
Montmorillonite experiments
Montmorillonite is investigated fewer times when compared to illite, one of the only
relevant studies is conducted by Malhotra et al. [51]. Malhotra et al. [51] use thermal
soaking and thermal shocking combined with FTIR to better understand the intrinsic step of
montmorillonite decomposition, using thermal shocking in the range from 290 < T < 1100K
for 24 h. They found three regions could be associated with dehydroxylation while a fourth
could be attributed to thermal dissociation for the remaining hydroxyls [51]. From thermal
soaking (isothermal experiment), the proposed mechanism is contracting sphere movement
(CSMM) which indicates surface nucleation, followed by growth over the surface. Malhotra
et al. [51] finds a rate constant of k = 0.039h−1 for CSMM (R3, see table 4) model.
A second study is conducted by Drits et al. [52], they try using a multi-cycle heating and
cooling method, to obtain data at different partially dehydroxylations for different TOT clay
minerals containing trans- and cis-vacant layers. Drit et al. used approximately 25 mg of
sample for each multi-cycle program, at 2 K/min. The TG process were composed of heating
up to a specific temperature, for example 496 °C, then letting it cooling down to 35 °C and
then heating up 25 °C higher than the previous heating (496 °C). This heating cycle were
performed until reaching 800-900 °C. Drit et al. [52] observed that tv-montmorillonite (Otay
montmorillonite) had varying activation energy with degree of hydroxylation (DT).
cis-
vacant montmorillonite (Wyoming montmorillonite) showed a rather stable activation energy
for the entire dehydroxylation degree, except close to 0 and 100 % DT. These observations
were made from Drit et al. [52] based on figure 13.

Experimental materials and methods
Based on the literature review, it is evident that more research is required on the reaction
kinetics of phyllosilicates to better understand it and to develop reliable kinetic models for
simulating clay calcination processes. All experiments in the following section are performed
using STA, and the goals can be divided into four main objectives.
• Develop procedures to perform stable and reliable STA experiments
• Provide TGA based experimental data that can be applied for determining kinetics for
the thermal decomposition of clays
• Develop a heating program so each step in montmorillonite conversion can be distin-
guished
• Propose mechanistic behaviour of the clay mineral and kinetic parameters

Muscovite was not available as a fine powder such as kaolinite and montmorillonite. 0.5

a mortar and pestle. However, using a mortar and pestle introduced many different possible
contamination sources. As can be seen in figure 14a, the mortar and pestle were already
contaminated from previous usage.
The use of mortar and pestle did not achieve great
success, as it was difficult to crush the lenses and grind them to small enough particles (see
figure 14b); therefore, an agate mortar was used instead, which resulted in better grinding.
After grinding, the mass was brushed from the mortar and pestle into a sieve with a filter
< 90µm, seen on figure 14c with muscovite lenses, however, it is not known what the brush
had previously been used for, thus increasing the amount of possible contaminations. The
brush was knocked against the table to remove other particles before use. The aim was to
minimise the number of times the muscovite lenses were transferred between the sieve and
mortar, since small particles could disappear due to charged surfaces (static).

to keep each sample to be analysed between 5 ± 0.1mg, which was achieved. The spatulas
were cleaned between each use in order to minimise contamination. Some clay particles were
observed to fly off the spatula because of static, it was also observed once that the particles
would leave the container and attach to the bottom of the lid, see figure 16.
For cleaning of the crucibles, nitrile rubber gloves of type EN 16523-1 were used to not
contaminate them. Using gloves the crucible was grabbed and washed with demineralised
water, followed by drying using a paper towel which was handled with tweezers, and lastly
the crucible was left to dry overnight.

10.4
Kaolinite conclusion
As proposed by Ondro et al.[34] and Ptáček et al. [22], kaolinite follows a third-order
reaction model, with respect to conversion. This thesis found an activation energy of 187
kJ/mol and a pre-exponential factor of 2.91 × 1010s−1 [53, 34]. However, there is only a
slight difference between R2 for F2 and F3, so kaolinite could also be governed by a second-
order reaction model. The reaction is two hydroxyl groups reacting, therefore a second-order
mechanism is physically more meaningful.

Discussion
15.1
Uncertainty introduced by mass
A considerable obstacle in the field of clay kinetics is the sample mass, as pointed out
throughout this thesis.
In principle, the sample should be only a few particles in order
to obtain pure kinetics parameters for the material examined and minimise the effect of
heat transfer. Depending on the theoretical mass loss and the current knowledge of the
investigated clay, the mass can be decreased. For example, kaolinite experiments can be
performed at a low sample mass, because of the high mass loss (13.95 % theoretical mass
loss) and previous knowledge, while montmorillonite needs a higher sample mass because of
the low mass loss (around 5-6%) and inexperience, such that noise plays a less significant
role, and different steps can be clearly seen.
15.1.1
Kissingers method discussed

exponential factor), while having the same slope (activation energy).

15.2
Data treatment
As mentioned in the section with analysis of montmorillonite, Kissingers is a method
inadequate for describing clays with high noise-to-mass-loss ratio and limited knowledge
about behaviour because it can lead to false results, i.e. negative activation energies. Many
studies have also used Kissingers to propose kinetic parameters, especially the activation
energy. The reason for only proposing an activation energy actually lies within the sample
mass, as seen in figure 17, the increase in mass shifts the curve in the same way as an increase
in the heating rate. Since the TG signal is shifted, so does the DTG, so the DTG peaks shift
to a higher temperature but maintain the same slope while changing the interception with
the y-axis, meaning that the activation will still be correct, but the pre-exponential factor
cannot be proposed. An example is plotted in figure 38, where the two lines have the same
slope (activation energy) while having different interceptions with the y-axis (pre-exponential
factors). From this argumentation, Kissingers method might be decent for determining the
activation energy regardless of the sample mass.
15.2
Data treatment
Calculating the conversion will depend to a certain degree on experimental noise. If the
initial point and final point for alpha (ξi, and ξf) is a noise point, either positive or negative
noise, from the actual weight of the sample, then alpha becomes influenced by the noise.
During the development of the code for fitting different mechanisms, it was discovered that
noise could result in either negative conversion or conversion slightly above 1, since this is
not possible, conversions above 1 were automatically set to 1, while negative conversions
were set equal to 0. This method introduces further uncertainty, due to the data being
manipulated, but helped a lot with the optimisation.
15.2.1
Data weight
The STA machines used also have some standard value for the number of data points
collected per minute in the isothermal period and the dynamic period. Therefore, using a
lower heating rate resulted in more data points and thus weighing more when minimising
the error in the model fitting code. This means that data at heating rates like 50 and 100
K/min have less influence compared to 5 K/min.
15.3
Distributed activation energy
An aspect that is not studied here is the possibility for a distribution of activation
energy, since there can be different conditions for bound water depending on the layering of
the clay sheets and how far or close they are to positive anions. In an article by V. Osipov
[58], the ion-electrostatic bonds in air-dry montmorillonite were calculated for mono-, di- and
trivalent cations and found that the increase in cation charge increases the ion-electrostatic
bond. The composition of phyllosilicates such as montmorillonite and muscovite can vary,
as shown in table 3, making it possible for water to bind differently to different cat-ions with
varying strength, meaning that some water could be released earlier compared to other water
molecules. In figure 39 examples of water storage between phyllosilicate sheets can be seen

Therefore, a distribution of activation energies might be a decent solution to describe the
behaviour of phyllosilicates with varying composition; however, this will require research with

The approach of using a distribution of activation energies complicates the process
further; it would be easier with an apparent activation energy, which was also one of the
main objectives for this thesis, to describe the behaviour with relatively simple models.
15.4
Feasibility of models
The behaviour of the models were extensively studied in this thesis, the apparent be-
haviour does not necessarily mean that it is what actually happens at a molecular level. In a
reaction, there will be a transition state with an activated complex, and using transition state
theory (TST) one could approximate all possible complexes and determine the most reason-
able pathway [60]. If TST gives logical results, it can give great insight into the pathways,
however, it requires extensive knowledge about the structure of the species participating in
the reaction. Clay minerals are quite difficult to define, the variation in composition makes
it possible to have many different configurations throughout the clay mineral, such as the
crystalline structure, lattice geometry, and TST needs to take into account all the possible
variations to ensure precise results [60]. The feasibility of the mechanism/model is not stud-
ied here because it is beyond the scope of the thesis, however, it would give great insight
into the behaviour of clays.
15.5
Environmental disturbance
During the timeline of this project construction was also carried out some days, resulting
in vibration that could be felt in some of the rooms, this can also have affected the data, this
could be the reason for the fluctuating results seen in figure 28 for 10 K/min. Even wind
drafts and temperature difference will have a influence on the sensitive STA machines.

Conclusion
In an attempt to contribute to the understanding of the production of activated phyllosil-
icates, this thesis attempts to describe the kinetics of de-hydroxylation of pure phyllosilicates
such as kaolinite, montmorillonite, and muscovite, with the use of model-fitting on exper-
imental data obtained from thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). The study aims to derive
kinetic parameters and propose a mechanism for the clays studied, in order to provide kinetic
data that can be applied in more complete models that can be used to simulate industrial
calciners.
Kaolinite was confirmed to follow a single-step third-order reaction model (F3) with
respect to conversion, with an activation energy of 1.87 × 105 J/mol and a pre-exponential
factor of 2.91 × 1010s−1, in agreement with the results presented in literature. However,
under flash-calciner conditions (heating rate of 1000K/s), the small variation between the
literature and the results of this thesis becomes apparent.
Montmorillonite showed a more complex kinetic behaviour, requiring a three-step mech-
anism to better capture its behaviour. The 3-step mechanism showed habits closer to expec-
tations compared to the 1-step mechanism. The three steps in montmorillonite are each best
described by third-order reaction model (F3) resulting in activation energies of 8.43 × 104 ,
5 × 105 , 2.26 × 105 J/mol and pre-exponential factors of 4.57 × 106, 2.28 × 106, 3.45 × 108
s−1, for the first step, second step and third step, respectively.
Muscovite showed similar tendencies like montmorillonite with regards to thermogravi-
metric (TG) experiments, indicating the need for a multi-step mechanism.
Third-order
reaction model (F3) also proved to be the best fit for muscovite with an activation energy
and pre-exponential factor of 6.06 × 105J/mol and 2.21 × 101s−1, respectively.
In a controlled and well defined mixture the method of constituents proved to be able
to describe artificial mixtures of phyllosilicates behaviour in TG experiments. However, that
was not the case for industrial clay samples. The samples also had a certain amount of
uncertainty attached to their composition characterisation methods.
This thesis contributes with kinetic parameters for montmorillonite and muscovite, that
otherwise have limited information, and supports the recent data in literatures for kaolinite.
The findings can help in the production of cement with phyllosilicates as SCMs consistently
achieving a high quality.

Future work
17.1
Sample mass influence on model-fitting technique
It has been discussed and shown how the Kissingers model is an appropriate way to
determine activation energy without being sensitive to the sample mass. However, model-
fitting has not been tested with respect to the mass of the sample; therefore, it would be
a favourable idea to determine how much sample mass could be used while still obtaining
kinetics that are close to the true kinetics, so experiments with 2:1 clays can be run with
the highest possible sample mass to reduce noise, while getting true kinetic parameters.
17.2
Equal data weighting
Future research should focus on having an equal sampling amount for the different
heating rates; otherwise, it will lead to the model being biased towards the experiments
with higher sampling amount when using the model-fitting method, like in this thesis. This
approach should ensure better predictions for higher heating rates, but might also make it
deviate more from lower heating rates.
17.3
Different 2:1 clay compositions
As discussed in this thesis, then 2:1 clays like montmorillonite and muscovite can have
different cations in their lattice, therefore future research should investigate if and how much
the cations impact the mass curve. This will lead to a better understanding of 2:1 clays and
the possibility for a better heating procedure to produce results usable for kinetic derivations.
This observation was also made by Drit et al. [52].
17.4
Implementation in larger models
Although having kinetics is a major step in advancing forward toward optimising cement
production, this thesis work is only on a laboratory scale. Moving forward, the kinetics should
be implemented into models, which includes heat transfer and flow behaviour (computation
fluid dynamics, CFD), this will results in much more complex simulations but will result in
behaviour close to industrial scale. Teklay et al. showed that the particle size distribution
can significantly impact how long the residence time for each particle is [21].
