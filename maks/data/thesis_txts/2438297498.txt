Abstract
This project is based on loudspeaker modeling and subjective perception of distortion
on woofers. A non-linear model of a moving-coil loudspeaker was developed in Matlab
by using the differential equation solver @ode45. The model has been verified with
real measurements. The comparisons between model results and real measurement
have shown the displacement as the variable with the best matching. The sound
pressure level and the current showed poor agreement between the model and the
measurements.
A listening test was conducted using one octave tone burst signals as stimuli, in
order to estimate the voltage thresholds at which a loudspeaker distortion becomes
audible.
Four loudspeaker systems were used for this purpose.
The method did
not show clear differences in the sound pressure level across loudspeakers systems,
when they are driving at the voltage thresholds estimated from the listening test.
The distortion patterns of the four loudspeakers were investigated to see if common
patterns or similarities are found across them. Distortion patterns, when loudspeakers
are driving at their voltage thresholds, do not show evident similarities. The third
order harmonic, HD3 was the distortion metric that better fit across the distortion
patterns.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Loudspeakers are one of the most important devices within the audio chain. Pro-
fessional and Home Audio demand high sound quality on their loudspeaker systems.
Professional audio includes sound recording, sound reinforcement systems, audio mix-
ing, music production, etc. Home audio refers to high-fidelity systems, HI-FI systems,
which are designed to play high-quality sound, but at home. In Professional Audio
and Home Audio, the loudspeakers must be able to play properly in the whole audible
frequency range. To achieve this purpose, the frequency range is split in two or three
different bandwidths, commonly called ways. Proper loudspeakers must be used for
each of the ways. Thereby, woofers are used for the low frequencies, mid-range drives
for the mid frequencies, and tweeters for the high frequencies.
1.1
Motivation
This project focuses on woofers. Woofers are loudspeakers that operates in the low
frequency range. They are normally baffle or mounted on a box, for a better perfor-
mance. Woofers have been developed for more than 50 years for Professional Audio.
In contrast with consumer audio, Professional Audio and Home Audio are the two
main areas where woofers have a key role in the overall sound quality of a loud-
speaker system. Nowadays, the automotive industry has also become an important
client for loudspeaker manufacturers. The automotive industry is demanding higher
sound quality in their cars, in order to provide a better listening experience to their
costumers.
A woofer is a non-linear system.
Its response depends on the amplitude level

2.1
The Auditory System
The human auditory system is divided into three main parts. The outer ear, the
middle ear and the inner ear.
Figure 2.1 shows a scheme of the human auditory
system. The outer ear is formed by the pinna and the external auditory canal, or
ear canal; and the ear drum or tympanic membrane. The sound comes through the
air into the ear canal reaching the tympanic membrane. The ear canal is a crooked
tube of approximately 2.5cm length, but this varies from one person to another. The
tympanic membrane has a diameter around 7.5 to 9mm and it connects the outer ear
with the middle ear. The pressure difference between the ear canal and the middle ear
causes the tympanic membrane to vibrate, and vibrations are transmitted into the
middle ear along three tiny bones called the hammer, anvil and stirrup- also known
as the malleus, incus and stapes.
These three bones constitute the middle ear,
which acts as an impedance matching between the outer and the inner ear, making
easier the transmission of the sound. The middle ear ends at the stapes, which are
connected to the cochlea through the oval window.

The oval window is the entrance to the inner ear, which includes on the semi-
circular canals and the cochlea. The semicircular canals are related to the sense of
balance and not to the sense of hearing.
The cochlea, however, is highly related

activated when the basilar membrane is excited, generating electrical pulses that are
transmitted to the brain. Higher stages in the auditory system are also important to
understand the whole behaviour of the auditory system and the perception of sound.

2.1.1
Frequency and Time Resolution
The frequency resolution of the auditory system is highly related with the properties of
the cochlea. The excitation of the basilar membrane is frequency dependent. Different
regions of the basilar membrane are excited according to the frequency content of the
incoming sound. If the incoming sound is based on one single frequency, a small region
of the basilar membrane will vibrate, and only the hair cells located on that specific
area will generate electrical signals. For instance, a 200Hz signal will excite the area
close to the apex while an 8kHz signal will excite the area near to the base. On the
other hand, a broadband signal will excite different regions of the basilar membrane.
This behaviour of the basilar membrane is modeled as an auditory filterbank. The
shape of each of the auditory filters that conform the auditory filterbank was analyzed
by Patterson(1976) by listening experiments, [3]. Figure 2.3 shows the shape of a
single auditory filter centered at 1kHz. The shape of the filter can be fitted by the
PolyFit procedure or by SLD method used by Glasberg and Moore(2000). [3]. A filter
with this shape can not be expressed with a single number, unlike the rectangular
shape filter. An alternative parameter is considered for this purpose. The Equivalent
Rectangular Bandwidth, ERB, expresses an equivalent bandwidth that contains the
same power in an equivalent rectangular band, that the power in the real band. An
equation describing the value of the ERB as a function of center frequency, F, is
defined in Glasberg and Moore (1990) as:
ERB = 24.7(4.37F + 1)
(2.1)
where the value of ERB is specified in Hz, but F is in kHz. [4].

2.1.2
The Masking Effect
The masking effect is a psychoacoustic phenomenon that occurs when two sounds are
presented but one of the sounds masks and makes the other sound inaudible. Masking
effect occurs in everyday life. Imagine chatting with a friend close to a road and a
motorbike pass by close. The sound from the motorbike will force you to talk louder if
you want your friend to understand you. This is the masking effect. The sound from
the motorbike, called as the masking signal, has masked your voice and your friend
might had not heard you. There are three types of masking, which are illustrated in
Figure 2.4:
• Simultaneous masking. It is the most common type of masking. Both sounds
are presented at the same time and one masks the other. The masking effect is
upwards in frequency. A masker signal centered at 1kHz will mask sounds that
are centered at 1.5kHz and 2kHz, more than sounds that are placed at 700Hz.
• Forward masking. A sound might get masked from a masking signal that
was present before.
This means that when a masking signal is present and
suddenly stops; masking effect remains and a second sound coming right after
==PAGE:23==

the masking signal stopped, could still get masked. This effect can last more
than 100ms.
• Backward masking. A sound that reaches the ear might get masked from a
second sound that reaches the ear later than the first sound. The time of the
backward masking comparing with the forward masking is short, around 20ms.
Masking effect is used in psychoacoustic experiments to estimate the auditory
filter bank on the basilar membrane and other properties of the auditory system.
The masking effect increases upwards in frequency as the level of the masking signal
rises up.

2.3.3
Perception of distortion
The distortion metrics, such as the HD and the IMD, are objective parameters that
try to characterize the loudspeaker response in the non-linear domain. The second
and the third order harmonic, HD2 and HD3, as well as the total harmonic distortion,
THD, are typically used for this purpose. These objective parameters might not be
correlated with subjective perception of distortion. Musical instruments such as the
electrical guitar, are played through an amplifier that add a lot of distortion to the

• Asymmetric non-linearity: It adds lots of even order harmonics to the output

2.4.1
Moving-Coil Loudspeakers
A moving-coil loudspeaker is an electro-mechanical transducer. It converts an electri-
cal signal into an acoustical signal. The electrical signal is converted into a mechanical
force which causes the loudspeaker’s diaphragm to move and radiate sound. Figure
2.10 shows the cross section of a typical moving-coil driver and its main components.

is immersed in a magnetic field generated by the magnet and the pole pieces. The
dust cap covers the central part of the diaphragm and prevent dust from entering the
driver.
The voice coil is placed in an air gap where the magnetic field is constant and
crosses the voice coil at right angle. If a current is applied to the voice coil, the
current flows perpendicular to the magnetic field and a mechanical force is produced.

2.4.3
Non-linearities of a Loudspeaker
The intrinsic non-linearities of a loudspeaker will produce distortion at the acoustical
output when the loudspeaker is operated in the large signal domain. This section
introduces the most important nonlinear parameters of a moving coil loudspeaker
with special focus on the Bl factor.
The three dominant nonlinear parameters of a moving coil loudspeaker are the
force factor, Bl, the compliance Cms and the inductance Le. These parameters are
dependent on the position of the moving coil. In the small signal domain, low am-
plitude signals are applied into the voice coil which experiences small displacements
around its rest position (commonly at x = 0). Bl, Cms and Le can be assumed as
constants. However, when the loudspeaker is driven with high amplitude signals, the
large signal domain, the voice coil moves far away from its rest position and the non-
linear parameters become highly dependent on the voice-coil position, Bl(x), Cms(x),
Le(x). Non-linear parameters will have more impact on low frequencies as they cause
larger displacements on the diaphragm as it was seen in the previous section.

to its surround. A voice coil surrounded by air offers lower inductance than a voice
coil surrounded by an iron. This is because the permeability in the iron is higher than
in the air. So that, Le(x) will be higher when the diaphragm moves inwards than
when it does outwards. The permeability is bigger inside the motor than outside in
the air.
The main parameters, causes and symptoms of the three main non-linearities are
well further explained by W.Klippel on his paper, [13].

Forward Euler

step and the next time step.

The Forward Euler Method only uses a single step between the actual

Midpoint Method, where an intermediate step is used in between the actual time and
the future time step. The Forward Euler Midpoint Method improves the accuracy
respect to the standard Forward Euler Method. Nevertheless, the Runge Kutta model
still offers more accuracy and less error and it was the solver used in this project.
The model was implemented using the Matlab function @ode45. This function is a
differential equation solver 4th order, based on an explicit Runge-Kutta (4,5) formula,
the Dormand-Prince pair. [16].

Non-linear parameters presented in this section were obtained for Spk81 and Spk86
in free air. This is not a very common situation for woofers, as they are normally
baffled or mounted in a box.
A woofer driving in free air, do not radiate sound
properly, particularly at low frequencies. A woofer in free air can be modeled as a
dipole source. Two spherical waves are generated from the dipole, a forwards wave
and a backwards wave. The two waves travel in opposite directions; they have a 180
degrees phase difference. In this way, destructive interference will appear in equal
distance points to the source, generating the undesirable response known as a comb
filter. This problem can be solved by baffling the driver or mounting the driver in
a box. These solutions allow to isolate the backward wave from the forward wave;
removing the destructive interference.

CHAPTER 4
Non-Linear Model
Validation
This chapter presents the results obtained by the loudspeaker non-linear model. The
model was executed with the loudspeaker in free air and mounted on a box. The
outcomes from the model are compared with real measurements in order to validate
the efficiency and accuracy of the model. The displacement of the diaphragm, the
current through the voice coil and the sound pressure level are the magnitudes used;
they are analyzed in the time domain and in the frequency domain.
4.1
Overview
The non-linear model is based on the differential equations that define the behaviour of
the loudspeaker. These equations assume the main non-linearities of the loudspeaker.
To solve these equations, as the non-linear model does, the mechanical and electrical
parameters of the speaker must be known in advance.
They can be obtained in
different ways. Some of the linear Thiele and Small parameters can be extracted
by simply measuring the electrical impedance of the loudspeaker. The added mass
method has been used for so long to calculate the moving mass of the loudspeaker.
Nowadays, the use of a laser is preferred to calculate the moving mass and the force
factor, as it permits the speaker to totally perform as it is, free of any added mass.
Furthermore, the series power coefficients for the polynomials that represents the
main non-linearities of the loudspeaker must be estimated to input them into the
model. There is no clear alternative to Klippel System for obtaining the series power
coefficients for Bl(x), CMS(x) and LE(x).
The loudspeaker parameters used in the model during this project are available
on Appendix B and C.

CHAPTER 5
Listening Test
To characterize subjectively the distortion of a loudspeaker system, a listening test
with human listeners must be conducted. This chapter presents the listening test
performed in this project. The goal of the listening test is to determine the voltage
thresholds, from which the distortion generated by the loudspeaker becomes audible.
Two different approaches were initially considered as potential alternatives for the
listening test. The pros and cons of both alternatives are discussed here. The results
and an analysis of the listening test are included at the end of the chapter.
5.1
Different approaches. Recordings & Live set-up
Two different approaches were considered for the listening test: Recordings and Live
Set-Up. By Recordings different samples of the loudspeaker system driven at different
voltage levels are recorded to use a posteriori in a listening test. The recordings are
presented to the test listeners over headphones. With this approach, the test subjects
hear between some different recorded samples that the test conducter has previously
selected, according to the listening test’s target. By a Live Set-up the test subject
is present in a room with the loudspeaker system and he/she is asked to adjust the
level of the power amplifier until distortion from the loudspeaker is perceived.

Analyzing frequencies in the lowest area of the audible range is a handicap. For

• The recording and playback system, as the headphones, will modify the signal

and the differences in the seal will produce different headphones playback. The

• The recording method involves a noticeable extra workload due to the record-

only requires to set the proper maximum voltage levels of the amplifier as well
as the maximum SPL at listener positions.
• Having samples from recordings allows to implement efficient listening tests
as several questions might be asked to the test subjects: comparison between
sounds with and without distortion, comparison between sounds with distortion
from the same or from a different loudspeakers, etc. Besides, these questions
can be made in a way that the test listener is not involved with engineering
concepts, which sometimes are hard to deal with. Instead, he/she might get
asked: Which of these three sounds is different? or Which of these three samples
sounds better to you?. The Live Set Up requires a training for the listeners to
get familiar with the sound of the stimuli and the method, and to recognize how
distortion sounds.
The Recording approach was checked and several recordings were conducted in the
small anechoic chamber at DTU. The goal was to check if the recordings would be able
to give the same impression of distortion as being physically in front of the loudspeaker.
The recordings were playback over two different headphones systems. They sound
different as their frequency response and the coloration added by the headphones
were different. None of the headphones gives the same listening experience as being
physically in front of the loudspeaker. The Live Set Up was briefly tested by two
people and their sensations were more favourable. By turning up and down the level
of the amplifier the loudspeaker gives an straight forward sound and listeners seem
to be more comfortable with this method. The Live Set Up was the method used to

The crest factor, in dB, is defined as the level difference between the peak value and
the RMS value of a given signal. The three signals have the same peak value, 1V.
However, the RMS value is different as the signals do not have the same length. For
longer signals, the RMS is larger and therefore, the crest factor is smaller. For shorter
signals (higher central frequencies of the tone burst) smaller rms values result in a
higher crest factor.
5.2.4
Method
The four loudspeaker systems were tested at three different frequencies, 32Hz, 63Hz
and 125Hz. These frequencies were chosen in order to cover frequencies below, close
and above the resonance frequency, fs. Two different ways for rating the distortion
on the loudspeaker were used:
• From the maximum level and going down. The stimuli is presented with
the power amplifier at its maximum output level. The test subject is asked
to slowly turn down the volume control of the amplifier, until the distortion
disappears from the sound.
• From zero and going up. The stimuli is presented with the power amplifier
at its minimum output level. The test subject is asked this case to carefully
turn up the volume control until distortion is perceived.
Four repetitions were conducted for each of the three frequencies. Two repetitions
were from the maximum level and going down whereas the other two repetitions were
from zero and going up. From a pilot test it was seen that starting at 125Hz and from
the maximum level and going down, it made easier the performance for the listener.
As the sound generated from one octave tone burst signal centered at 125Hz is louder
than for the 32Hz or 63Hz; 125Hz was set as the first frequency to be tested. Similarly,
starting from the maximum voltage on the amplifier and going down, makes easier
to the listener to establish the first of the four voltage thresholds estimated at each
frequency.
It was not possible to record the voltage and the current on the loudspeaker
while the test subject was moving the volume control of the amplifier for rating the
distortion. For this reason, when the test listener had set the level on the amplifier,
he/she was asked to do not move the volume control. Thus, the stimuli was applied
again into the loudspeaker but in a shorter duration version, with only four tone bursts.
In this way, the voltage and the current through the loudspeaker were measured at
the same conditions as the test listener set.

Discussion on the results
The standard deviation, particularly at 32Hz, shows values about 5dB on the voltage
thresholds. Several reasons might explain this big standard deviation.
The background noise in the IEC Listening Room was significant at low frequencies.
The air conditioner system was switched off to minimize the background noise, but
the sound pressure level measured was still around 58dB SPL, at 20Hz. This causes
a signal noise ratio, SNR, close to 0dB, between the pure tone signal at 32Hz and the
background noise. Therefore, measurements at 32Hz are not really realistic. At 63Hz
and 125Hz, the SNR between the signal and the background noise was higher than
20dB, and therefore, results are more consistent. Background noise also affected the
performance of the listener test. Some of the test subjects shared the same impression
as it was not easy to detect the distortion at 32Hz. For one particular listener, it
was an ardours task to even heard the stimuli at very low levels. The loudspeaker
was exceptionally moved closer for this listener, at 32Hz, to let him to end up with
the test. At 63Hz and 125Hz, listeners were more comfortable as the stimuli was
easier to heard. Another reason for a big standard deviation might be that the test
listeners were not standing in a fixed position. They did not have their heads at the
exactly same position. They were sit in the same place in the room, but they were
free to move their heads and therefore, they were not placed at the same position and
distance from the loudspeaker.
These two effects might be corrected, placing the loudspeaker closer to the listener
test, improving the SNR. Another noise source was the computer running the Matlab
script inside the room. It was desired to keep the computer outside the room, but it
was not possible.
According SPL thresholds, all loudspeaker systems produce similar sound pressure
levels at their voltage thresholds, showing no clear differences each other. The second
and third order harmonic distortion, HD2 and HD3, of the sound pressure level and
the current, were calculated for the four loudspeaker systems driving at their voltage

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
It has been seen that results from a non-linear model do not always agree with real
measurements. This might be expected, as the model does not include all the non-
linearities that are physically present in a loudspeaker.
The three assumed main
non-linearities, Bl(x), CMS, LE, might dominates the non-linear character of a loud-
speaker system, but other factors, such as the dependency of the suspension on the
frequency, creep factor, or the heating of the voice coil, are not considered in the
model. Thus, the model does not estimate accurate values for the fundamental and
the harmonics.
However, if differences in the values of the fundamental and the
harmonics, between the model and the measurements, are equivalent, the distortion
metrics might still fit well. In this project, an analysis in the frequency domain is
conduct. The second order harmonic distortion, HD2, and the third order harmonic
distortion, HD3, are usde to compare the non-linear model performance with real
measurements. In general, comparisons show better matching for HD3 than for HD2.
There is a strong matching at frequencies above the resonance frequency of the
system, fs. The frequencies above fs were, 63Hz, for woofers in free air, and 125Hz
for woofers mounted on a box. At other frequencies, the matching was not so obvious,
and similarities appeared at isolated cases. The electrical impedance at frequencies
above the resonance frequency is dominated by the mechanical mass, MMS.
At
frequencies below fs, the electrical impedance is dominated by the mechanical com-
pliance, CMS. MMS is a constant parameter but CMS presents non-linearities, and it
changes as a function of the voice coil position. The good matching at high frequen-
cies between the model and the real measurements, indicates that the model perform
good at frequencies above the resonance frequency, where MMS dominates. On the
other hand, the poor agreement showed at frequencies near to or below fs, shows an
incorrect performance of the model at low frequencies, where CMS dominates. Both
MMS and CMS are estimated by Klippel System and they are used as inputs in the
non-linear model. The estimation of CMS in the non-linear domain is more sensitive
to errors than the calculation of the MMS, in the linear domain. In this way, if CMS
is not well estimated, the model will perform with erroneous inputs and the results
will be incorrect, specially at frequencies below the resonance frequency. The large
signal parameters obtained for the woofers mounted on 11 liters and 22 liters boxes
that were presented in the report, showed a reduced displacement range, Xprot, and
a bit weird shapes for the polynomials. A revision in the calculation of the large

signal parameters with Klippel System must be done, with the woofers mounted on
the two different volume boxes, to make sure that the main non-linearities are well
represented by the calculated 4th order polynomials. Klippel’s module LSI Woofer
+ Box can provide better estimations of the polynomials than Klippel’s module used
along this project, LSI Woofer Driver.
The state variable that shows the best comparison between the results of the
model and measurement is the displacement. The sound pressure level is strongly
affected from the acoustics of the room. The current through the voice coil fits good
for specific cases but it shows big dissimilarities for others. The displacement shows
the best agreement between the model and measurements, both for HD2 and HD3.
In this project, the displacement was only tested with the woofers in free air. Further
validations should be done by measuring the displacement with the woofers mounted
on a box. This test would help to understand if the displacement is a proper variable
for validating a loudspeaker model.
The loudspeaker model presents some limitations. As the model does not include
all the physical non-linearities present in a real loudspeaker; the results from the
model will not be the same as in real measurements. This is an intrinsic issue when
modeling physical systems. Models are commonly reliable within a certain area, or
limited range. Beyond these limits, the model will not predict correct results. The
loudspeaker model developed in this project includes the main non-linearities of a
loudspeaker, such as the Bl factor, the mechanical compliance CMS, and the induc-
tance of the voice coil, LE. The model does not estimate accurate results for the
fundamental and the harmonics, assuming the real measurements as the correct val-
ues. However, some distortion metrics, such as HD2 and HD3, can show comparable
results between the model and the measurements. Through this project, the third
order harmonic, HD3, showed better correlation than HD2. Higher order harmon-
ics, or different distortion metrics such as the total harmonic distortion, THD, or
the intermodulation distortion, IMD, should be analyzed, as they could be useful
to validate and check the limitations of the model. Another limitation of the model
might be the importance of a correct estimation of the 4th order polynomials, that
represents the main non-linearities of a loudspeaker. Polynomials are used as inputs
in the model and therefore, the performance of the model is totally dependent on
these 4th order polynomials.
A listening test with the Live Set-Up configuration and tone burst signal as stimuli
provides a straight forward method to assess distortion in loudspeaker systems. 12
non trained listeners participated in the listening test. Some deviations are observed

other factors might explained these deviations. The background noise was an impor-
tant factor that added extra difficulties to the performance of the test. At very low
frequencies, the signal to noise ratio, SNR, was very poor due to the air conditioning
system, which presented a noise about 57 dB SPL at 20Hz. The computer inside

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The first of the two research objectives of this project was to implement a non-linear
model as a potential tool for loudspeaker design. The main non-linearities of the
loudspeaker were considered as well as the eddy currents and the current dependency
of the inductance. The Matlab function @ode45, a differential equation solver based
on Runge-Kutta methods, was used to implement the model. A validation was con-
ducted by comparing the model with real measurements, in two different situations:
woofers in free air and woofers mounted on a box.
The comparisons have shown
strong matching for the displacement of the diaphragm, specially for the third order
harmonic, HD3. The measured sound pressure level has not fit well with model pre-
dictions, as the acoustics of the room were not considered in the non-linear model.
The current through the voice coil fits well between the model and measurements but
only at some particular conditions. The displacement was the variable that showed
the best agreement between model predictions and real measurements. However, in
this project, the displacement was only measured with the woofers in free air. A
further validations should be conducted on the model, by mean of the displacement,
with the woofers mounted on a closed box. If the real displacement fits well with the
model when woofers are mounted on a box, the displacement would become a useful
state variable for validating a non-linear model.
The second of the research objectives was to investigate the perception of distor-
tion on the woofers. A listening test using tone burst signals was conducted, in order
to determine the voltage thresholds levels, at which distortion becomes audible. Four
loudspeaker systems were used for this purpose. The Live-Set Up method is a straight
forward method to assess the distortion on loudspeakers. The background noise is
a determinant factor when judging distortion at very low frequencies. The sound
pressure level, generated by the loudspeakers at their voltage thresholds, has shown
similar values for all the loudspeakers. In this way, the Live Set Up method might
not be a good method to detect deviations in the sound pressure level, for different
loudspeakers at their voltage thresholds. However, only two woofers were tested in
two different volume boxes and more loudspeakers should be tested for drawing a
stronger conclusion. The distortion patterns were also compared across loudspeaker
systems. In general, distortion patterns present dissimilarities among loudspeakers.
The third order harmonic, HD3, showed more comparable results than the second
order harmonic HD2. Further investigation in the distortion patterns should be done

7.1
Future Work
The non-linear model developed in this project should be further analyzed, and it
might get verified with other distortion metrics, such as higher order harmonics, or
the THD or IMD. The ratio between the HD2 and the HD3 might also provide
some clues across different distortion patterns.
Further analysis in the non-linear
model could be also done by using synchronized swept-sines as input signals. By
mean of this signal, the called Higher Harmonic Frequency Responses, [18], can be
obtained and the impulse response of each of the harmonics can be detected, [19].
This would help to analyze a non-liner system as a loudspeaker, from a different per-
spective, in the time domain. The synchronized swept-sine can be also used to verify
the accuracy of the model by mean of comparing with real measurements.
An effort must be done to include other factors that characterize physically the
loudspeakers behaviour, such as the viscoelasticity of the suspension of the loud-
speaker. Furthermore, other possibilities for solving the state-space model are avail-
able, such as the Grunwald-Letnikov method, which is based on fractional derivatives,
[20].
Other possibilities for a listening test can be tried out. Different type of signals
might be employed as stimuli. The use of tone burst signals demands a prior training
to allow the listeners to get familiarized with the sound, and its distortion. By using
musical signals as stimuli, the listeners might not demand a training, and they would
feel more conformable than with the tone burst signals. However, distortion may
be harder to be detected for a musical signal. A musical signal has wider frequency
content comparing with one octave tone burst signals. For this reason, the masking
effect would be higher for a musical signal than for a tone burst signal. The listening
test over headphones still remains as an alternative for judging distortion of a loud-
speaker. In this case, the headphones and the audio equipment involved, must be
carefully considered to avoid any influence in the final results.

The tone burst signal used as the stimuli for the listening test, was first considered to
measure the sound pressure levels. The tone burst signal was analyzed at the voltage
threshold levels, for the four loudspeaker systems. At threshold levels, the current
and the voltage spectrums show no harmonics, and therefore no distortion. Figure
D.1 shows the voltage and current spectrum for a one octave bandwidth tone burst
signal, centered at 32Hz, at 14dBV, which was the threshold level estimated for the
Spk86 mounted on 22liters box, at 32Hz.
Figure D.1: Voltage and current spectrum for a tone burst signal with a voltage level
equal to the mean voltage threshold level, for Spk86 mounted on 22liters, at 32Hz.
The tone burst signal used as stimuli was extremely short in the time domain to
be properly analyzed in the frequency domain. There is no distortion in the spectrum.
Thus, it was agreed to use longer signals. A pure tone signals centered at 32Hz, 63Hz
and 125Hz, were used to measure the sound pressure levels, as an alternative signal
for the tone burst. The pure tone signals were playback for 30 seconds across the
four loudspeaker systems. 9 different voltages were used, covering the linear and non-
linear domain of the loudspeaker. The voltage and the current on the speaker, and
the sound pressure level were recorded. The spectrum of the pure tone signals show
