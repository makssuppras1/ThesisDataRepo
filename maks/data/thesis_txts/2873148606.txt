Abstract 
Background: Obesity is a rising global health concern that not only increases the risk of chronic 
diseases but also worsens outcomes during viral infections like influenza. Although the respiratory 
complications of influenza A virus are well-studied, its potential impact on cardiac health, 
especially in the context of obesity, has not been extensively studied. This study investigates the 
effect of IAV infection on cardiac gene expression and histological changes in lean and obese pigs 
in order to understand the link between viral infection and obesity.  
Materials and methods: The study was done in male Gottingen minipigs, which were divided 
into four different groups: Lean-Mock, Lean-Flu, Fat-Mock, and Fat-Flu. Heart tissue samples 
were collected and analyzed using microfluidic qPCR to study the expression of genes in different 
treatment groups. Moreover, a histological examination using hematoxylin and eosin staining was 
performed to evaluate structural alterations in the heart tissue between the groups. 
Result: The result demonstrated that expression of genes was altered in different treatment groups.  
It was studied that the genes such as SERPINE1, HSP70.2, ICAM1, and TNFRSF6B were 
significantly upregulated in the obese pigs following influenza infection, indicating that viral 
infection causes prolonged inflammatory responses by weakening the immune system. The Fat-
Flu group showed the most pronounced gene expression changes during IAV infection, indicating 
that obesity leads to worse conditions in heart. This was further supported by histological analysis, 
which confirmed more visible changes in heart tissue in the Fat-Flu group compared to others. 
Additionally, it was also observed that fibrotic, adhesion molecules, and muscle/contractile genes 
were differentially regulated in the heart, indicating a metabolic condition when a pig fed a high-
fat diet. This indicates that obesity causes an altered expression of cardiac genes in the heart. 
However, it was also studied that during IAV infection in lean pigs, showed few gene expressions, 
which indicate the inflammatory response remains well-regulated in the heart. 
Overall, this study demonstrates that obesity enhances the adverse cardiac effects during influenza 
infection at both the molecular and tissue levels. These findings highlight the importance of 
considering obesity as a risk factor not only for respiratory disease but also for cardiac health. 
Moreover, the results support the relevance of the swine model for studying how metabolic 
conditions like obesity influence the body’s response to infectious disease.

Introduction 
Influenza, commonly known as the flu, is one of the most widespread and serious viral illnesses 
across the world, resulting in major seasonal morbidity and mortality each year (Moghadami, 
2017). “According to the World Health Organization (WHO), in 2025,” influenza causes an 
estimated 290,000 to 650,000 deaths each year and affects 3 to 5 million people globally (Infleunza 
(Seasonal), 2025). The influenza A virus (IAV) can infect people of all ages and is responsible for 
yearly epidemics and sporadic pandemics. However, some groups are more vulnerable to serious 
illnesses than others, such as children, the elderly, people with underlying medical conditions, and 
those who are extremely obese (Treanor, 2023).  
Obesity is considered a growing global health concern diseases not only raise the risk of chronic 
diseases, but it also increases vulnerability to infectious diseases like influenza (Darku MD MLS, 
2024; Khanna et al., 2022; Magdy Beshbishy et al., 2020; Obesity and Overweight, 2025) . Obesity 
related chronic low-grade inflammation impairs immune system function and increases 
susceptibility to severe flu infection (Honce & Schultz-Cherry, 2019). The body's capacity to 
effectively respond to viral infections is weakened by this immune impairment, which is 
characterized by impaired interferon responses, disturbed cytokine signaling, decreased leukocyte 
recruitment, and delayed antigen-specific activation. (Hulme et al., 2021). As a result, obese 
individuals experience longer viral shedding, a slower rate of recovery, and a higher risk of 
complications from influenza (Honce & Schultz-Cherry, 2019; Treanor, 2023). This was made 
evident during the COVID-19 pandemic and the 2009 H1N1 "swine flu" pandemic, when people 
who were obese were much more likely to experience serious illness, hospitalization, and 
complications, including cardiovascular-related ones (Kwong et al., 2011). 
Even though influenza is most commonly associated with respiratory symptoms, there is mounting 
evidence that it can also result in serious cardiovascular problems. The virus's association with 
adverse cardiac events has been highlighted by epidemiological studies that show an increase in 
hospital admissions for acute myocardial infarction, myocarditis, and heart failure during peak 
influenza seasons.(Warren-Gash et al., 2009). These risks are increased in obese people, who are 
also more vulnerable to related cardiovascular problems and severe influenza infections (Karlsson 
& Beck, 2010). 
==PAGE:13==
2 
 
The interaction between obesity and influenza poses a critical challenge to public health and 
clinical care. Even though there is mounting evidence that influenza infections increase the risk of 
cardiovascular disease (Skaarup et al., 2023), it is still unclear what molecular processes lead to 
these worsened outcomes, particularly when obesity is involved. Cardiac tissues are extremely 
vulnerable to viral stressors and systemic inflammatory mediators. There is a significant 
knowledge gap, though, as little research has looked at how obesity affects the heart's reaction to 
influenza (Siegers et al., 2020). Addressing this gap is important to develop more effective 
strategies for protecting vulnerable populations from both the respiratory and cardiovascular 
impacts of influenza. 
A lot of research has been done on how the respiratory system reacts to viruses and how obesity 
affects that. However, not much research has been done on how viruses affect cardiac gene 
expression, especially in people who are overweight (Gutiérrez-Spillari et al., 2020; Honce & 
Schultz-Cherry, 2019). The purpose of this thesis is to fill this knowledge gap by examining 
whether IAV infection changes the expression of cardiac genes, which could result in pathological 
heart conditions. An excellent large animal model that accurately captures obesity and influenza 
virus infections, along with related comorbidities, will be used to study this. This framework led 
to the establishment of the following hypotheses: 
Obesity exacerbates the impact of Influenza A Virus infection on cardiac gene expression by 
amplifying pro-inflammatory and stress response genes. 
Obesity amplifies the histopathological changes in cardiac tissue induced by influenza A virus 
infection, which may be associated with an increased risk of cardiovascular complications. 
Influenza A Virus infection results in the dysregulation of genes involved in cardiac remodeling 
and fibrosis, with greater changes observed in obese pigs.

1. Background 
1.1 Obesity 
Obesity is one of the most common noncommunicable disorders that pose significant public health 
problems(Khanna et al., 2022). It is a growing global health concern, affecting over 650 million 
adults worldwide(Magdy Beshbishy et al., 2020) It is defined as the accumulation of abnormal or 
excessive fat, which interferes with maintaining an ideal state of health (Ellulu et al., 2017). The 
most common causes of obesity are heredity and diet. A high-fat, high-protein, calorie-dense diet 
that is mostly composed of processed, sugary, and carbohydrate-rich foods with a noticeable 
deficiency in vitamins, minerals, and fiber has been linked to global urbanization. In addition, 
changes in body composition, insulin and glucose levels, metabolic systems, and immune system 
dysregulation, long-term use of this diet can lead to weight gain and an elevated BMI. Furthermore, 
the significant inter-individual variance in body weight that influences how people react to the so-
called "obesogenic" environment, i.e. an environment that encourages weight gain and has a major 
genetic component(Hulme et al., 2021). 
1.1.1 Obesity-induced Inflammation and Immune Dysregulation 
Obesity is a condition characterized by excessive fat accumulation and a chronic inflammatory stat 
that substantially disrupts normal immune systems. It promotes the infiltration and activation of 
immune cells within adipose tissue, where these immune responses dysregulated, contributing to 
systemic inflammation and metabolic disturbances(Kawai et al., 2021). When adipose tissue, 
particularly visceral fat, becomes metabolically active, it secretes various pro-inflammatory 
cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), interleukin-6 (IL-6), and monocyte 
chemoattractant protein-1 (MCP-1), as shown in Figure 1. These chemicals aid in the formation of 
chronic, low-grade systemic inflammation that hinders the effective operation of the immune 
system. In obese people, chronic inflammation results in multiple levels of immune dysfunction, 
confirming that immunological dysfunction involves compromised responses from T cells, B cells, 
and macrophages. Because of these changes, immunological surveillance and defense systems are 
compromised, increasing the risk of infection and decreasing the effectiveness of immunizations 
in obese people (Alarcon et al., 2021; Kawai et al., 2021; Magdy Beshbishy et al., 2020).  
==PAGE:15==
4 
 
Furthermore, the weakened immune response results in "immunosenescence," a condition in which 
the body's capacity to fight against infections is reduced. This leads to increased viral shedding, 
longer infection duration, and worsening illness. These immunological dysfunctions result in 
increased hospitalization and death rates in obese persons when viral infections like influenza and 
COVID-19 are present(Honce & Schultz-Cherry, 2019; Mal et al., 2022)

1.2 Obesity and Cardiovascular Health  
Obesity significantly increases the risk of cardiovascular disease (CVD), which continues to be 
one of the world's major causes of morbidity and death (Powell-Wiley et al., 2021). The persistent 
inflammatory environment associated with obesity contributes to endothelial dysfunction, a key 
early event in the development of atherosclerosis. This dysfunction is accelerated by elevated 
==PAGE:16==
5 
 
levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines, which impair nitric oxide synthesis and, in turn, increase 
arterial stiffness and vascular resistance (Magdy Beshbishy et al., 2020). In addition to vascular 
effects, obesity significantly impacts cardiac structure and function. Obese people have higher 
metabolic and circulatory needs, which raise cardiac output and blood volume. These 
hemodynamic changes place sustained pressure on the heart, contributing to left ventricular 
hypertrophy (LVH) and greater stress on the myocardial wall (Alpert et al., 2016). This cardiac 
remodeling is often accompanied by interstitial and perivascular fibrosis, driven by profibrotic and 
inflammatory mediators such collagen genes (e.g., COL1A1, COL3A1), matrix metalloproteinases 
(MMPs), and transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β1) (Boudina & Abel, 2007; Van Gaal et al., 
2006). Moreover, metabolic reprogramming in obese hearts favors fatty acid oxidation over 
glucose use. This change can lead to contractile dysfunction and heart failure because it raises the 
formation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) in mitochondria, encourages oxidative stress, and 
affects myocardial energetics (Boudina & Abel, 2007) .  
Furthermore, adipokines like resistin and leptin worsen vascular inflammation and sympathetic 
nervous system activation, making people more susceptible to arrhythmias and hypertension 
(Poirier et al., 2006). Studies also shows, in obese people, influenza vaccination has demonstrated 
cardioprotective benefits, lowering mortality and acute coronary syndromes. However, in obese 
populations, the effectiveness of standard-dose influenza vaccinations may be limited due to 
compromised immune responses, requiring adjuvanted vaccines or higher-dose formulations to 
improve immunogenicity(Gutiérrez-Spillari et al., 2020). 
1.2.1 Impact of Obesity on Cardiac Structure and Function  
Numerous cardiovascular diseases have been linked with obesity, which has a significant and 
complex effect on the structure and function of the heart. It causes a persistent condition of 
hemodynamic overload by increasing cardiac output and blood volume, which causes left 
ventricular hypertrophy (LVH) and chamber dilation. The heart has to work harder to meet the 
metabolic needs of excess adipose tissue, which results in this structural adaptation(Wong et al., 
2004). LVH refers to the thickening of the ventricular walls and is a common result of both pressure 
and volume overload, eventually decreasing the heart's function (Wong et al., 2004). Over time, 
these changes may progress to systolic dysfunction (impaired ability of the heart to contract) or 
==PAGE:17==
6 
 
more commonly, diastolic dysfunction, where the heart has difficulty relaxing and filling properly 
during diastole (Nagueh et al., 2008). 
Obese people are more likely to experience diastolic dysfunction due to interstitial fibrosis-induced 
cardiac stiffness and decreased relaxation, which can result in heart failure with preserved ejection 
fraction (HFpEF) (Carbone et al., 2017). A type of heart failure occurs when the heart's ejection 
fraction, or the proportion of blood that is expelled from the ventricle with each beat, stays normal 
(≥50%), but heart failure symptoms are present because of poor ventricular filling and high left 
atrial pressures (Paulus & Tschöpe, 2013). This is made worse by related comorbidities that 
aggravate myocardial remodeling, including insulin resistance, systemic hypertension, obstructive 
sleep apnea, and activation of the sympathetic nervous system and renin-angiotensin-aldosterone 
system (RAAS) (Powell-Wiley et al., 2021).Notably, obesity alone is associated with increased 
left atrial size, increased Left Ventricular (LV) mass, and changed myocardial strain patterns even 
in the absence of hypertension or diabetes, confirming the theory that adiposity has a direct 
cardiotoxic effect(Peterson et al., 2004; Wong et al., 2004). 
These cardiac changes are a major public health concern, especially considering the increasing 
incidence of obesity worldwide. To reduce long-term cardiovascular risk in this population, early 
detection and focused intervention are crucial, especially for blood pressure management, weight 
loss, and the treatment of related metabolic diseases (Khan et al., 2018). 
1.3 Obesity as a Risk Factor for Viral Infections 
Obesity has been identified as a key factor in determining the severity of infectious diseases, as 
evidenced by the 2009 H1N1 influenza pandemic and the current COVID-19 outbreak. People 
with a body mass index (BMI) of ≥30 kg/m² are considerably more likely to be hospitalized, 
admitted to critical care, and die from viral infections, according to several epidemiological studies 
(Honce & Schultz-Cherry, 2019; Mal et al., 2022). This study is further supported by research 
showing that obesity significantly exacerbates the impact of infectious diseases due to its effects 
on immune function and inflammation (Zhao et al., 2020).  
Obese individuals commonly exhibit a persistent pro-inflammatory state characterized by the 
expansion of hypertrophic and hypoxic adipose tissue, which actively secretes inflammatory 
==PAGE:18==
7 
 
mediators. These mediators interfere with normal immune regulation, leading to reduced 
effectiveness of key immune cells, such as macrophages and lymphocytes. This immune 
dysfunction weakens both innate and adaptive immune responses, increasing the host’s 
vulnerability to infections such as influenza and COVID-19 (Kawai et al., 2021; Magdy Beshbishy 
et al., 2020). Additionally, obesity has also been linked to lower vaccination effectiveness, a higher 
risk of severe disease, and clinical care issues such as imaging, surgery, and patient mobility. 
Chronic inflammation caused by excess adiposity may potentially promote viral persistence and 
mutation, increase the possibility of more virulent viral types, and facilitate transmission across 
populations. Overall, obesity increases the risk of infection while also contributing to more severe 
disease progression and poorer clinical outcomes.(Honce & Schultz-Cherry, 2019; Magdy 
Beshbishy et al., 2020) 
1.4 Influenza 
The Influenza virus belongs to the Orthomyxoviridae family, which includes IAV, Influenza B 
virus (IBV), Influenza C virus (ICV), and Influenza D virus (IDV), as well as Thogovirus and 
Isavirus (infectious salmon anemia virus). Among these, the three influenza virus genera (IAV, 
IBV, and ICV) are characterized by segmented, negative-sense single-strand RNA (ssRNA) 
genomes, meaning that their viral RNA (vRNA) is non-coding and must be transcribed to positive-
sense coding ssRNA upon infection(Taubenberger & Kash, 2010a).  
IAV and IBV share a similar structure, but ICV stands out due to its formation of long cord-like 
structures on infected cell surfaces and its single major surface glycoprotein, the hemagglutinin-
esterase-fusion (HEF) protein, as opposed to the HA and NA of IAV and IBV(Bouvier & Palese, 
2008). Both IAV and IBV are accountable for the seasonal influenza epidemics in humans, which 
occur annually due to the antigenic drift in influenza viruses. These viruses infect the epithelial 
cells of the upper respiratory tract and are the predominant cause of respiratory infections in 
humans. Fever, muscle aches, sore throat, coughing, and headaches are the common symptoms, 
particularly affecting children, the elderly, or individuals with a compromised immune system. 
Such groups are more vulnerable to secondary infections, including viral or bacterial 
pneumonia(Julkunen et al., 2000). 
==PAGE:19==
8 
 
Based on host species, IAV can infect a wide variety of hosts, including humans and animals, 
causing respiratory infections. In contrast, ICV primarily causes mild upper respiratory symptoms 
in humans, especially among children, and has additionally been detected in pigs. Meanwhile, IDV 
is known to infect a broader range of hosts, including cattle, ruminants, pigs, and occasionally 
humans, causing respiratory illness (Su et al., 2017). Similarly, IBV, like IAV, can also cause 
respiratory disease in humans. However, the main difference between IBV and IAV lies in their 
host specificity and infective capacity. Humans represent the natural host for IBV, whereas aquatic 
birds serve as the primary reservoir for IAV(S. S. H. Huang et al., 2014) . However, IAV is 
recognized as a significant pathogen with a substantial zoonotic infection, with its ability to adapt 
and shift hosts has repeatedly triggered global pandemics (Iuliano et al., 2018). 
1.4.1 Influenza virus 
Influenza A virus is a highly significant pathogen with notable zoonotic potential, frequently 
infecting various hosts including birds, swine, equine, and humans. Its rapid ability to cross species 
barriers, also known as "host-switching," poses a significant risk for generating pandemics, 
particularly when novel strains emerge to which the human population lacks immunity (Roberts 
& Krilov, 2022).The classic example is the 2009 H1N1 influenza pandemic, originating from 
swine; it quickly adapted genetically, enabling efficient human-to-human transmission and 
resulting in widespread global infection. Transmission of IAV primarily occurs through respiratory 
droplets, with seasonal epidemics typically driven by antigenic drift, involving minor genetic 
changes. However, pandemics usually arise through antigenic shifts—substantial genetic 
reassortments that introduce novel viral strains with distinct antigenicity(Dhanasekaran et al., 
2022). Moreover, pandemics caused by IAV are characterized by increased geographic dispersion, 
higher infection rates, and increased severity, notably affecting the younger population compared 
to typical seasonal influenza(Neumann & Kawaoka, 2015; Roberts & Krilov, 2022). 
IAV is an enveloped virus characterized by a complex structure that is crucial for its infectivity 
and pathogenicity. They possess a lipid membrane derived from host cells, which is essential for 
their entry and exit from infected cells. The two major surface glycoproteins, hemagglutinin (HA), 
neuraminidase (NA), along with a small number of the matrix 2 (M2) ion channel protein, are 
embedded in a lipid bilayer (Figure 2) (Mostafa et al., 2018). Here, HA is a glycosylated type 1 
==PAGE:20==
9 
 
integral membrane protein that functions as both the viral receptor-binding protein and fusion 
protein. It specifically interacts with N-acetylneuraminic acid, a type of sialic acid (SA) present on 
host glycoproteins with binding preferences varying by host species. IAVs adapted to humans 
exhibit a stronger affinity for α2-6-linked SA. Once the virus attaches to its receptor, it is 
internalized, and the acidic conditions within the endosome induce structural changes in HA, 
promoting membrane fusion and the release of viral ribonucleoproteins (RNPs) into the cytoplasm. 
HA functions as a trimer, with each unit undergoing proteolytic cleavage to form disulfide-linked 
HA1 and HA2 polypeptides, a necessary process for viral activation. This cleavage is carried out 
by host serine proteases, such as tryptase Clara in humans, which recognizes a conserved Q/E-X-
R motif (J. Chen et al., 1998). The second most abundant protein is NA, a sialidase enzyme that, 
together with HA, facilitates viral release by cleaving sialic acids on host cells and viral 
glycoproteins, preventing particle aggregation. It is a major antigenic target and the site of action 
for antivirals (Taubenberger & Kash, 2010b). Currently, there are 18 known subtypes of HA and 
11 subtypes of NA. Among these, H1, H2, H3, N1, and N2 are known to have caused human 
epidemics. Due to IAV's ability to infect various animal species and cross species barriers, it poses 
a significant zoonotic risk. In pigs, three zoonotic subtypes have been identified: the swine 
influenza H1N1 subtype, the H3N2 “human-like” virus, and the reassortant H1N2 subtype (T. K. 
Anderson et al., 2021; de Vries et al., 2020). 
The IAV genome, consist of eight single-stranded viral RNA (vRNA) segments that encode a 
minimum of 18 proteins (table 1). Each vRNP complex is composed of three viral polymerase 
proteins: polymerase basic 1 (PB1), polymerase basic 2 (PB2) and polymerase acidic (PA) as well 
as nucleoprotein (NP)(Yang et al., 2024). NPs ensures that the protein complex and the viral 
genome are bound together in a needle-like structure consisting of polymer NP scaffolds of double 
stranded helix. The RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp), consisting of PB2, PB1 and PA, is 
important for the virus, as IAV is a negative-sense RNA and therefore relies on RdRp for both 
replication and transcription when inside the host cell (Dawson et al., 2017a; Hutchinson et al., 
2010; Mostafa et al., 2018)

1.4.2 Anti-viral innate immune responses 
Innate immune system, which plays a crucial role in detecting microorganisms and tissue damage 
through specialized receptors encoded in the germline, and remains unchanged in somatic cells. 
This contrasts with adaptive immune receptors, such as immunoglobulins and T-cell receptors, 
which arise through genetic rearrangements in lymphocyte precursors (Biondo et al., 2019). 
During influenza infection, the innate immune response serves as the body’s first line of defense, 
acting immediately to restricts viral replication before the adaptive immune system is activated. 
==PAGE:23==
12 
 
Since influenza primarily affects individuals who lack pre-existing antibodies due to mutations in 
the infecting viral strain, early control of the virus relies entirely on innate immunity. Generally, it 
takes typically five days for the adaptive immune system to mount a response by generating virus-
specific T-cells and antibodies which make the initial immune reaction crucial in determining 
disease progression(Hermans et al., 2018; Krammer & Palese, 2013) 
Therefore, an effective innate response is essential for initiating immediate defensive actions and 
for stimulating the subsequent generation of antibodies and T cells. Even though innate responses 
are essentially protective, they can cause collateral damage to the host and are responsible for 
many of the pathological manifestation of influenza. This is especially noticeable when highly 
virulent viral strains, like some pandemic or avian strains, cause potentially fatal infections. These 
strains are frequently linked to pneumonia and elevated cytokine production (de Jong et al., 2006; 
Lee et al., 2011)  
During IAV infection, the host employs various pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) (Figure 3) 
such as Toll-like receptors (TLRs), RIG-I like receptors (RLRs), nucleotide-binding 
oligomerization domain (NOD)-like receptors (NLRs), cyclic GMP-AMP synthase (cGAS), and 
Z-DNA-binding protein 1 (ZBP1) to sense the infections. Upon recognizing viral components, 
these receptors trigger downstream signaling pathways that lead to the production of type I 
interferons (IFNs) and pro-inflammatory cytokines. This response establishes an antiviral state that 
inhibits viral replication in surrounding cells and activates interferon-stimulated genes (ISGs) to 
exert further antiviral effects. The process also recruits immune cells to the infection site to assist 
in clearing the virus. Nevertheless, IAV has developed strategies to evade and counteract these 
innate immune responses, creating a complex interplay between host defense mechanisms and 
viral antagonism (An et al., 2024a). The following section will cover the principal mechanism of 
the innate antiviral immune response with regards to IAV infection.

1.4.1.a Pathogen Recognition Receptors (PRRs) 
Toll-like receptors (TLRs) serve as essential pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) in the 
identification of pathogens both on the cell surface and within intracellular compartments, 
including endosomes and lysosomes(Takeshita et al., 2006). Specifically, TLR1, TL2, TLR4, 
TLR5, and TLR6 are located on the cell membrane and are responsible for recognizing pathogen-
associated molecular patters (PAMPs) derived from bacteria, fungi, and protozoa. In contrast, 
TLR3, TLR7, TLR8, and TLR9 are located in endosomal and lysosomal membranes, where they 
predominantly recognize nucleic acid PAMPs derived from viruses such as influenza A viruses 
(IAVs) (Kawai & Akira, 2010; Kumar et al., 2011)Among these, TLR3, TLR7, and TLR8 play 
significant roles in detecting IAV components in endosomes during the process of viral replication. 
Here, TLR3 is known to recognize double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) in endosomes and may also 
identify newly discovered RNA structures found in phagocytosed cells infected with IAV (Schulz 
et al., 2005)Whereas, in plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDCs), TLR7 identifies ssRNA from 
internalized influenza virions, initiating downstream signaling through the adaptor protein myeloid 
differentiation factor 88 (MyD88) (Lund et al., 2004)This activation leads to the stimulation of 
==PAGE:25==

NF-kB and interferon regulatory factor 7 (IRF7), thereby promoting the production of pro-
inflammatory cytokines and type I interferons (IFNs). However, in macrophages and conventional 
dendritic cells (DCs), TLR3 engages with TIR-domain-containing adapter-inducing interferon-β 
(TRIF), leading to the activation of serine-threonine kinases IKKe and TBK1. These kinases 
phosphorylate IRF3, thereby regulating IFN-B expression(Le Goffic et al., 2007; Mifsud et al., 
2021) 
Intracellular ssRNA and transcriptional intermediates of IAV within infected host cells are detected 
primarily by retinoic acid-inducible gene (RIG-I). Additionally, melanoma differentiation-
associated gene 5 (MAD5) plays a role in sensing non-self RNA and transcriptional products of 
IAVs in the cytoplasm(Pichlmair et al., 2006a). Upon recognizing PAMPs, RIG-I undergoes 
activation, resulting in the exposure of its caspase activation and recruitment domains (CARDs). 
These domains are subsequently regulated through dephosphorylation or ubiquitination, processes 
facilitated by E3 ligases such as tripartite motif-containing protein 25 (TRIM25)(Munir, 2010). 
This modification facilitates the CARD-dependent interaction between RIG-I and mitochondrial 
antiviral-signaling protein (MAVS), initiating downstream signal transduction at the outer 
mitochondrial membrane. Consequently. Essential transcriptional factors such as interferon 
regulatory factor 3 (IRF3), interferon regulatory factor 7 (IRF7), and nuclear factor kappa-light-
chain-enhancer of activated B cells (NF-kB) are activated. This cascade ultimately leads to the 
production of multiple interferons (IFNs) and pro-inflammatory cytokines, which enhance the 
host’s antiviral response(Yoneyama et al., 2015). 
NOD-like receptors (NLRs), including NLR family pyrin domain-containing (NLRP3) are also 
intracellular PRRs. NLRP3 is widely expressed in various immune and non-immune cells, 
including dendritic cells (DCs), macrophages, neutrophils, monocytes, and human pulmonary 
epithelial cells (An et al., 2024b; X. Chen et al., 2018a). Upon activation, NLRP3 oligomerizes 
and serves as a nucleate for the recruitment of the adaptor ASC and pro-caspase-1 to assemble the 
NLRP3 inflammasome. This causes the activation of caspase-1 and the proteolytic maturation and 
secretion of IL-1beta and IL-18, as well as the cleavage of gasdermin D (GSDMD), which tigers 
pyroptosis by forming membrane pores and the release of immune factors such as galectin-3; an 
effector protein of the NLRP3 inflammasome(Y. Chen et al., 2019; Niu & Meng, 2023). 
Additionally, IAV replication also generates Z-RNAs and promotes type I IFN responses. IFN

signaling upregulates Z-DNA-binding protein 1 (ZBP1), which acts as a sensor for viral Z-RNAs 
(not shown in Figure 3). Once upregulated, ZBP1interact with receptor-interacting protein kinase

ultimately leads to the activation of apoptosis and inflammatory cell death pathways, contributing 
to host’s antiviral defense (Basavaraju et al., 2022). 
1.4.2.b Cytokines, Chemokines and Interferon Stimulated Genes 
During IAV infection, the host immune system responds by activating various transcription factors, 
including NF-kB, interferon regulatory factor 3 (IFR3), and IRF7, which translocate into the 
nucleus and induce the transcription of genes encoding pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, 
IL-6, IL-beta), type I interferons (IFN-alpha and IFN-beta), and type III interferons (IFN-λ1, IFN-
λ2, IFN-λ3, and IFN-λ4) (Iwasaki & Pillai, 2014) . These cytokines and interferons are critical 
mediators of antiviral defense, shaping both the innate and adaptive immune responses. 
Additionally, cytokines such as TNF-alpha, interleukin-6 (IL-6), and interleukin-1 beta (IL-1beta) 
play significant roles in controlling IAV infection by promoting inflammation, recruiting immune 
cells to the infection site, and enhancing antiviral defense mechanism (Tisoncik et al., 2012). 
Similarly, chemokines play a vital role for coordinating the immune response by recruiting 
immune cells to infected tissues. For instance, CCL2 (monocyte chemoattractant protein-1, MCP-
1) and CXCL10 (interferon-gamma-inducible protein-10, IP-10) are upregulated during IAV 
infection to attract monocytes, macrophages, and T cells to the lungs, helping to control viral 
spread (Lin et al., 2008). However, excessive chemokine signaling or cytokine production led to 
lung pathology and contributed to disease severity. 
Another crucial antiviral immune response is interferons (IFNs). There are 2 types of interferon, 
Type I interferons (IFN-α and IFN-β) and type III interferons (IFN-λs), that are expressed rapidly 
in response to IAV infection(Davidson et al., 2014). IFN-α/β binds to IFN-α/β receptors (IFNAR), 
triggering a systemic antiviral response, whereas IFN-λs bind to IFN-λ receptors (IFNLR), 
primarily affecting epithelial cells in the respiratory tract (X. Chen et al., 2018b; Hemann et al., 
2017). Both IFN types activate the Janus kinase-signal transducer and activator of transcription 
(JAK-STAT) pathway, leading to the induction of ISGF3 complex, which binds to interferon-

1.4.3 Modulation of innate immune response by viral proteins 
Influenza A viruses have adopted different strategies to evade the antiviral nature of the host innate 
immune system. Many defense mechanisms have been activated by the host innate immune 
system, which restricts IAV replication and infection. Therefore, for the successful replication of 
IAV in host cells has to counteract host antiviral activities, mainly the production of IFN and the 
==PAGE:28==
17 
 
activities of IFN-induced host proteins that inhibit virus replication (Van de Sandt et al., 2012). In 
particular, the viral protein, mainly PA-X and NS1, are the major factors that evade the innate 
immune response and virus pathogenicity (Nogales et al., 2018). Here, NS1 inhibits RIG-1 
receptor signaling by various means (Pichlmair et al., 2006b). For instance, NS1 protein blocks 
the recognition of 5’-phosphorylated viral ssRNA by RIG-I receptor. In addition, NS1 also 
prevents oligomerization of TRIM25 by interacting with the coiled-coil domain and so inhibits 
TRIM25-mediated RIG-I CARD ubiquitination, which is essential to transmit signals through the 
MAVs to elicit interferon responses (Gack et al., 2009; Rajsbaum et al., 2012). Furthermore, NS1 
suppresses the transcription factors IRF3, NF-kB, and AP-1, hence limiting IFN synthesis and 
subsequent induction of IFN-stimulated gene products (Ludwig et al., 2002). Similarly, the NS1 
protein also inhibits NLRP3 inflammasome activation by preventing the cleavage of pro-
interleukin (IL)-1Beta and pro-IL-18 into their mature form, where NS1 interacts with NLRP3 and 
impairs ASC speck formation and suppresses ASC ubiquitination, processes relevant to 
inflammasome activation (Chung et al., 2018). Another important target of NS1 is the 
phosphatidylinositol-3-kinase (PI3Kk) signaling pathway, where it directly interacts with p58beta 
subunit, resulting in the activation of Akt pathway, which eventually increases the rate of viral 
internalization, inhibits apoptosis (Hale et al., 2006). Thus, IAV employs NS1 and PA-X proteins 
to effectively counteract host antiviral response by inhibiting key signaling pathways, suppressing 
IFN responses, and modulating inflammasome activation. With these mechanisms, IAV escapes 
the host innate response, thereby enhancing its pathogenicity within host cells. 
1.5 Swine as a Model for Human Cardiovascular and Metabolic Diseases 
Animal models are an important tool in influenza research as they help researchers to understand 
virulence mechanisms and produce more effective vaccinations, preventative methods, and 
treatments. The experimental model to be utilized should be determined by the species' traits and 
similarities to humans, as well as how each host's restrictions interfere the least with the parameters 
evaluated (Rajao & Vincent, 2015). Swine have emerged as useful models for studying human 
cardiovascular and metabolic illnesses due to their anatomical and physiological similarities, 
notably in heart size, coronary circulation, and metabolic function. This makes them excellent for 
mimicking cardiovascular illnesses and evaluating surgical methods, including as genetically 
altered cardiovascular components and xenotransplantation(Hou et al., 2022).  
==PAGE:29==
18 
 
Swine models are relatively easier to establish than other large animal models, and the well-
developed swine industry supports their use in translational research, making them valuable for 
preclinical testing and advancing new diagnostics and treatments to clinical application. In 
addition, pigs are instrumental in infectious disease research with significant implications for 
human health. For instance, studying fundamental viral mechanisms in pigs has contributed to the 
development of human treatments and vaccines, including those targeting coronavirus-related 
illnesses. Swine serve as reservoirs for zoonotic diseases, ranging from bacterial (e.g., 
salmonellosis) to viral (e.g., influenza) and parasitic (e.g., toxoplasmosis)—thus supporting the 
"One Health" concept, which emphasizes the interconnectedness of human and animal health 
(Decaro & Lorusso, 2020). Pigs also offer over 80% similarity to humans in immune parameters 
and share significant genomic and protein homology, providing advantages over traditional mouse 
models in infectious disease research (Meurens et al., 2012). In the case of influenza, pigs are 
particularly valuable because they are susceptible to the same viral subtypes (H1N1, H1N2, 
H3N2), share similar epidemiological patterns and viral adaptation mechanisms, and exhibit 
comparable infection (Mehle & Doudna, 2009). These characteristics make pigs highly suitable 
for exploring the pathogenesis of influenza, vaccine development, immune response mechanisms, 
and potential therapies. Although other models like guinea pigs and mice are used for influenza 
research due to their cost-effectiveness and handling ease, they often lack the clinical relevance 
and immunological integrity offered by swine (Lunney et al., 2021). Thus, the swine model stands 
out as a powerful translational tool to bridge the gap between animal studies and human health 
outcomes, particularly in cardiovascular, metabolic, and infectious disease research.

2. Experimental Background 
2.1 qPCR 
Quantitative real-time PCR (qPCR), a fluorescence-based approach, is a key tool in molecular 
diagnostics, life sciences, agriculture, and medicine because of its capacity to identify and quantify 
extremely minute quantities of nucleic acids in a wide range of samples. Due to its simple 
underlying principle, user-friendly approach, short turnaround time, and outstanding sensitivity 
and specificity—often attained in a single-step assay—qPCR has become the standard method for 
nucleic acid measurement. In addition to its widespread use in scientific research, it is valuable in 
diagnostic applications such as detecting and quantifying microorganisms, analyzing gene copy 
numbers, identifying transgenes in genetically modified organisms, assessing the risk of cancer 
recurrence, and supporting forensic analysis(Artika et al., 2022; Bustin et al., 2009).  
Today, its application has been extended to RNA analysis through the use of reverse transcriptase 
enzyme, which converts RNA into complementary DNA (cDNA) because DNA is more stable 
than RNA, and also the presence of RNase in the surroundings will increase the chances of RNA 
degradation. To ensure that enough template is available for accurate quantification, a pre-
amplification step is often performed after cDNA synthesis, especially when working with low-
input samples or in nanoliter reaction volumes. Once sufficient cDNA is obtained, the qPCR 
process continues with the standard PCR cycling steps: denaturation, annealing, and elongation, 
while enabling real-time monitoring of DNA amplification throughout each cycle. Unlike 
conventional PCR, which only provides end-point detection, qPCR allows for the accurate, 
sensitive, and rapid quantification of DNA during each cycle of amplification(Kifaro et al., 2022). 
qPCR enables the measurement of the initial quantity of target RNA. This capability is crucial for 
applications such as gene expression analysis, where precise mRNA quantification is needed such 
as for assessing influenza viral RNA load in clinical samples. Additionally, qPCR eliminates the 
need for post-processing, significantly reducing the risk of cross-contamination from previously 
amplified products (Artika et al., 2022). The following section provides a comprehensive overview 
of qPCR, the various components involved in qPCR, as well as the technologies used for analysis 
and detection. 
==PAGE:31==
20 
 
2.1.1 RNA Extraction and Purification 
The extraction of RNA typically begins with the collection of tissues from the studied organisms, 
which are immediately preserved in RNA later, an aqueous, non-toxic RNA stabilization and 
storage reagent. RNA later protects cellular RNA from degradation and allows long-term storage 
of biological samples. Stabilization at this early stage is crucial because RNA is highly unstable 
and susceptible to rapid degradation by RNases. Without proper stabilization, gene expression 
patterns can be altered, making accurate analysis impossible. Therefore, RNA stabilization is an 
absolute prerequisite for reliable gene expression analysis (Thermo Fisher Scientific, n.d.-a) 
RNA purification follows similar principles as DNA purification but requires greater care to 
prevent degradation. Using the miRNeasy Mini Kit (Qiagen, #217004), RNA is isolated through a 
specialized buffer system that enables selective binding of RNA to a RNeasy silica membrane. 
First, the biological samples are lysed and homogenized in the presence of a highly denaturing 
buffer containing guanidine thiocyanate, which rapidly inactivates RNases and ensures the 
integrity of the RNA. Ethanol is then added to the lysate to create conditions that promote RNA 
binding to the membrane. The lysate is transferred to a RNeasy Mini Spin Column, where total 
RNA binds to the membrane while impurities pass through. The column is subsequently washed 
with two buffers: Buffer RWT, which removes non-specifically bound contaminants such as 
proteins, fatty acids, and carbohydrates, and Buffer RPE, which removes residual salts. The result 
is highly purified RNA suitable for downstream applications such as qPCR, microarrays, or RNA 
sequencing (QIAGEN, n.d.) 
2.1.2 RNA quality, quantity, and integrity 
In RT-qPCR experiments, two of the most critical factors are the purity and integrity of the RNA. 
Impurities in RNA samples can inhibit both reverse transcription (RT) and polymerase chain 
reaction (PCR), leading to variable and inaccurate quantification results. RNA purity and integrity 
are distinct parameters, and both must be assessed to ensure the RNA meets the minimal 
acceptance criteria for downstream applications. For RNA quality assessment, both quantity and 
purity are commonly evaluated using spectrophotometric ratios: The A260/A280 ratio should 
range between 1.8 and 2.0. Ratios below 1.8 may indicate protein contamination, which can reduce 
PCR efficiency. The A260/A230 ratio helps detect residual contaminants such as phenol or 
==PAGE:32==
21 
 
guanidine isothiocyanate, a chaotropic salt commonly used in RNA extraction. These compounds 
can inhibit enzymatic reactions and negatively impact data quality (Life technologies, n.d.; Taylor 
et al., 2010; Thermo Fisher Scientific, n.d.-a) 
RNA integrity is typically assessed using the Agilent RNA 6000 Nano Kit in conjunction with the 
Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer. This system separates RNA molecules on a microfluidic chip via 
electrophoresis, allowing for fluorescence-based identification of ribosomal RNA subunits (18S 
and 28S) and degradation products. The software (2100 Expert Software, Agilent Technologies) 
evaluates the 18S:28S rRNA ratio and generates a RNA Integrity Number (RIN), which ranges 
from 1 (fully degraded RNA) to 10 (completely intact RNA). Average RIN values might differ by 
sample type, and for heart tissue samples, a RIN between 5 and 7 is commonly observed (Fleige 
et al., 2006). A RIN ≥ 5 is generally considered acceptable for RT-qPCR and is associated with 
high reproducibility (Fleige et al., 2006). 
2.1.3 cDNA synthesis and pre-amplification 
Before running qPCR, cDNA synthesis and pre-amplification are critical steps to ensure the 
accuracy and quantifiability of gene expression analysis. During cDNA synthesis, RNA samples 
are first treated with a genomic DNA (gDNA) Wipeout Buffer, which contains DNase to remove 
any residual gDNA. This is essential, as gDNA contamination can lead to false-positive results or 
inaccurate quantification. Because RNA is highly unstable and prone to degradation by ubiquitous 
RNases, it cannot be used directly in qPCR. Moreover, qPCR utilizes DNA polymerase, such as 
Taq DNA polymerase, which requires a DNA template. Therefore, reverse transcription (RT) is 
performed to convert RNA into complementary DNA (cDNA) using a reverse transcriptase 
enzyme in combination with oligo(dT) primers (which bind to the poly-A tail of mRNA) and 
random primers (which provide broader transcript coverage). This conversion stabilizes the 
genetic material and prepares it for accurate amplification (Taylor et al., 2010) (Life technologies, 
n.d.) . 
To enhance detection sensitivity and quantifiability, preamplification is performed after cDNA 
synthesis. This step increases the concentration of specific target sequences in the sample before 
qPCR analysis. Preamplification involves a limited number of PCR cycles using a pooled primer 
set identical to those used in the downstream qPCR reaction. Each target gene can be amplified up 
==PAGE:33==
22 
 
to 1,000-fold or more, allowing for the analysis of multiple targets without compromising 
sensitivity. Following preamplification, the reaction mixture is treated with an exonuclease to 
degrade any remaining primers. This prevents primer-dimer formation and reduces background 
signal in the subsequent qPCR step, ensuring higher specificity and reproducibility in gene 
expression quantification (Okino et al., 2016). 
2.1.4 Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) 
 
Figure 4 Principle of the chain reaction.  
(a) The stages in a PCR cycle. (b)A sequence of PCR cycles (Evrard et al., 2009). 
Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is a technique in molecular biology that is used to amplify a 
single or a few copies of a sequence of DNA/cDNA across several orders of magnitude, generating 
thousands to millions of copies of a particular DNA sequence. These copies of specific DNA 
segments are called amplicons. The basic PCR principle involves a chain reaction and one DNA 
molecule, which is used to produce two copies, then four, then eight, and so forth (Figure 4). This 
continuous doubling is accomplished by specific proteins known as polymerases, enzymes that are 
able to string together individual DNA building blocks to form long molecular strands.  The 
exponential amplification of a very small amount of template DNA is achieved using a heat-stable 
DNA polymerase and an automated heat block that is capable of rapid changes of temperature 
(Wages, 2005).  
==PAGE:34==
23 
 
The template DNA molecule is initially denatured to two single strands by heating to a high 
temperature (typically 90–95°C) in the first stage of the PCR cycle. Two small synthetic 
oligonucleotides that are complementary to sequences on opposite strands of the template 
molecule and that flank the DNA segment to be amplified serve as primers for the DNA 
polymerase. The second stage of the PCR cycle consists of cooling the reaction, which permits the 
annealing of the single-stranded oligonucleotide primers to the denatured template molecule. The 
third stage of the reaction is the extension of the new strand from the annealed primer in a 5′ to 3′ 
direction by the heat-stable polymerase. This is performed at the optimum temperature for the 
polymerase (68–72°C). The most commonly used polymerase is the enzyme isolated 
from Thermus aquaticus (Taq DNA polymerase). Each successive cycle effectively doubles the 
amount of DNA product. The three-stage cycle of denaturation, annealing, and primer extension 
is repeated 25–40 times in a typical PCR procedure. The product of such a reaction is a large 
quantity (10−9 to 10−8 mol l−1 in a 10–100 μl volume) of a double-stranded DNA molecule whose 
length is determined by the distance between the primer sites on the original template molecule. 
Typically, PCR products or amplicons are 100–3000 bp in length, although much longer (up to 
∼50000 bp) amplifications are possible under specific conditions. PCR products are visualized by 
electrophoresis, followed by staining with fluorescent dyes or by hybridization to labeled 
oligonucleotide probes (Spurgeon et al., 2008).   
In contrast, microfluidic qPCR involves amplifying small DNA fragments ranging from 70 to 200 
base pairs in order to achieve high efficiency and quick amplification due to the small reaction 
volumes and fast temperature cycling provided by microfluidic devices. To achieve amplification 
effectiveness, primers are designed with identical melting temperatures (Tm), often around 60 °C. 
This allows for consistent denaturation and annealing throughout several reactions in parallel. The 
amplification process is seen in real time using fluorescent detection techniques such as SYBR 
Green dye, which binds nonspecifically to double-stranded DNA, and TaqMan hydrolysis probes, 
which offer sequence-specific detection. These fluorescence signals are continually monitored 
throughout temperature cycling, allowing for precise measurement of nucleic acids (Zhang & 
Ozdemir, 2009). The integration of these functionalities inside microfluidic systems boosts 
sensitivity, minimizes reagent use, and speeds up data gathering, making microfluidic qPCR 
extremely appropriate for applications that need quick and high-throughput DNA analysis. 
==PAGE:35==
24 
 
The primers in PCR are specific for each gene of interest and are designed with a focus on both 
specificity and efficiency of amplification. Unspecific primers can produce extra unnecessary PCR 
products, whereas inefficient primers do not double the amount of amplicon per cycle, according 
to the theoretical optimum for each PCR cycle. Specificity and efficiency of the primers are at their 
most optimal when the length of said primers is between 18 and 24 oligonucleotide bases, with the 
melting temperature (Tm) set at close to 60 °C. In addition, the percentage of guanine-cytosine 
(GC%) content of the primers must be considered, as the Tm correlates with the GC% content, 
meaning the higher GC% content in the primers, the higher Tm, and vice versa. Too low Tm affects 
the specificity of the primer, and too high Tm affects the efficiency. The optimal GC% should be 
between 50% and 60%. The above-mentioned parameters are all important for designing primers. 
Various computer programs can be used in primer design, such as Primer3 (Primer3 – v.0.4.0). 
Once designed, the primer sequences can be blasted to ensure specificity to the gene of interest 
(BLAST). The fluorescence signal of a qPCR reaction must be measured during the exponential 
phase of the PCR. The cycle at which the fluorescence crosses a defined threshold is termed the 
cycle threshold (Cq) value, which can be used for quantification. (Spurgeon et al., 2008; Taylor et 
al., 2010; Thermo Fisher Scientific, n.d.-b). Additionally, a post-amplification melting curve 
analysis can help assess reaction quality by detecting genomic DNA contamination or the presence 
of primer-dimers, which appear as additional peaks separate from the target amplicon. 
PCR efficiency is determined from a 1-3 dilution series of the starting material. The Cq values 
from the dilution series are plotted on a log scale against the known starting cDNA concentration. 
The slope of the regression line correlates to the dynamic range of the amplification curve, and 
thereby the efficiency.

DNA binding dyes detect all double-stranded DNA (dsDNA), making it necessary to include a

amplification, and gDNA contamination. A melting curve is obtained by increasing the heat and 
measuring the fluorescence during the process, by which the denaturation of the DNA double helix 
can be analyzed, as seen in Figure 5 (Baaske et al., 2007) 
Technical variation in qPCR can be due to PCR inhibitors, which can affect the sensitivity and the 
kinetics of the qPCR. Therefore, a set of controls is required when performing qPCR. The 
expression profile is validated with a set of internal controls, such as reference genes. Reference 
genes are genes that are constitutively expressed in cells from specific tissues. By obtaining results 
from the same tissue, the reference genes are a great source of control, and the genes of interest 
are normalized to the reference genes. The non-template control (NTC) is a negative control, 
whereas the negative reverse transcriptase (-RT) control contains no reverse transcriptase enzyme. 
These two controls are frequently used to examine any contamination, primer dimers, or gDNA 
present (Angela Pérez-Novo et al., 2005; Laurell et al., 2012; Vandesompele et al., 2002). 
2.2 Histology and Hematoxylin & Eosin (H&E) Staining 
2.2.1 Histology 
Histology is the microscopic examination of the cellular and tissue structure of animals and plants. 
It is a fundamental discipline in both medical and biological sciences, with important applications 
==PAGE:37==
26 
 
in diagnostics, pathology, forensic investigations, and scientific research. Histology allows for 
microscopic examination of tissues to detect abnormalities, distinguish between healthy and sick 
states, and get a better understanding of cellular activity and tissue organization. It has evolved as 
a result of the development of numerous procedures and staining protocols that improve tissue 
viewing and interpretation. The histological examination process is detailed, with several essential 
steps required to prepare tissue samples for microscopic study (Musumeci, 2014). 
Fixation: The initial and most important step in tissue preparation for histological examination is 
fixation. Fixation preserves tissue as close to its native form as possible, preventing enzymatic 
deterioration and autolysis. This is often performed via chemical agents such as Neutral Buffered 
Formalin (NBF), which stabilizes the tissue by crosslinking proteins(Alturkistani et al., 2015). 
While fixation is helpful for structural preservation, it can sometimes denature proteins and nucleic 
acids, impairing subsequent analysis(J. and R. G. Anderson, n.d.). 
Processing, Dehydration, and Embedding: Following fixation, the tissue is processed, which 
involves dehydration, clearing, and infiltration. Dehydration is frequently done by removing water 
from tissue with graded ethanol solutions. This process is required to prepare the tissue for 
embedding because water is not compatible with paraffin wax. Clearing agents, such as xylene, 
are subsequently used to eliminate alcohol and promote paraffin infiltration. Here, embedding in 
paraffin wax creates a solid matrix to support the tissue during sectioning(Titford, 2009) 
Sectioning and Slide Preparation: After embedding, the tissue block is cut into thin slices with 
a microtome. These thin slices, which are typically only a few microns thick, are subsequently put 
on glass slides. Sectioning allows for a thorough examination under a light microscope by 
providing the essential transparency and detail for diagnostic reasons. Staining is then carried out 
to improve the visibility and contrast of cellular features(Alturkistani et al., 2015; Cai et al., 2014).  
2.2.2 Hematoxylin and Eosin (H&E) staining method 
Among the different staining procedures available, H&E staining remains the most widely used 
and essential technique in routine histological examination. Hematoxylin, a basic dye, gives acidic 
structures like nuclei a blue to purple color, highlighting the chromatin and nuclear features. Eosin, 
an acidic dye, stains basic cytoplasmic and extracellular matrix components pink to red. This dual-
==PAGE:38==
27 
 
staining approach provides a thorough contrast that clearly differentiates cell shape and tissue 
architecture(Bancroft & Layton, 2013). 
Basic Steps for H&E staining: The standard H&E staining procedure consists of multiple 
successive phases that achieve maximum staining quality and clarity (Table 3). The paraffin-
embedded tissue sections are first deparaffinized with xylene, then rehydrated with ethanol in 
descending concentrations (100%, 95%, 70%) to water. The slides are then treated with 
hematoxylin for a set period of time to allow for nuclear staining. This is followed by rinsing and 
removing excess discoloration with an acid alcohol solution. The slides are then immersed in a 
bluing solution, such as ammonia water or lithium carbonate, to increase the contrast of the 
hematoxylin stain. The sections are then counterstained with eosin to reveal the cytoplasmic 
components. After staining, the slides are dehydrated with increasing alcohol concentrations and 
cleaned in xylene before being mounted with a coverslip(J. and R. G. Anderson, n.d.). 
 Table 3 Routine (conventional) H&E staining procedure(Ankle & Joshi, 2011). 
 
 
 
==PAGE:39==

The H&E staining process is widely regarded for its effectiveness, cost, and adaptability. It 
provides a quick and reliable way to assess tissue morphology and detect pathogenic alterations. 
Despite its extensive use, H&E staining has limits, as it does not identify all cellular components 
or molecular markers. In such circumstances, additional staining techniques such as Periodic Acid-
Schiff (PAS), Trichrome, or immunohistochemical procedures are used to provide further detail 
and specificity (Alturkistani et al., 2015). Thus, histology remains a critical component of 
diagnostic and research pathology in which tissue samples are carefully processed and stained, 
particularly utilizing the Hematoxylin and Eosin staining procedure, which provides essential 
insights into tissue structure and function.

3.2.1 Primer design 
For the design of primers targeting host genes, both Ensembl (http://www.ensembl.org/index.html) 
and Primer3 (version 0.4.0) (https://bioinfo.ut.ee/primer3-0.4.0/) were utilized, along with

target gene was retrieved from Ensembl and input into the Primer3 software. Primer lengths were 
specified between 70 and 100 nucleotides, with melting temperatures (Tm) set between 59°C and

To prevent primer binding to genomic DNA, primers were designed to span introns whenever 
possible; if this was not feasible, primers were positioned on exons flanking a large intron. The 
designed primer pairs were then evaluated for specificity to the target gene using BLAST searches

3.2.1 cDNA synthesis and pre-amplification (mRNA) 
Extracted RNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA using the QuantiTect Reverse Transcription Kit 
(Qiagen #205311) following the manufacturer's instructions. The cDNA synthesis procedure 
included DNase treatment and a reverse transcription master mix. Here, the reverse transcription 
master mix was prepared by mixing RT, RT Primer Mix, RT Buffer, and RNase-free water in a 
ratio of 0,75:0,75:3:1,5. After DNase treatment, the samples were stored at 4 °C. The resulting 
cDNA was diluted 1:10 with low EDTA TE buffer before pre-amplification and stored at -20 °C. 
A detailed description of the cDNA synthesis protocol is provided in Appendix 5. 
3.2.2 Pre-amplification and exonuclease treatment 
The pre-amplification master mix was prepared using primers (Appendix 3) at a concentration of 
20 µM. The pre-amplification reaction consisted of 3 µL TaqMan® PreAmp Master Mix, 2 µL low 
EDTA TE buffer, and 2.5 µL of a 200 nM primer mix. Samples were then subjected to 19 cycles 
of amplification using a thermocycler. For exonuclease treatment, the enzyme was diluted from 
20 U/µL to 4 U/µL, followed by incubation at 37 °C for 30 minutes, 80 °C for 15 minutes, and 
finally held at 4 °C. The resulting pre-amplified and exonuclease-treated cDNA was diluted 1:10 
in low EDTA TE-buffer and stored at -20 °C. A detailed description of the pre-amplification and 
exonuclease treatment protocols is provided in Appendix 6.

3.2.3 Quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) 
Quantitative PCR (qPCR) was performed using the Fluidigm 96.96 Dynamic Array IFC (BMK-
M-96.96, Standard Biotools). The assay mix was prepared by combining 3 µL of 2X Assay 
Loading Reagent Mastermix (Standard Biotools, 85000800) with 3 µL of 20 µM gene-specific 
primer pairs. The pre-sample mix consisted of 3 µL of 2X TaqMan Gene Expression Mastermix 
(Applied Biosystems, PN 4369016), 0.3 µL of 20X DNA Binding Dye (Fluidigm, 100-0388), 
0.3 µL of EvaGreen 20X (VWR, BTIU31000), and 0.9 µL of low EDTA TE-buffer (VWR, 
APLIA8569.0500). For each sample, 4.6 µL of pre-sample mix was combined with 1.5 µL of pre-
amplified cDNA. 
The 96.96 IFC chip was primed using Control Line Fluid and the Chip Prime 138x program on the 
HX IFC Controller (Fluidigm). After vortexing and centrifuging both mixes, 5 µL of assay mix 
was loaded into the left inlets, and 5 µL of sample mix into the right inlets of the chip. Air bubbles 
were carefully removed before loading. The loaded chip was processed using the Load Mix 136x 
program and run on the BioMark System (Fluidigm). The qPCR was performed using the 96x96 
protocol with a 10 min hot start, followed by 35 amplification cycles. Additional details of the 
qPCR procedure are provided in Appendix 7.

3.2.5 Pre-data processing 
Initial data processing was conducted using Fluidigm Real-Time PCR Analysis Software (v4.8.1, 
Standard BioTools Inc.), where amplification and melting curves were visually inspected to assess 
technical variation. Samples displaying abnormal Tm or unusual amplification profiles were 
excluded. To evaluate gDNA contamination, –RT controls were analyzed, and samples with more 
than 5% gDNA were removed. NTC was also reviewed to detect potential contamination, and any 
affected samples were excluded if there were less than five Cq values between the NTC and sample 
amplification curve. Then qPCR efficiencies between 80% to 120% and a R2 > 0,95 was accepted 
for subsequent data analysis inGenEx™ software (v7.1.1.118, MultiD).   
In order to remove technical variation, the dataset was normalized to reference genes. Here, 
normalization was performed using four reference genes—B2M, GAPDH, RPL13A, YWHAZ—
whose expression stability was assessed using NormFinder and GenNorm algorithms. Cq values 
from cDNA replicates were evaluated, and one or both of the replicates were excluded if more than 
15% deviated beyond the accepted range (–1.5 to +1.5). Averages were calculated for accepted 
replicates, and missing values were imputed using the highest observed Cq value plus one. Genes 
with over 20% missing data were excluded from the analysis.  
3.2.6 Data analysis 
A two-tailed t-test was performed to test for any significant expression differences in heart tissue. 
It was performed to test any significant expression differences between the groups: Fat-Mock vs 
Lean-Mock, Lean-Flu vs Lean-Mock, and Fat-Flu vs Fat-Mock in heart samples. A standard error 
of the mean (SEM) was calculated. A gene was considered statistically significant if p-values 
<0.05. A correlation analysis was conducted to study between the primers. The strength of a 
correlation is measured by the correlation coefficient r, and 0.6 ≤ r ≤1 indicate variables which can 
be considered highly correlated (Correlation Coefficients, 2005).

5. Discussion 
The present study investigated the effect of IAV infection on cardiac gene expression and 
histological changes in pigs fed a standard diet and a high-fat diet, similar to lean and obese 
conditions. The molecular and histological studies of different treatment groups show that distinct 
genes are regulated and their structural changes in the heart at different conditions The following 
section will discuss whether the results of this study's qPCR and histology analyses fit the research 
objectives. 
5.1 Altered gene expression in heart tissue based on the two different diets in 
pigs 
To evaluate whether high-fat diet and low-fat diet cause any changes in cardiac health, here, pigs 
are given a standard diet, indicating good health, and pigs fed a high-fat diet represent a metabolic 
condition. When comparing the gene regulation of healthy and obese pigs, the qPCR results show 
that several genes are regulated differently in the heart, indicating metabolic condition might affect 
the function of genes in the heart. It was observed that pigs on a high-fat diet show significantly 
higher levels of TGFB1, COL3A1, TNNC1, and TNNT2 than pigs fed a lean diet (Figure 7,8 & 
9). 
It has been found that obese pigs have higher levels of the genes TNNC1 and TNNT2, suggesting 
that obesity might affect the role of these genes in the heart. The sarcomeric gene TNNT2, which 
mediates the calcium-dependent interaction between myosin and thin filament, forms a part of the 
troponin complex with troponin C (TNNC1) and controls heart muscle contraction (Li & Hwang, 
2015). Also, a study supports that obesity contributes to asymmetric growth in hypertrophic 
cardiomyopathy (associated with diastolic dysfunction and changes in contractile mechanics) by 
increasing heart mass and myocardial wall thickness (X. Liu et al., 2022). Therefore, this study 
supports that obesity alters the expression of TNNC1 and TNNT2 in the heart. However, 
myocardial wall thickening was not studied during histological analysis; this might be incorporated 
for future studies for further validation or to support our findings. Similarly, upregulation of 
TGFB1 and COL3A1 in obese pigs indicates an obesity-driven fibrotic response in the heart. To 
support this, a study suggests that TGFβ1, a master regulator of fibrosis, is activated under obese 
==PAGE:54==
43 
 
conditions through downstream signaling pathways that promote collagen synthesis, including 
COL3A1, contributing to extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling and collagen deposition. This 
might explain the reason for the increased expression of both TGFB1 and COL3A1 in the present 
study is due to the obese condition(X. Liu et al., 2022). Additionally, the increased levels of CCL26 
in obese pigs further support an inflammatory condition in cardiac tissue. CCl26 is typically 
elevated during inflammatory and immune responses, and its increased levels can further 
upregulate TGFB1, thereby amplifying the fibrotic signaling cascade(Dong et al., 2023) which 
supports the finding in present study. Surprisingly, ICAM1, an adhesion molecule, seems to be 
regulated in non-obese conditions. Although there are studies reported that ICAM1 increases 
adiposity(M. Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2005). In our case, however, the temporal link to pressure 
overload might result in the induction of ICAM1 expression in the hearts of lean pigs, which might 
indicate the elevation of ICAM1 in healthy pigs, which was reported in a study (Salvador et al., 
2016). In case of ICAM1, the necessity of more research is needed to be done in lean pigs to 
determine whether obesity affects this gene. 
In conclusion, the qPCR results of Lean-Mock and Fat-Mock were supported by histological 
examination (Figure 9), showing an increased abundance and enlargement of adipocytes in the 
cardiac tissue of pigs fed a high-fat diet, along with minor endothelial layer disruption. The results 
of this study, which compared the diets of healthy and obese pigs, indicate that a high-fat diet 
contributes to inflammation in the heart that results in alterations of gene expression. On the other 
hand, finding like ICAM1 gene regulation in the heart suggests that two different conditions need 
more research. 
5.2 Altered gene expression in heart during IAV infection in pigs under healthy 
conditions 
The purpose of this study was to determine whether there was any alteration in the expression of 
cardiac genes during IAV infection in a non-obese state, indicating a healthy state. In this study, 
it's interesting to note that during influenza infection, only two genes appear to be altered in a pig's 
heart when fed a lean diet (Figures 10 & 11). This indicates that there is a minimal effect on the 
heart during influenza infection under healthy conditions. 
==PAGE:55==
44 
 
In this study, CCL26 levels were found to be two to three times greater in lean IAV-infected pigs 
than in their mock (Figure 10). It suggests that during an infection, there are more eosinophils 
found in the heart tissue. We know that an IAV infection causes more eosinophils to recuricate in 
the airways, which accelerates the virus's removal (Samarasinghe et al., 2014). In addition, it is 
commonly recognized that CCL26 (eotaxin-3) is a strong eosinophil chemoattractant and that it is 
increased in peripheral blood and the esophagus epithelium during active inflammation (Merves 
et al., 2014). Thus, in this study, IAV contributes to eosinophils to be produced, which subsequently 
move through the peripheral circulation. These eosinophils may be drawn to the heart by CCL26, 
which is produced by cardiac fibroblasts and macrophages, raising CCL26 levels(Diny et al., 
2016). Besides, infected lean pigs in the present study also had 30 times higher levels of ITIH2 
than lean pigs without infection (Figure 11). It suggests that the heart has adapted a defence 
mechanism against IAV infection by limiting tissue damage brought by CCL26-mediated immune 
activation, which was already observed in this study with a higher amount of CCL26. Additionally, 
a study reported that the acute-phase protein ITIH2 contributes to maintaining the integrity of the 
extracellular matrix during inflammation and can also strengthen the innate immune response to 
influenza viruses (Bell et al., 2019; Tang et al., 2024). 
Histological examination of Lean-Flu (Figure 12) samples revealed intact endothelial cells and 
preserved arterial structure. This implies that the higher ITIH2 levels might have a protective effect 
by maintaining the stability of the extracellular matrix (ECM) and limiting tissue damage even 
during active immune responses  (Schiattarella et al., 2021). These data demonstrate that, in the 
absence of metabolic conditions, the inflammatory response remains self-limiting and well-
regulated in the heart.  
5.3 Altered Gene Expression in Heart tissue after Influenza Infection in Pigs 
with a High Fat diet 
In this study, it was hypothesized that obesity increases the impact of IAV infection by increasing 
the expression of pro-inflammatory and stress response genes in the heart. In order to study this 
effect, the gene expression between pigs fed with a high-fat diet in the presence or absence of 
influenza infection was studied, which represents the metabolic condition along with viral 
infection.  
==PAGE:56==
45 
 
The molecular analysis shows differential expression of genes in obese pigs when infected with 
influenza. Figure 13 shows the upregulation of ICAM1, IKBKB, HSP70.2 and TNFRSF6B in a 
pig with influenza infection following an obese condition when compared to the Fat-Mock (obese 
condition). The alteration of genes in an obese pig in the presence of influenza indicates a 
pathological and cellular stress environment, which leads to a significant increment of stress 
proteins HSP70.2 in this study. The significant rise in HSP70.2 was also seen in viral replication 
and oxidative damage(Mardan-Nik et al., 2016). In our case, although the influenza virus was not 
detected in the heart tissue, a higher level of HSP 70.2 was observed, possibly indicating cardiac 
cells respond to cellular responses caused by viral infection. Also, obesity is a known cause pro-
inflammatory state in the body(Kawai et al., 2021). At the same time, influenza infection weakens 
the immune system, making it harder for the body to manage stress and inflammation 
effectively(Zhao et al., 2020). Thus, resulting in a chronic inflammation and metabolic stress 
environment in infected obese pigs leads to a stronger activation of stress-related proteins HSP70.2 
in the heart. Similarly, the increased level of IKBKB, a catalytic component of IKK complex, may 
be due to the activation of cardiomyocyte-specific IKK2/NF-κB, which results in immune cell 
infiltration (Maier et al., 2012), which was high during viral infection. This was also observed in 
histological analysis of fat-flu (Figure 12), showing disruption of the endothelial layer in heart 
tissue, indicating immune cell infiltration. Thus, the upregulation of IKBKB in our study in 
infected obese pigs mainly indicates structural changes in the heart. Additionally, there was 
upregulation of serine protease inhibitor SERPINE (also called PAI-I). Although there is a study 
suggesting that SERPINE inhibits IAV spread by extracellular IAV protein cleavage (Dittmann et 
al., 2015), no direct viral replication was observed in heart tissue in this study, indicating that the 
only cause of the SERPINE overexpression is obesity. This was supported by other research that 
demonstrated that obese individuals have high levels of SERPINE1(Kaur et al., 2010; Zhu & Hua, 
2025). 
 Interestingly, the level of ICAM1 is high in obese pigs infected with influenza, which indicates 
that upregulation of ICAM1 in this study was solely due to obesity due to the absence of virus in 
heart and it was also studies that ICAM1 expression elevated in a mice fed with high fat diet 
indicating ICAM1 positively correlated with abdominal fat deposition and macrophage infiltration 
(Brake et al., 2006) . This result was also supported by a histological study in Fat-Flu (Figure 14) 
==PAGE:57==
46 
 
in which immune cell infiltration was observed around the arterial wall. The increased level of 
TNFRSF6B in infected obese pigs when compared to their mock was observed. A study suggests 
the protective role of TNFRSF6B (Decoy receptor 3), by reducing influenza virus-induced 
macrophage activation, lowering inflammation(M.-T. Huang et al., 2015). Therefore, in the present 
study, inflammation caused by obesity might trigger an increase in TNFRSF6B levels, as a 
protective response by the body. Interestingly, three genes CHUK, COL3A1, and DBP, were 
upregulated in pigs with an obese condition without influenza infection, indicating that obesity 
causes an increased expression of these genes in response to a pro-inflammatory state in this pigs. 
Although IKBKB and CHUK are both members of the protein kinase family involved in NF-κB 
signaling (Fiordelisi et al., 2019), their differential expression between fat-mock and fat-flu groups 
suggests they may play distinct roles in the heart. The upregulation of CHUK in the fat-mock group 
appears to be driven by obesity. It was also noted that an obese pig without an infection had a high 
DBP level. DBP is a transcription factor that regulates Bmal1 to regulate the circadian clock 
mechanism(Maury et al., 2021). Since there was a lack of evidence that the DBP gene directly 
contributed to obesity, it was challenging to determine what exactly caused the rise in DBP in 
obese condition.  
Thus, studies between the Fat-mock and Fat-Flu group suggest that the influenza infection in obese 
pigs worsens metabolic stress in heart by weakening the immune response in the body, resulting 
in chronic low-grade inflammation, a common feature of obesity, where immune cells become less 
effective at controlling the pro-inflammatory environment in cardiac tissue.

6. Conclusion 
In this study, the impact of IAV infection on cardiac gene expression and histological changes was 
investigated in lean and obese pigs. The results showed that the high-fat diet results in alteration 
of the expression of several genes, including inflammatory and tissue remodeling genes, 
suggesting that the heart in obese animals is in a pro-inflammatory state. When infected with 
influenza, these effects were further amplified, particularly in genes related to fibrosis, immune 
response, and stress response. Histological analysis also revealed visible changes in heart 
architecture, especially in the Fat-Flu group compared to other groups. This indicates that viral 
infection further exacerbates metabolic conditions by weakening the immune response and causing 
a prolonged pro-inflammatory response in the heart.   
Overall, the data support the hypothesis that obesity exacerbates the cardiac effects of influenza 
infection, both at the molecular and tissue levels. The combination of metabolic stress from a high-
fat diet and viral infection appears to increase the risk of inflammation and tissue remodeling in 
the heart, which might potentially lead to a higher risk of cardiovascular complications. 
However, it is important to acknowledge some limitations in this study. The sample size was 
relatively small and inconsistent between the groups, which may influence the strength of the 
results. Another limitation is that this study only focuses on gene expression at the mRNA level, 
without measuring whether these changes led to differences in protein levels or actual heart 
function. Despite these limitations, this study provided important insight into how obesity may 
worsen the outcome of viral infections in terms of heart health.

7. Future Directions 
Future studies should consider using a large number of animals and include multiple time points 
after infection to better understand how influenza affects the heart over time. This would help to 
identify whether the changes observed are part of an early response, recovery phase, or a sign of 
long-term damage to the heart. In addition, it would be valuable to validate gene expression results 
at the protein level using techniques such as immunohistochemistry or western blotting. This 
would confirm whether the molecular changes lead to actual structural or functional effects in the 
heart. Finally, assessing heart function through imaging techniques or physiological measurements 
could provide a clearer understanding between molecular changes and actual cardiac performance.

Appendix 4: Detection of viral RNA in heart tissue  
Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) was performed using the Rotor-Gene Q system (Qiagen) 
and viral RNA primers (Flu5, Flu 29 and Flu 33) to detect the presence of viral RNA in heart tissue 
samples collected from H1N2-infected animals. Total RNA was extracted from heart tissue and 
reverse transcribed into complementary DNA (cDNA) following standard protocols (described in 
Appendix 4).  Two different concentration, i.e., 0,1 and 1 were used. Melting curves were then 
generated after each run to confirm a single PCR product. The presence of viral RNA was assessed 
based on amplification curves.
