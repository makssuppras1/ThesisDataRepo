Abstract
In this thesis, the Magnetic Anisotropy Energy (MAE) and Dzyaloshinskii-Moriya Interaction (DMI)
of a GaAs-Fe-Au (001) slab were calculated, using the ﬁrst-principles method, Density-Functional-
Theory (DFT), as implemented in the commercial software suite QuantumATK. Both of these were
calculated using Andersen’s local force theorem, to calculate total-energy diﬀerences of diﬀerent spin-
structures. It was found that both the GaAs-Fe and Fe-Au interface, enhances out-of-plane MAE,
with the former being the stronger of the two. It was also discovered, that the preferred spin-spiral
chirality of the slab, is anti-clockwise. The calculated microscopic DMI of 0.53 mJ m−2, was found to
agree rather well with experiments.
A new method for calculating DMI in a DFT-software, using a localized basis set, were also derived,
implemented and tested. The derived fully-relativistic method, were based on the method of inﬁnites-
imal rotations by Liechtenstein, Katsnelson, Antropov and Gubanov (LKAG), which is widely used,
to great success, to calculate the Jij of many materials. The method correctly predicted the direction
of the DMI-vector in a number of systems, such as a Cr-trimer on a Au (111) surface, a W-Fe (110)
slab and a Co-Pt (111) slab. The method also correctly showed enhancement of the DMI at surfaces,
interfaces and close to elements with strong spin-orbit coupling. The method however overestimated
the magnitudes of the DMI-vector for all systems considered in this thesis. The cause for this was not
found during the period of this project, but it was believed to be caused by the non-orthogonality of
the used basis set.

Introduction
The ﬁrst portable computer was the IBM 5100 released in 1975 which had a whopping 64 kB of
RAM and weighted 25 kg. 45 years later a smartphone weighing as little as 163 g is readily available
with 8 GB of RAM. This development have been made possible through continuous downsizing of
components, a process so continuous that Gordon Moore predicted that the number of components
would double every year. This prediction, coined ”Moore’s law” have held to this day, but is now
on the verge of declining. One technology which could potentially avert this decline, is the use of
quasi-particles, called magnetic skyrmions, as the next-gen memory technology.
Skyrmions were proposed by T.H.R. Skyrme as particles which can be characterized by a topological
integer and are topologically protected in the sense that any continuous deformation of the ﬁeld will
leave this integer unchanged.2
Figure 1: Experimental observation and control
of Skyrmion phase.3
Magnetic skyrmions are then a type of skyrmion
consisting of chiral spin-structures such as vor-
tices. In Fig. 1 a so-called N´eel-type skyrmion
is shown. Magnetic skyrmions show promise as
a memory technology, due to the ability to cre-
ate/annihilate them with a spin-polarized cur-
rent, see Fig.
2, and due to their long life-
time, caused by their topological protection,
which in turn would lead to very stable mem-
ory.
Magnetic skyrmions can be stabilized by
a number of mechanisms, such as long-range
magnetic-dipole interactions, antisymmetric ex-
change, more commonly called Dzyaloshinski-
Moriya Interaction (DMI), frustrated exchange
or four-spin exchange interaction.4 Of these, DMI
is the most promising mechanism for memory ap-
plications, due to the resulting small size of the magnetic skyrmions (5 nm - 100 nm), as compared
to the resulting size from magnetic-dipole interactions (100 nm- 1 µm), and the ability to control the
skyrmion with conducting electrons.5
Figure 2:
Experimental write and subsequent
deletion of individual Skyrmions.3
In order to ﬁnd systems suitable for memory ap-
plications, it is advantageous to be able to pre-
dict skyrmion behaviour in the system a priori.
To do so, the Heisenberg model is employed, and
simulations such as Monte-Carlo simulations are
carried out. The results of such simulations de-
pends critically on the parameters of the Heisen-
berg model, which needs to be determined from
ﬁrst-principles methods for the given system.
The purpose of this thesis is exactly that, to de-
rive, implement and test a method to calculate
the DMI parameter a priori, using the state-
of-the-art commercial simulation software suite
QuantumATK from Synopsys®.

2.2
Density Functional Theory (DFT)
Solving the many-body problem in Eq. (7) is an exceedingly demanding and untractable task, to
exemplify how demanding, one can roughly estimate the memory required to save the many-body wave
function. Assume that a wave function can be reproduced by merely 10 measurements of its value in
each of the cartesian coordinates. Then the one-electron wave function takes up 10×10×10×8 B = 8 kB
of memory, here a single measurement takes up 8 B. For a three-electron wave function, since the
electrons interact, their positions are interdependent and it cannot be factorized as a product of three
one-electron wave functions. As such it requires 109 × 8 B = 8 GB to store a three-electron wave
function. As can be seen, the memory requirement of the many-body wave function grow way too
quickly, for it to be feasible to store it for any physical system containing many free electrons.

The self-consistent cycle is started by making an initial anzats for the electron density, the better
the anzats, the quicker the cycle will reach self-consistency. A way to make a decent anzats, is by
constructing the initial density from a Linear Combination of Atomic Orbitals (LCAO) using Eq. (12).

density, which can then be used as the initial density of the next iteration. This cycle is then continued
until some convergence threshold is reached. After self-consistency have been achieved, the ground
state electron density can be used to calculate a number of system properties, such as magnetization,
band diagrams, atomic forces etc.

2.4
Spin-lattice Hamiltonians
In order to analyse and design properties of complex systems, the complicated many-body Hamiltonian
is often replaced by a simple model Hamiltonian. Depending on the model Hamiltonian, a great range
of properties of the system can eﬃciently be derived to a reasonable precision. Therefore a lot of
work have been done on making good mappings between the many-body Hamiltonian and such model
Hamiltonians. This thesis is a work in that direction, with the simple model Hamiltonian being the
spin-lattice Hamiltonian, known from statistical physics as the Heisenberg model. The simpler version
of this model, called the Ising model, was the simplest model to predict phase transitions, and have
been extensively studied and exactly solved in one and two dimensions.15,16

Symmetries of DMI
A necessary requirement for DMI is Spin-Orbit (SO) coupling, since without it, spin-space is decoupled
from real-space. Consider a unitary transformation U, which mirrors spin-space but not real-space.
Consider also a spin-spiral, like the ones shown in Figure 5a. Since spin-space and real-space are de-
coupled, U can be chosen so that it mirrors the spin-spiral into its chiral counterpart, without changing
the real-space environment. Therefore the Hamiltonian is invariant under the unitary transformation
U†HU = H, and the two spin-spirals are degenerate.
SO coupling is a necessity but not a suﬃciency for DMI, in the case of a symmetric environment,
the mirror transformation still leaves the Hamiltonian unchanged, even with SO coupling. Therefore
a symmetry-breaking environment is needed together with SO coupling, in order to have DMI. The
symmetry breaking environment can, for example, be a surface, as is seen in Fig. 5b. An isotropic
surface was considered here, but if the substrate is anisotropic, and has symmetry-breaking in-plane
features, the two other mirror symmetries seen in Fig. 5b can also be broken.

1. If a center of inversion exists in the middle of two sites i and j, then dij = 0.
2. If a mirror-plane exists, which are perpendicular to Rij = Ri −Rj, and passes through the
middle of the two sites i and j, then dij is parallel to this mirror-plane.
3. If a mirror-plane exists, which contain both sites i and j, then dij is perpendicular to this plane.
4. If a twofold rotation axis, perpendicular to Rij, passes through the middle of the two sites i and
j, then dij is perpendicular to this axis.
5. If a n-fold axis (n ≥2) along Rij exists, dij is parallel to Rij.

Force Theorem
Andersens local force theorem, ﬁrst thought up by Andersen27 and later coined ”The Force Theo-
rem”,28,29 states that the total energy diﬀerence, from the addition of a small perturbation such as
the spin-orbit coupling, can be determined within DFT from the band-energy diﬀerences between a
well-converged scalar-relativistic Kohn-Sham calculation, and a single Kohn-Sham iteration with spin-
orbit coupling, using the density of the well-converged calculation as the input density. This theorem
works due to the total energy functional being variational in the electron density, and a systematic
cancellation of terms up to second order in the change in the electron density δn.

of the self-consistent calculation, an input density is supplied and an output density is constructed.
The total energy at each step, is a functional of both of these densities, since the eﬀective potential is
calculated from the input density.

density used as input to generate n1, is denoted n0.

The advantage of using the Force Theorem to calculate the MAE (and other quantities), is that

Discussion & comparison to experiments
Considering the results shown in Table 1, one peculiarity really sticks out. As was discussed in the
preceding section, the [110]- and [1¯10]-directions are equivalent for the Fe-Au slab. As such, the DMI-
energies of the spin-spirals in these directions should be the same. But not only is the out-of-plane
component fairly diﬀerent in the two directions, the component perpendicular to the spin-spiral is
almost 5 times as large in the [1¯10]-direction, as in the [110]-direction.
A possible cause for this, is the relaxation of the structure. It is possible that relaxing the Fe-Au slab
broke a symmetry. DMI is known to vary greatly, with small changes in the atomic lattice, so even a
small dislocation of an atom could have a huge eﬀect.
It could also have been a supercell issue. In the DFT-calculation, it is assumed that the potential
is periodic, with same periodicity as the supercell. But considering the fact that spin-orbit coupling
stabilizes localized spin-structures that are several nm big, a sub-nm supercell will never be able to
replicate such a feature. In a previous work with a Wolfram-Iron (110) slab, shown in App. B, the
choice of supercell was seen to have a huge inﬂuence on the DMI. The method to check if this was the
cause, is fairly straightforward though, one just needs to use a 2×2 supercell, instead of 2×1 for one
spin-spiral and 1×2 for the other spin-spiral. This was however not attempted in this project, due to
the size of such a computation.
Another issue that has to be considered, is the free iron surface of both slabs. DMI is known to
be strong at surfaces, due to the symmetry-breaking of the free surface. But in the results reported
in Table 1, it was impossible to ﬁlter out the DMI of the free iron surface from the DMI from the
interfaces.
A ﬁnal issue with this method of calculating the DMI-vector, is the assumptions that were taken in
order to derive the DMI-vector mappings. In some systems these assumptions are justiﬁed, but in
many systems, DMI between higher order neighbours, as well as through next-nearest and further
away non-magnetic atoms, are not negligible. While they can technically be included in the method,
bigger and bigger wavelength, and thereby supercell size, is needed in order to map onto the diﬀerent
DMI-vectors.
As of yet, these results have only been discussed based on symmetries and well-known properties of
the DMI. As with all a priori calculations, the ultimate test is the comparison to experiments. Mr.
Andr´e Thiaville have reported a DMI in a GaAs-Fe-Au (001) system, with a iron-layer thickness of
1 nm, which is of D = 0.4 mJ m−2. At the time of writing, this result has not yet been published and
all credits for this result goes to Thiaville and his group.

4
Derivation of LKAG formulas
In a paper published in 1987, A. I. Liechtenstein, M. I. Katsnelson, V. P. Antropov and V. A. Gubanov
derived an expression for the isotropic exchange coupling constant Jij of the Heisenberg Hamiltonian.33
This expression, called the LKAG equation after its discoverers, have been widely used in the com-
munity and successfully implemented in QuantumATK, though in a diﬀerent notation.34 The original
derivation have since been expanded to the non-collinear case and even attempted used at deriving
an expression for the DMI.35–37 These DMI expressions were however derived in a Multiple Scattering
Theory (MST) formalism, and is therefore not directly applicable to QuantumATK’s LCAO formal-
ism. A more transferable approach was attempted by V. V. Mazurenko and V. I. Anisimov in Ref.

Test of LKAG formulas
In order to test the LKAG formulas Eq. (98) and Eqs. (106), (107) and (115) for the DMI vector, the
DMI were calculated for some systems whose DMI are well-known in the literature. These systems
were also previously analysed using the supercell method.
This analysis is seen in App.
B. The
ﬁrst of these is a slab consisting of a monolayer of BCC Iron (110) on top of BCC Wolfram (110).
This interface has a lattice mismatch of 6.9% and a strong DMI, due to the magnetic Fe atoms and
the strong spin-orbit coupling of the heavy W atoms. A relaxed and well-converged version of this
structure, was taken from the previous work in App. B, where three layers of wolfram were used
in the slab. In the relaxation of this system, wolframs lattice parameter were imposed on the iron
monolayer, and a supercell of 2×1 simple unit cells in the [001]-direction were considered. The spin-
orbit conﬁguration were acquired like with the Force Theorem, which is with a very well-converged
scalar-relativistic spin-polarized structure, and then a single Kohn-Sham iteration including spin-orbit
coupling. The spins were set collinear in-plane along the [1¯10]-direction.

5.2
Symmetry tests
A necessary requirement for any DMI-vector calculation method, is that the resulting DMI obeys
Moriya’s symmetry rules listed in List 1.
In order to test this, a couple of simple systems were
constructed, in which the direction of the DMI-vector can be inferred directly from the symmetry
rules. None of the systems considered in this section were relaxed, as relaxations could break the
perfect symmetries that allows the inference of the direction of the DMI-vector. Convergence with
regards to the DFT-parameters were not checked either, as this should not be necessary, when only
the direction of the DMI-vector, and not its magnitude, is of interest. The values of the DMI-vectors
will still be reported, for completeness, but should as such not be taken seriously.

plane. According to Moriya-rule  2, the DMI-vector is parallel to this mirror-plane. Considering the
mirror-plane spanned by the 3 atoms, according to Moriya-rule  3, the DMI-vector is perpendicular
to this plan. As such the DMI-vector of the chromium trimer should be strictly out-of-plane compared
to the trimer. Checking if this is the case:

CONCLUSION & OUTLOOK
6
Conclusion & Outlook
The goals of this thesis were twofold.
The ﬁrst was to analyse a GaAs-Fe-Au (001) slab, which
is a system promising for memory applications, as a device using magnetic skyrmions as information
carriers. This was partly successful, as the calculated microscopic DMI of 0.53 mJ m−2 is in reasonable
agreement with the experimental value of 0.4 mJ m−2. It was only partly successful though, as the
reason for this agreement with experiments was assumed to be caused by a cancellation of errors.
It was also found, that both the GaAs-Fe interface, and the Fe-Au interface, enhances out-of-plane
magnetic anisotropy. The GaAs-Fe interface MAE enhancement was found to be double in size to
that of the Fe-Au interface.
The second goal of this thesis, was to derive, implement and test a method, to eﬃciently calculate the
DMI-vectors. This goal was also only partly achieved, as the initial method failed and the subsequently
derived method still had some issues. The fully relativistic LKAG method, derived in this thesis, does
show great promise though, as it is easily carried out and is usable both in continuous systems and
molecules. The method showed all the correct physics, obeying Moriya’s symmetry rules, as well as
showing enhancement of the DMI between magnetic atoms, at surfaces, and close to heavy elements
with strong spin-orbit coupling. It did however not yield satisfactory values, for the systems tested
in this thesis. This was believed to be due to a wrong handling of the non-orthogonality of the used
basis sets.
To ﬁnd the ﬂaws in this newly-derived method in the future, it would be advantageous to work with
simpler systems, such as dimers, or the Cr-trimer brieﬂy looked upon in this thesis. Doing so would
allow for much faster testing, and the direction of the DMI-vectors could be directly inferred from the
Moriya symmetry rules. Of the tests to be done, it would be interesting to check the importance of
including spin-orbit coupling self-consistently, which was not possible for the duration of this project.
It would also be interesting to try out other basis sets, in particular it would be interesting to try
out a orthogonal one, to check if the methods ﬂaw really does lie in a wrong handling of the non-
orthogonality.
Once the ﬂaws of the method have been ﬁgured out, it would be interesting to analyse the GaAs-Fe-Au
(001) slab with this method, to check if the success with the supercell method, were indeed due to a
cancellation of errors.
