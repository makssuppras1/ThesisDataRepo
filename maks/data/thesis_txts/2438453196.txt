Abstract
Capacitive micromachined ultrasound transducers (CMUT) is a device used to emit and sense
ultrasonic waves which is useful for ultrasound imaging in the medical industry. The traditional
piezoelectric device suﬀer from impedance mismatch limiting the performance and complicates
the fabrication process by adding a number of layers to improve the mismatch, therefore CMUT
is an advantageous choice for the next generation.
Recently the CMUT has been fabricated using wafer bonding which compared to sacriﬁcial
release has some advantages: processing time, number of processing steps and separation of
cavity geometry and plate thickness. The conventional use of SOI wafers for fabrication of
CMUTs utilizes the etching selectivity in KOH (potassium hydroxide), however the SOI wafers
(silicon-on-insulator wafers) have a non-uniformity of ±0.5 µm thus eﬀecting vital properties
like the resonance frequency and the pull-in voltage especially. The boron etch-stop also utilizes
selective properties in KOH, however it uses the selectivity between silicon and highly doped
silicon. Since a CMUT was previously fabricated using a double LOCOS process (local oxidation
of silicon) with a plate thickness of 2.02 ± 0.03 µm, thicker plates were investigated in this
project; a process ﬂow was developed for plate thickness optimization and the doping process
was further improved for two doping times. A process ﬂow for fabricating CMUT and for
displaying the top plates was developed.
The theory for planar doping was presented and the junctions depth was found to be propor-
tional to
√
t. Theoretical and practical values were found for the surface roughness during the
presentation of the theory for fusion bonding. The etching theory was explained and modelled
by using an empirical formula in an Arrhenius form. During the investigation several char-
acterization methods were applied and the working principle for each method was presented
separately from the results.
A process ﬂow for plate thickness optimization was developed; during the plate thickness op-
timization the overall process was improved with regards to backside protection and surface
contamination minimization. The etching process was closely monitored for a doping time of
1 h in two diﬀerent KOH etching mixtures at two diﬀerent temperatures, and the interpolated
etching rates were calculated. The etching process ended with a visual assessment of decrease
in hydrogen production which indicated a decrease in etching rate. 1 h of doping resulted in a
mean plate thickness of 1.41 ± 0.28 µm. The knowledge of the etching process was applied for
a doping time of 6 h. The oxygen content was adjusted to avoid substrate damage on one hand
and on the other non-uniformity resulting in a mean plate thickness of 4.30 ± 0.34 µm.
A process, for fabricating a CMUT device using a RIE (reactive ion etching) for cavities and
applying the plate doped for 6 h, was developed and executed until the wafers delaminated

Chapter 1
Introduction
Capacitive micromachined ultrasound transducers (CMUTs) were ﬁrst introduced in the 1990’s
[18][29], since then CMUTs have been researched considerably because of the numerous poten-
tial advantages to the traditional piezoelectric ultrasound transducer, which is used for a number
of applications, for example ultrasound imaging. The most crucial advantage is the improved
impedance match [13][20][23], which eﬀects the amount of energy transferred at a medium
boundary when an acoustic signal propagates through the boundary. As a consequence of the
impedance mismatch, the fabrication process of piezoelectric transducers is complicated and
limits the geometry of the device in turn limiting bandwidth and electric impedance [23]. The
fabrication of CMUTs is thus less complicated and the impedance match is better. The concept
of a CMUT is sketched in ﬁgure 1.1; a bias is applied over two metal electrodes covering the
front and back of a cavity made of an insulating material, which induces a capacitance due
to an accumulation of charges between the plates. The applied bias can be a combination of
direct (DC) and alternating (AC) bias to induce vibrations in the clamped plate and thus emit-
ting waves with a wavelength above 2 MHz for ultrasonic imaging. When the bias is a direct
bias, incoming waves actuates the plate which changes the distance between the electrodes thus
eﬀecting the measured capacitance.
Figure 1.1: Skecth of the principle of a CMUT with a cavity with a height of gv in an insulating
material with a thickness of gi. A thin plate with thickness h covers the the cavity with a radius
of a if the cavity is round and L if the cavity is square[13].

1.1
CMUT fabrication
The ﬁrst CMUT devices were fabricated by using the sacriﬁcial release method, which consists
of depositing the membranes layer on top of a sacriﬁcial layer and then removing the layer
underneath the membranes thus creating underlying cavities[14]. The biggest drawback of this
method is the limitation of long etch times for the cavities[20]. The alternative method to
the sacriﬁcial release method is the fusion bonding method, which simpliﬁes the process by
avoiding complicated etching channels followed by sealing the cavities inducing long etch times
due to the capillary eﬀect[20]. Another advantage of the bonding method is the separation of
the deﬁning parameters of the dimensions of the membrane and the cavities [20].
The precise process can vary from device to device, but mainly the process consists of the
following steps:

4. Thinning handle wafer as shown in ﬁgure 1.2(c)
5. Metallization shown in ﬁgure 1.2(d)

A CMUT is often characterized by two important parameters; the resonance frequency is an
acoustic parameter and the pull-in voltage is an electrical parameter [21][22][36]. Both param-

frequency band of the CMUT, no wave is emitted or detected. However the CMUT is quite
broadband thus this is less of an issue, unless the bandwidth is decreased drastically due to
thickness increase [13]. Such an increase in plate thickness can improve the sensitivity of the
device consequently decreasing the bandwidth [13]. Traditionally the resonance frequency is
also used to evaluate the coupling coeﬃcient which is an expression for the eﬃciency of the
energy transfer between the wave and the device.

1.3. BORON ETCH-STOP
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.3
Boron etch-stop
Since non-uniformity eﬀects important CMUT properties like the pull-in voltage and the reso-
nance frequency to previously explained great extent, another etch-stop technique could provide
a more favorable uniformity in a cheaper way. Such an etch-stop requires a high selectivity be-
tween silicon and a material suitable to produce a thin top plate regarding mechanical and
electrical properties. KOH (potassium hydroxide) has anisotropic etching properties in silicon
and a high selectivity between highly doped silicon and silicon with a low doping concentration.
Furthermore, the etch-stop technique has the low cost advantage[12] and extensive substrate
damage is avoided, which is not a possibility with dry etch stop techniques. The concept of
selectivity based on doping concentration was ﬁrst reported in 1969[31], but the best known
publication is Seidel et al. [31], who also tested ethylenediamine pyrocatechol as an etchant
despite its toxic properties.
A CMUT was previously successfully fabricated using a double local oxidation of silicon (LO-
COS) process to deﬁne cavities and a top plate thickness of 2.02 ± 0.03 µm[28], which should
give a theoretic pull-in voltage ratio of 0.99. The pull-in voltage of the aforementioned device
was 242 V; the theoretical minimum pull-in voltage is 236.7 V, which is a signiﬁcantly better
performance than a device based on SOI wafers. The device based on boron etch-stop tech-
nique provided a resonance frequency of 10.9 MHz ± 2.8 %, which is an improvement compared
to the SOI wafer device. Since the resonance frequency is proportional with plate thickness,
the non-uniformity would eﬀect the device linearly. The fabrication of the CMUT with boron
etch-stop techniques uncovered some issues in the process. First, the surface of the top wafers
was roughened and stressed during the doping process, which then was optimized by increasing
the oxygen content and doping the backside. The maximum doping time investigated had a
plate thickness of approximately 2.5 µm and was 90 min[28].

Theory for Process Development
The purpose of this chapter is to provide the reader with knowledge and understanding of the

theory for doping, bonding and etching crystal silicon wafers is explained. An earlier doping

2.1
Doping
Doping is the introduction of impurities into the substrate. The impurities can be interstitial
or substitutional or crystal defects of diﬀerent kinds.
Doping can occur by using diﬀerent
methods and is necessary to change the electrical properties of the device. Ion implantation
is a preferred method due to its precise control of dopant level and junction depth [24]. The
disadvantage is mainly excessive damage of the substrate material due to the energy enriched
particle bombarding the sample[24]. Planar source doping is a batch process taking place in a
furnace at high temperatures above 1100 ◦C. The silicon substrate wafers are placed in a boat
between source wafers, which emit doping species into the surrounding atmosphere. The doping
ﬁlled air is then transferred to the substrate surface and because of the high temperature, the
doping diﬀuses into the substrate. The high temperatures can be a limiting factor for the process
before the doping as it could melt or contaminate the silicon bulk by diﬀusing into the substrate
if the surface was covered by a thin ﬁlm like photoresist or metal. In this chapter the theory of
diﬀusion of doping into the substrate is explained. Previous work is presented to prepare the
reader for any results related to this phenomenon like concentration and measurements thereof.

2.4
Theory of Characterization Methods
During the process development, diﬀerent characterization methods were used, and in this
section the working principles of the machines are explained.
2.4.1
AFM
The precision and the resolution of AFM are especially applicable to measuring surface proper-
ties such as the surface roughness on materials e.g. silicon [10]. AFM is based on the attractive
and repulsive forces between one atom and another, which is also called intermolecular forces
most often described by the Lennard-Jones potential [10]. Diﬀerent modes can be used with an
AFM: contact modes, non-contact modes and tapping modes [10]. In this project, the tapping
mode is applied. In the contact mode, the probe tip touches the surface and is therefore in the
repulsive forces regime; this mode is also the most abrasive and damaging of the modes though
it is the mode with the highest resolution. The non-contact mode operates in the attractive
forces regime and the tip is thus inﬂuenced by long range Van der Waals forces, which is propor-
tional to r−7 (r is distance between two atoms). Due to the rapid decrease in force, the mode
is diﬃcult to operate despite the gentle qualities of this mode. The tapping mode combines
the previous two modes, keeping the substrate protected, however the resolution is maintained.
The working principle of the instrument is sketched in ﬁgure 2.17; the height of a cantilever
with an attached tip is measured with laser and a photodetector, whose feedback is calibrated
via the computer. The sample is located on a piezo operated stage, which is controlled in the
x-, y- and z-direction.

roughness after doping[3].

The photoluminescence mapper has a white light source directed toward a rotating sample via

2.4.3
SEM
A SEM probes a sample by using electrons and the energies of these. The layout of the in-
strument is sketched in ﬁgure 2.19; electrons are emitted from the gun, they are focused in
the electromagnetic condenser lens, the deﬂection coil controls the scanning spot and the ﬁ-
nal lens focuses the scanning spot. The energy of the electron beam can vary in the interval
0.1 keV-30 keV[10]. When the electrons hit the sample, they scatter randomly within a speciﬁc
interaction volume; with a higher acceleration voltage, the electrons have more energy to scatter
and thus the volume becomes deeper and more pear-shaped as shown in ﬁgure 2.20(a)[10]. Vari-
ous species can be measured with diﬀerent detectors as illustrated in ﬁgure 2.19 and 2.20(b)[10].
The backscattering electrons generated from elastic scattering usually have an energy above
50 eV. Because of the high energy, the electrons can be generated deeper into the substrate,
which is noted by tBSE in ﬁgure 2.20(b). Due to the probing depth of this species, it is opti-
mal to investigate the density of the sample instead of the topology[10]. The topology can be

Process Development
In this chapter, the initial investigation and optimization of the development of the etch stop

3.1
Overview of the Fabrication Process
The process ﬂow roughly consists of 5 steps; these steps are sketched in ﬁgure 3.1 and are listed
below:
1. A 4”, n-type 350 µm thick silicon wafer was doped in a furnace where temperature,
doping time, gas ﬂow and gas composition are adjustable parameters.
2. A 110 nm thick oxide layer was grown on a 4”, n-type 525 µm thick bottom silicon wafer.
3. The two wafers were bonded by using fusion bonding, also called direct silicon bond-
ing.
4. The backside of the bottom wafer was protected by a 200 nm thick nitride layer, though
a mechanical device was tested as shown in ﬁgure 3.2.
5. The bonded wafer was ﬁrst roughly etched, followed by a selective etch.
The ﬁrst step in the process is doping. Doping of the top wafer is necessary to generate the
stop layer, whose concept was previously explained. In ﬁgure 3.1(b) this step is sketched and
in ﬁgure 2.1 the wafer placement in the furnace is sketched; the wafer was RCA-cleaned prior
to doping. The parameters for the doping are listed in table 3.1[28] where the time and gas
composition can be altered to investigate plate thickness and optimize the etched surface. The
recommended gas ﬂow for a furnace diameter of 16 cm is 7 SLM to avoid backstreaming[34],
though others have reported that a lower gas ﬂow can be applied without compromising the
result[28]. The critical parameter regarding the gas ﬂow is the oxygen ratio of the total gas
ﬂow. The recommended ratio is a percentage of 2.5 % oxygen, however by increasing the ratio,
the surface damage is minimized resulting in a better bonding of the wafers due to a smoother
surface[34]. The ratio is therefore increased to 7.4 % oxygen[28]. The doping step was followed

3.7
Summary
In this chapter the overall process ﬂow for top plate optimization was improved in various areas;
backside protection, bonding and contamination minimization. The etching process was opti-
mized for plates doped for 1 h; the bonded wafers were etched in two diﬀerent mixtures where
the mixture, with the highest selectivity, had a weight percentage of 20 % and a temperature
of 80 ◦C. This process produced a plate with a mean thickness of 1.41 ± 0.28 µm. The doping
process was improved for 6 h doping by increasing the oxygen ﬂow to 8.8 % of the total gas
ﬂow, which produced a plate with a thickness of 4.30 ± 0.35 µm. An oxygen content of 10.3 %
was also tested and the non-uniformity was higher. Pyramid-shaped hillocks were observed for
both doping times; a relation between base area and height was derived and it was observed
that the height appeared to increase with doping time.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The objective of this project was to optimize the fabrication process of top plates for CMUTs by
using KOH etch-stop which utilizes the selectivity of highly boron-doped silicon. Two process
ﬂows were developed during this project: one for plate thickness optimization and one for a
relatively fast CMUT fabrication to display the plates. The process of optimizing top plate
thicknesses included oxidization of the bottom wafer, doping of the top wafer, bonding of the
two wafers and etching roughly and selectively of the bonded wafer. The theory hereof was
presented; the theory for the doping included an explanation of planar source doping and by
using previous concentration data a simulation resulted in two junction depths of 2 µm and
6 µm for doping times of 1 h and 6 h respectively. In practice, commercial boron source wafers
dopes the silicon wafers in a thermal process . The theory of bonding limited the range of the
surface roughness. The physical principle for KOH etching was presented and a model for the
etching rate in silicon was developed based on experimental data.
During the execution of the plate thickness optimization process two overall improvements were
found: protection of the backside of the bonded layer in the form of a 200 nm nitride layer and
optimization of the bonding quality by minimizing surface contamination.
Due to previous work, the etching process was optimized using a doping time of 1 h. The etching
process was investigated for two KOH mixtures for plates doped for 1 h using the total thickness
of the bonded wafers; 28 % KOH, which is a standard concentration, at 70 ◦C and 20 % at 80 ◦C,
which have the highest selectivity proven by the lowest surface concentration after etching. XPS
was tested as a method of detecting the surface concentration however this method could only
distinguish silicon and silicon oxide, therefore SIMS was used for this purpose. The interpolated
etching rates dropped visibly when the stop layer was reached; the stop layer could therefore
be detected in other ways than by subjective visual assessment. The mean plate thicknesses
were 1.50 ± 0.49 µm and 1.41 ± 0.28 µm respectively which were approximately 0.5 µm lower
than expected; the diﬀerence could have been due to the subjective estimate of when the
etch-stop has been reached. The relation between the mean plate thicknesses and the surface
concentrations was slightly diﬀerent from previous work and from the speciﬁcations provided by
the source wafer manufacturer, however the surface concentration was high than the expected
threshold which is consisting with a thinner top plate.
The doping time was increased to 6 h to increase the plate thickness. The doping process was
optimised in regards to oxygen when the doping time was 6 h due to pits in the surface measured
using AFM. The surface roughness was also measured using AFM; it showed a slight increase
of roughness with an increase of doping time. The plate thickness for this time was between

Chapter 6
Outlook
Since the top plate disintegrated during the etching of the CMUTs, the obvious continuation
would be to improve the process with focus on improving the stress and the bonding. The stress
can be investigated by measuring the wafer bow and is likely caused by the compressive stress
of the boron layer causing the wafer into a dome-like shape; if the waferbow is unavoidable,
a bowl-like shape is more preferable during the bonding and this can be achieved by ﬂipping
the top wafer so the previous bonding side becomes the etched side. Another solution could
be depositing a layer to counteract the stress during bonding to improve the bonding quality
to remove it before etching. Besides being dependent on the curvature, the bonding quality
also depends on the surface roughness which was not thoroughly investigated in this project; it
was measured during the initial investigation, however it could have changed with a new batch;
this could be improved by optimizing the oxygen content further. Additionally, The bonding
quality could be improved by changing the layout of the lithography mask. The cavity mask
could be improved by reducing the number of devices on a wafer thus increasing the bonding
area and the safety margin at the edge of the wafer; this could serve multiple purposes: the
increased bonding area strengthens the bond, and, if the KOH ﬂows into one cavity, it can
be contained by a safety margin. The edges could also be protected by a combination of the
mechanical holder from the start of this project and a nitride layer on the backside of the wafer
to counter for the limits of the mechanical holder. The method of bonding could be changed;
Recently anodic bonding has been investigated, though it is less predictable, the method is
less eﬀected by contamination. However anodic bonding is equivalently eﬀected by the bonding
area in comparison with fusion bonding thus increase of the bonding area would be the primary
concern on the level with the stress.
If the stress and the bonding are improved, a device can hopefully be fabricated characterized
acoustically and electrically.
A secondary point to optimize would be the purity of the etching solution to avoid the formation
of hillocks; this could be done by controlling the introduction of CO2 to the solution and could be
characterized in an optical microscope. A fascinating result in this project was the inconsistency
between the measurements and the simulation which could be improved by further investigating
the doping proﬁle at diﬀerent doping times; however this proves diﬃcult due to the limited
access to a SIMS thus other methods for determining the doping concentration are required.
The four point probe can measure the sheet resistance however the sheet resistance depends on
an eﬀective resistivity or thickness which means that the relation between concentration and
thickness is unknown. The doping process could further be investigated with respect to the
