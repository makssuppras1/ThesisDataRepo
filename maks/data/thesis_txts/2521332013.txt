Abstract
Wastewater Treatment Plants are an essential part of the modern overarching san-
itation system. The current control strategies have proven to be inadequate espe-
cially during events of heavy rainfall. There are strong incentives of economical,
social, scienti’¼üc and environmental nature to continuously improve these control
strategies.
The fundamental goal of this thesis is to model the sludge blanket height in sec-
ondary clari’¼üer tanks. This is achieved using both 1) theoretically based determin-
istic advection-diffusion partial differential equations and 2) empirically driven
stochastic differential equations. The ’¼ürst part of the thesis is spent on the former
model. The investigation is of mathematical nature, through the presented theory.
The second part of the thesis develops the stochastic model. Its purpose is entirely
application-based, and is used directly to predict sludge blanket heights, based on
measurements obtained from Damhus├źen Renseanl├”g.
The deterministic modelling demonstrates the effects of various parameters, and
shows sensible sludge concentration pro’¼üles which con’¼ürm the possibility that
sludge blankets can form. Furthermore, two methods to determine the sludge
blanket height are demonstrated. The dynamical solution curves to various com-
plex forms of the partial differential equation are presented. The solutions behave
over all well, but display certain ’¼éaws in the implementation which require cor-
rection.
The stochastic modelling iteratively improves a model by expanding its parame-
ters into more sophisticated function expressions via random walks. The standard
deviation on the model estimates is reduced from an initial value of 0.35 meters
to 0.30 meters at the three-hour prediction level. The models are clearly seen to
be improving visually for a three-hour prediction horizon as well. The best per-
forming model increases the fraction of measurements within its 95% con’¼üdence
interval by roughly 0.10 up from 0.85 for a one to ten hour prediction horizon.

Introduction
Over the last century humans have become increasingly aware of their role as
stewards of EarthŌĆÖs climate. It is accepted today as an axiomatic truth that our ac-
tions in fact have an enormous environmental impact, and that this impact needs
to be dramatically reduced in the future. Waste Water Treatment Plants (WWTPs)
are an essential part of controlling pollution of natural water bodies and serve as
an integral part of the modern overarching sanitation system. Their primary pur-
pose is to treat wastewater in order to avoid undesired environmental impacts on
surface waters and groundwater (Tchobanoglous et al. 2003). The treatment pro-
cess can include various steps e.g mechanical, chemical or biological. The focus of
this thesis will primarily be on the ’¼ünal step in the treatment process, namely the
sludge settling. This step is carried out in a speci’¼üc basin, the secondary clari’¼üer,
however its performance is strongly correlated with the previous treatment step
and the incoming water. The previous treatment is conducted in an aeration tank.
This basin-tank pair comprises the setup of the so-called activated sludge process.
Incoming wastewater is treated in the aeration tank (henceforth referred to as the
biological tank) by the bacteria already present in the wastewater, which reacts
(under suitable conditions) with the organic material abundantly present in the
water to create ’¼éocculents. The water/sludge mixed liquid is subsequently chan-
neled to the secondary clari’¼üer tank (SCT) where clari’¼üed water (denoted ef’¼éuent)
is seperated from the sludge by gravitational settling, and released back into seas,
rivers or streams. The dynamics of this settling process is in most situations such
that a seperation is clearly visible at a very particular depth (as a consequence of
so-called hindered settling (Tchobanoglous et al. 2003)), resembling the seperation
of water from oil. The depth at which this clear seperation occurs is referred to as
the sludge blanket height (SBH).
To promote high quality surface and ground water strict legislative demands are
enforced upon WWTPs to make sure that the concentration of speci’¼üc nutrients
is kept at a minimum, regulated at ministry level with substantial taxations and
potential ’¼ünes should they fail to meet these demands (Henze et al. 2014) (Skat-
1
==PAGE:9==
teministeriet 2020). It is seldomly problematic under relatively dry conditions to
properly operate well designed WWTPs whose dimensions match the catchment
area they are serving, however it is ’¼ünancially infeasible to design WWTPs that
can handle such extreme events and general cost-optimization draws substantial
attention e.g. (Peter A. Vanrolleghem et al. 1996), (Benedetti, Bixio, et al. 2006)
and (Benedetti and P. A. Vanrolleghem 2006). When such events occur the incom-
ing quantities of wastewater greatly surpass the capacity of the WWTPs causing
partial loss of control. In the worst-case scenario it can be necessary to completely
bypass the incoming wastewater around the facility. This means entirely skipping
the biological treatment altogether. Bypasses are usually done either to avoid the
bacterial mass quality deteriorating as a consequence of dilution or because of
an extraordinary high sludge blanket resulting in poor ef’¼éuent quality. The latter
emphasizes the necessity for accurate sludge blanket control strategies, though in
general terms better SBH control will also enable WWTPs to run more optimally,
at capacities closer to their designed limits. Such control strategies will inevitable
be an integral part of modern controlled facilities with common control strate-
gies between catchment areas, sewer systems and WWTPs (Thyrsing 2019). It is
necessary if this technology is to be pursued that models with improved predic-
tion accuracy are developed. This argument provides a justi’¼ücation for the work
and research presented in this thesis, the development of improved sludge blanket
height models.
It is the modelling of the behaviour and dynamics of the secondary clari’¼üer that is
the scope of this thesis. The objective is, more speci’¼ücally, to be able make accu-
rate predictions of the location (depth) of the SBH and in long-term to incorporate
these models into on-line control algorithms to improve the current solutions that
are observed to operatore poorly during rainy periods. The problem has been
addressed extensively in the litterature in the nineties for one-dimensional mod-
els, notably by (Tak├Īcs et al. 1991) with further development by (Hamilton et al.
1992) and (Watts et al. 1996), who have shown reliable SBH prediction results
using a discretization of a governing partial differential equation. These models
do however not appear to have been incorporated fully into industrial solutions
despite the passing of two decades. More extensive higher dimensional models
(usually based on computational ’¼éuid dynamics theory) have also been developed
but are generally used for design optimization etc. They are usually unsuited for
control applications (that require frequent updating) due to the demand of high
computational speed when intended for on-line use (Grijspeerdt et al. 1995), and
are further unsuited for this work due to the nature of the available data - one-
dimensional height measurements.

Chapter 2 present an overview of the Damhus├źen Renseanl├”g WWTP from which
the data used has been collected (height measurements, various water ’¼éows and
suspended solids (SS)). The chapter also elaborates on both the treatment process
and the secondary clari’¼üer. In Chapter 3 and Chapter 5 respectively, the thesis
works toward establishing two models (based on differential equations), 1. in-
spired by the work of (Tak├Īcs et al. 1991), (Hamilton et al. 1992) (Watts et al.
1996) and others, derived from physical principles and another 2. which is de-
rived emperically (driven primarily by the measurements). The former model (.1)
is a partial differential equation that arises from solid ’¼éux theory and its solutions
describe the concentration pro’¼üles in the secondary clari’¼üer, which can be used
to indirectly retrieve the sludge blanket height. The solutions are obtained us-
ing a high convergence spectral-method scheme. The details of the theory and
implementation regarding this scheme are described and the associated numeri-
cal solutions are presented. The emperical model (.2) is obtained from propos-
als based on a qualitative understanding of the physical processes and takes the
form of a stochastic differential equation (SDE). It is gradually expanded, ’¼ürstly
by hypothesizing extensions, and later by iteratively expanding its embedded pa-
rameters into functions of the inputs. A few function expressions are investigated
and the resulting models are compared using standard statistical measures. The
embedded parameters in the resulting SDEs are estimated using the R/C++ pack-
age Template Model Builder (TMB) by (K. Kristensen et al. 2016) which employs
a maximum-likelihood-estimate (MLE) approach.
The parameter estimation is
achieved using measurements over the course of ten days. The performance of the
individual models are evaluated by inspection of their accuracy over a 3-hour pre-
diction period. The thesis ends with a summarized discussion of the two methods,
a conclusion and an outlook of the potential of the work.
It should be noted that the work presented here only uses as inputs to its mod-
els the incoming ’¼éow from the biologic reactors as well as measurements of the
suspended solids. Inclusion of other drivers of sludge blanket growth such as de-
sign parameters, bacterial growth dynamics or sludge characterisics were deemed
outside the scope of this project, and is probably more suited for a project with
a duration of several years. It is however important to remember that while in-
clusion of more parameters and dynamics potentially increases model accuracy
and performance it also increases complexity and computation time, which might
make it unsuited for control.

Theoretical Model
The solid ’¼éux theory is a well-known mathematical formulation of the dynamics
of (hindered) settling. It was initially proposed by (Kynch 1951) and combines
the mass conversation equation (a time-dependent partial differential equation)
with the assumption that the settling velocity is determined solely by the local
concentration. The primary goal of this chapter is to mathematically investigate
the dynamics of the secondary clari’¼üer and visualize how various parameters affect
the sludge concentration pro’¼üles. In the ’¼ürst section the governing differential
equation will be presented. In the second section an introduction to the numerical
solution method, a so-called spectral method, is given and the remainder of the
chapter is spent presenting and discussing various increasingly complex forms of
the partial differential equation.

Summary and Discussion
In the previous chapter the solid ’¼éux theory and mass conversation equation have
been introduced and the resulting partial differential equation has been solved in
various forms using an effective spectral scheme. The general intuition of the clari-
’¼üerŌĆÖs behaviour was mirrored in the solutions with regards to the effect of diffusion
and settling parameters. Realistic values namely gave rise to sudden concentration
changes that could be interpreted as the formation of a sludge blanket. Two meth-
ods to determine the location of the sludge blanket from the concentration pro’¼üles
were also presented. They performed well initially but produced erroneous results
in the last simulations in Figure 3.12 and Figure 3.13 (and are therefore omitted).
The temporal solutions of the PDE revealed the effect of in’¼éow, return ’¼éow and
suspended solids concentration, and responded in general according to expecta-
tions, though various elements of the solutions should be discussed further.
It is clear that there are numerous assumptions underlying the modelling ap-
proach. Firstly due to the one-dimensional modelling of an in fact three-dimensional
problem several assumptions must be made. The primary ones are 1) that the
’¼éows distribute uniformly across the cross sectional area of the clari’¼üer, and 2)
that horizontal ’¼éux contributions are neglectable. These assumptions in general
simplify the otherwise likely chaotic behaviour that liquids can exhibit, and there-
fore the possible turbulence caused by high in’¼éows or return ’¼éows are neglected.
Another deeply embedded assumption is also that the liquid exhibit homogenous
identical particles with identical properties for instance with respect to buoyancy,
35
==PAGE:43==
’¼éocculation and bacterial growth. This is known in the literature to be a gross sim-
pli’¼ücation. It is important to be aware of these assumptions because they might be
interesting in a model development perspective to be used for control. Here it is
crucial to only introduce the principle variance variables as a compromise between
complexity and computation times.
It is natural to ask what the current model is might be lacking, and which ’¼éaws
its solutions display relative to what is expected to be observed. Admittedly, while
the current implementation serves well to develop an understanding of the clari-
’¼üer, it is believed that alterations are necessary if more realistic scenarios are to be
modelled. Firstly the literature suggests, in accordance with the authorŌĆÖs obtained
experience during derivation and implementation, that careful considerations are
necessary when dealing with the boundary and inlet (Ekama et al. 1997). Clearly
the "valleys" seen at the inlet position in the solutions in e.g. Figure 3.12 and 3.13
reveal exactly the opposite effect that the inlet ought to have. This alleged ’¼éaw is
also not seen in the literature solution curves e.g. (Watts et al. 1996) and (Tak├Īcs
et al. 1991). An additional undesirable side effect of this is that interferes with
the ability to detect a sludge blanket through the gradient because ’¼üctitious large
gradients appear around the inlet point. Another challenge appears to be that the
characteristic time of the dynamics, i.e. the temporal velocity of the pro’¼üle, is
much higher than expected. This seems to be backed up by the actual height mea-
surements from Damhus├źen Renseanl├”g, which are presented in high resolution
in Chapter 5, because they show the reverse behaviour, a rather slow develop-
ment. It is true that the measurements do not directly depict the concentration
pro’¼üle but should arguably be strongly correlated to it. A possible contribution to
this problem could be the currently used term for the gravitational settling. This
has also been addressed in (Tak├Īcs et al. 1991) who alters the form of vs in 3.2
to reduce the settling velocity in low concentration zones arguably to account for
variety in the sludge particle size distributions. The ’¼éux term also causes some
concern. The current implementation dominates when the concentration is high,
because the ’¼éux is proportional to the concentration, while the contribution from
gravity goes to zero. It seems plausible that a decreasing effect in the uppermost
part of the clari’¼üer must be implemented to circumvent this issue.
Since computation times are crucial for control applications it is a must that the
solutions can be obtained fast enough, and therefore that the PDE can be solved
reasonably fast. This is especially due to the fact that multiple evaluations are
needed when applying control algorithms. This was initially quite promising with
computation times around a second for the solutions with constant ’¼éow values,
36
==PAGE:44==
but was seen to increase dramatically with the time-varying ’¼éows. The solution
time for N = 30, N = 50 and N = 80 was approximately 20, 30 and 60 seconds re-
spectively, which is much too high. It is however likely that this can be improved
by a more effective implementation. It is also likely that ’¼üxing the previously
mentioned ’¼éaws in the model would cause less numerical instability and there-
fore allow the use of a smaller N without producing step-size errors in the ODE
solver.
The established model has provided the necessary basics to model a secondary
clari’¼üer, but further development is necessary before it can be applied to large-
scale plants. The possible future prospects of the model are discussed further in
the concluding Chapter 7.

Intermission
This chapter serves as a formal transition between the two methods of the thesis,
namely the partial differential equation approach and the stochastic differential
equations approach. The nature of these two methods vary substantially and so
too do their aims. The reader will therefore likely notice that the methodology,
tone of writing and precision changes somewhat. This intermission is intended
as a sort of warning. Let us brie’¼éy consider and outline the major differences be-
tween these two approaches before continuing:
PDE:
The former partial differential equation model is highly quantitative and originates
from physical principles and theory. It is in a sense more complete because of the
fact that it yields information beyond just the sludge blanket height, but therefore
it is also, in its current form at least, unable to be employed in any real-world
scenarios to accurately predict blanket heights (not in opposition to the objectives
of the chapter), despite that this certainly is the long-term goal (see Chapter 7).
SDE:
The to-be presented stochastic differential equation model relies much more on
qualitative observations, intuition, understanding and hypothesis of the system
dynamics, but is primarily driven by empirical observations. The expansion and
improvement of the model relies on user ideas and inputs, and attempts to incor-
porate information obtained through statistical analyses. The model is constructed
precisely with the purpose to attempt to predict sludge blanket heights, nothing
else, and yields therefore no further information about the secondary clari’¼üer sys-
tem. In this sense is it naturally tuned for this speci’¼üc task.
With the following differences outlined, we are now ready to continue with the
work, and present the SDE approach.

Summary and Discussion
In this chapter an introduction to stochastic differential equations and stochas-
tic state space models have been given, with a special focus on the Ornstein-
Uhlenbeck process. The chapter also contained an introduction to the software
package Template Model Builder (TMB) which is implemented in R through C++
function templates. It is exceptionally ef’¼ücient at estimating large systems of ran-
dom effects and parameters through the use of the Laplace approximation. These
theories are combined together with measurements obtained from Damhus├źen
Renseanl├”g. These measurements are obtained at high temporal resolutions (ev-
ery minute) which is a particularly important underlying assumption for the sta-
tistical calculations to be accurate. The assumption is that the sampling frequency
is much faster than the system dynamics which enforces the fact that the condi-
tional transition densities of the state remains Gaussian in the sampling interval.
The primary objective of the chapter was to develop a stochastic model for sludge
blanket height predictions and to demonstrate how such a model could be itera-
tively improved to attain the best possible predictions. In a long term perspective
the methodology was also a novel approach to developing better models for con-
trol strategies effectively continuing along the path laid out by (Brok et al. 2019).
The model construction relied initially of a qualitative understanding of the system
dynamics, and later employed a methodology presented by (M├Ėller et al. 2011)
to iteratively expand its embedded parameters. In total, ’¼üve models of increasing
complexity and performance were presented. They were compared against each
other using various statistical measures, root mean squared prediction errors (RM-
SPE) and through visual inspection.
The modelsŌĆÖ accuracy were primarily measured using a three-hour prediction hori-
zon. The associated RMSPE revealed that the standard deviation of the height
estimates was reduced from roughly 0.35 [m] 0.30 [m] over the course of the ’¼üve
models. The ’¼ünal and best performing model was able to improve upon the frac-
tion of observations within its 95% con’¼üdence interval by roughly 0.10 from 0.85
67
==PAGE:75==
to 0.95 when compared with the initial model, for prediction horizons between
one and ten hours.
The work was carried out using only the in’¼éow and the suspended solids concen-
tration to the biological reactor of the wastewater plant. It seems sensible that the
in’¼éow and the suspended solids concentration explain the principle variance (in-
dicated by the ’¼ündings of (Peng et al. 2007)) though it is known that more subtle
dynamics can be in’¼éuencial. It is also worth mentioning that the challenge is gen-
erally regarded as a dif’¼ücult one, for a multitude of reasons, as were previously
mentioned in Chapter 2.
It was originally the intention to expand all parameters as functions of the input
variables however it was discovered that the expansion method could not be car-
ried out in all cases due to numerical challenges. This was the case for the rate
parameters ╦£╬Ė and ╬Ė. The challenges could not be solved by the most common so-
lutions using transformations to the real-axis and improved initial solver guesses,
however this only demonstrates that these numerical procedures are by no means
trivial, and utmost focus is necessary when performed. It is therefore also of great
importance when developing algorithms that are to be implemented for automa-
tion, that such situations are acknowledged and circumventions planned. It is nec-
essary to further the understanding of why the problem occured only for the rate
parameters. It was however evident that an additional naive approach could be
taken, simply by "guessing" relationships. This fact simultaneously emphasizes the
methodŌĆÖs high degree of versatility, where many different function expressions and
hypotheses might potentially work just well, and at the same time this could be
characterized as a drawback. It is (arguably) generally preferred to have models
from underlying physical principles instead of relying on non-interpretable mathe-
matical functions, even if the performance is good. This primary reason for this is
the loss of transparency and hence the dif’¼üculties in adapting the developed model
to new scenarios, other wastewater treatment plants, or something third. It is for
this speci’¼üc reason that it would be interesting to fuse the thesisŌĆÖ two presented
methods. Indeed, the prospects of a combination between the deterministic partial
differential equation approach and the statistical stochastic approach is discussed
brie’¼éy in Chapter 7.

Conclusion
This thesis has developed models to investigate and predict the sludge concentra-
tion pro’¼üles and sludge blanket heights in a secondary clari’¼üer.
A partial differential equation, speci’¼ücally on the form of an advection-diffusion
equation has been solved to obtain sludge concentration pro’¼üles in the secondary
clari’¼üer. The solutions are obtained using a Legendre spectral method which dis-
plays very high convergence. The equation was solved in increasingly complex
forms, which concluded with a dynamical sludge concentration PDE with input
variables as measurement time-series obtained from Damhus├źen Renseanl├”g. The
implemented numerical method as a whole showed stability and good properties
though became computationally slow towards the end, in the most complex set-
ting. A few corrections to the model is proposed, e.g. reconsidering boundary
and inlet equations to correct certain "valleys" seen in the solutions. It was also
deemed appropriate to expand the gravitional settling velocity function to reduce
the overall settling velocity at lower concentrations, which appeared too fast. Two
methods to recover the sludge blanket height from the concentration pro’¼üles are
also presented. They perform ’¼üne in the initial cases but fail in the complex set-
ting. Further investigation into how to best determine the sludge blanket height is
needed.
A stochastic state space model has been iteratively expanded to increase its sludge
blanket height prediction accuracy. The expansion is initially based on qualitative
hypotheses and underlying intution of the secondary clari’¼üer dynamics. This gives
rise to three models of increasing complexity. The best performing model is further
expanded by modelling its embedded parameters as random walks plotted against
the two available input variables, the in’¼éow and the suspended solids concentra-
tion. In this way potential relationships between the parameter and the variable
can be parametrized and replaced into the model for an improved effect. The
procedure succesfully expanded teh long term height mean ┬Ą, but experienced
numerical dif’¼üculties for the two rate parameters ╦£╬Ė and ╬Ė. The issues could not
69
==PAGE:77==
be solved, so the parameter expansion was instead conducted by a naive hypoth-
esis as a linear function of the suspended solids, and this hypothesis was seen to
improve the model signi’¼ücantly. The dif’¼üculties emphasize the necessity for im-
proved understanding of the properties and requires of Template Model Builder.
In tota ’¼üve stochastic models were developed of increasingly better performance.
The ’¼ünal model achieved a 0.05 [m] reduction in the standard deviation of its
prediction estimates, roughly equivalent to a relative decrease of 15%. The model
predictions with uncertainty contained the observations in more than 95% of the
cases, though a valid critique would be that the uncertainties are quite large rela-
tive to the height changes observed.

Outlook
The work presented in this thesis has intentionally been split into two fundamen-
tally different methods. Where the former employs a deterministic partial differ-
ential equation to "exactly" model the entire sludge concentration pro’¼üle in the
secondary clari’¼üer, the latter uses sophisticated estimation methods to model only
the sludge blanket height based on actual measurements. A potential combination
of the two methods could possibly take advantage of their individual strengths and
create an exceptional sludge blanket model.
It is proposed here to construct a stochastic partial differential equation (SPDE) by
extending the developed partial differential equation into a stochastic state-space
model. This is undoubtly a great challenge, for the potential observation equation
would be much more complex than those presented in this thesis. This is partly be-
cause of the fact that the state equation is a partial differential equationthat must
be solved, and partly because the observation equation becomes quite complex.
The "measurement" of the sludge blanket must be determined from the concentra-
tion pro’¼üle slution, and hence using the presented gradient method for instance,
the observation equation would take the form

Simple TMB C++ Template
#include <TMB.hpp>
template<class Type>
Type objective_function<Type>::operator() ()
{
DATA_VECTOR(Y);
// Observations
DATA_VECTOR(iobs);
// Observation Indices
// Parameters
PARAMETER_VECTOR(X);
// Random Effects
PARAMETER(Theta);
// Rate
PARAMETER(Mu);
// Mean
PARAMETER(Sigma);
// Model Noise
//Measurement Noise
PARAMETER(SigmaY);
// Measurements Noise
Type ans=0;
// ans is the negative log-likelihood
vector<Type> dt(tsim.size()-1);
for(int i=0;i<dt.size();i++)
dt(i) = tsim(i+1)-tsim(i);
// Include likelihood contributions from state transitions
for(int i=0;i<dt.size();i++){
ans -= dnorm( X(i+1) , X(i) + Theta * ( Mu - X(i) ) * dt(i) , Sigma*sqrt(dt(i)), 1 );
}
// Include likelihood contributions from measurements
for(int i=0;i<Y.size(); ++i){
int j=CppAD::Integer(iobs(i));
ans -= dnorm(Y(i),X(j),SigmaY,1);
}
return ans;
}
