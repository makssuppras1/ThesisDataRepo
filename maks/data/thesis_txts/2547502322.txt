Abstract
CO oxidation is one of the most widely applied catalytic reactions used for converting the toxic molecule
CO to the more harmless CO2. Today, the standard CO oxidation catalyst consists of the Platinum
Group Metals (PGM) as they eﬃciently facilitate the reaction without succumbing to poisoning. The
major issue with these metals as catalysts for CO oxidation is that they require temperatures above
200 ◦C in order to become active. Gold nanoparticles have shown catalytic properties at room temper-
ature, providing an interesting alternative to PGMs. One major downside to gold is that it is diﬃcult
to obtain stable nanoparticles as they are prone to sintering.
Alloying Au with Ti has been proposed as a possible solution to the stability issues of pure Au, and
in this project, this alloy is further investigated. The particles are deposited using a nanobeam cluster
source in series with a time of ﬂight mass ﬁlter and tested using a microreactor. This setup allows for
the deposition of small quantities of a very well deﬁned system with a high degree of of knowledge of
the particles size which may be tested in the microreactor. During the project, it was found that the
microreactors themselves are contaminated as microreactors without deposited particles showed high
activity for CO oxidation at temperatures around 200 ◦C. It was found that the contamination was
partially removed by sputtering of the microreactor in Ar+ for 40 min. Alternatively, a layer of TiO2
on the microreactor volume also appeared to solve the problem.
It was found that 2.5 nm AuTi nanoparticles on TiO2 had signiﬁcantly higher mass activity than other
support-particle combinations. AuTi on SiO2 showed ﬁne mass activity and stability, but reproduction
of the samples proved to be a issue. Analysing the active and inactive microreactors by ISS and XPS
suggests that something happens during bonding or testing which results in practically no gold being
present on the surface of the reactor volume. This issue of reproducibility is an ongoing subject of
investigation.
The size study of AuTi on SiO2 is largely inconclusive as the aforementioned issue with reproducibility
obstructed reaching a conclusion.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Climate change
The worlds climate has always been subject to oscillatory change as shown in ﬁgure 1.1 where the
atmospheric CO2 concentration is shown over the last 800,000 years. However, a drastic deviation
from the normal oscillations has occurred approximately within the last 200 years, corresponding to
the onset of the industrial revolution. This worrying trend is closely tied to an increase in the global
average temperature caused by our consumption of fossil fuels and the associated increase in CO2
emissions as indicated in ﬁgure 1.1, and even though the global society is painfully aware of this,
there is no indication that the trend of increasing fossil fuel consumption will be reversed within the
foreseeable future as our high standard of living is intimately connected to a high energy consumption.
Most of the world agrees that this cannot continue, and in 2015 all the world’s countries (except Syria,
Nicaragua and USA) signed the Paris agreement, agreeing to limit the average temperature increases
relative to preindustrial levels to no more that 2 ◦C [2].
To reach this goal, many sources of renewable energies are being explored in combination with diﬀerent
technologies needed in order to utilise this energy, where one promising candidate for the latter is
catalysis where electrical energy can supply the energy needed to facilitate a chemical reaction. The
research community allocates a get deal of resources to catalysis, and a promising method for early
stage testing of catalysts is a microreactor in witch very small amounts of catalyst can be tested with
great precision.

1.1. CLIMATE CHANGE
Figure 1.1: Development of global primary energy consumption by source from 1800 to 2018. The

Catalysis is not only used within the energy sector, but is also a key part of countering air pollution
due to the ability to cause a chemical reactor to occur with lower energy costs. One important piece of
technology using catalysis is the catalytic converter used in automotive engines in which heterogeneous
catalysis is applied to break down poisonous compounds such as CO and NOx to harmless compounds.
This application has had a big impact on the pollution especially in cities.
In the 1940-1950s, the air quality in many major cities was in a very bad shape due to the increase in the
number of automotive engines [4]. To solve this problem, catalytic converters were implemented as part
of the exhaust in vehicles where unburnt hydrocarbons and CO was decomposed. Today, such catalytic
converters are mandatory for any automotive vehicle. However, one challenge for catalytic converters
still stand: They are not eﬃcient at temperatures under 200 ◦C, resulting in signiﬁcant emissions as the
vehicle starts and heats up [5]. To solve this problem, Au has been identiﬁed as a possible substitute for
the more common CO oxidation catalysts as it has shown activity at temperatures as low as −70 ◦C[6].
However, Au as a catalyst has several other challenges, and this project has tried to take on some of
them.

1.2. HETEROGENEOUS CATALYSIS
1.2
Heterogeneous Catalysis
In general a catalyst is a material which facilitates a chemical reaction without consuming itself. This
is done by forming bonds with the reactants whereby the reaction can take place and then releasing
the products. A catalyst can be in a multitude of forms such as single atoms, molecules and enzymes.
[7].
This project is based on heterogeneous catalysis in which the catalyst and reactants are in diﬀerent
phases; eg. a solid catalyst and gas phase reactants. In ﬁgure 1.2 a potential energy diagram is shown
illustrating the working principle of catalysis. The catalytic reaction pathway is the solid black line,
the the red and yellow circles illustrate the molecules, and the catalyst surface is illustrated with a solid
orange line. The ﬁrst step is that the catalyst surface adsorbs the molecules, causing the intermolecular
bond to weaken. This facilitates the process of dissociation of a molecule such as oxygen or nitrogen,
O2 →2O∗or N2 →2N∗. This is often a key step in the process of heterogeneous catalysis as breaking
the molecular bond is needed before other bonds can formed. The dissociated species can now react on
the surface and form products which can thereafter leave the surface, leaving room for new reactants
to adsorb. This process is by far more complicated than the uncatalysed pathway (illustrated with the
dashed line) but far less energy costly.
A catalyst’s energy of adsorption is a very important parameter. If the catalyst binds reactants and
products too strongly, the products cannot leave the surface after formation. If the catalyst binds
reactants and product to weakly, the reactants would not stay at the surface for long enough do
dissociate and react further. The optimal adsorption energy is therefor a compromise.
Figure 1.2: Illustration of a potential energy diagram showing the elementary steps in a heterogeneous
catalytic reaction. The solid line is the catalysed pathway where the dashed line is the uncatalysed
pathway. Illustration made with inspiration from [7].
Nanoparticles
The size of the catalyst particles is of signiﬁcant importance due to diﬀerent eﬀects. Many of the
optimal catalysts are not very abundant and therefore also expensive [8]. Therefore, one would like to

optimise the surface area to mass ratio as catalysis takes place at the surface. Furthermore there are
various eﬀects when particles become suﬃciently small, and an example of this is gold as a catalyst
for CO oxidation as introduced in section 1.3.1.
Surface site
On any crystal surface a number of diﬀerent adsorption sites are present. The reactivity (adsorption
strength) of diﬀerent sites varies due to the local electron structure in the vicinity of the adsorption
site. One important parameter for this is the coordination number and therefore number of nearest
neighbours of the site. The corner sites have the lowest coordination number and according to the
d-band theory, this results in the highest reactivity. Subsequently edge sites have intermediate coordi-
nation number and therefore intermediate reactivity. Lastly the facet sites possess less reactivity than
the other sites. Reactivity is a measure of how strong a molecule binds to the surface which is not the
same as activity. Activity is the sum of how well a catalyst catalyses a reaction, and it is inﬂuenced
by a number of parameters such as reactivity, reaction kinetics and desorption. How catalytic activity
is declared is a matter of much discussion, but often for small research systems, the term turn over
frequency (TOF) is used as a measure of the number of reactions taking place per site per unit time.
The number of edges and corner sites increases with decreasing particle size due to geometric eﬀects.
If one would like many edge sites, a small particle size would therefore be preferable.
A good example of how diﬀerently a material can behave due to the size eﬀects is gold. Gold is known
as the most noble element[9], but when the particle size becomes suﬃciently small, it becomes a very
active catalyst for CO oxidation[10], and in section 1.3.1, gold as a catalyst will be discussed.
Sintering
A downside to nanoparticles is that they can move more on the surface and they thereby become prone
to sintering. The sintering eﬀect is the phenomenon where smaller dispersed particles combine into
fewer, larger particles, eﬀectively reducing the surface area. This process may be due to two mechanisms
or a combination hereof. One is the Ostwald ripening (OR) where larger particles consume smaller
particles whereby they become bigger, minimising the total surface energy. Particle migration and
coalescence (PMC) is a phenomenon where particles move around dictated by Brownian motion and
coalesce into larger particles if they happen to move too close to each other [11]. It is diﬃcult to
determine which of the two mechanisms is dominant and this may also change during the course of a
reaction. Typically, small particles have better mobility on the surface and can thereby move around
easier; this makes sintering more of a problem for small particles such as the nanoparticle systems
studied here.

The reason why gold is active has long been a subject of debate, and it was speculated that the
support might play a dominant role in the supply of oxygen in order to form active oxidic gold sites
[19]. Such theories have been disproved by studies combining theoretical and experimental work to
rule out support interactions as the primary eﬀect on the activity as shown in ﬁgure 1.5 [20, 21] while
identifying the number of undercoordinated Au atoms as the key variable in this regard. Brodersen et
al. studied which sites are the active ones for CO oxidation and how the shape of the clusters inﬂuences
this. It was found by DFT calculations that the activity is heavily dominated by under-coordinated
corner atoms (coordination number 6 or lower) [22]. However, the support does have an eﬀect on the
activity, and it is known that gold nanoparticles are more active on reducible oxide supports such as
Co3O4 and TiO2 [23, 24] whereas they have poor activity on non reducible substrates such as SiO2.
The current understanding is therefore that the fraction of undercoordinated Au atoms is the primary
descriptor for the activity of the Au catalyst. However, a number of other factors also play a role in
the activity of which the choice of substrate is one of them. Why the TOF drops when decreasing the
particle size below 3 nm is not known, but Valden et al. hypothesises the inﬂuence of an increasing
band gap for very small particles as part of the explanation [16]. Another explanation is that stabilising
the very small particles becomes increasingly diﬃcult the smaller the particle size, making testing very
diﬃcult.

There is, however, a downside to gold as a catalyst as it has been shown to have stability issues [16].
These problems have been veriﬁed both experimentally and theoretically to be due to sintering of the
gold particles [25, 26]. The importance of the support on the stability has been investigated, but on
the two most commonly used supports (SiO2 and TiO2), Au has been shown to be prone to sintering
[27, 28]. To increase stability, chemically synthesised Au particles were attempted alloyed with Ir on a
TiO2 support, showing an increased stability [29, 30]. Au-Ag alloys have been shown to have improved
stability and higher activity for CO oxidation at room temperature [31]. The activity of this alloy
appears to be unaﬀected by the support, whereas the stability does depend on the choice of support
with the best results obtained for TiO2. Au-Cu alloys on a SBA-15 (a mesoporous silica catalyst
support[32]) have also shown higher stability [33] than pure gold. It has been shown that a strong
metal–support interaction (SMSI) may make the gold nanopaticles more sintering resistant [34]. The
SMSI can be enhanced by a pretreatment for gold particles on a TiO2 substrate by annealing them
in H2 atmosphere, resulting in a higher stability [35]. That a pretreatment in H2 for gold particles
had a positive inﬂuence of the stability has been known since 1997 [24], and indications that such a
pretreatment also increases the activity has also been reported [31].
A study into alloying Au with Ti made by our group (SURFCAT) together with collaborators found
that mass selected Au-Ti/SiO2 clusters exhibit increased stability against sintering as a result of an
anchoring interaction with the support [36].

2.1
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS)
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is based on the photo-eclectic eﬀect explained by Einstein in
1905. It is a well known principle where electromagnetic radiation is absorbed in a solid under the
emission of a photoelectron as shown in ﬁgure 2.1.
The surface is bombarded with photons, and with suﬃcient energy an electron in the solid may absorb
the photon energy and escape the solid with a characteristic energy reﬂecting the energy of the photon
as well as the binding energy of the electron.
The experiment is performed in ultra-high vacuum (UHV). The beam is typically monochromatic with
an energy in the range of 1 to a few keV which will result in photoionisation from the surface atoms
which will thus emit electrons. This will also happen in the bulk of the material but due to the emitted
electrons having a relatively low energy, their mean free path is small and only electrons close to the
surface will be able to escape the solid without energy losses.
The energy of the emitted electron (Ekin) relative to the vacuum level will be given by equation 2.1
where hν is the photon energy of the incident X-rays, EB is the binding energy of the electron in the
solid relative to the Fermi level and Φ is the work function of the analyser [37].
Ekin = hν −EB −Φ
(2.1)
It is thereby possible to measure the photoelectron intensity as a function of binding energy and since
the binding energies are characteristic for each element it is possible to determine atomic compositions.
Furthermore the binding energy is also aﬀected by chemical state, allowing one to analyse the oxidation
state of the surface.
For quantiﬁcation, the peaks are ﬁtted with a Shirley background correction.

2.3
Microreactor setup
The microreactor setup oﬀers a unique platform for measuring very small amounts of catalyst. Using
a nanobeam cluster source one can deposit nanoparticles of well deﬁned size and in well deﬁned
quantities. Unlike a traditional reactor setup, one can in the microreactor analyse all the products
using a quadrupole mass spectrometer (QMS). Therefore, the cluster source in combination with the
microreactor oﬀers a great way to produce and test model catalyst systems in order to obtain insight
on the fundamental aspects of how the catalyst works.
In this section, the microreactor chip will ﬁrst be described. Then, a short description of the particle
deposition process will be given followed by an account of the anodic bonding process used for sealing
oﬀthe reactors. Lastly, an overview of the gas handling system is given along with an introduction to
the working principle of the QMS used for product detection.

Magnetron sputtering & Gas-phase Aggregation
Magnetron sputtering is a method used to sputter atoms of a desired material. A negative bias is ap-
plied so that there is a potential of ≈−250 V on the target. Argon is lead into the chamber where the
strong electrical ﬁeld ionizes the Ar gas and the positively charged Ar atoms are accelerated towards
the target where they cause sputtering events. The sputtering leads to two events: One is the release
of electrons that helps to maintain the plasma, and one is the sputtering of the target material into
the vacuum. Magnets are placed behind the target in a circular array, creating a magnetic ﬁeld that
conﬁnes the electrons in a ring above the target, and the Ar+ manly hits the target in this area [42].
Some of the sputtered oﬀatoms escape the plasma and enter the aggregation zone. In the aggregation
zone, the atoms agglomerate into clusters assisted by He atoms which facilitate the formation of metal
dimers while providing thermal conductivity between the atoms and the liquid nitrogen cooled walls.
After the particles escape the aggregation zone, they are accelerated to prevent further aggregation.
The particles are also focused into a beam.
Time-of-Flight mass ﬁlter
The mass separation happens in a time-of-ﬂight mass ﬁlter. An illustration of the working principle
can be seen in ﬁgure 2.7. The particles entering the time-of-ﬂight primarily have a velocity in the
horizontal direction. By applying an electrical ﬁeld as a shot pulse in the vertical direction at plate 1,
the particles obtain a velocity in the vertical direction as they pass through a slit. The particles with
a lower mass to charge ratio (m/z) will be aﬀected more by the ﬁeld pulse than the heavier particles.
They therefore accelerate more and bend further away from the horizontal direction.
Shortly after, an oppositely directed electrical ﬁeld pulse of the same magnitude is applied on plate 2
to neutralise the vertical component of the velocity. After the second pulse, the beam is distributed
based on the mass to charge ratio (m/z) of the particles in the vertical direction. Using an exit slit,
one can choose a very ﬁne interval of mass to charge ratio. The size of the interval can be tuned by
changing the size of the exit slit.

2.3.3
Anodic bonding
After deposition of particles on the reactor, a pyrex lid is used to seal the chip through anodic bonding,
resulting in the micro channels where the gas is to ﬂow while making the chip easier to handle and
prevent contamination of the reactor volume.
Anodic bonding is an electrochemical process where the principle is to have a high voltage drop around
400 V to 1000 V between silicon and a borosilicate glass so that the glass is negatively charged with
respect to the silicon. The process is carried out with temperature at the cathode of approximately
300-450 ◦C [45]. This makes ions of Na+ and K+ travel from the anode to the cathode, producing a
depletion region which results in a strong electrical ﬁeld. This ﬁeld pulls the two materials towards
each other, close enough that oxygen from the glass may diﬀuse into the silicon and oxidise it, forming
strong covalent bonds which are entirely gas tight. A sketch of the process can be seen in ﬁgure 2.8.
Figure 2.8: A sketch of the mechanism of silicon-glass anodic bonding. Inspiration for the sketch from
[45].

2.3.5
Quadrupole mass spectrometer
A quadrupole mass spectrometer (QMS) is used to analyse the gas exiting the microreactor. The
principle of a QMS is to analyse the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). First, the gas from the measurement
is ionised to obtain charged molecules and atoms. A source slit is placed to get a well deﬁned beam,
and after the slit, a quadrupole analyser consisting of two pairs of cylindrical rods is placed. One pair
is negatively charged and the other is positive charged. They are placed opposite to each other as
shown in ﬁgure 2.11. A radio frequency (RF) voltage is applied with a DC oﬀset voltage on each
of the rods: The two positive rods with +RF+DC and the negative rods with -RF-DC. The applied
RF and DC ﬁelds will eﬀect the particles diﬀerently depending on the m/z ratio. A heavier ion will
not respond as quickly to an oscillating potential as a lighter ion. For an ion to make it through the
quadrupole, it needs to be stable in both the x and the y direction. This requires a very speciﬁc m/z
ratio depending on the applied voltages. Therefor, in order to analyse the full spectrum of masses, a
scan over all mass-to-charge ratios is necessary. In reality one scans over a number of selected m/z.
The signal is then ampliﬁed using an electron multiplier, which enhances the signal, and is measured
as a current.

Results and Discussion
In this section, the results of this project will be presented together with a discussion of the data.
First, an overview of the experimental procedure regarding the microreactors is given. Then, a short
section on how the temperature is measured and the temperature uncertainty is presented. Thereafter,
an overview of how blank reactors behave during CO oxidation is given. Lastly, data from the AuTi
study is presented including both a size study and the eﬀect of substrate along with an investigation
of the issue of data reproducibility.
3.1
Experimental overview
A number of steps proceed the testing of the microreactor. Firstly, they have to be fabricated, and
the fabrication process describing how Danchip makes the microreactors can be found in appendix 6.5.
The microreactor substrate is silicon covered by a grown oxide layer, also at the reactor volume which
was used as support for the Au and AuTi studies. To investigate the inﬂuence of support material,
additional microreactors were fabricated with a TiO2 substrate deposited at the reactor volume in a
magnetron sputtering chamber. The layer of Tio2 was only deposited at the reactor volume with a
thickness of around 50 nm.
The handing of the cluster source, and thereby the deposition of the particles, was performed by mas-
ter’s student Jens Ringsholm and ph.d students Niklas Secher and Karl Toudahl. The most essential
steps of the deposition process will be presented here without diving into the ﬁner details. A more
detailed description can be found in [18, 50]. Prior to deposition, the sample is cleaned by 1 keV Ar+
sputtering for 40 min to ensure a more well deﬁned surface without any carbon contamination. Two
diﬀerent targets were used in this project: One was a pure Au target of 99.99% purity used for the
deposition of pure Au nanoparticles, and one was a 50/50 composition of Au and Ti used for the
deposition of Au-Ti particles. All samples were made with a coverage of 5% of a monolayer in order to
obtain a reasonable activity as a function of temperature while ensuring a decent distancing between
the particles and avoid two particles landing at the same position. The particles were all deposited in
the soft-landing regime (where the clusters impact the substrate with low enough energy to deposit on
the surface without penetrating it) with a bias of 48 V.
The microreactor was bonded using the cold bonding setup, and thereafter placed in the microreactor
setup where the testing takes place.
A CO oxidation measurement in the microreactor proceeds as follows.
23
==PAGE:31==
3.1. EXPERIMENTAL OVERVIEW
1. An argon ﬂow is passed through the microreactor and the tubes adjoining it whereafter the
system is pumped down. This is done twice in order to thoroughly ﬂush out any impurities in
the system.
2. To avoid back diﬀusion of gas, a 2:1:1 ﬂow of O2:Ar:CO is applied for 20 min before changing
to a 1:1:1 ﬂow of O2:Ar:CO. As seen in ﬁgure 2.10, CO and Ar is on the same gas line whereas
O2 is on an other. The doubled ﬂow of O2 is applied to ensure even pressure on the two lines to
avoid that the Ar and CO back diﬀuse into the O2 gas line.
The 1:1:1 ﬂow of O2:Ar:CO is passed though the reactor for another 20 min to ﬁll the reactor
volume and ensure a stable ﬂow.
3. The temperature is ramped to either 250 ◦C with a ramp of 4 K/s using the thermocouple to
control the temperature, or to 350 ◦C with a ramp of 10 K/s using the RTD to control the
temperature. The diﬀerence between the two methods will be made clear in section 3.2.
4. The temperature is held constant for a duration between 20 minutes and 1 hour.
5. The temperature is ramped down to room temperature using the same ramp.
The product gas from the reactor outlet is analysed in a QMS, and an example of the QMS data
obtained may be found in ﬁgure 3.1.

Measuring the Temperature
The temperature is controlled relative to the temperature measured by either the resistances temper-
ature detector (RTD) or a thermocouple (TC). This means that the chosen method inﬂuences the
temperature.
In the beginning of the project, for some of the microreactors, a connotation to the RTD could not
be established and the TC was therefore used to control the temperature. This means that the gold
titanium project was entirely done using the TC. Later on, it was found that for most of the microre-
actors, the RTD actually worked.
The RTD and the thermocouple do not measure the same temperature as the RTD measures signiﬁ-
cantly higher temperatures as seen in ﬁgure 3.2 in which a common temperature proﬁle for the AuTi
project is shown where the temperature is controlled by the TC and set to ramp up to 250 ◦C with a
rate of 4 K/min.

are for three diﬀerent measurements on three diﬀerent reactors.

Discussion
An explanation of why the temperature is so diﬀerent in vacuum compared to at 1 bar could be that
the thermal conductivity of the air gap in between the TC soldering and the pyrex lid scales with the
pressure. Any small distance between TC and surface would therefore result in a larger temperature
diﬀerence in vacuum where gas diﬀusion contributes less to the thermal conductivity.
Conversely,
the RTD, should not be inﬂuenced by the pressure as thermal conductivity is dominated by phonons.
Therefore, it seems plausible that the RTD measures a temperature closer to the actual temperature in
the reactor volume. In this project, the stability of the RTD measurements has been decent, however,
the RTD still looses connection in approximately 10% of the measurements which must then be redone,
so the longer annealing time of the microreactors appears to have had a positive eﬀect.
At the start of a measurement, the temperature measured by the TC is used as a room temperature (T0)
reference measurement. One must ensure that this measurement is carried out either at atmospheric
pressure or room temperature as the TC measurement cannot be trusted at elevated temperatures in
vacuum.
When the thermocouple is used to control the temperature, the variance in the temperatures measured
by the RTD at the highest temperatures (250 ◦C as measured by the TC) between diﬀerent reactors
seems to have an uncertainty of ±25 ◦C estimated from ﬁgure 3.2. A lower uncertainty is expected at
lower temperatures due to the increase in temperature diﬀerence between the RTD and the TC as the
temperature is increased.
All things considered, it is recommended to use the RTD for future measurements as an uncertainty of
up towards 50 ◦C is simply too much in thermal catalysis even though roughly 10% of the measurements
must be redone. The thermocouple should still be in place in case the RTD looses contact as it can
give an idea of what the temperature was during the remainder of a measurement.

3.3
Blank reactors
A blank reactor is a chip which is bonded as received from fabrication in the cleanroom. This implies
that there is no catalyst deposited on the reactor, and one would therefore expect no catalytic activity.
However, as will be made clear, this was not always the case which caused a lot of headaches, and in
order to provide some insight into the work with the blank reactors, the work with the blank reactors
will be chronologically described in the following. A table of all blanks tested can be found in appendix
6.2
A blank reactor was tested in the begging of this project (BMR1) showing a very low activity. At the
same time, a microreactor (BMR3) with 50 nm thick TiO2 layer deposited on the reactor volume was
tested for CO oxidation at a TC-controlled temperature of 250 ◦C and showed a similarly low activity
as the previously measured silicon blank. This very low activity measured only at high temperatures
is tolerable and considered unavoidable.
After 3 months where a lot of measurements had been conducted, the blank silica reactor (BMR1) was
tested again, this time showing a much higher activity. Two explanations for this were considered:
1. The reactor might unintentionally have been swapped with a diﬀerent reactor with deposited
particles.
2. Carbonyls might be present in the CO gas ﬂow which would lead to nickel contamination which
could explain the increase in activity over time [51].
.
Option 1 can never truly be ruled out, but it seems highly unlikely as great care is taken to
separate and organise the reactors after use.
Option 2 would be surprising as none of the other
microreactors’ activity increased over time. Nickel contamination would increase the activity of all
samples and therefore question all measurement made in the setup. However, in order to rule out
carbonyl contamination, a carbonyl ﬁlter was implemented in the setup on the CO line just before
the mass ﬂow controller to be sure that it could not be nickel causing the activity. A new blank
reactor (BMR4) was thereafter tested and showed a lot of activity up to full conversion. The shape of
the measured CO2 signal was signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from what had been observed for previous blanks
microreactors, but also for reactors with gold. A plot of the blank (BMR4) together with an active
AuTi on SiO2 can be found in ﬁgure 3.4. The result was extremely concerning as CO appears to be
converted very eﬃciently into CO2, and such a good conversion suggests contamination from the likes
of platinum or palladium. Another blank from an old batch from 2018 was also tested and showed the
same result.
The next step was to investigate where the contamination comes from. The ﬁrst suspicion was aimed
towards the tubing installed between the carbonyl ﬁlter and the mass ﬂow controller which was not
new. Therefore, the carbonyl ﬁlter and the pipes were removed again, and a new blank reactor (BMR6)
was thereafter tested and showed full conversion, leading to the two following options.
1. The contaminant from the tubing could have diﬀused into the rest of the system.
2. The contaminant could be in the microreactors themselves.

reproducible for deposited particles of the same size. Previous work on other cluster source deposited

3.4.2
Stability
To investigate how a microreactor behaves over time, four CO oxidation experiments have been pre-
formed in sequence on the same AuTi/SiO2 with 2.5 nm nanoparticles. The temperature is controlled
using the thermocouple and the temperature is ramped up to 250 ◦C, held constant for 1 hour and
then ramped down. The result can be seen in ﬁgure 3.12 Where the calibrated CO2 signal is plotted
against the temperature. A run is an experimental sequence corresponding to either a ramp up or a
ramp down in temperature. The data has been cut oﬀwhere the reactor goes to full conversion as this
is where the system becomes mass transport limited (full conversion). One should notice the activation
step taking place around 250 ◦C without which the reactors appear completely inactive. Furthermore
it is seen that the activity seems to decrease over time while remaining somewhat active across the
runs.

3.4.3
Analysis of Substrate
To investigate the inﬂuence of the substrate, microreactors were fabricated with Au/TiO2 and AuTi/TiO2
and compared to reactors with Au/SiO2 and AuTi/SiO2, all with particles of 2.5 nm. The mass ac-
tivity is shown in ﬁgure 3.13. The ideal manner in which to plot the activity would be using the
turn over frequency. However, as the number of surface sites is unknown, using the Mass activity has
been chosen instead. This is done by using the calibrated CO2 signal divided by the argon signal and
divided by the mass of gold deposited on the reactor. For the AuTi particles, the deposited mass has
been estimated by calculating the volume of each particle using the average size found through STEM
as shown in section 3.4.1. This volume is translated into a mass per particle using the density of Au
(ρAu = 19.3 g/cm3), and since the number of deposited particles is known, the total deposited mass
is easy to ﬁnd. For pure Au particles, the process is simpler as the mass of each deposited particle is
selected when depositing particles in the cluster source to be the same using the time of ﬂight mass
analyser. The mass per particle is therefore simply multiplied by the number of deposited particles.
The data has been cut oﬀwhere the reactor goes to full conversion as this is where the system becomes
mass transport limited.

3.4.5
Reproducibility issues
Reproduction of the the active AuTi/Si2 microreactors has proven to be a challenge. As an example,
the active 2.5 nm reactor has been attempted reproduced three times using diﬀerent reactors with no
luck (the result can be found in ﬁgure 6.5) in appendix 6.4. One might argue that it may be the
three reactors without activity that measures correctly, but something is causing the activity on the
active 2.5 nm reactor. It has been a mystery why some reactors are active and others are not. It has
been investigated whether the RTD temperature was higher for the active reactors compared to the
inactive, but no but no such connection could be made out as indicated by ﬁgure 6.6 in appendix 6.4.
In order to investigate what distinguishes the active reactors from the inactive ones, four reactors were
opened by cutting the pyrex lid and thereafter removing it from the reactor volume, exposing the
reactor volume so that it is possible to investigate the surface by ISS and XPS. This was done for two
active reactors with particle sizes of 2.5 nm and 4.5 nm respectively, and two inactive reactors with the
same sizes of nanoparticles. The four microreactors were measured by XPS and ISS where a survey
scan was made at three diﬀerent points on each reactor. An XPS survey scan for each microreactor
can be seen in ﬁgure 3.15. It is observed that there is a clear gold signal as both the Au4f and Au4d
peaks are clearly visible for the two active reactors which is not the case for the two inactive reactors.

Chapter 4
Conclusion
In conclusion, Au and AuTi nanoparticles have been deposited by a nanobeam cluster source on
microreactors with either SiO2 or TiO2 supports. This procedure allows one to analyse a very small
and well deﬁned amount of catalyst with a high degree of precision.
It was found that there is contamination on the microreactor itself which performs CO oxidation at
approximately 200 ◦C as measured by the RTD placed on the backside of the microreactor. By ISS
and ICP-MS, it was found that the contamination could be platinum or palladium or a combination
thereof. However, no good explanation from where palladium might originate has been proposed. It
was found that by sputtering the microreactor for 40 min in Ar+ the measured activity decreases to
an acceptable level. Likewise, through deposition of a layer of TiO2 on the microreactor volume, the
measured activity decreases to an acceptable level. Furthermore, gold is found to be active at lower
temperatures than the contamination, and the contamination’s activity will therefore not inﬂuence the
result of the AuTi study.
In the AuTi study, it was found that the activation of AuTi/SiO2 occurs at temperatures close to
250 ◦C measured by the TC corresponding to 350 ◦C measured by the RTD. The results indicate that
the activation occurs sharply around 350 ◦C as measured by the RTD which illustrates the importance
of measuring the temperature accurately, and this is only possible using the RTD. An investigation into
the stability of the AuTi/SiO2 showed that the activity over a four hour period decreased slightly but
not drastically. The study of diﬀerent particle-support combinations using 2.5 nm nanoparticles of Au
and AuTi found that AuTi/TiO2 had a signiﬁcantly higher mass activity than the other combinations,
and that Au/SiO2 was largely inactive. Au/TiO2 had a similar mass activity as AuTi/SiO2 until a TC
temperature of 100 ◦C where Au/SiO2 went to full conversion illustrating that there is less available
sites per unit mass than what is the case for AuTi/SiO2. A problem was that it was not possible to
reproduce the activity for AuTi/SiO2.
In the size study of AuTi/SiO2, it was found that the microreactor with 5.5 nm particles had a higher
mass activity followed by the reactor with 4.5 nm and 2.5 nm sized particles. The reactor with particles
of 3.5 nm was not active, likely due to the the mentioned reproducibility issue, but this should be
attempted reproduced to see if the 3.5 nm particles ﬁt the trend. As it was not possible to reproduce the
active measurements on the microreactors with nanoparticles of 4.5 nm and 2.5 nm, it is very diﬃcult
to draw a conclusion on the size study, and to continue this study, the issues with reproducibility
must be identiﬁed. The active and inactive microreactors with particle sizes of 4.5 nm and 2.5 nm have
been analysed by ISS and XPS post testing where it was found that the active microreactors had a
high gold signal both in XPS and ISS. Conversely, no gold signal could be observed for the inactive
microreactors. A possible explanation is that the Ti might creep over the gold and encapsulate it due
to the SMSI, a phenomenon which depends on the temperature during activation. Through ISS depth

Chapter 5
Outlook
This project has been part of an ongoing project investigating the AuTi alloy for CO oxidation. It has
contributed with knowledge of the microreactor itself as it was found that there was contamination
on the microreactors. Furthermore, temperature measurements have been performed showing that
the RTD temperature is stable with few exceptions, indicating that the RTD measures the correct
temperature. With this knowledge a good foundation has been laid for further work on the AuTi
project.
One of the ﬁrst things which should be performed is to make a new microreactor with 2.5 nm AuTi
nanoparticles on TiO2 as only one of these microreactors have been tested. Furthermore, a size study
of AuTi/TiO2 would be very interesting as this system might be more stable due to the SMSI. A size
study of Au/TiO2 would also be needed to provide a good frame of reference.
To look further into the issue of reproducibility, the inactive AuTi/SiO2 microreactors could investi-
gated by microscopy techniques as one hypothesis is that the deposited gold particles have formed local
large particles. To obtain more stable AuTi/SiO2 reactors, varying the Au/Ti ratio of the particles
may be a possible solution. In order to do this, a new target with a larger amount of Au would be
needed.
The data deﬁnitely suggests that the RTD should be used to control the temperature as these tem-
perature measurements are much more reliable. Indications have been found that this is especially
important during the activation step in order to obtain a temperature of 350 ◦C as it was seen that the
CO2 signal peaks sharply at this temperature. If the TC is used, one should increase the temperature
to ensure that one obtains proper activation.
To avoid contamination on the microreactor, the reactor should be sputtered in Ar+ for 40 min as this
was shown to drastically decrease the activity. To further ensure that the eﬀect of the contamination
on the activity is insigniﬁcant, one could consider depositing a layer of SiO2 on the microreactor in a
magnetron sputtering chamber as it was done with TiO2. A SiO2 target has already been delivered
and it should therefore straightforward to perform this experiment.
In literature, it has been documented that pretreatment in O2 or H2 can make Au/Ti2 more stable.
Therefore, it would be interesting to investigate the inﬂuence of such pretreatments on our system.

6.3
TOF calculation
The turn over frequency (TOF) has been calculated. In order to do that, some assumptions have been
made. Firstly, the particles do not sinter and are assumed to be half spheres on a ﬂat surface. The
diameter of the gold in the AuTi particles is estimated form the ﬁgure 3.11. Secondly, we assume that
Au exposes it’s (111)-surface, an assumption which is known to be crude. Lastly, it is assumed that all
sites are equally active for CO oxidation. The last assumption is known from literature to be false, but
a more correct calculation would be very demanding. In equation 6.1, the TOF calculation is shown
where ρsurface is the surface density for gold which is calculated to be 13.9 atoms/nm2. nparticles is
the number of particles deposited on the surface, Nsites is the estimated number of sites on the reactor
surface and lastly NA is Avogadro’s number.
