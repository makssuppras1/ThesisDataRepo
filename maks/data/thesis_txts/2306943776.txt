Abstract: The urgent issue of declining fish stocks has created a need for new livelihood opportunities in 
the rural communities of Ghana. The aim of this project, conducted in collaboration between Ghanaian and 
Danish researchers, is to create a sound foundation for green growth and new business opportunities in 
Ghana. The vision of the project is to establish seaweed farming in Ghana and subsequently processing of 
the seaweed into value-added products and bioenergy. Ghana has 540 km of coastline with many rural 
areas and cultivation of seaweed would create a possibility to utilizing the large coastal areas and reducing 
poverty at the same time.  
The green seaweed specie Ulva Fasciata is naturally found in Ghana and therefore chosen for cultivation 
and as the feedstock for the biorefinery. The process technologies chosen for the biorefinery have been 
selected due to the desire of the Ghanaians to produce enzymes and bioenergy locally in Ghana. The 
biorefinery is going to use indigenous enzyme-producing microorganism in an on-site enzyme production, 
which is then used in the saccharification process of Ulva Fasciata to produce bioethanol. The co-produced 
streams of wet biological material are used in either Microbial Fuels Cells to produce electricity, in 
Anaerobic Digesters to produce methane or/ and used as fertilizer.  
An Emergy Assessment has been conducted to illuminate the systems reliance of renewable, non-
renewable and imported resources. Three different cultivation scenarios has been investigated to see how 
the `Off-bottom` cultivation method performs compared to the `Floating Line` method. Furthermore, how a 
women-owned farm is different from a man-owned farm it is size, materials and labour inputs. The 
harvested seaweed is washed, dried and stored before transported to the biorefinery. The extensive 
amount of steps in handling and processing the seaweed are heavily adding to the emergy memory of the 
products resulting in high joint transformities of; 2,46E+05 sej / J produced electricity and compost and 
2,30E+05 sej/ J methane and compost and 1,60E+05 sej/ J for fertilizer. Seaweed may seem like a very good 
candidate for biofuel production due to its high production of biomass and independence of arable land, 
fresh water and fertilizer. However, it is not as green as it may look like when seen through a Sustainability 
Assessment perspective. The production of bioenergy from seaweed is heavy reliant on non-renewable 
inputs and even though the feedstock sequential is utilized by different technologies to produce enzymes 
and different kinds of energy, the amount of non-renewable inputs cannot make up for the production of 
bioenergy with the present yields and conversion efficiencies.  
Based on the data used in this study it is concluded, that seaweed is not a suitable choice as primary 
feedstock for bioenergy production. However, seaweed could be cultivation of human consumption or used 
for extraction of value-added products and then utilized as a secondary feedstock for bioenergy as the last 
process operation utilizing the resource.

Resumé: Den stigende tendens med faldende fiskebestande i vandene langs Ghanas kyst, er et stort 
problem for de lokale fiskere og deres familier og har skabt et behov for nye indkomstmuligheder i 
landdistrikterne. Formålet med dette projekt, gennemført i et samarbejde mellem ghanesiske og danske 
forsker, er at skabe et solidt grundlag for grøn vækst og nye forretningsmuligheder i Ghana. Visionen med 
projektet er at etablere tang landbrug i Ghana og efterfølgende ekstrahere høj-værdi produkter og 
bioenergi fra tangen. Ghana har en 540 km lang kyststrækning med mange landdistrikter og dyrkning af 
tang ville skabe en mulighed for at udnytte de store kystområder og mindske fattigdommen på samme tid.  
Den grønne tang art Ulva fasciata findes naturligt i Ghana og er derfor valgt til dyrkning og som råvare til 
bioraffinaderiet. De procesteknologier som er udvalgt til bioraffinaderi er valgt på baggrund af et ønske fra 
ghanesiske side om, at producere enzymer og bioenergi lokalt i Ghana. Bioraffinaderi skal udnytte de 
naturligt tilstedeværende mikroorganismer på Ulva fasciata til at producer enzymer lokalt på fabrikken og 
efterfølgende bruge disse in hydrolysering af tang i ethanol fermenteringsprocessen. De resterende 
produkter fra enzym -og ethanol produktionen er udnyttet som substrat i tre forskellige scenarier, 
bestående af enten mikrobielle brændselsceller til produktion af elektricitet, biogasanlæg til produktion af 
methane og/ eller anvendes af det resterende biologiske materiale som gødning.  
En Emergy vurdering er foretaget for at belyse systemets afhængighed af fornybare, ikke-fornybare og 
importerede ressourcer fra det omgivende samfund. Tre forskellige dyrkningsscenarier er blevet undersøgt 
for at se, hvordan de hver især præstere i et energiregnskab. Desuden er det undersøgt hvordan et kvinder-
ejet dyrkningsområde er anderledes fra et mands-ejede område i forhold til størrelse af dyrkningsareal, 
materialer og arbejdskraft. Den høstede tang er efterfølgende vasket, tørret og opbevaret før transporteret 
til bioraffinaderiet. Det omfattende antal af forarbejdningsprocesser resultere i en stor mængde energi 
investeret i produkterne og derfor høje transformiteter for at producere bioethanol og enten; elektricitet 
og kompost (2,46E+05 sej/ J) biogas og kompost (2,30 sej/ J) eller ren gødning (1,60E+05 sej/ J). Så selvom 
tang kan virke som et godt substrat til produktion af bioenergi på grund af sin høje produktion af biomasse 
og uafhængighed af langbrugsjord, frisk vand og gødning er det er imidlertid ikke så ”grøn” en løsning fra et 
energiregnskabs synspunkt. Produktionen af bioenergi fra tang er dybt afhængige af ikke-fornybare 
ressourcer og selvom tangen er anvendt af forskellige teknologier til at producere enzymer og forskellige 
former for energi, kan mængden af ikke-fornybare ressourcer ikke gøre op for produktionen af bioenergi 
med de nuværende udbytter og konvertering effektivitet.  
Det står derfor klart fra Emergy analysen, at tang ikke er egnet som primær råvare til produktion af 
bioenergi. Men tang kunne potentielt dyrkes som fødevare eller bruges til at udvinde høj-værdi produkter 
og derefter som sekundær råvare til produktion af bioenergi.

2 INTRODUCTION 
2.1 WHY WE SHOULD CARE!  
As the 21st century progresses, it becomes clear that global environmental change is happening creating 
new `wicked` situations, that defy complete definition but result in lasting damage (Moser et al., 2012; 
Rauland et al., 2015). Wicked challenges such as; decline of biodiversity, dwindling water supplies and 
climate change appear resistant to resolution due to their incomplete, contradictory and changing basis. 
Their underlying dynamics, which has fuelled these challenges, are a combination of economic and 
population growth coupled with a change of lifestyle heavily reliant on high energy inputs and extraction of 
natural resources (IPCC, 2014; Rauland et al., 2015). Wicked challenges have a high degree of scientific 
uncertainty, lack of problem definition and unknown consequences, which all together equals a serious 
headache for the world’s scientists, governments and non-governmental organisations (Rauland et al., 
2015). A catalyst for wicked challenges is anthropogenic greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, which have 
reached levels not seen in at least 800,000 years, reported by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate 
Change (2014). The high concentration of GHGs in the atmosphere influence the climate system and have 
widespread consequences for both ecosystems and human lives (IPCC, 2014). A source of GHG are fossil 
fuels (coal, crude oil, and natural gas), which has since the industrial revolution been the primary source of 
energy and the foundation for the development of modern society (Agostinho and Ortega, 2013). A 
development that is still progressing as the world’s energy demand is predicted to reach 399 million barrels 
of oil equivalent per day by 2040, compared to the 268 million barrels of oil equivalent used per day in 
2013 (International Energy Agency, 2015). However, the steady increase in consumption of fossil fuels for 
industrial processes and transportation has had major consequences for the natural reserves and 
contributed to 78% of the total GHG emission increase from 1970 to 2010 (IPCC, 2014).   
An increasing request for energy from a rapidly expanding global population mixed with rising 
environmental concerns represent a grand challenge for nations worldwide (Wilson and Lee, 2016).  
Concerns regarding atmospheric pollution`s effects on climate change have created interests in alternative 
energy sources, that can meet the energy demand while mitigating climate change as lower GHG emission 
alternatives (Kamp and Østergård, 2011; Suganya et al., 2016). The growth is mainly set to be in the 
developing countries where populations, as well as the individual energy use, will increase the most 
(International Energy Agency, 2015). The conversion of renewable sources into energy in an economically 
efficient, social responsible and environment sustainable way is therefore a challenge faced by 
governments and businesses worldwide to provide energy security and avoid the consequences of climate 
change (Golberg et al., 2014; Rauland et al., 2015).

2.1.1 ENERGY FROM BIOMASS AS AN ALTERNATIVE TO FOSSIL FUELS 
To combat the negative impacts of fossil fuels on the environment, energy from biomass has obtained a 
growing interest as a source of biofuels (Aitken et al., 2014; Ghadiryanfar et al., 2016). However, the extent 
to which biofuels can displace fossil fuels as an economically feasible and energetic-environmentally sound 
alternative, depends on the cultivation, processing and production phases from feedstocks to fuel, and to 
which extent that involves directly or indirectly use of non-renewable energy inputs (Macedo et al., 2008).  
Current production of biofuels from sugar-rich feedstock, produced in large scale in countries such as Brazil 
and the USA, has received negative attention on the basis of the feedstock and production processes 
(Suganya et al., 2016). The criticisms mainly regard the use of fertile land for cultivation of energy crops 
such as sugarcane, corn, soybean, wheat or sugar beet that set a “food vs fuel” dilemma due to competing 
for arable land (Ghadiryanfar et al., 2016). The sustainability aspects related to energy crops impact on 
biodiversity, water use and high energy inputs, often from fossil fuels, has furthermore caused concern of 
the adverse environmental effects of biofuels production (Golberg et al., 2014; Suganya et al., 2016a).  
Due to these criticized issues affecting the trust in biofuels as a sustainable alternative to fossil fuels, it is 
crucial to carefully choose appropriate raw biomass feedstocks and process technologies to ensure 
production of biofuels with environmental benefits (Golberg et al., 2014). Research and development 
programs to convert vegetal biomass into bioenergy, and especially liquid fuel for transportation, are 
therefore initiated worldwide (Agostinho et al., 2015; Golberg et al., 2014). The largest increase of energy 
demand will be in the developing countries in the coming decades and a key challenge is therefore to 
provide a sustainable biomass supply and development of appropriate technologies to convert biomass 
into bioenergy using resources as efficiently as possible (Golberg et al., 2014). 
Bioenergy derived from biomass should be produced under the criteria of sustainability (The Natural Step) 
as biofuels are not renewable per definition. A comprehensive assessment of the different aspects related 
to the strategy adopted for the production of biofuels is therefore essential to identify the chosen 
feedstock and appropriate technological route (Agostinho and Ortega, 2013). Designing a production 
system from an economic point of view is important. However, reaching social and environmental benefits 
are fundamental for a sustainable production (Agostinho and Ortega, 2013; Agostinho et al., 2015). These 
sustainability issues of production processes are inter –and transdisciplinary challenges. They require 
natural scientists to work together with social scientists to design socially responsible and economically 
feasible processes, combined with willingness from governmental actors to provide the needed support to 
integrate the solutions with existing systems (Sievanen et al., 2005).

2.1.2 SEAWEED AS A SUITABLE CANDIDATE FOR BIOENERGY 
In the search for a suitable feedstock candidate for bioenergy production that can be sustainable cultivated 
and efficiently converted into bioenergy, seaweed has gained growing interests due to its potential 
environmental benefits compared to terrestrial crops (Aitken et al., 2014; Lee et al., 2014). Cultivation of 
seaweed offers several advantages compared to terrestrial crops for energy production (Lee et al., 2014): 
There is no need for arable land, fertilizer or fresh water. Offshore seaweed production is not dependent 
on terrestrial land, but instead requires access to coastal areas for cultivation (Lee et al., 2014), as 
illustrated in Figure 1 from a village in Indonesia. The use of fertilizer is neglected and instead dependent on 
the availability of nutrients in the seawater. Irrigation is also not necessary, however, seaweed is 
dependent on the weather conditions which affect the temperature and salinity of the seawater that may 
change in the seasons (Fasahati et al., 2015; Langlois et al., 2012). In the rainy season seaweed is extra 
vulnerable due to frequent heavy rainfalls and freshwater runoff from the surrounding streams, which may 
cause die-offs in shallow waters as the salinity content fluctuates (Crawford, 2007; Msuya, 2006). 
 
Figure 1. Aerial view of a seaweed cultivation site in a village in Indonesia (Damian Carrington, 2012). 
Seaweed has the advantage of high biomass production because of its continuous growth throughout the 
year combined with high growth rate, which give seaweed a higher biomass productivity compared to most 
terrestrial crops (Aitken et al., 2014). Seaweed furthermore efficiently takes up nutrients from the seawater 
as it is capable of nutrient uptake over the whole surface area, lacking internal nutrient transport, because 
nutrients can be directly absorbed (Fasahati et al., 2015). An example is the brown seaweed Saccharina 
latissimi, which can take up 21 g nitrogen per kg dry weight (dw) and 4,5 g phosphorus per kg dw (Langlois 
et al., 2012). As excess amount of nutrients in coastal areas cause eutrophication, seaweeds uptake of 
these nutrients could improve the quality of the water (Aitken et al., 2014). In addition to uptake of 
nutrients, seaweed take up dissolved CO2 from surface waters and stores it until its released in the 
combustion of bioenergy (Langlois et al., 2012; Fasahati et al., 2015).

2.1.3 FARMING APPROACH AND TECHNIQUES 
The optimal condition for cultivation of seaweed is in a semi-enclosed shallow lagoon or in quiet water, as 
strong waves or a powerful wind can easily cause breakage of seaweed branches that are then washed 
away (Crawford, 2007). For the cultivation of seaweed, different approaches are available. By direct 
harvest, the naturally occurring seaweed drifting from the ocean shores is collected, without any energy 
input in the cultivation phase resulting in low production costs. However, the availability and composition 
of seaweed species and quantities vary seasonally and are unsuitable for a continuous feedstock supply 
(Golberg et al., 2014).  
Intensive seaweed farming in ponds onshore constitutes a reliable and easily regulated system with high 
biomass yield. However, high energy input is required to build and maintain the system (Golberg et al., 
2014). Another possibility is extensive seaweed farming, which constitutes a controlled farming system in 
the shallow water that is close, or relatively close, to the shore. It does not require the same extensive 
energy input in the construction and maintains, but also yields 3-4 times less than intensive cultivation 
(Golberg et al., 2014). The farming system is also more exposed to marine life eating or damaging the 
seaweed by causing severe wounds on the seaweed (Msuya, 2006). However, extensive seaweed farming is 
widely used in tropical regions. 
There exist different techniques for extensive seaweed farming. Among the most used are the Floating line 
method and Off-bottom method (Figure 2). The Floating line method is used at deeper waters, which give 
the advantages of a more consistent salinity and avoidance of die-offs that is common in the shallow 
waters (Crawford, 2007). However, floating materials are required and the farm is more exposed to motor-
driven boats and less simple to manage (FAO, 1990). The Off-bottom methods make the plot easy to 
construct and manage for the farmers, as it is cultivated in the shallow waters close to the shore. However, 
the seaweed is vulnerable to rough weather and more difficult to move (FAO, 1990).

2.1.4 CREATING ALTERNATIVE LIVELIHOOD OPTIONS FOR COASTAL COMMUNITIES 
Ghana’s fishery sector plays a significant role for the economy and employs 10% of the population in 
aquaculture, inland and marine fisheries (FAO, 2016). A big part of those are poor and vulnerable 
Ghanaians living in rural coastal communities dependent either directly or indirectly on fishing for their 
livelihood (FAO, 2016). However, most of Ghana’s fishery resources are heavily overexploited, effected by 
pollution and destructive fishing and as a result the sector has recorded a decline in production over the 
last decade (FAO, 2016). The rural fisher families are largely dependent on the fishing industry for survival 
but the decline in fish stock forces many out of business (The World Bank, 2016).  
Nevertheless, Ghana has an extensive 550 km coastline (FAO, 2016) with potential for growth of seaweed 
(GhanaWeb, 2015). Cultivation of seaweed could benefit both the communities and ecological systems as it 
may reduce fishing pressure and generate an alternative livelihood for local fisher families as well as uptake 
nutrient run-offs from agriculture or fish farming activities (Aitken et al., 2014; Msuya, 2006). Seaweed 
cultivation is therefore an opportunity to reach socio-economic and environmental benefits, when seen as 
one part of a larger strategy of diversifying economic activities for coastal communities (Rhein-Knudsen et 
al., 2015; Sievanen et al., 2005). Seaweed cultivation for carrageenan production is already a popular 
alternative livelihood approach to provide an extra income for fisher families in tropical countries due to its 
small investments requirements and simple techniques (Valderrama et al., 2013). The development of 
offshore seaweed farms has been demonstrated in Tanzania, Philippines and Indonesia where mostly red 
seaweed is cultivated and sold for extraction of carrageenan  (Msuya, 2006; Valderrama et al., 2013). 
Seaweed farming provides communities a possibility to generate socio-economic benefits to coastal 
communities with limited livelihood options (Valderrama et al., 2013). Beside the needs for alternative 
livelihood opportunities, energy security for rural communities could make them independent of potential 
public and private conflicts regarding energy sources. However, the bioenergy supply should not only be 
economically feasible, but even more importantly prove social and environmental sustainability (Golberg et 
al., 2014). Small-scale production is suggested to be the best option to gain higher energetic-environmental 
performance (Agostinho and Ortega, 2013) and identify the best addressed solution at the local level within 
local communities where action can occur at a rapid rate (Rauland et al., 2015). In order to reach energy 
security in rural communities in developing countries, adaption of methods for local small-scale production 
of bioenergy are therefore crucial (Golberg et al., 2014). The use of those methods in a decentralized 
production of energy would have the potential to benefit the local population by adapting to future 
resource scarcity and increasing availability of labour (Kamp and Østergård, 2016). Utilization of local 
labour supply and renewable resources to replace present energy sources may thereby be a way to 
contribute to empowerment of rural areas (Kamp and Østergård, 2016).

2.1.5 BIOREFINERY WITH SEAWEED AS FEEDSTOCK  
A biorefinery is by the International Energy Agency (IEA) Bioenergy Task 42 defined as; “the sustainable 
processing of biomass into a spectrum of marketable products and energy” (Jong and Jungmeier, 2015). 
Biorefinery is a pathway for capturing solar energy and carbon dioxide via photosynthesis into organic 
matter, which is then harvested and processed into useful biomass-derived products such as liquid fuel, 
electricity, chemicals, materials food and feed (Golberg et al., 2014). Biomass is essential for a green 
production of chemicals and fuels as it is the only carbon rich materials available (Lee et al., 2014). The 
utilization of seaweed as raw materials in a biorefinery is therefore a promising alternative to fossil 
resources for production of energy and chemicals, thus mitigating climate change and enhancing energy 
security (Baweja et al., 2016). 
Seaweed is a promising feedstock for biofuel production, as it does not possess the major drawbacks that 
are associated with the current feedstocks for generations of biofuels. Seaweed does not compete directly 
with arable land that could be used for food crops and furthermore seaweed contains very little lignin 
compared to lignocellulosic feedstock (Golberg et al., 2014). However, the direct use of seaweeds for 
bioethanol production is not cost-effective (Baweja et al., 2016). Issues related to creating both 
economically feasible and sustainable processes for bioenergy purpose has created concerns (Langlois et 
al., 2012). The future of bioethanol from seaweeds therefore depends on the diffusion of new technologies, 
integrated into a balanced and complete utilization of seaweed biomass (Baweja et al., 2016) in a combined 
manner to both extract value-added products and produce bioenergy (Langlois et al., 2012). Several reports 
on macro algae biorefinery concepts have been published and the development is ongoing (Golberg et al., 
2014; Kemausuor et al., 2014; Kumar et al., 2013; Lee et al., 2014; Seghetta et al., 2014; Suganya et al., 
2016a; Trivedi et al., 2015). 
The products possible to produce from a biorefinery are determined by the choice of raw material, which 
makes the design of a biorefinery a complex task (Golberg et al., 2014). It is extremely important to invest 
in research and development of biorefineries of seaweed to jointly apply different process technologies to 
successfully produce seaweed-based bioenergy. It is essential to recycle and optimize processes to utilize 
the resource most efficiently by maximizing the material recovery to get the maximum output of supplied 
input – especially of the non-renewable sources (Agostinho and Ortega, 2013; DANIDA, 2014; Fasahati et 
al., 2015). However, a comprehensive analysis of all the component of a biorefinery is needed to ensure a 
holistic approach that is sustainable in all its aspect of its implementation in rural communities in 
developing countries.

2.1.6 CHARACTERISATION OF DIFFERENT SEAWEED SPECIES 
Seaweed is a macro alga that in comparison to microalga, which have lipid content up to 50%, only 
constitutes ±5 % lipid of the total dry weight. Therefore, seaweed as an energy crop is dependent on 
efficient conversion of its carbohydrate content (Fasahati et al., 2015). However, there is a broad variety in 
the chemical characteristics among seaweeds species and between species, which makes them suitable for 
different purposes. There are three major kinds of seaweed; red, brown and green algae with different 
composition used in a variety of industries illustrated in an overview in Table 1.

2.1.7 PRODUCTION OF ENZYMES FOR BIOETHANOL PRODUCTION 
2.1.7.1 
Enzymatic saccharification 
A key process in the conversion of biomass into products is the degradation of complex carbohydrates into 
simple sugars. From a sustainability point of view, saccharification of seaweed is best proceeded by 
enzymatic catalysis as it degrades seaweed into products with minimum use of water and energy 
(Agostinho et al., 2015). Saccharification by enzymes is traditionally produced by microorganisms cultivated 
in a nutrient-rich media as used in commercial applications today (Zhuang and Marchant, 2007). However, 
solid state fermentation is an alternative that involves much less liquid reducing the downstream process 
steps and thereby simplifying the process, which is much needed as the cost of enzyme remain a key issues 
in the production of bioethanol (Hong et al., 2013; Zhuang and Marchant, 2007). 
 
2.1.7.2 
On-site vs. off-site production 
The high enzyme dependency for bioethanol production has led to research in on-site enzyme production 
to both manage costs and a steady and sufficient enzyme supply to the increasing bioethanol production 
(Hong et al., 2013). Furthermore, off-site production of enzymes have high energetic-environmental costs 
due to extra processing steps required for downstream purification, resulting in reduced yields as well as 
addition of preservatives add extra energy inputs to the production of enzymes. On-site production may 
therefore be a way to eliminate the energy requiring purification, which required more input to 
compensate for the enzyme lost in the purification steps (Hong et al., 2013). Furthermore, a possibility to 
create jobs, keep the cost low and minimise import by keeping the enzymes production within the 
biorefinery boundaries. This, however, sets a number of challenges for the locally available skills and 
technical support services, the availability of local nutrients, a steady source of energy and additional 
transport of feedstock to the factory.  
 
2.1.7.3 
Issues related to enzymes produced for seaweed saccharification 
In the production of products from lignocellulosic biomass, lignin is the largest barrier and this complicates 
the saccharification process. As seaweed does not contain lignin, it is possible to skip harsh pre-treatment 
methods (Fasahati et al., 2015). Seaweeds face other barriers such as the limited availability of known 
enzymes with the ability to degrade polysaccharides in seaweed and microorganism efficiently converting 
those into biofuels (Golberg et al., 2014). As seaweed polysaccharides are structurally complex and diverse 
in chemical composition from terrestrial plants, they require different approaches and organisms (Trivedi et 
al., 2015). One of the main challenges is therefore to identify suitable microorganisms to perform 
hydrolysis, saccharification and fermentation of all types of carbohydrates present in seaweed (Fasahati et

2.1.8 MICROBIAL FUEL CELLS FOR ELECTRICITY GENERATION 
Microbial Fuel Cells (MFC) are constructed around the natural respiration process of bacteria degrading 
organic material to generate energy. The biochemical process is today widely use in wastewater treatment 
plants to generate electricity via anaerobic bacteria, which break the organic material producing electricity 
and reducing the amount of sludge (Foley et al., 2010). MFCs are considered a promising future technology 
as it utilizes the waste stream from organic material for conversion into energy by functioning as a living 
fuel cell (Sun et al., 2014). However, it remains to be demonstrated how lab scale MFCs can be scaled up to 
be utilized for industrial application (Foley et al., 2010). Research projects are therefore now looking into 
how to tackle that barrier and a MFC are included in this study to elucidate an upcoming technology, which 
is currently not extensively documented in combined biorefineries in the literature. 
2.1.8.1 
Principles of bioelectricity generation in MFCs 
The generation of an electric current by microbial conversion of organic material happens as illustrated in a 
schematic drawing in Figure 3: 
 
Figure 3. Organic substrate enters the MFC via the anode chamber and creates a biofilm on the anode of bacteria breaking down 
organic material producing electrons in the oxygen-free environment. Because there is no electron donor present, the electrons are 
instead transferred to the anode into a circuit and further to the cathode. The cathode chamber is separated from the anode 
chamber and allows oxygen to enter. The electrons, transferred to the cathode chamber via the circuit, react with the oxygen 
present and thereby work as a final electron acceptor ending the reaction. Cation and anions are allowed to cross the ion membrane 
to maintain the charge of the system. Illustration from (Sun et al., 2014). 
To be able to harvest electrochemical energy from bacteria, a MFC consists of electrodes that enable 
bioconversion of the substrate via a biochemical system. The composition of the biological rich substrate is 
very important for the overall success of a MFC. The organic substrate not only works as fuel but also 
provides and sustains the microbial community that is essential for efficient electricity generation (Sun et 
al., 2014).  The anode chamber of the reactor is maintained under anaerobic conditions and fed with 
organic substrate containing a variety of bacteria. A mixed variety of microorganisms grows on the anode 
creating a biofilm (Sun et al., 2014). They completely oxidize the organic substrate via anaerobic

respiration, in which electrons are donated by an organic substrate and transported though a serial 
electron transport chain from intracellular to extracellular before reaching the anode which serves as the

The anode is connected via an electrical external circuit that allows the elections to be transported to the 
cathode. In the cathode chamber oxygen is present to act as terminal electron acceptor (Sun et al., 2014). 
The electrical power is harvested in the electrical circuit connecting the anode and cathode (Foley et al., 
2010; Offei et al., 2016; Sun et al., 2014). The cathode and anode chamber are separated by an ion 
exchange membrane securing the anode is the only intermediate electron acceptor in the anode chamber 
and restricting oxygen to the cathode chamber. 
 
The ion exchange membrane allows ions to pass through thereby maintaining the charge balance of the 
system (Sun et al., 2014). Not all types of MFC require an ion exchange membrane. The membrane-less 
single chamber reactor performs with a higher power density compared to two-chamber systems, as the 
ion transport is more efficient without the membrane. The free ion transportation improves microbial 
activity and supplies sufficient protons in the cathode, increasing the reaction. The low internal resistance 
of membrane-less air cathode reactor therefore generally make them produce the highest power density 
(Sun et al., 2014). 
 
For MFC to be a green and sustainable alternative for bioenergy generation, it needs to be constructed, 
operated and maintained with economic viability, environmental consideration and social responsibility. 
The use of local materials and consideration of suitable anode electrode material with both large surface 
area as well as proper electrical conductivity and possibility for biological growth should be taken into 
consideration (Offei et al., 2016).

2.1.9 INDUSTRIAL APPLICATIONS OF HIGH-VALUE COMPOUNDS FROM SEAWEED 
2.1.9.1 
Seaweed as food 
Seaweeds has been consumed by humans since ancient times, but is presently becoming more and more 
popular as it is an excellent source of protein, minerals and vitamin A, B1, B12, C, D, and E, riboflavin, 
niacin, pantothenic acid, and folic acid. Seaweeds presently contribute to 1% of the world’s food supply, 
which will most likely increase in the future. We might furthermore expand our use of seaweed species as 
we today only use 145 species as a food source out of nearly 10.000 marine algal species present on Earth 
(Baweja et al., 2016). 
2.1.9.2 
Extraction of high-value hydrocolloids  
Currently, cultivating seaweed for the only purpose of bioenergy is not financially profitable or 
environmental beneficial enough in itself. However, as seaweed contain a range of different high-value 
compounds, extraction of those might make the production process more feasible (Langlois et al., 2012). 
Both red and brown seaweeds species can be cultivated for extraction of value-added products such as the 
hydrocolloids; carrageenan, alginate, and agar and many other products for thickening material in food, 
pharmaceutical, cosmetic, textile and biotech industries, which have proved to be good source of revenue 
(Baweja et al., 2016; Msuya, 2006). The global production of hydrocolloids has reached 100,000 tons per 
year with a gross market value about US$ 1.1 billion and are currently extracted from seaweed cultivated in 
many developing countries (Rhein-Knudsen et al., 2015). Alginate is extracted from brown seaweed, 
whereas carrageenan and agar are extracted from red seaweed. Hydrocolloids are the main commercial 
seaweed extract from where carrageenan is the most important component typically consistenting of 25% 
of the total dry weight (Langlois et al., 2012).  
2.1.9.3 
Ulvan - a Polysaccharide from Green Seaweeds 
The cell wall of green seaweed contain the economically important compound “ulvan” with industrial 
applications and a rich sources of omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids (Baweja et al., 2016). However, at the 
moment the green seaweeds remains largely unexploited, but this might change as the demand for novel 
materials and technologies is increasing as well as research into these unexploited marine green species 
(Alves et al., 2013). There are presently limited reports on the use of effluent streams following high-value 
compound extraction from seaweeds. Nevertheless, they may be used as source of feedstock for bioenergy 
production in the future in a combined biorefinery (Kumar et al., 2013).

2.2 SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT OF BIOENERGY 
To be sustainable is defined by Oxford Dictionaries as “able to be maintained at a certain rate” and 
elaborated as “Conserving an ecological balance by avoiding depletion of natural resources” (Oxford 
Dictionaries, 2016). The general assembly of UN did in 1987 define sustainable development as 
“development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations 
to meet their own needs” (Brundtland, 1987). However, what it exactly means to meet the needs of people 
today without depriving future generation needs can be up to discussion.  
2.2.1 THE GREEN ECONOMY 
Though, we know for sure that sustainability is much more than the depletion of natural resources. It is the 
capacity of our human society to continue indefinitely within the natural cycles of the earths and its 
spheres. Responsibly environmental, economic and social concerns in rural development are crucial for 
sustainable development as human well-being inextricably are linked to the status of our environment 
(Hensen et al., 2010).  
 
Figure 5. Illustration of the different views of the relationship between economy, society and environment. (Cato, 2012). 
As illustrated in Figure 5, the understanding of the interconnected relations between society, economy and 
environment can be interpreted in different ways. The left picture illustrate the conventional economic 
view of the interaction between economy, society and environment. To the right, the green economics 
paradigm is illustrated, with the inner circle representing the economy that is wholly enclosed within the 
society, as the economy is built on the society system (Cato, 2012).  
The vison is to improved human well-being and social equity, while significantly reducing environmental 
risks and ecological scarcities. However, green economies paradigm is not based on a vision of radical 
changing the way we live by consuming less, but rather the idea of developing low-carbon -and 
environmentally friendly technologies to be able to largely continuing our way of living – with the help of 
technologies to use resources more efficiently.

2.2.2 BARRIERS TO A SUSTAINABLE SOCIETY  
Behind a less technology-based vision of a future are the  organization The Natural Step, which has defined 
the four major barriers, which we need to overcome to create a sustainability society (Bradbury, 2007): 
o Relatively large flows of materials from the earth´s crust, such as fossil CO2 and heavy metals 
 
o Accumulation of substances created by society, such as antibiotics and endocrine disruptors 
 
o Physically inhibit nature´s ability to run cycles creating deforestation and draining of 
groundwater tables 
 
o Barriers to people meeting their basic needs worldwide 
 
These four barriers are all a sign of how we are undermining our environment at an alarming rate creating 
barriers toward sustainable development.  Barrier we need to overcome in the transition to a sustainable 
society (Bradbury, 2007). In order to be sustainable an economically viable, environmentally compatible 
and socially responsible production process should be developed that eliminate waste and our current one-
way linear flow of resources, which has brought the problems of today. As Albert Einstein formulated it, we 
need another way of thinking to solve these problems than the one that created them. It is not sufficient to 
solve design challenges by a technological fix in a simplistic approach. We should rather look at the world in 
a whole-system perspective to be able to study the system`s components and couplings rather than 
studying individual parts (Magnin, 2009).  
2.2.3 BIOENERGY POLICIES FOR SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT 
The establishment of biorefineries based on seaweed feedstock should be a step-wise incremental change 
to secure a steady implementation of farming approaches and technologies for a long-term sustainable 
development (Kamp et al., 2016b). In order to be able to bridge new energy technologies to rural 
communities in developing countries, specific policies are useful to tackle economic and social challenges. 
To integrate bioreﬁneries into coastal communities, business networks and governments in both developed 
and developing countries have adopted national legislation strategies for climate change mitigation. Their 
strategies are based on promoting clean, safe and low-carbon energy sources due to global policy debates 
on energy and climate (Golberg et al., 2014). They promote investments in clean technologies including 
renewable technologies such as wind power, solar power, hydropower or biofuels as sustainable 
alternatives to fossil fuel based energy sources. To facilitate the development of technologies that can help 
increase resilience to the impacts of climate change, first the Kyoto Protocol and later Paris agreement was 
established for research and promotion of renewable forms of energy and enhancement of energy 
efficiency (Unfccc, 1998; United Nations, 2015).

2.3 OBJECTIVE OF THE STUDY 
For the most efficient use of resources in the progress of sustainable development, this study aims to 
quantify the emergy memory in cultivation and processing of seaweed into bioenergy. In addition, evaluate 
the possible effects of seaweed production on coastal communities based on studies from other developing 
countries. The biorefinery are a hypothetical system based on present knowledge and technologies scaled 
up to industrial scale and consists of multiple technologies in an effort to evaluate different solutions for 
cultivation of seaweed and bioprocessing into bioenergy. The system is based upon scientific literature and 
personal communication with researchers from the SeaBioGha product (DANIDA, 2014).  
The main effort of the project is to conduct an Emergy Assessment of a conceptual biorefinery for 
conversion of seaweed into bioenergy in Ghana. Regarding the cultivation of seaweed three different 
scenarios will illustrate the difference in emergy input and productivity for the cultivation phase for a 
women owned Off-bottom plot(S1), a man owned Off-bottom plot(S2) and Floating line plot(S3), 
respectably. Furthermore, the processing of seaweed into enzymes and bioethanol are evaluated, as well 
as the additional treatment of the effluent streams treated into either bioelectricity by MFC (P1), biogas by 
anaerobic digestion (P2) or used directly as fertilizer (P3). See Figure 6 for a schematic illustration of the 
scenarios. 
The evaluation permits a ranking of three cultivation methods and thee different biorefinery approaches, 
which can hopefully be useful in the consideration prior to implementing the project. The method of the 
study is to account for the origin of all material, emergy and information of the process that encompasses 
all stages of the production and use of seaweed as a source of biomass for enzymes and bioenergy 
production.

3.1 THE CASE STUDY  
In the case of seaweed cultivation and bioprocessing in Ghana, an Emergy Assessment lists the resources 
inputs and thereby assess the amount of energy needed for seaweed processing as well as content of 
renewable, non-renewable and imported emergy required for each scenario. The idea on during an Emergy 
Assessment on the system is to, assess the environmental performance of the technological options with 
respect to resource use efﬁciency in a biophysical perspective and the degree to which each approach 
depends on renewable resources.  
As a biorefinery, consist of human-influenced processes it is necessary to accounts for labour inputs to be 
able to quantify the labour intensity of the different phases of cultivation and production of bioenergy. 
Thereby get the full picture by including labour inputs contribution to the overall environmental impact of a 
biorefinery.  
Labour can be accounted for as direct labour, the human contribution to managing the processes directly 
accounted for in man-hours. Or indirectly labour, all the external input humans have contributed to in the 
making of input from outside the system stated in monetary cost converted into global man-hours using an 
average global conversion rate (Kamp et al., 2016a, 2016b). Emergy Assessment will systematically include 
labour inputs along with material and energy inputs. All renewable energy sources from outside the 
systems as well as materials and labours are included in the analysis.

3.1.4 PRE-TREATMENT 
As seaweeds do not contain lignin, the carbohydrates are more easily accessible and any form of harsh pre-
treatment is therefore skipped (Fasahati et al., 2015). Instead, mechanical pre-treatment are performed by 
milling and grinding the seaweed (Trivedi et al., 2013). The dry milling attritor runs in batch mode, with the 
feedstock charged into the mil at the top, grinding the seaweed into an even mass, which are discharged 
through the bottom of the attritor. 
3.1.5 PRODUCTION OF CELLULASE FOR BIOETHANOL PRODUCTION 
Green seaweed Ulva fasciata has been selected by multiple studies (Chen et al., 2015; Khan and Hussain, 
2015; Trivedi et al., 2015, 2013) as a potential feedstock for bioethanol production due to its high

3.2 METHODOLOGY 
3.2.1 EMERGY ASSESSMENT (EMA) 
The reason for conducting Environmental Sustainability Assessments is to illuminate the use of resources 
“upstream” of the production of the product and to evaluate the effects of the product on the surrounding 
environment “downstream” the production process (Ulgiati et al., 2006). By using Environmental 
Sustainability Assessments on different technologies for a specific product, it is possible to evaluate the 
different production processes and identify inputs, which are responsible for undesirable outputs (Kamp 
and Østergård, 2016). Different impact assessments generally focus on either “upstream” methods or 
“downstream” methods and a combination of impact assessments is therefore needed for a holistic 
evaluation of the product. 
Emergy accounting, also called a Emergy Assessment, is a methods within Environmental Sustainability 
Assessments that focus on the “upstream” resource flow needed to produce a product or service (Odum, 
1996). Emergy accounting stands out from other Environmental Sustainability Assessments as a systematic 
methods that include all (energy) flows going into the system, accounted for as materials, energy or human 
labour (Kamp et al., 2016a). Emergy accounting quantitatively assesses ecological and technical systems by 
identifying every input source as either a flow-limited renewable resource (e.g. solar radiation, deep heat 
and tidal energy) or a stock-limited non-renewable resource (e.g. fossil resources) or labour inputs required 
for manufacturing the product(s) (Wright and Østergård, 2016). 
3.2.1.1 
Origin of Emergy accounting 
The Emergy methodology was developed by H. T. Odum on the principles of thermodynamics and system 
theory (H.T. Odum, 1996). The aim of an Emergy assessment is to investigating the amount of solar energy 
equivalent required to produce a certain product or service, taking into account all direct inputs and 
indirect preceding inputs needed to support the production of a product or service (Odum, 1996; Brown 
and Ulgiati, 2004). By accounting the inventory of all relevant energy flows of a particular process 
systematic, it is  thereby possible to quantitatively assess the ecological sustainability of a product (Kamp 
and Østergård, 2011).  
An Emergy assessment quantifies different inputs by normalizing all products and services to a unit that 
represent the quantity and quality of work created and maintained by the system (Pulselli et al., 2008; 
Kamp et al., 2016).

3.2.2 UNIT EMERGY VALUES

Emergy Values (UEVs). The different sources of energy are converted into solar equivalent joule (sej) by a 
solar transformity factor comparable to the amount of emergy per unit of the given source of energy, 
defined by Odum as “the solar emergy required to make 1 J of a service or a product.” (Agostinho and 
Ortega, 2013; H.T. Odum, 1996). These values is indicators for the efficiency of a specific resource and 
calculated as the emergy support embedded in the resource divided with the available energy in the 
resource (Kamp and Østergård, 2013). As a products transformity is an expression of the quality of the 
output itself, for the higher the transformity, the more emergy is required to make the product flow 
(Agostinho and Ortega, 2013).

3.2.3 EMERGY ALGEBRA 
The emergy methodology follows a set of rules referred to as emergy algebra (Brown and Herendeen, 
1996). The four algebra rules are the foundation of emergy accounting and used as a starting point for the 
continuing development of the ongoing process advancing the Emergy methodology to fit various systems 
and scales. The four rules are defined by Brown as follows: 
1. All source emergy to a process is assigned to the process’ output(s); 
2. Co-products from a process have the total emergy assigned to each pathway; 
3. When a pathway splits, the emergy is assigned to each ‘leg’ of the split based on its percent of the 
total energy flow of the pathway;  
4. Emergy cannot be counted twice within a system:  
a. Emergy in feedbacks cannot be double counted;  
b. Co-products, when reunited, cannot be added to equal a sum greater than the source 
emergy from which they were derived 
The emergy theory is based on the foregone “memory” of resource inputs and each output of the system 
therefore have all the emergy require for its production assigned to it. However, as emergy cannot be 
counted twice within a system an elaboration of rules for co-products in small system perspective is 
needed (Kamp and Østergård, 2016, 2013). There is an issue in fulfilling the rules of attributing the full 
emergy to each co-product regardless of the fact that multiple product shares the use of resources (Kamp 
and Østergård, 2013).  
3.2.3.1 
System Expansion and allocations strategies to account for co-products 
By System Expansion, the system boundaries are enlarged to include additional functions to make the 
different output transformities comparable with other products (Kamp and Østergård, 2013), Figure 
14. However, by using the System expansion method with substitution it may be challenging to find 
complementary alternatives to the output of the system and cause high probabilities for uncertainties and 
inaccuracies. Allocation is another methods to account for co-products and is based on the physical 
relationship between different outputs as mass or energy content (Kamp and Østergård, 2013).  
When it comes to calculating transformities in a system with co-products there are different ways to go 
around it. By using system expansion, the different outputs from a multifunctional system are included 
within the system boundaries. Whereas by using allocation the environmental burdens is distributed 
among the co-products of the multifunctional system (Kamp and Østergård, 2016).

high contribution of non-renewable and imported energy in the studied system, while low values indicate 
high contribution from renewable energy inputs. ELR values lower than 2 as generally seen as low impact 
system, values between 2 and 10 have a moderate impact, while values higher than 10 indicated a system 
with a large environmental stress (Agostinho and Ortega, 2013).

corresponding emergy flows to the studied system. The list of inventory is based on the data, calculations 
and assumptions found in Appendix A, while illustration of the different activities can found in Appendix B. 
To be able to fit all indicators and inputs into the tables some phases are given on two pages, given by part I 
and part II. 
The emergy table contains all inputs and corresponding energy memory, structured in a systemic way 
throughout the process. The cultivation of seaweed, as well as the washing, drying and storage of seaweed 
are divided into the three different scenarios. S1: Women managed farms cultivated via the Off-bottom 
method, S2: Men operated farms by the Off-bottom method and S3: Cultivation of seaweed with the 
Floating Line method independent of gender. The direct comparison of every resource input into the 
different cultivation methods paint a picture of the differences in resource use and specific emergy for the 
individual products given in sej per mass.  
All inputs from direct labour is regarded as low consumption UEV, based on the results from to Kamp et al., 
(2016a) while the indirect labour is calculated in global UEV (global avg. man-hours) based on the same 
study.  
Following indicators are included in the tables:  
 
“%” as the percentage of the individual inputs from the overall emergy memory of the phase  
 
“Global Ren. Fraction %” is the global renewability fraction percentage. Indicating part of the 
resource, that is considered to be based on renewable sources.  
 
“Local fraction %” is local fraction percentage and is an indication of the energy memory that 
comes from a local source.

For the energy memory of the cultivation phase (Table 7), the renewable inputs only constitute 2% of the 
total emergy flow, which is an exceptionally low percentage. The reason for that is the relatively small 
farming plots, but wide range of materiel and labour inputs. The non-renewable inputs nitrogen and 
phosphorus is considered non-renewable, as they most likely would be nutrients run-off from agriculture 
and aquaculture in the area. They constitute of ~10% of the emergy flow and are thereby a significant 
source for nutrients uptake from the surrounding ecosystem.  
The percentage of material input in the cultivation plots is responsible for 17 to 23% of the total emergy 
flow depending on the scenario. For the purchased inputs, the largest difference between the scenarios are 
found in the larger percentages of total emergy for the women owned plots(S1), as the cultivation area is 
noticeably less. Nevertheless, is the need for material and transport the same as S2, resulting in a higher 
percentage of purchased inputs. 
The UEV for the three scenarios rank S2 lowest, 2,28E12 sej/ year, S1 has the highest of 2,59E+12 sej/ year, 
while S2 is in between with 2,32 sej/ year. The most favourable scenario is S2 as it has the largest 
production area and most efficient use of resources. 
Regarding the amount of global renewable resources can only 14% of the emergy flow can be accounted 
for as a global renewable fraction (Table 8). Nevertheless, almost all the inputs are local. Overall, making 
the cultivation phase a heavy on labour and local material.

The next phase consists of all the post-harvest activity consisting of washing, drying and storage of the 
harvested seaweed (Table 9).  
4.1.2.3 
Washing phase 
The washing of the seaweed is carried out on the beach in wood washtubs. The activity is dependent on the 
non-renewable input of groundwater, electricity for pumping the water and timber for building the 
washtubs. However, the water and materials only consist of 5%-6%, out of 60-62% for the washing phase, 
and it is instead the direct and indirect labour that consist the greatest energy memory, calculated as their 
money to emergy ratio converted to global man-hours. The washing phase is intensive, as it requires a large 
input to wash all the seaweed free of salt, which can cause serious complication in the fermentation tanks 
and anaerobic digesters. 
4.1.2.4 
Drying phase 
The drying phase is reliant on the renewable emergy flows from the sun to dry the seaweed. The activity is 
carried out on platforms near the beach and build of wood post and palm leave mats. The wood is assumed 
to be harvest in a nearby forest and therefor assumed to be 50% global renewable. The largest emergy flow 
is from the direct labour required to spread the seaweed one the mats in an even thin layer (see Appendix 
B for illustrations of the process). The direct labour is assumed to be 10 % renewable, while indirect labour 
16% renewable. It is a rather passive process mostly reliant of the sun and time for the seaweed to dry.  
4.1.2.5 
Storage phase 
After drying are the seaweed packed in sacks and stored in a woodsheds. Only purchased materials are 
used in the process and timber consist of the greatest input. The storage of S2 need twice the amount of 
space for storage and as timber is an energy intensive process it greatly influences the transformity in the 
end 
In general, the emergy memory for S2 is largest in the feedstock dependent steps, which increases with the 
increasing amount of input, whereas the items less dependent on the amount of input is favouring a larger 
production. However, in generally S2 has the most efficient use of resources and both The UEV for the 
washing, drying and storage phase(3,16E11) and UEV incl. the cultivation phase(2,60E12) are small 
compared to S1(3,21E11) for washing, drying and storage phase and UEV(2,91E12) incl. cultivation S3 is in 
between with UEV of (3,08E11 sej/year) and 2,63E12 sej/year.  
In total 25% of the inputs are of global renewability fraction, as a resource from the forest and groundwater 
constitute a considerable fraction (Table 10).

4.1.2.9 
The biorefinery building and pre-treatment of seaweed 
The biorefinery consist of a 1.500 m3 building made with concrete and bricks, setting the framework for the 
biorefinery. The resource input going into the building consists of purchased inputs incl. direct and indirect 
labour. 
The first process all seaweed entering the biorefinery reaches is the pre-treatment of dry milling consisting 
of input from the steel reactor and electricity for the milling process. The electricity is assumed to consist of 
47% global renewable fraction, whereas steel is assumed to have a global renewable fraction of 5%, Table 
12, as earlier studies from Ghana concluded (Kamp & Østergård, 2016 Kamp et al., 2016). 
4.1.2.10 Production of enzymes for bioethanol production 
The production of enzymes for further use in the production of bioethanol is based on non-renewable 
inputs of the groundwater used to create the right conditions for the fungi to thrive in the fermentation 
tank. Furthermore, nutrients are added to create the right environment for the fungi and are the 
temperature increased to 40 °C. Even though water is seen as a non-renewable input, it is assumed to a 
have a global renewability fraction of 100 as well as be 100% local. As it is pumped locally and a renewable 
source when seen as part of the water cycle. Stainless steel is used for the tanks and the operational cost 
for the materials is calculated as well as the specialized labour needed. The fungi needed to produce 
enzymes is excluded from the study as it is assumed to naturally be present on the seaweed.  
4.1.2.11 Production of bioethanol - saccharification, fermentation and distillation 
The production of bioethanol is based on the stream of enzyme soup from the fungi cultivation, as well as 
fresh seaweed entering the tank and added nutrients rich water to create the right process conditions. 
Enzyme saccharification is happening simultaneous with fermentation for which the yeast Saccharomyces 
cerevisiae is added. It has not been possible to find a transformity for yeast, instead the oil needed to 
produce the yeast is included, as shown by example in the study of Seghetta et al., (2014).

5 SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS 
5.1.1.1

6 DISCUSSION 
6.1.1 RESULTS FROM EMERGY ASSESSMENT 
6.1.1.1 
Results from the cultivation phase 
The resource efficiency of three scenarios for seaweed and the subsequent three processing scenarios were 
compared based on sej pr. yearly production. The emergy assessment of the cultivation scenarios 
represented by the three farming methods of the women-owned Off-bottom plots (S1), man-owned Off-
bottom plots (S2) and the use of the Floating Line method (S3) illustrate three different methods of 
cultivation seaweed. As the Off-bottom plots cultivated by men is double the area of a women-owned plot 
(3,8 vs 1,9 ha), the on-site renewability fraction is correspondingly bigger. The Floating line method has 
with the in-between size of 2,06 ha and on-site renewability fraction between the S1 and S2 scenario. The 
production of seaweed biomass was set as 0,35 kg/ meter line per cycle for all three scenarios (Crawford, 
B., 2007) and the production was therefore fully dependent of the size of the plots.  
For the emergy memory of the cultivation phase, the renewable fraction only constitutes around 2%, which 
is an exceptionally low percentage compared to other Emergy analysis of agricultural systems (Kamp and 
Østergård, 2011; Kamp et al., 2016b; Seghetta et al., 2014; Wright and Østergård, 2015). The reason for 
that is the relative small farming plots, but a wide range of materiel inputs as well as labour inputs. The on-
site renewable inputs are only calculated as the exact area of cultivations of the seaweed. However, it 
could be argued that the cultivation of seaweed takes up energy from a larger area than the plots they are 
cultivation on. As the cultivation will most likely happen in a lagoon area protected from strong waves, the 
renewable energy from the tide, rain and deep heat be affected by the whole lagoon area and carry with 
them nutrients and CO2. 
The UEVs for the cultivation phase rank S2 as the most efficient use of resources with the low transformity 
of 2,27E+12 sej/ year. Scenario S3 is only a slightly higher with a transformity of 2,31E+12 sej/ year followed 
by S1 with a transformity of 2,59E+12 sej/ year. S1 had the smallest cultivation area but still requires as 
much machinery and fuels as the two other scenarios and primary therefor ended with the largest UEV. The 
purchased inputs from society are for that reason the category with widest difference between the three 
scenarios.  
The non-renewable inputs, represented by the nitrogen and phosphor taken up by the seaweed in the 
growth phase make up approx. 10 % of the emergy flows. The large amount of nutrients run-off from the 
surrounding area is therefore beneficial for the cultivation of seaweed and a possibility to turn an 
environmental problem into a resource once again since it would be possible to return the nutrient back to

the fields as fertilizes or be stored in products instead of accumulating in the ocean. The beneficial trade of 
recycling phosphor back to the land and the fact that it is a “leftovers” resource from another process, it 
could be discussed if some kind of “emergy discounts” would be suitable as cultivation is a beneficial 
method to take up excess amount of nutrients in the seaweed and bring it back to land as fertilizers or used 
again stored in energy carries.   
Labour constitutes the largest emergy flow of 69-72 % (Table 10) as cultivation of seaweed is a labour 
intensive process. Especially tying of seaweed to the lines in preparation of a cultivation cycling and the 
harvesting of seaweed is labour intensive and the most dominant emergy contribution in the whole 
cultivation phase. Thereby are cultivation of seaweed fit for purpose, when it comes to job generation in 
rural areas, as it requires a small amount of material, which are almost all relatively accessible for people in 
rural area. The boat for harvesting represent most expensive input, however as one boat can cover a large 
area and therefore can be shared between many firmest, it becomes less of an obstacle.  
The direct labour inputs are throughout the study assumed to be 10% renewable as assumed in a study by 
Kamp et al., 2016b. However, one could argue that people living in rural areas might be more dependent on 
locally produced goods and service and have greater tendency to rely on renewable inputs than people in 
cities. In addition, they might be further away from the emergy intensive infrastructure provided in the 
urban areas as hospitals, electronics and fossil fuel based technologies as well as a more meat rich diet. 
However, it has not been possible to find sufficient evidence of this and the assumption of 10% renewable 
is therefore retained. 
6.1.1.2 
Results from the washing of seaweed 
There are no on-site renewable inputs in the washing activity, but on-site non-renewable input in the 
consumption of groundwater for washing the seaweed, as well as the purchased input of electricity used 
for pumping the water and material used to construct the washtubs. However, it is the indirect labour 
inputs (service), which accounts for the largest emergy flow especially all the emergy embedded in the 
groundwater and electricity used for the washing phase. 
6.1.1.3 
Results from the drying, storage and transport phase 
Drying of seaweed is a rather passive process utilizing the sun to dry the harvested seaweed and can in 
general be regarded as a low-energy required process constitution 6-11% of the emergy going into the 
washing, drying and storage phase. The seaweed is stored in sacks in woodsheds until transported to the 
biorefinery. The emergy memory for the timber itself, as well as all the indirect labour and man-hours going 
into build it, is the largest inputs. Timber is a widely used input in the whole cultivation and post-harvesting 
process and access to a forest as well as sustainable collecting of timber is crucial for a balanced use of

6.1.1.4 
Results from the biorefinery 
For the enzyme production numbers from Seghetta et al., (2014) was used. However, it is a very simple 
modelling of a system and from the supplementary material, it appears that no media was added and all 
the numbers of size is calculated from the amount of seaweed present. However, to create optimal 
condition for bioethanol fermentation it is important for the microorganism to have optimal living 
conditions. Therefore, media working as a buffer to reach these condition are crucial and should not be 
neglected. The calculations in this study is based on an interpretation of the reported results including few 
assumptions. 
No references to transformities for nutrients, yeast or microorganism or in general for biological processes 
in a biorefinery setting have been found. In literature transformities have all typically been neglected as it 
does not consist of a great volume. However, it does represent a great amount of knowledge and research 
which is then neglected. It is therefore important to include enzymes in the Emergy Assessment as they 
represent a large amount of energy memory.  
6.1.2 ENZYME PRODUCTION AND THE EMERGY STORED WITHIN 
An Emergy Assessment of enzyme production has only been conducted a few times in literature (Agostinho 
et al., 2015; Coppola et al., 2009) and still many assumptions and neglecting of inputs is happening.  This is 
a significant problem for the analysis of enzymes contribution to production of bioethanol as Agostinho et 
al., (2015) concluded that the enzymes only contributed to 0,49 % of the total emergy. However, as 
enzymes often contribute to 25-50 % of the coast of bioethanol. There is a clear missing link.  
The reason for the high price of enzymes is all the included research and development going into 
production of enzymes as well as the downstream processing steps. However, when these are neglected it 
is not a mystery why Agostinho et al., (2015) get so favourable results for enzyme production. The true cost 
of enzymes has to my knowledge not been presented, but is a study in itself that will hopefully be 
conducted in the future. 
6.1.2.1 
Energy embodied in enzymes 
When assessing the energy input of enzyme production multiple dimensions are important to consider. 
Besides the obvious economic costs of production, enzymes have a high investment of research and 
development by all the intellectual investments embodied in enzyme production through years of scientific 
research. The energy of this information embedded in the research and development process is often 
disregarded due to “operational difficulties in the quantification” (Agostinho et al., 2015). However, if the

costs of enzymes from an off-site supplier should be able to be compared with on-site production an 
estimation of the “information” in enzymes are crucial as the costs given by off-site commercial supplies 
also include the research and development the company has invested into developing enzymes.  
To be able to evaluate the emergy stored in of genetic information as well as the emergy needed to build 
and maintain organisms required a great understanding of the complexity and average emergy demand for 
genes and organism. After the idea of Odum and refined by Jorgensen and Brown (2004) they found that 
“as the complexity of the biological carrier of information increases, the emergy costs of maintaining the 
carrier increases faster than the information carried”. However, beside a few studies is there still room for 
improvement and development of strategies to account for microorganism in Emergy Assessments, which 
is needed to get the true energy cost of yeast, fungi and other microorganism used in production of 
bioenergy and material from biomass in general. 
Both public and private research efforts are now going into strategies for seaweed conversion by enzymatic 
catalysis. Research into genetic engineering strive to develop microorganism able to utilize the different 
carbohydrates of seaweed for biofuel production (Fasahati et al., 2015). A strategy for accounting for 
genetic research intensive microorganism is therefore needed to evaluate these new technologies. 
6.1.3 SUSTAINABLE PRODUCTION OF BIOENERGY 
The seaweed-based biorefinery should be put into a sustainability assessment in order to fully understand 
its energy performance and how much of that energy is from local and renewable sources. However, a 
systems energy performance does not necessarily say anything about conservation and sustainable 
management of coastal environments. It is furthermore important to evaluate the social and 
environmental aspects of a sustainable assessment of a bioenergy in coastal evaluation such as; land-use 
conﬂicts, proper infrastructure and location of biorefinery and potential displacement of local communities 
and businesses to fully understand the system (Golberg et al., 2014).  
It is therefore important to evaluate and regulate bioenergy to avoid potential negative impacts on 
biodiversity and surrounding ecosystems in general and furthermore, include indigenous and local 
communities in the development of holistic solution (Golberg et al., 2014). Work still needs to be done to 
get an overview of the full external effect of biorefineries based on seaweed feedstock. In order to secure 
socio-economic as well as environmental benefits principles for sustainable production and criteria most be 
set up (EPFL Energy Center, 2008).

6.1.4 IMPACT OF SEAWEED FARMING ON SOCIAL AND ECONOMIC STRUCTURES 
Even through more cultivation of women is a more resource intensive process, it positively influences the 
whole family and create empowerment for women. A man owned plot requires more hired labour and 
therefore need to hired labour, whereas women managed plots often can be managed by the family itself. 
The impact of seaweed farming on social and economic structures are important to fully understand its 
potential eﬀects on the coastal ecosystem and on the livelihoods of indigenous and local communities 
(Golberg et al., 2014). These socioeconomic considerations play a critical role in the successful and 
sustainable transfer and adoption of technologies and their products in developing countries. However, 
that requires a deep understanding of the local context of social structure, political environment and 
infrastructural development. 
As seaweed farming is not currently being done in Ghana, studies in similar countries regarding climate and 
economy is useful. A suitable comparison to Ghana might is Tanzania that is also in the tropical region of 
Africa. Studies from Zanzibar have demonstrated how families, which earlier relying on fishing, subsistence 
farming and small-scale businesses, now successfully have changed to seaweed farming as their major 
source of income (Msuya, 2006). Zanzibar is an example of a seaweed cultivation site that, with some 
impact on the ecosystems, has help diversifying economic activities in coastal communities (Msuya, 2006). 
However, Tanzania as well as other African countries are small players on the seaweed cultivation market 
as it is the Asian pacific region that largely dominated by a long tradition of seaweed farming which have 
had positive socio-economic benefits by both reducing overfishing as improving the livelihoods for the 
communities in the coastal areas (Rhein-Knudsen et al., 2015). On mainland Tanzania and many other 
places, local fishers initially using destructive fishing methods as dynamite fishing and use of poison, 
stopped doing so when they started seaweed farming (Msuya, 2006). Income generated from seaweed 
used for daily needs as well as school uniforms and books have been reported in Indonesia and the 
Philippines. By creating job in coastal communities give youth a change to be employed in the local 
communities as opposed to move to the city to look for a job, thereby potential reducing rural-urban 
migration (Msuya, 2006).  
6.1.5 SOCIAL EMPOWERMENT OF WOMEN 
The positive effect of seaweed farming on the economic and social well-being of coastal communities is 
largely driven by women as they are more likely to continue seaweed farming in the long run than men are 
(Msuya, 2006). Seaweed farming has great potential of involving women and children as seen in the 
Philippines, Indonesia and Tanzania (Crawford, 2007; Sievanen et al., 2005). Seaweed forming in Tanzania 
has giving the women a possibility to generate their own income and given them economic purchasing

power and greater controls over resources and freedom to make their own decisions (Crawford, 2007). 
Also, the balance within the household is changes as they earn an independent income.  
 
As women often consist of 85-90% of the workforce as they to a greater extent than the men continue 
seaweed farming and have more patient to complete the cycle of planting, weeding, harvesting, drying and

only give the family another way to earn an income but also provide the women a change to independence 
as they become more independent from their husband incomes and usher a change in gender balance 
within the household (Msuya, 2006).  
6.1.6 LIMITATION OF METHOD 
An emergy analysis can describe the energy flow through the system and the percentage of renewability 
and locality of the produced product. However, it does not say anything about the biodiversity which might 
be effected if large area gets cultivated with one seaweed species that favours certain fish species 
decreasing the variation of marine life. Emergy Accounting is therefore only one way of assessing the 
sustainability of a system and should therefore by seen as a puzzle in creating a bigger picture. 
When a LCA is conducted, standard programs are available to streamline the way the analysis is conducted. 
However, that is not the same for Emergy accounting, which is conducted according to the individual 
authors. An Emergy accounting program would in the future be very useful to apply the same framework 
around different emergy assessments. However, it would require great amount of data and work to agree 
on a standardized way to conduct Emergy Assessments. Nevertheless, to be able to compare transformities 
it is essential to streamline the way of conduction Emergy Assessments. 
6.1.7 FUTURE PROSPECTS FOR BIOETHANOL PRODUCTION 
It has been suggested in multiple studies (Baweja et al., 2016) that bioenergy production from seaweeds 
will reduce the usage of fresh water, fertilizer, and agricultural land and with potential to extent the 
biorefinery to produce high-value products in an environmentally friendly manner. In the present scenario, 
seaweeds may be a key link between energy, local environments, and climate change mitigation. However, 
from the results of this project it does not seem to be plausible. As it is currently unlikely that bioethanol 
from seaweed will be capable of competing with conventional bioenergy feedstocks (Aitken et al., 
2014).Production of bioenergy from seaweed could beneﬁt by including high value co-products (e.g. 
hydrocolloids) with the generation of bioenergy, which could improve the sustainability and economic 
viability of the production (Aitken et al., 2014).

7 CONCLUSION 
Seaweed may seem like a very good candidate for biofuel production due to it high productivity of biomass, 
lack of lignin and independent of arable land, fresh water and fertilizer. However, it is not as green as it may 
look like in an energy-environmental perspective. The process is heavy on non-renewable input and even 
though the feedstock sequential is utilized in different technologies to produce enzymes and different kinds 
of energy. The amount of non-renewable inputs cannot make up for the production of bioenergy with the 
present yields and conversion efficiencies.   
Cultivation of seaweed in rural communities in the tropics has in many regions of Asia shown the ability of 
creating alternative livelihood options. Mostly red seaweed is cultivated for carrageenan production. The 
processing of seaweed for high-value product might also be energy and environmentally favourable as well 
as it is also known to be economically favourable. However, due to the state of the projects those aspects 
of seaweed conversion was not included in this study.  
The results were obtained from the process of cultivating green seaweed for bioenergy production and the 
extraction of other high-value products from seaweed were therefore omitted. Instead extensive seaweed 
farming was modelled, as it has been conducted in other regions, constituting the framework of a 
hypothetical cultivation of seaweed in Ghana, which is the focus of this study. 
As clearly indicated by the Emergy analysis and the subsequently measured parameters all enhance the 
conclusion of not seeing seaweed as a potential feedstock for bioenergy production. Even through the 
numbers are not near how it would probably be in a real life system, if all data could be measured and not 
assumed based on the conceptual biorefinery. It is clear that it would be a comprehensive process to 
produce enzymes and bioenergy from seaweed no matter if the yield is improved by 10% or even 50% over 
the next years.
