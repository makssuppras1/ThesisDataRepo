Abstract
In this project, the linear and non linear properties of the highly accurate, highly dispersive
Boussinesq model, presented by Madsen et al. (2002), will be investigated. The current formu-
lation is pseudo spectral, which means that the space derivatives are found via spectral method,
and time derivatives are time marched with the Runke-Kutta 4th order scheme, or in preliminary
non linear cases, the built in Matlab ode45. The linear testing showed the results as expected,
that the celerity was accurate to kh = 25. The preliminary non linear testing against the stream
function solution, [Fenton, 1988], was very good, especially for the 2-step model which was a
signiﬁcant improvement compared to the results reported by [Andersen, 2016]. The highly non
linear focused wave cases by Johanneson & Swan (2002) were also simulated to good agreement.
However, for the focused wave cases using a JONSWAP spectrum, the 2-step model was unable
to simulate cases higher than ηfocus = 4 m. The reason for this was that instabilities were form-
ing in the trough region, even at very low wave heights, which resulted in the model blowing
up. The 1-step model was able to simulate accurately at ηcrest = 22 m, however, a run with
ηfocus = 27 m was completed as well. The latter showed signs of breaking down, as instabilities
in the crest and trough regions could be spotted. The last test was the simulation of a multi
directional irregular wave ﬁeld. The linear analysis of the grid showed that the 512 × 512-grid
with ∆x = ∆y = 5 m was the best layout in terms of the balance in accuracy and computation
time. The spacing of sampling points in x- and y-direction was also investigated, and this was
determined to be 16 points, to ensure that the sampling points were statistically independent.
Thus the results were not only dependent on the spacing of the sampling points, but also de-
pendent on the grid size. The linear result with this grid layout were quite satisfactory, and the
statistics matched the linear statistics of the Rayleigh distribution and Gumbel distribution, for
the exceedence probability and highest crest exceedence probability, respectively. In terms of non
linear runs, the model was able to produce non linearity up to 2nd order in a multi-directional
irregular wave ﬁeld.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background motivation
Madsen et al. (2002) presented the highly accurate non linear highly dispersive Boussinesq equa-
tions for water waves in terms of horizontal velocities and a vertical velocity, which would be used
as a variable. Both a 1-step and 2-step model were presented. The formulation utilizes exact
kinematic and dynamic surface conditions as well as bottom condition. [Bingham et al., 2009]
and [Jamois et al., 2006] introduced the equations as a velocity potential, in order to reduce the
number of variables. The expansion point for the velocities/potential is crucial for the accuracy
of the formulation.
[Madsen et al., 2002] show, that accuracy of the celerity of the waves is
dependent of this parameter, as well as the internal kinematics, but that will not be discussed
here. Previously, the model was set up as a ﬁnite diﬀerence model, see e.g. [Fuhrmann, 2004].
The motivation for using such numerical models is to be able to simulate real life scenarios, of
e.g. freak waves, in order to accurately predict the internal kinematics of such events, and thus
calculate the forces such events will exert on ships or oﬀshore structures.
[Andersen, 2016] implemented the highly accurate Boussinesq equations presented by [Madsen et al., 2002]
in a pseudo spectral manner. The implementation was done with velocity potential, as proposed
in [Bingham et al., 2009], which reduces the computation time, since it eﬀectively reduces the
variables that have to be found by 1/3. The 1- and 2-step model were both implemented into
the model setup. Both these models have their advantages. The 2-step formulation is more
accurate, hence it is also signiﬁcantly slower to compute. The formulation is a set of highly
complicated diﬀerential equations. The beneﬁt in this implementation, is that the derivatives
are very easy and fast to compute, and it is implemented in such a way, that coeﬃcient matrices
are not needed to be stored, thus reducing the required storage signiﬁcantly, allowing for larger
problems to be solved as well.
The 1-step model is to the authors knowledge not changed signiﬁcantly from the implementa-
tion of [Andersen, 2016]. However, as was seen in Andersens results, the 2-step model was not
functioning very well. The cases that were run had high ﬁltering implemented, which may have
reduced the quality in the results. Thus, David Fuhrman changed the formulation slightly in
order to stabilize the model.
1.2
Research scope
The scope of this thesis, is to test the models ability to simulate irregular non linear wave
ﬁelds. Such simulations would be a good way to investigate the velocities and forces that are
present in such a sea state. The current Boussinesq formulation is a good way to simulate such
sea states, as the model has been shown previously to be quite accurate when compared to 2-

in [Johanneson and Swan, 2000]. As mentioned above, Andersen (2016) did not have good re-

The progression of this thesis is as follows: First, the governing equations that are used in the
model are presented, followed by a brief account of how the numerical setup is made. After
that, the models properties are tested, to see if these yield satisfactory results. First the linear
properties are tested and compared to linear theory. Deviations are expected to appear in the
celerity of the wave, as it in the present formulation only is accurate up to kh ≤25 for the
surface elevation, and internal kinematics for kh ≤12.
The natural follow up is to test the model against the non linear stream function, see [Fenton, 1988].
To test the non linear properties of the model, and the accuracy of the velocity proﬁles for even
more demanding cases, the focused wave cases reported by Johanneson & Swan (2002) will be
simulated. The simulations will be compared to the data reported by Johanneson & Swan, in
forms of surface elevation at the time of focus and the velocity proﬁle for that given wave height.
All these non linear simulations are performed with the 1- and 2-step model, and the results are
compared.
Afterwards, the model will be tested on a larger domain, where a waves described by a JON-
SWAP spectrum will be used. The waves will be given phases and directions, such that all the
waves will focus at one place in time and space, so a large focusing event will occur, which can
emulate a freak wave in terms of velocities and wave heights.
Finally, the multi directional irregular wave case will be investigated. Here the waves are given
random phases and directions. The same JONSWAP spectrum as for the focused wave case is
used. The subject of interest is the statistics of the waves that are simulated (simulations are
1 hour to get enough data). This case will be tested linearly and non linearly. For the linear
case, it is expected that the crests will follow a number of diﬀerent distributions: If the entire
data set is used, it is expected that the probability of the crest heights will follow a Rayleigh
distribution, and if data series from each sampling point are treated individually, it is expected
that the highest crests will follow a Gumbel distribution. For the non linear cases, the Gumbel
distribution is exceeded. If the data is treated as one long series, for moderately non linear cases
the exceedence probability of the crests is expected to follow Forristall 2nd order distribution.

Chapter 2
Governing equations
In this chapter, the theory used in the numerical model will be presented. The basic under-
lying assumptions will be stated, and the high-order Boussinesq equations will be presented.
[Madsen et al., 2002] presented this with regards to the velocities, but in this work, the re-
derivation conducted by [Bingham et al., 2009] with regards to the velocity potential will be
utilized. Both formulations utilize exact free surface boundary condition and solve the exact
Laplace equation is solved by expanding an inﬁnite series around an arbitrary z-level.
The
present high-order Boussinesq formulation gives accurate surface elevations for wavenumbers
up kh = 25, while the kinematics such as the vertical and horizontal velocity is accurate for
wavenumbers up to kh = 12.
The advantage with the potential formulation is due to the
reduced CPU time by a factor of 2, as this reduces the variables needed to solve for in half.
2.1
Assumptions
In the following sections, the liquid considered will be inviscid, irrotational and incompressible.

the conservation of volume. The incompressibility is deemed a fair assumption, since a surface
ﬂow is considered, and the velocity of the particles are small compared to the speed of sound
in the ﬂuid [Svendsen and Jonsson, 1976]. A cartesian coordinate system is adopted, where x-

previously stated in mind, that the ﬂow is incompressible, the ﬂow through the box must be

3.1
Introduction
In this chapter, a brief overview of the numerical implementation of the model will be given,
and how the high order Boussinesq equations are solved.
The equation that are used in the model are presented the chapter about governing equations,
and the system that has to be solved is outlined here as well. The implementation in this report
is via a pseudo spectral method, which was implemented by [Andersen, 2016]. A brief outline of
the implementation will be given in this chapter.
The Boussinesq equations are a set of PDE’s (Partial diﬀerential equations), which are time and
space dependent. Above the model was mentioned to be implemented in a pseudo spectral man-
ner. In this case that means, that the spatial variables are found with spectral diﬀerentiation, and
the time dependent variables are found via ﬁnite diﬀerence. This sacriﬁces spectral accuracy, but
if the time steps are small enough the global accuracy should be satisfactory [Trefethen, 2000].
In fact, if the time variables were treated spectrally instead of time marched, the computational
expense would increase greatly. The model uses Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) to solve the
diﬀerential equations. When the spatial variables are discretized the system of PDE’s can be
reduced to a set of ODE’s. The Runge-Kutta 4th order time stepping scheme has been used for
the demanding cases, where the built in Matlab ode45 has been used for the less demanding
cases. That is because to the ode45 uses a variable time step and time stepping scheme (4th or
5th order Runge-Kutta) to minimize the error (user speciﬁed).
Since the Boussinesq equations utilizes FFT to solve for the space dependent variables, periodic
boundaries are used for the domain. This is done, because Fourier series only work with periodic
boundaries, which is used for all domains and grids throughout this thesis.
The system of Boussinesq equations given in equation (2.23) is solved in each point with a Krylov
subspace iterative method that is built-in in Matlab called GMRES-algorithm (generalized
minimal residual).
3.2
Spectral diﬀerentiation
The spectral diﬀerentiation is used to approximate the spatial derivatives of the model. In con-
trast to the ﬁnite diﬀerence, which uses the nearby functional values to approximate derivatives,
the spectral method uses the functional values. That means, that if there is much spatial varia-
tion in the function, the grid has to be resolved on a very ﬁne grid, increasing CPU cost. The
spectral method is gives better accuracy on coarse grids. The spectral methods have a very
high convergence speed for the error, referred to as spectral accuracy [Trefethen, 2000]. Thus

Filtering
Some ﬁltering was required to ensure that the waves run steadily.
As default, the ﬁltering
parameter was set to Ncut = 3, which was also found to be the best to use with the 2-step
model. However it was found, that for the 1-step model a more suitable ﬁltering parameter
was Ncut = 4, which eliminated the high frequency noise. The ﬁltering is applied in Fourier
space, where wave number components which are resolved with a speciﬁed amount of points pr.
wavelength or less would be solved, in order to eliminate high order wavenumbers. The ﬁltering
process is about ﬁnding a balance, since you remove some wavenumber components, and thereby
remove wave energy. Thus, if too much wave energy is removed from the spectrum. In that way,
too much ﬁltering may make the results worse. Through this report, Ncut = 3 is used for all
2-step runs, and Ncut = 4 is used for all 1-step runs.
3.5
Implementation of solver
As mentioned previously, the GMRES solver was used to solve the linear system shown in equa-
tion (2.23). The input of into the GMRES solver has to be a square matrix A with size n × n,
and the right hand side must be a vector b with length n. For large domains, this will require a
very large matrix to store all these values. Therefore, [Andersen, 2016] made a function which
makes this matrix-vector product, in order to avoid generating this matrix. Thus, the function
replaces the matrix A in GMRES, making the process matrix free. The details in the matrix
vector product function will not be described in detail here.
A similar approach is made for the preconditioning process, which is required to solve the prob-
lem in the Krylov subspace. The key for a good preconditioning matrix is that it has to resemble
A, however also be easy to solve. The GMRES preconditioner is given as M, and is used to
solve the system M −1Ax = M −1b. In the same way as with the coeﬃcient matrix A, a function
is made which for an input b returns the output M −1b. The function replaces M in GMRES,
and the preconditioning is therefore also matrix free. The details in the preconditioning function
will not be described in detail here.
For details on these functions, the reader is referred to [Andersen, 2016].

locity proﬁles from diﬀerent waves, σ = −0.5 is the better option.
The linear comparisons of the surface elevation and the celerity show a good agreement between
the model solution and the Stokes solution.
This is the ﬁrst step in any numerical simula-
tion, as there is no point in proceeding with non-linear simulations if the linear properties
are not in order.
In addition, the accuracy that is achieved in the linear simulation serves
as an indication of the accuracy expected in the non-linear simulation. Thus, a similar accu-
racy is expected for the non-linear simulations as have been demonstrated thus far. However,
according to [Madsen et al., 2002], the internal kinematics are only accurate up to kh = 12.
Since the model displays accuracy in agreement with the tests that have been conducted, e.g.
[Madsen et al., 2002], it is concluded that the model is able to conduct the linear simulations
satisfactory.

4.3.2
2-step method
In this section, the stream function solution is compared to the results from the simulation
utilizing the non-linear 2-step Boussinesq method. As opposed to the 1-step formulation, the
velocity formulation for the 2-step method is described in two steps - a Pad´e approximation is
utilized from the seabed to the still water level, while a Taylor expansion is utilized from the
still water level to the free surface. Thus, the 2-step method requires more CPU, but is more
accurate with regards to the predicting the surface elevation as well as the internal kinematics.
Figure 4.4 shows the results of the surface elevation simulations, compared to the corresponding
stream function solution.
As is seen in Figure 4.4, the surface elevations for all the cases is very well predicted. Worth
noting, in contrast to the results in Figure 4.3, there is no notable phase diﬀerence between
the stream function solution and the solution obtained from the 2-step Boussinesq formulation.
This is because the 2-step predicts the internal kinematics of the wave better than the 1-step
model. The reason that there is no phase lead in particular, is because the 2-step model also
more accurately predicts the celerity of the wave. Another thing worth noting, is that the 2-step
model also was able to run for a longer time, than the 1-step method. This is, again, due to the
2-step method being more accurate, and thus being able to satisfy the set tolerance (10−6) in
the Matlab function ode45.

4.4.3
Comparison
It has been demonstrated, how the Boussinesq model, 1- and 2-step, can compare to the stream
function solution. However, there are diﬀerences in the accuracy, in which the two implemen-
tations perform. With regards to the surface elevations, it can be seen, that the 1-step model
develops a signiﬁcant phase lead in the surface elevations. This is due to the 1-step model not
being able to calculate the wave celerity accurately. On the other hand, the 2-step model does
not develop any signiﬁcant phase lead. Even though the 1-step model develops a phase lead
compared to the stream function solution, the surface elevation is simulated very accurate. The
2-step solution predicts the surface elevation perfectly as well. As earlier mentioned, the 2-step
model was able to simulate more periods than the 1-step model. This is not due to 2-step model
being more stable than the 1-step model, but rather it is more accurate, hence it is able to satisfy
the prescribed tolerance better.
With regards to accuracy, the 2-step model is clearly better, which can be seen by compar-
ing e.g. Figures 4.8 and 4.12. In general, all the initial velocities match the prescribed velocity,
which was expected. However, there are some small discrepancies, which means that the model
solution simply overestimates the velocity right at the peak slightly compared to the analytical
solution. This is supported further by the fact, that the two distributions match perfectly apart
from at the maximum. For h/L = 0.05, the 1- and 2-step models show quite diﬀerent results at
the last period. The 1-step slightly underestimates the peak velocity, while the 2-step slightly
overestimates the velocity.
Apart from the case of h/L = 0.05, the 1-step model generally
overestimates the velocity at the peak wave crest, while the 2-step model predicts the velocity
perfectly for h/L = 0.10, 0.11 and 0.12, respectively. The diﬀerence in the 2 models is due to the
respective formulations of the velocity. The 1-step model treats the entire water column equally,
with Pad´e approximants, while the 2-step model utilizes Pad´e approximants from the sea bed
to the still water level, and Taylor approximations from the still water level to the the free surface.
In general, the models, 1- and 2-step, have shown satisfactory results when comparing to the
surface elevation. One negative point, would be the 1-step models inability to simulate as many
periods as the 2-step model. However, as mentioned previously, this is not due to instability in
the model, but rather due to the high demand of accuracy implemented into the ode45. Keeping
in mind that the simulations performed were highly non linear, the results were accurate, and
apart from the inability of the 1-step model to run as many periods as the 2-step model, these
tests conﬁrm that the 1- and 2-step models can be used satisfactory for non linear cases.

5.1
Introduction
In this chapter, a series of focused waves will be investigated in the Boussinesq model. Surface el-
evations and velocity proﬁles will be examined, as these form the basis of predicting forces associ-
ated with focused waves, which could represent e.g. freak or rouge waves. The results will be com-
pared to the experimental results, that have been well documented in [Johanneson and Swan, 2000].
The validation and experimental setup is also well documented herein. Johanneson and Swan
investigated alot of diﬀerent cases, that range from a narrow to a broad spectrum of frequencies.
These are named D and B, respectively. They also used a intermediate spectrum labelled C, but
that will not be included in this thesis. This is due to the extreme cases being deemed suﬃcient
in validating the model. In total, Johanneson and Swan tested 84 cases - they used the 3 men-
tioned frequency spectra, and for each of these, diﬀerent input amplitudes, see equation (5.1) for
normalized amplitude, and six diﬀerent directional spreading factors. Many of the wave cases
were reported to be near breaking, which shows that these cases are highly non-linear events.
Table 5.1 shows the frequencies, the decay of the spectra that were used in the experiments, and
the fundamental frequency (spectral repeat period). The B spectrum includes non-dimensional
wave numbers of of 2.53 ≤kh ≤13.25, and the D spectrum includes non-dimensional wave
numbers of 3.32 ≤kh ≤7.55. As mentioned earlier, the present model can provide accurate
surface description for approximately kh ≤25, and accurate internal kinematics for approxi-
mately kh ≤12. Thus, the model will be tested to the limit. The cases are simulated from
t = −20 s to t = 2 s, but the most non-linear cases are simulated to t = 4 s. All the cases are
designed to focus at t = 0 s, but due to non-linearity, the time of focus is shifted. The basin
has a constant depth of h = 1.2 m, and the simulations are run on a 512x512 grid, with a grid
spacing of ∆x = ∆y = 0.05 m.

5.4
Comparison
With regards to the surface elevation, the 1- and 2-step models were able to predict the surface
elevation very well. Especially around the focused peak, the model results were very satisfactory.
However, slight discrepancies could be observed at the neighbouring crests and troughs. This
was especially the case for B52ud and D61ud (more so for the D61ud), and it could be seen
for the 1- and 2-step results. The other cases were generally good. However, when the ﬁgures
are examined closely, it is apparent that the 2-step model yields results that are more accurate
than the corresponding 1-step results. One clear example can be seen when examining ﬁgures
5.2f and 5.3f. If ﬁgure 5.2f is considered, it can be seen quite clearly, that the modelled result
exceeds the measured result at the crest, whereas that is not the case if ﬁgure 5.3f is considered.
Table 5.4 conﬁrms this fact. It was however expected, that the 2-step model would be more
accurate than the 1-step model, as the 2-step model has a more accurate formulation, involving
derivatives up to the 10th order. Apart from the D93s4 case, there is not too much diﬀerence in
the surface elevation accuracy in the 1- and 2-step models.
Comparing the velocity proﬁles from the 1- and 2-step models, there is not much doubt, that the
2-step model is able to produce more accurate results. This is apparent, when e.g. comparing
ﬁgures 5.4f and 5.5f. If the upper part of the ﬁgures is examined, it can be seen, that the match
between the measured data and the modelled data is better. This also holds true for the other
cases.
One thing that is worth noting though - regardless if the 1- or 2-step is considered, is that the
modelled velocity proﬁle seems to be overestimated at the peak of the wave crest, for all the
cases. Fuhrman and Madsen (2006), as well as Bateman et al. (2002) have reported numerical
results on the same cases, as mentioned earlier. According to the results shown herein, the same
trend was detected, where the velocity was overestimated near the free surface, especially at the
crest. Since this error is quite systematic, Bateman et al. (2002) suggests, that there might
be some inconsistency between the spatial measuring points, and the points where the results
are found in the numerical method. The numerical method can more easily detect the highest
wave crest and ﬁnd the corresponding velocity distribution, while that feat is not as easily done
in the wave basin. Small spatial diﬀerences will have an inﬂuence on the found result. Thus,
Bateman et al. (2002) suggest that the error relates to the spacing of the wave gauges, which
cannot ensure the same precision as the numerical model can achieve.
By the reasoning stated above, the 2-step model is clearly the better of the two. However, one
thing that also has to be factored into the consideration, is the CPU time that is required to
complete the simulations. The CPU time required for the simulations is listed in table 5.3. Here
it is apparent, that the 1-step model is by far superior in speed. This was however expected, as
it has a simpler formulation. Thus, any conclusion would have to take both the accuracy that is
wanted, an the time there that is available into account. With regards to this project, time was
not really an issue, and thus it has to be concluded, that the 2-step model would be the better
choice going forward as the main model that is used, as the accuracy has to be in focus.

6.1
Introduction
As mentioned, the simulations in this chapter are based on a JONSWAP spectrum. The physical
layout of the domain is as follows: the depth is constant at h = 45 m, the number of grid points
is nx = ny = 1024, and the spacing is constant for both directions as ∆x = ∆y = 5 m.
The spectrum is truncated at khcut = 14, with a peak non-dimensional wave number of peak
frequency fp = 0.06666, corresponding to a peak non-dimensional wave number of kph = 1.035.
This means, that for the peak wave number, the case is intermediate water depth, and for the
cut wave number (the tail of the energy spectrum), the case is deep water. The boundaries
between shallow and deep water are usually given as kh < π
10 for shallow water, and kh > π
for deep water. The cut frequency has been chosen in order to eliminate high order noise, which
consists of short period waves. The waves with high frequencies can make the model unstable.
In addition, the wave number cut oﬀis on the tail of the spectrum, so only a small amount of the
energy that is not taken into account. The focusing event linearly decided to occur at t = 0 s,
and the simulation starts at t = −120s. The simulations are however run further than t = 0,
since the waves will focus at a later time due to non-linear interactions, as was experienced in
chapter 5. The time step was chosen, such that waves corresponding to the peak frequency and
the cut frequency were satisfyingly resolved. That was calculated from the Courant number,
Cr = 0.5. The Courant number is deﬁned in the following way:

7.1
Introduction
In this chapter, a wave ﬁeld with multi directional irregular waves will be examined. The waves
that are used are based on a JONSWAP spectrum. First thing that has to be considered, is
how large the grid has to be. This is done in linear mode, as there are various ways to compare
the gathered data to theoretical distributions. The grid size analysis will be done in two ways
- correlation analysis, which is basically the same analysis that is made in turbulence theory
[Sumer, 2013], and crest statistical analysis. The crest analysis includes a comparisons to the
Rayleigh distribution, Forristall 2nd order theory and the Gumbel distribution. The comparison
to Forristall 2nd order theory is only conducted for the non-linear cases, as the linear runs are
expected follow the Rayleigh distribution to some point, where the data start to break oﬀ. All
runs are made in the 1-step model, as the 2-step model broke down during the focused wave
cases shown in the previous chapter.
With regards to the physical layout of the domain, three diﬀerent domain sizes will be consid-
ered: 256 × 256, 512 × 512 and 1024 × 1024. These will be tested to see, which one can be used
for the analysis of non-linear waves. The ﬁnal domain size will be as small as the analyses will
allow, as the increase in domain size leads to extended CPU times. Two spacings have been
used, namely ∆x = ∆y = 5 m and ∆x = ∆y = 10 m. The latter was only used for 256 × 256
grid size, to see if results comparable to the 512 × 512 grid could be achieved.
With regards to the spectrum details, the reader is referred to section 6.1, as the same peak
period, peak frequency, cut frequency etc. are used in this model setup. The waves are generated
with random phases and directionality. The linear runs are run with a signiﬁcant wave height
of Hm0 = 9.4 m, for all grid sizes. The signiﬁcant wave height for the non-linear cases are varied
from 1.0 to 7.0 m, and the wave height that has been used in the diﬀerent runs is shown in the
respective ﬁgures. All runs have been simulated for 1 hour. The same criteria has been used for
the Courant number, but as two grid spacings have been used, two times steps have been used
(∆t = 0.14 s for ∆x = 5 m and ∆= 0.28 s for ∆x = 10 m).

7.2.1
Correlation analysis
As mentioned, correlation analysis will be done on the linear results of the domain. The corre-
lation analysis follows the theory proposed by [Sumer, 2013]. In general, there are two ways of
doing the correlation analysis, namely space correlation and time correlation. The correlation
analysis that is presented in [Sumer, 2013] relates the ﬂuctuating components of the velocity (u′
or v′) at one point in space to another, this can be presented as follows:
f(r) = u′(x)u′(x + r)
u′2
(7.1)
g(r) = v′(x)v′(x + r)
v′2
(7.2)
These are called two point double correlation functions, and they have been normalized, so that
for r = 0, f(r) = g(r) = 1. As r →∞, in turbulence, the correlation tends to go towards zero.
In the present case, this has been adapted such that the surface elevation signal is analysed
instead, across the grid points in x-direction, and it is implemented in the following way:
f(r) = η(x)η(x + r)
σ
(7.3)
where σ is the variance of the wave heights. This is expected to have similar traits as the cor-
relation for turbulent ﬂuctuations, that f(r) →0 for r →∞. Since the boundary conditions
are periodic, the correlation calculation has been implemented in such a way, that values are
cycled, such that the correlation is periodic. Sumer (2013) explains the correlation coeﬃcients
as follows: The higher the correlation coeﬃcients, the more likely the two measurements belong
to the same coherent structure. In the case of the waves, this can analogously be seen as the
larger the correlation coeﬃcient for the surface elevation, the more likely the two measurement
points belong to the same wave crest.

Crest statistical analysis
In this section, the crest analysis for the three grid sizes will be presented. The crest analysis
involves diﬀerent tools, in order to determine if the crests follow linear theory. The analysis in-
vestigates two general aspects, and their inﬂuence on the results: namely the size of the domain,
and the spacing between the sampling points. The size of the domain is pretty straight forward,
and serves the same purpose as the correlation analysis. The spacing analysis is made in order
to determine how many grid points have to be in between sampling points to ensure, that the
two sampling points can be considered independent.
To conduct this analysis, the raw signal of the surface elevation is not suﬃcient, as only the
wave crests will be treated. Thus, the zero down crossings of the signal will be recorded, and
only one crest is allowed for every zero down crossing. Thus, only one crest will be recorded for
every wave length.
The ﬁrst step in the analysis is making a histogram. That is pretty straight forward in Matlab,
once the crest heights have been found. The crest heights are gathered into bins of a given size,
and the result is plotted in a bar plot. The next step is to see, if the distribution of the crest
height matches the theoretical Rayleigh exceedence probability distribution, given as

7.2.3
Discussion and selection of grid layout for non-linear runs
As seen from the results above, very diﬀerent results can be obtained for the diﬀerent layouts
of the grid. With regards to the correlation analysis, the worst option to choose would be the
256 × 256 grid with ∆x = 5 m. That is clear considering, that it is the only layout that does
not plateau in the middle of the domain. It appears, that it is about to plateau, but that can
not be certain. The 512 × 512 and 1024 × 1024 both oscillate around zero for a large portion of
the domain, and are thus deemed good to use. The correlation analysis however becomes very
diﬀerent for the 256-grid, if the grid increment is increased from 5 m to 10 m. By doing that,
the model does get a similar plateau in the center of the domain, as was seen for the 512- and
1024-grid. It would be expected, that all the grid sizes would have reached 0, considering that
the peak wave length compared to the length to the center of the domain. That is however, as
the analysis shows, not the case. However, as it would seem that the correlation was approaching
0, and that the analysis is conservative when comparing the peak wave length to the domain
size, the 256 × 256 grid was also included in the crest analysis.
When looking at the various histograms across the domain sizes, it can be seen, that they are
very similar. Only when the grid increment, ∆x, is changed, a slight diﬀerence in the distribution
of waves can be seen. That is seen by the columns representing 3 and 4 meters having evened
out. Apart from that, no real diﬀerence can be seen. When sorting the crest heights obtained
for the individual sampling points, the crest heights and corresponding exceedence probabilities
scatter around the Rayleigh distribution. There is not much that can be learned by looking at
all the data in this manner, other than by observing where the scatter is positioned, as will be
seen later. If the results are linear, they will tend to scatter as has been seen in the previous
sections, however, if the result simulation is non linear as seen later, the scatter will shift towards
the higher
ηc
Hm0 . It is hard to determine, if the grid size or spacing of sampling points has any
inﬂuence on these results.
By lumping the data together into one long time series, the diﬀerences start showing. First
considering the 256 × 256 grid with 4 points spacing, it can be seen that the sampled data,
shown ﬁgure 7.5, follows the Rayleigh curve nice until around 10−1, where the sampled data
starts breaking oﬀ. When the spacing is increased to 8 points (ﬁgure 7.7), it appears that the
curve breaks oﬀat a later point, however not signiﬁcantly. If the grid size is increased, signiﬁcant
changes can be observed in the joined realizations from all the sampling points compared to the
Rayleigh distribution. As seen in ﬁgure 7.11, the sampled data only starts breaking oﬀfrom
the Rayleigh distribution at about 6 · 10−3. Compare that to the 256 × 256-grid with 4 points
spacing, the improvement is obvious. For the 512 × 512-grid, improvement could also be seen
when increasing the number of grid points. For 16 points spacing, see ﬁgure 7.14, the sampled
data starts breaking oﬀat probabilities around 2 · 10−3. Considering the last grid that was
investigated, the 256 × 256-grid with ∆x = 10 m, the same tendency could be seen. There was
an improvement in the way the sample data matched to the Rayleigh curve, in a similar fashion
as was seen for the 512 · 512 grid. That was also expected, as those two grid layouts are equally
long, even though the 512 × 512 has a ﬁner resolution.
The comparison of the highest sampled wave heights compared to the Gumbel, a similar trend
can be seen. First considering the 256 × 256-grid with ∆x = 5 m, there is a clear shift from the
expected wave height distribution, which the Gumbel distribution represents. And the output
of sampled data does not seem to be depending on spacing of the sampling points. This is
pretty clear, when comparing the 4, 8, 16 and 32 points spacing for all the tested grid sizes in
fact. The general tendency and position of the wave heights does not change as the spacing is
increased, but the density of points only decreases as the spacing is increased. Some change in
the progression of the sampled data is observed, but that is due to some sample points and their
respective highest waves being taken out of the statistic. In that way it can be concluded, that
the highest wave height analysis of the sample points is indeed not dependent on the spacing of
the points, but rather it is dependent on the domain size.

cases that are simulated non linearly have signiﬁcant wave heights of Hm0 = 4, 6 and 7 m. The

the domain layout that was used. Some smaller signiﬁcant wave height cases were made for the
256 × 256-grid (Hm0 = 1 m and Hm0 = 2 m), and those can be seen in appendix C. According
to the linear analysis, it was seen that not much was gained by including a spacing of 4 points
between the sampling points. Thus all the analyses start with a spacing of 8 points. The analysis
will start with the smallest domain.

7.7. DISCUSSION OF NON LINEAR RESULTS
91
7.7
Discussion of non linear results
As was demonstrated in the linear analysis, the 256×256-grid with 5 m spacing was not suitable
for the given wave spectrum. The low amplitude non linear runs, Hm0 = 1 m and Hm0 = 2 m,
both showed very similar trends as were seen in the linear runs. E.g. when comparing to the
highest crest for each sampling point to the Gumbel solution, the sampled crest heights were
systematically lower than predicted by the Gumbel solution. However, even though this result
is clearly wrong (since the linear runs for 512 × 512 and 256 × 256 with ∆x = 10 m aligned with
the Gumbel solution), the fact that the low signiﬁcant wave height (∼linear) showed similar
statistical results as the linear runs, means that the model in non linear mode is capable to
produce linear results if the input signiﬁcant wave height is low enough. One more implication,
was that since the sampled crest height were lower than those compared by the Gumbel and the
breaking oﬀfrom the Rayleigh distribution for the joined realizations, that the non linear eﬀects
do not come into eﬀect for as low wave heights as for the other domain.
The 256×256-grid with ∆x = 10 m showed better results in the linear runs, and not surprisingly,
also showed better non linear results. The biggest advantage by using this grid size, is the speed
at which the simulations are carried out. Due to keeping the Courant number at 0.5, the time
step ∆t = 0.28 s, which reduced the time steps needed into half. The disadvantage is though,
that the grid is very coarse, both temporal and spatial. This may have the eﬀect, that some
higher wavenumber components cannot be resolved as well as for the ﬁner grid. The main reason
to try out this grid size, was to see if the physical domain size would have an inﬂuence on the
result. That was most certainly the case, as the results from this case resembled the results from
the 512 × 512-grid more than that of the 256 × 256-grid with ∆x = 5 m. The wave distributions
seen in the histograms are not entirely equal either, which is most likely due to how this model
setup is resolved. With regards to the joined realizations, it is seen that the non linearity is
better represented in this model setup than the small domain, which can be seen both for the
joined realizations from all the sampling points as well as the highest crest comparison to the
Gumbel distribution.
The 512 × 512-grid showed the best results of all the cases. It showed good agreement with
2nd order theory at a signiﬁcant wave height of Hm0 = 4 m, where for larger signiﬁcant wave
heights the sample data started to exceed the crest heights predicted by the Forristall 2nd order
distribution. The simulations did not at any point highly exceed the 2nd order theory when
comparing the joined realizations, which implies, that the runs did not become fully non linear,
before the model blows up. Hm0 = 7 m was the highest signiﬁcant wave height that could be
achieved for all of the domains.
Seeing that the 512 × 512-grid was the one that could produce the most non linear results, it
shows that the domain size does have an inﬂuence on the waves that are simulated. That was
also seen with the linear analysis, so that is not a surprise. The reason must be, that when
the domain is only roughly 4.5 times the peak wave length (nx · dx = 256 · 5 m = 1280 m),
that the entire domain is not independent, thus the waves inﬂuence on each other will be more
pronounced. Thus in these cases, the simulations will consistently have a lower distribution of
waves, compared to what would be expected from statistical calculations. This fact is supported
by the results that were obtained by the larger domains, as well as the correlation analyses.
To sum up, it was found in the linear- as well as the non linear analysis, that the 256 × 256-grid
with ∆x = 5 m was too small for this particular spectrum of waves. The results for the 256×256
with ∆x = 10 m were more similar to the ones that were obtained with the 512 × 512, but did
not become as non linear. The CPU time for the 256×256 with ∆x = 10 m was however greatly
reduced compared to the 512 × 512 grid. However, it seemed that higher wave heights were
found for the 512 × 512-grid, but that may also be due to there being more sampling points.
And due to the wave being more poorly resolved in the 256 × 256-grid with ∆x = 10 m, higher
crests and thus more non linear events were seen in the 512 × 512 domain.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
Another velocity proﬁle was found than the one [Andersen, 2016] showed in his thesis, but it was
sadly found at a too advanced stage to implement it into the model, and rerun the simulations.
In this report, the linear properties of the wave were well documented. It was seen, that for
the given expansion point of σ = −h
2 , the celerity found in the model corresponded very well
to the analytical solution provided by [Madsen et al., 2002], which shows the accuracy of the
celerity obtained with the Boussinesq formulation compared to Stokes 1st order theory. The
Boussinesq formulation is accurate for kh up 25, where a deviation of about 2% is observed.
It was also found, that the accuracy improved for longer simulation periods, which was also
expected, as small variations are evened out when simulations are made for longer periods of
time. The surface elevations showed the same story, see ﬁgure 4.1. There it was shown more vi-
sually, as the reader can see that one wave clearly has lower celerity than the other for growing kh.
With the linear properties of the model proven to work, more challenging non linear were exam-
ined. The model was set to simulate the stream function results, based on [Fenton, 1988], and
since the runs were non linear, the 1- and 2-step model were investigated. The runs used the
variable ode45-time stepping scheme, and both models had the same error tolerance as input.
The results were good for both model setups in terms of the wave heights that were simulated,
however the 1-step model had higher celerity than the stream function solution, that could be
seen for the runs H/L = 0.05 and 0.1, which ran for 100 and 50 periods, respectively. The more
steep cases appear to have a good celerity, but keep in mind that those cases only maintained
the required accuracy for 3 periods. On the other hand, the 2-step solution simulated the same
celerity for all the steepnesses. With regards to the velocity proﬁles, the same trend was found,
as the 2-step formulation of the velocity was better than the 1-step, as was expected, since
the 2-step velocity proﬁle has a more accurate formulation from the still water level to the free
surface. With regards to CPU time though, the 1-step model is far superior. However as these
cases are not very time consuming, the more accurate model will also be the most preferable.
Since no more analytical models were available to test the model further, the next step in test-
ing was to use the 1- and 2-step model to simulate the highly non linear, and well documented,
cases, that were carried out in a laboratory by Johanneson and Swan (2000). Overall, the nu-
merical results matched very well with the experimental results. Only the case of B52ud was
a bit oﬀ, since an input amplitude of A = 61 mm had to be used, in order to get matching
surface elevations. However, Bateman et al. (2001), as well as Fuhrman and Madsen (2006)
reported the same issues in matching the surface elevation when using the reported input am-
plitude. Bateman et al. used an input amplitude of 57.2 mm, while Fuhrman and Madsen used
an input amplitude of 59 mm. In this report, the used input amplitude was 61 mm. For all
the other cases, the same input amplitude was implemented as the one that was documented in
[Johanneson and Swan, 2000]. Similarly as with the streamfunction results, there was diﬀerence
between the results obtained by the 1- and 2-step model. With regards to the surface elevation,

the 2-step model had better results than the 1-step model. This is particularly seen in the D93s4
case, as the 1-step model gets a slightly higher surface elevation than was documented. The 1-
and 2-step model had deviations in the neighbouring crests for both uni-directional cases, but
as the focused peak is very accurate, these results were deemed acceptable. With regards to the
velocity proﬁles, the 2-step was better for all cases. It was noticed however, that all the velocity
proﬁles (except B52ud) had higher velocities at the crest. Bateman et al. (2002) suggested that
the discrepancy is due to the measuring of the velocities. In contrast to the model where it
is straight forward to get the velocity at the crest, it is not as straight forward to measure the
velocity at the crest. Bateman et al. (2002) therefore suggest that the error is due to the relative
spacing of the measuring gauges. Since the error is so systematic, this is deemed to be a fair
assessment.
The next case was to investigate the model with a JONSWAP spectrum, where the initial con-
ditions were arranged so that the linear focusing of the wave occurred at t = 0 s. The model
was tested in linear mode to ensure, that the input amplitude would correspond to the focused
wave height. That was the case, however, the focusing time was bit oﬀ, namely tfocus = −0.24 s.
That was the same result for all the linear cases. The small amplitude non linear runs showed
the same for the 1-step model, but not the 2-step model. The linear amplitudes were not ’spot
on’, but they were accurate to the 3rd digit for ηfocus = 1 and 2 m, and 2nd digit for the case
of ηfocus = 5 m. The 1-step non linear runs for these small amplitudes showed slightly diﬀerent
results for η = 1 and 3 m, with a shift of focus in space for η = 5 m, and a larger focus crest
elevation. The exceedence of these results were accepted, as the focusing event consists of all the
wave components focusing in one spot in time and space, and thus the non linear contributions
will be more distinct. The 2-step case could not be simulated for ηcrest ≥4 m. Compared to the
linear run, the focusing was also shifted in time for amplitudes as small as ηfocus = 3 m. The
breakdown is documented in the respective section, and was due to disturbance in the trough
region a long time before the focusing event. The disturbance turned in to a needle shape, which
blew up the model in the end. The reason for this disturbance is unclear, but it is at least
certain that it is not due to breaking of waves, since this blow up occurred at such low crest
heights. Thus, the 1-step model was adopted for further investigations. A trend could be seen
in the 1-step model, that the more non linear the cases became, the focusing event was shifted
further downstream, and occurred at a later time. This is seen in ﬁgure 6.5. The highest stable
simulation that was found was for ηcrest = 22 m. After this, disturbances could be seen in the
surface, however not of the same sort as was seen in the 2-step model. The model broke down
at ηcrest = 31 m. The reason for this breakdown is believed to be a combination high frequency
disturbances and wave breaking.
The aim of this work was to simulate non linear irregular waves, and that was the last part of
this thesis. First it was found via. correlation analysis and linear crest analysis, that the best
suited grid layouts were the 512 × 512-grid with ∆x = 5 m, as well as the 256 × 256-grid with
∆x = 10 m. The spacing was also investigated, and it was found that the spacing of 16 · 5 m or
8 · 10 m was the optimal spacing. The spacing had most impact on the exceedence distribution
of the crest heights. In addition it was found, that when comparing the highest crests from each
sampling point to the Gumbel distribution, the result was actually not dependent on the spacing,
but rather the size of the domain. That means, that the independence of the sampling points
was not only dependent on spacing, but also dependent on the domain size. The 1024×1024-grid
with ∆x = 5 m would be best according to the correlation analysis. No crest analysis was made
on this grid, since the ﬁrst run had error in the storage, and not enough time was available to
simulate this case anew. Thus it was not pursued. With regards to the non linear results, it was
found that the 1-step model could simulate cases up to 2nd order theory, however, not to fully
non linear. The 512 × 512 grid appeared to be closest to being fully non linear, for Hm0 = 7 m.
This shows that the non linearity that could be achieved was also dependent on the domain size.
Since the domain size is eﬀectively the same for 256 with ∆x = 10 m, it would be expected that
the results were similar. However since the grid is coarser, the wave distribution can also have
changed, since some wave components may have been lost. The maximum signiﬁcant wave height

Appendix C
Multi directional irregular waves
C.1
Crest analysis
In this section, the remaining plots that were not included in the main report are shown. These
