Abstract
We consider how to handle inference of spherical data, and how this differs from infer-
ence on regular data. In particular we consider how to use anisotropic distributions
on the sphere, as an extension of the isotropic inference on the sphere as presented
by S. Hauberg [Hau], and the anisotropic distribution on the 2-dimensional surface
of a 3-dimensional sphere.
We show how the similarity between the spherical normal distribution, and the regular
multivariate normal distribution, presents opportunities to utilize currently existing
tools to work with the spherical distribution in some aspects. We present how the
essential part of the spherical normal distribution consists of a normalization constant
only dependant on a concentration parameter, and introduces a method to estimate
this constant. We present an Expectation Maximization algorithm as a base frame-
work to handle spherical data and work with the estimation of the normalization
constant. We demonstrate how the spherical normal distribution perform a cluster-
ing using the EM framework, and do an empirical comparison with the commonly
used von Mises-Fisher distribution.
We end up concluding the Spherical Normal
Distribution yield a more detailed segmentation compared to the von Mises-Fisher
Distribution, but at the cost of slower computational times. In higher dimensional
data neither of the methods appears to be stable, and a conclusion is hard to draw
based on the results.

CHAPTER 2
Motivation
The world is becoming increasingly dependant on the ability of machines to act in a
human-like fashion. This often requires machines to become aware of their surround-
ings, separate data etc. often achieved by a variety of clustering techniques, which is
a way of separating data points into different groups based on certain structural as-
pects of the data. Each clustering technique have it’s own strengths and weaknesses,
but a common factor in most of them, is the dependency on the underlying statistical
model. Some data have constraints that restricts it to be of a certain shape, one such
constraint is when data is normalized to have unit length. The uniform length makes
it so the data points only vary by their direction, the branch of statistics that capture
this particular constraint is called Directional Statistics. While at first glance, it may
not be entirely obvious where data with unit length appears, but the applications are
surprisingly common. Just to name a few of the data, in chemistry protein shapes
can be described by a parametrization of a sequence of points all with unit length. In
computer vision surfaces can be described by a collection of normal vectors, allowing
for distinguishing them on the unit sphere, with flat surfaces being concentrations
of points and curved surfaces appearing as bands across the sphere. When trying to
automatically separate generic documents, one approach is to do a count of specific
words, if this histogram of words is normalized to unit length, they are forced onto
the unit sphere and the nature of the histogram is then spherical. When performing
clustering on these data, one of the most essential concepts, is distance between any
two points. A thing that is normally trivial, given the old saying: ”The shortest path
between two points, is a straight line”, but this only holds true in a euclidean space.
Spherical data is not located in a euclidean space, but on a curved subspace embed-
ded in a euclidean space, this gives rise to a problem being a straight line would pass
though space that is not part of the curved subspace the data is located in. This
is equivalent to measuring the distance between two cities located on different con-
tinents on earth, by following a secant line instead of following the curve of earth’s
surface.

Method
The original inspiration for this project is based on some interesting work done on
a Dirichlet Process Tangential Gaussian Mixture Model[Str+15]. The paper by J.
Straub et. al. made some good points in comparing advantages and disadvantages of
multiple different methods and their proposed technique. One of the opposing meth-
ods in the paper by J. Straub et. al. is the popular von Mises-Fisher distribution
(vMF), in this thesis the vMF distribution is used as a baseline result to compare
against and will be described in Section 4.1. There are some considerations that is
worth mentioning before the theory is explained. First off, the data observed is spher-

since some of the first methods made use of euclidean distances, not centering the
data would introduce errors.

The depth data of the NYU dataset, has been collected using a Microsoft Kinect camera,
the range chip used by the Kinect does not support a very high resolution, which results in
the jagged ranges noticed in Figure 5.2. These jagged ranges is a source of error in methods
working on this kind of depth data. The idea of handling the depth data as spherical data,
is to take the surface normals on the depth data. This will result in a series of 3-dimensional
vector of unit length. The number of vectors is dependant on the number of pixel, but is
reduced due to the approximation of surface normals requiring multiple points. When all
surface normals from Figure 5.2 has been approximated, it will yield a sphere as seen on
Figure 5.3. The idea of mapping the surface normals to the tangent-plane and working on
the data in a more manageable form, will give disc shaped dataset as seen on
A very noticeable feature of the surface normals, is the very large gap in data on the
sphere. It might not be entirely obvious at first glance why this is the case, but it is logical.
When the Kinect camera captures the depth image, the infrared rays from the chip only hits
surface oriented towards the camera, one example is Figure 5.1. There is a pillar in the left
side of the image, in theory the surface normals from this pillar would cover a thin band
around the sphere. But since the Kinect camera only captures the side facing towards the
camera, a large portion (around half) of the pillar is not visible to the camera. While this
is a structural feature present in all depth image datasets, it will not be present in every

CHAPTER7
Conclusion
In the beginning of the thesis, the goal was defined to be able to estimate Z2(Λ). This has
been solved by developing a sampling-based estimation, which works very well, for lower
dimensional data, and depending on the initialization it seems to work well for high dimen-
sional data. It causes issues if a naive guess of the covariance/concentration parameter is
given, which causes to wide a spread of data when sampling. It could be reduced significantly
by using a truncated normal distribution to draw the samples from, but since the truncated
normal distribution has a fairly complex normalization constant, this would cause a lot of
issues on the algebraic side of the problem[WM10].
Overall the estimation of anisotropic Normalization Constant for the Spherical Normal Dis-
tribution (Z2(Λ)), has been solved in a satisfactory manner. Futhermore the goal was to
test the Spherical Normal Distribution and compare it to the von Mises-Fisher distribution.
When working in 3-dimensions they perform comparably, The Spherical Normal Distribu-
tion have a slight edge on quality of the clustering while von Mises-Fisher is a fair bit faster
to run.
Lastly one of the goals of this paper was to provide a useful framework to make use of the
Spherical Normal Distribution. This has also been accomplished in form of Expectation
Maximization, and a small toolbox of methods to find a set of parameters that maximize
the likelihood.

The derivation of the gradient of the log-likelihood with respect to the factorization of
the covariance, currently does not work properly in my implementation, despite the math
appears to be correct, this implies some bug in my code that has gone undetected so far,
this would speed up the Manopt optimization as it would not have to use a Finite Difference
approximation.
While my efforts of finding a closed form solution to the anisotropic normalization constant
in a generalized dimensionality has been futile, the hope of finding a such expression still
persists. It would improve the stability of the methods significantly, due to some instability
of the sampling in high dimensions. Finally a good initialization of the parameters could
improve the performance, potentially quite significantly, currently a K random points are
taken as initial guesses of µ and a covariance is estimated for each in a naive fashion, this
could be improved by running another clustering on the data (ideally one that also estimates
the number of clusters), and use the results as a starting guess.
