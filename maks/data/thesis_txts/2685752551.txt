Abstract 
 
In the last 70 years, the production and use of plastic in the world 
have increased exponentially, thanks to the comfort, convenience, 
and versatility associated with it. However, it is estimated that 
about 80% of plastic ends up discarded or incinerated, without the 
possibility of recycling and therefore of creating additional value 
from products or resources, but instead, increasing the pollution 
(Roland Geyer, 2017). For this reason, the idea of circular economy 
is progressively expanding throughout the world in all sectors. 
This thesis provides a general description of the problems related to 
plastics and its production. Then, the innovative concept of the cir-
cular economy is presented with the relative advantages associated 
with it, which contrasts with the currently most widespread model 
of the linear economy, according to which, objects are produced, 
used, and thrown away. 
To give a practical view of these concepts, the analysis of two case 
companies, called RecycleBalls and ReBounces, and their methods, 
have been conducted. These companies are two of the most inno-
vative in their field, providing services and products to increase the 
sustainability of tennis, extending the life cycle of tennis balls or the 
materials used to produce them. 
Moreover, to show the consequences of applying these concepts, 
the Kjøbenhavns Boldklub, a tennis club located in Copenhagen, 
was taken as an example. The analysis revealed that not only would 
they guarantee an improvement from an environmental point of 
view, shown through the calculation of a Carbon Footprint, but also 
from an economic point of view there would be advantages.  
 
 
Nevertheless, the challenges in exporting the methods of the two 
US-based companies to the rest of the world, are also highlighted, 
showing the greatest difficulties in doing so for the method used by 
RecycleBalls, which requires collaboration between several ele-
ments, while the other one results easier to replicate even through 
self-made options. 
Hereby, the thesis contributes to its main purpose, fulfilling the goal 
of presenting new ways to increase the sustainability of tennis, a 
sport that has followed a linear model since it was created, without 
however renouncing to have economic advantages.

1. Background 
 
Nowadays, having a world without plastic would be almost 
impossible. All kinds of plastics are increasingly used in 
different sectors such as packaging, transports, construction, 
etc. 
It is strong, lightweight, and moldable and it is used in 
thousands of products to add comfort, convenience, and 
safety to our everyday lives.  
Thanks to the enormous benefits that plastic brings with its 
combination of low cost, durability, versatility, and high 
strength to weight ratio, since 1950 when the world produced 
only 2 million tons per year, by 2015 its annual production 
increased nearly 200 times reaching 381 million tons (Figure 1) 
(Roland Geyer, 2017). 
Plastic production, according to studies reported by the Ellen 
MacArthur Foundation, is also expected to double again by 2030 
and almost quadruple by 2050. (EMF, 2012) 
Despite all the positive qualities that plastic has; this material 
brings nevertheless significant negative externalities 
concentrated in three main areas: 
 
Greenhouse gas emissions, subsequent from its production 
and after-use incineration. 
 
Degradation of natural systems, as a result of leakage. 
 
Health and environmental impacts from substances of 
concern. 
 
Figure 1 - Global plastic production from 1950 to 2015. (Ritchie, 2018) 
 
1.1 Greenhouse gas emissions 
Millions of tons of greenhouse gases are associated with the 
production and use of plastics. These gases are produced during 
the processes of extraction and transportation of the necessary 
materials, and later by the actual manufacture of plastic, which is 
made from resources that derive from fossil fuels and gas. 
According to the estimation of researchers, only in 2019, the 
production and incineration of plastic, created more than 850 
million tons of greenhouse gasses into the atmosphere, and this 
number is expected to rise to 2.8 billion tons by 2050. 
Also, our oceans are in danger, in fact, they represent the largest 
natural carbon sink for greenhouse gasses leaving a deadly legacy. 
Sunlight and heat cause the plastic to release powerful gasses, 
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
and as our climate changes, the planet gets hotter and the plastic 
breaks down into more methane and ethylene, increasing the 
climate change and perpetuating the cycle. (Major, 2019) 
 
1.2 Degradation of natural system 
The leakage of plastics in the environment, especially in the 
ocean, is one of the most important problems that must be 
solved. Every year more than 8 million tons of plastics leak into 
the ocean, same as dumping the content of one garbage truck into 
it every minute, and these numbers will only increase if nothing 
will be done to change it. Plastic has even been found in the 
deepest place on Earth, the Mariana Trench, around 11 kilometers 
below sea level. (Graham, 2019) 
Plastics can remain in the ocean for hundreds of years in their 
initial form, and even longer in the form of smaller particles called 
microplastics. This means that plastic accumulates over time and 
it has been calculated that in 2050, the total weight of plastic in 
the oceans could exceed the weight of fishes. 
Ocean plastics considerably impact marine natural capital, in 
Europe, where the leakage is also quite limited compared to other 
continents, such as Asia, potential costs for coastal and beach 
cleaning could reach 630 million euros per year. In addition to the 
direct economic costs, they do not have to be forgotten the 
potential impacts to livelihoods and health, food chains and other 
economic and societal systems. 
 
 
 
1.3 Substances of concern 
Another critical point concerning plastics, is related to the 
impurities and contaminants that are released from them, 
becoming a risk of adverse effect on human health and the 
environment. Plastics are produced mixing polymer and a complex 
blend of additives like stabilizers, pigments and plasticizers which 
might contain also unintended substances (BPA, PVC). 
The concerns and uncertainties are related to the potential conse-
quences of a long-term exposure to these substances and their 
combined effect considering that about 225’000 tons of these addi-
tives leach out of the plastic into the environment every year with a 
potential increase to 1.2 million tons per year by 2050, suggesting 
the need of additional research and increased transparency. (EMF, 
2016) 
These data shows that even if plastics can bring a lot of benefits, it 
is crucial to address the negative externalities, both during produc-
tion and the after-use treatment. 
For these reasons, the problem of plastic and pollution has cap-
tured the attention of the public, governments and various compa-
nies and associations all over the world. Other than trying to find 
solutions that can be scaled up, there is a growing recognition that 
addressing the symptoms of this crisis through clean-ups alone is 
not sufficient anymore. 
Now, but even more in the future, it will be necessary to change the 
current linear take-make-dispose model and rethink the way of de-
signing, using, and reusing plastic, adopting circular models in which 
pollution and waste of resources is reduced to the minimum. 
To help achieve this, there are more initiatives every year that seek 
to engage the largest number of businesses by promoting the circu-
lar vision. 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
One of these, for example, is the New Plastic Commitment, led by 
the Ellen MacArthur Foundation in collaboration with UN Environ-
ment.  
Thanks to this commitment, more than 400 organizations around 
the world have adopted a circular vision for plastics since the pro-
gram was launched, i.e., October 2018. 
The general ideas are to eliminate the plastic that is not needed, to 
innovate the design of the one that is needed so that it can be re-
used or recycled, and finally to circulate everything that is possible 
to circulate so as not to make it become waste. (UN-Environment, 
2019) 
 
1.4 Research Questions  
This thesis follows the idea of the circular economy and through the 
examination of two companies, named RecycleBalls and Re-
Bounces, it tries to propose new ideas to reduce the plastic waste 
associated to a specific case, such as the use of tennis balls.  
The study of these two companies will show the costs and benefits 
that the stakeholders would have in case they decide to adopt 
these methods, also comparing them with the current linear mod-
els, which are largely the most used in every part of the world. 
The thesis, therefore, raises and tries to answer the two following 
research questions: 
 
How can current linear business models be replaced by 
circular models? Case study on tennis balls.  
 
Which changes are needed from a behavioral and 
organizational aspect? 
To answer these two questions, the research will be based on the 
potential recirculation of the materials that make up tennis balls, 
which are mainly synthetic and natural rubber. 
The case studies will be analysed from different perspectives and 
the research objectives will be addressed as follow: 
1. Analysis and description of products and services that the 
case studies propose to the stakeholders, through a Circular 
Economy Business Model Canvas, and different tools 
designed from the CIRCit Norden research project. 
2. Analysis of the costs and benefits that stakeholders would 
have utilizing the circular models offered by the two case 
companies. 
3. Examination of the environmental impact that ReBounces 
and RecycleBalls’ circular models have. 
4. A suggestion of possible improvements to the 
aforementioned circular methods.

ferent areas. (CIRCitNorden, 2020)

2.4 Circular Economy Business Model Canvas 
The business model canvas is a tool that was initially conceived in 
2004 by Alexander Osterwalder and developed in 2010 by Oster-
walder himself with the collaboration of Yves Pigneur, Alan Smith 
and other 470 experts. (Osterwalder, 2010) 
The Business Model Canvas is a strategic model used for the crea-
tion and development of business models. It is literally a visual tem-
plate that shows the infrastructure, products, customers, suppliers, 
and other elements that distinguish a company. One of the main 
objectives of this tool is the ability to offer an overview of the inter-
connections present within the model as well as a quick representa-
tion of the main elements that make up a business model. 
In particular, the two presented in this thesis are Circular Economy 
Business Models which consist in an adaptation made by the CIRCit 
Norden research project, which represent companies with a funda-
mentally different way of producing and consuming goods and ser-
vices trying to find a way to switch towards a more resource-effi-
cient and circular economy, reducing the environmental pressure 
that comes from economic activities. CIRCit also developed a circu-
lar economy-driven business model configurator, an excel tool, 
which aims to facilitate designing business model concepts for the 
circular economy following a step-by-step guide and that has been 
used to develop the business models of RecycleBalls and Re-
Bounces. (P. Pieroni, McAloone, & Pigosso, 2019)

The Circularity Assessment tool is an excel-based tool developed by

1. Preparation  
It is important to have a good overview of the product lifecycle with 
the associated environmental aspects during all the lifecycle stages. 
Then, it is necessary to have a clear idea of the different circular 
strategies and a good understanding of the design guidelines which 
are showed and briefly described in Appendix A. 
 
2. Evaluation of the concepts 
The circularity assessment tool calculates the total circularity poten-
tial score for at least two concepts. This score is calculated multiply-
ing two main criteria: “importance” of each design guideline and its 
“level of fulfilment”. 
The importance criterion refers to how important the design guide-
lines are in the development of each specific product and the selec-
tion of the scores is qualitative based on the product development 
goals and the environmental and circularity goals of the company 
taken in consideration. This criterion can have a score that goes 
from 0 to 3 divided in this way: 
 
(0) Not important 
(1) Slightly important 
(2) Moderately important 
(3) Really important 
 
The level of fulfilment criterion, instead, refers to how much the 
general design guidelines are applied and fulfilled for each of the 
concepts and it is possible to choose between the following scores: 
 
(1) Yes, the guideline has been completely fulfilled by this 
concept. 
(3) Somehow but can be improved. 
       (5) No, the guideline has not been fulfilled by this concept

3. Literature background 
 
3.1 Circular economy 
During the evolution of the industrial economy, we always 
followed one basic characteristic established at the beginning of 
the industrialization: a linear model of resource consumption 
that follows a “take-make-dispose” pattern. 
Many companies, in the last few years have noticed that this 
model increases the exposure to risks, in fact, there are five 
types of risks when a company depends on the conventional 
linear economic model: 
 Ecological: production of goods at the expense of the 
productivity of our ecosystem, which can endanger the 
provision of vital ecosystem services, such as water, air, 
and soil cleaning. 
 Fluctuation of raw material prices: since 2006, the 
fluctuation of raw materials prices has spiked. This 
creates problems for suppliers of raw materials and for 
the market, and it discourages investment in the 
extraction and processing of materials, that translates 
into a higher price rise over time. 
 Critical materials: a lot of goods are produced 
with scarce material. Different industries make 
intensive use of critical materials that are 
available only in limited extent, for their 
production processes. 
 Increase in material demand: due to the population and 
welfare growth, a huge increase in material demand is 
expected which creates a big problem together with the 
limited supply of raw materials and a shorter lifespan of 
the products, that comes from a process of “positive 
feedback”, which means that the consumers want new 
products faster, reducing the life span of the old ones. 
 Interdependence: with the increase of trades, a lot of 
different products are interconnected because 
countries with scarcity of a product and a surplus of 
another, can trade them for each other. In this way 
when there is scarcity of a raw material, this affects the 
prices and availability of a lot of goods. (Shyaam 
Ramkumar, 2018) 
This situation leads to the search for a new industrial model 
that can further decouple sales revenue, the Circular Model. 
The circular economy is a model of production and consumption 
designed to benefit the environment, the society, and the 
businesses. In contrast to the linear model, a circular economy is 
regenerative by design and it aims at reducing waste to a minimum, 
when a product reaches the end of life, its materials are kept in the 
cycle whenever possible to be used again and again, creating 
further value. In this way it is drawn a precise distinction between 
the consumption and the use of materials, changing the model in 
which manufacturers or retailers hold the ownership of their 
products, acting as service provider that sells their use and not their 
one-way consumption. 
There are few principles on which the circular economy is based.  
Design out waste and pollution. The product has to be designed to 
fit within a biological or technical material cycle, with its design 
made to facilitate disassembly and refurbishment. In this way, the 
concept of waste is limited.  
Biological waste is natural and non-toxic, which means that the 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
nutrients can be re-introduced in the atmosphere. Technical 
components are designed to be re-used with minimal energy and 
highest quality retention, with the possibility, in some cases, to 
have some improvements. Restorative loop is the head start idea. 
Rely on renewable sources’ energy.  Systems should try to run only 
through utilization of renewable sources, this would make sure that 
the pressure is put on the true bottleneck of our resource 
consuming society, considering that there is no shortage of labor 
and renewable energy in the long term. 
Build resilience through diversity. Braungart says: “Natural systems 
support resilient abundance by adapting to their environments with 
an infinite mix of diversity, uniformity and complexity. The 
industrial revolution and globalization focused on uniformity, so our 
systems are often unstable. To fix that we can manufacture 
products with the same flair for resilience by using successful 
natural systems as models” (Braungart, 2002). Which means that in 
a fast and uncertain evolving world, modularity, versatility and 
adaptivity are features that need to be prioritized to survive to 
possible external shocks. 
Waste is food. Taking in consideration the biological nutrient side, 
the heart of the idea is to reintroduce products and materials back 
into the biosphere using non-toxic and restorative loops, while for 
the technical nutrient side, the idea is to use the upcycling, which 
means that it is possible to also have an improvement in quality. 
Think in systems. Elements are considered in their relationship with 
their infrastructure, environment, and social context. In circular 
economy, it is important to understand how the single parts 
influence one another within a whole, and the relationship of the 
whole to the parts. The system thinking emphasizes flow and 
connection over time with the potential to include regenerative 
conditions instead of focusing only on the short term or on the 
single parts. (EMF, 2012)

3.2 Plastic in Circular Economy 
According to what has been said in Chapter 1, our relationship with 
plastic needs rethinking and some actions are needed to be able to 
do that. So, how do we create a circular economy for plastic? 
In the plastic vision of the future, one of the vital characteristics is 
that plastic cannot become waste or pollution. To achieve this, it is 
necessary to eliminate all the useless plastic items, due to the 
demand set to double over the coming two decades, it will be 
impossible to keep this growing flow of plastic in the economy and 
out of the environment. That is why we need to reduce the amount 
of material that needs to be circulated.                                         
Another really important point is to innovate and reuse models, to 
be sure that the plastics we need is recyclable, compostable, and 
reusable. In the last few years, in fact, there was a huge increase of 
focus from businesses and governments on reuse, in the form of 
research initiatives and reuse-focused startups.                          
Finally, circulation. The idea is to keep all the plastic items we use 
out of the environment and inside the economy for as long as 
possible. This is achievable developing a system which includes 
collecting and sorting, a physical chemical and biological breakdown 
process, and finally the rebuilding of a material that is reintroduced. 
There is a difference from material re-circulation and material re-
use, the ladder means that the shape of the plastic item is 
maintained, and it is re-used as it is, while in the first one the shape 
is not maintained, and circulation occurs through a breakdown 
process. (EMF, 2016)

3.3 Types of Recycling 
One of the key principles of the circular economy is that products 
and materials, are circulated at their highest value all the time. 
Plastic items are reused when possible (circulating the plastic 
item) and then recycled (circulating the plastic materials). 
Nevertheless, today, after more than 40 years since the 
introduction of the recycling concept, most of the plastic items are 
recycled into lower-value applications which also represent their 
final use before they cannot be recycled again. 
It is possible to distinguish three broad types of recycling: 
mechanical closed-loop, mechanical open-loop, and chemical 
recycling. 
The Mechanical recycling closed loop is the most value-preserving 
loop, in which, polymers are kept intact instead of breaking them 
down as in the chemical recycling, preserving more value. This 
allows to keep the quality of the materials at a similar level by 
cycling materials into the same application (e.g., Plastic cups to 
plastic cups) or into applications that require materials of similar 
quality. As such, mechanical closed loop makes it possible to 
preserve the value of the material and to maintain the range of 
possible applications in the future adding more loops. 
The Mechanical recycling open loop is a method that cannot 
continue forever due to the inherent quality loss of plastic during 
mechanical recycling. The mechanical open loop plays an 
important role anyway because polymers are kept intact, but the 
inferior quality and material properties allows only applications 
with lower demands. The strategy of cascading to the highest-
value applications each cycle could help to maximize the number 
of possible cycles and the preservation of the value. 
Finally, the Chemical recycling, breaks down polymers into single 
monomers that can become building blocks for the production of 
new polymers. Considering this, the chemical recycling is less value 
preserving that the mechanical, and chemical recycling 
technologies are not really widespread or economically viable for 
most common plastic items. However, this technology could 
enable after-use plastics to be upcycled again into virgin-quality 
polymers, becoming an option for materials for which mechanical 
recycling is not possible (e.g., Plastics that cannot be cascaded 
anymore). (EMF, 2016)

Step 1 – Core Formulation and Production solution 
The core formulation for pressurized balls is based on a high

 Clay: 40 
 Magnesium carbonate: 8 
 Calcium carbonate: 20  
 Zinc oxide: 2.5 
 Sulphur: 3.5

The specific amount of the mix of synthetic and natural rubber, var-
ies between the different companies that produce the balls, and it 
reaches the factory in bales of 150-550 kgs.     
It is then kneaded to make it softer to work and to ensure that the 
solution will flow through the different steps as expected. Then the 
different powders are added to the rubber to give the required 
properties such as strength and colour. The rubber compound sub-
sequently is put into a tank with petroleum solvent and it is left to 
soak until it becomes a sticky dough after some hours. Finally, the 
dough is diluted by stirring with more solvent until it reaches the 
desired consistency. 
 
Step 2 – Prepping the rubber 
After the rubber compound is ready, steel rollers kneed the rubber 
and thin it before machines heat and cut them into pellets, which 
will be left to cool down afterwards. 
 
 
 
Step 3 – Curing the Half-Shells 
The pellets are loaded into a hydraulic press which turns them into 
a hemispherical half shell and partially cures them for 2 and a half 
minutes at 150ºC. 
 
 
Figure 9 - Rubber half-shell. 
Step 4 – Edge Buff 
The edge of the half shell is roughened with a grinding wheel to 
provide a key for the adhesive which is next applied.  
A vulcanizing rubber solution is applied to the edge of the buffed 
half shell.

Step 5 – Making the Core 
Regarding pressurized balls, there are two methods that can be 
used to inflate them. The first one, is using chemicals, which can be 
accurately controlled, and it happens after the core has been as-
sembled. The chemicals that are used for this process usually are 
sodium nitrite and ammonium chloride, which ends up producing 
nitrogen during the molding process. 
The second method is much more complex, and it happens using 
compressed-air inflation bringing together the two shell halves and 
trapping the air inside. This method is divided as follows: 
I. 
The press closes until the edges of the half shells are not 
quite touching. 
II. 
The internal platen area is isolated from the atmosphere by 
a rubber sealing ring around the periphery of the platens. 
III. 
Compressed air is introduced at the required pressure to the 
platen area. 
IV. 
The press platens are brought together trapping 
compressed air between the half shells forming the final 
pressurized core. 
V. 
The platens are finally heated to vulcanize the rubber 
solution and then left to cool down. 
Every tennis ball usually is pressurized at 0.83 Bar and they are sold 
in cans to maintain the balls pressure. 
 
 
Figure 11 - Creation of the core. 
 
Step 6 – Applying the adhesive 
The core is buffed to have a rough surface and afterwards, a rub-
ber-based adhesive will coat it, through a tumbling operation, en-
suring uniform quantity of adhesive across the core.

Step 7 – Cutting and Applying the felt 
There are two different types of cloth used for tennis balls. Melton 
cloth, with high wool content, and Needle cloth, with a greater con-
tent of synthetic fibers (nylon) and cheaper to produce. 
An automated machine will therefore cut sheets of felt 2.5 meters 
wide and 70 meters long into panels with a “dog bone” shape. Once 
this sheet is stacked, the felt is dipped in a yellow vat of adhesive, 
which will help it to adhere to the core and finally the edges of the 
blanks are coated with vulcanizing solution, which will form the 
seam of the ball. 
 
 
Figure 13 - Felt of the tennis ball. 
 
With adhesive on the back of the felt and the core, an automated 
machine applies the two felt panels to the core at about 5 centime-
tres wide and 15.2 centimetres long, and they are rounded on both 
ends. 
 
 
Figure 14 - Application of the felt on the tennis ball. 
Step 8 – Molding 
The ball is then placed in a molding press and heated for 11 minutes 
curing together the rubber solution on the core and on the back of 
the cloth. The heat and pressure also cure the rubber solution in 
the join between the two covers and molds it into a smooth seam. 
Afterwards, the ball is cooled ad removed from the press. The 
molding process leaves the cloth smooth and compressed with a 
ridge around it, where the mold closed upon it.

The balls are sent in an industrial washing machine with a steam

minutes of hot air, which causes the cloth to fluff, giving a raised 
and softer surface, and cancelling the ridge around the ball. 
 
 
Figure 16 – Steaming of the ball.

Finally, the balls are hand-inspected and stamped with their respec-

with a pressurization of 0.14 bar higher than the balls, since a can 
holds air 50 times better.

As it is possible to see in the Market and Product Decision Tree, in Figure 
29, the two case companies, follows two different recirculation strategies. 
ReBounces, follows the lines of Refurbishing. This mean that its purpose is 
to extend the use-cycles of the old products by returning its functionali-
ties to a satisfactory working condition. 
Refurbishment does not claim that the products have to return to as-new 
conditions and in this case, the refurbishing activity is the re-pressuriza-
tion of the old tennis balls through the utilization of the Green Tennis Ma-
chine, in a way that will allow them to bounce as new, but still with the 
outside felt more worn than the new ones. 
This process is often mistaken with Remanufacturing, but the main differ-
ence from it, is that Refurbishment is usually less costly, less rigorous, in-
volve less disassembly and reassembly and a lower production volume. 
Instead, RecycleBalls utilizes the strategy of Cascading. It can be charac-
terized as repurposing of materials, accepting declining levels of mechani-
cal properties and it results in a material that is not suitable to be re-
turned to the initial use-cycle. 
Cascading is typically used in applications that require large flows and it 
means that product materials are re-used to extend resource timelines. 
However, in cascading, a new application usually has a lower demand for 
resource quality.  
In a cascade strategy, a material starts its use-cycle at the highest possible 
quality like in RecycleBalls’ case where the rubber compound starts its cy-
cle to form the core of the tennis ball, then the quality of the material nat-
urally declines over time, due to its utilization. Afterwards, in this specifi-
cal case, the rubber compound is grinded to form the Green Gold and re-
utilized in a new application before its quality falls too low. The quality 
not only declines because of natural processes, but it could happen also 
due to potential impurities of the materials to be cascaded, such as the 
non-perfect separation between the core that must be grinded and the 
superficial part with the felt. 
One of the main benefits of the cascading, is that it can be used to retain 
the value of large number of materials that would end up as waste if not 
used for other applications, but it needs to be kept in consideration that 
the treatments are non-reversible. In this case, in fact, once the rubber

In general, companies that need the utilization of circular business

sources with a consequent reduction of industrial and consumer

the refurbishing of old products is taken into consideration, it is 
possible to reduce the extraction of natural sources and the genera-
tion of waste up to 80% relative to the manufacture of new prod-
ucts. (Pieroni, 2020)

nations, 2020)

gets a previous stage, finding a way to prolong their utilization in 
the same field and for the same purpose. 
Considering that these two companies target different moments of 
the tennis balls’ life, it is possible to say that neither of them ex-
clude the other one, making it possible to find an even more sus-
tainable and circular solution, through the combination of both 
their models, in a way that will increase the benefits from each ten-
nis ball. 
As shown in Figure 43, the idea of this new concept starts in the 
same way as before, with the creation and manufacture of the balls 
with the same methods. Then, during their utilization, when they 
lose their internal pressure and the bouncing characteristics start to 
decrease, the Green Tennis Machine is utilized. 
This, will rejuvenate the tennis balls, allowing the players to utilize 
them again more than one time, instead of buying new ones, reduc-
ing virgin material utilization for creation of new balls, and reducing 
waste that would come from their disposal. 
After the utilization of the Green Tennis Machine for the necessary 
number of times, when the outside felt is disrupted and it would 
Figure 43 - Combination of the two models. 
==PAGE:60==
 
60 
 
not be possible to re-pressurize the tennis balls anymore, instead of 
incinerating or disposing them in landfills, their materials could be 
cascaded. 
Utilizing the Play It Green machine to grind the tennis balls, felt and 
core will be divided and shredded, and the rubber can replace the 
EPDM plastic granules for the production of new tennis courts, once 
again reducing the utilization of virgin raw materials and waste pro-
duction, considering that the core doesn’t have to be disposed. 
 
8.1 Benefits of the new model 
The use of the new suggested model brings the following benefits 
summarized in this list: 
 reduction of the budget used to buy tennis balls, for both 
individual players and tennis facilities. 
 since fewer tennis balls are purchased, given the longer life 
of each one of them, consequently there would be a 
reduction in the virgin raw materials used for the 
production of new balls. 
 reduction of disposal costs by tennis facilities, which 
donating balls do not have to pay any tax for their disposal. 
This point does not affect individual players because, for 
the smaller amount of tennis balls used, they would not 
have to pay anything for their discard. 
 reduction of EPDM plastic granules production, as its use in 
the production of tennis courts is replaced with the 
cascaded material from the old tennis balls. 
 significant reduction of waste, considering both what 
should have been incinerated or landfilled, as the majority 
of the balls’ material that reach the end of life is no longer 
discarded, but it stays in the loop. 
 
8.2 Mitigation of the weak points 
In Chapter 6, using the circularity assessment tool, four weaknesses 
are found in the methods used by ReBounces and RecycleBalls. 
By combining their models, a problem that was related to Re-
Bounces is eliminated. This problem consists in the final discard of 
the tennis balls due to the disruption of the external felt, after they 
have been re-pressurized the maximum number of times. The 
core’s material, in fact, will be cascaded to produce the shock pad 
for the new tennis courts. 
As for the other three problems, also described in Chapter 6, they 
remain, making it necessary to find alternative solutions. 
The first one, consists in a failure to use the shredded felt of the 
ball, which is instead discarded, and it can be mitigated by using this 
material for the realization of other products, such as slippers, hats, 
or, as RecycleBalls is studying to do, even though they are still in the 
research and development phase of the process, mixing it with cot-
ton and polyester, to be able to use it for the production of t-shirts. 
The second problem that is still present, is related to the quality of 
the shredded materials, not yet perfectly separated, with a division 
of the 99%. This will require a further period of research and devel-
opment to perfect the machine used to complete this task, trying to 
bring this value to 100% of material division, which consequently 
raises the quality of the cascaded elements. 
==PAGE:61==

The last problem found, consists in the quality decay of the ball’s 
felt during its utilization, which is not restored to the initial condi-
tions during the re-pressurization process, as happens instead for 
the bouncing characteristics. This problem follows the natural de-
cay of materials over time and for this reason, there is probably no 
solution other than purchasing new tennis balls when it happens. 
 
8.3 Comparison with current linear model 
After the proposition of improvements, to compare the suggested 
joint circular model to the initial linear one, the Checklist of Sustain-
ability Qualifying Criteria is used.  
The framework of this tool contains five categories, such as value 
proposition, creation, delivery, capture and transformation, used to 
show how value is treated in traditional profit-oriented business 
models and in sustainable business models. All these categories 
with relative guidelines can be seen in major detail in Appendix B 
while the instruction for its utilization can be seen in Chapter 2. 
The selection process of the scores for each category is qualitative 
based on personal reflections of the author of this thesis on the 
twenty-six sustainable qualifying criteria that define the categories. 
Figure 44 shows the results of the tool in a radar graph. The red line 
represents the values scored from the Final Circular Model sug-
gested, while the yellow line represents the values of the Linear 
model currently utilized everywhere in the world. 
The linear model has a really low score in each category presented 
due to its high unsustainability, considering that the tennis balls 
lifecycle is basically the same as the one used in 1874, when tennis 
was invented, and according to which the balls are simply manufac-
tured, used, and thrown away, with the company that is producing 
them that has only an economic focus. 
The final circular model suggested, instead, has different scores for 
each category as can be seen hereby. 
Value proposition represents how stakeholders and customers’ 
economic, environmental, and social needs are addressed by the 
business or network of businesses in relation of different time 
frames such as the beginning of life, middle of life and end of life. It 
also includes the description of services and products used to ad-
dress the targeted needs. 
In this section, the circular model reaches a total score of 20 out of 
26 thanks to the various services offered that allow to extend the 
tennis balls’ life, rejuvenating them through re-pressurization, and 
decreasing the amount of waste generated, cascading the end-of-
life material for another application such as the creation of new 
tennis courts through collaborations with partners companies. The 
missing points are due to a lack of targeting social needs.

Value creation addresses how value is created by the supply chain 
or how value is co-created in the value network. It covers the defini-
tion and management of the resources, processes, partnerships, 
and physical infrastructures of the network. 
In this section, the total score is 9 out of 12 because most of the 
principles mentioned are fully or partially embedded in the system.  
Value delivery addresses the customer, or more in general, the 
stakeholders interface management and it includes the definition 
and management of aspects related to the relationship with stake-

This section has a score of 3 out of 4, because are considered both 
the needs of the customers, reducing the expenses for tennis balls,

Value capture addresses how the value is retained or recovered by

The use of the Checklist of Sustainability Qualifying Criteria gives a

9. Feasibility and Further Work 
 
This chapter will analyse the feasibility and the different challenges 
that any tennis facility or any individual player would have to face in 
case they are interested in adopting the methods suggested by this 
thesis. 
9.1 ReBounces’ method 
Without a doubt, the method of re-pressurizing tennis balls is the 
easiest to adopt. In fact, it is only necessary to buy a Green Tennis 
Machine with the capacity of balls related to the own needs, with 
individual players who can buy the smaller versions, which have a 
much lower cost, while the tennis facilities, using a greater number 
of balls, can adopt one of the bigger versions. 
The only other cost linked to them is the re-pressurizing cost of the 
machine, which as explained in Chapter 5, and as calculated by Re-
Bounces, results to be only 0.07 DKK for each ball, making this 
choice extremely affordable by anyone. 
 
9.2 Personal experiment 
The author of this thesis has also experimented the construction of 
his own repressurizing machine, following the description of the 
model presented by ReBounces.  
To do this, a 5 gallons Pin Lock Cornelius Keg was used, linked with 
the appropriate connector and inflation valve to an air compressor. 
Then, 50 flat tennis balls have been introduced into the charging 
chamber at atmospheric pressure. After the balls were added, the 
keg was closed, and the pressure increased to 50 psi by adding an 
appropriate quantity of air through the utilization of the compres-
sor. Once the keg was charged, the pressure has been monitored 
with a pressure gauge. After four days, the pressure in the chamber 
was bled off until an acceptable level, to be able to open the keg 
and check the results of the experiment.

9.2 RecycleBalls’ method 
Within the United States, where RecycleBalls is positioned and the 
right infrastructure and network is already present, there are no 
particular difficulties in adopting their method. In fact, any individ-
ual with more than 100 tennis balls to donate, or any tennis facility 
with more than four courts, can become part of their program, re-
ceiving free shipping labels, and receiving a tax receipt to claim the 
tax-back. 
Outside the United States, however, the situation is more compli-
cated. Being a very innovative solution in this field, there are not 
many countries or many companies that offer the possibility to use 
this method. Taking Europe as an example, a similar solution is 
adopted only in France, in a project called "Operation Balle Jaune", 
thanks to which the French Tennis Federation in collaboration with 
the waste management company Coved, is able to collect 1.5 mil-
lion tennis balls per year, through the utilization of two trucks that, 
to optimise the carbon balance of the campaign, travel a nation-
wide tour of France to pick them up. Afterwards, these balls are de-
livered to two Coved facilities, grounded down into granules, and 
separated from the felt by a process of suction, ready to be used to 
create sports surfaces for hospitals and social, healthcare, and edu-
cational institutions. (Villette, 2016) 
Therefore, if a tennis facility wants to adopt this method, some 
steps would be necessary: 
==PAGE:66==
 
66 
 
1) Gather a sufficient number of balls, either used only by one ten-
nis facility or collected cooperating with more than one facility in-
terested in doing so, in order to create the necessity for the cas-
caded materials, considering, as explained in Chapter 7, that for a 
single tennis court at least 20,000 balls are needed. 
2) Find a plastic recycling company that can shred the balls dividing 
also the felt from the core. 
3) Find one of the various companies that produce cushioned sport 
courts, which is interested in using the materials recovered from 
the balls. 
4) Based on the number of balls that need to be sent to the recy-
cling facility, find an agreement with a shipping company 
For a single individual it would not be possible to adopt this 
method, for the reason explained in point number 1. This means 
that, at least until a network similar to the one in the United States 
is created, which allows private donations from every individual 
player with more than a certain number of balls to donate, it would 
be necessary to do it through the collection that takes place in the 
tennis facilities. 
As it is possible to see, spreading this method to other countries 
that do not already have the right structure, is more complicated 
than adopting a machine for repressurizing balls, but following the 
guidelines, hopefully in the future, it will be a common practice. 
 
 
 
 
 
9.3 Further Work 
Should this thesis be developed further, there are some areas, in 
particular related to the RecycleBalls method, that would need fur-
ther study. 
Without having the chance to meet the founders of RecycleBalls 
and the Play It Green Machine, it was not possible to know in detail 
how their machine works, therefore a more in-depth study on every 
step the machine is going through to shred the tennis balls, would 
be necessary, to be able to replicate it somewhere else in the world 
or to improve it, so that felt, and rubber can be divided by the 100% 
instead of the actual 99%. 
Another point that would need further study concerns the treat-
ment of the felt once it is separated from the rubber. At the mo-
ment, it is normally disposed of trough incineration, so further stud-
ies are necessary to find options that could use felt at a lower qual-
ity condition than the initial one. 
Finally, although the general guidelines have been given, it is neces-
sary to analyse in which countries it would be more appropriate to 
start using these methods, according to the presence of modern re-
cycling facilities, which are able to divide the felt from the core, and 
sport flooring companies that are interested in using the shredded 
rubber.

10. 
Conclusion  
 
This thesis explores the possibility of decreasing the consumption of 
virgin plastic through the application of circular economy principles, 
supported by a specific case taken as an example.  
To achieve the circularity, it is necessary to avoid the consumption 
of the resources, recovering them in a continuous and long-lasting 
system, with the aim of keeping them in the cycle at their highest 
potential, as long as possible. 
The implementation of a circular system is based on the idea of the 
five principles introduced in Chapter 3, such as, designing out waste 
and pollution, relying on renewable energies, building resilience 
through diversity, reintroducing materials back into the biosphere, 
and considering elements as part of a bigger infrastructure instead 
of only as single parts. From the practical point of view of compa-
nies that want to employ the circularity, these principles translate 
into the utilization of one or more strategies of recirculation of 
products, materials and parts, and extension to new cycles, men-
tioned in Chapter 6. 
The case considered examines how two companies such as Recy-
cleBalls and ReBounces, can increase the sustainability of a sport 
like tennis, which has remained practically unchanged from this 
point of view since it was invented, and in which only a small minor-
ity of the tennis balls produced annually are recycled. 
In this case, it is necessary to change the current linear methods by 
adding loops during the life cycle of the products, refurbishing the 
tennis balls, bringing them back to a condition similar to the initial 
one as many times as possible. Later, when they reach their end of 
life, the cascading strategy is applied, in order to use waste as a re-
source, reutilizing the materials from which they are composed for 
other purposes, such as in this case, the production of new sport 
floorings. 
To make these variations, changes are needed from a behavioural 
and organizational point of view, in fact, circular economy is not 
only a commitment to recycling products at their End of Life, but it 
is a complete shift in the way society think about the creation and 
the consumption of products.                                                                      
The circular economy requires the society to change behaviour 
about how to source, create, use, and dispose the materials and 
products used. 
Concerning the case taken as an example, with regard to individual 
players and tennis facilities, they no longer have to normally dis-
pose the tennis balls, because in this way, their life cycle would end 
without creating additional value, which instead could be retrieved 
adopting the two strategies described above.  
Another important point is to create awareness of these possibili-
ties to the greatest number of possible stakeholders. This can be 
done, for example, by advertising in tennis facilities through explan-
atory signs, social networking and using clearly visible containers to 
gather the old balls, so that they create curiosity in those who no-
tice them. 
This thesis, therefore, on the one hand, provides a clear vision of 
how we can increase the circularity of this sport, also showing the 
benefits from an economic and environmental point of view. The 
economic benefits can be summarized in a decrease in the budget 
used for purchase of tennis balls and their disposal, followed by the 
possibility of requesting a tax-back based on the number donated. 
For the environmental benefits, instead, there is a decrease in the 
carbon footprint related to the minor production of balls, due to 
the re-pressurization of the old ones, and the lower use of EPDM 
granules necessary for the production of new sport floorings, which 
==PAGE:69==
 
69 
 
translates in a potential decrease in the carbon footprint for each 
court, built with cascaded materials, of 10.583 kg of CO2, that can 
be compared to 2,2 years of CO2eq emitted by an average person. 
(Roser, 2020) 
On the other hand, however, it shows the challenges in "exporting" 
these strategies outside the United States, the country where the 
two case companies are located and where there is already a suita-
ble network to adopt them. As for the re-pressurization of the balls, 
its adoption does not present any particular difficulty, both in the 
case of adopting the machine marketed by ReBounces, or in the 
case of own-made production. 
For the cascading of materials, however, the greatest difficulties in 
achieving it are highlighted, as there is a need to establish a net-
work that guarantees cooperation between donors, recycling facili-
ties and sport flooring construction companies, and for which the 
main guidelines are given to make it happen. 
The export of these strategies to the rest of the world certainly rep-
resents a wide spectrum of challenges and possibilities, making this 
thesis only the first step towards the increased circularity of tennis 
and the rise of methods to reduce plastic waste, with the need for 
further work to discover and mitigate the challenges associated 
with their exportation throughout the world.
