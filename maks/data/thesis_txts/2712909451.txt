Technical summary
Due to the progressive take-make-consume-dispose culture the world is experiencing today, there
is a massive need to conserve virgin materials and focus on re-circulation of materials, also known
as circular economy. One of the solutions to keep materials in the circular loop is by industrial
symbioses: a collaboration between enterprises where waste and other by-products are exchanged.
Gladsaxe Municipality is an interesting area for several reasons: it is an area that is very progressive
on sustainable development and green transition and it contains a significant amount of production
enterprises. This makes it an applicable place to start an industrial symbiosis. Based on an extensive
literature review and conversations with relevant experts on the area, a methodology is made for
conducting an industrial symbiosis in a well established industrial area, namely Gladsaxe Municipality.
Out of 30 contacted manufacturing enterprises in the municipality, eight were selected for an interview.
The interviews are conducted based on a screening tool from Symbiosis Center Denmark (Symbiosis
Center Denmark, 2022). The interviews included data collection on their different waste fractions,
the amounts, related costs and waste handling companies. It also involved the enterprises general
green opportunities and readiness to take the necessary actions towards sustainable development.
The material inputs, water usage and waste water amounts was also mapped. Many different waste
fractions are identified from the mapping, but five fractions are identified and analyzed, since they
are either represented at all enterprises or because the quantities are very significant. The five
fractions are: sludge, wood, plastics, bio-waste and textiles. From the conducted interviews it became
clear, that none of the enterprises’ waste fractions match the input of another enterprise. It also
became clear that it is primarily low quality and non-valuable waste fractions that are identified
at the enterprises, that does not already have the best environmental and economical disposal solution.
Therefore, other important measures can be made in regards to waste handling and sustainable
development within the eight enterprises in the municipality. An extensive analysis of each waste
fraction has been conducted based on the waste hierarchy and the different disposal methods within
it: reduce, reuse, recycle, recover and dispose. The analysis is based on conducted life cycle analyses
on each waste fraction, found throughout the literature review. For all waste fractions, the top levels
in the waste hierarchy should be of highest priority. Afterwards, it is possible to move downwards in
the hierarchy to examine which recycling or recovery methods and technologies are available. For
each enterprise a recommendation was made based on their waste type in regards to the hierarchy,
e.g. new technologies to invest in, change of waste handling companies or simply more sorting
options. Aside from the waste hierarchies, a set of guidelines are conducted to provide enterprises
with counseling on the internal environmental opportunities and what actions they can do themselves,
to improve their waste management and overall environmental impacts.
A conclusion from this thesis is that an industrial symbiosis is not necessarily the best environmental
and economical solution for enterprises. In the case of Gladsaxe Municipality, where none of the
eight enterprises can share resources, it is a better solution for them to follow the waste management
guidelines and optimize their recycling of resources. It can also be concluded that the overall
readiness to take action on sustainable development throughout the eight enterprises is high. This is
partly due to the comprehensive work of Gladsaxe Municipality involving enterprises in committees,
sustainable groups and overall sustainable development goals for the area. It is also due to the general
==PAGE:5==
societal pressure of having sustainable products, a responsible consumption and recycling options. It
can be concluded from this thesis, that despite the best efforts from enterprises, big and small, a lot
of recycling technologies are not fully developed (e.g. for paint sludge and textile waste), and many
sorted waste fractions are continuously being exported to other countries. Extensive life cycle analyses
on different waste handling options, including transportation, as well as environmental supply chain
management and CO2 calculations, will be the best tools to support the waste hierarchies, thus
creating a complete sustainability guide for manufacturing enterprises.

Sammenfatning
Grundet den progressive ’brug og smid væk’-kultur verdenen oplever i dag, er der et større behov
end nogensinde for at bevare råmaterialer og fokusere på recirkulering af materialer, også kendt
som cirkulær økonomi. En af løsningerne til at holde materialer inde i det cirkulære kredsløb er
ved industriel symbiose: et samarbejde mellem virksomheder, hvor affald og andre biprodukter
bliver udvekslet. Gladsaxe Kommune er et interessant område af flere årsager: det er et område,
der er meget progressivt i forhold bæredygtig udvikling og den grønne omstilling, og så indeholder
området mange produktionsvirksomheder. Dette gør det teoretisk set til et passende sted at starte
en industriel symbiose. Baseret på et omfattende literaturstudie og samtaler med relevante eksperter
på området, er der blevet udviklet en stepvis metode til at lokalisere mulighederne for en industriel
symbiose i et etableret indusiområde, nemlig Gladsaxe Kommune.
Ud af de 30 kontaktede produktionsvirksomheder i kommunen er otte udvalgt til interview. Inter-
viewene er udført på baggrund af et screening værktøj inspireret af Dansk Symbiose Center (Symbiosis
Center Denmark, 2022). Interviewene inkluderer dataindsamling af forskellige affaldsfraktioner,
mængderne af dem, relaterede omkostninger og aftagere af deres affald. Spørgsmålene relaterer sig
også til virksomhedernes generelle grønne muligheder og parathed til at tage de nødvendige skridt
imod bæredygtig udvikling. Materiale input og ouput, vandforbrug og spildevandsmængder er også
kortlagt. Mange affaldsfraktioner er identificeret fra kortlægningen, men fem fraktioner er identificeret
og analyseret, fordi de enten er representeret ved alle virksomhederne eller fordi mængderne af dem
er store. De fem fraktioner er: slam, træ, plastik, bio-affald og tekstiler. Fra interviewene er det
tydeligt, at ingen af virksomhedernes affaldsfraktioner matcher med materialeinputtet i en anden
virksomhed. Det er også tydeligt, at det primært er lav-kvalitets og lav-værdis affaldsfraktioner, der
er identificeret ved virksomhederne, som ikke allerede har den bedste miljømæssige og økonomiske
afsætningsmulighed.
Derfor er andre vigtige tiltag nødvendige i forhold til affaldshåndtering og bæredygtig udvikling hos
de otte virksomheder i kommunen. En omfattende analyse af de fem udvalgte affaldsfraktioner er
blevet lavet på baggrund af affaldshierakiet og de forskellige afsætningsmuligheder: forebyggelse,
direkte genbrug, genanvendelse, forbrænding og deponi. Analysen er baseret på livscycklusanalyser
fra litteraturstudiet for hver affaldsfraktion. For alle affaldsfraktionerne er det den øverste del af
affaldshierakiet, der er af højest prioritet, og dermed også i disse niveauer det er interessant at
undersøge, hvilke genanvendelsesmuligheder der er, og hvilke teknologier der er mulige. Der er
til hver virksomhed givet en anbefaling baseret på deres affaldstyper i forhold til affaldshierakiet,
f.eks. investering i nye teknologier, potentielle aftagervirksomheder for affaldet, eller blot at sortere
i flere fraktioner. Udover affaldshierakierne, er der blevet udviklet nogle retningslinjer for at give
virksomhederne rådgivning på de interne miljømuligheder, de har, og hvordan de bedst kan forbedre
deres affaldshåndtering og miljøpåvirkning.
Det kan ud fra dette projekt konkluderes, at en industriel symbiose ikke nødvendigvis er den
bedste løsning for de otte udvalgte virksomheder i Gladsaxe Kommune, idet ingen af de specifikke
virksomheder kan dele resourcer på nuværende tidspunkt.
Derfor er det en bedre løsning for
dem at følge affaldshåndtering guidelines og optimere deres genanvendelse af resourcer, indtil
potentielle partnerskaber bliver identificeret i kommunen. Det kan også blive konkluderet at den
==PAGE:7==
overordede parathed til at tage de rette skridt mod en bæredygtig udvikling er ekstrem høj for de
otte virksomheder. Dette er blandt andet grundet det store stykke arbejde Gladsaxe Kommune har
gjort ved at involvere virksomheder til events, bæredygtighedsgrupper og FN’s verdensmål. Dels er
der også et generelt samfundsmæssigt pres om bæredygtigt forbrug og genanvendelsesmuligheder
både fra forbrugere og politikere. Det kan ydermere konkluderes, at der på trods af bedste evner fra
virksomhederne, store som små, mangler yderligere genanvendelses teknologier (f.eks. for malerslam
og tekstilaffald), og mange sorterede affaldsfraktioner bliver stadig eksporteret til udlandet. Udførlige
livscyklusanalyser af de forskellige affaldshåndteringsmuligheder og CO2 beregninger vil være de
bedste redskaber til at supplere affaldshierakierne, som i sidste ende skal rådgive virksomheder om
de bedste håndteringsmuligeder af deres affaldsfraktioner.

1. Introduction
Over the past few decades global environmental impacts have increased immensely, which is primarily
due to the rapid population growth worldwide, economic growth and a progressive take-make-
consume-dispose culture. This increasing trend places a massive pressure on the global resources
and leads to significant amounts of waste to be handled. Therefore, there is an increasing focus
on re-circulation of materials to reduce the demand for virgin raw materials and to get as many
materials as possible back into the material loop, also known as circular economy. It is relevant to
examine how to change patterns in society from a linear system to a more circular system. There
are several ways to keep materials in circulation. One solution could be industrial symbioses, which
is a collaboration between companies where waste and other by-products are exchanged. Thus,
one company’s waste will become someone else’s resource. This way of keeping the materials in
circulation is an important tool to ease the pressure on society’s waste management and to make the
best possible use of important resources (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019).
Gladsaxe Municipality has a successful cooperation and partnership with the enterprises that are
located in the municipality. This is among other seen by the Gladsaxe Business District association
which involves sustainability, growth, green transition, networking and urban development (Gladsaxe
Erhvervsby, 2022). However, as of today there is no extensive mapping of the waste streams in the
municipality which is necessary to identify if an industrial symbiosis can be implemented.
Therefore, the purpose of this master thesis is to map waste fractions and examine potential symbioses
between selected enterprises in Gladsaxe Municipality, as well as the best handling options for the
observed waste fractions among the enterprises. A literature review on industrial symbioses in
Denmark and other cases from the Baltic Region will be conducted along with fieldwork in the
form of interviews and data collection from eight selected enterprises in Gladsaxe Municipality
including: PPG Coatings Denmark A/S, Jespers Torvekøkken ApS, Beck & Jørgensen A/S, Ferrosan
Medical Devices A/S, Kjell Pedersen Enterprise A/S, Ironpump A/S and Novozymes A/S. This will
result in a proposal of the most optimal way of utilizing the various resources in the mapped waste
fractions and a discussion of the potential for possible industrial symbioses including opportunities
and barriers. In addition, in order to increase the circular economy in the Gladsaxe Municipality
guidelines for the enterprises are conducted. These deal with the most optimal environmental actions
that the enterprises can take e.g. in relation to their waste management.
This master thesis include the following learning objectives:
• To obtain knowledge from existing literature e.g., industrial symbioses, circular economy and
waste hierarchy
• To cooperate with enterprises in Gladsaxe Municipality and to navigate in business settings

1.1
Problem statement
How can an industrial symbiosis be implemented in Gladsaxe Municipality in regards to identifying
and overcoming environmental and economic barriers as well as applying sustainable and efficient
solutions for sharing resources?
Along with the main research question several guiding questions are subject to discussion:
• What previous knowledge is there on developing industrial synergies/symbioses in cities,
municipalities and district areas?
• What types of enterprises can be a part of an industrial symbiosis and what is their incentive
to join one?
• What tools and measures are used to screen enterprises for their ability to join an industrial
symbiosis?
• What kind of waste fractions can be identified from selected enterprises in Gladsaxe Municipality
and is it possible to share waste between the enterprises?
• Which factors will influence the industrial symbiosis development?

2. Theoretical framework
This chapter will provide an exposition of the most relevant theories and concepts pertaining to

2.1
Sustainable Development Goals
In 2015 all members of the United Nations (UN) adopted the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable De-
velopment which is a blueprint for peace and prosperity for the planet and the people living on
it, both now and in the future (United Nations, 2022). The agenda is set to protect the planet
from degradation which includes sustainable consumption and production, sustainably managing its
natural resources and urgently act on climate change. By doing so the planet can support the needs
of the present and future generations (United Nations, 2022). Based on the agenda 17 Sustainable
Development Goals (SDGs) have been composed which acknowledge improving human lives while
protecting the environment. The challenge of climate change is global as well as local as cities has a
big responsibility in regard to the green transition. Four of the 17 SDGs are highlighted as being
the most relevant to the aim of this thesis. These are:
SDG 11 Sustainable cities and communities
When we reach 2050, approximately 70% of the worlds population will be living in cities, thus
creating a need for them to be sustainable more than ever for the sake of the planet. In collaboration
with different businesses, there is a need to find specific infrastructures, technologies, services and
financing solutions, together with an overall strategy that will support the optimization of urban
systems to create inclusive, safe, sustainable and disaster resilient cities, which is the purpose of
SDG 11 (United Nations, 2022).
SDG 12 Responsible consumption and production
Along with an increasing population, the economy will also grow making more people join the middle
class over the next approximately 20 years (United Nations, 2022). For the individual person this
development is positive, it is however a big constraint on the Earth’s natural resources. Therefore,
societies around the world needs to find a way to meet the individuals needs and ambitions without
compromising the needs of future generations and the limits of the planet (United Nations, 2022).
SDG 12 ensures sustainable consumption and production patterns for all. This is especially lifestyles
in industrialized societies. In collaborations with businesses there is a need to find solutions that
implement and facilitate sustainable consumption and production both within the product life cycle
and the use of them.

SDG 13 Climate action
Today, climate change is inevitable and caused by human activities around the world. CO2 and other
GHG emissions is a big factor contributing to the changing climate. The goal of the United Nations
Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) is to seek efforts to limit the global increase
in temperature to 1.5◦C above pre-industrial levels (United Nations, 2022). To do so, the world must
reconstruct its energy network, industries, transport, food and forestry systems. The enterprises
play a big role, and they can contribute to SDG 13 by taking urgent action to combat climate
change and its impacts, by decarbonizing their activities throughout the supply chain, production
and consumption (United Nations, 2022).
SDG 17 Partnerships for the goals
SDG 17 strives to strengthen the means of implementation and modernize the global partnership for
sustainable development (United Nations, 2022). This is among other done by multi-stakeholder
partnerships that organize and share knowledge, competences, technology and financial resources to
reach the SDG for all countries, especially the developing ones. If businesses are cooperating on
reaching the SDGs there would likely be a higher potential for success.
2.2
Circular economy
Today, the world economy is experiencing a progressive linear take-make-consume-dispose culture.
Resources and materials are extracted from the Earth, processed into products and at some point
thrown away as waste when it no longer has a value to the consumer. This culture puts a huge
pressure on the planet as we have difficulties handling the large amounts of waste that Earth’s 7.7
billion inhabitants are creating. The world is however now realizing and acknowledging this problem
and seeking for solutions. In 1987, the World Commission on Environment and Development made
a report called ”Our Common Future” which is guiding principles for sustainable developments, the
document came to be known as the ”Brundtland Report” after the commissions chairman. The
report is based on the three fundamental pillars of sustainability: social, economic and environmental
(World Commission on Environment and Development, 1987). The social pillar involves enterprises
upholding norms and values that encourage legitimacy and respect for the right of the individual.
The economic pillar involves enterprises’ competences to develop and endorse a responsible economy,
where profit goes hand in hand with collaboration on sustainability strategies. The environmental
pillar is the one that has the biggest focus in this project and it involves protecting the Earth by
minimizing risks and impacts on the environment from enterprises’ activities (World Commission on
Environment and Development, 1987).
Overall, there is a significant call for a transition from a linear economy to a circular economy, where
as many materials as possible are put back into the loop, and waste is greatly minimized (Ellen
MacArthur Foundation, 2019). Circular economy is a solution framework that handles different
challenges like diversity loss, climate change, pollution and waste. Circular economy is about
decoupling economic growth and activities from CO2 emissions and consumption of finite resources.
Figure 2.1 shows a scenario with circular economy actions over time, and the relationship between
economic growth, human wealth and population growth, CO2 emissions and extraction of raw
materials. It shows how this relationship will be decoupled from each other concurrently with an
increased focus on circular economy and sustainable actions.
Page 4 of 108
==PAGE:18==
Figure 2.1: A future scenario when following the principles of circular economy, that shows the
relationship between economic growth, CO2 emissions and consumption of finite
resources over time (Lendager Group, 2018)
There are three phases of decoupling. The first phase is ’no decoupling’, which takes place when
both the growth rate of resource use and impacts are lower than the economic growth. The next
phase is called ’relative decoupling’ and is when raw material extraction and GHG emissions grow
less rapidly than economic growth. The last phase is called ’absolute decoupling’ and is the ideal
phase from an environmental perspective. Here, extraction of raw materials and GHG emissions will
be declining while economic growth will continue to rise.
In addition, there are two different types: resource decoupling and impact decoupling. Resource
decoupling refers to reducing the use of virgin materials per unit of economic growth. Similarly,
impact decoupling reaches negative environmental impacts are reduced per unit of economic output.
The Ellen MacArthur Foundation was one of the first pioneers when it came to developing and
promoting Circular Economy. Figure 2.2 is a visualization of circular economy made by Ellen
MacArthur Foundation (2019), and is also known as the Butterfly diagram. The diagram provides
an overview of the flow of materials in the economy. There are two main cycles: the biological
(illustrated with green lines) and the technical (illustrated with blue lines). The left part of the
diagram involves preserving natural capital where the renewable resources are balanced and the
finite resources are promoted. The right part is about increasing the functionality of products,
components, and materials as well as keeping them circulating in the economy to the capacity limit.
Finally, there is a need to develop efficient systems that reduce the volume of waste that ends in
landfills (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019).

==PAGE:19==
Figure 2.2: Butterfly diagram: a visualization of circular economy (Ellen MacArthur Foundation,
2019)
When referring to recycling of products/materials, a distinction is made between two types of
recycling: open-loop and closed-loop recycling. Open-loop recycling relates to all recycling processes,
which are converted into both new products and at the same time generate waste. An example of an
open-loop recycling process is plastic bottles, which are converted into other products, where the
lid or label is most often discarded because it is made of a different type of material. Closed-loop
recycling is a process in which products are recycled back to the same product or a similar product
with the same value, without significant waste production/material loss. An example of a closed-loop
recycling process is aluminum can recycling, as they can be recycled to new cans with little/no
material loss (General Kinematics, 2022a).
In regards to implementing circular economy in an enterprise, it is crucial to examine their supply
chains. The challenge of closing material loops and reproducing natural resources relates to the
complexity of the products and the length of the supply chains (MacArthur et al., 2013). Global
supply chains account for roughly 80 % of all carbon emissions (Montag et al., 2021) which is why
they are among the key unit of action.
Handling of waste is important for the individual person as well as enterprises, especially the ones
that have a production as they will often generate larger amounts of energy and materials. Focusing
on circular economy within an enterprise will capture new forms of value, build resilience and achieve
social and environmental targets (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019). One way to optimize circular
economy and have as many materials as possible reentering into the loop is to implement industrial
symbioses between selected enterprises that can help each other reuse and recycle materials.

2.3
Resource Management
Today, one third of all waste in Denmark is sent for incineration, which makes Denmark one of the
top countries in Europe that incinerates the most waste. On a daily basis, valuable materials flow
into cities and businesses and eventually end up as waste and this is not sustainable in the long run,
as many of those resources are scarce e.g. minerals, fuels, plants soil etc. (National Geographic, 2011).
From a city perspective, there must be a greater focus in relation to creating a society that takes
this into account and focuses on regeneration and strives for net zero waste ambitions. According
to the UN, the world has already used up the raw materials available every year in August, which
means that every year we recover resources from the stock of the future (Earth Overshoot Day, 2022).
The EU Waste Framework Directive has introduced a so-called ”waste hierarchy” which, in order of
priority, lists the most preferred actions in relation to waste management, and the different end-of-life
options, which describe the phase when a product is classified as waste. Waste prevention is the
most important option, and therefore at the top of the reverse pyramid, as seen in Figure 2.3. Then
comes reuse, recycling, energy recovery/incineration and finally disposal/landfill as the very last
resort (European Commission, 2021).
Figure 2.3: Waste hierarchy with prioritization of waste management actions going from reduction
of materials as the best solution and ending up with deposit as the worst solution
(European Commission, 2021)
The purpose of the prioritization is to try to keep waste management in the upper part of the
hierarchy, which is most resource-efficient and environmentally friendly. The waste hierarchy is an
important tool for showing what to strive for, but it cannot stand alone, and in most cases it will
require more in-depth analyzes of the individual cases. A Life Cycle Analysis (LCA) can be a tool
to determine the best option to handle waste in specific cases based on emissions linked to the life
cycle such as raw materiel extraction, manufacturing and transportation, use and end of life of a
product/service. In many LCA methodologies the potential global warming or greenhouse effects
is quantified using global warming potentials (GWP) is often expressed as CO2 eq. (equivalents)
(Environmental Protection Agency, 2005). A LCCA (Life Cycle Cost Analysis) is also a useful tool
to analyze the overall cost impacts for a project. This helps to select the plan that ensures that the
facility will provide the lowest cost along with good quality and function (Whole Building Design
Guide, 2016).
Another important concept in resource management and circular economy is cradle-to-cradle, which
is a concept that refers to a product being reused/recycled, thus ”closing the loop”. In contrast
Page 7 of 108
==PAGE:21==
to cradle-to-gate, which is the linear form of consumption and disposal where products end up as
waste. These concepts are largely related to the waste hierarchy of Figure 2.3, with cradle-to-cradle
operating on the top two levels after the reduction step, and cradle-to-gate operating on the bottom
two levels.
When examining resource management, it is also important to look at GHG emissions for enterprises
and their environmental impact. According to the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard, an enterprises’
actions are divided into three scopes. Scope 1 is related to the direct emissions from an enterprise,
which are the result of their activities, that they are in control of themselves, e.g. fuel combustion
from company facilities and company vehicles etc. Scope 2 relates to the indirect emissions associated
with energy consumption from a utility provider such as their purchase of energy of electricity,
steam, heat/cooling etc. Finally, there is scope 3, which is the most interesting for this project.
This relates to all indirect emissions and is also known as the ”holy grail” of emissions. These
emissions are found in the value chain of the specific enterprise and includes both upstream and
downstream emissions e.g. emissions associated with waste generation, waste management, trans-
portation etc. (Plan A, 2020). According to Greenhouse Gas Protocol (2022) reporting of scope 1 and
2 are mandatory, whereas scope 3 is voluntary and at the same time also the most difficult to monitor.
There is also a focus on resource depletion, which is a term for when resources are depleted faster
than they can naturally be regenerated. This is due to several factors such as an increased population
and an increased prosperity leading to an over-consumption of natural resources, together with
improved technologies which has made it possible to utilize more of the Earth’s resources. In this
connection, the term ’Earth Overshoot Day’ is used, which is the day of the year when the Earth’s
resources, which can naturally be restored in one year, have been exhausted. Thus, it is an expression
of when the population’s demand for natural resources exceeds the amount that the Earth can
regenerate. The Global Overshoot Days is July 29th, which means that the Earth’s population
consumes 74% more resources than the Earth can regenerate in one year. An Overshoot Day has
been calculated for each country, which is calculated based on what day their resources are depleted,
if all of humanity had a consumption to the same extent as the population of the given country.
Denmark’s Overshoot Day is at the end of March, with a 14th place and uses significantly more
resources per citizen compared to other EU countries (Earth Overshoot Day, 2022).
2.4
The Industrial Symbiosis
Industrial ecology is the general term for the study of material and energy flows through industrial
systems (Frosch and Gallopoulos, 1989). In Figure 2.4, the broad definition of industrial ecology
is explained and put into perspective in relation to its connection to industrial symbiosis, which is
the main focus in this project. The concept of industrial ecology is included under the main term
sustainability, and can be divided into three levels: micro level (enterprises and consumers), meso
level (inter-firm, local ecosystems and industrial networks) and macro level (cities, nations and EU).
Page 8 of 108
==PAGE:22==
Figure 2.4: An illustration of the definition of industrial ecology and its connection to industrial
symbiosis. Modified from Chertow (2000)
In biology, a symbiosis is defined as: ”a symbiotic relationship between individuals of different species
in which both individuals benefit from the association” (Biology Online, 2022).
The word industrial symbiosis is thereby inspired by this biological process, as the purpose is also to
create a place-based symbiotic relationship between two or more actors. The definition of industrial
symbiosis is: ”the process by which wastes or by-products of an industry/industrial process become
the raw materials for another” (Trokanas et al., 2014). By working together, the enterprises thus
gain collective benefits that can potentially be higher than they would have been if the enterprises
acted alone. These kinds of collaboration can also help promote social values.
Industrial symbiosis refers to a network of two or more enterprises, where one’s waste or by-product
becomes another’s resource. Industrial symbiosis has both economic and environmental benefits, as it
creates circular flows of underused resources (e.g., materials, energy and water) between enterprises
and thereby make opportunities for the industrial sector to save money, reduce consumption and the
demand for raw materials, thus minimizing its ecological footprint. It is an approach which aim to
increase resource productivity, green growth and move towards a circular economic society (Mikkola
and Teräs, 2022).
Industrial symbiosis can be formed in two ways: a top-down approach where the motivation is
coming from the municipality or another influential stakeholder, or it can be developed from a
bottom-up approach where enterprises start to cooperate in symbiotic networks (Fraccascia and
Giannoccaro, 2020). An example of an industrial symbiosis that is designed based on a top-down
approach is eco-industrial parks. An eco-industrial park is a community of enterprises located in
the same area, which together want to optimize environmental, economic and social aspects, just
like industrial symbiosis. The difference is that often eco-industrial parks are designed from scratch
so that everything can be planned to go well together, which can be an advantage over industrial
symbiosis, which is often created in already established industrial areas. Eco-industrial parks are
especially popular in China, where they have large areas and the finances to build industrial parks
from the beginning of the project and consider resource and energy sharing on before-hand, also in
terms of infrastructure between enterprises (Hong and Gasparatos, 2020). The definitions of the
various terms from the literature will be further discussed, differentiated and explained in chapter 3.
Self-organized industrial symbioses are examples of the bottom-up approach. A classic example
of a bottom-up approach is the world-famous and first working symbiosis in the world, namely
Kalundborg Symbiosis in Denmark from 1970s. Some manufacturing enterprises formed a network
Page 9 of 108
==PAGE:23==
where they had the opportunity to share resources such as water, energy and materials between
them. Some examples of the interconnections between the enterprises include reuse of waste water
and coolant water from Kalundborg Refinery to Kalundborg Forsyning. From here, Saint-Gobain
Gyproc reuses the steam from the power plant to the drying process of their gymsum boards (Shi,
2020). This will be further elaborated in chapter 3: Literature review.
One of the cornerstones of an industrial symbiosis is the exchange of resources between enterprises.
By observing several types of industrial symbiosis, Chertow (2000) has developed a classification of
five material exchanges, which contain spatial and organizational aspects:
Type 1
Through waste exchanges: this exchange can for example be seen in municipal recycling programs
(separation of glass, paper etc.). This is usually a one-way exchange and is generally focused at the
end-of-life stage.
Type 2
Within a facility, firm, or organization: this exchange is occurring inside the boundaries of an
organization rather than with a collection of outside parties. This can be on-site actions such as
waste reusing of materials, water treatment, heat recovery etc. It can also be by extending material
exchanges along the supply chain of a product within a specific organization. This could be when an
enterprise invests in other markets to utilize their own by-products, e.g. a sugar refinery include
alcohol production as a way to derive profit from their own waste.
Type 3
Among firms collocated in a defined eco-industrial park: as previous described, these are often “built
from scratch” or new developments but can also be retrofits of existing industrial area and mostly
seen in China and the US.
Type 4
Among local firms that are not collocated: this exchange is often seen in areas that were not originally
planned to be industrial parks, but the proximity of the enterprises made it possible to utilize each
other’s by-products. An example of this kind of exchange is the focus of this thesis and is seen in
areas such as Kalundborg and potentially also Gladsaxe Municipality.
Type 5
Among firms organized “virtually” across a broader region: there are probably very few enterprises
that will relocate to enter into an industrial symbiosis collaboration, as there are costs of moving and
a number of unknown factors. This type of exchange operates within larger distances at a regional
level, but is still location-based. This means that there are greater opportunities to find potential
by-products to share, as more enterprises can be included. The focus is more on the ”virtual” link
rather than the close location. However, there are also a lot of barriers in this kind of exchange, as the
logistics can both complicate the process, be more costly and be heavy in relation to the environment.
Page 10 of 108
==PAGE:24==
So overall, industrial symbiosis can take place at different levels, both locally and regionally. With
an increased distance between enterprises, the opportunities for sharing energy and water are limited,
as there is little or no economic benefit from transporting these streams over longer distances. It
will therefore be more efficient between enterprises in proximity to each other. Whereas material
by-products can often be shared over longer distances and still provide benefits both economically
and environmentally (Chertow, 2004).
There are different tools and approaches when establishing an industrial symbiosis. One of them is
called: Input-Output Matching (Chertow, 2004). This approach is about mapping energy, water and
materials of each enterprises’ input and output, and afterwards trying to match the enterprises based
on their input and waste. The other approach is called: Stakeholder Processes (Business-Based).
Industrial symbiosis can be an important tool for promoting the circular economy and can at the
same time contribute to improvements for some of the SDG targets, as previously described.

Waste management and legislation
This section involves how, some relevant selected waste fractions, is handled in Gladsaxe Municipality,
what kind of legislation there is concerning it, as well as the costs of disposing it.
2.6.1
Waste water
Waste water, whether its from households or the industry, most often has to be treated before being
discharged to other water environments. Depending on the industry, enterprises either produces
biological or non-biological waste water and sludge. The treatment of biological waste water is
executed in different steps and will often begin with transferring to an equalization tank where the pH
value is regulated and a mechanical treatment will begin (Krüger, 2022b). This treatment transfers
the water through filters that works as sieves where large particles are separated. Afterwards, the
water is biologically treated where nitrogen, phosphorous and organic material are removed from the
waste water. This happens with aerobic processes where oxygen is added to the water and bacteria
is used to eat the dissolved organic matter, and CO2 will be emitted through bubbles out of the
tanks. Depending on the composition of the waste water, anaerobic treatment can also be used
(Krüger, 2022b). Then, the waste water will undergo a chemical treatment where most metals are
removed by adding iron trichloride (FeCl3) where the iron will undergo sedimentation and can be
separated along with the rest of the sludge. When the sludge is removed the final product will be
the clean waste water that can be discharged to other water environments. During the treatment
process, biological sludge is produced. This is transported to a digester where it will decay over
20-25 days, and afterwords as much water as possible is removed from the sludge. Finally, the sludge
is either incinerated or used as fertilizer in agriculture (Vandetsvej, 2022).
The process of treating waste water either happens at a waste water treatment plant, or at the
enterprise itself if it generates large amounts of waste water. The enterprise will go through different
steps of the treatment process and finally end up with sludge that is transported to the incineration
plant. If the enterprise only produces non-biological sludge, it will most often be sent to incineration
and not utilized as fertilizer as can be done with the biological sludge. If it is profitable for the
enterprise, they can also invest in different technologies such as a treatment technology that will dry
the sludge, reducing the amount and improving the quality (Krüger, 2022a).
Waste water legislation
Commercial properties in Gladsaxe Municipality that produce waste water, has to pay a water fee
and water discharge fee and properties that are discharging particularly contaminated water can be
charged with an extra fee. Enterprises that are discharging waste water from the production will
need a sewage permit from the municipality (Gladsaxe Kommune, 2022d). According to Novafoss
A/S, which is the water provider for Gladsaxe Municipality, the price for water is 23.15 DKK per m3
and the price for waste water in 2020 is 30.2 DKK per m3 (Novafoss A/S, 2022). There are certain
requirements for the waste water in regards to clogging and corrosion of the sewage system and the
treatment plants processes (bacteria that degrade nutrients in the waste water can be inhibited by
other contaminants) (Gladsaxe Kommune, 2022d).

2.6.2
Materials
According to the statutory order of waste (affaldsbekendtgørelsen) it is the municipalities that
decide whether a material or substance can be deemed as waste, and whether it is: hazardous
waste, packaging waste, waste suitable for material recovery, suitable for incineration or suitable
for deposition (Miljøministeriet, 2021). Disposal of waste should happen in accordance with the
following waste hierarchy: reduction, reuse, recycling, recovery and disposal (Miljøministeriet, 2021).
Waste from households and enterprises in Gladsaxe Municipality has to be sorted into the following
fractions: general waste, food, paper, cardboard, plastic foils, hard plastic, glass, hazardous waste,
iron and metal, electronics, PVC, clinical hazardous waste, garden waste, construction waste and
soil. Some of the waste fractions will also have to be sorted further depending on the treatment
plant that receives and handles it (Gladsaxe Kommune, 2022b). Hazardous waste can be produced
by several different industries. It is called hazardous as it poses a substantial or potential threat,
e.g. to public health or the environment by being flammable, explosive, infectious, toxic, poisonous,
acidic or corrosive (Miljøministeriet, 2021). Non-hazardous wastes include all other wastes that do
not fit the definition of hazardous wastes.
Vestforbrænding handles waste from their 19 owner-municipalities, one of them being Gladsaxe Mu-
nicipality, and transform it into energy and district heating (Vestforbrænding, 2022). All enterprises
has to sort their waste where it is distinguished between reusable and non-reusable waste. The
non-reusable waste will be incinerated or be deposited. The price for incinerating the non-reusable
waste in Vestforbrænding is DKK 370 per ton in 2022 (Vestforbrænding, 2022).
When it comes to different types of waste, it is plastic that is the main source of fossil CO2 emissions
from waste incineration. This is because plastics are made of oil. To put this into perspective
Kostyantyn et al. (2019) made a report in 2019 stating that 420,000 tonnes of plastic was incinerated
in 2016 in Denmark, resulting in 960,000 tonnes of CO2 emitted into the atmosphere. This is the vast
majority of the 1,300,000 tonnes of CO2 equivalent associated with the Danish waste management
sector in all of 2016. However, this number is both including the Danish industry and households.
The amount of total waste from the Danish industry was in 2017, 1 million tonnes where 72 % of that
was recycled, 23 % incinerated and 5 % deposited (Miljøstyrelsen, 2019). According to Kostyantyn
et al. (2019) 52 % of the recovered plastic waste in Denmark is exported, while the remaining 48 %
is treated in Denmark.
Plastic waste in Gladsaxe Municipality is most often sorted at the enterprises and collected by a
third party, such as Marius Pedersen A/S, Cas cas group A/S etc., and driven to Vestforbrænding’s
delivery area (Gladsaxe Kommune, 2022b). The plastic is then reloaded and transported to the
sorting plant where different types of plastic are sorted. Afterwards, the separated plastics are pressed
into boxes for the different types: LDPE, PP, PE, PET etc. The different boxes are transported to
different treatment sites where it is cleaned and reduced to flakes that are then separated into colors.
Then the flakes are made into pellets which are used in the production of new plastics, that can be
remelted into for example new plastic bottles (Vestforbrænding, 2022).
Another common type of waste that is also handled at Vestforbrænding is wood. In 2021 they
handled around 31,500 tonnes of wood waste, that is being crushed and afterwords sent to a treatment
plant that is utilizing the wood waste for wood pellets and chipboards (Vestforbrænding, 2022).
In Denmark it is Kronospan A/S that receives 75 % of the clean wood waste that is collected
in Denmark and turn it into chipboards (Kronospan A/S, 2021). However, Vestforbrænding also
displaces parts of their wood waste to Germany (Teknologisk institut, 2019b).

from DTU even estimates that this figure to be even higher DAKOFA (2019).
Material management legislation
Enterprises and households have to follow the rules of sorting waste, recycling and disposal according
to the statutory order of waste (affaldsbekendtgørelsen) chapter 9, 10 and 11 (Gladsaxe Kommune,
2022b). These include ”waste producing enterprise’s waste handling”, ”special rules for hazardous
waste from enterprises” and ”professional constructors screening, mapping and reporting of construc-
tion waste” (Miljøministeriet, 2021). It is the enterprises’ own responsibility to sort their waste so
that the parts that are recyclable are being utilized. When waste is incinerated or deposited, the
enterprises pay a waste fee, and if materials are recycled the fee is reduced (Gladsaxe Kommune,
2022b). A private enterprise has to use a private waste transportation company that is registered in
the Energy Agency’s (Energistyrelsen) waste registry. The tax for landfills in Denmark is 887 DKK
for mixed waste (RenoNord, 2022)

3. Literature Review
This chapter covers an in-depth review of the existing literature of industrial symbiosis from different
case studies and practically organized symbioses. A lot of research and projects have been done on
industrial symbioses on a larger and smaller scale, and this chapter will touch on the most well-known
and relevant literature in the field within the scope of this thesis. This chapter is an essential part
of the research process, as it provides insight into the literature, knowledge and research in the
field, and a clear overview of where this thesis can contribute to existing research, or possibly fulfill
so-called gaps in the literature.
To find the relevant literature and research in the field, a pre-systematic literature review has
been made, which primarily used the following databases: DTU Findit, ResearchGate and Elsevier.
Various search techniques and keywords have been used to narrow down the field of literature, such
as: ”Industrial Symbiosis”, ”Eco-industrial parks”, ”Industrial Ecology” and ”Industrial Cluster”. In
addition, the literature has been sorted so only literature in Danish and English has been used. This
has been done, as cases from Denmark are especially relevant for this project, as many processes and
waste fraction could be similar, thus providing a lot of knowledge on the area. At the same time,
it is important to look at cases in other countries, as there may be new discoveries or they might
have used a different approach, which could be an inspiration for this project as well. Furthermore,
cases in different size scales and how well-established they are in the development process have been
included. Both newly started industrial symbiosis, ongoing and functioning symbioses are considered.
It will provide a realistic image of what barriers and opportunities one can face when establishing an
industrial symbiosis.
In the literature, there are several definitions of what an eco-industrial park is, as the term is used
interchangeably. In some literature it is defined as a ”community in a limited area”, where companies
utilize each other’s waste streams through symbiotic exchanges of by-products. This is fairly similar
to the definition of industrial symbiosis and is used interchangeably depending on the author. In
this thesis, the definition of an eco-industrial park is taken from Alhilali (2017): ”An earmarked
area for industrial use at a suitable site that ensures sustainability through the integration of social,
economic and environmental quality aspects into its siting, planning, operations, management and
decommissioning”. There are a lot of projects around the world with eco-industrial parks. China
and USA are especially up front, when it comes to projects regarding eco-industrial parks. However,
these are not included in the literature review, as the majority of their symbiosis is built from
scratch in large parks, where connecting as many enterprises as possible, by sharing resources, are
incorporated from the beginning (Yu et al., 2014). That is not the case for this project, and it is
therefore out of scope in relation to the purpose of this project. Furthermore, this is also seen for
industrial clusters/eco-clusters, which are also used extensively. In this thesis, the definition of an
industrial cluster is: ” A geographic concentration of interconnected firms, suppliers, and institutions
in a particular field. ” Which means that there is most often a recurring theme in a cluster, e.g.
Page 15 of 108
==PAGE:29==
that it is primarily about a specific material such as wood or glass, and then there are a lot of actors
who collaborate on this in different ways (Chertow and Park, 2019).
The purpose of this project is to map waste fractions and investigate the potential for industrial
symbiosis between enterprises in Gladsaxe Municipality. Therefore, the literature review will be
divided into different case studies, which have been inspiring and relevant to the project in Gladsaxe.
Therefore, a natural selection has been made in the literature, which has ended with the following
projects: Kalundborg Symbiosis, the Baltic Sea Project which includes several industrial symbiosis
projects, and Industrial Symbiosis North (Aalborg Industrial Symbiosis).
3.1
Baltic Sea Project
The Baltic Industrial Symbiosis project is an EU project funded by the Interreg Baltic Sea Region.
The project ran from 2019 to 2021, and aimed to promote and inspire industrial symbiosis in the
Baltic Sea area. The project involved the following partners, who will be further described in this
section: Kalundborg Symbiosis (Denmark), Paper Province (Sweden), Digipolis (Finland), Ressource
City (Denmark) and Thams Industrial Cluster (Norway) (Baltic Industrial Symbiosis, 2021).
3.1.1
Kalundborg Symbiosis
The world’s first established and most recognized industrial symbiosis is located in Kalundborg.
It is one of Denmark’s largest areas with industrial enterprises. The project with Kalundborg
Symbiosis has stretched over 50 years and is constantly evolving with new enterprises joining. The
first symbiosis connection was established in 1972, and consisted of a pipe between Statoil and Tissø
(Jacobsen, 2006). In the following decades, many enterprises have joined, and today Kalundborg
Symbiosis consists of 13 partner companies, who have created 25 internal streams between each
other, as seen on Figure 3.1.

Kalundborg Symbiosis is a world famous constellation and often referred to as a best practice
example of an industrial symbiosis (Baltic Industrial Symbiosis, 2021). The symbiosis is managed by
a secretariat in collaboration with the Board of Directors. In addition, the enterprises also have
influence as they are a member of an Advisory Board, which is involved in the further development

635,000 tonnes of CO2, 104.9 million DKK in socioeconomic savings, 180.1 million DKK in economic
savings and three million m3 of water annually (Baltic Industrial Symbiosis, 2021).
3.1.2
Paper Province
The Paper Province is an industrial symbiosis network for the pulp and paper industry located
in Värmland in Sweden. The motivation for starting Paper Province was an initiative between
Karlstad Municipality and some private and public stakeholders, which was later reorganized into an
economic association for private members. The symbiosis currently consists of 90 member enterprises,
including pulp and paper mills as well as chemical industry enterprises. The cluster includes both
large and small enterprises, which cover the entire value chain from paper and pulp production to
universities, municipalities and packaging companies. Although many of the members are in fact
competitors, they have nevertheless found an added value in collaborating on knowledge sharing and
bio-economy, which can ultimately be seen on the bottom line. Although there is a decrease in the
paper production worldwide, Paper Province has managed to have a growth rate three times higher
than the Swedish average over the past six years (Nordregio, 2022).

3.1.3
Digipolis
In 2013, in the northern Finland, an industrial symbiosis has been conducted in the Kemi-Tornio
region, where by-products and waste are shared. Here, they use residues consisting of branches from
silvicultural practices from forestry to bio-fuel production, as well as recovery of metals from various
types of steel and ferrochrome production. They are particularly focused on: shared use of service,
joint gathering and removal of waste and collective transport of goods and people (Iida Holck, 2022).
The stakeholders consist of companies in the forest and steel industry, service companies, research
institutions and municipal authorities.
3.1.4
Ressource City
Ressource City is an industrial symbiotic glass cluster located in Næstved in Denmark. The symbiosis
is based on glass waste, which is collected and sorted in the area. The annually amount of glass
waste is 125,000 tonnes, which is recycled into approximately 800 new glass bottles. By doing so,
they reduce the need for virgin materials and save around 18,000 tonnes of CO2 per year (Ressource
City, 2022). In addition to recycling glass waste, the glass factory, Holmegaard A/S, contributes
more than 90% of the district heating in the area called Fensmark. Furthermore, the glass dust that
cannot be recycled is used for insulation and road filling by the enterprise Dansk Celleglas ApS.
3.1.5
Thams Industrial Cluster
Thams Industrial Cluster is a community of 30 members, all of which focus on contributing to
increasing circular economy and contributing to green transition, as well as increasing companies’
competitiveness. The industrial symbiosis was built by Trøndelag County Council, who identified a
need for closer cooperation on resources between companies (Symbiosis Center Denmark, 2022). An
example of a symbiosis in the cluster is between a smelting works and an abattoir. The smelting
works have an engine for melting rocks that contain silicon, which uses electricity and results in lots
of surplus energy. In close proximity, an abattoir, that produces meat, is located. The hot water from
the smelting engine is used in the meat production to save electricity (Thans Industrial Cluster, 2022).
Per-Erik Sørås, who is the mastermind behind Thams Industrial Cluster, states: ”Industrial symbiosis
requires companies from various industries and several industrial symbiosis have consequently grown
successfully in areas where there is already established industry.” (Symbiosis Center Denmark, 2022)
3.2
Industrial Symbiosis North
Industrial Symbiosis North is located in Aalborg and consists of 17 actors including Aalborg Portland
A/S, Royal Greenland A/S, Fodercentralen Limfjorden, I/S RenoNord, the Port of Aalborg, etc.
The purpose of the network is to ensure an optimal framework for green ideas and solutions, resource
optimization and green infrastructure. It is run by the facilitating organization Green Hub Denmark
which helps to convey the story of the development of the symbiosis and helps to constantly create

has a symbiotic cooperation where the sand in Limfjorden is collected, to make space for ship entry,

but now a much more profitable solution, both environmentally and economically, is found. The
waste is transported to Fodercentralen where it is utilized for feed to minks (Industriel Symbiose
Nord, 2022). According to Industriel Symbiose Nord (2022), Aalborg industrial symbiosis has an

3.3
Findings
There is a big difference on how the projects were conducted and who facilitates them. The role
of the facilitator is extremely important as it is the backbone of whether the industrial symbiosis
survives after locating the potentials for resource sharing. Symbiosis Center Denmark (2022) has
published a guide to facilitators of industrial symbioses and clusters. The purpose of the guide is
to act as a knowledge-based approach through practical advice. The guide is based on knowledge
from experiences and different best practice examples from industrial symbioses and clusters in the
Baltic Sea area as described in the previous section. The case studies from the previous sections can
contribute to knowledge from their experiences in relation to understanding the different phases
when conducting an industrial symbiosis and which opportunities and barriers they may encounter
in this process.
Mapping of waste streams
According to Kari Poikela (2022) an important action when establishing an industrial symbiosis
is the mapping of waste streams from enterprises. This is the first step in the construction of an
industrial symbiosis to get an overview of the available resources in the area. A good example of
the importance of this is from a case study in Austria, when two researchers investigated an area
for the possibility of sharing resources. Hundreds of thousands of tons of materials were discovered
here, including gypsum, steel, wood, etc. (Chertow, 2007). Next step is then matchmaking of the
enterprises and, by going through these steps, trying to establish an industrial symbiosis. The
literature points out a lot of potential opportunities/drivers and challenges/barriers one will meet
along the way trying to set up resource synergies between enterprises.
Drivers
Various drivers for establishing an industrial symbiosis are seen in the literature. Ellen MacArthur
Foundation estimates that closing material loops in the EU could save up to $630 billion (Wormslev
et al., 2014b). Chertow (2007) mentions the biggest motivating factor as being a desire of profit and
competitiveness for the companies. Then she mentions other drivers such as social, environmental
and regulatory benefits, which can vary depending on the type and size of the enterprise and what
country they are located in. According to Symbiosis Center Denmark (2022) there are different
benefits that are important when constructing an industrial symbiosis which can be divided into
the three pillars of sustainability: environmental, economical and social. For the environmental
dimension it is: conserving virgin resources, reduction of CO2 emissions and other GHGs and
reduction in waste quantities. For the social dimension it is: creation of local jobs and potentially
a larger customer base for the local companies. For the economical dimension it is: reduced costs
for resources and waste disposal, an extra income from waste products, better resilience against
unstable resource prices and contribution to the SDGs (Symbiosis Center Denmark, 2022).
Another important benefit that industrial symbiosis can provide when companies come together to
share waste is that they are part of a local and flexible partnership. This could potentially also lead
to collaborations at several levels which will place the individual enterprise stronger than if they
were to get things through on their own. Such an ideal community can be difficult to set up, and it
thus requires a lot from the facilitating organization and communication with the enterprises. The
facilitator can contribute with technical knowledge, research and solutions. However, developing
and maintaining a strong network also requires an understanding of social aspects such as mutual
Page 19 of 108
==PAGE:33==
trust, good communication and familiarity among the members. Furthermore, it requires a strong
focus on innovation to make the symbiosis network survive, as well as to be attractive and skilled at
recruiting new companies to join the community (Symbiosis Center Denmark, 2022).
As mentioned earlier, from the literature it is generally seen that industrial symbioses differs a lot
depending on country, industry, enterprises, etc. According to Symbiosis Center Denmark (2022),
there is no perfect recipe for starting an industrial symbiosis. Therefore, the best strategy is to do
research on other symbiosis and get inspiration from their actions and accomplishments. Thus, it is
possible to learn from other symbiosis cases’ successes and opportunities, as well as their challenges
and the barriers they have encountered along the way. Symbiosis Center Denmark describes the role
of the facilitator organization as: ”As you will experience, the process is characterized by trial and
error, and requires that the facilitator has an entrepreneurial mindset, a proactive attitude, patience
in the process, work hard, believe in the idea, and, have a pinch of luck” (Symbiosis Center Denmark,
2022). Furthermore, a strategy might be to include enterprises that already prioritize a sustainable
agenda and actions for green transformation. They are more likely to have a positive attitude
towards entering into an industrial symbiotic collaboration. An important point from Symbiosis
Center Denmark is that small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) are more in need of help from a
facilitating organization because they often do not have the ability and resources for projects that
are outside their core business activities (Symbiosis Center Denmark, 2022).
Barriers
Although there are many measurable advantages of industrial symbiosis, it is limited how many ac-
tually become reality, which is due to the many barriers that the literature also touches on (Chertow,
2007). It is especially barriers regarding business development which relates to technical, operational,
financial, regulatory, organizational and behavioral challenges that arise when enterprises are working
across sectors. The technological barriers that enterprises might face are in situations where the
right technology might not be available for recycling specific waste products from their production
(Frelle-Petersen et al., 2013). Frelle-Petersen et al. (2013) gives an example of a case with Coloplast
A/S who are having large amounts of waste that goes to incineration and some less hazardous streams
that are not utilized. A workable solution has not yet been found, and therefore the waste ends up
in an incineration plant and as hazardous waste at Fortum A/S, respectively. So even though the
motivation has actually been present for Coloplast A/S, the technology has hindered practice in reality.
Another barrier may be the access to information and knowledge about industrial symbiosis and the
availability of resources, thus becoming aware of the potential and opportunities of entering resource
synergies with other companies. As well as ensuring clean, homogeneous and continuous flows of
waste, to ensure stable deliveries of materials (Frelle-Petersen et al., 2013).
According to Velenturf and Jensen (2016) another significant challenge is the social factor, which is of
great importance, as industrial symbiosis involves a cooperation between two or more enterprises. In
general, the literature on industrial symbiosis shows that trust and geographical proximity are some
of the key words for the development of a symbiosis network (Chertow and Ehrenfeld, 2012; Ashton
and Bain, 2012). Kalundborg Industrial Symbiosis is a good example of this. Their collaboration
started as a bottom-up development, as local companies in a geographical proximity worked together
on sharing resources. According to Ashton and Bain (2012) and Castiglione and Alfieri (2019),
the success was partly due to the motivation from the enterprises, but also to a large extent that
they were located close to each other and that they were already part of a small community that
has helped to create awareness and trust in each other. Another important reason why proximity
is important is in relation to the exchange of resources, as it can be both expensive in transport
Page 20 of 108
==PAGE:34==
costs and emissions over longer distances. Therefore, the value of the materials must often be
relatively high for it to be worthwhile to transport over longer distances. Whereas local areas have
the opportunity to share resources of lower value, while it still becomes a valuable sharing, as it
remains in a closed loop and is not discarded. A disadvantage of restricting an industrial symbiosis
to a limited local area is that the size and continuity of the waste streams might be limited, and
this is a crucial factor to consider when constructing an industrial symbiosis (Velenturf and Jensen,
2016).
Summary of findings
To sum up, the literature indicates that there are both many opportunities and benefits when
establishing an industrial symbiosis. It requires hard work, as many technical and non-technical
barriers might be encountered during the process. This requires a good facilitator organization,
which can keep the wheels turning and be innovative in creating new symbiosis. The benefits for

4.1
The good idea
The first step in an industrial symbiosis is getting the good idea. The idea behind this project
has evolved due to a high number of manufacturing enterprises in Gladsaxe Municipality. There
was also a wish from the municipality to map as many waste fractions as possible to gain an
even better understanding of the sustainability potential in the municipality and its enterprises.

4.4
Identifying potential enterprise connections and general guidelines
This part of the industrial symbiosis identified potential connections between two or more enterprises
where waste fractions from one enterprise can become a resource to another enterprise. This step
Page 25 of 108
==PAGE:39==
also involved the enterprises’ motivation, readiness and willingness to participate in an industrial
symbiosis. Mutual trust and shared ideologies between enterprises are among the key encourage-
ments in a symbiotic model (Symbiosis Center Denmark, 2022). There should as a minimum be
two participants to conduct an industrial symbiosis and it requires that the two enterprises can
be matched. The facilitating organization will facilitate the dialogue between enterprises so that
all possibilities for cooperation are considered. They will also assist in the evaluation of economic,
environmental, resource and organizational possibilities and barriers as well as make suggestions on
how to break down those barriers (Wormslev et al., 2014a).
However, since this project was time limited, such an agreement was conducted during this project,
thus the final step in this project will end here. However, it is desired that the execution of the
industrial symbiosis would be continued after this project.
An important factor for increased
industrial symbiosis activities is the development of long-term public support from the regional
authorities, or perhaps the government, for increasing sustainability and circular economy actions
such as industrial symbiosis (Baltic Industrial Symbiosis, 2021). Even though the final step of the
industrial symbiosis project will be this one, guidelines for the enterprises are conducted as a result
of the waste mapping and knowledge gained throughout the project. The guidelines will provide the
best possible solution for the enterprises’ waste fractions according to the waste hierarchy presented
in section 2.3. The guidelines are presented in chapter 6: Waste utilization potentials.
4.5
Evaluation, test and proof of concept
The fifth step is the evaluation of the industrial symbiosis concept, where the practicalities are assessed
and evaluated. This includes extraction of materials from one enterprise, as well as transportation
of them and the usage of materials from the recipient enterprise. There is a need to evaluate if
the practicalities of the industrial symbiosis are continuous, efficient, reliable and economically,
socially and environmentally valuable. The relevant legislation, fees, taxes and policies are also
taken into account in this step. Finally, the last part of this industrial symbiosis step is to create
a pre-commercial agreement between the partners in the industrial symbiosis (Baltic Industrial
Symbiosis, 2021).
4.6
The remaining steps
As explained earlier, the final step of this project conducting an industrial symbiosis is ”identifying
potential enterprise connections and general guidelines”. However, it is the hope and wish that
the project will continue for further research and development. Therefore, the remaining steps,
based on Baltic Industrial Symbiosis (2021), will briefly be explained in this section. The sixth
step is the ”sustainability assessment finalized” of the project, where the focus is to analyze the
impact on resources, the environment and the Sustainable Development Goals which are explained
in section 2.1. The seventh step is the ”system qualified” where it is made sure that the productions
are in accordance with obligations, standards and certifications.
The eighth step is ”partners
committed”, this is quite self explanatory as this is where the partners of the industrial symbiosis are
committing full scale to the project, and the enterprises are developing a common communication and
business strategy. The ninth and final step is the ”commercial production and resilient partnership”.
Here the production is ready to be commercial including a commercial agreement as well as the
industrial symbiosis and the exchange of resources generates a resilient partnership that includes a
dynamic network that will in time allow development for more symbiotic exchanges (Baltic Industrial
Symbiosis, 2021).

4.7
General limitations
When conducting this project, a number of limitations were made. First of all, the project is time
limited to five months which includes enterprise selection, interview processes with several companies,
data collection, literature review on other industrial symbiosis cases and finally a suggestion to symbio-
sis opportunities in Gladsaxe Municipality and guidelines for different waste fractions. Compared to
other cases of industrial symbioses both inside and outside of Denmark, five months is a short period
of time to conduct a symbiosis. In comparison, the Kalundborg Symbiosis had its founding year of
facilitating organization in 1996 (Baltic Industrial Symbiosis, 2021). Therefore, it is expected that the
industrial symbiosis project in Gladsaxe Municipality will need much more time to be fully carried out.
This project is also limited to be conducted within Gladsaxe Municipality, thus limiting the project
to a specific geographical scope. This is due to the high concentration of manufacturing enterprises
located in the business district in the municipality and their great interest in the SDGs, green
transition and resource efficiency.
The project is also limited to only investigate the material and water inputs, consumption and
outputs of the enterprises. When conducting an industrial symbiosis, the energy, heating and cooling
is also a big factor that could be interesting and important to include. However, the mapping of
energy among the enterprises in the municipality is an initiated process conducted by Rambøll A/S.
This is being done since the district heating network is expanding in the municipality making it
possible for more buildings to connect to it (Gladsaxe Fjernvarme, 2022).
Another limiting factor is that approximately half of the enterprises in the Gladsaxe Municipality
are advertisement protected. This means that they cannot be contacted directly by salespeople
or people with other inquiries. This will limit the industrial symbiosis in the municipality since a
potentially good partner for the industrial symbiosis cannot be identified and contacted. It is also a
limiting factor that some enterprises simply do not have the time and resources to participate in this
project. It requires time, resources and people to collect the needed data regarding waste quantities
and costs and to participate in the interviews. Unfortunately, this is the case for some enterprises in
the municipality.

6. Waste utilization potentials
This chapter contains descriptions and examples of how different waste fractions can be utilized.
There are different ways to utilize waste fractions from enterprises whether it is internally, by
involving and sharing with other enterprises, recycling it in another way, or if it is by utilizing it
as energy recovery. For each major waste fraction mapped in the selected enterprises in Gladsaxe
Municipality, a prioritized waste hierarchy is made with descriptions of each step in the hierarchy
related to the six largest waste fractions, that were discovered from chapter 5: Data collection: waste
water, sludge, wood, plastics, bio-waste and textiles. The enterprises that produces the specific
waste stream is mentioned under each section and it is evaluated what utilization method is best
and obtainable for that specific enterprise.
From the data collection it was discovered that almost all companies had waste water in larger or
smaller quantities. Waste water is a special type of waste fraction as it cannot be recycled, heat and
energy recovered nor deposited. Therefore, it is not included as a waste hierarchy in the following
sections, as the possibilities with waste water are few. The possibilities for waste water only include:
reduction of the amount generated, cleaning it and recycle it in an enterprises’ own production,
share it with other enterprises, or discharge it into the sewage system. It is recommended for all
enterprises with larger amounts of waste water, to investigate the opportunities for minimizing the
amount of it or recycle it, if possible, as the economic and environmental benefits are considerable
(The World Bank Group, 2022). Today, there are many technology opportunities for waste water
treatment on site. In a conversation with Krüger A/S and Alfa Laval A/S, both of which provide
water treatment technology solutions, it became clear that these are not too costly and relatively easy
to set up (Krüger Veolia, n.d.). However, this depends on each enterprises’ finances and willingness
to invest in such a technology. By recycling treated waste water it is possible to ease the pressure
on freshwater supply from groundwater reservoirs, as well as treatment plants (Micronics, 2022).
In addition, it is also possible to avoid waste water discharge fees, which in the long run will likely
pay off after investing in a water treatment technology. However, waste water is part of the sludge
utilization waste hierarchy, given that sludge arises as a by-product from waste water, thus it will
also be possible to separate the waste water and purify it. This will be further discussed in the
section regarding sludge utilization potentials.
Generally, there is a need to have a certain quantity of the specific waste fraction for it to make sense,
both environmentally and economically, to move upwards in the waste hierarchy. Different utilization
methods require different technologies, transportation, energy etc. Having a small quantity of a
waste stream might not be profitable to utilize in certain ways (Niras A/S, 2014).
Optimally, it would be preferable to make a LCA and LCCA for all scenarios of waste management
in order to assess which method pays off the most environmentally and economically (see description
of the methods in section 2.6). As these methods are time-consuming and require large and accurate
Page 59 of 108
==PAGE:73==
amounts of data on processes, quantities and costs, this has not been possible and is not included
in this project. However, it is supplemented with results from the literature, if available. Instead,
a waste hierarchy is set up for each of the five selected waste fractions: sludge, wood, plastics
(HDPE and LDPE), bio-waste and textiles. The different methods for handling the waste have
been assessed in terms of qualitative descriptions of the advantages and disadvantages regarding
the environment and financial costs in relation to the different levels in the waste hierarchies.
This has been further supported by available LCA results from the literature, which have some
limitations, as LCA is case specific, but these limitations will be mentioned in the context of the
specific case. Waste hierarchies are useful to give an overview of waste management methods
according to which step they belong to. The method also has its limitations in that the specific
environmental impacts are not quantified. Instead a qualitative description has been made of the
advantages and disadvantages of the environmental consequences as well as the associated economic
actions. In special cases, there may be higher consumption of materials, energy and transport during
an action in one of the upper steps in the waste hierarchy compared to what is exploited when
extracting virgin materials. As previously mentioned, transport is not taken into account in this
project, nor is it expected to be particularly significant as the geographical scope is within Glad-
saxe Municipality. However, in an ideal study, it would be relevant to look into transportation as well.
Both environmentally and economically, the best option in most cases is to minimize the amounts
of waste in enterprises. Conducting an industrial symbiosis relates to the next two levels of the
hierarchy: reuse and recycling. In some cases it can also be heat and energy recovery if there is
invested in a joint biogas plant, which is seen in Kalundborg Symbiosis, where the plant utilizes
leftover organic products from Novo Nordisk A/S and Novozymes A/S.
6.1
Non-biological sludge
In general, there are good opportunities for sludge in relation to trying to optimize the treatment
and utilization processes, or incubate it in an industrial symbiosis, if possible, as the economic and
environmental yield can be increased. Today, a lot of non-biological sludge is incinerated, dumped
into the sewer system or discarded as hazardous waste if it contains toxic components (DANVA,
2009). Therefore, there is an enormous potential to improve the waste management of this waste
fraction.
Non-biological sludge is generated in a variety of productions that uses water. Depending on the
type of sludge there are different ways to utilize it. In this project, the focus is on non-biological
sludge, since the largest amounts of sludge from the data collection consisted of paint sludge from
the paint manufacturers PPG and Beck & Jørgensen. The case for IRON Pump is slightly differ-
ent as the sludge contains different metals, and is therefore not included in the waste hierarchy,
but is explained separately at the end of this section. Sludge is a difficult waste fraction to de-
fine on a general level, as it is generated in enterprises with very different productions and water usage.
Non-biological sludge can vary a lot in water content, consistency and the amount of hazardous
substances, depending on what the water has been used for in production contexts. As mentioned in
section 5.1 most paint types are either oil-based or water-based and have different characteristics.
Paint sludge are mostly determined by the usage of the paint and the chemicals added, such as: pH
boosters, anti-foaming substances, biocides, ammonia etc. The different methods have been listed
and prioritized according to the actions in the waste hierarchy as seen on Figure 6.1 below.

Reduction and reuse of sludge
The top priority is reduction of sludge within the production. The enterprise should evaluate if it
is possible for them to reduce the amount they are generating. For PPG and Beck & Jørgensen,
this could e.g. be to reuse the waste water and sludge from the rinsed paint tanks into the next
production of the same paint color. As mentioned in section 5.1, this does however, depend on the
size of the production and how long it takes before the same color is produced again, as there can
be complications regarding microbial activity and decay. For IRON Pump, a solution could be to
purchase prefabricated goods that are made from material that would not dissolve when being tested
for leakage in the water basins, however, this will require studies on the various metals and has not
been possible to find data on in the literature. Otherwise, a technology that can extract the metals
from the water is needed. Such methods are already available, and a study done on sewage sludge
shows that the costs are less than sending sludge for incineration, if investments in the technology
Page 61 of 108
==PAGE:75==
have already been made. However, it should be noted that there may be differences in the handling
of biological sludge and non-biological sludge according to the extraction of metals. Another solution
could be to invest in a decantation technology which can dry the sludge, thus reducing the amount
and volume of the sludge. A decanter is a centrifugation technology that can separate solids from
two liquids e.g. separating water and oils from the sludge (Alfa Laval Nordic A/S, 2022). This
technology will also be useful in relation to the next step in the waste hierarchy, which is direct
recycling of sludge.
Recycling of sludge
Generally, there is limited research and studies reported on how paint sludge can be recycled
(Salihoglu and Salihoglu, 2016). But a LCA study made by Dunmade (2012) concludes that recycling
paint sludge has a markedly lower environmental footprint than depositing it as well as producing
the same quantity of virgin paint. There are different ways to recycle sludge from paint production.
One possibility is to use it as a filler in cement, which can either be used for road filling, mortar or
other types of concrete (Ahmad et al., 2022). New research shows that sludge, including paint sludge,
can be utilized in the production of mortar as an aggregate replacement (Avci et al., 2017). Mortar
is used as a binding material when building with brick, block, and stone to keep them together.
It is usually composed of cement, fine sands and lime. When producing mortar with sludge it is
important that the sludge is dried to a minimum of 90% before it can be used (DANVA, 2009).
The advantage of using sludge in mortar is that it can be used directly after being dried, and does
not require incineration, which is a level further down in the waste hierarchy below recycling. A
disadvantage is that the sludge has to be dried before utilization, thus investments must be made
in a technology such as decantation, as mentioned earlier. In addition, it also requires research in
investigating and developing the recipe for mortar, that contains paint sludge, in terms of physical
and chemical properties (Tavares et al., 2014).
Recovery of sludge
The dried sludge has a great heat value and can be used for energy production when it is incinerated.
The calorific value of sludge is approximately 12 GJ/tonnes dry matter (Niras A/S, 2014). When
sludge is incinerated, the energy it produces will be converted to heat for the district heating system.
This could result in CO2 reductions as it can replace coal and other fossil fuels for the district
heating production DANVA (2009). Incineration of sludge is however, a relatively expensive method
of disposal due to the high water content if it is not dried (DANVA, 2009).
Disposal of sludge
Finally, landfill is the last solution and lowest priority for any type of waste, including sludge. It is
possible to deposit the sludge together with other waste types, it is however, difficult to handle due
to its consistency (DANVA, 2009). If the sludge is not dried before deposited for landfills it can
generate instability in the landfill slopes (O’kelly, 2005). However, depositing sludge to landfills does
only occur in the case where it is not possible to utilize the inorganic by-product from incineration
and/or oxidation. It is also a very expensive solution and the worst one for the environment.
6.1.1
PPG Coatings Denmark A/S
The biggest waste stream and cost for PPG is the sludge from the waste water with an expense of
224 thousand DKK. Today, PPG is having their sludge collected and incinerated by Vestforbrænding.
Therefore, there is a need to utilize the sludge in the most efficient way both environmentally and

detect the cost for investing in such a treatment technology, but as IRON Pump has large costs of
disposing of their sludge, it could be an option both economically and environmentally.

Recycling of wood
Wood waste that cannot be directly reused can be processed into a variety of products, such as
furniture or building materials. The wood can be reshaped by, for example, gluing larger pieces
together to fulfill a new function. However, for it to make sense to recycle wood waste into new
products like this, there has to be a demand for it. Other recycling uses are sawdust and wood
chips that are turned into wood pellets, Another solution is to turn wood waste into wood chips for
landscaping or trail stabilization.
In Denmark, a lot of wood waste is reconstructed into chipboards, which are made primarily of wood
waste and serve a lot of different purposes in the building industry (Niras A/S, 2015). As mentioned
in chapter 2: Theoretical framework section 2.6, Kronospan A/S is a Danish company that produces
chipboards with 90% recycled wood waste that is collected at recycling stations (Kronospan A/S,
2021).
Overall, the market for wood waste in Denmark is difficult. There are not many enterprises in
Denmark that can receive large quantities of wood waste which results in the waste being transported
to Germany, e.g. Vestforænding displaces parts of their wood waste here (Miljøstyrelsen and COWI
A/S, 2016). However, it is observed that many of the European markets, such as Germany, also
experiences an overflowing market of wood waste (Teknologisk institut, 2019b). In Denmark the only
enterprise that receives large quantities of wood waste is, as mentioned, Kronospan A/S for their
production of chipboards. They receive 75% of the clean wood waste that is collected in Denmark.
In regards to environmental effects, the difference between incineration and energy utilization of
wood, and production of chipboards with recycled wood is little, since the incineration of wood is
politically determined to be a CO2 neutral energy form, but this will be further discussed in the
next section. However, recycling of wood entails bigger environmental savings than incineration, in
regards to the use of fossil fuels and resources in general (Teknologisk institut, 2019b). It is critical
that the amount of wood waste is increasing and the enterprises that receives them cannot keep up,
as the market is full and possibly flooded. It is crucial that there will be more receivers of wood
waste and broader utilization options in the future (Teknologisk institut, 2019b). The supply of
wood waste to enterprises such as Kronospan A/S, has to meet the demand for chipboards.

Recovery of wood
The disposal of wood waste is often incineration, which is generally a cheap way of disposing wood
waste. However, there is a challenge when it comes to the environmental impact from potential
impregnated wood with hazardous substances in the incinerator (Teknologisk institut, 2019b). A
higher control of the sorted wood is therefore required. According to Teknologisk institut (2019b) it
is cheaper for the recycling stations to have the wood waste collected for chipboard production than
incineration due to incineration fees. There are many discussions on whether incineration of wood,
e.g. wood pellets, are a green fuel source (Bruun et al., 2019). Arguments for, is that wood does not
release CO2 from the fossil pool. In addition, it is a carbon neutral method as long as the trees are
replanted to absorb CO2. Arguments against are backed by a study from 2018 by Sterman in the
journal, Environmental Research Letters, shows that burning wood emits more CO2 emissions than
burning fossil fuels, as wood has a higher water content even when it is dried (Norton et al., 2019).
The study also showed that it can take up to 104 years (depending on the tree species) for new trees
to grow and absorb such an amount of CO2 so wood burning becomes a better option than fossil
fuels (Niller, 2021).
Disposal of wood
Finally, the last solution for wood waste is landfilling, which has the lowest economically and
environmentally gain (Niras A/S, 2015).
Impregnated wood is landfilled since the hazardous
substances are not decomposed by the incineration and remain as flue gas waste (Niras A/S, 2015).
When landfilling there is a risk of the substances contaminated the soil and groundwater and the full
potential of the wood waste is not utilized. Wood waste has a high degradable carbon content which
means that it will produce CH4 and CO2 when deposited (Pier and Kelly, 1997). Furthermore, it
can cost up to DKK 896/ton for depositing wood.

6.3
Plastics
Plastics are essential for modern day life and is represented, in one way or another, in every single
enterprise selected for this project. Denmark is in the top of 30 European countries when it comes
to sorting industrial plastic packaging with 64% sorted (Teknologisk Institut, 2019a). However, as
mentioned in chapter 2: Theoretical framework, 52 % of the recovered plastic waste is treated outside
of Denmark. Based on the mapped data for the companies in Gladsaxe Municipality, two types of
plastics were observed in particular, namely plastic wrap made of a soft plastic type called LDPE
(low density polyethylene), and plastic containers consisting of HDPE (high density polyethylene),
which is a type of hard plastics.
HDPE is one of the most common materials used in manufacturing since it is much stronger than
other plastic types (Accel Polymers, 2020). Common use of industrial HDPE is storage boxes,
pallets, containers and drums, IBC-containers and utility pipes which are also the materials that
are identified in this project. Aside from being the most commonly used plastic, it is also the most
commonly recycled plastic (Accel Polymers, 2020). Due to the strong structure of the plastic it is
possible to reuse and recycle it safely for different purposes. This makes it a dependable, effective,
and an enduring product with many applications. Studies show that HDPE can be reused and
recycled for new containers at least ten times (Microdyne Plastics Inc, 2018).
LDPE is both a flexible and soft product to work with but it is also very strong and resilient which
is one of its keys forces. Compared to HDPE it is a relatively cheap plastic material that is good
at resisting moist and other solvents (Plastindustrien, 2022). LDPE is often used in wrapping
applications due to its transparency, flexibility and toughness. In this project it is identified as
plastic wrap, packaging and general coverings.

Recycling of plastics
Recycling of hard and soft plastics, respectively, both has advantages and disadvantages. Recycling
HDPE is both advantageous to the environment but also to the manufacturing facilities. Research
shows that it is less costly to produce a product with recycled HDPE than it is produce it from
new virgin materials (Accel Polymers, 2020). Bataineh (2020) made a LCA to quantify the overall
environmental performances of mechanical recycling of HDPE. The LCA showed that it is pos-
sible to achieve GHG emission savings between 25% and 75% when recycling HDPE (Bataineh, 2020).
When recycling plastics there will be less costs regarding extraction and production of virgin ma-
terials, and today the technology for sorting of plastic waste is mainly mechanical. The sorting
is first done based on the specific polymers, color etc. and afterwords it is cleaned for potential
impurities (Teknologisk Institut, 2019a). The cleaner the plastic sorting, the easier it is to recycle,
thus providing a better quality for new plastic products.
LDPE can also be recycled, but flexible products are more difficult to recycle than firm. Soft plastics
such as film and bags have a tendency to be contaminated with the item that it is packaging or
wrapping (Plastic Experts, 2022). The harder the LDPE form is the easier it is to recycle.
As mentioned in Figure 6.3, there are also several challenges when recycling plastics. First of all,
sorting it correctly into the right fractions is crucial. Second of all, there is a great challenge due to
the composed plastic materials/types since they can be difficult to separate (Teknologisk Institut,
2019a). This means that the recycling of the plastics is compromised by the additives and fillers
in it. These compounds gives the plastic its properties and appearance. The composition with
many additives in plastics make the mechanical sorting extremely difficult (Teknologisk Institut,
2019a). However, LDPE recycling machines are in place and they can granulate the plastic into
small pieces and afterwords into pellets (Plastic Experts, 2022). These pellets can be transformed
into new LDPE products.
Recovery of plastics
Pyrolysis is a process in which the collected plastic waste is degraded, usually without the addition
of any air or oxygen, into fuel (diesel) (Teknologisk Institut, 2019a). Whether the plastic is bound
for pyrolysis is evaluated through the environmental and economical consequences of the production
of fuel (Teknologisk Institut, 2019a). This evaluation determines whether the plastic should continue
to live on in the circular loop or utilized for fuel. By utilizing plastics for fuel, the ability to
recycle the plastics are lost which is why it should only be incinerated if it cannot be recycled
(Teknologisk Institut, 2019a). Several enterprises in Denmark are practicing pyrolysis of plastic waste.
Studies show that the plastic-derived diesel produced by pyrolysis is suitable for use in conventional
compression ignition engines (Mani et al., 2011). The smelted plastic from the pyrolysis can also be
utilized for new plastic products. However, the disadvantages of pyrolysis is the fact that it is an
energy-intense process that generates ashes that must be disposed of.
Incineration of plastic both has advantages and disadvantages. Plastics are made of hydrocarbons,
just like oil, which is more energy-dense than coal, thus in fact making it great for energy recovery
(National Geographic, 2019). This energy is used for heating and powering houses through the district
heating system. The incineration is also a fast and easy way of decreasing the quantity of plastics.
However, the main problems concerning incineration of plastics are the release of hazardous substances
such as dioxins but many modern plants does have filters to capture it (National Geographic, 2019).
Page 69 of 108
==PAGE:83==
Another disadvantage is that there is no conservation of the plastic resources when incinerating them.
Plastics are too valuable a resource, both in general and in an industrial symbiosis, to be incinerate.
Disposal of plastics
Landfill is, like for all other waste types, at the bottom of the waste hierarchy. Conventional plastics
degrade very slowly in landfills and it depends on the plastics properties and the landfill conditions
(DEFRA, 2011). Today, there is little knowledge of plastics contribution to GHG emissions from
landfills. However, a study has shown that polyethylene (which both HDPE and LDPE consist
of) does emit CH4, but is likely to be an insignificant component of the global CH4 budget (Royer
et al., 2018). A portion of the CH4 can be captured for energy recovery (DEFRA, 2011). Another
consequence of disposal of plastics is the potential of landfill leachates of microplastics (Silva et al.,
2021). However, this depends on the landfill conditions and methodologies adopted. Today, the
knowledge of the factors effecting the release of microplastics from landfills is scarce.

of food waste. A way to reuse food, that is not rotten and can be eaten directly, can e.g. be to hand out

results in a saving of 0.8-1.4 kg CO2-eq./kg avoidable food waste. The result from the LCA depends
on the content of the bio-waste, but in general the literature shows that reduction of bio-waste has a
significantly greater environmental saving than biogas and incineration.
Recycling of bio-waste
The most recognized and profitable way to recycle bio-waste is through composting. Composting
is considered recycling, as a new product is created from the bio-waste. Compost production is
rather easy and profitable to implement at different scales. Bio-waste consists of organic material
and nutrients which makes it suitable as fertilizer. Most bio-waste can be spread as fertilizer on
agricultural land in Denmark, the rest is being processed at an anerobic digestion plant or being
incinerated. When bio-waste is used as fertilizer the nutrient content is recycled which can improve
the quality of soils with sustainable and renewable fertilizers. Bio-waste contain nitrogen, potassium,
magnesium and sulfur which are very important nutrients to recycle back into the soil (DANVA,
2009). Phosphorous is an important nutrient for food production and since it is a scarce resource
that only can be extracted in a few places on Earth, it is crucial to recirculate back into the soil.
Some of these nutrients are added to the fields by commercial fertilizer where it would be a bigger
environmental benefit to recirculate them from bio-waste. When utilizing bio-waste for agricultural
purposes it has to meet the specific criteria and not containing hazardous pollutants and heavy
metals. Another advantage is that compost can often be made at the company level and small scale,
and therefore is easily accessible and a cheap investment (Bolwig et al., 2021).
Biogas using bio-waste
Biogas, also known as anaerobic digestion, is a combination of recycling and recovery, and is therefore
located in between the ”recycling” and ”recovery” section, as it is producing both energy and heat,
while the residual product from biogas production which is called digestate is a nutrient-rich soil
improver. This can be used directly on agricultural land as fertilizer, which provides nutrients to the
soil and crops. Digestate products will in most cases add profit and are thus both an environmentally
and economically efficient way of handling bio-waste (US EPA, 2022). Biogas is a cheap way of
handling the bio-waste, once the plant is built. There are many biogas plants in Denmark, thus
transporting bio-waste will not require large environmental costs.

Recovery of bio-waste
By incinerating bio-waste both electricity and heat are produced which can replace fossil fuels as
previously mentioned. However, by using other treatment technologies such as composting or biogas,
as mentioned above, the energy utilization can be significantly improved, while at the same time
recycling nutrients from the organic material.
The Waste and Resources Action Program made seven LCAs with different geographical scope for
example including Australia, USA and Norway. The following bio-waste treatment options were
compared: composting, anaerobic digestion, incineration, and landfill. The results from the seven
LCAs unanimously show that anaerobic digestion and composting are the best solutions when
considering Global Warming Potential (GWP) and depletion of natural resources. This is because
composting is rich in nitrogen and phosphorus and thus can substitute fertilizer. Composting is
therefore favorable when taking into account the eutrophication impact category. However, anaerobic
digestion performs better in depletion of natural resources, as it recovers energy depending on in
which country the study was conducted in relation to the energy mix, where substituted energy from
fossil fuels has greater benefits (The Waste and Resources Action Programme, 2022).
Disposal of bio-waste
Landfill are the poorest waste handling options for bio-waste treatment. Bio-waste is extremely bad
to put on landfills, as organic material generates methane, when it decomposes under oxygen-free
conditions, which is a potent greenhouse gas (The Waste and Resources Action Programme, 2022).

(Dansk Affaldsforening, 2022). Research shows that clothes that are directly reused and that replace
the production of new clothes have an energy saving of 15-30 times the energy that can be achieved
by incinerating the clothes (ARGO, 2022). When growing cotton, large amounts of pesticides and
water is used. In addition, the production of fibers such as polyester, acrylic nylon and others are
made by chemical processes, that is often based on coal, crude oil or natural gas. The fibers must
then be spun and woven before they can be used as textiles. Furthermore, chemicals are often used to
strengthen the fibers and dye the textiles. All these processes together are very energy intensive and
this is due to the large CO2 emissions (Dansk Affaldsforening, 2022). This point is also supported
by an LCA made by Johnsen (2021) shows that the environmental benefits of recycling, even in the
form of low quality products such as cloths and insulation, are significant in relation to the fact that
textile waste ends up being incinerated.
EU countries have decided that in 2025 they must sort textile waste. The market for textile waste is
complicated, as textiles are often composed of many types of materials, which can make it difficult
to recycle or transform into new fibers or materials. Therefore, there are not many companies that
have entered the market to receive discarded textiles. Today, the market is primarily driven by small
green businesses that are passionate about changing the way the textile waste is handled (WasteTech,
2021).

Reduction and reuse of textiles
Reducing textile waste at an industrial level can be difficult, as textiles are most often discarded
because they are damaged in some way. However, one can consider to explore the possibilities of
investing in good quality textiles such as working clothes, tablecloths, tableware, etc., to secure a
longer lifetime on the products. However, it is impossible to foresee if these may be ruined anyway
before they are being worn-out Johnsen (2021).
Reuse is the second-most valuable form of handling textile waste, as the textiles can be disposed
of without any kind of processing, which can be both economically and environmentally costly.
According to Watson et al. (2018), it is estimated that approximately 10% of the textiles in Danish
companies are reused or recycled based on a research done on mapping of textile flows in Denmark.
This can be due to several factors. Reuse of e.g. work wear and uniforms can be a challenge, as
there may be limited demand.
Actions in relation to reuse textiles can e.g. be donations to companies that can use the textiles
directly such as designers. It is often seen that clothes can be exported for direct reuse to relief
organizations outside the EU, but here it is no longer possible to keep track of the end-of-life of the
textiles, and research shows that most of these textiles end up being incinerated or landfilled when
it has served its time (Europa Parlamentet, 2022).
Recycling of textiles
If it is not possible to reduce or reuse the quantities of textiles, then recycling can be an option.
Here, the textiles can be sorted into fabric types and transformed into raw materials, which can
then be used to produce new textile products. This can e.g. be cloths for the industry, filling in
furniture seats, industrial felt, etc. Mats and carpets made from both natural and synthetic fibers
can be recycled into sports surfaces or insulation (DEFRA, 2011). Today, there is a great focus
on research to develop new technologies for unraveling textiles to be able to use the fibers in new
textiles. However, there is still lack of technologies that do not damage the fibers to such an extent
that it does not affect the quality of the new products. Research shows that clothes that are recycled
have an energy saving of 3 - 10 times the energy that can be achieved by incinerating the clothes
(ARGO, 2022). Sending 1 tonnes textile materials for reuse or recycling are on average expected to
save over 3 tonnes CO2 equivalent (DEFRA, 2011).
Page 77 of 108
==PAGE:91==
Recovery of textiles
Textiles that for some reason can neither be reused nor recycled end up being incinerated. This
includes wet and dirty textiles that can end up ruining the rest of the textile fraction by molding,
or it can be textiles that consist of too many other non-textile items that make the recycling
process too complicated Johnsen (2021). A challenge for textile waste is that there is not much
legislation/requirements in the area in relation to how to handle discarded textiles, and therefore a
lot of clothes end up being incinerated, because it is often the easiest and cheapest solution (Watson
et al., 2018). The environmental impact of incinerating textiles depends on whether the fibers are
natural or synthetic (mostly oil-based) (DEFRA, 2011). The natural fibers will replace fossil fuels in
the incineration plant. Research shows that the energy generated by recovering textiles is not as
high as the energy saved when reusing or recycling (DEFRA, 2011).
Disposal of textiles
In Denmark, it is not often that landfills are seen as an option in relation to textile waste. This only
happens in cases where the waste is very toxic. Textiles consisting of natural fibers will biodegrade
in a landfill, which produces CH4 emissions (DEFRA, 2011). Especially azo dyes can be a challenge
at landfills, as certain azo dyes under anaerobic conditions, which occurs at landfill, can split off
arylamines (Brüschweiler and Merlot, 2017). In addition, heavy metals in certain types of textiles,
which are primarily used as color pigments in the textiles, can also be released and end up in the
leachate during the decomposition processes at the landfill. All of these factors make landfill the
worst environmental solution for disposal of textiles.
6.5.1
Elis A/S
Elis A/S is the only company in this project that has large quantities of textile waste with an
amount of approximately 400 tonnes annually. As previously mentioned, more than 70 % of Elis’
discarded textiles are incinerated, with just under 30 % being reused or recycled in one way or another.
According to the Figure 6.5, it would be of great financial and environmental benefit for Elis to
invest time and research in finding collaborations that can purchase their large quantities of textiles.
As mentioned earlier, this can be difficult as the market for recipients of textile waste is almost
non-existent. A research of the competitors in the market, shows that their biggest competitor DFD
A/S (De Forenede Dampvaskerier A/S), which is also a laundry service, has entered their own value
chain and started producing designs of their discarded textiles and selling them. This would be
associated with high costs in terms of knowledge, designers, production facilities, marketing and in
general to enter a completely new market, but at the same time it could also be beneficial for Elis in
the long run, as they would have complete control over the end-of-life use. However, there are major
uncertainties associated with this strategy in that the fashion industry can be a difficult market to
navigate, as one must constantly follow the latest trends and stay relevant.

Waste management guidelines
This section provides sustainable guidelines for the enterprises interviewed in this project as well
as other enterprises that find it relevant to engage in more green opportunities for their business.
The guidelines are a tool developed based on the industrial symbiosis literature review, conducted
interviews with the eight enterprises and waste mapping in the municipality. The purpose of the
guidelines is for enterprises to get an overview of what sustainable measures and opportunities they
are able to take with the five selected waste fractions: sludge, wood, plastics, bio-waste and textiles.
As Gladsaxe Municipality is highly promoting green businesses and sustainable solutions, some of
the enterprises in the municipality are already well aware and progressive in this direction. These
guidelines will support enterprises’ actions in relation to waste management and the optimal way to
go if the enterprises want to maximize the outcome of their impacts on the environment primarily
focusing on the enterprises’ waste.
These guidelines should be used for manufacturing enterprises that have the selected types of waste
and want to handle these in the best possible way. A further development of guidelines will be
favorable by adding more waste fractions and a prioritized guide on how these should be handled
most optimally. However, there is a general trend towards the waste hierarchy’s structure with
reduction as the best solution and disposal as the worst.

6.6.1
The four guidelines steps:
In the following sections, each step in the guidelines will be described.
Step 1: Track emissions
This step is conducting scope 1, 2 and 3 calculations for the enterprise. It is important to investigate
which processes that are causing the largest GHG emissions and work towards creating sustainable
solutions in that area. As mentioned in chapter 2: Theoretical framework section 2.2, supply chains
account for 80 % of all carbon emissions worldwide. Therefore, it is crucial that enterprises, both
big and small, must perform an extensive GHG emission inventory to make sure that they are not
missing great opportunities to move in a sustainable direction (cf. section 2.3). GHG emission
accounting has not been the main focus in this project, but as it is a crucial part of an enterprises’
environmental impact it has been included in the guidelines. In addition, it is still extremely relevant
to the project as scope 3, which are the indirect emissions that the company does not control
itself, deals with waste generation in relation to waste sent to landfill as well as the extraction and
production of virgin materials, which will be further explained in the next section regarding supply
chains (Bernoville, 2020).
Step 2: Supply chain
Both in the literature and during this project, it has become clear that the choice of suppliers and
partners are extremely important and have a great influence on both emissions and resources. Many
enterprises often start by investigating their own operations, which is also important as it is tangible
and easier to take actions. However, it is still in the power of enterprises to influence the supply chain
since they can place requirements on e.g. what type of pallets they want to receive their materials on,
or which type of packaging (McKinsey, 2017). In addition, they can examine the suppliers’ processes
and control whether they have any certifications, which can be a benchmark for their green profile.
Environmental supply chain management involves mapping the supply chain and determining the
environmental impact of an enterprises’ product with use of a LCA tool. A benchmark is to examine
whether the suppliers live up to specific standards and certifications such as ISO14001, Nordic
Ecolabel, the Swan label etc. This requires manufacturers to comply with environmental laws and
make continuous environmental improvements (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2010). It
is important to place requirements on suppliers to investigate where the products are manufactured,
and to consider the possibility for local suppliers. Finally, enterprises can make collaborations with
their suppliers by, for example, have them collect packaging that can be reused when they deliver
new products.
Step 3: Map waste fractions
A crucial step in making an overview of an enterprises’ waste, is to map which waste fractions they
generate, as well as how large the quantities are and the costs related to the various waste fractions.
This is a comprehensive step, and it has been discovered in this project that there is a big difference
between whether enterprises have knowledge of their exact amounts of waste and where it ends up
once it has been discarded. The benefits of waste mapping are numerous. Mapping of waste fractions
can help enterprises determine hot spots and localize processes that can be optimized, reduce waste
and add value to the waste for it to be utilized as a resource. This generates both greater economic
and environmental benefits (Kurdve et al., 2017). The four actions regarding mapping of waste
fractions are described in details below.
Page 81 of 108
==PAGE:95==
Definition of steps/processes
The first action when creating an overview of an enterprises’ waste fractions, is to define the steps
and processes involved in the production. This means defining all processes that produce waste, e.g.
waste water, waste from production, packaging, damaged objects etc.
Identification of waste fractions
After creating an overview of steps and processes in the enterprise, the waste fractions must be
identified. This can be done by gathering the information and processes in a MFA or other kinds
of overview-creating tool that can keep track of all the waste fractions and from which process the
waste is produced.
Data collection on waste quantities and costs
Next, data must be collected in relation to quantities and prices for collection and treatment of each
of the waste fractions. This is important in relation to the next step, which involves examining the
largest amounts of waste, or the most costly, which often relate to the hazardous degree of the waste
and how expensive it is to treat or dispose of.
Identification of hot spots
By becoming aware of where the company is discarding the largest amounts of waste, it is possible to
identify the bottlenecks in relation to an enterprise’s waste management. By doing so, they become
aware of where they can begin to minimize the amounts of waste, thus take action in the upper level
of the waste hierarchy. Reducing waste both contributes to financial and environmental savings
at the enterprise from avoiding collection and treatment of the waste. There is great incentive
for enterprises to take action on this matter when looking at their financial costs regarding waste
management and what they pay/receive for getting rid of their waste. Here, it is possible to sort out
valuable waste fractions such as metals, plastic wrap and hard plastic etc. and get paid for specific
waste fractions.
Step 4: Waste Management
Waste management is the final step and also here the biggest outcome can be identified both in
terms of financial savings and by keeping the resources in circulation instead of discarding them.
These guidelines take the five examined waste fractions into account and the best waste handling
options according to the waste hierarchy. For further details on each of the handling potentials see
sections 6.1 - 6.5.

7. Discussion
This chapter will cover all of the reflections and considerations that has been made during this
project, as well as tying the general learnings of industrial symbioses from the literature review with
the collected data and interviews with the eight enterprises.
7.1
Limitations made for the project
As mentioned in chapter 4: Methodology, it is important to limit this project as it could have gone
into many different directions. The basis of this project was that Gladsaxe Municipality wanted
a general waste mapping of all the enterprises in the municipality. As there are more than 1,000
enterprises in the municipality, this would have been a comprehensive project, which in the end would
only have been on an explanatory level if the entire municipality’s waste fractions were quantified.
Therefore, as mentioned in chapter 4: Methodology section 4.7, the scope of the project has been
limited to larger manufacturing enterprises in Gladsaxe Municipality. The enterprises have been
contacted in collaboration with Gladsaxe Municipality and asked if they would participate in the
project. From the beginning, the purpose was to include many enterprises with a lot of different
waste types. This was done to avoid bias about which waste fractions each individual enterprise
discarded, thus no risk of excluding companies with greater potential in relation to entering an
industrial symbiosis than first assumed. Aside from waste mapping it has also been important to
investigate the enterprises in terms of green opportunities, readiness to be more sustainable and
resources in relation to investing in green solutions or collaborations.
Eight selected enterprises were interviewed where the five largest waste fractions were identified
and analyzed. All enterprises, except for Kjell Pedersen Enterprise A/S, sorted into more than five
different waste fractions but to focus the project, only the five largest fractions were selected for
further examinations. The examinations of the waste fractions were done based on literature, research,
conversations with relevant experts such as Stena Recycling A/S, Krüger A/S, Cas Cas Group A/S
etc., and supported by available LCA results on how to handle the specific waste fractions. The
management of the waste was prioritized according to the waste hierarchy with the advantages and
disadvantages of each utilization method is evaluated and listed in connection to the environmental
impacts. In addition, the economic advantages and disadvantages were also described, as in many
cases it will not be a realistic scenario to make large investments in environmental management if
it is not profitable for the enterprises. It has not been possible to obtain exact prices on all the
different waste management technology as they are often confidential due to competitiveness, and
therefore the cost tables for each waste hierarchy are made as a descriptive analysis with advantages
and disadvantages.

7.2
Industrial symbiosis
As mentioned in chapter 4: Methodology, there are different steps when conducting an industrial
symbiosis and no course of action is the same for each case. The aim of this project has been to
identify potential symbioses from selected enterprises in the municipality. Through the literature,
it became clear that it could be a major challenge to create industrial symbioses in an already
functioning business area, where the enterprises do not necessarily match in terms of their inputs
and outputs. According to Symbiosis Center Denmark (2022) the success of an industrial symbiosis
is most of all depended on luck and timing. The eight selected enterprises interviewed in this
project were first of all chosen because they are all generating products and potentially significant
amounts of waste, but also because they had time, interest and resources to participate in the project.
As mentioned in chapter 3: Literature review section 3.3, Kari Poikela (2022) addressed the
importance of mapping waste fractions from enterprises when establishing an industrial symbiosis.
The same applies to Gladsaxe Municipality. There was a need to gather information about what
types of waste the different enterprises had and, very importantly, how much. As described in
chapter 3: Literature review, there are already established industrial symbioses in Denmark. However,
as mentioned earlier, no course of action when conducting an industrial symbiosis is the same. The
methodology in this project is primarily based on knowledge gained from the literature review and
what actions other facilitators have done. In addition, the method is based on guidance and advice
from the Symbiosis Center Denmark. All these findings have contributed to the methodology of
the project, which, although not all cases are the same, can function as a recipe or guidelines for
approaching a municipality and creating potential industrial symbioses. The methodology used
in this project (literature review, performing interviews, waste mapping, green opportunities and
readiness evaluations of enterprises and the generated guidelines) has arisen on the basis of own
experiences and learning from other projects.
Barriers
Naturally, this project met some barriers along the way. This thesis created the foundation for
establishing potential symbioses in Gladsaxe Municipality, and DTU has in collaboration with
Gladsaxe Municipality functioned as the facilitating organization, were time was spent to familiarize
the enterprises with the concept of an industrial symbiosis and what data was required from them
for the project to get started.
A considerable barrier arose in the process of establishing contact with relevant enterprises and
getting them involved. A number of enterprises found the project exciting and relevant, however, due
to time pressure or not having the right people to participate, they declined. Others simply did not
want to participate due to the lack of interest. Out of the approximately 30 contacted enterprises
eight were interviewed and worked with. Had it been eight other enterprises, the project might have
taken another course of action. This is also clearly seen in the development of the project, as it was
depended on which companies would be involved and which inputs and outputs each of them had,
and the enterprises inputs and outputs had to match for potential symbiosis to be realized.
Once the waste mapping for the eight enterprises was conducted, it became clear that the largest
waste fractions that were not already reused or recycled were low quality waste such as single-use
pellets, sludge etc. It is the waste that enterprises either do not get any profit from, or waste
fractions, such as small combustibles, that they do not know where to dispose of elsewhere. Since
many of the clean and valuable waste fractions, such as metals, glass, cardboard etc., are disposed of
correctly (e.g. reuse or recycling) it leaves the low quality and non-valuable waste fractions to this
Page 84 of 108
==PAGE:98==
project. This is where another barrier is met as these fractions are significantly more difficult to
utilize either internally within the enterprise or with a neighboring enterprise.
After investigating the different waste fractions and determining what the best disposal solution
is for them, different technologies can be utilized for different wastes. This involves for example a
decantation technology for separating and drying paint sludge or a biotank or biogas facility for
organic waste fractions such as bio-waste. These technologies are however, in most cases, relatively
expensive for an enterprises to invest in. It will often require that the enterprise can see a profit on
the long run by utilizing these kinds of technologies, thus saving money on disposal and incineration,
for making these large investments. Yet, this is where another barrier is met, as in many cases it is
not enough that it is a better solution for the environment, it also has to be profitable for the enterprise.
As mentioned in chapter 3: Literature review, another barrier for enterprises can be lack of the
right recycling technology for certain waste types Frelle-Petersen et al. (2013). In addition, it was
also seen that a market for buyers of a specific waste fraction is necessary for it to make sense to
reuse/recycle the fraction in question. Otherwise it is neither an environmentally nor economically
profitable solution by reusing/recycling the waste, if it just accumulates and end up being incinerated
or deposited anyway. Both of these barriers affect Elis A/S in terms of finding purchasers for their
discarded textiles. This became clear after the interview with Elis. As of today, Elis only recycles
28.9 % of their textiles which is partly due to the lack of recycling technologies. As mentioned in
section 5.8, there is a need for more research on how to recycle different fibers such as silk, acrylic and
polymide. But also partly because there are not many purchasers of damaged textiles who either re-
pair, recycle or shred the fibers so that they can be recycled in other low-quality textiles such as cloths.
The same issue relates to wood waste: there are simply not enough technologies and uses for disposed
wood shavings or low-quality wood, e.g. single-use pellets. As mentioned in chapter 6: Waste
utilization potentials, the market for wood waste shavings, both in Denmark and many other places
in Europe, is not very large. Today, the primary use of wood waste shavings is for the production
of chipboards and there is a limited need for chipboards in general. This results in a need for new
technologies that can utilize wood waste to other valuable products, or more purchasers who can see
a business in recycling wood waste, but this will require a demand in such products.
The geographical scope of the project has also limited the possibilities for industrial symbiosis. On
the one hand, this project is conducted in a municipality which, first of all, has a relatively large geo-
graphical area with a lot of different types enterprises from different industries, sectors and purposes,
meaning that they produce very different products/services, thus waste fractions. On the other hand,
as mentioned in chapter 3: Literature review, a disadvantage of restricting an industrial symbiosis
to a limited local area can also be a limiting factor in terms of the size and continuity of the waste
streams. This is a crucial factor to consider when constructing an industrial symbiosis (Velenturf
and Jensen, 2016). In comparison with Kalundborg Symbiosis, the eight enterprises in Gladsaxe
Municipality did not fit as well in regards to match the enterprises’ inputs and outputs in relation to
utilization of waste. In Kalundborg it was local enterprises, in a geographical proximity, that worked
together on sharing resources. They were already part of a small community and were located close
enough together to exchange materials, energy and water. Gladsaxe Municipality basically has the
same prerequisites, as they are located in a business district also in a geographical proximity. In
addition, they have the opportunity to be part of the collaboration called Gladsaxe Business District,
which as mentioned earlier, is a community among companies that try to improve sustainability and
communication among members. In contrast to Kalundborg Symbiosis, Gladsaxe Municipality has a
number of challenges. The municipality has a lot of other institutions and operations such as road
systems, schools, buildings, train stations, green areas etc., that can become obstacles and generates

==PAGE:99==
complications to potentially arranging and organizing large pipelines and other ways of transporting
materials and water from enterprise to enterprise. This is explained in the five classifications (type
1-5) of material exchanges, as mentioned in chapter 2: Theoretical framework. Chertow (2000) states

Generally, when conducting an industrial symbiosis it has been a realization through the literature
review and conducted interviews with the enterprises that small and medium-sized enterprises are
more in need of help from a facilitating organization than the large ones. They often do not have
the abilities and resources to join projects that are outside of their core business activities. Ferrosan

They were able to provide exact information on the environmental impacts from their production

relation to having their residual biomass transported from their enzyme production to their shared
biogas plant with Novo Nordisk A/S, which is part of Kalundborg Symbiosis.
As mentioned in chapter 3: Literature review, Chertow (2007) states that the biggest motivating
factor for participating in an industrial symbiosis and closing material loops is a desire of profit and
competitiveness for the enterprises. That is also what is discovered after interviewing the enterprises.
At the workshops with PPG, all of the waste fractions were listed with their related costs per year.
On this list, they clearly marked the few waste fractions that had the highest costs in regards to
disposal and collection (e.g. waste water and the related sludge). These fractions are the ones that
they are going to focus on in regards to finding better and more sustainable disposal solutions. In
general, a large focus on profit and costs in relation to waste was observed among the enterprises. It
was observed that the enterprises were generally good at sorting and storing valuable materials such
as metals, and to some extent plastics and cardboard, whereas unprofitable waste fractions most
often ended up in small combustibles. Most of the enterprises have already established agreements
with waste handling companies that take care of the valuable waste fractions such as metals and
plastic wrap. Some also have agreements to get waste collected free of charge, so it it not an expense,
e.g. PPG who have their IBC containers picked up from an enterprise in Norway.
An industrial symbiosis is highly dependent on cooperation and trust between enterprises. Therefore,
when two enterprises that produces the same type of product, and are competitors, it can be
challenging to be trustful in the the collaboration. There are many benefits to collaborate on waste
with enterprises that have similar waste fractions. They can, for instance, invest in a common
treatment facility, recycling technology etc. Such a solution could be an option between PPG and
Beck & Jørgensen, as they have the same type of waste water with paint residues in it. A solution
for the two painting enterprises could be to invest in a decantation technology for sludge. However,
that also comes with some complications as this technology would physically have to be located on
one of the enterprises ground. This would require the enterprises to have good cooperation and

Opportunities
After conducting the interviews of the eight enterprises and investigating how to handle the different
waste fractions according to the waste hierarchy, different opportunities for the enterprises arose. As
an industrial symbiosis between the eight enterprises in Gladsaxe Municipality is difficult, as none
of the enterprises waste fractions match the input of another enterprise, other important measures
can be made. From the waste mapping it became clear that there are large quantities of single-use
pallets that are being disposed of from almost all companies, and Kjell Pedersen Enterprise, who is
a carpenter’s workshop, had large amounts of wood shavings to be discarded.
A solution could also be to join forces on specific waste fractions in so-called clusters. Here, enter-
prises’ smaller quantities of a specific waste fraction can be combined into larger quantities and
thereby the recipient of the waste is ensured a continuous waste flow of an certain order of magnitude.
In Gladsaxe Municipality there is a potential to collect collective waste fractions since the enterprises
have some of the same waste fractions. This includes e.g. wood (single-use pallets), LDPE and
HDPE plastics and waste water. From the literature review (see chapter 3: Literature review),
several cases were discovered where companies worked together in a cluster focusing on a specific
type of waste. This is seen e.g. in Resource City, which is a symbiotic glass cluster. Here, all glass
waste from both households and industrial enterprise are collected and treated at the glass factory,
Holmegaard A/S. This is a clear example of a cluster that only functions due to the multiple actors
that contribute. Another slightly different example is from Paper Province in Sweden, where the
industrial symbiosis network relates to the pulp and paper industry, where residues from mills are
used for fossil-free bioplastics, fish feed and energy. In this cluster they have included the whole
forest value chain, where everything focuses on wood cutting, the production of pulp and paper, and
who can purchase or produce something from side-streams from these processes. In the same way,
Gladsaxe Municipality could focus on a type of waste and cooperation on e.g. wood waste or plastic,
so that the quantities that can be delivered become larger. There are a number of intermediaries
for the treatment of e.g. plastic and wastewater in Gladsaxe Municipality, but this will be further
discussed in section 7.5: Research gaps.
It is also important that the enterprises are evaluating the companies that are handling their waste
(such as Vestforbrænding, Cas Cas Group A/S, Stena Recycling A/S etc.). There are a lot of different
technologies out there for disposing different kind of waste and some are better than other. From the
conducted interviews, it has been noticed, that the enterprises often choose the easiest and cheapest
way of disposing their waste. This is where it is crucial to look for other opportunities with their
waste. In a conversation with Niels Nielsen from Holbæk Erhvervsforum, he states that one of the
biggest problems is the sorting phase and separating the materials to get as clean fractions as possible.
There is a number of intermediary enterprises that can assist in the correct treatment of wastes. An
intermediary enterprise that is also located in Gladsaxe Municipality is Krüger A/S that provides
technologies for drying sludge by decantation, as mentioned earlier. They have been contacted during
the project to provide information on their technologies for the enterprises that produce paint sludge.
PalleKoncept ApS also function as an intermediary, as they help restore broken single-use pallets and
EUR pallets, which have been found at almost every enterprise in this project. Stena Recycling A/S is
also an intermediary enterprise that has been consulted with during the project. They provide a vari-
ety of different waste handling services where recycling is their main focus. They were also interesting

7.3
Guidelines
The guidelines are an outcome of the overall gained knowledge from this project. The purpose of the
guidelines is to give the enterprises some guidance regarding the internal environmental opportunities
in the enterprise. It also involves what actions they can do themselves to improve their waste
management, environmental impacts and overall comply with the SDGs that Gladsaxe Municipality
are working with (cf. chapter 2: Theoretical framework section 2.1). The guidelines provide an
overview for manufacturing enterprises in terms of what sustainable opportunities and measures
they can implement.
The importance of scope 1, 2 and 3 calculations as well as supply chain considerations have been
highlighted during this project both from the literature and discussions with the enterprises involved.
Supply chain management is an important tool for an enterprise to influence the majority of the
emissions that are often indirectly associated with the enterprise through the supply chain. This also
complies with SDG 17 Partnerships for the goals, which is one of the four SDGs that are highlighted in
this thesis (cf. section 2.1). Supply chain covers all the activities related to producing and delivering a
product/service. It concerns all the actions from extraction of virgin materials to processing, produc-
tion and delivery of the final product. This is also illustrated by the butterfly diagram on Figure 2.2.
As previously mentioned, it is also expected that approximately 80 % of GHG emissions originate
from the supply chain. Putting demands on suppliers can in most cases be easier said than done.
Today, supply chains are increasingly involving more players from around the world, making it even
more difficult to keep track of and ensure a sustainable supply chain. Therefore, the guidelines clarify
how to get an overview of your supply chain, while keeping focus on the important actions to be taken.

The overall options with the developed guidelines are that it can help manufacturing enterprises
focus on the important actions when lowering their environmental impact, while providing tools
on how to deal with their waste management and the waste fractions from their production. The
guidelines have been determined in connection with with this project, thus the main focus is on waste
management as well as the largest waste fractions seen in the eight enterprises involved Gladsaxe
Municipality. Therefore, there are some limitations to the guidelines as they do not include all

can be used by all manufacturing enterprises will require an extensive study on waste management
and more types of wastes to be included. In addition, costs have not been taken into account in

require assistance of an external expert, whereas mapping waste internally within the enterprise
may be more affordable. However, for each waste fraction in relation to their handling options, cost
tables have been made for each waste fraction, in chapter 6: Waste utilization potentials, in relation
to advantages and disadvantages, which can help enterprises decide whether it will require excessive
costs in relation to their resources.
7.4
Waste hierarchy
The set-up of waste management potentials of the five different waste fractions has been manageable
and, in most cases, it also makes sense to follow the first steps in the waste hierarchy. However,
as previously mentioned, there may be cases where it can be an environmentally better action
with energy recovery rather than recycling. At the moment this concerns e.g. paint sludge, as the
recycling potential by turning the sludge into mortar is not fully explored. According to Van Ewijk
and Stegemann (2016) dematerialization of the economy, which is about reducing the pressure on
the earth’s resources and consuming fewer materials, can be made measurable and can serve as a
guide for managing input materials and output waste using waste hierarchies. In a waste hierarchy
setup, material flows are primarily taken into account, which are easier to measure in relation to
environmental impacts. In addition, it contributes to a reduced material consumption; which both
relates to minimization of raw materials and minimization of waste, if the top levels (reduce, reuse
and recycle) in waste hierarchy are followed. The use of waste hierarchy is a simplified method
of describing the usually complex connection between environment and economy (Van Ewijk and
Stegemann, 2016). Thus, this simplified method has some limitations, as it primarily focuses on
output waste and not input materials. It is implicitly included at the top level, which is to reduce
waste, but it is not the main focus, which is more on waste management when materials have already
ended up as waste. In addition, reuse, recycling and recovery do not result in total dematerilization,
as these actions still allow secondary material flows to increase. However, in this project, the waste
hierarchy has been made as a tool for manufacturing enterprises, which have great influence on
their own inputs and outputs, thus minimizing their amounts of waste by changing their production
methods, putting requirements on their suppliers, etc.
In this project, LCA results have been used backup the argumentation in relation to the waste
hierarchy and to support why the various actions are placed as they are done. In addition, the LCA
results also support the environmental advantages and disadvantages that are pointed out for each
potential utilization method. An LCA study from Cleary (2009) that examines municipal solid waste
management options and compares it in relation to the waste hierarchy in relation to determining
its validity. The result showed that the LCA tool in this specific case “tends to confirm” the waste
hierarchy actions Van Ewijk and Stegemann (2016). As previously mentioned, a comprehensive LCA
analysis would be preferable for each waste fraction in relation to the different handling options,
Page 89 of 108
==PAGE:103==
but as this method is time-consuming and requires large amounts of accurate data, and is very case
specific in terms of geography, waste management, energy mix etc. However, in this project, there
has been great focus on addressing this issue by quantitatively explaining the various possibilities as
well as the advantages and disadvantages of the waste management of the different waste fractions.
It has neither been possible to perform LCA for all utilization methods in this project, nor find
many relevant LCA in literature, which will be further discussed the next section, that examines the
discovered research gaps during this project.
As described in the chapter 2: Theoretical framework, there are different types of recycling, including
open-loop and closed-loop recycling, and the waste hierarchy does not take the different types into
account either. This may be relevant as closed-loop recycling maintains their value indefinitely as
it is recycled back to the same or similar product without deriving significant amounts of waste.
Whereas open-loop recycling is usually recycled for something with a purpose other than the original,
and in many cases may require more treatment and/or components that cannot be recycled, e.g.
labels, caps etc. Open-loop recycling is often referred to as down-cycling or reprocessing (General
Kinematics, 2022b). An advantage of the waste hierarchy is that it focuses on actions such as
reduction, reuse, recycling and recovery, so waste does not end up in landfills. In addition, it is a
powerful communicative tool for explaining to enterprises and citizens, what the environmentally
best options are. So if waste hierarchies are assisted by CO2 calculations or LCA, it will be an
almost complete guide and communication tool.
7.5
Literature and research gap
Conducting this project included an extensive literature review on industrial symbioses in Denmark
as well as other Baltic countries. After performing the interviews and waste mapping it became
clear that there are certain gaps or unexplored areas in this field. There is a limited amount of
literature and research on how to handle low quality waste fractions such as wood shavings/pellets,
paint sludge and textiles. This may be because the current technology is not fully developed to be
able to recycle these fractions. For example, this is seen for the paint sludge, which is identified
both in PPG and Beck & Jørgensen. Here there is limited literature on the use of this kind of
sludge, compared to other types of sludge (e.g. sewage, biological), as the paint sludge is a niche
by-product. Therefore, more knowledge and research is needed in the the specific fields so more
utilization methods can be explored, and more alternative solutions for e.g. drying and incinerating
paint sludge if it is not possible to reuse it in new production of paint.
Even though Symbiosis Center Denmark made a guide for facilitating an industrial symbiosis and a
screening tool to obtain relevant information from the enterprises involved, there is no comprehensive
guide to the execution as well as shared experiences from such processes. This project contributes
with know-how regarding the start-up of a project on industrial symbiosis in an already established
business district. It provides more data regarding different types of enterprises, what waste fractions
they have, and how they currently handle them. In addition, it gives an overview of the opportunities
and barriers from the literature and combines it with own experiences. Furthermore, the study
contributes to part of a larger mapping of the waste fractions in Gladsaxe Municipality.

8. Conclusion
The eight enterprises interviewed in this project are very different in terms of the products they
are providing as well as the waste they are generating. However, five large waste fractions were
identified from the waste mapping and interviews of the enterprises in Gladsaxe Municipality. The
initial aim of this project has been to identify potential symbioses from selected enterprises in the
municipality. However, conducting an industrial symbiosis in an established business district can
be a significant challenge for several reasons. First of all, none of the enterprises’ waste fractions
matched the input of another enterprise. Second of all, it was primarily low quality and non-valuable
waste fractions that were identified at the enterprises. Yet, there is great potential in improving the
waste management of these fractions, as it is often some of the same waste types in manufacturing
enterprises from e.g. packaging and storage. However, it requires further research and improvement
of technologies to be able to increase reuse/recycling of these fractions.
Since conducting the industrial symbiosis has been challenging, other important measures can be
made in regards to waste handling and recirculating materials. A solution for selected enterprises in
the municipality could also be to join forces on specific waste fractions in clusters, which can ensure
a continuous flow of a waste stream to the recipient of the waste. If a cluster is not possible, each
enterprise is able to get an overview, via the conducted guidelines, of what sustainable measures and
opportunities each enterprise is able to take with the five selected waste fractions: sludge, wood,
plastics, bio-waste and textiles. For each enterprise a recommendation is made based on their waste
fractions in regards to the waste hierarchy, e.g. new technologies to invest in, change of waste
handling companies or simply more sorting options. The guidelines are the optimal way to go if
an enterprises want to maximize the outcome of their impact on the environment according to the
enterprises’ waste, supply chain and overall emissions. Based on the conducted interviews, it can be
concluded that the choice of suppliers and partners is extremely important for an enterprise and has
a great influence on both emissions and resources.
Overall, this thesis functions as a recipe for approaching a municipality with the purpose of potentially
creating an industrial symbiosis. All of the findings from the literature review, conversations with
relevant experts on waste management, conducted interviews with manufacturing enterprises and
general waste mapping, have contributed to the methodology of the project. This project contributes
with know-how regarding the start-up of a project on industrial symbiosis in a well established
business district. It provides more data regarding different types of enterprises and their waste types,
how they currently handle them and how they can optimize their actions.
A conclusion from this thesis project is, that an industrial symbiosis is not necessarily the best
environmental and economical solution for enterprises. In the case of Gladsaxe Municipality, where
none of the eight enterprises can share resources, it is a better solution for them to follow the waste
management guidelines and optimize their recycling of resources. It can also be concluded that the
Page 91 of 108
==PAGE:105==
overall readiness to take action on sustainable development and green transition throughout the
eight enterprises are generally very high. This is partly due to the comprehensive work of Glad-
saxe Municipality involving enterprises in committees, sustainable groups and overall sustainable
development goals for the area. It is also due to the general societal pressure of having sustainable
products, a responsible consumption and recycling options. It can be concluded from this thesis, that
despite the best efforts from enterprises, big and small, a lot of recycling technologies are not fully
developed for low-quality waste fractions (e.g. for paint sludge and textile waste), and many sorted
waste fractions are continuously being exported to other countries. Extensive life cycle analyses on
different waste handling options, including transportation, as well as environmental supply chain
management and CO2 calculations, will be the best tools to support the waste hierarchies, thus

The establishment of an industrial symbiosis is still possible if further work is done on the project.
The guidelines are developed on the basis of the research during this project, and is a good tool for
detecting their environmental impacts and actions in order to locate their hot spots and focus their
resources as best as possible with a focus on waste management.

9. Recommendations for further research
This project has been the building foundation for an industrial symbiosis in Gladsaxe Municipality,
and served as a starting point for a further mapping and investigation of the possibility of potential
symbioses among selected enterprises. The project can continue in different directions, as it includes
many important aspects and many opportunities and challenges that have arisen along the way. As
previously mentioned, it has been important to clearly define the scope of the project to keep focus
on the overall purpose. This has also resulted in many opportunities to continue working on the
project.
First of all, there is the obvious continuation of the project that involves waste mapping more
manufacturing enterprises in the municipality. The municipality still has an ambition to get an
overview of the types and quantities of waste available in the municipality. In addition, it could
also be interesting to have more focus on enterprises with a need for input of the waste fractions
already seen among the selected enterprises such as wood, plastics, bio-waste, textiles and sludge.
As mentioned earlier, this project would probably have looked different if eight other enterprises had
been included. During the project it became clear that there are significant amounts of wood waste,
mostly in form of wood shavings and single-use pallets, that are not being utilized. For further
research it could be interesting to have a focus on a specific waste fraction, such as wood, which
was seen among the eight selected companies. This is especially in relation to the problem with
broken EUR pallets and single-use pallet, as well as including some enterprises that can use the
waste fraction in their inputs. This includes BAUHAUS A/S, Østerbro Tømmerhandel ApS and
Københavns Listefabrik as all of these enterprises have large amounts of wood, both as input and
discarded wood waste. By including more enterprises that are a ”better fit” for a potential industrial
symbiosis, it will be a more focused analysis. The possibilities to go further in the methodological
steps of the implementation of an industrial symbiosis as explained in chapter 4: Methodology,
section 4.6.
Aside from expanding the number of enterprises in the project to investigate possible symbiosis, it
could also be interesting to go even more in depth with the eight selected enterprises. As mentioned
in chapter 4: Methodology, energy use is not a part of this project. However, the inclusion of
energy would be interesting for the project, as Gladsaxe Municipality is currently having the district
heating system expanded. Several of the enterprises are producing surplus heat that could be
reentered into the district heating system, and will contribute with heat to the rest of the network.
In addition, Rambøll A/S is conducting an energy mapping, which examines the potential for
energy sharing between the enterprises in Gladsaxe Municipality. For example, there is a possi-
bility for PPG to utilize surplus heat from a data center, located on the other side of the road,
to heat their tanks when producing paint. That would be an example of a symbiotic exchange
between enterprises that produces and uses heat. However, this can also have some complications,
as energy exchange is locally determined, as it is challenging to exchange energy over longer distances.
Page 93 of 108
==PAGE:107==
Moreover, LCAs for each waste fraction would be a useful tool to support the waste hierarchies.
The LCAs would provide a clearer and more precise analysis of the waste management solutions in
regards to the overall environmental impacts. The recycling potential for each of the eight enterprises
could also be calculated. In chapter 5: Data collection, the total amount of waste for each enterprise
was divided into three categories: hazardous waste, non-hazardous waste and recycled waste. It could
also be interesting to determine the overall recycling potentials for the enterprises. This includes all
of the waste that is currently being incinerated but actually has a potential to be recycled, such as:
sludge, plastics, wood etc. This would require information about the content of small combustibles,
which can be difficult to determine.
Overall, this project has many interesting and relevant discussions and problems that are very
important to address. Re-circulation of materials back into the utilization loop and reducing the
demand for virgin raw materials, is crucial to decrease the global environmental impacts on our
Earth.
