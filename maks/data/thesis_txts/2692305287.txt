Abstract
This thesis aims to model and assess the nuclear heating in a MSFR reactor
and freeze-plug. A neutronics model was developed using Serpent 2 Monte
Carlo Code, and validated against previous studies. Burn-up calculations
were run to determine the depleted fuel inventory and long-term reactor
behaviour, as well as the eﬀects of fuel salt reprocessing. Finally, a new
energy deposition calculation mode in Serpent 2 was used to evaluate the
heating contributions in the reactor core and freeze-plug. The assessment
shows heating contribution consistent with previous studies, and that a
signiﬁcant amount of the neutron and photon energy is deposited in the
ﬁrst part of the freeze-plug.

Introduction
In 1932, British physicist James Chadwick conﬁrmed the existence of a
charge-neutral nuclear particle – the neutron – based on the reaction of α-
particles with beryllium atoms. Spurred by Chadwick’s discovery, Austrian
Lise Meitner and German Otto Hahn, while investigating the transmuting
properties of this new particle in 1938, ﬁrst observed the process of nuclear
ﬁssion [1, 2]. Four years later, in 1942, the ﬁrst man-made sustained nuclear
chain-reaction was conducted at the Chicago Pile-1 reactor in a squash
court of an unused stadium, signalling humankind’s new dominion over the
nuclear forces. Hereafter, the Second World War eﬀorts to weaponise the
atom led to great progress in understanding nuclear power, and in the 50s
and 60s the ﬁrst generation prototypes of nuclear power reactors were con-
structed — usually with the dual-purpose of production of ﬁssile isotopes
for nuclear weapons programmes. Some of these pioneering reactors were
the graphite-moderated Magnox, the heavy-water-moderated CANDU, and
the ﬁrst commercial power plant: the light-water-moderated Shippingport
Atomic Power Station.
The second generation of nuclear reactors followed in the 70s up through
the 90s, where the bulk of commercial plants were built. Still today, the pre-
dominant reactor design is the solid-fuelled, water-moderated Pressurized
Water Reactor (PWR) with the Boiling Water Reactor (BWR) a close second.
At the end of the 90s, evolutionary improvements to the existing reactors
coalesced into the third generation of advanced power reactors, mainly in
the form of enhanced safety features, standardised designs, and improved
eﬃciency [3].
At the beginning of the new millennium, a collection of countries formed
a symposium, the Generation IV International Forum (GIF), which set out to
create a framework for the co-ordination of research and development of
the future of nuclear power — selecting six reactor designs for furthering as
the to-be fourth generation of nuclear systems, seen in Figure 1.1 [4]. One
of the reactor designs selected is the molten salt reactor (MSR), based on the

1.1
The Molten Salt Fast Reactor
The ﬁrst studies into molten salt reactors was carried out at the aptly named
Molten Salt Reactor Experiment (MSRE), constructed at Oak Ridge National
Laboratory (ORNL) in the early 1960s [7]. The thermal research reactor used
a liquid ﬂuoride-based salt and was graphite moderated. The experiment
ran successfully for 4 years, proving the feasibility of liquid-fuelled reactors.
Research continued up through the 1970s with plans for a successor reactor,
the Molten Salt Breeder Reactor (MSBR), but eﬀorts stalled and it was never
built, due in part to a priority of liquid-metal fast-breeder reactors [8]. After
the shutdown of the MSBR project, research into MSRs was sporadic and
did not lead to construction of any additional reactors.
During the 2000s, interest in MSR designs renewed and international
collaborative eﬀorts coalesced. The Molten Salt Fast Reactor (MSFR) concept
was ﬁrst developed at CNRS in France and is one of the fast-spectrum
MSR designs studied by the GIF MSR committee [7]. The fast-spectrum
reactor has multiple advantages for nuclear sustainability such as breeding
capabilities, nuclear waste burning, and strong safety speciﬁcations [5, 9].
A MSFR reference system conﬁguration was developed as part of the
Evaluation and Viability of Liquid Fuel Fast Reactor Systems (EVOL) project

1.2
Freeze-plug
One important safety component of the MSFR concept is the freeze-plug, a
valve made from frozen fuel salt in a channel at the bottom of the reactor.
In case of an accidental rise in fuel temperature or loss of power, the frozen
salt plug is designed to melt, allowing the fuel salt to drain into safe storage
tanks where it will cool oﬀ. The system is required to work passively, and
thus must be able to melt from the decay heat of the reactor alone, yet not
melt during normal operations. The freeze-plug was ﬁrst studied and used
in the early MSRE, though for the purpose of a corrosion-resistant valve
mechanism for salt ﬂow control [12, 13]. The MSFR freeze-plug concept
was studied in the EU SAMOFAR project that ran from 2015 to 2019, and
continued in the current SAMOSAFER project [5, 14].

1.3
Objectives of Thesis
The main objective of this thesis project is to develop a neutronics model of
the MSFR using Serpent 2 Monte Carlo code, and use it to assess the nuclear
heating in the reactor core and freeze-plug, as well as how this is aﬀected by
depletion and reprocessing of fuel salt.
The main work of the thesis is divided into four parts. First, the Serpent 2
code is presented along with the MSFR model developed for the assessment.
In the second section, the created neutronics model is validated against
previous results and benchmarks. In the third section, model burn-up
calculations are performed and the evolution of reactor state and inventory
is explored. The fourth section introduces a new coupled neutron-photon
transport energy deposition mode of Serpent 2 and uses it to calculate the
nuclear heating contributions in the reactor core and in the freeze-plug.

Literature review
During the work of this thesis, two relevant papers concerning freeze-plugs
were read and presented. In this chapter, a brief review of the key concepts,
procedures, and results established in these papers are presented.
2.1
Oak Ridge Freeze-valve Experiment
Development of Freeze Valve for use in the MSRE [12]
M. Richardson (1962)
In the 1960s, a prototype nuclear reactor using molten salt was constructed
at ORNL. The researchers found themselves lacking a reliable mechanical
valve for the reactor testbed. An evaluation study was undertaken on the use
of freeze-plugs as valves to control the ﬂuid salt ﬂow, testing three diﬀerent
types of freeze-plug designs.
The initial part of the experiment was carried out using a vertical test setup
with an upper head tank and a lower sump tank, connected by the test valve.
The molten salt is moved between the tanks using a gas pressure diﬀerential
system. Using fanned room air on the valve, the salt can be frozen, forming
the freeze-plug and preventing ﬂow between the tanks. To reestablish ﬂow,
the plug can then be melted by heating the valve pipe. Three diﬀerent valve
heating methods were studied and compared: direct resistance heating,
induction heating, and Calrod-heaters clamped directly on the valve.
During the test, the salt was repeatedly frozen and melted for 100
cycles. All valve types performed without complications. Melting times
were recorded as function of the power input, with the Calrod-heated valve
having the fastest melting time per power input – an average of three
minutes for a 1.6 kW input.

Based on these initial results, the Calrod-heated design was selected for
further study in the MSRE Engineering Test Loop, a circulation setup used
in the test reactor, see Figure 2.1. In the setup, freeze-plugs were used to
control the ﬂow of salt from the operating system into either of two drain
tanks using a single pipeline. The freeze-plug valve was cycled 40 times
without issue. A blockage elsewhere in the loop prevented the opening
of the line but was ascertained not to be due to the freeze-plug design or
operation. Operational test showed the valve to function satisfactorily under
forced-drain conditions.
In conclusion, the experiments proved it possible to use freeze-plugs
for a molten salt valve system — in particular the MSRE. Calrod-based
heating control was determined to be most suitable due to its simplicity
and the design was successfully implemented and operated in the MSRE
Engineering Test Loop.

3.1
Basic Nuclear Physics and Neutronics
The atom consists of a positively-charged nucleus, composed typically of
both protons and neutrons, surrounded by a negatively-charged electron
orbital. Protons and neutrons are hadrons – or more speciﬁcally baryons –
held together by the strong nuclear force through the interaction of gluons.
It is the energy stored in this bond that holds the key to nuclear power.
3.1.1
Nuclear energetics
The total energy of a particle in motion is given by the relativistic mass-
energy relation
Etot = mc2 =

which includes the kinetic energy of the particle’s motion at velocity v and
its rest-mass energy m0.
In a reaction where nuclear particles interact to form some products,
the total momentum and energy of the initial particles are conserved, but
some of the energy can be converted into mass or some mass into energy.
The total change in mass then determines whether the reaction has a net
increase or decrease in kinetic energy – what is known as the Q-value of the
reaction:
Q = (Minit −Mﬁnal)c2 = ∆Mc2
(3.2)

reaction is the mass defect. When protons and neutrons bind together to
form nuclei, the bound nucleus has a lower energy state than its individual
constituents, resulting in a reduced rest-mass. This mass defect is also
the binding energy of the bound system, the energy holding together the

3.1.2
Neutron-matter interaction
In a nuclear reactor the ﬁssion process depends fundamentally on neutrons
and their interaction with elements in the reactor. Because of the neutrons
charge-neutrality, it is unaﬀected by the electric charges of orbital electrons
and protons, and can interact directly with the nucleus of an atom. Such
interaction can happen in several ways, often denoted by the binary reaction
notation (n,x):
Scattering: The neutron can scatter oﬀthe target nucleus either through
elastic scattering (n,n) leaving the nucleus in its ground state, or
through inelastic scattering (n,n′) where energy is transferred from
the incoming neutron to the nucleus leaving it in an excited state. The
excited nucleus quickly decays through γ-emission to its ground state.
Capture: The incident neutron can be absorbed by the nucleus in what
is called radiative capture, increasing the number of neutrons in the
nucleus and usually exciting it considerably, which then rapidly decays
by emitting highly energetic capture γ-rays, (n,γ). Other times, a
neutron is absorbed and another particle is emitted, such as for (n,α)
or (n,p) reactions.
Fission: When a colliding neutron is absorbed by a nucleus, the result is a
highly excited and unstable compound nucleus. If the incident neutron
has suﬃcient energy, the compound nucleus will be able to ﬁssion into
two highly charged ﬁssion fragments. First, the unstable compound
nucleus oscillates and deforms, until the ends scissions into two pieces,
repelling each other. Due to the enormous excitation, neutrons are
“boiled oﬀ” the fragments, sending out additional prompt neutrons.
The leftover excitation energy is emitted as prompt γ-rays, and as the
fragments slow down they become ﬁssion products. Additionally, some
nuclides are able to undergo spontaneous ﬁssion.
In a ﬁssion reaction, the amount of ﬁssion energy Eﬁss released is equal to
the Q-value of the reaction plus the kinetic energy of the incident neutron.
The energy is released predominantly as kinetic energy in the ﬁssion frag-
ments, but is also carried by gammas, betas, neutrinos and anti-neutrinos.
Crucially for ﬁssion chain-reactions, the ﬁssion reaction also releases ad-
ditional neutrons capable of initiating their own ﬁssion reactions. The
average number of neutrons released per ﬁssion ν is dependent on the ﬁs-
sion nuclide, the energy of the reactant neutron, and the ﬁssion products
produced, ranging between approximately 2.5–3 neutrons per ﬁssion for
nuclides commonly used in nuclear fuels. Most neutrons are produced
immediately during ﬁssion, called prompt neutrons, while a small fraction
β is emitted by neutron decay of the ﬁssion products seconds to minutes
after the ﬁssion event, termed delayed neutrons. Fission products responsible

3.1.4
Description of neutron population
Neutrons are emitted in a reactor with a multitude of energies and direc-
tions, and as such are in a state of non-equilibrium. Accordingly, particles
(or rather particle densities) are transported around the system driven by
the gradients in density and energy. Because of the huge number of particles,
it is convenient to describe the neutron population in terms of probability
distributions instead of each individual neutron. In such a way, in a given
volume element the change in neutron population is equal to the net trans-
port of particles in and out of the volume, plus the amount of neutrons
produced in the volume and minus the amount lost. The Boltzmann trans-

3.2
Basic Reactor Physics
Nuclear ﬁssion power is based on the ﬁne-tuned control of self-sustaining
ﬁssion chain reactions, generating massive amounts of energy inside a
nuclear reactor. Numerous diﬀerent reactor designs exist but almost all
of them use the ﬁssion energy to drive steam or gas turbines to generate
electrical energy. For reactors operating with a thermal neutron spectrum,
only certain ﬁssile nuclides can fuel the ﬁssion process, e.g. 233U, 235U and
239Pu. Other fertile nuclides can be used to breed ﬁssile fuels by neutron
capture. In the fast spectrum, the capture-to-ﬁssion ratio

3.2.3
Circulating-fuel reactors
In contrast to conventional reactor designs, where the nuclear fuel is em-
bedded in solid fuel rods in the core, liquid-fuelled reactors like the MSFR
present a radically diﬀerent framework where both fuel and coolant make
up the ﬂuid core. This also results in some distinctive characteristics related
to circulating-fuel reactors. Importantly, the reactivity feedback mecha-
nisms are such that transient changes in the system properties tend to die
down rather than increase. For safe and stable power plant operation, the
total feedback coeﬃcient must be negative.
The thermal feedback coeﬃcient is a measure of the reactivity sensitivity
to thermal changes in the core. The two main contributions to the thermal
coeﬃcient are the density coeﬃcient, related to the thermal expansion of
the fuel, and the Doppler coeﬃcient, related to the Doppler broadening of
resonances in the absorption cross section spectrum due to the thermal
energy of the nuclides in the fuel. In the MSFR, both of these are negative

4.1
Introduction
Several neutronics codes exist for solving neutron transport and simulating
reactor behaviour [10, 23, 24]. Some codes are based on trying to solve the
neutron transport equation deterministically by discretisation or through
diﬀusion theory approximation, while others are based on stochastic sam-
pling using the Monte Carlo method. In this chapter, the Serpent 2 particle
transport code is presented in some detail along with the general setups and
inputs used in relation with this thesis.
4.2
Presentation of Serpent 2
Serpent 2 is a three-dimensional, Monte Carlo reactor physics code devel-
oped by VTT Technical Research Centre of Finland since 2004. In 2012 the
Serpent 2 version was released and is still under continuous development;
the latest update to the code being version 2.1.32, released in February of
2021 [25, 26]. While not directly open source, the source code is distributed
free of charge for non-commercial research usage.
Serpent 2 is used by numerous international organisations and universi-
ties, and has been used to study many classic reactor types, as well as several
research and future reactor designs. With its burn-up calculation routine
and coupled multi-physics capabilities, it is also used for fuel performance
studies, medical physics, and fusion research.
Since the majority of reactors to this day have been using solid fuel, most
of the eﬀorts to develop neutronics codes have been dedicated to them, and
very few simulation tools are designed to deal with the speciﬁc conditions of
liquid-fuel reactor types such as the MSFR. The radical change to a ﬂowing
and circulating fuel pose a challenge for the conventional nuclear codes.
While Serpent 2 is still geared towards current solid-fuelled reactors, it has
been used in code veriﬁcation of MSRs such as the Brovchenko et al. bench-

4.2.2
Solving the Bateman Equations
Serpent 2 features burn-up calculation routines to solve the Bateman deple-
tion equations (3.15) and track the isotopic inventory of the reactor model.
Two diﬀerent computational methods are available: a linear chains method,
and the Chebyshev Rational Approximation Method (CRAM) which is used
by default [29]. The CRAM method is based on the exponential matrix
method of solving systems of ﬁrst-order linear diﬀerential equations, with
the solution:
n(t) = eAtn0
(4.3)
where n(t) is the nuclide concentration vector, n0 the initial concentrations,
and A the matrix of burn-up Bateman coeﬃcients. The CRAM method uses
Chebyshev rational functions to compute an approximate solution [30].
The Bateman equations solution requires a constant transmutation cross
section, but this is not generally the case as changes to nuclide composition
aﬀects the self-shielding of materials. Therefore, the burn-up must be
divided into steps and assume a constant cross section at each step. Changes
in the cross section between steps are then approximated by an algorithm.
The simplest algorithm uses constant extrapolation of the ﬂux and cross
section values at the beginning of the step. This is also called the Euler
method. The default time-integration scheme in Serpent 2 is the predictor-
corrector, using a constant extrapolation and linear interpolation correction.
First the ﬂux and cross section values are predicted similarly to the Euler
method, then the values are calculated at the end of the step, and the
initial prediction corrected linearly. Then the actual step is taken, using
the corrected values. While more accurate, the predictor-corrector method
also requires an additional calculation of ﬂux and cross section increasing
computation time.

5.1
Introduction
The ﬁrst objective was to validate the created Serpent 2 MSFR model de-
scribed in Section 4.4 against previous studies on similar models. The MSFR
neutronics benchmark from 2019 by Brovchenko et al. [10] was chosen as
a suitable reference study for comparison and validation of the Serpent 2
model, as the benchmark study uses the same simpliﬁed model design as
Auﬁero [19] and compares results from diﬀerent numerical codes, including
Serpent 2.
5.2
Methodology
A selection of neutronics parameters from the benchmark study were chosen
for comparison:
• Eﬀective multiplication factor keﬀfor a given initial composition;
• Critical amount of initial ﬁssile inventory in fuel;
• Neutron ﬂux and reaction rates in the fuel;
• Eﬀective delayed neutron fraction βeﬀ;
While the benchmark also investigates a TRU-started reactor model, only
a 233U-started model was evaluated in this thesis. The JEFF-3.1 nuclear
data library [35] was used for the validation calculations to comply with the
POLIMI Serpent 2 evaluations in the benchmark study.

6
Burn-up Studies
6.1
Introduction
During its operation, a nuclear reactor consumes ﬁssile material, irradiates
fertile material, and induces important changes in the isotopic composition
that aﬀects the reactivity of the system. To study and validate the long-
term behaviour of the MSFR model and obtain spent fuel conﬁguration for
nuclear heating assessment, reactor depletion simulations were performed
and compared to the literature.
6.2
Methodology
6.2.1
Simulation setup
Serpent 2 burn-up calculation mode permits tracking the evolution of in-
dividual isotopes as well as changes in the general reactor parameters.
Radioactive decay data and ﬁssion product yield data from the JEFF-3.3
database was used for all simulations. Main calculations using either JEFF-
3.3 and ENDF/B-VII.1 (see Section 4.3.1) cross section data were run with
the critical initial composition found in Section 5.4.3.
The dep input enables the depletion calculation mode and allows for
deﬁning the total depletion time steps in units of days, either by interval
or cumulative. At the beginning of the irradiation cycle, sub-daily steps
were used to capture initial reactor changes as well as the saturation of
135Xe poison. The step size was steadily increased over the ﬁrst burn-up
year, and hereafter a step size of 73 days was used for the ﬁrst 12 years.
Lastly, 146 and 292 days steps were used for the remainding time, for a total
depletion time of 26 years. The default CRAM mode with the CRAM order
of 14 was used for burn-up, along with the default predictor-corrector time
integration scheme.1

6.2.2
Reprocessing
In the studies of Brovchenko et al. [10], Auﬁero [19], and Pedersen [36],
fuel reprocessing is used to extract ﬁssion products from the fuel salt. In
the MSFR, an on-line helium bubbling system removes noble metals and
non-soluble gaseous ﬁssion products, and through oﬀ-line fuel treatment
cleans out soluble lanthanides [9]. In the aforementioned studies, this is
emulated by a simpliﬁed reprocessing scheme, removing the non-soluble
ﬁssion products with a certain rate, and the soluble with another. While
Auﬁero and Brovchenko et al. use the same simpliﬁed scheme, there was
confusion as to what removal rates were used: Auﬁero assumes a time
constant of τ = 30 seconds for the non-soluble ﬁssion products, whereas
Brovchenko et al. describes a half-life of t1/2 = 30 seconds. Both studies
present the soluble removal rate as a 40 L/day extraction.

Results
Unless noted, presented results are based on JEFF-3.3 database calculations.
6.3.1
Reactor behaviour in depletion
The burn-up simulations produced several insights into the performance of
the MSFR model reactor. The ﬁrst inquiry was into the reactivity evolution.
In Figure 6.2, the estimated keﬀis plotted for each step of the depletion dur-
ing the total 26 years simulation. In the ﬁrst half year there is a pronounced
drop in reactivity before the reduction slows down. This initial drop is
caused by the gradual diversiﬁcation of the reactor inventory and buildup
of ﬁssion products and neutron poisons, e.g. the minor actinides 233Pa and
241Am which both have (n,γ) cross sections in the order of 0.1 to 1 barn in
the intermediate-fast energy region. For the next approximately 12 years,
the keﬀdecrease with a slightly declining slope. In the long term, after 6000
days of burn-up, the slope starts to increase for about 5 years before ﬁnally
decreasing again. Some divergence is seen between the JEFF-3.3 and the
ENDF/B-VII.1 libraries, owing to cross section data diﬀerence. Pedersen
[36] simulated burn-up of similar time periods of 20 years, while Auﬁero
[19] and Brovchenko et al. [10] have simulation times much larger, up to 200
years, but included a refuelling scheme and reactivity control algorithm.

6.3.2
Inventory
A selection of important nuclides was tracked during the burn-up. Figure
6.5a shows the evolution of uranium isotopes masses in the reactor fuel. At
the outset, only ﬁssile 233U is present in the fuel salt, which then is slowly
consumed, and other isotopes are produced — mainly 234U, the non-ﬁssile
precursor to the ﬁssile 235U. Within the 26 years of simulation time, the
uranium inventory does not seem to reach an equilibrium state completely.
The initial 5000 kg of 233U have been approximately halved, and the 232U
and 234U appear to be depleting, while 236U still is increasing.
Likewise, Figure 6.5b shows the evolution of the plutonium inventory.
Here all the plutonium isotopes are still increasing at the end of the simula-

sensitivity to the cross section databases, the thesis and benchmark results
are found in good agreement.

Reprocessing
The results of the model implementing the simpliﬁed reprocessing scheme

6.4
Summary
The evolution of reactivity and reactor inventory over 26 years depletion
was calculated using the Serpent 2 burn-up mode. Reactivity was found
to experience a initial drop and hereafter decrease somewhat steadily. Ex-
pected reactor behaviours such as blanket breeding and xenon buildup were
observed. Roughly 1.7 tonne of 233U was bred in the blanket. Inventories for
uranium, plutonium, and some minor actinides increased to levels compa-
rable with previous studies but did not reach a stationary equilibrium state.
The removal of ﬁssion products had a substantial eﬀect on the reactivity
evolution and prolonged the physicality of the reactor, while inventories
were only mildly aﬀected.

7
Nuclear Heating Assessment
7.1
Introduction
The freeze-plug passive safety system is highly dependent on precise heat
distribution in the reactor in order to melt in a suﬃcient time frame. In the
freeze-plug, heating due to multiple mechanisms could inﬂuence viability
and performance. The energy deposition in the MSFR model was simulated
and studied. By default, Serpent 2 energy deposition calculations are sim-
pliﬁed and limited to the site of the ﬁssion reactions. To simulate accurate
and non-local heating due to both neutrons and photons in the fuel and
non-fuel materials, the advanced energy deposition mode was used, see also
Section 4.3 and [31].
7.2
Methodology
7.2.1
Serpent 2 edepmode 3
Getting the improved energy deposition mode 3 in Serpent 2 to function
required a substantial eﬀort and much trial and error. First of all, addi-
tional photon physics data ﬁles are necessary to enable photon transport
calculation. This data is supplied as a zip-ﬁle on the Serpent 2 wikipage
and the directory is set using the separate set pdatadir input command.
As described in Section 4.3.1, special cross section ACE-ﬁles are needed to
calculate the correct nuclear heating and is only available in a ENDF/B-VII.1
database. The ﬁles were obtained by request on a speciﬁc forum post on
the Serpent 2 developer forum. Suitable data paths, linking the library with
the ‘.xsdata’-ﬁle, were also necessary and required editing generously per-
formed by supervisor Amalia Chambon. This data was input by declaring
the regular data path in the set acelib command.
Despite these inclusions, the simulation would still not run without
further supplementary data [43]. Photo-atomic interaction libraries taken

7.2.2
Heating assessment with depleted inventory
Changes in the inventory due to burn-up could potentially impact the contri-
butions to the nuclear heating in the reactor. For this reason, nuclear heating
simulations were also carried out using a depleted material composition.
The depleted inventory of the model was obtained from burn-up sim-
ulation with the set printm 1 command, making Serpent 2 output the
full isotopic composition of the model materials for each burn-up step in
separate ﬁles. Based on results from the burn-up studies, and due to the lack
of refuelling in the model, the accuracy and physicality of the reactor model
beyond the decadal time range is questionable. Pedersen [36] describes
how long simulation become unphysical as the keﬀdrops below-critical, and
therefore chose only a ﬁve-year simulation time. With this in mind but
being slightly more lenient, the material compositions were extracted at
year 12 of the burn-up. Material compositions were created using depletion
setting with reprocessing.
For consistency with the isotopic data of the depletion simulation with
the coupled neutron-photon transport simulation, it was necessary to run
the burn-up using the same modiﬁed ENDF/B-VII.1 cross section library.
Decay and ﬁssion yield data from JEFF-3.3 were used as before.
7.2.3
Heating distribution in freeze-plug
A modiﬁed model was created, adding a single, cylindrical freeze-plug
channel based on Tiberga et al. [13] design, at the approximate location of
the MSFR freeze-plug system, see Figure 7.1. The freeze-plug was formed
as a cylindrical channel parallel to the z-axis with a 10 cm radius, and a 100
cm height extending from the bottom of the lower reﬂector to the bottom of
the fuel. The salt channel was surrounded by a 2 cm thick pipe cladding of
structural material. The freeze-plug was placed at a radius of 125 cm from
the system centre, displaced along the positive x-direction.
Detectors were used to measure the energy deposition along the freeze-
plug channel in both salt and surrounding pipe. Due to complications with
Serpent 2 detector transformation abilities, a Cartesian box-bin detector was
used to tally the heating in the salt and pipe. Finally, simple heating rate
assessments based on the physical properties of the salt and pipe material
were calculated using material data based on [10, 44, 45].

deposited while non-ﬁssion neutron energy accounts for just around 1%.
Just about 75% of the energy, 86.4 kW, is deposited in the ﬁrst 20 cm of the
channel. After 50 cm, the energy deposition density has fallen from about
25 W/cm3 to only roughly 0.7, and 98% of the energy has been deposited.
The total energy deposition in the salt was 115.4 kW.
In the pipe of the freeze-plug the energy deposition density is much
lower, with a maximum of 3.4 W/cm3 at the inlet of the channel. Due to its
thinness and the distance from the fuel core, relatively few particle reach
the pipe, leading to higher statistical error in the results as seen in Figure
7.4b. Seeing as the pipe material is not ﬁssionable, no direct ﬁssion energy
is deposited in it. Instead, almost all of the heating comes from photons
with a small fraction in the upper part due to neutron heating. Qualitatively,
the fall-oﬀof pipe heating is similar to the fuel salt, if not a slight bit faster.
4.2 kW of energy is deposited in the ﬁrst 20 cm of the pipe. The total energy
deposition in the pipe was 5.4 kW.

7.4
Summary
A new coupled neutron-photon transport energy deposition calculation
mode was employed and used to assess nuclear heating contributions in
the MSFR model. An overwhelming majority of the heat was deposited in
the fuel, with ﬁssion energy as the dominant heat source and 9% being due
to photons. Gamma heating was dominant in the blanket, reﬂectors, and
neutron shield. Using a depleted inventory, increased ﬁssion and photon
heating was observed in the blanket and reﬂector materials. Most of the
heating in a simple freeze-plug channel was found to deposited in the ﬁrst
20 cm. Simple temperature calculations found a numerical estimate for
heating rates in salt and pipe.

Discussion
The results of the validation in Section 5 indicates that the Serpent 2 MSFR
model behaves as expected from previous benchmark and studies. Some
small discrepancies were found, e.g. the thorium capture rate, but overall
the results are in good agreement with both theory and benchmark. Addi-
tional parameters are presented in Brovchenko et al. [10] that could have
been compared against, such as the thermal feedback coeﬃcient and neu-
tron generation time, but these were omitted to save time, and progress to
the more speciﬁc studies. Likewise, the TRU-started conﬁguration could
have been modelled and compared for additional conﬁdence in the model
validation, but would have extended the work and time needed undesirably.
The nuclear data library sensitivity reported by Auﬁero [19] is corroborated
by the comparison of reactivity evolution using diﬀerent databases and
codes. The JEFF-3.1 library is found to result in divergent reactivity curves
and a notable diﬀerence in the critical initial composition, most likely stem-
ming from issues with the 233U capture cross section data as identiﬁed by
Auﬁero.
Burn-up depletion calculation results of Section 6 show reactor dynamics
and inventory changes that generally are in line with the comparable results
available. A signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the thesis burn-up model and
the models of Auﬁero and Brovchenko et al. is the custom-made reactivity
control and equilibrium search algorithms, that allow calculation of long-
term equilibrium inventories. As the reactivity drops in the absence of
refuelling or reactivity control, the burn-up become less stable and estimates
become unphysical. Without access to these additional routines, comparison
beyond the ﬁrst few years of burn-up was made diﬃcult. Even with a
simpliﬁed thorium and uranium refuelling scheme as used by Pedersen
[36], reproducing the Auﬁero [19] reactivity evolution was found to be
ineﬃcacious as evidenced by [36, Figure 12]. Obtaining or replicating the
custom routines were not within the scope of this thesis, but it is believed
that these features or similar will be implemented in a future version of

Serpent 2. Nonetheless, the reactivity and depletion results for the ﬁrst
decade or so correlates well with the compared studies, and several of
the expected eﬀects such as blanket breeding and actinide buildup are
demonstrated. Since the initial composition of a reactor is a highly transitory
state and not reﬂective of the composition in a working reactor, the depletion
results also provided inventory data for the further heating assessment.
The result of the simpliﬁed salt reprocessing model supports the un-
derstanding that salt treatment and reduction of ﬁssion product buildup
increases reactor eﬃciency signiﬁcantly. The eﬀects on depletion inventory
are less pronounced though. Implementing a more advanced reprocessing
scheme that extracts bred uranium from the blanket and re-inserts it in
the fuel would probably result in more accurate estimates for long burn-up
times, but was decided against in the absence of good documentation of the
Serpent 2 mass ﬂow functions. While fuel salt treatment also aﬀects isotopic
evolution and reactivity, it is perhaps most important in limiting hazardous
radiotoxic isotopes and lowering the reactor decay heat, as described by
Auﬁero [19]. These elements were not studied in this thesis work but would
be an obvious choice for further study had there been more time.
The nuclear heating assessment in Section 7 shows that the major com-
ponent of energy deposition is from ﬁssion energy release in the fuel as
expected. About 9% of the fuel heating and 96% of reﬂector heating stems
from gamma radiation, ﬁgures that are consistent with previous accounts of
10% gamma heating in fuel and 90% in structural material [46]. Non-ﬁssion
neutron reactions were a minor component in heating, except for the boron-
carbide shielding where it makes up three quarters of the contributions.
This is sensible considering that boron shielding material is used because of
its large cross section and attenuating properties. The heating assessment
using a depleted inventory conﬁrm the breeding and ﬁssioning in the blan-
ket also found during the burn-up studies, with a large increase in ﬁssion
energy deposition in the blanket compared to the initial inventory heating.
This is not entirely a realistic scenario though, as bred 233U and actinides
in the blanket would be extracted continuously during operation and fed
back into the core for burning. For the simpliﬁed reprocessing scheme
implemented, the results support the expected breeding of ﬁssionable nu-
clide in the blanket. Further studies using a more realistic reprocessing and
refuelling would give a more convincing image of blanket heat deposition.
The simple freeze-plug model results show that a majority of the freeze-
plug salt and pipe heating is being deposited in the ﬁrst 20 cm. The cal-
culated heating rates are very simple as they do not account for any heat
transfer, but give a numerical estimate of the importance of the nuclear heat-
ing during operation. Given time, a more elaborate temperature distribution
treatment like those presented in [47] would have been beﬁtting. Naturally,
a more representational evaluation would require a full 3D multi-physics
model of the core and freeze-plug, and account for the heat transfer between

the online Serpent 2 wiki is the main source of guidance for using the code,

Complication with cluster crash
Unfortunately the DTU Risø HPC cluster Jess, where the Serpent 2 simula-

of the thesis work, and DTU AIT services shut down all access permanently.
Luckily, most of the work-ﬁles were able to be salvaged and duplicated
onto the Sophia cluster. This was not the end of the problems though, as
Serpent 2 installations and nuclear data libraries had to be reestablished
on the new cluster system, and diﬀerences in the system conﬁguration and
available MPI libraries made this diﬃcult and time consuming. Nuclear
databases had to be transferred to the cluster and converted into proper
formats. Additionally, Serpent 2 mesh plotting was rendered impossible
due to a missing GD graphics library. Approximately two weeks were lost
on getting back ﬁles and Serpent 2 availability. While unlucky, there was no
way of predicting such an accident.

as the circulation of precursors results in potentially signiﬁcant heating
outside of the core. It was found however that Serpent 2 lacks appropriate

9
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to assess the nuclear heating in the MSFR and freeze-plug.
A neutronics model of the MSFR was developed using Serpent 2 code, and
was validated against a benchmark study. Based on static calculations, the
model was found to agree well with both theory and comparable results.
Burn-up calculations provided long-term behaviour of the model and in-
ventory depletion, in agreement with previous study results. The eﬀects
of reprocessing were found to increase reactor eﬃciency signiﬁcantly. By
utilising an advanced coupled neutron-photon energy deposition mode, the
nuclear heating contributions in the MSFR and freeze-plug could be esti-
mated for the initial and depleted inventory states, and energy distribution
and heating rates calculated for the freeze-plug channel.
9.1
Perspectives
While the results are limited by the simpliﬁed, static design, which does not
account for transport of delayed neutron precursors, the heating assessment
provides a useful benchmark for the heating contributions when running
coupled neutron-photon simulation, as well as gives an indication of the
heating rate in the freeze-plug. A comparison of the coupled mode deposi-
tion with other neutronics codes would clarify the veracity of the results.
The Serpent 2 code is currently limited in its capability of studying local
precursor concentration and estimating delayed neutron heating contribu-
tion, though it is being developed for a future version. Additionally, the
reactivity control and equilibrium search algorithms used in the comparison
studies should preferably be incorporated into the oﬃcial Serpent 2 code.
Ideally, a multi-physics study combining the Serpent 2 code with a
thermal-hydraulics code would be employed to model the ﬂow and con-
centration of precursors in the freeze-plug, as well as heat extraction and
transfer among components. As the new energy deposition mode of Serpent
2 has not been validated for burn-up calculations, a burn-up comparison
could likewise be the aim for future studies.
