Abstract
Megasynthases are industrially relevant enzymes responsible for the production of a large variety
of products. In this project, I developed a discrete diffusion model, named Axolotl, to aid in the
efforts of creating novel functional megasynthases. Based on previous work in developing the
models SEDD and MD4, different diffusion formulations were explored both in an absorbing state
and uniform diffusion setup. Additionally, a novel diffusion formulation was derived, cox1, where
the diffusion process is conditioned on a sample 𝑥1 from the prior. Of the formulations explored,
cox1 was the worst, showing the highest generative perplexity, while the other formulations showed
comparable results to each other. SEDD-uniform showed marginally better results when trained on
a small dataset specialized for megasynthases. Axolotl was used to generate synthetic A-domain
pockets for four small NRPSs, which were subsequently cloned, transformed into S. cerevisiae,
and expressed, to assess Axolotl’s output in a laboratory setting. While none of the enzymes could
be confirmed to be active by HPLC-MS, in silico analysis indicates that Axolotl has learned valuable
representations of megasynthates. As Axolotl can still be scaled up significantly, both in model size
and training, further training is expected to lead to higher experimental success. All the code for
the implementation of Axolotl is freely available on github

1
Introduction
Megasynthases are large enzymes responsible for the production of natural products through com-
plex biosyntheses, where chemical moieties are successively added to the compound as if on
an assembly line. These enzymes and their corresponding products are incredibly diverse, with
a wide range of applications. Some examples include: Cyclosporin[1], a nonribosomal peptide
(NRP) used in organ transplantation because of its immunosuppressive activity, Fuelimycin[2], an
energy-dense polyketide (PK) which could be used as rocket fuel, and 𝛽-keto-𝛿-lactones (BKDLs),
a class of engineered polyketides (PKs) that can be used to produce the renewable plastics known
as polydiketoenamines (PDKs)[3].
With sustainability in mind, megasynthases could be incredibly useful for biomanufacturing, allowing
for the programmable production of complex compounds from sustainable feedstocks, otherwise
produced from petrochemicals. For this to become a reality, it is necessary to decrease the time
it takes to design a novel megasynthase, increase their efficiency, and ensure that engineered
megasynthases remain functional.
Engineering of megasynthases is often done by domain swapping. A domain from one enzyme
is swapped for a domain from another enzyme to provide new functionality to the megasynthase.
This produces a chimeric enzyme, which usually only works when exchanging domains from closely
related enzymes, and does not generate high titers unless subsequently optimized. This reshuffling
of the natural diversity can provide novel megasynthases, but one could envision better workflows,
that create megasynthases where many more of the intricate and complex dependencies between
domains are taken into account.
The holy grail of megasynthase engineering would be a full understanding of the interactions be-
tween domains, and the ability to create a megaynthase responsible for the production of any
desired compound. Unfortunately, massive datasets of megasynthases labeled with their products
do not exist, and the labeled data would add another layer of complexity to already very complex
modeling. This project therefore focused on a simpler, but still arduous, task. As there is a lot
of unlabeled protein sequence data for megasynthases, the focus was on producing a generative
model for megasynthase-like sequences, able to in-fill a megasynthase protein sequence where
a part of the sequence is missing. The model then provides a sequence which mimics naturally
occurring megasynthases, without replicating the original sequence.
For this, the use of discrete diffusion models was explored. These are diffusion models operating in
a discrete state-space, in this case that of protein sequences. This leads to the following problem
statement:
How can discrete diffusion models aid in the engineering efforts to create novel
megasynthases?
Different discrete diffusion formulations have been explored for generative modeling of protein se-
quences; with some notable example being EvoDiff[4], DPLM[5], and ESM-3[6]. Discrete diffusion
also provides a way of prompting models in a non-autoregressive manner, which from a protein
engineering standpoint is very useful. For instance given a prefix, a suffix, and a sequence length,
the model can in-fill a part of a sequence, conditioned on the prefix and suffix. It can actually be
conditioned on any subset of a sequence, where the choice of which residues to regenerate and
which to keep from the original enzyme, is left as a design choice when prompting the model.
Recent work on discrete diffusion models within the field of natural language processing (NLP) has
shown great promise in improving generative perplexity and, in this project, the focus was on two
discrete diffusion formulations, SEDD[7], and MD4[8]. The improvements in NLP can be attributed
to two novel loss formulations; one based on score entropy, and another based on mean prediction.
These same loss formulations were employed on protein sequence data. For NLP, it seems that
1

==PAGE:15==
mean prediction, as done in MD4, is superior to score entropy, as done in SEDD. Additionally, it
seems that the field of NLP is shifting its attention to absorbing diffusion formulations as opposed
to uniform diffusion. However, as shown in this project, when applied on protein sequence data, it
appears that uniform diffusion using score entropy still has merit. In the interest of time, the model,
named Axolotl, was trained directly on a specialized dataset for megasynthases. I envision Axolotl
being trained on a larger dataset, such as UniRef50 which contains ~200.000.000 sequences, to
create a foundational model for protein sequence generation. Subsequently one could finetune
this model on specific protein families or traits by using low-rank adaptations (LoRAs) or similar
finetuning methods[9]. All the code for the development of Axolotl is available on github under the
MIT license[10].
This project combines prior work in discrete diffusion with protein sequence data to train a model
for generation of megasynthase protein sequences. The sequences were then experimentally val-
idated in a laboratory setting by expressing some sequences conditionally generated by Axolotl.
Although it could not be proved that the megasynthases created were functional, in silico model-
ing shows that Axolotl has learned local sequence dependencies, but struggles to create a fully
coherent megasynthase without any conditioning.

Background
2.1
Megasynthases
The generative modeling in this project is done with the goal of creating novel NRPSs and PKSs,
two important classes of megasynthases responsible for the production of an enormous variety of
natural products. The two classes of enzymes both function as assembly lines, successively adding
more functional groups to a compound. The structural diversity of naturally occurring polyketides
(PKs) and nonribosomal peptides (NRPs) is directly related to the diversity of the megasynthases
responsible for their production. This in turn originates from the many different types of domains
that make up the enzymes, and the intricate way these domains interact with each other. There also
exist NRPS-PKS hybrid enzymes, which have both the functionality of NRPSs and that of PKSs.
Given this incredible diversity, it is therefore no wonder that megasynthases have been studied
extensively, with the aim of unlocking their full potential, and being able to program their function.
Figure 2.1: The mechanism of PKSs and NRPSs. Through multiple cycles the enzymes add chem-
ical moieties to a growing compound. After n cycles the product is released from the enzyme.
The different megasynthase domains are responsible for the reactions: acyl carrier protein (ACP),
peptidyl carrier protein (PCP), acyltransferase (AT), 𝛽-ketoacyl synthase (KS), 𝛽-ketoacyl reduc-
tase (KR), dehydratase (DH), enoyl reductase (ER), thioesterase (TE), adenylation (A), condensa-
tion (C), methyl transferase (MT), epimerase (E), reductase (R), and cyclase (CYC). The figure is
adapted from the work by Cano-Prieto[11].
2.1.1
Polyketide synthases (PKSs)
PKSs are naturally occurring enzymes responsible for the production of polyketides, a class of
natural compounds with alternating methylene and carbonyl groups together known as a ketide.
PKSs function as an assembly line iteratively elongating a carbon chain by Claisen condensation.
The growing chain can be modified by specific domains of the PKS, for instance reducing the
carbonyl to a hydroxyl, an alkene, or an alkane. These functionalities are provided by 𝛽-ketoacyl
reductase (KR), dehydratase (DH), and enoyl reductase (ER) domains. Additional modifications
3

to the polyketide include methylation and cyclisation which can be provided by methyl transferase
(MT) and cyclase (CYC) domains respectively. After being synthesized the final polyketide is either
released from the PKS, usually mediated by a thioesterase (TE) domain, or it is transferred to
another enzyme to finish the synthesis. An overview of the mechanism of PKSs can be seen in
figure 2.1.
2.1.2
Nonribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPSs)
As the name suggests, NRPSs are responsible for the production of peptides without involvement of
ribosomes. Instead of the usual translation of mRNA to peptide sequence, the peptide sequence is
determined by the domain architecture of the NRPS, which builds the peptide in an iterative fashion.
Additionally, NRPSs can incorporate non-canonical amino acids and hydroxy acids into the NRP.
Within each module of the NRPS the choice of amino acid to incorporate is usually attributed to the
A domain, however, the rest of the NRPS should also be compatible with this choice. When the
peptide has been synthesized, it is released from the enzyme, either by a TE, R, or CYC domain.
An overview of the mechanism of NRPSs can be seen in figure 2.1.

Discrete diffusion attempts to learn a data distribution 𝑝0 by defining a continuous-time Markov chain
(CTMC) which transforms samples from 𝑝0 into sample from 𝑝1, where 𝑝1 is a known distribution.
The CTMC can be seen depicted in figure 2.3 where a data sample 𝑥0 is transformed into one
from the prior, 𝑥1. The CTMC defines how the sample is perturbed, usually either with absorbing,
or uniform diffusion. Absorbing diffusion moves all samples towards an absorbing state, whereas
uniform diffusion moves to a random sample where all categories have the same probability. A
model is then trained to reverse this process, and thereby transforming transforming samples from
𝑝1 into new samples from 𝑝0.

3
Materials & Methods
3.1
Model architecture
The model architecture is shown in figure 3.1. As in SEDD[7], the model is based on the DiT archi-
tecture[25], which is an encoder-only transformer conditioned on the time-step1 and a label. The
label signifies whether the sequence is of eukaryotic or prokaryotic origin. Dropout of the label
with 𝑝= 0.1 is employed, so that classifier free guidance[26] can be used upon inference. The
conditioning from this label is added to the conditioning from the time-step. This time-step is also
used in the noise scheduler described in section 2.5 to sample the input tokens. The noised tokens
and conditioning are used as input to the DiT blocks. Every block has multi-head attention (MHA),
with rotary positional embeddings (RoPE)[27] to provide positional information, instead of the tradi-
tional sinusoidal positional encoding[13]. FlashAttention is used, which is a common optimization
of the attention mechanism with lower memory requirements which also runs faster[28]. As in the
original DiT implementation[25], adaptive layer normalization with zero initialization (adaLN-zero)
is used to provide the conditioning through scale and shift parameters. As in SEDD[7], there is also
added a dropout layer with 𝑝= 0.1. Each DiT block also has a feedforward layer and there are skip
connections allowing data to flow around both the attention mechanism and the feedforward layer.
After passing through all DiT blocks, the output passes through one last adaLN-zero, and a linear
layer. Finally, it is post-processed based on the kind of diffusion process, whether it is absorbing
or uniform diffusion, see section 2.6.
3.2
Datasets
The datasets used in this project come from two sources. One is UniProt[29], which is home to
the family of UniRef clustered datasets[17]. The other is InterPro[30], which provides an API for
retrieving sequences based on specified domains.
The datasets were clustered by sequence similarity and no sequences were discarded. Every entry
in a dataset is a cluster of sequences. The custom dataloader described in section 3.3 is in charge
of choosing a random sequence within the cluster, processing it, and batching multiple sequences
together[31]. To see how the datasets are created, see appendix E.1 or the readme on github. The
datasets are after clustering split into training-, validation-, and test-sets.
3.2.1
UniRef
UniRef50 is a database created after the clustering of all UniProt sequences using CD-HIT[32] at
50% similarity threshold. The usual UniRef datasets have discarded all but one sequence in every
cluster. Therefore, to keep all sequences and implement a custom dataloader, the UniRef dataset
was retrieved and processed locally. This is done using the SPARQL endpoint for UniProt and the
query found in appendix E.2. The dataset has ~40M clusters, of which ~4000 clusters are set aside
for both validation-, and test-set.
3.2.2
InterPro
A specific dataset for training on megasynthases was created using the InterPro API[30]. A descrip-
tion of how to generate an InterPro dataset can be found in the readme in the github repository. The
InterPro dataset was in this case made by using the ACP-like superfamily with the entry number
IPR036736. This dataset has ~150.000 clusters, of which ~1.500 clusters are set aside for both
validation- and test-set.

3.3
Dataloader & sequence packing
The sequence lengths of the datasets are very skewed towards shorter lengths than that of megasyn-
thases, with the vast majority of sequences in UniRef being < 1024 amino acids long. To accom-
modate megasynthases the model needs a very large context length, as most megasynthases
are > 1024 amino acids long. Megasynthases even include the largest known natural enzyme,
PKZILLA-1, which is a megasynthase of 45.000 amino acids[33]. To capture most megasynthases,
a sequence length of 4096 is chosen for foundational models, and 1024 for hyperparameter search.
A naive implementation would use padding tokens to pad all sequences to the same length, but this
would lead to an excessive amount of padding tokens, slowing down training significantly. There-
fore, sequence packing is employed to get rid of padding tokens entirely[31].
The custom dataloader is implemented in pytorch[34]. See the implementation on github for more
details. This dataloader is responsible for loading the data from the chosen dataset and dividing it
among the chosen number of cores. When the dataloader is iterated over, a minibatch is created
in the following way:
1. A random sample is drawn from a random cluster in the dataset.
2. The length of this sample, capped at the model context length, is added to a running total
length.
3. Step 1 & 2 are repeated until a desired maximum length is reached.
4. All the samples are truncated to the context length, and batched into a minibatch using py-
torch[34] nested tensors[35].
5. The running total is reset to 0, and the process is restarted for the next batch.
This ensures that every batch has the same number of tokens, even though sequences are not of
the same length, which should provide with lower variance in the loss. It can however give a larger
variance in GPU memory usage, as this is highly dependent on sequence lengths.

3.5
Evaluation
For evaluation of the model the loss is calculated on a batch from the validation data set every 100
steps, without backpropagation of gradients. The loss is very volatile, especially for uniform diffu-
sion formulations. Therefore, generative perplexity is calculated, which is used in both SEDD[7]
and MD4[8] to compare model performances. In the NLP literature GPT-2 can be used for calcu-
lating perplexity. This project instead uses ESM-2[38], which is a protein language model trained
with the masked language modeling (MLM) objective.
A fixed sequence length and batch size is used to generate a batch using a model checkpoint, with
no class label to have no guidance of sequences. ESM-2-t12-35M-UR50D is used for calculating
perplexity, which has 12 layers, 35M parameters, and is trained on UniRef50. To calculate gen-
erative perplexity on ESM-2 for the batch of generated sequences, copies of the sequences are
created with each token masked out and perplexities using only the logits of masked positions are
computed[39]. The code for calculating perplexity can be found in appendix E.3.

Expression of NRPSs
To validate Axolotl, artificial variants were created of four NRPSs by replacing the A-domain pocket
with ones generated by Axolotl. The generated sequences were retrotranslated and incorporated
into the original four NRPSs. These artificial proteins were then expressed in S. cerevisiae BJ5464-
NpgA (MATα ura3-52 his3-Δ200 leu2-Δ1 trp1 pep4::HIS3 prb1-Δ1.6R can1 GAL). This is a leucine,
tryptophan, uradine, and uracil auxotrophic strain, with a phosphopantetheine transferase inte-
grated (PPTase), which is necessary for activation of NRPSs. Plasmids for in vivo assembly of
NRPSs were constructed by Dr. Coco Xie in a different project. These four plasmids include the se-
quence of four different wildtype NRPSs under expression of the GAL1 promoter, with the A-domain
pocket being exchanged for a URA3 marker. This marker is flanked by two BsaI restriction sites
for excision and subsequent in vivo assembly under counter-selection of the URA3 marker using
5-fluoroorotic acid (5-FOA). The in vivo assembly is depicted in figure 3.2. The repair templates for
the A-domain pockets were produced by conditional generation using Axolotl with the rest of the
amino acid sequence as context. This amino acid sequence was codon optimized for expression
in S. cerevisiae, and ordered as gene fragments from GenScript with flanking regions for in vivo
assembly into the relevant plasmid, while also making sure to have no BsaI sites in the sequence.
After cloning, three biological replicates were picked from the plate for expression. They were
transferred to 3 mL SC-URA medium, added 0.2% glucose and 1.8% galactose, to induce the
GAL1 promoter, and incubated at 30°C for 3 days at 250 rpm. The cells were spun down, and
metabolites were extracted using 1:1 methanol and MilliQ water. After a subsequent spin-down to
remove any solids that might have gotten transferred over, the samples were analyzed by HPLC-MS
at the Novo Nordisk Centre for Biosustain.

To qualitatively asses the model output, unconditional generation of sequences was done using

in-filling of a single protein domain, keeping the rest of the sequence static.

5
Perspectives
To properly experimentally validate Axolotl, one could train a larger model, or design a simpler
task for easier validation. An example could be to train Axolotl to generate fluorescent proteins,
which would have a much more efficient assay. However, it would be unclear how well a successful
generation of a fluorescent protein would translate to generation of megasynthases. Alternatively,
one could choose a smaller region of the megasynthase to regenerate, with the simplest task being
point-mutations. There are also more optimizations to be done for the training of Axolotl. For
instance, generative perplexity is expensive for ESM-2, as it requires an evaluation for every token
in a sequence. This is because it has an MLM objective. However, by finetuning ESM-2 to output
generative perplexity, one can make a version of ESM-2 that outputs generative perplexity by just
a single pass per sequence, which would make evaluation significantly quicker[39]. I envision a
foundational model being trained in the same framework as Axolotl, to make an open source model
from which different specialized models can be created by fine-tuning on smaller datasets of interest.
Inspired by the work in image generation models, LoRA or another parameter-efficient method
can be used to combine different specialized models[46]. For instance, a LoRA for thermostable
proteins can be combined with a LoRA for carbohydrate-active enzymes (cazymes) to create a
model generating thermostable cazymes. This could create an ecosystem around Axolotl where
researchers can pick and choose between different LoRAs to create the model they need for a
specific task, or they can create their own. Of course, the first LoRA I would create, would be one
for megasynthases.

6
Conclusion
The goal of this project was to use discrete diffusion models to aid in the efforts of creating novel
megasynthases. Taking inspiration from domain swapping, the generative model Axolotl was cre-
ated for conditional generation of megasynthase-like sequences. It is a score-based discrete diffu-
sion model using an absorbing state transition matrix, trained on a dataset of megasynthase-like
sequences. Mean prediction was also explored as an objective, along with a uniform transition
matrix. Due to time-constraints these other formulations could not be considered for the imple-
mentation of Axolotl. Of the different discrete diffusion formulations evaluated in this project, a
score-based model with a uniform transition matrix seems to yield the best generative perplexity,
although this model is not significantly better than the next-best models. Future versions of Axolotl
might therefore be trained on a larger scale with different diffusion formulations to further elucidate
the best setup for discrete diffusion of protein sequences. A novel diffusion formulation, cox1, was
also derived, which uses a diffusion process conditioned on a sample from the prior, 𝑥1. However,
cox1 had the highest generative complexity of the formulations evaluated.
Axolotl was evaluated by generating artificial NRPS sequences. In an unconditional setup, gener-
ating a full megasynthase, Axolotl creates sequences that according to InterProScan contains a
plausible domain architecture. When evaluated using AlphaFold3, these sequences however have
much lower pLDDT values than that of natural sequences. Axolotl was also used for in-filling, by
regenerating A-domain pockets for four different NRPSs of an A-PCP-TE domain architecture. The
artificial sequences were retrotranslated and incorporated into the wildtype NRPSs instead of their
natural A-domain pocket. This resulted in 16 different artificial NRPSs, which were all expressed
in S. cerevisiae. The supernatant of the resulting cultures were analyzed by HPLC-MS, which un-
fortunately did not show production of relevant NRPs. The reason for this could be that the NRPSs
were non-functional, that the novel A-domain selected for a precursor which was not present in the
media, or that the NRPSs were not properly expressed.
In silico modeling shows that the conditionally generated domains have a low predicted RMSD value
of 0.522 when evaluating just the generated part of the sequence. This metric, although biased due
to the conditioning, indicates that Axolotl has learned how to generate NRPS-like sequences when
prompted for in-filling, although the model might not have trained enough to create fully functional
enzymes. Axolotl shows no signs of overfitting, and the generative perplexity is still on a downward
trajectory, so it is very possible that further training will result in a version of Axolotl which would
produce functional megasynthase domains by in-filling.
