Abstract
This project focuses on the collection and processing of image data to obtain highly accurate
maps using an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV). The project investigated the designing, assembly
and calibration of a fixed-wing Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) with the purpose of performing
accurate survey missions. Data was collected in Værløse Flyvestation (Værløse Airport) and the re-
sulting Digital Surface Model (DSM) and Orthomosaic model produced from the collected data were
analysed and compared to data from Hans Christian Andersen Airport (HCAA), where Ground
Control Points (GCP) and Post Processed Kinematics (PPK) was implemented. The intention was to
evaluate the accuracy obtained from mounting different cameras. In this project two cameras were
investigated; the Phase One IXM-GS120 and the Sony A7R IV.
The results showed that the cameras encountered the same issue of estimating building roof
edges accurately. However the Phase One IXM-GS120 was capable of achieving an accuracy below
4 millimeters on all axes by using Ground Control Points (GCP) as the ground truth. By comparison,
the Sony A7R IV was capable of achieving an accuracy below 25 millimeters. It was observed that
Phase One IXM-GS120 camera equipped with a 80mm lens produced the most accurate results.

Introduction
Aerial mapping is an effective tool for various applications, such as monitoring the environment, ur-
ban planning and disaster management. Manned airplanes has been the traditional method to collect
aerial images, however in recent years, Unmanned Aerial Vehicle’s (UAV) have become much more
accessible and reliable, making unmanned an alternative platform to manned. Fixed-wing UAV’s
have the design shape of common airplanes, using large wings to create lift, giving the benefit of
longer duration of flight time compared to copters, for example. Fixed-wing UAV’s are portable
allowing for mapping in remote areas, as well as being much cheaper to operate than traditional air-
planes.
Achieving accurate resulting maps, whether it is a manned or unmanned platform, depends on the
accuracy of the Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) position and the orientation of the vehicle.
A higher confidence in the location and attitude of the images taken equates to a higher confidence of
the resulting processed models. Methods such as Real Time Kinematics (RTK), Post Processed Kine-
matics (PPK) and Ground Control Points (GCP)’s can be used to increase the accuracy of the UAV
position estimation.
Advancements in camera technology has brought down the dimensions of cameras that are capable
of capturing enough details from standard flight altitudes. This reduces payload weight, increasing
the possible flight time. The small form factor, and high quality cameras allow UAV’s to produce
acceptable results for commercial applications.
1.1
Motivation and Purpose
This project is focused on developing a test platform for the Quality Assurance (QA) department for
the company Phase One. Phase One is a camera company that also provides payload solutions to
customers that want to integrate a Phase One camera on their UAV platform. The Phase One camera,
IXM-GS120, shown in Fig. 1.1, was developed with the purpose of capturing images with high shutter
speeds. A high shutter speed allows the camera to capture rapidly moving objects without motion-
blur, therefore integrating the IXM-GS120 on a fixed-wing UAV would test the camera’s capabilities
of capturing images with no blur at high speeds.
Figure 1.1: Phase One IXM-GS120[30].

The purpose of this project is therefore to;

Standard UAV System
All fixed-wing UAV systems consists of several core components: Battery, Motor(s), Flight Controller

The MAVLink Protocol
The standard messaging protocol UAV’s use to communicate is called MAVLink [25].
The MAVLink protocol can be manually written in many different coding languages, but for the
scope of this project, software such as MissionPlanner is used, which has implemented the MAVLink
protocol.
2.3
Ground Control Station (GCS)
The ground control station is software that fetches transmitted telemetry data from the UAV, and
allows the pilot to change on-board parameters while in flight.
The GCS also has built-in features for planning detailed autonomous missions and uploading them to
the FC. Most GCS’s have integrated tools to plan flights. There are several Ground Control Station’s
such as QGroundControl[34], MissionPlanner[26], and UgCS[42].

2.5
Ground Control Points (GCP)
Ground Control Points are markers on the ground where the coordinates are known to a very accu-
rate degree. The GCP are used in post-processing, allowing the processing software to have markers
in the data which increases the accuracy of the resulting models. This is especially useful when a
large area is surveyed.
In Fig. 2.4, two examples of GCP are shown. In general, GCP should have a high contrast in them so
that they can easily be identified in the images taken.

The downside of GCP is they are not simple to set up. The location GCP are placed in must be
measured accurately to perform well for post-processing, so setting up GCP’s in remote locations
might prove difficult, limiting the use of them.
2.6
Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS)
Accurate positions estimates are found by calculating the distance from a minimum of three satellites
to the GNSS receiver. The distance is calculated using;
distance = speed · time
(2.5)
==PAGE:13==

A satellite sends a data signal to the GNSS receiver. The satellite and the GNSS receiver are equipped
with advanced clocks to measure the time of transmission and the time of arrival. Knowing the travel
time of the signal, the speed of the signal (speed of light), as well as the exact position of the satellite
at the time of transmission yields an accurate distance from the satellite to the GNSS receiver.
Combining the calculated distances from a minimum of three satellites produces an accurate 3D po-
sition estimate. If less that three satellites were used, it would not produce an exact estimate in 3D
space, instead it would give a plane or sphere of possible positions. This is known as triangulation.
To further increase the accuracy of GNSS signals, Differential Global Navigation Satellite System
(DGNSS) was developed. DGNSS works by setting up a second, stationary GNSS receiver in the
vicinity of the primary GNSS receiver. The second receiver would be capable of adjusting for certain
errors such as receiver clock errors, satellite clock errors, atmospheric distortion, and orbit errors[10],
which would be applied to the primary GNSS receiver. From DGNSS two main variants have been
formed, known as Real Time Kinematics (RTK) and Post Processed Kinematics (PPK).
2.6.1
Real Time Kinematics (RTK)
Real Time Kinematics (RTK) uses real time data to adjust the position estimate of the primary GNSS
receiver. The secondary GNSS receiver, capable of eliminating certain errors, would transmit the off-
set to the primary GNSS receiver in real time, actively adjusting the position estimate over time.
This method of obtaining accurate geo-tags for the images is widely used, because it can be imple-
mented in remote locations as long as the base station is stationary and has several satellite connec-
tions.
2.6.2
Post Processed Kinematics (PPK)
Post Processed Kinematics (PPK) involves performing post-processing of the GNSS data. Contrary to
RTK, there is no real time connection between the primary and secondary GNSS receiver. Instead the
raw GNSS data of the two receivers is saved, after which the logs would be extracted, and the GNSS
positions estimates would be computed using separate software[27].

3.1
System Requirements
The requirement for this project was to be able to perform a survey mission with a Phase One IXM-
GS120 camera. Therefore the system has been designed according to the specifications of the camera.
The two important factors were the weight, and the physical dimensions of the camera. The IXM-
GS120 has the dimensions 90x90x67 mm[19]. The lens’ supported for survey mapping are the 35mm
and the 80mm lens so the maximum payload weight was therefore 1170 g[19].
The camera was mounted in NADIR, meaning that the payload bay must be able to contain a 90x90
mm object. The length of the camera changes according to the lens configuration, and was therefore
not as important for the system design.

3.3.2
Power Design
The general power design was split into three branches, marked on Fig. 3.1 by the ’CORD TRIPLE
SPLIT’. Two branches were for the individual motors, and one branch was to supply power to the
payload. On these branches, power modules were installed, to provide the necessary power outputs
for various other components.
There were three power sources going to the Flight Controller as shown in Fig. 3.1. The power coming
from Power Module 1 was the main power source for the FC, however, the servo rail was powered in-

dependently by the ESC’s UBEC. Both ESC’s were connected to the servo rail in case a motor burned
out and the UBEC could not supply power to the servo rail. If only one ESC was connected to the
servo rail, all control of the servos would be lost.
The RFD 868x module was powered by an independent power source, Power Module 2. While in
theory, the RFD module could be powered by the FC, it would add a weakness to the system in case
the FC would not be able to supply power to the RFD 868x module. The Flight Controller supplied
power to other modules already, so to make sure the RFD module received the power it required, a
dedicated power supply reduced the risk of failure.
3.3.3
Data Communication Design
The telemetry data communication between the camera and the Flight Controller (TELEM2) consisted
of camera status information as well as commands sent from the FC to the camera. The cable used
was provided by Phase One. All telemetry data from the FC, camera as well as the GNSS was com-
bined and sent out from TELEM1 to the RFD 868x module. From here it was sent to the RFD TXMOD
mounted on the RC controller. A laptop was connected to the RFD TXMOD over a WIFI hot-spot to
obtain the data sent from the FC, as well as being able to send commands back to the FC.
The RC data communication, unlike the telemetry data, was only one way, from the RC controller
to the FC. The RC controller sent data to the RFD TXMOD, which was transmitted to the RFD 868x
module onboard the UAV. From there, it sent the RC data to the FC through the PPM_SBUS_PROT
in the servo rail. This data was then converted to commands that were sent to the servos and motors.
IO_CH 1, 2, 3, 4 and 7 sent signals to the servos RUD, ELEV1, ELEV2, AIL 1, and AIL 2 respectively.
IO_CH 5 and 6 sent throttle commands to the motors, as shown in Fig. 3.1.
3.3.4

There are several components that were powered by either the ESC’s or power modules, so it was
important to make sure that these power sources could supply the correct power to the hardware.

manufacture.
The tests were performed using the PicoScope 5000 series[31], together with a Fluke i30s AC/DC am-

450mA without any peripheral devices[9]. The only peripheral device connected to the FC was the
GNSS module.
The current of the Flight Controller with the GNSS module attached was measured, shown in Fig.
3.2. The current consumption spiked at 0.8A. Power Module 1 was capable of supplying a maximum
of 3A [33], which meant the supply from the power module was enough.

components were still fully functional. Usually, full throttle would only be used for take-off, therefore
20 seconds was long enough of a stress test to validate its performance.

System integration

4.5
Airspeed Sensor
The airspeed sensor was capable of detecting pressure differences which was used to estimate air
speed. The airspeed, in contrast to ground speed, takes the speed of the wind into account while in
flight. The sensor itself was mounted in the nose of the UAV, where there was no turbulence from the
propellers.
The airspeed sensor was also calibrated. ArduPilot provided an automatic calibration process for the
sensor. The UAV was left in LOITER mode (circular flight around fixed point), which allowed the
airspeed sensor to gather data in all wind directions, which was essential for calibrating. This also
required the weather conditions to have constant wind speed.

In general, fixed-winged UAV’s are capable of performing survey missions at higher speeds, as well
as having a longer flight time, compared to copters. Copters used for surveying tend to fly at speeds
around 5 m/s[20], whereas the fixed-wing in this project had a target cruise speed of 19 m/s, which
greatly increased the efficiency of the survey mission. This was also indicated by Tab. 6.1, where the
flight time for the fixed-wing in Værløse Airport was approximately 18 % longer compared to the
copter in Case Study 1, however the amount of area covered was 383.6 % larger, with over double the
amount of images taken.
This project encountered an issue with boundary estimations of building edges. Research shows the
use of Light Detection And Ranging (LiDAR) has the potential of improving this issue[45]. LiDAR
transmits an electromagnetic pulse which is reflected by the earth or object, to be received again by
the LiDAR instrument[21], which is used to model the environment. Data collection with LiDAR im-
plemented, prove much more capable of estimating building boundaries[45], however LiDAR comes
at the cost of increased weight, and power consumption, making LiDAR impractical for fixed-wing
UAV applications.
Post-processing software such as Pix4DMapper allows for the alteration of the point cloud. The
wrongly estimated building edges could be removed manually before the processing of the Digital
Surface Model and Orthomosaic Model. This would improve the accuracy of the edge estimations,
however the manual work to correct the wrongly estimated points is tedious and does not scale well
for larger projects.
The boundary estimation issue could be remedied by implementing an alternative mount configura-
tion, which positions the camera at an angle to the ground surface, which is called Oblique. Surveying
using the Oblique mount configuration results in more accurate results, especially for buildings[28].
For Copters, in most cases, the camera is mounted on a gimbal, which makes it simple to implement
the Oblique mount configuration. Whereas for fixed-wing UAV’s, the maximum payload weight and
dimensions are smaller, and therefore the implementation of a gimbal would be more challenging.
As future work, it would be significant to research the possibility of implementing a Phase One cam-
era on a fixed-wing, in the Oblique mount configuration to investigate the effect it would have on
boundary estimations of buildings, as well as the general map accuracy.
All missions flown in this project was done in single grid. However, performing cross-grid surveying
produces more accurate results[38], which, if implemented in this project, could further improve the
accuracy of the resulting models.

DISCUSSION
From the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) analysis of the Hans Christian Andersen Airport (HCAA)
flight missions, the 80mm lens proved to significantly improve the map accuracy, in line with the
expected results based on the significantly lower GSD. Therefore, as future research, it would be sig-
nificant to investigate the mapping accuracy of a Phase One IXM-GS120 camera equipped with the
Phase One RSM 150mm AF lens. This would increase the payload weight and dimensions signifi-
cantly, which would require modifications to the hull of the UAV, and possibly require an upgrade of
the motors to lift the extra weight.

7
Conclusion
This project investigated the construction and application of a fixed-wing Unmanned Aerial Vehi-
cle (UAV) with the purpose of performing accurate survey mapping missions. A fixed-wing was
designed, built and various sensors calibrated to enable the UAV of performing autonomous sur-
vey mission flights. The platform was tested and validated thoroughly before attempting to fly with
a payload. A Phase One IXM-GS120 camera was integrated into the UAV payload, and success-
fully used to collect data, resulting in three processed models; a point cloud, a Digital Surface Model
(DSM), and a Orthomosaic Model.
The Digital Surface Model and Orthomosaic Model was analysed and compared to data collected
from Hans Christian Andersen Airport (HCAA), and found to suffer from the same flaws, specifi-
cally in relation to the estimation of building edges. Various other types of areas were analysed and
the results were positive in the estimation of topographical features.
From the flight missions performed in HCAA, an Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) analysis was done
to evaluate the estimation of three Ground Control Points (GCP). The Sony A7R IV was able to achieve
a horizontal accuracy of 10.35mm and a vertical accuracy of 24.34mm. For the Phase One IXM-GS120,
the results showed a maximum map horizontal accuracy of 3.8mm and a vertical accuracy of 2.90mm.
The comparison between Flight 1 and Flight 2 showed notable improvement in the vertical (Z-axis)
RMSE for the Phase One IXM-GS120 equipped with a 80mm lens, in comparison to the 35mm lens.
During the development of the project, it became clear that further research could be done to improve
the accuracy, such as performing cross-grid flight missions as well as investigating the effect of posi-
tioning the camera in an Oblique mounting position. This could possibly improve the accuracy of the
resulting Digital Surface Model and Orthomosaic model.
Furthermore, the increased accuracy of the 80mm lens equipped on the Phase One IXM-GS120 could
suggest that the accuracy of the mapping mission could be further improved by equipping the Phase
One RSM 150mm AF lens, however that would require serious modifications to the UAV design.
