Abstract
The construction industry is being criticised for insufﬁcient growth in productivity and
a part of the reason is given as lacking digitisation. Being one of the largest industries
in the world, the challenges of the industry need attention. This thesis presents an ex-
ploration of opportunities for application of blockchain technology to target some of the
challenges found in the construction industry. Blockchain technology promises to im-
plement trust and transparency in digital applications and these properties are in focus of
this study. Based on a study of literature and interviews with industry professionals two
concepts of blockchain technology application in the construction industry have been
developed. The concepts show two different use cases for blockchain technology, both
beneﬁting the construction industry in terms of improved data management and over-
view of projects. This study ﬁnds that blockchain technology can be become valuable
to the construction industry in the future if implemented together with an update of the
work processes in the industry.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The construction industry of today has been given the label of being ripe for dis-
ruption. In a recent report published by the McKinsey&Company, it is stated that the
construction industry is lagging behind other industries in terms of digitisation [1]. Fur-
ther, growth in productivity in the construction industry is lower than the world average
economic growth and has been for the past two decades now. This is pointed to as a
great challenge for an industry employing close to 7% of the working-age population
of the world [2] and accounting for about 5% of GDP in developed countries and more
than 8% in developing countries [22]. McKinsey&Company point to the complexity
of the construction industry as being one of the reasons for the poor adaption of di-
gital technologies. The complexity arise from the geographically disperse projects with
many different stakeholders and participants ranging from large consultancies to small
sub-contractors all working together and sharing information. It is further stated, that
the ﬁrst movers in successful digitisation in the construction industry are likely to build
a sustainable competitive advantage. In the current state of the construction industry,
large construction projects typically overrun the planned deadline by 20% and are up to
over 80% over budget [1].
As these problems continue to gain attention from academics and consultancies
around the world, a new digital technology has appeared out of the online open-source
communities. This technology is blockchain technology and from its origin in 2008,
blockchain has risen to be one of the most hyped technologies of today [20]. Block-
chain technology was introduced in a white paper by Satoshi Nakamoto [41]. The paper
describes the digital cash system bitcoin, which is built on the blockchain infrastructure.
Despite being published together in the same paper, it is very important to distinguish
between bitcoin and blockchain technology. The ﬁrst software implementation of the
system described in the paper was released in 2009, also by Satoshi Nakamoto. To this
day, Satoshi Nakamoto is anonymous and it is not known whether it is a single person
or a group behind the release of the paper. Blockchain is an infrastructure for listing
transactions or records of other digital events in a chronological order. The unique in-
novation of blockchain is that it is impossible to tamper with the records, once they have
been added to the blockchain. The list of records or transactions is called a ledger, with
a reference to the bookkeeping used in banks and by merchants to keep track of ﬁnan-
cial transactions. The blockchain ledger method differs from traditional ledger keeping
as, on the blockchain the ledger is public to all participants in the blockchain network,

Today, the most famous application of blockchain technology is still the bitcoin,
which is just one among many other less known digital currencies. The number of di-
gital currencies, or cryptocurrencies, is ever increasing and today (Feb 2018) more than
1,500 different cryptocurrencies exist [9]. Besides being used to run the bitcoin, block-
chain technology is showing a lot of promise for applications beyond cryptocurrencies
and for this reason, it is getting a lot of attention from enterprises across many different
industries. Yet, the construction industry is not a front-runner in blockchain research
and the study presented in this thesis will be examining if that should change.
1.1
Motivation
While the construction industry is lagging behind other industries in digitisation, KMPG
points to distrust between constructors and building owners as another reason for the
budget and deadline overruns [36]. For a combination of digitisation and increasing
trust, the blockchain technology is an interesting technology to consider. Many new
digital technologies are getting a lot of attention today and blockchain is one amongst
these [20]. The Economist printed a 2015 article under the title: "The trust machine" ex-
plaining how the implementation of blockchain technology has the potential to change
how people and businesses cooperate [48]. This is also found elsewhere, as blockchain
is promising to implement trust between parties in digital transactions [25]. When col-
laborating in a project, trust is of ever increasing importance and this is especially true
when it comes to business. In the digital age, implementing trust in applications on the
World Wide Web has shown to be a challenge. The construction industry is already ex-
periencing complexity in projects and the increase in digital collaboration adds further
challenges to the already complex relationships [1]. This challenge is very well-known
in the construction industry, where large construction projects are conducted between
many companies and usually have even more stakeholders in terms of governments,
neighbours, users, etc. [40]. A recent survey shows that trust in the four institutions:
government, media, business and NGOs, is decreasing around the world [7]. The survey
is published in the Edelman Trust Barometer Global Report and reports the populations
of different countries’ trust in businesses as shown in ﬁgure 1.1.

1. Introduction
1.1. Motivation
Figure 1.1: The Edelman Trust Barometer: Populations trust in businesses doing what is right by
percent. Scores below 50% represent general distrust, and scores above 60% represent general
trust. The year-to-year change (Y-Y Change) is the change from 2016 to 2017 [15].
The emergence of blockchain technology has been compared to the rise of the in-
ternet in the 1990’s. Initially, the internet revolutionised the way people communicate
by introducing email servers and clients. Following this period the rise of e-commerce,
social media and various other applications have changed business processes across al-
most all industries along with creating entirely new industries. In comparison, bitcoin
is now getting much attention as the email did when it ﬁrst started being adopted [4].
Blockchain technology is showing a lot of potential in handling digital transfers and
records, and is being said to change the way that valuable assets are being handled di-
gitally [39]. In the digital age, the ability to digitally transfer the ownership of an asset or
send a message has so far been dependent on intermediaries facilitating these actions as
a service. This has forced any two or more interacting parties to rely on, and trust-third
party facilitators like banks, real estate agents and lawyers, which in many cases are
adding cost, time and complexity to their business processes. Blockchain technology is
looking to change this by ﬁnally allowing for a digital peer-to-peer transaction, where
ownership of any asset can be transferred in a secure way that prevents the sender from
accessing the asset once it has been transferred. This ability can be extended to prevent
fraud in documentation, as signed documents will be stored on the blockchain such that
it is visible to all relevant parties and yet protected using cryptography. Any changes
to the document will be rejected by the blockchain network unless participants reach
a consensus, agreeing that the changes are valid. Blockchain is creating a transparent
vault in which valuable data can be safely hidden in plain sight [39].

1.2. Research Statement
1. Introduction
1.2
Research Statement
The purpose of this thesis is to examine how blockchain may contribute to solving
some of the challenges of the construction industry. The aim is to provide one or more
well-described concepts that are founded on blockchain technology. To achieve this the
leading research questions for the master’s thesis is:
• How can blockchain technology be applied to create value in the construction
industry?
With the sub-questions:
• What is the current state of the construction industry?
• What are the advantages and disadvantages of using the blockchain technology?
1.3
Scope
This project focuses on exploring the potential of applications of the blockchain tech-
nology in the construction industry. The construction industry will be studied through
available literature and interviews with construction industry professionals. Based on
the current state of the construction industry, a study of the properties and current use
cases of blockchain technology will be evaluated. The study was performed in the
period from 18th September 2017 to 25th March 2018.
Blockchain technology as an infrastructure for applications will be in focus for the
project and the current application of blockchain technology for cryptocurrencies such
as bitcoin will only be used in terms of explaining the technology.

Blockchain Technology
Blockchain technology is one of the most hyped new technologies of today [20]. To
explain why this is the case and later ﬁnd out how the blockchain technology might be
relevant to the construction industry, the blockchain technology will be explored through
out this chapter. The ﬁrst thing that one should know about blockchain technology is
that it is simply a database technology. A database is a system that handles digital in-
formation in a structured way making it possible to recall the information when it is
needed. What makes blockchain technology different to other database technologies
is that blockchain technology is especially good in handling data about digital transac-
tions. Digital transactions can be thought of as a transaction that does not involve cash
or physical goods. This can be the transaction of money through an online bank or send-
ing an email. Both are examples of transactions of digital information between two or
more parties. Handling digital transactions have been a challenge since the introduction
of the World Wide Web, as it is very hard to control if the digital asset that one is re-
ceiving is actually unique. For any recipient of a digital asset, whether this is an email,
a ﬁle, a document or any digital proof of ownership, it is impossible to know if the same
asset has been sent to other recipients or uploaded on a website for the whole world to
download [12]. This problem is also known as the double-spending problem. When
digital assets hold value, third-party intermediaries are commonly used to facilitate the
transfers. For the construction industry these intermediaries are often banks when it
comes to transfers of ﬁnancial values and lawyers when it comes to facilitating con-
tracts holding the information on materials, man-hours and deadlines that are agree to
between different parties. The inclusion of third parties usually increase cost and time
of the transactions. Using blockchain technology could potentially decrease the need
for third party intermediaries and the interacting parties should be able to save time and
money [39]. Before moving on, a comparison of traditional databases and blockchain is
presented in the next section.
3.1

One of the problems that the blockchain technology seems to have solved is the double-
spending problem. The double-spending problem is a well-know problem in the digital
world and as indicated above, it relates to the issues of knowing whether a digital asset
is unique and that you are the only owner of it. To handle this problem, third parties as
e.g. banks are trusted to facilitate digital transactions of money [39].

3.2. Blockchain Properties
Based on the description of blockchain technology that has been presented so far,
the most important properties of the blockchain technology and thereby also blockchain
applications will be presented. The the focus will be on the three properties: trust,
transparency and computational logic.
Trust
Through decentralisation of information and the application of cryptography, a system
that ensures the integrity of records is found using blockchain. Thereby, the users of the
blockchain applications can trust the data from the system without having to trust their
counterparties. It is impossible to register a transfer of an asset of which one is not the
owner. Furthermore, the transaction history cannot be manipulated. The design of the
system opens the door for instant peer-to-peer transactions between non-trusting parties
without the need of a trusted third party to facilitate the transaction [8].
With the internet, sharing and communicating has never been easier but it is a com-
mon problem to keep track of the original or most up to date copy of ﬁles or records.
When an email is sent from person A to person B it is copied from one server to an-
other. By this, both the sender and recipient holds a copy and it can be very hard to
prove which copy is the original in case of a dispute. Blockchain technology offers a
solution to this problem and can be view as a single source of information available for
both parties [23].
Transparency
When assets are registered on a blockchain their history from day one will be recorded
in the ledger. Assets can be cryptocurrencies like bitcoins but it can also be digital re-
gisters of ownership of a physical object. Digital registers of ownership on a blockchain
are called tokens. The tokens represent ownership of an asset which can be claimed or
transferred only by the proved owner of the token. Again, cryptography is used to pro-
tect against counterfeits as to prove ownership. It is easy to imagine assets which could
beneﬁt from being represented with tokens on a blockchain in order to add transparency
of the ownership history and origin. This could be expensive assets, such as diamonds,
in order to keep track of the origin and type to avoid buying counterfeits. On the other
hand, bikes, electronics and other items which are often stolen and sold through second-
hand marketplaces could also be represented with tokens on a blockchain [38]. The
full overview of all metadata related to the assets that are registered on the blockchain
will create full transparency of the history of these assets. Assets can also be data ﬁles
or contracts, where the metadata will reveal who has updated or changed any data at a
certain point in time.

3.2. Blockchain Properties

Computational Logic
Keeping in mind that blockchain technology is an infrastructure for building applic-
ations, small programs can be built to facilitate automation of certain tasks. On the
blockchain, these programs are called smart contracts. Unlike ordinary signed paper
contracts which are legally binding, a smart contract is a program or script written in
computational code. Smart contracts can be built on the blockchain and thereby func-
tion as a transparent and tamper-proof agreement between two or more parties. Once
the contract has been initiated, it can not be altered and execution depends on different
parties authenticating that a digital or physical event has occurred. After the authentic-
ation, the smart contract can be set up to carry out the next steps in a predeﬁned process
[8].
A simple analogue to a smart contract is a vending machine. When the vending
machine is given a coin equal to a certain value, a soda is released. This can easily be
written in a smart contract and one can imagine it to be used when two parties are doing
a trade containing a number of transactions. Where multiple transactions are concerned,
there exist interdependent actions which can only be initiated when certain terms in the
smart contract are met. Consider one party which is selling goods to a second party. The
transactions are interdependent so that the 70% of the payment is made when the goods
are shipped and the remaining 30% is paid when the goods are received. All bills and
documents associated with these actions can be set to automatically be release to the
appropriate recipients. These conditions can easily be described using logical computer
code. Once the code is loaded into the blockchain, it cannot be changed and thereby
the agreement between the two parties is enforced by the blockchain. Any dishonesty
in the sending or receiving of goods will result in payments being withheld. If the
smart contract needs to be terminated, the option of termination must be coded into the
original code of the smart contract.
The properties that have been described apply to blockchain systems in general.
Though, different types of blockchain systems can be created, depending on how it is to
be used. The different types of blockchains will be presented in the next section.

3.3. Types of Blockchain Systems
3.3
Types of Blockchain Systems
When talking about different types of blockchains three types are to be considered. The
differences are the found in who is allowed to join the blockchain and thereby who can
see the information. The three different type of blockchains are:
• Public Blockchain
• Private Blockchain
• Hybrid Blockchain
Public blockchains allow anyone who wishes to join the network to download the
ledger and set up a node. Thereby, all information on a public blockchain is accessible
for everyone. The bitcoin blockchain is an example of a public blockchain. For other
purposes, a less public infrastructure might be preferred. In these cases, a private block-
chain can be chosen. A private blockchain requires approval from the existing nodes
or the owners of the system for new users to be added. This approach is typically pre-
ferred for enterprise applications and the information on a permissioned blockchain is
only available to those who have gained permission to see it. For this reason private
blockchains are also often referred to as permissioned blockchains. [30].
In public blockchains, participants do not know each other and thereby a very se-
cure consensus mechanism is needed. The consensus mechanism is the way in which
nodes agree that new blocks of information should be added to the blockchain. On
permissioned blockchains participants are known, as they have been given access and
the consensus mechanism can simply be an approval from the relevant participants. On
public blockchains the most common consensus mechanism is the one used in the bit-
coin network called Proof-of-Work (PoW). For a more technical description of the PoW
consensus mechanism consult Appendix D. In short, PoW ensures that an amount of
computational power is put into solving a very hard mathematical problem, that is given
the new block of transactions as input. This process is also called mining. The idea
behind this process is that all nodes are contributing computational power to the main-
tenance of the blockchain, and thereby they are using electricity that must be bought in
the real world. To ensure an incentive for the nodes to do this, they are rewarded with
newly generated bitcoins [41]. In this way the nodes are all working towards the same
goal of maintaining and staying true to the system. Despite the fact that the PoW con-
sensus mechanism is the reason that the double-spending problem has been solved, it is
often criticised for not being environmentally friendly. The maintenance of the bitcoin
network is estimated to consume around 30 TWH per year by late 2017. This exceeds
the total power consumption of Ireland, a country of 4.7 million people [26].

3. Blockchain Technology
Besides the purely private or public blockchain technology setups, a hybrid block-
chain system is also an option. A hybrid blockchain system is a system that combines
the properties of the public and the permissioned blockchains. The hybrid blockchain
can be preferred in cases where some transactions can be visible to all users and some
cannot [34].
To summarise, when a number of participants agree to make up a blockchain net-
work amongst them they need to decide on the type of blockchain they need. The
primary consideration relates to who should have access to the information. For bit-
coins a public system makes sense as the participants do not know each other which
calls for the very secure consensus mechanism to ensure integrity of their transaction
data. Using the public blockchain allows them to create a ﬁnancial system without a
bank facilitating their transactions as the trust and transparency they need is provided
by the blockchain system. Enterprises might be less interested in revealing all of their
data and therefor permissioned or hybrid blockchains are commonly chosen. These sys-
tems allow for access control but can still provide the transparency and trust needed
amongst the participants. In either case, the foundation of the blockchain system is still
the decentralised ledger that provides a chronologically ordered list of all transactions
and only allows new data to be added after a consensus is reached. From the strong
association of bitcoin and blockchain, the view on blockchain technology has some-
times been blurred due to bitcoins being an anonymous payment method used at online
markets outside of government control. However, despite hacks and crashes of bitcoin
exchanges, the blockchain technology itself has not yet been successfully comprom-
ised [39]. Based on the introduction of blockchain technology that has been presented
a preliminary discussion of the use of blockchain technology is conducted in the next

1. Do you need a database?
Blockchain technology is a database technology and therefor3 the ﬁrst natural
questions to ask is if a database is needed. If a database is not needed, there is no
use for blockchain.

3. Blockchain Technology
2. Are multiple parties writing to the database?
Blockchain is most relevant to cases where more than one party can write inform-
ation to the database. On the blockchain, data is stored in multiple locations rather
than in one central database, and all changes to the database have to be conﬁrmed
by all participants. Therefore, this extra use of capacity has to be justiﬁed by the
need for some of the blockchain properties as described in section 3.2.
3. Are the parties using the database known and trusted?
When the ledger on the blockchain is updated it is clear to all who did what. This
will help non-trusting parties to collaborate as no single party can make changes to
the data without it being visible to all. If this property is not needed, a blockchain
might not be needed.
4. Do the parties want or need a trusted intermediary?
On a blockchain system, the code is the intermediary and there is not a lot of
room for negotiation with the system. If the parties sharing the database do want
a trusted intermediary, as e.g. a bank or lawyer, blockchain is probably not a
solution to their problem. The relevant properties to prefer the blockchain in this
case are potentially lower costs and faster transactions.
5. Are consecutive transactions between parties registered and dependent on
each other to be accepted?
Another true strength of the blockchain system is the property of adding com-
putational logic to transactions. If transactions are interrelated in a way that one
transaction will only occur when a conﬁrmation of an event is given, the block-
chain technology can be used to build transparent systems doing just this.
6. Can the parties agree on a set of rules for entering and accepting transactions
to be registered?
As the blockchain system will be acting as the intermediary, a set of rules for who,
how, and when transactions will be accepted must be coded into the system. All
transactions will be checked against these pre-deﬁned rules and will be rejected if
they fail to comply.
7. Can the parties agree on picking validators for approving transactions?
In order to have a functioning blockchain system a number, of validators must be
chosen. In a public blockchain all nodes in the system act as validators. In per-
missioned blockchain systems, validators must be chosen as the system is created
between the parties. Public and permissioned blockchains are described in section
3.3.

8. Is it clear who is backing the assets that are represented on the blockchain?
The ﬁnal condition relates to the interface between the assets represented on the
blockchain and the physical state of these assets. It must be clear where and how
the owner of an asset according to the blockchain can go and claim these assets in
the real world.

Framework 1: A Blockchain Payment System
Implementing a payment system on a blockchain is a well-tested use case. Imagine a
building owner holding the ﬁnished building program, moving on to the planning/design
phase. At this point, the building owner has a good idea of the budget and will soon have
a detailed plan of the building project. Now, the blockchain payment system is initiated
and the budget for the project is loaded into the system. By doing this, the money
needed to pay the different actors working on the project is digitally represented in the
system. This representation can be thought of as digital casino tokens. By them-selves,
the digital tokes do not hold any value, but they can be exchanged for real money at a
ﬁxed price per token at any time.
When the building owner hires architects, engineers and contractors, they are given
an account on the blockchain payment system and receive their payment in tokens.
These tokens can be used to buy further services from other actors in the project, or they

4.5.1
Concept 1: Project Overview and Evaluation
As a development of the payment framework, a concept that will provide an overview
of projects and generate data for evaluation of construction projects is now presented.
The concept aims to provide a detailed overview of construction projects. The users
of the system will register the progress of the individual tasks through a simple applic-
ation on a mobile device, making it very easy to use in the ofﬁce or at the building site.
The overview of the projects will enable real-time monitoring of project progress and
allow for analysis of the projects after they have been completed. The result of using
this system is that learning and experience are quantiﬁed and can be used to improve
new projects. Patterns of errors or delays that might not be found today will be visual-
ised and in the end, help to avoid delays in coming projects.
In the building program, the initial project plan contains a range of tasks to be com-
pleted with an estimated duration for each task. This plan develops over time and dif-
ferent participants in the project will be assigned to complete different tasks. All project
participants will get an account on the new project overview system and can access their
account from a computer or a mobile device. In the system, the users will ﬁnd the tasks
that they have been assigned with a start date and a deadline. When the task is com-
pleted, the user will register the completion of the task in the system.
The system is set up by the administrator and once project participants join the pro-
ject they are added to the system. If the new users are new to the system they will need
to download an application on their mobile device or log in online through a website.
The project plan is loaded into the system and as the project plan develops in detail the
new information is included. As an administrator in the system, one can see the pro-
gress of the project that is given as an output. A simple example is presented in ﬁgure
4.12, where four tasks are planned and according to the user’s inputs, an overview of
the progress is given.

Concept 2: Project Data Management and Approvals
As a combination of the data management and approvals frameworks a data manage-

The data management concept seeks to enhance the handling of digital information
in construction projects. Documentation and speciﬁcations are linked to the relevant
parts of the building models to ensure that changes to either the documentation or the
models are notiﬁed to the users. This link should help to avoid misunderstandings and
errors where outdated information is used. The system is set up to monitor the doc-
umentation and models to make sure that users always access the latest versions. The
aim of the concept is to improve quality in construction projects by lowering the number
of errors and mistakes happening due to miscommunications and lacking discipline in
work processes. Through the setup of logical links in the model updates, certain work
processes can be enforced and project templates corresponding to the chosen project
ﬂow can be implemented.
To avoid misunderstandings, logical links are implemented on the blockchain. When
a user of the system adds changes to a document, the users of the corresponding parts
of the building model is given notice. The link is illustrated in ﬁgure 4.16.

of the construction industry propose by McKinsey&Company. In this evaluation, it was
found that the concepts do cover some of the aspects in the proposed solutions, but also
that the concepts alone do not provide a unique solution to increase the lagging growth

5.2
Future Work
To expand the understanding of the challenges of the construction industry and their
potential relations to the properties of blockchain technology further research is needed.
This research could be conducted in collaboration between academia and the construc-
tion industry to ensure inclusion of the stakeholders. In the process of gaining adoption
form the industry, a focus on developing a change management focused implantation
plan is also suggested.
To enhance the potential impact of blockchain technology in the construction in-
dustry, decentralisation of work processes in terms of risk sharing and project respons-
ibilities should be examined. Blockchain technology allows for much more peer-to-peer
focused processes as compared to the more hierarchical structure of building projects.
The concepts provided by this thesis have only been evaluated through one ﬁnal
interview and a comparison to the solutions to the challenges in the construction in-
dustry proposed by McKinsey&Company. Before implementation, further evaluations
are needed. The evaluation and implementation should be conducted on smaller projects
initially.
5.3
Conclusion
The exploration of the potential of blockchain technology in the construction industry
presented in this thesis has shown that blockchain could very well become relevant to
the construction industry in the future. The two concepts constitute applications of
blockchain technology that creates value in terms of learning and overview of projects
and enhanced data management. These concepts are suggestions for applications, and
the construction industry might ﬁnd other ways of incorporating the properties of block-
chain technology: trust, transparency and computational logic, to increase productivity
in the future. The nature of projects in the construction industry, where stakeholders
from many different trades work on a joint project could yield a range of interesting
use cases for blockchain technology. For implementations of blockchain technology to
reach its full potential of creating value in the industry, the current work processes must
be addressed. Current IT-systems do not succeed in providing their full value to the
industry and neither will a blockchain system if it is not adopted fully.

5.3. Conclusion
The construction industry is lagging behind other industries in productivity growth.
The comparisons made across different industries do not always make sense due to the
difference of their natures, but it is clear that this issues of the construction industry need
attention. The size and complexity of the construction industry do not allow for a single
conclusion to summarise the current state of the entire industry. Though, it has been
found that the construction industry generally calls for digitisation and digital processes
should be on the agenda for most companies today. The survey by Software Advice
in section 2.3 showed how 50% of SMBs looking for new software currently relies on
manual processes. For this thesis, Niras has served as the contact with the industry.
Niras is using different digital tools, though it became clear from the interviews that
they also experience errors and mistake caused by manual processes. This leads back to
work processes being an issue, along with the reluctance towards new systems, that was
also indicated in the quantitative study from the workshop.
Blockchain technology provides opportunities for digital applications that has not
previously been possible. Implementing peer-to-peer trust and transparency is the prom-
ise of the technology that has been proven through different applications already. One
of these applications is the very hyped bitcoin. The massive hype and the link to crypto-
currencies are not contributing to the view of the technology as an infrastructure. As an
infrastructure, blockchain does not need massive attention from the users of blockchain
applications. In the end, blockchain technology is simply a database infrastructure, and
it is important to consider if it is the right infrastructure on a project-to-project basis. In
terms of storing and fast recalling of information, blockchain is not yet the best solution
and for this reason, combinations with traditional databases are going to be relevant to
many cases.
A blockchain system alone is not going to solve all of the challenges of the construc-
tion industry. KMPG points to distrust between participants of construction projects as
one of the reasons for the budget and deadline overruns [36], and in this space, the
properties of blockchain technology do provide some opportunities. The Hype Cycle
by Gartner suggests that blockchain applications will reach the plateau of productivity
within 5-10 years, and this time frame could be realistic for blockchain applications in
the construction industry as well.

Appendix D
Blockchain technology - A technical descrip-
tion
To fully understand how blockchain works and why it is gaining so much attention, a
more technical walk-through is presented. The distributed system was explained earlier
and the concept of nodes running the software that deﬁnes and maintains a common
ledger will remain. Until now the consensus mechanism and validation of transactions
have mostly been referred to as cryptography. These concepts will be explained further.
Both the transaction validation and the consensus mechanisms are known technologies,
but the combination of them is what is the core of blockchain technology.
Joining the Blockchain
For the blockchain to function a digital signature system is required to authorise and
sign transactions. The digital signature serves as an identity on the blockchain. Digital
signature systems are based on public key technology which was initially introduced in
1976 [13]. The digital signature system consists of three parts: a key pair, a signing
algorithm, and a veriﬁcation algorithm. The key pair consists of two different keys
where one is the public key and the other is the private key. The keys are generated to
be used with a speciﬁed cryptographic function so that any message that is encrypted
with the public key can only be decrypted using the private key. The public key can
freely be distributed on a network whereas the other should be kept secret. Thereby,
all participants are holding two keys each and all participants have a signing and a
veriﬁcation algorithm to sign and control the validity of transactions.
Digital Signature Systems
The keys are used with the signing and veriﬁcation algorithms. When a message is
sent from node A to node B, node A signs the message with its private key and thereby
generates a signature. This can be illustrated by the following equation:
S(m,Kprivate) = s
where S is the signing algorithm, m is the message, Kpritave is the private key and s is
the signature. When the message arrives at node B it is easy to verify that the message
was actually sent by node A and that the message has not been altered by giving A’s
public key, the signature and the message as inputs to the veriﬁcation algorithm. This
action can be illustrated by the following equation:
V(m,s,Kpublic) ∈{yes, no}

The dotted line in ﬁgure A.6 represents the boarder between the private and public
sphere for every node in the blockchain network. As shown, node A holds a public key
and a private key. When a transaction is to be made, the transaction is broadcast to all
nodes in the network. This is done by sending out a message to the network contain-
ing the information about the transaction. The message is signed using the signature
algorithm and the private key. When nodes in the network receive the message, they
can check the validity of the transaction using the veriﬁcation algorithm. Due to the
cryptographic link between each set of public and private key, it is easy for everyone to
ensure that only the true owner of the private key corresponding to the public key that
is requesting the transaction to be made, has in fact sent the message.
Transactions on the Blockchain
On the blockchain, public keys serve as receiving addresses. This means that in the case

When the message is broadcast to the nodes on the network all of the nodes verify
that the transaction is valid by checking that node A has received the transactions listed
under previous transactions in ﬁgure A.7 and that these are not previously spent. If the
transaction is validated the nodes start to work on adding the transaction to the ledger.
In order to add the transaction to the ledger, the majority of the nodes must agree to add
the transaction. This process is the consensus mechanism. Different consensus mech-
anisms exist and on a private blockchain, a simple acceptance from the other nodes can
be sufﬁcient, whereas on a public blockchain the consensus mechanism is important for
ensuring that it is not possible to spend any assets twice [8].
In order to add the transaction to the ledger, it must be placed in a block that is
successfully referenced to the latest accepted block on the blockchain. Blocks are com-
monly referenced to the previous block using a hash function. A hash function is a
cryptographic function that turns data of any size into data of a ﬁxed size. Hash func-
tions are one-way functions meaning that it is very hard or impossible to calculate the
input that was given to the hash function from the output, but when a certain input is
given, the hash function always returns the same output. Thereby, the output can easily
be used to verify the integrity of the input, but the output itself does not reveal the input.
On the Bitcoin network, the hash function is the SHA-256 which gives an output in 256
bit which corresponds to a string of 64 hexadecimal characters. Hexadecimal consists
of the numbers 0-9 and the letters a-f, representing the numbers 10-15. The output of
a hash function is referred to as the hash of the input [14]. Any changes to the input
will result in a completely new output hash and to illustrate the sensitivity of the SHA-
256 hashing function a test is shown in table A.1. A text input is given and the output is

Current Blockchain Applications
Applications of blockchain are already existing. The development of the technology
is highly centered around the open source community, just as it was seen with the in-
ternet when it started. Different commercial, off-the-shelf blockchain platforms like
Hyperledger and Ethereum are available, on top of which applications can be built. The
blockchain systems are built on the basic business concept of network, assets, transac-
tions, participants and contracts [49]. In the following a number of applications that are
or soon will be implemented is going to be presented.
Payment Systems
Bitcoin is the most famous of the blockchain payment systems in existence. What block-
chain payment systems offer is direct account-to-account transfers without the need of
a bank serving as an intermediary. Different consensus protocols are being used for dif-
ferent systems, whereas Bitcoin is being criticised for the lack of scalability to handle
many transactions [10], other systems are set up differently. The Bitcoin protocol is
limiting the number of transactions per block and with a ﬁxed time between approval
of new blocks of around 10 minutes there is going to be an upper limit. This limit to
the capacity allows for a maximum around 4-7 transactions per second, whereas Visa
Europe handles up to 1627 transactions per second in comparison [51].
The beneﬁts from a direct payment system include a potentially low cost of transac-
tions, due to the removal of fees charged by intermediary banking institutions, and also
the availability for people who would otherwise not have access to banking due to their
geographical position [25].
Preventing Fraud
The company Everledger has created a blockchain in which diamonds are registered.
When a diamond is found in a mine, it passes through a range of distributors before
ending up in a store where it is sold to a customer. Previously, each participant in this
chain from the mine to the customer has had its own record of where the diamond was
bought and to whom it was sold. With Everledger, a diamond is registered on the block-
chain with an ID and pictures of it so that the end customer can be provided with a full
log of the diamond’s history. Beneﬁting from the immutability of the blockchain ledger,
customers can be guaranteed that they are not buying diamonds mined in a war zone
and also be ensured that they are not buying stolen goods [3].

Dispute Resolution
IBM Global Financing (IGF) is a ﬁnancing institution within IBM offering ﬁnancing
solution for their partners to pay their suppliers. In the case of goods not being de-
livered, IGF serves as the middleman in handling the dispute. This dispute handling
process ties up a lot of capital and for IGF this is up to $ 100 million at a time. One

resource handling systems which make ﬁnding the source of the dispute hard. By im-
plementing a blockchain solution, IGF has created full transparency and all participants
have permissioned access to the ledger on the blockchain. The result for IGF has been
a reduction in dispute resolution time from an average of 40 days to less than 10 days
which also implies an improved cash ﬂow [32].
Supply Chain Efﬁciency
The Danish shipping company Mærsk has initiated a blockchain project in their ship-
ping division in collaboration with IBM. In a study, they have concluded that paper-
work and point-to-point communication costs up to DKK 1,900 for a container shipped
from Africa to Europe. By implementing a blockchain they believe that a lot of these
costs could be saved [5]. The approach is similar to the IGF case described in sec-
tion Appendix E where the silo information structure adds complexity to the interac-
tions between trading partners. A blockchain system is created where smart contracts
automatically collect the approvals needed from different agencies and places all the
information on a shared interface between the supply chain participants. This system is
estimated to be able to save Mærsk Shipping up to DKK 20 billion per year while also
reducing delays and fraud [31].

National Cultures by Hofstede Insights
The descriptions of the country comparison tool provided by Hofstede Insight are com-
posed by Hofstede Insights [27]. They can also be found on their website, but are added
to this appendix for the convenience of the reader.
Power Distance
This dimension deals with the fact that all individuals in societies are not equal – it ex-
presses the attitude of the culture towards these inequalities amongst us. Power Distance
is deﬁned as the extent to which the less powerful members of institutions and organ-
isations within a country expect and accept that power is distributed unequally. High
scores indicate that people accept the hierarchical order where as low scores indicate a
preference for decentralisation of power.
Individualism
The fundamental issue addressed by this dimension is the degree of interdependence a
society maintains among its members. It has to do with whether people’s self-image
is deﬁned in terms of "I" or "We". In Individualist societies (high scores) people are
supposed to look after themselves and their direct family only. In Collectivist (low
scores) societies people belong to in groups that take care of them in exchange for
loyalty.
Masculinity
A high score (Masculine) on this dimension indicates that the society will be driven by
competition, achievement and success, with success being deﬁned by the winner / best
in ﬁeld – a value system that starts in school and continues throughout organisational
life.
A low score (Feminine) on the dimension means that the dominant values in society
are caring for others and quality of life. A Feminine society is one where quality of life is
the sign of success and standing out from the crowd is not admirable. The fundamental
issue here is what motivates people, wanting to be the best (Masculine) or liking what
you do (Feminine).

Uncertainty Avoidance
The dimension Uncertainty Avoidance has to do with the way that a society deals with
the fact that the future can never be known: should we try to control the future or
just let it happen? This ambiguity brings with it anxiety and different cultures have
learnt to deal with this anxiety in different ways. The extent to which the members of
a culture feel threatened by ambiguous or unknown situations and have created beliefs
and institutions that try to avoid these is reﬂected in the score on Uncertainty Avoidance.
High scores indicate a large degree of uncertain avoidance and low score the opposite.
Long Term Orientation
This dimension describes how every society has to maintain some links with its own
past while dealing with the challenges of the present and future, and societies prioritise
these two existential goals differently. Normative societies, which score low on this
dimension, for example, prefer to maintain time-honoured traditions and norms while
viewing societal change with suspicion. Those with a culture, which scores high, on the
other hand, take a more pragmatic approach: they encourage thrift and efforts in modern
education as a way to prepare for the future.
Indulgence
One challenge that confronts humanity, now and in the past, is the degree to which small
children are socialised. Without socialisation, we do not become “human”. This dimen-
sion is deﬁned as the extent to which people try to control their desires and impulses,
based on the way they were raised. Relatively weak control is called “Indulgence”
(high scores) and relatively strong control is called “Restraint” (low scores). Cultures
can, therefore, be described as Indulgent or Restrained.
