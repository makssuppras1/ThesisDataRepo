ABSTRACT
Nowadays, controlling climate change has already become the consensus in the international
community. Many practical solutions have been carried out, aiming to achieve some
prominent targets in the following decades. However, the consensus is merely the starting
point of environmental protection action. Novel manner, methods, and patterns are still
awaiting exploration to motivate global economic sustainable development. Recently, the
rapid development of electric vehicles(EVs) has brought many opportunities and challenges.
The combination between renewables and EVs could efficiently decrease the emission level
of air pollutants, and battery charging could provide some potential ancillary services.
Meanwhile, the phase unbalance problem exists widely under power system operation due
to various factors, which are prone to capacity waste and energy loss if the management is
inappropriate.
The project’s general aim is to analyze and improve the performances of EV smart charger
clusters built with a distributed control architecture. It has a starting point, the previously
developed model designed in the autonomously controlled distributed chargers(ACDC)
project.
The model can minimize the negative impact on the transformer/grid and
maximize the engagement of renewables while fulfilling user charging requirements. The
thesis focuses on the further development of local control logic, adding functionalities such
as concurrent charging and coordination of single-phase and three-phase charging, with
the scope of adding phase balancing as a possible ancillary service.
In this thesis, a physical parking lot is selected as the background scene archetype,
located on the Risø campus of the Technical University of Denmark. Part of the physical
demonstrations is the proposed controlled charging strategy with autonomous phase
switching. It mainly consists of three parts, spike elimination, phase-switching decision,
and conflict avoidance. The simulation model is implemented in Matlab Simulink.
A group of user connection profile from a real office parking lot is accepted to validate the
proposed strategy, and the performance is assessed under the medium and high three-phase
EV penetration. The simulation result shows that our strategy could limit the total
power consumption under the limitation of the power reference. In addition, the system
unbalance (loading of phases) is reduced by 35.71% and 27.63% under the medium and
high penetration separately. Additionally, the compatibility of the strategy is verified by
the involvement of network communication delay and the EV start-up characteristics.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background and Motivation
In 2015, the Paris Agreement was adopted at the United Nations Climate Change
Conference, covering several vital points facing global climate change, such as mitigation,
adaptation, finance, technology, ability construction, and transparency. Various countries
around the world proposed their implementation solution. In June 2021, the European
Climate Law wrote into law the goal that Europe’s economy and society will become
climate neutral by 2050 and reduce net greenhouse gas emissions by at least 55% by
2030 [3]. China also announced that it would provide more effective policies to have CO2
emissions peak before 2030, and carbon neutrality will be achieved by 2060 [4]. Although
the Paris Agreement represents the main direction of global green transformation, it is only
the minimum action required to protect the environment. Therefore, the new development
principle shall be built: innovative, coordinating, ecological, open, and shared. The historic
opportunity can be seized, containing a new round of technical revolution and industrial
transformation. Then, a significant resultant will be formed to push the global economic
recovery after the pandemic and facilitate sustainable development.
The popularization of electric vehicles (EVs) is a powerful method to reduce CO2 emissions
in many countries, and most vehicle manufacturers are also engaged in this competition.
Recent reports show that the global EV stock has hit 10 million mark in 2020 [1]. Some
predictions show that more than 100 million EVs will be on the road until 2035, and
according to the International Energy Agency, the target of EV production will be 548
million by 2040 [5].

emission, including CO2, will be reduced. However, it leads to an enormous load demand
because the average battery size is anticipated to be 70 - 80kWh in high-income countries[6].
The charging demand, more specifically the charging power, is a tall challenge that needs
to be addressed to prevent overloading or voltage issues in the grid [7]. On top of that,
distribution system operators(DSOs) are facing a high integration demand for distributed
renewable energy sources (DERs), such as wind turbines and solar photovoltaic panels [8].
Thus, making grid planning a dilemma between expensive reinforcement costs and exploiting
demand side flexibility [9]. Demand side flexibility, with EVs in particular, is a promising
tool to mitigate such challenges [10]. Here, smart charging of EVs is more feasible than
integration of residential battery storages [11]. The authors of [12] have listed potential
ancillary services EVs can provide via unidirectional smart charging.
Besides, phase unbalance is widespread worldwide [13]. More than half of low-voltage
substations suffer from a significant asymmetric load combination in the UK. For example,
the highest phase load is up to 50% higher than the lowest phase [13]. Furthermore, phase
unbalance is the primary reason for overheating and premature failures in induction motors
[13]. Its main reasons are obvious, such as an uneven allocation, random load behavior,
network structural asymmetry, or the occurrence of asymmetrical faults. It will lead to
some consequences, such as network capacity waste, extra energy loss, and device damage
[13]. In Denmark, the three-phase connection is dominant for end users, and DSOs are
challenged to guide users’ local electrical installation, leading to a high degree of load
imbalance [14]. The same situation appears when EV is charging because it is hard to
guide users to connect their EV symmetrically. Fortunately, the time of vehicle-charger
connection is much longer than the need of actual charging process [15], which provides
some coordination spaces to mitigate the imbalance. Moreover, an ancillary service could
be provided to DSO, and load balancing on a larger scale becomes possible.
In this thesis, an EV cluster charging unbalance problem will be discussed, and a solution
implemented by Simulink will be proposed. The investigation aims to reduce the load
imbalance within a parking lot and dynamically schedule the allocation of the charging
phase. A potential flexibility service will be provided based on the charging control strategy
in a distributed architecture, and a group of actual user data will be accepted to validate
the model.
1.2
Literature Review
So far, multiple methods have been proposed to keep phase balance, such as analytical
solution, optimization method, heuristic algorithm, particle swarm algorithm, genetic
algorithm, and game theory. Wang [16] reveals that phase balancing can be equivalent
to an integer partitioning problem, which is belong to an NP-complete problem. The
article has summarized several different heuristic algorithms, such as exhaustive search
and backtracking algorithm, greedy algorithm, simulated annealing, genetic algorithm,
and dynamic programming, and compared them. Each algorithm has its unique merits.
For example, dynamic programming has the best performance but also the slowest. The
limited potential option will lead the genetic algorithm collapse to an exhaustive search
when the user scenario is not large enough. Alam [17] has proposed a dynamic mitigation

approach with community energy storage to avoid the disadvantage of other approaches.
The neutral voltage and the total neutral current are involved as an index of unbalance.
Sun [18] has proposed a distributed real-time algorithm that involves energy storage in
the whole system, and its performance is better than other greedy algorithms. Hong [19]
has proposed a centralized operation strategy to minimize the system loss and relieve the
phase overloading problem using the DC micro-grid installed capacity, which is realized
by a typical iterative algorithm and can be considered as a distribution system power
quality improving supplementary method.
Chen [20] has proposed a game-theoretic
approach performed by plug-in electric vehicles, which introduced financial incentives,
phase balancing remunerations, and encouraged vehicle owners to assist the grid operator
in dispatch. Although it is a non-smooth non-cooperative game, the existence of its Nash
equilibrium is demonstrated by transforming it into a smooth extended game, and both
stakeholders will benefit from it financially. Jimenez [21] has proposed a Deterministic
Crowding algorithm to switch the single-phase customers’ connection by using an imbalance
target value and minimizing the number of required switching customers.
On the other hand, some articles also discussed the economic cost when the system is
kept unbalanced. For example, Kong [22] has proposed a customized cluster-wise Gaussian
process regression method to predict the overall unbalance cost when the data is scarce
in a low voltage network that only needs the annual peak current and the overall power
consumption over a year. Li [23] has proposed a novel method to calculate the long-
term marginal cost pricing for the network operator, based on the unused capacity to
offer a forward-looking economic evaluation reference. Ma[24] has proposed the accurate
additional reinforcement cost calculation formula and proved that: a slight imbalance could
cause a high cost on the main feeders; the predicted cost will increase exponentially when
utilization is almost 100%; the main feeder is more susceptible to imbalance.
1.3
Scope and Limitation
The investigation scene will be limited to a parking lot containing eight chargers. Each
charger has two sockets that can be connected arbitrarily by EV users. Therefore, there
could be up to 16 vehicles involved in our scene simultaneously. The electrical power and
communication architecture has been defined either, as shown in Figure 1.2.
The parking lot is based on a distributed control architecture [25], which includes a cloud
aggregator, a smart meter, an enabled virtual aggregator (VA), seven disabled virtual

PROBLEM FORMULATION
2.1
Charging Patterns
EV charging patterns can be classified as two categories, uncoordinated and coordinated.
The uncoordinated charging, also called dumb charging, means that the EV will start the
charging process immediately when connected to the charger and continue the charging
until it is fully charged or disconnected [5]. The load demand for uncoordinated charging
depends on the connection profile [27], or as known as coincidence factor, which means
that it does not have any automotive control logic. Reserved load capacity relies on the
numerical sum of charging power. The number of potential mountable charger sockets
will be low when the load capacity is limited at the distributed network. The peak load
could be frightful while EVs connect to our chargers intensively, but there will be some
forgotten capacity on less connection period. Fortunately, the method can ensure charging
performance and satisfy the load demand.
In order to solve the disadvantage of uncoordinated charging, controlled charging emerged.
Initially, it is based on the integrated phase modeling, which means the alternating current
system is symmetrical at every moment and simplifies the three-phase AC circuit to
a single-phase circuit, which ignores the difference between the three phases. In this
condition, the controlled charging strategy focus on involving the power reference as an
external limitation and dynamically adjusts the charging power of each charger, making the
total power demand limited, which lays a solid foundation for a variable power reference.
However, this version still has some apparent defects. For example, it does not consider
the charging situation in reality, such as the single-phase charging EV. When single-
phase charging EVs connect to our parking lot, phase unbalance will be brought into the
distribution network. The cost of the distribution system operator(DSO) will increase
either.
Based on the aforementioned controlled charging strategy, an advanced method is proposed,
which involves the independent phase modeling to mirror the truth and phase switching
trying to weaken the potential consequence to the distribution network. Significantly, the
independent phase modeling faithfully simulates the three-phase AC system structure,
revealing the load imbalance problem in diverse scenarios.
It can assist chargers in
estimating the load assignment situation within the parking lot and compensate for
the imbalance autonomously based on available information. Furthermore, a behind-of-
meter(BTM) ancillary service can be provided to DSO by setting the power reference of
three phases separately, balancing the load in a larger area and enhancing the economic
benefit. Additionally, a balanced load assignment will cut the wastage of circuits and
devices down and improve the benefit further.

2.2
Control Architecture
The control architecture of EV charging strategy can be roughly divided into three
structures, centralized control, distributed control, and decentralized control. Compared
to a entirely decentralized architecture, a distributed architecture requires direct
communication between control elements. Compared to a centralized architecture, the
autonomy of remote control elements increases [25].
• The centralized architecture refers to taking the cloud aggregator as the decision-
making and scheduling center, treating a single charger as the role of a sensor and
an actuator to collect information and execute actions according to the scheduling
commands issued by the cloud.
• In the distributed architecture, the control strategy is dispersed in most of the control
elements to improve the robustness of the whole system. Likewise, it can make the
system highly extensible, which implicitly requires control elements to be standalone
and operate autonomously.
• The decentralized architecture means that the scheduling behavior is completely
delegated to the charger’s control algorithm. The charger makes corresponding
output adjustments on the basis of its own measurements and information collection.
The information flow of the centralized architecture is comparatively simple, so it needs to
deploy more computing resources and a high-speed communication network between the
cloud and local actuators. This also allows it to have the best monitoring of the system and
can guarantee an optimal and fair energy assignment. However, its stability has pitfalls,
and the failure of a key element can cause the entire system outage.
The decentralized architecture is highly flexible and suitable for large-scale deployment. Its
complete decoupling between control elements makes it less dependent on communication
network, but this also leads to control algorithms that can only attempt to obtain local
optimal decision based on limited information and may sometimes miss the global optimum.
The distributed control architecture combines the advantages of both of them, excellent
expansibility, strong stability, and high-quality scheduling decisions. All control elements
can execute their algorithms autonomously and communicate with other elements.
Therefore, all of them can obtain sufficient information to assist in finding the global
optimal decision and effectively avoid the situation of converging to the local optimum.

as voltage unbalance reduction, capacity management, and loss reduction. BTM services
are provided by the service provider to users, which could contain load coordination and

Power Supply
Our parking lot will house eight chargers with two plug sockets in each. According to the
IEC-61851 standard [28], a socket should be able to supply the charging current to EV
from six to 16 amps, that is, 1.38 to 3.68 kW, in each phase, and these chargers satisfy

the grid capacity is limited to 43kW on three-phase, i.e., 14.33kW on each phase. It is
a contradiction between the grid’s capacity and the chargers’, which is a chance for the
virtual aggregator and the controlled charging strategies to show their effect.

3.2
Spike Elimination
A new EV connection will bring an abrupt transient power spike to the charging system.
When the total power has stabilized around the reference power, an unexpected transient
spike could go far beyond the limitation, increasing the risk of system overload. As a result,
a spike elimination method is proposed, shown in Figure 3.3.
Here, a concept called the primary phase is defined. The primary phase is the supplying
phase while an EV is charging in single-phase mode. Because the phase connection has
been rotated on every socket, the primary phases of the socket are different. As shown
in Figure 1.2, the virtual aggregator will read the power reference as an essential input
parameter at first and wait for a new EV connection. If an EV is ready to charge, it
will determine whether the three-phase charging is possible. In the case of three-phase
charging, the power reference will be minor by the maximum charging power; otherwise,
only the power reference on the primary phase will be reduced by the same amount. Then,
the calculation result shall be compared with zero. If it is less than zero, the new power
reference will be zero; conversely, it will be the calculation result.

The primary phase was defined in the preceding subsection, and the secondary phase also

3.4
Conflict Avoidance
Although the method of phase switching can effectively solve the unbalance problem, it still
introduces some new potential risks. For example, when all charging stations in the parking
lot are continuously trying to switch their charging modes to balance the power demand,
since they cannot fully communicate with each other, competition or game phenomenon
will be inevitable, which will eventually lead to uncontrollable oscillations at the point of
common coupling. Therefore, two conflict avoidance methods are proposed, a synchronous
conflict avoidance mechanism and an asynchronous conflict avoidance mechanism.
3.4.1
Asynchronous Mechanism
Asynchronous collision avoidance is a notice-response coordination mechanism based
on the communication network, which is mainly composed of request messages and
acknowledgement(ACK) messages. The virtual aggregator plays the role of the information
aggregation and forwarding center, the server side; the role of the charger is the executor,
the client side.

The virtual aggregator will work on the wait state in the most of time. If any kind of notice
or request is received, it will return the corresponding ACK message. When the total power
demand message is arrived, it will calculate the power reference error and send it to all
chargers.
Figure 3.4. The State Machine of Virtual Aggregator
The state machine diagram of VA is shown in Figure 3.4. Its principal function is to receive

receives an operation notice message from any charger, it will log the message and return
an ACK by broadcasting.

3.4.2
Synchronous Mechanism
A synchronization coordination mechanism can also effectively solve the problem of
competition and game among chargers. As shown in Figure 3.9, the method allocates the
time slices for chargers to adjust their supplied power. Twenty seconds is a time slice, of
which the first three seconds are prepared for charger controller to switch the charging
mode or adjust the output power. The next 17 seconds are used for the system to absorb
the shock caused by the controller adjustment and reach a new steady state. The next 20
seconds will be assigned to another charger controller and do the same things.
20 seconds is a time circulation. The first 3 seconds are assigned to charger controller’s
switching decision, and the following 17 seconds are used to wait for the stabilization of
the system.
Figure 3.9. The Timeline of the Synchronous Conflict Avoidance
3.4.3
Comparison
When comparing the two conflict avoidance mechanisms, the asynchronous one is based
primarily on an object-oriented design. The sequence of events determines the order of
behaviors. However, the synchronous mechanism is based on a process-oriented design,
whose actions are determined by a timeline. The asynchronous mechanism has more
incredible implementation difficulty but can achieve better performance of spike elimination
and phase balance. The synchronization mechanism is easier to be implemented while it
also brings a complex time slice calibration. An appropriate time slice should be determined
considering stability and operational efficiency.

Study Cases
There are four cases designed to evaluate the validation of proposed strategy:
• Case 1: Medium three-phase EV penetration.
• Case 2: High three-phase EV penetration.
• Case 3: Wireless network transmission delay involved with medium penetration.
• Case 4: EV start-up characteristics involved with high penetration.
First, a basic and typical study case is defined, which contains 50% three-phase EV
penetration, and these EVs are unevenly assigned across the three phases. The case of low
three-phase EV penetration is ignored. Because too few three-phase EVs means that no
matter how VA dispatched, there is not enough capacity to compensate for the imbalance.
Secondly, a case of high three-phase EV penetration is defined. Perceptually, the more
three-phase EVs there are, the more effective our proposed charging strategy will be. But
a high three-phase EV penetration implies that load imbalance would be less severe than
medium penetration. Therefore, the performance of phase balancing by our strategy may
become more perfect in terms of results, but may become smaller in terms of improvement.
According to the electrical architecture of parking lot, a wireless communication network is
designed to carry control signals between smart meter, VA and charger controllers. However,
in the real world, due to the large number of electrical and communication equipment
in the parking lot, the electromagnetic compatibility environment is often complicated.
The distance between VA and the furthest charger cannot be ignored. Although, taking
WiFi network as an example, the maximum transmission delay within 200m defined in
802.11 protocol should be less than or equal to 1 microsecond [31]. But in actual condition,
considering the packet loss, re-transmission and the calculation consumption of the sending

6
CONCLUSION
6.1
Summary
This thesis proposed a controlled charging strategy with autonomous phase-switching
function for an aggregation of EVs within a parking lot scene and implemented it in a
simulation model for validation and verification.
At the beginning, the differences between uncoordinated charging and controlled charging,
integrated phase modelling and separated phase modelling are compared, illustrating the
formulation of proposed strategy. The main types of control architecture are explained,
distinguishing the centralized, decentralized, and distributed structure. The potential
flexibility services provided by EVs are listed in the end, providing a perspective of EV
charging.
Then, assumptions of background scene, i.e. the parking lot located on a working place, are
described, and our strategy is described on theory horizon. Due to a heavy relation with
physical parking lot, there are many limitations restraining the potentiality of development.
The proposed strategy mainly consists of three parts, spike elimination, phase-switching
decision, and conflict avoidance. Additionally, in terms of the conflict avoidance part,
an asynchronous mechanism and a synchronous mechanism are proposed at the same
time. However, because of the inherent characteristics of selected simulation tool, the
asynchronous mechanism is abandoned despite it will perform better than the other. The
advantage of synchronous mechanism is easy-implemented and robust, making the system
stable.
In the simulation model chapter, all control elements are abstracted to a logical model, such
as smart meter, virtual aggregator, charger controller, and EV. All of implementation are
basically on the basis of the description in methodology chapter. Especially, the efficiency
of converter is involved in the model of EV increasing the authenticity.
Finally, a group of user profile is accepted, which comes from a real office parking lot, and
four study cases are used to evaluate the performance, which is medium three-phase EV
penetration, three-phase EV penetration, wireless network transmission delay involved
with medium penetration, and EV start-up characteristics involved with high penetration.
According to the simulation result, our strategy is able to keep the total power demand
under the limitation of power reference compared to uncoordinated charging strategy.
35.71% and 27.63% of charging load imbalance are reduced under medium penetration
and high penetration, and the strategy has a great compatibility of communication delay
and EV start-up characteristics. Besides, economic benefit is discussed in terms of two
directions, capital expenditure, and operating expenditure.

6. Conclusion
6.2
Future work
Because of the time limit, there are some regrets remained until the end of investigation:
• The spike elimination function still has some room for improvement. A separate
time logic can be involved for the newly connected EV and other charging EVs. The
new EV shall have a higher priority to occupy the power capacity reserved by power
reference decrease, and then, it will have a better spike eliminating performance.
• The asynchronous mechanism could be implemented by an object-oriented simulation
tool, which matches the distributed control architecture better, and can provide a
higher expansibility.
Additionally, the completed work also could be extended to an advanced horizon with
some extra elements:
• A group of dynamic power reference in a larger scene could be established to offer a
load balancing service to DSO. The coordination strategy between several parking
lots and other points of common coupling could be interesting.
• A group of more precise and diverse EV simulation model could be abstracted based
on a comprehensive market investigation, which could significantly enhance the
validity of further strategy design.
• The hardware design of charger produced many limitations. A set of more flexible
and controllable charger hardware will change the strategy design from the underlying
layer.
• The distributed renewables generation, such as PV panel, local wind turbine, or
energy storage device could be involved in the further investigation, improving the
self-consumption or system balancing.
