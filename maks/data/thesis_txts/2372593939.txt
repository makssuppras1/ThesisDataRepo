Abstract
This thesis deals with the adjustment of the static and dy-
namic properties of the tilting-pad journal bearings.
Two
diﬀerent design solutions to adjust the properties will inves-
tigated and compared to a conventionally operating tilting-
pad journal bearing for diﬀerent static loads, geometerical
preloads and journal angular velocities. The two designs are:
a static force behind the lower pad and oil injection through
the lower pad. The properties are investigated experimentally
on a single degree of freedom, 2 pad, load on pad, tilting-pad
journal bearing by performing static analysis to determine
the pivot point reaction forces and journal position inside the
bearing and experimental modal analysis to determine the
stiﬀness and the damping of the bearing. Oil injection was
concluded to give the most agressive adjustment of the static
and dynamic properties due to a signiﬁcant change in the jour-
nal equilibrium position, whereas the static force behind the
lower pad result in a moderate adjustment of the properties.
The experimental ﬁndings have been compared to numerical
simulations and an overall agreement in the tendencies of the
results were obtained.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In the oil and gas industry there is a great demand for increased eﬀeciency and productivity in
order to stay competitive against competing ﬁrms and to protect the environment. This puts
a demand on increased performance of rotating machinary. That being for example increasing
operational velocities, reduction of wear, increased stability, increased eﬃciency and decreased
down-time.
In turbo machinary it is also desirable to reduce the overall weight. Simultaneously increasing
the operational angular velocities and decreasing the weight will eventually lead to a decreased
stability margin. It is therefore important to design machine elements that can dissipate the
vibrational energy in order to increase stability and minimize the vibrations. Such machine
elements can for example be bearings.
Bearings are structual machine elements used in rotating machinary to support a moving ma-
chine part with the least amount of friction. The motion is often with respect to a non-moving
surface and the bearing therefore provides the interface between moving and non-moving parts.
Bearings are therefore used extensively in rotating machinary and a large variety of diﬀerent
bearings exist. Bearings can be divided into two diﬀerent kinds of contact types; Rolling ele-
ment bearings and sliding bearings. In rolling element bearings the contact between the bearing
races rely on rolling elements. These types of bearings have low friction but may suﬀer from
instability at high angular velocities. Sliding bearings have its bearing surfaces sliding against
each other usually with a ﬂuid inbetween. Such bearings are refered to as ﬂuid bearings. A
widely used type of ﬂuid bearing is the plain journal bearing which rely on continuous lubrica-
tion, either selﬁnduced or by injection. Such a bearing consists of a rotor or journal (rotating)
and a bearing bush (stationary). The thin oil ﬁlm between the journal and the bearing bush
provides both low wear since surface to surface contact is eliminated and a damping eﬀect from
the squeeze ﬁlm eﬀect of the ﬂuid. Problems with self-induced vibrations may however occur at
high speeds. Another problem is the cross coupling in stiﬀness between two diﬀerent directions
of movement. A movement of the journal in the vertical direction causes a movement of the
journal in the horizontal direction. The vibrations and the cross coupling can be reduced by
modifying the bearing bush geometry by cutting oil grooves or making the bore lemon shaped.
A superior design, however, is the tilting-pad journal bearing (TPJB). The bearing bush is cut
into segments (pads) which are free to rotate independently around a pivot point. An illustra-
tion of a four pad, load on pad, TPJB can be seen in Figure 1.1. The pads rest on a pivot

converging wedge. This results in insigniﬁcant cross coupling and for some loading conﬁgura-

journal is in the vertical direction. The static and dynamic behaviors of these kinds of bearings
have been investigated thoroughly by many diﬀerent authors and it is possible to predict the
properties of the bearings via simulations. The theoretical models of TPJB’s however usually
predict more damping than is actually measured via experiments. This is thought to be due to
an inappropriate assumption of rigid pads and pivot points [6]. The pivot point will, as a result
of the hydrodynamic forces inside the bearing, deform elastically and the bearing clearance will
increase. Even small changes in bearing clearances have a large eﬀect on the properties of the
bearing as the clearances in journal bearings are usually very small.
A common characteristic for conventional journal bearings, and for that matter conventional
TPJB’s, are that they operate completely passive meaning that no controlling in theory is
necessary. Opposed to, example, an active magnetic bearing which rely on a well designed
controller. This however means that the once the journal bearing has been dimensioned and
constructed the properties of the bearing cannot be modiﬁed during operation. That being for

properties of TPJB’s.
A well investigated method to alter the bearing properties is to inject high pressure oil into
the bearing and thereby alter the pressure distribution in the oil ﬁlm. This has been done in
previous work by Santos [13] and have been concluded to give good results with regards to
adjusting the dynamic properties.
Another possible way of adjusting the properties of a bearing is described by Santos [14].

2.1
Working Principle of Journal Bearings
Journal bearings belong to the category of sliding bearings refered to as axial ﬂuid bearings.
The bearing relies on a hydrostatic pressure build up in the thin oil ﬁlm between the journal
and the bearing bush. This pressure build up forms as a result of the oil being forced into
a wedge by the rotation of the journal. The diﬀerence in the radius of the journal and the
bearing bush, R and Rs respectively, as well as the eccentricity e determines the magnitude
of the wedge. This is scematically shown in Figure 2.1, where the diﬀerence in journal and
bearing bush radii is greatly exaggerated to illustrate the wedge. Normally the clearance in

The pressure proﬁle sketched in Figure 2.1 is given as a result of two diﬀerent types of ﬂow;
Coutte ﬂow and Poiseuille ﬂow. Zooming in on the bearing gap and initially disregarding the
wedge, meaing that the journal and the bearing center coincide, one will see a Coutte ﬂow, see
in the top of Figure 2.2. The two surfaces are assumed parallel and separated by a narrow gap.
It is assumed that the ﬂuid is only ﬂowing in-plane reducing the problem to 2d. The upper
surface is the surface of the journal and the lower surface is the surface of the bearing bush.
The upper surface moves with the velocity U = ΩR while the lower suface is stationary. The
ﬂow is fully developed and laminar meaning that the velocity distribution of the ﬂuid is the
same whatever position, along the gap, is chosen. The ﬂow proﬁle is obtained by assuming an
incompressible, isothermal and Newtonian ﬂuid. The no-slip boundary condition governs the
ﬂuid velocity at the surface of the journal and the bearing and makes it equal to the surface
velocity. Since the ﬂuid is Newtonian the velocity distribution can only be triangular. There is
no change in pressure as there is no pressure source.
A purely pressure driven ﬂow is called a Poiseuille ﬂow and is illustrated in the middle of
Figure 2.2. Both the upper and the lower surface are in this case stationary while a pressure
source forces the ﬂuid through the gap inbetween the two parallel surfaces.
The pressure
decreases linearly and the same assumptions for the ﬂuid as stated previously are still valid.
The no-slip boundary condition shapes the pressure proﬁle into a parabolic shape, see Figure 2.2.
The gap is now changed into a narrowing wedge of a small angle with the same assumptions
of ﬂuid behavior and no-slip boundary condtion as described previously. The upper surface,
still the journal, moves with the velocity U = ΩR, while the lower surface, bearing bush, is
stationary, see in the bottom of Figure 2.2. The boundary velocity forces the ﬂuid into the
gap, but maintaining the triangular velocity proﬁle along the gap will violate ﬂow continuity
since the ﬂow is in-plane. Therefore the velocity proﬁles have to be modiﬁed. The proﬁle in the
ﬁrst position, in the widest gap, must bulge backwards, while the proﬁle in the third postion,
in the narrowest gap, must bulge forwards. Refering to the Poiseuille ﬂow a forward bulge is
assosiated with a decreasing pressure. This is the case for the proﬁle in the third position. In
the ﬁrst position the pressure must therefore increase since the proﬁle bulges backwards. It is
clear that in one speciﬁc position the velocity proﬁle must take a triangular shape. This is the
case for the second position and this position is also associated with a zero pressure gradient
as in the pure Coutte ﬂow. The maximum pressure will therefore be in this speciﬁc position
along the gap. This nature of a ﬁrst increasing and then decreasing pressure suggest that the
wedge possesses a load carrying capacity. This is also the case, but the performance is greatly
dependent of the wedge angle, which normally has to be relatively small. The force on the
journal is found by integrating the pressure over the area. The main power loss in this kind of
bearing comes from heating up the oil and the shearing of the oil.
Alternatively the load carrying capacity can also be obtained using oil injection. This makes the
bearing hydrodynamically lubricated and also has the nice feature, if well designed, of reducing
wear in the bearing during start up, as the journal at rest has contact with the bearing surface

The described principal of operation of a journal bearing captures the actual operation to some
extend. It was implicitly assumed that deformations of both the journal and the bearing surface
do not take place. This is however not true for actual bearings since some deformation of both
the journal and the bearing surface takes place. Also the end ﬂow of oil escaping out of the
bearing ends was neglected as the problem was reduced to a 2d problem. Oil will to some
extend ﬂow out of the bearing ends and the oil pressure will drop to ambient pressure.
Since journal bearings are highly dependent on the ﬂuid forces from the oil ﬁlm the bearings are
sensitive to changes in oil viscosity. Oil viscosity tends to decrease with increasing temperature
and with decreasing oil viscosity the load carrying capacity of the bearing decreases. With
decreasing viscosity the shear forces in the bearing however decreases and the power loss in
the bearing is reduced. Normally cooling in journal bearings are necessary and this is easily
obtained by cooling the feed oil going into the bearing.

2.2
Modiﬁed Reynolds Equation
The pressure distribution in the oil ﬁlm between journal and bearing bush is governed by
Reynolds equation. Reynolds equation is derived from the Navier-Stokes equation and is a
partial diﬀerential equation describing the pressure distribution in a thin viscous ﬂuid. The
equation has been expanded by Santos and Russo [15] in order to model the pressure modiﬁed
by the oil injection. This equation will be refered to as the modiﬁed Reynolds equation (MRE).
The full derivation of the MRE will not be carried out here as it is out of the scope of the
report. The equation will however be presented and the assumptions of the equation will be
stated.
In order to model the TPJB one has to deﬁne a series of diﬀerent coordinate systems as the
TPJB is a complex 3d geometry. In total there are four diﬀerent coordinate systems. This
is shown for the j-th pad in Figure 2.3 and Figure 2.4. The pad rotation is given by α and
the translation is given by ξ. The coordinate systems are: 1: An inertial frame of reference
deﬁned as I(Xr,Yr,Zr). This coordinate system is stationary. 2: An auxiliary frame B(x,y,z)
deﬁned for the pivot point oriented at an angle ϕ relative to the inertial frame of reference. 3:
A moving coordinate system B′(x′,y′,z′) attached to the j-th pad in the pivot loaction. This
system is free to follow the pad as it either translates or rotates. The system is oriented at an
angle α relative to auxiliary frame B. 4: A cylindrical coordinate system ¯B(¯x,¯y,¯z) mapping to
the pad surface using the angle β relative to B′(x′,y′,z′) and the pad thickness l0.
The MRE will be described in the frame ¯B as this allows for the direction of the injection ﬂow
proﬁles to remain constant. The main assumptions of the MRE are:
• Newtonian and incompressible ﬂuid
• No change in pressure in the radial direction
• The body forces of the ﬂuid are negligible
• Rigid pad and journal

Opposite the direct modiﬁcation of the pressure in the oil ﬁlm originating from the oil injection

Introducing a static force behind the pads eﬀectively compresses the bearing and forces the
pads closer to the journal i.e. reduces the oil ﬁlm thickness h. It should be noted that the term
governing the oil injection is set equal to zero, F = 0, when a static force is put behind the
pads. The two diﬀerent design solutions to adjust the properties of a TPJB are therefore seen
to work in two diﬀerent ways.
Knowing the pressure distribution of the oil ﬁlm, the ﬂuid ﬁlm forces may be found by inte-
grating over the area of the pad. The MRE may not be solved analytically so a numerical
implementation is necessary. This has already been done and implemented in a TPJB program
lastly updated by Mønsted [10] to also include the pivot deformation ξ, see Figure 2.3. The
pivot point is modelled as a spring. This program can be used to predict the static and dynamic
properties of TPJB’s. The dynamic properties are determined via a pad pertubation method
described by Allaire [1]. This results in a full system with a square stiﬀness and damping
matrix of the size n × n, where n is the amount of degrees of freedom (DOF) of the TPJB.
These DOF’s are in our case the translations of the journal and the translations and rotations
of the tilting-pads. To be able to compare these dynamic properties with the ones determined
experimentally, it is prefered to do a reduction of the full system matrices, so that the dynamic
properties of the bearing are described only by the DOF’s of the journal. The reduction method
used in the program was originally proposed by Lund [9] and assumes that the pad movement is
synchronous with the journal movement and that only the journal is subjected to an excitaion
force with the frequency ω. This allows for the dynamic coeﬃcients to be described in the
frequency domain ω, which is same domain the experimentally obtained frequency response is
described in. The internal working and governing equations of the TPJB program will not be
presented here. For further reading the reader is refered to [10].
It has however been necessary to extend the current program so that the static force behind the
pads can be modelled as this function is not readily available. This has been done by applying
a force equivalent to the force from the pressure in oil in the housing cylinder, see Figure 1.2.
This force unloads the pivot point.
All numerical simulations made with the TPJB program are presented in the chapter concerning
the numerical results.

Guiding Brackets for the Tilting Pads
During the initial testing of the bearing it was noted that the guiding brackets for the tilting-

pivot insert would solely rest on the brackets resulting in a very low stiﬀness measured pivot
stiﬀness. As the loading would increase the brackets would deﬂect elasticly and at some point
the pad would actually be resting on the pivot point resulting in an increased pivot stiﬀness.
To solve this problem the guiding brackets were modiﬁed with larger guiding holes so that no
forces in the vertical direction can be taken up by the guiding brackets. The main function of
the guiding brackets is to prevent the tilting-pads from being dragged around in the bearing
by the rotation of the journal. The modiﬁcation does not alter this function as the guiding
holes are made oval which allows for vertical movement of the pivot insert. The original and

4.2
Static Testing
This section describes the methods used to determine the static properties of the bearing. Due
to high temperature sensitivity of the force transducers mounted inside the pads it has been
determined to perform the static analysis of the reaction forces in the bearing by increasing the
rotational speed of the journal linearly from 0 to 82 Hz in 280 seconds keeping the temperature
of the sensors somewhat constant, but still slow enough to let the journal ﬁnd its equilibrium
position. The journal position inside the bearing is measured by allowing the journal to ﬁnd its
equilibrium so that the temperature inside the bearing is constant. This has been experienced
to take some time since, especially, the angular velocity of the journal Ωhas a strong inﬂuence
on the operating temperature of the bearing, see Appendix A, Equation A.5.

When the frequency response of a signal is calculated averaging may be used to ﬁlter out noise.
The method used in this project follows the method described by Welch [18]. Initially the signal
is divided into blocks. Welch’s method then uses two diﬀerent tools in the averaging proces,
overlap and windowing. This reduces noise in the power spectra in exchange for a reduction in
the frequency resolution.
The fundamental assumption when doing Fourier transformations is that the signal is continous
and periodic. This is however not the case for real life data as this is discontinous. In Welch’s
method this is worked around by applying a weighting function or window to each block. The
window gives most inﬂuence to the middle of signal in the block and forces the signal at the
ends of the block towards zero. This makes the signal seem continous. Applying a window
however gives rise to loss of information since part of the signal is reduced. To avoid this the
blocks overlap each other. In this report a Hanning window will be used.
To illustrate the eﬀect of averaging an example is shown in Figure 4.6. The test was conducted
with a preload mp = 0.48, angular velocity Ω= 0 and a static force fr = 500 N. For test
settings see subsection 4.3.2. Three diﬀerent block sizes are used (full signal length = 1 block,
half signal length = 2 blocks and 4096 points ≈233 blocks). 50 % overlap and a Hanning
window have been applied. It is clear that by increasing the number of blocks the amount of
noise in the frequency response is reduced. Even at 2 blocks a great amount of noise is removed.
At ≈233 blocks almost all noise is removed and the frequency response is smooth.
All of this may seem ﬁne, but one has to be carefull when increasing the number of blocks as
information might be lost in the process. Unless otherwise stated a block size of 4096 points,
50 % overlap and a Hanning window will be used on all recorded data to calculate the FRF.

4.3.2
Test Settings
In order to obtain good and usefull measurements for the dynamic analysis one has to consider
the test settings such as sampling frequency, measuring time and shaker settings (signal type,
signal magnitude, period and minimum and maximum frequency). All have inﬂuence on the
recorded data. This section will brieﬂy explain the main thoughts of the values for the chosen
settings.
In the work presented in [16] a similar experimental campaign was conducted on the excact same
test rig as described in this report. It was concluded that a faster convergence was achieved
by sending a harmonic swept (chirp signal) to the electromagnetic shaker instead of a random
signal. A chirp signal is therefore chosen as the excitation signal. The period of the signal is
set to 30 s. In order to increase the coherence the chirp signal must be repeated a number of
times. The total measuring time is therefore set to 4 minutes or 240 seconds giving a total of
8 repetitions. This is two more repetitions than concluded satisfactory in [16].
The fundamental natural frequency ωn of the system at rest (Ω= 0) is in the vicinity of 170
Hz depending on the static load. It has therefore been concluded that the chirp signal must
be in the frequency range of 30 Hz to 300 Hz in order to capture the full frequency repsonse.
According to the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem the sampling frequency must be at least
two times the frequency of interest. By setting the sampling frequency to 2000 Hz this is easily
achieved.
The last shaker setting is the magnitude of the signal. According to [2] the amplitude of the
journal movement must be kept within 20 % of the radial clearance of the bearing in order to
comply with the assumption of linear stiﬀness and damping coeﬃcients of the oil ﬁlm. The
above requirement is satisﬁed by having a maximum amplitude of the dynamic force of ≈40
N. By decreasing the maximum amplitude the time for convergence of the coherence function
is greatly increased due to noise from diﬀerent souces like the electrical motor and vibrations of
the entire structure. By increasing the maximum dynamic force to more than ≈40 N nonlinear
eﬀects may arise due to violation of the assumption of linear stiﬀness and damping coeﬃcients
of the oil ﬁlm. In Figure 4.7 an example of nonlinear behavior can be seen. The operational
conditions are a preload mp = 0.48, angular velocity Ω= 81 Hz and static loading fr = 900 N.

4.3.3
Removing Unbalanced Response
As with most rotating machines an unbalance will be present in the rotating parts due to
manufactoring tolerances, wear, etc. This unbalance will result in a harmonic excitation of the
structure with the frequency Ωequal to the angular velocity of the journal. A Fast Fourier
Transformation (FFT) of the signal will reveal the harmonic excitation and its harmonics as
peaks will be present at nΩ, where n = 1,2,3,4,... This is clearly seen in Figure 4.8 as peaks are
present at 81 Hz and 162 Hz. The power of the harmonic decreases as the order n increases. In
order to lower the power of the peaks a number of diﬀerent methods can be applied. One is to
determine the displacement of the journal as function of angle of the journal. In this way the
displacement originating from the forced repsonse can be subtracted from the recorded data.
Another method is to apply a digital ﬁlter to the recorded data. A Notch ﬁlter will be suitable
in this situation since it lowers the power at speciﬁc narrow frequency band. A Notch ﬁlter will
be used in this report due to its simplicity compared to the ﬁrst method. Since the stiﬀness
and damping coeﬃcients are a function of the angular velocity, temperature of the oil, static
loading, etc., the forced response will change according to the operational parameters. A large
number of correction functions (displacement as function of rotor angle) should therefore be
made which seems like a enormous taks.
The Notch ﬁlter is applied at the speciﬁc frequencies nΩ, where n = 1,2,3,4. An example
can be seen in Figure 4.8 where the power of the harmonic excitation is lowered considerable
comparing to the FFT of the un-ﬁltered data as the peaks at 81 Hz and 162 Hz are greatly
reduced. This may seem ﬁne, but as with averaging one has to be carefull when ﬁltering the
data. Information might be lost Problems may also arise when the harmonic excitation or one
of the harmonics coincide with the natural frequency of the system since a Notch ﬁlter will
remove information at the resonance peak of the system. The Notch ﬁlter will be applied to
both the displacement signal and the force signal.

Chapter 5
Experimental Results
In the following section the results obtained from the static and dynamic measurements will
be presented. Since the dynamic coeﬃcients are a function of the excitation frequency only a
selection of the dynamic properties will be presented due to the amount of obtained data. All
of the static properties will be presented. All the dynamic results are presented in Appendix
D.
The chapter wil be divided into three diﬀerent sections and each section will be divided into a
subsection containing the static properties and a subsection containing the dynamic properties.
The ﬁrst section deals with the conventional TPJB and the inﬂuence on static load, journal
angular velocity and preload. The second section covers the inﬂuence of the static force behind
the lower pad and the third section deals with the inﬂuence of the oil injection going through
the lower pad.
5.1
Conventional TPJB
5.1.1
Static Properties
The inﬂuence of the static load fr on the reaction forces from the pivot points fpivot,1 and fpivot,2
is studied. By increasing the static load fr the lower pad is more heavily loaded resulting in a
larger reaction force from the pivot point. This is illustrated in Figure 5.1 where the preload
is kept constant at mp = 0.48 and mp = 0.73 and the static loading is varied with the values
500 N, 700 N and 900 N. The reaction forces from the pivot points are plotted as function of
the angular velocity of the journal Ω. The reaction force from the upper pivot point decreases
due to the fact that the journal is forced away from the upper pad and agianst the lower pad
deforming the tilting point. The forces inside the bearing follows Equation 3.9 which says that
the diﬀerence between the reaction forces from the lower pivot point and upper pivot point
fpivot,1 and fpivot,2 must equal the static load on the journal fr. That comply to some extent
with the measured results. As the angular velocity of the journal Ωincreases the reaction forces
from the lower and upper pivot point also increases, at least for mp = 0.73, due to an increased
oil ﬁlm pressure, until around 45 Hz where the bearing gradually loses its load carrying capacity.

The conﬁguration of the bearing is now switched over to oil injection through the lower pad.
Again the section will be divided into two parts. The ﬁrst contains the static properties and
the second contains the dynamic properties. By introducing oil injection it is expected that
the journal will be forced to a new equilibrium position. During the experiments it was noted
that the high pressure pump could not supply the desired tank pressue poil for the oil injection
due to an increased oil ﬂow to the bearing. This was caused by unproper setting of the servo
valve as this should had been more closed instead of fully open. This has resulted in a decrease
in the tank pressure to around 20-25 bar. The results presented for 30 bar and 60 bar tank
pressure are therefore close to equal to each other. In the discussion of the results the tank
pressures will, however, still be refered to as poil = 30 bar and poil = 60 bar in order to see if
there is any diviation nonetheless. Again the results for the conventionally operating bearing
will be presented so that the results may be compared to each other.

The inﬂuence on the dynamic properties of the bearing is studied. Again the bearing stiﬀness
and damping are plotted as function of the excitation frequency ω. The angular velocity is ﬁxed

N. For the case of mp = 0.48 a relatively large increase in stiﬀness is observed at low loadings.

At a preload of mp = 0.73 a slight increase in bearing stiﬀness is observed and is relatively

pivot points when introducing oil injection. The damping has also increased slightly and seems
to be relatively unaﬀected by changes in the static loading. The highest increase in damping
is obtained at a preload of mp = 0.48.
5.4
Comparison of the Diﬀerent Design Solutions
To summarize the ﬁndings from the privious three sections a short comparison of the results
will be carried out.
At a preload of mp = 0.48 the upper pad is unloaded at high loadings due to the relative large
radial clearance. Introducing the static force behind the lower pad increases the reaction force
from the upper pad slightly making the upper pad ”active”. Introducing oil injection increases
the reaction forces signiﬁcantly as the journal is forced upwards inside the bearing and close to
the upper pad. The largest eﬀect is therefore seen for oil injection. The equilibrium position of
the journal is also changed fundamentally for oil injection as the journal now travels downwards
inside the bearing as the angular velocity of the journal increases. For the conventional bearing
the journal travels upwards with increasing angular velocity of the journal. With the static force
behind the lower pad the bearing is beeing slightly compressed and therefore the equilibrium
position of the journal is relatively unchanged.
For the static force behind the lower pad a tendency in the behavior of the dynanic properties
has been observed. By increasing the stiﬀness of the bearing the damping is decreased. The
static force behind the lower pad has resulted in an increase in stiﬀness K and an decrease in
damping C for almost all cases except at the high loading fr = 900 N and the preload mp = 0.48.
Here the opposite behavior has been observed. This is caused by the non-existing reaction force
from the upper pivot point, that eﬀectively makes the bearing a single-pad bearing.
In all cases oil injection result in an increase in stiﬀness K and an increase in damping C. Oil
injection is therefore concluded to be the most aggressive form of adjusting. The increase in

Chapter 6
Numerical Results
This chapter covers the numerical simulations. A selection of the experimental results from
the previous chapter are compared to numerical simulations made by the TPJB program. A
description of the program was previously given in section 2.2. The simulations are set up to
match the test rig conﬁguration with 2 pads and load on pad. The geometrical parameters
from Table 3.1 are used to simulate the bearing. The journal has been restricted to only move
in the vertical direction to emulate the test rig.
To limit the amount of ﬁgures it has been determined to only compare a selection of the
experimental results with numerical simulations. The preload mp = 0.48 is completely left out
due to the fact that the upper pad is unloaded in a large amount of the experiments due to the
relative large radial clearance. It has not been possible to do simulations with the upper pad
unloaded as the solution will not converge. This is a generel problem with the program. If the
forces inside the bearing are too small a solution may not be found. All the presented results in
the following sections are therefore made with a preload of mp = 0.73. The oil temperature in
the bearing is set equal to the mean value of the measured lower and upper pad temperature,
see Appendix C. In this way the viscosity of the oil can be calculated using Equation 3.6 and
used in the simulations.
6.1
The Inﬂuence of the Pivot Stiﬀness on the Dynamic
Properties of the Bearing

Chapter 7
Discussion
The following chapter will summarize and present several topics related to the validity of the
obtained results, both experimentally as well as numerically.
• Reaction forces from the pivot points
The force transducers inside the tilting pads have proven diﬃcult to work with. The
transducers are working outside of thier design domain as they are made for bolted ap-
plications. The calibration of transducers are therefore gradually lost. This is caused by
a change in sensitivity of the transducer over time as they are not properly seated inside
the pad. The transducers are also made for large forces so only approximately 1/20th of
the actual measuring range is utilized in the experiments conducted in this project.
The reaction forces from the pivot points are obtained experimentally by increasing the
angular velocity of the journal linearly over 240 seconds from 0 Hz to 82 Hz. This also
introduces an error as the bearing does not have time to reach a equilibrium.
• Oil injection pressure
The injection pressure of the oil going into the bearing has been assumed to be equal to
the tank pressure. This assumption may not be completely valid as an pressure drop in
the hydraulic system is expected due to an oil ﬂow. The actual injection pressure will
therefore be lower than the tank pressure.
• Converged temperatures and temperature measurements
Some waiting time is needed in order to let the bearing ﬁnd its equilibrium with regards
to the operating temperature of the bearing. A relative large amount of oil is present
in the oil tank underneath the test rig. This oil is circulated as it is pumped from the
tank onto the bearing surface and afterwards returned to the tank. All of this oil need to
reach a working temperature before the temperature will be constant inside the bearing.
Every time a parameter is changed (angular velocity of the journal, static load, injection
pressure, etc.) the system will have a new equilibrium temperature. This temperature
may take some time be stable which means a waiting time of approximately half an hour
is expected as parameters are changed. In the experiments some of the temperatures may
not have fully reached an equilibrium. This is especially true for high angular velocities
of the journal as the temperature of the bearing dependent on the temperature squared,
see Appendix A.
67
==PAGE:81==
CHAPTER 7. DISCUSSION
68
Another thing is that the temperature of the oil ﬁlm has been assumed to be equal to
the temperature of the pad as the temperature measurements are made for the pad. This
may not be strictly true as some resistance in the conduction of the heat will be present
in the pad. The oil ﬁlm temperatures may therefore be slightly larger in magnitude than
the temperature measurements. This will have an eﬀect on the calculated oil viscosity.
• Oil viscosity
The viscosity of the oil in bearing is calculated based on the pad temperature and the
Walther formula. As with every mathematical model the viscosity based on the Walther
formula may deviate from the actual behavior of the oil viscosity. The oil viscosity is used
in the calculation of the Sommerfeld number and in the numerical simulations, so these
results might be aﬀected.
• Air bubbles in the oil
During the experiments it was noticed that there was an increased formation of air bubbles
in the lubricant oil as the angular velocity of the journal would increase. While under
operation it has been possible to extract oil from inside bearing by dismantling the high
pressure line for the lower pad at the servo valve. Oil would then be forced backwards
through the injection nozzle and the oil line by the pressure in the oil ﬁlm between the
journal and the lower pad. An increasing amount of small air bubbles in the oil has been
experienced as the velocity increases. This violates the assumption of an incompressible
ﬂuid as the air bubbles, now trapped inside the oil, will lower the bulk modulus of the
oil considerable, causing the oil to be less ”stiﬀ”. Air bubble formation is already seen at
around 30 Hz. The formation of bubbles of air in the oil is thought to be caused by the
rather unsophisticated low pressure lubrication system. Oil is sprayed onto the journal
via two sets of nozzles. As the angular velocity increases the oil is splashed around inside
the bearing housing due to the velocity of the journal, causing small air bubbles to form
in the oil. This oil is then dragged inbetween the journal and the tilting pads by the
rotation of the journal. This unfortunate behavior was however ﬁrst noticed relatively
late in the project and after most of the measurements were conducted. All measurements
are therefore aﬀected by this problem.
• The SDOF assumption
Initially it was assumed that the test rig could be described as a SDOF-system with the
up and down movement of the frame as the governing mode. This has however proven
not to be the case as other resonance peaks have been oberserved in the experimental
FRF’s. Other modes could for instance be torsion of the frame. The displacement sensor
at the end of the frame 10 is not directly positioned below the dynamic force transducer
3, meaning that the torsion will be picked up by the displacement sensor. A way to
measure the response originating from the torsional mode is to install accelerometers in
the coners of the frame, as these now will pick up the accelerations from the torsional
mode.
• Number of repetitions
In the dynamic measurements it was determined to run with a 30 second period for the
shaker excitation signal and with a total of 8 repetitions due to previous investigations
made in [16]. The eﬀect of either lowering or increasing the number of repetitions have
not been investigated in this report so it can not be said with certainty that the results
==PAGE:82==

69
will change by increasing the number repetitions. It is however noted that the coherence
is close to 1 in the interval of excitation frequencies from 50 Hz to 200 Hz. This implies
that the measurements are largely free of noise and that there is good agreement between
H1(ω) and H2(ω), which suggest good agreement between the driving force f(t) and
measured displacement Y (t).
• Bearing stiﬀness and damping
One of the main challenges has been to isolate the bearing dynamic coeﬃcients from
the dynamic coeﬃcients of the test rig. As of now the experimental dynamic coeﬃcients
are obtained for the complete system (test rig) due to the loaction of the dynamic force
transducer 3 and the high accuracy displacement sensor 10. The dynamics of the frame
itself is therefore included in the results. Due to the setup, the frame will be in a series
conﬁguration with the bearing, eﬀectively meaning that the weakest link will dominate
the results.
In order to account for this a reference stiﬀness and damping has to be
determined. Due to the nature of the series conﬁguration of springs and dampers the
calculated bearing stiﬀness K is highly dependent on the actual value of the reference
stiﬀness kref. The same thing can be said about the damping of the bearing C. Through
the static measurements it has been possible to determine a value for the reference stiﬀness
kref so that the bearing stiﬀness K may be estimated. With the current methods it has
not been possible to determine a reference damping cref. This means that the damping of
the bearing C presented in the results sections is in fact the damping of the total system
c.
• Constant stiﬀness model
A constant stiﬀness model for the pivot points was used in the numerical simulations.
The assumption may however not be strictly correct as it was concluded that the pivot
stiﬀness was in fact nonlinear and changes as a function of the pivot point reaction force.
The numerical results may therefore not follow the actual behavior of the real physical
bearing as the pivot stiﬀness is assumed constant.
• Damping of the pivot point
So far the pivot point has only been modelled as a spring. It is however expected that the
pivot point will possess some damping properties due to the dissipation of energy as the
elastic deformations take place. The damping may be modelled as stiﬀness proportional
damping, as the damping is directly linked to the deformations of the pivot point. In
Figure 7.1 the damping of the pivot point is included in the mechanical model of the
bearing investigated in this report. It is clearly seen that the pivot damping cpivot is in a
series conﬁguration with the damping of the oil ﬁlm coil, so by including the damping of
the pivot points the damping of the bearing C will be lower than for the bearing modelled
without a damping of the pivot point.
• Include the dynamics of the hydraulic system
The dynamics of the hydraulic system has not been addressed in this report. This might
have an inﬂuence on the simulations as the hydraulic system will both possess some
stiﬀness and damping that will contribute in some way to the stiﬀness and damping of
the total system.

Chapter 8
Future Work
• New low pressure oil system
A new low pressure oil system for the bearing should be designed. In conventional journal
bearings in the industry the bearing is commonly enclosed so dirt may not enter the
bearing. The enclosement is usually achieved by a radial shaft seal. In this way the
bearing can be ﬁlled with oil and the amount of air in the bearing can be minimized.
This will also solve a generel problem with test rig, as oil fumes are whirled out into the
air covering everything in a thin oil ﬁlm.
• Guiding brackets made of steel
New guiding brackets should be made of steel with an oval hole that allows for a vertical
movement of the tilting pads. The current guiding brackets are made of aluminium and
the oval holes are made by hand.
• New force transducers inside the pad
The current force transducers mounted inside the tilting pads have proven diﬃcult to work
with. It is therefore suggested that some other transducers should be used. Preferable,
the transducers should have a lower measuring range. This will greatly decrease the error
in the measurements. It might prove diﬃcult to ﬁnd new transducers with the exact
same dimensions as the current transducers. To be able to install the new transducers
one therefore has to redesign the pads so the transducers can ﬁt inside the pad.
• Investigate diﬀerent pivot stiﬀnesses
In the numerical results it was concluded that the pivot stiﬀness has great inﬂuence on
the dynamic coeﬃcients of the bearing. A natural choice would therefore be to investigate
this experimentally. The stiﬀness of the pivot point can be changed by having the pivot
inserts made in another material than steel. Such pivot inserts already exist and are made
of aluminium and brass. The stiﬀness of the pivot point will therefore decrease as the
new pivot inserts are made of materials with a lower Youngs modulus than that of steel.
• Measure actual oil ﬁlm thickness
The design of the pads allows for a displacement sensor to be mounted inside the pad. The
displacement sensor measures the distance to the journal. The static properties presented
in this report only covers the position of the journal relative to the unloaded center of
the bearing. Knowing the distance between the journal and the pads (oil ﬁlm thickness)
71
==PAGE:85==

72
it is possible to determine the deﬂection of the pivot points and thereby the clearance of
the loaded bearing.
• Investigate cause to loss in load carrying capacity
The current conﬁguration of the test rig does not allow for the investigation of the tilting
angles of the tilting-pads α. The tilting angles are currently the best explanation for the
loss in load carrying capacity. To be able to investigate the tiling angles one could install
displacement sensors on each side of the pivot points so that the tilting of pad can be
monitored.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
During this master thesis experimental as well as numerical work has been carried out in order
to determine the static and dynamic properties of a single degree of freedom, two pad, load on
pad, tilting-pad journal bearing subjected to diﬀerent design methods in order to adjust the
bearing properties under diﬀerent operational conditions. The diﬀerent design methods has
been a static force behind the lower pad and direct oil injection through the lower pad.
Preliminary experimental work conducted on the bearing shows that the pivot stiﬀness of the
pivot points of the tilting-pads is in fact nonlinear. This corresponds well with the assumption of
a line-contact governed by Hertzian contact theory. The ratio of the pivot stiﬀness determined
via Hertzian contact theory and experiments, however, is around 20 at a static loading of
the bearing of 700 N. The analytical pivot stiﬀness is therefore considerably larger than the
measured pivot stiﬀness. This suggest that other components like the pad and the journal etc.
also deﬂect elastically.
Due to deformations of the test rig it has not been possible to properly isolate the dynamic
properties of the bearing from the dynamic properties of the test rig. A reference stiﬀness has
been introduced in order to reduce the inﬂuence from the test rig. A reference damping has
however not been possible to determine.
During the experimental work it was noticed that the bearing would lose its load carrying
capacity at around 35-40 Hz or 2100-2400 RPM. The explanation for this behavior may be
found in the tilting angles of the tiling-pads. At a current state the experimental facility does
not allow for the measurements of the tilting angles, so a ﬁnal conclusion may not be drawn.
The loss in load carrying capacity is not captured in the numerical simulations.
At a geometrical preload of mp = 0.48 the bearing eﬀectively functions as a single-pad, tilting-
pad journal bearing due to the relative large radial clearance. As the bearing operates conven-
tionally the upper pad is completely unloaded. At a preload of mp = 0.73 a pressure in the
oil ﬁlm forms between the upper pad and the journal. This is observed in the measured pivot
point reaction forces.
By introducing a static load behind the lower pad the reaction forces from the pivot points will
increase signiﬁcantly compared to a conventional bearing. The bearing will be more compressed
so the journal will not be able to move as large a distances inside the bearing as compared to a
73
==PAGE:87==
CHAPTER 9. CONCLUSION
74
conventional bearing. This causes a decrease in the change in eccentricity ratio. For the case of
a preload of mp = 0.73 the stiﬀness of the bearing increases due to an increase in pivot reaction
forces and the damping decreases. For the case of mp = 0.48 the static force behind the lower
pad results in a decrease in bearing stiﬀness due to the absent of a reaction force from the
upper pad. The damping increases.
By introducing oil injection through the lower pad the equilibrium position of the journal
changes complete as the journal is forced closer to the upper pad than the lower pad. The
reaction forces from the pivot points increases signiﬁcantly and becomes larger than for both
the conventional bearing and with a static force behind the lower pad. Both the stiﬀness and
the damping of the bearing have increased compared to the conventional bearing. It is therefore
concluded that the oil injection yield the most agressive adjustment of the bearing properties
whereas the static force behind the lower pad only result in a moderate adjustment of the
properties.
Simulations show the same tendencies as observed experimentally except for the case of oil
injection. Here the stiﬀness is predicted to be lower than for the conventional bearing. The
deviation is thought to be caused by an inpropper assumption of a constant pivot stiﬀness in
the numerical simulations. The stiﬀness and damping of the bearing are generally larger in
magnitude for the simulations than in the experimental results. This has been concluded to
be caused by inpropper isolation of the bearing dynamic properties from the test rig dynamic
properties.
