Abstract
Simulation of laminar to turbulence transition using RANS models
This work attempts the validation of the Wilcox kω transitional turbulence model imple-
mented by B. E. Larsen using OpenFoam. The model is based on the Reynolds-Averaged
Navier Stokes Equation. The importance of the model is that it can model the transition
from laminar to turbulent ﬂow, which is not well known.
The innovation on the model is that it is implemented using the Open Source software
OpenFoam. In order to perform the validation, the model is tested in diﬀerent cases,
from basic to more advanced experiments. The analysis shows that it accurately models
transition in the simplest cases, but more complex experiments have not been able to
be ﬁnished.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The study of ﬂuid mechanics is used in many diﬀerent areas. Those applications go from
the everyday household tools such as the vacuum cleaner, to the basic functioning of
the human body, e.g. the blood movement through the veins and arteries, the breathing
and other multiple examples in this area, to the more advanced technology used in the
aerospace industry. Thus, it is really important to understand the nature and behaviour
of the ﬂuid mechanics in many ﬁelds of knowledge. To analyze the mechanics of a ﬂuid,
mainly in liquid or gas state, it is necessary to understand the individual properties of
the ﬂuid, such as pressure or temperature, and also the characteristics of its motion,
like the velocity or external forces or pressure applied to the ﬂuid. All those conditions
together generate speciﬁc ﬂow conditions, which can be analyzed for each designed case
and therefore, predict the behaviour for every diﬀerent application.
Considering the rate of change in pressure with time, three regimes are diﬀerentiated:
steady, pseudo-steady, and transient state. The ﬁrst regime corresponds to a constant
mass ﬂow rater in the domain, where the pressure is also constant in time (dP
dt = 0).
In the pseudo-steady the system behaves as a closed system with no ﬂuid movement
between boundaries. This regime is deﬁned by a constant pressure change rate (dP
dt =
constant).
The ﬁnal regime, the transient state, is characterized by a time varying
pressure (dP
dt = f(t)) with no movement restrictions on the ﬂuid [1].
The ﬂow conditions can also be deﬁned through a dimensionless parameter called the
Reynolds number (Re), which expresses the ratio between the inertial and the viscous
forces acting on the ﬂuid particles [2]. Using the Reynolds number, a given ﬂow can be

classiﬁed into diﬀerent regimes of which, the two main categories are the laminar and
turbulent ﬂows. However, more sub-categories can be used based on the way the ﬂuid
particles interact between each other, and also on the predominant forces in the ﬂow.
Any engineering project must consider the ﬂow regime, since laminar and turbulent ﬂows
have a completely diﬀerent behaviour. In the laminar regime, the ﬂow runs in parallel
layers with no interference between them. In this case, the predominant forces are the
viscous forces . In the turbulent regime, the ﬂow runs chaotically, and thus the layers
mix, creating an irregular ﬂow with eddies, swirls and instabilities. The predominant
forces in this case are the inertial forces [3][4]. The change from laminar to turbulent
regime is denominated transition, and it is a very complex process with many unresolved
unknowns. The main analysis of this document is based in the analysis of this phase,
through the validation of a transitional turbulence model designed by David C. Wilcox
[5] and implemented by B. E. Larsen during the development of his PhD project.
Since Osborne Reynolds published his work about the motion of water in parallel chan-
nels in 1883 [6], many studies have been carried out with the goal of a better under-
standing of the transition between regimes. In the beginning, those studies were mainly
experimental, and the results were used to formulate mathematical correlations to apply
the theory and perform the necessary calculations. The objective of laboratory experi-
ments is to recreate the real world characteristics, simulating the conditions in a scaled
level. This experiments have great value, but the main problem with them are their lim-
itations. The main constraints are produced by the equipment and by amount of time
necessary for each one of the test. Another limitation is the complexity of creating an
accurate set-up, because a slight modiﬁcation can recreate uncertain results. Moreover,
while collecting the results the conditions of the environment must remain undisturbed,
to avoid compromising the results. Nonetheless, these experiments are really important
because they recreate the physics realistically.
In the 1950s, following the improvements of technology and computational power, the
use of numerical analysis to recreate ﬂuid ﬂows gained importance. The main limitation
of these methods is the diﬃculty to recreate the real conditions. Thanks to the work
carried out by the Los Alamos National Laboratory between 1957 and the late 1960s [7],
it is possible to perform computer based numerical analysis to model ﬂuid ﬂows. This
branch inside the ﬂuid mechanics area is called Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD).
To generate an experiment using a CFD software, ﬁrst it is necessary to implement

a mathematical model that will recreate the physics of the experiment in the most
precise manner. To test the validity of the model, it is evaluated for diﬀerent cases and
then compared to already performed analytical or empirical analysis. Using CFD, it is
possible to reproduce most the ﬂuid mechanics experiments, but the computational cost
and the amount of data produced increases proportionally with the complexity of the
model. Nowadays, with the improvements of technology, the limits of CFD are pushed
every day. This translates in a more competitive outcome compared to the traditional
experimental analysis, caused by the simple reproduction of the cases, and therefore a
reduction in monetary and time costs. Additionally, CFD simpliﬁes the analysis of the
results, because it facilitates the tracking, control and evaluation of all the variables
involved in the experiment.
1.1
Project motivation and objectives
This thesis project idea originates from the implementation of the Wilcox k −ω tran-
sitional model carried out by B.E. Larsen. during his PhD project. The model was
implemented in OpenFoam, aiming to simulate transition in waves. The model was not
used in the project, but it was already ﬁnished when it was discarded. The model was
only tested in the ﬂow over a ﬂat plate case, but only to conﬁrm that transition was
being simulated. Therefore, it is necessary to perform an formal validation, testing the
model over diﬀerent cases, from basic to more complex experiments. In this project
three diﬀerent cases are set up to probe that the transition is correctly modelled and
that the model is ready to be used in more complex cases. All the numerical calculations
are veriﬁed by comparison with experimental data gathered from the literature review.

of the k−ω transitional model by Wilcox (2006) [8] using the Reynolds-Averaged Navier
Stokes (RANS) approach, which was implemented in OpenFoam by B.L. Larsen. To fully
understand all the concepts and procedures used throughout the document, the theory
in which the procedure is based and all the case set-ups are presented in the following

A general ﬂuid ﬂow is described by its governing equations, which are the mass con-
servation and the momentum conservation. In this document, the analyzed ﬂuid is an
incompressible (constant density) and Newtonian (constant viscosity) ﬂow. This type of
ﬂuid is described by fundamental partial diﬀerential equations called the Navier-Stokes
equations [15]. Since the direct solution of this equations is not computationally viable,
to obtain a valid approximation of the ﬂuid ﬂow, the equations are time averaged. The
resulting equations are commonly known as Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS)
equations.

a ﬁxed volume is zero. For the type of ﬂow studied in this document, Newtonian and
incompressible ﬂow, and considering the mean values of the parameters, the conservation
of mass is given by the continuity equation. Which, in Einstein notation and Cartesian

2.2
Closure problem
Considering a case where the ﬂow is laminar, that ﬂow is described using four equations,
the continuity equation (Eq. 2.4) and the three Navier-Stokes equations of motion (Eq.
2.5). To solve the case, there are 4 unknowns, i.e. the three velocity components and
the pressure. Since there are four equations for four unknowns, the system is considered
closed.
As explained in section 2.1, if the ﬂow is turbulent, the mean ﬂow is also characterized
by four equations: the continuity equation (Eq. 2.4) and the three RANS equations
of motion (Eq. 2.6). Altogether, there are ten unknowns, which are the three velocity
components, the pressure and the six independent components of the Reynolds stress
(−ρu′
iu′
j). The diﬀerence between the number of equations and unknowns means that
the system is not closed, and there is a need of additional equations in order to close it.
This task is carried out through the design of a turbulence model that uses an adequate
amount of equations to describe the eﬀects of turbulence in the mean ﬂow accurately
[12].
2.3
Turbulence models
Turbulent ﬂows can be computed using diﬀerent methods. The main diﬀerentiation is
based on how the ﬂow is solved. The process can be carried out either by solving the
RANS equations with the right solver for speciﬁc turbulent quantities, or by directly
computing them. There are many diﬀerent models in use [9] [16], but the most important
are:
==PAGE:24==
Theoretical background
10
• Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes Models
– Algebraic models.
– Turbulence-energy equation models.
• Computation of ﬂuctuating quantities
– Simulation models.
The algebraic models are the simplest models of all, and are also known as zero-equation
models. The term zero-equations means that the model is implemented employing only
the equations for energy, momentum and mass. This method expresses the Reynolds
stress as a product of the eddy viscosity and the mean strain rate, applying the Boussi-
nesq eddy-viscosity approximation (Section 2.3.1). The turbulence (eddy) viscosity, is
calculated in terms of a mixing length. The most commonly used algebraic method is
the mixing length model, developed by Prandtl in 1925 [17]. It is the simplest model,
but the main disadvantage is that the model is considered incomplete because, despite
considering the ﬂow history, there is a need to specify a ﬂow dependent property, such
as a turbulent length scale [18][19].
The turbulence-energy equation models are more advances models which can be complete.
This means that the only knowledge of the ﬂow required in advance are the initial and
boundary conditions. In this type of models, as in the algebraic models, the Reynolds
stress is deﬁned as a product of the eddy viscosity and the mean strain rate, but the
turbulence viscosity is computed in terms of the turbulent kinetic energy (k = 1
2u′
iu′
i)
instead of a mixing length. There are two types of turbulence-energy equation models:
One-equation and two-equations models. The latter was ﬁrst analyzed by Kolmogorov
in 1942 [20], modelling the turbulent kinetic energy (k) in combination with the rate of
energy dissipation (ω). This model is therefore called k −ω model. Other two-equations
models exist, such as the k −ϵ model [21].
The simulation models are the most advanced models, where the turbulence process is
directly computed, without including the previously mentioned closure problem. Some
examples are the Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS), the Large Eddy Simulation (LES)
and the Detached Eddy Simulation (DES). In a DNS research, a complete 3-D and time-
dependent solution of the Navier-Stokes and continuity equations is pursued. This type
of simulations achieve numerically precise solutions of the equations of motion, and
==PAGE:25==
Theoretical background
11
hence the statistics obtained form the results can be used to validate model model
estimations. DNS models are only used nowadays for low Reynolds numbers, due to
the computational cost involved in the simulation, but with the advances in technology
and the increasing computational power, the range is expanding [9]. In LES models,
the smallest eddies are modeled and the large eddies are computed. The premise is that
large eddies carry most of the Reynolds stress, are directly aﬀected by the boundary
conditions, and therefore, they must be computed. The small-scale turbulence can be
overlooked, since its contribution to the Reynolds stresses is lower and consequently
less critical [8]. The Detached Eddy Simulation (DES) method is an alternative that
combines the best features of LES and RANS models creating a cost-eﬀective procedure.
This model was introduced ﬁrst in 1997 by Spalart et al. [22]. DNS models handle the
boundary layers and thin shear layers with a RANS approach, and the rest of the ﬂow
through a conventional LES [23].
To perform the objective of this document, only the k −ω model is used, so no further
explanation of the rest of the models is carried out. For a more thorough explanation
of the other turbulence models, the work from Wilcox (2006) can be consulted [8].

2.4.2
Transition
For a given ﬂow, it is possible to predict if the regime is turbulent or not. The parameter
that deﬁnes that is the critical Reynolds number. It is easy to understand that for low
or high Reynolds numbers the regime is clearly deﬁned, but there is a region where the
ﬂow is neither fully laminar or fully turbulent. This is the transition region.
The desire to understand the physical mechanisms at work in the development from
laminar to fully turbulent ﬂow, i.e. the transition or the also called onset of turbulence
[30], has occupied engineers, physicists and mathematicians for the past century. The
main goal of the work in the prediction of the transition is to answer the questions of
where, when and why it takes place. The enigma is not the same , but it is related, to
the understanding of the turbulent ﬂow itself, since the comprehension of the last stages
of transition and the initiation of turbulence, can throw some light on the problem
of modelling turbulent ﬂow, due to its close relation. The prediction of the point of
transition from smooth to turbulent ﬂow is a problem of immense importance to industry.
The turbulent ﬂow is deﬁned by a wide range of scales in the regions of circulating ﬂow
known as eddies. The eddies are able to transport heat, momentum and particles, feature
of great importance in the industry, e.g provoking transition in a chemical reaction can
ensure the complete mixing of all the components. Another example of the usefulness of
a better understanding of the transition process is related to the aeronautics industry.
A turbulent ﬂow increases the drag resistance, therefore, an aircraft whose design allows
the ﬂow around its wings to be laminar can save costs, either by allowing the plane to
carry more passengers or just saving fuel [31].
==PAGE:31==
Theoretical background
17
The most important feature of the phenomenon of transition is the increased diﬀusivity
in the ﬂow. The main reason of its complexity is that besides the presence of laminar and
turbulent ﬂow at the same time, there is also the interaction between the two phases,
which is usually looked over to analyze deeply the fully laminar and turbulent regimes.
Many research groups have analyzed this problem, however, the important eﬀect of the
transition is not yet included in the majority of CFD simulations. The reason for this
is that transition modelling is more complicated to implement in CFD models, even
though a large amount of publications are available on the subject.
There are several reasons for this situation, such as the fact that the transition process
involves a wide range of scales and it is very sensitive to physical ﬂow features. Transition
occurs mainly through three diﬀerent mechanisms [32], which are:
• Natural transition: Small disturbances in the laminar boundary layer, which
becomes unstable and transition starts;
• Bypass transition: Caused by large disturbances outside the boundary layer;
• Separation-induced transition: Caused by separation of the laminar boundary
layer
The transition to turbulence can happen inside a range of Reynolds numbers, depending
on lots factors, such as the surface roughness, heat transfer, vibration and some other
disturbances.
As explained at the beginning of this chapter, the Reynolds number
expresses the ratio of the inertia forces to the viscous forces. Taking that into account,
at low Reynolds numbers the viscous force is dominant, so small disturbances in the
velocity ﬁeld will be absorbed and not allowed to increase. As the Reynolds number
increases, some small perturbations appear and the ﬂow can become unstable. At this
point, transition emerges. In case the disturbances are very small, the transition to
turbulence can occur at a higher Reynolds number than the critical value. This means
that the point of transition cannot be deﬁned by a single Reynolds number, but a range
of values. Bearing that in mind, it is possible to delay transition to larger values than the
theoretically expected by controlling the disturbances in the experiment environment.
At some point, the Reynolds number reaches a value where it is impossible to keep the
laminar regime because under those conditions even slight disturbances will be rapidly
ampliﬁed into turbulence [33].

2.5.2
Solving the case
Solving the case consist in running the simulation, applying all the conditions set in the
pre-processing stage, and developing a solution using a selected solver. In this analysis,
two diﬀerent solvers have been used, depending on the case: the standard pisoFoam and
pimpleFoam. Following the essence of OpenFoam, the solvers are modiﬁed to adapt to

the requirements of each simulation in Chapters 4 and 5, where a body force is added
to the solver to simulate an oscillatory ﬂow and a body force driven ﬂow respectively.
To ensure the numerical stability and the temporal accuracy solving the case, the sim-
ulation must converge. To achieve it, the Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy condition must be
satisﬁed. This condition is deﬁned in terms of a coeﬃcient called the CFL coeﬃcient or
Courant number:
Co = U ∆t
∆x ≤Comax
(2.33)
where ∆t is the chosen time-step, U is the magnitude of the velocity through a single
cell and ∆x is the cell size. The CFL number represents the ratio between the distance
travel by the information at each time step and the cell size.
If the ratio is larger
than 1, the information may get lost, therefore the solution may not converge and
the results become erroneous. For an explicit solver the ratio should be lower than 1,
and for implicit solvers the value can be larger than 1. Both the pisoFoam and the
pimpleFoam solvers are semi-implicit solvers, hence, for the analysis, a value of 0.5 is
chosen as Comax. The more restrictive the limit is, the more accurate the solution, but
the computational time increases. Therefore, it is necessary to ﬁnd the balance between
accuracy and computational cost. It is important to consider the CFL number specially
when a reﬁnement in the mesh is carried out, since the time-step must be adjusted in
order to avoid an increase in the value of the CFL number.
The selection of the solver is based on the ﬂow conditions, and the set-up of the cases.
Both the pisoFoam and the pimpleFoam are used in the project analysis, since both of
them can adapt to the conditions. The two solvers are transient solvers for incompress-
ible turbulent ﬂows. The diﬀerence between them is the used algorithm. The pisoFoam
solver uses the PISO algorithm, where the calculation is limited in the time step due to
the aforementioned CFL number, which cannot be larger than 1. This criteria must be
fulﬁlled for all the cells. To achieve this requirement, all the cases in this analysis are set
considering the worst case scenario, i.e. the smallest cell size. The pimpleFoam solver
uses the PIMPLE algorithm, which is a combination of the SIMPLE and PISO algo-
rithms. This combination allows the user to use bigger time steps, i.e. the CFL number
can be larger than 1. Due to this feature, it is one of the most used solvers in Open-
Foam. Another advantage of the pimpleFoam solver is that it can work in PISO mode
setting the nOuterCorrectors parameter to 1. The greatest advantage of the PIMPLE
==PAGE:37==
Theoretical background
23
method compared to the PISO method is that it has a better performance with complex
geometries [35]. The third case analyzed in this document, the ﬂow over a cylinder, has
more complex geometry than the initial tests, hence the pimpleFoam solver is used in
that case, with the PISO mode activated. The selection and set-up of the solver is done
in the controlDict dictionary.

Post-processing
This phase includes all the processes carried out by the user after the simulation is
completed, i.e. managing and visualizing the outputs. In this case, for the analysis and
visualization of the results, software external from OpenFoam is used, such as Matlab,
for data processing, or paraView for the three-dimensional analysis.
The following chapters explain the procedure carried out in each of the cases analyzed
to perform the model validation. For every case, the necessary additional theoretical
background and the OpenFoam case set-up are explained, and the results are analyzed
and discussed. Three cases are analyzed and the results are compared with the theoret-
ical expected results, from basic experiments, to more advanced tests. Those cases are:
ﬂow over a ﬂat plate with steady current, ﬂow over a ﬂat plate with oscillatory ﬂow and
ﬂow around a cylinder.

This chapter analyzes the boundary layer development over a ﬂat plate where the ﬂow

5.1

The ﬂow around a cylinder is described by the Reynolds number, which is deﬁned in

Figure 5.2 shows the boundary layer development in the case where separation occurs. In
the front part of the cylinder, there is a favourable pressure gradient where the pressure
acts in the same direction as the ﬂow. On the other half of the cylinder there is an
adverse pressure gradient, i.e. the pressure pushes in the opposite direction of the ﬂow
motion. The adverse pressure gradient, makes the velocity gradient steeper, hence, the
velocity near the wall decreases, and the boundary layer grows thicker. At some speciﬁc
point, the wall shear stress turns zero due to this eﬀect. After that point, the shear
stress becomes negative and the ﬂow reverses. The reversed ﬂow cannot go back along
the wall, so a new region of recirculating ﬂow develops. The point where the shear stress
becomes zero is called the separation point (Fig. 5.1.a). The second region is the wake
region, which is a large region of separated ﬂow that develops downstream. It is a highly
turbulent region, which eventually dissipates in the ﬂow [43].

When the boundary layer separates form the surface due to the aforementioned adverse

phenomenon called vortex shedding. The vorticity is passed into the shear layer from
the boundary layer developed along the cylinder, and the ﬂow inside it rolls up in the
same sign as the vorticity. In each of the sides of the cylinder, vorticity has a diﬀerent
sign, hence vortices rotate in diﬀerent direction depending on the side of the cylinder.
The vortices are unstable for Re > 40, where the laminar vortex street starts to form
(Fig. 5.3).
Figure 5.4 depicts the process of the vortex shedding. Due to the small disturbances
which appear in the shear layer, one vortex will eventually grow larger than the other.
The larger vortex (A) grows to a point when it is able to drag the opposite vortex
(B) through the wake. As vortex B approaches the opposite shear layer, its vorticity
will cut oﬀthe vorticity supply to vortex A from its boundary layer, shedding vortex
A. After that, vortex A is convected downstream. This process becomes cyclic, so the
shedding alternates between both sides of the cylinder, forming vortex C. It is important
to mention that, following the previous explanation, it is clear that vortex shedding only
occurs when there is interaction between the two shear layers. Therefore, it is possible
to inhibit the interaction if needed.

5.3
Analysis of the results
As mentioned before, in order to solve this case accurately, the best procedure to perform
this experiment is to execute parallel running. Otherwise, the computational costs and
the simulating time of each simulation, especially in high Reynolds number, takes to
much time and memory. unfortunately in this case, parallel running has not been used.
Despite several tries, and after discussion with the co-supervisor and the support service
from the DTU G-bar, it is concluded that the use of the special boundary conditions
employed in the set-up, the model is not prepared to run in parallel. Therefore, all the
simulations from now on are executed using serial running. Before even starting, this is a
tremendous issue, since the analysis is restricted by computational limitations. Despite
this fact, the aforementioned cases are designed and carried into execution.
Considering the above, the mesh design is initially created, as in previous cases to resolve
the ﬂow up down to the viscous layer. In the case of the turbulent experiments designed
in this analysis, the idea has been discarded, because the necessary mesh is not viable
due to the time restrictions of the thesis. therefore, even though it is not accurate, the
ﬁnest mesh employed is the mesh corresponding to the Re = 1e5.
Another issue is the initial unknown about the eﬀect of the body force, since the deriva-
tion of its value and calculation did not ensure the expected results. During the analysis,
it has been proved that the ﬂow stabilizes at a higher value than the calculated. Diﬀerent
approaches have been used to reach the steady state, such as extending the simulation
in time or increasing the initial velocity 25% its initial value.

sualize the ﬂow. The laminar simulations match the theoretical behaviour. The high
Reynolds simulations do not allow an exhaustive inspection, since at the moment of

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This work attempts the validation of the Wilcox kω transitional turbulence model im-
plemented by B. E. Larsen using OpenFoam. The model is tested in diﬀerent scenarios,
from basic to more advanced experiments. First the sensitivity of the model is analyzed
testing it by using a wide variation of initial parameters. It is probed that the model is
robust and does not suﬀer big changes even against a large variations of the parameters.
In the second test the Wave boundary layer over a ﬂat plate is simulated, analyzing the
transition through the development of the bed shear stress and the oscillating velocity.
The theoretical results are achieved probing that the model can model both laminar and
fully turbulent ﬂows.
The third case shows that the model can recreate the ﬂow around a cylinder for low
Reynolds numbers, but due to the particular conditions of the set-up and procedure, a
statement for higher Reynolds numbers cannot be written.
6.1
Recommendations for future works
In order to achieve the full validation of the model, the third case should be re done,
in order to use the correct initial conditions. After that, for further analysis, the next
experiment would be to set-up the case of the ﬂow around the cylinder in an oscillatory
ﬂow.
