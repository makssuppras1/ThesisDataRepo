Abstract
The importance of high-eﬀiciency and low-maintenance machines are increasingly critical
for improving production, particularly in Power-to-X and electricity sectors, in a soci-
ety facing environmental challenges. This thesis presents a comprehensive study on the
thermal effects on an Axial Active Magnetic Bearing test rig. The modifications of the
test rig, that are imperative for the study of the thermal effects during the experiments,
are presented. The theoretical foundations for an AMB system is presented, with var-
ious engineering disciplines including mechanical- and electrical engineering. A Linear
Time-Invariant (LTI) State Space model is derived and the expected thermal effect is
anticipated through existing literature. The experimental methodologies for achieving
thermal steady states, conducting step responses, and performing transient experiments
are thoroughly presented, including potential pitfalls and challenges.
The test rig has some anomalies and notable behaviours that are highlighted through the
data, the tilting motion introduce some challenges and demonstrate amplification of the
axial movement due to a steady state tilt at the equilibrium position as a result of the
misaligned PMBs.
The experiments demonstrate how the thermal effect causes the rigid rotor to drop under
various experimental conditions. The rotor dropped at ≈52 °C in experiments heating the
same Bearing Electromagnet (BEM) but at different temperatures. It was demonstrated
how the thermal effect has a more prominent impact on the upper BEM, opposed to the
lower BEM.
The derived mathematical model is optimized to fit experimental data, and later modified
to include the thermal effect in the LTI state space model. The optimized model is used
to tune a new PID controller with improved stability, decreased overshoot and settling
time. The activation of the tilting motion through the step responses were demonstrated
to increase the amplitude in the axial direction. This behaviour is not included in the
mathematical model due to simplifying assumptions. A large difference in the optimized
inductance and the model predicted is found, the increased inductance is explained by
more prominent fringing due to the relatively large air gap.
A primitive draft of time-scheduled PID gains are designed and implemented in the Lab-
VIEW UI and validated through a single experiment.
Overall, the master’s thesis demonstrates the impact of increased temperatures on AMB
systems as a result of the increasing resistance. A temperature dependent Linear Time-
Invariant State Space model that is fitted the new test rig is included.

Introduction
This thesis focuses on the thermal effect on Active Magnetic Bearings (AMB) and the
stability that is achieved with a PID position feedback controller under different thermal
conditions. The working principle of the Active Magnetic Bearing System studied in this
thesis, and generally, is shown in Figure 1.1. It comprises of the rotor that is levitated at
a desired distance s0 from the bearing electromagnets. The position is measured with a
no contact type sensor, this setup uses an inductive type sensor. The signal is fed to PID
controller comparing the signal with a desired reference position, and the controller cor-
rects accordingly. The control signal, is handled by the amplifiers for the electromagnets;
in this setup they are motor drivers with an H-Bridge circuit configuration.

1.1
Motivation
Active Magnetic Bearing systems are utilized in many different industrial applications,
they are excellent when high speeds, low maintenance or clean operational conditions
are required. The control system enables the option to actively dampen critical natural
frequencies in high speed applications, enabling better control over the system.
AMBs require little to no maintenance, which is proven by several manufacturers, for ex-
ample SKFs systems deployed on the seabed in Norway, extracting oil and gas [1]. Siemens
with their SIMOTICS Active Magnetic Bearing [2] system in High Voltage applications
for steam turbines. Yet another example is Copenhagen Atomics that are researching in
Molten Salt Reactors (MSR), a visit to the company in Copenhagen showed how they use
an AMB to circulate the molten salt in a high temperature application. Their use case

did not require high eﬀiciency, but rather low maintenance, due to regulations around
Nuclear Facilities, maintenance of equipment is extensively complicated for safety reasons,
and therefor a maintenance free system is desirable. Another application is for High-Speed
Flywheel Energy Storage systems that aim at stabilizing the load on the electric grids, it
has never been more relevant with the addition of wind- and solar power supplies.
A Finland based AMB manufacturer, SpinDrive, has cooperated with Technology Agency

With the environmental challenges the world is facing, eﬀiciency of the energy- and pro-

becoming more feasible due to the abundant energy produced at times of no consump-
tion, the Power-to-X becomes more relevant with the increased eﬀiciency from AMBs.
The production of hydrogen for instance, requires high performing compressors for large
scale production, and later when hydrogen is used for the production of ammonia for
fertilizer in the food production and synthetic fuels.
1.2
Literature Review
The industry has shown that Active Magnetic Bearings can be employed in applications
affected by very high temperatures. A study from Texas A&M University College Station
and NASA Glenn Research Center (GRC) by M. W. Mohiuddin et al. are considering
a magnetic thrust bearing in high temperature applications [4]. Their AMB setup has
an air gap of 406µm, significantly lower than the air gap in this study. The bearings are
heated to 538 °C (1000 °F) and also study the effect on the magnetic force when the rotor
is spun to 5000rpm. The rotation of the rotor is not considered in this thesis, but their
results presented no to very little impact on the axial load force on the axial magnetic
bearing. The temperature range in their study is far higher than the scope of the studied
conditions in this thesis. They include the magnetic permeability of the materials, but
it should not be required in the low temperature range of 20 −80 °C. They show the
increase in temperature to 538 °C, decreased the magnetic force by 65% compared to the
initial force at room temperature, 25 °C. They conclude the cause was partly explained by
the increased path reluctance, consequently decreasing the magnetic flux and force as a
result of the decreasing permeability of the core material. The additional decrease in the
force stems from the electrical resistance in the coils. They further showed the resistance
in their setup increased linearly with the temperature. Due to their large temperature
range, the decrease in magnetic permeability will be more prominent and is therefor not
considered here.
Another study by A. J. Provenza et al. investigated a High Temperature Characterization
of a Radial Magnetic Bearing for Turbomachinery [5]. They investigate the thermal effect
on a radial bearing systems with an air gap of 620µm at room temperature.
Their
results are in line with [4], the increase in temperature to 538 °C caused a reduction in
the magnetic force of 51% −55% with respect to the room temperature. The air gap
increased quite significantly from 620 µm to 792µm at 538 °C.

1.3
Scope of Project
The goal of this master’s thesis is to investigate the thermal effect on an Active Magnetic
Bearing system. The test rig used in this thesis required modifications on the physical
setup to accommodate the thermal experiments, the changes are thoroughly presented
and the CAD drawings for the components are attached in the Appendix. Additional
changes are done to the LabVIEW UI and a user guide is attached in the Appendix.
Once the modifications were complete, a hand tuned controller was implemented in the
setup with reasonable performance, enabling the thermal steady state experiments.
A mathematical model governing dynamics of the mechanical- and electrical system is
derived and linearized around the operational point and represented by a Linear Time-
Invariant State Space model. A PID controller is implemented to stabilize the inherently
unstable behaviour from the Active- and Passive Magnetic Bearings. The model limita-
tions due to linearization and simplifying assumptions are presented thoroughly.
Based on the mathematical model and other studies [4] for instance, the parameters that
should be included in the study to suﬀiciently govern the thermal effect is presented.
The experimental methodologies are presented on a step-wise guide basis as it was used
during the conducted experiments. The experiments include thermal steady states, step
responses and transient vibrations of the rigid rotor.
The experiments gave insight to anomalies where a possible explanation is given.
A
small tilt is present when the rotor levitates at the equilibrium position. Additionally,
a periodic oscillatory behaviour is present throughout all experiments when the rotor
levitates, a possible cause is substantiated, although additional work is required to handle
and determine the cause.
The thermal steady state experiments illustrate the impact of the thermal effect. The
position of the rotor is monitored throughout the thermal steady state, providing vital
information about the AMB system’s stability. The rotor is dropped in several experi-
ments as a result of the increasing resistance, consequently decreasing the current flow
and magnetic force. The resistance is estimated at the steady state temperature and is
used in an optimization process to fit the mathematical model to the experimental results.
Several parameters are studied in the optimization process to achieve a suﬀiciently fitted
LTI state space model. The temperature effect is then incorporated in the state space
model through the temperature-resistance relation.
During the optimization process, it was discovered that the model’s predicted inductance
was significantly different. The optimized value for the inductance was validated with an
LCR meter. The optimized and temperature dependent state space model is used to tune
a new controller for the AMB system. A comparison between the previous controller and
the simulations from the model is made.
Finally, a draft of time-scheduling the PID gains to account for the thermal effect is
implemented. A single experiment illustrates that it works, but it is not further validated
nor optimized.

Active Magnetic Bearing Setup
To study the thermal effect on an Active Magnetic Bearing System, a test rig that was
previously studied by Andreas Westerberg & Alessio Dallabona in [6] is modified. This
test rig configuration, shown in Figure 2.1, requires modifications to study the thermal
effect. Moreover, the previous AMB setup encountered several challenges related to the
rotor. The shaft was bend, leading to imbalance of the rotor. But more importantly,
the discs proved non-parallel with one another other and were non-perpendicular to the
shaft on the rotor, affecting both their interaction with the electromagnetic field and the
position measurements.

2.1.1
Bearing Electromagnets
The bearing electromagnets, 4 and 5 in Figure 2.1, produce a magnetic field by the
flow of an electric current through a coil wound around a ferromagnetic soft iron core
to increase the field strength. The magnetic field’s intensity can be actively controlled
through the change of current, allowing it to be utilized for rotor levitation. The setup
consists of two bell shaped electromagnets of Ø80mm from Isliker Magnete AG, additional
information can be found in the datasheet F.1. For electromagnets, it is generally desirable
to have high magnetic permeability, good magnetic saturation, low hysteresis losses and
low coercivity to decrease the remanence. The datasheet does not include any information
of the properties for the core material. This means the magnetic permeability, hysteresis
losses, coercivity and point of saturation are all unknowns. It is not a problem for this
study, because literature shows the primary factors are the increase in the coil resistance
and reduction in the magnetic permeability due to increased path reluctance. However, a
low remanence is desirable, as it will otherwise introduce an additional magnetic field in
the setup further complicating an already non-linear behaviour. The point of saturation is
assumed to be at or beyond the maximum current specified in the datasheet at 1.5A that
protects the rectifier, it is far beyond the operational conditions in this setup, meaning it
is not a limitation. The ideal material properties are found in electrical steel, it is known
for the high silicon and low carbon content achieving all the above characteristics. It is
commonly used in applications with magnetic fields in for example transformers, electrical
motors and active magnetic bearings. It comes in sheets of varying thickness’. When these
are laminated and stacked, a core with very good properties is created. Additionally, very
little losses from the surface eddy currents are present, as these are broken by the ’edges’
formed by the stacked sheets.
To ensure the utilization of the simpler mathematical model for the interaction between
the electromagnets and the rotor, it is imperative to establish a closed flux path and
ensure it does not near the edges of the end discs in the setup as it can lead to undesirable
behaviour. The optimal case is illustrated in Figure 2.5.

2.1.2
Passive Magnetic Bearings
In this particular setup, Passive Magnetic Bearings (PMB) are employed as bearings in
the radial direction, 1 and 2 in Figure 2.1. They consist of a stator with 16 equally
spaced rod magnets aligned in a circular array and with two stacked ring magnets mounted
on the rotor. The poles for all the magnets all point in the same direction. The PMBs
exhibit non-linear force/displamement characteristics that can be derived using the Biot-
Sawart Law [7] for magnetostatics. The magnetic field B is often depicted as a current
carrying wire, although, for a permanent magnet, it is replaced by a dipole moment, that
is estimated from the material and the grade of magnetisation. The PMBs introduces an
additional unstable behaviour described by Earnshaw’s Theorem. In this study, the model
is linearized around the equilibrium, and for this reason, the experimentally determined
values in the vicinity of the equilibrium position determined in [6] , will be used for the
initial model.
2.1.3
Position Sensors
The position of the rotor is measured with four analogue inductive sensors positioned in
a square with their center’s 41 × 41mm apart, the 3 in Figure 2.2. This enables them
to not only measure the vertical position, but also use the individual signals to estimate
the tilting of the rotor. The sensor itself generates a magnetic field that interacts with
the conductive material, in this case the end discs made of steel. When this rotor moves
relative to the sensors it induces a voltage in the coils according to Faraday’s Law of
electromagnetic induction. The magnitude of this voltage is proportional to the distance.
The sensor is a KJ8-M18MN50-ANU from Pulsotronic and is limited to a range between
1−8mm, and an output voltage of 1−9V. This output voltage requires a voltage divider
to step down the sensor output in order to comply with the analog input limits of 5V as
outlined in subsection 2.1.6. Additionally, the temperature drift of the sensor should be
noted as it is ±5% which is relatively large. It is considered to have no impact on the
measurements because the sensors are shielded below the end discs. The linearity of the
position sensors are also relatively large at ≤5%. The full datasheet can be found in F.4.
The reference position signal for the PID controller is a mean value of the four position
sensors. The four sensors were previously installed, however, recalibration was necessary
due to significant deviations observed in their measurements. Some of their solderings
have been redone to ensure suﬀicient contact.
2.1.4
Current Sensors
The current measurement is not directly used as a parameter for the control process, but
the data is used to verify the mathematical model. The current sensors are from LEM, a
CTSR 0.6-P, the 9 in Figure 2.3, with a measuring range of up to 30A. The measuring
range is determined by the voltage input to the Vref pin. The sensor is based on the hall
effect, measuring the magnetic field generated by the current flowing through. The sensor
output voltage is proportional to the current flowing, with a linearity of ≤1.3%. The full
datasheet can be found in F.3. Opposed to the position sensors, the current sensor is not
close to the heat source, because of this, the temperature dependency is not considered.
The current sensors were already installed and good accuracy were observed, although a
bad solder has been redone for the voltage divider on the MXP A/B boards connected to
myRIO-1900.

2.2.2
Temperature Sensor
To accurately measure the temperature of the bearing electromagnets to determine the
impact on the dynamics, the temperature of the coil and iron core is of interest. How-
ever, it proved hard to measure the core temperature of the coil windings. At best, the
surface temperature of the coil windings’ enamel could be measured, but that required
disassembling the electromagnet and a less invasive method was desirable.
The solution that is implemented, is measuring the temperature of the steel core with a
PT100 in the electromagnet. It is done without machining the electromagnet, but through
the M8 mounting hole in the bottom. The M8 countersunk bolt has been drilled through
the hexagon socket and the entire length of the bolt in which the PT100 is mounted.
There is no thermal paste in the hole, and this will increase the thermal resistance further
due to the additional interface between the electromagnet, air and the PT100, but at
steady state, it should be leveled out.
To enhance the accuracy of the temperature estimation of the coil, two additional PT100
sensors are installed at the inlet, positioned just before the heated air enters the enclosure
surrounding the electromagnets.
The selection of the PT100 sensor was based on its superior accuracy, rapid response time,
and ease of integration. While initially considering contactless sensors, such as infrared
sensors, their relatively large tolerance and complexity compared to the PT100, moreover,
their inability to accurately measure the temperature of both the coil and the steel core
ultimately discarded them as an option.
The PT100 consists of a platinum part with a resistance of 100Ωat 0°C. The implemented
versions in this setup comes with a 4-wire configuration that accounts for the lead wire
resistance to ensure the temperature of the PT100 is the true temperature. Additionally,
the sensors are Class A, that deliver a tolerance of ±0.15°C, which is still within the
ADC resolution for both types of analog inputs. In order to utilize tolerance, it is crucial
to maintain a current through the sensor below 1mA. To do so, a large resistance is
required, this also reduces the output voltage to a level, that requires a high resolution
on the analog inputs to fully use the Class A tolerance. This can be solved with a custom
amplifying circuit with for example an INA128. Or the easier and complete solution is
to use the LKM 214 Transmitter, that amplifies the output voltage to a level between
0 −10V for a temperature range of 0 −150°C for the electromagnets and 0 −200°C for
the inlets. The full datasheet of the sensors and transmitters can be found here
F.5 and F.6.

3
Theory
The derivation of the mathematical model for the Active Magnetic Bearing System is
derived. It includes the three translational directions and two tilting angles for the rotor.
The AMB system exhibits nonlinearities due to the magnetic force, tilting of the rotor
and other complex dynamics, for example the Passive Magnetic Bearings. They arise
from intricate couplings between the rotor dynamics and the electromagnetic dynamics.
Moreover, Active Magnetic Bearing systems inherits unstable behaviour that can only be
stabilized with active controlling, introducing additional dynamics.
Furthermore, this particular setup utilizes two PMBs to center the rotor.
These are
characterized for their unstable behaviour according to Earnshaw’s Theorem [7]. Elec-
trostatic forces alone, cannot create a stable equilibrium configuration, and the controller
shall therefor stabilize this unstable characteristic.
Although the equations are non-linear, a good approximation can be achieved by lineariz-
ing them around a convenient operational position, as long as the system is operated in
the vicinity.
Considerations like eddy-current losses, hysteresis and saturation is not considered.
The expected thermal effect on the AMB system will be outlined briefly at the end of this
chapter, and further shown with the results from the experimental work.
3.1
Mathematical Model
The aim is to derive the equations of motion for the Rotor-Bearing system. To do so a
reference system, position vectors and kinematic relations are formulated.
3.1.1
Mechanical System
The mechanical system is modelled as a rigid rotor with 5 degrees of freedom with the
translational x, y, z and the rotational ϕ and θ respectively. The rotation around the
z-axis, denoted as ψ, is not included, because the rotor is not spun.
The mathematical model governing the dynamics of the Active Magnetic Bearing sys-
tem includes the radial- and axial forces from the Passive Magnetic Bearings (PMBs)
and the dynamics from the bearing electromagnets.
The AMB is only acting in the
z-direction, consequently, there are no means of controlling the other directions. The
stiffness’ throughout the system, and the damping from the AMB is shown in Figure 3.1.
None of the dissipative forces in the mechanical system, like wind resistance and eddy
currents in the PMBs, are included. The damping effect arises from the back EMF as a
result of the interaction between the rotor and bearing system. The rotor is shown with
the PMB stiffness’ and their distances from the center of mass, cm. The AMB stiffness’
are illustrated for the individual bearing electromagnets, BEMs, depicted with either a U
or L for upper and lower magnets. It illustrates how each of the two bearing electromag-
nets both contribute to the dynamics. However; when the bearing magnets are modelled
later, they are considered as one unit, and the ki, ks, ku and L are modelled as one for
the BEMs in the z-direction.

3.1.4
Model Limitations
The final linearized model has been derived by using simplifying assumptions. They are
all collected for both the mechanical- and electrical system in their respective boxes below.
The linearization process means it does not accurately estimate the behaviour when not
at, or in the vicinity, of the operation point. As a result of the omitted air resistance and
other dampening effects in the mechanical system, the model will predict marginally stable
conditions for the system, which may not accurately reflect the real world behaviour. For
this particular study, it is assessed to be of little importance as the axial direction does
include damping through the back EMF.
Assumptions for the Mechanical System
• Rotor is assumed a rigid body as a point mass with mass moment of inertia’s.
• Air resistance is neglected in both translational and rotational movements, it
is considered insignificant for the purpose of this study.
• Eddy currents generated in the PMB are neglected.
• In the linearization process, the tilting is assumed to be small angles.

Assumptions for the Magnetic Bearing System
• Magnetic saturation is neglected, the saturation point is assessed to be con-
siderably higher than the operational range in this setup.
• Fringing is not considered, because the end discs has a significant overlap of
the BEMs to handle this effect.
• Flux leakage is neglected because it is considered insignificant.
• Eddy currents are not included, the movement in the z-direction is relatively
limited and no rotations are present.
• Reluctance induced by the magnetic field in the end discs are not included,
it is considered insignificant.
• A uniform flux density is assumed across the BEM cores.
• Any hysteresis from the changing magnetisation of the material is not in-
cluded.

3.3
Expected Thermal Effect
With the mathematical model and the corresponding assumptions with the equations, the
thermal impact on the stability of the AMB system can be predicted. The four important
quantities for the AMB system are the stiffness’ in Equation 3.27. They depend on the
following parameters µ0, n, A, i0 and z0, each with different magnitudes. The number
of windings, n, are not changing with temperature and the value from the datasheet for
the BEMs are used without further validation. The magnetic permeability of vacuum is
constant and does not depend on temperature. The area does depend on the temperature,
but with a thermal expansion coeﬀicient for a ferromagnetic material at 10 −13 µm
m°C [14],
assuming iron, it is neglected.
The air gap z0 is also related to the thermal expansion, it is however assumed to have
little to no impact despite the ks stiffness is inversely proportional to the cube of the air
gap. Ultimately, an increase in temperature of 40°C results in just above 1% increase in
ks, and even less for ki, ku and L.
The last parameter of interest is the resistance in the coil, which will be the main param-
eter studied in this thesis. The resistance is linearly proportional to the temperature, and
the resulting current is expected to follow the same pattern. The resistance determines
Axial Active Magnetic Bearing System
35
==PAGE:47==
the flowing current together with the control voltage, and it is therefor important to de-
termine its relation to a temperature change. The bias current evidently has an impact on
the system’s dynamics through the four quantities, but also directly through the control
current.
The temperature range is limited to avoid damage to the enamel insulation according to
its insulation class. The only significant thermal effects in this study is expected to be
the change in coil resistance, but at higher temperatures additional thermal effects can
occur.
A study from A&M University College Station and NASA Glenn Research Center are
consideing a magnetic thrust bearing in high temperature applications [4]. They heat
the bearing to 538 °C (1000 °F). They show a decrease in the magnetic force to 65% of
the initial force at room temperature. The cause was partly explained by the increased
reluctance path as a result of the decreasing permeability of the core material and the
electrical resistance in the coils. Because they consider a larger temperature range, the
decrease in permeability will be more prominent.

temperature in the coil windings are diﬀicult to estimate accurately without numerical
simulations. A simulation or calculation is not carried out, but the following is rather an
illustration to show how the implemented way of measuring the temperature is assessed
to underestimate the true temperature of the coil.
The electromagnet is a symmetric circular body with the major heat source being the hot

Additional Thermal Effects
In high temperature applications, several hundred degrees Celcius, the material properties
of the ferromagnetic core changes. There are nonlinear effects like the curie temperature,
when the material exceed this temperature, the magnetisation undergoes a transition to
paramagnetic properties. Increased temperatures decrease the magnetic permeability of
the material, µFe, consequently resulting in a weaker magnetic field due to the increased
path reluctance. Finally, the saturation limit decreases. These factors are neglected in
the model.

5.3
Discussion
The equilibrium position tilting is proposed to be a consequence of the PMBs as they
might not be perfectly aligned in the circular array. Moreover, the ring magnets on the
rotor might be slightly misaligned after assembly. It is not considered an issue during
the study, although it causes non-uniform air gaps, this effect is neglected and the AMBs
also illustrated the capability of handling this during step responses.
The oscillatory
behaviour is proposed to be a result of two coupled effects. One is from the tilted rotor in
the equilibrium position, because the active control current sustaining the stability of the
rotor is constantly adjusting the position toward the ideal vertical position, introducing
small perturbations. Another effect can be a resonance frequency interfering with the
control output frequency and the system’s natural frequencies. This constant battle of
stability and small perturbations in the system, together with the relatively low stiffness’
in the system, enables these tilting mode shapes that cannot be damped effectively by
the 1DOF control system.
It might be possible to solve this by introducing a bandstop filter at the frequencies to
attenuate the frequency of the periodic oscillatory behaviour in the PID output signal to
determine if the controller is the cause. This can also introduce its own challenges due
to the phase lag that will be introduced to the control and feedback loop because of the
Axial Active Magnetic Bearing System
53
==PAGE:65==
filter dynamics. The above are propositions and require additional studies to investigate
further. For this study, where the thermal effect on the AMB system is determined, these
challenges are ignored.

6.1.4
Discussion
The vertical shift from the reference is proposed to be partly a result of the measurement
method of the temperature. The temperature of the coil windings may not be accurately
determined by measuring the core of the ferromagnetic material.
The assumption of
reasonably close and uniform temperature profile throughout the BEMs may not be as
accurate as originally assumed. To acquire a better insight to this challenge, numerical
tools are used to determine the direction of heat flux and the thermal distribution of
the heat in the BEMs. This can improve the accuracy of the estimates from the core
temperature sensors. Additionally, the calibration of the current sensors require additional
validation to find a possible measurement error and an explanation to the underestimation
of the resistance.

Although some of the resistances are below the prescribed values in the datasheet, they
show a somewhat similar trend as the reference stemming from the coppers thermal
resistance coeﬀicient. In the mathematical model, the coil resistance is assumed to be
the same in both BEMs, the experimental data illustrated the difference in resistances
because of the increased power dissipation in the upper BEM as a result of the differential
control regime. When heat is unevenly distributed among the BEMs, it results in varying
resistances between the two. The assumption is accurate when the heat in the AMB
system is approximately even distributed between the BEMs. Assuming this, the system
is of the simpler form, a Single-Input-Single-Output (SISO) model.
The back EMF were considered in the estimates of the resistance, but its impact is assessed
to be insignificant. The position data, when heat is added to the lower BEM at 80°C, has
more movement than the others at the end, but the data show no apparent effect, since
the movement is around an equilibrium where the mean value of the velocities should be
approximately zero and hence the back EMF will cancel out.
The step responses show the performances of the PID controllers can be improved. The
overshoot is relatively large in the step responses that are shown and this is further dealt
with in the parameter optimization process.

6.2.5
Discussion
The optimization process generally converged towards good results between the experi-
mental and theoretical model. When the system was later used, in some cases it demon-
strated the frequency of the system had not adjusted with the other parameters. This
was primarily experienced when compared to experiments with only a single input of
heat, which is partly explained by the underestimation of the resistances. The thermal
conditions were not suﬀiciently high to cause changes to the magnetic permeability, which
increases the path reluctance as outlined in the literature review. Initially the optimiza-
tion only considered the air gap, magnetic permeability and axial PMB stiffness, but the
optimized value for the inductance is three times larger than the original parameter. The
initial optimization process did not find a suﬀicient match with the mathematical model.
The significantly higher inductance was validated with an LCR meter and the results
were almost on par with the optimized value. The equation governing the inductance

in the AMB system does not have any variables that can explain this deviation. The
tilting mode shapes are coupled to the BEMs, although the model suggests it is not. It is
caused by the imperfections in the alignment of the PMBs. The governing equation for
the inductance are based on the assumption of small air gaps. Comparing the air gap in
this test rig to other setups, the air gap size in this test rig is 25 times larger than the
test rig in [11] for instance. The air gap is not the only determining factor, but also the
magnetic force. When the air gap increases, the fringing effect becomes more prominent
which increases the effective area for the inductance in the AMB system.

while the system is running can introduce perturbation that can cause instabilities. As a

changing back and fourth in the temperature transition phase. This method is not flawless
and lacks robustness. For instance, if the desired temperature is between two ranges, it

Conclusion
The main goal of this master’s thesis was to investigate the thermal effect on an Active
Magnetic Bearing system.
The test rig has been improved with better measurement
surfaces for the position sensors and a new rotor with improved rigidity. Temperature
sensors have been fitted to enable the experimental work. Lastly, the test setup has a new
and improved LabVIEW UI, focusing on the PID controller and the thermal experiments
and with a Time-Scheduling UI.
Then a linearized LTI state space model governing the dynamics of the mechanical- and
electrical system was derived with the incorporation of a differential PID control regime,
stabilizing the inherently unstable behaviour from the Active- and Passive Magnetic Bear-
ings. The model’s limitations were presented on the basis of the simplifying assumptions.
The coil resistances were studied to determine the thermal impact on the AMB system.
Long thermal steady state experiments, 2 −3 hours, were conducted to ensure the values
had settled suﬀiciently. The thermal steady state experiments demonstrated the conver-
gence as desired. However, the resistance estimates had relatively large deviations from
the prescribed values in the datasheet, that were also validated with a multimeter at the
internal steady state conditions. Furthermore, in the experiments where heat was only
added in one of the BEMs, the opposite BEM illustrates a particular underestimation of
the resistance. No possible explanation has been found for this underestimation in the
opposite BEM. The estimated values show a linear trend comparative to the reference,
stemming from the coppers thermal resistance coeﬀicient, although the absolute value
deviated from the reference. Other explanations to the deviations in the resistance es-
timates have been assessed. A possible cause can be a drift in the current sensors as it
was calibrated early on. It is also a possibility the H-bridge in the motor driver does
not directly throughput the voltage according to the PWM signal, as assumed from the
beginning.
The thermal steady state experiments also illustrated the increasing instability of the
AMB system when it dropped the rotors three times in different experiments and had
increased oscillatory movements during two other steady state experiments.
A small tilt is present when the rotor levitates at the equilibrium position, it is a result of a
non-uniform magnetic field from the PMBs, that is caused by misalignment in the arrays.
It may also be the new rotor that has introduced a change in the PMB configuration
through tiny movements of the ring magnets on the rotor. The impact is likely small, but
it could be the cause of the periodic oscillatory behaviour every 50s which is evident in
the thermal steady state experiments. This is due to the small moments introduced by
the BEMs when maintaining the equilibrium position, coupling the tilting mode shapes
and the BEMs through the misalignment of the PMBs. Implementing a bandstop filter
that attenuates the frequencies in the oscillatory behaviour in the position can determine
if it is caused by the controller.
The optimization process used the mean value of the estimated resistances in the exper-
iment for the internal thermal steady state. The optimized value for the inductance is
Axial Active Magnetic Bearing System

==PAGE:92==
three times higher than the original parameter. The significantly higher inductance were
validated with an LCR meter. The results were almost on par with the optimized value.
The equation governing the inductance in the AMB system does not have any variables
that can cause this deviation, the simplifying assumptions were revisited. The air gap in
the studied test rig is assessed to not fulfill the assumption for small air gaps while also
considering the magnetic force. When the air gap increases, the fringing effect become in-
creasingly prominent, consequently increasing the effective area for the AMB inductance,
which in turn increases the inductance.
A new PID controller is designed with the use of the optimized mathematical model. The
PID controller demonstrates improved stability, decreased overshoot and settling time.
The activation of the tilting motion through the step responses were demonstrated to
increase the amplitude in the axial direction. A behaviour that is not included in the
mathematical model due to simplifying assumptions.
A primitive draft of time-scheduled PID gains is designed and implemented in the Lab-
VIEW UI and presented through a single experiment.
7.1
Future Aspects
With the completion of this master’s thesis, opportunities have presented themselves for
future improvements on the test rig and additional studies.
Academic Aspects
• Numerical simulation of the heat distribution and heat flux direction in the BEMs,
to determine the core temperature deviation from the coil winding temperature.
Derive a model estimating this deviation.
• Improve the resistance estimates through validation of the current sensor calibra-
tions and voltage throughput in the motor driver. Include also the underestimation
of the resistance for the BEM opposite the heat source.
• The air gap size is comparatively larger than other AMB systems, the fringing
effect might alter the inductance in the system. Further investigation into this can
help determine the cause of the large deviation in the inductance and improve the
mathematical model.
Practical Aspects
• Validation of current sensor calibrations due to the large discrepancies in the resis-
tance estimates.
• Validate the inductance linearity dependency on the air gaps with an LCR meter
and the additional spacers for the rotor that decreases the air gap to 3mm.
• Implement of a bandstop filter to determine if the attenuation of the periodic oscil-
latory behaviour is caused by the controller.
• Improving the alignment of the PMBs can decrease the tilting motions resulting in
improved stability.

Run-Out Measurement
The run-out of the end discs in the axial direction is measured using a rigid bench with
rollers, see Figure E.1. A run-out gauge is mounted on an independent part. This run-out
Mitutoyo gauge is placed on the disc, 5mm from the edge and then it is zeroed. The rotor
is then gently spun one entire rotation by rotating the wheels, and not touching the rotor.
Several rotations are done to ensure repeatability.
Figure E.1: Rigid bench with rollers for run-out measurement.

is equally spaced around the ”mean” position of the disc.
1. Upper had roughly ±500µm
2. Lower had roughly ±350µm
The new and improved rotor design resulted in
1. Upper has a run-out of roughly ±20µm
2. Lower has a run-out of roughly ±16µm
The Mitutoyo Gauge has an MPE of 5µm, giving relatively large uncertainties in the new
rotor run-out measurement.

5.0 A Throttle Control H-Bridge
The 33926 is a SMARTMOS monolithic H-Bridge Power IC 
designed primarily for automotive electronic throttle control, but is 
applicable to any low-voltage DC servo motor control application 
within the current and voltage limits stated in this specification.
The 33926 is able to control inductive loads with currents up to 
5.0 A peak. RMS current capability is subject to the degree of 
heatsinking provided to the device package. Internal peak-current 
limiting (regulation) is activated at load currents above 6.5 A 1.5 A. 
Output loads can be pulse width modulated (PWM’ed) at frequencies 
up to 20 kHz. A load current feedback feature provides a proportional 
(0.24% of the load current) current output suitable for monitoring by a 
microcontroller’s A/D input. A Status Flag output reports 
undervoltage, overcurrent, and overtemperature fault conditions. 
Two independent inputs provide polarity control of two half-bridge 
totem-pole outputs. Two independent disable inputs are provided to 
force the H-Bridge outputs to tri-state (high-impedance off state). An 
inverted input changes the IN1 and IN2 inputs to LOW = true logic.
Features
•
5.0 to 28 V continuous operation (transient operation from 5.0 to 
40 V)
•
225 m maximum RDS(on) at 150 °C (each H-Bridge MOSFET)
•
3.0 V and 5.0 V TTL / CMOS logic compatible inputs
•
Overcurrent limiting (Regulation) via an internal constant-off-time 
PWM
•
Output short-circuit protection (short to VPWR or ground)
•
Temperature dependent current limit threshold reduction
•
All Inputs have an internal source/sink to define the default (floating 
input) states
•
Sleep mode with current draw < 50 µA (with inputs floating or set to 
match default logic states)

Application information
Filtering, decoupling CTSR transducer
Supply voltage Uc (5 V):
The CTSR transducers have internal decoupling capacitors, but in the case of a power supply track on the application PCB having 
a high impedance, it is advised to provide local decoupling, 100 nF or more, located close to the transducer. 
Reference Vref
Ripple present on the Vref pin can be filtered with a low value of capacitance because of the internal 499 ohm series resistance. 
The CTSR transducers have an internal capacitor of 22 nF between Vref pin and Gnd pin and the maximum filter capacitance value 
which could be added is 1 µF. Adding a larger decoupling capacitor will increase the activation delay of degauss.
Output Vout
The CTSR transducers have an internal low pass filter 470 ohm/22 nF; if a decoupling capacitor is added on Vout pin, the bandwidth 
and the response time will be affected. In case of short circuit, the transducer CTSR can source or sink up to a maximum of 10 
mA on its output Vout.
Using an external reference voltage
If the Vref pin of the transducer is not used it could be either left unconnected or filtered according to the previous paragraph 
“Reference Vref”.
The Vref pin has two modes Ref out and Ref In:
•	 In the Ref out mode the 2.5 V internal precision reference is used by the transducer as the reference point for bipolar 
measurements; this internal reference is connected to the Vref pin of the transducer through a 499 ohms resistor. It 
tolerates sink or source currents up to ± 5 mA, but the 499 ohms resistor prevents this current to exceed these limits.
•	 In the Ref In mode, an external reference voltage is connected to the VREF pin; this voltage is specified in the range 2.3 
to 4 V and is directly used by the transducer as the reference point for measurements. The external reference voltage 
Vref must be able:
o
o either to source a typical current of               , the maximum value will be 3 mA when Vref = 4 V.
o
o or to sink a typical current of               , the maximum value will be 0.4 mA when Vref = 2.3 V.

Primary nominal residual current and primary nominal current
The primary nominal residual current is the sum of the instantaneous values of all currents flowing through the aperture of the 
transducer.
The primary nominal current is the current flowing through any conductor placed into the aperture of the transducer.
The presence of a primary nominal current DC or AC leads to an additional uncertainty.
For example, with a primary nominal current of 30 A the uncertainty referred to primary is typical 4.3 mA.
CTSR transducer in Test mode
When the Vref pin is forced at a low level voltage between 0 and 1 V and is maintained at this level, the output voltage Vout of CTSR 
transducer exhibits a fixed value (see specification) as if it measured a primary test current.
The activation time of test mode is min 30 ms. The CTSR transducer can be maintained in test mode as long as needed for 
checking that it is fully operating.
CTSR transducer in Degauss mode
The CTSR transducers go in degauss mode automatically at each power on or on demand by using the Vref pin.
At power on:
A degauss is automatically generated at each power on of the CTSR transducer; during degaussing the output voltage Vout is 
maintained at 0.3 V typ. (max 0.5 V). After c.a. 110 ms, the output voltage Vout is released and takes the normal operation level in 
relation with the measured primary current.
Using VREF pin:
When the pin Vref is released from the Low level voltage defined in the Test mode above, there is a rising edge on Vref which 
generates an automatic degauss.
The activation of degauss takes typically 40 µs after releasing Vref pin, then degauss lasts typically 110 ms.

Definition of typical, minimum and maximum values
Minimum and maximum values for specified limiting and safety conditions have to be understood as such as well as values 
shown in “typical” graphs. 
On the other hand, measured values are part of a statistical distribution that can be specified by an interval with upper and lower 
limits and a probability for measured values to lie within this interval.
Unless otherwise stated (e.g. “100 % tested”), the LEM definition for such intervals designated with “min” and “max” is that the 
probability for values of samples to lie in this interval is 99.73 %. 
For a normal (Gaussian) distribution, this corresponds to an interval between -3 sigma and +3 sigma. If “typical” values are not 
obviously mean or average values, those values are defined to delimit intervals with a probability of 68.27 %, corresponding to 
an interval between -sigma and +sigma for a normal distribution. 
Typical, maximal and minimal values are determined during the initial characterization of a product.

TYPE 214 INSTRUCTIONS FOR USE 
The Type 214 is a analoger transmitter for Pt100/1000 temperature sensors. It converts the temperature-
dependent resistance to a standard voltage signal of 0 .. 10 V in order. It is designed for mounting on 
DIN rail in 35mm, but can also be mounted in other suitable housing. 
OPENING  THE HOUSING AND LOCATION OF CONTROLS 
To open the clearview cover this must be carefully pressed inwards 
on the narrow sides and lifted off.  The location of the 
potentiometers can be seen in the adjacent figure.  The 
potentiometers are protected against inadvertent adjustments.   
 
INPUT CIRCUIT FOR THE SENSORS  
 
In the 2-wire connection the resistance of the leads 
affects the results measured. Therefore this circuit should 
only be selected if the leads are short or accuracy 
requirements are low.   
The zero potentiometer can also be adjusted to correct for 
lead resistance.  The span potentiometer should be 
adjusted as little as possible.  A jumper must be connected 
between terminals 1 and 2, and between 3 and 4, for each 
measuring transducer. 
 
 
By splitting one input lead in the current and 
signal path the resistance of the lead 
elements in the 3-wire connection does 
not affect the results measured.  This circuit 
represents a compromise between the two 
and four lead circuits. 
 
 
By splitting both input leads in the current and signal path 
the resistance of the lead elements in the 
4-wire connection does not affect the results measured. 
This circuit thus offers the greatest accuracy, but also 
requires the most wiring resource.  In the first instance it is 
envisaged for use when the distance between sensor and 
measuring transducer is greater.

möglichst nicht verstellt werden. Zwischen die Anschlüsse 
1 und 2 bzw. 3 und 4 muss jeweils am Messumformer eine 
Brücke geklemmt werden. 
 
Durch Auftrennung einer Zuleitung in Strom- und 
Signalpfad geht der Widerstand der entsprechenden 
Teilleitung bei der Dreileiterschaltung nicht in das 
Messergebnis ein. Diese Schaltung stellt einen 
Kompromiss zwischen der Zwei- und Vierleiterschaltung 
dar. 
 
 
Durch Auftrennung beider Zuleitungen in Strom- und 
Signalpfad geht der Widerstand der Leitungen bei der 
Vierleiterschaltung nicht in das Messergebnis ein. Diese 
Schaltung bietet deshalb die größte Genauigkeit, aber 
auch den größten Verkabelungsaufwand. Sie ist vor allem 
bei größerer Entfernung zwischen Sensor und 
Messumformer vorzusehen.

TYPE 214 INSTRUCTIONS FOR USE 
The Type 214 is a analoger transmitter for Pt100/1000 temperature sensors. It converts the temperature-
dependent resistance to a standard voltage signal of 0 .. 10 V in order. It is designed for mounting on 
DIN rail in 35mm, but can also be mounted in other suitable housing. 
OPENING  THE HOUSING AND LOCATION OF CONTROLS 
To open the clearview cover this must be carefully pressed inwards 
on the narrow sides and lifted off.  The location of the 
potentiometers can be seen in the adjacent figure.  The 
potentiometers are protected against inadvertent adjustments.   
 
INPUT CIRCUIT FOR THE SENSORS  
 
In the 2-wire connection the resistance of the leads 
affects the results measured. Therefore this circuit should 
only be selected if the leads are short or accuracy 
requirements are low.   
The zero potentiometer can also be adjusted to correct for 
lead resistance.  The span potentiometer should be 
adjusted as little as possible.  A jumper must be connected 
between terminals 1 and 2, and between 3 and 4, for each 
measuring transducer. 
 
 
By splitting one input lead in the current and 
signal path the resistance of the lead 
elements in the 3-wire connection does 
not affect the results measured.  This circuit 
represents a compromise between the two 
and four lead circuits. 
 
 
By splitting both input leads in the current and signal path 
the resistance of the lead elements in the 
4-wire connection does not affect the results measured. 
This circuit thus offers the greatest accuracy, but also 
requires the most wiring resource.  In the first instance it is 
envisaged for use when the distance between sensor and 
measuring transducer is greater.
