Abstract
One of the most common chronic diseases that affect people’s lives is diabetes. One
of the core challenges within diabetes treatment is the inability to continually monitor a
diabetic’s blood sugar in a non­invasive manner, and since invasive technologies cannot
be used for continuous monitoring due to the discomfort brought on by invasive glucose
measurements, a non­invasive blood glucose measurement has long been needed. The
most widely used techniques in research are Terahertz Time­Domain Spectroscopy (THz­
TDS) and Fourier­Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR). In this study, we looked into
the feasibility of combining two non­invasive technologies to detect glucose concentration.
Using the THz­TDS, a Partial Least Squares (PLS) model produced a reading of 99.58%
in zone A + B of the Clarke error grid. The FTIR results demonstrated that the cuvette’s
transmittance limits the acquisition of glucose information in the mid­IR and far­IR.

Introduction
According to the World Health Organization (WHO), diabetes mellitus is a metabolic dis­
order characterized by abnormal blood glucose levels, caused by either a failure of the
pancreas to secrete insulin as a result of autoimmune β­cell destruction or an abnormal
response of body cells to insulin as a result of a progressive loss of β­cell insulin secretion
[1]. Insulin is the hormone that is secreted from the pancreas to control glucose levels
in the blood. A shortage of insulin or the inability of cells to respond to it, will cause hy­
perglycemia (high blood glucose levels), which is a clinical indication of diabetes, where
taking more insulin than needed can lead to hypoglycemia (low blood glucose levels) [2].
The diagnosis of diabetes mellitus is confirmed through the use of laboratory tests that
measure blood glucose levels. Figure 1.1 shows the diabetes diagnosis threshold levels.

2
Background
Researchers have developed non­invasive glucose sensors that are dependable, quick
painless, and cost­effective, for the convenience of patients to often test their glucose
level. Different kinds of non­invasive techniques have been introduced over the past two
decades such as non­optical and optical methods [12]. Among the non­invasive glucose
monitoring techniques, the optical methods produce the best measurements [13]. Opti­
cal techniques such as near­infrared, mid­infrared or Raman spectroscopy exhibit high
selectivity for glucose sensing considering the complexity of the blood/tissue properties.
Additionally, the targeted biological tissue in optical approaches is less exposed to irri­
tation [12]. Therefore, the most recent non­invasive optical sensors for blood glucose
monitoring are the topic of this chapter.
2.1
Non­Invasive Optical Techniques
In this section a brief explanation of optical techniques is provided, along with a summary
of their advantages and disadvantages.
2.1.1
Near­Infrared (NIR) Spectroscopy
Near­infrared (NIR) spectroscopy is a method for gathering quantitative information about
a sample and its components. It uses radiation with a wavelength between 750­2500 nm,
it’s radiation can penetrate the skin beyond 0.5 mm [12]. The blood’s absorption of NIR
light is increased by the C­H, O­H and C=O bond in glucose molecules. It has peaks at
particular wavelengths. The Beer­Lambert law formula makes it possible to determine
glucose absorbance from its concentration [14]. The transmission and reflection modes
can be used to take NIR measurements and blood glucose levels can be calculated by
measuring changes in light intensity brought on by the absorption or scattering of glucose
molecules from NIR light, which is dependent upon the amount of glucose present in the
tissue [12]. Simplified representations of the transmission and reflection modes of NIR
spectroscopy are shown in figure 2.1. The NIR spectroscopy setup consists mainly of a
sample exposed to NIR radiation, a photo­diode to measure the attenuated light waves th­

as temperature and humidity may also have an impact on the measurement results [7].
Therefore, alternative non invasive optical techniques for measuring glucose are getting
more attention.

2.1.3
Raman Spectroscopy
Raman spectroscopy uses a monochromatic light source that ranges from visible to MIR
to determine the sample content based on the Raman effect [13]. Monochromatic light
produces scattered light that travels in all directions. When it strikes an target, the major­
ity of this radiation, known as elastic or Rayleigh scattering, has the same wavelength as
the incident light, while the remaining radiation, known as inelastic scattering or Raman
scattering is very little and has a different wavelength. The Raman shift is the resultant
difference in wavelength. This shift gives information about bodily fluids rotational and
vibrations properties to monochromatic light. The molecular concentration of the bodily
fluids affects the vibration of the molecules [13]. The fundamental design of a Raman
spectroscopy, is displayed in figure 2.2. It consists of a collimnating lens that collects the
scattered radiation and directs it to a filter that only allows the Raman scattered light to
be detected by the detector. The signal is processed by the computer, which outputs the
corresponding Raman shift.
The sharp narrow peaks that shows in the band response, make this spectroscopy excep­
tional and make it easier to separate the signals. Unfortunately, interference from other

2.2
Terahertz Time­Domain Spectroscopy (THz­TDS)
The electromagnetic radiation known as THz has a frequency between 0.3 and 10 THz.
This frequency range is higher than the microwave but lower than infrared making it at
the border of the electronic and optical. This so­called terahertz gap was previously chal­
lenging to access due to inadequate device capability, high strong thermal background
noise and air absorption [17].
Data collection in the THz frequency range offers various benefits, where many sub­
stances, such as pharmaceuticals, have distinct fingerprints in this range and biological
samples are not ionized by terahertz radiation. Terahertz Time­Domain spectroscopy as
(THz­TDS), is a spectroscopic method for identifying the characteristics of a sample that
has been exposed to brief terahertz radiation pulses [18]. Which makes it a candidate for
monitoring blood glucose levels.

2.3
Fourier­Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR)
MIR spectroscopy seems to be the best method for detecting glucose concentration, ac­
cording to the advantages and disadvantages discussed in section 2.1. However, The de­
velopment of the third generation IR spectrometer, the Fourier Transform­Infrared Spec­
trometer (FTIR), has a number of prominent benefits [24]. FTIR has a substantially greater
signal­to­noise ratio than earlier infrared generation. All frequencies have a quick scan
time. Extremely high resolution is present. FTIR Spectrometers have taken the place of
IR Spectrometers as a result of these benefits [25].
The optical instrumentation in an FTIR spectroscopy consists of an infrared light source, a
computer, and the Michelson interferometer, which consists of two mirrors, a beam split­
ter, and one detector. The source emits radiation that the sample is exposed to while it go­

The Michelson interferometer, is the main component of FTIR spectroscopy, it divides
a single beam of light into two, which sends half of the incoming light to a stationary
mirror and the other half to a moving mirror. The two beams from the moving mirror and
stationary mirror are then recombined and travels to the detector through the sample.
The interferogram, or interference pattern, is generated when the combined beam strikes
through the beam splitter [24]. The pattern varies relative to the position of the moving
mirror. The interferogram is later converted to a spectrum through a Fourier Transform
[25].

4.1
Principal Components Analysis (PCA)
In today’s data­sets, there are frequently a significant number of variables. Due to the
fact that the human mind cannot simply process data on such a huge scale, inspecting
each of the variables independently becomes challenging. When a data­set has a high
number of variables, there is frequently a great deal of overlap between them [30].
Principal Components Analysis (PCA) is a statistical technique commonly used to trans­
form the variables into a new collection of variables known as components. PCA achieves
this goal by projecting the original data onto a lower­dimensional subspace that preserves
the majority of the variation between the data points [31].
Quantification of glucose levels in aqueous solutions using a combination of infrared and terahertz
spectroscopy techniques

==PAGE:30==
PCA is defined mathematically as finding a linear combination of the original variables
with the maximum variance. This indicates that we will generate a new component called
t. This t will be calculated by multiplying our initial variables (X1, X2, ...) by a weight for
each of our variables (w1, w2, ...). t = Xw can be written as a formula, with a unit length
limit on w. The mathematical goal is to discover the values for w that maximize the vari­
ance of t [32].
Finding the components that optimize variance is simple with PCA. This is ideal if we want
to reduce the number of variables while maintaining a high level of variance. However,
we may not always be interested in maximizing variance, we may wish to offer our newly
formed dimensions the most helpful interpretations.
4.2
Partial Least Square (PLS)
Since PCA focuses on maximizing variance, there might be some certain information’s
that are crucial where the variance is minimal; in this case, PCA may not be the best ap­
proach; instead, one might use Partial Least Square (PLS), which focuses on maximizing
covariance.
PLS is similar to PCA, where the variables are projected onto a lower­dimensional space.
One disadvantage of PCA is that the dimensionality reduction is not supervised, which
means that certain critical variables may be lost. The variables with the largest variance
would be kept by PCA, however it’s likely that variables with minor variances are important
in determining the target y. PLS enables for dimensionality reduction in a similar fashion,
but by taking into consideration the target y [33].
There are subcategories within PLS, and PLS Regression is the most used model, it’s
the foundation of the other models in the PLS family. It’s a regression model that applies
when the dependent variables are numerical, and when the dependent variables are cat­
egorical, one will utilize Discriminant Analysis as an alternative [34].

corresponding to the invasive measurements’ method. These error grids are separated
into five risk regions depending on he clinical important of diabetes conditions. The dis­
tinction is that PEG analysis distinguishes between type 1 and type 2 diabetes [46].

age using MADR and we are not interested in distinguishing between type 1 and type 2
diabetes, the CEG was used to evaluate our PLS model.

crucial for estimating blood glucose levels, which could be the cause of not improving the
performance.
After creating all of these different models, we need to choose the best one. We need
to look at the highest R2 and the lowest RMSE value and that’s model 1, while model
5 comes in second place. However, this does not necessarily mean model 1 is the best
model; model 1 uses 12 components, whereas model 5 uses 5, in terms of dimensionality
reduction model 5 is the best model. So models 1 and 5 were selected for more evaluation.
Although we had mentioned in Chapter 4 that PLS should perform better than PCA, we
had developed PCA models for these models using their respective numbers of compo­
nents, and the resulting R2 values are respectively 0.82 and 0.72; this demonstrates that
PCA did not perform better than PLS.

FTIR Results and Discussion
Figure 5.5 displays the mid­IR and far­IR of the FTIR measurments. The orange line,
which represents the cuvette without any solutions, shows that the cuvette has a signal in
the mid­IR up to 2000 cm−1, but not in the far­IR. Nearly no signal is visible in the mid­IR
and far­IR on the green and red lines, which correspond to cuvetter containing 3.5 mg/mL
and 100 mg/mL of glucose solution, respectively. This suggests that the cuvette stops
transmitting at 2000 cm−1. The relevant glucose measurements may be in the range of
2000 to 500 cm−1.
The problem is that the FTIR spectroscopy used in the studies was designed for measure­
ments of solid materials rather than measurements of liquid solutions utilizing transmission
mode. FTIR machines that measures aqueous solution uses reflection mode and does
not require a holder. Therefore, we needed to locate a holder so we can carry out our
FTIR measurements.

ordered one to investigate if there would be any signal in the 4000–2000 cm−1 range.

6
Conclusion
In conclusion, aqueous glucose solutions’ THz­TDS and IR absorption spectra have been
investigated. We have demonstrated that THz­TDS measurements are an easy and ac­
curate tool for determining glucose concentrations. The glucose concentration was de­
termined by a PLS model that produced a reading of 99.58% in zone A + B of the Clarke
error grid. Our findings for the FTIR are consistent with the idea that the cuvette’s transmit­
tance prevents glucose­specific information from being obtained in the mid­IR or far­IR.
We want to conduct the experiment again in the future utilizing an FTIR system in the
reflection mode for aqueous solution.
