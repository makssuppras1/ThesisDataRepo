Abstract
Today, autonomous mobility, particularly autonomous vehicles, is advancing rapidly. In
addition, several projects in recent years have focused on utilizing waterways for trans-
portation. This project aims to implement a SLAM solution that ensures precise localiza-
tion and mapping for the harbour buses in Copenhagen, along with reliable detection of
the docking platforms.
The project utilizes data acquired from two round trips on the harbour bus route. The
solution employs the FAST-LIO2 algorithm, combined with a detection module based
on point cloud registration.
The accuracy of the solution is evaluated through RTK
measurements and GNSS data.
The project demonstrates that centimeter-level accuracy in both localization and mapping
can be achieved using FAST-LIO2. Additionally, RANSAC registration of a template of
the docking stations is proved effective for precise localization within the maps generated
by the algorithm.
Furthermore, it is shown through a proof-of-concept experiment that relocalization into
the maps generated by FAST-LIO2 can be integrated to enhance the system’s reliability.

Chapter 1
Introduction
We live in exciting times when it comes to autonomous mobility. Over the past years,
significant development has happened in this sector caused by progress within sensor
technology, machine learning and computational capabilities as well as more invenstments
in the sector. Especially when it comes to Autonomous Vehicles (AVs), we see companies
such as Waymo operating in San Francisco and Los Angeles [1], and closer to us AVs
operated in a suburb of Oslo in collaboration between Holo, Ruter and Mobileye [2].
Also when it comes to utilizing waterways for transportation, several projects have emerged
in the recent years. Examples include the autonomous ferry in Stockholm that transports
passengers between Kungsholmen and S¨odermalm [3], Denmark’s first autonomous har-
bour bus, the GreenHopper [4], and ASKO Maritime’s use of autonomous ’sea drones’
to ferry trailers across the Oslo fjord [5]. These examples highlight that also technolo-
gies for autonomous maritime freight are beginning to develop, leading to new business
opportunities.
Each day, urban ferries and harbour buses make multiple stops to transport passengers,
with docking maneuvers being one of the most critical operations. This affects passenger
safety, navigator workload, and energy consumption.
Fully or partly automating this
process could positively impact all three areas, making the exploration of methods to do
so both relevant and interesting.
This thesis will be focusing on solutions for harbour busses in the Copenhagen harbour.
LiDAR-IMU SLAM it utilized to achieve high-precision 3D mapping and localization of
the harbour bus. Additionally, point cloud registration methods are employed to detect
docking stations within the generated map.

1.1
Problem Definition
This project aims at implementing a Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM)
solution that allows for precise localization and mapping of the harbour busses in the
Copenhagen harbour utilizing multiple sensor modalities and state-of-the-art SLAM al-
gorithms. Furthermore, the project aims at finding a precise measure for detecting the
docking stations on the route of the harbour bus to allow for accurate navigation to the
docking positions.
Today, harbour busses like the one seen on Figure 1.1a are manually maneuvered and
transport passengers between 11 stops on a route that goes from Orientkaj to Teglholmen
as seen on Figure 1.1b. The current harbour busses have room for 80 passengers and
operate at a service speed of 8 knots. In 2021, the harbour busses transported around
450.000 passengers between the stops [6].

This project is based on data recorded on the harbour bus with the sensor platform

The solution is therefore limited to utilizing sensor modali-

Automatic Identification System (AIS).
Additional requirements to the solution are the assumption that GNSS is not available and
that the localization of the harbour bus during docking should have centimeter accuracy.

Literature Review
1.2.1
SLAM Methods
Generally, SLAM methods can be divided into two groups based on the data they rely on.
Visual-based methods can provide 3D representations of the environment using RGB-D,
stereo cameras and structure from motion by extracting characteristic features from images
and recognizing them in subsequent images. In comparison, LiDAR-based methods collect
consecutive scans and register them to generate a 3D point cloud of the environment.
Because the LiDAR data itself provides 3D information along with the fact that they
can measure distances of hundreds of meters, have a large field of view and generally are
less sensitive to light conditions and surface characteristics, LiDAR-based methods are
particularly relevant for doing 3D mapping [8].
The implementation method of the algorithms can again be divided into three main cat-
egories: filter-based, graph-based and deep learning-based. Filter-based approaches treat
the SLAM problem as a state-estimation problem commonly utilizing various versions of
the Kalman filter or particle filters in the implementation. Graph-based approaches com-
monly treat the SLAM problem as a pose-graph optimization, where nodes represent poses
and landmarks and edges the constraints between them. Deep learning-based approaches
uses neural networks and deep learning and can be used on all parts of the SLAM problem
including describing features, aligning measurements and making estimates [9].
Visual-Based SLAM
Barros et al. [10] provides a comprehensive survey of visual SLAM algorithms divided
into three categories: visual-only, visual-intertial and RGB-D SLAM.
Visual-only SLAM algorithms are based on 2D image processing and may use monocular
or stereo cameras - examples of algorithms are MonoSLAM [11], DTAM [12], ORB-SLAM2
[13] and CNN-SLAM [14]. Common problems for visual-only SLAM algorithms are the
initialization of the system, pose estimation and estimating drift and scale some of which
can be compensated by using stereo cameras.
Visual-Inertial SLAM algorithms incorporate an IMU either to estimate the orientation
and changes in the sensor’s position (loosely coupled) or by adding it to the motion
equation to get a state estimation considering both IMU and camera data (tightly coupled).
This adds more information to the algorithm but also a higher complexity - especially in
the tightly coupled case [10]. Examples of Visual-Inertial SLAM algorithms are MSCKF
[15], OKVIS [16], VI-DSO [17] and ORB-SLAM3 [18].

RGB-D SLAM algorithms present advantages to the two methods described above in-
cluding color image data and a depth map without any pre-processing. Therefore, the
general approaches of these algorithms are also different from the ones mentioned above,
where most algorithms uses Iterative Closest Point (ICP) algorithms to locate the sensor
and fusing the depth map to achieve 3D reconstruction [10]. Examples of RGB-D SLAM
algorithms are KinectFusion [19], SLAM++ [20], DVO-SLAM [21] and RGBDSLAMv2
[22].
Due to the nature of the data of monocular and stereo cameras, most of the algorithms
above are filter-based due to the high necessity of making estimates of e.g.
depth in-
formation. Examples of graph-based algorithms are SLAM++ and DVO-SLAM whereas
CNN-SLAM is an example of the utilization of convolutional neural network for prediction
of the depth.
LiDAR-Based SLAM
LiDAR sensors are playing an increasingly important role in autonomous robots and are
heavily used in AVs - e.g., in both real-world implementations by Waymo and Mobileye
mentioned above [1, 23]. Moreover, recent developments are enabling commercialization
and mass production of more light-weight, cost-effective and high-performance solid-state
LiDARs, making it a more available technology [24].
LiDARs are exterioceptive sensors emitting laser pulses and calculating the position and
distance of objects from the time of flight of the reflected laser pulse. The field of view
of a LiDAR is commonly high and combining this with the number of scan lines, LiDARs
are able to generate very high amount of data. Generally, working with LiDARs poses
four problems [25]:
1. Large amounts of data require high computational efficiency in online solutions.
2. Feature point extraction may cause problems in some structure-less environments.
3. Motion distortion caused by sampling during motion.
4. Long measuring range, but low resolution between scan lines.
Fusing LiDAR and IMU data is a popular way of dealing with motion distortion. Some
of the state-of-the-art LiDAR and IMU based SLAM algorithms are RTAB-Map [26],
LeGO-LOAM [27], LIO-SAM [28], hdl graph [29], DLO [30] and FAST-LIO2 [25].

1.2.2
Detection Methods
When it comes to detection methods for the docking stations on the route of the harbour
bus, mainly three approaches were considered. Standard image processing techniques, like
template and feature matching, can be used for image data, while point cloud matching
methods suit LiDAR data. Additionally, deep learning techniques are applicable to both
data types, as demonstrated in [34] and [35]. In fact, utilizing deep learning methods on
RGB data for object detection and tracking has received heavy attention from academia
in the past years [36].
The focus of this project has been on exploring relevant methods that are not based on
deep learning.

Image Processing
A widely used method for object detection in images is template matching. Hashemi et
al. [37] provides an overview of advances and and applications of template matching in
image analysis.
The methods spans from na¨ıve matching, where a template is slid over an image to find
the best match, to more sophisticated feature matching methods that creates descriptors
around specific points in the image and matches these. This makes the solutions invariant
to scale variations and rotation.
Common feature descriptors are Scale-Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT) [38] and Oriented
FAST and Rotated BRIEF (ORB) [39]. Various matchers such as Fast Library for Ap-
proximate Nearest Neighbors (FLANN) and Brute Force (BF) [40] can then be used to
find feature matches between the template and the image.
Point Cloud Registration
Point cloud registrations methods are mainly divided into two types: local and global
registration. Global registration does not need an inital alignment of the point clouds,
whereas local registration is used for increased precision of the alignment of two point
clouds based on an initial registration.
Common algorithms for local point cloud registration are ICP [41, 42], ICP-derived meth-
ods such as described in [43, 44] and probabilistic methods such as described in [45, 46].
The difference between ICP and probabilistic methods is that probabilistic methods model
the point clouds as probability distributions, commonly Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM),
reformulating the registration problem as a way to minimize two data distributions. They
therefore construct more densely weighted correspondences, which is more robust but also
requires more memory and computing [47]. Recent works like [43, 44], therefore focus on
improving computation efficiency through efficient data structures and fast, approximate
methods.
A common method for global point cloud registration is Random Sample Consensus
(RANSAC) [48], which is based on model fitting.
Just like ICP this method is itera-
tive and often evaluated using closest point queries, resulting in growth of runtime with
increasing number of points.
Several methods have therefore been explored aiming at
reducing the runtime. An example of this is Fast Global Registration (FGR) which does
not involve iterative sampling, model fitting or local refinement.
Feature descriptors capturing unique local geometric properties are commonly used in
point cloud registration to efficiently and accurately identify and match corresponding

A widely used descriptor is Fast Point Feature

1.3
Methods and Tools
Going back to the definition of the problem in Section 1.1, there are four key requirements
to the SLAM algorithm:
1. The aim of the project is a solution that can be implemented on a harbour bus, and
the algorithm must therefore be able to run online.
2. The route of the harbour bus does not include frequent loop closure, as seen on
Figure 1.1b, and the precision of the SLAM solution must therefore not depend
heavily on this.
3. The distance between individual stops on the route are hundreds of meters, so the
solution should optimally be able to build and maintain big maps.
4. Since centimeter accuracy of the localization of the docking position is a requirement,
the algorithm must provide accurate results.
It was decided to use a LiDAR-based SLAM solution because of the LiDAR directly
providing 3D data without any pre-processing along with the many recent research results
within LiDAR SLAM. Evaluating the requirements above with respect to the algorithms
in Table 1.1, at a first glance DLO and FAST-LIO2 were considered since they are both
light-weight and not depending on loop closure.
DLO requires a 3D point cloud as input and optionally also IMU data. The IMU data
is used for inital gravity alignment and to increase point cloud registration precision. In
DLO, scan matching consists of two steps: scan-to-scan between the current and previous
LiDAR scan for an inital alignment, and scan-to-map for a registration between the current
scan and past environmental knowledge (map). Generalized-ICP with RANSAC outlier
rejection is used for both registrations. The pose of the robot is found through the scan-
to-map transform and checked against certain metrics to determine if it should consitute
a new localization update [30].
In comparison to this, FAST-LIO2 uses a tight coupling of the LiDAR and IMU to ensure
accounting for motion distortion.
It performs direct registration of the LiDAR points
onto the map with on-tree downsampling to avoid loss of information through an initial
downsampling. Moreover, the algorithm has been tested and works well for map sizes
of up to 2000 × 2000m [25].
Along with multiple initiatives to increase runtime, this
shows promising for all four requirements mentioned in the beginning of this section. The

Image processing as well as point cloud registration was explored for detection of the dock-

1.4
Contributions
At the beginning of the project, the available resources were:
• A dataset collected on the harbour bus from September 24, 2023 including two trips
back and forth on the route of the harbour bus.
• The sensor platform used for the data campaign in September in case of the need to
acquire additional data.
• Electronic Navigational Charts (ENC) of the inner Copenhagen harbour.
For the SLAM implementation, a finalized ROS1 version of FAST-LIO2 was available
through [52]. Moreover, a not-finalized ROS2 implementation was available through [53].
Based on these, a clean, dockerized and fully functioning version of FAST-LIO2 running
in ROS2 was implemented. The contributions and fixes made are described in detail in
Section 4.2.
This project contributes with a thorough evaluation of the precision of the maps that
can be generated using FAST-LIO2. Because the ENC charts proved not useful for this,
an experimental campaign was performed with high-accuracy differential GPS equipment
to generate a ground truth for evaluation of the maps. Moreover, the project shows the
applicability of this type of LiDAR-inertial SLAM in maritime environments.
Following this, the project contributes to the understanding of the usefulness of standard
point cloud registration algorithms for object detection in point cloud maps along with an
analysis of the applicability of them in a live solution.

Data Synchronization
As mentioned above, the sensor data was recorded using sensor-handler-and-logger
[60], which contains python classes used to read and store data from the different sensors.
Because of its structure, some of the data was recorded with different timestamps.
The timestamps of the IMU data started at time zero while the timestamps of the re-
maining sensors used in this project were in UTC+0 time. The IMU timestamps thus
had to be modified to be synchronized with the remaining data. This was done using a
python script structured as seen in Algorithm 1. To relate the timestamps to UTC+0
time, the time of the last modification of the csv file storing the IMU data was used. This
timestamp was used to backpropagate through the data and update the timestamps by
subtracting the difference between the recorded timstamps from the UTC+0 time.

Both base station and Septentrio determine their position based on the phase of the GNSS
signal. They then communicate through the NTRIP network to identify errors and make
corrections.
RTK is a highly accurate technique used to determine the position of a receiver using the
phase of the carrier signal of the satellite-based positioning system. It involves using a
base station, which is a stationary reference receiver with a known location, and a mobile
receiver, which in this case is the Septentrio. This is illustrated in Figure 3.5.
The receiver on the base station calculates its location from the signal received from the
GNSS satellites and compares this location to its known location to identify any errors and
generate a correction signal. This correction signal is then transmitted real-time to the
Septentrio that uses it to improve its own computed position from the GNSS constellation
[73].
In the case of the Septentrio, the used communication protocol is Networked Transport
of RTCM via Internet Protocol (NTRIP). As the name indicates, NTRIP communicates
over the internet, where the base station runs the caster software and the mobile receiver
runs the client software [74]. The Septentrio is connected to the internet via a sim card.

4.1
FAST-LIO2 Architecture
FAST-LIO2 [25, 52] builds on the framework of FAST-LIO [77] presented by the same au-
thors. Most of the fusion framework is directly inherited only with the addition of online
calibration of the LiDAR-IMU extrinsic parameters. To address the growing computa-
tional issue, FAST-LIO2 furthermore introduces the ikd-Tree data structure for maintain-
ing the map whereas the map in FAST-LIO is based on feature points.
FAST-LIO2 differs from other LiDAR and IMU SLAM algorithms by not utilizing scan
matching, loop closure, feature extraction or downsampling during pre-processing.
It
instead offers two key novelties to ensure fast, robust and accurate LiDAR navigation:
1. Direct registration of LiDAR points to the map without feature extraction.
2. ikd-Tree [78] data structure for maintaining the map, enabling incremental updates
and dynamic re-balancing.

Measurement Model
The measurement model applies the backpropagation block seen in Figure 4.2. The core
purpose of this is to account for the continuous motion occuring during a LiDAR scan.

done based on the IMU measurements - thus the tightly coupled Kalman filter.

Mapping
This section describes how the mapping and map management is performed in FAST-
LIO2. The map is defined as a cube with the side length L, set in the cube side length
parameter of the config. The ikd-Tree data structure described in the next section is used
to organize the map points. It grows dynamically by merging new scans of point clouds
and the side length L is used to prevent unbound size of the map by only maintaining
points within this region in the ikd-Tree. From the tests made in [25], it was found that
FAST-LIO2 performs well for L ≦2000m.

ikd-Tree Data Structure
The data structure ikd-Tree (incremental kd-tree) is a binary search tree designed to
offer efficient nearest neighbor search, incremental map-update (point insertion, on-tree
downsampling, points delete) and dynamic rebalancing at minimal computation cost [78].
==PAGE:53==

Generally, kd-trees are binary search trees where each node represents a k-dimensional
point instead of holding just one value like in a regular binary tree. Each layer of the tree
partitions the points in space alternating between the axes.
Figure 4.5 shows an example of the construction of a two-dimensional kd-tree. Here, it is
seen that the space it recursively partitioned by the x and y axis. Node A is the median
node of the x axis and thus chosen as the root. The left and right child of A are then
the median nodes on the y axis of the two sub-spaces created by the partition, B and E,
respectively. This structure makes kd-trees very efficient for nearest neighbor search, but
is also what makes dynamic insertion and deletion difficult. This is one of the problems,
the ikd-Tree addresses.

Point-Wise Insertion
As seen from the system overview in Figure 4.2, new points are inserted on the map using
point-wise insertion. The input to the point-wise insertion is a point, p, from the state
estimation module and a downsampling resolution, l. l is set in the filter size map
parameter in the FAST-LIO2 config.
The first part of this algorithm partitions the map space into cubes of length l and finds
the cube CD that contains p. All points contained in CD are then found and compared
along with the new point p. The point closest to the center of CD, pnearest, is found by
comparing the distances of all points to the center point pcenter. All existing points within
CD are then deleted after which pnearest is inserted in the tree. This is called on-tree
downsampling and ensures that the map is kept at a certain resolution. The partition of
the space and the points contained in CD is illustrated in Figure 4.6a.
Inserting the point pnearest is done recursively, where the tree is searched from the root
node until an empty node is found.
During recursion, points in the non-empty nodes
are compared to pnearest along the division axis for further recursion and the attributes
treesize and range, mentioned above, are updated with the latest information. A balance
criterion is also checked an maintained for the sub-trees updated with the new point to
keep the balance property of the ikd-Tree. This is described in more detail below.

deleted are removed during the rebuilding process of the tree. Moreover, box-wise deletion

in the second thread. Nmax is by default set to 1500 in FAST-LIO2.

updates (point insertion and deletion), but not for queries on the subtree. This ensures
that the mapping process proceeds at the odometry rate without interruption during the

K-Nearest Neighbor Search
The last thing worth mentioning regarding the ikd-Tree used to keep the map, is how the
KNN-search algorithm differs from other k-d tree libraries. The range information of the
tree nodes is used for pruning. A priority queue q is kept during the search to store the
current k-nearest neighbors and their distance to the target point. During the search, the
minimal distance dmin from the target point to the cuboid CT of a given node is calculated.
If dmin is not smaller than the biggest distance stored in q, no further processing of the
node and its offspring nodes is done. All of this reduces the amount of backtracking and
thus improves the time performance.

Dockerization
The first addition to the implementation was a dockerization of the workspace. Doing so
offers significant advantages in terms of maintaining consistent development environments
across various systems, effectively mitigating the common issue of discrepancies between
different machines. FAST-LIO2 has a long list of dependencies both in the form of C++
libraries and ROS drivers making the intial setup of the environment quite cumbersome.
Thus, the dockerization was done to ensure easy management of dependencies and to ease
use of the implementation for others in the future.
The Dockerfile sets up a ROS2 Humble environment with the necessary requirements
of the algorithm. A shell script is then used to execute the container with two shared
volumes: a data directory to ensure easy access to rosbags, and the FAST-LIO2 ROS2
package itself to be able to make changes to the algorithm without having to rebuild the
container.
Time Synchronization
One of the requirements mentioned in the documentation of FAST-LIO2 [52] is that it
should be ensured that the LiDAR and IMU data is synchronized before input to the
algorithm.
To do so, a ROS2 node time sync was implemented using the ApproximateTimeSynchronizer
functionality of the ROS2 message filters library as seen below:
self._ts = ApproximateTimeSynchronizer ([ subscription_imu ,
,→subscription_lidar ], queue_size , time_slop)
self._ts. registerCallback (self. _time_sync_callback )
Here, subscription imu and subscription lidar refers to subscribers to the raw IMU
and LiDAR data. The time slop defines the allowed difference between the timestamps
of the data. As mentioned in Section 2.2, the frequency of the IMU data was 400Hz and
the frequency of the LiDAR 20Hz. The time slop was therefore set to 0.01s to ensure
close synchronization without loss of data points.
For each pair of samples, the time sync callback updates the time stamps of the two
messages to the most recent of the two and publish a new IMU and LiDAR message to
the topics \imu\sync and \lidar\sync. These topics can then be used as inputs to the

Publish GPS Path and Transform
The world/map frame of FAST-LIO2 is camera init as seen on Figure 4.11b. This frame
is initialized based on the first IMU frame received by the algorithm, which means that
each initilization of the algorithm has a different transform to a known reference in the
world.
It is therefore necessary to determine the transform from camera init to a real world
reference such as GNSS data that can be represented in the NED frame based on the initial
orientation of the IMU. As mentioned in Section 2.1.2, the IMU’s output in the ENU
frame with respect to local magnetic North. Since common reference systems are with
respect to true North, this need to be accounted for.
The deviation between local magnetic and true North (magnetic declination) of the po-
sition where camera init is initialized was obtained from the World Magnetic Model
[83]. For each dataset, the GNSS coordinate corresponding to the timestamp of the first
\Odometry message was found and the magnetic declination on the day of the data ac-
quisition found for the location. For both locations, the declination was around 4.9◦- so
actually quite substantial.
The transform from the camera init to NED was then based on the mounting of the
IMU on the sensor platform as seen on Figure 2.8, the magnetic declination and the
transform between ENU and NED coordinate frames. This transform is implemented in the
publish ned node seen on Figure 4.11a.

Relocalization in Map
First, it should be mentioned that the relocalization in a map generated by FAST-LIO2
has not been the focus of this thesis. Thus, the results presented in this section should
merely be understood as a proof of concept of the possibility to do so.
The implementation used here is based on [85] and is running in ROS1 Noetic.
The
implementation has been dockerized utilizing the Docker image in [79] and can be run
following [86].
The basic concept of the implementation is to fuse a low frequency (0.5Hz) global regis-
tration with the high-frequency FAST-LIO2 odometry (10-100Hz). This ensures that the
system is still computationally efficient, while eliminating possible accumulative errors on
the odometry.
The global registration is performed by cropping the map in the FOV of the LiDAR
and using ICP point cloud registration between this and the current LiDAR scan. The

transformation is used if the fitness of the registration is above a certain localization
threshold (per default set to 0.8). The algorithm is dependent on a rough inital position
in the map.

4.4
Results and Discussion
This section presents the localization and mapping results achieved with the FAST-LIO2
algorithm utilizing the September Harbour Bus Dataset. The docking maneuver is quite
delicate in the sense that having a 23.3m long harbour bus [6] not reaching the docking
position correctly can cause big damages. The evaluation in this section will therefore focus
on how well the precision lives up to the project goal of achieving centimeter accuracy and
how precisely this accuracy can be quantified.
As mentioned in Section 3.2, the RTK campaign was performed along two parts of the
route of the harbour bus: by Orientkaj and between Det Kgl. Bibliotek and Knippelsbro.
Two datasets are available at each of these locations as can also be seen from the data
availability plot on Figure 2.7. The performance evaluation in this section will be based
on those, where Dataset 1 refers to the dataset recorded earliest in the day and Dataset
2 to the latest recorded dataset for each of the locations. All results presented in this
section are generated online by FAST-LIO2 by doing live playback of the data.
First, the mapping performance will be described. Here, various distances in the built
maps will be compared to the corresponding distances derived from the measured points
from the RTK campaign described in Section 3.2.
Second, an evaluation of the localization performance is made where the paths generated
by FAST-LIO2 are evaluated compared to the GNSS data in the dataset.

5.2
Image Processing
The first strategy for the detection module was to make a simple solution based on com-
puter vision and the image feed from the sensor platform. For this purpose, template
matching and feature matching methods were explored.
There are several challenges when it comes to achieving robust matching in image pro-
cessing such as rotation, deformation, scale variations, partial occlusion and illumination
variation [89]. In the case of the harbour bus, the following challenges are relevant: 1)
rotation due to the vessel’s movement in the water, 2) scale variations due to the ves-
sel’s distance to the docking platform and 3) occlusion and illumination variations due to
varying weather conditions.
All images used in this section are from the September Harbour Bus Dataset.
More
specifically, a data sequence from Orientkaj was used because of the harbour bus’ straight
approach towards the docking platform.
5.2.1

that match a predefined template or pattern. It involves sliding the template over the

5.2.2
Feature Matching
Feature matching is a technique in image processing used to identify and correspond similar
features or key points between different images. Generally, it contains four steps:
1. Feature Detection: Key points, such as corners, edges, or blobs, are detected in both
images.
2. Feature Description: Each detected feature is described using a vector that captures
the local image structure around the key point.
Descriptors are designed to be
invariant to transformations such as scaling, rotation, and illumination changes.
3. Feature Matching: The descriptors from the two images are compared to find cor-
respondences.
4. Filtering Matches: Initial matches are often filtered to remove outliers using criteria
such as the ratio test (comparing the best and second-best matches) or geometric
constraints.
Various combinations of feature descriptors (SIFT, ORB) and matchers (FLANN and BF)
were tested out.
SIFT features [38] are distinctive local image features widely used in computer vision
tasks. These features are invariant to scale, rotation, and illumination changes, making
them robust for tasks like object recognition and image stitching. SIFT detects keypoints
in an image and describes them based on their local image structure. This tends to be
computationally intensive due to the Gaussian scale-space pyramid and gradient calcula-
tions involved in keypoint detection and descriptor computation [91]. In comparison, ORB
==PAGE:84==
5.2. IMAGE PROCESSING
72
features are designed for efficiency, utilizing binary descriptors and fast keypoint detection
[39].
The Brute-Force matcher matches the descriptor of one feature in the first image with all
other features in the second image using a distance calculation and returns the closest one.
In comparison, the FLANN matcher is optimized for fast nearest neighbor search [40].
It was not possible to get a reliable performance with the ORB features or BF matching.
Using just the placement of the detected docking platform in the image allows for simple
visual servoing where vessel control just aims at the direction of the platform. However,
since the docking maneuver is quite delicate in the sense that both speed and direction of
the harbour bus need to be carefully controlled, it was necessary to estimate the distance
to the docking platform to do proper path planning.
Since the camera is a monocular camera, a method of estimating depth was needed. Here,
the idea was to use knowledge of the actual size of the template, the homography of the
detected docking platform and distance through motion.
For the perspective transform of the template onto the images in the camera feed (homog-
raphy), OpenCV’s findHomography function [92] was used on the keypoints. The size of
the bounding box along with the known size of the object in the template could then be
used to find the distance to the platform. However, the size of the bounding box turned
out not to be precise enough to achieve a reliable distance measure to the platform.

5.3
Point Cloud Registration
This section explores the use of a template of a docking platform to detect the docking
position within the point cloud map. The templates are based on the standard platform
layout that 8 of 11 docking platforms share as described in Section 5.1.
Point cloud registration offers a key advantage to finding the transform because the unit
of the map is in meter and based on actual measurements from the LiDAR on contrary
to using RGB images for detection where the distances must be estimated.
5.3.1
Methods for Point Cloud Registration
Many algorithms exist for doing point cloud registration. As mentioned in Section 1.2,
point cloud registration is a standard tool used in many LiDAR SLAM algorithms to
match consecutive LiDAR scans and thereby obtain the motion between them.
For the point cloud registration, different algorithms were used. Since the registration
should run online, only feature-based algorithms were used. FPFH features were used as
feature descriptor while FGR and FPFH-based RANSAC were used for the registration.

Fast Global Registration
FGR is an algorithm designed to quickly and accurately align partially overlapping 3D
surfaces.
It differs from traditional methods such as ICP and RANSAC that require
iterative sampling and model fitting by operating directly on candidate matches that cover
the surfaces. It does not perform correspondence or closest-point updates but instead
accomplishes in a single stage what is commonly done in two [94].
For this project, Open3D’s implementation of the algorithm [95] has been used.
The
general outline of the algorithm is as follows.
First, the scale of points in the two point clouds is normalized by subtracting their means
and dividing by a calculated global scale. This is done to ensure benefits such as enhanced
runtime, consistency of the point clouds by ensuring they are on the same scale, and stable
calculations because working with either very small or very large numbers is avoided.
Then, an initial matching of features between two datasets is performed using KD-Tree
nearest neighbor searches and cross-checking to return a list of pairs of indices where
the features match. The cross-checking ensures that the matching is bidirectional and
robust. An advanced matching layer can be added on top where point triplets from the
initial matching are selected at random, verifying their geometric consistency based on a
scale constraint, and returning a list of matching pairs that satisfy these constraints. The
candidate correspondences for each pair of surfaces Pi, Qj where i < j are called Kij.
The next step is an iterative process where the transformation between the point clouds is

FPFH-Based RANSAC
RANSAC is an iterative technique to estimate parameters of a model by random sampling.
It was first published by Fischler and Bolles in 1981 [48] and has since been applied in
different versions on various usecases. A general overview of the method is outlined in
Algorithm 4.
A subset of the data is picked at random and fitted to a predefined model. All data is
then tested and evaluated against the model. This is repeated until the number of inliers
divided by the number of points exceeds a given threshold. The model is then estimated
with all inliers before the algorithm terminates.
This architecture means that outliers are automatically detected as they are the datapoints
not used in the final fitting of the model. A benefit of this is that outliers don’t have an
influence on the result of the algorithm. Naturally, RANSAC can also be interpreted as
an outlier detection method.
Open3D’s implementation of RANSAC for global registration [96] has been used for this
project. This implementation is utilizing FPFH descriptors just like FGR described above.
In each iteration of the algorithm, ransac n random points are picked from the template
point cloud. Their corresponding points in the map point cloud are then detected by
querying the nearest neighbor in the FPFH space.
Two pruning algorithms are used to reject false matches quickly:
CorrespondenceCheckerBasedOnDistance checks if aligned points are close (less than
the specified distance threshold), while CorrespondenceCheckerBasedOnEdgeLength
checks if the lengths of any two arbitrary edges (line formed by two vertices) individu-
ally drawn from source and target correspondences are similar by checking if one of the
following is true:

Solution Architecture
This section describes the architecture of the algorithm implemented to obtain the trans-
form from the harbour bus to the docking station using point cloud registration.
Figure 5.8 illustrates the concept. The first transform goes from the Odometry to the rough
placement of the docking station in the map. Point cloud registration is then performed
between a template of the docking station and part of the map around the station. These
two transforms are then combined to get the transform from the Odometry to the docking

measures:
1. Fitness: Number of inlier correspondences divided by the number of points in the

RMSE
The RMSE is the squared sum of the difference between the predicted and observed values
divided by the number of observations. In this case, the difference is the distance between
a given point in the point cloud map and its macthed point in the docking template when
applying the transform from the registration result.
When evaluating the RMSE, it is expected to see the deviations grow along with the voxel
size. This is because the sampling of the point clouds do not guarantee a precise overlap
of the voxels in each cloud. It is key that the error is lower than the voxel size but errors
within the range of it are to be expected. It is seen on Figure 5.13 that all RMSE values
are below the voxel size.
The mean Rate of Change (ROC) for the RMSE of the docking templates is between 0.62
and 0.75. The highest ROC belongs to the combined template followed by Bryggebroen,
Nordre Toldbod and Nyhavn that, as mentioned above, have a slightly different layout
than the rest of the platforms.
Looking at Figure 5.13b it is again seen that the deviation range is smaller for the combined
template than the rest - so again it provides more reliable but also slightly worse results.

Runtime
The runtime is the time the algorithm takes to run and provide a result.
The initial
pre-processing of obtaining the FPFH features is not included because the runtime of this
is negligible. Moreover, since the templates do not change, the FPFH features can be
calculated and stored beforehand.
Looking at runtime as presented in Figure 5.14 it is seen that the runtime is below 1s for
all templates for voxel sizes of 0.2m and above. For voxel sizes above 0.4m, the runtime
is below 0.1s in all cases. This means that a frequency of 1-5Hz can easily be achieved
for the detection module. Since the harbour bus will not be at high speed during docking
(around 0.5 knots at final approach) and the control of heading is also not super fast, this
means that the solution can run online.
Mainly one datapoint stands out from the rest - namely the combined template for a voxel
size of 0.1m where the runtime is 14s which is clearly out of bounds for the solution.
It can also be noted that the worst performers in Figure 5.12a and 5.13a are also the ones
with the highest runtime in Figure 5.14a.
The deviation ranges in Figure 5.14b are all quite small. However, the general tendency
of the range of the combined template being smaller also applies here.
Generally for Figure 5.12, 5.13 and 5.14, it is seen that the randomness of RANSAC does
not seem to produce results that are way off. This can be seen by comparing the deviation
range in e.g. Figure 5.14b, that is based on the min and max of all 100 runs for each
datapoint, to Figure 5.14a where each datapoint is the mean of 100 runs. The spread in
both cases is very similar.

6.1
Combining the Results
This section evaluates the results of the project based on the four requirements mentioned
above.
1. Accuracy
The first requirement to the solution was to achieve centimeter accuracy of the localization
and mapping during the docking maneuver.
In Orientkaj, the mapping of the front of the docking platform had a deviation of 0.18-
0.22m from the length measured with a measuring tape. By Det Kgl. Bibliotek it was
0.05-0.13m while 0.02-0.23m by Knippelsbro. As discussed in Section 3.2.3 and 4.4.1, the
use of the measuring tape, the precision of the LiDAR and the selection of endpoints in the
point cloud map to calculate the length from introduces some error. As the points were
chosen to yield the maximum result this means that the actual result could be shorter and
thus decrease the error.
Regarding localization, the performance at the docking platform by Knippelsbro had a
deviation of 0.09m and 0.08m, respectively, when compared to the GNSS data. By Ori-
entkaj, the first dataset provided a localization error of 0.09m by the docking platform
while the second dataset provided an error of 0.44m when compared to the GNSS data.
As mentioned in Section 4.4.2, the positional accuracy of the GNSS data is 5m. However,
the RTK campaign showed that there is a good satellite coverage in these areas which
gives an indication on the quality of the available GNSS data.
Combining the mapping and localization results, it is evident that the accuracy is indeed in
the centimeter range. As discussed in Section 4.4, the best-guess for the required precision
for the harbour bus to correctly dock in the ramp should be within 0.10-0.20m from the
center of the platform. Based on this, FAST-LIO2 does provide results in the precision
range required of the SLAM module.
2. Real-Time Operation
The second requirement to the solution was that it should be able to run online on the
harbour bus. This is necessary, if it should work in an autonomous system.
All SLAM results described in Section 4.4 were achieved using live playback of the data in
the September Harbour Bus Dataset. The odometry rate of FAST-LIO2 was 20Hz which
corresponds to the frequency af LiDAR data. The SLAM module thus lives up to the
requirement of real-time operation.

The detection module should be able to run simultaneously. As described in Section 5.4,
the detection algorithm based on RANSAC registration can easily run at a frequency of
1-5Hz. This should be a sufficient frequency of input to a control algorithm.
As mentioned earlier, all results in this project were achieved on an Intel Core i7 processor
with 16M cache. A similar - but also better - computational capacity is not a problem for
the use case of this project. When building a harbour bus able to carry many passengers,
both budget and storage room on the vessel itself will allow for the necessary computational
power required by this solution.
3. Platform Detection Precision
The third requirement to the solution was a precise method to detect the docking positions
to find the goal position of the harbour bus and allow for precise navigation.
Using RANSAC-based point cloud registration in the maps generated by FAST-LIO2 with
various versions of templates proved to be able to achieve high and reliable accuracy.
As seen on Figure 6.1, the final implementation of the solution includes a relocalization
module.
This is to reduce errors in the mapping.
As described in Section 4.4.1, the
mapping performance seems to be influenced by the amount of traffic around the harbour
bus. Having a precise map without this extra noise will thus make the errors smaller.
Moreover, since the route of the harbour bus is not changing, it makes a lot of sense with
a pre-built map.
The relevance of doing point cloud registration for the docking platform in this map should
be discussed since relocalizing in a given map ensures that the exact position where the
docking platform is located is always known. It is of course worth mentioning that the
precision that can be achieved by the relocalization module has not been investigated to
the same level of detail as the detection module. Both the relocalization and detection
module make use of point cloud registration and with the results presented for both this
seems like a valid method to use. In the future it is therefore worth investigating if both
modules should be used or if they can be combined or enhanced.
4. Sensor Modalities
The last requirement to the solution was that it should utilize multiple sensor modalities
and work without available GNSS.
This project demonstrates that it is possible to achieve precise mapping and localization
on most parts of the route of the Copenhagen harbour bus using just the two sensor
modalities IMU and LiDAR.

However, as mentioned in Section 4.4, there are places on the route where the water is so
wide that the coast line is outside the FOV of the LiDAR. As demonstrated, this results
in FAST-LIO2 failing, when run without relocalization.
As the main focus of this project is on the docking maneuver, this does not cause big
problems since the module functions in all areas close to the docking platforms. However,
should the harbour bus be able to sail fully autonomously on the entire route, integration
of more sensor modalities is necessary. Based on the available sensors on the platform,
using both GNSS or XBR makes sense.
When the harbour bus moves into wider waters, the need for high positional precision
decreases. The use of GNSS would therefore make sense here, as the covariance can be
increased in the sensor fusion algorithm in these areas.
Throughout the entirety of the September Harbour Bus Dataset, the GNSS data has been
consistent without any major deviations with only one exception.
Using this data in
areas where FAST-LIO2’s capabilities are limited is a promising approach, as it should be
possible to integrate into the Kalman filter of FAST-LIO2.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
7.1
Conclusion
The objective of this project was to implement a SLAM solution that allows for precise lo-
calization and mapping for the harbour busses in Copenhagen. The solution was developed
using the FAST-LIO2 algorithm combined with a detection module based on point cloud
registration. The precision of the solution was evaluated through an RTK measurement
campaign as well as GNSS data.
The project showed that it is possible to achieve centimeter accuracy of both localization
and mapping when applying FAST-LIO2. FAST-LIO2 had been tested on dense environ-
ments [25] and this project showed that the algorithm also performs well in environments
with distances in the 50-100m range to the features to be mapped.
Using RANSAC for registration of a template of the docking stations proved to offer a
precise and fast way to establish the position of the docking station in the map. Fur-
thermore, experiments revealed that despite some variations in the layout of the docking
platforms, accurate outcomes could still be achieved by employing a single template for
platform detection, rather than utilizing individual templates for each platform.
Additionally, it was shown through a small number of proof of concept experiments that
it is possible to implement a relocalization module for the maps.
This opens up for
further developments of the FAST-LIO2 algorithm for assuring even better performance
and precision by fusing additional knowledge of the environment into the state estimation.
Overall, this thesis demonstrates that LiDAR SLAM, widely used for AVs, is also highly
applicable in maritime environments similar to the ones in this project, where there are
objects and features to map within the LiDAR’s field of view.

7.2
Future Work
The work presented in this thesis leads to three primary areas of further research: mapping
of the harbour, further development of the relocalization module and object detection.
Mapping of the Copenhagen Harbour
As described in Section 2.3, no maps of high accuracy are available for the inner Copen-
hagen harbour. This was the main reason for the RTK data campaign. Based on the
evaluation of FAST-LIO2’s mapping capabilities against the RTK data, it has been shown
that it is possible to achieve maps of centimeter/meter accuracy.
Given these findings, a logical starting point would be to create a complete map along the
entire harbor bus route. This would establish a more accurate foundation than what was
available in the beginning of this project.
Accurate coastline maps of the harbor are not only beneficial for implementing an au-
tonomous harbor bus but also for various other applications such as coastal planning and
monitoring.
Further Development of Relocalization
As mentioned in Section 4.3, the relocalization module has not been the focus of this
thesis.
The natural first step would be to make a ROS2 implementation of it. Because of the
ROS2 implementation of FAST-LIO2 developed in this project, this should be a fairly
simple task. It would require ensuring that the python scripts making the registration to
the map can run in the new environment, but the FAST-LIO2 data it is fused with should
be delivered directly by the implementation from this thesis.
Moreover, it would be interesting to make more experiments into the reliability of the
module as well as its capabilities when it comes to relocalization after sensor fallouts.
Object Detection
Finally, it would be interesting to increase the situational awareness of the harbour bus
by also detecting moving objects such as boats passing by.
As described in Section 4.4.1, boats are at the moment being added to the map by FAST-
LIO2 which affects the mapping precision. To address this, an initial step could involve
filtering out moving objects, as they do not contribute to the static map. This could be
==PAGE:109==

implemented in a similar manner to the relocalization module where objects in the LiDAR
scan not found in the map or located within water areas are disregarded.
Moreover, while obstacle avoidance may not be imperative due to established rules of
maritime navigation, maintaining continuous tracking and awareness of potential hazards
would undoubtedly enhance operational safety.
