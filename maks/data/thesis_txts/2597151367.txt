Abstract
Abstract
Background: Cardiac activation refers to the propagation of electrical impulses that
travel through the heart. This process leads to a series of depolarization and repolar-
ization states of the heart chambers. The ventricular depolarization is generated by
specialised conducting cells, named Purkinje fibers, located in the inner ventricular
wall. According to the type of Purkinje fibers pattern, mammals can be divided into
two groups. The first group is represented by primates and carnivores. Common
primates are humans while carnivores include animals such as lions. This group has
Purkinje fibers distributed only in the endocardial layer of the ventricles.
The second group is formed by ungulates: pigs and horses are examples of animal
species belonging to the ungulates’ order. Subendocardial Purkinje cells (SEPj) and
intramyocardial Purkinje cells (IMPj) are found in the ventricular wall of ungulates.
Objective: The main purpose of the study is to assess differences related to the types
of Purkinje fibers patterns through electrocardiography and vectorcardiography.
Methods: Electrocardiograms of humans, lions, horses and pigs are compared.
In addition, 2-D and 3-D vectorcardiograms are reconstructed to evaluate the spatial
location and direction of the QRS vector loops.
Results: Humans and lions show a similar QRS morphology characterized by high-
amplitude R waves and low-amplitude S waves. With reference to these animals, pigs
and horses have lower R waves and higher S waves amplitudes. The angle of the
highest intensity vectors of QRS loops is positive with respect to lead I in humans
and lions while it is negative in horses and pigs. The spatial orientation of QRS loops
is similar between humans and lions as well as between horses and pigs.
Conclusion: Deep, penetrating Purkinje fibers in the ventricular wall of ungulates
result in a single burst of ventricular activation that alters the ECG and vectorcar-
diogram significantly. The difference of Purkinje fibers patterns in mammals is the
result of an evolutionary process that influenced the ventricular activation in order
to allow animal species to guarantee an efficient pump function.
Keywords: Cardiac activation; Purkinje Fibers; Electrocardiography; Vectorcar-
diography

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Cardiac activation in animals has been broadly studied. Different electrophysiology
techniques such as patch-clamp, electrocardiography and vectorcardiography have
been used to understand the behavior of the cardiac cells that spread the depolariza-
tion and repolarization waves through the heart chambers.
Vectorcardiography is a fast and cheap diagnostic tool which provides a deeper insight
into the electrical phenomena that occurs during the depolarization of the ventricles
[1]. The role of vectorcardiography is considered crucial in improving heart diseases
diagnosis and treatment planning [2].
Ventricular activation is the last step of the cardiac activation process. The depo-
larization wave terminates at the Purkinje fibers network which is a cluster of cardiac
cells that ensure the ventricular activation.
Comparative studies have been conducted in order to discover properties and be-
haviors of various Purkinje fibers patterns in different animals.
The purpose of the project is to present a comparative model that could be used
as a reference for electrophysiological studies related to evolutionary differences in
animal species.
==PAGE:12==
2
1 Introduction
1.2
Purpose of the study
The present study aimed to assess the ventricular activation of animal species char-
acterized by different Purkinje fibers patterns through the use of electrocardiography
and vectorcardiography.
The project was divided mainly into two parts. The first part consisted in the si-
nus rhythm recording of the animal species in two modalities : 6-lead ECG and
xyz-lead ECG.
The second part focused on the evaluation and comparison of electrocardiograms and
vectorcardiograms of the recorded animals. Lead II was used as a reference lead for
the evaluation of the 6-lead electrocardiogram. Two-dimensional vectorcardiograms
were built from 6-lead ECGs while spatial vectorcardiograms were reconstructed by
xyz-lead ECGs.
Based on the cardiac conduction system, mammals can be classified into two groups.
The first group of animals is represented by carnivores and primates while the second
group is formed by ungulates. The depolarization of the ventricles in the first group
is influenced by the subendocardial Purkinje fibers while deep penetrating Purkinje
fibers distinguish the second group of animals.
The study relied on two hypotheses:
• Primates and carnivores are distinguished by the same cardiac vector orientation
in both frontal plane and three-dimensional space.
The vectors’ orientation
differs from the one of ungulates.
• The Beat-to-beat variation of cardiac vectors in ungulates is higher than the
beat-to-beat variation of cardiac vectors in primates and carnivores.
The first hypothesis is based on the anatomical difference. It might be reasonable to
think that primates and carnivores have a different vector orientation with regard to
ungulates.
Regarding the second hypothesis, it is closely connected to the first one. Deep pene-
trating Purkinje fibers depolarize simultaneously the masses of the ventricles.
The single burst of activation might be more sensitive to QRS complex orientation
changes hence a higher beat-to-beat variation in ungulates is expected.

Background
2.1
Cardiac Activation
2.1.1
Heart conduction system
The heart is an organ specialised in pumping and regulating the blood circulation in
the body. During the cardiac cycle, the contraction and relaxation of the heart ven-
tricles help the blood to flow in the circulatory system. The heart muscle contracts
autonomously since it is capable of generating and conducting its own electrical im-
pulse through the myocardium; this feature makes it unique [3].
Figure 2.1: Cardiac conduction system of the human heart[4].
Figure 2.1 shows the conduction system of a human heart. Special cardiac cells,
named pacemaker cells, initiate the electrical wave propagation through the heart.
Pacemaker cells are found in the SA node which is a cluster of cells located in the wall
of the right atrium. Once the impulse has been generated, the electrical activation
travels through the Bachmann’s Bundle and the internodal pathways: the former
==PAGE:14==

helps the contraction of the left atrium while the latter brings the impulse to the AV
node. The AV node lies at the lower back section of the interatrial septum, which
divides the right from the left atrium. The impulse then travels into the AV bundle
where it is slowed down to allow the atria to complete the ejection of blood before
the contraction of the ventricles. From the bundle of His, the current moves to the
left and right bundle branches and terminates in the Purkinje fiber network. These
specialised fibers allow the instantaneous excitation of both ventricles which contract
and eject blood into the circulatory system.
2.1.2
Action potentials
The electrical conduction in the heart occurs through the cardiac cells which are sub-
jected to an action potential. Two different states characterize the action potential
of a cardiac cell : the depolarization and repolarization. The depolarization is the
change that occurs when the cell shifts to a positive internal charge while repolariza-
tion happens when the internal part of the cell is less positive than the external part.
The change in the cell polarity depends on three major electrical charge carriers:
sodium, potassium and calcium ions.
Figure 2.2: Action potential of a cardiac cell[5]
Figure 2.2 represents the five different phases that make up the action potential pro-
cess. Phase 0 represents the upstroke of the action potential and the depolarization of
the cell. In this phase, Na+ channels open to the inward flow of Na+ causing a shift
of the membrane potential from -90 mV to +20 mV. Phase 1 is characterized by the
closure of the Na+ channels with a decrease of Na+ permeability. Some cardiac cells
present a spike-and-dome morphology of action potential due to an abrupt outflow
of potassium ions during phase 1. Transient outward potassium current (Ito) causes
the membrane potential to slightly become more negative before the following phase.
Phase 2 starts with the inward flow of Ca++ due to an opening of the Ca++ channels.
In Phase 3 Ca++ channels close while a rapid efflux of K+ establish a repolarization
of the cell. Phase 4 is known as resting potential state, in which the sodium-potassium

pump takes out Na+ from the cell and allows K+ to enter the cell.
The action potential of a cell is characterized by a refractory period which is de-
fined as a time interval during which a cell cannot be stimulated to produce another
action potential. Cardiac cells are distinguished by two refractory periods: absolute
and relative. The absolute refractory period starts at phase 0 and ends at phase 3.
During this time interval the cell is unable to generate a new action potential.
The refractory period follows the absolute one and during this time phase a stronger-
than-usual stimulus can induce the cell to a new action potential.
Two different types of action potentials characterize the conduction system of the
heart: the slow response and the fast response.
The slow response is found in the pacemaker cells of the SA node and in the conduc-
tion fibers of the AV node. Slow response action potential cell are distinguished by a
Ca++ plateu.
The fast response occurs in the myocardial cells of the atria, the ventricles and the
Purkinje fibers. Its main feature is fast opening of the Na+ channels.
Figure 2.10 illustrates the different action potentials of the cardiac cells. The dura-
tion of the action potential varies. The Bundle branches have the longest refractory
period and the longest plateau phase. This long duration allows the ventricles to
finish the contraction and avoid an early repolarization which could lead to heart
arrhythmias.

2.2
Electrocardiography
Electrocardiography is the study of the electrical activity of the heart. The impulses
generated by the pacemaker cells and conducted through the heart create a potential
difference that varies in time and space. The potentials difference between two area
are detected and recorded by an ECG device.
2.2.1
ECG trace
Figure 2.4: ECG trace of humans [7].
The electrocardiogram trace represents the depolarization and repolarization states of
the heart chambers. The three major deflection recorded by an ECG machine are: P
wave, QRS complex and T wave. P wave reflects the depolarization of the atria. The
QRS complex and T wave indicate respectively the depolarization and repolarization
of the ventricles. The repolarization of the atria is not visible since it generates a
small voltage wave and it is hidden by the QRS complex in the ECG trace [8].
The health status of an heart can be assessed by the study of time interval and ampli-
tude of the waves. The PR interval is the time period between the onset of the P wave
and the beginning of the QRS complex. An abnormal duration of the PR interval
reflects a disturbance of the AV node conduction. The QRS complex is the com-
bination of three different deflections and reflect the direction of the depolarization
wave in the ventricles. A ventricular conduction dysfunction or an heart disease may
results in a fragmented QRS complex (fQRS) in the ECG trace [9]. The isoelectric
line segment ST is the interval between ventricular depolarization and repolarization.
When a myocardial ischemic damage occurs it leads to a deviation of the isoelectric
line. QT interval represents the time length of the depolarization and repolarization
of the ventricles. The duration of the QT interval is strictly connected to the heart
rate, in fact an increase of heart rate shortens its length [10].

2.2.2
The electrocardiogram
The electrocardiogram is a diagnostic tool used in both human and veterinary medicine.
The first practical electrocardiogram was devised by Einthoven in 1895 and later on
implemented by Wilson and Goldberger. Since the body acts as a conductor of the
electrical currents, the electrocardiogram system records the cardiac potentials that
occurs during the depolarization and repolarization of the heart cells.
The sum of the electrical forces of the heart at any moment is associated to a vector
with a magnitude and direction. The resultant cardiac vector is considered to lie at
the centre of an hypothetical equilateral triangle oriented in the frontal plane of the
body and formed by three leads : lead I, II and III. Lead I is formed by the potential
difference of the right arm (RA) and left arm (LA), lead II is between the right arm
(RA) and left leg (LL), lead III is made up by left arm (LA) and left leg (LL). The
limb leads are formed by bipolar electrodes. A positive voltage results in a positive
deflection of the electrocardiogram trace while a negative voltage means a negative
deflection of the lead trace.
Figure 2.5: Representation of the Einthoven’s triangle
The implementation of the Einthoven’s triangle included three more leads in the
frontal plane : aVF, aVL and avR. These three leads are found by the monopolar
electrode configuration.
They record the voltage with respect to Wilson’s central
terminal which is located in the centre of the heart and considered as a negative
pole. The Wilson’s central terminal (WCT) is built by connecting each terminal of
the limb to a 5kΩ resistor. Considering the ECG as an ideal voltmeter and applying
Kirchhoff’s law where there is no internal current flowing (2.1), the Wilson’s potential
is calculated through Ohm’s and Kirchhoff’s laws.

2.3
Vectorcardiography
Vectorcardiography is a method that displays the electrical forces generated during the
depolarization and repolarization of the heart. Its usage is important to understand
how the sum of the electrical forces moves in the body.
Vectorcardiography has
been demonstrated to be more efficient than electrocardiography in predicting heart
diseases such as myocardial infarction [14]. The first vectorcardiogram concept was
developed by Mann in 1920.
The electrical forces of the heart were displayed as
a set of consecutive vectors that recreate a loop. However, only the QRS complex
was defined since the other deflections were considered too small for the analysis
[15]. The most used vectorcardiography method at present is the three-dimensional
vectorcardiography that shows the heart electrical activity on three planes: frontal,
sagittal and transverse. This technique was first devised by E.Frank in 1954 using
seven electrodes [16]. The vectorcardiography loop is based upon the fact that cardiac
vectors occuring during the cardiac cycle have an origin on a common zero point
which is identified by the values of the isoelectric lines of the ECG trace. The loop
is formed by vectors that leave and return to this point forming a loop shape. The
vectorcardiogram records different loops based on ECG principle components : P
wave, QRS wave and T wave [17]. A 2D vectorcardiogram is obtained by matching the
values of two different leads recorded simultaneously. The union of two leads results
in the mean electrical axis which express the net direction of the depolarization and
repolarization wave. Typically 2D vectorcardiogram axes are displayed by using lead
I and lead aVF or lead I and lead II. The resultant loop of a 2D vectorcardiogram
gives information of the electrical activity on the frontal plane. In healthy humans,
the average mean electrical axis on the peak of the QRS complex lies approximately
at +60 degrees with respect to the x axis [10].
Figure 2.9: Human 2D vectorcardiogram loops formed by lead I and lead aVF. Dotted
lines mark different waves loops.

2.3 Vectorcardiography
11
The three-dimensional vectorcardiography, better known as the spatial vectorcar-
diography, gives a deeper insight of the heart electrical forces due to the addition of
transverse plane signals which are not considered in the 2D vectorcardiograms.
The three dimensional loops of a vectorcardiogram are given by the union of signals
coming from three leads : x,y and z. The first two leads record potentials on the
frontal plane of the body while z represents the transverse plane lead.
In humans, transverse plane signals are recorded placing two electrodes around the
torso. The negative pole electrode is placed on the chest while the positive pole elec-
trode is placed on the back. On the other hand, it is possible to reconstruct a spatial
vectorcardiogram from a 12-lead ECG. In 1990 Kors developed a method which is ca-
pable to reconstruct a three-dimensional vectorcardiogram from standard limb leads
and precordial leads of a 12-lead ECG [18].
Figure 2.10: 3D vectorcardiogram of a human. Dotted lines mark different waves loops.

2.4
Ventricular activation process
The ventricular activation process is the last step of the heart conduction system that
leads the ventricles to contract and eject blood in the circulatory system. The electri-
cal depolarization wave is transmitted by the bundle of His from the atrioventricular
node to the ventricles of the heart.
The bundle of His originates in the anterior-
inferior end of the interatrial septum where it divides in the left and right bundle
branches and terminates in the Purkinje fibers network.
Purkinje fiber are the largest cardiac cells characterized by a rapid conductivity that
carries the action potential along the ventricular wall [10].
It has been demonstrated that mammals are characterized by a different ventricular
activation process due to various Purkinje fibers distribution [19].
Based on the type of Purkinje fibers pattern, mammals can be divided into two groups.
One group is represented by primates and carnivores such as humans, lions and dogs
while ungulates and large animals constitute the second group [19]. Even-toed ungu-
lates such as bovine and pigs are grouped together with odd-toed ungulates like horses
in the Purkinje fibers pattern distribution. Two types of Purkinje fibers cells have
been found in ungulates : subendocardial Purkinje cells (SEPj) and intramyocardial
Purkinje cells (IMPj) [20].
Previously studies demonstrated that primates and carnivores are characterized only
by subendocardial Purkinje cells (SEPj) [20] [21].
The intramyocardial Purkinje cells are deep penetrating Purkinje fibers that runs in
the myocardium and lead to an instantaneous depolarization of the ventricular mass
[19][22].

Endocardial, myocardial and epicardial layers of the left ventricles of a lion and
a horse are compared in Figure 2.11. The presence of Purkinje fibers cells of a lion is
visible only in the endocardial layer of the heart, as shown in Figure2.11a.
Subendocardial Purkinje cells (SEPj) and intramyocardial Purkinje cells (IMPj) of
a horse are shown in Figure2.11b. The presence of deep and penetrating Purkinje
fibers is visible in the three ventricular layers.
Purkinje fibers have a diameter about 5.5–16 µm larger than normal muscle fibers [23].
The main part of the a Purkinje fiber cell is composed by a structure-less sarcoplasm
with a small presence of myofibrils. Purkinje cells are characterized by one or more
larger nuclei. Multiple cells are connected together through intercellular junctions
named desmosomes.
The different Purkinje fibers patterns influence the cardiac activation of the ventricles.
Primates and carnivores are characterized by three phases of ventricular depolariza-
tion, as shown in Figure2.12a.
The first step starts from the depolarization of the interventricular septum (IVS) from
the left to the right side of the heart. In the second phase the depolarization wave
moves towards the apex of the ventricles and finally moves towards the base of both
ventricles in a apicobasilar direction.
Ungulates are distinguished by two states of depolarization, as shown in Figure2.12b.
The ventricular depolarization initates with the activation of the endocardium on the
left ventricle and then a single burst of activation depolarizes the ventricles simulta-
neously. The rapid depolarization from the endocardial to the epicardial layer of the
ventricles is the main responsible of the little contribution to the QRS complex at
this stage.

2 Background
The two types of Purkinje fibers patterns result in contrasting QRS complexes of
the different animal species seen from lead II. Figure 2.13 shows a schematic represen-
tation of the electrical forces involved in the various stages of the heart conduction
system in humans and horses. The QRS complex in horses is often represented by
an rS-morphology. It is characterized by a low-amplitude Q and R wave and a high-
amplitude S wave. The R wave in humans is detected when the depolarization wave
spread towards the apex of the ventricles. In horses, the R wave is nearly invisible
due to the deep penetrating Purkinje fibers pattern. The simultaneous depolariza-
tion of the ventricular wall is represented by purple vectors that move in the opposite
direction and neutralize each other. This process results in a small deflection of the
R wave in lead II.
Figure 2.13:
Schematic representation of the heart conduction system in humans and
horses seen by lead II [24].
The Electrocardiogram trace shows relevant dissimilarities in the ventricular acti-
vation process between subendocardial and deep penetrating Purkinje fibers.
However, vectorcardiography is considered the best way to display and quantify the
sum of all electrical forces generated during the ventricular activation.
The main objective of this study is to define a spatial orientation of the ventricular
depolarization wave in different animal species characterized by a specific type of
Purkinje fibers pattern.

Discussion and
Limitations
The aim of the project was to evaluate any differences in cardiac activation of animals
characterized by different patterns of Purkinje fibers.
Ungulates animals have deep penetrating Purkinje fibers that span the free wall and
spread a fast impulse [21].
On the other hand, primates and carnivores have subendocardial Purkinje fibers that
spread the impulse across the ventricular wall in a cardiomyocyte-cardiomyocyte man-
ner [26].
The ventricular depolarization generates potentials which produce an electrical field
in the surrounding tissues detected by an ECG device[6].
In this study both electrocardiography and vectorcardiography were used in order
to assess electrical parameters of the cardiac activation in the four animal species
analysed.
5.1
ECG evaluation
The first analysis was made on the animals’ sinus rhythm recorded by a 6-lead electro-
cardiogram. Six standardized electrocardiography leads is widely used in veterinary
medicine[27]. Lead II was chosen as the reference lead in all the animal species and it
was used to compare time intervals and amplitudes of the electrocardiogram waves.
The one-way ANOVA test showed significantly differences among the species in both
amplitude and time intervals of the waves.
The core of this analysis was the evaluation of the QRS complex waves amplitude.
As shown in Table 4.1, humans and lions are characterized by a high-amplitude R
wave followed by a low-amplitude S wave.
Large mammals have a similar ventricular morphology, besides they show a thicker
left-ventricular wall than the right one [28].
==PAGE:50==
40
5 Discussion and Limitations
The anatomical structure of the ventricles and the endocardium-to-epicardium di-
rection of the ventricular activation are the main cause of the QRS morphology in
humans and lions. The electrical activity generated by the ventricular depolarization
is more pronounced on the left side of the heart. This means that the sum of the
depolarization waves points towards the left and a marked positive R wave is detected
on lead II [27].
Table 4.1 shows that horses and pigs have values comparable between them but signif-
icantly different with respect to human and lions. The ventricular activation process
of ungulates such as horses and pigs is different from the one described for humans
and lions. The electrical activity of the deep penetrating Purkinje fibers system, that
characterize these two animal species, results in several vectors with opposite direc-
tion that neutralize each other in the apex area of the heart recorded by the ECG as a
small amplitude R wave. Furthermore, when the depolarization wave moves from the
apex to the base of the heart, the sum of electrical activity results in a high-amplitude
negative S wave[29].
Table 4.1 shows that horses have long time intervals. This can be due to the fact
that the heart rate of horses is slow since it is influenced by a high vagal tone which
directly increases the duration of the waves [30]. As a matter of fact, Table 3.1 reveals
that domestic swines have an heart rate twice as high as the horses and this can be
a limitation for the time interval comparison of the species.
Regarding the different length of the RR intervals among the species, the heart-rate
corrected QT interval (QTc) was calculated in order to assess the duration of the
ventricular depolarization and repolarization in the animals. Bazett’s formula was
preferred to other methods since it has been used most often in humans because of
its simplicity to estimate the QT interval [31]. However Bazett’s formula is more
efficient for heart rate of 60 BPM [32] and it might over-correct at high heart rates
and under-correct at low heart rates [33]. This reflects the fact that a proper com-
parison of the waves’ time intervals cannot be obtained since animal species show a
significantly different heart rate among them.
A further investigation was made on the possible presence of a notch on the QRS
complex in the ECG trace.
Figure 4.2 shows an ECG trace of a lion where it can be noticed a notch following the
QRS complex. The existence of a notch depends on a transmural voltage gradient
that is created by endocardial and epicardial cells of the ventricular wall. An early de-
polarization of the epicardial cells of the ventricles occurs due to a transient outward
potassium current (Ito) which anticipate the plateau phase of the action potential.
Ito results in a spike-and-dome morphology of the cell action potential [34]. It has
been demonstrated that abnormal levels of Ito lead to arrhythmogenic disease and
heart failure [35][36].
Table 4.1 illustrates that a notch was found in 7 lions and 1 human while no presence
was recorded on neither horses nor pigs.
It is reasonable to say that the existence of a notch is straight related to the pattern

of the Purkinje fibers. In horses and pigs, the ventricular depolarization is simultane-

VCG evaluation
5.2.1
2D vectorcardiography
The spreading wave of the electrical activity occurring in the heart has a direction
and a magnitude and it resembles a vector. This analysis was made on vectorcar-
diography since it shows in a clearer manner than ECG the magnitude and spatial
orientation of cardiac vectors at every moment [1].
The aim of this analysis was to display and evaluate the QRS vector loop in two ways:
2D-frontal plane and 3D space.
Figure 4.3 displays representative 2D vectorcardiogram loops of one animal per each
species analysed. The QRS loops of the animals are different in terms of size and
shape. However QRS loops of both human and lion stand in the positive side of
the aVF axis in the hexaxial reference system while loops of both horse and pig are
mostly on the negative side.
The cardiac vector of the loop which was considered to be the most representative
one was the highest intensity vector. The average position of the highest intensity
vectors is shown in Table 4.2.
Humans and lions have comparable values between them, in fact, they have a positive
highest intensity vector angle with respect to lead I. Horses and pigs result signifi-
cantly different from humans and lions but closed to each other: they both form a
negative angle with reference to lead I.
This suggests that the different Purkinje fibers patterns influence the position of QRS
loop in the hexaxial reference system.
The representative cardiac vector resulting from the sum of the electrical forces during
the ventricular depolarization show a positive angle in the subendocardial Purkinje
fibers pattern and a negative angle in the deep penetrating Purkinje fibers.
Table 4.2 displays the mean value and standard deviation of the highest intensity
vector’s angle in the four species.
It is important to note that pigs present a high standard deviation. In fact, one pig
showed an angle of 100° which differs from the mean value of the group. A popular
explanation is that the orientation of the cardiac vector depends on several factors
such as heart diseases or anatomical and functional status of the ventricular conduc-
tion system [37][38].
The study recruited only health subjects, however no specific health checks were made

on the animals. Further investigations were made on the ECG trace of the animal.
Lead aVF shows an higher wave amplitude voltages than lead I and its QRS complex
morphology is characterized by a high R amplitude wave and a low S amplitude wave
that result in a QRS loop standing in the positive side of lead aVF axis.
Since ECG recordings on pigs were made on the same day, no relation to electrodes
misplacement was assumed. We speculate that this might be due to an uncommon
intraventricular depolarization occuring in the heart of the pig which lead the cardiac
vector to stand in the positive side of the aVF axis and considered as an outlier.
In this analysis, the variation of the Highest intensity vector over ten consecutive
loops was studied. Figure 4.4 shows the beat-to-beat variation of one representative
lion and horse. The lion has a cardiac vector variation of 1.1° while the horse has a
variation of 2.8°. Table 4.2 shows that lions have the lowest beat-to-beat variation
and they are statistically significant different from horses. However it is reasonable
not to relate this to the different Purkinje fibers patterns that characterize the two
animal species since both humans and pigs show comparable values between them.
One limitation of this analysis is that the highest intensity vector not always rep-
resents the best way to quantify a QRS loop. One concern about its use is that in
circular shaped loops, the highest intensity vector can be represented by multiple
vectors with almost the same magnitude but with different positions and directions.
This means that a small variation of the loop could results in different orientation of
consecutive highest intensity vectors of a single animal. For this reason, part of the
project was to identify vectors that could represent the QRS loop in a proper way.
The Peak cardiac vector and QRS mean vector have been studied but not reported
in the final results. The Peak vector was considered the cardiac vector of the highest
point of QRS complex in lead II while QRS mean vector resulted from the mean of all
the cardiac vectors that form the loop. The former was discharged since the animal
species are characterized by a different QRS complex and the evaluation of the high-
est voltage point of the trace was inaccurate. QRS mean vector was not considered
since it was straight related to the length of the QRS interval and it is subjected to
BPM changes. Another vector considered was the one pointing towards the center of
mass loop. However it was not included in the final results since it was difficult to
calculate the exact centre of mass in irregular shape QRS loops.
From this standpoint, highest intensity vectors were considered to best represent the
QRS loops since no circular shaped loops were found during the analysis.

3D vectorcardiography
The last analysis aimed to have a better understanding of the QRS loop in a three
dimensional space. The spatial vectorcardiography confirmed the results obtained in
the previous analysis: the position of the highest intensity vectors match between
humans and lions as well as between horses and pigs.
In this analysis, the spatial orientation of the cardiac vectors, which form the QRS
loop, is defined by two angles: azimuth and elevation.
Table 4.3 illustrates that the elevation and azimuth of the highest intensity vector
in humans and lions is equivalent. Horses and pigs are comparable between them
and significantly different from humans and lions.
The magnitude of the highest
intensity vectors in horses and pigs is mostly affected by signals coming from the
transverse plane. The highest intensity vectors of humans and lions depend mainly
from the frontal plane coordinates.
We speculate that this might be due to the
direction of the electrical forces during the depolarization.
The highest intensity
vectors in horses and pigs reflect the moment in which the depolarization wave has
an apicobasilar direction in the interventricular septum and it is mainly recorded by
the z-axis electrode. The direction of the highest intensity vectors in humans and
lions is marked by the endocardial to epicardial ventricular depolarization wave.
A schematic representation of the highest intensity vectors’ direction in humans and
lions is shown in Figure 5.1.
The predominant cardiac vector in both humans and lions has a right-left downward
direction.

As discussed, the direction of highest intensity vectors in the animal species is due to

Highest intensity vectors of deep penetrating Purkinje fibers mainly relies on the api-

Purkinje fibers animals are mainly distinguished by an endocardial-to-epicardial de-
polarization wave direction.
The difference in the cardiac vector orientation can only be attributable to evolution-
ary processes in mammals.
Primates and carnivores are examples of animals that have adapted identically to
ensure an optimal pump function.
Ungulates, despite being four-legged animals as carnivores, have developed a com-
pletely different kind of ventricular activation which allows them to have an efficient
ejection fraction.
An apparent limitation of the method naturally includes the recordings of the electro-
cardiograms as well as the vectorcardiograms reconstruction. The aim was to create
vectorcardiograms comparable for all the animal species. The different anatomical
structures makes it hard to place the electrodes in the same spots for all the animals.
Regarding this limitation it could be argued that the exact position of the lead is con-
sidered not crucial as long as the electrodes are positioned along the mean electrical
axis [39].

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In the present project, cardiac activation of different animal species has been investi-
gated through electrocardiography and vectorcardiography.
From the electrocardiography analysis, it turned out that humans and lions have a
similar QRS complex and result statistically significant different from horses and pigs.
The orientation of QRS complex loops in both frontal-plane and in three-dimension
space was analysed. The direction of the Highest intensity vector, chosen as the most
representative vector of the QRS complex loop, is comparable between humans and
lions as well as between horses and pigs.
In conclusion, the orientation of cardiac vectors depends on the type of Purkinje
fibers pattern. Vectorcardiograms of deep penetrating Purkinje fibers animals are
mainly influenced by the depolarization wave pointing towards the base of the heart.
Subendocardial Purkinje fibers are represented by cardiac vectors moving from the
endocardium to epicardium of the ventricles.
An hypothesis of this study was that the beat-to-beat variation was assumed to be
larger in ungulates than primates. However the hypothesis was rejected since no sta-
tistically significant difference was found among the animal species neither in 2D nor
in 3D vectorcardiograms.
The project sets as a model that could be used for studies related to evolutionary
differences of the heart in animal species. The present project aims to argument that
the main reason of the different QRS morphology and vector direction is due to evo-
lutionary different necessities that brought the animals to have an effective systole
and pump the blood out to the circulatory system.
However, further investigations on more animal species belonging to class Mammalia
are necessary to confirm our results.
