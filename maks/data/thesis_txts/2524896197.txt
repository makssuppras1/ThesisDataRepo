Abstract
The purpose of this project was to synthesize and deﬁne the required process for the synthesis
of new sulﬁde materials, to be used as photo-electrodes for water splitting. This research
was based on an theoretical study performed as a collaboration of the SUFCAT and CAMD
groups from the Department of Physics in the Technical University of Denmark. The study
was a ﬁrst principle computational screening of ternary materials, based on the desired
properties needed to be potentially used in a photoelectrochemical devise for the production
of hydrogen.
From all the possible materials, it was chosen to work with ZnZrS3 which is comprised
of earth abundant materials with the possibilities for large scale production. The material
was synthesized in a thin ﬁlm form, since it is the most relevant for the said application.
All attempts entailed a two step process fabrication process. First the deposition by physi-
cal and/or chemical deposition methods nominally magnetron sputtering and chemical bath
deposition of a precursor metal and metal oxide/chalcogenide ﬁlm. Followed by the sulfur-
ization of the precursor ﬁlm by thermal annealing under constant H2S gas ﬂow.
Each step in the fabrication process was characterized and evaluated by a variety of ana-
lytic methods. Most notably Scanning electron microscopy coupled with Energy Dispersive
X-Ray Spectroscopy, X-Ray Diﬀraction and other optical measurements.
Initially the precursor ﬁlms were metallic Zirconium and Zinc oxide deposited via mag-
netron sputtering. Preliminary results revealed two competing problems. The oxygen present
in the ﬁlm was reacting with the zirconium forming the very stable zirconia (ZrO2) which
needed very high annealing temperature in order to be reduced. Whereas Zinc being volatile
after 900o C meant that the conditions to reduce the Zirconia could not be reached. In
order to facilitate this issue diﬀerent deposition methods were used in order to deposit a zinc
sulﬁde ﬁlm on top of a metallic zirconium ﬁlm. By this method a new problem arose since
the two layers were not able to mix. Similar results were acquired by depositing a stacked
ﬁlm consisting a layer of zinc oxide on top of a zirconium ﬁlm which was later sulfurized.
As a last resort, after a sputtering system dedicated to sulﬁde materials was build in DTU
Risø, a new set of precursor ﬁlms was fabricated consisting of Zinc sulﬁde and Zirconium.
Even after creating these ﬁlms that had minimum residual oxygen in the ﬁlms the material
was not able to be synthesized.
During all thermal treatments all samples where phase
separating, indicating that the binary phases are more stable than the predicted metal
ternaries.
From all the experiments performed during these months, we concluded that this material
is not possible to be synthesized due to thermodynamic limitations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This project covers a wide range of topics, each a ﬁeld on its own. The learning objectives
of the project determined by my supervisors and myself, deﬁned the pedagogical goals set
to achieve during this period. Those objectives included the understanding on my part, of
the fabrication and characterization methods utilized throughout the research.
This report is structured in three main sections, spread out in 6 chapters. The ﬁrst part
is a description of the motivation behind this project, both for the general concept of the
ﬁeld of photoelectrochemical water splitting but also for the theoretical study that has fueled
this research. The next section contains a brief description regarding the physical concepts
for each of fabrication methods used but also the implemented characterization tools. The
experimental procedures used throughout this project, along with the acquired results are
explained and thoroughly discussed in the last and ﬁnal section.
For each of the methods used, one can ﬁnd a number of textbooks that cover the subject
in detail. This is the reason why it is deliberately chosen to discuss parts only relevant for
the project in question, and tried to avoid mathematical derivations and formulas that may
not have being of use.
During this period and for each method tried, a large number of experiments was per-
formed, each of them logged in detail. Again, only the more relevant and important results
are included in this report.
1.1
Motivation
The ever increasing energy needs of our society is governing for a sustainable, cheap and
abundant energy source with as minimum environmental impact as possible. From all the
available renewable energy sources, harvesting solar energy is one of the most promising
options due to absolute magnitude of energy reaching earths surface (4.3 ·1020 J/min [1])
but also due to the decentralized nature of this power source. However, as with most of
renewable energy sources, the daily and seasonal ﬂuctuations of the solar radiation give rise
to the problem of storing and transporting the harvested energy, especially during times
when the production exceeds the demand. These ﬂuctuations, coupled with the need for a
High-Energy dense alternative to the conventional fossil fuels, has led the scientiﬁc research
to focus on methods of converting the solar energy into chemical energy.

One approach to this problem is to store the harvested solar energy into the form of
chemical bonds like H2, in a similar fashion that nature does with plants and the process
of photosynthesis. This can be achieved electrochemically in one of two ways, either by
coupling a Photovoltaic (PV) to an electrochemical cell or by an integrated system where a
semiconductor/water interface exists. The latter case is the more desirable scheme aiming
at minimizing the signiﬁcant fabrication and systems costs involved in the use of separate
electrolyzers wired to p-n junction solar cells[2].
Ever since Honda and Fujishima[3] ﬁrst reported using TiO2 as the semiconductor ma-
terial to photoelectrolyse water, there has been a great interest into improving the process
and the solar-to-hydrogen conversion eﬃciency of the early design. In the last 40 years many
diﬀerent semiconductor materials have been evaluated as well as many diﬀerent scheme sys-
tems, with some reported up to 5% of solar energy conversion[4].
Therefore, the purpose of this project was to synthesize a novel semiconductor material
and evaluate it’s feasibility as a light absorber for water splitting. The choice of the material
was based on the ﬁndings of a study conducted in DTU Physics by Kuhar et al [5] where
a ﬁrst principle computational screening between hundreds of potential candidate materials
suitable for water splitting was performed.
1.2
Theoretical background
The overall water splitting reaction can be split in the two half reactions of eq 1.1. An
oxidation step called the Oxygen Evolution Reaction (OER) and then a reduction step
called the Hydrogen Evolution Reaction (HER).

The water splitting reaction is an endothermic reaction, that requires 237 kJmol−1 or
1.23 V per electron. This energy is required in order to drive the reaction and it can be
either provided by an external power source (electrolysis) or ideally by sunlight (Photolysis
and photo-electrolysis).
For the case of photolysis, when light illuminates a semiconductor material of band-gap
Eg, photons of energy Eph larger than Eg can be absorbed. These electrons in the material
upon absorbing the photon energy can be excited from the valence band of the material
to it’s conduction band. During this process, a pair of electron and hole charge carriers is
created that can be then consumed in the redox reactions of eq 1.1.
In order for a semiconductor material to be able to drive the oxidation and reduction
reactions of equation 1.1, a band-gap larger than the redox potential for water splitting
is needed.
However, it is also required to have its band edges aligned to the respective
potentials. This translates that the material must have a band-gap larger than ∆E = 1.23 eV
and the top of the valence band needs to be situated below the reduction potential for the
HER reaction (0 eV vs NHE), while the bottom of the conduction band must be above the
oxidation potential for the OER reaction (1.23 eV vs NHE).

In reality though, the electron-transfer processes at semiconductor/liquid junctions pro-
duce energy losses due to the concentration and kinetic over-potentials needed to drive the
HER and the OER[2]. These energy losses dictate that the semiconductor band-gap required
to drive the overall reaction has to be larger than the value of 1.23 eV per electron hole pair
generated. Previous studies have reported that the band-gap must lie within the range of
1.6-2.4 eV or 775-516 nm [6],[7]. Focusing on the global solar radiation spectrum for AM
1.5 (ﬁg 1.1), it is clear that in this range, most of the available photons are be utilized
since anything below the band-gap energy of the semiconductor will not be absorbed, thus
reducing the overall eﬃciency.

A diﬀerent approach to the problem is to utilize two diﬀerent semiconductors with a
combined band-gap energy larger than the 1.6-2.4 eV needed to eﬀectively drive the water
splitting reaction. The principle behind this scheme is to have a large band-gap material
(LBG) layered in front of a small band-gap material (SBG). In this setup the LBG material
will absorb the large energy photons (small wavelength), allowing the small energy photons
(large wavelength) to be transmitted though and in turn be absorbed by the SBG material.
In Figure 1.2 we can see an illustration of such a design. Light illuminates the LRB side that
will absorb the low energy photons of the spectrum. Subsequently allowing all high energy
photons with energy larger than its band-gap to pass though and be later be absorbed by
the SBG material. This way a broader part of the spectrum can be utilized. On each side
of the devise the photons absorbed will be used in one of the two redox reactions. Both
photoabsorbers can be used to facilitate either one of the two redox reactions (OER or
HER). Both approaches have their own advantages and disadvantages, a detailed discussion
on both was performed by Seger et al[8].
According to Shockley and Quiesser[9], each photo-absorber suﬀers from a free energy
loss of ≈400 mV compared to the band-gap voltage. Since, this scheme utilizes two photo-

1.2.1
Computational Screening of materials
Kuhar et al[5], recently implemented a computational screening using ﬁrst principle electronic
structure calculations where a systematic investigation for the class of the ABS3 (A and B are
cations) i.e. sulde perovskite structures, is performed. The screening assessment is based on
the thermodynamic stability of the resulting material, the bang-gap energy and the position
of the band edges relative to the water redox levels. All calculations are performed with
the use of Density Functional Theory (DFT) in the Projector Augmented Wave (PAW)
formalism, as implemented by DTU’s electronic structure code GPAW. For the calculations
of each material, the six most common prototype structures found on the ICSD database[10]
were used along with the cubic and the distorted cubic. These six structures are:
1. Hexagonal (P63/mmc) structure of BaNiO3
2. Orthorhombic (Pna21) structure of YScS3
3. Orthorhombic (Pnma) structure of GdFeO3
4. Orthorhombic (Pnma) structure of NH4CdCl3/Sn2S 3
5. Monoclinic (C12/m1) structure of FePS3
6. Monoclinic (P1c1) structure of PbPS3

Although the structure of PbPS3 and the cubic and distorted cubic are not perovskite
structures, there are included in their calculations due to their frequent occurrence in the
ICSD database. These crystal structures are used as guidelines for the atomic positions of
the A and B atoms for each material. For all structures both ABS3 and BAS3 combinations
are used, meaning that for each material a total of 18 structures are evaluated.

Synthesis
For the synthesis of the material a two step process was used. First a precursor thin ﬁlm was
deposited using mainly two methods, Magnetron sputtering and Chemical Bath Deposition.
After the precursor ﬁlm was produced then it was sulfurized by thermal annealing under
constant Hydrogen Sulﬁde gas (H2S) ﬂow.
In the following sections the methods used through out this project are discussed in
detail.
2.1
Sputter Deposition
Sputter Deposition is a physical vapor deposition (PVD) technique used for thin ﬁlm depo-
sition. The basic principle of the method is that highly energetic ions are directed onto a
target, comprised of the material needed to be deposited, by colliding with the material the
ions eﬀectively cleave away atoms or particles from it. These in turn are deposited onto a
desired substrate situated in front of the target. There are various sputtering methods but
the relevant to the project are the following:
• DC Diode Sputtering.
• RF Diode Sputtering.
• Magnetron Sputtering.
2.1.1
Basic Principles
All sputtering processes require a vacuum chamber mainly due to two reasons. First, the
highly clean environment that will contain only the materials of our selection, ensures the
purity of the end result. Secondly the need for a vacuum can be better understood though
a parameter called inelastic mean free path. The mean free path is the average distance
that a particle travels between two sequential collisions. Since the sputtering method is a
ballistic process, in order for the sputtered atoms to reach the substrate, the mean free path
of the sputtered atoms needs to be comparable to the distance between the Target and the
substrate. This can be achieved by removing the air from the deposition chamber with the
use of a vacuum pump and a sealed vessel called a vacuum chamber.

2.1.2
DC Diode Sputtering
The DC diode sputtering system is the basic sputtering model, consisting of a a pair of planar
electrodes; the cathode which is kept at a low potential and the anode, either grounded or
biased.
The substrates are placed in the anode side and the target is positioned in the
cathode side that is facing the plasma, whereas the opposing side of it, is water cooled.
When the partial pressure of the process gas reaches the required values (∝mTorr, exact
value depends on the target) the plasma is then ignited by applying a DC potential. In
the presence of the electric ﬁeld, the positively charged Argon ions are directed towards the
cathode and collide with the target surface. This results in the target atoms being ejected
from the surface and coat the substrate in the opposing side of the chamber.
In DC diode sputtering electrons are accelerated from the cathode and ionize the process
gas. To sustain the discharge, the partial pressure of the process gas must be kept at an
optimal level. For instance, if pressure level is too low and the electron mean free path is
comparable the electrode distance, the plasma cannot be sustained. On the other hand, if
the chamber pressure is too high then the mean free path of the electrons will not be long
enough to gain the required energy causing ionization of the Ar atoms.
Despite the simplicity and advantages of DC diode sputtering, it also comes with a few
drawbacks that are addressed with the other two sputtering methods . These disadvantages
are:
• In order to sustain the glow discharge, the working pressure must be high enough since
the electrons driven by the electric ﬁeld leave the discharge onto the cathode. This
high pressure leads to low deposition rates.
• For the case of insulating targets, the glow discharge cannot be sustained because of a
positive surface charge build-up on the target surface.

2.1.3
RF Diode Sputtering
To mitigate the problem of charge build-up on the surface of insulating targets the DC power
source can be replaced with a AC power source. The alternating ﬁeld of the AC power
neutralizes the positive charge accumulated on one half-cycle by the electron bombardment
of the next cycle. Frequencies generally used for these alternating ﬁelds are typically in the
Radio Frequency (RF) range 1 kHz103 MHz; with most common sputtering applications
performed at a frequency of 13.56 MHz since it is one International Telecommunications
Union allowed bands[19, 20].
Since electrons are more mobile than the heavier ions, the alternating RF ﬁeld will aﬀect
them more and subsequently more electrons will be hitting the target than ions, thus creating
a self bias. Ions will then diﬀuse from the plasma as only a negative DC bias voltage is present
on the cathode and sputter the target as in the case of DC diode sputtering[21].
All diode sputtering systems require an impedance matching network between the net-
work and the chamber in order to achieve the maximum power transfer between the power
source and the electrical load. The impedance of the glow discharge is typically of the order
of 1 to 10 kΩwhereas the impedance of most RF generators are 50 Ω[21].
Similarly to the DC diode, sputtering RF systems have their own set of limitations, advan-
tages and disadvantages. The main disadvantages are the following:
• The added expense of the impedance matching network makes RF sputtering systems
more expensive compared to DC systems.
• Because of the alternating nature of the applied ﬁeld in RF systems, deposition rates
are typically slower than for DC systems since the process occurs only for one of the
half cycles of the discharge.
• Because of the slower deposition rates that RF systems suﬀer, RF depositions require
much higher voltage to achieve the same result as with DC, thus overheating often
becomes an issue.
2.1.4
Magnetron Sputtering
In normal diode sputtering (both DC and RF), the trajectory of an electron is deﬁned only
by the applied electric ﬁeld across the chamber. As mentioned above, the plasma discharge
needs to be sustained by a constant collision of electrons with Ar atoms and in order to
achieve that a working pressure in the order of a few Pa is needed. Another approach is to
try to contain the electrons inside the discharge for as much as possible in order to increase
the ionization probability of each electron. This is done by coupling a magnetic ﬁeld to the
electric ﬁeld. This approach is called magnetron sputtering and the main advantage of this
method is the lower operating pressure that greatly increases the deposition rate and ﬁlm
quality[19], according to table 2.2.
There are mainly two diﬀerent magnet geometries used. The ﬁrst one utilizes a cylindrical
magnet while the second makes use of a planar magnet. The latter is the most commonly
encountered mainly due to its convenience. Both geometries operate under the same basic

used for this project, it will be the subject for our analysis.
For the case of a circular target there,is a permanent planar magnet placed right behind it.
This magnet consists of a central disk magnetic pole and an annular pole, thus the magnetic

This is one of the main disadvantages of magnetron sputtering. When the erosion track

more expensive and precious materials such as Platinum this can be a major disadvantage.
There are many approaches for optimization of the magnetic ﬁeld design as a solution to this
problem, but even with an optimized magnet design, target utilization remains less than 50%
for stationary magnets. By more complex magnet geometries under the target, higher target

subsequently less heat is generated on the target.

2.2
Chemical Bath Deposition
Chemical Bath Deposition (CBD) or Chemical Solution Deposition (CSD) is one of the sim-
plest, reproducible and inexpensive deposition methods available. In order for the deposition
to take place, it is only required a source of heat, a container to hold the chemical solution
and a means of mounting and submerging the substrate in the solution.
All deposition techniques have their advantages and drawbacks and CBD is no diﬀerent.
Compared to other deposition techniques such as Sputtering, CBD suﬀers from slower growth
rates but also from the inherit impurities due to the chemical solution that can migrate to the
deposited ﬁlm in the form of contaminants such as Oxygen or Carbon. Lastly, the deposited
ﬁlms often show a high roughness factor and lack the uniformity and crystallinity achieved
with other methods. Thus a post treatment, such as a soft anneal in a inert atmosphere,
is often needed. But due to the simplicity of the method and the possibility to perform
depositions on large areas, the method is well suited and employed by the PV industry since
it can be easily employed at industrial scale.
The CBD process utilizes a chemical reaction in order to deposit a solid thin ﬁlm through
controlled precipitation of a solution. Typically in a CBD process the substrate is submerged
in a alkaline solution consisting of a metal ion, a chalcogen source , a base and a complexing
agent. The end result of the deposition is highly depended on the choice of recipe and the
deposition parameters. The method has been used for many years, with the ﬁrst reported
application being in 1984 by J.E.Reynolds and the deposition of Lead Sulﬁde(PbS) [22].
Nowadays there is a wide range of materials being deposited using this method, mainly
metal chalcogenides (e.g. CdS, ZnS,ZnSe, MnS)[23]. Since the method has been thoroughly
investigated for the past years, especially in the ﬁeld of PV’s, a plethora of recipes exists.
Many recipes contain similar ingredients but small variations in the ingrident concentration
can lead to radically diﬀerent results. Thus, in order to tune and optimize the deposition, it is
important to have a basic understanding of the chemical and physical parameters underlining
the method.
2.2.1
Requirements for CBD
There are four basic requirements that need to be met in order for a material to be able to
be deposited with CBD. These requirements are presented below:
• The compound should be able to form through ionic reactions precipitates.
• The formed compound should be insoluble in the solution.
• The formed compound should be chemically stable in the solution.
• Depending on the formation mechanism (see section 2.2.2), the generation of the com-
pound or the decomposition of the precursors should occur relatively slowly.
Most of the chemical solutions used, consist of one or more metal salts M n+ (M=
Zn,Cd,Sb e.t.c.), a source for the chalcogenide X (Group 16 elements e.g. S,Se,Tb) and
a complexing agent in a aqueous solution . The complexing agent (or ligand) is used in order
to prevent precipitation of metal hydroxides (M(OH)n) and to reduce the concentration of
free metal ions which in turn prevents the rapid precipitation of the desired products [24].

Basic Mechanisms of CBD
According to Hodes[24], the deposition of metal chalcogenide can occur through four diﬀerent
proposed mechanisms. Since, for the purpose of this project a Zinc Sulfate ﬁlm was deposited
via CBD, it will be used as an example in order to illustrate the four proposed mechanisms.
The deposition of ZnS was performed using a standard aqueous solution of Zinc sulfate
(ZnSO4), ammonia (NH3) and thiourea (SC(NH2)2).
These four mechanisms were initially proposed for the well studied deposition of Cadmium
sulfate by a similar solution where instead of ZnSO4 salt, a Cadmium sulfate salt (ZnSO4) is
used. But the basis of the theory can be expanded to more metals, especially for the case of
Zinc, since both Zinc and Cadmium are Group 12 metals with similar electronic structure and
chemical properties. The main diﬀerence between the two metals is the solubility products of
the chalcogenides and hydroxides of the two metals, with zinc hydroxides being more stable
against sulphurization, compared to cadmium. This indicates that ZnS deposition is slower
process compared to CdS depositions.

In homogeneous nucleation collisions between ions or molecules form the so called em-

kinetically stabilized by lowering the solution temperature eﬀectively increasing their lifetime
even up to the point where they grow to a size which can be thermodynamically stable.
For the case of heterogeneous nucleation. Embryos that may have been re-dissolved can
adsorb in an open surface and then grow either through surface diﬀusion processes or by ad-
ditional collisions from the solution. Since the energy required to form the interface between
the surface and the particle is smaller than of homogeneous nucleation, this mechanism is
energetically favored.
Adhesion of the deposits on a substrate is caused mainly due to attractive Van Der Waals
forces between the substrate and the deposit. After a nucleus has adhered onto a substrate,
then ﬁlm growth will be accelerated since deposition is more diﬃcult on a clean surface. On
the substrate the crystals will continue to grow until blocked by neighboring crystals or by
the absorption of a substance from the solution that can can cause the termination of the
ﬁlm growth and the reason for the impurities in that can occur in the process.
2.3
Heat Treatment
Post deposition thermal annealing is a very common practice for the preparation of multinary
semiconductor materials used for photo-voltaic devices. As any literature search will reveal,
almost always some heat treatment is employed during the fabrication process.
Thermal annealing is used to provide to the system the necessary energy for the atoms
to diﬀuse and rearrange them selves into the material. This rearrangement can lead to the
formation of new crystalline phases, a process which may require the added energy in order
for the formation reaction to occur at a reasonable rate. The energy provided to the system
is used in order to overcome kinetic barriers of the formation such as the braking of existing
atomic bonds and the formation of new. A necessary thermodynamic requirement is that
the new phase needs to be the most thermodynamically stable of the possible multinary
structures. This translates into the resulting material having the lowest free energy amongst
all other possible multinary structures.
Besides the formation of a new and potentially more stable phase, heat treatment is also
used to facilitate crystal growth and allow the annihilation of defects[25]. When the primary
re-crystallization is complete, grain growth eﬀectively lowers the materials free energy by
reducing its total area of grain surface.
Both the grain growth and the annihilation of
defects are attributes that are preferable for thin ﬁlms especially for the case of PV. Larger
grain size means less grain boundaries and grain boundaries are defects that scatter carriers,
thus reducing their mobility. Therefore smaller grains result in a higher density of defects

3.1
X-Ray Diﬀraction XRD
Often the properties of a material, especially of a crystalline solid, can be linked back to
the periodic arrangement of its atoms, the so called crystal structure. Materials made of
the same element can have radical diﬀerences in mechanical and electrical properties, due
only to the diﬀerence in the arrangement of their atoms. The most common example to
illustrate this diﬀerence, is Diamond and Graphite. Both materials comprise entirely from
Carbon atoms, but Graphite is electrically conductive and brittle, whereas Diamond is non
conductive and the hardest material naturally found on earth. X-Rays have a wavelength
in the order of inter-atomic distances (λ ≈0.1-10 nm), that makes them ideal for exploring
the structure of materials 3.1 since they are able to produce diﬀraction patterns(eq 3.1).
X-Ray diﬀraction (XRD) is an non-destructive analytical method that can give quan-
titative and qualitative information of the atomic and molecular structure of a crystalline
material. XRD is used to identify crystalline structures, phases and other structural parame-
ters such as crystalline stress and average grain size. The method is based on the constructive
interference of a monochromatic X-Ray beam, as it is being diﬀracted at the lattice planes
of a crystal, at speciﬁc angles that fulﬁll the Brag condition (eq.3.1):
nλ = 2d sin θ
(3.1)
Where λ is the wavelength of the incident X-Rays, d is the distance between two crystal
planes, θ is the angle of incidence of the beam and n is a positive integer. Since the diﬀraction
patterns depends on the spacial distribution of the atoms inside the lattice, these patterns
can be used as a unique ’ﬁngerprint’ for each material. Many extensive databases of the
X-Ray diﬀraction patterns for known crystalline samples exist, such as the ICSD (Inorganic
Crystal Structure Database).
One can use these databases in order to perform a phase
investigation within a given sample.

There are many diﬀerent instrument conﬁgurations, each aimed at probing speciﬁc prop-

An X-Ray radiation source from materials such as Cu, Cr, Mo and Ar is used in order

at the path of the reﬂective beam. By positioning both the detector and the X-Ray source
into diﬀerent diﬀraction angles all possible diﬀraction planes of the investigated sample can
be probed. The result of a measurement of this kind is the sample’s diﬀraction pattern, a
plot of the X-Ray intensity picked up at the detector as a function of the angle 2 (measured
relative to the incident beam direction).
One of the most commonly used X-Ray source is the kα lines of Cu (λ = 0.15418 nm,
E = 8.05 keV), that has an average penetration depth for most materials in the order of
tens of µm[29]. Typical XRD scans are in the 2θ range of 2-70o but can extend even more
depending on the material and experimental needs.

Instrumentation

source.

• Divergence slit, that controls the spread of the beam.
• kβ ﬁlter, that absorbs the kβ radiation and only allows the kα1 and kα2 radiations to
go thought.
• Selection or anti-scatter slits, that reduce the diﬀusion or scattering due to amorphous
or air scattering.

3.2.1
Scanning Electron Microscopy - SEM
In Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM), a focused electron beam is rastered over the sur-
face of a sample. Then by detecting the signal of the primary back-scattered or secondary
electrons as a function of the beam position, a topographic image of the sample can be
acquired[31]. Contrast in the image can arise from the position of the detector but also from
the signal which is being detected. In SEM, there are two main signals used depending on
the nature of the detected electrons.
• Back-scattered electrons (BSE) High-energy electrons originating from the primary
electron beam and are elastically back-scattered out of the sample. Since elements
with a larger atomic number scatter electrons more strongly than lighter elements
they appear brighter. Thus contrast in the BSE detection mode originates from the
so called Z-contrast and can yield information on the topographical diﬀerences in the
chemical composition of a sample.
• Secondary electrons (SE) are the product of the ionization of the atoms in the sample
from collisions with the primary electrons of the electron beam. Since they are the
product of inelastic scattering, they are lower in energy compared (≈5-50 eV) to the
BSE. Due to that, they originate from just a few nanometers of the sample’s surface
[32]. The brightness of the signal is depended on the number of SE arriving on the
detector and also the position of the detector, which in many cases is positioned next
to the beam source. This position of the detector can have a shading eﬀect in the
resulting image. Furthermore, since the features are imaged according to the number
of SE picked up by the detector, as the angle of incidence of the beam increases the
activated area increases and more SE are produced. Thus, steeped surfaces and corners
appear brighter compared to ﬂat and feature-less surfaces.
There are many ways of producing electrons, two of the most commonly encountered
in microscopes are thermionic emission guns and ﬁeld emission guns. Thermionic emission
guns rely on the thermionic emission of electrons from a heated ﬁlament (typically made out
of Tungsten) reaching temperatures greater than 2700 K. Whereas ﬁeld emission guns where
a sharply pointed emitter is held at a negative potential relative to the anode so that the
resulting potential diﬀerence if suﬃcient to drive the electrons to exit the material. There
is also a third type of emitter called Schottky gun which is a combination of the two. Of
course each type of electron emitter has its advantages and disadvantages.
After the electrons are generated, they are accelerated through an anode and produce
a highly energetic and tunable electron beam. Thereafter, a series of magnetic lenses and
apertures condenses and focuses the beam towards the sample stage, and with the help of
a scan coil, the beam is able to scan the surface. After the interaction with the sample,
a detector picks up the signal from the sample and with the use of a computer software
it is able to link this signal to the position of the beam and produce the resulting image.
In order to avoid unwanted scattering of the electrons with atoms in the atmosphere, the
whole apparatus including the sample is enclosed in a vacuum chamber pumped down to the
desired pressure.

Ellipsometry is a method used to determine physical properties of a materials that can lead
to the change of the polarization of incident light. This method is used for determining
the roughness, uniformity, thickness and dielectric function of materials and especially for
thin ﬁlm applications.
The theory behind the method was introduced in 1887 but due
to the mathematical complexity of the method it wasn’t popularized until the available
computational power made it possible[29].
The method gets its name from the fact that, linearly polarized light becomes elliptically
polarized when reﬂected from a surface, at an oblique angle of incidence. This change in the
polarization state of a known light is the measured quantity of the method.

σ and π polarization. Light that is σ polarized has the electric ﬁeld component polarized
parallel to the plane of incidence and π polarized light perpendicular. The enabling prin-
ciple of ellipsometry is that π and σ polarized light reﬂect diﬀerently, thus resulting in the

5.1
Project Outline
The synthesis of all samples was in principle a two step process. It begins with the deposition
of a thin ﬁlm of metallic (Zr) and ceramic (ZnO or ZnS) precursors on a fused silica substrate.
Next the precursors are sulfurized, by thermally annealing in a quartz tube furnace, under
constant H2S (5% in Ar) ﬂow. The gas ﬂow employed was in the range of 65-420 sccm,
depending on the process.
In order to ensure that the process atmosphere was depleted from oxygen as much as
possible, before the annealing the system was pumped down to a rough vacuum (≈8-13
mTorr).
To further ensure the lowest possible residual oxygen levels after reaching the
lowest possible pressure level, the system was ﬂushed with Ar gas and pumped down again.
During the deposition, a thin ﬁlm of the precursors needed to be created with an atomic
stoichiometric ratio of Zn/Zr equal to 1.
This was achieved by choosing the sputtering
parameters for each target, mainly the sputtering power, pressure and time. Depending
on the sputter system used each time, the optimal sputtering parameters were deﬁned by
using a microbalance determining the deposition rate of each target and try to match them.
For the case where there was no microbalance to measure the deposition rate, an estimate,
based on the nature of each target was made prior to the deposition. After performing a
test deposition the stoichiometric ratio was checked by EDX analysis and then tuned the
parameters for the next deposition accordingly. The thickness of the ﬁlms were measured
form the SEM image of a cross-section of a deposited sample. After a few attempts the
deposition parameters to be used, could be deﬁned and used in later processes.
The ideal process, in order to have a better mixing of the metals would entail co-sputtering
pure metallic precursors on the substrate and then sulfurize them by annealing in H2S.
Unfortunately, as discussed in section 2.1.1.1, due to Zn having high vapor pressure it was
not possible to use metallic zinc in vacuum without contaminating the system. To overcome
this limitation it was chosen to sputter from a ceramic ZnO target by means of RF magnetron
sputtering, since ZnO is more stable in vacuum compared to metallic zinc. By doing that,
large amount of Oxygen was inevitably introduced into the ﬁlm and that immediately created
two competing problems in the fabrication process:
• When the Oxygen from the ZnO is introduced with the Zr, Zirconia (ZrO2) is formed

5.3
CBD of Zinc sulﬁde
In order to limit the amount of Oxygen deposited in the precursor ﬁlm, it was decided
to change the deposition route that was initially followed. That is why it was chosen to
chemically deposit via CBD, a ZnS layer on top of a Zr thin ﬁlm that was previously deposited
via DC magnetron sputtering. By adopting this approach it meant that:
• An extra step was added in the synthesis of the material since it is ﬁrst needed to
deposit the Zr ﬁlm and then deposit on top of that the ZnS ﬁlm.
• Due to the nature of the CBD method itself, impurity elements could potentially be
introduced (mainly C and O) into the ZnS ﬁlm, that could bring about new issues to
be dealt with.
• By this two step fabrication process, the two metals are deposited in layers that needed
to be mixed during the thermal sulfurization. This process has its own thermodynam-
ical limitations, and diﬀusion of the two metals could potentially be a problem.

Electroplating ZnS
Besides chemically depositing a ZnS ﬁlm another deposition method was used, called elec-

Electroplating is a method that utilizes electric current in order to reduce dissolved metal
cations that in turn can form a thin metal ﬁlm coating on an electrode. This method can
be expanded to the deposition of metal chalcogenides such as ZnS. For the purpose of this
project, a solution containing ZnSO4 and Sodium sulfate (NaSO4) was used to deposit a

metallic Zr. It was realized that the Zr surface was hydrophobic, possibly due to a surface
oxide layer that as discussed before Zr is prone to form. Since one of the requirements of the
method is that the surface to be coated besides being conductive, needs to have some degree
of wetting. All attempts on the Zr surface yielded a ﬁlm with poor adhesion with only a few
big crystals being deposited that ﬂaked oﬀafter the surface was naturally allowed to dry.

Sacriﬁcial ZnS powder annealing
In order to limit the evaporation of the Zn from the ﬁlms during thermal treatments at high
temperature a sacriﬁcial ZnS powder was used. Along with the samples a small quartz boat
containing ZnS powder was placed in the center of the furnace. One of the co-sputtered ZnO
and Zr precursors was placed one centimeter downstream. As the powder was in the hottest
zone of the furnace the ZnS would evaporate and then condensate on the surface of the ﬁlm
that was in a slightly lower temperature. This was done in order for the ﬁlm to reach a
high enough temperature and perhaps be able to reduce the ZrO2 present, while the Zinc
evaporating from the ﬁlm could be compensated by the powder.
In order for this method to work a two zone furnace is needed in order to have a hot zone
for the ZnS powder to evaporate and a slightly cooler zone for the sample to be placed. In
the setup available this was diﬃcult to achieve and although as it was discussed in section
4.3 the furnace used had a large temperature gradient it was diﬃcult to accurately measure
and place the sample accordingly.
As a result samples annealed at 900 and 1000oC, using sacriﬁcial powder, had a thick
and rough ZnS ﬁlm on their surface that was on top of the deposited ﬁlm appearing not
to have reacted. Perhaps the temperature of the position that the ﬁlm was placed was not
ideal for the process in hand.
ZnO/Zr stacked ﬁlms
Besides the stacked ﬁlms that consisted of a ZnO layer on top of a Zr ﬁlm discussed on
section 5.6, the reverse case was also considered. The motivation behind this case was, that
perhaps the Zr over-layer would act as a cap for the zinc present in the ﬁlm, reducing or
even stopping this way the evaporation of the zinc during the anneal. If this was the case,
high enough temperature may be reached in order to remove the oxygen present and create
one of the predicted ternary phases.
Preliminary results for these stacked ﬁlms were discouraging. Samples annealed at high
temperatures had lost all their Zn content and the annealing process had destroyed the ﬁlm
partially or even totally peeling oﬀ.

for two hours hold time. Samples that had a faster ramping up rate of 10oC/min had the
ﬁlm completely peeled oﬀ, leaving nothing but the substrate. Whereas samples that had a
slower ramp rate of 2.5oC/min, were only partially destroyed. The yellow region in the ﬁlm
is pure ZnS whereas the red region in the middle was found to be ZrS2 with no Zn content.

Titanium nitride buﬀer layer ﬁlms
Besides the quartz substrate used as a substrate for all depositions. A Titanium nitride (TiN)
coated quartz substrate was used, for the stacked Zr/ZnO but also for the co-sputtered ZnS
and Zr samples discussed in sections 5.6 and 5.5. This layer was used ﬁrst of all in order to
investigate if it could act as a buﬀer layer between the quartz substrate and the Zr layer,
but also to be used as a back-contact for the devise in the case the material was eventually
synthesized.
The motivation behind this TiN layer was that since Zr is prone to oxidize and as it was
found, for both the cases of the layered ﬁlms in sections 5.3 and 5.6 that the Zr stayed in
the bottom layer. It was hypothesized that perhaps the Zr in the ﬁlm was trying to reduce
the SiO2 ﬁlm and that was the reason for the two layers not mixing. The TiN layer could
possible prevent that trend, by acting as buﬀer layer between the substrate and the ﬁlm.
But all samples yielded similar results as the ones deposited directly onto a quartz substrate.
Photoluminescence Spectroscopy Measurements
Even though the material in question was not synthesized, all samples that had a band-gap
close to 2 eV where analyzed by photoluminescence spectroscopy (PL). Photolumienescence
is deﬁned as the emission of light after excitation by another light source. PL spectroscopy
is a non-destructive technique that probes the electronic structure of materials by detecting
the intensity of light emitted by the sample at diﬀerent wavelengths when it is exposed to a
single, ﬁxed excitation wavelength. The PL response of a material is a ﬁrst step indication
in the material quality and if it can be used in a solar cell, since it gives information on the
lifetime of the carriers in the material.
All the samples measured failed to yield any PL response, regardless of the synthesis
method used, rendering them not useful for further investigation.

Chapter 6
Conclusion- Discussion
During this project, it was attempted to synthesize one of the predicted ternary phases of
ZnZrS3, to be potentially used as a Large band-gap semiconductor in a two photon water
splitting devise. All experiments performed during this period consisted of ﬁrst depositing
metallic and ceramic precursors in a thin ﬁlm form, since it was the most relevant for the
use. After the deposition the precursor ﬁlm was sulfurized by thermally annealing in an
H2S atmosphere. In total, four main routes were followed for the deposition of the precursor
ﬁlms:
• Co-sputtering ZnO and Zr.
• Chemically depositing a ZnS ﬁlm on a Zr coated substrate.
• Layered sputtering of ZnO and Zr ﬁlm.
• Co-sputtering ZnS and Zr.
All the results acquired throughout this project, strongly indicate that the most stable
state for the system is for the materials to form their respective binaries instead of one of
the predicted ternary phases. This is based on the distinct phase separation observed for the
two metals in their respective sulﬁde or oxide phase, regardless on the state the metals were
deposited. Although for some of the ﬁlms prepared the optical band-gap was in the predicted
range, EDS but also XRD analysis did not give any indication that the predicted materials
where present in any of the samples. In all the XRD diﬀraction patterns most of the peaks
where identiﬁed as one of the predominant crystalline planes of one of the most commonly
encountered binary oxide or sulﬁde phases of the two metals. In some cases where not all
the peaks could be accounted for, the remaining peaks could not be identiﬁed as none of the
peaks in the theoretical patterns. Finally all materials that had band-gap values in the range
that could be used for a LBG material in a 2-photon water splitting devise had their photo-
luminescence response measured in order to be considered as an alternative. Unfortunately
all of the samples failed to produce any detectable PL response rendering them un-usable in
such a devise
After exhausting all possible scenarios, it was concluded that the material in question is
not possible to be synthesized, at least with the available to us materials and methods.

Initially, it was believed that the oxygen present in the deposited precursors was causing
problems in the ﬁnal treated ﬁlms. Mainly due to the very stable ZrO2 present coupled with
the volatility of Zn after a certain annealing temperature. This assumption steered all further
investigations into ﬁnding ways to reduce the oxygen content in the ﬁlm. Either by limiting
the amount of oxygen deposited via diﬀerent deposition methods (CBD, electrodeposition
and lastly by sputtering ZnS), or by reducing the deposited oxides into their respective
sulﬁdes when thermally treating the precursors (Stacked ZnO/Zr ﬁlms).
By both routes the precursor ﬁlms were stacked in layers, always with zinc present either
as an oxide or as a sulﬁde on the top layer. This was done of course since metallic Zr was
possible to be deposited via magnetron sputtering as opposed to zinc that at that time was
only possible to be sputtered in its oxide form.
As it was discussed in sections 5.3 and 5.6 although by both methods, ﬁlms with signiﬁ-
cantly lower oxygen content were produced. All treated samples remained phase separated,
despite trying a combinations of various temperatures and annealing times. When it was
possible to co-deposit ZnS and Zr via magnetron sputtering, it was believed that it would
be possible to form one of the predicted ternary phases of two metals. But yet again the two
metals appeared phase separated upon heating.
The chain of events described was unfortunate, since if it was possible from the beginning
to sputter ZnS instead of ZnO, this conclusion would have been perhaps realized sooner. In
all precursor ﬁlms oxygen was always present and subsequently it was not easy to discern
what was the reason behind the observed phase separation. After all, as it was discussed in
section 1.2.1 the study that this project was based upon had considered the stability of the
ternaries over their respective metal chalcogenide and not their oxides.
Perhaps, although unlikely, the material could be synthesized in situ, by co-sputtering
from a ZnS and a ZrS2 target, while the substrate is under heating. Since all the materials
will be in the correct stoichiometry and by providing all the necessary energy in the system
by heating, the most stable ternary phase could be potentially be created. When considering
the conditions for the sputtering chamber build in Risø, this scenario was considered but
such a target could not be procured.
Density Functional Theory is still in its evolution phase, limited by the available com-
putational power. Although the theory is rapidly evolving along with the the progress in
computers, it still needs to balance between the level of accuracy it can provide and the
computational demands of the calculation. Perhaps this is the case for this material and a
re-calculation of the systems that was considered is required.
