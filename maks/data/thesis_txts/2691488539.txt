Abstract
This master’s thesis investigates whether a car-free Roskildevej will help Frederiksberg Municipality reach
its sustainability goals by 2030. Through a sustainability and mobility assessment, the thesis examines the
advantages and disadvantages of such a change, its impact on the mobility and sustainability of the area, how
it will inﬂuence the surroundings, and ﬁnally the size effect of changing Roskildevej. The investigation ﬁnds
that the current state of Roskildevej is sustainable with a Sustainability Performance Score (SPS) of 2.90 on a
scale from 1 to 5, which leaves much room for improvements. Furthermore, the investigation ﬁnds that a car-
free Roskildevej will affect the sustainability and mobility of the area positively with a 25%-point difference
between best and worst outcome in the Effect Assessment Score (EAS). A baseline is created for the current
state with respect to the visions of Frederiksberg Municipality and the UN’s Sustainable Development Goals
(SDGs) and contains 12 sustainability indicators, which are evenly distributed and weighted between urban
sustainability and sustainable mobility. In the baseline, the three indicators ‘number of cars’, ‘size of green
area per citizen’, and ‘parking’ achieve low sustainability scores. For the best outcome of the future scenario,
all indicators have an improving effect on Roskildevej’s EAS except for the indicators ‘size of green area’
and ‘distance to green area’ which have the same score as the baseline, causing no difference between the
best and worst outcome. For the worst outcome of the future scenario, the indicators ‘parking’ and ‘travel
time in peak hour’ score lower than the baseline. The results suggest that the decision to cut off car trafﬁc
from Roskildevej can serve as a pioneering project for Frederiksberg in its ambition to create a liveable city
and achieve its sustainability goals for 2030. Nevertheless, Frederiksberg Municipality has chosen to proceed
with a road redevelopment for Roskildevej which has the future potential to be the ﬁrst step towards a car-
free zone. The conducted mobility and sustainability assessment can be applied to any new implementation
of Roskildevej as it could serve as an ongoing control of the road and area’s condition and development. All
in all, a change in the layout of Roskildevej may not only create a better coherence between Frederiksberg
Garden and Søndermarken but might also help to increase the area’s urban sustainability.

Introduction
The world is currently experiencing great changes due to the climate, forcing small and large key players in
society to incorporate a more green mindset of varying scale for the sake of future generations. This thesis
focuses on a small section of such a change, namely the branch of Roskildevej at the hill Valby Bakke (from
now on referred to as Roskildevej) in Frederiksberg municipality (from now on referred to as Frederiksberg).
Frederiksberg is located in the heart of Copenhagen, Denmark, with Valby Bakke situated in its southern part
together with the two parks: Frederiksberg Garden and Søndermarken. The municipality plan of Frederiks-
berg includes the wish of changing the layout of Roskildevej [Frederiksberg Kommune, 2020]. See Figure
1.1 for visual understanding.
Figure 1.1: The location of Frederiksberg Municipality surrounded by Copenhagen Municipality and focus area of
Roskildevej on Valby Bakke.
Frederiksberg sees Roskildevej as a barrier between the two recreational areas and wishes to change the
1
==PAGE:17==
scope of the area [COWI, 2020]. In 2020 Frederiksberg hired the consulting engineer ﬁrm, COWI, to
investigate 5 different trafﬁc scenarios: two different tunnels, a car-free road, and two road redevelopments
[COWI, 2020].
Frederiksberg has a vision of being sustainable by 2030, following the 17 Sustainable Development Goals
(SDGs) by the UN [Frederiksberg Kommune, 2020]. Frederiksberg acknowledges that many of its qualities
are due to green areas. Frederiksberg wants to increase the number of pedestrians, cyclists, and effective
public transport, while decreasing car usage [Frederiksberg Kommune, 2020]. By building more and better
bicycle paths with sufﬁcient bicycle parking the space for cars will decrease, though Frederiksberg also
wants more parking lots [Frederiksberg Kommune, 2017]. Frederiksberg wants to improve the air quality
and noise levels and to be a safe city with a focus on accessibility [Frederiksberg Kommune, 2020].
The thesis provides answers to the research questions of whether the modiﬁcations of Roskildevej will
help Frederiksberg to reach its sustainability goals for 2030. The aim is to create an assessment of the
sustainability and mobility of Roskildevej at Valby Bakke, ﬁrstly by creating a baseline in relation to the
visions of Frederiksberg and the SDGs, and secondly, by assessing the present conditions and a car-free
scenario through a generated sustainability indicator set. By use of qualitative and quantitative methods, the
mobility and perceptions of the area will be found. The report will investigate whether the scope size is big
enough to derive any sustainable results.
1.1
Problem statement
The following research questions have been made based on the future vision of Frederiksberg and the SDGs:
How will the change of Roskildevej affect the sustainability and mobility of the area?
• How good is the mobility and sustainability of the area now?
• What are the advantages and disadvantages of changing Roskildevej?
• What inﬂuence will the change have on the surroundings?
• Is the scope of Roskildevej too small for Frederiksberg to actually achieve its sustainability goals?
The sustainable indicator set will be based on the three sustainability domains. Both the present and future
state will be assessed on an easily understandable scale. The present scale being from 1 to 5, 5 equalling
very sustainable, and the future scale being from -2 to +2, -2 equalling very negative, 0 equalling no change
of state, and +2 equalling very positive. The scales are made different due to the fact that the future is not
directly measurable.

Theory
In this chapter, the choice of scenario is presented to substantiate the chosen theories of sustainability and
mobility. Furthermore, the chapter introduces some of the problematics experienced in an urban environ-
ment.
2.1
Choice of scenario
Frederiksberg hired COWI to carry out an analysis of ﬁve different trafﬁc scenarios of Roskildevej to inves-
tigate the possibilities for a more coherent connection between Søndermarken and Frederiksberg Garden.
Based on the COWI [2020] report, scenario C - closing of car trafﬁc - was chosen to be the most interesting
case for this thesis as the scenario involves more drastic measures towards a more human scaled city. The
scenario spoke in favour of a comfortable city made for the natural human travel speed and for a better
inﬂuence of our lifestyle [Alonso, 2017]. The car-free scenario had higher chances of complying with the
SDGs, due to the immediate improvements of air quality and noise levels as well as making Valby Bakke
more inclusive, safe, resilient, and sustainable [United Nations, 2021a].
2.2
Sustainability
Since sustainability ﬁrst appeared in ’The Brundtland Report: Our Common Future’ in 1988, the term has
been widely used [Keeble, 1988]. The report deﬁnes sustainable development to be obtained if “it meets
the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs”
[Keeble, 1988]. This broad deﬁnition, however, has later received criticism for being too weak in its loosely
deﬁned base [Verma and Raghubanshi, 2018], resulting in others redeﬁning the term, including UN with
its SDGs. Sustainability is divided into three pillars: economic, environment, and social, originally seen as
three independent and equally important pillars. This perception, however, has been challenged as some ar-
gue that they are closely interlinked [Lehtonen, 2004]. Some argue the three individual sustainability pillars
3
==PAGE:19==
should be viewed as overlapping circles with equal weighting, known as the Sustainable Development, since
each domain depends on each other to keep working [Verma and Raghubanshi, 2018]. Yet, some claim the
Sustainable Development to be an outdated sustainability model which does not recognise the fact that soci-
ety depends on the environment, and the economy being a social section. This type is called the Bioeconomic
Model and states that the Sustainable Development does not comply with energy conservation, meaning the
laws of thermodynamics [Morandín-Ahuerma et al., 2019]. See Figure 2.1 for a visual explanation.
Figure 2.1: Two sustainability methods. Left: Sustainable development, equal grouping of environmental, social,
and economical [Keeble, 1988]. Right: Bioeconomic Model, system oriented with three differently weighted groups
[Morandín-Ahuerma et al., 2019].
The Bioeconomic Model does not state the environmental pillar to be more important than the others, as the
social and economic pillars often are good starting points if not conﬂicting with the environment [Lehtonen,
2004]. The pillar of environment clariﬁes the opportunities and limitations of nature in regards to human
activities. Turcu [2013] deﬁnes sustainability as a normative choice with no well-deﬁned concept and urban
sustainability to be a contradiction, as urban areas are epicentre of consumers of resources. Urban growth
should be a balance between economies of scale and scopes, together with environmental degradation and
leakage [Verma and Raghubanshi, 2018]. The whole confusion of sustainability with no precise deﬁnition
led to the development of the SDGs with associated targets for nations to prioritise between the goals under
accepted norms [United Nations, 2021a].
2.2.1
The Sustainable Development Goals
The SDGs were ﬁnalised by the United Nation in 2015 containing 17 goals and 169 targets all with the
objective of producing a universal set of goals, meeting the challenges within the environment, politics, and
economics worldwide [United Nations, 2021d]. The SDGs help deﬁne how the world should look in 2030
4
==PAGE:20==
with strategies improving health, education, reduction of inequality, economic growth, and tackling climate
change [United Nations, 2021c]. The SDGs should be the compass of each country for future planning
while being a part of a global commitment. The issues addressed by the SDGs are all correlated with
climate change in relation to sustainable development and with a strong focus on the implementation of the
SDGs. However, the SDGs have still received some criticism for not being scientiﬁcally robust enough.
A study made by Sustainable Development Service Network showed that 54% of the targets need a more
speciﬁc scope and that only 29% of the targets had well-developed scientiﬁc background, while 17% of the
targets need signiﬁcant improvements [Verma and Raghubanshi, 2018]. Denmark wants to reach the SDGs
through the municipalities and regions as they hold 70% of the public budget as well as being the authorities
on social services, education, health, environment, and technology [United Nations, 2021b].
2.3
Sustainable mobility
The transportation network is one of the cornerstones of a society [Larsen et al., 2013]. From 2012-2025
the population of Copenhagen is expected to increase with 100,000, which will put a large pressure on the
different transportation modes [Larsen et al., 2013]. Frederiksberg alone is expected to have a 10% increase
from 2012-2025, meaning 18,517 more citizens [Tetraplan, 2019]. The economic growth, competitive per-
formance of companies, and lifestyle of today is very dependent on a well-functioning transportation system
[Larsen et al., 2013].
Congestion is a known problem to the city [Larsen et al., 2013]. The trafﬁc congestion increases even with
the modernisation of the transportation system. This means the car usage demand needs regulations for the
quality of mobility not to be lowered [Larsen et al., 2013]. Increasing the demand for transportation will have
a considerably negative effect on the environment if no steps are taken towards a more sustainable mobility
[Gudmundsson, 2003]. Sustainable mobility can be interpreted in different ways with mobility itself being
deﬁned as “the ability of an individual to move about”, while linked to the notion of movement, accessibility,
liberty, equity, and need [Zeitler, 1995]. Sustainability is a complex matter and its interpretation might
inﬂuence the measures of sustainable mobility, as enhanced sustainable mobility means linking mobility,
transportation, and land-use [Banister, 2008]. The all-important point, when improving sustainable mobility,
is to base the transport system on the needs of the users and push the future trafﬁc growth mainly towards
bicycling and public transportation. By implementing sustainable mobility through beneﬁting vulnerable
road users and public transport, it is possible to decrease CO2 emission and give the citizens more sustainable
options [Sultan et al., 2016].
Sustainable options lead the user away from non-renewable energy-consuming transport modes, such as
cars, if the alternative becomes a more convenient choice. Good interconnections make the shift between
5
==PAGE:21==
transportation modes natural and simple [Grinsven et al., 2019]. The travel behaviour of people is impacted
by the urban spatial structure, making people very path-dependent which is difﬁcult to alter, including both
the private traveller and the planners [Hrelja et al., 2013, Næss, 2012]. Different attitudes towards travel
time exist; on one side, the users desire shorter a travel timer, on the other, the users desire a liveable city
with slower trafﬁc [Banister, 2008].
Land development and improved infrastructure are closely linked. Land becomes more usable through in-
frastructure, increasing the attractiveness of the land, creating a larger travel demand, which again leads
to further land development [Kasraian et al., 2016]. A high population density of a capital creates a good
foundation for an effective transport system, including public transport. A dense city is a good thing when
looking at the socioeconomic potential as the distance to important locations is shortened [Larsen et al.,
2013]. The congestion problem can be solved on a short-term basis by using the principle of station proxim-
ity, securing large enterprises close to stations, and a joint parking strategy for the entire capital [Larsen et al.,
2013]. A more long-term solution regarding trafﬁc congestion could be nationwide road-pricing, enabling
regulation of the car trafﬁc in accordance with the demand [Larsen et al., 2013]. The road-pricing should be
an electronic fee from the state based on GPS measures of the drivers’ use of the road-network with time,
date, and distance [Larsen et al., 2013]. According to Larsen et al. [2013] in 2025 public transport in the
metropolitan area must handle 15% more trips than in 2012, while the number of car trips will increase by
10%, and bicycle trips by 3%.
Improved bicycle lanes and public transport systems are the bearing pillars for better mobility in the inner
city. The trafﬁc ﬂow should be made more efﬁcient on already existing roads while still considering e.g.,
air pollution [Larsen et al., 2013]. For instance, cycle superhighways (Supercykelstier) make more people
choose the bicycle even for longer distances. A sufﬁcient bike parking capacity around the city is necessary
when wanting to prioritise the bicycle, especially at nerve centre stations as the integration between the
bicycle and public transport is essential.
2.4
Recreational areas
Green spaces, or recreational areas, have a large effect on humans and the experienced urban space, and
they should be distributed uniformly across an urban area to minimise the distance from dwelling to park
[Mwendwa and Giliba, 2012]. The need for recreational areas has become even more important with the
large urban growth as parks contribute to more biodiversity, mitigating climate change, etc. [Li, 2020].
Green areas counteract global warming both by absorbing CO2, making better air quality, and by reduc-
ing the risk of urban heat islands [SMVdanmark, 2021]. However, many cities have been built towards
6
==PAGE:22==
increased car-usage, urban sprawl, and, thereby, altered travel behaviour, leading to more health problems
[University of Delaware, 2021]. Recreational areas have a direct link to better health - both physical and
mental, while also holding an important social aspect, as they can strengthen a community by creating safer
and cleaner neighbourhoods and better community atmosphere [California State Parks, 2005]. Green spaces
are positively linked to quality of life; the longer and more frequent a person visits a park, the better their
self-reported well-being will be [Li, 2020]. Mental health is directly linked to the size and number of green
areas, as residents of housing areas with few green areas (14.90 m2 per inhabitant) have a 16-27% higher
chance of depression and suicides, compared to locations with more green areas (33.31 m2 per inhabitant)
[Min et al., 2017]. The risk of stress increases with 42% when living more than 1 km away from green area
[SMVdanmark, 2021]. The amount of green area within a 1 km radius affects the risk of depression; people
living in an area with 10% of green within 1 km radius have a 25% higher chance of depression than those
living in an area of 90% of green within 1 km radius [Min et al., 2017].
The above-mentioned factors become very relevant as Frederiksberg is the municipality with the lowest
number of green areas per citizens in Denmark. Copenhagen Municipality has the second lowest score with
33.2 m2 per citizens which still is 60% more than Frederiksberg with only 13.4 m2 per citizens [SMVdan-
mark, 2021]. Though the population of Frederiksberg has increased in the past years, no extra space has
been allocated to recreational areas. In the municipality plan of 2017, Frederiksberg wishes that no citizens
have more than 500 meters to the nearest green area [Frederiksberg Kommune, 2017]. This wish is very
dependent on Frederiksberg’s perception of a green area and the size of such. In the suggestion of a new
municipality plan of 2021 Frederiksberg proposes a reduction of the maximum distance to a green area to
300 meters with the green area being a minimum of 2,000 m2 [Frederiksberg Kommune, 2021a]. An area of
2,000 m2 is deﬁned as a small green area by Teknik og Miljøforvaltningen [2018] with no improvement to
the microclimate of the cities. One could argue that Frederiksberg is setting the bar too low in regard to the
minimum size of green areas. The citizens of Frederiksberg have access to few larger green areas, namely:
Frederiksberg Garden and Søndermarken in the south, Grøndalsparken in the west, and two of the Lakes
in the east. This means much of the northern part of Frederiksberg has little access to large green areas.
It should be noted that Frederiksberg contains many residential areas with private gardens, minimising the
house owners’ need for access to public parks.

2.5.1
Air pollution
Air pollution holds a serious negative effect on human health, leading to substantial socioeconomic and
environmental consequences [Jensen et al., 2020]. Green areas hold a big resource in improving the air

quality through ﬁltering and air change [SMVdanmark, 2021]. Though Frederiksberg does not exceed the

due to outdoor air pollution [Jensen et al., 2020].

2.5.2
Urban heat islands
Urban heat islands occur in the city and are deﬁned as the air temperature difference between city and
rural land, making cities uncomfortable in warm periods [Armson, 2012]. As cities grow bigger due to
urbanisation the urban environment changes too, creating its own urban climate. Building materials are
usually impermeable, watertight, and of a darker colour, meaning the water cannot evaporate when the sun
is heating up the city. Dark materials, such as pavement, are good at storing the energy from the sun with
8
==PAGE:24==
temperatures up to 88 degrees [Poórová et al., 2019]. Furthermore, the taller built environment of the cities
alters the wind speed by either slowing it down or channelling the air with the risk of containing high levels
of pollution [Armson, 2012]. Heat islands can cause health problems and in general create discomfort for the
citizens, as well as contribute to a larger energy bill and more pollution [Poórová et al., 2019]. By planting
more vegetation, preferably trees, the urban heat islands can be reduced. Green areas alter the energy balance
of an area by cooling the environment through utilising solar radiation and water evaporation. Trees also have
the beneﬁt of acting as shading [Armson, 2012]. A 10% larger green area decreases the temperature by 1.2
degrees, or 3.7 degrees in the city [SMVdanmark, 2021]. The type of vegetation also affects the temperature.
For instance, grass has a lower Normalised Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) than trees, meaning how
much light is absorbed by the type of vegetation. The higher the NDVI, the lower the temperature [Bühler
et al., 2010].
2.5.3
Noise pollution
Noise pollution composes a critical public health issue, on some points being the second largest urban
environmental risk to health, second only to air pollution [Det Europæiske Miljøagentur, 2020]. The Danish
Environmental Protection Agency (Danish EPA) has judged street noise levels in cities acceptable for the
environment and health if between the mean value limits of 58-63 dB [Miljøstyrelsen, 2007]. That is an
interval up to 10 dB more than what WHO recommends. A 6 dB decrease is perceived as a 50% decrease in
noise level [Soni and Soni, 2016]. Even if compared to the Danish EPA limit, Roskildevej has a noise level
of up to 75 dB during the day and 65 dB at night, [Miljøstyrelsen, 2021], meaning the street only meets the
Danish guideline at night.
At least 1.6 million healthy years of life are lost due to street trafﬁc noise in Europe [WHO Regional Ofﬁce
for Europe, 2017]. High noise levels can lead to auditory effects, e.g. hearing loss and tinnitus, but is
also a stressor, which can lead to psychological and physiological distress such as cognitive impairment
in children, cardiovascular disease, annoyance, and sleep disturbance [WHO Regional Ofﬁce for Europe,
2017]. The relative risk of ischaemic heart disease due to trafﬁc noise is 8% [1-15]95% per 10 dB increase
in noise levels between a mean noise level of 40-80 dB [WHO Regional Ofﬁce for Europe, 2017]. Trafﬁc
noise has a relative risk of increasing strokes with 14% [3-25]95% per 10 dB increase of noise levels between
a mean noise level of 50-72 dB. The Guideline Development Group of WHO strongly recommends keeping
the noise from street trafﬁc below 53 dB during the daytime and 45 dB at night-time, all numbers being
mean values [WHO Regional Ofﬁce for Europe, 2017]. However, the present urbanisation and demand for
mobility makes it very unlikely that the number of people exposed to noise will decrease in the future [Det
Europæiske Miljøagentur, 2020].

2.6
Car-free zones
Copenhagen, including Frederiksberg, is already known to be a city with a large focus on its bicycle-
infrastructure. In the past years, more streets have been altered from their car-oriented layout towards being
more bicycle-friendly. However, Ramboll [2019] still found that 70% of participants in the questionnaire
favoured better bicycle-infrastructure and 59% favoured more car lanes to be reserved for vulnerable road-
users. Furthermore, 48% of the surveyed favoured transforming some parking spots into room for public
transport, bicycles, and green areas [Ramboll, 2019].
90% of the vehicles on the streets of Frederiksberg are private cars, making the private car-owner a major
focus group for Frederiksberg [Danmarks Statistik, 2021b]. Frederiksberg wants to make more people trans-
fer to an electric vehicle instead of a conventional car [Frederiksberg Kommune, 2017]. This, however, will
neither solve the problem of congested streets nor be more environmentally friendly. In general, electric ve-
hicles weigh more than conventional cars due to their batteries, and if all car users had to transfer to electric,
the air pollution would increase, even though the air would be perceived cleaner at street level due to the
lower levels of NOx [Jensen et al., 2020]. Frederiksberg would mainly beneﬁt from the change on a purely
political level. Many studies have concluded that increased car-mobility has an overall negative economic
impact on society [Soni and Soni, 2016]. Even though a lot of studies state that more space for pedestrians
and bicycles would strengthen the local businesses, the myth of the damage less car space would cause still
stands and render change impossible [Soni and Soni, 2016, Copenhagenize, 2019]. In the Christmas of 2018,
Madrid closed the city centre for cars, increasing the sales by 9.5% on their main street [Copenhagenize,
2019].
People tend to avoid areas in the city with heavy car trafﬁc, even though these areas are often central and
important for the economy. To facilitate the socioeconomic system and availability of an area, pedestriani-
sation of the streets is both a sustainable and effective, low-cost solution [Soni and Soni, 2016]. The number
of pedestrians increases signiﬁcantly along with public transport usage when creating pedestrian-friendly
space [Soni and Soni, 2016]. Pedestrianised infrastructure is only a solution if coupled with effective public
transport and bicycle infrastructure. City centres closed for vehicle trafﬁc are usually livelier, making the
citizens take pride and feel ownership of the area. People socialise more in the car-free streets, creating more
activities, reducing crime rates and the feeling of loneliness [Soni and Soni, 2016].
As a result of the pandemic of COVID-19 in 2020 many cities closed off areas temporarily to give room
for pedestrians and cyclists. However, many cities, such as Paris and Athena, are planning on keeping the
changes, due to the many beneﬁts, e.g. better air quality and less trafﬁc congestion [The Guardian, 2020].
Pedestrianisation is closely linked to improvements of the environment; for instance, air quality can become

the infrastructure is incorporated when creating car-free zones, the car trafﬁc will just be led elsewhere.

2.7
Megatrends
Some behavioural patterns of people are not dictated at a local basis, contrary they are affected by macroe-
conomic and geostrategic forces called megatrends. These megatrends shape the world and the collective
present and future [pwc, 2017]. Megatrends are important for societies in terms of shifting progress, giving
a hint of the possible future by showing the biggest challenges and opportunities of society [Retief et al.,
2016, pwc, 2017]. Each society or business should work along with the megatrends as they are too big to
be altered or stopped [Retief et al., 2016]. The megatrends are all interlinked, and each megatrend should
not be worked with isolated. Retief et al. [2016] explain on p. 53 that ’[...] changing demographics is also
reﬂected and inﬂuenced by urbanisation, and power shifts are made possible by accelerated technological
innovation’.
The current megatrends affecting all societies are listed below:
• The shift of global economic power is changing away from western dominance towards a more rebal-
anced global economics [pwc, 2017]. This means the world is becoming more bi-polar with no single
dominant player [Retief et al., 2016]. The megatrend suggests a better future through alliance for
common interests rather than only focusing on self-interests [Retief et al., 2016], along with shifting
the power from the elites to the broader population through the internet [Retief et al., 2016].
• The demography of countries is changing both towards rapid ageing and fast-growing young societies.
Some countries experience explosive population growth while others are in decline [pwc, 2017]. Both
scenarios have a large impact on the business world as well as the society due to the large pressure the
old populations put on the tax base and the demand young populations put on education, employment,
etc. [Retief et al., 2016].
• The urbanisation has accelerated over the past 70 years with 50% of the world’s population living
in cities by 2017; that will increase with 44% by 2050 [pwc, 2017]. This trend will burden the
infrastructure of older cities in the developed world to the utmost, while countries with emerging
13
==PAGE:29==
economies will experience a demand for massive investments in smart infrastructure [pwc, 2017]. The
massive urbanisation underlines the importance of far-sighted urban planning for more sustainable and
liveable cities [Retief et al., 2016].
• The innovation in technology has opened up for new investment opportunities and increased the pro-
ductive potential [pwc, 2017]. Technological innovation will proceed to accelerate; however, the
challenge will be the provision of contributing to the well-being of humans [Retief et al., 2016].
• Climate change is causing extreme weather and rising sea levels, affecting both cities and the coun-
tryside. The extreme progress of societies and businesses globally, seen in each megatrend, is closely
linked to the changing climate [Retief et al., 2016]. Furthermore, climate change is closely linked
to the shortage of resources [pwc, 2017] which have increased with 50 % from 2014-2030 in use of
global water, energy, and food demand, exceeding the limits of the earth’s resources [Retief et al.,
2016].
The COVID-19 pandemic in 2020-2021 affected many of the megatrends - some positively and others neg-
atively. The physical global interaction was limited by closed borders, making the digital technological
interaction bloom through social media, online communication platforms, etc. The lockdowns drove the
inhabitants of larger cities to recreational areas, resulting in an awareness of the lack of nature in the cities.
2.8
Sustainability assessment
A baseline comes in handy when wanting to assess the degree of sustainability of an area. The baseline
provides a point for inventorying the current situation of the area. The baseline can help clarify which
ﬁelds need changes and possible strategies. A baseline made for assessing sustainability is a crucial device
for sustainable urban planning and can work as a foundation [paleBLUEdot, 2021], and gives a holistic
view of all parameters. Working without a sustainability baseline could result in a too narrow ﬁx of the
situation, and worst-case scenario, leading to negative effects on other parameters [iSpring, 2021]. A well-
structured and tangible baseline enables better community dialogue and an understanding of the community
issues [Verma and Raghubanshi, 2018]. The baseline idea originates from the scenario methodology. The
scenario methodology is a data-driven optimisation that compares the changes of an area with previously
collected records of scenarios [Campi et al., 2018]. Later decisions are based on the most optimal outcome
of the scenario. The scenario methodology is an effective method for scrutinising future outcomes and
investigating assumptions [Chermack, 2005]. Scenario methodology acts like the best tool for preparing and
altering the unknown future in a rapid changing world, linking hypothetical future events with historical and
present events [Chermack, 2005].

Case area
This chapter presents Roskildevej as the case area, the history, and the current conditions and usage of the
road. This is done to give the reader an understanding of the area and how a change would affect it.
Roskildevej is an 800 meters long road in southern Frederiksberg with Frederiksberg Castle and Copenhagen
Zoo situated at its centre. The castle was founded at the beginning of 1700 together with Frederiksberg
Garden and Søndermarken [Danmarks Naturfredningsforening, 2021]. The cultural offer, Cisternene, lies
inside Søndermarken. Originally, the two gardens were one, but a road was built in 1776 to accommodate
a direct route from the capital to Roskilde [COWI, 2020]. Roskildevej changes to Vesterbrogade after Pile
Allé with many shopping facilities.

Roskildevej is deﬁned as a distribution road, meaning it is the link between thoroughfare roads and local
streets [Byens vejnet, 2021]. It is very important to maintain the classiﬁcation of the road for the users not

Methods
This chapter will explain the general methods used to collect numerical data and the methodologies used
to answer the problem statements of this thesis. A combination of qualitative and quantitative methods was
used for the data collection such as observations, interviews, questionnaires, and the creation of sustainability
indicators.
4.1
Literature review
A literature review was conducted to understand and explore all the aspects of the subject, e.g., sustainable
mobility, pollution, the visions of the municipality and state, etc. The literature review gave an overview
of the existing research and its possible knowledge gaps. Different literature on academic theories was
combined to support the objectives of this study [Saunders et al., 2007]. Through the literature review, it was
possible to condense relevant data from many different sources into a complete overview of the subject with
the use of critical reading. Without a critical approach, the literature review could risk being a noncritical
listing of previous research [Saunders et al., 2007]. The publications were found from various sources such
as DTU FindIt, Elsevier, and Google Scholar by use of different keyword combinations.
4.2
Empirical Methods
The empirical method was used to investigate the scoped area to derive veriﬁable evidence. This was done
through the mixed-method approach with both qualitative and quantitative analysis for an in-depth exami-
nation of the subject, as the chosen technique would dictate the obtained results [Saunders et al., 2007]. The
different data collection types were linked to their own analysis procedure, either done parallel or sequen-
tial. The use of mixed methods did not only gather more data but ruled out the risk of the ’method effect’,
meaning the disadvantages of each method (explained further down) [Saunders et al., 2007]. However, the
more methods used, the greater the unanticipated outcomes.

Mobility observations
An assessment of the mobility of the area was made, containing an observation of the movement of people,
size and patterns, interactions between people and other road users, the atmosphere of the area, and structural
features and functions. The focus of the observations was mainly on the top of Valby Bakke by the two
entrances to Frederiksberg Garden and by the main-entrance to Zoo, see Figure 4.1 for exact locations.
Figure 4.1: Observation points at Roskildevej [Krak, 2021].
Several observations were performed on different days of the week to collect the most representative data of
18
==PAGE:34==
Roskildevej [Saunders et al., 2007]. The ﬁrst observation was on a weekday in week 7 (the school winter
holiday week). This day was chosen as it was presumed more people would be using the park facilities,
making the movement of people more coherent and clearer. The second observation was conducted on a
weekday in the presumed lunch break between 11.30-12.30. This time and date were chosen as many people
use their break time to get some fresh air and exercise. The third observation was made on the weekend on a
sunny day, as it was presumed many people would be using the facilities both due to the weather and being
their day off. All observations were gathered by note-taking. Maps of the areas were prepared for drawing
the patterns of people’s movement directly on site.
The quantitative method was used for the mobility observation with the naturalistic observation approach
[Stamp and Mikkelsen, 2007]. This type of method was chosen due to its direct look at social behaviour and
none-manipulate data [Cherry, 2019]. A disadvantage of this method, however, is the observer effect, where
the behaviour can be interpreted subjectively by the observer, and the natural behaviour of people might be
affected by the consciousness of being observed [Saunders et al., 2007]. The qualitative method was used for
the observations of people, atmosphere, and area [Stamp and Mikkelsen, 2007]. These general observations
were done by ﬁeld notes. Important details can be captured with this method; however, the data can be mis-
interpreted if not written down immediately [Aarhus Universitet, 2019]. Both the naturalistic and ﬁeld note
observations were made as objectively as possible and conducted with minimal interaction. Observations
can be very time-consuming and, therefore, costly, meaning the observation data can be difﬁcult to replicate
[Saunders et al., 2007]. Observations are the best method for quantifying behaviour; however, they do not
touch on why the patterns occur [Saunders et al., 2007].
Mobility is not only the quantitative description of congestion and the number of people on bicycles but
equally the everyday mobility and unnoticed micro-action between the players of the street [Jensen, 2015].
The mobility of people is staged by the physical settings, dictating the frame for the social interactions and
choices as well as how it unfolds. Technology within transit affects the everyday mobility staging of people
through both possibilities and limitations. Analysing mobility can be carried out by viewing the mobility of
the road-users as a river. The observer ’elevates’ themselves up to see the larger picture and monitor the ﬂow
of people, thereby, detecting centres and clashes and prioritised routes [Jensen, 2015]. Another method to
analyse everyday mobility is by viewing the people as performing a ballet, meaning the actions and bodily
signals between the road-users to best operate in a common space. Each person analyses the space in a
concrete and situation-based way [Jensen, 2015]. The analysis of the mobility of Roskildevej was done with
focus on the interaction between different and similar road-users. The communication between parts was
observed together with the ﬂow of both pedestrians and trafﬁc. Each spot with full-stop motion was noted if
not within the intended physical settings. The reasons behind the choice of path were decoded. Finally, the
19
==PAGE:35==
scale of the mobility problems was found through all the above-mentioned factors.
4.2.1.1
DepthMapX
The software depthMapX was used to investigate the spatial layout of the area and how it interlinked [Space
Syntax, 2021]. DepthMapX supported the created observations with science-based analysis of social be-
haviour in the built environment by visualising the reasons behind it. The software enabled the linkage
between patterns of movement as well as the road-hierarchy. DepthMapX was used to analyse three dif-
ferent types of maps: visibility, axial, and segment. The visibility map was analysed for the area’s Isovist
(the visible area from a given point). The visibility map used the footprint of the built-up area, including
buildings, opaque fences, and vegetation. The Isovist illustrated the possible view from a given point in the
area, giving a thorough exploration of the given location. The axial map illustrated the ﬂows of movement
in the area by looking at the connectivity of the streets and scale of integration. The axial map showed
how the network of streets worked together and their importance in the grid. The segment map depicted the
streets’ line structure by segmenting them and their relations to angular change, meaning a straight connec-
tion between streets would be 0 degrees. People tend to take the most direct route and choosing a turn of
more than 90 degrees becomes very strange. The segment map analysed the least angle segment of the area
through a metric radius and depicted the potential of the streets as a thoroughfare in the whole network. The
maps were decoded through a colour scale with red being a high through movement potential and blue the
opposite.

Questionnaire
The quantitative method was used by conducting two questionnaires, one for the users of the park areas, the
other for the business owners at Vesterbrogade. Both questionnaires were anonymous. The questionnaire
for the users of the parks was conducted as an interviewer-administrated, structured interview on site. The
questionnaire of the business owners was conducted by responding to a self-administrated, internet-mediated
questionnaire. The quantitative questionnaire types were chosen to ensure enough data. The business owners
at Vesterbrogade were found through Google Maps, and only the business owners lying within the borders
of Frederiksberg were contacted, meaning the stretch of Vesterbrogade between Pile Allé and Platanvej.
The structured interview of the users was carried out on both a weekday and on the weekend to ensure
no population groups were under-represented. The questions were read out in the same tone and order to
prevent any indications of biases [Saunders et al., 2007]. Closed-ended questions were used together with
opinion variables to create descriptive and explanatory data of the perception of the area. Attribute variables
were used to spot perception groupings due to e.g., age or gender. The questionnaires were created through
Google Forms by use of different answer types, such as rating scales from a scale of 1-5. The participants

5.1
Sustainable Development Indicators
Indicators are deﬁned as variables representing a broader issue or matter of interest while reﬂecting speciﬁc
characteristics or problems [Gudmundsson et al., 2015]. The intangible structure of sustainability can be
given form through indicators and become an informational device to inﬂuence governments [Gudmunds-
son et al., 2015]. The indicators must embrace the diversity of the involved disciplines to resonate with
different stakeholders. However, indicators are not pure, as they are subjectively chosen to cover certain
aspects. Even though indicators still help achieve sustainability goals and to inform the public and policy-
makers of the current standpoints [Verma and Raghubanshi, 2018].
Sustainability assessments indicate the current state of sustainability, quantiﬁes it, give feedback on imple-
mentations, and ﬁnds the best measures of sustainability [Verma and Raghubanshi, 2018]. Unfortunately,
sustainability assessments are not easily transformed for other case studies due to the area-speciﬁc indica-
tors. To be able to make a comprehensive sustainability assessment of an area, the sustainability indicators
should embrace all three environmental domains - economic, social, and environmental. The process, how-
ever, can be difﬁcult due to the complexity of the relationship of the domains and the lack of understandable
measures of all three [Maclaren, 1996]. As mentioned in Section 4.4 the creation of indicators is iterative.
The process of settling on a ﬁnal set of indicators is explained below.

5.1.2
Scoping
The target audience of this sustainability assessment is Frederiksberg Municipality. The object of creating
the assessment is to give Frederiksberg a tool for measuring the extent of improvement of mobility and
sustainability of Roskildevej. The indicators must, therefore, be precise, detailed, and with high scientiﬁc
validation.
The number of indicators was based on the duration of this thesis with its time limitation regarding data
collection and the ability to make a holistic result, together with a comprehensible number of indicators.
Therefore, the number of indicators was chosen to be between 8 and 12. The number of indicators are data-
dependent, as the source must be both stable, available, and relevant for the domains of sustainability. The
baseline and sustainability assessment must be able to be used again to measure other scenarios for the same
area. The data for the baseline should be the latest available and only use realistic simulated data for the
future. The temporal bounding of this report was, therefore, not larger than necessary. The spatial bounding
of this study is southern Frederiksberg since the changes do not affect all of Frederiksberg. Adjacent areas
will be mentioned but lie outside the scope.
5.1.3
Framework
The indicators were made through a framework that links the indicators together and organises how they
are selected and used [Gudmundsson et al., 2015]. Combination of frameworks was used for this thesis. A
combination of frameworks eliminates each framework’s disadvantages as well as consolidating the advan-
tages [Maclaren, 1996]. The creation of the indicators was a three-step procedure. Initially, the scopes of
the indicators were selected by use of the goal-based framework, ensuring the SDGs were incorporated in
the indicators [Maclaren, 1996]. The goal-based framework made it possible to measure the sustainability
changes both good or bad. Secondly, the main sustainability problems expressed by Frederiksberg created
the indicators by use of the issue-based framework, which was chosen to ensure all the visions of Frederiks-
berg were included for the evaluation of the sustainability of the area. This framework makes the needed
changes more tangible [Maclaren, 1996]. Finally, the indicators were categorised into the three sustainabil-
ity domains by use of the domain-based framework, ensuring all aspects of sustainability were included
[Maclaren, 1996].
5.1.4
Potential indicators
The potential indicators were found by brainstorming for the relevant SDGs and visions of Frederiksberg.
The brainstorm was conducted with urban sustainability and sustainable mobility in mind to make sure all
potential indicators were found and covered. At this stage, no indicators were ruled out as being too far from
the scope or with too little data available.

5.1.5
Selection criteria
When selecting the indicators, it relied solely on general evaluation points for viability and validity [Ma-
claren, 1996]. Good sustainability indicators should meet the following criteria [Wolf et al., 2015, Maclaren,
1996, Gudmundsson et al., 2015]: Robust, relevant, effective, available, understandable, and practical. This
means that an indicator should be appropriate and realistic when measuring a given scenario [Gudmundsson
et al., 2015]. Furthermore, the indicators should be easily understandable as the user might not be an expert
in all stated ﬁelds [Ghellere et al., 2017]. All irrelevant indicators were discarded to capture the essence of
the circumstances. By a clear deﬁnition of the problems of interest the most representative indicators could
be found. Poor choices would materialise in the utilisation stage [Gudmundsson et al., 2015].
The simpliﬁed selection approach, SMART criteria (Speciﬁc, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, and Timely),
were used to choose the indicators [Gudmundsson et al., 2015]. The method was an easy grading approach,
however, with less scientiﬁc support.

Measures of recreational areas
As mentioned in Section 5.1.2 only southern Frederiksberg is of interest, therefore, nothing above Fin-
sensvej, Smallegade, and Gl. Kongevej will be included. Southern Frederiksberg is 4.15 km2, which is
approximately 50% of Frederiksberg’s total area. Green areas adjacent to dwellings on the border of south-
ern Frederiksberg were included if they were within 300 meters (of said dwellings). Green areas below 2
hectares are deﬁned as too small to have an impact on the microclimate of a city [Teknik og Miljøforvalt-
ningen, 2018]. Bühler et al. [2010] found that urban heat islands can only be reduced by green areas of a
minimum of 3.5 hectares.
To measure if the vision was achieved for the citizens of southern Frederiksberg a minimum size of a green
area was set to 4 hectares. This size was chosen due to the aforementioned deﬁnition, being above the
minimum size for heat reducing parks, and by measuring the size of other popular recreational areas in
Copenhagen, such as Litauens Plads or Folkets Park. Critical situated areas of dwellings were found through
the maps on Krak [2021]. The open-source tool, Ruteplanner [iForm, 2021], was used to ﬁnd the distance
from the door of each critical dwelling to the nearest park. Existing streets and smaller paths were used for
the measurement, meaning shortcuts were not included. Each address of the dwelling of interest was noted
down. Residential areas with their own garden were not seen as critical dwelling even if a larger green area
was more than 300 meters away.

Movement patterns
The movement pattern of pedestrians can be seen as a river, as pedestrians tend to choose the most direct path
between A and B. Each person’s choice of route was drawn on a map. See the black lines in Figure 6.1 for the
river patterns. Roskildevej has fences, walls, or dense thickets along most of its length. Both Søndermarken
and Frederiksberg Garden have minor openings, making the movement of people more abrupt. The low
fence at Søndermarken took many pedestrians by surprise, making the river ﬂow uneven. See dotted circle
in Figure 6.1. The southern side of Roskildevej has few stretches of good sidewalk and from Frederiksberg
Castle to Pile Allé, the pedestrians must walk through Søndermarken or cross to walk on the northern side
of Roskildevej. This also comes as a surprise to some pedestrians walking eastwards, often resulting in an
unwanted ballet between cyclists and pedestrians. The river of the cars is relatively smooth, mainly paused
by the light junctions. Most of the vehicles drive within the speed limit, making the distance assessment
easy for the other road users. The hillside to the east seems to make the cars hesitate when driving west, this
could be due to a lack of clear visibility of the hilltop. The sidewalk in front of the Zoo entrance is wider
than the rest of Roskildevej. The cluster in front of Zoo together with the counter-terrorism boxes take up
much of the space on the sidewalk. This distorts the river of pedestrians trying to move along Roskildevej,
resulting in congestion and people using the parking spots and bicycle paths as shortcuts. The waiting time
at the ofﬁcial pedestrian crossing is around 20 seconds from the time the ’pedestrian pushbutton’ (the button
at a pedestrian crossing for green light) is pushed with 10 seconds to cross.
Other ﬁndings
The atmosphere of the area was observed to be lively, however, a bit stressful at the crossing - both the ofﬁcial
and self-made. Frederiksberg Castle seems very grandiose when seen from Frederiksberg Garden but not
very noticeable from Søndermarken. The space in front of the entrance to the castle is very narrow and
people often use the bicycle path to get past other pedestrians. Due to the different degree of fencing along
Roskildevej, the road has a tunnel-like vibe with only one genuine opening at Søndermarken. The main
facilities of Roskildevej all have their entrances at the centre of Roskildevej, making the ends of the road

6.3
Future scenario
This section describes the possible outcomes for the scenario of removing car trafﬁc from Roskildevej. The
scenario will be split into the two categories of best and worst outcome, when needed. The analysis of the
car-free scenario will contain a mobility analysis made through depthMapX of the changed road network
and each indicator with its EAR score.
6.3.1
Mobility
By closing Roskildevej for car trafﬁc, the barrier between the two parks will be removed and pedestrians
would gain direct crossing opportunities, depending on the bicycle- and footpath network. The former
clusters of people, trying to cross Roskildevej, would disappear along with the unwanted ballet between
pedestrians and visitors waiting in line to the Zoo. The area without cars would become safer and less
stressful for the light road user, making the counter-terrorism boxes in front of Zoo unnecessary if other
precautionary measures were made at each end of Roskildevej. With the pedestrians not inﬂuenced by
vehicles, the movement pattern would become more ﬂuid, especially if the walls and fences between the
parks were removed or minimised. The atmosphere around Roskildevej would be expected to become less
stressful and the southern entrance to Frederiksberg Castle would be expected to seem more grandiose with
a castle plaza that better presents the building. The large grass area at the beginning of Søndermarken could
become a more popular place for taking stay. However, if the bicycle path is not well integrated it would
become a new barrier between the two parks. This is especially important to keep in mind with the projected
cycle superhighways on Roskildevej. Furthermore, if good footpaths are not made along Roskildevej the
east and west moving pedestrians might still have a lower rank in the hierarchy in the ballet with north and
south moving pedestrians. The future Roskildevej should contain multiple footpaths for improved mobility
of the area. This would ﬁx the former lack of sidewalk and its problem with the pedestrians’ movement on
the southern side of Roskildevej. The river of cars becomes less direct as cars will be forced to take a detour
around the parks if the congestion is not dealt with. As seen in Figure 2.2, Valby Langgade would take most
of the car trafﬁc, creating an unwanted barrier between Frederiksberg and Valby.

most likely to stay the same, except for the pedestrians who used to take the detour to the ofﬁcial pedestrian

menting many bushes and trees around the bicycle- and footpaths.

Discussion
This chapter goes over the choice of SDGs and municipality visions, development of the indicators, and
results of the baseline and the future scenario. The discussion includes the advantages and disadvantages of
changing Roskildevej, the analysis of the area’s mobility and sustainability, the inﬂuence on the area, and
ﬁnally the size effect of the scope.
7.1
The creation of the assessment
The ﬁrst aim of this thesis was to measure the urban mobility and sustainability of Roskildevej and the
area around it. This was done by creating relevant and measurable indicators indicators that would match
the vision in the municipality plan of Frederiksberg along with the SDGs. Additionally, observations of
Roskildevej were made to gain a more wholesome understanding of the movement patterns of the area to
ﬁnd the problematic zones.
The worldwide climate problems were articulated politically through the creation of the SDGs. Solving the
expressed issues should be done by use of science and engineering. A solely engineering-based selection of
indicators would have inﬂuenced the result of the mobility and sustainability assessment to be more focused
on direct results with little inclusion of the users. Creating indicators on an engineering basis might be more
practical and realistic, but not necessarily better as the human scale would have been forgotten. On the other
hand, a solely political basis for the assessment would not have been beneﬁcial either, as the results could
have been too vague. This thesis was, therefore, created by balancing the two processes.
The selection process of SDGs was not only for the creation of the indicators but just as much to see whether
Frederiksberg had implemented the goals in the municipality plan. It was found that Frederiksberg had
incorporated many measures within urban sustainability. The municipality goals acted as inspiration for
the indicator brainstorm. Not all potential targets were used for the analysis due to this thesis’ deliber-
ate focus on changing the layout of Roskildevej. These goals were garbage disposal, cultural offer, CO2
73
==PAGE:89==
neutrality, climate-prooﬁng, accessibility, and Smart-City infrastructure [Frederiksberg Kommune, 2020].
Though relevant for urban sustainability, the goals were not found useful for the speciﬁc infrastructural case
of Roskildevej. For instance, Roskildevej is surrounded by absorbing green areas and has a steep hill, mak-
ing climate-prooﬁng against e.g., cloud bursts irrelevant. The Smart-City infrastructure is directed towards
a more artiﬁcial intelligent solution, which does not speak of a changed road layout. Furthermore, the scope
was to create 12 indicators, thus only the most tangible goals were selected for the clarity of the baseline
tool.
It would have been interesting to investigate the travel habits of the population to and from work in the
southern part of Frederiksberg, such as the percentage of people travelling by foot, bicycle, and public
transport. Such indicators could have spoken of the conditions of the transportation modes and the choices
of the population segment. This indicator was not considered further due to difﬁculties of separating the data
for southern Frederiksberg let alone Roskildevej. The indicator would probably have had more useful data
if the metro line from Ny Ellebjerg already existed due to the improved mobility. Instead, the indicators,
measuring the number of bicycles and cars on Roskildevej, was chosen as it was found to give a more local
insight of the conditions on Roskildevej.
The psychological well-being of citizens has come more into focus in political decision-making, especially
during the COVID-19 pandemic. With the knowledge of parks having a positive inﬂuence on humans’ state
of mind, an indicator accommodating this would be relevant. The indicator ‘psychological inﬂuence of
parks and their size’ was produced but ended up being discharged. This decision was made as no data was
available for the exact area of Frederiksberg Garden and Søndermarken and the indicator would demand
many interviews with both people in the risk group and experts. The indicator was instead made simpler by
focusing on the size of the green areas and how much green area each citizen has at their disposal. Other
indicators were deselected by similar considerations.
The current mobility and sustainability of Roskildevej and the surrounding area got an SPS of 2.90 on a scale
of 1-5. This means the area is at the low end of urban sustainability and mobility. Many of the indicators
receiving a low score were directly or indirectly connected to car trafﬁc. Fewer cars on the roads would
improve urban sustainability as cars have a negative impact on both the environment and society [Selzer
and Lanzendorf, 2019]. With fewer cars on the streets both the noise and heat levels of the city could be
reduced; the roads would not need as much space for cars, giving the opportunity to transfer former car
lanes into recreational space. Furthermore, fewer cars in the city mean less need for parking. Reducing the
number of cars could, thereby, improve the score of indicators with a non-sustainable score (Size of green
area, Parking, Noise pollution, Heat islands). The transition away from cars would be helped by intelligent
74
==PAGE:90==
technology to create ’smart mobility’ [Selzer and Lanzendorf, 2019]. The transition speaks directly into
one of the megatrends stirring the world towards more innovation in technology. The stigma of cars giving
people ultimate freedom and ﬂexibility needs to be altered for urban sustainability and mobility to succeed,
being even more important with increasing urbanisation.
7.2
Roskildevej now and in the future
The second aim of this thesis was to ﬁnd the advantages and disadvantages of the possible future layout of
Roskildevej. The created indicators for the baseline were used as measures to test possible outcomes by
dividing the scenario into best and worst outcome.
The business friendliness of the area had a large deviation between the possible outcomes. For Vesterbrogade
to gain advantages from the changes of Roskildevej both the business owners and the municipality must use
the shift to their beneﬁt. For instance, if the lowered number of cars on Vesterbrogade is not used to alter the
atmosphere of the street from being a busy car road to a popular shopping street for pedestrians, by improved
infrastructure and presentable business windows, Vesterbrogade will stay the same. Vesterbrogade might
even seem deserted with fewer people using the street as a thoroughfare. Some of the interviewed business
owners would welcome the change of the area to create a more open front of their shop, as it would be
possible to use the space at the facade, e.g., for having outdoor seating. Seating and clothes rails are known
to create a more diverse town life [Københavns Kommune, 2012]. The ﬁrst 80 cm from house facade to
sidewalk is free for the business to place items on, however, only a few businesses on Vesterbrogade do so
[Københavns Kommune, 2012]. Other business owners were more critical to the changes of the area, stating
fewer people would be able to come to the shop, or a wider sidewalk would increase their property tax.
Some interviewees might be inﬂuenced by habit with little interest in trying out something new; many of the
negative answers were from business owners who have had their shop at Vesterbrogade for the past 39-54
years.
Another indicator with a large deviation between best and worst outcome was parking. The indicator had a
wide gap between the scoring of the bicycle and car parking, due to bicycles still having access to Roskilde-
vej. How large the advantage of the number of bicycle parking will be is solely up to Frederiksberg. By only
matching the number of bicycle stands with car parking (+21%), Frederiksberg would only improve the load
rate conditions by 14%, though still an improvement to the current situation. More bicycle stands could eas-
ily be implemented which would prevent unwanted parking of bicycles at the walls of Frederiksberg Castle
or the fence of the two parks. Furthermore, more bicycle stands might have a nudging effect for choosing
the bicycle.
75
==PAGE:91==
The parking on Roskildevej will probably be a disadvantage for the area, as the probability is very small that
investors or Frederiksberg will build an underground car park. A question arises, whether less car parking is
an actual problem. If the conditions for cars are made worse through parking, the choice of car transportation
might become less attractive as the seeking time for a parking spot would be too long. This would decrease
the advantages of private car drivers, resulting in fewer cars on the roads and a reduction in congestion.
However, this does not mean that all parking spots should be removed from the city, as some visitors might
live far outside the city with little access to public transport. The city still needs to be accessible to all.
An alternative could be parking lots outside the city with direct access to different parts of the city through
public transport.
As seen in the EAS, indicator 1.4 ’Size of green area per citizen’ and 1.5 ’Living more than 300 meters
from a green area’, did not differ from the baseline. This speaks of two indicators being irrelevant for the
baseline of the investigated area. During other circumstances, the indicators should have been reworked in
an iterative process to obtain more representative indicators, as expressed in Section 4.4.
However, indicator 1.4 and 1.5 both relate to the visions stated in the municipality plan of Frederiksberg
and illustrate how well Frederiksberg is achieving its goals and depict how difﬁcult some measures are to
reach. From the indicator brainstorm two other similar indicators were found, namely, ‘the recreational
areas percentage of urban areas’ and ‘the psychologically and physically beneﬁts from park area’. These
indicators might portray another importance of Roskildevej as a green area.
If the inclusion of indicators 1.4 and 1.5 was important, the weighting of the indicators should have been
assessed individually for them to easier achieve a high or low score. This would make the indicators less
engineering based and more inﬂuenced by political decisions. By weighting the indicators, some of them
become more important than others, which shifts the neutrality. Knowing the weighting of indicators is
difﬁcult unless dictated by political persuasion.
The third aim of this thesis was to predict how the adjacent areas to Roskildevej might be affected by a
changed infrastructure. The closing of Roskildevej would, as Frederiksberg wished for in the municipality
plan, create a better coherence of the two parks. The two gardens and Zoo attract thousands of guests each
year, making them an important part of the city culture [Frederiksberg Kommune, 2017]. Frederiksberg
must weigh whether the large number of visitors is reason enough to close an important arterial road in
the southern Frederiksberg. Shutting down Roskildevej for car trafﬁc will inﬂuence both public and private
vehicle transportation. Deciding to close the road, anyhow, speaks of an interest in following the megatrend
of creating a more liveable city with less car focus.
The occupancy rate of vehicles (the number of people in a vehicle) in Denmark is 1.31 for private cars
76
==PAGE:92==
and just above 1 for both lorries, delivery vans, and motorcycles [Skougaard and Christiansen, 2015], the
numbers assumed to be equal for Frederiksberg. The distribution of vehicles at Frederiksberg is 90% private
cars, >1% lorries, 5% delivery vans, and 5% motorcycles [Danmarks Statistik, 2021b], and with Roskildevej
having 22,890 vehicles per day, the total number of vehicle users is around 10.76 million people a year. The
number of visitors in the two parks is assumed to be equal, as the size of the parks is even. The total number
of yearly visitors of both the parks, Zoo, and Cisternene are 7.3 million, this being a 32% lower rate of
people than the vehicle users of Roskildevej. This conﬁrms that Frederiksberg in a car-free scenario would
prioritise the none-vehicle based transportation in the municipality. With the megatrend urbanisation, the
need for liveable cities has become more urgent with a need for adjusting the city for the ’small user’ [Retief
et al., 2016]. Not prioritising this megatrend of liveable cities would only result in more congested roads as
urbanisation is inevitable. If space for vehicles is allocated, citizens will ﬁnd reasons to transport themselves
by car.
The prioritisation of the ’small users’ on Roskildevej will not only inﬂuence the coherence of the parks,
but also the travel time of both car drivers and public transport. For the travel time not to be inﬂuenced
negatively, the infrastructure needs to be improved. As mentioned in Section 2.6 larger road closings must be
accommodated by improved public transportation and light road user facilities. The possible delay time for
car drivers would be resolved as some of the commuters would swap to public transport instead. For instance,
1,000 car trips will be removed with the implementation of the metro line from Ny Ellebjerg [Metroselskabet,
2019]. The possibility of modifying the bus route of 7A to BRT, matching the new infrastructure, would
serve as the last link of improvement, reducing the travel time between 10-70% [Ingvardson and Nielsen,
2018]. The advantages of reduced travel time in peak hour would also be assisted by the cycle superhighway,
making bicycling more attractive.
7.3
Achieving sustainability goals of Frederiksberg
The last aim of this thesis was to determine whether the scope of Roskildevej was too small to achieve
the sustainability goals of Frederiksberg. As illustrated in Section 6.3, the closing of Roskildevej for car
trafﬁc has many different outcomes, however, both best and worst outcomes result in improved mobility and
sustainability of the area, see Figure 6.28.
Closing Roskildevej for car trafﬁc could act as a pioneering project, making an example of how Frederiks-
berg wishes the municipality to achieve their sustainability goals in the future. Roskildevej cannot solely
reach the visions of Frederiksberg, but it might operate in combination with other initiatives for creating
more urban sustainability in Frederiksberg. As explained in Section 6.3.5, the inclusion of Roskildevej as a
77
==PAGE:93==
park contributes very little to the green area per citizen in Frederiksberg; the size of the green area is directly
linked to the mental well-being of the citizens. However, the closing of Roskildevej could set the agenda
in shaping a more liveable city where the small user is prioritised, making more room for green breathing
spaces in the city, improving mental and physical well-being.
A new question arises when comparing the change of Roskildevej to the sustainability goals of Frederiks-
berg, namely, whether the visions of Frederiksberg are adequate for the SDGs. The visions of Frederiksberg
leap out as conceptions for a more beautiﬁed municipality, compared to the SDGs addressing more crucial
problematics in the world. Is this due to Frederiksberg being a part of a western country with less critical
issues affecting the everyday life of the people? Or Frederiksberg not being visionary enough or having the
guts for a drastic change? Or is it simply that the speciﬁc SDG indicators should be compared to the goals
of the municipality plan of Frederiksberg instead? Comparing more tangible measures would make it easier
to spot possible differences or insufﬁciencies. Frederiksberg is ’only’ a municipality and does not have the
same leeway as a nation. This makes it complicated to make the necessary drastic changes. However, the
State of Denmark sees the municipalities as the most suitable for implementing the SDGs [United Nations,
2021b]. Furthermore, many of the SDGs are aimed at developing countries to improve their living standards.
One thing directly affecting both developed and undeveloped countries is climate change, which seems to
be well implemented in the SDGs and visions of Frederiksberg. Changing the layout of Roskildevej would
be a change that lives up to the set visions as it shows a willingness to shift the perception of an area away
from being car-dominated.

7.5
Source of errors
This section will examine the possible source of errors affecting the outcome of the results. The source of
errors might both be due to unforeseen obstacles.
7.5.1
The pandemic of COVID-19
This thesis was written during the pandemic of COVID-19, including different phases of the lockdown,
which might have inﬂuenced some of the collected data.
As the pandemic shut down many indoor activities more people used the outdoor facilities. This could have
inﬂuenced the seen movement of people as the streams might have been larger than usual, creating more
and greater clusters of people. However, the larger quantities of people could also be representing the future
80
==PAGE:96==
scenario in a growing city with more citizens. With more people working from home, the car trafﬁc would
naturally be lower in the rush hour. The lower number of cars could have swayed the perceived observation
of the mobility on the street, as Roskildevej would seem less congested.
Zoo was closed from the 8th of January to the 4th of March, removing the normal number of visitors,
which could have inﬂuenced the number of parked cars and bicycles. The reopening of Zoo came with a
requirement of a negative and newly made COVID-19 test. The new queuing precautions at the Zoo entrance
and the temporary test centre at the former residence of the Zoo director could have changed the movement
pattern of pedestrians walking along Roskildevej. The test centre might have attracted more people to the
area than normally, inﬂuencing the number of bicycles parked outside bicycle stands.
Though face masks were worn, and distance kept, some people did not feel comfortable being interviewed
for the questionnaire in the park. This could have resulted in some opinions not being represented in the
data. The data of the questionnaire of the business owners on Vesterbrogade might have been inﬂuenced
by some of the shops being closed during the lockdown. Some business owners might have answered more
negatively to the questions due to having a lower number of customers.
7.5.2
Questionnaire
It was derived from the questionnaire of the business owners on Vesterbrogade that some of them misunder-
stood the questions. Some thought that Vesterbrogade too should be closed for car trafﬁc. One interviewee
answered:
"[...] many of our customers arrive by car from large parts of Zealand, and if our location becomes impos-
sible to access, no customers would come and we would die."
The questions only presented that Roskildevej would close for car trafﬁc, meaning the business owners either
thought a reduction in car trafﬁc of 32% was a massive intervention, or they simply misread the questions.
If this was a matter of misunderstanding the questions, the business owners might have responded more
negatively to the questions which would have tampered with the data.
7.6
Sustainability assessment as a tool
This thesis was a study of the sustainability and mobility of Roskildevej and the surrounding areas, made
as a monitoring tool for Frederiksberg to assess its sustainability goals for 2030. Though some of the
indicators may not be relevant for other cases, the baseline for Roskildevej could be an inspiration for
similar projects in Frederiksberg as the indicators were design based on Frederiksberg’s visions. Measuring
the sustainability goals of Frederiksberg through this model might even make general sustainability practise

both Frederiksberg and relevant parts in the municipality, such as corporations wanting to contribute to a
green agenda. This would support the goal, stated in the municipality plan of Frederiksberg, of entering

Conclusions
This master’s thesis serves as an assessment of the urban sustainability and mobility of Roskildevej. The as-
sessment is made as a monitoring tool for Frederiksberg Municipality to evaluate its sustainability goals for
2030. The assessment is conducted by creating 12 indicators corresponding to the visions of Frederiksberg
and the UN’s Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). These are combined with observations of the area
to provide an understanding of the problematic zones. Roskildevej, in its current state, achieves a Sustain-
ability Performance Score (SPS) of 2.90 on a scale from 1 to 5, which is considered low in terms of urban
sustainability and mobility. Many of the low-rated indicators are directly or indirectly linked to car trafﬁc.
The future scenario is divided into a best and worst outcome, as a car-free Roskildevej might have a wide
range of different outcomes. The future scenario has a 25%-point difference in its Effect Assessment Score
(EAS) between the best and worst outcome. This means a car-free Roskildevej has an improving effect on
mobility and sustainability of the area in both the best and worst outcome.
During the selection of the SDGs, it became clear that Frederiksberg Municipality already had incorporated
many of these measures into their municipal plan which served as inspiration for the indicator brainstorm
made in this thesis. The applied indicators are created and chosen to be tangible and easy to investigate,
hereby discarding irrelevant or too complex indicators. ‘Business friendliness of the area’ and ‘parking’ are
two indicators with large variations between the score of the best and worst outcome. The ﬁnal possible
outcomes of these indicators depend on how Frederiksberg Municipality ends up using a potentially car-free
Roskildevej to their advantage. Two indicators, namely, ‘the size of green area per citizen’ and ‘living more
than 300 meters from green area’ have identical scores in both the baseline and future scenario. This makes
them irrelevant to the studied area as they do not speak of any change, although both are directly related to
the visions of Frederiksberg.
The closure of Roskildevej for car trafﬁc would not only remove the barrier between the two parks on
either side of the road but also inﬂuence the public and private transportation in the area. Opposing vehicle
83
==PAGE:99==
dominance would follow the megatrend to create a more liveable city with a narrative of focusing on people
rather than cars. However, the infrastructure needs to be improved if travel time is not to be adversely
affected. In addition, the decision to cut off car trafﬁc from Roskildevej can serve as a pioneering project for
Frederiksberg Municipality in its ambition to create a liveable city where vulnerable road users are prioritised
over motorised trafﬁc. Frederiksberg Municipality has chosen a design proposal with a road redevelopment
of Roskildevej as the future scenario to solve the main problem of the barrier between the parks. This road
redevelopment will not fulﬁl the sustainability goals to the same extent as the car-free scenario but could
be considered the ﬁrst step in a smooth transition towards a car-free Roskildevej. In any case, the baseline
scenario analysed in this thesis should be used to assess the effects of the upcoming road redevelopment
on the area’s sustainability and mobility. Lastly, this thesis could serve as an inspiration for other relevant
projects in Frederiksberg Municipality or similar projects in other major Danish cities.

mobilitetsundersøgelse af Roskildevej på Valby Bakke. Frederiksberg Kommune ønsker at få en

andre overvejelser, som I kunne have om området og jeres rolle deri.
