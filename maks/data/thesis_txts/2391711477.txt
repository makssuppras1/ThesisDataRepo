Abstract 
 
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is the most prevalent neurodegenerative disease, most often 
affecting the elderly population. The exact pathomechanism of AD remains unknown, but 
neural degradation is attributed to the formation and accumulation of amyloid plaques, 
consisting of insoluble Aβ40 and Aβ42, and neurofibrillary tangles in the brain. The cause 
of the majority of AD cases is sporadic, but it is believed that a mixture of genetic and 
epigenetic factors predispose for the disease. Familial AD, which is very rare, has been linked 
to several mutations in three different genes: APP, PSEN1, and PSEN2. Allele variants of 
the gene APOE have further shown to influence the risk of developing AD, with the genotype 
APOE ε2/ε2 conferring the lowest risk, APOE ε3/ε3 having a neutral risk, and APOE ε4/ε4 
yielding the highest risk. 
Isogenic induced pluripotent stem cell (iPSC) lines with four different APOE genotypes, 
as well as an APOE KO line, and mature neurons derived from these iPSC lines were used 
as an AD disease model in this study. Mature neurons were generated through a neural 
progenitor cell intermediate or by using an NGN2 overexpression system. Novel genetically 
engineered isogenic iPSC lines were created by inserting the NGN2 gene and the trans-
activator M2rtTA into iPSC lines with an APOE ε3/ε3 and an APOE ε4/ε4 genotype. 
Moreover, the AD-related mutation L150P was inserted into the PSEN1 gene in iPSC lines 
with an APOE ε3/ε3 genotype and an APOE KO line. All five APOE cell lines were 
characterized for mitochondrial respiration, which has been shown to be impaired in AD. A 
trend in the oxygen consumption rate of the tested cell lines was observed, with APOE ε4/ε4 
having much maximum than APOE ε2/ε2. Expression of ApoE as well as Aβ40 and Aβ42 
in iPSCs and iPSC-derived neurons was examined. Results showed production of ApoE in 
iPSCs but none in mature neurons. ApoE-containing supernatant was used to influence Aβ40 
and Aβ42 production in iPSC derived neurons. Preliminary results, backed by other 
published works, show that ApoE4-containing supernatant has a bigger influence on Aβ 
production in neurons than ApoE2-containing supernatant.

og den rammer oftest den ældre del af befolkningen. Det er stadig uvist, hvad årsagen til 
AD er, men dannelse og akkumulering af fibrillær amyloidplak bestående af Aβ40 og Aβ42

1. INTRODUCTION

1. Introduction 
 
Over the last hundred years, the lifespan of the average person has increased immensely. 
This is due to changes in our lifestyle, work profile, access to better food and living 
conditions, and maybe most of all, breakthroughs in medical science and treatment. Diseases 
that were previously the cause of death for the majority of the population in the first world 
are now treatable or manageable with the advance of modern medicine. Examples include 
infectious diseases, type 1 diabetes, certain types of cancers, and heart disease. This increase 
in the average lifespan of a person has opened the door for many age-related diseases, such 
as hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and dementia (Kesteloot, 2004). A lot of research in the 
field of medicine and biotechnology has therefore focused on studying and finding methods 
of treatment for these more and more prevalent diseases. 
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is the most prevalent neurodegenerative disease causing 60–70 
% of all dementia-related diseases. As people live longer, the risk of getting AD increases, 
and about six percent of the general population over 65 years of age develop the disease 
(Burns & Iliffe, 2009). Since there is no curative treatment, and clear understanding 
regarding the pathomechanisms causing the disease have not been fully uncovered, there is 
a big incentive to study the disease and find a cure for it. To achieve this, disease models 
have been created using molecular biology and gene engineering in model organisms. These 
disease models are studied in order to find possible links between certain genes and mutations 
and a disease phenotype. Mutations in the genes APP, PSEN1, and PSEN2 have already 
been attributed to cause familial AD, and research shows that the allele variant ε4 of the 
APOE gene may also be a genetic risk factor for developing AD (Mahley et al., 2006). A lot 
of research has therefore been conducted trying to establish connections between these, and 
possibly other, genes to unravel the disease mechanisms of AD. 
This thesis is based on experiments using BIONi010-C induced pluripotent stem cell 
(iPSC) lines and iPSC-derived neurons as disease models for Alzheimer’s disease. Five 
BIONi010-C cell lines carrying different APOE allele variant were used. Existing isogenic 
iPSC lines were differentiated to mature neurons and used for phenotypic studies. Mature 
neurons were generated through a neural progenitor cell intermediate or by overexpressing 
the NGN2 gene in iPSCs. Genetically engineered cell lines were created as well for use in 
further research. 
Part of this project has been a direct continuation from the thesis work conducted by 
fellow Bioneer Master student Kennie R. Prehn (Prehn, 2017).

2.1 Alzheimer’s disease 
Alzheimer's disease (AD) is the most prevalent neurodegenerative disease affecting about six 
percent of the general population over 65 years of age (Blennow et al., 2006). This is when 
the onset of the disease usually presents; however, cases of early-onset AD can also occur for 
people in their forties (Harvey, 2003). AD usually presents itself with common signs of 
dementia, with patients having memory problems or getting confused more easily. These 
symptoms accelerate and get worse as the disease progresses and may include disorientation, 
changes in personality and mood, and problems with sleep and language (Blennow et al., 
2006). These symptoms are not only debilitating to the patients, but also affect their families 
and close relations. Diagnosis of the disease is based on behavioral analysis and the medical 
history of the patient combined with imaging techniques such as CT, MRI, or PET scans. 
These imaging techniques are more frequently used to exclude other neurological causes and 
are not used as definitive diagnostic tools to confirm AD (Schroeter et al., 2009). Diagnosis 
can only be completely confirmed post-mortem by examining tissue samples from the brain 
of the deceased. So far, no effective treatment option has been developed to cure AD, 
although many drug candidates and other treatments have been used to treat symptoms or 
are used in palliative treatment (Blennow et al., 2006). 
The neuropathology of the disease is characterized by neuronal degeneration and loss of 
synaptic connections in the cerebral cortex and other subcortical areas (Blennow et al., 
2006). Loss and death of the neurons is attributed to the presence of amyloid plaques and 
neurofibrillary tangles, which accumulate around and in the neurons (Figure 1). These 
plaques and tangles are visible when examining post-mortem brain tissue microscopically. 
Amyloid plaques are caused by insoluble amyloid beta (Aβ), a peptide derived from the 
amyloid precursor protein (APP). Depending on where the APP is cut, Aβ of different sizes 
can be produced. Aβ40 and Aβ42 are the fragments mostly associated with AD and can both 
be present in amyloid plaques (Masters et al., 1985). Neurofibrillary tangles are caused by 
accumulation of hyperphosphorylated microtubule-associated protein tau.

The overall etiology of AD in not well understood, even though the disease has been 
researched quite intensely and many different theories have been proposed. Some hypotheses 
include hypertension, infection, nutrition history, neurological injury, damaged 
mitochondria, or other epigenetic factors, even though the most likely cause is genetic (Burns 
& Iliffe, 2009). Alzheimer’s disease can be divided into four different types depending on the 
onset of the disease and whether or not it is sporadic or familial. The most prevalent type 
of AD is sporadic late-onset Alzheimer’s disease (LOAD), affecting patients of 65 years and 
above. The etiology of this type of AD is mostly unknown, but include some of the factors 
listed above. The second most prevalent type, affecting about 5 % of the general population, 
is early-onset Alzheimer’s disease (EOAD). Patients with this type of AD manifest the 
disease earlier than 65 years of age, sometimes even in their thirties or forties (Zhu et al., 
2015). The etiology for this type of AD is also fairly unknown. The most uncommon type of 
AD is early onset familial Alzheimer's disease (EOFAD). This type of AD is different from 
sporadic LOAD and EOAD in the regard that the patients inherit the disease in an

autosomal dominant manner. Three genes are attributed to cause this hereditary type of 
AD: amyloid precursor protein (APP), presenilin 1 (PSEN1), and presenilin 2 (PSEN2). 
Presenilin proteins form the sub-component of the γ-secretase responsible for cutting the 
APP, yielding Aβ40 and Aβ42 (Figure 2) (Buckinham, 2003). Many different mutations 
contributing to EOFAD have been uncovered in these three genes (Bird, 2012). All 
abovementioned types of AD have the same pathophysiological mechanisms and manifest in 
the same way. However, knowledge from studying the genes linked to EOFAD is used in 
trying to study the sporadic types of AD, and a lot of scientific effort is being put into 
researching these genes and their mutations.

2.1.1 Apolipoprotein E 
In addition to the link between APP, PSEN1, and PSEN2 and AD, the gene coding for 
apolipoprotein E (APOE) has been found to pose a predominant risk for the disease. The 
protein apolipoprotein E (ApoE) normally has a role in cholesterol and triglyceride 
metabolism in the brain and peripheral tissues (Kim et al., 2009). ApoE is produced

2. THEORY
 
6 
primarily by astrocytes in the brain and peripherally by the liver, but it may also be 
expressed and secreted by stem cells (Harris et al., 2004). ApoE works by transporting 
cholesterol into neurons via the ApoE receptor, and thus functions as the main supplier of 
cholesterol to the neuronal tissues (Mahley, 2016). APOE is a polymorphic gene, and three 
different alleles exist in humans: ε2, ε3, and ε4 (Figure 3). 
 
 
Figure 3. Representation of the protein ApoE. Receptor binding takes place between amino acid 136 
and 150 and lipid binding takes place between amino acid 244 and 272. The isoform-specific

ApoE2 has a cysteine on position 112 and 158 and occurs in about eight percent of the 
general population, whereas only four percent of AD patient carry the allele. It is therefore 
believed that this allele variant gives a reduced risk of developing AD. ApoE3 has a cysteine 
on position 112 and an arginine on position 158 and is found in about 78 % of the general 
population and 60 % of patients with AD (Liu et al., 2013). This allele variant is defined as 
neutral. ApoE4 has arginines on both positions 112 and 158 and occurs in about 14 % of the 
general population and 37 % of patients with AD. This is the allele variant which has been 
found to increase the risk of developing AD while also giving increased risk of developing 
other pathologies, such as cardiovascular disease (Lopez et al., 2014). However, it is not 
certain that a person with the ε4 allele will develop AD, just as the ε2 allele does not confer 
protection against the disease. A possible pathomechanism of the APOE ε4 variant might 
be an internal domain interaction (Chen et al., 2011). The domain interaction is caused by 
Arg-112 and mediates the production of a salt bridge between Arg-61 and Glu-255. This 
changes the conformation of the protein and it may thus interact differently with its 
biological targets. Another theory is that the different ApoE variants contribute to the 
clearance of Aβ to certain degrees in the brain, ApoE2 clearing most Aβ and ApoE4 clearing 
very little Aβ (Verghese et al., 2013). So far, many other theories have been proposed to link

2.2 Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells 
Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) are cells that are able to be generated from already 
fully matured cells. Work with stem cells has been conducted since the 1960’ies, but up until 
the breakthrough from Shinya Yamanaka’s research group in 2006, it had been very tedious 
to consistently produce pluripotent stem cells for research (Takahashi & Yamanaka, 2006). 
The generation of iPSCs is mostly done using fibroblasts and can be accomplished by 
introducing expression of specific genes encoding transcription factors, which then reprogram 
the mature fibroblasts to convert back to the stem cell stage. As few as four genes coding 
for different transcription factors can revert mature cells into iPSCs. These transcription 
factors are Oct4, Sox2, c-Myc, and Klf4 (Takahashi et al., 2007). Other factors have since 
been found to be able to reprogram the cells as well. The transcription factors are often 
introduced using viruses or plasmids (Yu et al., 2007). 
Pluripotent stem cells have been thought to make a big impact in research and 
regenerative medicine because the cells are able to propagate and be kept in cell culture 
indefinitely. Furthermore, they can be differentiated into every possible somatic cell type, 
e.g. cardiomyocytes (Lian et al., 2012), hepatocytes (Chen et al., 2012), and neurons 
(Chambers et. al., 2009; Zhang et al., 2013). This opens up for the possibility of generating 
tissue transplants without risk of rejecting the transplant. An advantage by using iPSCs, 
compared to ESCs, is that iPSCs can be derived from patients, making it possible to generate 
patient-specific iPSCs and culture tissue in order to study genetic diseases from certain 
patient groups (Grskovic et al., 2011). This also does not pose ethical problems, since the 
stem cells are generated from adult tissues and are not harvested from embryos. 
So far, iPSC technology is not widely used in regenerative medicine, but a lot of research 
in cell biology and disease phenotypes has been conducted. The pathomechanisms related to 
AD can e.g. be studied by using disease-specific cell models, where iPSCs are differentiated 
into neurons and astrocytes and then characterized. iPSC lines can be generated using 
fibroblasts from AD patients in order to uncover AD-related genes and mutations (Freude 
et al., 2014). Additionally, AD-related mutations can be introduced in healthy cells to try 
and induce a diseased phenotype (Seah et al., 2015). Such iPSC-derived neuronal culture 
can further be used to test novel pharmaceutical treatments tailored against AD (Grskovic 
et al., 2011; Figure 4).

2.3 Neuronal differentiation 
A normal way of studying a genetic mutation is by introducing the mutation to a set of cells 
and studying the phenotype of the cells. However, some genes are only active in certain cell 
types. The same can be postulated for a certain pathomechanism that might not be present 
in the wrong cell type. In order to study the function of a certain gene, the specific cell type 
relevant for the disease needs to be obtained. Human neural cultures seem to be the best 
basis to studying AD. However, primary neural human cultures are almost inaccessible. 
Therefore, neurons have to be procured in other ways. The development of induced

2.3.1 Induction of neuronal progenitor cells and mature neurons using the SMAD signaling 
pathway 
iPSCs can be differentiated to neural progenitor cells (NPCs), which are the intermediate 
step to producing mature neurons and astrocytes. The paper from Chambers et al. (2009) 
describes a highly efficient method of converting of human ESC and iPSC to neuronal cells 
by using dual inhibition of SMAD signaling. The initial inhibition of SMAD signaling uses 
SB431542 and Noggin, two signaling inhibitors of the SMAD pathway. This inhibition results 
in efficient and rapid production of NPCs, which can be used for further differentiation 
(D’Aiuto et al., 2014). The NPCs show clear change in morphology compared to iPSCs and 
also begin expression of several NPC-associated genes, such as SOX1, PAX6, and NESTIN 
(Valenzuela et al., 1995). Following this, neurons or astrocytes may be produced depending 
on the differentiation factors used to further induce the NPCs (Shi et al., 2012). Examination 
of neuron-specific expressed genes, such as TUJ1, or transcription factors such as TBR1, 
CTIP2, or Satb2, show maturation into adult neurons (Shi et al., 2012). 
To study brain-related diseases, both mature neurons and astrocytes are important to be 
studied, since neurons are very much dependent of the supportive function of astrocytes in 
the brain. Astrocytes have, other than providing nutrients and protection, been found to 
impact differentiation processes of neurons in the brain (Song et al., 2002). Since astrocytes 
are the main producer of ApoE in brain tissue, they are also relevant to study in order to 
understand the interplay of ApoE and how it affects AD risk. 
 
2.3.2 Rapid single-step doxycycline-dependent induction of functional neurons from iPSCs 
using reverse tetracycline-controlled trans-activator to overexpress inserted NGN2 gene 
Other than the differentiation process described above, scientists investigated other ways to 
produce mature neurons more quickly and efficiently. By overexpression of certain neuronal 
genes, it was shown that somatic cells could be directly transdifferentiated to neurons 
(Vierbuchen et al., 2010). Zhang et al. (2013) presented a way where stem cells were induced 
to functional neurons by overexpressing the gene NGN2 coding for neurogenin-2. Both 
embryonic stem cells (ESC) and iPSCs were demonstrated to be inducible in the study. A 
reverse tetracycline-controlled trans-activator (rtTA) dependent on doxycycline (Dox) was 
used as means of overexpressing NGN2. Furthermore, it was shown that the neuronal yield 
and purity approached 100 %, meaning that very few cells differentiated into other non-
specific cell types.  
ESCs or iPSCs in the study were transduced with a lentivirus constitutively expressing a 
rtTA and either one extra virus expressing an Ngn2+P2A+EGFP+T2A+puromycin fusion

2.4 TALENs 
Several molecular mechanisms can be used to edit a specific gene in living cells. Transcription 
activator-like effector nucleases (TALENs) are one such mechanism. TALENs are restriction 
enzymes that, when modified, can cut targeted sequences of DNA (Boch, 2011). Any DNA 
sequence can be cut, and together with the ability of a cell to perform homologous 
recombination, use of TALENs yield an excellent tool for editing DNA of interest inside 
cells. 
A typical way of introducing TALENs into cells is by cloning the TALENs into a suitable 
vector and then transfecting the vector carrying the TALENs into the cells. When TALENs 
cuts DNA, double-strand breaks (DSB) are introduced (Miller et al., 2011). By using several 
different TALENs, cuts can be introduced multiple places in the DNA simultaneously. The 
cell can then respond to the breaks in the DNA with different repair mechanisms (Figure 6). 
 
==PAGE:23==

If cuts in two separate chromosomes are introduced, chromosomal rearrangements may occur. DSB: 
double-stranded breaks; HDR: homology-directed recombination; NHEJ: non-homologous end 
joining; Taken from Joung and Sander (2013). 
 
One of these mechanisms is non-homologous end joining (NHEJ). NHEJ ligates DNA directly 
from either side of a DBS. Typically, there is no or at least very little overlap for annealing 
the DNA sequence. Errors such as insertions, deletions, or chromosomal rearrangements are 
therefore often included in the genome after NHEJ (Joung & Sander, 2013). If any such 
errors occur in a gene, the function of the gene can be altered or inactivated. Foreign DNA 
can also be introduced to the genome using NHEJ (Bedell et al., 2012). The foreign DNA 
can be introduced to the cell e.g. by transfecting the cell with a suitable vector. 
The other mechanisms used to repair DSBs is homology directed repair (HDR). HDR can 
be used by the cell when a homologous DNA template is present. HDR can thus also be used 
to introduce exogenous DNA in the genome of a cell (Miller et al., 2011). 
 
 
2.5 CRISPR–Cas9 
CRISPR–Cas9 is a system now commonly used to produce gene engineered cell lines. 
CRISPR stands for clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats, and it was 
==PAGE:24==
2. THEORY
 
12 
originally discovered as a part of the bacterial defense mechanisms against invading 
bacteriophages (Samson et al., 2013). CRISPR-associated (cas) genes encode proteins that 
target and cleave foreign DNA encoded in the CRISPR locus. This process is specific down 
to a single nucleotide. Several CRISPR–Cas systems have been discovered in bacteria and 
can be divided in three types: Type I, Type II, and Type III. Type II systems utilizing the 
protein Cas9 are the systems most frequently used by scientists to genetically modify cells 
(Rath et al., 2015).  
The mechanisms by which CRISPR–Cas functions can be divided in three general phases: 
adaptation, generation of CRISPR RNA (crRNA), and interference (Figure 7). 
 
 
Figure 7. Overview of the mode of action of the CRISPR–Cas system type II. Taken from Samson 
et al. (2013). 
 
Adaptation is the process where foreign bacteriophage or vector DNA is incorporated into 
the CRISPR locus. This foreign DNA, termed spacer DNA, is separated by short sequences 
of direct repeats which are duplicated each time when spacer DNA is incorporated (Bolotin 
et al., 2005). The DNA located in the invading bacteriophage or other vector DNA 
corresponding to the spacer DNA is called protospacer DNA. In type I and II CRISPR–Cas 
systems, it is important that the protospacer DNA is immediately adjacent to a sequence 
stretch dubbed the protospacer adjacent motive (PAM) in order to be incorporated in the 
CRISPR locus. Generation of crRNA is the process where the CRISPR locus is transcribed 
into one primary pre-crRNA transcript. After processing, the primary transcript produces 
several shorter crRNAs, which each comprise repeat and spacer DNA. Together with the 
==PAGE:25==
2. THEORY
 
13 
translated Cas proteins, CRISPR–Cas complexes are assembled in the cell, which can then 
recognize sequences complementary to the spacer DNA associated with the CRISPR–Cas 
complex. Interference takes place upon recognition of a complementary protospacer sequence 
(Marraffini & Sontheimer, 2010). The sequence is cut specifically by nuclease activity 
conferred by the CRISPR-Cas system and the invading DNA can be inactivated. 
The CRISPR–Cas9 system used to gene engineer cell lines takes advance of the system 
described above. Plasmids can be constructed containing the Cas9 protein, single guide RNA 
(sgRNA), and repair template DNA needed to introduce specific changes in target DNA. 
The sgRNA consists of the crRNA, needed for recognizing the target DNA sequence in the 
genome, and of trans-activating crRNA (tracrRNA), which binds to the crRNA and forms 
an active complex necessary to bind to the Cas9 protein (Figure 8). Upon expression of the 
system in the cell, the targeted DNA sequence is cleaved by nuclease activity of the Cas9 
complex. The repair template may then be integrated into the genome using homologous 
recombination resulting in the sought genomic change. Using CRISPR–Cas systems, gene 
editing has been performed in both prokaryotic and eukaryotic organisms (Ledford, 2016).

2.6 Mitochondrial respiration 
Mitochondria take part in and regulate the energy metabolism of the cell and also contribute 
to cellular death. Mitochondrial dysfunction and oxidative stress has previously been linked 
to the pathophysiology of AD (Moreira et al., 2010). Research has shown that oxidative 
stress can be present in many brain regions and that oxidative damage can take place before 
amyloid plaque formation. Mitochondrial function may therefore very well be a crucial aspect 
in the etiology of AD. 
One way to study mitochondrial respiration is to measure the oxygen consumption of the 
cell medium. Hereby, parameters of mitochondrial function may be studied. These 
parameters include basal respiration, production of ATP, proton leakage, maximal 
respiration, and spare respiratory capacity (Brand & Nicholls, 2009). A method to examine 
mitochondrial respiration in intact and functional cells is by using the Seahorse XFe 
Extracellular Flux Analyzer (Seahorse Bioscience, 2016). This apparatus allows for real time 
analysis of the metabolic flux in the mitochondria, registering oxygen consumption rate 
(OCR) and proton flux. Since the cells are still intact and viable at the time of the 
experiment, the measurements are more precise than previous methods using isolated 
mitochondria (Brands & Nicholls, 2009). The Seahorse XFe Extracellular Flux Analyzer 
further allows for injection of four different reagents that can change metabolic function in 
the cells, and cellular responses can therefore be registered in real time (Figure 9). After 
monitoring the basal respiration, an injection of oligomycin into the system can inhibit ATP 
synthase (complex V), thus lowering the OCR, allowing only for proton leakage in the 
mitochondria.

4.2.1 Thawing of iPSCs 
Culture plates were first coated with a Matrigel solution and incubated at 37 °C and 5.0 % 
CO2 for at least 15 minutes. Induced pluripotent stem cells stored in a cryovial were thawed

4.2.2 Maintenance of iPSCs 
Following the thawing of iPSCs, the cells were kept in the same culture dish for three to five 
days at a time. Culture medium was exchanged daily after visually inspecting the color and 
turbidity of the medium, and after inspecting the cells under the microscope for confluency, 
morphology, and contamination. After reaching the optimal confluency of about 80–90 %, 
the cells were propagated into a new culture dish coated with Matrigel dilution. Propagation 
of iPSCs was done in two different ways: either using Accutase or EDTA as the cell 
detachment agent.

minutes at 37 °C and 5.0 % CO2 until cell detachment was visible under the microscope. 
Accutase-treated cells were supplied with equal amounts of FBS-containing medium to 
inhibit the Accutase. The detached cells were transferred to either a 2 mL Eppendorf tube

5.2.6 Assessment of mitochondrial respiration in iPSCs and neurons 
The object of the following experiments was to assess mitochondrial respiration in iPSC lines 
and induced neurons by measuring the oxygen consumption rate in the mitochondria. It was 
especially interesting to assess whether the effect found by Prehn (2017) in his neurons could 
also be detected in iPSCs or if it was a neuron-specific effect  
Three separate experiments were performed, using iPSCs of all BIONi010-C cell lines, 
induced Diff.D2 neurons from the cell lines BIONi010-C6 APOE ε2/ε2 + NGN2, BIONi010-
C4 APOE ε4/ε4 + NGN2, and BIONi010-C3 APOE KO + NGN2, and induced Diff.D3 
neurons with the same three cell lines as before, but having been treated with iPSC 
supernatant. All initial cell culture work was performed at Bioneer A/S, whereas the OCR 
experiments were performed by Blanca I. Aldana at the Department of Drug Design and 
Pharmacology at the University of Copenhagen. The results obtained there are presented in 
this thesis with her permission. 
In short, each OCR experiment commenced with three readings to establish initial basal 
OCR levels. Oligomycin, an inhibitor of ATP synthase, was then added to the cells, followed 
by the addition of FCCP to disrupt ATP synthesis and allow for the measurement of the 
highest possible respiration of the cells. The final addition of the inhibitors of mitochondrial 
complexes I and III, Rotenone and Antimycin A, fully prevent the electron transport chain, 
thus letting OCR levels sink to the absolute lowest non-mitochondrial respiration levels. 
Values from the experiments were standardized against total protein content of the cells.

6. DISCUSSION
 
62 
6. Discussion 
 
This section will highlight the results obtained during this project and will discuss them by 
comparing them to other published work and by critically assessing the validity of the 
results. 
 
6.1 Generation of novel gene-edited cell lines with NGN2 system or PSEN1 L150P 
One part of this project was to genetically engineer novel cells lines, which could be used in 
future projects. 
Using TALENs, the cell lines BIONi010-C2 APOE ε3/ε3 and BIONi010-C4 APOE ε4/ε4 
had the gene NGN2 and the trans-activator M2rtTA inserted into the safe locus AAVS1 in 
the genome. The cells were isolated in pure culture and cryopreserved. The nucleofection of 
BIONi010-C2 APOE ε3/ε3 with the plasmid carrying the M2rtTA proved to happen at a 
very low yield, needing multiple rounds of nucleofection in order to yield positive clones. 
Nonetheless, positive clones, which could be induced to mature neurons upon induction with 
Dox were generated. None of the novel positive NGN2 clones were submitted to quality 
control analyses upon isolation in pure culture. This will have to be done eventually, before 
the novel clones can be used in research. These analyses can include RT-qPCR and 
immunohistological analysis to examine for mature neuronal markers in the induced neurons. 
Using CRISPR–Cas9 technology, cell lines BIONi010-C2 APOE ε3/ε3 and BIONi010-C3 
APOE KO had the AD-related mutation L150P inserted in the gene PSEN1. These two cell 
lines, together with cell lines BIONi010-C APOE ε3/ε4 (WT), BIONi010-C6 APOE ε2/ε2, 
and BIONi010-C4 APOE ε4/ε4 nucleofected and selected earlier (Prehn, 2017), were 
cultured in pure culture and cryopreserved. Nucleofection with the CRISPR–Cas9 system 
happened at a low yield, but produced enough positive to ensure enough candidates for the 
screening process. Novel genetically engineered PSEN1 L150P cell lines had the PSEN1 gene 
DNA sequenced in order to verify that the mutation had been inserted in the correct position. 
All five clones showed to have the mutant version of the gene. However, other QC analyses 
were not completed. 
 
 
6.2 Neuronal differentiation and rapid single-step neuronal induction 
Mature neurons used for the experimental part of this project were produced using the neural 
differentiation protocol developed by Shi et al. (2012), using NPCs as an intermediate step

63 
between iPSCs and mature neurons, or the single-step NGN2 overexpression induction 
process developed by Zhang et al. (2013). Some of the neurons used for this study were based 
on differentiations produced previously (Prehn, 2017) and these neurons were analyzed for 
mature neuronal markers using immunohistochemical staining and RT-qPCR. The analysis 
showed expression of the neuronal marker Tuj1 or transcription factors CTIP2, TBR1, and 
Satb2 (Prehn, 2017). The same analysis was not carried out in this project, since the protocol 
was considered established. 
The same can be stated about the induced neurons produced using the NGN2 
overexpression system. No induced neurons were tested for maturation markers or pure 
neuronal markers, since this was already done and established earlier (Prehn, 2017). 
Furthermore, the purity and maturity of the neurons using this induction method has 
previously proven to approach 100 % (Zhang et al., 2013). 
The only selection criteria used to choose neurons prior to experimental use, was a 
thorough evaluation of the neuronal morphology by microscopy. Only neurons in pristine 
morphological condition were used. 
 
 
6.3 Chromosomal analysis of previously engineered cell lines with NGN2 
overexpression system 
The iPSC lines BIONi010-C6 APOE ε2/ε2, BIONi010-C4 APOE ε4/ε4, and BIONi010-C3 
APOE KO with the NGN2 overexpression system had previously been genetically engineered 
(Prehn, 2017). These clones were subjected to chromosomal analysis in order to verify that 
no chromosomal alterations were present in the engineered cell lines. Chromosomal analysis 
showed a normal male (XY) karyotype for all three tested cell lines. Even though no 
structural or numerical abnormalities were found when analyzing the chromosomes, the 
analysis did not include small genetic abnormalities such as minor deletions or other 
molecular changes. The lines BIONi010-C6 APOE ε2/ε2 and BIONi010-C3 APOE KO were 
used for the experiments in this project, but BIONi010-C4 APOE ε4/ε4 was discarded after 
suspicion of unspecific gene modification. This was due to the observation that the cells grew 
faster compared to the other cell lines. 
 
 
6.4 Quantification of ApoE in supernatant from iPSC and neurons 
Supernatant from iPSCs and neurons was analyzed for ApoE content. The iPSC supernatant 
would be used in later experiments and was therefore aliquoted and stored at –80 °C. Results 
==PAGE:76==
6. DISCUSSION
 
64 
from the ApoE ELISA assay showed that quantifiable amounts of ApoE was present in 
supernatant from APOE ε2/ε2 and APOE ε4/ε4 iPSCs, whereas APOE KO iPSC showed 
no detectable ApoE. This served as a good control to prove that no ApoE is indeed produced 
in the KO. Results from the Diff.J13 neurons showed that minute amounts of ApoE was 
present in the supernatant, but the absorption levels were in the lowest part of the limit of 
detection and may thus be attributed as background. These analyses could only detect ApoE, 
which was excreted in the supernatant, and did not ensure detection of ApoE still inside the 
cells. 
Treatment of neurons with ApoE containing medium was based on the experiments from 
Huang et al. (2017), who showed that different APOE genotypes can trigger the release of 
Aβ. However, Huang et al. used supernatant from glial cells which were not available in this 
study. Supernatant from iPSCs was chosen, because these cells have showed to yield a higher 
expression of ApoE. The initial plan was to use supernatant from iPSC-derived astrocytes, 
as these cells are the main source of ApoE in the brain. Therefore, iPSCs were previously 
differentiated to astrocytes using a commercially available kit from Stemgent (Prehn, 2017). 
However, the differentiated astrocytes did not show expression of ApoE (data not shown). 
As such, supernatant from iPSCs was chosen. It is important to state that the concentration 
of ApoE was 1,000 times lower compared to the concentration Huang et al. had used in their 
study. Further, the posttranslational modifications of ApoE in iPSCs can differ from those 
in astrocytes. In future studies, a better source for supernatants could be chosen; ideally 
from glial cells with higher amounts of ApoE. 
 
 
6.5 Quantification of Aβ40 and Aβ42 in protein lysate from neurons 
Neurons from both established differentiation strategies were analyzed for Aβ40 and Aβ42 
production in this study. The experiments were performed in order to compare results with 
the recently published research from Huang et al. (2017) showing that ApoE very likely has 
a role in the production of Aβ in the brain. In more detail, the study describes how the 
binding of ApoE to the ApoE receptor activates the dual leucine-zipper kinase, resulting in 
the activation of MKK7 and ERK1/2 MAP kinases (Huang et al., 2017). cFos is 
phosphorylated upon ERK1/2 activation, inducing the transcription factor AP-1, which then 
enhances the transcription of APP and increases the production of Aβ. The final production 
of Aβ was dependent on the ApoE variant, and the study showed that supernatant with 
ApoE4 resulted in a higher production of Aβ than supernatant with ApoE3, which in turn 
stimulated more than ApoE2 supernatant. Since Aβ accumulation in the brain causes the 
==PAGE:77==
6. DISCUSSION
 
65 
amyloid plaques ultimately leading to neural death, it can be claimed that ApoE4 is a larger 
risk factor for AD than ApoE2 and ApoE3. 
Two separate experiments were performed in this study in order to replicate the findings 
from Huang et al. However, conclusive results could not be achieved from either of the 
experiments. The first experiment using Diff.J9 neurons had used ApoE-containing 
supernatant from iPSCs, but the ApoE content in the supernatant had not been measured. 
Only the density of the iPSCs was standardized. It was therefore uncertain, whether or not 
the resulting production of Aβ40 and Aβ42 in the neurons was due to the ApoE variant or 
due to different amounts of ApoE in the supernatant. The second experiment using Diff.D7 
neurons did quantify the amounts of ApoE in the supernatant, but the concentration used 
for the experiments was 1,000-fold less than the amount used by Huang et al. to treat the 
neurons. The Huang et al. paper stated that they used 10 µg/mL, whereas only 10 ng/mL 
was available in the iPSC supernatant harvested in this study. It is possible that this 
concentration would not induce any difference in the production of Aβ. Furthermore, the 
data collected from the Diff.D7 experiments had too much variation in the data in other to 
be statistically significant.  
Even though no conclusive results could be drawn from these experiments, a lot of 
knowledge regarding the execution of the experiment has been acquired and future 
experiments can be planned better. 
 
 
6.6 Detection of mitochondrial chain enzymes in neurons 
Neurons from the Diff.J9 differentiation were used for immunoblotting experiments to detect 
mitochondrial complexes I–V. The experiments were based on the paper from Chen et al. 
(2011), where ApoE4 was shown to reduce the levels of all mitochondrial complexes in 
neurons derived from brain-cortices of neuron-specific enolase promoter-driven transgenic 
mice. The study found no changes in mitochondrial respiration when exposing the same 
neurons to ApoE3 (Chen et al., 2011). The proteins VDAC1 and mtHSP70 were used as 
internal controls and actin was used to normalize the data. The paper postulates that the 
effects of ApoE4 are caused by the ApoE4 domain interaction (see section 2.1.1). It was 
shown that mutant ApoE4 missing the domain interaction did not cause mitochondrial 
dysfunction in neuroblastoma Neuro-2A cells. Furthermore, a small molecule disrupting the 
domain interaction in cells expressing ApoE4 restored mitochondrial respiration (Chen et 
al., 2011).  
The results from the first western blot showed that less mitochondrial complex IV was 
produced in APOE ε4/ε4 neurons compared to the other cell lines. This supports the findings 
==PAGE:78==

66 
presented by Chen et al. (2011). The same tendency was replicated a second time, but the 
quality of the gel image was bad; presumably due to poor proteins transfer from the gel to 
the nitrocellulose membrane under the second western blot. Of all five mitochondrial 
complexes, only complex III and IV were detectable in the western blot. No bands were 
observed at the expected sizes of the other complexes. After re-reading the product 
information sheet of the OXPHOS antibody cocktail, it was discovered that some of the 
complexes would not be detected in the blot if treated at temperatures close to 100 °C during 
sample preparation. A trial run was therefore performed to test for optimal sample 
preparation conditions. The OXPHOS product information sheet indicated that all five 
mitochondrial complexes would be detectable at 50 °C, but only bands of complex III and 
IV were detectable in all tested sample preparation conditions. This could of course allude 
to none of the other complexes being present in the samples, but this is highly unlikable 
since the neurons would not have been viable without all mitochondrial complexes in the 
cells. Due to these technical problems, the experiments will have to be repeated to get 
consistent and valid results. 
 
 
6.7 Assessment of mitochondrial respiration in iPSCs and neurons 
The paper from Chen et al. (2011) also presented results from an oxygen consumption rate 
experiment performed to assess mitochondrial respiratory function. They showed that 
maximal respiratory capacity was lower in Neuro-2A cells producing ApoE4 compared to 
their ApoE3 counterpart.  
The OCR experiments conducted in this study used iPSCs, induced Diff.D2 neurons, and 
induced Diff.D3 neurons treated with supernatant. The results from the iPSC experiments 
were not useable since the cells seemed to be inhibited by either the Oligomycin or FCCP. 
The results from the experiment using Diff.D2 neurons had a clear trend showing that 
neurons with the APOE ε4/ε4 genotype had a significantly lower maximal respiration 
compared to the other tested genotypes. As such, the results from Prehn (2017) and Chen 
et al. (2011) were reproducible in the first experiment using NGN2 differentiated neurons. 
Results from Diff.D3 neurons showed the same trend as previously, but the experiment have 
to be repeated with the new APOE ε4/ε4 clone in order to generate valid data.

7. CONCLUSION
 
67 
7. Conclusion 
 
A lot of effort has been put into unraveling the disease mechanisms of AD and finding a 
cure for this terrible disease. Research in several mutations of the genes APP, PSEN1, and 
PSEN2 has yielded a lot of usable knowledge so far, and iPSC disease models have made it 
easier to study diseased neural tissues in vitro. 
In this study, novel genetically engineered isogenic iPSC lines were created by inserting 
the NGN2 gene and the trans-activator M2rtTA into iPSC lines BIONi010-C2 APOE ε3/ε3 
and BIONi010-C4 APOE ε4/ε4. Moreover, the AD-related mutation L150P was inserted 
into the PSEN1 gene in iPSC lines BIONi010-C2 APOE ε3/ε3 and BIONi010-C3 APOE 
KO. These lines were characterized for mitochondrial respiration, which has been shown to 
be impaired in AD. These characterizations were carried out by oxygen consumption rate 
(OCR) experiments and immunoblotting. Results from the OCR experiments indicate an 
observable trend in the OCR regarding the APOE genotype of the tested cell lines, with 
APOE ε4/ε4 having much lower maximum respiration than APOE ε2/ε2. However, results 
from the immunoblotting experiments show inconclusive results and will therefore have to 
be repeated using more replicates to ensure valid data. Expression of ApoE as well as Aβ40 
and Aβ42 in iPSCs and iPSC-derived neurons was determined using ELISA assays. Results 
showed quantifiable amounts of ApoE in iPSC supernatant from the cell lines with the 
APOE ε2/ε2 and APOE ε4/ε4 genotype. No ApoE was present in the APOE KO nor in 
supernatant from mature neurons. ApoE-containing supernatant was used to influence Aβ40 
and Aβ42 production in iPSC derived neurons. The results did show that ApoE4-containing 
medium may have a bigger influence on neurons than ApoE2-containing medium, but the 
results showed a big variance in the data and will have to be repeated in order to get 
statistically significant results.

8. FUTURE WORK
 
68 
8. Future work 
 
There is still a lot to be learned about Alzheimer’s disease. The experiments performed in 
this study were done in part to replicate results attained by other researchers and to confirm 
the effect of the different ApoE variants on an AD phenotype. Each experiment will have to 
be repeated again to yield statistical significant and valid data. 
Double-mutation studies have been proven to be a powerful tool to investigate disease 
phenotypes. The insertion of the AD-related mutation L150P in the gene PSEN1 in the 
BIONi010-C cell lines could therefore yield an enhanced disease phenotype, which could 
prove to give usable insight into the pathomechanisms of AD. 
Prehn (2017) had succeeded in establishing a protocol for LDH-analysis and CellROX 
studies used to measure mitochondrial reactive oxygen species under conditions of oxidative 
stress in the cell. These experiments were not performed during this project period, but could 
prove to yield intriguing results. 
Astrocyte differentiation protocols could be tested and established in order to be able to 
culture mature astrocytes, either in culture alone or together with neurons. The latter culture 
method would approximate the physiological conditions in the brain, thereby yielding better 
testing conditions. 
Collaborations between Bioneer A/S, universities, and other research institutes have 
already yielded exciting studies related to AD, and it is my belief that much more interesting 
knowledge will surface based on these collaborations.

Preparation of NeuroBasal Medium (NBM) 
This SOP describes the procedure for the preparation of NeuroBasal Medium (NBM). NBM 
is used for mature neurons after neuronal maturation. It is also used for the preparation of 
Neural induction medium (NIM), Neural expansion medium (NEM), and Neural maturation 
medium (NMM).  
1. 
Mix 250 mL of DMEM/F12 with 250 mL Neurobasal medium in a sterile glass bottle 
2. 
Add 5 mL of N2 Supplement 
3. 
Add 10 mL of B27 Supplement 
4. 
Add 5 mL of Glutamax 
==PAGE:89==
10. APPENDIX
 
77 
5. 
Add 500 µL of Penicillin/Streptomycin 
6. 
Keep the medium in the fridge and protect from light 
7. 
Warm up to room temperature prior to use 
 
Preparation of Neural Induction Medium (NIM) 
1. 
Use NBM as the base medium 
2. 
Add SB431542 to a final concentration of 10 µM 
3. 
Add LDN 193189 to a final concentration of 0.1 µM 
 
Preparation of Neural Expansion Medium (NEM) 
NEM is used for the expansion of neural progenitor cells (NPCs).  
1. 
Use NBM as the base medium 
2. 
Add FGF-basic at a final concentration of 10 ng/mL to NBM 
 
Preparation of Neural Maturation Medium (NMM) 
NMM is used for the terminal differentiation of NPCs to cortical neurons. NMM can be 
stored for up to 2 weeks at 4 °C (longer storage not tested). 
1. 
Use NBM as the base medium 
2. 
Add BDNF to a final concentration of 20 ng/mL 
3. 
Add GDNF to a final concentration of 10 ng/mL 
4. 
Add db-cAMP to a final concentration of 50 µM 
5. 
Add L-AA2P to a final concentration of 200 mM 
 
Preparation of Poly-L-Ornithine and Laminin coated plates 
1. 
Add 1 mL of 1:10 or 1:5 diluted PLO (in DPBS) to one well of a 6 well plate and 
incubate either for 4 hours at 37 °C or overnight at 4 °C  plates with PLO can be 
sealed with parafilm and stored in the fridge for later use (one month works, longer 
storage not tested) 
2. 
Remove the PLO and wash 1X with DPBS 
3. 
Add 1 mL of 1:200 or 1:100 diluted Laminin (in DPBS) to the well and incubate 
either for 4 hours at 37 °C or overnight at 4 °C  plates with PLO/Laminin can be 
sealed with parafilm and stored in the fridge for later use (10 days works, longer 
storage not tested) 
4. 
Remove the Laminin – do not wash the plates. Plates are ready to use
