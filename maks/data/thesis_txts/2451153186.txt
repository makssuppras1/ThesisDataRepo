Summary
Shell Eco Marathon is a contest in fuel eﬃcient vehicles. The contest is held in diﬀerent parts of
the world and involve university students competing in how far their vehicles can run on 1 litre of
gasoline. DTU Roadrunners is a group of students from DTU competing in this contest. The team is
highly eﬃcient with their car Dynamo 14.0. Because of the casting mould being worn out the design
of the car is revisited for improvements.
The aerodynamic forces on the car are important for the cars fuel eﬃciency. Computational Fluid
Dynamics are used to simulate the ﬂow over diﬀerent designs of the car.
A Reynolds-Averaged
Navier-Stokes simulation coupled with the k-ω turbulence model is set up to solve the turbulent ﬂow
of the simulation. The simulation is validated against 2 wind tunnel data set from 2 Fagprojekts.
The Fagprojekt from 2011 in which the present design was developed and a Fagprojekt from 2018.
3 individual designs relating to external inﬂuences on the design are simulated. These simulations
are; with a windscreen wiper, with body transitions and a mounted LED strip. The windscreen
wiper with a 10◦yaw angle inlet ﬂow shows a 19.6 % increase of the downforce. The body transition
simulation shows no signiﬁcant change in the drag but a change in the lift of 2.0 %. The cosmetic
LED strip simulation shows a decrease in the downforce by 26.7 % making the LED strip a good
addition to the car.
The adjoint method is used on the car to pinpoint areas with optimization potential. 6 areas are
pinpointed. These are; the camber of the body, the extension of the rear end, wheel fairings, the
transition between the shell and the windscreen, the front wheel case and the inclination from the
bottom to the rear end. The ground clearance is simulated for diﬀerent heights and a height of 140
mm is chosen, because this solution increases the drag by 4.1 % and decreasing the downforce by
36.3 %. The camber of the car is simulated with diﬀerent designs for the new ground clearance. A
camber changed by 140 mm from original is chosen. This new camber decreases the drag by 5.8 %
and increases the downforce by 48.9 %. This makes an overall decrease in drag of 1.9 % and decrease
in downforce of 5.2% when comparing the optimizations of the front and height with the original
design.
A small improvement to the drag and lift, and 5 areas with potential for improvement have been
achieved in this project.

1
Introduction
The Shell Eco Marathon (SEM) is an annual event happening 3 places in the world: North America,
Asia and Europe. The SEM is a contest in fuel eﬃciency. 2 main categories is present at SEM, the
Urban Concept and Prototype category with a set of subcategories for the fuel type. The diﬀerent
fuel subcategories include gasoline, diesel, ethanol, hydrogen and more. All the mileage from the
diﬀerent fuel categories is converted into km/L gasoline. There are diﬀerent rules for the cars to
uphold in the categories. The Urban Concept car have to resemble a normal car, and have to be able
to hold a suitcase among other rules. The Prototype category is more open, without rules for how
the car should look.
1.1
DTU Roadrunners at SEM
The DTU Roadrunners have contested in SEM since 2004 in both the Prototype and the Urban
Concept category on various fuel types with great success. DTU Roadrunners are thus holder of 1
ﬁrst place in the Prototype category in 2009[1] and 10 ﬁrst places in the Urban Concept category
latest in 2018 (Figure 3). Various designs of their cars exist. The current design of DTU Roadrunners
Urban Concept car Figure 2 was designed by a group of students in 2011 and is running on ethanol.
This new design performed better than the old car design, seen in Figure 1, from 2009 giving a
reduction in the cars drag area coeﬃcient of 41 % [4].
Each year a new top have been made, mainly because of rule changes from SEM. Casting a new body
wears on the mould. The old mould is nearly worn out which is why the design of the car is desired
revisited.

1.2
Project Aim
The aim of this project is to study the design of the Urban Concept car and search for improvements
with regards to the aerodynamic design of the car. This is done using Computational Fluid Dynamics
(CFD) to simulate the ﬂow over the ecocar numerically. This tool can be used to search for a better

Governing Theory
3.1
Aerodynamic Forces
A body experiencing a ﬂow will be exposed to 2 forces, the pressure force and the shear force. The
pressure force is acting normal to the body, contributing to the lift and the drag of the body. The
shear force is acting tangentially to the body contributing only to the drag force. The direction of
the forces can be split into the same axis as the coordinate system; the drag, the lift and the side
yaw force.
The drag is the force acting in the parallel line of the ﬂow on the z-axis. The drag is the main
contributor to the aerodynamic forces a body have to overcome to move forwards.
The lift is the force acting upwards normal to the drag. The lift is normally thought of as a positive
addition to the forces of a body. When negative, lift is also known as downforce. This is common in
car designs due to the ability of downforce keeping the car down on the road and traction high. In
air planes positive lift is used to keep an air plane ﬂoating in the air without the use of external force.
Yaw force is the side wind force. This is normally present in crosswinds. The Yaw force is usually
adding mostly to the lift with a small addition to the drag.
Nondimensional force coeﬃcients have been made in order to compare the ﬂow speciﬁcations of a
body. These are the drag coeﬃcient CD, the lift coeﬃcient CL and the yaw coeﬃcient CY shown in
Equations (1) to (3).

3.2
Drag
The sole purpose of a streamlined car is to reduce the drag. The drag force is the force applied by the
wind when it hits the car. Often when the drag is mentioned people only think of the force hitting the
car in the direction parallel to the ground. However the drag can be divided into 5 diﬀerent inputs
to the overall drag of a car. Equation (4)[7] shows the drag components of the total drag.

Here Dpress,sep is the pressure drag, Dskin is the skin friction, Dpress,BL is the pressure drag in the
boundary layer, Dind is the induced drag and Dint is the interference drag[7].
3.2.1
Pressure Drag
The pressure drag is usually the biggest contribution to the overall drag of passenger cars and is
also the ﬁrst area optimized when working with streamlined cars. The pressure drag is inﬂicted by
the pressure diﬀerence happening behind the car. This phenomenon is known as separation. When
the ﬂow separates from the body a lower pressure is left behind. This lower pressure is acting as a
vacuum pulling the car backwards. Inside the vacuum the ﬂow is turbulent, which generates vortices
absorbing additional energy from the ﬂow. The ﬁrst and most important challenge to overcome when
designing a streamlined car is to postpone the separation of the ﬂow.
3.2.2
Viscous Drag
The viscous friction from the boundary layer is usually the highest contribution to the overall drag on
streamlined cars [7]. The viscous friction is a combination of 2 contributions, the skin friction and the
boundary layer pressure drag. The skin friction is caused by the viscosity of the ﬂuid running over the
shell. Because of the no-slip condition on the surface the car is experiencing a small resistance when
running trough the air. The viscous drag contribution is higher, due to the ﬂow staying attached to
the surface of the car longer than usual.
The second contribution of the viscous drag is the boundary layer pressure drag. This pressure drag
is similar to the pressure drag occurring from separation. However this pressure drag is formed due to
the boundary layer thickening as it is formed down the body of the car. This formation is visualized
in a pressure coeﬃcient (Cp) plot. The pressure coeﬃcient is seen to be equal to 1 at the stagnation
point. The Cp is then dropping until the point where the velocity reaches maximum. The Cp will
after reaching maximum increase towards 1 again but because of the boundary layer thickness the
Cp is not able to reach a full pressure recovery resulting in a small wake behind the car.
3.2.3
Induced Drag
Induced drag is the result of lift generated on the car. This can either be upward lift or downward lift
also called downforce. Lift on a car can form vortices around the car due to the pressure diﬀerence
between the ﬂow over and under the car. This is the reason lift is attempted eliminated. Lift however
in the form of downforce can be a necessity in a race to give the tires a better grip on the road and
keep the car on the ground. In a SEM race downforce is undesirable due to the low velocity, when
comparing to other car races.
3.2.4
Interference Drag
Interference drag is caused by changes to the smooth perfect design unreachable in reality. This is
everything from fabrication defaults, stickers, transitions between body parts, holes to car lights and
windscreen wiper. Elimination is not possible, but the interference drag should be minimized. Some
of the inputs to interference drag will be investigated in Section 7.

3.5
Body Modiﬁcations
3.5.1
Ground Clearance
A big part of a streamlined vehicle is the shape. Some of the shapes with the lowest drag assembles
airfoils. Airfoils perform well in free air due to having no net lift, resulting in no induced drag. This
is because the ﬂow is symmetric on the top and bottom of the airfoil. When the airfoil is moved closer
to the ground the air under the airfoil will move at a higher local velocity than the ﬂow over the top.
This is causing a lower pressure on the bottom of the airfoil also known as the venturi eﬀect. The
lower pressure on the bottom of the airfoil is then producing a downforce on the airfoil. The venturi
eﬀect can be countered by cambering the lower part of the airfoil. If the right camber is applied
the ﬂow is able to move under the airfoil without generating a higher velocity. Figure 6 shows an
illustration of how camber works on an airfoil.
Figure 6: Illustration of camber on airfoil
The camber and ground clearance are dependent on each other. If the ground clearance increases,
the lower the camber have to be and vice versa.

3.6
Computational Fluid Dynamics
Fluid dynamics is a study of motion and is based on the three conservation laws: conservation of
mass, conservation of linear momentum and conservation of energy. These 3 laws are also known
as the continuity equation, Newtons second law of motion and the ﬁrst law of thermodynamics.

equations signiﬁcantly. The temperature is assumed constant for the simulation, which remove the

3.6.1
RANS
4 possible ways in CFD to solve the Navier-Stokes equations exist. These are the Direct Numerical
Simulation (DNS), Large Eddy Simulation (LES), Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) and
Detached Eddy Simulation (DES). The ﬁrst, DNS, solves the full unsteady Navier-Stokes equations.
This method require incredible amounts of computational power, which is why only a few in the
world are able to solve a ﬂow with DNS. The second, LES, solves the ﬂow in the same way as DNS
but requires a much coarser mesh. Because of this only large eddies can be captured by this model.
The third, RANS, is the most commonly used model as it works with a good compromise between
computational power needed and accuracy of the solution. The fourth, DES, is a hybrid model using
the LES great accuracy away from the surface and the RANS accuracy, at lower computational cost,
at the surface. LES requires more computational power than RANS, but should be more accurate
for problems involving both surface ﬂow and ﬂow away from the surface.
RANS is chosen as the model used in this project because of it’s great ability to be cost eﬀective and
accurate.
RANS is decomposing each of the solution variables in to an averaged and ﬂuctuating component,
φ = ¯φ + φ′ [16], where φ represents any variable being velocity, pressure or energy.
Using the RANS averaging changes the simpliﬁed Navier-Stokes equations to Equation (11)

K - ω Turbulence Model
Three eddy viscosity models exists in Star-CCM+. These are the Spalart-Allmares model, the K-ϵ
model and the K-ω model. The extensive work done in [9] has shown that the K-ω model is best suited
for modelling eddies of automotive simulations, hence the K-ω model is chosen for the simulations.
The K-ω turbulence model solves 2 equations to determine the turbulent eddy viscosity. These are the
turbulent kinetic energy k and the speciﬁc dissipation rate ω. The biggest ﬂaw of the standard K-ω

3.7
Adjoint Method
Automatic tools are needed when designing a car. This is in order to get a tool which are able to show
and optimize a mesh for a given parameter, without simulating a great number of iterations. A such
tool is the adjoint solver. The adjoint solver is providing direct instructions to the areas which have
optimization potential via cost functions for the chosen optimization parameter, here the drag. The
adjoint simulation is simulated after a primal solution have converged. This is because the adjoint
solution is using the gradients of the primal residuals. A converged solution with low residuals is
therefore needed for the adjoint simulation to reach convergence.
The adjoint method have restrictions. Explicit models have to be chosen in order to run an adjoint
simulation. These are:
• Two Dimensional or Three Dimensional
• Steady
• Gas or Liquid
• Coupled Flow
• Ideal Gas or Constant Density
Turbulent or laminar can be chosen, the adjoint analysis is however only valid for inviscid or laminar
ﬂow, turning turbulent viscosity, if chosen, into a passive quantity [16].

Validation Conclusion
The values in Table 8 is diﬀering a lot from the wind tunnel validation values Fagprojekt 11 and 18
[4, 15]. The values from the 2 Fagprojekts also diﬀers a lot from each other. The values validated
against is the values they chose as their ﬁnal values, however a high variation is seen in the test data
of the Fagprojekts. Referring to Table 9 the placement of the car in the wind tunnel can have a big
impact on the measurement results. It is hard to obtain a perfect alignment in a experimental setup
making it highly likely that the car have been misplaced by a small degree in the 2 Fagprojekts which
also account for the diﬀerence in their values. The Fagprojekts do not account for which height the
car has been placed at in the wind tunnel. The height might possible be a lot bigger, than the 1 cm
the car is placed of the ground in the validation. The lift increase as a cambered body moves further
from the ground. A high distance between the wheels and the ground in the wind tunnel might cause
the positive lift measured for both of the Fagprojekts.
The simulation is simulated with a very ﬁne mesh in order to capture all the small diﬀerences. It is
shown that the Y+ value is far below 1 ensuring a ﬁne resolution of the boundary layer. The size of
the domain is large enough and the physical models have been shown in [9] to be the models best
suited for automotive simulations.
Based on all of the above the solution of the validation is deemed plausible.

Conclusion
The windscreen wiper has been simulated for yaw angle 0 and 10◦. A windscreen wiper is making low
drag change for both yaw angles with the highest change being 1.1 % at 0◦yaw angle. This change
can be contributed to the design of the windscreen wiper, which is seen to form horseshoe vortices.
The biggest change is the lift at 10◦yaw angle. The CLA is changing with 19.6 % when the ﬂow
interact with the windscreen wiper from the side. The only way of reducing this lift is to reduce the
size of the windscreen wiper. A ﬂat streamlined design of the windscreen wiper, would give better
ﬂow conditions over the windscreen wiper reducing the impact on the ﬂow.
7.2
Transition of Body Parts
The transitions of the body parts are a place where high interference drag can happen. The car have
to be made in at least 2 parts, which is the upper and lower part of the car. The upper part has to
be split, because easy acces to the engine is needed. The rear end of the lower part is split. This
part is then glued together with the rear end of the upper part to eliminate the transition between
them. This is done to give an easier joint between the rear upper part and then lower part of the
car. This gives 3 parts where interference drag can happen at the transitions. Figure 41 is showing
the car with the 3 transition lines. The ﬁrst transition separates the upper and lower part of the car
in the point of stagnation, the second transition separates the front and back of the car just behind
the middle of the car and the last transition separates the lower rear end from the lower part of the

Adjoint Optimization
The adjoint method has been used in order to give an indication of areas of interest. The adjoint
solver can also be used with the geometry morpher to change the geometry of the car automatically.
This option is not chosen due to the morpher being unstable leading to unsolvable geometries. The
adjoint method is hence only used to show where manual design changes should be done.
8.1
Adjoint Simulation Setup
The simulation is ﬁrst simulated with the segregated ﬂow solver until a converged solution has been
reached. The physic models are hereafter changed to the coupled solver and simulated until con-
vergence has been reached again. The simulation is runned like this in order to obtain a converged
solution. When a converged solution has been reached by the coupled solver the adjoint ﬂow model
can be chosen. A low residual converged solution is important for the adjoint ﬂow solver. Figure 49
shows the residuals of the adjoint simulation.
Figure 49: Residuals for the segregated ﬂow solver, coupled ﬂow solver and adjoint solver.
The continuity residuals of the segregated ﬂow solver converge down to 10−7 and the coupled ﬂow
solver converge to 10−9. This is a low convergence which should be good for the adjoint method.
However as can be seen in Figure 50, which is a blow up of the residuals, the residuals of the cost
function only change the ﬁrst 100 iterations. This is because the adjoint solver could not reach a
converged solution after this point. The solution reached is not the most exact solution, however it
can still give an overview of the most critical areas.
The adjoint method is used with a cost function which is based on the drag. The adjoint solution
drag is plotted with respect to continuity in this instance also known as mass conservation. The
values of the plot can be interpreted as following; the positive values indicate areas where removing
mass will lower the drag, and negative values indicate areas where adding mass will lower the drag
of the car.

Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis has been to look for improvements to the DTU Roadrunners Dynamo 14.0.
The aerodynamic forces have a large inﬂuence on the joint forces the car engine have to overcome.
In order to study the aerodynamic forces on the car CFD have been used to simulate the ﬂow over
the car. This has been done in phases.
Validation
First the simulation have been set up using the work done in the master thesis by Neus Teixido
[9] and the dissertation by A.Z. Fourie [10]. Teixido compared the diﬀerent turbulent models with
the Ahmed body and concluded that RANS with the SST K-ω turbulent model was best suited for
automotive simulations. Fourie compared the diﬀerent turbulence models with a generic airfoil and
concluded on the same models as Teixido. After the physical models had been chosen, the validation
mesh were set up. Here the inﬂuence of the diﬀerent parameters was simulated for the best result.
This resulted in a meshed domain upholding the recommendations of the Wall Y+ value.
Two wind tunnel data sets were supplied for validation. This was from a Fagprojekt in 2011, wherein
the current design of the car was produced. The other wind tunnel data was produced by a Fagprojekt
in 2018. The CDA and CLA from the validation simulation was compared with the values from the
2 Fagprojekts.
The validation values showed a high diﬀerence for both of the Fagprojekts.
The
highest diﬀerence from the validation was calculated for Fagprojekt 11 CLA at 10◦yaw angle. Here
the diﬀerence was 2842.2 %. In comparison the 10◦yaw angle CLA diﬀerence for Fagprojekt 18
was 1527.5 %. This a huge diﬀerence from the validation which would imply errors in the validation
simulation. A big diﬀerence is however also seen between the experimental values of the 2 Fagprojekts.
Investigating the data the values have a high variance. Both the Fagprojekts have a positive lift on
the car which result in the high diﬀerence, however none of the Fagprojekts mention the height the
car is placed from the ground in the wind tunnel. The ground clearance inﬂuence on lift is shown to
be high which would directly inﬂuence the values of the wind tunnel experiment. A lower diﬀerence
is calculated for the CDA of the validation and Fagprojekt 18. Here it only diﬀers by 34.4 % for 0◦
yaw angle and 43.6 % for 10◦yaw angle. This is still a big diﬀerence, but can be accounted for by the
challenge in placing the car straight in a wind tunnel. On account of these sightings the validation
simulation is deemed representative and valid for the ecocar.
Individual Simulations
3 simulations have been made for 3 cases representing changes made to the design after manufacturing.
These are the addition of a windscreen wiper, the transitions between body parts and the addition
of a cosmetic LED strip to the car.
The windscreen wiper is shown to have no signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the drag and a small inﬂuence
on the lift at 0◦yaw angle. The CDA is changing by 1.1 % and the CLA is changing by 4.1 %. A
bigger change is seen for the lift at a 10◦yaw angle. Here the diﬀerence is 19.6 %. The change in lift
suggests the windscreen wiper needs a new streamlined design.
The 3 transitions of the body parts are simulated. The transitions are causing small vortices, but no
signiﬁcant change to the drag and lift occurs. The transitions are closed which is not the case for the
real car. Here small gabs exists between the transitions. The gabs should be closed by tape in order
to prevent ﬂow to enter the cabin of the car. The last transition could aﬀect the separation of the
boundary layer, if the separation was delayed past the transition.
The last individual simulation made was for a cosmetic LED strip placed on the car. The simulation
showed no signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the drag but a huge improvement to the lift. The CLA was increased
by 26.7 %. This is a positive inﬂuence to the lift, bringing the lift closer to 0. This improvement
might be because the LED strip interfere with the vortices generated on the body. The small vortices
created by the LED strip might also be strong enough to induce positive lift to the car.

simulation unusable. An indication of areas suitable for optimization could however be used. The
Adjoint solution showed high positive values on the camber, start of the windscreen and on the wheel
case of the car. This indicates areas where removing mass would improve the drag. Negative values
existed on the rear of the car, which indicated, addition of mash would improve the drag.
Design Optimizations
On account of the adjoint solution and theoretic knowledge a number of design optimizations was
developed and simulated. The ground clearance was changed and simulated. This was done in order
to look for a ground clearance with great inﬂuence on the drag and lift of the car. The original ground
clearance of the validation simulation is 120 mm. The ground clearance was investigated for 130-190
mm. Increasing the ground clearance showed an increase in both the drag and the lift. The drag was
increasing with a small gradient until a ground clearance of 140 mm. The Lift was increasing with
a bigger positive gradient. The ground clearance of 140 mm was chosen. This gave an increase to
the drag of 4.1 % and to the lift 36.3 %. The increase to the drag is a negative point. However the
increasement in ground clearance, pulls the wheels downwards making room for improvement above
the wheels. This is an area which show optimization potential according to the adjoint solution.
With the ground clearance increased the camber of the car was now simulated. 7 diﬀerent designs
of the camber is made. The designs range from 50 to 230 mm from the original camber. The new
cambers improve the Cp curve of the ﬂow. The spike from the camber is removed and instead a lower
and smoother curve is shown. The camber designs show a decrease in drag but an increase in lift.
The camber design chosen for further improvement was the 140 mm design. This design showed a
decrease in drag of 5.8 % but a decrease in lift of 48.9 %. The 140 design was the best, taking into
account the drag decrease and the lift decrease.
Five additional areas were pinpointed for optimization.
This was the extension of the rear, the
addition of wheel fairings, the incline from the bellypan to the rear end, change to the front wheel
case and change to the transition between the shell and the start of the windscreen. CAD design
and simulations was made for rear extension, the wheel fairing and the bellypan inclination. The
simulations was however obstructed by the divergence of the rear extension simulation. Diﬀerent
simulation parameters was simulated in order to get a stable simulation with no luck. At the point
of this reports deadline no solution had been found. The time used on the rear extension simulation
hindered the production of simulations for the wheel fairing and bellypan simulations without the
rear extension. This also hindered the production of CAD and simulations of the wheel case and
windscreen optimization.
