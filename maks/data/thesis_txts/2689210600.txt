Abstract
People with hearing impairment are commonly challenged when carrying out daily
tasks, like social interaction. Communicating with others involves both auditory
and visual attention. However, people with hearing impairment have diﬃculty in
hearing conversational speech and require the use of vision for purposes other than
those needed by people with healthy hearing.
Visual attention is the ability to
focus consciously on a task, when a person directs their gaze towards the sound
target of interest, which is eventually important for visual information.
Recent
studies have shown that in-ear electrodes capturing electroencephalography (EEG)
in the ear can provide a steering signal, as it estimates the eye gaze relative to
the head, when the head is ﬁxed. Therefore, this thesis focuses to investigate the
brain correlation between target of interest and direction of eye gaze applying ear
EEG using neural networks. The ear EEG and eye gaze data were obtained from
experiments conducted at Eriksholm Research Center prior to this study. During
the experiments, the participants ﬁrst had to focus their visual attention on an LED
target changing regularly, and then follow a conversation. Eye gaze detection may
provide a steering signal for hearing instruments (HI) to enhance sounds from the
direction where the eyes are pointing to and suppress other sounds. This thesis
evaluates the usability of deep learning models to classify eye gaze directions: left,
middle, and right. The achieved accuracies run up to 95.69% using raw data and
100% for ﬁltered data. The models are also optimized to increase their compatibility
with edge devices by lowering numerical precision, alas resulting in lower accuracy.
Deep learning models applied into HIs show promising results to improve the quality
of life for hearing impaired people.
Keywords: Electroencephalography, Electrooculography, Neural Network, Classi-
ﬁcation, Signal Processing, Hearing Instruments, Real Time

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
”To answer a popular question, if we deﬁne sound as traveling ﬂuctuations in air
pressure, then yes, a tree falling in the woods with no one nearby does indeed make a
sound. If we deﬁne sound to be the electrical signal transmitted by the mechanisms
of our inner ears to our brains, then no, that tree falling in the woods makes no
sound.” - Richard G. Lyons [1].
1.1
Background
Our ears guide us in our everyday environment; from warning us against dangers
to letting us to listen to music and communicate with others. The behavior of a
person during a conversation typically involves both auditory and visual attention.
However, hearing-impaired people require the use of vision for purposes other than
those needed by healthy hearing people. Visual attention is the ability to focus
consciously on a task. The person directs their eye gaze towards the sound target
of interest, which is important for visual information. On average, a person makes
two to four eye movements per second to extract new information from their visual
environment. Hence, detection of the gaze may provide a steering signal for future
HIs that is necessary for eﬀective social communication.
Focusing on a speciﬁc speaker when multiple sound sources are present is not easy
- not even with the smart and intelligent HIs we have in the present day. The HIs
today are characterized by remarkably advanced algorithms, which can enhance the
sound perception of the listener. These algorithms consist among others of beam-
formers to obtain signals from a speciﬁc direction, streaming of auditory signals from
remote microphones, and advanced auditory scene analysis to segregate multiple
speakers. However, there is one essential element these complex algorithms cannot
accomplish yet, which is how to navigate and steer the algorithms in situations with
multiple sound sources. One potential method to identify selective auditory atten-
tion is by recording the brain signals retrieved by electrodes positioned in the ear
canal which reﬂects the neural responses in the brain. This is called ear EEG [2].
This could beneﬁt the HI users and improve their listening experience. If a HI can
decode where the user wishes to attend, it will be possible to monitor which sounds
the user want to amplify only by focusing on the source of the sound.
1.1.1
Brain Hearing
Hearing is thinking. Our ears collect the sound, but it is our brain that actually
understands and recognizes it. Good hearing helps our brain stay ﬁt throughout
life and avoid many further health problems. If our brain does not get the sound
information it needs, we will ﬁnd it more challenging to understand what people are
saying and what is happening around us. New research in hearing [3] has shown
that the brain needs access to the whole sound scene in order to work in a natural
way.

1.1
Background
1
INTRODUCTION
Other scientiﬁc discoveries [4] have shown that the subsystems inside the hearing
center work together to help the brain make sense of sound. Figure 1 shows the
steps during sound processing from ear to brain:
Figure 1: The steps during sound processing from ear to brain. The subsystems work
continuously and simultaneously together to ﬁnd and enhance cues that best segregate
sound sources. The sound processing by the brain is a constant interaction between the
subsystems [4].
This process is further described in below:
1. Neural Code includes the most peripheral parts of the auditory system, i.e.
the ear. When sounds reach the inner ear, they convert into a neural code of
information such that the brain can make sense of it. It is then transferred by
the auditory nerve into the hearing center of the brain, which is also known
as the auditory cortex.
2. Orient and Focus occurs mainly within the auditory cortex, a region in the
brain specialized in detecting and separating sounds. The ﬁrst step in this
process is to orient, where the brain scans the whole sound scene to identify
the inputs and establishes an overview of the sound objects around us. If
we dismiss the role of the orientation subsystem, we miss out an important
cue and become less capable of following conversations and understand our
surroundings. The next step is to focus, which identiﬁes the sound we want to
concentrate on, while enhancing the perception of the object of interest and
ﬁltering out irrelevant sounds.
3. Recognize is where the brain makes use of accessible working memory to
identify the sound. At this stage, the brain extracts meaning and controls
complex cognitive processes such as speech understanding, attention switching
and memorization.
This stage also obtains and integrates knowledge from
other senses such as visual input [5].

1.1.3
Electroencephalogram
The brain consists of millions of neurons which play an essential part for controlling
behavior of the human body with regard to internal and external sensory stim-
uli. Electroencephalography (EEG) is an electrophysiological monitoring method
to record electrical activity of the brain. It is a technique that detects the brain’s
electrical activity over a period of time using electrodes placed on the scalp or in
the ears.
The electrodes are entirely located in or around the outer ear for ear
EGG, resulting in a much greater invisibility and wearer ﬂexibility opposite to full
scalp EEG. The major drawback is the signiﬁcantly reduced signal amplitude and
reduction in the amount of brain regions in which activity can be measured. Ear
EEG is a good candidate for inclusion in a hearing device, however, due to the high
complexity of ear EEG sensors, this has not been done yet.
The cells in the brain communicate via electrical impulses and are active all the
time. EEG measures voltage ﬂuctuations resulting from ionic current within the
neurons of the brain. Human behavior can be visualized in terms of sensory states
such as eye movement, lip movement, remembrance, awareness etc. These situations
are associated with speciﬁc signal frequency which helps to identify the functional
behavior of the complex brain structure. This activity is shown as wavy lines on an
EEG recording in ﬁgure 3 [11]:
Figure 3: Scalp EEG and ear EEG sensor with corresponding recordings measured in

and ”4” indicating the exact location [12].

1.2.1
Auditory Attention Identiﬁcation
The ﬁrst description of the term cocktail party eﬀect was by the British scientist,
Colin Cherry, in 1953 [13], where it was used to refer to the challenge of focusing on
a particular sound source, often a speech stream, while suppressing other unwanted
sounds in a noisy and complex background. Cherry performed a series of experiments
in which test subjects listened to two diﬀerent speech signals simultaneously through
both ears from a single loudspeaker. The participants then had to separate the two
sounds, by noting down one of the messages. In the second series of experiments,
the test subjects were presented to diﬀerent speech signals to each ear.
The ability to maintain auditory attention in a cocktail party is one of the capabilities
that the human brain possesses - to tune to a single sound and suppress all others.
In [14], the user was able to focus on one single talker and easily switch attention.
However, the user also overheard interesting information from another talker. The
results showed that the performance in both listening comprehension and target
detection, was reduced signiﬁcantly as the number of simultaneous audio channels
increased.
To distinguish and segregate voices is a well-known challenge for all HIs and their
users, since HIs lack this capability. However, using new technologies, including
advances in deep learning, might bring us closer to this goal.
1.2.2
Electroencephalography
Several studies [15–19] have shown that EEG signals are a suitable method for
identifying the attended speaker, which reﬂects the neural responses in the brain
with high temporal resolution. Such steering signals must be extracted from the
hearing-impaired person to reﬂect the auditory attention and intention in various
situations.
A study from Eriksholm [2] reveals that by creating a speciﬁc transfer function or
decoding algorithm, it is likely to identify and separate the attended speaker by
correlation of the decoded EEG signals with the audio streams. Even though it
has high scientiﬁc signiﬁcance, it is not the case for the HI industry, due to the
fact that the EEG signals are recorded from a grid of electrodes covering the entire
scalp. However, to overcome this issue, the brain signals can also be retrieved by
electrodes positioned in the ear canal.
Widex demonstrated in [20] that it is possible to record EEG signals from both
customized and generic earpieces, where the customized earpiece had the highest
signal quality in terms of Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR). The superior signal quality
was due to the much higher degrees of freedom to place the electrodes in the ear.
Thanks to the imminent integration of recording and signal processing system into
an ear-worn device, we are taking a signiﬁcant step towards discreet, robust and
user-friendly brain monitoring devices.
The feasibility of ear EEG for developing an endogenous Brain-Computer Interface
(BCI) system that uses self-modulating brain activity was investigated in [21]. EEG

1.2.3
Electrooculography
The behavior of a person throughout a conversation usually includes both auditory
and visual attention. Visual attention entails that the listener directs their eyes
towards the sound target of interest. By tracking the position of the eye movement,
useful interfaces can be developed that helps the user to listen better.
Recent studies [22,23] present various feature extraction and classiﬁcation techniques
to classify the eye movement tasks. One of the most popular and robust techniques
for measuring saccadic eye movement is EOG. A saccade is deﬁned as a rapid move-
ment of the eyes between two or more phases of ﬁxation in the same direction.
A saccade normally takes about 200 milliseconds [24] and peaks around 60 Hertz
(Hz) [25]. Fixation duration typically ranges from 150 to 300 milliseconds [26]. Eye
movements can be detected by placing electrodes in the area of the head around
the eyes. Pairs of electrodes are typically placed either above and below the eyes
(vertical eye movements), or to the left and right side of the eyes (horizontal eye
movements).

Eriksholm Research Centre [27] investigated the steering of audio input in HIs by eye

3. An ideal condition where the eye gaze was accurately detected through an eye
tracker and the audio was correspondingly steered (eye tracker steering).
The scoring of the correctly repeated words per sentence was measured using Danish
DAT (Dagmar, Asta, and Tine) open-set database [28], where two aspects of the
score were considered: the score of individual words, and the score of full sentences
that were correctly repeated as presented in ﬁgure 5:

and ﬁnally, the eye tracker steering had the best performance in terms of accuracy [27].

gaze direction, which is relevant to our study, since we use the eye tracking data as

1.2.4
Biosensors In Hearing Instruments
Head movement can easily be implemented inside a HI using an accelerometer, a
gyroscope and a magnetometer. This will enable the hearing-impaired people to
freely move their head and not having it ﬁxed. Eye gaze estimation with biosensors
in HIs was investigated in [29]. The experiment was executed in two setups:
1. The participants had their head ﬁxed on a chin-rest.
2. The participants were free to move their head.
To estimate the head orientation, sensors consisting of an accelerometer, gyroscope
and magnetometer were embedded in the Behind-The-Ear (BTE) shell. The head ro-
tation angle was estimated by applying Extended Kalman Filter to the signals from
the Magnetic Inertial Measurement Unit (Mag-IMU), which is an electronic device
that measures an object’s speciﬁc force, orientation, and angular rate using a combi-
nation of accelerometers, gyroscopes, and magnetometers [30]. The ear EOG signal
was modeled using saccades, head movement, and a drift (slow movement).

gaze for all nine possible transitions in the head-ﬁxed condition:

Deep Learning
Sound source separation in noise is a complicated problem in terms of analyzing var-
ious sensor data. However, human brain solves this task with fascinating simplicity
and precision. Signiﬁcant advances in this domain have been achieved using a variety
of deep learning techniques, where mapping of an input to a target output is realized
through learning complex non-linear relations captured within the network param-
eters. These methods achieve cutting-edge performance even at very low latency,
which is crucial for resource-constrained embedded devices such as HIs [31].
Deep learning has shown excellent potential in understanding EEG signals to learn
good feature representations from raw data. A study [32], reviews 156 papers ap-
plying deep learning to EEG published between January 2010 and July 2018. The
papers were separated into three categories based on whether preprocessing steps
were used: “Yes”, “No” and “N/M” (not mentioned). The results are shown in
ﬁgure 8:
Figure 8: EEG processing choices.
Number of studies that used preprocessing steps,
such as ﬁltering (top), number of studies that included, rejected or corrected artifacts
in their data (center), types of features that were used as input to the proposed models
(bottom) [32].
Preprocessing EEG data usually comprises a few general steps, such as downsam-
pling, band-pass ﬁltering, and windowing.
Throughout the process of reviewing
papers, they found that a diﬀerent number of preprocessing steps were employed in
the studies. It is mentioned that a substantial amount of preprocessing was required
for assessing cognitive workload using deep learning. A considerable proportion of
the reviewed articles (72%) employed at least one preprocessing method. This result
is not surprising, as applications of Deep Neural Network (DNN) to other domains,
such as computer vision, usually require some kind of preprocessing like cropping
and normalization as well.
Artifact handling techniques were used to remove speciﬁc types of noise, such as oc-
ular and muscular artifacts [33]. As emphasized in the research, removal of artifacts
may be crucial for achieving good EEG decoding performance. However, cleaning
EEG signals might be a time-consuming process. Almost half of the papers (47% )
did not use artifact handling methods, while 23% did. Given those results, we are

Time Series Classiﬁcation
TSC is a challenging problem in data mining. In conjunction with the growth of
time series data accessibility, hundreds of TSC algorithms have been proposed. A
recent article [34] describes a study on the current state-of-the-art performance of
deep learning algorithms for TSC. It provides an overview of the most successful
deep learning applications in various time series domains. Deep learning approaches
for TSC can be separated into two main categories: the generative and the discrim-
inative models:
Figure 10: Overview of diﬀerent deep learning approaches for TSC. The two main groups,
the generative and the discriminative models, and their respective subgroups, auto en-
coders, echo state networks, feature engineering and end-to-end [34].
In ﬁgure 10 the deep learning for TSC with its two main categories, is shown.
Generative models usually exhibit an unsupervised training step that precedes the
learning phase of the classiﬁer. This type of network has been referred to as model-
based classiﬁers in the TSC community. Some of these generative non deep learning
approaches include auto-regressive and kernel models. Discriminative deep learning
models are classiﬁers (regressors) that directly learns the mapping between the raw
input of a time series and output a probability distribution over the class variables
in a dataset. Several discriminative deep learning architectures have been proposed
to solve the TSC task.

2.1.3
Participants Tasks
The test subjects were asked to do three tasks:
1. Follow a louder voice: The test subject had to follow either the monologue
or dialogue of whoever was talking loudest (∼12 decibels (dB) diﬀerence
to ensure clear identiﬁcation of target source). The sound intensity of the
loudspeakers was modiﬁed to create a variation in the participants saccades of
diﬀerent sign and amplitude.
2. Follow a dialogue: The test subject had to follow a dialogue between two
of the three people, where the sound intensity was not modiﬁed.
3. Steer a dialogue: The test subject had to follow a dialogue between two of
the three people, where the sound intensity was modiﬁed based on the target
output, the person currently speaking was ampliﬁed by 12 dB in the direction
of the attended target.

Preprocessing
Data preprocessing refers to transforming raw data into a form that is better suited
for predictive modeling. It is necessary in cases where irrelevant information, like
noise and/or unreliable data is present. The recorded data is stored in a CSV ﬁle,
which consists of rows, columns, and values. Non numeric values are replaced with
sampled numeric values preceding them.
The data is transformed from time domain to frequency domain applying the Short-
time Fourier transform (STFT) that perform time-frequency analysis, which is used
to design the cutoﬀfrequency of the lowpass ﬁlter. STFT is a sequence of Fourier
transforms of a windowed signal, whereas the standard Fourier transform provides
the frequency information averaged over the entire signal time interval. The STFT
provides information on how the frequencies in the signal change over time, which
it is used to generate representations that capture both the time and frequency
content in the signal. It works by dividing the signal into several small segments

Postprocessing
Within the ﬁeld of machine learning, there are two main types of learning: super-
vised, and unsupervised. The main diﬀerence is that supervised learning is done
using a ground truth, where we have prior knowledge of what the output values
for the samples should be. Therefore, the goal of supervised learning is to learn a
function that, given a sample of data and desired outputs, best approximates the re-
lationship between input and output observable in the data. Unsupervised learning,
on the other hand, does not have labeled outputs, so its goal is to infer the natural
structure present within a set of data points [41].

we mapped input to output labels. Dataset 1 is, however, unlabeled. As ﬁgure 22

3.1
Artiﬁcial Neural Networks
Deep learning is part of a wider group of machine learning methods based on artiﬁcial
neural networks that was inspired by human brain (neurons). DNN is a trainable
algorithm that allows machines to solve complex problems. It has been applied to
ﬁelds including computer vision, speech recognition, natural language processing,
and audio recognition, where it has produced results equivalent to and, in some
cases, superior to human performance.
This project deals with TSC:
1. Deﬁnition: A univariate time series X = [x1, x2, . . . , xT] is an ordered set of
real values. The length of X is equal to the number of real values T.
2. Deﬁnition: An M-dimensional multivariate time series, X = [X1, X2, . . . , XM]
consists of M diﬀerent univariate time series with Xi ∈RT.
3. Deﬁnition: A dataset D = (X1, Y1), (X2, Y2), . . . , (XN, YN) is a collection of
pairs (Xi, Yi) where Xi could either be a univariate or a multivariate time series
with Yi as its corresponding one-hot label vector. For a dataset containing K
classes, the one-hot label vector Yi is a vector of length K where each element
j ∈[1, K] is equal to 1 if the class of Xi is j and 0 otherwise.
We proceed with the third deﬁnition as our task involves training a model on a
dataset in order to map a space of possible inputs to a probability distribution over
the class variable values (labels).

series will be aggregated over the entire dimension resulting in a single real value.
This is similar to applying a local pooling with a length of a sliding window equal
to the length of the input time series.
Generally, a global pooling operation is
implemented to signiﬁcantly reduce the number of parameters in a model, resulting
in smaller and less computationally demanding models.

An epoch deﬁnes the number of times an algorithm iterates through the entire

• Overﬁtting refers to a model that has been over-trained on the particulars

• Optimum refers to a model performing satisfactory on both training and

Architecture
This section presents our work, the fundamentals and choices made to build the
networks. Several architectures were studied and designed. For this project it was
decided to move forward with the FCN (CNN with FC as last layer) as they were
the best suitable candidate among other DNNs for several reasons:
1. CNNs use weight sharing, require fewer parameters than FC networks, and
converge faster. The ability of CNNs to extract useful features with a relatively
small number of parameters outperforms other DNNs of similar size.
2. FCNs are suitable for edge devices due to their small and straightforward
network architecture.
3. Extensive research [69,70] has been conducted in FCN optimization methods,
further enhancing their eﬃciency in power limited devices like HIs. FCN com-
bine the classiﬁcation strength of a CNN with the ability to learn meaningful
features that improves the classiﬁcation task solved by FC layer.
The three FCN models developed during this project for TSC of ear EEG, are
presented and discussed below:

right.
• Model 2 (M2): a deep complex network based on dataset 2 where the goal is
too a three-class classiﬁcation task: whether the test person looks left, middle,

4.1

Hyperparameters have high impact on the size and performance of the network. The
optimal set of hyperparameters minimizes the network size, maximizes accuracy,
and avoids overﬁtting of the dataset. The number of hyperparameters needed to be
deﬁned throughout the design process of the network architecture was high. Having
a large number of hyperparameters to tune has its pros and cons. On one hand, it
provides a high degree of freedom allowing the network to be ﬁne-tuned. On the
other hand, the hyperparameter space is exponentially increased with the number
of free hyperparameters, i.e. ntrain = lnpara

REAL-TIME SYSTEM CONSIDERATIONS
5
Real-Time System Considerations
Design of real-time computing systems is one of the most challenging and complex
tasks in the engineering world. Like any computer-based system, it must integrate
HW, software, human interaction, and data elements to properly achieve a set of
functional and performance requirements. Deep learning models are notorious for
being large and computational heavy. It is always a challenge to ﬁt these models into
edge devices, especially HIs that usually have frugal memory. However, there are
a number of ways to address these problems. But ﬁrst, we will discuss the reasons
behind choosing embedded devices over the cloud:
• Privacy and security: Data can be stored locally, avoiding exposure when
developing private algorithms and software.
• No connectivity or internet issues: When models run locally on the de-
vice, there is no transmission of data, and response time and bandwidth con-
sumption are cut down signiﬁcantly.
• Minimal latency and power consumption: The modern accelerator units
with their improved architecture reduce power consumption considerably and
remove latency since everything happens on-board.
Henceforth we will talk about the inference process [71]. In the following sections two
aspects of real-time processing, quantization and group delay (GD) are addressed,
to better understand the requirements and limitations associated with these sys-
tems.
5.1
Quantization
Quantization is the process of reducing the number of bits that are used for repre-
senting a number. With 32-bit ﬂoating-point being the default for most applications,
including deep learning, it turns out that DNNs can work with smaller data types,
such as 8-bit integers, resulting in no or negligible loss of accuracy [72].
5.1.1
Advantages
Supporting inference with quantized types in any machine learning framework like
TensorFlow (TF), has many advantages, which are listed below:
• Memory requirements are reduced straightforward by a factor of four,
by going from 32-bits to 8-bits. Lighter deployment models mean less storage
space for storing the weights and biases, and reduction in power consumption
when transferring the data, since energy consumption is heavily dominated by
memory access.
• Smaller bit-depth for representing parameters results in lighter memory
requirements. This means less frequent RAM access to read the parameters,
which usually consume a lot of time and power.

• Gain further speed-up by using integer computation instead of ﬂoating-

metic.

• Symmetric Mode maps the exact min/max of a ﬂoat range to a quantized

Figure 45: 8-bit integer symmetric quantization (signed) [74].
Using asymmetric quantization, leads to a fully utilized range since the min/max

5.2
Group Delay
In this section, we would like investigate the delay for our lowpass ﬁlter. For a
system, GD is a performance property that helps to characterize time delay, which
is the amount of time for the various frequency components of a signal to pass
through the device from input to output. When a ﬁlter has linear phase, all the
frequencies within that signal will be delayed the same amount in time [76]. The
GD measurement shows how much these frequency components are misaligned in
seconds. If a ﬁlter does not have a ﬂat GD, then all the frequency components will
not experience the same delay through the ﬁlter. GD is deﬁned as the negative
derivative of a ﬁlter’s phase response:
τg(ω) = −dθ(ω)
dω
(40)
Our lowpass ﬁlter’s GD is shown in ﬁgure 46:
Figure 46: GD for lowpass ﬁlter. For signals below the cutoﬀfrequency (10 Hz) there is
almost a constant group delay of around 22.5 samples. A signal below the cutoﬀfrequency
will simply be delayed by approximately 22.5-27 samples. Above 50 Hz, group delay is
less than one sample.
The phase response of the Butterworth ﬁlter from ﬁgure 19 is far from linear, result-
ing in a non-constant GD, as shown in ﬁgure 46. This means that is has diﬀerent
values for diﬀerent signal frequencies and frequency components will no longer be
time-aligned. Note that the GD decays with increasing frequency, and is steeper at
low frequencies. The GD is at 22.5 samples in the passband of the ﬁlter, and peaks
at around 27 samples for frequencies close to 10 Hz, which is the cutoﬀfrequency
of the ﬁlter. In fact, this shows a common phenomenon, which is that ﬁlters have
more or less a ﬂat GD far from their cutoﬀfrequency. With a saccade lasting 200
ms, a GD of approximately 25 ms is considered to be acceptable.

Filters have a signiﬁcant amount of phase shift, and as a ﬁlter order increases, the
delay increases as well (the slope of the phase curve gets steeper). One may ask, if it
is possible to construct a device that does not generate a phase shift, and the answer

each. When a signal gets delayed through a ﬁlter, the same signal (signal of interest,
within the passband of the ﬁlter) will be recreated after the delay, presumed all its
frequency components are delayed with the same amount. In other words, we want
our signals to maintain their shape as they pass through a device [77].

formance. An optimal window size should be capable of capturing all the necessary
signal artifacts used in the classiﬁcation task. In this case, it was worthwhile to
experiment with diﬀerent number of convolutional layers and window sizes. Based
on the research in section 1.2, we chose to experiment with 2-4 convolution layers,
looping over window sizes of 100, 200, and 300, as ﬁxations lasted around 150 to
300 milliseconds, and saccades 200 milliseconds in average.

7
Discussion and Future Work
The aim of this study was to detect the direction of the eye gaze based on ear EEG
using deep learning. In this section, we both refer to the various challenges in the
diﬀerent stages of the development encountered throughout the project, and present
as well as discuss improvements to the existing methods are presented and discussed.
This project can be expanded and improved in several ways, hence new proposals
to try diﬀerent methods or ideas are outlined.
7.1
Data Quality
The expression ”garbage in, garbage out”, has existed since the computer was in-
vented. However, since the invention of machine learning and its strong need for
data quality, the expression is more relevant than ever. Machine learning is very
data-sensitive, and because of this, the quality of data has a huge impact on its
success. High quality data is required for most models to work properly - especially
for challenging tasks like eye gaze classiﬁcation presented in this thesis.
Thomas C. Redman, one of the original pioneers in data quality management cau-
tions us that ”bad data can rear its ugly head twice - ﬁrst in the historical data used
to train the predictive model and second in the new data used by that model to make
future decisions” [79]. Training data governs the performance of machine learning
systems. Quality data returns quality results; bad data does not.
• Data is missing: Occasionally, areas of recordings are left blank as the
information is missing, resulting in non-numeric values.
Solution: One way to solve this issue is to eliminate all data that has non-
numeric values, however, having too many missing samples would lead to a
skewed dataset. Synthetic data is also an option, involving creating data by
algorithms to imitate the properties of real data. Thereby, synthetic data helps
to complete the training data. Hence, we applied synthetic data.
• The data is inaccurate: When working with machine learning, cleaning the
data before training might be necessary. If the training data is full of errors,
noise, and outliers, the system will have a hard time to detect the underlying
patterns. Even an insigniﬁcant error can lead to a negative impact.
Solution: The data we obtained was very noisy, containing non-numeric val-
ues and outliers. This was processed and cleaned by implementing ﬁlters and
removing the inaccurate samples from the dataset.
• The data is biased: Bias is a type of error when certain classes in the
training data are over-represented or heavily weighted. For M3 with saccades,
the number of saccade samples was underrepresented.
Solution: To add a more balanced perspective to the saccades we penalize
the model by imposing additional cost implementing class weights [80] in M3.

7.2
Labeling Scheme
Most data is not labeled, which was also the case for the dataset used in this study.
Labeling the data is done to get a target that the model should predict. The goal of
our labeling scheme is to label the eye tracking data, so it can be used as a reference
for the ear EEG data (see section 2.2.1, postprocessing).
The labeling scheme had limitations in labeling the eye tracking data due the re-
quired manual inputs, aﬀecting its performance. In total, four input variables had
to be provided: a threshold and three baselines. Setting a wrong baseline leads to
a mislabeled dataset. A too low threshold results in no class labels, while a too
high value results in a class being mislabeled as another class. We tried to minimize
this error by monitoring the results: not a big concern for dataset 1 as the eye
tracking data had low ﬂuctuations. However, for dataset 2, there was a huge risk of
mislabelling, especially, when the baselines varied and were not ﬁxed at −30, 0, 30
degrees, as seen in ﬁgure 29. This is caused by the participants’ eyes targeting a
person instead of a LED. Furthermore, more errors occurred when labeling the ﬁx-
ations and saccades for dataset 2. When labeling ﬁxations between the targets, the
labeling scheme labels all states above the threshold as saccade, which is not always
a correct labeling as illustrated in ﬁgure 33.
Dedicating more time to get more accurately labelled data might improve the be-
havior of the model signiﬁcantly, but this task was out of the project scope. In the
following, several methods are introduced for labeling data.
Suggestion:
• Semi-Supervised Learning incorporates supervised and unsupervised learn-
ing, labeling large amounts of data with a small already labeled dataset.
• Transfer Learning is a technique, where a pre-trained machine learning
model is used to label the data. Here, a model that has been trained on a
similar dataset is applied to the one intended for labeling, and ﬁne-tuned to
achieve the required accuracy.
• Internal Labeling, also known as in-house labeling, is when experts within
the company label the data.
• External labeling is when dedicated vendors or workforce outside the com-
pany perform the labeling. [81].

7.3
Uncertainty Quantiﬁcation
Machine learning involves working with incomplete and imperfect information. We
will never have all the observations, otherwise a predictive model would not be
required. Uncertainty can be classiﬁed into two categories:
• Aleatoric uncertainty, also known as statistical uncertainty, coming from
random processes varying each time the same experiment is executed. This
type of uncertainty is irreducible.
In this study, aleatory uncertainty was
given by the inherent randomness of the sensors, accuracy, resolution, and
precision of the devices. Eye movement of the participants is also considered
as aleatoric uncertainty. Given our model input, there is no way of knowing
the exact output of a concrete evaluation, as there is always a certain amount
of variance in the result.
Suggestion: Reducing aleatoric uncertainty requires inﬂuence on the way
data is collected, since it is inherent in the physical system and can only
be quantiﬁed in a statistical sense.
A technique that can be used is data
augmentation. The goal is to obtain additional examples for the dataset, so
that the deep learning model can learn from a larger and preferably better set
of examples. It should also be noted that data augmentation during training
could help reducing the epistemic uncertainty, as it contributes to a network’s
learning experience [82].
• Epistemic uncertainty, also known as systematic uncertainty, refers to the
deﬁciencies produced by limited data or lack of knowledge: inaccurate mea-
surements, as the model neglects certain eﬀects, or particular data has been
deliberately hidden. This type of uncertainty is reducible [83].
Suggestion: Epistemic uncertainty can be diminished by improved measure-
ments, model formulation, and/or by increasing the sample size of data. Doing
more test experiments with more test participants would get us a better idea
of how these parameters inﬂuence the prediction. Eventually, epistemic un-
certainty would fade away given enough data spread across the input domain.
A machine learning model will always have some error, sometimes summarized as
”all models are wrong”. This is a common aphorism in statistics often expressed
as ”all models are wrong but some are useful”, attributed to the British statistician
George Box [84]. This does not only includes the model itself, but also preparation
and choice of data, training hyperparameters, and the interpretation of model’s
predictions.

to Coral AI board for real-time inference. The performance should be evaluated in
terms of achieved accuracy, model size (parameters, precision), latency (algorithmic,
computational, throughput), power consumption as well as feasibility of running the
model on a HI platform.

CONCLUSION
8
Conclusion
The behavior of a person during a conversation typically involves both auditory
and visual attention. However, people with hearing impairment are challenged and
require the use of vision for purposes other than those needed by healthy hearing
people. For visual information, a person directs their eye gaze towards the sound
target of interest. Hence, detection of the gaze direction may provide a steering
signal for future HIs making them more eﬀective for social communication.
In this project, experiments were conducted at Eriksholm Research Center and
several methods were analyzed, regarding detection of eye gaze direction based on
ear EEG recordings using neural networks.
In order to reach this objective, an
algorithm was developed to label the eye tracking data as a reference for the ear EEG
data. Butterworth and Notch ﬁlters were applied to remove noise or unnecessary
frequencies to prepare the data for predictive modeling. The preprocessed data was
then fed into three models. The architecture and topology of the models were derived
from related works, where similar conﬁgurations yielded positive results.
Model 1 and 2 are applicable for classifying data into three classes (left, middle,
right), while model 3 is opted for two classes (ﬁxation, saccade). Data 1 is charac-
terized by head-ﬁxed participants having their visual attention on a LED, gazing to
left, middle and right, whereas data 2 is characterized by head-ﬁxed participants fol-
lowing a conversation. Dataset 1 was used for model 1, while dataset 2 was used for
model 2 and 3. The described models were able to eﬀectively classify the data with
high accuracy: model 1 using raw data reached 95.69% accuracy versus 100% using
ﬁltered data. The classiﬁcation accuracy for model 2 using raw and ﬁltered data
were 91.35% and 73.59%, respectively. Finally, model 3 achieved 94.34% accuracy
using raw data, and 86.11% and 86.61% using ﬁltered data (Notch and Butterworth
ﬁlter, respectively). Lowering numerical precision increased models compatibility
with edge devices by reducing the size of model 1 by 22.22% and model 2 and 3 by
71.81%. The primary goal of model size reduction was to make them ﬁt into HIs
devices due to the limited memory at the cost of lower accuracy.
The intention of this project was also to serve as inspiration for future works that
combine deep learning with classical signal processing.
This can be: recording
additional data, developing diﬀerent labeling scheme algorithms, and investigating
other types of neural networks.
The overall results shows a promising perspective of applying deep learning models
for ear EEG data classiﬁcation into HIs. There is no doubt, that better perform-
ing HIs will signiﬁcantly improve the experience of the surrounding world for the
end-user and thus enabling them to interact more responsively to their environ-
ment.
