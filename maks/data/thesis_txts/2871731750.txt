Resume
For at overholde diverse klimamål og aftaler, hvilket medfører et stigende behov for ved-
varende energi, er det nødvendigt at indfase og implementere nye energiformer i samfundet
såsom hydrogen. Dette kandidatspeciale undersøger hvilke problematikker der opstår, hvis
hydrogenbiler introduceres i samfundet. Dette gøres ved først at forklare relevant teori,
hvorefter gasudslip simuleres, der udføres risikoanalyse på hydrogenbiler i tunneler, ana-
lytisk beregning af eksplosioner i tunneller, fysiske eksperimenter udføres, eksperimenterne
simuleres med CFD og til sidst bruges simuleringsmodellen på en forsøgsopstilling, der
forestiller en hydrogenbeholder.
I projektet blev diverse risici ved hydrogenteknologien defineret. Dette inkluderer gasud-
slip, antændelse af hydrogen, hydrogeneksplosioner og eksplosion af hydrogenbeholder
under højt tryk. I simuleringen af hydrogenudslip i et indelukket rum opnåede hydro-
genkoncentrationen et brandfarligt niveau, men ikke et eksplosivt niveau.
Risikoanalyse af hydrogenbiler i Femern Bælt-tunnelen viste, at der var meget lav sandsyn-
lighed for hydrogeneksplosioner eller eksplosion af hydrogentanken. Trykket fra en hydro-
gentankeksplosion i Femern Bælt-tunnelen blev beregnet, hvilket viste at konsekvenserne
er store.
Eksperimenter med heptan- og benzinbrande blev udført for at få data om den termiske
påvirkning og effekten af en afskærmende stålplade. Stålpladen var i stand til at reducere
en stor del af varmestrålingen. Eksperimenterne blev replikeret i brandsimuleringspro-
grammet FDS, hvorefter simuleringsmodellerne blev sammenlignet og verificeret med den
eksperimentelle data. Til sidst blev den verificerede simuleringsmodel for heptanbranden
brugt til at undersøge en forsøgsopstilling, der forestillede en hydrogenbeholder. Denne
simulering viste, at hydrogencylinderen hurtigt blev opvarmet til meget høje tempera-
turer, samt at det er problematisk, hvis den varme røg fastholdes omkring cylinderen.

Part I
Introduction
In figure 1 the primary energy production in Denmark from 1990 to 2022 is shown. The
figure shows that the total primary energy production has been declining since 2005,
however roughly one third of the primary energy production is still from crude oil, which
has a negative impact on the environment.
Figure 1: Primary energy production in Denmark from 1990 to 2022[17] (Blue = crude
oil. Yellow = natural gas. Green = sustainable energy)
In order to achieve the international goals set by the European Parliament of 42,5%
sustainable energy and the national goals set by Denmark to reduce the emissions with
70% in 2030 compared to 1990 and being completely climate neutral in 2050, it is necessary
to develop and transition to new sustainable energies.[18]
One such promising type of energy is hydrogen, that could transform the transport sector,
be used to heat buildings and be stored, all without emitting CO2. Hydrogen is advan-
tageous since the only byproduct from using it is water, the existing infrastructure can
be adapted to hydrogen, it can be used to produce other fuels and the energy density of
hydrogen is higher than that for batteries.[20]
In 2021 only 2% of the energy mix in EU was hydrogen, and 95% of this hydrogen was
produced with fossil fuels. Clean/sustainable hydrogen is produced through electrolysis
of water by using electricity that originates from sustainable sources, such that no CO2 is
released during the production. Research shows that 20% of the energy mix in EU could
be hydrogen in 2050.[20]
1
==PAGE:8==
However, hydrogen is not very significant in the total energy supply at the moment, as it
is facing challenges with production capacity, infrastructure and safety[20]. The European
Hydrogen Incidents and Accidents database HIAD 2.1, shows that recent hydrogen inci-
dents include hydrogen explosions at power plants, pipelines, refineries, storage facilities,
fueling stations and hydrogen transportation trucks. Furthermore, there were numerous
fires and near-misses.[21] This shows the critical need to analyze these risks in order to
fully implement hydrogen into the societies.

Recent hydrogen incidents
A couple of relevant incidents resulting in damage will be described. These incidents are
described in the European Hydrogen Incidents and Accidents database HIAD 2.1[21].
1.1
Explosion of a tank at hydrogen components manufacturer
Austria, 2023. Explosion during a hydrogen tank test. Massive damage at test facility
from pressure and a small fire. Investigation ongoing. Probable cause is over-pressure in
the tank.
1.2
Explosion during hydrogen supply in a chemical company
Germany, 2022. Hydrogen carrying vehicle was supplying hydrogen to a chemical plant,
but the hydrogen was transferred to the wrong pipe and sent into a silo, creating an
explosive atmosphere that ignited.
1.3
Release and fire from a hydrogen pipeline
US, 2021. Hydrogen release and ignition resulting in a property loss of 4.500.000 USD.

placement).
1.5
Explosion at the hydrogenation tower of a refinery
China, 2015. Hydrogen release and ignition resulting in a property loss of 500.000 euro,
4 deaths and 2 injuries.

2
Aim of this study
This study aims to explore the events leading up to a hydrogen explosion or vessel burst
and evaluate methods to avoid the occurrence of such, by analyzing the process of a
hydrogen vessel exposed to fire. Furthermore, research on hydrogen gas dispersion and
blast impact will be conducted. However, detonation tests are outside the scope of this
project.
3
Research goals and methods
The research goal is to assess the thermal impact on a hydrogen vessel exposed to fire and
the potential consequences that may follow. A mock-up model will be created to collect
data on how the vessel is heated, creating a basis for analysis. This setup will likewise
be modeled with computational fluid dynamics (CFD) software, and the results will be
compared.
In addition, gas dispersion will be modeled with CFD and hazards will be identified based
on explosive concentrations and analytical formulas will be used to calculate the size of
the potential vessel burst. Furthermore, risk analysis will be conducted.
These goals are summarized below:
• Fire and explosion hazards identified
• Risk analysis of hydrogen hazards
• Temperature profile from mock-up test
• Temperature profile from CFD simulation
• Validation of CFD model
• Gas dispersion CFD simulation
• Analytical calculation of vessel burst
This project will analyze hydrogen vessels in vehicles that are exposed to fire. The nu-
merical simulations will be performed with Ansys Fluent and Fire Dynamics Simulator 6
(FDS). Several simplified models of relevant cases will be analyzed.
4
Prior work
Several studies have been conducted for hydrogen expansion, deflagration, and deflagra-
tion to detonation (DDT), but there are less studies on boiling liquid expanding vapor ex-
plosion (BLEVE), pressure vessel burst (PVB), vapor cloud explosions (VCE) and hydro-
gen cloud size effect. Furthermore, the precision of blast models such as TNT-equivalent is
poor and the CFD precision varies. The most frequently used CFD programs to simulate
hydrogen explosions are FLACS, OpenFOAM and Fluent by ANSYS.[45]

4.1
Experimental vessel burst test of a hydrogen vehicle
4.1
Experimental vessel burst test of a hydrogen vehicle
Byoungjik Park and Yangkyun Kim conducted a field experiment of a vessel burst of a
hydrogen vehicle in an unconfined area. The hydrogen tank contained 2.1 kg hydrogen at
70 MPa. The vehicle was exposed to fire while the thermal pressure relief device (TPRD)
was deactivated, resulting in an vessel burst. After the vessel burst, a piece of debris with
a weight of 23.2 kg was found 55.8 m away from the car. Maximum overpressure of 173
kPa was observed 3 m from the tank. Analysis showed that the maximum overpressure
would cause structural damage and eardrum rupture.[9]
4.2
TPRD activation time
Bi et. al conducted 3 pool fire tests on a type 3 hydrogen tank with a volume of 210 L
and a nominal working pressure of 35 MPa. In test 1 the TPRD was deactivated and
explosion occurred after 784 seconds. In test 2 the TPRD activated after 666 seconds and
in test 3 it activated after 596 seconds. In test 1, 2 and 3 the diameters of the TPRD
were 4, 4 and 3,6 mm respectively.[6]
4.3
Analysis of hydrogen leakage in tunnels
Zhang et. al. found that longitudinal ventilation in tunnels can decrease the explosion
risk of a hydrogen leak and that the most economical solution is a ventilation speed of
3-5m/s. In addition they determined that valve failure of the hydrogen supply was the
main cause of hydrogen fire and explosion. Furthermore they found that the fire hazard is
greatest when the hydrogen is leaking downwards and lowest when it’s released upwards
as it is diffused along the ceiling by the ventilation.[50]
4.4
Hydrogen-air mixtures diluted with inert gases
Yang. et. al. researched the effect of inert gases on hydrogen-air mixtures in confined
areas and found that increasing the amount of carbon dioxide in the hydrogen-air mixture,
reduces the explosion risk.[61]
4.5
Water mist suppression of hydrogen jet fire
Shen et. al. performed numerical simulations of hydrogen jet fires on ships. They found
that fine water mist with a particle size of 30 µm can reduce the temperature of hydrogen
jet fires and that increasing the particle size decreases the suppression effect. They also
found that the suppression effect decreases with increasing wind speeds, due to flame
intensification from the wind.
However, the water mist was unable to extinguish the
hydrogen jet fire.[62]
4.6

most simple atom with only one proton and one electron. Atoms with only one electron
tend to be highly reactive, which explains why hydrogen is highly reactive and naturally
merge into H2.[40]

5.4
Toxicity
Hydrogen is not toxic in itself, but can cause oxygen deficiency as it rapidly replaces
the oxygen in the air.
When the oxygen level drops below 19.5%, effects of oxygen
deficiency are present, which include rapid breathing, reduced mental alertness, reduced
muscle control, bad judgement, reduced sensation, fatigue and death. This is usually
only a problem in confined areas where the ratio of the leak size to the room size is
large. Whereas an unconfined area wouldn’t pose an oxygen deficiency threat due to the
buoyancy of hydrogen.[40]
5.5
Diffusivity
Considering hydrogen’s low boiling point, hydrogen leaks will evaporate rapidly. In ad-
dition, the hydrogen molecule is the smallest gas molecule, which enables it to diffuse
through membranes and materials that are impermeable for other gases.[40]
Figure 6 shows diffusion coefficients for gases in air. Hydrogen at 20℃has a diffusivity
of 0.756 while hydrogen at 400℃has a diffusivity of 3.238.[53]
Figure 6: Diffusion coefficients for gases in air[53]
5.6
Flammability
Fuel, oxygen and ignition are necessary for a fire or explosion to occur. Flashpoint is
a measure of the temperature when the fuel is producing enough vapor to create an
ignitable mixture, corresponding to the lower flammable limit. At temperatures below
the flashpoint, not enough vapors are produced to create a flammable mixture.
The
flashpoint for hydrogen is -253℃while the flashpoint of gasoline is -43℃.[40, p.23]

Flammability

where the gas can be ignited, which is limited by the amount of air and the amount

a mixing with the adjacent air, resulting in a flammable mixture. Similarly the upper
explosive limit (UEL) and the lower explosive limit (LEL) can be defined. These limits are
shown in figure 7. Figure 7b shows that the flammability limits vary with the temperature.
Compared to gasoline that has a flammability range from 1 to 8%, hydrogen is highly

Power-to-X (PtX)
Power-to-X (PtX) is a term for technologies that can transform electricity to hydrogen.
It is also known as electrofuels (e-fuels), which refers to the electrolysis process. Ener-
gistyrelsen has evaluated that PtX is an important technology in order to reach the 70%
reduction of emissions in 2030, and especially to become climate neutral later on. [19]
6
How a hydrogen car works
Hydrogen fuel cell vehicles (HFCV) or fuel cell electric vehicles (FCEV) are terms for
vehicles that utilize fuel cells to power electric motors. For vehicles, Proton Exchange
Membranes or Polymer Electrolyte Membranes (PEM) are usually used as the operating
temperature is low (80℃), which is beneficial due to less wear on components.[42]
6.1
Fuel cell
A fuel cell is a device that combines fuel and air electrochemically to produce electricity.
Usually, hydrogen is used as the energy efficiency is very high, transforming more than
80% of the energy in the hydrogen atoms into electricity. Each fuel cell generates a small
amount of electricity, therefore they are combined into a unit called a stack.[32]
The concept of a hydrogen fuel cell is shown in figure 8.
Figure 8: Hydrogen fuel cell concept (Edited from [51])
11
==PAGE:18==
The energy production with a fuel cell is explained below:[32]
• Hydrogen is supplied at anode
• Air is supplied at cathode
• Hydrogen releases electrons and becomes hydrogen ions
• The electrons pass through a circuit from anode to cathode, generating energy to
power the electric motor
• Hydrogen ions transfer through a membrane in the electrolyte over to the cathode
where they combine with oxygen and electrons, producing water.

Hydrogen fuel tank and TPRD
The trigger temperature of the thermal pressure relief device (TPRD) is 110℃± 5℃[43].
Bei Li et al. tested the activation of the TPRD in their experiment and found that the
pressure dropped to 0.1 MPa in 80 seconds. The diameter of the TPRD nozzle was 4 mm,
initial tank pressure 33.8 MPa, vessel volume 210 L, vessel type 3.[6]

improve the weight ratio between hydrogen and vessel material, but has higher manufac-
turing costs. Type 4 vessels typically have operating pressures up to 700 bar and even
better hydrogen weight ratio.[2]

Part VI
Discussion
Simulation of hydrogen leak in garage
The leak was simulated with both Fire dynamics Simulator (FDS) and Ansys Fluent
and the results showed that the hydrogen concentrations were higher in Fluent, but both
CFD-models yield results that are below the explosive range (18-60%). The hydrogen
concentration below the ceiling was 8% in FDS and 12% in Ansys Fluent, which is inside
the flammability range for hydrogen (4-75%).
The simulation was simplified and assumed a steady leak rate, simple geometry, no furni-
ture in the garage, no doors, no open/leaking windows and no ventilation. This simplified
setup lacks the complexity of real world scenarios, while still generating valuable insights
in hydrogen gas dispersion and identification of hazards. FDS is unable to model hy-
drogen jet fires and explosions, as it relies on the Navier-Stokes equations that are only
valid for mach numbers below 0,3[34], which shows the importance of further developing
this CFD-software. Future studies should explore ignition scenarios and the correlation
between leak ratio and hydrogen concentration in the garage.
Hydrogen risk modeling inside tunnel
The yearly risk of at least one hydrogen vessel burst inside the Fehmarnbelt tunnel was
calculated to 0,5%, the risk of delayed ignition was 0,049% and the risk of instant ignition
was 0,084%. This calculation assumed that all cars are hydrogen driven, the hydrogen
tanks are full, severe accidents could lead to leaks and 50% of the fires result in thermal
pressure relief device (TPRD) demand. Furthermore, the only fire cause evaluated in the
risk tree, is from vehicle crashes, which shows the need to analyze the risks regarding the
hydrogen fuel cell and all the other components that can lead to a fire. These components
were not evaluated as the mechanical and electrical failure rate is very low in new cars
and the hydrogen cars were assumed to be 2 years old on average. This risk would change
in the future as the hydrogen cars age and needs to be evaluated for older hydrogen cars.
Hydrogen vessel burst inside tunnel
The damage from a 3 kg hydrogen vessel burst inside the Fehmarnbelt tunnel was esti-
mated based on a CFD model that takes both the mechanical and chemical energy into
account while also relating the blast pressure to the geometry and wall friction loss. The
calculation showed that there would be 8 fatalities, 42 seriously injured, 2003 slightly
injured and the safe distance is approximately 5,7 km from the explosion site.
This
calculation assumed that the hydrogen tank is full, which is very unlikely.
A limitation of the used model is that it doesn’t account for obstacles and ventilation,
which could have a major impact on the blast pressure decay.
Future studies should
include this.
Experiments
During the experimental fire tests, the heat flux and adiabatic surface temperature (AST)
was measured with thermocouples. During test 3 with thermocouples directly exposed
66
==PAGE:73==
next to the fire, the average heat flux was 3622 W/m2 and the average AST was 200℃at
T40_Bot, while the average heat flux was 2498 W/m2 and the average AST was 160℃at
T40_Top. In test 4, inserting the metal sheet as shielding in front of the thermocouples
reduces the heat flux with 91% and AST with 75% for T40_Bot, while it reduces the
heat flux with 91% and AST with 73% for T40_Top.
During test 5 with thermocouples directly exposed above the fire, the average heat flux
was 22977 W/m2 and the average AST was 504℃at T_Above, while the average heat
flux was 1054 W/m2 and the average AST was 100℃at T40_Above. In test 6, inserting
the metal sheet as shielding in front of the thermocouples reduces the heat flux with 53%
and AST with 34% for T_Above, while it reduces the heat flux with 66% and AST with
46% for T40_Above.
This shows how simple passive shielding can be used to delay the heating of the hydrogen
vessel, which in turn would delay the activation of the TPRD, which gives firefighters
additional time to respond to the fire.
The device that measured the weight of the fuel failed several times during the experi-
ments, which shows that the old lab equipment could be prone to inaccuracies.
FDS simulation of experiments
The simulation of test 1 with the heptane fire yielded results that followed the experi-
mentally measured results very well. This simulation was based on the experimentally
measured heat release rate (HRR) that was calculated based on Janssen’s method. The
simulated heptane fire was thus verified and used for the simulation of the cylinder exposed
to fire.
During the gasoline fires, the HRR wasn’t measured. The HRR was instead determined
with a simple formula that relies on the measured mass loss rate, pool area, theoretical
combustion efficiency and theoretical heat of combustion. Slight changes in these theo-
retical values greatly impact the HRR, which can be seen on the simulation results, that
deviated significantly from the experimental results. The HRR along with the radiation
fraction and the emissivity of the thermocouple engulfed in the fire was changed. Although
the deviation remained significant between the experimental and simulation results, the
relative change in AST when the steel plate was introduced, was relatively consistent.
FDS simulation of cylinder exposed to fire
In scenario 1, the cylinder was exposed to to a fire in an environment where the hot
air/smoke could flow freely, while in scenario 2 there was a metal plate with edges placed
above the cylinder, which trapped some hot air/smoke, similarly to a fire underneath a
car.
The average AST at the middle bottom surface remained approximately unchanged in
both scenarios, while the average AST at the middle top surface increased by 38% when
the steel plate was introduced. This shows the importance of ventilating the hot air and
smoke away from the hydrogen vessel. The average AST at the bottom middle surface
quickly reached values above 500℃, indicating the need for quick response or activation
of mitigation strategies like the TPRD device.

Part VII
Conclusion
This master’s thesis analyzed various aspects that are crucial for hydrogen vehicles and
hydrogen explosion safety, including: gas dispersion, risk modeling in tunnels, calculation
of blast pressure decay, experimental testing and computational modeling.
Fire and explosion hazards evaluated and identified
CFD simulation of hydrogen dispersion in a simple single car garage without ventila-
tion showed that the hydrogen release following the TPRD activation created flammable
atmosphere, but didn’t reach the explosive range.
Risk analysis of hydrogen hazards in tunnels
The risk analysis looked at the events following a crash of a hydrogen car and the potential
hazards, providing a thorough evaluation of the risks that are specific to hydrogen vehicles
in tunnels. The yearly risks of vessel bursts and explosions were relatively low.
Consequence from hydrogen vessel burst inside tunnel calculated
The consequences from a hydrogen vessel burst inside the Fehmarnbelt tunnel was quanti-
fied with analytical calculations for blast pressure decay, highlighting the severity of such
events.
Experimental data obtained
Experimental data were obtained for a heptane fire and several gasoline fires, where
thermocouples in both directly exposed and shielded conditions were tested. Furthermore,
the oxygen concentration was measured during the heptane fire, enabling the calculation
of the HRR. The results showed that introducing the shielding steel sheet, greatly reduces
the heat flux and the AST.
Validation of CFD model for the experiments
The simulation results were directly compared with the experimental data from the fire
tests, validating the CFD model for the heptane fire, but the deviation for the gasoline
fire tests were too significant. Nevertheless, the model still captures the relative effect of
introducing the shielding steel sheet, with the decrease in AST being relatively consistent
with the experimentally measured decrease.
Simulation of cylinder exposed to fire
CFD simulations of a metal cylinder above the heptane fire were conducted in order to
determine the the thermal exposure in two different scenarios. In scenario 1 the air could
flow freely around the cylinder opposed to scenario 2 where a plate that traps some of the
hot air/smoke was introduced above the cylinder. The introduction of the plate increased
the average AST at the middle top surface of the cylinder by 38%, while the middle
bottom surface remained approximately unchanged.
