Abstract
This thesis investigates pattern formation in a Keller-Segel model with chemokinesis.
The model describes how bacteria respond to a chemoattractant secreted by the
bacteria themselves. Firstly, a simple form of the model is studied. We establish
under which conditions patterns must occur, and study the conﬁguration of steady
state patterns using pseudo arc-length continuation. Applying phase plane analysis,
we ﬁnd that the shape of patterns is dictated by a heteroclinic connection.
This
gives rise to bacteria aggregations with a ﬁxed height and a non-zero bacteria density
between aggregations.
Secondly, logistic bacteria growth is added to the model.
It is investigated how
the wave length of patterns, i.e.
the distance between bacteria aggregations, can
be related to a linear stability analysis. We demonstrate the ability of the model
to generate a variety of spatio-temporal patterns. We ﬁnd that temporally varying
patterns undergo repeated collapses and emergences of bacteria aggregations, but not
coalescence of diﬀerent aggregations. We observe how varying the growth rate leads
to period-doubling before the onset of spatio-temporal irregularity.
Lastly, the obtained results are compared to Keller-Segel models with chemotaxis in
order to determine the main similarities and diﬀerences between the two diﬀerent
ways of modelling bacteria and chemoattractant interaction.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In nature, pattern formation is ubiquitous. Animal coat markings exist in all sorts of
patterns, chlorophyll is non-uniformly distributed in leaves, and diﬀerent types of rock
can be recognized by their structure. Smaller parts are organized into an intricate
whole forming a distinct pattern. In some cases patterns are the result of the response
to a signal such as pheromones or food sources. Some patterns are symmetric, where
others seem more random, but in all cases we could ask: what inﬂuences pattern
formation?
This thesis is an examination of how pattern formation can be modelled as a result
of an organism’s interaction with a chemical substance. It studies the behaviour of
a bacteria population which interacts with a chemoattractant concentration. The
bacteria themselves exude a chemical substance, the chemoattractant, which in turn
causes the bacteria to move. Thereby each bacterium inﬂuences the location of the
other and this response to the chemoattractant signal leads to the formation of spatial
or spatio-temporal patterns. The mathematical model is a system of two coupled
non-linear partial diﬀerential equations (PDEs). The model we will study here is
a variant of a Keller-Segel model [Edelstein-Keshet, 2005; Keller and Segel, 1970],
where chemotaxis has been replaced by chemokinesis.
After introducing the model and providing a short background into Keller-Segel type
models, sections 1.1 and 1.2, we will describe the reasoning behind the chemokinesis
substitution and how it diﬀers from a classical chemotaxis model in 1.3. The last
sections, 1.4 and 1.5, account for the aim of the thesis and give an outline of the
thesis structure.

1.2
Background
In the seminal article by Keller and Segel [Keller and Segel, 1970] they construct a
chemotaxis model for the interaction between slime mold amoebae and the substance
cAMP. The model in its most simple form is
˙a = b −a + a′′
˙b = −(−µb′ + χba′)′
(1.2)
as described in [Edelstein-Keshet, 2005]. Here, χ is the chemotactic sensitivity and µ
is the motility of the slime mold. The term −µb is a diﬀusion term describing random
motion, whereas the term χba′ describes the directed motion (advection) of slime
mold due to their ability to detect cAMP gradients. Keller and Segel suggested that
pattern formation, or aggregation, could be viewed as an instability of the uniform
solution and used linear stability analysis to identify the mechanisms which lead
to such instability. In this form of the model, degradation and secretion of cAMP
is linear and motility and chemotactic sensitivity are constants. It is the simplest
spatial model which describes feedback between a population and its environment
(cAMP) and has been widely studied [Hillen and Painter, 2009]. The model along
with numerous variations, has been applied to a variety of topics within mathematical
biology. From its original application to slime mold, Dictyostelium discoideum, it has
been used to describe pattern formation of bacteria, for example Escherichia coli or
Salmonella typhimurium [Tyson et al., 1999]. By including a haptotaxis term, the
model has been adopted to model angiogenesis, i.e. the formation of blood vessels
in response to a chemical signal [Anderson and Chaplain, 1998]. Furthermore, it has
been used in the study of ants and their response to pheromones [Amorim, 2014], or
even to model how criminal behaviour spreads in urban areas [Short et al., 2008]. An
overview of the diﬀerent variations of the Keller-Segel model, which also mentions
many diﬀerent applications, can be found in [Hillen and Painter, 2009]. Besides such
case studies, the model in diﬀerent variations has been studied in order to determine
its mathematical properties such as existence of solutions, whether or not solutions
blow up in ﬁnite time e.g. [Childress and Percus, 1981] or stability properties e.g.
[Kang et al., 2007].
In this thesis we have adopted the terminology related to a bacteria population and its
interaction with a chemoattractant, since the application of these types of models to
bacteria populations seems to be widespread. Due to the mathematical and thereby
general nature of the study (as opposed to concentrating on a single case study),

1.3
Taxis versus Kinesis
Chemotaxis is used to describe directional motion in response to a chemical substance.
Chemokinesis on the other hand describes the undirected response to a chemical
substance. The diﬀerence in modelling the two phenomena lies on the microscale,
in the way individual bacteria respond to the chemoattractant: if the bacterium
is able to detect chemical gradients and thereby directs its motion towards higher
concentrations of the chemoattractant, the motion is chemotactic. If the bacterium
on the other hand, is only able to sample the intensity of the chemoattractant at a
single point and cannot determine chemical gradients, but still modiﬁes its movement
in response to the chemical concentration, the motion is chemokinetic.1 In our case,
this modiﬁcation of behaviour is a lowering of diﬀusivity when bacteria experience a
more favourable level of chemoattractant. In other words, the bacteria move less, the
more satisﬁed they are with their chemoattractant level.
If we consider an Itô stochastic diﬀerential equation [Øksendal, 2005, chapter 5] gov-
erning the movement of each single bacterium, we see the mathematical diﬀerence
between taxis and kinesis. Let the position of a bacterium at time t be given by the
random variable Xt, governed by

1.4
Aim of the Thesis
This thesis seeks to identify and explain the dynamics of a Keller-Segel model with
chemokinesis. We will aim to clarify the mechanisms that drive pattern formation
and how patterns are inﬂuenced by diﬀerent factors (i.e.
when varying diﬀerent
parameters). Among these factors we study the eﬀect of varying domain size and
bacteria density. The work includes two diﬀerent versions of the chemokinesis model:
the basic chemokinesis model and the chemokinesis model with population dynamics.
As explained above, the models are valid under the assumption that the bacteria
we are considering are not able to detect chemical gradients, e.g. because they are
too small to do so. The basic model also relies on the assumption that the eﬀects
of population dynamics, i.e. reproduction and death, can be ignored. This is the
case if bacteria proliferation has halted, as in the aggregation phase of slime mold,
or if bacteria growth happens on a signiﬁcantly slower time-scale than the pattern
formation phenomena we wish to describe [Hillen and Painter, 2009].

As accounted for above, the Keller-Segel model with chemotaxis has been the subject
of many studies. To the best of our knowledge, the corresponding chemokinesis model
has not been devoted much attention. This makes the chemokinesis model an obvious
choice of subject, allowing for the exploration of new results while at the same time
drawing on the vast body of material describing the chemotaxis model. Furthermore,
the model readily lends itself to semi-numerical analysis such as continuation, permit-
ting both theoretical and numerical explorations of the model. All in all, this makes
the chemokinesis model an appealing subject of study. The work done here is focused
on giving an overview of model behaviour based on analytical and numerical results.
Throughout the report we have sought to communicate these results by illustrative
ﬁgures, and by formulating statements rendered probable by the results, although
not formally shown. Following this approach, we have not devoted time to the more
theoretical routes which could have been taken, such as proving existence and unique-
ness of solutions, analytically determining stability of steady state patterns etc. The
tools employed consist of linear stability analysis, phase plane analysis, discretization
and numerical solution of PDEs, and pseudo arc length-continuation. The models
are considered in one spatial dimension throughout the thesis.

2.4 Steady State Solutions

2.4
Steady State Solutions
In the previous section we have identiﬁed an interval of K-values where the uniform
solution is unstable and a spatially non-uniform stable solution therefore must exist.
These solutions are patterns with areas of higher bacteria density interspersed with
areas with few bacteria. As mentioned earlier, we will use the term patch as a synonym
for bacteria aggregation or areas with a high number of bacteria. The rest of this
chapter is devoted to examining the following questions:
• For which K-values do steady state patterns exist?
• Is there a general conﬁguration of a steady state pattern and how does this
depend on domain size L?
• What characterizes the level of bacteria and chemoattractant inside and outside
patches?
The analysis is done by ﬁrstly transforming the PDE system at steady state into
a Hamiltonian system of ordinary diﬀerential equations (ODEs) and explaining the
types of solutions which are relevant in this context.
Afterwards, pattern conﬁg-
uration is identiﬁed by using pseudo arc-length continuation [Doedel, 2007].
By
comparing the results of pseudo arc-length continuation with the solutions to the
Hamiltonian system, it is shown that the Hamiltonian framework provides a simple
way of describing steady state behaviour.

3.2
Dynamics under the Standard Parameter Set
Based on the linear stability analysis we expect the introduction of a growth term to
destabilize the one-patch steady state solutions we saw for r = 0. Since k = 0 is no
longer an unstable mode when r > 0, we can therefore expect patterns to have one
speciﬁc wave length in steady state. This wave length will be independent of domain
size (provided that the domain is large enough to encompass it), in contrast to the
basic model, where we saw that the steady state patterns always had wave length L.
The number of patches to expect in steady state when including population dynamics
should therefore depend not only on the carrying capacity, K, and the strength of
the growth rate, r, but also be growing with domain size. A priori, we do not know if
the dominating wave length will again be the longest possible (i.e with wave number
given by the leftmost root of the dispersion relation), the wave length corresponding
to the fastest growing mode, or somewhere in between.

Phase I
In the ﬁrst phase, the patch is formed. The onset of a patch attracts bacteria
from the surroundings such that the patch width is increased, while the number
of bacteria around the patch goes down.
Phase II
At some point, the surroundings are depleted of excess bacteria and the bacteria
inﬂux to the patch stops, initiating phase two. This means that the bacteria
outside the patch are evened out into a locally stable uniform conﬁguration (not
necessarily zero). The number of bacteria inside the patch is well above the car-

then quickly move towards the interior of the patch to keep their chemoattrac-

behaviour results in patch collapse.
Phase III
In the third phase, the intermediate phase between patches, bacteria are dis-
persed almost evenly over the whole domain. The bacteria density is increased
due to proliferation until the uniform solution is no longer stable and patch
formation starts over.
The dominating mechanism driving spatio-temporal patterning is that each patch
dies out in isolation, undergoing the three phases, while staying at the same location.

3.3.1
Temporally Periodic Patterns
When we consider the temporally periodic patterns in ﬁgure 3.6 we see that they all
have patches which are clearly spatially aligned. Patches re-emerge at the same loca-
tion where they die out. Initially, patches form at random locations, but are shortly
after aligned so that they are evenly distributed over the whole domain – just as the
patches of steady state patterns are evenly distributed. The period of temporally
periodic patterns are largely controlled by the growth rate. This is intuitively clear:
the death of bacteria causes the patch to collapse so the larger the growth rate, the
faster the bacteria die out and the faster the patch breaks down. Figure 3.9 shows
the growth rate versus temporal period of all temporally periodic patterns generated.
Regardless of the other parameter values, the period is roughly inversely proportional
to r.

• In the beginning of phase one, the fast transformation from a uniform bacteria

time scale in order for [b] to increase before the patches are formed.

3.4
Chemokinesis Model with Population Dynamics Results
We have used the linear stability analysis to determine, which parameter values render
the uniform solution unstable and to identify a small growth rate, r, as a contributor
to pattern formation. For the standard parameter set, we have shown how the wave
length, i.e. the distance between bacteria patches, falls in the interval of unstable
modes predicted by the linear stability analysis.
When also varying the parameters of the diﬀusion term, the chemokinesis model with
logistic growth presents complex spatio-temporal patterning. These spatio-temporal
patterns present a high degree of spatial regularity while the patches collapse and
re-emerge, but never merge. Temporally periodic patterns experience synchronized
patch collapse over the whole domain. The majority of irregular solutions also show
periods of time, where all patches have disappeared simultaneously and left the do-
main to be dominated by a uniform solution.
The period of temporally periodic
patterns is to a large extent controlled by the growth rate and by choosing r as
bifurcation parameter, we present a series of period-doublings leading to irregularity.

CHAPTER 4
Outlook
This chapter is devoted to the discussion of the results of the thesis. In section 4.1
we compare with the results of the Keller-Segel chemotaxis model and identify the
main similarities and diﬀerences between a chemotaxis and a chemokinesis approach.
Afterwards, in section 4.2, we present some of the many other investigations that
could have been done. Lastly, the thesis ends with a conclusion in section 4.3.
4.1
Discussion and Main Contributions
The main reason to explore the eﬀects of chemokinesis is the desire to model bacteria
which are not able to detect chemical gradients. As accounted for in the introduction,
and shown throughout the thesis, the fact that each bacterium is not able to direct
its motion towards higher chemoattractant levels, still allows for directed motion of
the bacteria population as a whole.
We ﬁnd that the mechanisms which drive pattern formation in the chemokinesis and
chemotaxis models without population dynamics are similar: When considering the
basic chemotaxis model presented in section 1.2 in the same non-dimensionalized
framework as we have used for the chemokinesis model, a linear stability analysis, see
[Edelstein-Keshet, 2005, Chapter 11], reveals that low motility µ and high chemotactic
sensitivity χ promote patterns, just as a low minimal diﬀusivity D and a high reaction
rate γ does in the chemokinesis model. In both cases the basic idea is that bacteria
must be able to respond fast to chemoattractant levels, otherwise they will be spread
out to a uniform state by diﬀusive forces.
A notable diﬀerence between the two
models is that the chemokinesis model admits a range of bacteria densities, K, which
form unstable uniform solutions, whereas the chemotaxis model form spatial patterns
once the bacteria density is over a certain threshold (but with no upper limit). For
the basic chemotaxis model, k = 0 is also the lowest unstable wave number, which
leads us to think that chemotaxis also generates steady state patterns with one single
bacteria aggregation. Simulation of both models show transient behaviour where a
quasi-equilibrium with several patches is initially formed which then undergo patch
merging, or coarsening [Bose et al., 2013], decreasing the number of patches with
time, which also supports this assumption.
An overview of the chemotaxis model is given in [Horstmann et al., 2003], where many
variations of the model are described: For the basic chemotaxis model – known as the
minimal model – [Childress and Percus, 1981] has shown that in one dimension, steady
state patterns do not form Dirac δ-function spikes, that is, the bacteria population
stays bounded. This is similar to the behaviour we have experienced in the chemoki-

nesis model.1 In higher dimensions, however, the basic chemotaxis model develops
singular δ-function spikes, a behaviour which seems unlikely with the chemokinesis
model, as long as the bacteria always have non-zero diﬀusivity and do not constantly
seek towards higher chemoattractant concentrations, i.e. this property of the model
depends on the functional form of Db(a).
What has proven to be especially beneﬁcial in the study of the basic chemokinesis
model, is the transformation of the system into a Hamiltonian system. This has lead
to the recognition of the heteroclinic connection as the model for patch shapes in
patterns. A similar transformation is possible for the basic chemotaxis model, again
at the expense of one additional parameter (which we called C). In [Horstmann et al.,
2003] the relationship between the mean bacteria density and the new parameter
is shown (a ﬁgure similar to ﬁgure 2.5 right) and the phase plane analysis of the
chemotaxis system is done in [Bose et al., 2013]. These references show that – in
our terminology – three ﬁxed points never coexist for any value of C, and we can
rule out the existence of a heteroclinic orbit. However, when the uniform solution
is unstable, there are two ﬁxed points, a saddle and a center, and the Hamiltonian
system admits homoclinic orbits. Bose et al. have shown numerically that a one-
patch pattern is a stable steady state solution, but they have not compared this to
the level curves of the Hamiltonian and it would be of interest to determine, if one
homoclinic orbit (or more) directs patch shape, just as the heteroclinic connection
does for the chemokinesis model. In general, the chemotaxis model and its variations,
e.g. [Kang et al., 2007], [Hillen and Painter, 2009], [Murray, 2002] (but also [Hillen
and Painter, 2011] where the population dynamics are included), exhibit patterns
with spike shaped patches and zero (or near zero) bacteria density outside patches.
When adding a logistic growth term to the model, it is no longer possible to study
steady state behaviour through a transformation into a Hamiltonian system. [Hillen
and Painter, 2011] has studied the chemotaxis model with logistic growth numeri-
cally under a variety of parameter conﬁgurations. Just as we have demonstrated for
the chemokinesis model, they ﬁnd both spatially periodic steady state patterns as
well as regular time-periodic patterns and spatio-temporal irregular solutions. They
determine that the spatio-temporal patterning happens through a sequence of emerg-
ing and merging events. This is very diﬀerent from the mechanism behind spatio-
temporal chemokinesis patterns, where patterning happens through a sequence of
patch collapse and emergences interrupted by intervals of uniformly distributed bac-
teria. Bacteria which behave chemotactically form aggregations which drift towards
each other and coalesce, whereas chemokinetic bacteria form stationary patches that
each collapse due to overcrowding, see ﬁgure 4.1 for a comparison. Both models show
examples of period-doubling in a path of bifurcations from a steady state pattern to
an irregular solution.

4.2 Other Investigations
53
4.2
Other Investigations
Working on the project, many interesting ideas have turned up which are outside the
scope of the thesis. It would have been interesting to analyze some aspects of the
model further:
• Initial investigations lead us to believe that a standard choice of parameters
D and γ for the basic model is representative of the whole parameter space,
as long as they permit patterns to appear i.e. satisfy the pattern condition
(2.2). This has however not been made fully clear and could have been the
subject of further investigation. Similarly, other choices of the diﬀusion term
Db(a) could be considered.
Before settling on the used form of Db(a), we
explored the simpler form Db(a) = (a−ap)2, but the zero diﬀusivity of bacteria
experiencing their preferred level of chemoattractant leads to a singularity in the
Hamiltonian and to bacteria spikes of inﬁnite height. Also a exponential form
was considered, Db(a) = e−a in which case the bacteria always seek to increase
their experienced chemoattractant level. In some sense, this resembles the basic
chemotaxis approach more, but it introduced several numerical diﬃculties and
was quickly abandoned.
• The results from the linear stability analysis has served as a reference point
throughout the thesis, despite the fact that it does not necessarily say anything
about the behaviour of steady state patterns which are far from the uniform
solution. Taking this into account, we could have proceeded to study amplitude
equations [Cross and Hohenberg, 1993] to study what happens around the bifur-
cation point where the uniform solution changes stability. This could possibly
allow us to clarify, which wave lengths dominate long term behaviour.
• The main focus of the thesis has been steady state patterns. The transient
patterns that arise pose a separate topic of investigation. Especially interesting
would be to study the merging of patches. Following the idea in [Bose et al.,
2013] one could numerically explore the dominant eigenfunctions of a lineariza-
tion around periodic multi-patch patterns. For the chemotaxis model this has
lead to predictions about which of two merging phenomena takes place: either
patches will drift towards each other or bacteria will gradually migrate from
one patch to the other. Both of these merging phenomena have also been ob-
served in the chemokinesis model. Furthermore, one could consider studying
if merging time is dictated by the distance between patches. Throughout the
report, stability of solutions has been determined numerically by computing
eigenvalues of the Jacobian at the solution. The stability of patterns and patch
merging times could be studied using asymptotic analysis as it is done for the
chemotaxis model in [Kang et al., 2007].

considered

4 Outlook
• Outside the predicted pattern-generating interval of bacteria densities, we saw
that stable and unstable patterns coexisted with a stable uniform steady state.
In these bistable areas, we assume that small perturbations of the uniform
solution must die out, but that larger perturbations lead to patterns.
The
question is how to quantify ”small” and ”large” in this case – can for example
the variance be used to determine whether a given initial condition will lead to
patterns or not?
• When considering steady state patterns, we have regarded one full patch in
the middle of the domain to be qualitatively the same as a pattern with two
half patches at either end. If these are considered to be separate solutions, the
bifurcations at K = K−and K = K+ resemble subcritical pitchfork bifurcations
and it would have been interesting to show this formally.
For the chemokinesis model with population dynamics we have presented numerous
examples of its rich dynamics and several questions still remain open as how to
understand the pattern formation of this model:
• In order to fully determine the conﬁguration of steady state patterns under
the standard parameter set, pseudo arc-length continuation was considered –
analogous to what was done for the basic model. However, as mentioned, we
expect the wave length of steady state patterns to be ﬁnite, independent of
domain size and with a wave number somewhere between the lowest unstable
mode and the fastest growing. It is therefore desirable to make the step length
∆x a variable of the system as well, such that when solving for the solution and
the parameters K and r, we also solve for ∆x and thereby implicitly for the
wave length of the solution. This posed quite some diﬃculties and in the given
time it was not possible to make the continuation converge. I suspect that some
of these diﬃculties are related to the fact that we try to trace out a whole plane
of solutions in (K, r) space instead of a single branch of solutions which should
be easier to follow.
• When considering the spatio-temporal patterns, the simulations of the model
indicated that the same mechanisms that destabilize the uniform solution (low
D, high γ, low r), destabilize the steady state patterns and generate spatio-
temporal patterns when becoming more outspoken. Investigations remain, as
how to quantify this in order to track down which parameter sets lead to spatio-
temporal patterns and which lead to steady state patterns.
• In order to further examine the spatio-temporal patterns, we might attempt
to study the stability of the diﬀerent types of solutions. Following [Hillen and
Painter, 2011] this could be done numerically by considering the impact of a
small perturbation and whether or not this perturbation dies out. As seen for
the chemotaxis model, this could give indications about the sign of Lyapunov
exponents and possible sensitivity to initial conditions.

4.3 Conclusion
55
4.3
Conclusion
The Keller-Segel model with chemotaxis is one of the best known models describing
self-organization of bacteria colonies. It is based on the premise that bacteria move-
ment is directed. This thesis was devoted to examine the Keller-Segel model with
a diﬀerent type of micro-scale behaviour, chemokinesis. This undirected movement
of each bacterium creates a directed response in a bacteria population, which then
moves towards areas with more favourable levels of a chemical substance. We have
seen how this generates a multitude of diﬀerent patterns. Relatively simple patterns
are formed when we consider the chemokinesis phenomenon at a time scale where
bacteria proliferation does not play a role. When also regarding bacteria growth, the
simple mechanism of the bacteria – that they move less, the closer they are to their
preferred chemoattractant level – creates complex spatio-temporal patterns.
The chemokinesis model is intriguing due to the rich dynamics it presents, and we have
seen how it readily lent itself to both theoretical and numerical mathematical tools.
It serves as a prototype of how a simple feedback mechanism between individuals
and environment leads to patterns in the overall population. Patterns are a universal
phenomenon which is often encountered in nature and biology, but also outside the
natural sciences there is a need to model diﬀerent pattern phenomena. In some sense
kinesis is a weaker assumption on individual behaviour than taxis, since it implies a
more limited knowledge of the environment, and just as the Keller-Segel chemotaxis
model has found applications far beyond the initial application to slime mold, the
chemokinesis model might prove useful in settings, where the individuals cannot be
assumed to detect gradients in their environment.

APPENDIX E
Project Plan
Project objective
The aim of this project is to study the behaviour of a Keller-Segel type model with
chemokinesis. The Keller-Segel model is a reaction-diﬀusion equation with chemotaxis
which has, among other things, been used to model how bacteria react to a chemo-
attractant. In this setup, we will replace the chemotaxis term with a chemokinesis
term. This ensures that in steady state, the bacteria density does not tend to inﬁnity
at one single point. It also allows for a comparative analysis when investigating how
the chemokinetic model behaves compared to the chemotaxis model.
The analysis of the model falls into two main parts: with and without population
dynamics.
Outline
For the chemokinesis model without population dynamics, I expect to incorporate
and modify previous work done during the special course in June. Furthermore I will
do additional analysis on the model. The topics to be considered are
1. nondimensionalizing, linear stability analysis and the inﬂuence of domain size
on the stability of the uniform solution.
2. reducing the system to a coupled ODE by introducing a new parameter and
studying the Hamiltonian system that emerges using phase plane analysis.
3. Studying how the shape of steady state patterns depend on an underlying pa-
rameter, e.g. by doing pseudo arc length-continuation when varying the bacteria
density, K.
4. investigation of the stability of steady state patterns for parameters where the
uniform steady state is unstable. Speciﬁcally, I would like to investigate whether
a solution which traces out one period on the domain is always stable.
5. Analysis of the merging of two patches, thereby quantifying the metastabili-
ty/ stiﬀness of the system. This could possibly be done by asymptotical analysis
(cf. [Kang et al., 2007]).
The analysis for the model including population dynamics includes
1. linear stability analysis and pattern-generating parameter regions.

start studying the patterns of the model with population dynamics before Christmas.

some work went into recomputing results, since the model was slightly changed as was
