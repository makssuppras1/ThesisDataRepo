Abstract
Non-steroidal anti-inﬂammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are among the most widely used pharmaceutical
compounds worldwide and examples of such include the mild analgesic ibuprofen (IBU). IBU is
commonly used to alleviate mild symptoms of fever and muscular pains, and is increasingly being
used and abused by amateur and professional athletes [1,2]. Studies have suggested that IBU ex-
hibits beneﬁcial eﬀects on skeletal muscle repair after injury [3]. However, serious adverse eﬀects
have emerged in the recent years and usage of IBU has consequently been subject to scrutiny in
the public and the scientiﬁc community. Taking into account its increasing use against muscular
pains, unraveling of the regulatory eﬀects of IBU on skeletal muscle repair is therefore of great
importance [1,4,5].
RNA sequencing data derived from muscle biopsies of the Mackey et al., 2016 study was acquired
for the purpose of this project. During this study, a group ingested 1200 mg/day IBU and an-
other group received placebo (PLA) tablets. Biopsies taken on the day of electrical stimulation
(ES)-induced injury and two days later were used in this project. Through a combination of
diﬀerential gene expression analyses (DGEA), functional enrichment, gene co-expression as well
as protein-protein interaction (PPI) network analyses, the eﬀects of IBU were investigated.
The DGEA yielded signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed genes involved in heat shock responses,
ﬁbroblast proliferation, promotion of cell survival, immune responses, and AP-1 transcription
factor activities.
These included the genes HSPA6, HSPA1A, FOS, FOSB, and FOSL1.
The
ﬁndings were complemented by the gene co-expression analysis and the PPI network analysis
conducted with state-of-the-art Intomics in-house tools. This indicated that not only were the
genes diﬀerentially expressed but were likely involved in the same biological pathways and were
physically interacting. Processes indicative of skeletal muscle repair after injury were therefore
found to be signiﬁcantly upregulated and enriched in this study. However, these ﬁndings could not
be attributed directly to the prolonged use of IBU with statistical signiﬁcance. Clear batch eﬀects
were also observed which might have reduced potential signals from IBU to such an extent that
they were not detectable. Based on the results of this study, there was not substantial evidence
to support signiﬁcant eﬀects of IBU on skeletal muscle recovery after ES-induced injury. Further
research is therefore needed to unveil the potential eﬀects of the NSAID on muscle regeneration.

Introduction
Non-steroidal anti-inﬂammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are among the most commonly used pharmaceu-
tical compounds worldwide [1,4]. More than 50% of the adult Danish population was prescribed
NSAIDs during the period 1997 - 2005, and 29.6 million adults in the USA reportedly used NSAIDs
on a regular basis in 2010. The only over-the-counter NSAID available in Denmark is the widely
used, mild analgesic ibuprofen (IBU). IBU is only sold in small packages with a maximum of 20
tablets and in low dosages (200 mg) without prescription in Denmark [4]. However, it is available
at 800 - 1.200 mg for non-prescription purchase in many other countries and is also the most
frequently prescribed NSAID [5–7].
IBU is widely used to ameliorate symptoms of fever, arthritis, and chronic as well as acute pains
[1,8]. It is increasingly being used and abused in many countries including Denmark, and is the
analgesic of choice for amateur and professional athletes for the alleviation of muscular aches
[1,2]. However, in the recent years, the use of IBU has been placed under scrutiny due to raised
concerns regarding adverse eﬀects. Reportedly, these range from increased cardiovascular risk to
reduced fetal reproductive development, and most recently compensated hypogonadism in adult
males [1, 4, 5]. It is therefore of paramount importance to reach a greater understanding of the
regulatory eﬀects of the accessible and increasingly utilized drug IBU.
4.1
Ibuprofen
Figure 1: Structure of ibuprofen [9].
The mild analgesic IBU, also known as 2-p-isobutyl-
phenylpropionic acid, is at low dosages indicated to re-
lieve minor inﬂammation and aches such as toothaches,
dysmenorrhea, and muscle pains (Figure 1). At higher
dosages, IBU is commonly used for long-term treatment
of chronic degenerative inﬂammatory diseases such as
rheumatoid- and osteo-arthritis [5,10,11]. It is normally
administered orally and is primarily bound to plasma
proteins at therapeutic concentrations. IBU is admin-
istered as a racemic mixture of (R)- and (S)-enantiomers with the (S)-IBU carrying most of
the anti-inﬂammatory activity [5, 10, 12, 13]. However, 50 - 65% of (R)-IBU is inverted to the
(S)-conﬁguration by α-methylacyl-coenzyme A racemase [14]. The therapeutic concentrations of
IBU vary from ∼10 - 50 mg/l (49 - 243 µM), depending on the treatment. The drug is almost
completely metabolized upon administration where it is mainly converted to inactive metabolites
through oxidative metabolism by cytochrome P450 proteins (CYPs). IBU has a relatively short
plasma half-life of ∼1 - 3 hours, deeming frequent administration a necessity in order to maintain
therapeutic concentrations and the desired eﬀects to which IBU is ascribed [10,12,15].
4.1.1
Mechanism of action
IBU is able to enter the central nervous system (CNS) and accumulate at peripheral sites where the
proposed actions of the drug are required [10,13]. The mechanisms by which IBU is transported
intracellularly are still unclear. However, since it is a weak acid and lipid soluble, it has been
proposed that speciﬁc transporters may not be needed for the drug to cross membranes [10].
1
==PAGE:9==
Inhibition of prostaglandin synthesis.
Once transported into the cell, IBU elicits its main
eﬀect by inhibiting the enzymes cyclooxygenase (COX)-1 and COX-2 in a non-selective and re-
versible manner [4, 11]. These are encoded by the genes PTGS1 and PTGS2, respectively [10].
COX-1 is constitutively expressed in most cells and is mainly involved in housekeeping functions,
such as epithelial cytoprotection in the gastrointestinal (GI) tract and haemostasis. The expres-
sion of COX-2 is inducible by cytokines and shear stress with upregulation of expression within
several hours of acute inﬂammation [11,16]. COX-1 and -2 are also known as prostaglandin (PG)
G/H synthases and catalyze the ﬁrst committed step in the biosynthesis of prostanoids from the
unsaturated fatty acid, arachidonic acid. Prostanoids are lipid mediators that play an important
role during normal physiology and disease, and are widely synthesized in response to stimuli.
Examples of the generated prostanoids include the PGs, PGE2 and PGF2α, as well as the prosta-
cyclin PGI2 and thromboxane (Tx) A2 [10,11,16].
The prostanoids PGE2 and PGI2 have pro-inﬂammatory properties that include promotion of
increased blood ﬂow, leukocyte inﬁltration, and edema formation. Furthermore, upon activation
by the E2 and I prostanoid receptors, PGE2 and PGI2 increase vascular permeability [16]. Thus,
the anti-inﬂammatory properties of IBU are largely attributed to the inhibition of formation of
these two lipids [10]. Furthermore, COX-1 and -2 expressed in the spinal cord of the CNS release
PGs in response to peripheral pain stimuli. In this case, PGE2 and PGI2 reduce the threshold
of stimulation of nociceptor sensory neurons which leads to augmentation of pain perception [16].
An increase in membrane excitability and synaptic eﬃcacy is also obtained, which causes pain
hypersensitivity and a state of central sensitization [17]. Inhibition of the two PGs by IBU will
therefore also ameliorate mild symptoms of pain [10].
The prostanoids PGF2α and PGE2 can cause abnormal uterine hypercontractility and hypersen-
sitization of pain ﬁbres during menstruation [18]. The inhibition of formation and subsequent
release in the endometrium is therefore responsible for the eﬃcacy of IBU during dysmenorrhea
[10, 18].
Furthermore, upon T prostanoid receptor-binding in COX-1-producing platelet cells,
TxA2 causes vasoconstriction by acting on vascular smooth muscle cells, and enhances platelet
activation and recruitment [16]. It has been suggested through in vitro studies that IBU exerts
transient anti-platelet properties by reversible inhibition of COX-1 in platelets [10, 15]. Both in
vivo and in vitro studies have also shown that IBU exhibits anti-inﬂammatory properties through
inhibition of neutrophil aggregation and degranulation as well [10].
Scavenging of free radicals.
Another proposed anti-inﬂammatory action of IBU is the ability
to scavenge free radicals [10]. Immune cells such as macrophages (MPs) and neutrophils produce
speciﬁc free radicals in response to inﬂammation during the oxidative burst. Examples include
reactive oxygen species (ROS) such as hydroxyl radical HO , and reactive nitrogen species (RNS)
such as peroxynitrite anion ONOO– and nitric oxide NO. HO and ONOO– are highly reactive
radicals, involved in deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) damage and lipid oxidation. Furthermore, NO
is generated by tissue-speciﬁc NO synthases (NOSs), and is implicated in smooth muscle relaxation
and immune regulation. NO has also been shown to aﬀect synaptic plasticity in the CNS [19].
Inhibition of nitric oxide synthesis.
In vitro studies have suggested that IBU can also acti-
vate or inhibit NO production by speciﬁc NOSs. Furthermore, a study on healthy humans revealed
that therapeutic concentrations of IBU led to a reduction of exhaled NO, indicating inhibitory
eﬀects of NO production [10,19].
Thus, inhibition of prostanoid synthesis, immune cell regulation, scavenging of ROS and RNS,
and ﬁnally alteration of NO synthesis are among the pleiotropic anti-inﬂammatory and analgesic
processes to which the drug IBU is ascribed (Figure 2) [10,16,19].

4.1.2
Side eﬀects
In recent years, the use of IBU has been scrutinized due to raising concerns regarding adverse
eﬀects. These include less severe side eﬀects and serious long-term consequences, both common
for NSAIDs and currently only related to IBU. Like many NSAIDs, IBU is associated with an
increased risk of GI hemorrhage and ulceration [10,11,15]. This is largely attributed to the acidic
carboxyl moiety in the chemical structure of IBU. Furthermore, the inhibition of COX-1 produced
by platelets and on the GI mucosal layer undermines the physiological role of cytoprotective
PGs that otherwise maintain homeostasis and GI health [11]. This is especially associated with
prolonged use of IBU at high dosages [15]. Due to this, an interest has sparked in converting the
carboxyl group in IBU and other carboxyl-containing NSAIDs to an amide moiety in order to
reduce side eﬀects associated with their use [11].
Cardiovascular adverse events.
Of other side eﬀects associated with the prolonged use of IBU
is an increased risk of cardiovascular adverse events that include myocardial infarction, stroke,
and heart failure [4, 10, 11]. This is especially the case for selective COX-2 inhibitors and to a
3
==PAGE:11==
lesser extent the non-selective NSAIDs. As an example, a dosage-dependent risk between cardio-
vascular events and the COX-2 selective rofecoxib was found, which eventually led to its complete
withdrawal from the market in 2006 [4, 20]. A recent case-time-control study on the association
between NSAIDs and out-of-hospital cardiac arrest revealed that an association was also found
with the non-selective IBU. However, the short-term trial investigated high dosages with a daily
use of 2.400 mg IBU, which is well-above daily practice [4].
Fetal endocrine and reproductive development.
Evidence have emerged that exposure
to mild analgesics such as IBU can result in negative eﬀects with respect to fetal endocrine
and reproductive development [1, 2]. IBU is widely used during pregnancy to alleviate common
symptoms, and is done so with and without prescription. However, consumption of IBU during
pregnancy has been reported to be less safe than other mild analgesics such as paracetamol,
and is not recommended from week 24 of pregnancy. This was also supported by a study that
showed administration of IBU during the second trimester increased the risk of cryptochidism,
which is the main risk factor for infertility in adulthood. Furthermore, congenital abnormalities
of the urogenital tract have also been associated with the usage of IBU during the ﬁrst trimester,
however with conﬂicting ﬁndings [2].
Compensated hypogonadism.
Due to its increasing use, especially among elite athletes, and
raising concerns over declining male reproductive health, a recent study published in 2018 investi-
gated the eﬀects of IBU on the human pituitary-gonadal axis. This included a clinical intervention
trial which was part of a broader study that also focused on muscle tissues, described further be-
low. During the trial, a group of healthy Danish men was administered 1.200 mg/day of IBU and
a group ingested placebo (PLA) tablets for six weeks [1, 3]. Interestingly, the study found that
levels of luteinizing hormone (LH) signiﬁcantly increased by 33% and the testosterone (TST)/LH
ratio decreased with 23% after 44 days of IBU usage. Pituitary LH stimulates the TST production
in Leydig cells, and the ratio between the two is often used as a clinical marker of Leydig cell
function. Therefore, the study suggested that prolonged use of IBU induced a state of compen-
sated hypogonadism, deﬁned as regular levels of TST and elevated levels of LH [1, 21, 22]. Due
to the increasing concerns regarding male reproductive health [1], the publication caught public
attention and IBU was scrutinized in news outlets such as The Guardian and CBS News [23,24].
Skeletal muscle recovery.
Preceding the above publication, a study on the role of IBU on
skeletal muscle regeneration after damage was published in 2016. As eccentric muscle contrac-
tions inﬂict more damage than voluntary, electrical stimulation (ES) of the muscles was employed
for the study [3, 25]. The inﬂuence of NSAIDs on muscle recovery after damage has previously
been studied, but no consensus on potentially beneﬁcial eﬀects of NSAIDs on the repair of acute
muscle injury currently exists among physicians in Denmark. However, the study hypothesized
that IBU would exhibit negative eﬀects on the regeneration of muscle tissue and extracellular
matrix (ECM) remodeling, the latter of which is a proposed key regulator of muscle repair and
protection against injury. Interestingly, the results of the study suggested that IBU increased the
levels of muscle cell regeneration and activation of muscle precursor cells following injury [3].
IBU is increasingly being used and is the preferred NSAID against muscle pains for amateur as
well as professional athletes [1]. Furthermore, the presented evidence suggest that sub-prescription
levels of IBU might have beneﬁcial eﬀects on muscle regeneration after damage. However, consid-
ering the well-established and emerging adverse eﬀects attributed to the accessible analgesic, and
its increasing use and misuse for alleviation of muscle pains by athletes, it is of great importance
to unravel the eﬀects of IBU on skeletal muscle recovery [1,3].

4.2
Skeletal muscle recovery
Skeletal muscles have evolved throughout time to allow for movement in humans and harbour
extraordinary regenerative capabilities following muscle tissue damage. Functional constituents
of the skeletal muscle include bundles of cylindrical ﬁbres, referred to as myoﬁbres, that generate
force by contraction. The multi-nucleated myoﬁbres consist of myoﬁbrils that are comprised of
sarcomeres. The sarcomeres contain thousands of actin- and myosin-ﬁlaments that produce mus-
cle force upon their interaction. Furthermore, precursor muscle cells, also known as satellite cells
(SCs), are located on the surface of the myoﬁbres between the basal lamina and plasmalemma
[26,27] (Figure 3).
Exercise-induced muscle damage has been studied extensively over the course of 30 years. The
condition includes transient structural myoﬁbrillar disruption, loss of muscle strength, muscle
soreness, and systemic eﬄux of myocellular proteins. Following tissue damage, a myriad of cell
types and chemical processes are initiated by the subsequent mechanical alterations and metabolic
stress. During exercise-induced muscle damage, overstretching of sarcomeres beyond their ﬁlament
overlaps can occur, which can lead to a term called "popped sarcomeres". This causes membrane
disruption and inﬂux of calcium in the cytosol through damaged sections of the membrane of
the myoﬁber bundles. Consequently, inﬂammatory cells such as neutrophils and MPs, vascular
cells, ﬁbroblasts, and SCs will interact within the ECM of the aﬀected muscles. These complex
interactions will subsequently determine the muscle tissue recovery [25,28].
In the wake of injury, the processes of skeletal muscle degeneration and necrosis of myoﬁbres are
initiated, where the latter is caused by the inﬂux of calcium that activates proteolysis of the ﬁbres
[27]. After myoﬁber necrosis, muscle regeneration is initiated where new muscle tissue is formed.
The three phases ascribed to the regeneration process subsequently take place; inﬂammation, tis-
sue reconstruction, and tissue remodeling [29–31].

4.2.1
Inﬂammation
Throughout the years, it has been increasingly accepted that inﬂammation is a key process dur-
ing muscular repair and regeneration [28]. During necrosis of injured myoﬁbres, the ﬁbres are
inﬁltrated by inﬂammatory cells such as neutrophils, mast cells, and MPs that phagocytize the
necrotic myoﬁbres and debris [28, 30]. In the ﬁrst 1 - 4 hours after damage, the site of injury is
heavily inﬁltrated by neutrophils that clear cellular debris and further stimulate pro-inﬂammatory
responses. Neutrophils have also been shown to secrete proteolytic enzymes, and the aforemen-
tioned ROS and RNS to promote inﬂammation. Furthermore, mast cells secrete histamines and
a range of chemoattractants that induce recruitment of immune cells such as MPs [28].
After 4 - 24 hours of tissue damage, MPs inﬁltrate the aﬀected tissue [28]. MPs have been shown
to play a pivotal role in tissue homeostasis and in doing so, are involved in both pro- and anti-
inﬂammatory processes, depending on their activation proﬁle [3, 32, 33]. Pro-inﬂammatory MPs
are initially recruited to the site of inﬂammation by chemoattractants. Of those include the pro-
tein urokinase-type plasminogen activator (uPA), encoded by the gene PLAU, that is secreted by
circulating monocytes at the site of injury [27]. The recruited MPs are subsequently activated
by cytokines, produced by type 1 T-helper (Th1) lymphocytes, and secrete ROS and cytokines
themselves in order to propagate inﬂammation and initiate myocellular proliferation [28,32]. The
cytokines secreted by the MPs include tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-α and interleukin (IL)-1β
[27]. Furthermore, pro-inﬂammatory MPs that have been activated by Th2-produced cytokines
are associated with ECM remodeling and angiogenesis, a process through which new blood vessels
are generated from existing vessels [28,32].
The described pro-inﬂammatory processes that take place as a result of injury may also cause
transient damaging eﬀects on the muscle tissue, such as the release of ROS. In an attempt to
counteract these eﬀects, metabolic stress responses are initiated during the inﬂammatory phase.
This is usually obtained by an upregulated expression of heat shock proteins (HSPs) [28,34]. Ex-
amples of such include members of the HSP70 family, such as HSPA6. Of interest, studies have
shown that expression of HSP70 is greatly upregulated within the ﬁrst 24 hours of muscle tissue
injury, including ES-induced damage [25, 34, 35]. Members of the HSP70 family are character-
ized as chaperones and their main function are to fold newly synthesized and denatured proteins
that are produced during cellular stress. Interestingly, HSP70s promote cell survival and protein
synthesis through the Akt/mTOR pathway. The chaperones are also involved in regulation of cy-
tokine expression when counteracting the transiently detrimental, pro-inﬂammatory eﬀects during
skeletal muscle recovery [35,36].
Besides the pro-inﬂammatory responses, anti-inﬂammatory processes are also activated during the
ﬁrst phase of skeletal muscle regeneration, as previously mentioned. The anti-inﬂammatory MPs,
activated by immune complexes and Toll-like receptor (TLR) ligands, secrete cytokines such as
IL-10 and transforming growth factor (TGF)-β to dampen the inﬂammatory response at the site
of injury. However, this is mainly occurring in the late phases of the inﬂammatory stage [32].
4.2.2
Tissue reconstruction
After inﬂammation and removal of cellular debris, the process of tissue reconstruction is initiated
[27]. Studies have shown that muscle regeneration and reconstruction cannot take place with
the absence of SCs, proving that the role of the precursor muscle cells during this stage is both
crucial and well-established [3, 26, 29]. Furthermore, GFs are intimately involved in this process
and include hepatocyte growth factor (HGF), ﬁbroblast growth factors (FGFs), insulin-like growth
factor (IGF)-I, IGF-II, and TGF-β [27,29].
6
==PAGE:14==
Studies have revealed that HGF is a substantial activator of cell-cycle reentry of SCs upon binding
to its receptor c-Met, encoded by the gene MET. It does so by promoting transition from the quies-
cent G0 state to the G1 phase through the p38 mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) and the
phosphoinositide 3-kinase (PI3K) pathways. Furthermore, FGFs released from damaged myoﬁbres
promote proliferation of SCs and the ﬁbroblasts, described further below [36,37]. IGF-I promotes
protein synthesis and cell survival through the PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling pathway, and is also
involved in activation and proliferation of SCs through the MAPK kinase (MAPKK) pathway [36].
TGF-β, which functions both as a GF and cytokine, displays inhibitory eﬀects on myogenic re-
sponses together with stimulating eﬀects on muscle regeneration. It is also involved in regulation
of ECM remodeling and reduction of inﬂammation, as previously mentioned. Furthermore, stud-
ies have shown that inhibition of TGF-β leads to a rapid short-term recovery of muscle strength
after injury but at the same time impairs long-term structural regeneration, which speaks to its
intricate role in muscular recovery [38].
In response to the described GFs and cytokines of which are released from damaged myoﬁbres
and inﬂammatory cells, the SCs begin to proliferate at the site of injury [27,31]. Committed SCs
begin to diﬀerentiate into myoblasts, which are deﬁned as undiﬀerentiated precursor muscle cells.
Furthermore, undiﬀerentiated stem SCs initiate proliferation after 24 hours into both myoblasts
and new SCs that will be present for the potential need for future regeneration [31]. Within the
ﬁrst days of injury, the newly generated myoblasts with subsequently fuse in order to form new
myoﬁbres. After 5 - 6 days, the new myoﬁbres will replace the necrotized regions of the injured
myoﬁbres and initiate migration through the connective scar tissue [27,29,31]. Furthermore, an-
giogenesis also takes place at the site of injury, induced by the pro-inﬂammatory MPs. Following
the regeneration of myoﬁbres, the remodeling phase of the reconstructed tissue is initiated [28,31].
4.2.3
Tissue remodeling
The ﬁnal stage of skeletal muscle recovery after injury is tissue remodeling. During this phase, the
newly formed myoﬁbres mature and become attached to regenerating myoﬁbres [27,36]. Cell-cell
contact is crucial for the diﬀerentiation and fusion of the newly generated myoﬁbres [39]. In order
to fuse, the scar tissue will retract and pull the ends of the regeneration myoﬁber fragments in
closer vicinity [31]. After the reconstruction, the new myoﬁbres must therefore migrate in order
to ﬁnd neighboring ﬁbres [39].
The described GFs, HGF and FGFs also act as chemoattractants for the migration of myoﬁbres
in the tissue remodeling phase. Furthermore, some of the ECM constituents such as integrins,
laminin, ﬁbronectin, and hyaluronic acid (HA) are involved in signal transduction between the
migrating myoﬁbres. Interestingly, the glycosaminoglycan HA is believed to enhance the diﬀeren-
tiation and maturation of SCs by interacting with its primary receptor cluster of diﬀerentiation
(CD) 44.
CD44 is a key regulator of numerous physiological processes such as inﬂammation,
angiogenesis, cell-matrix anchorage, and recycling of ECM proteins. The receptor is found on
migrating cells and regions of actin polymerization, and loss of CD44 has been associated with
inhibition of MP recruitment at the site of inﬂammation and fusion of the newly formed ﬁbres [39].
The ECM is suggested to be a key regulator of muscle repair [3]. The ECM is mainly comprised
of collagen types I and III in skeletal muscles, and provides the muscles with the ability to endure
stress and transmit force to the tendons (Figure 4) [40]. This structural constituent is primarily
synthesized by the aforementioned ﬁbroblasts that are characterized as stromal cells [31]. The
ﬁbroblasts ensure proper regeneration and remodeling of the aﬀected myoﬁbres by producing
ECM proteins and interacting with SCs [37]. Activation of the otherwise quiescent ﬁbroblasts
7
==PAGE:15==
is employed by the transcription factor (TF) complex activator protein (AP)-1. This complex is
involved in promotion of transcription of inﬂammatory cytokines and ﬁbroblast proliferation, and
is comprised of dimeric complexes such as members of the Jun and Fos families [41,42]. Further-
more, ECM production by ﬁbroblasts is also promoted by the cytokines TGF-β and TNF-α [40].
During the process of tissue remodeling, extensive remodeling as well as degradation of the
ﬁbroblast-produced ECM are also occurring. Several proteases have been implicated in this pro-
cess, but studies have shown that activation of zinc-dependent matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)
bear the main responsibility. These are under normal circumstances only expressed during events
where tissue remodeling occur, such as embryonic development and wound healing [19].
An intricate web of cellular and biochemical events between a heap of cell types occur during the
three phases of skeletal muscle recovery. As a result, the cellular architecture is under normal
circumstances restored approximately two weeks after injury, which truly is a testament to the
extraordinary regenerative capabilities to which the human skeletal muscles are ascribed [27,36].
The exact inﬂuence of the analgesic and anti-inﬂammatory properties of IBU on the skeletal mus-
cle regeneration after injury is still unknown [3,25]. Further research is therefore needed to unravel
the regulatory eﬀects of the NSAID on this intricate process [1].

4.3
Aims of project
IBU is increasingly being used and abused, and is the preferred NSAID against muscular pains by
amateur as well as professional athletes (Figure 5) [1,2]. As a frame of reference, the former chief
medical oﬃcer of the Fédération Internationale de Football Association (FIFA) stated in 2017
that approximately half of the professional soccer players at the last three World Cups routinely
administered NSAIDs, including IBU [44]. However, use of IBU has been placed under scrutiny
in the recent years by the scientiﬁc community as well as the public due to raising concerns with
respect to adverse eﬀects [1,4,5].
A previous study conducted by Danish scientists in 2016 suggested that IBU might have beneﬁcial
eﬀects on skeletal muscle recovery after ES-induced injury by expediting muscle regeneration.
However, further research is needed to elucidate the exact role of IBU during this process, as
there is still a lack of consensus among Danish physicians of its potentially beneﬁcial eﬀects for
muscle repair [3]. Considering the presented adverse eﬀects associated with IBU and its increasing
use, the aim of this project is to reach a greater understanding of the regulatory eﬀects of IBU on
skeletal muscle recovery. In doing so, an integrative transcriptomics approach is employed where
the speciﬁc objectives of this project are the following:
• Identiﬁcation of diﬀerentially expressed genes relevant to IBU and muscle regeneration
• Identiﬁcation of networks of co-expressed genes of which are potentially involved in the
same biological pathways
• Investigation of biological eﬀects of IBU at a protein-protein interaction (PPI) network
level by use of Intomics A/S in-house developed tools
• Gain a functional understanding of signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed genes and net-
works of biological interest through a functional enrichment analysis (FEA)
Through the use of bioinformatics and systems biology applications, signiﬁcant upregulation of
biologically relevant processes related to recovery from ES-induced muscle injury were found.
These included heat shock responses, AP-1 TF activity, promotion of cell survival, and ﬁbroblast
proliferation. No eﬀects on these processes were found to be directly attributed to IBU in this
study. However, since strong batch eﬀects were detected, further research is needed to elucidate
potentially regulatory eﬀects of IBU on skeletal muscle recovery.

4.4
Intomics in-house tools
Intomics A/S is a consulting company specialized in integrative bioinformatics and systems bi-
ology approaches for internationally renowned pharmaceutical enterprises.
The company has
developed state-of-the-art tools, including the proprietary PPI network InBio Map™that was
deemed best-in-class in a benchmark study conducted by Harvard scientists in Nature Methods
[45]. The InBio Map™database consists of more than 700.000 physical PPIs of nearly 17.500
human reviewed UniProt proteins [46]. In comparison, KEGG and Reactome pathways cover
around 6.700 and 7.700 human proteins, respectively [47]. Thus, a wider biological framework is
obtainable with the Intomics-developed database and is therefore utilized throughout this project.
In order to conduct PPI network analyses for the purpose of this study, networks are generated
from the InBio Map™database by the in-house tool VirtualPulldown. Each PPI is assigned a
conﬁdence score between 0 and 1 that represents a measure of the possibility of the interaction
being true, based on experimental support and number of publications in the PubMed database.
The conﬁdence score is thus used to create sets of high-conﬁdence PPIs for the downstream anal-
yses [45].
Once the networks have been constructed, SystemSignificance computes their signiﬁcance by
utilizing values associated with proteins in the system, such as log2 fold-changes (L2FCs) or p-
values. Initially, integrated scores or p-values of the systems are computed, depending on the
type of associated values and integration method of choice. Random systems are subsequently
generated up to 107 times where new p-values or scores are computed and compared to the orig-
inal system. The signiﬁcance of the system is ﬁnally computed as the fraction of all the random
systems that yielded more extreme scores or p-values than originally. Thus, through extensive
permutations, biologically interesting PPI networks generated by unsupervised, data-driven tests
by the Intomics tools can be further investigated.

5.2
Data preprocessing
Quality control (QC) of raw and preprocessed reads was done using FastQC version 0.11.7 [48].
In order to reduce the biased selection of random primers at the beginning of reads, especially
associated with RNA sequencing, the 5’ ends were trimmed [49]. Based on the per base sequence
content estimated by FastQC, the ﬁrst 15 positions of the reads were removed with prinseq-lite
version 0.20.4 [50]. Adapters were removed with cutadapt version 1.16 with an allowed error rate
of 10%. The same tool was used for 5’ and 3’ trimming with a minimum base call quality of
30 together with a minimal read length of 30 after a potential adapter cut and trimming [51].
Re-pairing of preprocessed reads was performed by pairfq version 0.17.0 [52].
5.3
Alignment and gene quantiﬁcation
For the quantiﬁcation of gene abundances of the preprocessed paired-end reads, RSEM version 1.3.0
was used [53]. However, before gene abundances were estimated, read mapping was done by call-
ing the STAR aligner version 2.6.1a [54] within RSEM. The hg38 human assembly [55] was prepared
and STAR indexed by rsem-prepare-reference. rsem-calculate-expression was used for read
mapping together with gene quantiﬁcation. 10 threads and the option –-paired-end were uti-
lized. Average read length and quality was determined by samtools stats [56].
5.4
Diﬀerential gene expression analysis
Diﬀerential gene expression analysis (DGEA) was performed by the R-implemented DESeq2 version
1.20.0 on the estimated gene abundances [57]. For this analysis and the following R-implemented
tools, Rstudio with R version 3.5.0 was utilized [58]. The data was imported to R with tximport
in order to normalize the counts on gene lengths and correct for potential changes in gene lengths
across samples [59]. Genes of which had summed counts below 10 across all samples were removed
prior to the analyses in order to reduce noise in the data.
5.4.1
Exploratory data analysis
Prior to the DGEA, an exploratory data analysis was conducted in order to investigate potential
batch eﬀects on the data. The data set was prepared with DESeqDataSetFromTximport, including
the full design information on the condition (ctrl/stim), group (PLA/IBU), and time (day 0/2).
Technical replicates were collapsed with collapseReplicates. The gene abundances were normal-
ized with varianceStabilizingTransformation (VST), in order to approximate homogeneity of
variance and account for diﬀerences in sequencing depths [60]. For the purpose of comparing the
samples in an unbiased manner without including prior information, blind = TRUE was applied.
A principal component analysis (PCA) was conducted on the VST counts using prcomp with scale
= TRUE, as the expression data diﬀered by orders of magnitudes. The principal components (PCs)
were visualized with ggplot2 and samples were colored by sequencing lanes (Appendix section
10.1, Table 4). A heatmap of the Euclidean distances of the 500 most abundant genes from the
VST matrix was also made together with hierarchical clustering of the biological data and batch
information [61]. This was done with Heatmap from ComplexHeatmap [62].
13
==PAGE:21==
The exploratory data analysis revealed that clustering of the samples occurred with respect to
the sequencing lanes rather than the experimental design (Figures 8 and 9). Batch correction was
therefore performed with svaseq from the sva library on VST counts where the option blind =
FALSE was applied [63,64]. This option ensured that the full design information of the study was
included [65]. PCA plots were made and the batch corrected data was saved for later use.
5.4.2
Diﬀerential gene expression
As it was of interest to investigate the diﬀerential gene expression (DGE) between combinations of
the experimental factors, the variables; condition, groups, and time, were combined into one (con-
ditionGroupsTime) and applied as the design. The sequencing lanes were included as a co-variate,
in order to account for the variability explained the batch eﬀect. DESeq was run for estimation
of size factors that correct for varying sequencing depths, along with gene dispersion estimation,
and linear ﬁtting of the data [57].
L2FCs and original p-values were extracted with the results function. In order to specify an
alternative hypothesis for the Wald test of signiﬁcance, the optimal threshold for the L2FCs
lfcThreshold was determined by testing the values; 0, 0.585, and 1. The following statistical
tests (n = 7) were made:
• CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0
• CtrlPLA0 vs CtrlIBU0
• CtrlPLA0 vs StimPLA0
• StimIBU0 vs StimIBU2
• StimPLA0 vs StimIBU0
• StimPLA0 vs StimPLA2
• StimPLA2 vs StimIBU2
The original p-values estimated by the results functions were adjusted for multiple testing by the
Benjamini & Hochberg (BH) method with p.adjust [66]. The number of tests was the summed
number of genes for all the listed comparisons. Genes of which had |L2FC| ≥lfcThreshold with
BH-adjusted p-values (pAdj) ≤0.05 were considered signiﬁcant. Based on the number of signiﬁ-
cant L2FCs, the optimal threshold was determined to be 0.585. Volcano plots of the L2FCs were
made with ggplot2, where the ensembl IDs were converted to gene IDs with biomaRt version 3.8
[67]. L2FCs, original p-values, and pAdjs were saved for later use.
5.5
Gene co-expression network analysis
The weighted gene co-expression analysis was conducted with WGCNA version 1.63 and the investi-
gation was based on the batch corrected VST counts [68]. The data consisted of 26.827 ensembl
IDs for the 72 samples, as genes with summed counts across all samples below 10 had been removed
and technical replicates had already been collapsed.

==PAGE:22==
5.5.1
Topological overlap
For the gene-wise adjacency calculations, the soft-thresholding power β, to which the co-expression
similarity of the genes was raised, was estimated ﬁrst [68]. Powers ranging from 1 - 20 were tested
with pickSoftThreshold, using the options blockSize = 8.000, and RsquaredCut = 0.85. Bi-
weight mid-correlations and the signed network type were applied for this and the following
calculations.
The lowest β to satisfy a scale-free network topology ﬁt index R2 of 0.85 was
determined to be β = 6 (Appendix section 10.4.1, Figure 10.4.1). Adjacencies calculated with
adjacency were transformed into a topological overlap matrix (TOM) with TOMsimilarity, in
order to reduce noise [68]. Clustering was performed on the dissimilarity matrix 1 - TOM with
hclust using method = "average".
5.5.2
Module identiﬁcation and investigation
In order to identify clusters in the network, cutreeDynamic was used with deepSplit = 2. The
optimal minModuleSize was estimated by testing values; 40, 75, and 100. Based on the number
of modules and their respective sizes, minModuleSize = 40 was chosen (Appendix section 10.4.2,
Table 7). Module eigengenes (MEs) were determined by moduleEigengenes and their dissimilar-
ities were deﬁned as 1 - cor(ME). In order to reduce redundancy, similar modules were merged
with mergeCloseModules using cutHeight = 0.1, and visualized by plotDendroAndColors.
To study how the modules related to the meta data (traits), a network of the MEs and traits
was made with plotEigengeneNetworks. Correlations and student asymptotic p-values, calcu-
lated with corPvalueStudent, were visualized with labeledHeatmap. The strongest module-trait
correlations were between condition (ctrl/stim) and brown (M1) and magenta (M2), which were
further investigated (Appendix section 10.4.4, Figure 22). Scaled gene expression proﬁles of the
two modules were visualized by barplots and heatmaps with Heatmap.
The relationship between gene signiﬁcance (GS) for the condition trait and the intramodular con-
nectivity of M1 (n = 2.426) and M2 (n = 800) was also investigated in order to detect potential
hub genes of biological interest. Node degrees were estimated with intramodularConnectivity
and the GS was calculated as the absolute correlation between condition and the expression data.
Genes with GS(condition) > 0.2, and an absolute correlation between the expression proﬁle and
ME(M1/M2) > 0.8 were saved for downstream analyses.
5.6
Protein-protein interaction network analysis
For the PPI network analysis of the expression data, Intomics in-house tools were utilized. A
global, data-driven investigation of all human reviewed UniProt proteins in the InBio Map™
database was conducted, followed by an analysis of the co-expressed proteins of M1 and M2.
5.6.1
Global network analysis
A naïve 1st order PPI network was constructed of the InBio Map™database (update 2018-09-01)
with VirtualPulldown version 3.11.1 using a conﬁdence score ≥0.1. SystemSignificance ver-
sion 3.11.1 was used to quantify the signiﬁcance of the networks where L2FCs and p-values from
the tests performed with DESeq2 were utilized. As the p-values were randomly permuted up to
107 times for the calculations, the original, unadjusted p-values were used. For the calculations
by SystemSignificance, the arithmetic mean was used as the integration method when using
L2FCs for the protein-associated values. The highest |L2FC| was utilized for genes that mapped
to the same protein. When using p-values, the Edgington’s integration method was applied and
the lowest value for duplicates was used [69]. In order to make the results reproducible, a random
15

seed of 42 was speciﬁed. A threshold of signiﬁcance of < 10−5 was used.
For redundancy reduction, similar signiﬁcant modules were merged with SystemMerger version
3.11.1. The overlap o between networks A and B was calculated as:
o =
w|A ∩B|
|A ∪B| + (w −1)|A ∩B|
The Sørensen-Dice coeﬃcient w = 2 was used and networks with o ≥0.5 were merged [70, 71] .
Lastly, network ﬁles were converted from extensible graph markup and modeling language (xgmml)
to cys format with CytoSession version 3.11.1 and visualized in Cytoscape version 3.7.0 [72].
5.6.2
Network analysis of co-expressed proteins
For the PPI network analysis of modules M1 and M2, 0th order networks were constructed with
VirtualPulldown. The generated networks of M1 (n = 1.768) and M2 (n = 711) were converted
to cys ﬁles with CytoSession and imported to Cytoscape. Sub-clustering was performed with
the Markov Clustering Algorithm (MCL) [73] from clusterMaker2, using default parameters [74].
As the initial MCL on M1 yielded a cluster of size n = 943, the tool was re-run. M1 and M2
sub-clusters of size of n ≥8 were tested for signiﬁcance with SystemSignificance, as previously
described. Clusters of signiﬁcance < 10−5 were further investigated.
5.7
Functional enrichment analysis
In order to gain a functional understanding of the signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed genes, an
FEA was performed with gProfileR version 0.6.7 [75]. The expression data was used as back-
ground, electronic annotations were excluded, and BH multiple correction with a threshold of
pAdj ≤0.05 was applied. The identiﬁed gene ontology (GO) terms were visualized in tag clouds
using REVIGO [76].
FEA of the PPI networks was performed by the Intomics tool SystemAnnotator version 3.11.1 that
analyzed the system for GO overrepresentation in the GO Annotation Database (UniProt-GOA),
and computed p-values with a one-sided Fisher’s exact test. GO terms of which had p-values that
were the smallest compared to all child terms were considered signiﬁcantly overrepresented when
using Bonferroni correction (p-value ≤0.05/n) [77]. Finally, these were visualized with REVIGO.

6.3.2
Diﬀerential gene expression
Following the exploratory data analysis, a DGEA was conducted on the samples with DESeq2.
The input was abundances estimated for 58.336 genes by RSEM. However, genes with summed
counts below 10 across all samples were removed prior the analysis, resulting in a list of 26.827
genes. The DGEs were calculated by performing 7 diﬀerent two-sided Wald tests of signiﬁcance
of the L2FCs, using a threshold of |L2FC| ≥0.585 with pAdj ≤0.05.
No DGE between baselines.
As the IBU group received 1200 mg/day of IBU two weeks prior
to the day of ES-induced injury (day 0), it was of interest to test whether IBU had an eﬀect on
the baseline samples. Therefore, the comparison CtrlPLA0 vs CtrlIBU0 was tested. The results
showed only one signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed gene with a L2FC of -23.39 (pAdj = 3.64E-2)
for the gene MMP1. The test therefore revealed that IBU did not cause signiﬁcant diﬀerences in
the baseline samples (Table 3).
Signiﬁcant DGE between ctrl and stim samples.
In order to study the response to in-
jury on the day of ES with usage of IBU, the statistical test CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 was made.
This comparison yielded the highest number of diﬀerentially expressed genes and resulted in 107
signiﬁcant hits. Interestingly, the highest L2FC of 7.65 (pAdj = 5.81E-06) was obtained for the
gene HSPA6, which encodes HSP70B’. This was followed by the genes FOSB, FOSL1, and FOS,
with L2FCs of 7.37, 6.56, and 6.52, respectively. These genes encode members of the Fos protein
family, and represent subunits of the AP-1 TF complex involved in proliferation of ﬁbroblasts and
promotion of transcription of inﬂammatory cytokines [41,42]. Furthermore, the gene HBEGF is
a strong mitogen for ﬁbroblasts and smooth muscle cells when expressed by Th cells, and was also
overexpressed in this comparison [78] (Figure 11a).
A signiﬁcant upregulation of the gene EGR1 with a L2FC of 6.31 was observed in response to
the injury with IBU usage. The gene encodes the TF, early growth response protein 1, and is
involved in the regulation of cellular proliferation and production of LH in the pituitary gland
[79–81]. A signiﬁcant upregulation was also found for the HSPs, HSPA1A, HSPA1B, and HSPA7.
Additionally, the gene IKZF3 was overexpressed in the stim samples and encodes a member of
the Ikaros family of TFs involved in lymphocyte proliferation [82]. Interestingly, an upregulation
was seen of the chemoattractant PLAU together with its primary inhibitor SERPINE1 [83]. The
gene ATF3 was also overexpressed. Of interest, this encodes a TF that is able to bind AP-1
promoters and repress transcription of subsets of cytokines, and is therefore able to reduce in-
ﬂammatory responses. Furthermore, ATF3 has been linked to repression of cell cycle genes in
ﬁbroblasts, such as cyclin D1, and can thereby halt progression from the G1 to S cell cycle phase
[42]. Signiﬁcant upregulation of cAMP-responsive element-binding protein (CREB) 5, encoded by
CREB5, was also observed. CREBs have been known to bind AP-1 and activate transcription [84].
To investigate the response to ES-induced injury without the usage of IBU, the comparison
CtrlPLA0 vs StimPLA0 was made. The highest L2FC of 7.56 (pAdj = 3.78E-02) was estimated
for the gene GRB7. This gene encodes an adapter protein that is implicated in the regulation
of cell migration [85]. Interestingly, this test also yielded signiﬁcantly upregulated HSPs, namely
HSPH1 and HSPA1L, but not HSPA6. Signiﬁcant overexpression of IKZF3 and CREB5 in the
stim samples without IBU usage was observed as well (Figure 11b).
DGE after two days of injury in the same group.
The comparison StimIBU0 vs StimIBU2
was made to study whether signiﬁcant changes had occurred in the expression proﬁles after two
days of ES-induced injury with the use of IBU. 12 signiﬁcant hits were found for this test, where
the highest L2FC of 6.12 (pAdj = 8.55E-3) was found for CD79A. Furthermore, a myosin-binding
protein, encoded by MYBPH, was also signiﬁcantly upregulated after two days, together with

==PAGE:29==
ATF3. An underexpression of SERPINH1 with a L2FC of -3.11 was observed, a gene of which
encodes the collagen-speciﬁc chaperone HSP47 [86] (Figure 11c).
To study the response in the stimulated samples after two days of injury without IBU, the test
StimPLA0 vs StimPLA2 was conducted. This resulted in 20 signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed

No DGE in stim samples due to IBU usage.

6.3.3
Functional enrichment analysis of diﬀerentially expressed genes
In order to functionally annotate the signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed genes, an FEA was per-
formed with gProfileR. The DGEA revealed that the statistical tests CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0,
CtrlPLA0 vs StimPLA0, StimIBU0 vs StimIBU2, and StimPLA0 vs StimPLA2 yielded DGE. An
FEA was subsequently performed on the signiﬁcant hits for each of these tests. No signiﬁcant GO
overrepresentation was found for the tests StimIBU0 vs StimIBU2, and StimPLA0 vs StimPLA2.
However, the results showed GO enrichment for the tests with the highest number of signiﬁcant
L2FCs, namely CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 and CtrlPLA0 vs StimPLA0.
The FEA of CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 showed that a large proportion of the biological process GOs
were child terms of ’response to unfolded protein’ (GO:0006986). Interestingly, these included
’response to cytokine’, ’response to IL-1’, ’response to wounding’, ’cellular response to stress’,
’response to oxidative stress’, ’response to GF’, and ’regulation of immune system process’. The
GO term ’regulation of protein kinase activity’ (GO:0045859) was also enriched, with child GO
terms such as ’negative regulation of MAPK cascade’ and ’activation of MAPKKK activity’.
For molecular function GOs, the parent term ’TF AP-1 complex’ (GO:0035976) was signiﬁcantly
enriched in the CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 comparison. This was supported by the signiﬁcant overex-
pression of the AP-1 subunit genes FOSB, FOSL1, and FOS, together with the AP-1 associated
genes ATF3 and CREB5 (Figure 11a). Furthermore, the cellular component terms ’HSP bind-
ing’ and ’HSP70 binding’ were also enriched, relating to the upregulation of HSPs observed for
this comparison. Of interest, overrepresentation of the GOs ’integrin’ and ’glycosaminoglycan
binding’ were also observed. These are constituents of the ECM, with HA being an example of a
glycosaminoglycan [39] (Appendix section 10.5.1, Figures 23-25).
The results of the FEA of the test CtrlPLA0 vs StimPLA0 showed enrichment of the biological
process GO terms ’regulation of cellular response to stress’ (GO:0080135) and ’cell cycle arrest’
(GO:0007050) (Appendix section 10.5.1, Figure 26).
6.4
Gene co-expression network analysis
In addition to the DGEA, it was of interest to conduct a gene co-expression network analysis
in order to determine modules of co-expressed genes, potentially involved in the same biological
pathways. This was done with WGCNA using the batch corrected VST counts with an input size
of 26.827 genes. Applying a minModuleSize of 40, 17 co-expressed modules were determined by
WGCNA (Appendix section 10.4.3, Figure 21). The modules were generally very large with most

Discussion
NSAIDs are among the most widely used pharmaceutical drugs worldwide and are administered
to alleviate mild symptoms of fever and arthritis together with chronic and acute pains. Examples
include the mild analgesic, IBU which is the NSAID of choice for amateur and professional athletes
for the alleviation of muscular pains. With the emergence of serious adverse eﬀects associated
with IBU, such as increased cardiovascular risk, reduced fetal reproductive and endocrine develop-
ment, and compensated hypogonadism in adult males, the use of IBU has been scrutinized in the
recent years [1,4]. However, studies have also suggested that IBU may have beneﬁcial eﬀects on
skeletal muscle recovery after muscular damage [3,25]. Considering the increasing use and abuse
of IBU against muscle pains by amateur and professional athletes, with the latter having caught
public attention, it is of great importance to unravel and elucidate the regulatory eﬀects of IBU
on skeletal muscle recovery after injury [1,2,23,24,44].
7.1
Bioinformatics and systems biology approaches
The development of high-throughput 2nd generation sequencing techniques within the last 20
years have vastly expanded the realms of bioinformatics and systems biology. The ﬁeld of network
theory has over time revolutionized the view on cellular biology and has increasingly become of
great relevance. Consequently, it has become possible to perform powerful transcriptome analyses
on biologically relevant issues, such as transcript quantiﬁcation and DGEA, together with PPI and
gene co-expression network analyses [53, 68, 91]. In order to study the eﬀects of IBU on skeletal
muscle recovery after ES-induced injury, an integrated transcriptomics approach of bioinformatics
and systems biology application was therefore employed. Evidently, the use of the tools included
in this study involved a series of decisions regarding design-setups and parameters of which can
inﬂuence the results and their subsequently interpretation. These will be discussed in the sections
below.
7.1.1
RSEM
To unveil genes of relevance for IBU and muscle regeneration after injury, alignment and transcript
quantiﬁcation of the provided RNA sequencing data was performed with the tool RSEM that called
the STAR aligner. RSEM utilizes the alignments produced by STAR as "candidate" sets of align-
ments and subsequently applies sophisticated statistical models to align and estimate transcript
abundances with high accuracy. The strength of utilizing RSEM for estimation of gene abundances
compared to many other tools is its ability to handle ambiguous mapping of reads. Furthermore,
it is also capable of handling 3’ bias of reads, an issue of which is often associated with RNA se-
quencing protocols [53]. Examples of other tools for abundance quantiﬁcation include Cufflinks
and Salmon, where the latter provides a computationally faster alternative to RSEM [59]. For abun-
dance quantiﬁcation by RSEM, gene-level estimations rather than transcript-level were conducted.
It has been shown that gene-level estimations are more accurate and show less variability than
transcript-level. Furthermore, inference of abundance on a gene level leads to improved statistical
performance and interpretation. Due to this, RNA sequencing data is mostly studied on the gene
level and it was therefore decided to estimate gene abundances in this project [59,92].
34
==PAGE:42==
An important issue to take into consideration is that it cannot be known through this study
whether the abundances of the transcribed genes were in fact reﬂective of the level of translated
proteins. During transcript quantiﬁcation, transcript abundances were used as "proxies" for pro-
tein levels.
However, studies have shown that the correlation between transcripts and actual
protein levels can be as low as 40% [93].
Discrepancies between RNA and protein levels will
therefore exist, and overestimation of abundances might occur.
7.1.2
DESeq2
In order to identify diﬀerentially expressed genes relevant to the response to ES-induced injury
with the use of IBU, the tool DESeq2 was utilized. The statistical tool uses the estimated abun-
dances to test for DGE under diﬀerent conditions. The beneﬁt of utilizing DESeq2 for estimation
of DGE is the fact that it takes within-group variability, also referred to as gene-wise dispersion
factors, into account as well as diﬀerences in sequencing depths across samples [57,60]. Similarly
to the widely used tool edgeR, DESeq2 assumes a negative binomial distribution of the counts.
However, they diﬀer in the estimation of the dispersion factor used for calculation of the relation-
ship between the mean counts and the variance. Comparative studies of commonly used tools
such as edgeR, DESeq2, PoissonSeq, and limma have shown an advantage of edgeR and DESeq2
for DGEA conducted on RNA sequencing data [57,94]. DESeq2 has also been shown to have high
sensitivity and precision, while at the same time maintaining low false positives, and was therefore
utilized in the study [57].
Prior to the estimation of DGE with DESeq2, the data was prepared and ﬁltered. In order to
reduce noise in the data, genes of which had summed counts below 10 across all samples were
removed. This number could arguably be changed, as one replicate of a sample with a count of
11 could be retained with this method even though no abundances were estimated for the other
samples. However, DESeq2 employs a series of independent ﬁltering and ﬁtting of the data to a
generalized linear model in order to weed out outliers [57]. Thus, the most extreme noise would
most likely be captured during the dispersion estimations by the tool.
After the initial ﬁltering, it was decided to collapse technical replicates for the analysis with
DESeq2. This not only increases the sequencing depth of the samples but also improves the statis-
tical power of the DGE estimations. As technical replicates are sequenced from the same library,
they do not constitute truly independent samples as is the case for biological replicates. It is
therefore inappropriate not to collapse technical replicates prior to the analysis, as this would
falsely increase the number of independent samples [95]. Furthermore, in order to improve the
statistical power, samples were grouped after condition (ctrl/stim), group (PLA/IBU), and day
(0/2) for the DGEA, as biological replicates were not available for the study, which will be dis-
cussed further below. This also meant that it was not feasible to study the eﬀects of IBU on the
level of individual test subjects, and it was not possible to account for the eﬀects attributed to
normal diﬀerences between the participants either.
In order to estimate DGE with DESeq2, Wald tests of signiﬁcance were employed. After L2FCs
have been shrunken to improve stability of the estimates, they are divided by their standard error
and the computed z-statistics are compared to a standard normal distribution during this test.
However, it is possible to test for signiﬁcant DGE with a likelihood ratio test (LRT) instead [57].
LRT is similar to analysis of variance (ANOVA) but the test is based on deviance instead. It
captures diﬀerences in the likelihood between a full and a reduced model and is recommended for
single-cell sequencing data [96, 97]. As the Wald test does not need a reduced model for DGE
testing and the provided data was not of single-cell origin, the Wald test of signiﬁcance was applied
in DESeq2.
35
==PAGE:43==
During the Wald test, it is investigated whether there is enough evidence to support the gene
in question is diﬀerentially expressed compared to a certain threshold. By default, a threshold
of zero L2FC is applied as the null hypothesis by DESeq2. The generated p-value will therefore
indicate the probability of obtaining a L2FC as extreme or even more extreme than would be ob-
served under the null hypothesis of zero L2FC. When using a threshold of zero for the statistical
tests, it is implied under the null hypothesis that the underlying biological processes are perfectly
decoupled from the experimental conditions of which are being tested. With this assumption, it
becomes possible to detect very small, non-zero L2FCs as being diﬀerentially expressed. As these
are most likely not of biological signiﬁcance, ﬁltering the L2FCs post hoc on a certain threshold
deemed biologically interesting by the user is often done. However, it is recommended to ap-
ply the threshold directly in the statistical tests instead because the computed p-value will then
reﬂect this criterion. By ﬁltering post hoc and not specifying a threshold, the p-value becomes
less interpretable because it corresponds to testing with the default threshold but is subsequently
ﬁltered on another [57]. It was therefore decided not to use the default of zero L2FC for the
null hypothesis but instead apply a threshold of 0.585 directly in the Wald tests. Often, a more
conservative threshold of 2 is used, but considering the already low amount of signiﬁcant hits
obtained with 0.585, it was deemed appropriate for the study.
When a data set is subjected to multiple testing, the false discovery rate (FDR) becomes inﬂated
[98]. As this was the case for the DGEA, the computed p-values were BH-adjusted for multiple
testing in order to reduce type I errors among the genes of which were deemed signiﬁcantly dif-
ferentially expressed. This was done for the summed count of genes across the 7 statistical tests
performed by DESeq2, to not only account for the number of genes being tested but also to account
for the number of comparisons [57,66]. As the experimental design of the study included the fac-
tors; condition (ctrl/stim), group (PLA/IBU), and day (0/2), many combinations of comparisons
were possible. However, with the increasing number of performed comparisons, more stringent
BH-adjustment of the computed p-values would consequently be required. In order to keep the
number of performed tests to a minimum and only consider comparisons of biological relevance
to the study, the 7 tests were performed (Section 5.4.2).
Multiple testing adjustment could also have been performed with the Bonferroni correction method
for the DGEA. This reduces type I errors but is sensitive to type II errors, i.e. not rejecting the
null hypothesis when it should have. Bonferroni correction is known to be too conservative on
large-scale multiple testing as observed in transcriptomics where the performed tests are highly
correlated. In this case, the BH-adjustment procedure is generally used and was therefore utilized
on the L2FC-associated p-values [98, 99]. However, use of FDR correction with the BH method
can lead to misleading interpretation of comparisons across diﬀerent experiments [99].
During DGEA with RNA sequencing analysis tools such as edgeR and DESeq2, it is assumed that
the estimated gene abundances in the data are equivalent to the actual abundances in the samples.
However, it is not possible to determine with certainty the completeness of the data, meaning that
it will remain unknown whether the lack of detected expression of a gene is indeed due to lack of
expression in the sample or rather because it was missed during sampling and sequencing [100].
Furthermore, a property of DGEA of RNA sequencing data is the diﬃculty of estimating DGE of
weakly expressed genes. This is due to the fact that noise in the data can cancel out potentially
signiﬁcant biological signals of weakly expressed genes, which could explain the low amount of
detected downregulated genes deemed signiﬁcant with DESeq2 in this project [57] (Figure 11).

7.1.3
WGCNA
To identify networks of co-expressed genes that are potentially involved in the same biological
pathways, WGCNA was used. WGCNA can be viewed as a data reduction scheme, much like a PCA,
but instead of imposing orthogonality of components in the data, it allows for biologically mo-
tivated dependency between the components.
This tool employs weighted topological overlap
measures to deﬁne modules of co-expressed genes, which has been shown to produce more cohe-
sive modules compared to utilizing unweighted connections between pairs of genes [68].
For the gene-wise adjacency calculations performed with WGCNA, the signed network type and bi-
weight mid-correlations were applied. This was done in order to maintain the signs of the gene
correlations to have modules of positively correlated genes, and to reduce the eﬀect of outliers.
It is possible to use Pearson correlations for the calculations instead. However, as bi-weight mid-
correlations are median-based rather than mean, which is the case for when applying the Pearson
correlations, these are less sensitive to outliers [101]. This correlation type was therefore applied
throughout the calculations with WGCNA.
In order to identify modules of co-expressed genes in the analysis, the parameter minModuleSize
was varied, which deﬁnes the minimal collection of co-expressed genes that constitutes a mod-
ule. Three sizes were tested; 40, 75, and 100. Due to the large sizes of the resulting modules,
it was decided to use a minimal module size of 40 (Appendix section 10.4.2, Table 7). How-
ever, instead of solely focusing on module sizes to establish the optimal value for the data set in
question, permutation tests could have been implemented instead and would be preferable. This
would also have provided a measure of the robustness of the module assignments of the genes [102].
7.1.4
SystemSignificance
For the investigation of biological eﬀects of IBU at a PPI network level, Intomics in-house devel-
oped tools were applied. The proprietary PPI network of InBio Map™was deemed best-in-class
in a benchmark study in Nature Methods in 2017. As a wider coverage of the human proteins is
possible when compared to other databases such as KEGG, InBio Map™was therefore utilized
for the purpose of this project [46].
VirtualPulldown was used to construct the 0th and 1st order PPI networks of the study, whereas
SystemSignificance computed the signiﬁcance of the generated networks. In doing so, a per-
mutation test was performed on the associated values to the proteins of the system by randomly
generating networks up to 107 times. One of the strengths of using permutations tests is that
instead of comparing the computed test statistics to a standardized statistical distribution, the
reference distribution is generated from the input data itself [103].
For the calculations, the
DESeq2-estimated L2FCs and original, unadjusted p-values were used as associated values to the
proteins of the systems. It was decided to utilize the original p-values that had not been adjusted
for multiple testing, as these were only used as "intermediate" values to score the randomly gen-
erated PPI networks by SystemSignificance. Multiple testing adjustment was performed on the
p-values from DESeq2 in order to reduce type I errors and to control the FDR. However, as the
p-values in this case were not used to infer the existence of a signiﬁcant signal from the proteins in
question, but rather used intermediately for the permutation test, it was arguably not necessary
to utilize the adjusted p-values in SystemSignificance.

7.2
Mackey et al., 2016
In order to study the regulatory eﬀects of IBU on skeletal muscle recovery after injury with an in-
tegrated transcriptomics approach, RNA sequencing data of muscle biopsies was acquired from the
Mackey et al. study. One of the strength of this study was that they investigated sub-prescription
levels of IBU within the recommended daily usage, namely 1200 mg/day. The eﬀects of IBU at
this dosage was therefore comparable to amounts that are widely and increasingly administered
in the general population both in Denmark and globally (Figure 5) [2,3]. The results of the study
suggested that prolonged usage of sub-prescription levels of IBU may promote activation of SCs
and expedite muscle regeneration after ES-induced injury [3].
15 men in the IBU group and 14 in the PLA group completed the double-blinded, randomized
intervention study. During the trial, the PLA group was administered PLA tablets indistinguish-
able from the analgesic and the IBU group received 1200 mg/day of IBU. The participant ingested
PLA/IBU tablets two weeks prior to being inﬂicted to ES-induced damage to one leg and four
weeks after. Blood samples and muscle biopsies were collected throughout the study. The biopsies
were taken on the day of ES-induced injury (designated as day 0), as well as 2, 7, and 30 days
later (Figure 7). Unfortunately, biopsies were not taken on the ﬁrst day of the trial (referred to as
day -14). It would have been preferable to have samples from this day as it could be considered
the true day 0 of the study and would have served as a more appropriate baseline. However,
the results of the DGEA with DESeq2 in this project did not show signiﬁcant diﬀerences when
comparing the CtrlPLA0 and CtrlIBU0 samples, indicating that IBU did not inﬂict signiﬁcant
changes to the baselines.
The ctrl samples were taken from one randomly assigned leg whereas the stim samples were col-
lected from the other. The ctrl samples taken on day 0 (CtrlPLA0 and CtrlIBU0) were taken prior
to ES, which ensured that no response to the ES would aﬀect the ctrl samples to which the stim
samples would be compared. The stim samples collected at day 0 (StimPLA0 and StimIBU0)
were taken 2.5 hours after the ES was completed on the other leg. The samples from day 0 and
day 2 were sequenced and utilized in this study. However, it would have been desirable to also
have sequencing data from biopsies taken several hours after the ES-induced injury in order to
capture responses biologically relevant to muscle tissue damage that do not occur immediately
after. This is exempliﬁed by neutrophils inﬁltrating the site of injury after 1 - 4 hours, whereas
MPs are usually recruited 4 - 24 hours later [28]. Furthermore, previous studies on skeletal muscle
regeneration in humans have suggested that sampling approximately 7 and 30 days after damage
is suitable for studying the inﬂammatory processes and SC activity during skeletal muscle repair
[32]. Therefore, it would have been preferable to have sequencing data from the biopsies taken at
day 7 and 30 in the Mackey et al. study as well. Due to the limited time points of the biopsies
utilized in this project, it is therefore possible that some relevant biological processes were not
captured.
Biological replicates were not taken during biopsy sampling of the 2016 study either, which re-
duced the statistical power of the downstream analyses of this project. However, natural and
ethical limitations are imposed on the number of samples that can be taken due to the invasive
and painful procedure of collecting muscle biopsies, and should be taken into consideration [3,32].
The procedure of the muscle biopsy collection likely also meant that the samples were not of single-
cell origin. Therefore it is not possible to deduct through this study from which cells certain genes
were potentially diﬀerentially expressed. However, taking into account the myriad of cell-types
involved in skeletal muscle recovery after damage, the usage of sequencing data originating from
multiple cell types may provide a broader insight into the intricate processes taking place [27,36].

7.3
Batch eﬀects
Prior to the analysis of the provided RNA sequencing data of the biopsies from the Mackey et al
study, an initial ﬁltering was performed on the samples. Many of the original samples contained
very low read counts, and it was therefore decided to ﬁlter on samples with a minimum read
count of 106. Only subjects with complete data sets after ﬁltering were kept in this study, which
resulted in 10 subjects in the IBU group and 8 in the PLA group. This meant that 5 subjects
from the IBU group were excluded, as well as 2 in the PLA group. After the initial ﬁltering, large
variations were observed in the number of reads between and within each subject kept in the study
(Table 2). The alignments performed during the gene abundance estimations also exhibited very
high variations in the percentage of mappable and uniquely mapped reads between the samples
(Appendix section 10.3, Table 6).
Before the DGEA, an exploratory data analysis was performed on the gene counts in order to
investigate potential batch eﬀects. Some of the relevant steps to take in exploratory data analysis
is to investigate possible batch eﬀects by a PCA or hierarchical clustering dendrograms with both
biological and batch variables. In doing so, it can be elucidated whether the observed variations in
the data stem from a true biological signal or batch eﬀects [61]. It was evident from the results of
the exploratory data analysis on the VST counts that batch eﬀects were indeed present, especially
with respect to samples from sequencing lanes 5 and 6 (Figures 8-9). One possible explanation
for the observed batch eﬀects and low number of reads in many of the original samples, could be
due to the age of the samples. The biopsies were collected between April 2009 - December 2009
but were not RNA sequenced until 2017. It was not known if the biopsies were stored as is or
if the RNA had been extracted from the samples, reverse transcribed, and subsequently stored.
Free RNA is generally regarded as unstable due to its susceptibility to degradation by RNA hy-
drolases, which is especially a risk during collection, handling, and storage of tissue samples [104].
The low quality could therefore be due to degradation of the RNA during the 8 years of storage,
especially if reverse transcription had not been performed. Furthermore, the batch eﬀect was also
driven mainly by sequencing lanes 5 and 6. Through personal communication it was informed that
samples that appeared to be of low quality were collected on the same lanes during the sequenc-
ing step. This could explain the diﬀerences observed by the samples sequenced on these two lanes.
In order to model and account for the size of the batch eﬀects in the DGEA, the sequencing lanes
were included in the design as a covariate. In order to avoid modelling the same batch eﬀects for
the downstream analyses by WGCNA, it was decided to remove batch eﬀects from the VST input
data. This was done with svaseq which have been shown to stabilize error rates for downstream
analyses [63]. However, when doing so, there is a risk of exaggerating eﬀects after batch correc-
tion that could possibly lead to overly conﬁdent ﬁndings [105]. Furthermore, it is not feasible to
conduct further exploratory data analyses after corrections because eﬀects beyond the expected
may have been removed. There is also the risk of removing true biological signals as a result of
the batch corrections [106].
7.4
Eﬀects of ibuprofen on skeletal muscle recovery
A combination of the DGEA, FEA, gene co-expression and PPI network analyses was employed
for the purpose of this project to investigate the regulatory eﬀects of IBU on skeletal muscle re-
covery after injury.
Interestingly, the results of the DGEA revealed that several heat shock, TF, and cell cycle regulat-
ing responses were signiﬁcantly upregulated as a result of the ES-induced injury. This included the
39
==PAGE:47==
HSP encoding genes HSPA6, HSPA1A, and HSPA1B, together with the AP-1 TF subunit genes
FOS, FOSB, and FOSL1 for the CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 analysis (Figure 11a).
Interestingly,
the co-expression module M1 of which was positively correlated to the condition trait (ctrl/stim)
included these genes as well, and therefore complemented the results of the DGEA. Furthermore,
the genes were also predicted to be potential hub genes as they showed high GS and intramodular
connectivity in the analysis by WGCNA. The two co-expressed PPI sub-clusters, M1S1 and M1S2,
were deemed signiﬁcant with SystemSignificance by use of the L2FCs and p-values estimated
for the CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 test. The ﬁrst sub-cluster contained cell cycle related proteins and
HSPs whereas the other was comprised of AP-1 subunits and AP-1 binding proteins. Furthermore,
the global, data-driven PPI network analysis yielded signiﬁcant networks containing these proteins
as well. The analyses therefore complemented each other and suggested that not only were the
HSPs and AP-1 TFs signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed for this test, but were also co-expressed
and interacted physically with each other. The FEA conducted on the diﬀerentially expressed
genes from CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 also showed overrepresentation of heat shock responses such
as responses to unfolded proteins and oxidative stress. It also showed enrichment of the AP-1
complex and regulation of the immune system.
The DGEA of CtrlPLA0 vs StimPLA0 yielded a 5-fold decrease in the number of signiﬁcant hits
compared to the IBU equivalent test, and generally resulted in lower and less signiﬁcant L2FCs.
However, signiﬁcant upregulation of HSPs was also observed in this case but not HSPA6. The
AP-1 binding protein CREB5 was also overexpressed and the sub-cluster M1S2 containing this
protein and the other AP-1 TFs was also deemed signiﬁcant for this test. Furthermore, the global,
data-driven PPI network analysis yielded the signiﬁcant network N2 that consisted of AP-1 pro-
teins as well. The FEA of the diﬀerentially expressed genes of the CtrlPLA0 vs StimPLA0 test
and N2 showed enrichment of heat shock responses and cell cycle regulation. This indicated that
the heat shock responses and upregulation of the AP-1 TFs were not exclusive to the samples
from the IBU group, and that many of the same biological processes appeared to be enriched for
both the PLA and IBU group.
It was expected that the condition trait would be the main driver of the biological signal observed
in the data, as this was arguably the most invasive intervention in the study. The group trait was
therefore not expected to show the strongest responses in this study but might exhibit secondary,
yet signiﬁcant, signals speciﬁc to IBU. As evident from the analysis of the module-trait correlations
by WGCNA, the strongest correlations were observed for the condition trait, followed by the group
trait (Appendix section 10.4.4, Figure 22). Conversely, the test CtrlPLA0 vs CtrlIBU0 by DESeq2
did not yield signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed genes or any signiﬁcant PPI networks from the
global analysis. This indicated that IBU did not have any signiﬁcant eﬀect on the baselines even
after two weeks of usage, as previously mentioned. Surprisingly, the comparisons StimPLA0 vs
StimIBU0 and StimPLA2 vs StimIBU2 did not yield signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed genes
either. Only when investigating the diﬀerences between the two conditions within the same group
did the DGEA and PPI network analyses yield signiﬁcant results. It was therefore not possible
to detect signiﬁcant responses to injury of which were speciﬁc for the IBU group in this project.
The lack of statistically signiﬁcant signals of the eﬀects of IBU in the analyses could be because
IBU simply does not exhibit regulatory eﬀects on skeletal muscle recovery after injury. It could
also be because of the quality of the data used in this study. As previously described, strong
batch eﬀects were evident from the data. The sequencing lanes were added as covariates to the
analysis by DESeq2 in order to model and account for batch eﬀects, and the batch eﬀects had been
regressed out of the input data to the WGCNA analysis. It is therefore possible that biologically
relevant signals had been removed from the data or reduced to such an extent that they were not
signiﬁcantly detectable.

7.4.1
Heat shock response
It was found that several biological processes were aﬀected by the ES-induced injury and included
heat shock responses, as previously described. HSPs were signiﬁcantly upregulated when com-
paring the ctrl to the stim samples, especially with respect to HSPA6. This gene was the most
signiﬁcantly overexpressed in the CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 comparison and was not signiﬁcant in
the PLA equivalent test. As previously described, this gene encodes a member of the HSP70
family. The signiﬁcant PPI networks, N3 and M1S1 also showed the presence of this protein and
showed that it was densely connected to other signiﬁcantly upregulated HSPs with high conﬁ-
dence interactions. HSPs are known to be upregulated during metabolic stress and inﬂammation
in order to counteract transiently detrimental eﬀects. Previous studies of ES-induced damage on
muscle tissues have shown that HSP70s are signiﬁcantly upregulated within the ﬁrst 24 hours
of injury, which corresponded to the ﬁndings of this study [25, 34, 35]. These are known to pro-
mote cell survival and protein synthesis together with regulation of cytokine expression [35, 36].
Interestingly, studies have shown that HSP70s are able to inhibit stress-induced apoptosis by
preventing an eﬀective assembly of a functional apoptosome. HSP70 has also been suggested to
inhibit apoptosis through binding to pro-apoptotic proteins such as c-Myc [107]. The encoding
gene MYC was also upregulated in the CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 test and was present in the PPI
networks N1 and N4. Furthermore, HSP70s can inhibit apoptosis through inhibition of protein
kinases that activate caspases [107]. This could explain the enrichment of regulation of protein
kinase activities for the CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 test. Thus, the evidence of this study suggest that
anti-apoptotic signals were present due to the signiﬁcant upregulation of HSPs as a response to
injury. However, it should be noted that HSP genes were not signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed
when comparing the PLA and the IBU group and any regulatory eﬀects on their expression could
therefore not be attributed to IBU with statistical signiﬁcance.
7.4.2
AP-1 transcription factor complex
Several genes of the AP-1 TF complex and AP-1 binding proteins were found to be signiﬁcantly
diﬀerentially expressed for both the CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 and CtrlPLA0 vs StimPLA0 tests.
These include FOS, FOSB, and FOSL1. Furthermore, they were present in the WGCNA-derived
module M1, with most of them being predicted hub genes. They were also present in the sig-
niﬁcant PPI networks M1S2, N1, N2, and N4. The FEA of these networks and of the CtrlIBU0
vs StimIBU0 test also complemented the previous ﬁndings by showing enrichment of AP-1 TF
activity. This complex is required for activation of ﬁbroblasts and is involved in promotion of
transcription of inﬂammatory cytokines [41,42]. Fibroblasts are necessary for proper regeneration
and remodeling of damaged myoﬁbres by producing ECM proteins and interacting with SCs [37].
Interestingly, the signiﬁcant PPI network N1 also contained AP-1 subunits and the FEA revealed
enriched signals of proliferation of ﬁbroblasts, cellular response to GFs, and responses to cytokines.
PI3K activities were also enriched in N1 which are involved in cell-cycle reentry of quiescent SCs.
This could be attributed to the presence of HGF and its receptor c-Met in the network N1 [36,37].
The signiﬁcant biological signal of the AP-1 TFs detected throughout the analyses of this study
were therefore indicative of skeletal muscle recovering processes being initiated in the samples.
The FEA of N1 also supported this by revealing enrichment of tissue development and cellular
diﬀerentiation signals.
7.4.3
Extracellular matrix remodeling
The ECM provides the muscles with the ability to endure stress and has been suggested to be a
key regulator of muscle repair. The study by Mackey et al. suggested that IBU would exhibit
negative regulatory eﬀects of ECM remodeling as a result of muscle tissue damage. However, the
41
==PAGE:49==
study found that IBU elicited a faster regeneration of damages myoﬁbres and ECM remodeling [3].
Signals relating to the ECM were also found throughout this project. The FEA of the signiﬁcantly
diﬀerentially expressed genes from the test CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 showed an overrepresentation
of integrins and binding of glycosaminoglycan, both of which are constituents of the ECM. Inte-
grins and glycosaminoglycan such as HA are involved in signalling transduction pathways between
migrating myoﬁbres during muscle tissue remodeling. HA is believed to enhance diﬀerentiation
and maturation of SCs through interaction with its primary receptor CD44 [39]. Interestingly,
this receptor served as the seed protein in the signiﬁcant network N1 of which exhibited enrich-
ment of ﬁbroblast proliferation, ECM organization and glycosaminoglycan catabolism. FEA of
N4 also yielded enrichment of ECM organization, most likely because CD44 also interacted with
the seed PLAU in this network. However, in concordance with the other ﬁndings of this study,
the enrichment of ECM remodeling signals were not signiﬁcantly attributed to IBU. The results of
this study with respect to expediting eﬀects of IBU on ECM remodeling did therefore not support
the ﬁndings of the Mackey et al. study.
Presumed that IBU exhibits expediting eﬀects on return to baseline muscle ECM after injury, it
is not clear whether this is a desirable property of the NSAID. Late onset of ECM remodeling
has been suggested to be of importance for protection against muscle re-injury. It is therefore not
clear whether a faster return to baseline ECM due to ingestion of IBU could lead to susceptibility
to muscle re-injury in the future, and it remains to be studied [25].
7.4.4
Inﬂammation
A signiﬁcant enrichment of inﬂammatory responses was observed in the FEA of the signiﬁcant
genes from the DGEA and the presented PPI networks. Substantial pro-inﬂammatory as well as
anti-inﬂammatory processes take place during muscle tissue regeneration as a result of injury [28].
IBU functions both as an analgesic as well as an anti-inﬂammatory drug. It could therefore be
interesting to study whether any inﬂammatory processes were signiﬁcantly aﬀected by IBU dur-
ing skeletal muscle recovery. IBU elicits its main eﬀect by non-selectively inhibiting prostaglandin
synthesis by the enzymes COX-1 and -2. COX-2 is inducible by cytokines and upregulation of
expression is usually seen within several hours of acute inﬂammation [11,16]. The encoding gene
PTGS2 was not found to be signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed and was not present in the sig-
niﬁcant networks during the PPI network analysis. However, this could be because the samples
after ES-induced injury were taken too soon to detect signiﬁcant upregulation of the gene.
Processes related to inﬂammation were signiﬁcantly upregulated in the DGEA, PPI network anal-
ysis, and the subsequent FEA. A signiﬁcant upregulation was observed for the gene PLAU for
the test CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0. The protein uPA was also the seed in the signiﬁcant network
N4 and interacted with CD44 in N1. This protein is secreted by circulating monocytes at the
site of injury and functions as a chemoattractant for MPs [27]. Furthermore, it has been sug-
gested to regulate interaction between leukocytes and the ECM at the site of tissue damage. It
it also critical for migration of leukocyte in damaged tissues, such as those caused by ES to the
muscles [108]. However, signiﬁcant upregulation was also observed for uPa’s primary inhibitor
SERPINE1, also present in N4, which is involved regulation of vascular ﬁbrinolysis [83]. This
could explain the enrichment of processes involved in negative regulation of blood coagulation as
well as negative regulation of plasminogen activation determined by the FEA of N4. Furthermore,
the cellular component, platelet α granule, was also enriched for this module, and is known to
secrete mitogenic and pro-inﬂammatory factors such as IL-1β to promote local haemostasis and
wound healing [90].
42
==PAGE:50==
Responses to IL-1 and oxidative stress were also enriched in the CtrlIBU0 vs StimIBU0 test. The
response to oxidative stress could be caused by the secretion of ROS and RNS by neutrophils to
promote inﬂammation in the initial stages of skeletal muscle recovery. Furthermore, this could
also be attributed to the secretion of ROS by recruited MPs to propagate inﬂammation. The
recruited MPs also secrete IL-1β to promote inﬂammation and initiate myocellular proliferation,
which could also explain the enrichment of responses to IL-1 [28,32]. Interestingly, IBU has been
suggested to elicit anti-inﬂammatory eﬀects by scavenging ROS and RNS that are secreted by
neutrophils and MPs during the oxidative burst [10, 19]. It has also been suggested to inhibit
NO production by tissue-speciﬁc NOSs. Enrichment of negative regulation of nitrogen compound
metabolic processes were observed for the network N4 but it could not be known if this was related
to the eﬀects of IBU on NO production by NOSs. It could also have been related to catabolism of
the ECM constituent, glycosaminoglycan as this is related to nitrogen compound metabolism as
well. As glycosaminoglycan was also enriched for N4, this was arguably a more likely explanation.
Thus, it was not possible through the analyses of this study to detect anti-inﬂammatory responses
attributed to the NSAID, IBU.
It should be noted that some inﬂammatory responses may also have been activated during the
biopsy collection procedure itself as well. Previous studies have speculated that local inﬂamma-
tion could be attributed to repeated collection of muscle biopsies rather than exercise. However,
evidence have shown that inﬂammation caused by the biopsy is minor when compared to that
resulting from exercise-induced muscle damage [28]. Furthermore, if minor inﬂammatory eﬀects
were caused by the biopsy itself, it could be argued that it was accounted for in the DGEA.
As suggested by the name, the DGEA only regards genes as being signiﬁcant if there is enough
evidence to support that they are diﬀerentially expressed in the test samples compared to the
baselines. Thus, inﬂammation caused by the biopsy procedure would be present in both sample
types and would most likely not be detected as diﬀerential.
7.5
Summary
The study published by Mackey et al. suggested that IBU expedited regeneration of skeletal mus-
cles and activation of the SCs after ES-induced injury. Activating signals of SCs and ﬁbroblasts
were detected through the integrative transcriptomics approaches of bioinformatics and systems
biology tools employed in this project. Signiﬁcant heat shock responses, AP-1 TF activities, ECM
related and inﬂammatory processes were found in this study as well. However, clear regulatory
eﬀects of IBU on these processes were not detected, as there were no signiﬁcant PPI networks
for the comparisons StimPLA0 vs StimIBU0 and StimPLA2 vs StimIBU2 or any signiﬁcantly
diﬀerentially expressed genes. Because there were no signiﬁcant diﬀerences between the PLA and
IBU group in the DGEA nor the PPI network analysis, there was not enough evidence to support
the ﬁndings of the Mackey et al. study from which the RNA sequencing data originated. Fur-
thermore, strong batch eﬀects were present in the data. Modeling of the batch eﬀects during the
DGEA and removal during the gene co-expression network analysis could therefore have removed
subtle, yet signiﬁcant biological signals attributed to the regulatory eﬀects of IBU on skeletal
muscle recovery. Furthermore, caution should be taken when drawing biological conclusions on
batch corrected data as these might lead to overly-conﬁdent ﬁndings [105,106].
IBU is increasing being used both in Denmark and globally, and especially so for the alleviation
of muscle pains by amateur and professional athletes [1, 2]. Future research is therefore needed
to determine potentially beneﬁcial or detrimental eﬀects of the anti-inﬂammatory drug on the
intricate processes taking place during skeletal muscle recovery after injury.

Future research
During this projects, a series of bioinformatics and systems biology tools were applied to elucidate
the role of IBU on skeletal muscle regeneration after damage. However, the DGEA could have
been conducted with another tool such as edgeR to test whether any signiﬁcant changes would
result from this. Furthermore, the PPI network analysis was conducted with the Intomics in-house
developed tools. The ﬁndings of this analysis could have been compared to a PPI network analysis
conducted with the STRING database as well. STRING includes both physical interactions as
well as functional and could therefore potentially add another layer to the ﬁndings of this study
[109]. In order to focus the analysis for the purpose of this project, only two modules were further
studied from the WGCNA analysis. However, it could be interesting to study other correlated and
anti-correlated modules of co-expressed genes and investigate whether they were related to IBU.
As previously mentioned, it could be of interest to have sequencing data from the biopsies taken at
day 7 and 30, in order to properly study the intricate processes taking place during tissue recon-
struction and remodeling at later stages. Furthermore, it could also be interesting to investigate
the eﬀects of IBU at several time points on the ﬁrst day of ES-induced injury. In doing so, it
becomes more clear what processes IBU aﬀect, if at all, and if IBU exhibits eﬀects on critical time
points. It would also be of interest to study the potential for susceptibility to muscle re-injury as
a result of the prolonged usage of IBU.
The gene co-expression analysis with WGCNA did not show any signiﬁcant correlations between the
ME and the meta data of the participants. This was the case for the age, BMI, and percentage
of type I muscle ﬁbres. However, it was not possible to study potential eﬀect of gender, as the
study from which the sequencing data was acquired only investigated the use of IBU on healthy
Danish men. It would therefore be interesting to study if potential changes would occur between
the genders.
For future research of skeletal muscle repair in muscle biopsy-derived data, it will be preferable to
have both technical replicates and biological replicates. This would make it possible to account for
diﬀerences observed in gene expression levels between people, and it would increase the statistical
power of the analysis. Furthermore, proper sampling, library preparation, and immediate RNA
sequencing should also take place, in order to reduce the risk of batch eﬀects that might warp
biologically interesting signals.
