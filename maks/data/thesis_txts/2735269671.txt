Abstract 
Since most people spend most of their time indoors it is of major importance to qualify indoor 
air quality and by extension quantify the levels of air pollution, especially in places where there 
are pollution sources. The qualifications of the indoor air can be done with the assessment of the 
indoor pollution while residential woodstoves/open fireplaces are operating within private 
residences in Greece. Furthermore, having a picture of the concentration levels inside the houses 
during the combustion, we can assess the health consequences, by examining health impacts 
that can be caused by indoor pollution. Last but not least, it is aimed to identify variations and 
examine the elements that influencing the indoor particle levels, for instance stove type (open 
fireplace or woodstove), refueling (adding some wood into the existing fire), opening windows 
or doors, all that, in order to obtain a deeper insight into their effects in indoor air quality. For 
that reason, an experimental campaign is organized in a north-east part in Greece, Kavala, 
between the 13th and the 24th of December. More specifically, households were recruited from 
the student’s social network in order to measure particulate matter and ultrafine particles 
simultaneously in 19 residents in Greece, with two instruments that are handed to the student 
by Teis Nørgaard Mikkelsen. The measurements were conducted both in open fireplaces (10 
houses) and in woodstoves (9 houses), clearly showing upward trend patterns in air pollution 
during operation of stove, in most cases, regardless of the stove type. Particulate matter shows 
an increase in 63% of the houses while ultrafine particles show a rise in pollution in 72% of the 
houses. Additionally, 77% of the households exceed the World’s Health Organization Air Quality 
Guideline for 1 hour average of ultrafine particles, with 4 of those houses exceeding it 
considerably. Concerning particulate matter, no clear difference is observed between 
woodstoves and open fireplaces in regard to which source pollutes the most. As for the ultrafine 
particles, results show that after operation concentrations are higher in households with 
woodstoves than open fireplaces. Findings presented in this study imply the need for more 
research into indoor air pollution, particularly in houses with open fireplaces/stoves, to further 
investigate the health implications of their operation. 
 
Key-words: Woodstove; Fireplace; Fine particles; Particulate matter; Ultrafine particles; Particle 
emission; Air pollution; Indoor air pollution.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1 Air pollution 
Air pollution is considered to be the contamination of indoor or outdoor environments through 
chemical, physical or biological processes that change the natural characteristics of the 
atmosphere. Among many sources of air pollution are household combustion devices, motor 
vehicles, industrial facilities and forest fires. The pollutants with the highest impact on public 
health include particulate matter, carbon monoxide, ozone, nitrogen dioxide and sulfur dioxide. 
Moreover, both outdoor and indoor air pollution can cause respiratory diseases in people 
(Ioannis Manisalidis 1, 2020).WHO’s data estimates that almost everyone in the world (99% of 
the population) breathes daily air that exceeds WHO guideline limits and contains high levels of 
pollutants, especially low and middle-income countries, which they suffer the most from high 
exposure levels (Air Pollution, 2024a). 
The last two decades air pollution emissions have been decreased, leading to a better air quality 
in the atmosphere. Despite this improvement, air pollution still stands as the largest 
environmental threat in Europe. According to the World Health Organization (WHO) it is 
estimated that human exposure to fine particulate matter and nitrogen dioxide cause 
approximately 238,000 and 49,000 premature deaths, respectively, in 2020. It is also observed 
that these pollutants are linked with asthma, heart disease and stroke. In addition to that, air 
pollution causes morbidity, meaning that people live with diseases related to long term exposure 
to air pollution. Air pollution has the biggest impact on society’s most vulnerable target groups. 
Older people, children and those with pre-existing health conditions are more susceptible 
compared to others. Air pollution is estimated to cause over 1,200 deaths annually in individuals 
under 18 years of age across member and collaborating countries of the European Environment 
Agency (EEA). Apart from health issues, air pollution can significantly affect Europe’s economy 
since it reduces life expectancy, it deprives workers of working days across different sectors, and 
it increases the costs in healthcare systems. Not to mention that it damages ecosystems and 
vegetation, as well as water and soil quality (“Air Pollution,” 2024b).

1.2 Household air pollution and health problems 
Indoor air pollution under no circumstances should be undermined. Quite the opposite, some 
key facts from WHO in 2022 clearly demonstrate the severity of indoor air pollution. In 2020 
household air pollution was responsible for around 3.2 million deaths. This number includes more 
than 237.000 deaths of children under 5 years old. Also, household air pollution combined with 
the effects of ambient air pollution lead to 6.7 million deaths annually. Concerning health, 
exposure to indoor air pollution can lead to noncommunicable diseases such as stroke, ischaemic 
heart disease, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease or even lung cancer. According to WHO 
women and children bear the greatest health burden from the use of technologies and polluting 
fuels in homes (WHO, 2023).  
Household air pollution arises from the use of inefficient and contaminant-emitting fuels and 
technologies within the domestic environment, giving rise to a spectrum of health-compromising 
pollutants. These include fine particulate matter capable of profound lung penetration and 
systemic entry into the bloodstream. More specifically, women and children bear a greater heath 
burden from polluting fuels, since they typically spend more time in homes and labor over 
household tasks such as cooking and collecting firewood, consequently they spend more time 
exposed to harmful smoke from polluting stoves and fuels. On top of that, gathering fuel 
increases the risk of musculoskeletal injuries and consumes considerably high amount of time for 
women and children, which can limit education as well as other productive activities. In rare cases 
with less secure environments, those two groups are in danger of injury and violence while 
collecting fuel. Most of the fuels and technologies used expose people to safety risks, for 
example, inhalation of kerosene. Due to the lack of access to electricity for over 750 million 
people around the world, there is a need for use of polluting devices and fuels, such as kerosene 
lamps for lightning, which make people exposed to high concentrations of fine particulate matter. 
Last but not least, WHO supports that spending time to use and prepare for inefficient and 
polluting devices constrains other opportunities for people’s health and personal development, 
such as leisure time, studying and other productive activities (Komalkirti Apte1 and Sundeep 
Salvia, 2016) 
1.3 Air pollution condition in Greece 
The current PM concentration in Greece is 2.8 times above the recommended limit given by the 
WHO 24-hour air quality guidelines value. 
It is believed that indoor air pollution in Greece is equally dangerous as the outdoor air pollution 
since air pollutants from the outdoor air invade into buildings/houses through ventilation, open 
doors and windows. The primary sources of outdoor air pollution in Greece are quite obvious 
including vehicle emissions, construction activities, factories, burning stubble, fossil fuel and 
wildfires, while the sources of indoor air pollution include wood, crop wastes, charcoal, coal, 
smoke, chemicals from cleaning products (Marilena Anastasaki 1, 2021).

1.4 Woodstoves and fireplaces  
Residential woodstoves and fireplaces differ a lot from any other means of heating and pollution 
source, since they can pollute the indoor environment directly because they are installed inside 
the house. Especially during winter people spend most of their time indoor, around 90-95% of 
their time, so it is really important to keep indoor spaces clean During the winter, when people 
tend to stay inside more to stay warm and may not ventilate as much, pollution from things like 
wood smoke becomes a big issue for indoor air quality (Press-Kristensen Kåre & Willumsen Tanja, 2022). 
 
1.4.1 Particulate matter (PM) and ultrafine particles from woodstoves 
Particulate pollution poses a significant global health risk, particularly through fine particles (FPs) 
and ultrafine particles (UFPs), which have very small diameters and large surface areas, allowing 
them to readily absorb metals, microorganisms, and other pollutants. These particles can invade 
the human body through different pathways and are quickly absorbed by cells, tissues, and 
organs. During combustion processes, various pollutants are released into indoor air, including 
particulate matter (PM). While some particles form through atmospheric reactions (i.e. 
condensation), others are emitted directly from sources. This study specifically focuses on 
ultrafine (PM0.1) and particulate matter (PM), recognizing their significance in indoor air pollution 
and associated with potential health risks (Jianwei Zhang 1 ⁎⁎, 2023).  
Particulate matter (PM): PM is shortcut for particulate matter or particle pollution, refers to a 
blend of solid particles and liquid droplets that are present in the air. Whilst some particles like 
dust, dirt, soot, or smoke are visibly large or dark, others are extremely small, requiring an 
electron microscope for detection (EPA, 2023). 
Ultrafine particles (PM0.1): Ultrafine particles (UFPs) are inhalable particles as well, with diameter 
less than 0.1 micrometers or even smaller. UFPs are widely distributed and have similarities with 
the FPs. They have smaller aerodynamic equivalent diameter, and they can artificially designed, 
synthesized or can be naturally exist in the environment.

1.5 Formation of ultrafine particles 
Ultrafine particles that are found in the ambient air typically originate from two different sources: 
primary and secondary. These particles vary in composition and size based on the profiles of their 
emission sources. Similarly with other type of particulate matters, ultrafine particles (also 
commonly referred as UFPs) are either directly released into the atmosphere as primary particles 
or created as secondary particles through the transformation of existing gaseous precursors 
(Sultan F.I. Abdillah a b, 2023). 
Ambient ultrafine particles are often formed through dynamic photochemistry processes (i.e. 
nucleation, condensation, and coagulation) of low-volatility gas compounds. Gaseous precursors 
which are mainly present in the ambient air (Sox, NOx) would react with OH- to form sulfuric acid 
vapors, which they self-combined with the help of water into a very small cluster of nucleation 
particles (1 nm). Under favorable conditions, nucleation mode particles are then condensed and 
coagulated (growth) into a newly formed UFP with various properties. Those UFPs formation

processes exhibit different characteristics than those experienced by larger particles (i.e. PM10, 
PM), and this attributes to the changes of shape, size, number, and surface area may occur faster 
in UFPs rather than in larger particles. However, apart from that, fresh emissions from unfinished 
fuel combustion and biomass burning can also contribute to as primary sources to the number 
concentrations of ambient UFPs (Sultan F.I. Abdillah a b, 2023). 
 
1.6 Indoor particle dynamics 
Indoor particle levels can be influenced by various factors, such as emissions from indoor sources, 
outdoor air ventilation, filtration, particle deposition on indoor surfaces and ventilation-driven 
removal from indoor spaces. In addition, within indoor environments, various processes such as 
mixing, interzonal transport, resuspension, coagulation, and phase change can also significantly 
impact particle concentrations and their effects.  
 
1.6.1 Brownian motion  
Brownian motion refers to the unpredictable movement of particles, resulting from continuous 
collisions with surrounding molecules. The significance of understanding the Brownian motion is 
because it provides us with evidence supporting the existence of atoms. Brownian motion can be 
influenced by four factors, namely temperature, particle number, particle size and viscosity. The 
larger the particles, the lower the velocity of Brownian motion, whereas smaller particles tend to 
experience more rapid movement as they propelled further by solvent molecules. Furthermore, 
high temperatures and a greater number of particles, contribute to an increased rate of Brownian 
motion. Since in this study the temperature not only varies from house to house, but also 
increases (in most cases) during the use of the open fireplace/woodstove, Brownian motion 
differs in every household (Hida, 1980). 
 
1.6.2 Coagulation 
When particles collide with one another, they often stick together, this process is called 
coagulation. This does not directly alter the concentration of particle mass, but it does change 
the distribution of particle sizes. Coagulation is a second-order phenomenon, meaning its rate 
depends on the square of the particle concentration. This differentiates it from most other 
processes affecting indoor particles, which are first order. The implication is that coagulation 
matters more when particle concentrations are high but is less significant when concentrations 
are low. The most significant mechanism that causes coagulation indoors is the brownian motion, 
which is progressively more effective as a transport mechanism for progressively smaller 
particles, such as ultrafine particles (Nazaroff, 2004).

Additionally, changes in indoor particulate matter can occur due to phase-change processes, 
where chemical substances transition between gas and condensed states, or vice versa. Research 
has identified at least four categories of these phase-change processes that impact indoor 
particles (Nazaroff, 2004). 
 
1.6.4 Interzonal Transport 
Air movement between rooms significantly affects the levels and behavior of indoor air 
pollutants. Despite its importance, this aspect of indoor air quality has received little attention. 
The factors that control the speed of airflow between zones are fundamentally understood. 
Pressure differentials created by wind, temperature variations, and the operation of fans drive 
this airflow. However, leakage between rooms restricts the extent of airflow induced by these 
pressure differences (Nazaroff, 2004). 
 
1.7 Combustion processes  
Speaking of particulate matter, ultrafine particles and indoor air pollution, it is of major 
importance to define exactly how the fine and ultrafine particles are emitted into the air and 
impact the indoor air quality in houses. This shift of the indoor air quality happens through the 
combustion process, or burning, which is a high-temperature exothermic chemical reaction 
between a fuel (reductant) and an oxidant (most of the time atmospheric oxygen). This process 
produces gaseous products that are oxidized, and it comes out in the atmosphere as smoke 
(either visible to human eye or not). Combustion does not necessarily always result in fire, due 
to the fact that flames are only visible when substances undergo combustion vaporize. After the 
activation energy that is provided to biomass (from a lighter or a match), the fire can be self-
sustaining since the heat from the flame provides sufficient energy. 
Combustion (fire) was the first chemical reaction (that can be controlled) discovered by humans, 
in the form of campfires and until today it remains the main method to produce energy. The 
combustion of biomass (and in this case wood) is highly associated with the production of an 
important amount of combustion particles. Among many particles that are released during 
combustion, this study will be focused on the release of PM and PM0.1 (Sitek et al., 2019). 
 
1.8. Ventilation and air supply principles  
The 3-hour measurement when the wood stove is burning is highly based on the fact that inside 
the room the pollutants are randomly directed in the air and this is due to a couple of reasons. 
Ventilation can be described as an engineering control, which facilitates the exchange between 
indoor and outdoor air, in order to refresh/remove/replace the existing atmosphere. Commonly 
it is used to remove the contaminants in the atmosphere such as gases and particles in order to 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
provide a healthier and safer indoor environment. Ventilation can be easily accomplished by 
natural ways, for instance opening a window or a door, or mechanical means (fans, blowers, air 
conditioners). Natural ventilation effects are not quite certain and there difficult to control, due 
to the fact that there is no information about the outdoor air, unless there is a station nearby. 
Consequently, the room airflow motion is entirely dependent on the indoor and outdoor 
temperature as well as the differences in the pressures. On the other hand, mechanical 
ventilation provides constant temperature, humidity and air quality in the room that is placed. 
In mixing ventilation, fresh air is supplied to urge an overall recirculation of air and promote 
sufficient mixture of contaminants, pollutants and fresh air. In other words, it is aimed to equally 
dilute the pollution level of the air, down to an acceptable level in all parts of the room (Gery 
Einberg, 2005). 
 
1.9 Objective of the study, research question and hypotheses  
 
1.9.1 Objective of the study 
The objective of this study is to measure indoor air pollution resulting from burning activities 
from stoves, with a specific focus on analyzing various emissions of particulate matter and 
ultrafine particles from woodstoves and open fireplaces. Since the combustion of wood results 
in the release of particles of various sizes into indoor environments (Guerrero et al., 2021), this 
study seeks to evaluate the concentration levels of particulate matter in depth, including PM1, 
PM2.5, PM4, and PM10, alongside with ultrafine particles of 0.1 diameter (PM0.1). 
Through measurements that are conducted inside houses with stoves in Kavala, Greece, the 
study seeks to provide a comprehensive understanding of the sources and levels of indoor air 
pollution during kindling. This is elaborated in more detail in the results chapter, in which the 
indoor air pollution levels are compared before and after the stove operation. Additionally, the 
research aims to assess potential health impacts on human health that are associated with the 
observed particle pollution, using the guidelines that are strictly set by the World Health 
Organization (WHO) as a benchmark for evaluating the consequences of indoor air quality and 
well-being of the people involved. Through this multisided approach, the study aims to contribute 
valuable insights to mitigate the health risks that are closely associated with indoor air pollution 
linked with kindling activities. 
Finally, the collected data from all houses in Greece, will be discussed and compared accordingly 
with the regulation and guidelines on fine and ultrafine particles from WHO. 
 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
1.9.2 Research question  
Given the existence body of literature prior to this one, there is no doubt that during the 
operation of woodstove/open fireplace there is an increase in indoor air pollution inside 
households. Consequently, the research question of this study is to assess the quantitative extent 
of woodstoves/open fireplaces contribution to the rise of the indoor air pollution, in other words 
how much a woodstove/open fireplace is contributing to increased indoor air pollution. 
 
1.9.3 Hypotheses 
Within the scope of this project, three hypotheses will be investigated and evaluate whether they 
hold true or not.  
Hypothesis 1: The operation of woodstoves/open fireplaces increases the level of pollution 
(number of particles) inside the houses. 
Hypothesis 2: There is a difference in the amount of concentration emitted between woodstoves 
and open fireplaces. 
Hypothesis 3: There is an association between the fluctuations of PM and PM0.1 throughout the 
3-hour period of combustion.  
 
 
1.10 Previous studies on the topic  
In later years studies have emerged and put emphasis on indoor air quality levels and on the 
pollution sources that degrade indoor air quality. 
 
In recent years, an increasing number of studies have emerged putting emphasis on the 
importance of indoor air quality levels and the sources of pollution responsible for its 
degradation. One such study, focusing on particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM1), surveyed 
residential woodstoves certified by the UK’s Department of Environment. The results showed 
that during stove operation, daily average indoor PM concentrations were substantially higher 
for PM2.5 by 196.23% and PM1 by 227.80% compared to a non-use control group. Also, hourly 
peak averages demonstrated significant rises, with PM2.5 being 123.91% higher and PM1 being 
133.09% higher than daily averages (Rohit Chakraborty, 2020). 
Similarly, research conducted in Portugal investigated the effects of biomass burning in both 
open fireplaces and woodstoves on indoor air quality. The findings highlighted that biomass 
combustion in indoor settings as a highly polluting process. In addition to that, significantly higher 
pollutant emissions were observed in open fireplaces compared to woodstoves, exceeding not 
only guidelines imposed by Portuguese legislation, bult also from WHO, particularly concerning 
particulate matter (J. Ascenção1, 2019). 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
Furthermore, a survey examining the effects of operating wood-burning fireplace ovens on 
indoor air quality, conducted in totally seven households and focusing on emissions from 
ultrafine particles, indicated that indoor air contaminants, especially ultra-fine particles, 
originated from wood burning fireplaces (Tunga Salthammer 1, 2014). 
A study from Norway, focused on the effects of wood stove operation on indoor air quality noted 
peaks in emissions of both fine and ultrafine PM during ignition and refill phases, particularly 
associated with the opening of the wood stove door. Ultrafine PM peaks were found to be higher 
and more frequent than fine PM peaks, even though the study was limited to two houses, one 
old and naturally ventilated, and one new with mechanical ventilation (Mathieu Hamon, 2019). 
In addition, a DTU student, a study in Denmark in 2023 resulted that an increase in the indoor 
particle concentration occurred during the operation of woodstoves in the vast majority of 
households, both in terms of ultrafine and particulate matter (PM0.1 and PM respectively). Yet, 
the evidence is clearer for PM0.1 since more data was collected regarding this pollutant (Johansen, 
2023). 
In a study of similar character, Press-Kristensen Kåre explored residential wood burning in 
Copenhagen and found out that it emits more particles annually than all road traffic in the city, 
despite that residential wood burning accounts for only approximately 0.5% of the city's energy 
consumption (Press-Kristensen Kåre & Willumsen Tanja, 2022).

2.1 Study plan  
To begin with, the study focuses on the different imission patterns of PM0.1 and PM from using a 
wood stove as a heating source in private houses in Kavala, Greece. The first step is to reach out 
to the owners of residential woodstoves and then the step-by-step approach is followed. This 
approach involves a brief introduction about the student’s current position in Denmark’s 
Technical University, his thesis title and objective, his mission in this thesis project and finishes 
with the request of the woodstove owners, which is the ignition of them for a three-hour 
measurement. Conducting indoor measurements and assessing the different patterns of 
particulate matter emissions from residential woodstoves is a significant and valuable insight for 
the scientific world. Given that, convincing people to allow a student to take measurements in 
their homes might require effective communication and a demonstration of the benefit of the 
study is also required. Consequently, a persuasive plan is needed and is given to people right 
before the entrance to the accommodation:  
• Importance of study: It is of vital importance to emphasize the importance of study. This 
can happen by mentioning potential health risks that are associated with indoor air 
pollution. 
• Highlight the knowledge gap on this topic: Explanation should be given that the available 
data for this topic is limited and that this study will contribute to the scientific world. 
• Assure safety and privacy: It’s common that people will be unaware of the instruments 
used, so they need to be informed, as well as their privacy will be respected. 
• Benefit of being a participant: People should be informed that by participating, they will 
gain useful insights about the air quality inside their house and its potential impact on 
their health. Also, after the measurements, recommendations can be provided to them 
which can lead to a healthier living environment. Finally, their participation in the study 
can ultimately contribute to reducing indoor air pollution. 
• Community awareness: One of the most important benefits will be the knowledge that 
can be spread in the local community, since (Kavala, Greece) is a small city with little 
scientific knowledge regarding air pollution and its environmental impacts. This 
knowledge can eventually lead into improved practices and regulations.

• Time commitment: It is also important to let them know that their participation will only 
be for three hours.  
• Trust and transparency: A good explanation of the equipment that is used will excite the

2.3 Procedure and rules (Protocol) 
 
• A few hours before and during the experiment, the owners should not cook, use candles, 
smoke, or vacuum inside the house. 
• Before the entrance to the house, a 5-minute measurement will be conducted to quantify 
the outdoor air pollution. 
• After the 5-minute outdoor measurement, p-Trak and Dust Trak are set on a table or chair 
close to the source (open fireplace or woodstove), and a 5-minute measurement will be 
conducted before the owners ignite the stove for the first time. 
• During the ignition all windows and doors should remain shut unless it is necessary. 
• During the operation of the stove owners can do anything else that they would normally 
do. 
• Owners should not be terrified or scared by the sound of the two instruments while they 
are measuring. 
• Regarding the frequency of re-ignition, it should be assured that the fire will be burning 
for three consecutive hours without putting off. 
Under those circumstances the experiment in each house is completed smoothly. In case one of 
the above steps is not followed, the experiment will keep going but it is crucial that it will be 
written down as a comment for the results analysis in a later chapter.

2.6.1 P-Trak® Ultrafine Particle Counter 8525, TSI® 
P-Trak belongs to the category of Condensation Particle Counter (CPC), which are specialized 
instruments that enlarge tiny particles to a visibly detectable size. This portable instrument is 
well-suited of tracking indoor pollutants and responsible for detecting and counting ultrafine 
particles, using a condensation particle counting(P-Trak Ultrafine Particle Counter 8525, 2024). 
Ultrafine particles are particles smaller than 1 micrometer that often accompany the presence of 
a pollutant. While the ultrafine particles are defined as having a diameter less than 0.1 μm or 100 
nm, the engineered nanoparticles are a subset of ultrafine particles with dimensions varying from 
1 to 100 nm. It should be noted that p-Trak uses the interval from 0.1 micrometer down to <= 20 
nm. Ultrafine particles are byproducts of combustion, for instance fireplaces, and other chemical 
reactions. On the other hand, nanomaterials are produced and used for industrial and high-tech 
applications. Manufacturing and using nanomaterials have no clear link with occupational health 
risks. Consequently, a need has been emerged to qualify the indoor air quality. Using TSI’S proven 
technology, the P-Trak gives direct, real-time measurement of ultrafine particle levels. Despite p-
Trak having multiple applications, in this study the purpose of its use is solely for combustion 
leaks, while residential woodstoves and kindled. (P-TRAk Ultrafine Particle Counter (Operation and Service 
Manual), 2022) 
As far as the unit is concerned, P-Trak’s unit is “pt/cc”, “pt” refers to a point, which is a unit of 
measurement, while “cc” stands for cubic centimeter, which is a unit of volume. 
 
As illustrated in Figure 1, particles are constantly entering the instrument and pass through a 
warm alcohol vapor. Then the mixture flows through a condenser section where the alcohol 
vapor condenses the particles and enlarges them into larger droplets. Each individual droplet 
then passes through a focal point of a laser beam, producing a flash of light. Finally, each flash of 
light accounts as one particle (Patricia B. Keady, 2000).

Operation of p-Trak  
When conducting measurements, the P-Trak followed the operational guidelines outlined in the 
manufacturer's manual, TSI® (TSI Incorporated, 2022). The specific methodology for p-Trak 
measurements will be detailed in the upcoming paragraphs. 
To begin with the P-Trak device was carried to Kavala (Greece), inside the carrying case, without 
any problem in the airport for its transportation. Before initiating each measurement with the p-
Trak, certain steps were taken to prepare the device.  
First and foremost, it was checked that the memory level is enough for the 3-hour measurement, 
including the 5-minute outdoor measurement and the 5-minute indoor measurement, before 
kindling. If the memory capacity is completely full (100%), then with a logging interval of 1 
(counting every second), the capacity is enough for approximately ten hours. To ensure that no 
problem would emerge during the measurement, after two houses the data was extracted from 
the student’s computer and then the memory was cleaned. Secondly, it was made sure that every 
household has an AC wall outlet into the living room or close to the living room, so that with the 
help of an extender cable the p-Trak will be charging the whole time. However, it was ensured 
that there is enough battery capacity in the AA alkaline batteries for a 3-hour measurement. At 
the same time, a check was performed on the alcohol level in the alcohol fill capsule to ensure 
that the alcohol level reaches the alcohol cartridge. If this wasn’t the case, then the isopropyl 
alcohol provided by TSI® was added to the bottle up to the point that the fill line of the cartridge 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
contacts the alcohol. Once this step is completed, then the alcohol cartridge is attached to the 
alcohol capsule of the device by being gently twisted into place.  
The p-Trak device is constantly carried in the carrying case and is removed every time a household 
is reached. So, the steps include the alcohol cartridge to be extracted from the fill capsule and 
placed into the P-Trak's cartridge cavity and twisted into place. The alcohol fill cartridge was 
slightly shaken above the capsule until it stopped dripping alcohol. Even though the charger was 
used, the AA was placed into the device to ensure that the p-Trak will be running for three 
consecutive hours. Following these steps, the condensation particle counter is also placed in the 
front part of the device and then the p-Trak is activated by pressing the ON/OFF button, making 
it ready for measurement. The settings are adjusted, choosing the “Data log mode”, specifically 
Log mode 1, and ensuring that the time and date correspond to real time. To initiate the process 
the log mode 1 tab is selected from the main menu and the Enter key button is pressed by the 
student. After the 3-hour measurement the Enter key button is pressed again on the keypad.  
The p-Trak is turned off from the ON/OFF button, the AA batteries are removed as well as the 
condensation particle counter, and lastly the alcohol cartridge is placed back to the alcohol fill 
capsule and the storage cap is placed back to the device to cover the cartridge cavity. After two 
houses in total the data was extracted to the computer to minimize the risk of losing it and to 
empty the memory of the p-Trak. The extraction of data takes place by switching on the p-Trak 
and connecting it to the computer using a cable. Then with the use of TrakPro software, provided 
by TSI®, the datafiles are exported as a TXT file and are stored into different folders depending 
on the house number (P-TRAk Ultrafine Particle Counter (Operation and Service Manual), 2022) 
 
 
 
2.6.2 DustTrak™ II Handheld Aerosol Monitor model 8532, TSI® 
The second mobile method for measurements that was used is the Dust-Trak™ II Aerosol Monitor 
8532. Dust-Trak is a portable, battery-operated device with data-logging abilities. This single-
channel, light-scattering laser photometer provides real-time measurements of aerosol mass. 
Using a sheath air system, it isolates the aerosol in optics chamber, ensuring clean optics for 
enhanced reliability and minimal maintenance. Despite its various applications (industrial, 
engineering evaluation, point source location, baseline trending and screening), in this study it is 
inclusively used for measuring aerosol concentrations inside houses with woodstoves/open 
fireplaces and to quantify the pollution level around the household. Dust-Trak’s measurements 
are displayed in the unit mg/m3 with a range of 0.001 to 150 mg/m3 and a resolution of ±0.1% of 
reading or 0.001 mg/m3. However, in this study, the unit is converted into μg/m3, since it is a 
more common unit that is used for particulate matter (DustTrakTM II Aerosol Monitor 8532, 2024). 
 
==PAGE:31==

Operation of Dust-Trak 
Prior to the measurement campaign the DustTrak™ II Aerosol Monitor 8532 was zero calibrated. 
This process was repeated by Teis Norgaard Mikelsen after the measurement campaign and no 
deviation in the concentration values is observed. In addition to that, it should be pointed out 
that no inlet attachments were used to measure particulate matter with a specific diameter, 
consequently, the sphere of the pollutants that are measured throughout the measurement 
campaign includes particulate matter such as PM1, PM2.5, PM4 and PM10. 
This instrument uses light-scattering laser photometer technology and is able to measure the 
concentration of several particle fractions. Unlike p-Trak, Dust Trak has a built-in battery which 
was never removed by the student, so a charger was of major importance and was carried within 
the bag in every single house, to ensure continuous operation. For the 5-minute outdoor 
measurement the built-in battery was sufficient. 
By pressing the turn ON/OFF button the instrument is switched on and the “START UP” screen is 
displayed initially, following the initial TSI® logo splash screen. Throughout both the initial and 
main screens, the Run Mode constantly remained in "MANUAL." The test-length is 3 hours and 5 
minutes, the log interval is 1 second (00:01 mm:ss), the number of tests is 1, no starting date is 
used, nor start time because is manually started by the student. The 5 additional minutes of the 
test accounted for potential delays in the kindling process within some households. As for the 5-
minute outdoor and indoor measurements, it was more practical by the student to keep the “Run 
Mode: Manual” and after 5 minutes the button “STOP” is pressed manually, and the 5-minute

4. Discussion 
In the following chapter the results are discussed from different angles, in the light of the three 
hypotheses made in the introduction chapter. Also, important findings from this study will be 
discussed as well as the limitations of the study. 
As far as the three hypotheses are concerned, namely taken, in hypothesis number one it is 
claimed that the operation of a woodstove or an open fireplace increases the pollution level 
inside the house. Then it is examined whether there is difference in the concentration levels 
between woodstoves (stoves with doors closed) and open fireplaces (stoves with no doors), in 
the context of hypothesis number two. Thirdly, given hypothesis number three, it is assessed 
whether there is an association between particulate matter and ultrafine particles that are 
emitted during the combustion process. This is examined through the ratio (ultrafine particles 
divided by particulate matter), and it is depicted with graphs.  
 
4.1 Increase in indoor Particle Concentration during stove operation 
To get a better picture of whether these hypotheses hold true or not, a couple of steps are taken. 
First of all, a 5-minute measurement is conducted around each household a few minutes before 
the woodstove/open fireplace is ignited for first time. Secondly, the outdoor concentration level 
is obtained through two stations in Thessaloniki to compare the concentration level outside of 
the house with concentration level in the city. Thirdly and perhaps most importantly, for 5 
minutes before the ignition of the stove the indoor air quality is assessed, measuring both 
particulate matter and ultrafine particles. To have as clear results as possible, owners are 
carefully instructed not to cook, smoke or use any candles before the measurement, as well as

represents the results from the woodstoves. Then, each blue bar stands for the outdoor 5-minute

4.1.1 Half-hour peaks and background concentration 
The fact that during the operation of a woodstove/open fireplace the concentration of pollution 
inside the house is increased (in most cases), can be proved by the comparison between the 
background concentration (5-minute indoor measurement) and the maximum 30-minute 
average concentration during the operation of the stove. More precisely, comparisons are 
conducted in open fireplaces separated from the woodstoves.  
Open fireplaces: Concerning the ultrafine particles in open fireplaces, in 8 out of 10 households 
(80%) the maximum half-hour concentration exceeds the background concentration level. The 2 
houses that the background concentration is not exceeded are houses 8 and 15. In house 8 the

very broad living room, so perhaps the pollution is distributed. 
As far as the particulate matter concentration is concerned, in 9 out of 10 households (90%) the 
maximum half-hour concentration exceeds the background concentration level. The only case in 
which the PM concentration slightly drops is house 11. 
Woodstoves: As for the ultrafine particles in woodstoves, the 5-minute background 
concentration is exceeded by the maximum half-hour concentration in all cases (8 out of 8 
households).  
Likewise, regarding particulate matter, in 88.9% of the cases (8 out of 9 houses), the background

4.1.2 Discussion of the research question  
Having analyzed the data obtained after the experiments, the research question can be 
addressed again and answered. In chapter 1.8 it is stated that indoor air pollution is increased 
post operating an open fireplace/woodstove given the literature prior to this study. It can be 
observed that there are four cases in total: emission of PM in open fireplaces, emission of PM in 
woodstoves, emission of ultrafine particles in open fireplaces, and finally, emission of ultrafine 
particles in woodstoves. In all four cases, by majority, an increase in indoor air pollution level 
occurred post kindling of the oven. Especially in houses such as H6, H13, H16 and H18 the 
concentration level of particulate matter skyrockets after the kindling of the stove, showing a 
strong contribution to increased indoor pollution. Similarly, in H1, H4, H7, H13, H14, and H19 the 
concentration of ultrafine particles experiences an extreme rise post kindling of the stove. These 
comparisons can be observed in figures 27 and 28, respectively.

Investigating the houses where the concentration either declines, remains stable, or experiences 
a slight increase, it is interesting to explore the possible explanations. As suggested by Kåre Press-
Kristensen, one possible reason for the observed phenomenon could be attributed to effectively 
functioning chimneys characterized with high ventilation. Such optimal ventilation conditions are 
believed to contribute to lower levels of pollution that are detected indoors during stove 
operation or keep the roughly the same concentration levels (Press-Kristensen Kåre & Willumsen 
Tanja, 2022). 
4.2 Emission differences between open fireplaces and woodstoves 
The biggest separation of the data collected, is the one between the open fireplaces, meaning 
fireplaces that have direct contact with the air of the living room without any doors, and the 
woodstoves which have doors, and they get closed after the woodstove is set for operation, 
trapping the air within the stove. In hypothesis number 2, it is assumed that the concentration 
levels for PM and ultrafine particles that are measured are higher in households with open 
fireplaces than those with woodstoves. Given the averages from each house and the total 
average from all houses, this holds true for particulate matter, however, not for the ultrafine 
particles (PM0.1). All those averages are displayed in tables in the chapter of results. More 
specifically, the average concentration of particulate matter in all houses with open fireplaces is 
158 μg/m3, while in woodstoves is 137 μg/m3. In this case, hypothesis number 2 is clearly proven 
correct. However, concerning the ultrafine particles, the average concentration in households 
with open fireplaces the average concentration of all the concentrations individually is 40898 pt 
/cc, whereas in woodstoves is 55345 pt/cc, clearly showing the second hypothesis is not correct.  
4.3 Association between PM and ultrafine particles

stoves results in a proportional emission of both particle sizes into the indoor atmosphere. To 
assess this relationship, the ratio between ultrafine particles and particulate matter is calculated 
across all data sets, as well as the regression analysis by calculating the R2. 
More precisely, the ratio is derived by dividing the concentration of the ultrafine particles with 
the concentration of the particulate matter, with the unit of the ratio being “(pt x m3) / (μg x cc)”. 
However, the focus is directed towards the association between PM and ultrafine particles, with 
minimal attention on the unit and the value of the ratio.

particles, but this is not the case. At the same time, the regression analysis shows an R2 value,

4.4 Ultrafine particles average concentrations and WHO guidelines 
It is extremely important for people to be aware of the indoor concentration levels, with a 
reference point that should not be exceeded. World Health Organization (WHO), having a large 
body of exposure science that was sufficient to formulate a series of good practices advice, has 
published global air quality guidelines concerning the ultrafine particles (WHO, 2021). In order to 
==PAGE:64==

address concerns regarding health and environmental impact of UFP, four good practices advice 
have been formulated. Aiming to direct national and regional authorities and research towards 
measures of reduction ambient UFP levels, WHO states clearly: 
1. Measure the particle number concentration (PNC) of ambient UFP within a specified 
range, starting from a lower limit (≤10 nm) without an upper limit restriction. 
2. Enhance the air quality monitoring approach by embodying UFP monitoring into the 
existing framework. Furthermore, integrate real-time number concentration (PNC) 
measurements across different size ranges at designated air monitoring stations. 
3. Differentiate between low and high particle number concentrations (PNC) so that so to 
form decisions regarding the prioritization of ultrafine particle (UFP) source emission 
control.  
4. Make use of emerging science and technologies to progress methodologies for evaluating 
exposure to UFP, with a special focus on their application in epidemiological studies and 
the management of UFP.

4.5 Explanation of peaks during stove operation 
As it is stated in recent research in 2022 (Press-Kristensen Kåre & Willumsen Tanja, 2022) and from 
observations during the measurements, it is believed that the rise in indoor air pollution from 
woodstoves/open fireplaces is caused by the following factors: 
1) Particles leaking through the stove door when open or through the open fireplace at any 
given time. 
2) Leaking seams in (hot) stoves and the smoke pipes. 
3) Sudden reverse ventilation down through the chimney.  
4) Dust burning on stove surfaces (characteristic smell) 
Therefore, any time that a peak is noticed in any of the experiments that have been conducted, 
can be attributed to one of those four reasons.

4.7 Limitations of the study and further investigation on the topic 
It is highly significant to describe the limitations of study, not only to acknowledge the things that 
restricted the study, but also to give an opportunity to make suggestions for further research in 
the future. 
Even though the sample size (households with stoves) is sufficient to find significant relationships 
while the open fireplace/woodstove is operated, a higher number of samples could have been 
more representative and facilitate to observe patterns more obviously. Ideally, more samples for 
both types of stoves, more samples of open fireplaces and more samples of woodstoves. In 
addition to that, it should be noted that the outdoor air around the household is just measured 
prior to the stove operation and not after the operation. In future studies, the outdoor 
measurement post operating of stove can be a significant addition, since fumes are emitted out 
of the chimney, and it can show whether there is a big difference between the concentration 
before and after the combustion.  
Furthermore, the background pollution inside houses is a clear limitation. In spite of instructing 
the owners not to cook, smoke, use candles or vacuum a few hours before the experiment (and 
during the experiment), the background pollution can still be of a problem, since one of those 
activities might have happened the previous days, so the source of pollution is not merely from 
the woodstove/open fireplace, while measuring with the two instruments (Dust-Trak and p-
Trak). As a consequence, due to this limitation and depending on the amount of background 
concentration, the different emission patterns that are formed might be different. For instance, 
households like H1, H7, H17, H18 are households with high background emission, since it is 
known that the owners are smokers and accordingly to (Slezakova et al., 2009) tobacco smoke 
clearly increases PM concentrations. 
Moreover, a lack of prior research studies on the topic can be considered as a limitation. Indoor 
air pollution is underestimated, and people do not pay much attention to the impacts that might 
cause. Lately, there have been reports that assess the effects on indoor air pollution during stove 
operation, yet further research on the topic is needed. In addition to that limitation, another 
factor that limits the prior research is the number of houses that the experiments are conducted, 
apart from (Johansen, 2023). 
Another limitation is that information regarding the type of wood has been given by the owners, 
though, in this research no further investigation is conducted. For further research, good food 
for thought would be to examine whether different types of wood lead to different amounts of 
emissions inside the house.  
As far as the zero calibration in Dust-Trak is concerned, despite that the Dust-Trak was zero 
calibrated before the campaign and after (and there was no deviation in the values), it is advisable 
to zero calibrate the instrument before every single measurement that is about to be conducted. 
Concerning the p-Trak, in H9 the p-Trak showed “Low alcohol” error, even though the wick had 
==PAGE:68==
68 
 
been soaked in alcohol before the measurement. So, it was turned off by the student for 5 
minutes and then it was turned on again, with the data in the excel being merged. 
Since air pollution is the single largest environmental health risk in the EU (EU Legislation in 
Progress, 2023), guidelines have been given to be met by 2030 concerning air quality standards 
for human health protections. More specifically, for PM2.5 pollutants the annual current standard 
is 25 μg/m3 and the revised standard will be 10 μg/m3, while the 24-hour limit value should not 
exceed 25 μg/m3 and the permitted exceedance is 18 times per year. As for PM10, the current 
standard of annual limit value is 40 μg/m3 and the revised standard for annual limit value will be 
reduced to 20 μg/m3, while the current standard for the 24-hour limit value is 50 μg/m3 and will 
be reduced to 45 μg/m3 by 2030 with permitted exceedances being 18 times per year. However, 
the limitation that is addressed is that all these standards by EU cannot be compared with 
average concentrations from DustTrak in this specific study, due to the fact that the 
measurements include PM of different/various diameters (EU Legislation in Progress, 2023). 
At the same time, both WHO and EU guidelines can be characterized as a limitation for this study, 
since the standards they set are for outdoor pollution, while this study merely focuses on indoor 
air pollution (from a specific source). 
Finally, the student did not directly measure the volume of each living room. Instead, the 
information given is relied on by the owners after the conduction of the experiment campaign. 
Consequently, the reliability and authenticity of the data provided to the student are 
questionable.

5. Conclusion 
All in all, taking everything into consideration, the experimental campaign that took place in 
Kavala, Greece, and this study can be an important inspiration for the research field, since not 
only it assessed the emission patterns both in open fireplaces and woodstoves, but it also 
assessed the quantification of the emission both regarding PM and ultrafine particles (PM0.1). 
As it was anticipated, during the operation of both woodstoves and open fireplaces, patterns 
clearly indicate the rise of both particles, in most cases, with some of them showing dramatic 
soar in concentration levels. However, the strongest evidence was found for the increase in 
PM0.1, as it increases in more houses when comparing the background pollution level with the 
post kindling average concentration. More specifically, in cases with woodstoves, during 
operation, in 6 out 9 houses (66%) the PM concentration is increased, while in 7 out of 8 houses 
(87.5%) the ultrafine concentration in increased as well. As for the open fireplaces, again, during 
operation, in 6 out of 10 houses (60%) the PM concentration rise, as well as in 6 out of 10 
households (60%) the ultrafine concentrations increase, respectively.  
Even though, two different categories of pollutants are measure simultaneously, it is clearly 
indicated that there is no relationship between them regarding emission.  This can be noticed 
when in some households the fine average concentration rises while the ultrafine falls, and vice 
versa. Additionally, statistical regression analysis indicates no significant correlation between 
emission of particulate matter and ultrafine particles (from a statistical point of view). As far as 
the WHO air quality guidelines are concerned, comparing the hourly average concentration for 
ultrafine particles that is given by WHO and their good practice statement (20000 pt/cc), 77% of 
the households (14 out of 18) exceeded this threshold. 
In conclusion, it is of great importance that a study of this character was conducted for first time 
in Greece, a territory in which the pollution level is high, with this number of houses (19) assessing 
patterns of particulate matter and ultrafine particles. This study of assessing indoor emission 
patterns during the operation of stoves, can be an incentive and trigger a series of other studies 
in the future that can assess health implications in more depth.
