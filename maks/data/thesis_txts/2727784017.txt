1
Introduction
The maritime sector plays a critical role in global trade, with ships carrying approximately
90% of the world’s trade. Given this scale, the sector is also a significant contributor
to greenhouse gas emissions, highlighting the urgent need for efficiency improvements
and decarbonization. One area where significant improvements can be made is in ship
scheduling, specifically concerning the problem of berth allocation. It involves not just
allocating space, but also scheduling times for each ship’s arrival, loading/unloading, and
departure. This task is known as the Berth Allocation Problem (BAP)[1], and has been
a subject of extensive research. However, the BAP becomes even more complex when
considering multiple ports and continuous berthing spaces. This problem, known as the
Multi­port Continuous Berth Allocation Problem (MCBAP)[2], is the focus of this thesis.
It involves determining the optimal berth allocation for each ship at each port they visit,
considering travel times between ports and service times at each port. This complex
problem has been previously addressed using exact methods such as branch­and­price
and software such as CPLEX, but these methods do not scale well for larger problem
instances.
Given the above, the aim of this master thesis is to develop and evaluate neighbourhood­
based meta­heuristics, particularly with the implementation of an adaptive Greedy Ran­
domized Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP) coupled with Local Search and Path Re­
linking. This GRASP algorithm aims to overcome the computational challenges associ­
ated with the conventional approaches offering scalable solutions for large problem in­
stances.
In the scope of this thesis, we implemented a GRASP algorithm with two main construc­
tion mechanism for each step of our heuristic : (1) Selecting Positions and Times for
a Single Non­Scheduled Visit and (2) Selecting among these Positions and Times one
Non­Scheduled Visit to place. Both mechanisms incorporate an adaptive mechanism
that employs three main tactics ­ minimizing finishing time, minimizing distance from the
preferred berthing position, and minimizing the global cost. We will also introduce a time
window constraint to effectively prune the search space and prevent inefficient solutions
inducing undue delays.
Further on, we present the development of a local search method inspired by the principles
of Large Neighborhood Search (LNS). This method involves removing and replacing visits
to improve the solution and a consolidation phase to minimize delays. A unique cost
window mechanism is also introduced in the Result and Analysis part, to enhance the
algorithm’s efficiency, focusing only on the most promising solutions.
This research also introduces a Path Relinking method aimed at transforming a con­
structed solution into a target solution drawn from an Elite Pool. This approach entails
creating a neighbourhood around the current solution and using the GRASP algorithm for
reconstruction, enabling intelligent solution rebuilding while making incremental improve­
ments towards the target solution.
In sum, this master thesis presents an extensive examination of the GRASP algorithm with
local search and path relinking, developed specifically to tackle the challenges of Multi­
port Continuous Berth Allocation Problem. This research aims to give valuable analysis
of this algorithm and extend the insights of the existing knowledge in ship scheduling
optimization.

2
Literature Review
Theoretically the MCBAP is a real challenge given the complexity of this problem. In the
literature, this was initially addressed from the point of view of a single port, the berth
allocation problem. The Berth Allocation Problem (BAP) has been a prominent topic in
port operations research since its introduction by Lim[1], who studied the problem in the
context of Singapore’s port for discrete berth spaces. This problem deals with the optimal
allocation of ships to berthing positions in a port, focusing on minimizing total service time
and, by extension, overall cost. Over the years, many modifications have been made
to the BAP’s constraints and optimization objectives, with numerous studies proposing
different methods of approach.
One of the earliest models for the Dynamic Berth Allocation Problem (DBAP) was pro­
posed by Imai et al.(2001)[3] where ship handling time was dependent on the assigned
berth. This model improved upon the static variant (SBAP) outlined in Imai et al.(1997)[4]
and applied a Lagrangean Relaxation (LR) leading to a linear assignment problem.
Monaco and Sammarra[5] adopted a similar approach, but provided a stronger LR ow­
ing to a reformulation of the problem. Around the same time, Nishimura et al.(2001)[6]
proposed a genetic algorithm approach, dividing the problem into subproblems based on
time, with results being comparable to Imai et al.(2001)[3].
Addressing the SBAP, Imai et al.(1997)[4] developed a solution that minimizes overall
handling time and dissatisfaction of the berthing order. This model was solvable in poly­
nomial time since it could be formulated as a linear assignment problem. Moving into
the continuous variant of BAP, there were contributions from Lim(1998)[1], Wang and
Lim (2007)[7], Guan and Cheung (2004)[8], and Kim and Moon (2003)[9]. These models
used various techniques, including graph representation, metaheuristics, LR approaches,
mixed­integer linear programs, and simulated annealing heuristics, with varying degrees
of success.
In recent years, studies have begun to consider integrating BAP into the planning of quay
cranes to further optimize operations (Imai et al. (2008)[10], Liang et al. (2009)[11], Meisel
and Bierwirth (2009)[12]). Despite being based on heuristics, these studies mark a signifi­
cant step towards improving the efficiency of berth allocation.These range from mathemat­
ical optimization to various heuristic and metaheuristic strategies, indicating the breadth
and complexity of the BAP.
Another critical issue in port operations research is the Vessel Scheduling Problem (VSP).
Unlike the BAP, which is concerned with berth allocation within a single port, the VSP ex­
tends this scope to multiple ports, optimizing the movement and speed of ships according
to a given schedule. Balancing operational costs against the punctuality of ships forms
a crucial aspect of the VSP. Over the past two decades, the VSP has been a focal point
of numerous research studies, Dulebenets (2021)[13] provides a comprehensive review
on the subject. Similar to the BAP, the VSP has seen an evolution in its constraints and
objectives to better represent various real­world scenarios.
However, real­world port operations often encompass elements from both the BAP and
VSP. This led to the conception of the Multi­port Continuous Berth Allocation Problem
(MCBAP), a problem that integrates these two aspects, offering a more comprehensive
perspective on port operations. The MCBAP requires optimal allocation of berths for each

Initial studies on MCBAP utilized mathematical optimization techniques, as demonstrated

Problem and data description
3.1
Description of the Multi­Port Continuous Berth Allocation
Problem
The Multi­port Continuous Berth Allocation Problem (MCBAP) is a optimization problem
that revolves around the effective allocation of berthing positions and timings for ships vis­
iting a set of terminals across different ports. This optimization encompasses operational
considerations that pertain to both the terminal operators and shipping carriers.
In this problem, we take into consideration a collection of ships, each of which is scheduled
to visit either all or a subset of the ports along its route. The sequence in which ships
visit these ports can vary. The core objective of MCBAP is to reach the most optimal
combination of berthing position and time to reduce the overall cost.
A vital component of the problem is the physical constraint of the berthing space available
at each terminal. This is essentially determined by the length of the quay at each terminal.
Handling time, which is the time taken to load or unload a ship, is dependent on the
berthing position and increases linearly as the deviation from an ideal berthing position
increases.
Ships have a pre­defined time window for service, known as a port call. This is scheduled
in advance, which helps in allocating berthing spaces efficiently and reducing congestion.
There is an allowance for a certain degree of delay in berthing, however, any delay can
incur additional prices as it might necessitate the allocation of unplanned resources, such
as additional workforce.
Another important aspect to consider is the relation between sailing speed and fuel con­
sumption. The relationship is non­linear, and a cubic function is often used to approximate
it. For our problem, we have discretized possible sailing speeds and assumed ships will
travel the distance between ports at one of these speeds. We also compute the corre­
sponding fuel consumption rates.

The objective function, as defined in equation (1), aims at optimizing the operational costs
for both carriers and terminal operators by diminishing the aggregate of waiting time, han­

Constraints (2) are employed to make sure that each vessel is berthed within the limits
of the available quay space. Constraints (3) and (4) employ auxiliary variables σc,c′ and
δc,c′ to establish the relative spatial positioning among pairs of vessels. The value of M
in these constraints can be restricted to the maximum of the finishing times among the
paired vessels.
Constraints (5) are vital to ensuring that berthing periods do not cross upon each other in
terms of both temporal and spatial dimensions.
Constraints (6) account for the calculation of the arrival time at a given port based on the
selected sailing speed between consecutive port visits.
Constraints (7) and (8) are ensuring that berthing commences strictly post­arrival and
within the permissible time window.
Constraints (9) calculate the delay when the expected time is exceeded, while constraints
(10) validate that the berthing times respect the maximum allowable finish times.
Constraints (11) are responsible for computing the handling time necessary for each ves­
sel during their respective port visits. Additionally, constraints (12) and (13) are key in
computing the deviation from the preferred berthing position, which have implications on
handling time.
Constraints (14) enforce the selection of a singular sailing speed for voyages between
ports, ensuring consistency in navigation.
Finally, constraints (16)­(19) define the domain of the decision variables, thereby setting
the mathematical foundation for the problem at hand.
In summary, the mathematical formulation encapsulates a comprehensive approach to
solving the Multi­port Continuous Berth Allocation Problem by considering a multitude of
factors and constraints that reflect the real­world challenges faced by terminal operators
and shipping carriers.

4
Methods
In this section, we present a meta­heuristic approach to address the multi­port continuous
berth allocation problem (MCBAP). The proposed approach consists of several compo­
nents: a Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP) as the foundation, a
local search technique built on top of GRASP and then a path relinking strategy to gener­
ate new solutions from an elite pool. The purpose of combining these techniques is to find
high­quality solutions for the MCBAP within a reasonable computational time, considering
the various constraints related to the berthing of ships from the set Nin at different ports
p ∈P and the associated costs.
4.1
GRASP Algorithm
The GRASP (Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure) algorithm is a well­known
metaheuristic introduced by Feo and Resende [17], widely used for solving combinatorial
optimization problems. In this section, we describe our adaptation of the GRASP algo­
rithm to solve the MCBAP. Our algorithm operates in two primary steps: (1) for each
non­scheduled single visit we identify an available position and time in the corresponding
port, and (2) we gather the selected position and time for all these visits and choose to
place one among them.

4.1.4
Enhancements to the GRASP Algorithm
Before talking about the other step of the algorithm, we already outline here a two en­
hancements employed to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of the GRASP algo­
rithm, as they directly impact the method :
Ordered Visit Selection
To ensure feasibility and reduce reconstruction efforts, the se­
lection of non­scheduled visits is done in an ordered manner. For a given ship, a visit is
considered for selection only if all visits that are supposed to occur earlier, as per speed
constraints and minimum berthing times, have already been scheduled. This ordered
selection is crucial in minimizing the creation of non­feasible solutions.
Time Window Constraint
A time window constraint is introduced in the
getAvailablePositionsAndTimes function to reduce the search space. During the first
phase a search is conducted within a time frame that is a certain percentage beyond the
minimum feasible time. This is defined as min_time+window×min_time, where min_time
is the minimum feasible time. This constraint efficiently prunes the search space by not
considering solutions that would create an excessive delay. Its contribution will be further
analyzed in the results section 5.2.5.
4.2
Local Search
After constructing a feasible solution through the GRASP algorithm, it is desirable to fur­
ther refine the solution to improve its quality. This is achieved through a local search
mechanism. Local search aims to explore the neighborhood of the solution obtained by
GRASP to potentially find better solutions. The time is used as a stopping criterion to en­
sure the algorithm does not run indefinitely. This section elaborates on the local search
technique employed, consisting of a pseudo large neighborhood search (LNS) [18] fol­
lowed by a consolidation step.

set of the ’worst’ (by cost) visits is removed and then some other random visits are also

4.3
Elite pool and path relinking method
After applying the GRASP with local search to construct and refine initial solutions, the
algorithm can further be enhanced by incorporating a Path Relinking method. Path Relink­
ing is an evolutionary approach that aims to discover high­quality solutions by exploring
trajectories that connect high­quality solutions to one another. The general idea is to iden­
tify a series of small modifications that can transform one solution into another, allowing for
an exploration of the solution space with the aim of getting closer to high­quality solutions.
To effectively implement Path Relinking, we employ an Elite Pool to store a collection of
distinct high­quality solutions. The Elite Pool ensures that we have a set of strong bases
for the Path Relinking process.
4.3.1
Creation of the elite pool
The creation of the elite pool is essential for the Path Relinking method [19]. The elite pool
is a collection of both diverse and high­quality solutions. The diversity ensures that the
solutions are not clustered around the same region in the solution space, this is achieved
by comparing the distances between the solutions.
To compute the distance between two solutions, the DistanceSols function is used. It
calculates the distance based on the difference in handling times and berthing positions
of all visits for each ship in both solutions.
Then the algorithm for creating the elite pool is as follows :
• Initialize the elite pool.
• For each new solution, calculate its distance to all solutions in the elite pool.
• Maintain a variable to keep track of the minimum distance of the new solution to the
existing solutions in the pool.
• If the elite pool is not full, add the new solution.
• If the elite pool is full, check if the new solution can replace a solution in the pool.
This is based on both the quality (cost) and diversity (distance) of the solutions. In
fact adding a new solution should not decrease the minimum distance in the elite
pool.

5.1
Introduction
5.1.1
Main analysis
In the forthcoming sections, my primary aim is to conduct a comprehensive analysis of
every component within our heuristic. To do this effectively, I have strategically segmented
the process into three distinct blocks: the GRASP algorithm, Local Search, and Path
Relinking. This approach provides a thorough evaluation of each component individually
before integrating them all together.
The initial focus will be exclusively on the GRASP algorithm. This first examination serves
to establish a robust understanding of the algorithm’s strengths and potential areas for im­
provements. Subsequently, Local Search will be introduced and integrated with GRASP.
The combined influence of these two components will then be assessed. Finally, Path
Relinking will be added into the mix, creating our full algorithm: GRASP + Local Search
+ Path Relinking.
While it is certainly beneficial to analyze the whole algorithm in its entirety, I believe that
this step­wise approach of isolating and then integrating each component serves a dual
purpose. It not only allows for a precise analysis of each part in isolation but also assists

contributing towards the overall effectiveness of the heuristic.

5.2.1
GRASP initial parameter setup
As seen in the theoretical part our process consists of two main phases: the first phase,
where we select a time and position for each non­scheduled visit, and the second phase,
where we choose to place one of this visit. In both of these phases, a set of parameters
guides the decision­making process and determines the trajectory of the algorithm. In this
section, we describe our initial parameter setup for the GRASP algorithm.
• For the first phase 4.1.2, where we assign time and position for each non­scheduled
visit, we give initially equal probability (1/3) to each of the three tactics ­ distance,
cost, and finish time.
To construct the corresponding Restricted Candidate List
(RCL) for each tactic, we use a set of alpha parameters ­ [0.001, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5].
Each alpha parameter has the same chance of being chosen initially (1/4).
• Alongside the tactic and alpha parameters, we also introduce a time window pa­
rameter (seen here 4.1.4 in the theoretical part) for the first phase. This parameter
determines the extent to which we explore the search space relative to the minimum
time found for each visit/position pair. We have set this time window parameter to
0.1, which implies that we do not explore beyond (min_time + 0.1 × min_time).
• For the second phase 4.1.3, we adopt a similar approach as the first phase. We use
the same tactics and alpha parameters initially.
In the following sections, we will analyse the main results of this algorithm and explore how
modifications to these parameters influence the performance of our GRASP algorithm.

5.3
Local Search Analysis
First of all as mentioned at the beginning of this analysis part, we will present in the main
document the results for this instance configuration but still provide graphs for other con­
figurations in the Annex :
• 30 ships
• 5 fixed ships out of the simulation
• A discretization of 10 meters
As observed in the previous section, the GRASP algorithm does not frequently gener­
ate optimal solutions. A closer inspection of the cost data indicates that the algorithm
often produces iterations with high costs, stumbling upon superior solutions only on rare
occasions. To circumvent this issue and enhance the algorithm’s efficiency, I decided
to implement a threshold for the local search. Given that the local search typically con­
tributes significantly to the algorithm’s computational demands, it seemed prudent to use
it only when a solution from the GRASP phase shows promise of being improved upon to
become a top­tier solution. This approach enables us to focus on enhancing solutions that
show the potential of becoming the best, thereby saving a substantial amount of computa­
tional effort. To this end, I introduced the ”local search window,” which essentially allows
the local search to be applied only to solutions within a specified cost range relative to
the current best solution. The implications of this parameter will be explored extensively
in this section.
The local search component of our GRASP­based algorithm consists of two main steps:
a pseudo Large Neighborhood Search (LNS) and a consolidation phase. In the pseudo
LNS phase, the algorithm identifies and removes a portion of the ’worst’ and random
visits. These visits are then reallocated based on there minimum finishing time for each
available position and on a predefined optimization criterion. It’s important to remember
than we select firstly the minimum feasible time for each visit and position (see 4.2.1).
This aspect really help to speed­up the local search (at the cost of diversification). For the
optimization criterion I did not add specific fine­tuning as we remove the ’worst’ solutions
based on cost and we replace them (similarly the random one) also based on the lower
cost first. The consolidation phase, on the other hand, focuses on shifting the visits earlier
in time, where feasible, to potentially reduce the total cost of the solution.
Specifically, we focus our investigation on two main parameters:
• Proportion of visits to remove: For the pseudo LNS we examine the impact of varying
the proportion of total visits removed as both the ’worst’ visits (defined by cost) and
random visits.
• Local search duration: The time allocated to the local search component of our
algorithm can significantly influence the ultimate quality of the solution. Balancing
computational resources and solution quality is a critical aspect of algorithm design.
In the subsequent analysis, we explore the impacts of local search window and these
parameters on the performance. This analysis allows us to build a comprehensive un­
derstanding of the local search process and how it contributes to the overall GRASP
methodology’s efficacy.

6
Conclusion and discussion
Throughout this thesis, we implemented an adaptive Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search
Procedure (GRASP) coupled with Path Relinking for the challenging optimization problem
of scheduling ships in a mutli­port environment.
Our proposed GRASP algorithm consisted of two essential steps: selecting a position/­
time for all single non­scheduled visit and selecting among these visits and their corre­
sponding position/time to construct the solution. In both steps, we utilized an adaptive
mechanism, choosing one of three tactics: minimizing finishing time, minimizing distance
from the preferred berthing position, and minimizing the global cost. The selection process
was regulated by evolving probabilities associated with each tactic, and the respective α
parameter values. Importantly, a time window constraint was incorporated to efficiently
limit the search space, preventing the consideration of solutions inducing excessive delay.
During the analysis of this part we found the ’distance’ tactic to be less efficient for the first
phase, opting instead for ’time’ and ’cost’ tactics. For the second phase, no tactic seemed
to significantly outperform the others, and thus, we allowed the algorithm to self­select tac­
tics. Regarding alpha parameters, we maintained the same values for all tactics as the
analysis did not lead to conclusive differences. Our experiments also pointed towards an
optimal time window range of 0.15 to 0.25 for efficient computational performance and
breadth of the search space. Therefore, we selected 0.2 as our time window setting.
These settings generally led to a more substantial improvement in smaller instances in­
volving 30 ships, indicating the adaptability of our tuning method across various instance
sizes.
The development of the local search consisted of a pseudo Large Neighborhood Search
(LNS) and a consolidation phase. The LNS phase was primarily aimed at improving the
solution by removing and replacing visits. This was achieved using two tactics: removing
the worst visits by cost and randomly removing other visits. The consolidation phase
focused on reducing any delays introduced during the construction and LNS phases.
Moreover, a cost window was introduced during the local search to filter the solutions
and control the computational effort, thus enhancing the efficiency of the algorithm by fo­
cusing only on the most promising solutions. The results showed that the Local search
proved effective at reducing delay and penalty costs, aligning with its design philosophy
to target minimal feasible times and minimize delays. However, it did not significantly
improve fuel costs, as reducing delays might necessitate faster operations and increased
fuel consumption. We found that it was in fact beneficial not to apply local search to ev­
ery solution, instead allowing the GRASP algorithm to independently construct solutions
and specifically high­quality ones. After the analysis we elected a smaller window size
of 0.1, enabling the GRASP algorithm to function independently while also applying local
search on well­performing solutions. Upon applying the local search to various configu­
rations, we found that larger proportions of visit removals yielded better performance and
interestingly, that we needed to remove more random visits to improve our local search.
Subsequently, we introduced a Path Relinking method, its goal was to transform a con­
structed solution into a target solution drawn from an Elite Pool. The process involved
creating a neighborhood around the current solution through partial deconstruction and
employing the GRASP algorithm for reconstruction. This approach was essential in in­
telligently rebuilding the solution with similar characteristics to the original while making
incremental improvements towards the target solution. In this stage, we did not observe a
Neighbourhood based meta­heuristics for the Berth Allocation problem.

substantial improvement in costs across different configurations. However, we did find a
variation in the number of processed solutions with an increase in the proportion of ships
removed and restricted this proportion of ships to be removed to 0.2.
Our concluding experiments revealed that the Local Search strategy contributed posi­
tively to cost efficiency improvements, whereas the Path Relinking strategy did not notably
drive down costs. This outcome can be attributed to the structure of our Path Relinking
approach, which closely mirrored the construction phase of our GRASP algorithm. As a
result, we were essentially generating a set of very similar solutions. This strategy did
not foster the development of varied and innovative high­quality solutions, explaining the
suboptimal impact on cost reduction.
This likewise illuminates some difficulties we encountered in generating ’optimal’ solu­
tions using our GRASP algorithm. Our ability to identify which cost factors to prioritize
allowed us to quickly derive satisfactory solutions and direct our Local Search, particu­
larly the LNS procedure, towards reducing delay and penalty costs. However, obtaining
the ’best’ solutions still presents a significant challenge. The computation­intensive na­
ture of the GRASP algorithm, even with the fine­tuning of certain parameters, often results
in only a handful of potentially optimal solutions following the local search. The relative
success of our local search further underscores that LNS­type methodologies [16] remain
the most effective in this context. Their strength lies in their capacity to start from already
satisfactory solutions and iteratively build upon them to produce superior outcomes.
