Abstract
Insulin pumps provide the necessary medicine to people with diabetes. However, size-
related annoyances push people to other treatment regimens, albeit health advantages of
using an insulin pump. A travelling wave pumping system could shrink the size of an
insulin pump. The requirement is that it can attain a flow rate of 6 mL
min. This thesis sets
out to answer whether this requirement is attainable and in the process characterise the
travelling wave pump. Although there are still roadblocks to overcome, it seems possible.
This thesis presents two-dimensional, analytical and numerical (finite element method)
studies of the characteristics of a travelling wave pump in a parallel-plate channel. Ana-
lytically, the travelling wave is applied as a cosine in a boundary condition and studied
in a long-wavelength and slowly-oscillating limit. Numerically, the travelling wave is first
applied as boundary condition and found to completely agree with the analytical solution.
In this limit, other wave shapes are also studied and seen to affect the flow rate. The
simulation is pushed to a high frequency limit to reach the desired flow rate. The simu-
lation is then expanded with a glass wall and a piezoelectric transducer, which generates
the travelling wave. All aspects are used in the verdict. The numerical simulations are
done within the commercial software Comsol.
Furthermore, the thesis strives to give insight in the process of transitioning from an-
alytics to numerics, detailing important aspects of the implementation in finite element
method as well as best practices obtained in the thesis work.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus
Insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus (IDDM, from here on just “diabetes”) is a chronic
condition that impact almost all aspects of life. As the National Service Framework in
Britain puts it, “As it is a life-long disease diabetes can have a profound impact on lifestyle,
relationships, work, income, health, well-being and life expectancy”. Insuﬀicient or com-
plete lack of insulin treatment can cause raised blood glucose levels (damaging tissues
throughout the body including kidneys, nerves, and eye and may cause kidney failure, loss
of sensation and blindness), increased risk to more common conditions (cataracts1, infec-
tions, soft tissue conditions, and skin conditions) and most severely coma and premature
death (due to diabetic ketoacidosis2 and cardiovascular disease). People with diabetes
more often suffer from depression and eating disorders. Diabetes effects pregnancies, too.
The risk of unwanted termination of the pregnancy is increased for women with diabetes.
Likewise, the risk of giving birth to a baby with a malfunction or the baby dying in its
infancy is increased. Diabetes also has large societal costs. 5 % of the total resources of
the National Health Service in England are used on the care of people with diabetes. This
is partly due to people with diabetes being hospitalised more often and for longer at a
time than the general population. The presence of diabetic complications (as described
above) can increase costs more than five-fold. [2] Furthermore, the number of people with
diabetes in the world is estimated to increase from 9.3 % of the population of the Earth
(463 million people) in 2019, rising to 10.2 % (578 million) by 2030 and 10.9 % (700
million) by 2040. [3]

1.2
Diabetes treatment and insulin pumps
The human and societal costs clearly emphasize the need to offer good insulin treatment.
Luckily, the breakthrough in isolation of insulin in 1921 has made insulin treatment pos-
sible for just more than a century. Insulin treatment was first done with the now-termed
“conventional treatment”, where insulin injections are taking twice per day from a syringe
and everyday routines such as mealtimes are planned to match the anticipated peaks in
the insulin profiles.
The newer regimen “intensive diabetes treatment” (sometimes re-
ferred to as “flexible diabetes treatment”), where patients get multiple injections or a
continuous dose of insulin, became possible with home glucose testing.[6] Since then, in-
tensive treatment has been shown to effectively delay the onset and slow the progression
of retinopathy3 along with the deterioration of kidney function and nerves when compared
to conventional treatment [8] and helps overcome barriers such as burdensome regimens
and fear of injections to initiate treatment [4]. Still, many people with diabetes choose
to not try or stop using an insulin pump. The main reasons are attributed to the size of
the pump, with reasons given that it is bothersome during sporting activities, it is un-
wieldy, and it is overly visible. Former pump users also state reduction in size as a main
precondition for resuming treatment via pump. [9]

One of the most popular insulin pumps is the Omnipod with a volume of 29

pumping systems miss critical driving elements.
1.3
The PRISMA pump
In 2022, D.-S. Park et al presented a landmark result for increasing the piezoelectricity
of thin-films[10]. They achieved a piezoelectric coeﬀicient of d33 ≈200, 000 pm
V , orders
of magnitudes larger than highest value measured in Gd-doped ceria ceramics (CGO), a
perovskites oxide. The effect was measured in the millihertz range and while significantly
lower in the kilohertz range d33 ≈100 pm
V , it is comparable to the best lead-based thin
films. The effect stems from a combined piezoelectric and electrostrictive response. This
provides the missing piece to create a new generation of travelling wave based insulin
pumps.
The goal of the European Union research project PRISMA is to develop a travelling
wave insulin pump that utilises electrostriction for the pumping system. In Fig. 1.2, the
principal mechanism is sketched. Ring electrodes actuate a ceria-based electrostrictor thin-
film, creating the travelling wave. Currently, it is estimated through initial experiments
that a relative wave amplitude of 1 % is possible. The short term goal of the PRISMA
project is to create a travelling wave micro-pump with significantly better performance
than current.
For the longer term, the pump should be capable of managing a multi
hormone system, thereby enabling the first fully functional artificial pancreas.
The targeted flow rate is 6 mL
min. To reach this with a relative wave amplitude of 1 %, it
has been estimated that a frequency of 100 kHz and wavelength of 6 mm is needed, when
using a flat geometry.

1.4
Focus of the thesis
In its simplest form, the aim of this thesis is to answer whether a travelling wave pump
could supply enough insulin to serve as the pumping system in an insulin pump. Put
simply: Is a flow rate of 6 mL
min possible?
In a wider sense, the aim of this thesis is to model a travelling wave pump, first
analytically and later numerically. The model will start as the simplest possible and then
grow in complexity. We therefore also present two models in this thesis: The single domain
and the triple domain model. The single domain only models the pressure and velocity
field in the fluid. Here, the travelling wave is simply applied as a boundary condition
on the velocity field. In the triple domain model, a wall and a piezoelectric are added.
Now the piezoelectric generates the travelling wave, much closer to how it would work in
the PRISMA pump. In both models, we model the channel in its unperturbed state as
a parallel-plate channel with x as the flow direction, y as the height of the channel, and
z as the infinite and out-of-plane direction. We only present an analytical model for the
single domain model.
For both models, the behaviour of the pump is determined by three central (dimen-
sionless) parameters.

1.5
Previous work
The most well-known work regarding travelling waves as pumping mechanism is by A. H.
Shapiro, M. Y. Jaffrin and S. L. Weinberg from 1969 [11]. However, they were interested
due to physiologic aspects of peristaltic pumping. They work with lower Reynolds numbers
and higher relative amplitudes. Jaffrin and Shapiro also authored a review on peristaltic
pumping in 1971, which works better as an introductory text[12]. A great advantage of
the texts is that they present both theory and experiments, thoroughly solidifying their
work as standard reference for peristaltic or travelling wave pumps.
Travelling pumps have also been manufactured in other contexts. Suzuki et al devel-
oped and fabricated a biochip with a travelling wave pump[13]. They intended to use it
as a low-cost disposable system for clinical diagnosis.
Numerically, travelling waves have also been studied before, but mostly in regards to
the shape of the velocity profile. This has been done by both Takabatake and Ayukawa [14],
who found agreement in experiments, and Selverov and Stone [15], who found agreement
with their analytical derivations.

Representations of Continuum Mechanics: Eulerian and
Lagrangian
In this thesis, we will work with two different representations of continuum mechanics:
The Eulerian and Lagrangian.
The reason we utilise both is that they have different
advantages.
In the Eulerian representation, we observe spatially fixed volume elements and describe
the fields in them by both space and time. It is therefore an advantage to utilise the
Eulerian in fluids, where fields describe the main properties and vary in both space and
time.
In the Lagrangian, we follow the volume elements and therefore only describe them
by their time-dependent position in space. It is advantageous in solids, where we describe
motion. We will use it to describe the mechanical deformation of solids.

3.1
Finite element method
Finite Element Method (FEM) is a numerical technique for solving problems that can be
modelled as partial differential equations. It is most useful, when the problems reach such
complexity that we can no longer solve them analytically.
In physics we may write the governing equation using a generalised flux J and a
generalised force F in the form
∇· J −F = 0.
(3.1)
J will depend of some field u(r, t), which again depends on the position in space r and time
t. The strong formulation of the problem is when we know u(r, t) so that it completely
fulfils Eq. (3.1) and the corresponding boundary conditions,
∇· J[u(r, t)] −F (r, t) = 0
(3.2a)
with a corresponding set of boundary conditions.
(3.2b)
These solutions are few, far between, and of simpler systems as we shall see in Chapter 4.1.
Instead we may have a weak formulation of the problem:
∇· J[u(r, t)] −F (r, t) = d(r, t)
(3.3a)
with a corresponding set of boundary conditions.
(3.3b)
Now, we have an approximate solution to our problem, but it does not fully satisfy the
equation. We are therefore left with a non-zero defect, d(r, t). This defect we obviously
wish to minimise.
To understand the minimisation process we need to introduce the
following concepts: The mesh and the test functions. A mesh is a discretization of our
geometry of interest. A mesh consists of mesh elements and mesh vertices. We may also
refer to a mesh cell, which is a vertex and the collection of mesh elements surrounding.
We can now focus on solving in a mesh element instead of solving the entire geometry.

3.2
Time-dependent solver
The solution to the Navier–Stokes evolves in time. Therefore it is natural that we use the
solver in COMSOL which can evolve the solution in time. COMSOL has multiple solver
for time-dependent problems. Of these, Backwards Differentiation Formula (BDF) and
the Runge-Kutta method (RK34) are noteworthy. Both function conceptually the same
way. They solve an initial value problem and use the initial solution to iteratively solve
up to some end time. In their simplest version, they both reduce to the Euler method.
We therefore shortly explain how one time evolves using the Euler method.
We start by defining our initial value problem in the form of an ordinary differential
equation with an initial condition as
dy
dt = f(t, y(t)),
y(t0) = y0.
(3.11)
We then define some small step size, tstep so that tn+1 = tn + tstep. We now have every
time step that we wish to solve for: [t0, tstep, 2tstep, …, tend]. We may now rewrite the
differential equation into an integral equation and use that tstep is so small that the integral
may be approximated using the rectangle method.
y(tn+1) −y(tn) ≈tstep f(tn, y(tn))
(3.12)
We start at n = 0 for which we know y(t0) and so the only unknown is y(t1). Now we can
iteratively calculate all the way up to our desired tend.
The previous solution always influence the next solution for either method. The initial
solution is therefore usually the hardest for the solver to determine. It is most often here
the convergence plot diverges. Once the solver starts solving iteratively, it usually succeeds
all the way up to tend. It is therefore also a good idea to introduce a ramp function as
described in Chapter 3.3 which simplifies the problem in the initial time steps.

3.3
Help functions
Getting a solution to converge does not only depend on defining all the physical aspects
correctly. Corners and edges often prove diﬀicult to solve in and along. As mentioned in
Chapter 3.2, the initial solution may also prove diﬀicult to attain. We therefore utilise a
family of functions to ease the computation. We may call this family ”help functions”.
A help function in time works as a time-ramping function, starting at 0 and slowly
growing in some way to 1, where it stays.
A simple example of this is a shifted and
truncated hyperbolic tangent. We always use this on the travelling wave. This means the
initial solution does not contain any travelling wave. In this thesis it was found that a ramp
function was either needed or greatly increased the computation speed. In Chapter 5.1,
it was also needed on the non-linear term of the Navier–Stokes. A time-ramping function
does affect the oscillations after the ramp time.
The help functions in space need to dampen the wave at the ends. Two were used in
tandem as one would dampen the wave more slowly and the other would eliminate any
numerical noise left. These were used in Chapter 5.1 and Chapter 5.2.

3.4
Deforming the mesh: Deformed Geometry and Moving
Mesh
The travelling wave causes our boundaries to move. In order to make sure the computa-
tional domain is equally changing in time, we need to deploy a deforming mesh. COMSOL
has two ways to deform the mesh. The first is called Deformed Geometry and the sec-
ond is called Moving Mesh. Deformed Geometry adds or removes points as the geometry
is changed. Moving Mesh induces a shape change in response to a deformation of solid
materials and therefore deforms the Lagrangian frame. Moving Mesh neither adds nor
removes points. Deformed Geometry was used to model the fluid, and Moving Mesh was
used to model the solids.

3.5
Mesh
In Chapter 3.1, we described what a mesh is. There are some deliberations on what makes
a good mesh that we will expand on here.
The standard method for generating a mesh is defining a size for the largest and
smallest mesh element. Comsol then generates a mesh with triangular mesh elements
that will fit inside the geometry on this basis. This approach may not be advantageous
in our case. When resolving a wave what matters is the number of mesh elements in the
wave. We may therefore instead move to a mapped mesh, where we define the number of
mesh elements horizontally and vertically. When there is only movement horizontally, we
may keep the resolution vertically low. We call the number of vertical mesh elements NV
and number of horizontal mesh elements per wavelength NH.
Another consideration is the relation between temporal and spatial resolution.
As
described in [28], disturbances should not be able to travel further than some fraction of
a mesh element within a time step. This ensures that the error of the time derivative is
smaller than the error of the spatial derivatives. Mathematically, this is formulated as the
Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy (CFL) condition,
CFL = c ∆t
∆x + c ∆t
∆y ≤CFLmax,
(3.13)
where c is the speed of the wave, ∆t is the discretization in time, ∆x and ∆y are the
discretization in space, and CFLmax is the solver-specific, limiting value. For the general-
ized alpha solver, CFLmax = 0.05. Since there has not been problems with propagating
numerical errors, an optimal value for the BDF solver has not been sought for. A study
in this would be of interest. However, at the current point we not that the simulations
have been able to converge. It is likely that Comsol is already handling this condition.

Let us start by answering the burning question: Can a travelling wave pump supply 6 mL

of insulin? Probably.
In our estimations and simulations of a fluid channel with travelling-wave as a boundary
condition, a parallel-plate pump could deliver up to 1.8 mm2

at the desired frequency

not take any pressure in the blood into account. Still, this leaves the estimation above
10−1 mm2
s .

to succeed.

is getting started.
