Abstract
In catalysis, the particle size distribution (PSD) of the catalyst often impacts the efficiency of
the catalytic reaction. Haldor Topsoe is working on a new catalyst and is interested in the
PSD of this catalyst. Haldor Topsoe developed a tool to study the PSD based on segmentation
of images using traditional computer vision techniques. This work propose to solve the
segmentation problem using deep learning in order to achieve a more precise estimate of the
PSD. UNet architectures and ResNet50 based segmentation networks achieve segmentations
masks of significantly higher quality than Haldor Topsoe’s tool. The segmentation masks
of the deep learning models result in an estimated particle size distribution much more
representative of the ground truth distribution than the PSD estimated from Haldor Topsoe’s
tool. The deep learning models are packaged into a neat software tool for the operators of
Haldor Topsoe to use.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Industrial chemical processes often utilize catalysis to increase production yield. Catalysis
is the process of accelerating a chemical reaction by adding a substance called a catalyst.
A catalyst works by offering an alternative chemical reaction route with lower activation
energy, making the chemical process less energy consuming and more efficient. For each
chemical process, a catalyst must be specifically designed for the corresponding process in
order to maximize the yield of the process.
Haldor Topsoe is currently researching a new catalyst for a specific chemical process.
The catalyst in this process is a metal, and these metal particles are deposited on a carrier
material. For this catalytic reaction, Haldor Topsoe discovered that the size of the metal
particles plays an important role for the performance of the catalyst. Grayscale images of the
catalyst particles can be captured with a scanning electron microscope (SEM). These images
enables segmentation of the particles to investigate the particle size distribution (PSD).
The particles appear very bright in the images and lies scattered around on a carrier
material. This carrier material is very uneven, causing folds and crevices where particles
are partly covered or out of focus. The three dimensional space being captured in a two-
dimensional image, causes an added perspective of depth, where some particles are located
in the background and other particles in the foreground. Particles in the background appear
smaller than they actually are, so in order to estimate the PSD as accurately as possible, these
must be excluded. When particles are very close to each other, their brightness frequently
makes it appear as if they are fused together as one. This makes it difficult to segment them as
seperate particles. The depth perspective also introduces a probability of particles overlapping
with each other, making it difficult to have all particles be in focus on an image. Together
the uneven nature of the carrier material, brightness of particles, and depth perspective,
significantly increases the difficulty of the segmentation task. Figure 1.1a shows an image of

Currently, Haldor Topsoe employs traditional computer vision techniques for segmentation

close to each other. Futher, the method tends to include particles that are in the background

This work investigates the use of state-of-the-art deep learning and tricks of the trade
for segmentation of particles. The goal is to get a more accurate estimation of the PSD, by
improving the separation of particles and gaining control over which particles to exclude
or include in the segmentation, while also reducing misclassification of carrier material.
Furthermore, this work experiments with novel data augmentation techniques using conditional
generative adversarial networks. Attempts will be made to improve particle seperation by
artificially generating SEM images of overlapping or closely neighbouring particles using
generative adversarial networks (GANs). This enables the segmentation model to be trained
on more images with difficult particles.
Finally, this work is compacted into a software tool for running deep learning experiments
and training runs. This software tool is developed for Haldor Topsoe such that users can
easily apply the deep learning models for segmentation of other datasets. The software
tool includes a graphical user interface, where the user can design and run deep learning
experiments with a variety of deep learning models and parameters.

1.1
Related work
Haldor Topsoe built an expert system using Fiji (ImageJ)1 which employs traditional
computer vision methods for segmentation of particles. The expert system was designed to
only work on images of this exact catalyst and with specific brightness and contrast settings
on the SEM.
Segmentation of images using deep learning has a repository of successful models. One
popular and widely used model is UNet [16]. The UNet model is an Encoder-Decoder
architecture with skip-connections between Encoder layers and Decoder layers. These skip-
connections help transfer multi-scale contextual information from the input encoding to
the output decoding. The UNet model is particularly used a lot in the medical field for
segmenting cells and medical scans. The UNet model lends itself to segmentation of particles
just as well.
Data augmentation using generative adversarial networks is a well known technique
studied in recent research [1, 2], and is successfully applied in many applications. However,
[1, 2] only studies the use of GAN augmentation for unconditional generation of samples
as a way of expanding the training set. They do not study the use of conditional GANs to
generate samples with specific patterns in order to teach a model to better recognize those
types of patterns.
1.2
SEM Particle Dataset
The primary data consists of 36 SEM images at a resolution of 960x1290 pixels capturing
the metal particles on carrier material. The images have been captured using back-scattered
electron signals (BSE). The SEM fires a beam of electrons at the catalyst sample, and back-
scattered electrons are beam electrons reflected back from the sample by elastic scattering.
The BSE signal is measured as the current of reflected electrons, and its intensity is strongly
related to the atomic number of the element that the electrons collide with. Areas that reflect
more electrons gets higher pixel values in the image. The element of the metal particles
and the element of the substrate are far apart on the periodic table meaning large difference
in atomic number. This makes BSE ideal for imaging this catalyst. The catalyst sample is
scanned at a working distance of 8-11mm. The working distance also influences the signal
strength, lower working distance gives sharper images but smaller depth of field. Each image
has been labelled to have a corresponding ground truth segmentation mask. The masks are
binary and contains a background class and a particle class. The background pixels are

2.1
Concepts and Methodologies
In computer science, a traditional algorithm with hand-coded rules is the primary technique
for problem solving. Typically, an algorithm is designed to solve one specific problem
and is based on heuristics and assumptions defined by humans. The hand-coded rules
and assumptions leads to the algorithm being very rigid, as the algorithm will break if the
assumptions do not hold. Further, the algorithm will not be applicable to problems or tasks
other than the one it was designed for.
A type of algorithm called a learning algorithm removes the need for strict assumptions
and hand-coded rules to solve a task, by learning from data or experience. A learning
algorithm can exist as a short computer program but also exist as a pure mathematical
formulation often termed as a statistical model. The field of statistics is primarily concerned
with learning algorithms as mathematical formulations to test hypotheses and formalize
mathematical relationships between variables in data. The comparatively new field of
Machine Learning is concerned with both computer programs and statistical models that can
learn to solve a task from data or experience without having to be explicitly programmed to
do so. Computer scientist Tom M. Mitchell gave a formal definition of algorithms studied
in machine learning: "A computer program is said to learn from experience E with respect
to some class of tasks T and performance measure P if its performance at tasks in T, as
measured by P, improves with experience E."1 This experience E is represented as data, and T
is the task the algorithm must solve, e.g to segment an image. The performance measure P is

In the field of machine learning there are two major types of learning tasks called
supervised learning and unsupervised learning but only supervised learning will be discussed
here. In supervised learning the task is to have a learning algorithm predict a value y given an
input x, and the algorithm learns to solve that task by looking at training data with examples
of x and y. The training data D consists of N independently and identically distributed sample
points, D = {(x1,y1),...,(xN,yN)}. During training on D, the algorithm learns to produce an
estimate ˆyi from xi, and the performance measure quantifies how much ˆyi deviates from the
actual yi, i.e. the error between the algorithm’s estimate and the ground truth. Gradient-based
learning is a technique to learn from a performance measure when trained on data in order to
improve at some task and is the technique used to train neural networks.
2.2
Mathematical Optimization
In order to be trained with gradient-based learning, the learning algorithm must be defined
as a mathematical formulation. The learning algorithm is often referred to as a model, and
is viewed as a mathematical function f : X →Y, that maps an input space X to an output
space Y. The performance measure is also defined as a function and is often inversed to
measure error rather than performance. This measure is given as L : Y, ˆY →R and maps a set
of ground truth values Y and set of estimated values ˆY to a real number. The higher L(y, ˆy) is,
the more error there is, and the worse the estimate ˆy is. L is often referred to as a loss function
since it measures an error or loss. To reach the optimal solution for the task of estimating y
given x, the model must give estimates that minimize the loss function L.

Kernels
Figure 3.5 also shows how the convolution between an image f and a kernel g produces
a new image f ∗g. In CNN terminology, this image is often called a feature map. From

the need for manual kernel selection, by learning the values of the kernel. A CNN trained
with gradient descent will learn the optimal kernels that best extract the relevant feature maps
for the specified task.
3.2.3
Convolutional Layers
Convolving and image with a single kernel only allows for a single feature map to be extracted.
Convolutional neural networks apply multiple convolutions to an input image in order to
learn multiple kernels that extracts a variety of different feature maps. A CNN does this by
convolving the input image with multiple kernels, and then convolving the feature maps with
multiple other kernels again. This way, the CNN extracts feature maps from feature maps,
essentially decomposing an image into a set of patterns with different levels of complexity.
Figure 3.9 illustrates this layering of convolutions throughout the CNN architecture. Figure
3.8 shows a set of learned 11×11×3 kernels from the early layers of a CNN trained by [17]
for the ImageNet classification challenge. One interesting thing to notice, is that the CNN
has learned kernels that extract meaningful features from an image such as edges, textures,
and color contrasts. All features which it then use to classify images.

to condense the feature map. A pooling layer with pooling window of 2×2 outputs a pooling
map half the size of the feature map, this way the feature map has been condensed into half
the size. The final CNN architecture consists of consecutive convolutional layers and pooling
layers, essentially condensing the input image into an information dense representation as
illustrated on Figure 3.9.

CNN Architecture for Image Segmentation
With a good understanding of how convolutional layers, pooling layers, and transpose
convolutional layers work, the popular convolutional UNet architecture for image segmentation
can now be properly understood. The name of the architecture is often referred to as a UNet,
as the architecture resembles a "U" when visualized. A UNet architecture includes an
encoder part, and a decoder part as seen on Figure 3.11. The encoder part consists of
multiple convolutional layers and pooling layers. The encoder takes an input image and
downsamples it into a dense feature representation. The decoder part consists of transpose
convolutional layers as well as regular convolutional layers. Through layers of transpose
convolutions and regular convolutions the decoder upsamples and transform the dense feature
representation into a multi-channel image such that it can classify pixels as illustrated on
Figure 3.2.
One important detail of this architecture is the concatenate-connections illustrated by the
black arrows. These are the skip-connections connecting layers of the encoder with layers of
the decoder. These connections allows information from different spatial resolutions of the
encoding layers to pass into the decoder, making it easier for the decoder to gain information
about object localization. Without skip-connections, the decoder would have to infer the
locations of objects in the input image from the compact feature representation alone, which
would be very difficult.

Chapter 4
Generative Adversarial Networks
Generative models is a category of models that are able to capture the continuous distribution
of a discretely sampled data distribution. The generative model can thus generate new original
samples from the modeled distribution, which has similar statistics as the training data.
The Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) was introduced by [6] and is a type of
generative model that captures the data distribution by simultaneously training two neural
networks to compete against each other in a minimax two-player game. One neural network,
called the Generator, is trained to capture the training data distribution, while another network
called the Discriminator, is trained to estimate the probability that a sample came from the
training data rather than the Generator. The training procedure for the Generator is to
maximize the probability of the Discriminator making a mistake, thus indirectly learning to
capture the training data distribution.
If a GAN is able to generate samples of sufficient quality that are nearly indestinguishable
from real samples, these samples can be used to expand an existing dataset. This chapter
will seek to explain the theory behind GANs and conditional GANs, for the purpose of
understanding how these models can be used to expand the SEM Particle dataset with
generated images of closely neighbouring or overlapping particles.
4.1
Adversarial Objective Function
The generator G learns a mapping from a vector of noise defined as the variable z ∼Pnoise(z)
to a marginal distribution PG(x). The generator G produce an output G(z) ∼PG(x) by taking
a vector of noise z as input. The goal is to learn a distribution PG(x) that is identical to the
real data distribution Pdata(x). The discriminator D(x) is a scalar in [0,1] that represents the
probability that x came from the training data distribution. The training procedure for D is to
maximize the probability of correctly discriminating between training samples x ∼Pdata(x)

Implementation and Design
The deep learning solution for segmentation of particles is based on the theory covered in
Chapters 2-4. Convolutional UNets will be the primary architecture explored for solving
this task, while experiments with the Pix2Pix GAN explore possibilites for augmenting the
dataset. All of the models are trained with gradient descent through backpropagation. This
chapter will cover the details of the implementation from development framework to data
processing, model design, training procedure, and model evaluation.
5.1
Platform and Hardware
The deep learning solution is developed in the Python programming language. The Python
ecosystem has large support for data science libraries and an active data science community.
Furthermore, Python underpins a lot of popular deep learning frameworks, such as TensorFlow
and PyTorch. The Tensorflow framework with the Keras interface is used to implement this
deep learning solution, due to its familiarity and performance.
Training large neural networks on high dimensional data such as images can require a lot
of computational power. The parallel processing power of graphical processing units (GPUs)
is leveraged in order to gain more computational power and accelerate the training of the
deep learning models. TensorFlow uses NVIDIA’s computing platform called CUDA1, to
distribute its computations to GPUs.
In order to keep up with the accelerated training speed on the GPU, the preprocessing
pipeline for the input data is multi-processed to four CPUs. This is to prevent the data loading
from becoming a bottleneck to the training process.

5.2
Data Pipeline
The input pipleline for the data consists of some rudimentary preprocessing steps along with
some classical data augmentation transformations.
Given a set of images, the data pipeline first shuffles the images to ensure independency
between them. Next, a series of data augmentation transformations are applied to each image
and corresponding mask. These transformations are displayed on Figure 5.1. First, a 256x256
patch is cropped from a random location in the image. Then the patch is transformed into
an RGB image with a random color, followed by a horizontal flip, a vertical flip, and a
rotation of a random degree between −180 and 180. Each of these transformations, except
for cropping, are applied with a probability of 50%, leading to an expanded dataset with
varying transformations across images. Cropping is performed every time. As discussed in
Section 3.1.2 and illustrated on Figure 3.2, the ground truth mask must be one-hot encoded.
This follows as the next step after augmentation. Finally, before going into the model, the
image is scaled to a pixel range of [0,1]. This increases stability of the training process, and
it is generally considered to be a standard part of the preprocessing pipeline.

5.3
Model Architectures
The segmentation tool includes multiple deep learning models, all designed to perform image
segmentation. Some models are more advanced than others, and in this section only the
design of the UNet model will be elaborated. The design follows many of the design choices
proposed by the original UNet paper [16], but introduces some minor changes.
The architecture of the primary UNet in this work is illustrated on Figure 5.2. The
network operates on five resolution levels denoted by the numbers on the left. The first level
is the input resolution and then four levels at increasingly downsampled resolutions. Each
level of the encoder consists of two 3×3 convolutional layers with ELU activation and a
2×2 max-pooling layer. The decoder levels consists of a 2×2 transpose convolutional layer
followed by two 3×3 convolutions with ELU. The final layer of the decoder also has a 1×1
convolution with softmax activation, such that it can be trained with cross-entropy loss and
dice similarity loss as described in Section 3.1.2 and 3.1.3. The convolutional layers on
level 1 each has 32 kernels. This number is doubled for each consecutive level, reaching
512 kernels for each of the convolutional layers at level 5. So, for each level the input is
downsampled by a factor of 2, due to the 2×2 max-pooling, while the number of kernels
are doubled. This means that at each level the localization information diminishes while
the contextual information increases. That is why the skip-connections are needed for the
decoder to better access localization information. The decoder upsamples the resolution at
each level, doubling the resolution while halving the number of kernels, until reaching 32
kernels and the original resolution at level 1.
ELU [4] was chosen as activation function over ReLU, as it allowed for slightly better
performance and faster convergence. ELU alleviate the "dying ReLU"2 problem and has a
normalizing effect on the networks activations, speeding up learning.
The segmentation tool of this work, provides three variants of the UNet: UNet2M,
UNet8M and UNet31M. The difference between these variants are the number of kernels at
each level. Table 5.1 summarize the kernels at each level for each of the variants, together
with the total number of parameters for each variant. The UNet variant in Figure 5.2 is
the UNet8M with ∼8 million parameters. This variant strikes a nice balance between its
parameters and the amount of available training data, and is typically a good default choice
of model. These UNet variants will be used for the experiments in Chapter 7, where they are
benchmarked against each other.

5.4
Training Process
There are many ways to train a model, and there are many parameters involved when
constructing a training procedure. This section will describe the training procedure and the
training parameters used for the experiments in Chapter 7
5.4.1
Training parameters
The primary parameters in a training process includes the optimizer to use, the learning rate,
number of epochs, batch size and many other things. Table 5.3 gives an overview of the
training parameters used in the experiments. These parameter values have been set mostly
by gut feeling and manual tuning. The ADAM optimizer [9] is an adaptive optimizer that
utilizes an exponential moving average of past gradients to scale the parameter updates.
This means that gradient descent has not been performed using exactly the update rule in
equation 2.3, but a slightly more advanced update rule instead. A learning rate of 1e−4 is used
together with a decay of 1e−6. The models seemed to quickly learn the difference between
background and particle in the first few epochs. Therefore, the initial learning rate is set to be
rather small, and a decay is introduced in order to reach convergence in a controlled manner.
Each training run covers 100 epochs. An epoch denotes the number of times all samples
from the training set has been passed through the network, and the batch size denotes how
many samples are passed through the network for each weight update. A small batch size has
a regularizing effect on the network, which is ideal when training on a small dataset to avoid
overfitting. Finally, the cost function used is a linear combination of cross-entropy cost CH in
equation 3.2 and dice similarity cost CDSC in equation 3.4. M denotes the number of classes
to segment, and CDSCi denotes the dice similarity cost for class i. The dice similarity cost is
averaged across classes 2 to M, because class 1, which is the background class, is excluded.
The linear combination coefficients 0.6 and 0.4 proved to strike a good optimization balance
between cross-entropy and dice similarity.

Recall from Section 5.2 that the data pipeline randomly crops patches of size 256 × 256

on new unseen data, that this data would have to be split into patches of 256×256 as well.

inputs of any size and simply slide its learned kernels across the input image, producing an
output mask with the same size as the input.

The estimated particle size distributions in previous experiments seems to approximate the

two images. Figure 7.16a shows an example of a spent catalyst, while 7.16b shows an example

When spent, the particles are merged together and form particles significantly larger than
those in an unspent catalyst. Figures 7.16a and 7.16b clearly represents two different particle
size distributions, which should be reflected in the PSD estimations from each method.

Discussion
What is to take away from the experiments in the previous chapter, is that Pix2Pix augmentation
and the additional border class seems to be tricks with some merit to them in regards to
improvement of segmentation quality. The Pix2Pix augmentation gave minor improvements
but only in regards to segmentation accuracy and not in terms of increased particle seperability.
This is worth further investigation in order to determine the limits of GAN augmentation
for segmentation problems, and the amount of generated data needed to be effective for
augmentation. Furthermore, the quality of the GAN samples plays a role on its effectiveness
for data augmentation, hence it might be wortwhile investing time in improving the GAN
model. NVIDIA recently published their SPADE method [15], a neural network layer that
allows deep learning models to synthesize photorealistic images given segmentation masks.
NVIDIA achieve impressive results with this method and it could potentially increase the
quality of the generated SEM images.
The apparent performance increase from the additional border class, makes it desireable
to expand the SEM Border dataset to include all samples of the SEM Particle dataset, and
not only a subset. This could potentially improve the results even further and indicate the
significance of a border class.
The natural brightness and contrasts did not yield any benefits, neither in terms of
segmentation quality or PSD estimation. It is worth keeping in mind that there are room for
improvements in regards to the labels and that this could yield better results if more time is
spent on the labels and their quality.
Another important observation from the results in previous chapter, is that the highest
particle IoU does not directly imply the best estimate of the particle size distribution. This
became obvious when nearly all the UNet models with highest particle IoU scores achieved
worse KLD than the ResNet50 FPN models with highest precision scores. This confirms that
false negatives do not skew the PSD estimate as much as false positives. In other words, it
is better to make mistakes that predict particles as background, than to make mistakes that
predict background as particles. Therefore, models with higher precision should be selected
==PAGE:82==
70
Experiments and Results
over models with higher recall or particle IoU when the true objective of the model is to
estimate the PSD and not to perform segmentation.
The primary source of error in these datasets comes from the inability to seperate
particles where the intensity of pixels seperating them is the same as the intensity of the
pixels belonging to particles. This suggests that the models put too much weight on intensity
features, and not enough weight on shape and context features. One might then ask: are the
models just thresholding? There is no doubt that a model base much of its decision-making
on pixel intensity, and it is obvious that thresholding pixel intensity is very effective on these
images as the particles are very bright compared to carrier material. However, from the
natural brightness and constrast experiment, it is clear that the model is able to distinguish
particles from carrier material using other features than pixel intensity. Secondly, deep
learning models are widley used to segment images from many different datasets, such as
traffic scenes for self-driving cars, and objects in natural images where thresholding is not
possible. Striking a good balance between brightness, contrast, and label quality might be
needed in order to have deep learning models reach their true potential on these particle
images. Nethertheless, the deep learning models will be useful when Haldor Topsoe collect
datasets of other catalysts where brightness and contrast are less extreme.
Perhaps cross-entropy and dice-coefficient are suboptimal choices for cost functions when
it is desired to completely separate particles. With cross-entropy and dice-coefficient the
pixels seperating particles do not incur more cost than any other pixel in the image, and when
only a small fraction of pixels makes up the border between particles, they are not prioritized
by the model in any way. The Kullback-Leibler divergence is a useful measure to compare
the estimated distribution to the ground truth distribution. The KLD measure is differentiable
and could therefore be used as a cost function. One approach is to have a segmentation
network produce a segmentation mask and output the estimated PSD. Then use KLD between
estimated PSD and ground truth PSD as a cost function, and backpropagate KLD cost
through the PSD to teach the network to produce segmentation masks that contains particles
representative of the ground truth PSD. The model would have to learn what elements in an
image are used for estimating the PSD. The model will not necessarily segment all particles
in the image but only segment particles that are necessary and best suited to estimate the
PSD. This technique would therefore not be applicable to every segmentation task but to
segmentation tasks where a distribution calculated from segmented objects are of interest.
The Kullback-Leiber divergence is a well known cost function and is used to train many
other deep learning models, such as regular classifiers or Variational Auto Encoders [10]
which also use KLD to estimate probability distributions.
==PAGE:83==
7.4 Summary of Experiments
71
With these particle images it might not be possible to perfectly solve the PSD estimation
task and to perfectly seperate particles using a pure deep learning approach. To further
improve the segmentation quality and PSD estimation of current deep learning models, it
will most likely require some post-processing like IoU thresholding or watershed.
==PAGE:84==
Conclusion
This work has demonstrated how deep learning models can be used to provide segmentation
masks well representative of the particle size distribution. Furthermore, the deep learning
models outperform Haldor Topsoe’s current method by a large margin, while also being
more flexible and applicable to any given dataset. It has been shown that GAN augmentation
improves both segmentation quality and PSD estimation, while an additional border class
improves segmentation quality alone. A discussion on Kullback-Leibler divergence as a cost
function has suggested a technique to train segmentation networks to directly estimate the
particle size distribution. This should be considered for future work.
The implementation is wrapped up into a neat software tool with a graphical user interface.
Designed for ease of use, the software tool lets operators at Haldor Topsoe leverage the power
of deep learning for other datasets or future problems.
