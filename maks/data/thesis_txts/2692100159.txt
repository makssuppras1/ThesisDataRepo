Abstract 
 
Many people are placed in refugee camps all around the world. The supply of food in these camps 
are often met while the fuel for cooking is often disregarded. This leads to heavy use of firewood 
and coal. Burning of these fuels leads to an unhealthy environment. The lack of access to clean 
cooking is found to kill 2.5 million people yearly. The aim of this work is to create a numerical 
analysis of the SunStore cooker made by Heliac, which allows for future improvements and set 
expectations in different environments. This is done by validation, where to model is compared to 
three different experiments. A literature study examining other numerical analyses have been made 
to improve the understanding regard the numerical analysis for melting of PCM. The sufficient mesh 
for the model was found to be a 500.000 cell mesh, which is locally altered at the heated surfaces. 
The model was validated against three experiments where it was found that the behavior of the 
model was similar but higher temperatures was obtained. Further work is needed to create a model 
which can represent the SunStore correctly. An optimization investigation was made where the 
prototype geometry was compared against three other geometries. From this it was found that the 
prototype can be improved, by decreasing the surface area of the internal structure.

Chapter 1  
Introduction 
 
1.1 Background 
As of 2020, 82.4 million people are forcibly displaced as a result of persecution, conflict, violence, 
human rights violation and serious public order disturbing events. 48 million of these are internally 
displaced people with aid from UNHCR and 26.4 million are refugees worldwide. The largest host 
country is Turkey where 4 million is currently displaced where several refugee camps are located [1]. 
In refugee camps all around the globe the typical energy source is firewood, with the addition of 
charcoal [2]. These sources of energy are very abundant and a switch to other alternatives, causes 
issues such as cost and sustainability, even though the switch would be beneficial for both the 
energy content and the convenience. The people living in refugee camps have several needs in 
which food security is included. This food security is the often met by the host government, or a 
support agency, during this, the food processing and utilization is often neglected, and cooking 
technologies are therefore essential [3].  
The supply of fuel for either space heating or cooking is not reasonable for the refugees to source on 
their own. This may be due to the lack of available fuel, security risks linked to the collection of fuel, 
government pressure, and environmental damage. Because of these factors the energy supply is 
often met by the host government or the support agency. Several recommendations are in place 
when supplying the fuel which includes that selected fuel should be unattractive for re-sale, and the 
distribution and the impact of the fuel supply should be monitored. These recommendations 
ensures that the specific users are using the fuel the intended way.  
According to the World Energy Outlook in 2019, it was found that in 2018 2.6 billion people were 
cooking with primitive stoves and without access to cleaner fuel, and 862 million were without 
access to electricity, which were related to 2.5 million deaths [4]. The primitive stoves are commonly 
identified as three-stone fire stoves, mud stoves, metal stoves and fired clay stoves. These stoves are 
found to produce a huge amount of particle matter due to combustion and with large degrees of 
thermal radiation loss. The radiative losses changed depending on the design of the stove, where the 
most common three-stone fire stove have the highest loss but is also the cheapest and most 
convenient to construct. Alternative stoves such as solar cookers have been made to improve the 
situation. These solar cookers are usually seen in three different types: the panel cooker, box cooker 
and the parabolic cooker. All these solar cookers rely on the solar irradiation, which means if no sun 
is present the cooker cannot function. This will in return mean that cooking can only happen outside 
during the day. As cooking outside is a necessity, the wind and ambient temperature plays a larger 
impact on the effectiveness of the cooker, though this has lesser impact on the box cooker. 
To accommodate the issue of need for solar irradiation, solar cookers using phase changing material 
(PCM) to preserve the heat have been made. These cookers utilize the latent energy of the PCM to 
preserve the heat, which allows the possibility of all day around cooking, cooking during clouds and 
==PAGE:11==
 
2 
in the evening [5] with an overall utilization efficiency improvement of up to 85%, with 15% 
reduction in heating time [6]. Using PCM in solar cookers have been found to have some limitations, 
one of which is the control of the temperature, as high temperatures cause higher mechanical stress 
and increased corrosion, while the input and output of heat from the PCM can be found difficult to 
determine. Solar cookers utilizing PCM can be designed in several ways where some is designed to 
be used while cooking where others store the heat in a container. 
A prototype for a PCM solar cooker has been developed by the company Heliac. This prototype 
consists of a cylinder filled with PCM along with a concentrating solar collector. The solar cooker 
utilizes the direct irradiation which are used to concentrate the sunlight onto the top surface of the 
solar cooker, where the cooker (SunStore) itself will function as a battery where the heat is stored 
until use in accordance with the users’ cooking routines. To ensure a minimum heat loss the cooker 
is placed inside an insulation case, during both charging and cooking.  
 
Figure 1: Heliac solar collector with on Sunstore battery. 
The solar collector has room of four SunStore batteries, and is tracking the sun in two axis, to assure 
an effective charging with no adjustments needed during the heating of the cooker. A protective 
aluminum shield is attached on top of the SunStore case to avoid burning of the insulation along 
with providing a cover for the SunStore to reduce heat loss. The PCM used in the SunStore is a 
eutectic mixture of sodium acetate and potassium acetate. This mixture defines the proportions 
between two substances for which the lowest melting point is found, this melting point is lower than 
both substances melting point [7]. Melting of the PCM is an essential part for the function of the 
solar cooker, but it is unknown to which degree the PCM has melted during charging of the 
SunStore. 
Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD) has over the years become an essential analytical tool, for most 
branches of engineering and scientific disciplines. CFD is commonly used as a tool for predicting the 
weather, while also used for optimization and innovation of Formula 1 cars. It can also help the 
understanding of why an accident have happened as was the case with King’s cross Underground 
Station Fire where 31 people were killed due to its rapid rise and flashover [8]. The possibility of 
basing a design problem on CFD simulations is that CFD can optimize the design and an optimal 
solution can be found. Though, the reached optimal solution is only as accurate as the model. The 
mesh, turbulence model and settings are essential for the simulation to be as accurate as possible. 
Multi object optimization problems are more problematic and difficult to overcome. These are 
solved through an iterative process where one variable is fixed with the other variables are 
constrained. Finding reasonable values for the constrains are difficult.

3 
Efficiency and robustness of the CFD model and simulation are essential as the cost of computational 
resources is high and can be judged by how few iterations are used to find the stable solution. The 
robustness of the model is essential as this measures the ability to find the solution without 
becoming unstable or getting stuck.  
1.2 Aim and structure of study 
This study extends the SunStore project for Heliac in an iterative process of understanding the 
SunStore and aims to help with future improvements. This will be done by an analytic numerical CFD 
model where multiple aspects of the current SunStore prototype are evaluated. The model attempts 
to predict the heat flow within the prototype to analyze the liquification process, the temperature of 
the PCM and container along with the heat transfer between different parts of the geometry. The 
setup of the model is performed using Ansys Workbench, where the numeric simulation is done 
using Ansys Fluent. To ensure accuracy of the model, three experimental investigations of the 
prototype have been conducted, these experiments will lay the foundation for the validation and 
accuracy of the model.  
In the next chapter (Chapter 2), the theoretical background of the CFD calculation is presented along 
with an investigation of other numerical calculations of PCM. Chapter 3 covers how the model is 
constructed along with the meshing and setup within Fluent. The mesh and Fluent setting are then 
investigated numerically in regard to creating an efficient simulation model (Chapter 4). The model is 
then compared against three experiments, covering heating in oven, heating in tracker and 
discharge to a pot (Chapter 5). Finally, a summery along with recommendation for further work and 
improvements is given in Chapter 6.

In this study, an iterative time advancement solution method of a 
Pressure-Based Segregated solver have been used. This algorithm is 
split into 7 steps which is shown in Figure 3. 
The algorithm starts by updating the fluid properties, the momentum 
equations are then solved sequentially, this gives the velocities of in 
each direction. The third step solves for the pressure correction which 
is given by the continuity equation which encapsules the continuity of 
mass. The mass, the velocity and the pressure are then corrected, and 
the algorithm solves the energy, turbulence, and other scalar 
equations. The 6th step is updating the source term arising from 
interaction between different phases. The 7th and last phase is 
checking for convergence, where the solution either moves to the 
next time step or iterates the solution again. 
 
2.2 Geometry and meshing method 
The geometry and mesh define the foundation for the accuracy of the model, and the method of 
which the simulations are calculated along with the efficiency of the simulation. In this project the 
solid modeling method has been used, where the geometric boundaries are defined and ANSYS 
meshing program generates the mesh automatically depended on the user settings. This method is 
preferred for more complex geometries and 3-D models but may be less effective compared to 
direct generation  [17]. 
 
Figure 4: Example of linear (L) and quadratic (R) elements. [18] 
When generating the mesh, the element can be chosen with a higher order (quadratic elements) 
which inserts an extra mid side node. The quadratic elements are more beneficial to use for 
elements with a degenerate shape, whereas the linear elements are beneficial for non-distorted 
elements and achieves a higher accuracy compared to a comparable quadratic element grid. For the 
linear elements, it is especially important to avoid tetrahedral shapes in critical areas. 
When meshing, four main considerations is made. These include the number of cells, the cell 
distribution, the cell quality, and the cell shape. The number of cells is in direct correlation with the 
computation time but will usually also result in a more accurate model. The number of cells required 
for a sufficient model is largely dependent on the complexity of the flow, and number of critical 
locations but is also highly influenced on cell shape.

2.2.1 Mesh density and quality 
The cell distribution within the model is critical to ensure that the number of elements does not 
increase to an absurd level. The cells should be distributed in a way to increase the cell density 
around critical location. These locations are usually found close to boundary layers, shallow areas, 
and in areas with high curvature as this is often where a high gradient of velocity, pressure, 
temperature, etc. is found [19]. Places where these gradients are found to be much smaller, a larger 
mesh can be made which in turn greatly decrease the amount of cells needed for computation. 
These methods can be seen applied in Figure 5. 
The mesh quality can be measured in different ways, though for a good quality grid it is usually given 
that the angle of the element is in the range between 20° and 160°, an expansion ratio less than 2, 
aspect ratio less than 10, and around 5 to 10 grid point in each direction of boundary layers [20]. 
The angular quality is given by the skewness of the cells, this will be the primary quality check. A 
more specific definition of the angular quality of the mesh is given in formula (2.17). The skewness is

2.2.2 Mesh methods and element shapes 
Depending on the geometry, different methods of meshing are possible, which will result in different 
types of cell shapes. The different 3-dimensional mesh types can be seen in Figure 7. 
The tetrahedron (tet) and hexahedron (hex) types are the primary type for meshing, where each 
type has its own benefits and limitations. The pyramid type is only found in hybrid meshes where a 
hex and a tet type meets and the prisms are formed when a tet mesh is extruded [22]. 
The easiest mesh to generate is a mesh based on tetrahedrons, this type of mesh is easy to generate 
and can be done for all geometries. This type of mesh is especially useful for extremely complex 
geometries or geometries which cannot be decomposed. Despite of these benefits, the tetrahedron 
mesh is not recommended as it is less efficient and needs more computational resources than the 
hex mesh, as the hex type can contain 6 tet type cells with the same edge length. 
The hex mesh is preferred whenever the geometry allows for the type to be applied. For the shape 
to be used, the geometry will need to be simple enough to be decomposed into sweepable bodies. A 
model is fully decomposed when the source and target face of each body are topologically identical 
and connected through continues edges and faces.  
For generation of hex mesh two types of methods can be used: the Sweep method and the 
MultiZone method. The sweep method is recommended when advanced size functions are used and 
all bodies are sweepable. The MultiZone method is more automatic, and is able to decompose the 
geometry itself, but lacks the more advanced size functions. In Figure 8, the MultiZone and Sweep 
method are shown, along with an attempted Sweep method on a non-decomposed geometry.

3.3.2 Calculation method, controls, and report definitions 
The solution method set for the model is the Semi-Implicit Method for Pressure Linked Equations 
(SIMPLE). This has been chosen due to a decent mesh and because of the robustness of the SIMPLE 
algorithm compared to its counterpart SIMPLE-Consistent (SIMPLEC) which is useful for faster 
convergence of uncomplicated problems [21]. The Pressure-Implicit with Splitting of Operators 
(PISO) algorithm have also been used as this method is suggested for transient problems and have 
both skewness and neighbor corrections which corrects velocities to satisfy the continuity and 
momentum more closely. This method does require more CPU computation but may lead to faster 
convergence. 
The spatial discretization gradient is set to least squares cell-based, this has been done as the 
accuracy and computational power benefits from this method, when the mesh is regular and 
structured which, is aimed for doing meshing. For the pressure, the PREssure STaggering Option 
(PRESTO!) is used and for momentum and energy, second order upwind have been chosen, as the 
simulation is seen to converge and are more exact than first order. The successive under-relaxation 
method given in have been used in the calculations. These factors have an impact on the ability for 
the model to converge as well as the calculation time. If these factors are set close to 1 the model 
might easily diverge, as the difference is allowed to be large. Smaller under-relaxation factors can 
have the opposite effect, as this can make the calculation time much larger, as the model is not 
allowed to make larger changes. Because of this, there will be an optimal set of under-relaxation 
factors. The factors will not influence the result, when the solution is converged and will therefore 
not be optimized, but only used to ensure convergence.  
In some simulations, it has been found that the under-relaxation factors had some impact on the 
simulation. This is assumed to be due to the scaled residuals reaching the set convergence indicator, 
as this limits each calculation model. The under-relaxation factors were adjusted throughout the 
simulation to improve the simulation based on the behavior of the model and the scaled residuals. 
The most stable under-relaxation factors for the start of the simulation are for the pressure 0.2, 
momentum 0.5 and energy 0.95, after which the under-relaxation factors may be increased as the 
simulation becomes more stable. The stability is judged, based on the scaled residuals along with the 
behavior of the report definitions and visual inspection.

3.3.3 Simulation 
It was found from the stability of the simulation, that during the early parts of the simulation where 
no melting has occurred, the model was more stable and converged easily, whereas when melting of 
the PCM initiated, the model became more unstable and did not converge. Because of this, the 
simulation has been sectioned into two main parts. The first part will run from the initialization at 
20°C and run until melting within the PCM is seen, after which the second part of the simulation is 
run with significantly reduced time steps. The time step determines the time between each 
simulated flow step and is included in the calculation in the first term of formula (2.7). This indicates 
that large time steps can result in an inaccurate result. To ensure an accurate simulation a time step 
analysis is performed and can be used to improve the efficiency of the simulation.

4.2 Time step independence 
To ensure an accurate and efficient simulation a time step independence investigation is conducted. 
The time step investigation examines how long the time step between each solution step can be 
while still creating a reliable solution. The time step investigation is split into two parts. The first part 
is before melting while the second part is after melting, this split is made due to the increased 
calculation complexity of melting. In the first part, four different time steps are investigated: 0.5 s, 1 
s, 2 s and 4 s. The time step investigation is made on the 500K locally altered mesh, to ensure a good 
mesh and low calculation time. The first part of the simulation is simulating 5200 seconds as this was 
found to be the time point of melting based on the mesh investigation.  
As mentioned previously the method of calculation will not change the results of the simulation but 
may improve the calculation time and efficiency. To ensure an efficient calculation the 
recommended method for transient problems PISO is evaluated. PISO should be more accurate at 
larger timesteps and when using a higher skewed mesh. The calculation method may take more 
computational power pr. iteration but will converge more quickly. In the first part, three PISO 
simulations have been conducted: a 1 second timestep, a 2 second timestep and a 4 second 
timestep. To evaluate convergence, a model for which the residuals convergence criteria has been 
significantly decreased to 1e-06 for all residuals except for the energy residual, which is set to 1e-12, 
as well as constant change of the under-relaxation factors is compared. As described previously 
these parameters is not the only parameters used to ensure convergence but are a good indication 
of convergence.

5.3 Oven experiment and simulation 
The first experiment conducted is the oven experiment where the battery alone, with a set oven 
temperature of 260 °C. The outer thermocouples are fastened to the battery with a piece of 
insulation covering the thermocouple to make sure that the thermocouples are not influenced by 
the temperature of the oven. This validation tries to validate the thermal properties of the materials 
as well as the geometry. 
The aim of the experiment is to validate the model by using the measured outer temperatures while 
the simulation is validated depended on the match between the recorded temperatures of the 
internal sensors and the simulated temperature at those locations. This is done by importing a 
transient table into Fluent, this transient table contains the time, from experiment start, as well as 
the recorded temperatures for the top, middle and bottom plate for each corresponding time step. 
These values are then used for the boundary conditions for the top plate, the side, and the bottom 
plate of the battery.  
==PAGE:43==
 
34 
This method eliminates the heat transfer from the oven air to the battery as this can be very hard to 
estimated and can variate heavily due to the air velocity around the battery. This way of modeling 
has some limitations as this approach will model the entire surfaces with each of their respective 
temperature given by the transient table. This will create a hard-edge temperature gradient at the 
corners of the battery, where two surfaces meet. This will limit the heat transfer in the direction 
between the surfaces. Despite of these limitations the method is deemed sufficient. This method 
can be made more accurate with the use of more thermocouples attached to the battery, as a 
temperature gradient over the surfaces can be made. Figure 34 shows the temperatures recorded 
from the oven experiment. 
It can be seen from Figure 34 that melting is mostly reached after 18000 s or 5 hours. This is found 
due to the large bend in the temperature, as the salt is mostly constant during melting and the bend 
indicates that melting has been completed. At the 18000 s mark the outer temperature is measured 
to 238.3°C, 241.6°C, and 239.7°C for the top, middle and bottom respectively. While the internal 
temperatures are measured to 234.4°C, 233.9°C, 233.7°C and 229.6°C for sensor 1, 2, 3 and 4. While 
after 18670 s, all thermocouples reach temperatures over 235°C indicated that the SunStore have 
been fully melted.  
As explained previously the temperature during melting is assumed to be constant, though this is 
not what is seen, instead the melting takes place over a temperature range. Because of this, the 
simulated temperatures may be higher than measurements recorded during the experiments prior 
to melting and lower after melting. This can be seen from Figure 35, as at a given value of stored 
heat prior to melting, a higher temperature is found for the ideal, while a lower temperature is 
found after melting.

5.4 Solar collector experiment and simulation 
The setup for the solar collector experiment is shown on Figure 40. For the experiment, a non-
selective surface is attached to the top of the SunStore. This surface has an estimated emissivity of 
0.9. To ensure good contact between the surface and the battery, a layer of graphite powder is used 
to fill any potential gaps. This is also where temperature sensor top – external is placed during 
charging. The external middle thermocouple has been placed halfway down the side of the 
SunStore, while the bottom external sensor is placed by the corner of the bottom plate. This location 
is chosen to ensure best possible contact to the battery. The internal sensors extrude approximately 
4 cm out from the bottom plate, along with the bolt head of the thermowell, to accommodate this 
the insulation has been cut in the bottom area where the thermocouples extrude from the battery.  
 
The solar collector experiment was executed Thursday the 3rd of June. The experiment started at 
approximate 8:30 and finished at about 14:00. The solar collector automatically tracks the sun as this 
is essential for the focus of the solar collector to hit the battery as only the direct normal irradiance 
(beam irradiance) is used, during this time the tracker was adjusted a few times to correct the focus 
point. In addition to the temperatures, the total irradiation is measured. To validate the model the 
measured temperature of the top plate can be used as a transient boundary condition. This method 
is adequate for the validation of the model as the validation is based on the temperature at different 
locations. Preferably, the beam irradiance is used for validation as the radiation and conductive heat 
loss of the top plate can be validated. The validation of the top plate enables the ability to use the 
simulation to predict the charging in any environment, where the beam irradiance is measured. As 
only the total irradiation is measured during the experiment, the direct normal radiation will be 
taken from DTU Climate station [40].  
Figure 41 shows the measured total solar irradiation (TSI)  on the surface along with the direct 
normal irradiance (DNI) and the diffuse irradiation measured on a plane normal to the sun (DI(N)). 
The DNI measures only the direct irradiation at an angle for which the normal vector points directly 
towards the sun, whereas the DI(N) only measures the diffuse irradiance. The diffuse irradiance is an 
indication of reflective irradiance from the sun and will be high when clouds are present. This will in 
return lower the DNI. The TSI measures both the direct and the diffuse irradiation at an angle for 
which the plane normal vector points directly towards the sun.

loss from the pot along with heat transfer rate into the water. Because of this further investigation 
of the discharge along with the simulation will be based on oil.

experiment, the battery is taken from the oven and put into the insulation box, during this time the 
surface of the battery decrease in temperature, this have an impact on the calculated heat transfer, 
as the surface will be adjusted during the first moments of the experiment, leading to a lower heat 
transfer than expected. The second limitation is found in the solidification of the PCM, as during 
solidification the temperature decrease will be smaller. This cannot be accounted for with the 
limited temperature measurements.

6.1 Internal structure optimization 
To improve the efficiency of charging the SunStore cooker, the inner geometry of the cooker has 
been investigated. In the investigation, three main aspects of the geometry are investigated, this 
includes the wall thickness of the rods, the longest and average distance from the rod surface and 
method of heating. This is done by altering the rod layout while keeping the volume of the aluminum 
and PCM the same as the prototype. The results from this optimization investigation, will not 
indicate real world performance as it was seen from the validation that the model is not 
representable for the performance seen in the experiment. Despite of this, it has been assumed that 
the models can be compared internally with the performance increase/decrease found can be used 
for future improvements of the SunStore. 
Multiple issues for the prototype have been found and will be addressed in the optimization process. 
The distance between the surfaces of the heat rods varies between 2.1 mm and 8 mm, and the salt is 
heated from four sides. Heating from four sides may not impact the melting time as the distance 
from each side is the same and the heat transfer will be identical. Additionally, the prototype is 
designed with a large number of enclosed volumes which will heavily impact the flow and natural 
convection inside the battery. 
The performance of each design will be based of behavior of the liquid fraction, temperatures and 
how the heat is transferred into the PCM. The temperatures are especially important as an upper 
limit of the potassium acetate is found at 60 K above the melting point [29] which indicates 
degradation of the salt at 295°C, this temperature limit is not found for the eutectic salt mixture 
where the melting point is notably lower. For the geometry investigation three different designs 
exploring the different parameters have been simulated. These designs are shown in Figure 56, 
where the arrow indicates the direction of heating.  
==PAGE:60==

The first alternative layout shown on Figure 56 (2) aims to better spread out the rods by using 
aluminum plates instead of the hollow square profile. The surface area and thereby the thickness of 
the plates will have an impact on how far the heat is transferred down into the battery. This is 
important due to the upper temperature limit of the salt. In addition, the layout aims to reduce the 
distance between the surfaces, as the thermal conductivity of the PCM in markedly lower. To 
balance the distance between the surfaces, thickness and the surface area, a thickness of 3 mm has 
been chosen. The layout consists of 13 plates of aluminum. In the layout shown on Figure 56, the 
plates go 22 cm down into the salt, leaving 1 cm to the bottom plate of the cooker. The use of plates 
compared to square profile enables better utilization of the heating area as well, as the plate is more 
evenly spread out. The plates can be adjusted in terms of length, thickness, and amount to find an 
optimal combination. This will not be investigated during this investigation and may change 
depending on the irradiation, as this will create comparable higher or lower temperature on the top 
plate.  
A second alternative geometry shown on Figure 56 (3) have been created to attempt to transfer the 
heat from the top plate down through the PCM as fast as possible to enable heating from the 
bottom. The design has a large rod which is 6.5 cm in diameter in the center of the battery. This 
circular shape maximizes the area to circumference ratio, which minimizes the heat transferred into 
the salt mixture. In the bottom of the large rod a plate used to distribute the heat, this plate is 20 cm 
in diameter and have a thickness of 1 cm. This layout leaves a large volume between the rod and the 
outer wall which does not have any surface close to it and relies heavily on the natural convection to 
melt. Optimizations of this design may be to add more horizontal plates along the large rod in 
heights where melting last occurs. 
To test the impact of heating from the bottom the geometry shown on Figure 56 (4) have been 
tested. This geometry is identical with the prototype in terms of heat pipes but is instead heated 
from the bottom with the air layer still located in the top of the geometry. This maximizes the 
natural convection which can occur for the prototype geometry. The reverse geometry may be

to the natural convection reducing the temperature near the heated plate, which is not true for the 
prototype and plate geometry where the highest temperatures are found near the heated plate. The 
large increase from the container seen for the reverse geometry is also expected, as large amount of

320 cm2, where the area for which the heat pipe is closest is only 
170 cm2. This concept is shown on Figure 62. This corresponds 
well with the container wall having a heat transfer rate 
approximately twice as large as the heat transfer from the heat 
pipe.

investigated with no visual change in the seen fluctuations. Further investigations for this geometry 
will need to be made. 
Overall, it is found that the large rod is notably worse than any of the other designs and is the only 
design to reach critical temperature within the simulated time frame. The reverse design is found to 
be effective for melting while keeping the temperature low. It was found that the flow is restricted 
by the heat pipe due to the enclosed area but does have a positive impact on the distribution of heat 
within the container. The best geometry was found to be the plate geometry but due to the 
fluctuations found in the PCM gain, this increase in liquid fraction may be caused by instabilities in 
heat distribution. This should impact the temperature recorded, but this is not seen. Further 
investigations of this geometry are needed. The geometry impacted the most by the container was 
found to be the large rod geometry followed by the reverse, where the reverse is heavily impacted 
by the heated surface. The dependency of the container for heating may be found less favorable as 
the thickness of the container wall may be reduced in future iterations. This wall thickness reduction 
will decrease the heat transfer in the container wall. On Figure 64 to Figure 70 the liquid fraction

Chapter 7 
 
Conclusion 
 
8.1 Summery and outlook 
The scope of this project was to create a CFD model which would help increase the understanding 
the heat distribution, flow, and liquification/solidification of the SunStore, in addition to validate the 
model based on three experiments. From the knowledge gained from CFD model and literature 
study three alternative designs for the internal structure is made and compared, which allows for 
recommendation of future improvement. This is essential for further improvements, and this gives a 
perspective on what works and what does not. 
In this project a geometry has been made that somewhat fit the prototype. The prototype was seen 
to have several different inconsistencies in the heat pipe layout and profiles. These differences have 
in this study been neglected, as this significantly increased the complexity of the geometry and 
therefore would make it more difficult to mesh. This resulted in an alteration of the PCM density 
from the presumed density of 1553 kg/m3 to 1402 kg/m3, which would locally heat up faster due to 
the reduced weight. 
The model presented have gone through a mesh independence investigation as well as a time step 
independence investigation. The mesh investigations compared five different meshes using the 
same geometry to determine the lowest mesh cell count for which an accurate result was obtained 
where a 500K mesh which have been locally altered was found to be sufficient. The time step 
investigation found that that while no melting is occurring within the SunStore a larger time step 
could be used to improve the efficiency of the simulation, for this part a time step of 1s was found 
appropriate. For the second part of the simulation where melting occurs a maximum time step of 
0.2s was found, this time step could create divergence in some model and a time step of 0.15s is 
therefore recommended. 
The oven experiment showed that a good correlation between the simulation and the experimental 
temperatures. It was found that the temperatures in the CFD simulation rose faster in the start of 
the experiment creating a maximum difference of 14°C. The temperatures which were measured 
using the internal sensor number 1, was found inconsistent as the heat would travel though the 
thermowell and impact temperature. 
The tracker experiment showed a larger discrepancy between the CFD simulation and the 
experimental data, where temperature differences between the simulation and the experimental 
data was found to be up to 40°C. And the model could not be validated based on the experiment. 
A thorough investigation of the charging experiment it has been found that for the entire charging of 
the SunStore the total efficiency was found to be 21.4%. The theoretical heat loss for the convective, 
conductive, and radiative heat loss was found to 0.41 kWh, 0.22 kWh and 0.65 kWh. Assuming these 
are correct and with an optical efficiency of the lens and cover glass was found to 0.6 [34], a focus 
==PAGE:69==
 
60 
efficiency was added which was found to be 0.6 to fulfill the stored heat calculated for the SunStore 
at 1.91 kWh. 
The cooking experiment used for validation was made using 5 liters of sunflower oil, instead of 5 
liters of water. This change was made due to the complexity of evaporation in the CFD modeling. 
From the CFD simulation a large rate in temperatures increase was found for the sunflower oil, along 
which a large rate in decreasing temperature for the SunStore. The model showed the same 
temperature behavior as seen in the experiment with two larger bends indicating the initialization of 
the solidification and the end of solidification. Furthermore, a larger heat loss was found in the CFD 
calculation compared to the theoretical value found for the experiment. 
Based on the experimental data a heat transfer rate between 2.5 [W/K] and 5 [W/K] was determined 
between the SunStore and cooking pot, with a theoretical total heat loss coefficient for the pot at a 
maximum 1.05 [W/K] and total heat loss coefficient for the battery between 0.2 and 0.1 [W/K]. 
These have been made assuming a constant temperature for the pot and SunStore respectively as 
only one temperature for each was measured but was found to correlate well with the calculated 
change in stored heat. 
Based on the current model which have been presented further work is needed to fully fulfill the 
desired result as the model could not be fully validated, this is true for the most important 
experiment which is charging in the tracker. Many uncertainties are found within the SunStore, this 
ranges from determination the thermal properties of the materials used, to how the SunStore is 
produced and how the crookedness and irregularities of the SunStore impacts the results. 
From the geometry optimization three alternative designs of the internal structure was tested. From 
this was found that an overall good spread of the heat pipes is first and foremost the most essential 
as lack of this can lead to critical temperatures within the PCM leading the degradation of the PCM. 
Furthermore, the results indicated that an optimal surface area to aluminum volume ratio can be 
found which would optimize the charging time, this ratio for the prototype was found to be too high. 
The results simulated was done on a vertical battery, and whether a change in tilt will impact the 
result needs further work. 
Future work within this project would include further investigations of the mesh of the model, this 
may be done using symmetry planes to reduce the geometry size. Further work will also include a 
more accurate representation of the SunStore with better definition of the thermal properties and 
all materials used within the SunStore along with the impact of the used silicone along the container 
wall and pour contact between the top plate and heat pipes.
