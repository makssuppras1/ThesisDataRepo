Abstract
We will in the following work look into the Discontinuous Galerkin Finite Element Method
and how it may be used in modelling the evolution of interfaces using the level set method.
We will go into details of the formulation of the discontinuous Galerkin method and
how it is used to solve hyperbolic conservation laws and higher order problems.
The Discontinuous Galerkin Finite Element Method will be used to solve the level set
equation and the reinitialization process. We will look into Hamilton-Jacobi equations,
some of their properties and how we may use the discontinuous Galerkin method to solve
them. We will use artiﬁcial diﬀusion to stabilize the method at discontinuities where a
decay of the modal coeﬃcients are used to detect the elements containing discontinuities.
Last we will go through some examples of the evolution of an interface in one dimension
and two dimensions. We will see why reinitialization is important and how well we may
capture the evolution of a linear standing wave in two dimensions using the discontinuous
Galerkin method and the level set method.

Introduction
Modelling of free surfaces is of particular interest e.g.
for the load of waves hitting
oﬀ-shore structures or the load of liquids in a container. Typically the liquid interacts
with air, but as the liquid typically is the dominating ﬂuid, we may consider this only;
known as a one-phase approach. An example of this is potential theory, where the ﬂow
is assumed to be irrotational and frictionless. By these assumptions the computations
simpliﬁes signiﬁcantly but we have to consider a domain which is varying in time and
also with varying boundary conditions at the free surface. This approach comes to short,
when we consider topological changes e.g. for breaking waves.
Another approach to the modelling of free surfaces is the two-phase approach.That
is, we consider both the air and the liquid. Here we may consider some ﬁxed domain
and avoid the use of boundary conditions on the free surface. We then have to take both
ﬂuids into account thus increasing the number of computations. We are on the other
hand not limited to make any assumptions on the ﬂow thus ﬂows around more complex
geometries may also be considered.
A place, where potential theory can not be used, is seen in Figure 0.1.
Here we
see an example of enormous waves hitting an oil rig in the North Sea. Here the waves
are breaking and hitting the structure with huge force. One issue here is that the ﬂow is
turning turbulent at the structure. That is, the motion of the ﬂuid gets rather chaotic and
with a large range of diﬀerent scales. For a direct numerical solution to turbulent ﬂows, we
demand so high resolutions that these simulations are impractical for the industry. Today
these types of ﬂows is mainly considered numerically using the simpliﬁed turbulence
models as Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) and Large Eddy Simulations (LES).
The work on simulations of ﬂows is known as Computational Fluids Dynamics (CFD).
Here we seek numerical solutions to ﬂow and especially two methods are widely integrated
in the industry - namely the Finite Element Method (FEM) and the Finite Volume
Method (FVM). Where the FEM oﬀers a method capable of easily achieving high order
by using high order global polynomials, the FVM are by construction conservative. Also
the FVM may easily be ﬁt to convection dominated ﬂows by introducing suitable ﬂuxes
e.g. an upwinding ﬂux.
An increasingly popular method in the CFD community is the Discontinuous
Galerkin Finite Element Method (DG-FEM). It oﬀers a high order method with the
conservative properties of the FVM. The method uses a local approach thus making it
especially eﬃcient for large-scale modelling of ﬂows in parallel implementations on GPU

Today the modelling of free surfaces using a two-phase approach is mainly done by
two methods. The volume of ﬂuid method and the level set method. They both model
the interface implicitly hence making them suitable for topological changes. They are
both used on unstructured grids making them suitable for complex free surfaces.
The volume of ﬂuid uses a scalar indicator function with values between 0 and 1
on each element. Here 0 indicates the ﬁrst ﬂuid and 1 indicates the second ﬂuid. For
elements with values in between, we have the surface. The volume of ﬂuid method is
known for its’ conservative properties but also for its’ problem in computing the surface
tension due to problems in determining the normal to the surface.
This is where the level set method has its’ strength. For the level set method, the
surface is modelled by a higher dimensional level set function, whose zero-contour models
the surface. The level set function is a signed distance function, where we measure the
distance to the surface. For one ﬂuid we consider a positive distance measure and for the
other we consider a negative distance measure. Although this seems promising we note
that the level set method may suﬀer for loss in mass.
Motivation and goals
We will in this work like to look into the world of two-phase modelling of incompressible
Navier-Stokes ﬂuids using a DG-FEM approach together with a level set modelling of the
interface. Using a combination of the Navier-Stokes equation and the level set method -
each solved using the DG-FEM - has huge potential when we model complex interfaces.
We may resolve the diﬀerent scales thus consider more complex ﬂow leading to better
understanding of ﬂuid ﬂows.
We will in the work see how we may formulate a DG-FEM solver for one-phase Navier-
Stokes but we mention here that the focus has been on the level set part and how we

Chapter 2
The Discontinuous Galerkin
Finite Element Method
The Discontinuous Galerkin Finite Element Method (DG-FEM) is a numerical method
which combines the ﬂexibility and hp-convergence of the FEM with the conservative
property and ﬂow eﬃciency of the FVM. The introduction to DG-FEM has been
accomplished by looking into the work by Hesthaven and Warburtons book of nodal
DG-FEM, [6]. We will go through details from this book in this chapter.
The DG-FEM are, as the name suggests, related to the FEM. We discretize the
domain into a ﬁnite number of elements and require that - on all elements - the residual
is orthogonal to all test functions in order to approximate the true unknown solutions
as well as possible.
We have a Galerkin approach meaning that the test functions are taken from the
same space as our basis functions.
It is at the choice of basis functions where the
diﬀerence between the DG-FEM and the FEM is the clearest. Instead of having global
basis functions with local support, we consider local basis functions deﬁned on each
element. In order to achieve this we would introduce additional nodes by repeating all
nodes at neighbouring faces. By this we introduce extra computation by increasing our
degrees of freedom. We will see an example of the repeated nodes later on. Although
the additional computational work seems unpromising we remind that all computations
are local thus the mass matrix is local and hence smaller. This local formulation makes
the DG-FEM an eﬃcient tool for parallel implementations.
In order to obtain unique solutions at the neighbouring faces, we will connect the
elements through an artiﬁcial ﬂux. These ﬂuxes may be utilized in order to take e.g.
advection into account by using an upwind ﬂux as in FVM.

Level Set Modelling
The level set method is used to model interfaces and tracking their evolution. It was
introduced by Osher and Sethian in 1988, [17] in order to track a moving front. The
main advantage for the level set method is that it tracks an interface implicitly as a
zero-level isocontour of a function. By this we will easier handle topology changes e.g.
breaking waves or two bubbles of water merging into one. The level set method is used
in several other disciplines e.g. image segmentation and computer graphics.

interface between two ﬂuids (e.g. air/water). It is implicit in that way, that the interface
between the two ﬂuids is an isocontour of a higher dimensional function.

4.1.1
Discontinuities
We have now seen that we get an (almost) optimal convergence using the LDG method
to solve the smooth Hamilton-Jacob equation. Solutions are nevertheless not limited to
being smooth. Several equations exist with kinks (discontinuous derivatives) and shocks
e.g. the Burger equation. The smoothness of the solution is well known to be a huge
importance for obtaining spectral accuracy but also for keeping the numerical solutions
stable - for high order methods in particular.
In order to ensure stability several choices exist. We may use ﬁlters to remove the
high order modes after the computations or we may use limiting which removes the
high order modes at the discontinuities introducing a low-order approximation here. As
these method fail to remove the problem or they aﬀect the accuracy, we would like to
do something else.

in order to smooth out the discontinuities. Although this method allows us to smooth

Advection of an interface
As a last part of this work we will look into the coupling of advection of an interface and
the reinitialization.
We will consider the level set equation
φt + V · ∇φ = 0
for a given velocity ﬁeld and where φ = 0 denotes the interface. The level set equation
will be solved using the strong form and an upwinding ﬂux.
In order to ensure a stable computation, we will employ reinitialization at every time
step. That is, we will reinitialize the level set function after each step of the advection of
the interface. In practice this is not always necessary, as the level set function might not
deviate from being a signed distance function that much in each step.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and perspective
We have throughout this work seen how we may formulate the high order DG-FEM for
solving hyperbolic PDE’s and how the ﬂuxes are given us a ﬂexible method. We have
looked into the use of the level set method for modelling of interfaces and especially the
reinitialization part. As the reinitialization is a so called Hamilton-Jacobi equation, we
have seen some of the properties of the Hamilton-Jacobi equation and how we may
formulate a DG-FEM approach in order to retain the high order. As these equations
might have discontinuous solutions, we have seen that by adding local artiﬁcial
diﬀusion, we are able to stabilize the scheme. The discontinuities was detected using a
discontinuity detector based on the decay of the modal coeﬃcients. We have then gone
through simple advection problems of interfaces using the level set method in both one
and two dimensions.
A take away from these experiments is that indeed we are to model the evolution of an
interface using the level set method. We may keep the function a signed distance function
using reinitialization but also we should be aware of the amount of reinitialization. To
much reinitialization is producing bad results. The amount of reinitialization should be
tuned to every problem.
Lastly we note here that the potential for a high order method capable of considering
the two-phase problem indeed is huge. With the development of a DG-FEM scheme for
the two-phase incompressible Naiver-Stokes equation and a DG-FEM scheme for the level
set method, the use of a high order method for solving complex free surfaces is indeed
plausible.

6.2
Perspective
We will end this work with some perspective of the use of high order methods for
simulation of free surface using the level set approach.
First we will denote the paper that was the ﬁrst insiration to this work, [10]. Here
the authors have made a incompressible Navier-Stokes level set solver for incompressible
two-phase ﬂows. The solver includes a two phase Navier-Stokes solver combined with
level set advection and reinitialization of the interface between the two phases.
The
method uses adaptive meshing in order to increase the resolution at the interface. The
entire formulation is based on the DG-FEM, where current work is on parallelization of
the solver onto GPU architectures using the uniﬁed multi-threading language, OCCA
[13].
The main drawback of the level set method is still the loss of mass. Many attempts
have been made in order to resolve this. Some to mention is Ollson and Kreiss who in
2005 created a conservative level set method, [14] and further developed it in [15]. Also
a coupling of the volume of ﬂuid and the level set have been tried several times. Some
to mention is [22] from 2000, [24] from 2004 and most recently [12].
Another new proposal for interface modelling came also recently. In [20] they used
a conservative phase ﬁeld approach for tracking an interface using high order methods.
The phase ﬁeld approach is similar to the volume of ﬂuids where a auxiliary variable
varies between 0 and 1 but here the interface is more diﬀusive and may have a thickness
over several elements. They apply both reinitialization and also local artiﬁcial diﬀusion
based on decay of the modal coeﬃcients.
