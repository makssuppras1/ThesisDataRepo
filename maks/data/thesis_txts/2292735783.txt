ABSTRACT 
 
The objective of this study was to investigate the association between the indoor climate in 
classrooms and two retrofit ventilation solutions, a decentralized mechanical system and a 
visual feedback panel system. In particular, the performance of the systems was evaluated by 
their effect on affects the pupils’ academic performance, their perception of the indoor climate, 
the intensity of building related symptoms. The study targeted both the heating and non-heating 
seasons.  
 
During two six-week periods in the heating and non-heating season the operation mode of the 
systems alternated between on and off in week-long periods. The hypothesis of the study was 
that the pupils’ perceptions, symptoms, and performance would improve in the weeks when the 
retrofitted system was in operation.  
 
The indoor climate in the classrooms was assessed by continuous measurement of the CO2 
concentration and temperature. Questionnaires were used to evaluate the pupils’ perception of 
the indoor climate and the intensity of symptoms and their performance was evaluated by tests 
integrated in their daily schoolwork. The results were compared with results from a reference 
classroom, were no altering of ventilation system were made during the experimental period.      
 
The air quality was improved in both retrofitted classrooms when the systems were operating. In 
the decentralized mechanical system the percentage of occupied time with CO2 concentration 
above 1,000 ppm was reduced from 73% to 29% in the heating season and from 46% to 22% in 
the non-heating season. In the classroom with a visual feedback panel the percentage of 
occupied time with CO2 concentration above 1,000 ppm was reduced from 62% to 57% in the 
heating season and from 34% to 18% in the non-heating season. Neither during the heating or 
the non-heating season did the system operation mode affect the classroom temperature.  
 
The operation mode of the systems resulted in only modest difference in the pupils’ perception 
of the indoor climate and the intensity of symptoms. However, during the non-heating season a 
significant improvement in the pupils’ perception of their concentration ability was seen in the 
classroom with the decentralized mechanical system when the system was in operation. 
 
In the heating season, the pupils were more accurate when the systems were in operation, 
although this was significant only in the classroom with the visual feedback panel. In the non-
heating season the error rate decreased significantly in the classroom with the visual feedback 
panel. No other significant performance outcomes were found.   
 
Overall, the decentralized mechanical system had the most positive effect on the measured 
indoor air quality and the visual feedback panel resulted in the largest improvement of the 
pupils’ performance.

1 INTRODUCTION 
 
In Denmark there is 10 years compulsory education, and almost all Danish children go to 
elementary school in all 10 years. Elementary school is the stepping-stone for the children’s 
further education and professional life and therefore optimal conditions for learning are utterly 
important. Furthermore, children spend more time in school than in any other place besides 
home.   
 
Many factors affect children’s learning. The most important is individual based, e.g. the child’s 
desire and ability to learn. This depends among other things on motivation, intellect and family 
background. Beside these factors also the teachers’ ability to teach influences learning. 
Teaching skills depend among other things on the teacher motivation and level of education. 
The society can do something about many of these factors for example improve the skills of the 
teachers’ by getting educated at the university, like the teacher for the high schools do. 
However, it is also relevant to optimize the frames around the children, which are not based on 
individual characteristics. This includes an improvement of the indoor climate, which may 
ensure a more homogeneous learning environment for the children (Kjeldsen et al. 2013). 
 
The law on educational environment states that students and pupils have the right to a good 
learning environment where learning can take place in a safe and healthy manner. Nevertheless, 
several studies show that there is not a satisfactory indoor climate in approximate 50 % of the 
Danish schools (Toftum et al. 2011; Clausen et al. 2014).  
 
It has been known for a long time that the indoor climate has a significant influence on human 
health, performance and well-being, e.g. (Wargocki et al. 2002). There have also been studies 
showing that children are particularly sensitive to the indoor climate, which results in higher 
absence among pupils who attend schools where the indoor climate is not acceptable (Bakó-biró 
et al. 2011; Mendell et al. 2013) Studies have also indicated that there is an association between 
the indoor climate and the performance of elementary school pupils(Toftum et al. 2011; Wyon 
et al. 2010; Bakó-biró et al. 2011; Petersen et al. 2015).  
 
The purpose of this project was to investigate the effect on the indoor climate, pupils learning 
and well-being for different types of retrofitted ventilation system in classroom. The study took 
place at Ravnsholtskolen.  
 
Ravnsholtskolen is an elementary school located in Allerød, north of Copenhagen. The school 
has a history of problems with the indoor climate. Allerød municipality made early studies of 
the CO2 concentration on the school and found a need to improve the indoor climate to comply 
with both official requirements and user needs. Originally, the school was ventilated by natural 
ventilation with manual opening of windows and door. To improve the indoor climate at the 
==PAGE:14==

school, four different types of ventilation systems were retrofitted in four different classrooms. 
This study will try to determine which system best suits the school in terms of improving the 
indoor climate, but also in relation to a possible improvement of the pupils’ schoolwork

In this study it is primarily two of the retrofitted system on the school that will be investigated:  
a decentralized mechanical ventilation system and a natural ventilation system that is based on 
visual feedback from a CO2 concentration sensor installed in the classroom. These two systems 
will be compared with a reference classroom without any retrofits installed.  
 
This project is part of a larger study, EFFECTIVE ENERGY-EFFICIENT CLASSROOM 
VENTILATION FOR TEMPERATE ZONES, ASHRAE 1624 TRP Technical Committee TC 
2.1, which is executed in the period October 2014 to February 2017.

2 OBJECTIVES 
 
The primary objective of this thesis was to study the association between retrofitted ventilation 
type and indoor climate in classrooms and how it affects pupil performance and well-being. 
 
The indoor climate was assessed based mostly on the CO2 concentration and temperature. Pupil 
performance was assessed through results from different performance tests, such as 
mathematical tests, grammatical logic tests and concentration tests. Additionally, the pupils’ 
perceptions of the indoor climate and the intensity of symptoms were evaluated by 
questionnaires.

3 BACKGROUND 
 
In the investigation of the indoor climate effects on pupil performance, it was relevant to 
describe the indoor climate in Danish schools, primarily to substantiate this investigation. It was 
also relevant to clarify what earlier research on this topic has shown.   
 
Indoor climate in Danish schools!
3.1
Pupils spend more than 30 % of their waking hours in school, and they must attend school and 
be present in the classroom even though the indoor climate is poor. 
 
The indoor climate is a broad term that includes different parameters; indoor air quality, thermal 
conditions, sound- and lighting. The gaseous and particulate compounds in the air can describe 
the indoor air quality. In rooms where humans are the main pollution source most often the CO2 
concentration is used to describe the air quality. The temperature and the relative humidity may 
characterize the thermal condition.      
 
The indoor climate in Danish schools has been tested in different studies. In two of the studies 
the CO2 concentration in classrooms was measured by the pupils themselves (Toftum et al. 
2011; Clausen et al. 2014). The first study was carried out in 743 classrooms distributed on 320 
different schools, which corresponds to 13 % of the schools in Denmark. The measurements 
were carried out in September and October 2009 (Toftum et al. 2011). The second study was 
carried out in 2014 in 785 classrooms distributed on 262 different schools, which corresponds to 
11 % of the schools in Denmark (Clausen et al. 2014). The two experiments were conducted in 
collaboration between DTU and Danish Science Festival (Dansk Naturvidenskabsfestival). The 
CO2 concentration in the classrooms was measured by drawing air through a CO2 measuring 
tube. The two studies were performed by the same methodology. 
 
The result from the studies showed that the CO2 concentration was only lower than the 
maximum acceptable level of 1000 ppm in 44 % and 40 % of the classes in 2009 and 2014, 
respectively (Toftum et al. 2011; Clausen et al. 2014). There has been a worsening of the CO2 
concentration over the five years, from a result that already showed an unacceptable CO2 
concentration in Danish schools. The distribution of the CO2 concentration from the most recent 
study in the five regions is shown on FIGURE 3-1 (Clausen et al. 2014). 
 
==PAGE:18==
 
6 
 
 
FIGURE 3-1: CO2 concentration in the regions (Clausen et al. 2014). 
There are many reasons why the air quality, is so poor in Danish schools. The most obvious is 
the lack of a functional ventilation system. Particularly schools with natural ventilation, which 
was approximately 50 % of the schools in the mass experiments, had the highest fraction of time 
with a CO2 concentration above 1000 ppm (Toftum et al. 2011). This is most likely due to the 
majority of the natural ventilation system working be manual opening of windows by the 
teachers and the pupils. This is not sustainable when trying to achieve an acceptable indoor 
climate and especially not in winter period (Wyon et al. 2010).  
 
Studies from other parts of the world also indicate that ventilation is inadequate in many 
classrooms, thereby leading to a poor indoor environment (Daisey et al. 2003). However, results 
from mass experiments in Sweden and Norway show that the CO2 concentration was under the 
acceptable level of 1000 ppm, in about 80 % of the classes. The better indoor climate is among 
other things a result of that in Sweden and Norway, only 6 % and 20 % of the schools, 
respectively have natural ventilation (Toftum et al. 2011).  
 
 
The effect on pupils of a poor indoor climate  
3.2
A poor indoor climate can affect pupil’s performance in the classroom in two ways; one where 
it affects the learning directly by removing focus from the school work due to discomfort and 
reduced motivation. The other effect is the health issue with poor indoor climate, which can 
result in higher absence of the pupils (Kjeldsen et al. 2013). Higher absence indirectly may 
result in poorer learning.   
 
==PAGE:19==
 
7 
 
The association between the indoor climate and the performance of the pupils has been tested in 
several earlier studies (Petersen et al. 2015; Bakó-biró et al. 2011; Kjeldsen et al. 2013). A study 
on the correlation between school ventilation system and results from the Danish National test 
scheme shows that schools with mechanical supply and exhaust, which typically have the best 
air quality, also have the best national test results. Schools with mechanical exhaust have the 
second highest test result and schools with natural ventilation, which typically have the worst air 
quality, have the lowest test result (Toftum et al. 2015). Another study has found a linear 
association between classroom ventilation rates and pupils’ academic achievement within the 
range of 0.9 – 7.0 l/s per person. For an increase of the ventilation rate of 1 l/s per person the 
percentage of pupils passing a standardized test is expected to increase by 2.7 % for reading and 
2.9 % for math. In the study one hundred elementary schools of two school districts in the 
southwest United States were included and the ventilation rate was estimated using CO2 
concentrations measured during occupied school days. (Haverinen-Shaughnessy et al. 2011; 
Shendell et al. 2004)  
 
Results from computerized performance tasks performed by more than 200 pupils showed 
significantly faster and more accurate responses when interventions increased the supply of 
outdoor air from about 1 l/s per person to about 8 l/s per person (Bakó-biró et al. 2011). A 
preliminary study resulted also in a significant (P<0.10) and positive association between 
classroom ventilation rate and test results in math (Shaughnessy et al. 2006). 
 
The latest research on the effect of increased classroom ventilation rate on the performance of 
schoolwork by pupils, have shown a significantly higher number of correct answers in 
performance tests when the fresh air supply was increased from an average of 1.7 l/s per person 
to 6.6 l/s per person. The increase of correct answers in performance test was; addition (6.3 %), 
number comparison (4.8 %), grammatical reasoning (3.2 %) and reading and comprehension 
(7.4 %). The experiment did not find a significant effect on the number of errors in any of the 
performance test. The research was based on field studies executed as a double-blind 2x2 
crossover intervention on two different Danish schools (two classrooms at each school). 
(Petersen et al. 2015)    
 
Higher absence of the pupils as a result of poor indoor climate has been documented in several 
studies (Daisey et al. 2003; Gaihre et al. 2014). For example an increase of 100 ppm CO2 was 
associated with a reduced annual attendance of 0.2 % roughly equivalent to 0.5 school day per 
annum, assuming schools are open 190 days per year (Gaihre et al. 2014). Other studies have 
shown that outdoor air supply rates below 25 l/s per person increase the risk of SBS symptoms 
(Sick Building Syndrome) and increase short-term sick leave (Wargocki et al. 2002). Schools 
with mechanical exhaust and schools with both mechanical exhaust and supply had lower 
absences among the pupils than schools with natural ventilation (Kjeldsen et al. 2013). This 
implies that schools with natural ventilation, which probably have the poorest air quality, have a 
higher absence rate. The reason of higher absence can in inadequately ventilated rooms with 
high person density, is an increased risk of transmission of airborne infection between the 
occupants (Daisey et al. 2003). Airborne infection may lead to common cold, sore throat and 
middle ear infection (Toftum et al. 2011). Other common diseases reported in schools with poor 
indoor air quality is asthma and SBS symptoms. Furthermore, one study found a statistically 
significant partial correlation between symptoms of headaches, dizziness, heavy headed,

tiredness, difficulties in concentrating and high CO2 concentration (1500-4000 ppm) compared 
with concentrations below 1500 ppm. (Daisey et al. 2003) 
 
Absence of the pupils may have a negative influence on their test result. In Denmark the classes 
with the highest absence generally performed poorest in the national tests and the classes with 
the lowest absence performed best, which is illustrated in FIGURE 3-2 (Kjeldsen et al. 2013).

Methods of ventilation  
3.3
Ventilation systems are often divided in to three groups; natural ventilation, mechanical exhaust 
and mechanical supply and exhaust (balanced ventilation).  
 
Studies have shown a significant correlation between the type of ventilation system and the CO2 
concentration (Toftum et al. 2015). Natural ventilation typically results in the highest CO2 
concentration compared with mechanical exhaust and mechanical supply and exhaust see 
FIGURE 3-3. (Kjeldsen et al. 2013)   
  
 
FIGURE 3-3: Average CO2 concentration in classroom distributed on ventilation system. (Kjeldsen et al. 2013) 
 
==PAGE:21==
 
9 
 
Data on classroom ventilation worldwide that included data from 287 mechanically ventilated 
and 900 naturally ventilated classrooms found that the mean CO2 concentration was higher in 
the naturally ventilated classrooms compared to the mechanically ventilated classroom. The 
mean CO2 concentration was 1410 ppm and 910 ppm for the naturally ventilated and 
mechanically ventilated classrooms, respectively, slightly below that in Danish schools 
(Santamouris et al. 2008; Wyon et al. 2010).   
 
The reason why natural ventilation often results in a poor indoor climate is because it is not 
controlled by anything other than the occupants’ perception of the indoor climate. It is up to 
them when the windows should to be opened. However, it is often difficult to detect poor indoor 
climate after spending longer periods of time in the same room. Additionally, the outdoor 
temperature also may affect the window opened pattern. When it is cold outside windows are 
rarely opened (Wyon et al. 2010). Natural ventilation with automatic control may provide better 
for the indoor climate. The control option can be automatically operable windows or something 
as simple as a visualized feedback on the CO2 concentration, which indicates to the occupants 
when to open windows. The visualized feedback has shown to reduce the time with CO2 
concentration below 1000 ppm with approx. 40-60 % in classrooms (Wohlgemuth & 
Christensen 2014).

4 LAWS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
 
There are different laws, regulations and recommendations for educational environments in 
school buildings and the work environment for the pupils and teachers. In Denmark, school 
buildings must meet the Danish building code (Bygningsreglementet) valid at the time of 
construction. Besides regulations on the building there are also environmental legislation for the 
teachers and pupils.  
 
 
Danish building code 
4.1
The code in force in 1979 when Ravnsholtskolen was built was the Danish building code 1977. 
It states that buildings must be ventilated in such a way that satisfactory health conditions are 
ensured. Furthermore, it states that in rooms with highly variable person load the outdoor 
airflow could be adapted to the load (BR 1977). The code at that time was thus not very detailed 
and did not include specific requirements to temperature and CO2 concentration.  
 
The Danish building code that is valid now is BR 2010. BR 2010 specifies more requirements to 
the indoor climate and ventilation of the building. BR 2010 prescribes that classrooms in 
schools must be ventilated with a ventilation system, which include supply, exhaust and heat 
recovery. Supply and exhaust must at minimum be 5 l/s per person and 0.35 l/s per square meter 
floor area, while ensuring that the CO2 concentration is not exceeding 0.1 per cent (1000 ppm) 
during longer periods. Both mechanical and natural ventilation systems are allowed, but if the 
supply of fresh air through windows is not sufficient there must be a mechanical ventilation 
system. (BR 2010) 
 
 
Occupational and educational health and safety 
4.2
There are two different environmental legislations that deal with educational environments; one 
for the teachers and pedagogues and one for the pupils. The Working Environment Act 
addresses teachers and pedagogues, and the Teaching Environment Act addresses pupils. Which 
one is valid in schools is not clear, since both can be considered to be applicable in the school 
environment, with both pupils taught and teachers.  
 
The Working Environment Act states that each workroom must have sufficient supply of fresh 
air without unpleasant draft, and the temperature during the work hours must be adjusted to the 
human organism. The temperature conditions in the workroom must be without unpleasant 
temperature asymmetry (Arbejdsmiljøloven). At-vejledning A.1.2 (The Danish Working 
Environment Authority – instructions) describes that a temperature between 20-22 °C is suitable 
==PAGE:24==
 
12 
 
at light physical activity such as in schools and the temperature must not exceed 25 °C. 
Furthermore, it states that if the occupant load is the major pollution source, the CO2 
concentration must not exceed 0.1 % (1000 ppm). If the concentration exceeds 0.2 % during 
longer periods, the air change rate is labelled inadequate. (AT-vejledning A.1.2). The Danish 
Working Environment Authority instructions are not mandatory, but they are based on the 
Working Environment Act which are. Therefore, if the Danish Working Environment Authority 
instructions are obey, the Working Environment Act is also obeying. 
 
The Teaching Environment Act states; “Pupils, students and other participants in public and 
private education are entitled to a good educational environment, so that the teaching can take 
place in a safe and healthy manner.” (Undervisningsmiljøloven). The intention is clear, but the 
phrasing is not clear when designing and running a school building, especially because there are 
no specific guideline values.  
 
 
Optional classification 
4.3
Besides the Danish Building Code and Environmental Legislation there are also optional 
classifications of the indoor climate. Such classifications are described in different standards, 
but the classes and class boundaries are not entirely identical in the different standards. Among 
these standards are; DS 3033, DS/EN 15251 and DS/EN/CR 1752. In this project focus will be 
on DS 3033, as it is the newest and therefore also considered to be the most updated standard.     
 
The classification in DS 3033 is divided into five quality-classes that range from “The excellent 
indoor climate (Class A++)” with high comfort to “The poor indoor climate (Class C)” with risk 
of negative health effects. Class A is defined as “The good, satisfactory indoor climate similar 
to the requirements in the Danish Building Code”, and therefore the minimum allowed 
classification to obtain. The classification is based on nine different parameters: ventilation rate, 
CO2 concentration, thermal conditions, radon, formaldehyde, particles, moisture/mould, lighting 
conditions and acoustic conditions. TABLE 4-1 shows selected class boundaries for schools. 
(DS 3033:2011)

5 METHODS 
 
In this study indoor climate parameters were measured in three classrooms with different 
ventilation types in an elementary school. The effect of changes in the indoor climate caused by 
the operation of the systems on pupil performance in different test and the pupils’ perception of 
the indoor climate were investigated as well. The experiment took place in two periods in the 
school year 2014/15, one in the heating season and one in the non-heating season.  
 
The school  
5.1
The experiment was carried out at the elementary school Ravnsholtskolen located in Allerød, 
North of Copenhagen. The school was built in the period 1979 to 1986, and has 541 pupils in 25 
classes with 2-3 classes at each grade. The school consists of multiple buildings and the 
experiment took place in the building marked with blue, in FIGURE 5-1. This building 
accommodates 3. - 5. grade.  
 
All classrooms have approximately the same floor area and room volume of 56 m2 and 160 m3, 
respectively. All classrooms have one large window and a door to the outside in the façade 
furthermore each room have a clerestory roof (increasing of the floor-to-ceiling height opposite 
the façade) with two windows. Around 24 to 27 pupils and one teacher occupy each classroom.  
The ventilation system on the school is currently manual opening of the windows and doors.

The experiment  
5.2
The experiment was a field intervention carried out in five identical classrooms on the school. 
The experiment was carried out in two periods each period consisted of 6 weeks in which 
ventilation system alternately were switched on and off. The first period was in the winter 
(heating season) and the second in the summer (non-heating season) in the school year 
2014/2015.  
 
Four of the classrooms had a retrofit ventilation system installed and in the intervention periods, 
the classrooms were exposed to two scenarios, one where the installed ventilation system was 
active and one where it was deactivated; each condition lasted for a week. The classrooms were 
exposed to each condition three times in the both periods. Furthermore, to test the effect on the 
pupils’ performance they executed different performance tests during each week. The pupils 
were not aware of the experiment and the purpose of the performance tests and the teachers 
were not told when the ventilation system was on or off. 
 
The overall design of the experiment was defined by the Research proposal “EFFECTIVE 
ENERGY-EFFICIENT CLASSROOM VENTILATION FOR TEMPERATE ZONES” (Toftum 
2013). However, some adjustment was made during the experiment period, based on experience 
collected in the first test period.      
 
The retrofit systems 
5.3
The experiment focused only on ventilation systems that can be retrofitted; this results in no 
comprehensive renovations of the school. Four different retrofits were installed on the school, 
however this particular study worked with only two of the systems and the reference room. The 
systems are; a decentralized mechanical ventilation system and natural ventilation based on 
visual feedback panel. Both systems are described further below. 
5.3.1 
Decentralized mechanical ventilation system (Airmaster)  
The decentralized mechanical ventilation system is an Airmaster 800 H. This ventilation system 
supplies and extracts air from only one classroom. The principle of the Airmaster is illustrated 
on FIGURE 5-2. The unit is equipped with a heat recovery unit. The temperature efficiency is

Indoor climate measurements  
5.7
Measurements of the indoor climate in the classrooms were carried out throughout both 
experimental periods. The indoor climate was characterized by measurement of the temperature 
and CO2 concentration in all the classrooms.  
 
Measurements were made with two different instruments: Netatmo and HOBO data logger with 
connected VAISALA. It is primarily results from the HOBO data logger with connected 
VAISALA CO2 transmitter there will be analysed. The air change rate was measured with tracer 
gas see section 5.8 for more information.  
5.7.1 
HOBO data logger with connected VAISALA CO2 
transmitter 
The HOBO data logger and the VAISALA are connected and placed on a wooden board and 
form one measurement station shown in FIGURE 5-8. The HOBO data logger model U12-012 
measures temperature and relative humidity and logs data from the VAISALA CO2 transmitter. 
The measurement range for the temperature was -20 °C to 70 °C and for the relative humidity it 
was 5 % to 95 %. The accuracy of the measurement for the temperature was ± 0.35 °C from 0 
°C to 50 °C and for the relative humidity +/- 2.5 % from 10 % to 90 %. (Appendix XIV)  
 
The VAISALA model GMD20 measures and transmits the CO2 concentration. The 
measurement range was 0 – 5,000 ppm, but it can be calibrated for other ranges: 0 - 2,000 ppm, 
0 - 10,000 ppm and 0 - 20,000 ppm. The accuracy of the measurements is ± (2 % of range + 2 % 
of reading). (Appendix XIV)

The VAISALA CO2 transmitter was calibrated before both experiment periods started. One 
measurement station was placed in all the classrooms on the wall not near any windows and was 
set to log every five minutes. The data was downloaded every week throughout the test periods.

5.7.2 
Netatmo 
The Netatmo weather station measures both indoor and outdoor climate related parameters. The 
outdoor parameter used is temperature, and the indoor parameter is temperature and CO2 
concentration. The measurement ranges for outdoor temperature -40 °C to 65 °C, indoor 
temperature 0 °C to 50 °C and CO2 concentration 0 to 5,000 ppm. The accuracy of the 
measurement is for outdoor/indoor temperature ± 0.3 °C and for CO2 concentration +- 50 ppm. 
The recording frequency is every 5 minutes. The weather station measures also relative 
humidity, pressure and sound but these measurements were not used in this study. (Netatmo) 
 
The Netatmo weather station is connected to the internet and it was possible to follow the 
measurement in real-time on a computer or smartphone. The logged data was downloaded to the 
computer. The outdoor station was placed on the school and there was an indoor station in each 
classroom.

5.10.4 
d2 - test 
The d2 test is an attention and concentration test, where the pupils had to identify all “d2”s. A 
“d2” is the character d with two dots, either under, over or one over and one under the d, see 
FIGURE 5-14. The test consists of 14 rows each with 47 letters. To perform the test, the pupils 
had 20 sec per row to mark all the d2-letters. Then continue immediately to the next row. The 
test takes 280 sec (4 min and 40 sec) to perform.      
 
==PAGE:36==

There are two types of errors: omission errors and confusion errors. An omission error is when 
the pupil omits to mark a d2 character and a confusion error is when the pupil marks a character 
that is not a d2 character. When the test is corrected these two types of errors are noted, and 
added to get the total error score. Total score is an expression for all processed characters.

This section presents the results of the analyses of the indoor climate, including CO2 
concentration and temperature, the performance tests and the pupils’ perception of the indoor 
climate in the heating season 2014. Furthermore, the measured ventilation rates in the 
classrooms are presented in this section.

were synchronized with the other classrooms. The classroom did not have any interventions in

comparison.

Indoor climate  
8.1
In all three classrooms, the CO2 concentration reached levels above the recommended 1,000 
ppm when none of the systems were running during the heating season. Under this condition the 
percentage of occupied time with concentrations above 1,000 ppm in all three classrooms was 
above 60%. In the non-heating season, the percentage of occupied hours above 1,000 ppm in all 
three classrooms was above 30% of the time when no systems was running. In Denmark the 
majority of the school year is in the heating season and therefore the results show an 
unacceptable indoor air quality when no retrofits were made. 
 
The retrofitted systems were able to lower the CO2 concentrations considerably when they were 
running. In general the CO2 concentrations were lower in the non-heating season for all the 
classrooms in both conditions, which was also expected. The low concentrations when the 
retrofitted systems were not in operation in the non-heating season were perhaps due to that the 
smaller difference between the outdoor and the indoor temperature in the non-heating season 
would affect the desire to open windows in the occupants (Gao et al. 2014). The average 
outdoor temperature in the heating season was 8.3 ºC and the average temperature in the non-
heating season 14.4 ºC.  
 
In the classroom with the decentralized mechanical system, the maximum airflow rate at 650 
m3/h was sufficient to keep the CO2 concentration below 1,000 ppm in the majority of the 
occupied hours. The average concentration when the system was operating was below the 
recommended level of 1,000 ppm in both seasons. The average CO2 concentration in the heating 
season achieved Class A+ and in the non-heating season Class A++ (DS 3033:2011). However, 
the percentage of occupied time when the CO2 concentration exceeded 1,000 ppm in 29% and 
22 % in the heating and non-heating season, respectively, which could mean that the system was 
undersized. The unit size was perhaps determined from a lower person load than the actual loads 
under the experimental period in the heating season. Nonetheless, the CO2 concentrations were 
considerably lower than if the system had not been there. This classroom had additionally a 
notably larger percentage of occupied time over 1,000 ppm when the system was not in 
operation compared to the two other classrooms in both periods, so the overall effect was higher 
than in the other classroom. Perhaps the occupants in the other classrooms had slightly better 
habits with regard to windows opening, because they also manually had to open the windows in 
the operation condition. Additionally, in the air change rate measurement the classroom with the 
decentralized mechanical system had the lowest air change rate when all windows closed and 
system turn off compared to the other two classrooms, which may explain the higher average 
CO2 concentration when the system was not in operation. The difference in the air change rate 
was probably due to that the classroom with the decentralized mechanical system has a different 
==PAGE:88==
 
76 
 
geographical direction than the other two classrooms, and therefore a different wind load on the 
facades, which effect the air infiltration. The occupant density was approximately the same in 
all three classrooms.  
  
In the classroom with the visual feedback panel the CO2 concentration was lower when the 
system were in operation mode as a response to the feedback indication resulting in the 
occupants opening the windows more frequently in both seasons. However, in the heating 
season the percentage of occupied time above 1,000 ppm was only lowered with 5 %, and the 
time above 1,500 ppm was lowered with 22 %. This could be due to pupils and teachers were 
instructed to first open windows one at a time when the visual feedback panel indicated that the 
CO2 concentration exceeded 1,000 ppm. Furthermore, the response time from the panel indicate 
a CO2 concentration above 1,000 ppm and to when the pupils and teacher begin open windows, 
could have been slightly long and therefore the percentage of occupied time were not lowered 
that much between the two operation modes. Additionally, another reason could be improper 
calibration between the visual feedback panel and the HOBO data logger with connected 
VAISALA CO2 transmitter, resulting in the visual feedback panel showed too low CO2 
concentration compared with what there was measured. Finally, a reason could also be that the 
pupils and teacher hesitated to open window when it was cold outside. This reason could 
explain, that the largest difference between when the system were in operation and when it was 
not, was first seen at concentrations above 1,500 ppm. The average CO2 concentration 
difference operation modes were almost the same in the heating and non-heating season, 199 
ppm and 179 ppm, respectively.  
 
The reference classroom there were no obvious difference seen between the weeks were the 
parallels classrooms systems were operating (“ON”) and the weeks were they was not operating 
(“OFF”) in the heating season, which indicate that the primary impact of the CO2 concentration 
in the retrofitted classrooms was due to the retrofitted systems. However, in the non-heating 
season percentage of occupied hours above 1,000 ppm decreased 13 % from the “OFF” 
condition to the “ON” condition. This difference could be related with the weather, which was 
better in the “ON” weeks, where the average outdoor temperature was about 2 ºC higher than in 
the “OFF” condition. Furthermore, the reference classroom had the lowest average CO2 
concentration compared with the two other classrooms non-operating systems weeks. A possible 
explanation for the lower concentrations may be that the occupants in this classroom were 
accustomed to manually airing out, as they never had an automatically controlled ventilation 
system or a visual feedback device indicating when to take action.  
 
In the two retrofitted classrooms there was no notable difference in the indoor temperature 
between operating and not operating conditions in the occupied hours for any of the seasons. In 
both seasons and under both conditions the average temperature in all three classrooms was 
within the recommended range (DS 474:1995). 
 
According to BR10 the ventilation rate at minimum must be 5 l/s per person + 0.35 l/s per 
square meter for new schools. This corresponds to 154.6 l/s for the reference classroom and the 
classrooms with the visual feedback panel (26 pupils and one teacher and 56 m2) and 149.6 l/s 
for the classroom with decentralized mechanical system (25 pupils and one teacher and 56 m2). 
The air change rate measurements showed that the reference classroom and the classroom with 
visual feedback panel fulfilled this requirement when all windows were opened resulting in a 
==PAGE:89==
 
77 
 
ventilation rate of 213 l/s. The classroom with the decentralized mechanical system did not fulfil 
the requirement with a maximum ventilation rate of 145 l/s but it was only about 5 l/s below the 
required ventilation rate. However, this could explain why the system did not keep the CO2 
concentration below 1,000 ppm.         
 
Pupil perceptions  
8.2
The pupils’ subjective perceptions of the indoor climate and the intensity of health symptoms 
did not vary much between when the retrofitted systems were in operation and when they were 
turn off for either of the seasons. However, some associations were seen between the pupils’ 
perception and the systems operation mode, for example was there a positive impact on the 
symptom stuffy noses intensity in the classroom with the visual feedback panel.  
 
In the classroom with decentralized mechanical system and during the heating season no larges 
difference in the perception of the indoor climate or symptoms was observed when the system 
was going from not operation to operating mode. However, the perception of the air quality had 
a small improvement when the system was going from no operating to operation mode however, 
not significant. Perhaps some of the pupils found it difficult to assess perceived indoor air 
quality.  
 
In the classroom with the visual feedback panel and during the heating season the pupils’ 
perception of acceptable temperature was significantly worse when the feedback was used (P = 
0.02). The difference in the classroom temperature when the system was operating and not 
operating were only 0.2 ºC and therefore this difference in their perception was unexpected. 
Perhaps the outdoor temperature or a feeling of draught when the windows were opened 
influences the pupils’ perception of acceptable temperatures. The pupils’ had a significantly 
lower intensity of the symptom stuffy nose when the system was in operation (P = 0.03). Week 
6 (system not in operation) had the worst outcome of the perception of stuffy nose. This was 
also the coldest week, so perhaps the pupils felt a stuffier nose in this week due to the colder 
weather. There was also an improvement of the pupils’ well-being when the system was in 
operation, although it was not significant.  
 
In the reference classroom and during the heating season, there were significant improvements 
in the intensity of the symptoms well-being and hurting eyes (P = 0.05 and P = 0.01). It is 
difficult to explain why this was the case with no alternating interventions in this classroom. 
However, a reason could be that a common cold were fluctuating in the weeks there were used 
as not operating weeks, and thereby the pupils felt their eyes was hurting and their well-being 
was not so good.    
 
In the classroom with decentralized mechanical system and during the non-heating season was 
there a significant improvement in the pupils’ concentration ability when the system was 
operating (P = 0.05), which could be an effect of the improved indoor air quality.   
        
In the classroom with the visual feedback panel and during the non-heating season no 
significant difference between when it was operating and not operating were found. Perhaps the 
improvement of the indoor air quality was too small to affect the pupils, especially because the 
CO2 concentration when the system was not in operation was below recommended.  
==PAGE:90==
 
78 
 
 
In the reference classroom and during the non-heating season there was a significant worsening 
of the symptom hurting eyes (P = 0.02), which was opposite the heating season. Again, there 
were no obvious reasons can why this was the case. Perhaps it had something to do with the 
indoor relative humidity, which have not been analysis in this project.  
 
In the non-heating season the difference in the CO2 concentration between the operation modes 
was less than during the heating season and even when the systems were not in operation the 
average concentration was below the recommended 1,000 ppm in all the classrooms. Perhaps in 
order to see the larges effects of the perception of the indoor climate and symptoms intensity the 
CO2 concentration must be above 1,000 ppm. The structure of the questionnaire was also 
altering between the two seasons, where the pupils had a finer rating scale in the heating-season, 
which could have affected the results.   
   
Overall, the modest difference observed between operating weeks and not operating weeks and 
the significant difference between the same division of weeks in the reference classroom in the 
pupils’ perception of the indoor climate and their symptom intensity, might suggests that the 
pupils find it difficult to make up their mind about how they percept the indoor climate and how 
they felt the different symptoms. Additionally, factors other than the change in the exposure 
caused by the operation mode of the retrofitted system could affect their health or well-being, 
such as for example noise from the system or changed to the indoor humidity. Furthermore, the 
pupils’ desire to answer the questionnaire or their general mood could also have affected the 
outcome. For example in serval of the completed questionnaires the answers consistently were 
either the saddest or the happiest smiley.      
 
Performance tests 
8.3
It was expected that the pupils’ performance would improve with a improved indoor air quality 
that was the consequence of reoperation of the decentralized mechanical system or use of a 
visual feedback panel system, as serval studies has shown such an association (Petersen et al. 
2015; Bakó-biró et al. 2011; Kjeldsen et al. 2013; Toftum et al. 2015) 
 
In the heating season, the results from all the different performance tests showed that the pupils’ 
in the classroom with the visual feedback panel performed the tests significantly more 
accurately when the visual feedback panel was used compared with the condition when it was 
not (P = 0.05). The pupils in the classroom with the decentralized mechanical system were also 
more accurate, although not significantly. The pupils in the two retrofitted classrooms worked at 
approximately the same speed under both conditions, slightly faster in operation. These results 
suggest a positive effect of the running of the systems, especially in the classroom with the 
visual feedback panel. It was unexpected that the effect was clearer in the classroom with the 
visual feedback panel, since this room had the smallest improvement in the air quality when 
going from not in operation to in operation mode compared with the classroom with the 
decentralized mechanical system. Perhaps the pupils in the classroom with the visual feedback 
panel felt the fresh air better because it was colder or the noise from the decentralized 
mechanical system affected the pupils in this classroom and therefore no significantly 
improvements in the pupils’ performance were seen.  
 
==PAGE:91==
 
79 
 
When the performance results were analysed separately for the different tests, other tendencies 
were seen. In the multiplication test both speed and accuracy varied significantly between the 
operations modes. In the reference classroom and the classroom with the visual feedback panel 
the pupils worked slower when the systems where operating, whereas in the classroom with 
decentralized mechanical system they worked faster. However, in both retrofitted classrooms 
the pupils were more accurate when the systems were operating and in the reference classroom 
the pupils’ were less accurate. The reasons why all classrooms had significantly results in speed 
and accuracy in the multiplication test could be that the test was only presented in two out of the 
six weeks, so it was only presented in one week where the system were in operation conditions 
and in one week where the system were not in operation. This could lead to that learning effect 
had a significant influence on the results outcome. In the classroom with the visual feedback 
panel the pupils were also significantly more accurate in the d2 test. There was no indication 
that the pupils made more or fewer errors in the different tests depending on system operation, 
and there was no obvious association between the speed and the percentage of errors (PE).  
 
The hypothesis that the pupils would be more accurate when the CO2 concentration was reduced 
was partly confirmed with the heating season results. However, only in the classroom with the 
visual feedback panel and in one test (d2) a significant improvement was observed when the 
system was in operation. The hypothesis was also that working speed will increase and the error 
rate will decrease when the retrofitted systems were in operation, this hypothesis could not be 
confirmed.  
 
In the non-heating season a significant improvement in the error rate in both tests was observed 
in the classroom with the visual feedback panel when the devise was in operation. However, at 
the same time in the same classroom the pupils’ had a significantly slower working speed in the 
mathematical test when the system was in operation. So perhaps the pupils’ were more 
concentrated in the weeks were the system was operating, so they were more concerned to be 
correct and thus they become slower. No other clear tendencies in speed or accuracy were seen 
between operation modes in any of the classrooms. Perhaps the modest difference in the 
classroom air quality between when the systems where operating and not operating and the 
generally low average CO2 concentration even when not operating was insufficient to affect the 
pupils’ performance.    
 
Other factors than the CO2 concentration may have affected the pupils’ performance. Perhaps 
therefore the results showed no overall improvement in the working speed or the percentage of 
error. In this study, the two retrofitted systems were installed to investigate their effect on the 
indoor climate and the energy consumption, without the possibility of careful control of other 
influencing factors. Such factors include the noise level, the temperature, the air humidity and 
also the risk of draught in the classroom, the time of day the tests were completed, e.g. if the 
pupils’ had been in another classroom with a better or poorer air quality just before the 
completion. The pupils’ mental state might also have affected the results, e.g. lack of 
motivation. This was expressed in some of the completed tests by comments from either the 
pupils themselves or the teacher saying that the test was stupid or that a particular pupil did not 
want to complete the test.

The project in general and limitations   
8.4
There was no placebo build into the experimental design, so the pupils were not blind to the 
state of the retrofitted systems and therefore aware of when systems were operating or not, 
especially in the classroom with the visual feedback panel. It had been better if the pupils had 
been kept blind to the interventions, to make sure that their knowledge of the systems did not 
affect the results. In the classroom with the decentralized mechanical system the set-up of a 
placebo would have been possible, but in the classroom with the visual feedback panel it would 
have been more difficult. 
 
The CO2 concentration around the time the tests or questionnaires were completed was only 
indirectly accounted for in the analysis. The pupils could have been in another classroom just 
before the test or the CO2 concentration when the tests were completed did maybe not resemble 
their average indoor air quality exposure during the week. This could have affected the results.  
 
Additionally, the teachers presented the tests and questionnaires to the pupils, although with 
instructions from the research team. It is nevertheless unclear how the explanation of the tests 
was done. Perhaps, some teachers indicated to the pupils that they should work fast as possible, 
whereas others may have indicated that it was more important to be accurate. Simply working 
with children may be challenging, as they easily get distracted. 
 
It is difficult to make a final recommendation regrading which ventilation system the school 
should select for an overall upgrade of the classroom environment. The decentralized 
mechanical system had the best effect on the measured indoor air quality and the visual 
feedback panel resulted in the largest improvement of the pupils’ performance. So it depends on 
which factors the school sees as the most important.

9 CONCLUSION 
Effects on the indoor climate and the academic performance in classrooms ventilated with either 
a decentralized mechanical system or a visual feedback system were seen in this investigation.  
 
In a reference classroom without retrofit and in the classrooms where the retrofitted systems 
were not in operation the CO2 concentration reached peaks levels above 3,000 ppm and average 
concentrations during the occupied time between 1,200 – 1,500 ppm, which is well above the 
recommended levels in the BR10 and the Working Environment Act.  
 
Analyses showed an improvement in the indoor air quality in both retrofitted classrooms when 
the systems were ON. The decentralized mechanical system improved the indoor air quality 
significantly in the heating season where the average CO2 concentration was lowered to 866 
ppm to a level below the recommended 1,000 ppm. The percentage of occupied time above 
1,000 ppm was reduced from 73% to 29% in the heating season and from 46% to 22% in the 
non-heating season. The visual feedback panel decreased the average CO2 concentration to 
1,052 ppm in the heating season, close to the recommended level. The percentage of occupied 
time above 1,000 ppm was reduced from 62% to 57% in the heating season and from 34% to 
18% in the non-heating season. The systems did not affect the temperatures when going from 
OFF to ON. The average temperatures in both seasons were within the recommended levels, 
according to the Working Environment Act and DS 474:1995.  
 
Analysis of the pupils’ perception of the indoor climate and the intensity of symptoms showed 
modest differences between the ON and OFF conditions. However, in the non-heating season a 
significant improvement in the pupils’ perception of their concentration ability was seen in the 
classroom with the decentralized mechanical system when the system was ON. 
  
In the heating season, the pupils were more accurate when the systems were ON, was although 
only significantly in the classroom with the visual feedback panel. In the classroom with the 
visual feedback panel both the error rate and the accuracy of the d2 test improved significantly 
and the pupils worked faster, although the increase in speed was not significant. In the non-
heating season there the error rate decreased significantly in the classroom with the visual 
feedback panel when the system was ON. No other significant performance outcomes were 
found in this season.

FUTURE STUDIES AND OTHER IDEAS  
 
During the work on this project several ideas on further analysis of the data and future studies 
came up. Due to time limitation on this project, it was not possible to realize all the ideas. In the 
following section the ideas for the future study and changed to the experiment are listed. 
 
• 
Study of the presence of pupils in the classrooms. This may explain the tendencies seen 
in the CO2 concentration and temperatures. In this study, school timetables were used 
as a guideline for the presence of pupils in the classroom, even though data showed that 
there were sometimes no pupils present in the classroom. The results of the average 
indoor climate parameters could probably have been different if there were only used 
data from when there were presences of pupils. 
• 
Study the noise in the classroom, both the noise level from the retrofitted systems and 
the noise from the pupils. If the noise from the systems were too high, it may disturb 
the teaching.  
• 
Study the window and door opening behaviour. This can perhaps explain the 
tendencies seen in the CO2 concentration and temperatures. 
• 
Change the arrangement of the interventions plan, so there were three successive weeks 
with the systems operating and three successive weeks were the systems were not 
operating.  
• 
See if pupils with different academic level are affected differently by changes in the 
indoor climate. Are pupils with lower academic level more affected by the indoor 
climate than pupils with higher academic level? Perhaps pupils with lower academic 
level might easily become distracted when the indoor climate is poor, whereas pupils 
with higher academic level find it easier to complete the tests even if the indoor climate 
was poor.       
• 
Find out what the pupils were told before completing the performance tests, if they 
were told to be fast or told to be more accurate.  
• 
See if the CO2 concentration when the tests or questionnaire were completed, could 
give another explanation to the results. In this project the average CO2 concentration 
over the weeks where the system was operating and average concentration over the 
weeks where the system was not operating used to describe some of the results.  
• 
See if the time of day of the weekday for completion of the performance tests and 
questionnaires influence the results of the pupils’ performance and perception.  
• 
Look more into the possibility of learning effect.  
• 
Look more into the results from the pupils’ performance test divided into pair of 
different operating mode successions. Perhaps the effect of the systems was higher in 
the first weeks than in the final weeks or the effect was higher going from operating to 
not operating or vice versa. &

#Load data  
Graph_4c <-

  header=TRUE, sep=";", na.strings="NA", dec=".", strip.white=TRUE)

  read.table("/Users/sisselhartelius/Dropbox/DTU Kandidat/Master/Test 
sommer/R/DSV/4c_math.csv",  
  header=TRUE, sep=";", na.strings="NA", dec=".", strip.white=TRUE) 
 
#Merge data  
All_tests_4c <- mergeRows(Graph_4c, Math_4c, common.only=FALSE) 
 
#Creating Speed model 
Speed.model = lmer(Snorm ~ Condition + Test + (1|Initials), data=All_tests_4c) 
 
#Summary Speed model 
summary(Speed.model) 
 
#Checking for Homoskedasticity 
plot(fitted(Speed.model),residuals(Speed.model)) 
 
#Checking for Normality of residuals 
hist(residuals(Speed.model)) 
qqnorm(residuals(Speed.model))

anova(Speed.null,Speed.model)
