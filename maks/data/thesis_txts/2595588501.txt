Abstract
In this report an analysis of a ventilation exhaust air heat pump is carried out. The heat pump
analysed is the Nilan Compact P which currently runs on R134a.
R134a has a high global
warming potential and as such it is desirable to ﬁnd replacements for R134a as a refrigerant.
This is the case as high GWP refrigerants are are being phased out, in EU this type of heat
pump must have a refrigerant with a GWP less than 750 by 2025 [1]. For this R1234-yf and R290
is analysed as alternative refrigerants to R134a in a model of the Nilan Compact P heat pump
developed in Engineering Equation Solver. The model was found to be lacking in terms of being
able to fairly compare the three refrigerants due to a signiﬁcantly more eﬃcient compressor
being chosen for R1234-yf than for the remaining refrigerants.
However general conclusions
could be made on the basis of the results and it was found that both R290 and R1234-yf would
be possible replacements for R134a. For R1234-yf it is likely that performance of the heat pump
would be worse than the other refrigerants. The analysis suggests that R290 could be a suitable
replacement for R134a assuming the safety concerns can be managed. Through the results of the
analysis interpreted with the too high isentropic eﬃciency for R1234-yf in mind it is concluded
that both alternate refrigerants would likely yield a reduction in heat exchanger sizes.

1
Introduction
Global warming is one of the greatest challenges of our time. In Denmark older space heating
technologies such as oil furnaces are for example projected to gradually be phased out [2]. As
replacements heat pumps are great tools in overcoming this challenge as they allow eﬃcient
conversion of electricity to heat or cooling. However the refrigerants used in the heat pumps
can be harmful to the environment. R134a is an eﬃcient refrigerant with no ozone depletion
potential (ODP) but has a drawback in it’s high global warming potential (GWP), a measure
for how big an impact the chemical has in terms of global warming compared to CO2.
The heat pump solution that will be investigated in this thesis is the Nilan Compact P. This is a
waste heat recovery system consisting of a passive heat regenerating counterﬂow air-to-air heat
exchanger. Furthermore consisting of a heat pump able to provide hot water and space heating
from the waste heat and even cool air inﬂow to provide air conditioning. The system can be
modiﬁed to accomplish these tasks but consists of the same components in each conﬁguration.
However not all components are used in each conﬁguration. The air ﬂows through the Nilan
Compact P is exhaust air from the house in which it is installed and ambient air being heated
before being discharged to the house. A ventilation exhaust air heat pump is advantageous as
it takes advantage of the heat that would otherwise be discharged to the ambient air due to
ventilation. This means that it essentially has a hot reservoir of exhaust air which could otherwise
not have any useful heat extracted from it. However with a heat pump this is possible, allowing
higher energy eﬃciencies to be reached for a house in which it is installed.
The analysis was conducted based on the central problem statement and accompanying research
questions:
Which low GWP refrigerant would yield the highest COP when replacing R134a in a
ventilation exhaust air heat pump while not being toxic and requiring only minor changes in
the system design?
• What refrigerants should be analyzed?
• What are the advantages of each analyzed refrigerant?
• What consequences does the use of alternatives have, also in terms of safety?

as they are both acting as condensers and evaporators depending on the cycle conﬁguration as

The modelling of the heat pump aims to compare performance of the heat pump running on

ysed. This should be coupled with other considerations e.g. the eventual change of component
size as to not yield unreasonably large heat pumps.

2.2
Refrigerant selection
In choosing alternate refrigerants only non-toxic refrigerants with no ODP was considered, how-
ever ﬂammable refrigerants were not excluded. With these criteria alternatives to the current
refrigerant in the heat pump had to be selected. An analysis of the system with the current
refrigerant, R134a, was conducted as well as to compare the results.
The alternative refrigerants chosen to be analysed as a replacement for R134a was R290 and
R1234-yf. The R134a-replacements were chosen with a goal in mind of resulting in the lowest
TEWI possible. This means a low GWP while not requiring a COP lowered too drastically.
R1234-yf is a hydro-ﬂuoro-oleﬁn (HFO) with a low GWP. It has been found to be suitable for
replacing R134a while yielding a decrease in performance both in [4] and [5]. Analysed systems
in these studies were heat a water-to-water heat pump and a heat pump for automotive use.
R290 is a hydrocarbon commonly known as propane. It was found in [6] that R290 could be used
with good performance in a domestic water heating heat pump on par with R134a. It should
be noted that R290 is a very ﬂammable refrigerant. These were the two alternate refrigerants
chosen for analysis alongside R134a.
2.3
Modelling Strategy
The Nilan Compact P exhaust air heat pump was modelled in Engineering Equation Solver
(EES) version 10.644. The modelling strategy used is to develop design models and operational
models. The aim of the design models will be to calculate the sizes of heat exchangers from
given dimensioning temperatures found in 1 along with a given hot water production capacity.
Only the DHW conﬁguration is modelled as design model. Through this the identical air heat
exchanger is ﬁxed in size along with the coil of the hot water tank.
Through the operational models the calculated component sizes are used as inputs and to calcu-
late temperatures that was previously given. As such the heat pumps can be seen in operation
under given circumstances. Since diﬀerent conﬁgurations are possible for the Nilan Compact P,
each conﬁguration should be modelled as a operational model.
Modelling the heat pump this way allows the diﬀerent refrigerants to have component sizes
that ﬁt them individually. This also allows a comparison of the component sizes between all
refrigerant. However, in doing this it is assumed that the whole system will be redesigned into
a new heat exchanger with diﬀerent compressors and heat exchangers. This means that this
analysis is not applicable to determine a replacement for R134a to retroﬁt into the current Nilan
Compact P.
2.4
Design model
The design model done is in the hot water production conﬁguration. This allows the length
of the coil on the hot water tank (HWT) to be determined along with the size of the heat
exchangers. The theory for calculations on the air condenser is presented as well. The design
model will not include this component as the DHW conﬁguration bypasses this component.
However, this theory is presented as well since it will be needed in implementing the component
in the operational model.

To determine the properties of the ﬂuids in each section of the heat exchanger the change in
enthalpy over the section is utilized. This is determined from the known heat transfer of the
section, the known mass ﬂow of ﬂuid and the initial enthalpy of the ﬂuid using a simple energy
balance:
˙Qsection = ˙m · (hsection,in −hsection,out)
(12)
Knowing the enthalpy along with the pressure of the ﬂuid allows the state of the ﬂuid to be
determined. The average enthalpy for the section is used in this evaluation. For the airﬂow the
humidity must also be supplied. Furthermore the ﬁlm temperature is utilized in determining
the properties of air and water which will also require the wall surface temperature touching
each ﬂuid.
The heat transfer occurring in the air heat exchangers, as well as in the hot water tank, is from
one ﬂowing ﬂuid to another over a wall. This means that to model the heat transfer in all
heat exchangers convection should be considered on both sides of the given wall as well as the
conduction through the wall. This is done by calculating the overall heat transfer coeﬃcient
and area, UA, in each section of the heat exchanger. Here the overall heat transfer coeﬃcient,
U and the heat transfer area, A, is written as one factor. This is due to this one factor being
used without the two individual values being computed initially. UA can be calculated from the

When splitting of the heat exchangers into discreet sections as to calculate the properties of ﬂuids
in each section, a suﬃcient number of sections must be determined. This is done qualitatively
by observing the change in size of the component as this further division of the heat exchanger
occurs. This allows that heat transfer in each section is suﬃciently low to produce results that
are of an adequate level of precision. However, a practical limit to this is both the resulting
calculation time and the maximum allowed variables in EES.
A smaller change of enthalpy over a given section corresponds to a more correct model. This is
since the properties of the ﬂuid will vary less over the section. This will make the assumption of
constant properties over the section more correct. However, a small enthalpy diﬀerence is not
a guaranteed by a relatively small heat transfer of a section. Since the heat transfer relates to
the enthalpy diﬀerence through the mass ﬂow of the ﬂuid, larger or smaller enthalpy change will
occur in each ﬂuid ﬂow depending on mass ﬂow.

Only for the heat pump running R1234-yf a compressor was chosen. The current compressor
in the NILAN Compact 9 being used for R134a was implemented. For the heat pump running
on R290 a compressor was already being considered by NILAN in potentially switching the
refrigerant to R290, and as such this was implemented. However, as seen from the number of
compressors needed to match the refrigerant ﬂow of the design model pattern emerges. It is
clear that several compressors needed as found in this analysis would not be an optimal solu-
tion in practice. This discrepancy from the actual physical system in number of compressors is
likely caused by the assumption of a hot water production capacity in DHW mode being too high.

relatively to the other refrigerants in the analysis. The preliminary results of the compressor
cycle was used to get accurate assumed isentropic eﬃciencies for the design model.

Hot water tank
It is seen from table 11 that only for R1234-yf a signiﬁcant change in capacity of the hot water
production in the DHW conﬁguration occurs. No refrigerant yields major increases in coil length.
It is found that a signiﬁcant decrease in the length of coil does occur in choosing R290. Due to
the higher isentropic eﬃciencies of R290 and R1234-yf the computed sizes of the components will
likely be overestimated as discussed in ??. This means that the resulting heat pumps using the
alternate refrigerants to R134a would likely result in a shorter coil. This is an advantage in terms
of a small reduction of material used in the coil, and thereby a very small reduction of materials
cost. This is likely insigniﬁcant, and since the size of the tank is not given by the size of the coil,
but rather by a given storage volume, this yields no real advantage. The loss of capacity for the
R1234-yf DHW conﬁguration could be problematic, but since the hot water production assumed
is likely overestimated it would not be a problem should the system be used in practice. How-
ever, a general loss of capacity could be a problem if the full capacity would have been necessary.
The given dimensioning temperatures from table 1 had to be changed so that the water going
into the water ﬂow is 55 ℃which results in water produced to be close to boiling without
ever reaching this point. This is justiﬁed in assuming that the HWT is fully loaded with 55
℃water in SH and SC conﬁgurations. The increase of Twater,out is then assumed to be fully
dissipated with no signiﬁcant change to the overall temperature in the HWT for simplicity of
further calculations. The water ﬂow is assumed to have the temperature gradient going from 55
℃to Twater,out close to the wall without it inﬂuencing the state of the HWT. This is coupled
with the increase of the temperature of the air going into the evaporator, Tevap,in, to 7.1 ℃for
the SH conﬁguration allows no boiling to occur in the water tank. The temperatures at which
hot water is produced in the SH conﬁguration are very high. Along with the assumption of
Twater,in= 55 ℃this yields a very low production of hot water in the SH conﬁguration. This
results in a low hot water production COP. This is the only way found to match the required
33
==PAGE:38==
mass ﬂow of air through the air condenser. In the SH conﬁguration for R134a speciﬁcally the
Twater,in is set at 76.97 ℃as to avoid boiling in the ﬁlm layer. Speciﬁcally for R134a a very
high discharge temperature out of the compressor, Tcomp,dis, was reached as seen in table 10
and the increase of Tevap,in was not suﬃcient. This assumption of a temperature of water going
into the water ﬂow being higher than the set water production temperature of 55℃is not a
good assumption. However, the heat transfer in the HWT is only about 16 % of the total heat
transfer of the hot refrigerant. As such while the assumption is not good it ultimately allows
results to be collected that should be reasonably representative of the heat pump running on
R134a in SH conﬁguration.

the resulting TEWI this contribution is mostly important for R134a due to its much higher GWP.
In determining the mass of refrigerant in the receiver an equal volume of the receiver was as-
sumed for each refrigerant. This would likely not be case and as such the mass charge of the
alternate refrigerants might vary somewhat. Since they both have low GWP this will be less
important in their ﬁnal resulting TEWI.
The charge of R290 is quite low,[26] provides an upper limit of 1.5 kg of a refrigerant with a
ﬂammability class of 3 which this does not exceed. This limit would likely be lower even, de-
pendant on the installation of the heat pump. This is unlikely to be problematic and is deemed
in compliance with safety standards. To ensure this the actual allowable charge for this speciﬁc
system must be calculated which depends on where it is installed. However, even though it
complies with the safety standard the risk introduced in using a ﬂammable refrigerant remains.
A reduction in charge could be obtained from varying the subcooling, as it is set in this analysis.

3.2.6
Discussion
TEWI takes into account both performance and inherent emission potential for each refrigerant.
This makes TEWI suitable as a quantitative measure of which refrigerants yields the best overall
results. However, some additional parameters beyond performance and GWP should be taken
into account. Most importantly this analysis has included R290 even though it is ﬂammable
and thus in safety group A3 [26]. This is a safety concern that should be addressed if chosen
as a replacement for R134a. Should R1234-yf be chosen as the R134a replacement the safety
concerns would not be as great. R1234-yf is in safety group A2L compared to R134a in safety
group A1 [26]. This due to the fact that R1234-yf is ﬂammable when at higher temperatures
from around 60 ℃[23]. Furthermore the physical changes necessary in the heat pump regarding
dimensions of components should be considered. A larger physical space required for the heat
pump would be a downside of the ﬁnal product.
In this analysis it was found that R1234-yf should outperform both R134a and R290 in terms
of indirect contributions to TEWI. This is likely due to the compressor chosen for R1234-yf
being quite a bit more eﬃcient than those for R134a and R290. This means that the results
showing that R1234-yf would yield high eﬃciencies should not be the sole reason for choosing
R1234-yf as they might well be misleading. This is the case since the superior performance of
R1234-yf is largely based on the better performance of the compressor. In a study of a water-to-
water heat pump it was found that substituting R134a for R1234-yf would yield a reduced COP
[5]. This trend would also be expected here, and as such would yield a signiﬁcantly higher TEWI.
Assuming that this reduction of COP would occur going from R134a to R1234-yf, instead of
the calculated increase, the indirect contribution to TEWI would be increased. The lowered
COPs might even yield similar TEWI to R134a occurring at an approximate overall reduction
of COPs by around 33 %, assuming no changes to direct contribution to TEWI. This scenario
36
==PAGE:41==
of 33 % redution of COPs is likely a bit conservative, but it showcases a choice that must to
be made when choosing between R290 and R1234-yf; safety versus performance. In choosing
the very ﬂammable refrigerant R290 a signiﬁcant reduction in TEWI in comparison with R134a
could be achieved. However, this could only ever occur if the safety risks are properly managed.
It is found that R290 would be a good replacement for R134a in terms of TEWI and compo-
nent sizes. Even when not considering the large diﬀerence in isentropic eﬃciency between the
R1234-yf compressor and the two other refrigerants, the TEWI for both R1234-yf and R290
are very similar. Thus R290 would be the preferred replacement assuming safety issues could
be managed and that R1234-yf had more representative isentropic eﬃciencies, comparable to
R134a and R290, resulting in worse performance.
No analysis of the impact of diﬀerent subcooling and superheating temperatures was conducted.
An optimization of these could lead to better performance and lower refrigerant charge. For
further analysis this would be important factors to investigate.
4
Conclusion
Through the analysis presented in this report it was found that R290 and R1234-yf could be
reasonably good replacements for R134a. They yield a decrease in TEWI of around 23% to 30%
respectively and results indicate that they would reduce the heat exchanger sizes. Due to the
analysis including a compressor for R1234-yf yielding unfairly large isentropic eﬃciencies and
therefore an unrealistically eﬃcient system, R290 is found to be the best replacement for R134a
of the analyzed refrigerants in terms of performance. Since its performance in terms of TEWI
is close to that of R1234-yf.
The results attained from the model used for analysis were found to be inaccurate to the ac-
tual heat pump that was to be analysed. This was deemed problematic, but ultimately not
completely invalidating the comparisons. However the aforementioned discrepancy in isentropic
eﬃciency between R1234-yf and the other refrigerants must be addressed.
It was found that the factor having the largest impact on the TEWI of the resulting system
were not the refrigerants GWP, but rather the resulting systems COP in the inﬂuence in indi-
rect TEWI. However the GWP were the determining factor as to seeing a diﬀerence in TEWI
between R134a and the two alternative refrigerants.
R290 was found to be the better replacement of R134a over R1234-yf through evaluation of the
results from the analysis, keeping the inaccuracies introduced by chosen compressor for R1234-yf
in mind. Due to the inaccuracies of the analysis this is cannot be concluded certainly, but all
results point towards R290 being a good option in replacing R134a.
