Title: Fundamental study in load reduction using cyclic pitch control schemes
Author: Christos Tsouknidas
Department: DTU Wind Energy
Summary (max. 2000 characters):
The reduction of loads has recently become an essential goal for the wind energy
industry. Wind turbine manufacturers and wind farm operators are investigating
ways to extend the life of the components by reducing the loads upon wind tur-
bines. Recently researchers managed to apply more sophisticated control schemes
and to test the theoretical potential of those controllers. One out of these methods
is the cyclic pitch.
Cyclic pitch is a method which was developed by the helicopter industry and
adapted to wind turbines. The term cyclic is the method where the blade pitch
angles are varied with a phase shift of 120◦.
Previous studies concluded that
pitching the blades with a phase shift it is possible to alleviate loads caused by
static or slowly varying wind structures such as wind shear or to some degree
alleviate more rapid time varying wind structures such a wake.
The objective of the thesis is to implement the Fourier harmonics and by adding
them to the collective pitch to create the cyclic pitch signal. The study consists
of the investigation of load reduction by optimizing the Fourier coeﬃcients. The
optimizations are performed under three diﬀerent inﬂow cases. Then, there is
a comparison between load reduction, energy losses and bearing race way dam-
age.
Moreover, optimizations had been done but this time the pitch signal is
constructed using cubic spline interpolation.
A detailed analysis of three cases is performed using HAWC2, where the NREL
5MW wind turbine model is used both in its stiﬀand ﬂexible version. The results
after the optimization of cyclic pitch angles are discussed and compared with the
previous work of Knud A. Kragh a former PhD researcher at DTU Wind Energy.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Wind turbines rated at 10 megawatts will have blades which are more than 70 meters
long [5].
Within that length, a turbulent wind may vary considerably.
A lot of
scientists and researchers have been studied the potential of reducing the loads caused
by the wind ﬂuctuations implementing new control schemes. A previous study of that
potential has been conducted by Knud A. Kragh [8] and it was the inspiration of my
Msc thesis. He investigated the potential of load reduction above rated wind speed
optimizing the pitch signal using cubic spline interpolation. He implemented and
iterative optimization procedure in order to reduce the fatigue loads caused by the
wind inﬂow minimizing the standard deviation of blade root bending moment Mbx.
Figure 1.1: The development of wind turbines the last 30 years, courtesy to [13]
1.2
Source of loads
Wind turbine manufacturers are involved in producing wind turbines with larger
rotor diameter which means higher rated power. The problem that manufacturers
are facing is that increasing the rotor diameter also introduces asymmetric loading of
the rotor blades. As the turbine blade sweeps around the rotor disc, it experiences
changes in wind speed and direction as a result of wind shear, tower shadow, yaw
misalignment and turbulence. As rotor sizes increase the importance of turbulent

1.3
The history of control schemes
The reduction of the loads has recently become an essential goal for wind energy
industry. Several studies the last decades have investigated load alleviation control
schemes. Implementing control techniques could reduce the loads which causing dam-
age, increase maintenance costs and can shorten the life of a turbine.
In the attempt of extending the life of wind turbines and reducing the cost of
energy there are some promising control schemes which will play an important role
on the future development of wind energy. A short overview of the developments on
the control schemes during the last 30 years and some promising future concepts are
described below:
1.3.1
Stall Controlled Wind Turbines(Passive)
Stall controlled wind turbines have the rotor blades bolted onto the hub at a ﬁxed
angle. The geometry of the rotor blade proﬁle has been aerodynamically designed
to ensure that the moment the wind speed becomes too high, it creates turbulence
on the side of the rotor blade which is not facing the wind as it seen in Figure 1.3.
This stall prevents the lifting force of the rotor blade. The basic advantage of stall
control is that one avoids moving parts in the rotor and it does not require a complex
control system. Around two thirds of the wind turbines currently being installed in
the world are stall controlled machines.

1.3.2
Active Stall Controlled Wind Turbine
Compared to passive; the active stall machines are pitch controlled machines, since
they have blades which can be pitched. However the control strategy is diﬀerent than
the modern pitch controlled wind turbines. For low wind speeds, the machines are
usually be programmed to pitch their blades like a pitch controlled machine at low
wind speeds in order to get large torque. When the wind turbine reaches the rated
power, the active stall-regulated turbine will pitch the blades in the opposite direction
than a pitch-controlled machine does.This movement will increase the angle of attack
of the rotor blades in order to make the blades go into a deeper stall. This aﬀects
the energy capture which usually drops for higher wind speed(Figure 1.4). For those
reason this regulation approach is a combination between pitch and stall controlled
wind turbines.

1.3.3
Pitch Controlled Wind Turbines
Most of the modern wind turbines are equipped with pitch controller. On a pitch
controlled wind turbine the pitch angle is controlled by a computer. According to
wind speed there are regions which describe the control strategy (Figure 1.5). The
controller checks the power output of the turbine several times per second. When the
power output becomes too high, it sends an signal to pitch actuator and it pitches
slightly out of the wind. Accordingly, whenever the wind drops again the blades are
turned back in order to maximize the capture of wind. Bellow rated wind speed the
computer will pitch the blades a few degrees every time the wind changes in order to
keep the rotor blades at the optimum angle in order to maximize output for the wind
speeds higher than the rated wind speed.
Figure 1.5: Description of control regions in a pitch regulated wind turbine
1.4
Control of future wind turbines
This section introduces the current advances and future developments of the control
schemes that it is likely to equip the wind turbines in the near future.
1.4.1
Adaptive Trailing Edge Flaps
Leonardo Bergami [1] on 2013 investigated the development of an active smart rotor
and he studied the Adaptive Trailing Edge Flap (ATEF) concept, which introduces a
continuous deformation of the aft part of the airfoil camber-line. Target of the work
is to alleviate the ﬂuctuating part of the aerodynamic loads.

1.4.2
Individual Pitch
Nowadays, ﬁeld testing of an individual pitch controller has been implemented in a
research stage. As part of the EU “UPWIND” project at NREL scientists[2] tested
this type of controller at CART-2 research turbine.
Figure 1.6: The research wind turbines in NREL,courtesy to [14]
1.4.3
Feed forward control
Again in NREL researchers used LiDAR and feed-forward algorithms to improve rotor
speed regulation and reduce costs of maintenance and operation[14]. This method is
based on rotor ﬂow measurements using LiDAR. The method is called feed forward
control because the system is controlled using preview measurements of wind speed.
The cup anemometer has been replaced by a LiDAR as the sensor which gives the
wind speed signal. The improvements oﬀered by feed forward control may extend
turbine life and reduce energy cost.
1.5
Cyclic pitch control and current study
As it was mentioned the cyclic pitch scheme it was ﬁrst studied and implemented
in the helicopter industry. It was already on 1982 when the Johnson investigated
the multi cycle pitch control of helicopter vibrations [6]. On 2005 Larsen [11] from
DTU Wind Energy researched a cyclic pitch controller which measures the mean tilt
rotor and yaw moments and adjust the pitch angle in order to reduce the blade root
bending moment.

1.6.2
Description of my study
The question that I was focused in this study is to answer: ” what we use a wake
detector for and how much a wake detector can help us”. In the other words if we
had the perfect LiDAR in our hands how much we could reduce the loads caused by
slow ﬂuctuating turbulence structures such an wind shear and tower shadow or from
more fast ﬂuctuating structures such as a wake on the half side of the wind turbine.
To answer those questions I based on the work of a previous Phd. researcher in DTU
Wind Energy [8] and his team. They have investigated the maximum load reduction
using cyclic pitch control by optimizing the pitch angles. The results from this study
cab be found on Figure 1.8. They have done the analysis using the case of wind shear
including tower shadow and made the optimization on stiﬀand ﬂexible model. In this
study three load cases are investigated and the results are compared to their work.

2.3.1
Finding the steady state
An important step for the optimization is the determine the steady state period.
Steady state is the period where the transients have been extinguished. Normally a
transient period is appeared in the simulation when the generator is turning on or a
natural frequency of the system is excited.
For that reason, an algorithm ﬁnds the periods in the blade root bending moment
signal and checks two consecutive periods. The blue line in Figure 2.6 shows the
blade root bending moment and the black one indicates the azimuth position. From

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In this study the load reduction using cyclic pitch signal was investigated. The goal
of the report was to reproduce the results from the reference study implementing and
optimizing the cyclic pitch signal which is given by Fourier harmonics and moreover
to add the case of half wake inﬂow. The method of Fourier harmonics was chosen in
order to investigate if we can go further in reduction of wind shear and tower shadow
induced load variations. As it is also mentioned in previous study the wind shear in-
duced load variations can be alleviated using a simple 1p pitch signal. In accordance
there is a signiﬁcant load reduction using 1st harmonic pitch signal but adding further
harmonics does not lead to further load alleviation. In the half wake operation where
the wind signal becomes more complicated including a rapid ﬂuctuations of wind it
is observed that using a 2p pitch signal results to a higher load reduction and it is
not beneﬁcial to use more complex pitch signal.
However in the studied cases the simulations are conducted using a deterministic
wind. A future study should be focused on adding more realistic wind inﬂows such
as an optimization of pitch angles using a turbulent wind ﬁeld.
Furthermore, even if the load reduction using cyclic pitch is proven based on sim-
ulation; it is quite diﬃcult and requires a lot of eﬀort, time and money investment
from researchers and industry to transfer the cyclic pitch concept to a experimental
prototype. Once due to the fact that the pitch actuators will experience a huge loads
by moving the big masses of the blades and secondly; even if the controller gives a
really fast response the modern actuators are not able to follow that signal by means
of acting speed and acceleration.
One alternative concept could be the implementation of cyclic pitch controller in
a partial pitch wind turbine such as the 2-bladed Envision 3.6MW. A pitch bearing is
placed at the end of the inner part of the blade which is located 20m from the hub[7]
and actually the part of the blade which is pitchable is 42-meters. Consequently the
pitch actuators need less eﬀort because the mass and the inertia of the pitcable blade
span is smaller compared to a common wind turbines. From this point of view, a
further study could include a simulation of cyclic pitch in a partial pitch controlled
wind turbine with in depth analysis of bearing aspects such as loads, energy required
and damage.
