Abstract 
The concept of sustainable development - which promotes a development similarly economically viable, 
socially positive, and environmentally healthy - occupies a prominent position within research fields, 
strategic policies and diplomatic conferences at international level. When dealing with the specific 
sector of urban transport, it appears that achieving sustainable infrastructure is a complex challenge 
which is important to face. Within the framework of this master thesis, the attention is paid to answer 
the following question: How to adapt infrastructures of transport to environmental, economic and social 
challenges? As such a question covers a broad range of topics, the study field is reduced to the question 
of transportation system for people in urban areas, focusing more precisely on the European context. 
This report aims to demonstrate that it is feasible to achieve sustainable transport infrastructures, and 
explore to what extent it can be done. After a first part intended to set up the basics with a reminder of 
the background on the subject, the second part of this work will be dedicated to the theme of 
sustainability assessment of transport projects. Indeed, the demonstration of the possibility to adapt 
infrastructures to the sustainable issues will be based on criteria provided by a sustainable rating system 
internationally renowned: INVEST. Sustainability assessment will be used as a support to argue the 
theory defended in this master thesis; and additionally the ins and outs of such an evaluation and its 
positive contribution to orientate decision-making process towards a sustainable approach for transport 
projects will also be analyzed and discussed. The question of the adaptability of sustainable 
infrastructure issues is firstly addressed theoretically within chapters 1, 2 and 3; then the demonstration 
is based on a double case study related to the Paris area context: the Urban Mobility Plan of Ile-de-France 
and the Grand Paris Express project. The case study and its context are introduced in chapter 4, before 
being evaluated with criteria from the INVEST tool in chapter 5, and finally analyzed and discussed in 
chapter 6.  
Based on the theory of sustainable transportation and its evaluation on the one hand, and 
experimenting theoretical observations on a case study characterized by typical challenges of modern 
European society on the other hand, this master thesis offers food for thought and observations about 
the evolution of transport infrastructures towards a sustainable model. The main topics discussed in the 
analysis phase are: evolution of assessment tools towards a common methodology to better take into 
account sustainability issues and effectively pilot transport projects, the need to consider the problem 
of project sustainability from several perspectives (micro and macro, all phases included), and finally 
demonstration of the social and environmental but also economic interests to implement sustainable 
transport infrastructures.

Introduction 
Since the emergence of the sustainability concept in the 1970s with the Stockholm conference ([7], 
[33]), the need to ensure economic and social development that does not damage the environment and 
therefore compromise economic, social and environmental well-being of the future generations has 
gradually become an international priority ([12], [33], [44]). 
A sector deserves special attention when it comes to sustainable development: transport. During the 
19th century, the transport revolution has played a major role in the society transformation [7]. In fact, 
the development of transport is closely linked to the modernization of society, as well as economic and 
social progress which humans still enjoy today. However, it turned out that this same transportation 
system have also contributed largely to environmental degradation of the planet and have intensified 
social inequalities, particularly related to inequalities regarding the quality and access to transport [7]. 
In addition, environmental degradation itself creates new negative effects from social point of view 
(saturation, annoyance, stress, social tensions) and from economic perspective (cost of air pollution, 
economic downturns, cost of noise pollution, degradation of cultural heritage) ([7], [19], [64]). Such a 
paradox - initial social and economic major progress leading to significant social, economic and 
environmental adverse impacts – led to address transportation system as a challenging and complex 
issue when discussing about sustainability.  
Such a topic led several authors to theorize the concept of sustainable transportation that will be 
introduced in this report ([42], [43]). Among the issues related to the sustainability of transport systems, 
the specific case of transportation in urban areas and its adaptability to sustainable development is 
crucial. Indeed, many cities in the world and particularly in Europe are characterized by a population 
augmentation in areas already very dense and within old cities whose original urban organization was 
not planned for as many people and such modes of transport [44]. Such characteristics underline the 
urgency of implementing sustainable solutions to overcome these difficulties but highlight 
simultaneously the complexity represented by the implementation of these solutions. 
The general research question which defines this master thesis is: How to adapt infrastructures of 
transport to environmental, economic and social challenges?  The term “infrastructures of transport” 
means here both “material” infrastructures that characterize transport - such as stations, airports, ports, 
roads - but also all the interconnected elements which provide the framework to support the whole 
transportation system. Thereby, within this master thesis it is important to address the issue of 
infrastructures of transport also in its abstract sense, which means consider infrastructure as an entity 
with many facets: the organization of the transportation system, its logistics, its strategies and the logic 
that characterizes it.  
The initial research question was voluntary covering a relatively large study scope. More precisely, the 
main objective of this study consists in demonstrating that infrastructures of transport can be made 
sustainable, while focusing on the specific sector of urban mobility, a challenging aspect of 
transportation when it comes to find sustainable solutions. Therefore, the general research question 
could be re-formulated as: to what extend and how urban mobility can be made compatible with the 
three dimensions of sustainability? 
To answer this general question, a sustainability rating and assessment tool broadly used and accepted 
will be used as a reference to define the “sustainability degree” of transport infrastructures and 
transportation solutions analyzed: the American tool INVEST, whose some criteria will be applied to a 
case study: the city of Paris and its challenges in terms of sustainable mobility. Indeed, after a theoretical 
approach about challenges regarding sustainable urban mobility and the need to have efficient 
assessment tools to discuss this topic, a detailed analyse of a study case will be made to illustrate theory,

1. Background: Sustainability assessment of urban transport 
1.1. Sustainability 
1.1.1. Definition 
Through the literature on the subject, it is commonly stated that the concept of sustainability appears 
for the first time in 1972 during the United Nations (UN) Conference on the Human Environment in 
Stockholm, which was the first international meeting discussing exclusively about environmental issues 
([7], [33]). A more advanced description of the concept is made in 1981 by the White House Council on 
Environmental Quality Report, where it is written: “If economic development is to be successful over the 
long term, it must proceed in a way that protects the natural resource base” [13]. But, that is in 1987 
that the Brundtland Commission of the United Nations produced one of the most commonly known 
definition of sustainable development, which is presented as a “development which meets the needs of 
current generation without compromising the ability of future generation to meet their own needs” ([12], 
[44]).  
Several definitions have been made regarding the concept of sustainable development and the goal of 
sustainability, but it can globally be described with the three dimensions of sustainability: 
Environmental, Social and Economic ([5), [7], [11], [33]). Sustainability is a concept that expresses a 
desire for economic and social well-being in harmony with a healthy planet. In other words, the 
sustainable development goal lies in the satisfaction and the improvement of human needs (social and 
economic) – both present and future - while satisfying or 
maintaining the well-being of the planet.  
The three dimensions of sustainable development appear to be 
strongly connected and interrelated, as the human well being 
sought (social and economic) depends on the planet’s condition 
(environment), which itself is highly dependent on the human’s 
behavior (social) and human’s activities (social and economic). 
These important interconnections between the   three key 
principles are well visible through the commonly used illustration 
of sustainable development: three circles joined together and 
whose common intersection area symbolizes balance that must 
achieve a sustainable behavior. 
  
1.1.2. A concept mobilizing and awaking consciences 
Since its emergence in the early 1970s, the concept of sustainable development has evolved, mobilized 
an increasing number people and starts to occupy a prominent place within the thoughts and decisions 
internationally. 
20 years after the Stockholm Conference, Rio de Janeiro (Brazil) was the site of the United Nations 
Conference on Environment and Development in June 1992 - known under the name "Planet Earth 
Summit". This conference enabled the adoption of a declaration which advanced the concept of rights 
and responsibilities of countries in the environmental field. The Rio Declaration on Environment and 
Development shows two major concerns emerged during the interval of 20 years separating these two 
conferences: the deterioration of the environment, including on its ability to sustain life, and 
interdependence increasingly evident between economic progress in the long term and the need for 
environmental protection [34].

Since 1995, the Conference of the Parties (COP) meets annually, bringing together international 
delegations from all continents. The COP is responsible for maintaining international efforts to address 
climate change. It reviews the implementation of the Convention and examines the commitments of 
the parties on the basis of the objective of the Convention, new scientific findings and experience gained 
in the implementation of policies in the field changes climate. It was during COP 3 in Kyoto (Japan) that 
was decided the Kyoto Protocol in 1997, an international agreement aimed at reducing greenhouse gas 
emissions. In 2015, the COP21 held in Paris. At the end of this Conference, 195 countries have adopted 
the first global agreement on climate, the Paris Agreement. Applicable to all countries and approved by 
unanimity of the participants, this agreement sets a target of limiting global warming between 1.5 ° C 
and 2 ° C by 2100 ([35], [36]). 
1.2. Transport as part of sustainable development 
1.2.1. Transport and sustainability 
In the nineteenth century, the Industrial Revolution refers to the historical process by which a society 
previously dominated by agrarian and artisanal sectors switched to a commercial and industrial society. 
This major transformation, which has affected simultaneously society, economy and environment was 
created largely thanks to the invention of the steam engine and was stimulated by the emergence of 
the railway sector in the 1840s. Thus, the development of modern transportation system is closely linked 
to the origins of today's society. After the steam engine, major innovations in terms of transport – car, 
aviation, reactor, nuclear propulsion - have largely contributed to the economic and social 
developments that led to the actual state of the world. Transport appears as inseparable from social and 
economic progress of the late 19th and 20th centuries: it is the means by which humans have access to 
goods and services, and by which they connect culturally and sociably to communities [7]. Due to 
transportation system evolutions, it became more and more easy to move persons, materials and goods 
within countries and internationally, which metamorphosed the structure of regional, national and 
international economies [7]. From social point of view, modern transportation system allowed an 
increasing amount of people to travel easier and farther, to discover new cultures and new societies, 
which step by step leaded to global major society changes.  More recently, this global process has been 
further enhanced through the growth of Information and Communication Technology (ICT). 
However, despite real positive contributions from the perspective of society modernization (economic 
and social positive impacts), transportation system also appears to be a major contributor to degradation 
of the environment. Indeed, the current environmental situation, especially regarding the use of natural 
resources and the impact of air emissions, is very preoccupying. In 2008, the United Nations (UN) 
Millennium Ecosystem Assessment acknowledged that, over the past 40 years, the current consumption 
rate of natural resources does not allow to meet the energy needs of future generations [29]. The 
transport sector is not solely responsible for this pessimistic conclusion but is largely involved in the 
problem. Indeed, according to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), in the world 
transport sector was responsible for 14.4% of the emissions of greenhouse gases in 2012. Moreover, in 
Europe this sector was responsible for the emission of 893 million tons CO2eq in 2012, corresponding 
to 20% of the total amount of CO2eq emitted in the continent [30]. The situation is even more alarming 
if the carbon footprint related to the construction of transport infrastructure (stations, airports, roads) 
is included in the total. 
Furthermore, transportation system also has negative social repercussions through the inequality it 
generates. Indeed, the different areas of the planet are not served equitably by modern transport 
networks. Whether within a country, from one region to the other, within a continent, from country to 
country, or at planet level, from one continent to the other, there are real and profound inequalities: 
certain areas find themselves isolated because poorly connected to transport networks which can largely

hampers their economic and social development. Considerable social tensions may result from these 
inequalities [7]. Thus, paradoxically, social problems result of global social progress allowed by transport 
evolution because this evolution has been inequitably developed.   
Additionally, environmental negative impacts of transportation system also lead to negative economic 
consequences. In fact, excessive pollution and air quality degradation it involves have a significant 
economic cost: costs related to treatment of diseases resulting from human exposure to pollution, costs 
associated with the necessary cleanup of polluted areas, economic slowdown during peak pollution are 
examples of these negative impacts. As an example, in 2015 a study conducted by World Health 
Organization (WHO) and the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) 
evaluated the economic cost of pollution at 1 430 billion euros per year in Europe, which is 10% of the 
EU GDP [19]. Thus, environmental problems linked to transportation system expansion tend to diminish 
its positive economic impact. 
These paradoxes related to transport and sustainability have led to consider transportation system as a 
key issue when thinking about sustainable development ([42], [44]). Thus, the concept of sustainable 
transportation emerged in parallel with the evolution of sustainability concept across the major 
environmental conferences, from Stockholm in 1972 to Paris in 2015 [7]. In fact, sustainable 
transportation can be defined as an “expression of sustainable development in the transportation 
sector” [43]. In 2002, BENFIELD and REPLOGE wrote the following statement about sustainable 
transportation, which nicely summarizes this section: “There can be no sustainable development without 
sustainable transportation. It is an essential component not only because transportation is a prerequisite 
to development in general but also because transportation […] contributes substantially to a wide range 
of environmental problems […]” [42]. 
1.2.2. Urban transport: a special challenge 
The case of urban transport represents a particular challenge when addressing sustainable 
transportation issues, generally resulting from the duality between old city’s structures and modern 
socio-economic challenges in urban areas. Indeed, these zones concentrate high population density, 
high density of existing infrastructure inappropriate for modern uses and growing demand for transport, 
thus constituting a complex tangle of challenges when it comes to reflect on a transportation network 
aligned with the socio-economic and environmental topics. 
In fact, mobility demands increase in all the major cities of the world, sometimes faster than the 
economic growth, and often faster than the reduction of energy consumption and air quality 
degradation. European big cities for example – which are known for their historical compact urban 
centers – face problems regarding the integration of adapted transport network in the urban areas while 
the suburbanization makes it increasingly urgent to find efficient solutions [44]. To stay on the case of 
European cities, three major issues can be identified concerning urban transport: traffic congestion, air 
quality degradation, urban organization not adapted to recent social and demographic changes. 
These three themes are extremely in interaction. Indeed, the air quality deterioration is partly due to 
traffic congestions which are themselves largely the result of overpopulation in old urban areas, which 
were not initially designed to accommodate both so many people and such a travel demand. Moreover, 
they have interconnected serious impacts from the perspective of the three dimensions of 
sustainability. Indeed, traffic congestion causes frustration, annoyance and consequent loss of time 
(social problems), while being the origin of direct (fuel waste) and indirect (work time lost) financial 
losses (economic problems), and of course it is synonymous of important pollution, both air and noise 
pollution (environmental problems). Similarly, air quality degradation in urban areas engenders obvious 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
environmental troubles while being economically problematic (anti-pollution measures prices, resident’s 
health costs) and sociably preoccupying (consciousness of living in an unhealthy atmosphere). 
Paradoxically overpopulation sometimes results itself partially from the attractiveness of a territory 
because of its accessibility and transport network. Thus the initial effectiveness of urban transport 
network can cause an increase in population density, which leads to the saturation of this same 
transport network, and therefore causes damages from social, economic and environmental 
perspective. 
1.2.3. A situation highlighting the need for efficient urban mobility planning policies  
As explained in this section, transport is a sector strongly connected with the three dimensions of 
sustainability: it allows to transport goods, connects people and facilitate society development (social) 
while being crucial from economic perspective, but similarly a huge factor of pollution and environment 
degradation. Therefore, it appears important to have efficient tools and methods to plan transport policy 
following sustainable requirements. 
Nevertheless, a difficulty which occurs when discussing sustainable transportation lies in the fact that 
transportation is not a closed, self-contained system [44]. Indeed, it is tightly interconnected with other 
sectors such as energy (when dealing with propulsion forms of the various transport modes for instance) 
or construction (construction of transport infrastructures and all the related impacts from socio-
economic and environmental perspective). This impossibility to reduce the transport sector in a closed 
area leads to a certain level of frustration when it comes to develop efficient and comprehensive 
solutions to sustainability problems related to transportation. As a result of this, a special attention as 
to be paid in the definition and implementation of sustainable transportation policies. Focusing on 
sustainable transportation issues in urban area, the concept of Sustainable Urban Mobility Planning 
(SUMP) will be presented in the next part of this study. 
1.3. Sustainable Urban Mobility Planning (SUMP) 
1.3.1. Brief history about sustainable urban mobility in European Union 
In Europe, the efforts made to implement urban policies pushing society along a path “more 
sustainable” led to the development of the European Conference of Ministers of Transport’s (ECMT) 
Sustainable Urban Travel (SUT) program between 1997 and 2001. This initiative sponsored a series of 
workshops dedicated to discuss the difficult integration of sustainable policies in urban areas [44]. This 
was a first major step regarding the conceptualization of sustainable urban transport policies in Europe. 
Subsequently, the European Commission published three documents concerning the planning 
framework for urban mobility in Europe: the Green Paper in September 2007, the Action Plan on Urban 
Mobility in September 2009 and the White Paper in March 2011. 
The 2007 Green Paper aims to orientate European transport policies towards a new culture of urban 
mobility. It states that European Union must play a leading role on this issue by promoting the sharing 
of good practices at all levels, by supporting the harmonization of standards, encouraging innovation and 
by financially supporting the countries and regions that need it most [10]. 
In 2009, the Action Plan for Urban Mobility aims to accelerate the adoption of sustainable urban mobility 
plan in the major cities of the European Union thanks to the support of the Commission to local 
authorities for the implementation of ambitious plans [10]. 
In 2011, the European Commission published the current most comprehensive expression of the EU 
transportation strategy objectives: the European Transport Policy White Paper, entitled “Roadmap to a 
Single Transport Area-Towards a competitive and resource efficient transport system” [7]. This

1.3.2. From major institutional documents to their concretization: the SUMP   
From the early 2010s, the European Commission decided to accelerate the adoption of Sustainable 
Urban Mobility Plans (SUMP) at EU level ([10], [45]). The concept of SUMP is defined as a "strategic plan 
designed to meet the mobility needs of people and economic activities within the cities and their 
suburbs, in order to achieve a better quality of life. It is based on existing planning practices and 
incorporates the principles of integration, participation and evaluation “[10].  
The Commission pushes for a balanced and integrated development of all transport modes, while 
encouraging a shift towards more sustainable modes of transport. The role of evaluation ante and post

1.4. Assessing sustainable transport planning 
The significant environmental impact of the transport and infrastructure construction sectors led the 
stakeholders of these sectors to address solutions in order to assess, analyze and reduce this impact. 
Indeed, it turns out that the last few years industrial sector for instance appears more concerned about 
sustainable development [14]. This relatively new appetite for sustainable development issues led to 
the need to measure and evaluate the environmental performance of projects, whether in construction 
or transportation projects.  
Several sustainable transport assessment tools and methods have been developed to meet this need. 
Main current methods, tools and approaches for transportation projects evaluation can be grouped in 
three main categories: 
- 
Traditional decision-making techniques, including Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) and Multi-criteria 
Decision Analysis (MCDA) 
- 
Specific environmental and social assessment techniques, including Life-Cycle Assessment and 
Social Life Cycle Assessment (SLCA) 
- 
Sustainability rating systems, scoring and awarding infrastructures projects in function of their

2. Sustainable Transport Assessment theory 
This section doesn’t aim to make a complete and exhaustive review of sustainable assessment theory 
but is dedicated to give a brief overview of the main sustainable transport assessment tools and 
methods used in the frame of transport projects evaluation. The major part of this chapter will be 
focused on sustainability rating systems, as a form of introduction to INVEST, American sustainable 
rating tool which will be further described in the next chapter and used to assess the study case in the 
final chapter. 
2.1. Traditional decision-making techniques 
This section will focus on Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) and multi-criteria approaches. These tools were not 
initially conceived for sustainability purpose but are among the most commonly used methods for 
decision making processes in transport project assessment, and these techniques integer sustainability 
dimensions increasingly.  
2.1.1. Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) 
CBA is well known and broadly used method when it comes to support decision-makers evaluating 
transport projects. According to some authors, CBA is considered as the basic tool in the transport field 
in the majority of European countries [54]. The CBA approach is classically employed as an ex-ante 
method – during planning phase – and consists in comparing several alternatives under the objective of 
maximizing social welfare. The technique is based on the monetization of user benefits such as travel 
time savings, well-being, or money-savings, compared to the cost of investment and other “negative” 
effects also monetized such as energy consumption, CO2 emissions and resources use. [33] 
As every method, CBA has its advantages and its drawbacks. Concerning the advantages, literature on 
the topic highlights the fact that CBA proves to be a “transparent, rigorous and formal appraisal tool”, 
which makes it a valuable support to assess sustainability ([33], [54]). One of the main interest of CBA 
lies in the fact that such a method provides clear, rational and tangible judgements of benefits and costs 
which allows to compare different alternatives for a transport project and to select the most adapted one 
based on quantitative and easily identifiable criteria. Thus, CBA appears to be useful and efficient to 
support decision-making during planning and design phases. 
On the other hand, main CBA drawbacks result from the difficulty to evaluate from a monetary 
perspective some projects aspects which are not evaluable this way. In fact, in the transportation 
evaluation context, there is for example complexity to quantify non-market goods such as road accidents, 
CO2 emissions, or people’s well-being. Due to these limitations, results from CBA assessment may be 
questioned and considered as “pseudo-objective” according to some authors’ analysis ([33], [54]). 
2.1.2. Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) 
Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA, sometimes just MCA in literature) is a widely used appraisal 
method, which allows to evaluate different options based on a multi-dimensional criteria framework and 
calculates rankings of these options in order to support decision-making process [56]. Such a technique 
is described by several authors as suitable when addressing complex problems characterized by high 
uncertainty, conflicting objectives, multi interests and perspectives, different forms of information and 
data, and the accounting for complex and evolving biophysical and socio-economic systems ([33], [55], 
[56]). Thus, MCDA appears to be an appropriate tool to support decisions based on sustainability 
assessment of complex projects such as urban transport projects. When it is used to address 
sustainability, MCDA approach usually includes identification of sustainability criteria, evaluation of each 
project’s alternatives based on these criteria, assignment of specific weighting coefficients to each 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
criterion, ranking of alternatives based on the criteria evaluation results, and finally selection of the 
“best” alternative from the MCDA perspective [33]. 
MCDA offers several advantages for decision-makers. Indeed, it allows to take into account several 
criteria simultaneously, including criteria dealing with aspects difficult to monetize or quantify such as 
macroeconomic impacts (economic dimension), social distributive effect of a transport project (social 
dimension), or negative visual impact (environmental and social dimensions) [33]. Moreover – as the 
last three example highlight - MCDA can explicitly deal with the three dimensions of sustainability which 
makes it very appropriate to support decision-making based on sustainability objectives. Furthermore, 
this method enables stakeholder involvement in the assessment process as the criteria are clear and 
transparent [33].  
Nevertheless, MCDA does have some issues as it is highlighted by articles dedicated to this topic: 
difficulty to evaluate qualitatively without an inherent subjectivity, complexity to identify precisely and 
accurately the impacts to assess, and the fact that the criteria weighting can be imputed of a certain 
level of subjective influence. Indeed, according to several authors’ criticisms [33], it appears that the 
use of weight is the main weakness of such an assessment methodology from objectivity perspective as 
it is difficult not to be influenced by subjective judgements when implementing the weighting 
coefficients. 
2.2. Techniques for assessing environmental/social impacts 
2.2.1. Life-Cycle Assessment (LCA) 
Life-Cycle Assessment (LCA) is defined as “a method used to assess environmental impact of a product, 
activity, or process” [33]. This technique is broadly-used by decision-makers; it consists of an 
environmental performance assessment of the whole project life-cycle, from “cradle to grave “, including 
material extraction, materials transport, construction environmental impacts, service, operations, 
maintenance, infrastructures end-of-life, waste management, and recycling.  
LCA provides valuable advantages when evaluating sustainability of a transport project due to its life-
cycle approach which reflects environmental issues from a quite exhaustive perspective. Indeed, as it 
focuses on the whole project life-cycle, this approach allows to include all aspects and to address the 
project assessment with objectivity. It is a solution to relieve criticisms about evaluation processes where 
a project is declared sustainable as it provides sustainable outcomes even if the implementation of the 
project (construction) has negative environmental impacts (for example: construction of a high-speed 
railway allowing to replace cars by trains (positive impact) but destroying landscapes and biodiversity 
on the other hand (negative impact)). 
However, on the other hand, some authors point that LCA is an incomplete tool as it focuses on 
environmental aspects and has more difficulties to address the other sustainable dimensions. In fact, 
general criticisms about LCA lies on the observation that such environmental-oriented tools have a 
“general objective of encouraging greater environmental responsibility within the construction industry, 
but not toward sustainability as a whole” [57]. As a consequence, it appears that LCA should be 
considered as an important part of an ideal complete sustainability assessment tool but cannot be 
viewed as a perfect sustainable appraisal method in itself. 
2.2.2. Social assessment approaches still under development 
Evaluation of transport projects’ social impacts is an aspect which is taking into account more and more 
often. However, it is still less commonly addressed than environmental and economic dimensions within 
the sustainability assessment tools. The integration of social aspects into LCA (which is called SLCA) is 
still under development and faces some difficulties.  
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
The main limitation when addressing sustainability assessment from social perspective lies in the 
complexity to define precisely the term “social impacts”, which is challenging as the adjective “social” 
may have a broad range of significations. Moreover, social impacts are mainly assessed qualitatively due 
to the lack of appropriate techniques to evaluate them quantitatively, which often lead to superficial 
social impacts assessment, and then increase the risk to obtain subjective social overview of a project. 
[33] 
2.3. Sustainability rating systems 
2.3.1.  Definition 
There is currently an important number of sustainability rating systems, produced by national and 
international organizations ([5], [14]). It appears that these systems were primarily almost exclusively 
used by construction and were slow to reach the usage patterns of transportation industry [14]. They 
vary in complexity and in context where they are supposed to be used, but globally the sustainability 
rating systems are conceived to “provide guidance, scoring and potential rewards for the use of 
sustainability best practices” [5]. They usually assess sustainability actions throughout five main 
categories: use of resources, energy, transport, water and waste [14]. Typically, sustainability rating 
systems focus on exemplary practices that are compatible with standards regulation but are above and 
beyond minimum requirements [14].  
Concretely, a sustainability rating system generally consists of a list of sustainability best practices which 
are associated with a common metric, often points, quantifying each enhanced practice in a common unit 
([5], [33]). It allows to directly compare the diverse characteristics of sustainable practices of a project. 
Classical measurements units of such a system are for example tons of recycled materials used in the 
project, ecosystem connectivity, pedestrian access, construction waste management, or life cycle 
assessment of the materials used ([5], [11]).When they are simple ones, these rating systems can 
consider every best practice with a unique scale. On the other hand, more complex systems provide 
different weight to practice assessed in function of their impact, their difficulty to be achieved or their 
priority ([5], [14], [31]). 
Due to their operating principle, some of these rating systems, such as INVEST (described in the 
following parts) where best practices collection take the form of a series of multiple criteria, may be 
similar to a sub category of multi-criteria decision analysis methods described previously. 
2.3.2. A crucial tool 
Similarly to other evaluation techniques discussed in this chapter, the sustainability rating systems offer 
a number of strengths. 
Firstly, through their structure, these systems provide guidance to engineering designers regarding the 
integration of sustainability over the whole life cycle of infrastructure projects, from design and planning 
to maintenance phase, including construction and operation. Through the guidance they provide, these 
tools may assist designers to select the most appropriate sustainable best practices. Such methods are 
generally appreciated because they are considered as “easy to understand, simple to implement, and 
highly flexible and adaptable” [33]. Thanks to this simplicity and easiness to be understood, rating 
systems allow for straightforward communication of sustainability objectives, efforts made, and 
achievement realized [5]. 
Moreover, as most of the sustainability rating systems are regionally based, they use to incorporate 
almost “naturally” the context-sensitivity nature of sustainability concept. Thus, due to these 
characteristics, the assessment performed is more accurate because it includes considerations specific 
to the project location. Indeed, for example, apply the British CEEQUAL system (Civil Engineering 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
Environmental Quality and Assessment Scheme) outside UK requires to adjust the weighting process to 
the local project characteristics; which highlights the regionally-based aspect of such a tool. [33]. 
Nevertheless, such an advantage may also be a weakness as it requires adaptation work when 
“exporting” the sustainability rating system to a region for which it was initially not conceived for. 
Furthermore, one of the most worthwhile advantage of these assessment methods lies in the fact that 
they define a quantitative process to address sustainability [33]. Indeed, through the use of points to 
evaluate each criterion, these techniques have the interest to measure sustainability with a common 
metric for the entire range of sustainable solutions and thus they make sustainability manageable clearly. 
[5]

2.3.4. Drawbacks of sustainability rating systems 
Despite clear valuable strengths, sustainability rating tools also suffer from some drawbacks and 
weaknesses. 
First of all, across literature on the topic, an observation is recurrent: sustainability rating systems lack 
objectiveness and transparency in the definition of criteria’s sustainability degree, as well as in methods 
regarding the credits weighting ([14], [33]). Indeed, as a conclusion of a review of six sustainable rating 
systems for infrastructures projects, Clevenger, Ozbek and Simpson highlight the following conclusion 
about the reviewed systems’ methodologies: “To what extent sustainability is achieved remains 
uncertain since consensus does not exist as to the definition of sustainability for […] infrastructure 
projects” [14]. 
In addition, although there are based on similar operation processes with comparable categories and 
overall methodology – as underlined in the “review” table above and briefly mentioned in the previous 
section –, there are important levels of variations between the different systems regarding the credits 
metering, the definition of achievement levels, and the weights granted to same categories ([14], [33]). 
Thus, each tool serves as an independent performance metric, with its own philosophy and conceptual 
approach of sustainability. Such an observation leads to difficulty when comparing rating systems, and 
this antagonizes the possibility to universalize transport sustainable assessment theory. Indeed, due to 
the context sensitivity characteristic of sustainability itself, each rating system tends to be developed at 
regional level with its own context characteristics [5]. It leads to a proliferation of numerous different 
rating systems, which have all a lot of strengths, but whose impossibility to be developed around a same 
consistent and identifiable framework constitutes a relevant weakness. Therefore, the objective to 
implement a common framework for these systems, a kind of universalization of the process, would be 
an asset. 
Moreover, several authors point out the fact that for the majority of them the rating systems are mostly 
focused on environmental topics and tend to lay aside economy and social dimensions of sustainability 
([5], [33]). In fact, in conclusion of review comparing 11 of the most commonly used sustainable rating 
systems, Veeravigrom, Muench and Kosonen highlight that, among the 11 systems reviewed and 
according to their analysis methodology, “there are 29 topics in the Environment dimension compared 
to only 21 in the Human and Economy dimensions combined” and that economy dimension topics are 
not addressed consistently in 4 of the 11 compared systems. [5].   
Furthermore, in the same review [5], authors blame rating systems because the majority of the 11 
compared promote “eco-efficiency” – the concept of producing goods and service while producing less 
pollution and waste – which is “a step towards sustainability” but does not provide a viable long term 
strategy. Indeed, reduce impacts related to design and construction phase is a crucial and positive goal 
but doesn’t address the sustainability performance of the overall transport infrastructure itself. Such an 
observation is directly linked to another recurrent criticism about rating systems: they usually measure 
design and construction but do not assess the global performance of the evaluated transportation project. 
And even when they evaluate operation and maintenance, they are more focused on operation details 
such as “operation vehicles fuel efficiency”, or “electrical energy efficiency for maintenance” (INVEST, 
OM-02 and OM-03, [2]), than on the global sustainable transportation concept. Thereby, such 
sustainability rating systems could be complemented by criteria encouraging a rethink of the whole 
process based on the finality of transport, such as travel time reduction considerations.

3. Sustainability of Transport Infrastructures: Research question theory 
3.1. Assessing sustainability to demonstrate its feasibility 
3.1.1. Research question 
The general research question of this master thesis - which is also its title - is: How to adapt 
infrastructures of transport to environmental, economic and social challenges?  Such an initial research 
question was voluntary covering a relatively large study scope. Moreover, it seemed interesting to 
mention the three dimensions of sustainability directly in the tile instead of using only the adjective 
“sustainable” in order to specify that the study will consider equally these three dimensions, which is a 
difficulty often highlighted by authors within the literature on the subject ([5], [14], [31], [33]). By making 
the choice to adopt a quite broad title, the idea was to use this research work to make a brief review of 
main issues regarding sustainability, urban transportation systems and the challenges related to the 
achievement of sustainable transportation models. Indeed, the main objective of this study consists in 
demonstrating that infrastructures of transport can be made sustainable, while focusing on the specific 
sector of urban mobility, a challenging aspect of transportation when it comes to find sustainable 
solutions. Therefore, the general research question could be re-formulated as: to what extent and how 
urban mobility can be made compatible with the three dimensions of sustainability? 
3.1.2. Analysis process 
The overall analysis performed in the frame of this master thesis aims to demonstrate that 
infrastructures of transport may be adapted to the 3 dimensions of sustainable development.  While 
the first two parts of this report were dedicated to frame the study - definition of sustainability as a 
general concept, then focus on sustainable transportation, introduction to the concept of Sustainable 
Urban Mobility Plans, and more detailed presentation and review of the sustainable transport 
assessment theory -, the next chapters will be focused on the study case used to support the overall 
theory of this master thesis, its evaluation, its analysis and a general discussion about the evaluation 
process. Indeed, to put examples on theoretical elements and confront the theories to reality, the 
following parts of this study will be based on an interesting study case when it comes to address the 
sustainability of urban transport systems: Paris and its region, also known as Paris metropolis, or Ile-de-
France. Inside this study case, two major projects are selected for partial evaluation and analysis: the 
Urban Mobility Plan of Ile-de-France (PDUIF) and the Grand Paris Express (GPE). Some specific aspects of 
these two projects will be selected and evaluated using INVEST, a sustainability rating tool introduced in 
the previous part which will be described more precisely in the next section.  
The PDUIF is the Paris’s SUMP. In France, SUMPs exist since 1982 under the name of Urban Mobility 
Plans (PDU) and are compulsory for urban areas over 100 000 inhabitants since 1996. The Plan which 
will be assessed and analyzed is the second one for the region Ile-de-France (Paris region). Such a plan 
includes a lot of aspects and specificities which would be interesting but also really long to analyze 
comprehensively, so only some specific topics of PDUIF will be selected to be assessed using INVEST and 
then analyzed and discussed. On the other hand, Grand Paris Express (GPE) is a major metro project 
involving the creation of 200 km of railways, the creation of four new subway lines, the extension of two 
existing lines and the construction of 68 new stations in the Paris suburbs. As for the PDUIF, due to the 
importance and the complexity of such a project, only some specific aspects will be analyzed. The 
selected topics for analyze and discussion will cover design, construction and operation phases. 
The aim is not the assessment of PDUIF or GPE as such but the demonstration of the feasibility of 
sustainable transport infrastructures through the evaluation of these projects. The assessment is used 
to support the overall analysis. And INVEST is used as reference tool, a yardstick of what a sustainable 
transport project should be. Indeed, through its collection of criteria, INVEST can be seen as a catalog of 
good practices in the field of sustainable transport system. In this way, the use of INVEST will enable to

3.1.4. Paris metropolitan area as study case 
In terms of mobility, Île-de-France is characterized by a high diversity of needs and travel patterns. 
The solutions provided must be adapted to the quotidian travels (to get to work or study place, go 
shopping, leisure activities), also adapted to tourists travels, and the need to access from anywhere in 
the country to the regional Paris’s poles of interest (airports, stations, universities, major sports 
infrastructures, etc). 
The diagnosis of the current situation of the Paris transport system and mobility practices shows strengths 
but also weaknesses. The importance of the existing public transport network is an undeniable asset. 
However, the use of the car still important and the almost systematic use of the road freight transport 
lead to a form of excessive dependence towards fossil energies. In addition, the capacity of the transport 
network, whether collective transport or road, is now insufficient and leads to too strong saturation 
problems, degrading the quality of life and quality of the air, and slowing economic growth . Finally, access 
to transport is unequal between the areas of the metropolis. 
Such a situation lead to interesting challenges in terms of improvement of transportation system 
towards more sustainability. During the decade 2010-2020 and even on a longer future period, two 
major projects tend to provide concrete solutions to solve the transport problems of Paris region 
mentioned above: Urban Mobility Plan of Ile-de-France– PDUIF in French -, and Grand Paris Express 
(GPE).

3.1.5. Study hypothesis, defining sustainability for the study case analysis 
For the purpose of this master thesis and based on sustainability definition presented in part 1, a 
transport project will be considered as “sustainable” when it can be demonstrated that that the project 
contributes to improve people wellbeing, favor economic development and fulfill the transportation 
needs in a way compatible with environmental needs. Furthermore, the criteria from INVEST will be 
considered as a reference and the goal to achieve to be able to claim the status of "project approach in 
line with the challenges of sustainable development" for the aspects evaluated and analyzed. 
3.2. A broadly used assessment tool: INVEST 
3.2.1. Introduction to INVEST 
As discussed in previous sections, during recent decades the need to adopt a sustainable approach to 
the transport sector development has gradually become obvious. This led to the creation of tools 
designed to frame transport projects and assess their sustainability, including the sustainability rating 
systems presented in Chapter 2. Among these rating systems, many were designed in the US as it is the 
case of the tool INVEST introduced in this section. 
INVEST, which means Infrastructure Voluntary Evaluation Sustainability Tool is a sustainability rating 
system created in 2009 by the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA) [2]. This voluntary, self-directed, 
web-based and free tool aims to provide a sustainable approach towards highways projects by helping 
decision makers to take judicious decisions regarding environmental, social and economic choices when 
it comes to improve the sustainability of transportations projects. INVEST has been initially created to 
be used by state departments of transportation (DOTs), local transport agencies and metropolitan 
planning organization (MPOs) ([2], [11]). 
According to INVEST’s documentation, the tool has been created and updated based on input, advice 
and feedbacks from professionals of transportation sector who used the tool in the frame of their 
projects.  As it can be seen through the methods defined by FWHA and through examples highlighted 
on the INVEST’s website, the tool aims to encourage and assist transport agencies to go beyond 
regulations and promote best practices. 
3.2.2. How does INVEST work? 
3.2.2.1. 
Generalities 
INVEST is a criteria-based sustainability rating system. It includes 81 criteria organized within 4 scoring 
modules, which are intended to help transportation professionals to evaluate programs and projects 
from sustainability perspective. Through INVEST’s website, FHWA helps users to identify, research and 
apply the criteria, and provides guidance to establish an evaluation method allowing to measure the 
progress toward more sustainable transport projects. 
Modules are conceived in such a way that each module is independent and may be evaluated separately. 
Each module contains between 14 and 33 criteria and each criterion focuses on a specific topic area.  
3.2.2.2. 
Modules 
The four INVEST’s modules aim to cover all the project phases. Therefore the users may pick criteria 
among modules untitled System Planning for States, System Planning for Regions, Project Development, 
and Operations & Maintenance. 
The System Planning for States (SPS) and System Planning for Regions (SPR) modules contain criteria 
aiming to assess an “agency’s system-level planning and programming policies, processes, procedures 
and practices” [11]. These two modules – whose distinctions, States and Regions, correspond mainly to 
US distinctions – are primarily dedicated to the assessment of an agency’s Long range Transportation 
Plan, which, at urban level, is more or less the US equivalent of the European SUMPS described in 
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
Chapter 1. SPS and SPR modules focus on how to conceive transportation planning documents that take 
into consideration the sustainability challenges. These two modules each contain 17 criteria, with a 
maximum of 15 points for each one, combined into a single scorecard [11]. 
The Project Development module covers the entire project development process, including similarly 
early project planning, preliminary and final design, environmental documentation, alternative analysis, 
and construction aspects. This module is provided with 7 available scorecards for the projects 
evaluation. Indeed, not all criteria are adapted to every project’s context, thus six of the scorecards are 
based on both the project location (rural or urban) and project’s type (paving, basic, extended, or 
scenic/recreational).  A seventh scorecard exists: a “custom” scorecard including 11 core criteria plus 
user-selected criteria to realize a custom self-evaluation of the project. The overall PD module contains 
a maximum of 33 criteria scored with a maximum between 2 to 15 points per criterion [11]. These 33

Concretely, each criterion consists of a mini questionnaire with binary questions (yes or no) or multiple 
choice questions. Each response corresponds to a specific number of points. The rating of the criterion 
is obtained by adding the points obtained based on answers. Then the scores for each criterion within 
the same module are summed to obtain the final score specific to each module.

4.1. Paris Metropolitan area 
4.1.1. Paris 
The city of Paris is the capital of France. It is located in the northern part of the country, in the heart of 
a vast fertile plain called Paris basin.  
For a long time, Paris has been the most populated city in Europe, it remains the most populated city in 
France, the third most populated urban area in Europe, and the 29th most populated of the world [29]. 
According to INSEE (the French National Institute about Statistics and Economic Studies), there was 
more than 2.2 million of inhabitants in the city in January 2013. The Paris area has been largely 
developed during the twentieth century, collecting 10.6 million inhabitants in January 2012 for the Paris 
agglomeration, and 12.4 million of inhabitants in January 2013 for the whole urban area (agglomeration 
and urban periphery) [23]. 
The city of Paris is divided into 20 municipal administrative divisions, called arrondissements, which have 
their own city halls and city councils. Such a particular city subdivision exists in the three biggest cities 
in France (Paris, Marseille and Lyon). 
The city proper has a high population density with 21 423 inhabitants per square kilometre according to 
INSEE data, with a maximum arrondissement population density of 42 074 inhabitants per square 
kilometre in the 11th arrondissement. The most populated arrondissement is the 15th one, with 238 190 
inhabitants in 2012 (28 088 inhabitants/km² in this arrondissement). 
4.1.2. Ile-de-France 
4.1.2.1. 
A dense region 
Paris is the centre of a vast metropolitan region called Ile-de-France, literally translatable as France’s 
Island in English. With 12 million of inhabitants, this region, also commonly known as “Paris region” or 
“Paris Metropolitan area” is the most populated region of France and concentrates itself almost 20% of 
the overall French population [3], [16]. 
This region alone includes 1280 municipalities, distributed over 8 departments, all on a total area of 
only 12,070 km² (smallest of the 13 French regions). It has a population density of 996 inhabitants per 
square kilometer. 
4.1.2.2. 
A major tourist hub 
The region, which hosts tourist sites such as the Eiffel Tower, Versailles or the Louvre, is a tourist center 
of significant size. Thus, by its rich cultural heritage and its leading position in business tourism, Ile-de-

Ile-de- La France. According to INSEE, the economic impacts of tourism in Ile de France in 2011 
represented € 39 billion and generate 395,000 jobs [21]. 
4.1.2.3. 
A third of the country’s concentration of wealth concentrated in less than 2% of 
the national territory 
In 2011, the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of the region was around 600 billion Euros. The region 
produces 30% of the wealth of the country with 20% of the national population, and this on less than 2% 
of the area of French territory [3], [16]. Regional activity is mainly directed towards the service sector 
(87% of total employment against 75% for the rest of the country). With 20% of executives against 7% 
for the rest of the country, the region is by far the one where the share of executives in the workforce 
is the most important of France. Their functions often radiate beyond the strict perimeter of Ile-de-
France and affect economic development in other regions [16].

4.1.3.2. 
Economic and social impacts of air quality issues in Ile-de-France 
In addition to having a direct significant environmental impact, exceeding pollution limits also has an

residents, regional commuters and tourists, (social impact) such as saturated public transport, delays, or 
impossibility to use their vehicles for half of the car owners [18]. Moreover, such measures necessary 
slow down the economic life which has impacts on the local economy. 
Furthermore, the health impact of pollution appears to be relevant, leading to 600 000 premature 
deaths in 2010 in Europe. And the health impact of pollution is also alarming from economic perspective. 
Indeed, according to a report from World Health Organization (WHO) and the Organization for

Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) published on the 28th of April 2015, the economic cost 
related to health impacts of air pollution has been estimated at 48 billion of Euros per year in France, 
which corresponds to 2.3% of the national Gross Domestic Product (GDP) [19]. Additionally, European 
Commission has initiated a litigation procedure against France for the non-respect of limit values for 
particles and for the failure of existing action plans, litigation procedure which will also have direct

Additionally, as study performed in 2016 by ADEME shows that noise pollution related to transport in

4.1.3.3. 
A less pessimistic situation when compared to the number of inhabitants 
Nevertheless, it should be noted that despite its high population density, its high production of wealth 
and the importance of its infrastructure, Ile-de-France is the region whose energy consumption per 
capita is the lowest in France, 92.4g per km per habitant in Ile-de-France against 127.7g per km per 
habitant in the rest of the country [16]. It is also the region where the CO2 emissions due to travel for 
work and studies are the lowest per capita. Indeed, in the region 44 % of the working people use 
collective transports to go to work, which is four time more than the percentage in the second region 
where public transport are the most used, Rhône-Alpes.  
This may seem paradoxical when considering the situation described previously about the air quality 
[18], [19]. But in reality this is explained again by the very high population density. Thus, a very large 
amount of pollutants are emitted into a low sized area which results in poor air quality. But this area is 
extremely populated, which leads to an apparently less alarming situation when the pollution is 
considered from the perspective of emission per capita.  
This observation highlights the necessity to use correct and adapted indicators when assessing 
environmental situation of an area. Indeed, even if statistics show that Ile-de-France inhabitants have a 
relatively good behavior in terms of pollutants emissions per capita, it appears that – due to the high 
density population – efforts still have to be done to improve air quality in the area.   
4.2. Urban Mobility Plan: PDU, a French pioneer system of SUMP 
4.2.1. 35 years of history 
The Urban Mobility Plan (Plan de Déplacements Urbains - PDU) was created by the French framework 
Law about internal transport (Loi d’Orientation des Transports Intérieurs – Loi LOTI) in 1982 [8]. It was 
only developed and systematized in 1996, when the French law about Air Quality (Loi sur l’Air et 
l’Utilisation Rationnelle de l’Energie – Loi LAURE) made it compulsory to produce a PDU for urban areas 
of more than 100 000 inhabitants [9].  
The PDU can be defined as “a global planning tool for mobility across an urban area, which defines 
organisational principles regarding transport and parking for both people and goods, covering all modes 
of transportation” [8].  It is also a scheduling tool as it prioritises and sets out how measures contained 
within it will be financed. It sets legal framework and guidelines which have to be taken into account in 
local urban development plans, as well as in the decisions and actions regarding transport taken by local 
public authorities and road network managers [8]. 
In  the 1980’s and 1990’s, the PDU started out as a general transport planning tool in urban area, 
focusing especially on the development of public transport and active transportation modes such as 
walking and cycling. More recently, during the 2000’s and early 2010’s, the PDU framework has been 
strengthened by several laws. Gradually, this planning tool took into account issues which used to 
receive insufficient consideration in the early decades of PDU’s history: environment and climate 
==PAGE:29==
28 
 
change, road safety, access to transport for people with reduced mobility, better integration of urban 
planning policies, transportation of goods, or parking management [8], [9]. 
35 years after its creation, articles about PDU shows that – through its evolution and its implementation 
in several cities in France – this planning tool has significantly and positively (from sustainable 
perspective) helped to change people’s mobility: increased use of public transportation, decreased use 
of car in city centres, and development of alternative modes of transportation [8]. In addition, besides 
the compulsory PDUs for towns of more than 100 000 inhabitants, several smaller towns decided to 
voluntary develop a PDU approach. Nowadays, there are nearly 80 compulsory PDUs implemented in 
France, and about as many voluntary PDUs adopted in medium sized towns. [9] 
Furthermore, the PDU exports to Europe and is one of the Sustainable Urban Mobility Plans (SUMP) 
promoted by European Union and its Action Plan on Urban Mobility. [10] 
4.2.2. A system with a legal framework 
The PDU is now defined in the French transport code. It can be defined as a general tool coordinating 
approaches specific to the various transport sectors, aiming for coherence within the whole transport 
system and framing the measures relating to mobility in an urban area.  
Since the 1996 Air Quality Act strengthened the application of PDU, its main objective consists in 
reducing car traffic by setting several measures such as developing public transport, promote active 
modes of transport (walking and cycling), and inscribe in the urban planning documents the necessity 
to organize urban development around existing or planned public transport infrastructures [9]. 
With the recent legislations passed in the 2000’s, the topics covered by PDU have been extended in 
order to integer automatically in all PDU’s specific measures such as the improvement of road safety, 
the protection of the environment and the access to transport infrastructures for people with disabilities 
[8]. 
The area covered by the PDU is under the jurisdiction of the Urban Transport Authority (Autorité 
Organisatrice des Transport Urbains – AOTU) and is called the Urban Transport Area (Périmètre de 
transports Urbains – PTU), which generally involves more than one single municipality. 
The Urban Mobility Plan is developed having recourse to a partnership-based process, involving several 
stakeholders, from public and institutional authorities to key players in civil society for both its 
production and its evaluation. Indeed, concerning the public authorities, the law stipulates the 
obligation to involve transport authorities and road network authorities at government, regional and 
departmental level.   Moreover, the municipalities located in the PTU need to be consulted on the PDU 
draft before any public consultation. Furthermore, at their request, the “representatives of transport 
occupations and users as well as associations for people with disabilities, chamber of commerce, and 
accredited environmental protections associations” must also be consulted on the PDU draft by the 
AOTU [8]. And finally, it is required to involve citizens in the frame of the public consultation process 
once the PDU draft is produced [8]. 
Concretely the PDU takes the form of a long document (300 pages for the PDU Ile-de-France 2010-2020), 
which includes all of the major objectives of the plan, specify the schedule for actions registered in the 
PDU and details the modalities about implementation, funding,  monitoring and evaluation of the actions. 
The law gives operational dimensions to the document in order to ensure that the actions inscribed to 
the PDU are well implemented, monitored by the AOTU. 
Additionally, the law makes it obligatory to evaluate the PDU every five years at least, but there is no 
specification regarding the evaluation methodology [8].

4.3.2. PDUIF 2010-2020 
4.3.2.1. 
Objective: a sustainable balance through an ambitious mobility plan 
The PDU of Ile-de-France (PDUIF), which is currently in effect, has been prepared by the Transport Union 
of Ile-de-France (STIF), as part of a broad consultation that involved all stakeholders implicated in 
mobility in the region [6]. STIF has the role of Urban Transport Authority (AOTU) in Ile-de-France. 
After a public inquiry and favorable opinion from the French Government, the PDUIF 2010-2020 was 
approved by the Ile-de-France Regional Council on the 19th of June 2014 [1]. 
According to the terms used by STIF [6], the new PDU aims to establish "a sustainable balance between 
the mobility needs, environmental and health protection, the preservation of life quality, taking into 
account the financial constraints ". Thus, according to the organization that manages it, the PDUIF is 
defined as an “action initiating tool for sustainable development”. Indeed, the definition of the 
sustainable development concept - whose goal is the satisfaction of human wellbeing (economically and 
socially), and the responsible use of natural resources, all while maintaining an harmony with a healthy 
planet over the long term ([5], [11]) – corresponds point by point to PDUIF’s objectives. 
The PDUIF sets the framework of mobility policy for the entire region Ile-de-France. The objectives and 
actions to be implemented by 2020 in terms of displacements of goods and people are defined by the 
plan. 
The implementation of the PDUIF actions relies on many actors, particularly public authorities such as 
departmental Councils, inter-communalities and municipalities. This is especially complex in Ile-de-
France, given the exceptional nature of the region compared to the rest of French territory; there are in 
fact 12 million people, 1280 municipalities and 8 departments in the region. This is, among other things, 
justifying that the implementation of PDUIF is led and coordinated by STIF - a competent, old and 
important professional structure - which also takes care of the evaluation of the plan. 
Furthermore, STIF and the Regional Council of Ile-de-France accompany financially and technically local 
authorities regarding all topics of PDUIF. 
4.3.2.2. 
Environment, health and safety as priority targets of PDUIF 
The PDUIF 2010-2020 faces a rather negative regional context in terms of air quality, noise pollution 
related to transportation and road safety ([6], [18], [19]). It aims to provide elements to resolve these 
problems. 
==PAGE:33==
32 
 
As mentioned previously, the air quality is problematical in Ile-de-France, with more than 2 million 
people affected by pollution levels exceeding regulatory thresholds [19]. The pollutants whose 
emissions levels are regularly above the limits in the region are particles (PM2.5 and PM10), nitrogen 
oxide, ozone and benzene. It turns out that road traffic contributes significantly to emissions of these 
pollutants, and in particular it is responsible for nearly a third of the direct emissions of greenhouse gases 
in the region [6]. 
Noise pollution is also critical in the area. In the densest part of the region, more than 1,6 million of 
people live in buildings exposed to road noise levels above the allowed limits during days ; and still 
800 000 inhabitants are concerned by these exceeding by night [6]. 
Regarding road safety, while the first PDUIF was ongoing, between 2000 and 2010, mortality due to road 
accidents decreased by 53 %, from more than 720 persons killed in 2000 to 356 in 2010 [25]. Since 2010, 
mortality is still decreasing but more slowly, 288 persons killed in 2013, a record year [26]. However, 
the decreasing observed concerns especially automobilists, but more vulnerable road users such as 
pedestrians, cyclists and motorbikes users are still the most exposed ones [6]. Thus, year 2014 had 
known a new increase of road mortality with a total of 314 persons killed in Ile-de-France this year; 
among these persons, there are 82 pedestrians, 16 cyclists and 95 motorbikes users, which corresponds 
to more than 60% of the total [26].

4.4. Grand Paris Express 
4.4.1. Context: strengthen transport supply to maintain regional dynamism 
The PDUIF serves as a guideline and provides frameworks to several urban mobility projects in Ile-de-
France region, from small ones to major ones. Among these projects, the PDUIF is used to orientate the 
works performed in the frame of the biggest French urban mobility project currently under 
development: the Grand Paris Express (GPE) [1].  
Over the past 20 years, in a broad context of reflection involving public actors, companies and 
associations, the observation was made that Ile de France faced a decline in its economic attractiveness, 
characterized by its competitiveness loss relative to other global cities, and slower economic growth [4]. 
These reflections led to address the adaptability of transport to recent urban developments in the 
region. Indeed, a global transport survey conducted in 2010 observes that 70% of trips in the region are 
now out of the city of Paris proper, which is a 50% increase in these flows by comparison to 1976 [39]. 
However, the construction of the RER (Regional Express Network - the rail transport system in the Paris 
suburbs) dates back to the mid-1970.  Then, strengthen the supply of transport from suburb to suburb 
appeared as a necessity. 
It is in this context that emerged several projects for new suburban subway lines (Orbital, Arc Express 
...) ultimately leading to the adoption by decree in August 2011 of a major project synthesizing the 
various projects: the Grand Paris Express [4]. 
4.4.2. The project outlines 
The Grand Paris Express project consists in the creation of four new metro lines in the Paris suburbs and 
the extension of two existing metro lines. This represents the realization of 200 km of new lines and the 
construction of 68 new stations ([3], [41]), whose 55 stations will be interconnected to the rest of the 
network [41]. Design and construction of the 4 new metro lines represents a total cost of 22.6 billion 
Euros ([3], [4]). When adding the costs of the existing lines extensions and renovations (partially covered 
in the frame of GPE project), the total cost is over 25 billion euros [3]. Then, by adding operation costs, 
maintenance and equipment renewal (estimated over a period of 50 years), the total amounts of the 
project is about 32 billion Euros. The funding of this project is mainly public (tax revenues, state grants, 
and local government contributions). The project is coordinated by a public company set up exclusively 
for this purpose: the Society of Grand Paris (SGP).  In connection with STIF, RATP and SNCF, the Society 
will manage the exploitation of the future network ([3], [4]). 
4.4.3. The future GPE network 
The GPE project involves several phases. The construction of the southern part of the future line 15 
started in 2015 and all lines should be delivered by 2030. The Paris bid for the 2024 Olympics Games 
will accelerate the completion of work to be able to provide transport infrastructure up to the event. 
The Erreur ! Source du renvoi introuvable. shows the layout of future lines of GPE; with, on the right, an 
illustrated calendar of the different project phases [41]. 
4.4.4. Creating jobs and wealth: main economic impact 
With a cost of 50 years over 30 billion Euros, does the GPE project worth such an investment? During 
the same 50-year period, the GPE will have annual 500 million Euros of tax revenue allocated by 
Parliament, more than 25 billion of tax revenue in total [3]. Added to this, there are other income 
(complex financial logistic), which allow a total between 40 and 80 billion euros of revenues over a 
period of 50 years according to SGP [3]. Moreover, the Society of Grand Paris (SGP) announces a project 
return rate around 7 to 8%, and estimates that the additional regional GDP generated by the project will 
be between 100 and 200 extra billion Euros (for a current GDP of € 600 billion) [3]. 
These data need to be considered with caution since they are published by an organization directly 
involved in the project success and because few independent studies are exploitable for comparison. 
==PAGE:38==
37 
 
Besides, the precise study of this aspect is not the subject of this thesis. However, it seems interesting 
to highlight those elements that reflect the integration of large-scale economic impacts when dealing 
with a major transportation project. 
Regarding employment, the Grand Paris Express project will generate between 10 000 and 15 000 direct 
jobs per year during construction, between 2015 and 2030 according to SGP estimations [3].  
In addition, Society of Grand Paris has made several projections about the creation of project-related 
employment: the number of jobs created directly or indirectly thanks to the project in 2030 would be 0 
in a low estimation, 115 000 in central hypothesis and 315 000 in top hypothesis ([3], [4]).  
Moreover, an article by Emile QUINET [4] provides an estimation of the number of jobs on the basis of 
economic studies previously conducted on similar projects [41]. Thus, according to this independent 
estimation, the 50% increase in the length of the public transport network (the actual metro network has 
a total length of about 200kms as well [41]) as part of GPE project should lead to an additional of around 
250 000 jobs through gains in terms of cost and time of transportation. This result is in the upper range 
of the study conducted by the SGP, which strengthens it. 
4.4.5. Improving mobility to reduce urban inequalities: one of the GPE’s social impacts 
The disparities between Paris proper and its outer suburbs are large, and even among suburbs areas 
there are great social inequalities, between east and west for example. Because they are poorly served 
by transport today, some area’s inhabitants are excluded from the labor market and blocked in 
unemployment situation. In the frame of the GPE project, the districts where the tax revenues are the 
lowest will benefit the most from GPE benefits in terms of travel time reduction. By opening up these 
urban areas through better access to the transport network, it is expected that they will have their 
attractiveness increased. [3] 
Indeed, the time savings thanks to GPE will for example reduce from 72 minutes to 27 minutes travel 
time to get from Clichy-Montfermeil to Roissy. Therefore, by reducing travel time, the new metro lines 
will in a sense “reduce distances” which is expected to have several interconnected positive aspects 
such as increased life quality (social), better access to leisure and culture (social), more productivity 
(economy), better attractiveness (economy), or car use reduction (environment) ([3], [4]). 
The time savings enabled by the GPE project and their sustainable impacts from social, economic and 
environmental perspectives will be described and discussed in more detail in Chapter 6 of this thesis. 
4.4.6. An eco-friendly project: environmental impact of GPE 
According to data provided by SGP [41], supported by statistics from INSEE and STIF, the Grand Paris 
Express will allow 90% of the inhabitants of Ile-de-France to live within 2kms of a station. By deploying 
new public transportation infrastructure in the whole metropolitan area, the future metro will 
encourage the change from car to public transport and will contribute - through 68 new stations – to 
the development of walking and cycling. Once carried out this project will then help to limit emissions 
of greenhouse gases and air pollution. 
Thus, long-term environmental impact of this project is expected to be positive but it is also important 
to consider the environmental impact of each project phase, including of course construction. The 
Society of Grand Paris took several environmental commitments within the framework of the 
construction, operation and management of the Grand Paris Express. 
The decisions taken by SGP in term of environmental preoccupation for the realization of this project, 
particularly the ones concerning the construction phase will be described and discussed more precisely 
in Chapters 5 and 6 of this thesis.

5. Study case evaluation 
5.1. Introduction to the evaluation 
As part of this master thesis, the evaluation of PDUIF and the Grand Paris Express (GPE) through INVEST 
is not a goal in itself but a means used to demonstrate the feasibility of transport infrastructures 
simultaneously integrating the three major issues of sustainable development. Evaluation is a “tool” to 
support and give credit to this thesis main question’s answer: how to adapt infrastructures of transport 
social, economic and environmental challenges?  
Moreover, this evaluation allows to address the complex issue of sustainability assessment of urban 
transport projects. Indeed, as detailed in previous sections, such an evaluation is crucial but difficult in 
practice, particularly because of the significant degree of subjectivity that can intervene in the 
assessment in proportion as it covers more aspects of the project. Thus, the evaluation which will be 
performed in this section will also be an occasion to experiment a broadly used assessment tool - INVEST 
– and to analyze to which extend it proves to be a good asset when working on major projects like PDUIF 
or GPE.  
As described previously, INVEST tool consists of four modules: System Planning for Regions, for States, 
Project Development, and Operation & Maintenance.  
Due to the particular context in which that assessment is part - means to demonstrate a theory and case 
study as “cogitation material” for the discussion about sustainability assessment of transportation 
systems – and considering the extent of the 2 projects - PDUIF and GPE - only 2 INVEST modules will be 
used: System Planning for Region and Project Development. Indeed, these are the two modules which 
are the most suitable for these two interconnected case studies. PDUIF is a document defining a legal 
framework for planning and manage transport systems at regional level, and the GPE is a large project 
under development that takes place in this same region and is therefore inscribed within the legal 
framework defined by the PDUIF. 
Furthermore, since the objective is not to credit a grade but to reflect on what does the use of INVEST 
bring as food for thought, only a dozen of criteria will be used and distributed over these two study cases. 
In parallel, only a few aspects of each of these two projects will be analyzed. This choice will leave enough 
room for a comprehensive analysis of the evaluation performed. 
5.2. Selection of focus aspects for analysis 
5.2.1. PDUIF 2010-2020 
When looking to the 34 actions declined in 9 challenges characterizing PDUIF 2010-2020 – once the 
challenge number 7 set apart due to its focus on freight and goods transportation (sector excluded from 
the evaluation) – three main axes can be identified ([6], and chapter 4.3.2). 
The first axis is about the evolution of the entire Ile-de-France transport system towards a better network 
from sustainable development perspective. This axis focuses primarily on the transport modes evolution. 
Thus, part of the actions to be implemented in the frame of challenges 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 consist in 
providing the means to achieve the objectives of PDUIF in terms of new repartition of transport modes 
and air quality improvement. Globally, it deals with reducing car use by encouraging a shift towards 
public transport and active modes.  
The second axis is about transforming and improving the existing transport system in Ile-de-France. This 
axis is primarily focused on improving transport infrastructure. Therefore, part of the actions to be 
implemented in the frame of challenges 2, 3, 4, and 5 are in line with the improvement and 
modernization of existing infrastructure. It consists in improving road safety, ensure accessibility of 
==PAGE:41==
40 
 
transport for all, upgrade the stations and ensure the implementation of high-quality transport 
infrastructure as reliable alternatives to the car. 
The third axis concerns the form to be taken by the governance and management of transport projects 
in the region. Expressed through the PDUIF challenges 8 and 9, this axis is more conceptual, it is about 
defining the decision making policy, and ensuring the involvement of all stakeholders, including - and 
above all – the future users. Like the previous two axes, this one is crucial because it ensures that the 
transport projects are established taking into account qualitative and not just quantitative criteria. 
Indeed, the two first axes help to provide transport projects with clear and quantified objectives in terms 
of accessibility for the disabled people, car use reduction, etc.; while the third axis allows to include more 
qualitative criteria in the pre-project phase throughout the importance given to the consideration of 
each party’s opinion. 
Each of these three axes is very interesting and they complete each other. However, for the PDUIF 
evaluation in the context of this master thesis, the analysis will focus on the first two axes. Indeed, this 
choice seems more suitable as the third axis comprises mainly qualitative information in the 
documentation of PDUIF, which may lead to subjectivity when assessing it. On the other hand, the first 
two axes are accompanied by clears data and quantified information, which makes them more suited 
to an accurate assessment. Moreover, this choice is motivated by the fact that public involvement in the 
decision making process - which is included in the PDUIF third axis - is experienced in a concrete and 
interesting way in the GPE project, and will be further analyzed as part of the GPE evaluation. 
Thus, the PDUIF aspects which will be analyzed in the INVEST evaluation are the ones addressed within 
the challenges 1 to 6:  Build a city more oriented towards public transport, walking and cycling; Make 
public transport more attractive; Promote active modes such as walking and cycling; Act on the use of 
individual motorized modes; and Ensure accessibility for all. 
5.2.2. Grand Paris Express  
Regarding the assessment of the Grand Paris Express (GPE) project, given the scale of the project, it is 
not possible here to make a comprehensive evaluation. Then, as mentioned previously, three particular 
facets of the project are selected to be evaluated and analyzed via INVEST. 
 
The first analyzed aspect concerns the involvement of all stakeholders, especially the public, during the 
Grand Paris Express conception phase. Indeed, Île-de-France inhabitants, who are the main future users 
of the new subway system, have been regularly involved in the project design through public surveys 
and regular public citizens meetings. This public involvement seems interesting to analyze because the 
Society of Grand Paris (SGP) insisted a lot on transparency of the project and has therefore brought a 
lot of efforts in the implementation of a system enabling the expression of future users and 
consideration of their expectations and demands. 
Moreover, this emphasis on the involvement of all stakeholders from the earliest phases of project fits 
perfectly into an integrated sustainable approach. Inform the public, involve it in decision-making 
process, listen to it and take into account its comments and questions are undeniably beneficial from a 
social point of view. Furthermore, this also ensures that the project does consider user expectations, 
which allows avoiding eventual modifying work once the project is completed but imperfectly if public 
expectations had not been integrated. Therefore, it gives to public involvement an economic dimension, 
because modifying works executed during operation phase may be particularly complex and costly in 
the metro sector. In addition, public involvement is accompanied by intensive communication 
campaigns towards the population, about the future metro network and its assets. Thus, users are well 
prepared for the arrival of the new metro which is susceptible to accelerate its future appropriation by 
the public and may then lead to accelerate the deferral process from cars to public transport, which 
represents an environmental asset. 
==PAGE:42==
41 
 
The second analyzed aspect relates to the consideration of environmental issues in the GPE project. 
Indeed, environment is central in the project preoccupations, either in the design phase, construction 
phase, operation phase and maintenance. According to the SGP President, Philippe YVIN, GPE must 
"help to develop a metropolitan area where urban spaces and infrastructures are more eco-friendly" 
[41]. As part of this analysis, attention will be particularly focused on the consideration of environmental 
issues in the construction and project development phases.  
By promoting a shift towards less polluting transport modes, Grand Paris Express will have a long-term 
positive environmental impact. However, immediately, resources should be deployed to minimize 
environmental annoyance of such a major project. In 2010, the Strategic Environmental Assessment of 
the Grand Paris Express was conducted in an area covering a zone of 3km around the future railway 
path (i.e. a total surface of 600 km²), where current regulations did not require more than a zone of 500 
meters around the path ([3], [41]). Society of Grand Paris aims to achieve exhaustiveness regarding 
environmental issues considered: noise, natural hazards, nature issues, agricultural issues, biodiversity, 
landscapes, air quality, circular economy or energy efficiency. This led the SGP to take "seven major 
environmental commitments", a kind of framework for the project’s environmental policy. These

- 
Aim for energy sobriety 
- 
Insert the infrastructures in their environmental, architectural and landscaping 
- 
Anticipate natural hazards 
- 
Promote soft mobility 
- 
Conduct eco-friendly construction sites 
-

It is also interesting to note that these engagements correlate with some of the 9 PDUIF’s challenges, 
which governs this project because it takes place in the same region. Thus, this GPE analysis gives an 
example of how projects in Ile-de-France may integer PDUIF’s objectives concretely. 
The third aspect analysed has already been mentioned previously: it is an aspect which occurs after 
project realisation and it concerns travel time reduction. As stated in previous sections, situation in Ile 
de France regarding access to efficient transport infrastructures is preoccupying, and is one of the 
justification of GPE project. For that reason, the reduction of time spent in transport, which will be one 
of the major effects of the project once its realisation completed, is at the core of the GPE project. The 
objective of this part from the GPE assessment and analysis will consist in demonstrating that travel time 
reduction through modernization of the transportation system in urban area has multiple and 
interconnected positive impacts from economic, social and environmental perspectives. This third aspect 
is not as adapted as the two previous ones to INVEST criteria, but it is still interesting to analyse and 
discuss. Due to that, the analysis of this aspect will be mostly qualitative and based on literature instead 
of being based on INVEST grading system. Furthermore, the fact that INVEST does not provide criteria 
to assess such issue will serve as cogitation guideline for the part dedicated to the “evaluation of the 
evaluation”. 
5.3. Methodology 
5.3.1. Generalities 
INVEST is - as described in the previous chapter - an American assessment tool initially dedicated to 
evaluate the sustainability of highways projects. So, it is normal that the questions asked in the criteria 
are not always adapted to the study case context: two French projects that do not involve highways. Thus, 
changes will be made to the criteria in order to apply them to the two case study project without having 
a biased evaluation. Some small changes will be made for each selected criterion during the evaluation 
==PAGE:43==
42 
 
process. Other changes, more global ones which concern all the criteria, are made before starting the 
evaluation and introduced in this section. 
The first of these ante-evaluation INVEST adaptations consists in “translating” the term “agency” to 
French context. Indeed, French and American organizations differ on this point, and the term “agency” 
in the INVEST’s questions will be replaced by STIF (Syndicat des Transports d’Ile-de-France) for the PDUIF 
evaluation, while it will be replaced by SGP (Society of Grand Paris) for GPE evaluation. In fact, these two 
organizations are in charge of managing, piloting and organizing every keys aspects of the two evaluated 
projects, which make them equivalent to an American agency in charge of managing a highway project.   
The second ante-evaluation adaptation that should be implemented is to declare that the references to 
US laws texts, codes or standards from American States will be replaced in this evaluation by their French 
equivalent when it is possible. For instance the criterion SPR-17 is about the National Environmental 
Policy Act (NEPA), a 1070’s US law; if this criterion was used, the NPA would be replaced by an equivalent 
French law such as the LAURE law mentioned above. In this case, this criterion will not be used because 
PDUIF, by definition, is automatically linked with the French equivalent of NEPA (LAURE law), same for 
GPE, as the project is inscribed in the PDUIF framework and has government as stakeholder. 
Another example of criterion context that should be adapted lies in the fact the some criteria refer to 
the specific American culture and landscapes. For instance, in the criteria PD 15 and PD 16, it deals with 
proximity of the construction area with America’s Byways, State Scenic Byway or Indian Tribe Scenic 
Byway. These criteria are not used for the case study evaluation because they do not match the aspects 
to which attention is given; however, if they had been used, the American references would have been 
replaced by equivalent from Parisian culture and characteristics, being admitted that it is unlikely to find 
Native American sites in the Paris area. 
Thereafter, other minor modifications and arrangements may be applied to criteria selected during the 
evaluation process, in order to be as adapted as possible with the case study assessed. The eventual 
changes in the criteria questions will be notified and explained prior to apply the criterion modified to 
the case study. Moreover, every modification or arrangement in the criterion formulation will be made 
so that the criterion should lose nothing of its original essence, which means that the criterion 
“principle” as defined by INVEST documentation should not be denatured.

These comparison tables of INVEST criteria give an interesting overview of the modules System Planning 
for Region (SPR) and Project Development (PD).  
Consequently, it is possible to observe that each of the two modules is quite complete and addresses 
several times each of the three basic themes that characterize sustainable development. Indeed, in the 
SPR module, the Social topic is addressed in 13 of the 17 criteria, the Economy topic is addressed in 14 
of these criteria, and the Environment theme is discussed 12 times. In the PD module, Social topic is 
addressed   in 19 of the 33 criteria, Economy is addressed   in 21 criteria, while Environment is addressed 
in 30 over 33 criteria.  In the SPR module, each topic is addressed almost equally, while the PD module is 
characterized by a predominance of Environmental issues.  This echoes the comments of the article "A 
global framework for sustainable roadway rating sysem" [5], written by Veeravigrom, Muench and 
Kosonen, who - after comparing the 11 main sustainability rating systems – stressed out that 
"Environment dimension is Largest by the number of topics” [5]. The fact that this predominance is 
present in the PD module may be explained by the fact that environmental issues during the phase of 
project development are very diverse, complex and cover several different areas (rain water, air quality, 
noise pollution, waste treatment, etc.) which requires many criteria. Similarly, if the importance of the 
three sustainability dimensions in the PD module is compared from the perspective of the maximum 
number of points allocated, it can be observed that Social dimension is addressed in criteria representing 
a maximum number of 86 points over 193 for the whole module, while Economic dimension represents 
a maximum number of 129/193 points and Environmental dimension a maximum number of 177/193; 
which confirms the predominance of Environment in this module. 
Regarding the adaptability of these criteria to the two French study cases, it can firstly be observed that 
it is a priori possible to apply these modules to both cases. Indeed, there are a total of 35 of 49 criteria 
that are applicable to at least one of the two cases.  
Moreover, it can be noted that the SPR module fits similarly to the two cases (6 "YES" and 7 "With 
arrangements" for PDUIF, 4 "YES" and 7 "With arrangements" for GPE), while PD module applies mainly 
to GPE project (9 "YES" and 11 "With arrangements" for GPE against 2 "YES" and 2 " With arrangements" 
for PDUIF). This seems logical since the PDUIF is a “global planning tool for mobility” [8] which – in this 
case – is made at regional level, which corresponds perfectly to the area covered by the module entitled 
System Planning for Regions. On the other hand, PDUIF has only a plan level, it defines projects 
frameworks but it is not a project in itself, which explains why the Project Development module is not 
adapted to it.  By contrast, Grand Paris Express is a major project managed by a company - SGP - which 
cares as much as the planning of the whole new transportation system represented by the GPE project, 
as well as the development of the project itself. Thereby, GPE project includes both a plan and a project 
level, which is why both modules are applicable to it.

6.1.2. PDUIF: A Mobility Plan globally on line with INVEST sustainable best practices 
6.1.2.1. 
Accessibility addressed ambitiously, affordability layaway 
The first PDUIF aspect assessed deals with how PDUIF takes in consideration the topics of accessibility 
to the transportation system and its affordability. As highlighted by INVEST criterion SPR-05, accessibility 
is a key point of PDUIF. It is not only the subject of one of the 9 PDUIF’s challenges [6], but also the 
theme fully developed in one of the three official documents constituting original PDUIF 2010-2020: the 
Appendix Accessibility, which is – as its name suggests – entirely devoted to this topic. The existence of 
that specific annex results from French law of 2005, which introduced the general principle of accessibility 
to all, and which states that this principle must be imperatively taken into account throughout the travel 
chain [48]. This document reminds the legal framework regarding accessibility to all users, makes an 
inventory of the current situation and lists the mapping of accessibility breaks to fix as a priority, it details 
solutions announced pursuant to PDUIF, and it specifies the technical conditions to implement 
accessibility solutions in public transports. Moreover, PDUIF challenge n°6, entitled "Ensure accessibility 
for all", is accompanied by key measures on this point: erase 80% of blocking situations for disabled 
people mobility on all urban roads (improve accessibility at bus stops, sidewalks improved, increase the 
number of parking places for disabled, etc.), bring accessibility to 258 stations capturing 90% of suburbs 
train traffic (lights, gates, waiting areas adapted), speed up the replacement of buses and acquisition of 
trams in line with new standards in order to overcome the fact that accessibility issues to some metro 
stations will be long to solve. 
However, if the physical and geographical accessibility is discussed in detail - with examples of concrete 
measures, quantitative and qualitative data enriched by the involvement of many stakeholders and 
details about the evaluation process and expected outcomes – affordability on the other hand is not 
clearly mentioned in the documents reviewed and is not subject to any challenge or any dedicated 
flagship action. But affordability is essential. Indeed, the most advanced transportation system will not 
engender the positive socio-economic and environmental impacts allowed by quality infrastructures if 
access is actually reduced because of prohibitive tariffs. According to the latest European comparison 
==PAGE:54==
53 
 
conducted by the Public Transport Union in 2012, the Parisian metropolitan area is ninth among 
European capitals regarding the price of a single ticket (high to low), with an average path 1.7 € (same 
price in 2016) against more than 5 € in London [53]. When making the relation between transport price 
and local living standards, an Australian study observed that to pay for a subway ticket, a Parisian with 
an average wage must work 22 minutes against 42 minutes in London and 38 in Berlin [52]. Thus, price 
situation is globally satisfying but there is room for improvements. As the major operator of 
transportation in Île-de-France, STIF has authority on this topic and should use PDUIF to set clear, 
advantageous and realistic objectives about how to increase affordability to transportation system, and 
how to use captivating pricing policy for public transport as a mean to promote sustainable mobility and 
raise awareness about sustainable issues. An interesting solution might be to establish a unique and 
attractive subscription system combining public transport throughout the whole region with 
subscriptions to electric vehicles sharing system (Autolib’) and bicycle sharing system (Velib’). Such a 
measure could be included in challenge n°1 “Build a city more oriented towards public transport, walking 
and cycling” as part of PDUIF. 
6.1.2.2. 
Air quality PDU’s objectives and their consequences on PDUIF challenges 
The approach to air quality topic within official documents constituting the PDUIF is singular. Indeed, on 
one hand the topic is discussed at the beginning of the document and frame all the measures put in 
place in the legal framework of the PDU. Thus, it is "to meet the regulations on air quality and national 
commitment to reduce by 20% greenhouse gas emissions by 2020" – commitment made in the frame of 
EU air quality engagements - that "PDUIF aims, in a context of global growth movements estimated at 
7% " to "a 20% growth of public transport", "a 10% growth of active modes", and "a 2% decrease of the 
car's use" [46]. And, then these quantitative major objectives lead to almost all the concrete measures 
and “actions” promoted and established by PDUIF throughout the PDUIF’s challenges, especially 
concerning the five first challenges. From this perspective, it could be said that PDUIF is driven by air 
quality clear, precise and ambitious objectives. However, on the other hand, air quality is surprisingly 
and paradoxically almost absent from the rest of the document. Indeed, “air quality” is written less than 
20 times in the 276 pages document (introduction excluded) and it is always to remind the EU air quality 
objectives. In fact, it is surprising that the relations between the 20% greenhouse gas emissions decrease 
and the different PDUIF actions are not explained and underlined while it seems obvious that they are 
linked together. 
Nevertheless, even if the way in which actions are structured around the air quality objectives is singular, 
it is true that the actions established via PDUIF are going “in the right and sustainable direction” according 
to what reveals application of INVEST criteria. Indeed, INVEST is used here as a catalog of best practices 
and evaluation mainly consists in checking if the best practices are incorporated into the PDUIF or not. 
It turns out that within PDUIF’s challenges 1-5, assessed by criteria 7, 9, 10 and 11 of the SPR module, 
with an average rating of 12.75 / 15, sustainability best practices are well implemented. 
Indeed, the actions to be implemented as written in the PDUIF fit into the framework of a sustainable 
transport system integrating with consistency the three dimensions of sustainability. Thus, it is about to 
build a city more favorable to walking, cycling and public transport while acting on use conditions of 
individual motorized modes to reduce the frequency of their use. Concretely this is reflected for example

6.1.2.3. 
Evaluation of PDUIF results and transparency of their diffusion  
Whatever the module used, INVEST’s criteria pay attention to the implementation of good practice but 
also to how they are monitored and how the effectiveness of measures implemented is assessed. 
Thereby, the agency's ability to “develop performance measures and monitor progress” as well as its 
capacity to “demonstrate sustainable outcomes” are very frequently addressed throughout INVEST 
criteria.  
This is a point on which STIF, which monitors conception and implementation of PDUIF, is very 
punctilious. Indeed, as mentioned earlier, the evaluation of PDU is required after 5 years according to 
the Transport Code. But in Ile-de-France it has been decided to go further and to opt for continuous 
evaluation of actions implementation and their global impacts on mobility and environment. The board 
formed by STIF and local public actors considered that it was "necessary to identify potential difficulties 
in implementation as soon as possible in order to assess the causes and to fix it without waiting several 
years" ([46], [58]). In fact, faced to the difficulties encountered in assessing the first PDU of Ile de France, 
STIF decided in 2007 to create a partnership and multimodal mobility observatory across the entire 
region called OMNIL (Observatory of Mobility in Ile de France). Then, OMNIL conduct the evaluation of 
the whole PDUIF process [58]. Concretely, OMNIL proposes a series of measures based on 33 
implementation indicators covering all the PDUIF’s actions, 10 indicators about the achievement of 
environmental and mobility objectives, and 4 indicators about the socio-economic and demographic 
context [46]. These indicators are listed on appendix C. 
Moreover, PDU evaluations have to be published publically and STIF fulfills this point, which corresponds 
to the transparency when demonstrating sustainable outcomes as promoted by INVEST. Indeed, OMNIL 
publishes PDUIF evaluations results on its website since the first September of 2015 [58]. For example, 
a document published on November 2015 in the frame of the continuous evaluation of PDUIF presents 
the provisory results of the policy about parking pricing and regulations as recommended by PDUIF. The 
evaluation report points out that two-thirds of the municipalities affected by PDUIF recommendations 
have already introduced new paid parking zones, which goes in the right direction. However the report 
also alerts on the fact that such a policy is not always generalized on all parking places in these same 
municipalities and is not always accompanied by appropriate measures regarding the shift towards 
other modes of transport. For this reason, OMNIL calls for strengthening the use of parking fee while 
intensifying the development of the public transport supply [59]. Such evaluation report conclusions 
highlight the wish from STIF for transparency and objectivity regarding PDUIF evaluation. 
Additionally, it is interesting to note that AIRPARIF (French organization approved by the Ministry of 
Environment for monitoring air quality in the region Île-de-France) has accompanied the STIF in the 
PDUIF development and has made a PDU ex -nte evaluation in 2011 with a focus on the impacts on air 
quality and emissions of greenhouse gases. This evaluation was performed for the account of STIF, and 
was taken into account before the PDUIF publication. On the conclusion of this evaluation, AIRPARIF 
estimated that a 17% reduction of greenhouse gases can be expected if the PDUIF actions are "globally" 
implemented. [49]

6.1.3.1. 
Public involvement: a key commitment from Society of Grand Paris 
The first aspect of the Grand Paris Express (GPE) project analyzed as part of this work is the public 
involvement in the design phase. Indeed, from the beginnings of the project, the Society of Grand Paris, 
in charge of project piloting, emphasized on public involvement throughout the project, with particular 
focus on the design phase. This desire for public involvement is even the origin of a kind of slogan from 
the SGP’s CEO Philippe YVIN: "The conversation developed with the Parisians is more that our 
trademark, it is our requirement." [3]. 
Concretely this public involvement is mainly reflected by an emphasis on intensive communication 
claiming to be transparent, listening public opinions and willingness to consider these opinions. The SGP 
began in 2010 an intensive public consultation about the Grand Paris Express [3]. The consultation takes 
place at several levels, it initially focused on the entire project, targeted consultations are also 
implemented for each of the four future new lines, and public consultations are also organized about 
future stations. In practice, the GSP is implementing a series of devices able to reach the greatest 
number of people possible: public meetings, meetings with economic actors and associations, creation 
of a section for collecting the opinions and issues on the internet, exhibitions, distribution of leaflets, 
major efforts on PR or advertising campaign [51]. 
Each section of the future metro lines is concerned by a major public inquiry. Each public inquiry was 
preceded by reinforced consultation period. Indeed, SGP has decided to organize a phase of discussions 
with the public in order to better prepare the public inquiry and inform as widely as possible about the 
project. These consultations were held under the aegis of a guarantor appointed by the National 
Commission for Public Debate (CNDP). They are now completed and have given way to full public inquiry 
which will be pursued during the works (it will then serve to inform and listen the public), and will also 
continue until the delivery of work [60]. The figure below illustrates how this public inquiry process work

6.1.3.2. 
Environmental commitments regarding infrastructures 
The second GPE aspect which is analyzed in the context of this master thesis is the consideration of 
socio-economic and environmental issues in the context of the construction of infrastructure for future 
metro lines (mostly station and tunnels). Given the substantial size of the project and its many aspects, 
only a few specific topics are examined, all using INVEST criteria as a reference guide of sustainable 
practices to implement on these topics. 
As underlined by criterion PD.07 (Habitat Restoration), SGP is committed to prevent, reduce or 
compensate environmental impacts linked to GPE infrastructures ([3], [41]). Thus, for example, such a 
commitment is traduced by the deployment of measures to reduce vibrations perception such as the 
choice of a deep underground metro (up to 50 meters underground) to naturally reduce train vibrations 
transmission; and also the installation of an anti-vibration sole between the floor and the rail to prevent 
the spread of these vibrations. Moreover, although environmental issues are moderated because of the 
underground type and the urban context of the project, the SGP commits itself to implement 
compensatory measures when a negative environmental impact is inevitable. For instance, in the case of 
line 15, impacts related to natural environment will be compensated by the restoration of broader 
ecological spaces in protected areas of the region. [41] It should be noted that for this criterion (PD-07), 
the fact that GPE gets only a portion of points is not due to problems in SGP approach but because of 
the incapacity to answer one of the criterion’s question due to lack of information. 
As highlighted in the criterion PD-17 (Energy Efficiency), SGP seeks to optimize the energy performance 
of the network and aims particularly to achieve energy sobriety regarding electricity. Indeed, electricity 
is the first energy expenditure of the network, whether for train traction power (600 GWh/year), stations 
power (350 GWh/year) or maintenance sites power (60 GWh/year). Thus, in terms of lighting, the SGP 
will firstly focus on natural light through large windows, atrium and skylight in its stations and 
maintenance sites, and also systematize the use of LED for artificial light coupled with devices allowing 
“intelligent” lighting management [41]. Furthermore, heat from the equipment rooms and data centers 
will be recovered and reused in dual-flow air units, thereby directly reducing the consumption of energy 
for heating, ventilation and air conditioning. [41] Despite these solutions, GPE cannot earn all the points 
in INVEST criterion because the metro is still super energy-consuming. A solution could consist in 
adapting INVEST standards to railway sector on this topic as it was initially designed to highways 
infrastructures which are less energy-consuming. 
GPE obtains 100% of the available credits for criterion PD-27 (Construction Noise Mitigation). Indeed, 
during construction phase, conscious that tunnel construction may be very prejudicial for construction 
site’s close residents, SGP is very attentive to manage carefully noises, dusts and vibrations during the 
construction phase. In fact, concerning noise, a plan for the traffic of works vehicles, supply and 
evacuation vehicles is established, communicated to the public and updated frequently. Furthermore, 
soundproofing devices are set up, with a particular focus on sites where tunnel boring machines are 
deployed [41]. 
SGP is also engaged in a large circular economy process for construction waste. Indeed, as noted in 
criterion PD-29 (Construction Waste Management), an Excavated Materials Evacuation Plan is 
==PAGE:58==
57 
 
requested from each contractor in order to favor the recovery of waste and excavation waste instead 
of burning or burying them. 
A Management Plan for Environment (PME) was established by the SGP to monitor and check the 
performance of all its environmental commitments during the construction phase. This plan established 
from the preliminary design lists all the arrangements made whether generic or specific, regulatory or 
voluntary, and is declined for each construction site. Updated at least quarterly, it is made in 
consultation with local authorities and regularly communicated to the public working or living near the 
construction zones. 
For some of the INVEST criteria applied to GPE, it can be seen that a large part of unallocated points are 
due to the fact that some information isnot available at this stage of the project, and sometimes some 
aspects may have not yet been taken into account by SGP because the project is still in its early stages 
on certain topics. This is not surprising because Project Development Module (5 of 6 criteria for GPE 
assessment) is more suitable for an evaluation performed at an advanced stage of the design phase and 
by a person involved in the project in order to get all the information available before they are public. 
The overall pack of project development measures described in this section are mainly related to 
environmental dimension of sustainable development but social and economic dimensions are also 
addressed.  Indeed, social dimension is for example directly affected by the consideration of noise 
nuisances and the measures taken to avoid messing up residents’ quality of life either during 
construction or operation phase. Similarly, the fact to develop a metro taking care to compensate for 
ecological damage and to minimize noise during and after construction should have a positive impact 
on local economic dynamism. 
6.1.3.3. 
Travel Time Reduction: How sustainable is the main objective of the project? 
A criticism is rather regularly made about the sustainability rating systems: they measure design and 
construction but do not focus on system performance [5]. INVEST is concerned by this drawback, and 
thereby it is quite difficult to use such a tool to assess the impact of Grand Paris Express on Parisians’ 
quality of life on a long term, focusing particularly on the topic of travel time reduction. Nevertheless, 
this aspect of GPE project is interesting to analyze and will be briefly discussed in this section, even if 
the analyze is not based on INVEST criteria.  
Just like all large-scale projects – even more a fortiori when dealing with projects within dense urban 
area – the Grand Paris Express aims to reduce travel time for its future users. In the case of GPE project, 
which connects the suburbs of the metropolis together and connects them to the center of Paris faster 
than it is actually the case, there are numerous impacts associated with reducing the transport time. By 
reducing the time required to get from one point to another through the deployment of 200kms of new 
Metro lines all around the capital, the GPE will enable to "shorten the distances" for millions of residents 
and for visiting tourists, to bring them closer to their workplace, closer to the leisure and cultural places, 
and closer to major infrastructures (both main airports will be much better connected to the city at the 
end of the project than it is the case actually). This reduction in transport time will a priori impact all 
users, but in the next part of this section, only residents of Ile de France will be considered. 
First, by reducing the travel time, the impact for residents will be firstly social: quotidian users would be 
quicker to get to their jobs, their place of study or place of entertainment, which should offer them 
comfort and induce a more pleasant and peaceful life from the perspective of transport. Moreover, 
reducing the traveling time required to get to work is likely to positively affect the feeling of the workers 
and even increase their productivity. In addition, gains on the working time should also be observed. 
Indeed, at the end of the project, 35 minutes will be needed to get from Champigny Centre (which is a 
very populated area in the Paris suburbs) to La Défense (which is the main Business pole of the region), 
against 60 minutes by public transport now [3]. Thus a person living in Champigny who works at La 
==PAGE:59==
58 
 
Défense will gain 50 minutes of daily transportation. The annual legal duration of working time in France 
being 1 607 hours and the average number of working days being 204 [61], this corresponds to a gain 
of 170 hours per year, more than 10% of the legal duration of working time. This double positive impact 
on the labor sector - potential impact on productivity and impact on the quantity of hours worked - 
should a fortiori have a significant impact on the economy and generate job creation in the region at 
medium and long term. 
Secondly, by reducing transport time and providing better service to some suburbs previously 
underserved by the existing transportation network, the GPE will enable to open up these suburbs. Such 
opening up is susceptible to have significant economic and social benefits. From a social point of view 
first, people previously underserved by transportation will have a more comfortable quality of life 
through greater freedom and flexibility in their travels within the region. From an economic point of 
view, this improved service will firstly allow residents to reach easier the business poles and then allow 
them to more easily find employment in areas previously inaccessible. Moreover, these suburbs 
themselves will be more easily accessible from the outside because better connected. They are 
therefore likely to welcome new businesses and to have a local economy that grows faster and better 
than in the absence of metro, which would again result in the creation of wealth and employment. 
Thirdly, the opening up of the suburbs and easier accessibility the center of Paris is likely to have another 
major socio-economic impact: mitigation of social inequalities. Indeed, the Grand Paris Express project 
will essentially open up the northern, east and southeast suburbs of the region which are also the most 
deprived ones. And easier access to central Paris and the rest of the territory should coincide with an 
easier access to cultural areas (museums, theaters), recreational zones, relaxation areas, and thereby 
promote cultural openness and social integration which is synonymous with positive effects on the local 
economy as well. 
Finally, the creation of a new extended metro system, efficient and designed in partnership with the 
public should lead in the medium and long term to a natural switch from the car to public transport for 
users previously poorly served by public transport. This will assist to achieve the objectives of this PDUIF 
[6], and certainly the upcoming one (2020-2030) in terms of improvement of the air quality, reduction 
of greenhouse gas emissions, and reduction of noise pollution related to cars; thereby a generally 
positive impact on the environment at medium and long term. 
However, a theory mitigates the magnitude of optimistic impacts explained in this section, without 
annihilate them. According to Jean POULIT - CEO of French Geographic Institute and recognized 
specialist in mobility issues - in the case of trips with economic role (from home to work), travel time is 
about 30 minutes and 10% of residents exceed one hour. And his theory, supported by studies on the 
subject, consists to say that this duration remains unchanged. Because of this invariance, applying the 
theory of J.POULIT on GPE project, users will then tend to find a new work further from their current 
workplace or to move to less expensive areas but further from their workplace, thus keeping the same 
travel time. According to this same theory, when a relevant new infrastructure is implemented, the 
average speed of travel increases, but the scope of the displacement increases in the same proportions 
leading to an expansion of available territory and an increase in the numbers of prized destinations 
whether for work or housing, resulting in longer distances traveled but faster leading to an overall travel 
time invariance.  As a result of this theory, it turns out that - quoting J. POULIT - it is "futile to seek to 
reduce travel time" [62]. But on the other hand, increase the territory total area with an invariant 
transport time allows users to access all forms of diversity, diversity of job opportunities and diversity of 
natural areas. Then, the well-connected and expanded territory becomes a place both source of 
economic vitality and environmental wellbeing. This observation confirms again the possibility to 
implement transport infrastructure which are at all levels consistent with the three dimensions of 
sustainable development.

6.2. Evaluation of the evaluation 
6.2.1. Adaptability of INVEST to the study case context 
When focusing on this “evaluation of evaluation”, it seems interesting to discuss the adaptability of 
INVEST to the study case context at three levels: adaptability to French context, adaptability to metro 
project, adaptability to large-scale projects. 
6.2.1.1. 
Adaptability to French context? 
INVEST is a sustainability rating tool conceived by an American administration (FHWA), which provides 
a “set of best practices intended to identify, recognize, and promote above-and-beyond efforts towards 
sustainability in transportation programs and projects”[2]. The American character of the tool is 
omnipresent within the criteria proposed by INVEST. In fact, as specified previously when explaining the 
evaluation methodology (Chapter 5), the tool is largely influenced by US geographic, geopolitical, and 
historic context (Indian Tribes in PD-16, United States National Register of Historic Places in PD-15), as 
well as US laws, standards and regulations (NEPA in PD-17). Furthermore, the categories division itself 
with the concept of states and regions refers to the geopolitical context of the United States.  
Thereby, at first glance, the tool is oriented to a strictly American usage, and difficult to adapt to another 
country. Nevertheless, as it has been done in this master thesis, it is possible - with some efforts of 
reflection ante-evaluation - to adapt a large proportion of criteria to French context, by leaving a very 
small number and keeping the most of the criteria by just "translating" the US regulatory and legal 
references, replacing them with their French equivalent.  Thus, despite a look a priori very American, it 
turns out that INVEST can be adapted to a foreign context, or at least the French context with some 
modifications. But these modifications affect only the method of criteria application and not the principle 
of these criteria. In this way, the assessment remains faithful to INVEST and to the catalog of good 
practices it promotes. 
FHWA could create a light version of INVEST "for foreign countries". Such a version would globally contain 
the same list of criteria with a few exceptions (less a few criteria impossible to adapt). In this version, 
the references to American context would be replaced by references non-dependent of the country. 
For example, in criterion PD-17, instead of referring to the NEPA, the criterion would refer to "legal texts 
about environment in force in the country where the project takes place”. 
6.2.1.2. 
Adaptability to projects with no relation with highways? 
From this study case perspective, INVEST is not only particular because it is American, it has also the 
particularity of having been initially developed to assess highways projects. However, in this master 
thesis, it has been used to assess a French metro project and a French urban mobility plan (which 
includes highways but not only).  
Similarly as the omnipresence of the American character mentioned in the previous section, references 
to the highways are also recurrent within INVEST criteria. Indeed, some criteria are dedicated only to the 
pavement (PD-22, PD-23, PD-24). In addition, the PD-17 criteria about energy efficiency is fully focused 
on the energy costs associated with lighting, which makes sense in the case of a highway project, but 
which makes the criterion more difficult to adapt to a metro project as GPE where energy costs are 
considerable and of multiple natures. 
However, as for the adaptability to foreign context, with some effort of reflection ante-evaluation, it is 
actually possible to adapt the criteria to a metro project or to a Sustainable Urban Mobility Plan by making 
some relevant modifications or changing standards when necessary. 
It is interesting to observe that, comparing PD module and SPR module which have been used in this 
study case, it appears that SPR module required less adaptations than PD module, whether it is about 
country context or about the fact that both PDUIF and GPE are not highways projects.  
==PAGE:61==
60 
 
Moreover, the PD module, whose criteria are more specific and less general than for SPR module, should 
maybe be extended with some new criteria if FHWA wanted to adapt it to different types of projects such 
as metro projects. Indeed, as it has been seen, some sustainable aspects related to highways projects 
are not really relevant for metro project assessment; but similarly some sustainable challenges related 
to metro projects are not really relevant in highway sector and would deserve to be included in an 
INVEST version dedicated to all types of transport infrastructures projects. For example it could be 
interesting to include in such an utopic new version of INVEST some criteria about dust management 
during construction phase, about the choice of adapted tunnel boring machines, about the accessibility 
and affordability to high-quality internet connectivity for transportation system users, about the choice 
of trains (materials used, price, comfort, energy efficiency), etc. More consideration about the positive 
sustainable impacts linked to travel time reduction could also be included. 
6.2.1.3. 
Adaptability to large scale projects? 
In the frame of this research work, INVEST criteria have been used to evaluate two very large scale-
projects. But is this tool adapted and calibrated for such projects? 
The following observations are more about PD module than SPR module. 
Throughout the evaluation performed, it appeared that for some criteria INVEST was not «going far 
enough» as an evaluation tool. Indeed, it seems possible to answer a question with a “YES” for two 
projects, and to earn corresponding points even if one of the two projects is clearly more advanced than 
the other. In the case of Grand Paris Express, it was possible to answer “YES” and obtain the maximum 
number of points for several questions, but at this stage SGP is still conducting studies on some 
evaluated topics and the points were earned on the single basis of SGP engagements: no data were 
required to prove or justify the quality level of these engagements. From this perspective INVEST proves 
to be a good catalog of best actions, but the “awards” obtained cannot be considered as really significant.  
INVEST criteria provide essentially qualitative questions on aspects both quantitative and qualitative. It 
is positive that the projects are assessed on both their qualitative and their quantitative aspects. 
Nevertheless, it would be even better if the questions themselves could be more quantitative and include 
more answers possible, based on data.   In fact, as an example, when INVEST asks if a plan for disabled 
people mobility is included in the planning process (SPR-05), it doesn’t really evaluate the quality of the 
plan: it is possible to answer YES to this question, to earn points and obtain the best award even if the 
plan for disabled people is finally not efficient enough. There is another example in criterion PD-29: the 
evaluated project is provided with a waste management plan, evaluation measures are done and the 
results communicated to public: then all points are earned by the project for this criterion. However, 
there is no quantitative indication about the quality of the waste management plan: what about is 
environmental impact? Is it an innovating plan? Does it worth the investment? Could a better plan have 
been used? Etc. 
A solution could consist in improving INVEST’s questions by adding more sub-categories within the 
answers possible, with a rating scale more based on the efficacy and efficiency of solutions offered by the 
project evaluated. 
6.2.2. Pertinence of the elected methodology 
In order to cover a large scope of complementary but different aspects characterizing transport 
infrastructures projects, and to let space enough for comprehensive analysis and discussion, the choice 
has been made to apply only 11 INVEST’s criteria to the study cases: 5 for PDUIF, 6 for GPE. It has been 
done following several steps: selection of aspects to focus on and analyze, criteria selection, and then 
criteria application. The criteria choice has been motivated by the wish to study several characteristics 
of assessed projects, doubled by the wish to cover the three dimensions of sustainability development. 
Indeed, the objective consisted in use this double evaluation to describe in what extend and how urban 
==PAGE:62==
61 
 
infrastructures of transport may be adapted to sustainability challenges. However, as criteria selection 
has been based on analyzed aspects selection, it has also been based on the capacity to adapt the 
selected criteria, i.e. based on the ability to get data allowing to apply these criteria. Nevertheless, by 
using such a methodology, there is a risk that the criteria selected correspond to the areas where the 
efficiency of assessed projects is quite easy to demonstrate. Therefore, there is a risk that the evaluation 
performed is slightly subjective, as the criteria selected correspond to the questions which are favorable 
for PDUIF and GPE. From this perspective, the evaluation performed cannot be received as 100% 
objective, which means that the awards (achievement levels) are not completely realistic: they cover 
only the analyzed aspects and not the overall projects.  
However, it might not be such a problem when considering this from the master thesis perspective. 
Indeed, as mentioned at the beginning of chapter 5, the evaluation performed here doesn’t aim to give 
an award to PDUIF or GPE, but it is a way to orientate, frame and enhance the discussion about 
adaptability of transport infrastructures to sustainable development. 
6.2.3. Difficulty to get a common framework to assess sustainability 
Evaluate sustainability of transport infrastructure projects is something crucial, particularly in the 
context of urban area; and which requires efficient tools and methods. As it has been seen (Chapter 2), 
there are dozens of tools and methods to achieve such evaluations. However, this plurality and diversity 
of existing tools may lead to some issues: if there are too many tools and methods, then the assessment 
becomes dependent of the tool selected, dependent of the country, dependent of a certain category of 
projects, which makes it difficult to compare. Furthermore, this context-dependence of assessment tool 
methodology leads to more complexity when defining of what should characterize a sustainable urban 
transport project. And such a definition is primordial to progress and reach sustainable transport 
systems. With too many tools, the attempts to evaluate may disperse and become less valuable, less 
legitimate and less relevant. Thus, there is a need to create a kind of global evaluation framework, a sort 
of common tool or general methodology which should be based on universal criteria in order to assess 
the sustainability of a project.  
Nevertheless, as the analysis of the study case underlines, just as the sustainability concept itself which 
is context-dependent, projects as well are context-dependent. Indeed, in order to use INVEST on a 
French case, it was necessary to adapt the questions asked in almost all the criteria selected, conserving 
essentially the criteria principles and the global operation principle of the tool.  Thus, a common 
“universal” sustainability assessment tool should stay adaptable and not too detailed, which could 
paradoxically lead to define a framework relatively “vague”.  
In fact, it seems that the perfect evaluation framework should similarly keep the best of the core from 
each tool detailed in part 2, while being as much as possible detached from references to standards and 
documents proper to each country. It means that the creation of a common assessment framework 
requires not to refer to legal and regulatory systems because these systems are not universal. Another 
solution could consist in working towards the implementation of universal rules, standards and legal 
frameworks regarding the sustainability issues, especially when considering air quality aspects. Indeed, 
air has no boundary, so air quality issues are international and should be treated with international 
standards, codes and regulations instead of focusing on these issues at regional or national level. Such 
an internationalization of air quality rules could be integrated in the objectives set up by the United 
Nations Conference on Climate Change.

Conclusion 
The place increasingly predominant occupied by the theme of sustainable development at major 
international conferences and at political level in a growing number of countries, coupled with the recent 
proliferation of sustainable assessment tools and methodologies, tend to emphasize a common 
awareness about the importance to ensure the development of economic and social well-being in 
harmony with a healthy planet. As mentioned in detail in the first chapter of this report, the transport 
sector represents a particular challenge when it comes to sustainable development. Indeed, its fast 
development accelerated economic and social development but due to significant direct adverse 
environmental effects, it is linked to a series of significant negative impacts on all levels: social, economic 
and environmental. It is therefore necessary to adopt effective and efficient political and technical 
strategies to counter these negative effects and adapt the infrastructures of transport to the challenges 
of sustainable development, and this is what constituted the starting point of this master thesis. 
The research lead to address three major topics: the Sustainable Urban Mobility Plans, with a special 
focus on Urban Mobility Plan of Ile-de-France (PDUIF); a concrete example of major transportation 
project currently under development, the Grand Paris Express; and thirdly it allowed to discuss the issue 
of transport project sustainable assessment, theoretically first and then experimentally through the use 
of INVEST on the study cases, analyzed and discussed.  
The study of PDUIF, which is a concrete example of implementation of EU recommendations regarding 
SUMP, reveals that the overall strategy policies about transportation issues may be consistent with 
sustainable development challenges. Indeed, the application of criteria from INVEST was used to validate 
the sustainability of actions undertaken within PDUIF framework. Furthermore, it also lead to identify 
points that deserve deepening. This is the case of reflection about subscriptions policy, with the 
suggested option to combine in a single subscription access to public transport, access to bicycles and 
vehicles shared self-service, and even potentially inclusion of carpooling modalities in such a concept. 
Such an innovative system would deserve to be addressed in more detail and has several potential 
benefits both social (flexibility, comfort), economic (cost savings for users) and environmental (carry ease 
towards clean transport modes). In addition, it is also interesting to note that significant new measures 
related to PDUIF’s challenge number 5 (about vehicles and air quality) were announced in June 2016: 
implementation by the Region Council of a bonus system to encourage professionals to opt for electric 
vehicles (up to 9 000€ bonus for a truck), and reduction by 20km/h of the speed limit on 4 major roads 
portions in the region (400000 vehicles/day on the roads concerned) from the 4th of July [65]. 
The evaluation of some aspects of the Grand Paris Express, followed by analysis and discussion about the 
project supports the possibility to adapt the infrastructures of transport to the challenges of sustainable 
development. Even if only some specific aspects of the project have been studied and despite the 
uncertainty on some issues related to the fact that the project is still in the study phase, the analysis of 
this concrete transport project example proves to be successful. In fact, whether during ante-project 
phase through the intensive involvement of the public, during construction via the inclusion of 
environmental and social issues beyond the regulations, or at long-term via the main objectives of the 
new metro (travel time reduction, reducing inequality, opening up suburbs), the three dimensions of 
sustainable development are omnipresent within what characterizes the content and the objectives of 
this large-scale project. One of the main challenges of this project could reside in its ability to evolve in 
order to be really well suited to the public demand and urban context at its scheduled end in 2030. Indeed, 
first reflection started in the early 2000’s and the project should be finished by 2030. Nevertheless, 
meanwhile the urban area is changing and is even likely to evolve more quickly and strongly than 
estimated in areas previously unserved and whose connection to efficient public transport has been 
announced as part of the project. 
==PAGE:64==
63 
 
Throughout the experimentation made with INVEST, the work achieved within the framework of this 
master thesis allowed to address the fundamental question of transportation project sustainability 
assessment. Indeed, get efficient evaluation methods is crucial as it enables to guide and support 
strategic, technical and economic decision-making process throughout the project while considering it as 
a whole with its intricacies and its multiple facets. This report highlights the importance of evaluating a 
transport project at several levels.  
Thereby, in the light of topics presented, analyzed and discussed in this master thesis, it appears that 
the evaluation of a project must firstly take into account the time scale of the project. In order to be 
comprehensive and reliable, the assessment should first be carried out at the earliest stages of the 
project, an ex-ante evaluation. Then, the assessment must be continuous during the development of the 
project and subsequently a post achievement review should be conducted to verify the effectiveness of 
the project implementation and achievement of objectives (ex-post evaluation). The completeness of 
the evaluation from a temporal point of view must also be reflected through the evaluated elements: 
all project phases have to be considered – from design phase to operation and maintenance including 
construction – and these phases should be addressed for each evaluation step, whether ex-ante 
evaluation, continuous evaluation or ex-post one. From this perspective, the overall approach towards 
transport sustainable assessment is similar to a Life-Cycle Assessment general approach.  
Moreover, it is also necessary to adopt an evaluative method of project that assesses sustainability by 
considering several size scales, from micro-evaluation to macro-evaluation. Thus, in the case of a metro 
project like the Grand Paris Express, the micro-evaluation must be comprehensive in the sense that 
every detail must analyzed based on sustainable issues, from the braking system of the future metros 
to the types of bulbs that will be used for stations lighting. However, such evaluation doesn’t constitute 
an exhaustive project assessment. The construction of High Environmental Quality stations, and the 
increasing consideration of socio-economic and environmental issues during the construction phase 
contribute to the sustainability of the whole project, but it is not enough, the project should be 
considered as a whole with not only its material characteristics but also its socio-economic and 
environmental role and purpose. Here comes the macro evaluation. In fact, assessment should also 
include project sustainability as a fundamental principle, that is to say, evaluate the project's impacts on 
society, landscape, lifestyles and the general organization of the affected area. 
Therefore, a transportation project must be sustainable at all levels, in all life cycles that compose it, 
whether it is about its infrastructures with the “material” meaning (stations, tunnels, trains, etc.) or 
transport infrastructures meaning overall transportation system. A sustainable transportation project is 
a project whose goals are sustainable and which is made in a sustainable way. 
From this point of view, the analysis performed in this master thesis therefore shows - based on the 
case study of the Paris region, its Urban Mobility Plan and its major metro project (Grand Paris Express) 
- that it is possible to achieve transport infrastructures adapted to sustainable development challenges. 
This must be done by giving great importance to the projects evaluation, by focusing to address with the 
same intensity each of the three dimensions of sustainability, by taking into consideration the 
interconnection between these dimensions on many aspects of transport projects, by considering each 
project phase, and finally by taking into consideration as much the project purpose that the specific 
characteristics of its realization.
