Summary  
This project is addressing the effects of increased fire load from upholstered furniture on fire 
safety in hallways, because the current guidelines in Denmark do not address this issue. The 
effects of increased fire load have been studied through a literature survey and experimental work 
using bomb calorimetry, mass loss cone calorimetry and a large scale study of furniture in the 
Room Corner Test setup. Furthermore, a cross-sectional survey was sent to local authorities to 
assess how objects were assessed in regards to their influence on increased fire load in hallways. 
This was confirmed, and it also revealed that most participants based their evaluation on material 
choice and ignitibility. 
Upholstered furniture has three main components; a frame, cover materials and cushions. The 
materials used in upholstered furniture can be divided into natural and man-made materials. 
Within the two categories the materials have very different fire resistance properties. Natural 
materials have a tendency to char when exposed to an ignition source, whereas man-made 
materials are more prone to melting. The fire resistance of each material can be determined 
through various fire resistance ignition tests. It is through regulations requiring these tests that 
countries have the possibility to control the fire resistance of furniture. The United Kingdom have 
created strict requirements for upholstered furniture, which has been contributing to the large 
decrease in fire related deaths over the last 25 years. Denmark does not require any fire resistance 
in furniture, except for furniture in large assembly halls. The lack of requirements towards 
furniture can be an accomplice in the unchanging risk of dying in a fire.  
A selection of sofa and chairs were tested in the full scale experiments, where the parameters of 
cushion thickness, fire load, flame retardants and ignition sources were investigated. The aim was 
to create recommendations towards stating limits to the upholstered furniture based on these 
parameters. The experiments were initiated by the following ignition sources in order of 
smoldering cigarette, match equivalent flame, two sizes of wooden cribs (crib 5 and crib 7). For 
naturally good fire resistance materials, such as wool blends and vinyl, the cigarette and match 
equivalent ignition sources were not a problem. The combination between cotton or 
thermoplastic fabrics and untreated polyurethane foam was ignited by the match equivalent 
flame test.  
The results indicate that materials treated with flame retardants have a lower ignitibility and 
obtain in an increased time between warning by an optical smoke detector to untenable 
conditions for humans occur. This results in longer reaction times for the occupants. 
Unfortunately, more smoke and carbon monoxide is produced by flame retardant materials 
compared to untreated materials. Furthermore, it was seen that low fire loads and thin cushions 
resulted in small fires. It was not possible to detect from the experiments if the size of the ignition 
source had an influence in the fire development, since only one experiment was conducted of 
each setup. The following recommendations for limitation of furniture parameters are based on 
the findings in the current study: 
- 
Fire loads should be below 300 MJ 
- 
Cushion thickness should be less than 5 cm 
- 
Flame retardant treated furniture is preferable in buildings with vulnerable occupants 
- 
Designs with low back rests and without armrests are preferred 
- 
Chairs are generally preferable to sofas

1 Introduction 
Modern buildings have many functional demands and should be very versatile in their use. This 
development has set higher demands on today’s regulations. The Danish building regulation from 
1961 was prescription based, but the development of new building materials and styles the need 
for performance based rules emerged. Through time the Danish building regulations has 
developed to a performance based document [1,2]. The gist of performance based requirements 
is that they are versatile, but this can also create some confusions to when they are obtained [2]. 
In the fire safety field, which is the main focus of this study, two main guides have been created 
to help the building developer comply with the building regulation and it is from these guides the 
problem for this thesis is based. The problem has root in following examples of the performance 
based requirements for escape routes: 
5.2(3) “Exits and escape routes must be designed for the people whom the exits 
and escape routes are intended to serve.” 
5.2(4) “Extreme temperatures, smoke concentrations, heat radiation or other 
factors that might obstruct evacuation must not be allowed to arise within the 
timescale within which the escape routes are used for evacuation.” 
These performance based descriptions are further elaborated in “Eksempelsamlingen om 
brandsikring af byggeri” [3], which is the document the Danish building regulation is referring to 
as examples of complying with the building regulation [4]. In this document it is stated that escape 
routes can be furnished for other proposes than escape, as long as the furnishings do not reduce 
the escape routes’ function or contributes significantly to the fire load or risk. This is not further 
specified. The lack of further definition creates problems when a new building must be approved 
by the authorities, because what is significant increase in fire load or risk? This is up to the local 
authorities to decide. There is a perception within the fire safety consultancy companies that the 
Danish regions have different positions of what is significantly and what is not. It is also known 
that specific employees with the authorities have their subjective opinion of the matter. This is 
not desirable for building owners or occupants since the safety level will not be the same across 
the country. 
This project will try to create a recommendation for how to ensure a reasonable level of fire safety 
if upholstered furniture is placed in connection with an escape route. To do this the problem was 
further analyzed through a survey1 send to the local authorities to define to problem and figure 
out which parameters are the most relevant to examine in an experimental study.  
The survey consisted of three main questions where the participants’ position regarding 
assessment of objects, fire load and useful parameters were evaluated. In Figure 1 the nine objects 
from the survey is seen. The three main questions are: 
1. Which objects are not allowed in an area of a building described with “not significantly increase 
fire load or risk”?  
2. Rate the objects based on their fire load from 1 to 9, where 1 is the lowest fire load and 9 is the 
highest fire load 
3. Which parameters decides if an object contribute with a significantly increased fire load or risk?

1.1 Material study 
From the cross-sectional survey it was found that material choice and their ignitibility was used 
by many to assess if objects would add to a significantly increased fire load or risk. These two 
parameters will be studied under one because they are interconnected through the burning 
behavior of cover materials and foams. The materials that will be studied are cotton, wool, leather, 
nylon, polyester, vinyl and flexible polyurethane foam. Furthermore, flame retardants can have a 
==PAGE:18==
8 | P a g e

major influence on fire behavior and are therefore briefly explained. Lastly an overview of the 
material parameters towards combustion is collected. 
An upholstered furniture comprises most often of three main components, the frame, cushions 
and cover fabric. The cover fabric is the outer shell which protects the cushion from wear and tear. 
It is in close contact with the cushion foam, and the two are normally the easiest to ignite on the 
furniture. The frame material are mostly solid materials or non-combustible, which means that it 
does not affect the ignitibility of the furniture, nor will it interfere with the initial growth phase of 
a fire [5]. For these reasons frame materials will not be studied. 
1.1.1 Flame retardants 
In Denmark flame retardants are not generally used, but if a reduction in ignitibility of furniture is 
demanded it will be necessary to change the practice in some way [6]. But this is not only possible 
by using flame retardants – interliners have also been used to achieve a better fire resistance. 
Interliners are a material with a high fire resistance that is used between the cover and the foam 
materials, these can also be treated with flame retardants. They will not be further explained as 
this is outside the scope of this project. 
The reason for using flame retardants are to minimize the risk of fires in buildings. Flame 
retardants have the following effects on furniture [7]: 
- 
delay of ignition time 
- 
reduction of fire propagation 
- 
reduction of heat released 
- 
reduction of mass loss rate 
- 
reduction of surface flame spread  
Flame retardants can be reactive or additive, this means the chemical is either added during the 
process of making the material or added after the creation of the material. The flame retardant 
treatments on materials afterwards do not form as good chemical bonds as if mixed into the 
material, which means that the weaker bonds can over time decompose or the molecules can be 
removed by wear and tear [6]. There is one application of durable addition of flame retardants 
called functional finish. A functional finish is polymerization of the flame retardants into the fiber 
voids or a direct reaction with the fiber polymer of the fabric. This gives a high durability of the 
flame retardants [8]. 
It is not possible to incorporate flame retardants in all materials, e.g. such as wool. For synthetic 
materials it is possible. In flexible polyurethane foam, which is used in many types of upholstered 
furniture, the process of adding flame retardants is simple. The flame retardants are added with 
the mixture of reacting ingredients before the foam sets. When adding the chemicals after the 
production, the furniture can be coated by flame retardants in a layer or getting a functional finish 
among others. On textile a coating can be applied on the front or backside of a fabric, when 
applied on the backside it will not interfere with the design or appearance.  
There are different kinds of flame retardants, the main groups are inorganic, brominated, 
phosphorus, nitrogen and chlorinated. Within these groups there are many different compounds, 
some which also utilizes molecules from the other groups to combine and enhance the flame 
retardant effects.  
 
Brominated: reducing flammability by releasing gaseous bromine atoms that interferes 
with the combustion process [9].  
==PAGE:19==
P a g e  | 9 
 
J e a n n e  B j e r r e  K i r k  
 
Phosphorus: forming a charring layer on the surface, which acts as a thermal layer 
between the fuel source and the flames[10].  
 
Nitrogen: Releases nitrogen gases into the gaseous phase to dilute the combustion gases. 
Furthermore, it forms stable compounds that stop the formation of pyrolysis gases [10].  
 
Chlorinated: reducing flammability similar to brominated fire retardants by releasing 
gaseous chlorine atoms that interferes with the combustion process [10]. 
 
Inorganic: Depending on the compound the inorganic flame retardant can hinder the fire 
with a combination of the effects also seen in brominated, phosphorus, nitrogen and 
chlorinated flame retardants [10]. 
Brominated and chlorinated flame retardants are also known as halogenated or organohalogen 
flame retardants. Halogenated flame retardants have been studied closely for their toxicity and 
environmental impacts. The molecules are very stable and bioaccumulative, which creates 
problems for humans and possibly animals [11]. Even though the chemicals have good fire 
retardant properties some have been banned by the Stockholm Convention on persistent organic 
pollutants, and some of these have even been banned by law in the EU, because of toxicity and 
environmental impacts [12].  
From the fire statistics and fire safety regulations in the UK it is clear that furniture with a lower 
flammability and higher ignitibility can save lives. To achieve this level of fire safety the 
manufactures used flame retardants, but in the future the manufactures might have to consider 
alternative solutions to comply with the regulations, because the general public’s concern towards 
health and environmental problems regarding flame retardants is growing. New ventures are 
within the nanotechnology or intumescent systems [13].  
The down side of flame retardant materials are that they will emit more smoke and CO compared 
to non-flame retardant materials [5], which is a problem since many people die of smoke 
poisoning during a fire.  
Brominated, chlorinated and nitrogen based flame retardants are not used in furniture produced 
in Denmark or by Danish producers, but it is not known which flame retardants that is used in 
furniture imported to Denmark [6].  
1.1.2 Burning behavior of cover fabrics 
Cover fabrics comes in various materials, thicknesses, tread counts, colors, textures, tensile 
strength, durability and so forth. There are so many different fabrics that it will be impossible to 
study all of them. Here, some of the most common cover fabrics used in upholstered furniture in 
Denmark have been chosen and divided into natural and man-made materials. The fire properties 
of the materials can be found in Appendix B. Flame retardants can be added to all of the cover 
fabrics, which can change their fire properties.  
In general, fabrics are considered thermally thin objects, which means that there is no 
temperature gradient in the thickness of the material and most of the time they are easy to ignite. 
The different materials have different behaviors when exposed to an ignition source. Sometimes 
it is desirable to create a composite material based on physical properties, but it is impossible to 
predict fire properties of the composite materials from the original materials [8].  
When creating a new fabric it is possible to enhance the fire resistance by giving the fabric a large 
density (more tightly weaved and thick), which is more likely to have a positive influence on the 
==PAGE:20==
10 | P a g e  
 
I n t r o d u c t i o n  
ignitibility, compared to a thin and fussy fabric [5]. Other features that enhances the fire resistance 
in fabrics are heavy char forming or a smooth surface. 
Many of the fabrics used for upholstery cover contain organic polymeric chains, which needs to 
decompose before flaming can occur. There are 4 processes which pure organic polymers can 
decompose [8]: 
1. End chain scission: individual monomers are sliced from the end of the chain. 
2. Random chain scission: Random cut in the polymer chain 
3. Chain stripping: attached atoms or atom group to the polymer chain can be sliced off. 
4. Cross-linking: New chemical bonds between the polymers, e.g. charring. 
The decomposition and thereby the flammability of polymer chains is also somewhat dependent 
on the chemical bonds between the atoms in the molecules. It is known that double bonds and 
aromatic rings will reduce the flammability because the presence of them reduces the hydrogen 
content and entails more bonds to break when decomposing. Furthermore, oxidation of the 
molecule reduces the energy of combustion, oxidation can be from O-H, C-O or C-X, X symbolizes 
a halogen [8]. 
1.1.2.1 Natural upholstered cover materials 
Natural upholstered cover materials come from fibers or animal hides, which can have natural fire 
protection properties, like wool. If a better fire resistance is required than what the natural fibers 
have to offer, flame retardants can be added as a coating on the finished fabric/cover.  
Natural fibers are woven into textiles. There are two main categories of natural fibers, which are 
plant fibers and animal fibers. The one best known from each category is cotton and wool, which 
are also the most common natural fabric fibers of upholstered furniture and will be described 
more in detail.  
Cotton consists of 90 % celluloses. Celluloses are a natural polymer consisting of linked glucose 
molecules. Cotton fabric is easily ignited and highly flammable. To naturally improve the fire 
resistance towards flame ignition sources a high density of cellulosic fabric is required, whereas a 
low density fabric has better resistance towards cigarette ignition sources [5]. 
Cotton can easily engage in a blended material. When cellulosic fibers are blended with 
thermoplastic fibers, the fire resistance of the fabric is increased [5].  
Wool is a durable and flexible material, that makes it well suited for upholstery fabric. 
Furthermore, it has naturally good flame retardant properties. When exposed to heat, the water 
content in the fibers is vaporized and thereafter the fibers char, which both are good qualities for 
reduced flammability [8]. Even though the material has natural fire resistance properties, these 
can be further enhanced by flame retardant treatment.  
One particular treatment is developed for wool fibers during the dyeing process. This is called 
Zirpro and it works by removing negatively charged molecule complexes from the fiber in an acid 
solution. The method does not affect any other natural properties of wools, such as soil resistance, 
color fastness and perspiration [7].  
The third natural material is leather, which is made from animal hides. Fr upholstered furniture 
most hides come from cows. A tanned treated leather cover can be exposed to a heat source of 
130-170 °C for 30 min without damages. Temperature above 200 °C will start the pyrolysis process 
and further increase in temperature could lead to shrinkage and crumbling of the leather [5].

Moreover, leather is prone to smolder, causing long lasting fires, which can ignite highly 
flammable materials, such as foams when used in upholstery [7,14].  
Leather can also be protected with flame retardants. Kozlowski et al. found that one flame 
retardant based on ammonium polyphosphate and boric acid could eliminate smoldering, 
increase the ignition time with 27 % and reduce the HRR with 20 % compared to tanned leather 
[14]. Unfortunately, the flame retardant treatment also caused 50 % more smoke production and 
a larger production of CO.  
1.1.2.2 Man-made upholstered cover materials 
There are two categories under man-made fibers, which are regenerated and synthetic. 
Regenerate fibers are made from natural fibers and wood. Synthetic fibers are made from mixing 
of chemicals; many are thermoplastic. The process of making the materials will not be explained, 
since it is not a part of this study. 
In general thermoplastic fabrics contract and crumble away from a flaming ignition source, which 
can reduce the possibility of ignition. Flame retardants are easy to incorporate in thermoplastics, 
which can lower the flammability by interfering with one of the combustion stages [8].   
The man-made fibers described below are generally used in blends with other fabrics and it is 
important to know that the fire properties of a fabric blend cannot be predicted from the fire 
properties of the individual fibers [8]. 
Nylon is a strong and very light fabric, that is easy to clean and remove wrinkles from. It has 
thermoplastic properties, which means that it can be shaped and reshaped. During flaming 
combustion, the fabric burns slowly while melting, which can cause burning droplets. When 
exposed to a small flame ignition source, the material can retreat from the flame exposing the 
underlying material [15]. 
Polyester is a very strong and durable material, that also is resistant to wrinkle, chemicals, 
stretching and shrinking. Furthermore, it is water repellant and dries quickly. Polyester has the 
same characteristics when burning as nylon.  
Nylon and polyester are mostly used as blends with other fibers when used for upholstered 
furniture. Their many good qualities make them well suited for creating a durable fabric, which 
keeps the blended fabric from changing appearance.   
Another popular material is polyester coated with vinyl or PVC, also known as synthetic leather, 
or simply pleather. Vinyl or PVC is very durable and can obtain many different properties 
depending on the chemical formula. Some of these added properties are waterproofing, soil 
resistance, and added strength. Vinyl has a very high limiting oxygen index, 45, which means that 
a small flame cannot ignite the material [16]. When the material is burning it produces a high 
concentration of CO. 
1.1.3 Burning behavior of polyurethane foam 
Foam is a major part of upholstered furniture. The foam is used to make cushions, which makes 
the furniture comfortable to sit in, especially over longer periods of time.  
Foams for upholstery furniture are lightweight materials. The most widely used foam is flexible 
polyurethane foam. This is an oil-based material that is created by mixing together chemicals. The 
chemicals react with each other. During the last stage of mixing, air will be trapped in pockets 
inside, which gives it the low density. Depending on the chemical used, different properties can 
==PAGE:22==
12 | P a g e  
 
I n t r o d u c t i o n  
be achieved, really soft and flexible or more rigid and stronger. The synthetic foams are easy to 
cut into the right size and shape for the furniture and adds to the comfort level.  
Generally, foams are highly flammable, which is a problem for fire safety. The solution from the 
manufactures have been to add flame retardants to the material. This can be done directly in the 
mixing of the chemical during foam production or as an added coating.  
When polyurethane is under combustion, it produces a vast amount of toxic smoke and flaming 
droplets. The smoke production is the most critical parameter for humans, since it is the largest 
killer in building fires. Burning droplets is also a concern, since a pool of melted foam can ignite 
other items, resulting in a large and uncontrollable fire.  
Fire retardant polyurethane foam might be more fire resistant, but when burning, it is producing 
more smoke than regular polyurethane foam [5]. Conventional flame retardant additives does not 
have an influence on the rate of heat release under a cone calorimetry test [17]. Furthermore, the 
experiments show quadruple production of CO compared to regular polyurethane foam. Checchin 
et. al. shows that modifying the polymer matrix with isocyanurate rings reduce the HRR with 83 % 
[17]. 
The dimension of the foam influences the burning behavior. A thick cushion will have a lower HRR 
compared to a thin cushion per unit mass [5]. Furthermore, increasing the density of polyurethane 
foam will increase the ignition resistance. 
The smoke production in foams are large. In flaming conditions, the smoke production is 
dependent on the ventilation rate to the fire. Under-ventilation causes increased smoke 
production. Furthermore smoke production also increases when the foam goes from flaming to 
smoldering combustion [5].   
Other foams are also used in upholstered furniture, polyester, polyether, and latex foam. Latex 
foam has the highest smoke production, and polyester foam has the lowest smoke production. 
The smoke production is very dependent on the exact chemical formula.  
1.1.4 Composite testing 
An upholstered furniture consists of foams and cover materials, and their individual fire properties 
can have negative or positive influence the burning behavior for the furniture composition.  
Compositions with regular polyurethane foam will generally perform well in fire resistance tests 
with match equivalent flame ignition sources when matched with wool fabrics, fire retardant 
cellulosic fabrics and vinyl coated fabrics [5]. Babrauskas et al. also has collected information 
about resistance to cigarette ignition seen in Table 1 [5]. Here the combination with acrylic, 
polypropylene and cotton/viscose fabrics do not pass the test, whereas wool/cotton, nylon, 
polyester, fire retardant cotton, and PVC do. The cover fabrics’ properties are more important 
than the properties of the foam when fire resistance testing with small ignition sources. If larger 
fire resistance is necessary only fire retardant wool and cellulosic fabrics should be used together 
with standard polyurethane. When using flame retardant foams, the fabric still plays a role in 
ignitibility, but it is the properties of the foam that will determine if the fire can be self-
extinguished combined with the size of the ignition source [5].

265 °C. The ignition temperatures are highest for wool and vinyl and they are also the materials

needs to be present to support the burning of the materials.

1.2 Enclosure fires 
The surroundings can play a large role in the development of a fire. The interactions between 
furniture and the surroundings are a large area of study and multiple books and articles have been 
written on the topic. Therefore, the basics behind the effect of enclosure size, thermal feedback 
from surroundings and smoke production in enclosures will be assessed.  
1.2.1 Enclosure size 
The enclosure size is one of the most influential parameters of the surroundings on the fire 
development. A fire in a small enclosure will have a faster growth than the same fire in a larger 
compartment. This happens because the small enclosure will heat quicker and fill up with smoke 
faster than the large enclosure, which both give more thermal feedback to the fuel to increase the 
burning rate. Even though this is known, it is not easy to determine when a fire will be significantly 
==PAGE:25==
P a g e  | 15 
 
J e a n n e  B j e r r e  K i r k  
influenced by the effect from the enclosure size that affects the fire development, because this 
also depends on the size of the fire and how the fuel is burning.  
The problem has been investigated multiple times. Hirschler & Janssens have collected a review 
of studies regarding enclosure effects on furniture fires [18]. The conclusion is that the effects 
from enclosure size can be ignored when the heat release rate (HRR) is lower than 200 kW. The 
tests are conducted in an open calorimetry setup and in a standard room, 2.4 m x 3.6 m x 2.4 m, 
or rooms of approximately those dimensions. It is also stated that burning furniture in an standard 
room will generally raise the HRR with 32 % compared to an open fire [18]. 
Furthermore, Parker et al. states that they did not see a difference in HRR under 600 kW, when 
testing chairs in the California technical bulletin 133 room, 3.66 m x 3.05 m x 2.44 m, and the 
standard room at NIST, 2.4 m x 3.6 m x 2.4 m [19]. The dimensions of the rooms are similar and 
could explain why fires must be larger to detect a difference in HRR than when comparing the 
standard room and a free burning.  
Xie et al. has investigated how the position of the furniture influences the HRR and temperature 
development in the standard room [20]. The boundary condition did have an effect as well as the 
design of the chair. When positioned against a wall the HRR had two peaks whereas the HRR only 
had one peak for a center position. Two tests were conducted in the center of the room, one with 
the chair facing the opening and another facing the opposite direction. The distance from the chair 
to the wall was 80 cm. Here the chair facing the opening has a higher peak heat release rate (PHRR) 
than the chair facing inwards. The explanation is complex fire dynamics interacting with the 
composition of the chair and the boundary conditions to increase the peak heat release rate.  
1.2.2 Thermal feedback from surroundings 
The surrounding materials are heated by the radiation from the fire plume and potential the hot 
smoke layer under the ceiling depending on the enclosure size. When materials are heated, they 
start to emit heat themselves, which will further increase the temperature in the enclosure. 
Depending on the thermal inertia of the materials in the enclosure, the effect will be larger or 
smaller.  
The largest radiative feedback is from the smoke gas layer. The feedback will not have a significant 
effect in the very early stages of the fire, but it will magnify with the fire development. If the fire 
spreads to other items in the enclosure the fires will influence each other’s development [21].  
It is not only the enclosure size that matters for the thermal feedback. Material properties also 
has an influence. Poulsen has studied the influence on HRR from two surface claddings: mineral 
wool and cement board [22]. The burning object was a block of polyurethane foam. It was found 
that in an enclosure of 3.08 m x 2.4 m x 2.4 m, the thermal feedback was greater when the room 
was lined with mineral wool than cement board. This resulted in a larger HRR and a faster fire 
spread. The thermal inertia of the mineral wool was 25 times greater than the thermal inertia of 
the cement board.  
1.2.3 Smoke production in enclosures 
Smoke production in enclosures depend on the ventilation rate. Over-ventilated conditions will 
not affect the smoke production, whereas under-ventilated conditions can have an effect on 
which gaseous products are produced. An example is a burner with propane. It will in under 
ventilated conditions produce a higher level of carbon monoxide compared to over-ventilated 
conditions [23].  
==PAGE:26==

I n t r o d u c t i o n  
Oxygen availability is one of the key parameters to combustion and by-products hereof. When not 
enough oxygen is present to react in the combustion process, the reactants will form other bonds 
than during a complete combustion. These other products could be CO and unburnt pyrolysis 
gases, which will be part of the smoke layer.  
1.2.4 Ignition source size and position 
Ignition sources can be many different things, but for this project some predefined standard 
ignition sources are used and will be described. Furthermore, the effects of the position to ignite 
an item will be discussed. There are two different types of ignition sources, smoldering ignition 
sources and flaming ignition sources. From the smoldering category the smoldering cigarette will 
be described and from the flaming category a small match flame equivalent, and two different 
sizes of wood cribs will be described.  
Ignition by a smoldering cigarette is the most common ignition source in upholstered furniture 
[24], therefore standards have been created and a standard cigarette defined to test fire 
resistance in furniture. The standard cigarette is 68 mm long and has a diameter of 8 mm and a 
mass of 0.85 g [25]. It does not have a filter or is utilizing the self-extinguishing technology that is 
required in cigarettes for sale in EU [26]. The cigarette can smolder for 20 min. During this time 
there is a heat output of 4-6 W and a temperature of 630-690 °C between the cigarette and the 
fabric [24]. The standard cigarette is designed to be similar to the hottest burning brand in the 
1970’s to test a worst case scenario. The cigarette that inspired the standard cigarette is not 
commercially available anymore, due to fire safety improvements in the industry. 
Cigarettes become a larger risk if they are covered while smoldering. This limits the heat loss to 
the surroundings creating an environment more prone to ignition [24]. During accidental fires 
caused by dropped cigarettes, there are little chance that the cigarette will fall under an object, 
but it can be partially covered by loose bedding or other objects, which can also enhance the risk 
of ignition [5]. The cigarette can only ignite upholstery materials if it is in direct contact with it. 
The small flame ignition source, match equivalent, is used to simulate ignition by a wooden match. 
The match flame has a heat release 80 W during 15 s and it radiates heat at approximately 20 
kW/m2 at a distance of 25 mm [27,28]. For this study propane gas is used for the flame, although 
EN 1021-2 specifies butane [25]. The differences in the burning behavior of the gases has been 
investigated from the burner tube described in EN 1021-2 [25]. Paul & Christian found that the 
two gasses burn very similarly [29].  
The larger ignition sources are wood cribs. Four different wood cribs are defined in the British 
standard BS 5852:2006 [30]. The cribs are constructed of the same material, pine wood. Crib 5 
weighs 17 g and has a heat release of approximately 1.9 kW, while crib 7 is larger and weighs 127 
g and has a heat release of approximately 6.4 kW [28,30].  
The standard wood cribs have been created to simulate ignition by newspaper. The wood cribs 
were created because they have a high reproducibility compared to crumbled newspaper and 
have similar burning behavior [27]. Ignition by a crumpled newspaper is difficult to replicate, due 
to multiple issues such as how to crumble the paper the same way and how the paper behaves 
during combustion. Even though the wood cribs are easier to reproduce, there are shortcomings. 
One problem is that cribs can collapse, causing uneven burning. The collapsing of the crib can 
result in different effects on the furniture. For an example collapsing towards the back rest can 
expose the fabric or foam to more direct contact with flames, which will enhance the risk of 
ignition. The crib can also be spread out over a larger area, which decreases the ignition chances 
==PAGE:27==
P a g e  | 17 
 
J e a n n e  B j e r r e  K i r k  
due to a larger heat loss to the surroundings. The exact effects are individual due to the complex 
fire dynamics of each furniture and the collapse of the crib. 
The location of the ignition sources, unrelated to the size, have an effect on how the fire develops, 
but it does not have a significant effect of the peak heat release rate on chairs. Fabrian found that 
igniting chairs at different locations with the same ignition source will have an effect on the time 
to reach the PHRR, but not the value of the PHRR [31]. Although this was found by Fabrian, he also 
reports of another study where the HRR was affected of the ignition source. Here a three seater 
sofa was ignited in the corner and in the center. The fire originating in the center was larger than 
the fire from the corner. This is possibly because the fire can spread in two directions when ignited 
in the center of a larger piece of furniture. When ignited in the corner the fire can only spread in 
one direction, which can create a smaller fire, because less material is burning simultaneously.   
1.2.5 Furniture design 
The furniture design also has a major impact on the fire development. In the design lies choices of 
material, construction and scale of the individual parts, which affect the fire behavior. It has been 
shown that wide furniture has lower HRRs than narrow furniture. Arm rest also makes a 
difference, if the arm rest have a large vertical surface, it will increase heat reflected back at the 
fire, increasing the fire growth. Having an opening between the seat and the back or arm rest, 
allows for a higher supply of air to the fire, which will yield a higher HRR [5]. 
The design of the furniture can increase the complexity of the fire dynamics in a room, which 
makes it hard to predict the behavior of furniture. There are several models that tries to describe 
the outcome of a furniture fire. Babrauskas have made two, one depended only on the mass, 
energy content and style of the furniture, where the other is also using data from a small scale 
cone calorimetry experiment [32,33].  
The project CBUF, which was founded by the European Union, created 3 models predicting the 
fire behavior of furniture [34]. All are based on small scale experiments with the cone calorimetry, 
mass loss and style factors of the furniture.  
1.3 Fire standards 
One way to define the fire resistance of furniture is to test the piece or components of it under 
standard conditions. Fire resistance tests of upholstered furniture are done in many different ways 
depending on the national traditions. The standards that are in focus are EN 1021 – part 1 and 2 
and BS 5852 [25,30,35]. Here the standards will be introduced and their methods explained. The 
requirements towards the standard in the studied countries will be regarded at a later point. This 
section is mainly to. 
1.3.1 EN 1021 – part 1 and 2 
EN 1021 – part 1 and 2 is European Standards, which are assessing the fire resistance in 
upholstered furniture components with a smoldering cigarette and a match equivalent flame 
[25,35]. It is used in Denmark, Sweden, Norway and the United Kingdom (UK), among other 
countries.  
The test is conducted as a small scale test in a rig, that holds two cushions with cover fabrics, which 
are positioned perpendicular to each other. For the cigarette test, the cigarette must be ignited 
so the end is glowing red, and immediately placed in the crease of the test materials. The match 
equivalent ignition source is a butane flame of approximately 3.5 cm that is applied in the crease 
of the setup for 15 s, before removal.

To pass the ignition tests the sample must comply with several points [25,35]. For smoldering and 
flaming ignition the component cannot:

combust rapidly 
-

-

-

of the furniture.   
1.3.2 BS 5852 
BS 5852 is a British standard and has not been adopted in the EU. The standard is also testing fire 
resistance, and it includes more ignition sources than EN 1021-1 and 2 [25,35]. There are two 
methods to gain the fire resistance class with BS 5852 [30], an upholstery composite or the 
complete item can be tested. If the upholstery composite is tested the setup is similar to EN 1021- 
part 1 and 2 [25,35], and when the complete item is tested the ignition sources are placed directly 
on the furniture.  
Upholstered composites are testing both the filling and covers of an upholstered furniture. It is 
placed in a test rig and the ignition source is placed in the crease for the required time. BS 5852 
includes eight ignition source categories (from 0-7) to test the material combinations [30]. Ignition 
source 1, 2, 5 and 7 are shown in Figure 8, as these are in focus in this study. The cigarette and 
match equivalent flame is the same as defined in EN 1021 – 1,2 [25,35].  
The British regulations decide which ignition source has to be used depending on the end use of 
the furniture. To pass the test there are different criteria depending on the ignition source, mostly 
it is defined by a maximum time for flaming or smoldering after ignition. 
As with EN 1021- 1 or 2 a certain combination of covers and fillings can be approved, but the 
confirmation of the individual materials/combinations cannot be transferred to a conclusion for 
the final assembly of the furniture [25,35]. For some end use it is acceptable to individually test 
components, and the complete furniture can then be approved for sale.

1.4 Regulations and statistics of fire safety 
Fire safety is becoming a topic of interest for organizations and the European Union. As of this 
day, no fire safety regulation is covering all member states of the EU. This means that each country 
has their own regulation of fire safety, which provides different levels of safety to the European 
citizens [36]. For this study, a comparison will be conducted of the Danish, Norwegian, Swedish 
and British regulations in relation to escape routes, interconnected fire statistics of number of fire 
related deaths and the initiatives the countries have taken to reduce the fire related deaths.  
1.4.1 Danish regulations 
The Danish Transport and Construction Agency regulates the construction sector through the 
building code (Byggeloven) and the Danish building regulation, bygningsreglementet [4,37]. The 
building code is a framework law and has an overall goal of ensuring safe buildings for occupants 
in respect to fire safety, security, and health [37]. The Danish building regulation is specifying the 
building code through performance based requirements. As a supplement for the building 
regulations the Danish Emergency Management Agency states preventive regulations that aim at 
reducing the fire risk in buildings and ensuring good access for emergency responds.   
The performance based requirements in the building regulation are exemplified as prescriptive 
examples in the guide “Eksempelsamling om brandsikring af byggeri” [3]. If a building cannot 
comply with the prescriptive examples, another guide, ”Information om Brandteknisk 
Dimensionering” [38], can be used to show compliance with the building regulation. Here 
performance based guidelines are described and through the calculation methods it has to be 
verified that critical conditions during a fire are not reached before the occupants have evacuated. 
This guide will not be explained further.  
In the building regulation the performance based requirement of safe evacuation for occupants 
during which they cannot be exposed to critical conditions such as high temperatures, smoke 
concentrations or radiant heat, which can prevent the evacuation, is found [4]. The pre-acceptable

solution to a safe escape in [39] is ensured by prescribing that a building shall be sectioned into 
fire compartments and sections. The compartments have to have 2 escape routes to ensure that 
persons are not trapped by a fire. From the door in a fire compartment the maximum distance to

In Denmark there are no laws regarding the fire safety of furniture for the regular consumer. 
However under the operational laws issued by the Danish emergency management agency, 
upholstered furniture placed in assembly rooms for more than 150 people must have furniture 
that complies with DS/EN 1021 part 1 – cigarette fire resistance test [25,40].  
1.4.1.1 Danish fire statistics 
Annually, 78 people die a fire related death, this is on average 15 people per million inhabitants 
in Denmark [41]. The development is seen in Figure 9. The Danish emergency management agency 
collects and discloses fire statistics [42]. The Agency defines a fire related death as a person dying 
during the fire or within 30 days of the fire. The most common death reasons are smoke poisoning 
and skin burns [41]. The statistics of fire related deaths are also including traffic accidents involving 
fire.  
From the statistics of fire related deaths divided into building type, seen in Figure 10, it is seen

In 2000 the Danish emergency management agency released an action plan to reduce the fire

December 2015. They have suggested 8 goals: 
1. Fewer fire related deaths 
2. No fires with more than 5 casualties 
3. More knowledge of fire 
4. No fire that paralyze infrastructure 
5. No fires with loss of irreplaceable cultural value 
6. Yearly fire related social loss reduced by 10 % 
7. Bisection of environmental impact from fire 
8. The Danes ability to prevent fire should be improved 
They want to achieve these goals by having smoke and stove alarms in all buildings, and have

commercial buildings to reduce the economic loss related to a fire. These recommendations are 
hopefully effective if/when they are admitted in society.

1.4.2 Swedish Regulations 
In Sweden the Swedish parliament issues the framework laws, the plan and building code, and the 
organization Boverket specifies the building regulations and guidelines for the building sector 
[28,44]. All new buildings and buildings undergoing alterations must comply with the newest 
building regulation. The current building regulation is Boverket’s Building Regulation (BBR) 22, 
which is an amendment to BBR 18 from 2011. The latest change in the fire safety section of the 
regulation was BRR 21 in 2014.  
The building regulation is elaborated through general recommendations stated in the law. The 
recommendations are voluntary, but if not followed it must be proved that the new solution will 
comply with the regulation. This can be done by an analytical calculation and will not be further 
explained here.  
The section for escape routes in the Swedish building regulation states that it must lead directly 
into the street or similar surroundings. Furthermore, it must accommodate the number of 
occupants and not be longer than a safe evacuation can take place before critical conditions are 
created from the fire. Moreover, they must be easy to pass through to ensure a quick evacuation 
[45].  
Since the building regulation only handles fixed installations, any loose furnishings are not 
considered, except that nothing is allowed to compromise the accessibility and spaciousness of 
the escape route. The furnishings are handled by the Swedish Civil Contingencies Agency. In 
domestic buildings furniture is required to apply with EN 1021 part 1 – cigarette fire resistant test 
[25,46], but there are no detailed recommendations for public buildings. Instead a holistic 
evaluation of the building is wanted to decide which furniture and other loose installations are 
allowed [28].  
Some regions have created their own pre-acceptable solutions to comply with the building 
regulation. In the Region Västra Götaland upholstered furniture placed in care facilities are 
expected to comply with both EN 1021 part 1 and 2 [25,28]. They have also recommended other 
initiatives to simplify the holistic approach for evaluating buildings. Other municipalities have 
adopted or created a similar approach to simplify the holistic evaluation of the fire safety of a 
building.  
1.4.2.1 Swedish fire statistics 
In Sweden there has been on average of 121 fire related deaths between 1999 and 2014 annually 
[47]. A fire related death is in Sweden is described as a person who died of fire related damages 
during the fire or within 90 days of the incident. The fire related damages can be both direct, such 
as burns or smoke poisoning, and indirect, such as fallen objects.  
The trend of fire related deaths per year is declining. In 1999 14.3 people per 1 million inhabitants 
died a fire related death, whereas in 2014 only 8.3 people per million died a fire related death. 
The development can be seen in Figure 9 [47]. Each fire has not become less threatening, on 
average 1.09 people die in a fire causing death, but the number of fires causing death has been 
reduced through the period. There are gender differences in fire related deaths. More men die 
due to fire than women, and the highest risk for men to die a fire related death is when they are 
between 45-64 years old, whereas most women die when they are above 80 years old. From 
Figure 12 it is seen that most people die in fires during Friday, Saturday and Sunday, with a high 
peak between 00.00 and 03.00 Sunday [47].

1.4.3 Norwegian regulations 
The Norwegian regulations also consist of a framework law that determines how the building 
sector is structured and building projects are planned [48]. An underlying regulation, TEK 10, 
specifies the technical requirements for buildings [49]. The regulation was adapted in 2010 and 
encompasses general requirements, nature impacts and outdoor environments and requirements 
for buildings [49]. 
According to TEK 10, escape routes must be clear and easily navigable with a sufficient height and 
width to accommodate a safely evacuation [49]. Furthermore, an escape route must always be a 
seperate fire compartment and be compartmentalized by the evacuation flow direction to avoid 
fire and smoke spreading during the calculated evacuation time [49]. 
It is the duty of the buildings operational management to ensure the function of the escape routes 
by prohibiting objects and remove existing objects in the travel route [50]. 
Under former regulations the escape routes were allowed to contain small rooms as open space 
for sitting or different kinds of receptionist work places. The requisite was that the escape route 
function was not reduced and that furniture or other objects could not be placed in the evacuation 
route [51]. 
Similar to Sweden, Norway has also adopted EN 1021 part 1 as a requirement for all upholstered 
furniture, but this is both for domestic and non-domestic buildings [25,52]. The regulation was 
issued in 1989 [53].  
In 2012 SINTEF NBL released a report suggesting stricter regulations for upholstered furniture. 
Their suggestion was to have the same level of safety as the UK, since it has been shown in reports 
that their regulations have been a contributing factor in the number of fire related deaths [54,55] 
Furthermore, it was suggested to differentiate between the fire resistant demands in the usage 
categories, to ensure the appropriate fire safety with the minimal changes. The regulations have 
not been changed to this date.

The United Kingdom has the highest level of fire safety in Europe, and their government has 
passed many fire safety regulations through the years. The regulations that this study will focus

These regulations cover building safety and fire safety regarding upholstered furniture. The British 
building regulation has many sections and under the section of “fire safety information” it is 
specified when it is necessary to meet the regulation concerning fire safety. Only if Fire Safety: 
Approved Document B also has requirements to the work that is to be done. The Approved 
Documents are guides to comply with the building regulation, in all there are 16 approved 
documents [58]. The Approved Documents entails a performance based description or specific 
examples of compliance with the British building regulation. 
The building regulation does not regulate furnishings and furniture, this is regulated for domestic 
upholstered furniture through the Furniture and furnishings (fire) (safety) Regulation 1988. The 
regulation has pinned out the needed fire resistance requirements for all furniture with upholstery 
tested according to BS 5852 and has created a labeling concept that all manufactures must follow 
[30]. The fire resistance requirement to the materials used in upholstered furniture are dependent 
on the material used and can vary between materials.   
Furniture in non-domestic building are regulated through Regulatory Reform (fire safety) Order 
2005. Here the requirements are not as straight forward as for the domestic furniture. One or 
multiple responsible persons must ensure fire safety of the entire building through obligatory risk 
assessments. Without the detailed requirements in the regulation, standards have been created 
to assist the preparation and risk assessment of the building. The British standard BS7176:2007 
has specifications for furniture depending on their end use. An overview is collected in Table 3.  
The standard BS7176:2007 is referring to other standards for the fire resistance tests of furniture. 
Here BS EN 1021 – part 1 and 2 and BS 5852 are used as tests for ignitibility and are discussed in 
section 1.3.  
Public health care buildings have their own guides under the Department of Health called the 
Health Technical Memoranda (HTM). The HTM 05-03 - part C provides details for expected fire 
safety in textiles and furnishings. The provision recommends that furniture should at least fulfill 
the same requirements as specified in Furniture and furnishings (fire) (safety) Regulation 1988 for 
domestic dwellings.

1.4.4.1 British fire statistics 
The development of fire related deaths from 1988-2014 is shown in Figure 9. In the UK number of 
fire related deaths are decreasing. In 1988 15 people died during a fire per million inhabitants. In 
2014 this number has declined to 5 fire related deaths per 1,000,000 inhabitants.  
The UK government has also worked hard with the area to bring down the fire related deaths. The 
regulations passed on fire safety of furniture has improved the fire statistics. Four years after the 
legislation was passed in the UK the number of fire related deaths started to decrease. A study 
was commissioned by the Consumer and Competition Policy Directorate, BIS, to evaluate the 
effect of the furnishing and furniture (fire) (safety) regulation 1988. The conclusion from the 
report was that the furnishing and furniture (fire) (safety) regulation 1988 has had an effect on 
fewer fire related deaths. The regulation is approximately responsible for 54 fewer deaths and 
780 fewer injuries per year [55].  
Another parameter that was discussed in the report was the usage of smoke detectors in dwellings 
[55]. The ownership went from 8 % of the British population in 1988 to 88 % in 2013, and this 
contributes to the reducing in number of fatal fires as well. Smoke detectors was found to have 
different effect depending on where the fire started. Fires in furnishings and furniture did not get 
as large an effect as other fires when a smoke detector is installed. Both had a drop in fatal fires, 
but the drop was larger for fire not originating in furnishings and furniture.  
Other factors influencing the fire statistics are fewer smokers and introduction of fire safe 
cigarettes [55,60]. The European Commission has found that when Reduced Ignition Propensity 
cigarettes are introduced in a region the fire related deaths can be reduced with up to 40 % [26].

1.5 Summary of literature study 
The literature study is the foundation of the project. It is here the problem is defined to be able 
to explore it through experimental work. The cross-sectional survey for the local authorities 
established that the material choice and ignitibility of the furniture are the most important 
parameters when the authorities are evaluating objects that are placed in escape routes or areas 
described with “not significant fire load or risk”. 
Material choice and ignitibility were studied together with furniture’s interactions with 
surroundings and use of flame retardants.  
The material choice study revealed that cover materials have different reactions to ignition 
sources depending on their origin. Natural materials are more prone to charring than man-made 
materials, which will melt. Wool can be mentioned as one of the best fire resistant materials 
without use of flame retardants, whereas cellulosic materials have a low fire resistance. Cover 
materials in combination with foam materials can be more or less fire resistant depending on the 
choice of materials. When thermoplastic cover materials are used, it creates a risk of exposing the 
underlying foam directly to the ignition source due to the melting behavior of thermoplastics. This 
is not desirable and can increase the fire risk. 
The use of flame retardants will increase the fire resistance and reduce the ignitibility of materials, 
but some of the flame retardants have been discovered to cause health problems for humans and

2 Experimental study 
The literature survey revealed that some materials are more desirable than others when looking 
at fire resistance of upholstered furniture. The purpose of the experiments is to investigate the 
influence of cushion thickness, fire load, flame retardants and ignition sources. These parameters 
are either inspired by the cross-sectional survey or the literature study and were chosen because 
they are easy identifiable to be used for recommendations on fire safety of furniture. The 
experiments were conducted in small and full scale setups as the bomb calorimeter, mass loss 
cone calorimeter and in the room corner test as of EN 9705 to test the burning behavior of 
furniture in enclosures [61].

taken out for testing in the bomb calorimeter. From the cover fabric a 10x10 cm sample was cut 
to be tested in the cone calorimeter setup. Examples of the samples are seen in Figure 15. The

2.3 Mass loss cone calorimeter 
The cone calorimeter is used to determine the ignitibility of the cover fabric and the burning 
behavior when exposed to a radiant heating source.  
2.3.1 Experimental setup 
The cone calorimeter setup consists of a cone apparatus with sample holder and scale. The setup 
is seen in Figure 16. The cone is placed beneath a hood with oxygen consumption calorimetry. The 
analyzers can detect oxygen, carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide and smoke density, which can be 
used to calculate HRR and visibility.  
The 10x10 cm samples that were cut from the cover fabric of the furniture are placed on mineral 
wool batts in the sample holder. The fabrics were secured to the batts with staples to limit the 
movement of the fabric while heating. Prior to heating the height of the sample holder is 
calibrated, so the distance between the sample and the bottom of the electrically heated cone is 
25 mm. 
The sample is placed beneath the heated cone protected by shutters. For these tests the cone is 
heated to 478 °C which corresponds to 15 kW/m2. Immediately after placement, the test is started 
by opening the shutters. Now the time to ignition by a pilot ignition spark is measured along with 
oxygen consumption calorimetry.

2.4 Sub conclusion 
From the heat of combustion, the total fire load has been found. It was found that chair 2, sofa 2 
and 3 were the pieces of furniture with the largest fire load. This was also expected based on their 
appearance and cushion thickness. Sofa 1 had the lowest fire load of the upholstered furniture, 
which was also expected, since both sofa 1 and chair 1 was chosen based on their low amount of 
combustible materials, which should yield a low fire load. 
Based on the results from the cone calorimetry experiments it is expected that sofa 3, chair 1 and 
2 and will ignite quickly when exposed to the ignition sources. The fast ignition of cover fabric 
from sofa 3 was a little surprising, since it is tested to be fire resistant to a match equivalent flame 
ignition source and the other fabrics that ignited slower were only tested to the cigarette ignition 
source. Of cause, there is a difference from the match equivalent flame to the radiant cone as 
ignition sources, which could lead to a different behavior from the materials in the full scale 
experiments than seen here.

combusted chair.

flames. This indicates that flashover was not the reason for fire spread.

2.5.4.2.5 Sub conclusion 
To sum up the observations of the experiments. The frames of the furniture do not combust until 
later in the fire, and these are therefore less important than the other components in the furniture 
for determination of the fire development. It was also observed that fires that travel around the 
furniture do not result in large fires. This is both sofa 1 and chair 3 an example of.  
It was seen that sofa 1 and 2 and chair 1 and 2 were the easiest furniture to ignite, see Table 13. 
This is due to the material choices of the cover fabric and the cushion foam. As explained in the 
literature study thermoplastic and cellulosic fabrics have a high ignitibility and flammability, 
whereas wool blends and PVC have better fire resistance. Furthermore, flame retardant materials 
lowers the ignitibility and flammability and both sofa 3 and chair 3 had flame retardant 
components.  
From this it is also easy to understand why the British fire safety regulations are effective. Using

furniture and the risk is larger.

2.5.4.2.6 Time available to extinguish the furniture for occupants 
Another parameter that is interesting to study is the time from the alarm sounds to it is impossible 
to extinguish the furniture by handheld devices, such as fire extinguisher and hose reels without 
large risk to the occupant. Here it is especially interesting to see, if flame retardants can prolong 
the time available to do so. A time lapse is set up in Figure 27, where sofa 2 ignited by match 
equivalent flame is compared with sofa 3 ignited by crib 5 and crib 7. Since the occupants only 
have their own senses to rely on when assessing if they can extinguish the fire, the time available 
will be determined only based on visual pictures from the fire.  
Sofa 2 ignited by a match equivalent ignition source, is evaluated to be possible to extinguish 
within the first minute after the smoke detector sounds. At this point the smoke production and 
the size of the flames are very large. Sofa 3 ignited by crib 5 does not pose a large threat to the 
occupants during the time it is under flaming combustion. Furthermore, the fire also self-
extinguishes 5 min after the smoke detector is activated. When sofa 3 is ignited by crib 7 it might 
be possible to extinguish it 2-3 min after the smoke detector is activated. Here the smoke 
production is large and the flames are manageable but both are less intense than for sofa 2 at 1 
min after the alarm.  
The same trends are seen for experiments with chair 2 and 3, where chair 2 rather quickly reaches 
a level, where it is impossible to extinguish without harm to persons. Whereas chair 3 never 
develops into a fire where it would be critical to extinguish it by handheld devices without 
protection.  
It is clearly seen that flame retardants in these cases will increase the time period in which it is 
manageable to extinguish the fire with a handheld device. Furthermore, the ignition sources have 
to be much larger to create the same fire development, which limits the risk of a fire. 
The time needed to locate the fire and collect the handheld extinguishing device depends on the 
usage category, the layout of the building and on the level of training of the occupants. Any 
approximate times have not been studied, but it is known that staff in health care facilities are 
trained in how to react to a fire, and they could especially take advantage of the prolonged time 
to react to such an event. Their prime objective is to secure the residents, which can be done by 
fire extinguishing or evacuation of the residents, whichever seems to be the best option.

2.5.4.3.3 Influence of flame retardants and ignition sources 
The obvious choice of furniture to study when assessing the effects of flame retardants on fire 
development is sofa 2 and 3, without and with flame retardants respectively. The design is similar 
and the furniture have similar properties as seen in Table 11, Table 12 and Table 15. First the 
development is compared with different ignition sources. Sofa 2 was ignited by ignition source 2, 
whereas sofa 3 was ignited by ignition source 4. The difference in the heat output of the ignition 
sources are significant. 
The fire in sofa 2 starts to develop rapidly after 110 s and reaches its peak at 350 s, when the fire 
is extinguished as seen in Figure 31. Sofa 3 has a rapid development from 190 s, but experiences 
a small decrease in HRR at 330 s, after consulting the visual results it is seen that the sofa has 
started to drip material onto the floor. This results in removal of heat from the sofa cushion, which 
can explain the small decrease in HRR. The sofa recovers the heat quickly and the HRR is soaring 
towards the PHRR from this point.  
The PHRR of the two sofas are approximately the same, and flame retardants do not seem to have 
an influence hereon. Babrauskas concluded that flame retardant foam does not have an influence 
on the PHRR [32]. But it does have an effect on the energy produced relative to time from ignition. 
In Figure 32 the accumulated heat release is shown for all tested furniture from the time the 
smoke detector is activated. It is here clear that sofa 2 releases much more energy than the fire 
retardant sofa 3. During the first minute after activation of the smoke detector the difference in 
released energy is not large, but hereafter the difference is highly noticeable. This supports the 
point that flame retardants delay the fire development.  
The fire development of sofa 2 and 3 are similar after they have reached 300 kW. Before 300 kW 
sofa 3 has a slower development than sofa 2. Since the sofas have the same designs and were 
ignited at approximately the same place, the difference must be the materials’ fire resistance from 
flame retardants. The foam in sofa 2 does not have any defined fire resistance, whereas the foam 
in sofa 3 is tested to crib 5 according to BS 5852 [30]. The fabric of sofa 2 is tested to DS/EN 1021 
part 1 – cigarette and sofa 3 is tested to DS/EN 1021 part 2 – match equivalent flame [25,35]. The

2.5.4.3.6 Risk of fire spread to other furniture 
The results have focused on the danger towards occupants so far, but it is also a problem if a fire 
develops into involving a whole building. In the Danish guide “Information om Brandteknisk 
Dimensionering” a threshold of 15 kW/m2 for risk of fire spread within 30 min is defined [38]. 
When looking at the irradiance from the experiments in Figure 41, it is clear that only the fire in 
sofa 2 had a large risk of spreading to other objects, because of the high irradiance achieved before 
the fire was extinguished. If the fire had not been extinguished the irradiance of about 30 kW/m2 
would most likely have continued for a longer time. 30 kW/m2 can ignited solid wood within a 
couple of minutes depending on the wood type.  
Both sofa 2 and chair 2 had measured irradiances of above 15 kW/m2, but C2 was self-
extinguishing and the risk of fire spread here was small, unless other objects were placed in close 
proximity. Sofa 3 had a lower measured level of irradiance than sofa 2 when extinguished, but this 
is most likely because the heat flux meter was covered in soot. Based on the HRR and the 
temperatures it is very likely that the irradiance from sofa 3 had the same peak irradiance as sofa 
2. Here the risk of fire spread is also large, which was also indicated by the fire spreading to the 
coffee table.

2.5.4.5 Discussion and sub conclusion 
The results have been compared in many different ways and it is essential to discuss how these 
findings can be used for determining the hazard posed by upholstered furniture in hallways in 
general.  
The experiments are conducted in a small room, where enclosure effects have been shown to 
increase the burning rate compared to a free burning [18]. This must be considered when relating 
the results to a hallway scenario, since the enclosure is relatively small compared to hallways used 
for escape. 
During the initial fire growth, until 200 kW, the enclosure effects can be ignored. This means that 
fire development seen in sofa 1 and chair 3 would be approximately the same in all surroundings. 
Chair 1 is on the threshold of being affected by the enclosure size. On the other hand, chair 2, sofa 
2 and 3 were definitely affected by the enclosure size and the results will represent a conservative 
fire development if the fires had happened in a hallway. Here it is assumed that the hallway is 
larger than the test enclosure.  
Other parameters such as temperature, smoke production and irradiance from the fire will also 
be affected. They all depend on the ventilation possibilities. In the experimental enclosure the 
opening is equivalent to a fully open door of the dimensions 0.8 m x 2.0 m. This lets smoke and 
heat escape the room through the upper part of the opening and pulls in fresh air at the floor 
which cools the enclosure. Although this is happening the effect is smaller than the energy 
released from the growing fire, and the main purpose of the opening is to supply the fire with 
more oxygen. 
There are two different ventilation scenarios for a hallway – with and without ventilation. A 
hallway without ventilation can create a ventilation controlled fire, which will limit the fire 
development and reduce the PHRR but produce more smoke. If the fire is large, it can consume 
all the oxygen in the room and die out. A hallway with ventilation are more likely to have a fuel 
controlled fire, which will reach a higher PHRR than a hallway without ventilation.  
If the volume in the hallway is so large that it can support a fuel controlled fire throughout the 
burning without the need for additional oxygen, it is irrelevant whether the hallway is ventilated 
or not. In the case of a large volume in the hallway and especially a high ceiling height, the 
enclosure effects will also be smaller and the fire development can be comparable to free burning 
experiments.  
The materials used in the hallway will affect the thermal feedback and the temperature 
development. Materials with a high thermal inertia will be able to store more heat and minimize 
the heat built up in the room which will slow the fire development down. Most hallways have a 
large thermal inertia, with materials such as brick, concrete, light weight concrete and gypsum 
board as these will meet the demand of fire resistance and spread on interior surfaces from the 
Danish building regulation. 
Chair 1 was on the boarder to create critical conditions in the small enclosure, but would probably 
not create critical conditions in a larger enclosure, such as a hallway. The low energy release from 
the chair would not be enough to heat up the surroundings. Furthermore, the smoke production 
was not large and in a larger enclosure it would only form a thin layer underneath the ceiling, 
which would not create a problem for occupants. The fire would on the other hand still give off 
the same irradiance. At a distance of 1.1 m this was 4 kW/m2 at the PHRR and at a distance of 2 m 
it was 1 kW/m2. According to the acceptance criteria occupants can pass by a fire radiating 10

kW/m2 assuming this takes less than 4 s. This means that it is fine to pass by at an even closer 
distance than 1.1 m. In conclusion it is most likely that chair 1 will not pose a threat in a larger 
enclosure. 
Chair 2 had a peak heat release rate of 500 kW, before the fire spread to the other chair. The 
acceptance criteria were exceeded before the PHRR was reached and even though the enclosure 
affected the fire development. It is doubtful that a free burning of the furniture would have led to 
a fire development that was not critical for the occupants.  
It is also important to consider the risk of fire. There is a larger risk connected to furniture with 
low fire resistance compared to furniture with high fire resistance, simply because the ignition 
sources can be smaller and still create a large fire. Sofa 2 was ignited by a small flame whereas 
sofa 3 had to be ignited by crib 7 to create a comparable fire. It would therefore be safer to place 
sofa 3 in a hallway compared to sofa 2, because it requires a large ignition source to become 
dangerous. It would not be advisable to place a large sofa in connection with an escape route, 
because it is possible to that it will result in critical conditions, which is against the Danish building 
regulation [4]. 
The furniture that could be permissible is thin cushioned upholstered furniture with a low fire 
load, below 5 cm and 300 MJ respectively. Preferably with solid plate frames and low back rests, 
as it has been reported in literature that this can lower the PHRR, which is also seen from this 
study. The design has a large influence on the complex fire dynamics when a furniture is burning 
and has to be considered when evaluating a piece of furniture. Furniture with arm rests generally 
creates a larger fire due to more thermal feedback. Furthermore, furniture with high back rest can 
result in a quick fire development because of quick flame spread upwards. 
Flame retardant materials can also reduce the risk of fire and can be advisable in spaces with a 
large fire risk. From the results they activate the warning system earlier due to a larger smoke 
production, during which more CO are produced compared to untreated materials. With an earlier 
warning the reaction time before critical conditions are reached is increased. The delay is more 
effective on HRR than smoke production.  
It has to be remembered that flame retardants do not reduce the PHRR when the entire furniture 
is on fire. Instead the PHRR is determined from many different parameters, but furniture with high 
fire loads are more likely to develop into a large fire.  
The experiments also show that both upholstered chairs and sofas can comply with the 
acceptance criteria even in small enclosures. Although chairs have an advantage to sofas, because 
they are generally smaller and can be placed apart to minimize spread to other units and thereby 
controlling the fire. It was seen in the experiments that the fire did not spread to the other chair 
in the setup with chair 1 and chair 3, with distances between the chairs of 10 and 8 cm 
respectively. 
To ensure that fire cannot spread between the furniture either a table or a larger distance can be 
added between the furniture. Tables of e.g. solid wood will need irradiance levels of 15 kW/m2 or 
more for several minutes before they ignite. This level of irradiance was not seen by the small fires 
and therefore a solid wood table can create a barrier between two upholstered furniture and 
reduce the risk of fire spread. It can also be considered to place furniture settings, e.g. two chairs 
and a table, with a safety distance between each other in a long hallway to limit the risk of fire 
spread between the settings and reduce the risk of a large fire.

3 Conclusion 
In the course of changing the Danish building regulation from a prescriptive to a performance 
based set of rules, the openness allowed for interpretation of the document, which have created 
confusion in some areas. Fire safety consultancy companies have experienced very different 
approaches from local authorities in how to define acceptable levels of fire safety in hallways, 
especially regarding furnishings. These differences are not desirable, because the fire risk can vary 
across the country. This project is therefore studying the effect on fire safety in escape routes 
furnished with various furniture. A survey distributed among selected local authorities are used 
to identify their approaches of assessing objects’ fire load and risk. Through literature the effects 
of material choice and surroundings in furniture fires are studied. In addition, the national 
regulations of fire safety regarding furniture were in Denmark, Sweden, Norway and the United 
Kingdom also studied and related to fire statistics. Based on this an experimental study was 
conducted that investigated the influence from fire load, cushion thickness, flame retardants and 
ignition sources in upholstered furniture fires in enclosures and answered the following questions: 
 
Can flame retardants, used in cover and foam materials in upholstered furniture, delay 
the fire development? 
 
How can an acceptable limit of the upholstered furniture parameters, fire load, cushion 
thickness and use of flame retardants be specified to ensure, that a piece of furniture will 
not significantly increase the fire load or risk in hallways? 
The conducted survey among the local authorities contributed to a verification of the problem 
experienced by the industry. It showed that there were regional and personal differences in how 
fire load and risk were perceived by the local authorities. Lounge furniture was rated with the 
highest fire load and the only object that most of the participants thought contributed significantly 
to the fire load and risk if placed in an escape route. Furthermore, the parameters ignitibility and 
material choice were most important to the participants when assessing the fire load and risk of 
an object.   
The parameters deemed important in the survey together with the effect of flame retardants and 
the interaction between the object and the surroundings had to be identified in the literature 
study. It was found that natural materials will char when exposed to ignition sources, whereas 
man-made materials have a tendency to melt. The melting behavior poses a large threat when the 
materials are used in upholstered furniture because it will expose the underlying material to the 
ignition source. This is not desirable and can increase the fire risk, especially if the newly exposed 
material have a lower fire resistance. Wool and wool blends were identified as the cover material 
with the best natural fire resistance. Ways to increase fire resistance is to change the density or 
thickness of the material or add flame retardants. 
Another way to ensure a high fire resistance in furniture is to regulate the area. The study of 
regulations and fire statistics revealed that strict fire safety regulations of furniture can have a 
positive effect on the fire statistics regarding fire related deaths, when compared to loosely 
defined furniture fire safety regulations. Furthermore, it is doubtful whether a requirement for 
fire resistance of upholstered furniture to a smoldering cigarette have any effects on the number 
of fire related deaths. This is mainly because many materials have natural fire resistance to this 
ignition source, and it will therefore not affect many pieces of furniture on the market. Only heavy 
cellulosic fabrics were reported to perform poorly when exposed to a smoldering cigarette.

P a g e  | 69

Since Denmark hardly has any regulations towards fire resistance in furniture, the need for 
guidelines determining the fire safety of furniture is large. From the literature study it was found 
that material choice, thickness of materials and flame retardant properties were important for the 
ignitibility and flammability of the furniture. These parameters were then studied in a full scale 
experiment that were conducted in the room corner test setup as defined in EN 9705 [61]. Here 
measurements of oxygen calorimetry, smoke production, temperature and irradiance are 
collected. It was found that flame retardants have an effect on the ignitibility of the furniture. 
Moreover, they extend the time between warning by an optical smoke detector to critical 
conditions occur. Flame retardants will also delay on the development of heat release rate longer 
than development of smoke production and give the material a larger smoke potential and result 
in a larger production of carbon monoxide during a fire.  
From the experiments it can be concluded that furniture with fire loads below 300 MJ, cushion 
thickness below 5 cm and a similar design as chair 3 and sofa 3 would be safe to place in a hallway, 
because they are far from creating critical conditions. This is not based on many experiments and 
it would be advisable to conduct more tests to see how furniture with different designs behave 
during a fire. From the study it seems that tall and narrow furniture burn faster and more violently 
than wide and low furniture.  
From this study it is concluded that individual chairs are more safe than sofas, due to a lower 
amount of combustible materials. Furthermore, chairs have to be placed very closely together to 
ignite each other during the growth phase, which limits the risk of fire spread and a hazardous fire 
development. Cushion thickness of 5 cm or less is considered to create small and manageable 
fires, that do not reach critical conditions and can be extinguished with a handheld extinguish 
device. Moreover, flame retardant furniture can be preferable when the furniture is used in a 
building with vulnerable occupants, because the chemicals can reduce the risk of a fire and 
increase the reaction time for the occupants and staff.  
This study did not have focus on the use of interliners between the foam and cover material. It 
would therefore also be interesting to study the use of interliners and their effect on fire resistance 
of furniture. Furthermore, a new branch of flame retardants based on nanotechnology have been 
developed and it would be interesting to test the effects of these compared to the regular flame 
retardants. Lastly to improve the recommendations for fire safety of furniture in hallways it would 
be interesting to look at a safety distance between furnishing groups in long hallways to minimize 
the risk of fire spread.
