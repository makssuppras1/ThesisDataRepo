Abstract
Surface water maps are very useful tools for decision and policy makers within the water
resources management area. Obtaining such maps and water body extents from remotely
sensed satellite imagery has been a complex problem to tackle in many aspects, from
the acquisition of observations to their segmentation and classiﬁcation. The increasing
reliability and time resolution of satellite data has made it possible to move from the
development of static water maps to constantly monitoring the evolution of water bodies.
This has lead to many applications such as estimating water availability, monitoring its
extent change in time, and controlling extreme events such as droughts and ﬂoods.
While multi-spectral imaging (MSI) systems are better suited for water detection, satel-
lites using these sensors have the setbacks of not being able to observe through clouds, as
well as their need for sunlight. This problem can be solved by using synthetic aperture
radar (SAR) instead. These systems have a capability of imaging regardless of night-time
and cloud-cover, which provides a great advantage for continuous reliable monitoring with-
out time-gaps. The issue with SAR imagery is that it has been seen to be more complex
to classify and to separate water pixels from land pixels. This project aims at tackling
this challenge and obtaining high-accuracy water extent maps from SAR observations. It
focuses on diﬀerent study areas in West Africa, within the operational ﬂood forecasting
and alerts in West Africa (FANFAR) project. Diﬀerent latitudes and longitudes are stud-
ied, showing diverse land-cover types surrounding the water-bodies, as well as particular
behaviours concerning seasonal water extent variation.
In order to face the challenge of water classiﬁcation from SAR imagery, diﬀerent techniques
are investigated.
Starting with a baseline approach of unsupervised thresholding, the
methodologies tested are change detection, k-means clustering, random forest classiﬁers,
support vector machines and Gaussian Naive Bayes classiﬁers. A performance assessment
is developed based on preliminary results in diﬀerent locations and a larger test in images
of three areas of interest (AOI) obtained throughout the year 2018. Random forests (RF)
and Gaussian Naive Bayes (GNB) seem to obtain the best results and are further examined
by implementing slightly modiﬁed versions including parameter tuning for RF and prior
information in a per-pixel basis for GNB. A comparison of the classiﬁcations from the latter
methods against multi-spectral imagery obtained close in time shows f1-scores over 90%
in almost all cases in two of the AOI. However, it evidences weaknesses of the techniques
in the last study area. These weaknesses are attempted to be overcome by a simple post-
processing using maximum extent water masks. Results are promising when considering
this post-processing, although the methods need further reﬁnement in order to be able to
map water bodies surrounded by all types of land-cover.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Context
Knowing the extent of water bodies close to civilization has been since ancient times of
utmost importance for humans, who rely on fresh water for most of their activities and
for communities and their environment to thrive. Fresh water availability is needed both
for rural and urban life but it is diﬃcult to track at a large scale. The increasing amount
of remote sensing data in the last decades has made this task much easier. Diﬀerent types
of sensors are well suited for the detection of water, although some need of more complex
techniques than others. Monitoring water extent change in time and controlling droughts
and ﬂoods or even using the data to try to model them are only some of the applications
that can be obtained from analysing remote sensing data over surface water.
This increase in interest for mapping the available water on Earth’s surface comes for
a reason. Freshwater in surface water bodies represents less than 1% of the totality of
water in the world (including salty water, which accounts for more than 97%) according
to Shiklomanov (1993). The rest of the water is stored underground in reservoirs that ﬁll
up at a much slower pace than they are in some places emptied, which means it is not a
resource to take for granted. The scarcity of this vital element justiﬁes for the necessity of
accurate and frequent monitoring worldwide. As human population rises, it is more and
more important to perform proper water resource management at global scale. Within
the last years, the United Nations (UN) have deﬁned the Sustainable Development Goals
(SDG), which aim at ensuring equal development of sustainable practices and clean energy
worldwide, as well as the eradication of hunger and poverty. In particular, SDG number 6
(UN, 2015) reads “Ensure availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation
for all”. This project could have an impact within these aspects as decision-makers would
obtain more accurate data of the availability of water.
Another aspect that has driven water mapping to gain momentum has been the increased
automation of all kinds of processes around the globe. From the acquisition of imagery,

through the fast cloud processing and down to the unsupervised analysis of it, the availabil-
ity both of observations and products ready to use for any type of consumer has grown ex-

have been substituted by faster, globally-scaled digital approaches. The expansion in the
technology that has made this possible has been key to implement diﬀerent techniques
such as the ones explored in this project.
An example of one of the ﬁrst missions to provide imagery from which maps of water
bodies could be produced on a global scale is NASA’s Landsat missions (Mannheimer and
Derdeyn, 1978). This is an example of a multi-spectral sensor built in a satellite. Regard-
ing imaging radar systems, the ﬁrst system sent to space for continuous environmental
monitoring was ESA’s European Remote-Sensing Satellite (ERS) mission with ERS-1
(ESA, 2020a).
Since then, the accuracy and resolution of both types of sensors have
been improving over the years. Synthetic aperture radars (SAR) have been developed
in diﬀerent bands of the microwave electromagnetic spectrum by diﬀerent space agencies
throughout the years (UNAVCO, 2019). Despite the importance of water mapping and
its broad range of uses, there has not been any satellite speciﬁcally designed to observe
and map surface water at a global scale yet.
This could change by ﬁnding a synergy
between the knowledge obtained from multi-spectral imagering (MSI), radar systems and
NASA’s new satellite mission Surface Water and Ocean Topography (SWOT) (Peral and
Esteban-Fernandez, 2018), which is expected to greatly extend the capabilities for land
hydrology (Biancamaria et al., 2016). In the case of this project, the Sentinel mission
already combining the capabilities of both SAR (Sentinel-1) and MSI (Sentinel-2) is the
main source of information.
With the technology available nowadays there are many diﬀerent approaches to mapping
water bodies accurately.
Some of them have better spatial resolution but worse time
resolution. Others sacriﬁce spatial resolution to be able to monitor more often. Diﬀerent
approaches are taken to ﬁt with the sensors obtaining the imagery, some with an added
complexity that makes it harder. As a consequence, there has not been a best practice
established for it. Nevertheless, multi-spectral imagery has often been investigated for
water extent mapping due to the high amount of information that can be extracted by
combining the diﬀerent bands. Indices such as the Normalized Diﬀerence Water Index
(NDWI, McFeeters, 1996) and its variants (Xu, 2006), or similar ones such as the Land
Surface Water Index (LSWI, Xiao et al., 2005), are examples of combinations of bands
which are particularly suitable to identify water. While MSI remains the easiest way to
map water globally, it still presents setbacks regarding the time-scales when it comes to
monitoring. Ideally, to monitor the water bodies throughout the year, a certain repetition
cycle has to be maintained. Unfortunately, MSI is highly aﬀected by lack of sunlight and
presence of clouds, thus making it diﬃcult to monitor with a consistent repetition cycle
which can be interrupted.

The other available option for global mapping is synthetic aperture radar imagery. SAR
antennas emit and receive at certain polarizations and provide either the complex return
signal or the intensity of the signal. This obviously reduces the amount of information
when compared to MSI, which makes it a more complex problem to identify any type
of land cover class (in the case of this project, water). The results of water delineation
obtained from SAR usually perform at a deprecated accuracy due to lower resolution,
lower information content and higher complexity of identiﬁcation.
Therefore, the main problem appears to be that both MSI and SAR imagery have draw-
backs when it comes to continuous water mapping. While the disadvantages of using MSI
cannot be tackled because they are related to weather or sunlight, the limitations in SAR-
based water detection should be able to be mitigated. In this project, the main goal is to
create a method that will map with a high accuracy the desired area of interest, and detect
water and non-water pixels, separating them into two classes. The detection should be
performed as unsupervised as possible, in order to make it viable for automatic monitoring
of surface water. It is also the intention of this project to do an accurate monitoring of
water with the highest possible resolution.

In order to understand the diﬀerences between the data sets used in this project, certain
background is needed regarding the types of sensors used and the observations acquired
from them.
First of all, visible and infrared imagery is covered, presenting the data
employed for both water masks and validation.

2.1
Visible and infrared imagery
Visible and infrared imagery captures the visible, near, mid, far and thermal infrared
bands of the electromagnetic spectrum. It has been commonly used for remote sensing
and mapping of planetary surfaces due to the fact that it is the spectral region of maximum
illumination by the sun (Elachi, 1987). The sun can be compared to a blackbody emitting
at an approximate temperature of 6,000 K, which as it can be seen in ﬁg. 2.1, it has its
maximum around the aforementioned bands.
The intensity of the electromagnetic waves reﬂected or emitted from the object or surface
being sensed are measured by sensors at diﬀerent wavelengths. However, when it comes
to remote sensing of planetary surface, or more precisely Earth’s surface, the waves ﬁrst
have to travel through the atmosphere. As they propagate through it, they get absorbed
in diﬀerent regions of the spectrum, also shown in ﬁg. 2.1, mainly caused by water vapour
(H2O), carbon dioxide (CO2) and oxygen in the form of ozone (O3).
The fact that
absorption occurs in some regions of the spectrum leads to a lack of transmittance in
those regions, which can be translated into a loss of information.

There are diﬀerent types of sensors which detect the radiation at these wavelengths: electro-
optical, photographic, multi and hyper-spectral. These sensors have been mounted on
UAVs, planes and ultimately brought into space by satellites.
Examples of the later
could be NASA/USGS’ Landsat mission (Short, 1982), or ESA’s Sentinel-2 mission (ESA,
2020c). These last two missions have been the most important ones in terms of quantity
and quality of data obtained, with a worldwide coverage during years.
There is yet another reason why the measurements from optical or multi-spectral imaging
could be ﬂawed or incomplete. It is that on top of the absorption by the atmosphere, some
of the radiation is reﬂected by the clouds or scattered by dust, gases and similar, or even
absorbed by the ground itself. Only a small percentage is actually reﬂected or emitted
(when referring to thermal infrared) from the surface and measured by the instruments.
However, even if a small fraction of radiation is received by the sensors, it is enough to be
able to diﬀerentiate between diﬀerent signatures from diﬀerent sources.
These signatures exist both for man-made structures such as buildings, roads and urban
infrastructures, and for biological materials such as trees and crops. Water also has its
own signature which can be diﬀerentiated from other sources. To take an example, the
chlorophyll in the plants absorbs all wavelengths while radiating back mostly in the green
and infrared wavelengths. This clear signature makes it feasible to diﬀerentiate plants
from other sources. There are other means of detecting certain types of land cover such
as indexes from the diﬀerent spectral bands. For vegetation, the Normalized Diﬀerence

2.1.1
Water masks
With the aim to provide information on water extent in a global scale of the past decades,
the European Comission’s Joint Research Center (JRC) and other contributors developed
an open-source Global Surface Water Explorer. This platform provides diﬀerent global
maps in ﬁles of 10° latitude by 10° longitude.
The diﬀerent maps are occurrence, oc-
currence change intensity, seasonality, recurrence, transitions and maximum water extent.
With these maps a lot of information on surface water can be extracted. The purpose
of delivering openly all this information is to support applications such as water resource
management, climate modelling, biodiversity conservation and food security.
These maps were obtained by using Landsat imagery and less conventional methods
(namely expert systems, visual analytics and evidential reasoning: Kartikeyan et al., 1995;
Keim et al., 2008; Yang and Xu, 2002) in order to tackle the challenge that is detecting
water throughout such an extended period of time, taking into account all the variability
that there is in the many types of water bodies to be mapped.
In the case of this project, these water extent masks were needed for labelling data points
for training and testing the diﬀerent methods and algorithms. Labelled data is of utmost
importance both for supervised methods and for preliminary accuracy tests (previous to
validation), but it is usually not readily available.
As far as this project is concerned, two diﬀerent maps of the diﬀerent study areas were
used: maximum water extent and occurrence.
Maximum water extent
The maximum water extent is derived from all 3.5 decades worth of observations by taking
each water body and ﬁnding its largest extent. This means that all that is not included in
the water class in this mask is therefore highly probably non-water, which means for the
purpose of this project: land. This is of particular interest when a high conﬁdence land
class would be needed for labelling, validation or other purposes.
As previously mentioned, in this case these water masks were used for creating labelled

Occurrence
The occurrence water mask was also derived using all observations available and then
assigning to every pixel a value between 0 and 100 corresponding to the percentage of
observations in which water was identiﬁed. This means that, to take an example, pixels
with values over 70 would have water identiﬁed in them over 70% of the time. This is of
particular interest because it means that by imposing a certain threshold to the occurrence
of water, one can obtain water pixels with a high conﬁdence. This high conﬁdence water
pixels can also be thought of as permanent water bodies which barely dry out. On the
other hand, pixels with a low occurrence can be associated with seasonal waters, mainly
depending on rain, temperature and climate.
Similarly to what was done with the maximum water extent maps to label land (or non-
water) data points, the occurrence mask was used to label water data points with a certain
conﬁdence. These pixels were then used for the training or testing of the methods. An

2.1.2
Validation data
Optical and multi/hyper-spectral images remain the best source to detect water (Pekel
et al., 2016) despite all the challenges that come with these sensors (cloud coverage or night-
time imaging not being possible). The available information from diﬀerent bands make
it possible to deﬁne a spectrum which most pixels of water will resemble, and therefore
select those with that same spectrum. Taking advantage of this, an optimum classiﬁcation
from optical images can be thought of as a reference data set against which a validation
can be performed.
For this project, the images needed to be as close as possible to the SAR imagery in time,
thus making sure that rain, drought or other meteorological events would not alter the
extent of water. The images also needed to be spread around diﬀerent seasons in order
to test the method for diﬀerent extents, backgrounds and conditions. The last obvious
condition for the images to work properly was that cloud coverage needed to be minimum
over the water body to identify, in order to make sure that the multi-spectral classiﬁcation
would be as accurate as possible.

2.2
SAR imagery
Although multi-spectral and optical imagery has been proved to work really well for water
delineation (Pekel et al., 2016), for cases such as long-term accurate monitoring of water
bodies, it is also interesting to have a data source that continuously monitors the area
of interest.
This is where microwave spectrum sensors such as SAR systems play an
important role.
Synthetic aperture radar imaging is an active remote sensing technique (Oliver, 2004).
Instead of only detecting what is naturally emitted (radiometers) or reﬂected by sunlight
(optical, multi/hyper-spectral), it emits radio waves in the microwave wavelengths and re-
ceives their return signal simultaneously. It works with similar principles as a conventional
imaging radar, only giving a ﬁner resolution due to its synthetically created larger antenna
(Oliver, 2004). The larger the antenna is, in general, the ﬁner the resolution, regardless if
it is physically or synthetically larger.
The principles of an imaging radar can be observed in ﬁg. 2.4. Taking the example of a
side-looking airborne radar (SLAR), which would be an imaging radar mounted on board
of an airplane or a satellite, its observations have two dimensions: range and along-track.
The system where the radar is mounted is moving with a certain velocity in the along-

likely to be identiﬁed as water by some methodologies. Finally, another aspect worth the
attention is the inherent noise-like feature that appears in radar imaging, called speckle.
2.2.1
Speckle
Speckle is not actual noise in images, but a pattern of both constructive and destructive
interference from the returned signal from the scatterers on the ground. At any time, the
system receives the backscatter from a series of scatterers in a resolution cell. All these
signals add coherently, which means that they can add both constructively or destructively
depending on the phases of these signals. This variation is then shown in the image as
inhomogeneities in supposedly homogeneous areas. The fact that the scatterers are not
exactly the same from one cell to the other makes SAR a system very sensitive to speckle
(Moreira et al., 2013).

Pre-processing
The pre-processing is an important step before actually performing the classiﬁcation of
water bodies on SAR imagery. Part of this pre-processing is already carried out before
downloading the observations. Every image acquired by the satellites have diﬀerent pro-
cessing levels available. In the case of the images used for this study, all images are in the
Interferometric Wide Swath (IW), high resolution Ground Range Detected (GRD) mode.
This would be considered a Level-1 product, where Level-0 would mean the raw data, and
Level-2 would be further processed products (in the case of Sentinel-1, products observing
the ocean). GRD products consist of focused SAR data that has been detected, multi-
looked and projected to ground range using an Earth ellipsoid model. Phase information is
lost. The resulting product has approximately square spatial resolution pixels and square
pixel spacing with reduced speckle at the cost of worse spatial resolution (ESA, 2020b).

For the radiometric calibration step, where three diﬀerent coeﬃcients can be obtained, the
decision was taken based on the notes from ESRI (2019):
• Gamma nought is typically used when calibrating the antenna. Since each range
cell is equally distant from the satellite, near range and far range are equally bright,
which is helpful in determining the antenna pattern in the output data set.
• Beta nought produces a data set containing the radar brightness coeﬃcient, which is
the ratio between the power transmitted and received by the antenna. It is related
to slant range, and is dimensionless.
• Sigma nought calibrates the backscatter returned to the antenna from a unit area
on the ground, and is related to ground range. Because the imagery is calibrated, it
can be directly compared with diﬀerent radar images collected from the same or a
diﬀerent sensor. Scientists tend to use sigma nought to interpret surface scattering
and reﬂection, and surface properties. Therefore, sigma nought was chosen for this
study.

Chapter 3
Literature review: Water extent
mapping
Water can be detected using the diﬀerent sensors previously explained. This has been
therefore largely used for many applications and using many diﬀerent methodologies as
technology advancement has made it possible. However, it has not always been like that.
Although the presence of water has been of utmost importance for civilization to thrive
since humanity existed, the opportunity to know with high accuracy the quantity of water
available has never existed until very recently. Satellite imagery of diﬀerent kinds has made
it possible for scientists all around the world to start mapping water bodies, starting by
the most extensive ones due to its considerable simplicity. For example, global mapping
of water bodies has already been done at coarser resolutions: Bartholomé and Belward
(2005); Carroll et al. (2009).
Particularly surface water extent mapping has in the recent years caught the attention of
both the larger enterprises that provide most of the imagery (NASA or ESA) and smaller
tech companies that develop their own constellations of satellites. A clear example of this
interest by larger enterprises would be the upcoming launch of NASA’s mission Surface
Water and Ocean Topography (SWOT) (Peral and Esteban-Fernandez, 2018). An example
of the latter is the constellation delivered by ICEYE, which as assessed by Antropov et al.
(2018), provides the opportunity to monitor ﬂoods and map land cover/use. We know
now thanks to this increasing interest in surface water mapping that inland water bodies
represent less than 4% of the Earth’s land surface (Downing et al., 2012, 2006).
Indeed water mapping has many applications. Knowing the availability of water for urban
or rural areas, although important, is not the only concern for both the science community
and companies or governments. Natural disasters is one of the main reasons for studying
water coverage and behaviour. Flood or drought monitoring have largely been studied
with the aim to help emergency response management and minimizing the damages to the
aﬀected areas. Although some purposes like ﬂood detection need methods with slightly

diﬀerent approaches, such as change detection, in comparison to regular mapping of water
bodies, with techniques such as classiﬁcation, in general the outcomes obtained are simi-
lar. The interpretation of the outcome taking into account the methodology used is the
important part when it comes to understanding the results.
The most commonly used method when surface water detection in SAR images is con-
cerned is thresholding. It is the simplest method of image segmentation which separates
the image between two clearly deﬁned classes, also known as binarizing the image. Thresh-
olding, as the name already implies, consists of selecting a threshold for pixel intensity that
will binarize the pixels depending on if the intensity is lower or greater than this thresh-
old. This simple approach is also very computationally eﬃcient, as no complex equations
are needed in order to perform it. The most basic thresholding methods simply select a
threshold after inspection of the histogram of pixel intensity. Due to the obvious level of su-
pervision, this method is practically not used. Instead, one of the concepts that is broadly
used is the Otsu threshold, named after Otsu (1979), who developed an unsupervised ver-
sion of thresholding. This method is applied to a certain polarization, and it is interesting
to note that in recent literature it has been found that cross-polarization (either VH or
HV) is usually better suited for segmentation of open-water and non-open-water due to
a larger contrast between these two classes (Cutler et al., 2015). Many diﬀerent versions
of thresholding, including a multi-dimensional approach and other variations have been
applied throughout the years, and it is still being used nowadays (e.g. Hardy et al., 2019).
Some inconveniences come with thresholding SAR imagery, such as the erroneous identi-
ﬁcation as water bodies of dark areas due to shadowing eﬀects, snow absorbing radiation
or even speckle.
Particularly in this type of methodologies, in images where the class to be identiﬁed is
underrepresented, most of the common thresholding methods fail to binarize the image
due to the lack of a bimodality in the histogram that characterizes the observation. This
drawback can be overcome by selecting tiles of the image where both classes to be separated
are represented with a similar frequency. This can be achieved through methods such as
the one proposed in Chini et al. (2017). The advantage of this method is the automatic
identiﬁcation of the region of interest and the adaptation of the tile size, in contrast
with other methods which were also automatic but with a priori set tile size (Bovolo and
Bruzzone, 2007). The latter approach can be a problem when applying the same algorithm
to diﬀerent areas as diﬀerent distributions and backgrounds may be found.
On the other hand, change detection is another very commonly used method for water
mapping, although with slightly diﬀerent applications such as time series analysis or ﬂood
detection. In general, change detection is mostly used for monitoring the changes in water
bodies, thus as a temporal trajectory analysis. By analysing long stacks of observations
trends and models can be obtained so that variability in certain aspects such as climate,
ﬂoods, rain season and many more can be taken into account. These techniques may also

be used to detect changes in shorter time scales, even when comparing only two images.
A great advantage of change detection is that it can be applied both to optical and SAR
data, although implemented rather diﬀerently. An example of an implementation of a
change detection-based algorithm to optical data is that carried out by Oliveira et al.
(2019), which is called Multi-Index Image Diﬀerencing Method (MINDED) and aims at
estimating ﬂood extent. The method MINDED is presented as an improvement to current
approaches to detecting water bodies mostly from ﬂoods. It uses the sensitivity of certain
indices derived from satellite images and it is based on a change detection method. From
optical data several indices can be calculated and a clear diﬀerentiation of water can be
done by taking a look at the behaviour of NIR-SWIR simultaneously with visible data. A
peak in blue-green is expected for ﬂoods together with a high absorption in SWIR. The
conditions vary a lot depending on many aspects though. As it is widely known, even
though the number of optical satellite constellations have increased, it is still not the
most reliable data set as they completely depend on cloud coverage and sunlight. That is
why solutions concerning microwave data (such as SAR) are being currently studied and
exploited. Their almost all-weather, day and night imaging capabilities make it the most
reliable source of data for ﬂood monitoring due to its urgency.
An approach using a more complex change-detection method on SAR imagery is presented
by Canty et al. (2019). This article is one of the few demonstrations of how GEE’s Sentinel-
1 archive can be used for change detection purposes (Kumar and Mutanga, 2019; Gorelick
et al., 2017).
The original Wishart-distribution based change detection algorithm was
designed to work for covariance matrices with p = 2 and p = 3, which means dual and full
polarization. However, in GEE the dual polarization covariance matrices from Sentinel-1
only include the diagonal elements, as the complex oﬀdiagonal elements are not available
in the archive. In Nielsen et al. (2019) and Nielsen (2019) modiﬁcations to the method are
carried out. This method is based on a test statistic, also known as omnibus test, which
can be factored into a sequence of test statistics in order to test if the change occurred in
the j-th observation (for long stacks of images).

algorithms, classiﬁcation can be applied to both optical and radar images.

training area either manually or by generating an AOI polygon input, the method can
detect all pixels having similar characteristics in all the multi/hyper spectral bands, thus
classifying them as the same class and rejecting the rest of the pixels to the other class.
There are other techniques that cannot be included in either of the aforementioned cate-
gories, as they use methods that combine them in diﬀerent ways, such as Hakdaoui and
Emran (2019), where they combine optical and radar data for a study in Morocco. In this
study ﬂash ﬂoods which occurred in an area near a city of Morocco from October 27 to
28, 2016 are analysed by combining the use of seven optical and radar images obtained
before and after the event. In order to obtain the water masks from optical data, a com-
bination of indexes that give information on soil moisture/dryness were employed. Even
though turbid and muddy ﬂoodwater has a diﬀerent reﬂectance from normal water, it can
be extracted using this combination of bands. Albedo, Normalized Diﬀerence Water In-
dex (NDWI), Normalized Multi-band Drought Index (NMDI) and Normalized Diﬀerence
Moisture Index (NDMI) are principal indices used for characterization of moisture/dry-
ness land change. All indices are normalized. Change detection is carried out between the
images before and after the event, considering the changes in the aforementioned indexes.
The combination of both types of data in this paper (Hakdaoui and Emran, 2019) ba-
sically consists of separately getting information from each source. From SAR data the
water extent can be calculated by means of segmentation using a given threshold. From
optical data accurate maps are developed and the index extraction is performed in order
to correctly interpret the images. Optical images are then compared to SAR images as
both of them are co-registered.

systematic errors were found due mainly to seasonal variations, environmental conditions,

Chapter 4
Methods
The aim of this chapter is to give an overview of the chosen methods to be investigated
during this project. As it has been shown in the previous chapter, there are many possibil-
ities for water mapping from satellite imagery, and new approaches are constantly being
studied and improved. While many of them consist in the use of data which depends
on sunlight and cloud cover (optical and multi/hyper-spectral), there are also many tech-
niques available for trying to identify water in radar (SAR) images. Some of these methods
have served as a baseline for the diﬀerent attempts tried during this project. This chapter
thus provides a theoretical overview of these methods as well as preliminary performance
tests for some of them.
4.1
Thresholding
Thresholding was chosen as a ﬁrst approach to surface water detection. Thresholding,
as the name itself implies, consists in selecting a threshold for pixel intensity that will
binarize the pixels depending on if the intensity is lower or greater than this threshold.
This simple approach is also very computationally eﬃcient, as no complex equations are
needed in order to perform it.
This methodology can be of particular interest when
delineating water bodies. Water is known to have a dominant specular reﬂection of the
electromagnetic waves sent by the radar, which yields a low backscatter coeﬃcient for most
of the water covered areas. However, wind, ﬂooded vegetation or partially inundated areas
can produce a higher return signal that can be misinterpreted as land. Still, thresholding
appeared as a simple technique that could be seen as a baseline classiﬁer of water. Having
in mind that the process had to be repeated for several images at diﬀerent time-points and
in many diﬀerent areas, a simple manual thresholding by histogram inspection was not a
feasible option. Instead, one of the concepts that is broadly used is the Otsu threshold,
named after Otsu (1979), who developed an unsupervised version of thresholding.

In order to improve the performance and overcome some of these limitations, two ap-
proaches were tried. The ﬁrst one tried to tackle the need for a bimodality in the distribu-
tion of the image pixels. Any satellite image covering both water and land should have two
diﬀerentiated maxima in their distributions corresponding to the diﬀerence in backscatter
intensity received from both classes (water and land). These maxima, however, can go
unnoticed if the ratio of pixels between the class with less representation in the image and
the class with more representation is too small. As the intention is to map water bodies
in land, it is usually the land class that predominates in the images. So if we take the
ratio Nwater/Nland where Ni is the number of pixels for each class i, and the result is too
low, the bimodality will go undetected and therefore the method will not work properly.
To overcome this issue, tiling of the images is a good solution.
Tiling consists in dividing the image in smaller patches of a certain number of pixels (X
pixels in one direction by Y pixels in the other direction, X-by-Y). After tiling, there
is a higher probability that the tiles where there is water will have a more balanced
representation of both classes, considering water is the minority class, as it will usually
be the case. In the tiles with no water the method will fail to binarize properly, but in
the tiles with water it will more accurately identify the bimodality and select a correct
threshold. To test this method, a polygon very close to the area of interest was drawn
and imported as Well-Known-Text (WKT) coordinates. Each tile, which would have its
coordinates assigned, was checked if it intersected the AOI. In case it did, the method
was applied. In case it did not, then the background SAR image was the output. This
way the problem of getting areas only covering non-water areas was mostly avoided. After
thresholding or outputing the background image for every tile, mosaicking was performed
in order to obtain the resulting image (ﬁg. 4.4). For this test case, tiles of 2000 by 2000
pixels were used. Manually changing the tiles is not the ideal case as it is something that in
order to obtain automation for diﬀerent areas to work should not be done, but for testing
the algorithm it served the purpose.
To improve the tiling method, in every tile it should be tested whether there is a bimodality
in order to know if the thresholding method should or should not be applied. There are
certain techniques for testing the bimodality of a histogram (e.g. Cataño and Climent,
2012).
Another way to improve it is the approach that Chini et al. (2017) took of a
hierarchical split-based tiling method, where each tile could in its turn be split into many
tiles until bimodality was found or discarded. Although it is an interesting approach that
could have been tried, other methods with diﬀerent ways of tackling the weaknesses of the
thresholding approach were tried.

4.3
Machine Learning methods
Machine learning has gained importance in many aspects of image analysis as well as it has
done in many other subjects. A clear example is the application for image classiﬁcation
and segmentation (e.g. Maity, 2016). Machine learning is the study of algorithms that can
make computers take certain decisions based on information given to them in the form
of sample data, also called training data (Bishop, 2009). These decisions or predictions
taken by computers after a “learning” process from the sample data make it an incredibly
useful tool to analyse images. There are many types of algorithms within machine learning
that can be used for the purpose of this project, and some of them have been tested and
are explained below. It is important to diﬀerentiate between supervised and unsupervised
methods. Supervised learning (Norvig and Russell, 2010) occurs when training a model
with sets of labelled data.
The model adapts depending on the diﬀerent features and
structures that the data might have once labelled. Unsupervised learning (Hinton and
Sejnowski, 1999), on the other hand, occurs when algorithms ﬁnd patterns or clusters
within the data and then dividing those into the desired classes. This second group of
methodologies is data-driven in the sense that the data itself is what drives the “learning”,
instead of forcing it with labelled data.
An important part of the development of these methods is the division of the data into
training data set and test data set, which includes the labelling and the cleaning of the
data.
This is included previous to the explanation of the methods themselves, but is
applied to each of them. Then, supervised methods are explored (i.e. Random Forests,
Support Vector Machines and Naive Bayes), and following them comes K-Means, as an
example of an unsupervised method used mainly for comparison.

4.3.2
Random Forest Classiﬁer
In order to understand random forest classiﬁers one has to ﬁrst know its basis. The model
is called a forest because it is made of trees, realistically enough. The trees are called
decision trees, and are formed of branches and leaves just like any other tree. Decision
trees are already a machine learning model themselves, and can be applied on their own
for a classiﬁcation problem.
Decision tree classiﬁers, ﬁrst described by Breiman (1984) try to ﬁnd patterns in the
features of data points. They are supervised machine learning models, which means that
they are created from a training set of labeled data.
Creating the tree is where the
learning in machine learning happens. The decision tree is built up by splitting the data
into diﬀerent branches recursively until it gets to the leaves, which are the node resulting
from the last split performed on the data. The splits into diﬀerent branches or leaves
are not random, but they depend on the information gain obtained from executing that
separation.
Commonly, to perform these splits the entropy of the parent and the possible child nodes
is taken into account. The entropy (E, eq. (4.10), where p and q are the fractions of the
two classes) is a measure of the homogeneity of the diﬀerent nodes. A completely homo-
geneous node will have an entropy of 0 and therefore be considered a leaf. A completely
heterogeneous node (50% of each class, in a binary tree) will have an entropy of 1. When-
ever entropy is larger than 0 a split must still be performed to achieve entropy of 0. The
decision on which split to take depends on the information gain that is obtained from a
parent node to its child node, which is calculated as the diﬀerence in entropies:
E = −p log2 p −q log2 q.
(4.10)
The largest diﬀerence corresponds to the largest information gain and is the split to per-
form. The information gain can also be deﬁned as the diﬀerence between the impurity
of the data before and after the split. This impurity is quantiﬁed by means of a concept
known as Gini impurity. To ﬁnd the Gini impurity, one has to apply the formula shown
in eq. (4.11). IG is the Gini impurity, i are the diﬀerent labels being i = 1, ..., k and k the
total number of diﬀerent labels. Ntotal is the total number of datapoints in the branch/leaf:

While the Gini impurity is an important parameter to take into account when building
decision trees, it is also worth taking into account the statistical signiﬁcance of such
measure. In other words, two diﬀerent leaves could both be purely one class, even though
one of them would only contain one datapoint and the other one would contain one hundred
datapoints. Now if only one datapoint from another class were to be included in both
leaves, one leaf would lose half of its purity, while the other one would lose less than
1% of its purity. Decision trees will not stop splitting the data until either purity or no
information gain is obtained, which makes these classiﬁers prone to overﬁtting (Sklearn,
2019c). In order to avoid this problem, pruning is a commonly used technique in decision
trees. Pruning consists in forcing the tree not to split anymore once a certain numbers
of splits have been performed, or once a minimum number of datapoints reach one node,
gaining in statistical signiﬁcance, computing time and reducing overﬁtting.
Sometimes pruning is not enough. This is why random forest represents an improvement on
this method. A random forest is an ensemble machine learning technique, which contains
many decision trees that work together to classify new points (Breiman, 2001). When
a random forest classiﬁes a new point, it gives that point to each of the decision trees.
Each of them report their classiﬁcation and the random forest returns the most common
classiﬁcation. Some of the trees in the random forest may be overﬁtting, but by making
the prediction based on a large number of trees, overﬁtting will have less of an impact.

4.3.3
Support Vector Machines
A Support Vector Machine (SVM) is a powerful supervised machine learning model used
for classiﬁcation developed by Cortes and Vapnik (1995). SVMs make classiﬁcations by
deﬁning a decision boundary and then seeing what side of the boundary an unclassiﬁed
point falls on. These boundaries are deﬁned by using a training set of labeled points.
The decision boundary in two dimensions is a line. Decision boundaries exist when your
data has more than two features as well. If there are three features, the decision boundary
is a plane instead of a line.
As the number of dimensions grows past 3, it becomes
very diﬃcult to visualize these points in space. Nonetheless, SVMs can still ﬁnd a decision
boundary. However, rather than being a separating line, or a separating plane, the decision
boundary is called a separating hyperplane.
The support vectors are the points in the training set closest to the decision boundary. In
fact, these vectors are what deﬁne the decision boundary. The distance between a support
vector and the decision boundary is called the margin. Because the support vectors are so
critical in deﬁning the decision boundary, many of the other training points can be ignored.
This is one of the advantages of SVMs. Many supervised machine learning algorithms use
every training point in order to make a prediction, even though many of those training
points are not relevant.
In the linearly separable case, the margin is deﬁned as the region between two hyperplanes
that maximize the separation of the two classes, also called a hard-margin.
And the
hyperplane that lies in the middle of these two is called the maximum-margin hyperplane.
In order to understand how the two hyperplanes are deﬁned, it can be imagined as a
two-dimensional problem for visualization. This can be visualized in ﬁg. 4.19. As a result,
outliers can be a problem. The size of the margin decreases when a single outlier is present,
and as a result, the decision boundary changes as well.

4.3.5
K-Means
K-Means is a clustering technique (Forgy, 1965), which means it is an unsupervised ma-
chine learning method. In this case, the method does not make use of labelled data but
infers the patterns from the data itself. It aims at classifying the given observations into
the desired number of classes. These classes are considered clusters, as they are determined
by identifying the nearest amongst the classes’ means.
The means, also called cluster centers or centroids, are initialized with a subset of the data.
The initialization of the method is a careful step to take into account as convergence de-
pends on it. In the case of this project, the initialization was performed using what is
called k-means++ algorithm developed by Arthur and Vassilvitskii (2007). This initializa-
tion uses a randomized seeding technique, which improves both speed and accuracy of the
method according to Arthur and Vassilvitskii (2007). Once the clusters are initialized, the
k-means algorithm aims at minimizing the within-cluster sum of squares of the distances
from each datapoint to the centroid of the class assigned to it. This is done by iteratively
recalculating cluster centers and distances until convergence is achieved. The distances
mostly used are the Euclidean distances.

Results and Discussion
This chapter aims at providing results on the diﬀerent methods explained in the previous
chapter and to discuss and assess which of them perform faster, more reliably, more
robustly and in diﬀerent conditions.
The only method that was ruled out of further tests was the one based on the change
detection using a test statistic based on the Wishart distribution that the covariance
matrices of the pixels of the image follow. This was due to the fact that the processing
needed to perform this method is diﬀerent, which would induce a bias in the comparison.
Furthermore, the preliminary results of the method previously explained did not seem as
promising as those of the rest of the methods. Due to this added complexity it was decided
to test all the machine learning algorithms and the Otsu threshold, the latter serving as a
baseline or starting point to compare with all the rest.
In the ﬁrst place, the results obtained are presented for an analysis using the aforemen-
tioned techniques for the same SAR observations spanning the whole year 2018 in three
diﬀerent regions of West Africa. The AOIs and dates used are summarized in Chapter 2.
In order to assess accurately the viability of every method and be able to compare them,
the same data needed to be used for all methods. Following that purpose, the same subset
of pixels for training and testing was used for every image analysed. The selection of train-
ing and testing datasets used for this case is very similar to the one previously explained
in section 4.3.1. In order to assess the accuracy of the methods after training, the labelled
dataset was split into training and testing data. 50% of the data was used for training the
models and the remaining 50% was used for testing them.
For this case, every method was tried without any added complexity, with the default
parameters for their Python implementation. This means that Random Forests had no
constraints on the parameters except for the number of estimators, which was 100 (Sklearn,
2019c). Gaussian Naive Bayes was applied without any prior information given (Sklearn,
2019a). Support vector machines were applied with the default C and γ parameters for

In both studies the way to assess the accuracy is by using the so-called f1-score. Among
the many metrics that were available for analysing the behaviour of the algorithms, f1-
score was simple enough while providing a good understanding on how good the ﬁt is.
It is one of the many metrics that can be obtained from the confusion matrix (ﬁg. 5.1).
While accuracy or balanced accuracy can give a more intuitive idea of how the algorithm
behaves, it can also lead to biases. For data sets such as the ones being used for this
project, with one class clearly predominating in the images (land) for most cases, both
accuracy and balanced accuracy can give high scores without actually performing that
well. An example of this can be an image with 80% of pixels being land. If the image
was to be analysed with the accuracy score, and the classiﬁcation would show all pixels
as land, the accuracy would still be up to 80%, which by itself does not look like a bad
result. On the other hand, when a classiﬁcation of the same image shows very good results
over water but a certain amount of misclassiﬁcations over land, a high score is shown by
a balanced accuracy, as the separated accuracy of the water class takes the same weight
as the one from the land class. Among other things, f1-score takes care of the weight of
unbalanced classes and overall classiﬁcation results.

Otsu thresholding. Considering the previous tests performed using Otsu thresholding,
the approach taken in order to properly compare to the rest of the methods was the
baseline of thresholding the histogram of the whole image, instead of the tiling version of

Random Forest. The implementation of this algorithm can be tuned for many param-
eters, but in order to test the basic version in comparison to the rest of the techniques,
the default parameters were used. The most important one is the size of the forest, which
was 100 trees. Sometimes it might not be necessary to have such a large number of es-

computation time. Other than the number of estimators, other parameters such as depth
of the trees, minimum samples of leaves and the way to decide the split were left to be
either unconstrained for the two former and the default one for the latter (which was the
Gini information criteria).
Support Vector Machine. Two diﬀerent implementations were tried in this case. The
ﬁrst implementation was a soft-margin linear kernel SVM, which would have the parameter
deﬁning the softness of the margin as an input left as the default one (C = 1). The second
implementation consists of an approach employing a radial basis function (RBF) kernel.
This kernel transforms the data points into inﬁnite dimensions and looks for a boundary
that ﬁts more or less the data according to the input gamma parameter. In the case for
the default implementation, γ = 1.
K-Means. K-means is an unsupervised method and as such the labelled data was not
needed. However, the same training data used for the other methods to build the classiﬁers
was used to initialize this classiﬁer, instead of using the whole image which would have
taken more time and computation power.
Iterations were left unconstrained and the
method to initiate the clusters was the default which was k-means++, which as previously
explained in general outperforms the rest of initialization techniques.
Gaussian Naive Bayes. Naive Bayes itself used without prior knowledge can be consid-
ered a Maximum Likelihood Estimator as previously pointed. The implementation using
Gaussian distributions for both classes, also called Gaussian Naive Bayes was the one
chosen for this implementation, without taking into account any prior information as it is

5.2.2
Prior information in Naive Bayes
Naive Bayes’ implementation in Python is a maximum likelihood estimator for which the
prior information can only be included as an input vector of two parameters, one per class
in a binary classiﬁcation such as the one performed for this case. If prior information in a
per-pixel basis has to be included in order to improve the classiﬁcation from this algorithm,
the implementation had to be programmed and adapted to allow for prior information to
be multiplied by the likelihood. The prior information used for the tests is the probabilities
obtained from the previous analysed image.
The ideal implementation is not the one that is shown in the following section, as there is
approximately one image every month. The use of a previous image with a large temporal
gap with the one being analysed can induce much more error as water extents might have
changed during that time due to weather and seasons. In areas where the water area
extent is similar for all cases it proves useful despite the time gap, whereas in areas where
there is a high variation of water extent, it is seen as a source of misclassiﬁcations.
Instead, the best practice when using this version of the Gaussian Naive Bayes is to use
the previous observation closest in time when analysing any image. Even for areas with
high variations in water extent, the classiﬁcations will most likely still beneﬁt from this
approach as the changes in the water bodies usually have slower time scales. Special cases
of ﬂash ﬂoods or heavy rains might have a negative eﬀect on the outcome of a classiﬁcation
using this approach. However, that is unlikely enough so as to still consider it.

5.2.4
Post-processing
In some cases the results have shown to be lacking accuracy mainly over land, where many
pixels are wrongly identiﬁed as water. This is mainly due to low backscatter in areas other
than the water bodies, which are confused with water. These low backscatter pixels might
be due to areas which are surrounding the lake and are either partially ﬂooded or covered
by vegetation, or areas with bare land or features in land which exhibit specular reﬂection.
This makes the water mapping task harder as larger water bodies seem to be identiﬁed
rather than their actual extent. In order to improve these methodologies without further
tuning or without having to change them, an approach with a very simple post-processing
is proposed.
78
Water extent mapping from classiﬁcation of SAR imagery in West Africa
==PAGE:95==
In order to ensure that the pixels over land which belong with high certainty to this class
are identiﬁed as they should, the maximum water extent masks previously used for the
selection of training data can be again exploited for this purpose. Once the classiﬁcation
is obtained, the maximum extent water masks can be multiplied by the classiﬁcation map.
Because both of them are binary maps, with water being identiﬁed 1 and land being
identiﬁed as 0, once the multiplication is done the classiﬁcation results are not altered
wherever there could be water, i.e. within the maximum extent of the water body. On
the other hand, wherever there is a high conﬁdent land-pixel in the water mask, that is
outside the maximum extent of the water body, the result of the multiplication is 0, which
will automatically change all wrongly identiﬁed pixels from 1 to 0. This methodology
might look aggressive as many pixels are completely discarded from being identiﬁed as
water, which could rule out events like ﬂoods or overﬂows. The purpose of this project
is not to ﬁnd about such events, although it should be able to identify them in case they
happened. Therefore, even though it might represent an improvement in some cases, it is
still a limitation that ought to be overcome with other techniques to be further explored.
Other techniques were tested for post-processing but showed some weaknesses in compar-
ison to the one chosen. The ﬁrst of them was a smoothing of the image where areas with
low density of pixels would be converted to the majority class. The inconvenient with this
technique is that for every image the window to check should be diﬀerent and adapt to it,
which adds a lot of complexity to the post-processing. However, it could be a promising
step to try if it was further investigated. The other methodology tried for improving the
results was to use erosion and dilation of the water masks in order to ﬁll these unho-
mogeneities. This last technique would blur and alter the edges of the water body. For
example, the Speckle ﬁlter chosen to be used was a Lee ﬁlter, which was adopted in order
to preserve edges.

6.1
Reference water maps
Among many techniques available in the literature, Nielsen and Müller (2012b) explains
a method based on an iterated version of the canonical discriminant analysis (CDA) in
which an optimal separation between classes is obtained. CDA aims at ﬁnding projections
in the feature space in order to ﬁnd maximal separation between classes. The code is
available for Matlab and has been used to obtain the reference water maps against which
the results are compared and scored. Even though the paper deals with hyper-spectral
data, the method can be applied to multi-spectral data instead. Sentinel-2 red, green,
blue, NIR, SWIR1 and SWIR2 bands are used for the MSI approach.
The algorithm works by ﬁrst selecting manually (or by imputing a range of pixels) a
polygon over the desired class to be separated from the rest; in the case of this project,
it is water. Then new training areas are iteratively selected by thresholding the canonical
variate (CV, in the case of two classes there is only one) obtained from the previous
iteration. A measure of the goodness of the ﬁt is obtained from the canonical correlation.
When this canonical correlation stops improving, the iterations stop.
For the case of one of the Sentinel-2 images obtained in Shiroro in 2019, the workﬂow of the
algorithm used can be understood by taking a look at the intermediate results obtained
in ﬁg. 6.1 and ﬁg. 6.2. In the former the ﬁrst iteration is observed, with the training area

6.2
Analysis of validation results
Once the reference water maps were obtained with the aforementioned technique, Sentinel-
1 images corresponding to a date within 3 days (either before or after) of the acquisition
of the Sentinel-2 observations were classiﬁed using Random Forest and Gaussian Naive
Bayes (with and without priors).
Knowing from the previous tests about the importance for accurate training data sets,
the implementation for the validation of the ﬁnal methods was tailored to each AOI. The
threshold for the occurrence water mask, which is what controls the conﬁdence of the
water pixels in the training data, was set to a value high enough to ensure very reliable
water pixels. This value was decided by visual inspection of the water masks thresholded
at diﬀerent levels. It has to be taken into account that this choice can be biased and not
completely reliable. This value is diﬀerent for every AOI, and was decided after inspection
of the water masks:
• For the case of Shiroro lake, a value of 92% was used. After having studied images
for this AOI over the whole year of 2018 in the previous section and some images
spread through 2019 in this section, it is clear that this water body exhibits a high
variation in water extent. That is why a high threshold is needed to ensure good
performance of the algorithms throughout the year.
• For the case of Bagré lake, the same value of 70% was used as it was done for the
previous tests. This is because if the value was increased as little as 5%, the number
of pixels dramatically decreased to showing only a very small area of the lake, which
was clearly not enough.
• For the case Guiers lake, a value of 95% was used. Not much variation is seen for the
water extent in this case, which makes it possible to increase the threshold without
compromising the availability of pixels over water. Having this high threshold en-
sures having mostly the pixels over the large water body instead of the river, which
seem to be more reliably water in all SAR images.
Another aspect that was changed as compared to the previous studies was the increase
in sampled data from the water class up to 5%, which also increased the sampled data
from land respecting the balancing of the classes as previously explained. This increased
the computation time as more pixels were used for training. However, it made the model
more resilient to diﬀerences within the classes and unhomogeneities. The rest was applied
as previously explained with the last modiﬁcations carried out for both Random Forest,
with the tuning of the input parameters, and Gaussian Naive Bayes, with the use of prior
information.
Once the classiﬁcations were obtained, they were compared to the water maps derived
from Sentinel-2 imagery. In order to be able to identify the correctly classiﬁed pixels but
at the same time to distinguish between comission and omission errors, or false positives

Chapter 7
Conclusions & Recommendations
To sum it up, this study shows how it could be possible to monitor large water bodies in
West Africa with a ﬁner temporal resolution than the one obtained with multi-spectral
systems by using SAR systems instead. Avoiding the issues related to cloud cover and
incapability of night time imaging would allow to monitor water bodies in a timely manner
even during the rainy season, obtaining information on water extent changes which are very
important both for water resource management and for ﬂood risk assessment. However,
there are certain limitations that arise. These limitations and the potential next steps are
underlined in this ﬁnal chapter. First of all, however, a summary of the performances of
the methodologies presented is drawn.
7.1
Performance summary
When all methodologies are considered, it is important to note that the change detection
approach was never compared to the rest of techniques due to diﬀerences within the pre-
processing, as well as the impossibility to obtain one water mask per image analysed. This
was due to the fact that many images are needed in order to perform a multi-temporal
change detection scheme, from which only one mask could be derived in a period between
one and two months.
The rest of methodologies were tested and compared on the same sets of images and
the results were presented in this report.
The diﬀerent implementations had diﬀerent
complexities, starting by the simplest method based on an unsupervised thresholding
approach. Even though it obtained high scores in some cases when tested against a subset
of the pixels of the images analysed, there was a clear decrease in accuracy when water
and land classes could not be diﬀerentiated in a pixel intensity histogram.
When the
VH polarization image, which was the one used for selecting and applying the threshold,
had a clear bimodality separating water pixels with low backscatter from land pixels
with high backscatter, the method achieved similar scores to the ones obtained by more
complex approaches. However, when a less clear separation existed, or even when the
Water extent mapping from classiﬁcation of SAR imagery in West Africa
97
==PAGE:114==
histogram appeared unimodal, this method clearly failed to separate water from land. An
important aspect to conclude from this project regarding the separation between classes is
that speckle ﬁltering was seen to improve the results by amplifying the diﬀerence between
water and land pixels. Considering that the aim was to be able to assess the water extent
of water bodies, the need for speckle ﬁltering with edge preservation was clear.
Using the thresholding technique as a baseline to compare with, it has been seen how
the other unsupervised method (K-Means) would achieve better results by including not
only VV polarization, but also the indexes and textures derived as further information.
The clustering algorithm commits less errors in general, although it has also been seen to
perform worse in some particular cases, usually related to images with a better separation
of water and land in the VH polarization image, while having a poorer separation in the
VV polarization. In general unsupervised methods still commit more errors than the rest
of the techniques tested, which are all supervised algorithms. It is unfortunate because
the labelling of data is not easy in large-scale image analysis like it has been attempted in
this project. Therefore, it would be ideal to have an unsupervised method performing at
high scores, competitive to the ones obtained from the supervised approaches.
The rest of supervised methods all seem to perform at high accuracies. A setback has been
found for Support Vector Machines, which seem to show a large variation in computation
time when compared to Gaussian Naive Bayes and Random Forest Classiﬁers. That is why
the methods that have been further explored have been the latter two. These supervised
methods seem to achieve the best results, although they highly depend on the training data.
When potentially incorrect labels are assigned to pixels used for training, both RF and
GNB commit more misclassiﬁcations, even performing visually worse than unsupervised
algorithms (K-Means), in some cases. Slightly modiﬁed versions of RF, including pruning
techniques and an assessment of the number of estimators, and of GNB, including prior
information in a per-pixel basis, have been tested against a reference data set of water
classiﬁcation from multi-spectral imagery.
The scores obtained for the ﬁnal methods have been found to be very high (over 90% most
of the times) for two of the study areas: Bagré and Shiroro lakes. It has been noted from
the results that the classiﬁcation performance most likely depends on the land-cover and
terrain characteristics of the surroundings of the water body. For the other area, Guiers
lake, much lower scores have been found prior to a post-processing step that has been
carried out with the JRC water masks also used for labelling data. This post-processing,
in general not much needed in the other cases, has been found to be a potential solution
for the last AOI. Even though this post-processing might seem very constrained, if the
results obtained with it were to be satisfactory, the temporal resolution obtained from an
approach like this one would improve the temporal resolution of such water masks, which
would go from monthly (Pekel et al., 2016) to at least a 12-day repetition cycle guaranteed
by Sentinel-1 SAR system.

7.2
Limitations and uncertainties
Water classiﬁcation from SAR imagery shows a clear improvement as compared to MSI
in terms of temporal resolution, avoiding cloud-cover related issues and being able to
obtain imagery during night-time. However, the classiﬁcation results still come at a cost
of lower resolutions and usually lack accuracy. Regarding resolution of the output water
masks, SAR imagery needs speckle ﬁltering in order to obtain classiﬁcations closer to the
ones obtained from MSI. In the case of this project, SAR observations already have lower
resolution than MSI before processing, and speckle ﬁltering only worsens this aspect. As far
as the lack of accuracy is concerned, in this project the accuracy assessment is referenced to
the classiﬁcation obtained using multi-spectral data from Sentinel-2. A better assessment
would be made if the water masks to be compared with were obtained through in situ data,
on the same day of acquisition of the SAR data and with the same resolution. However, as
this is obviously not possible due to the large-scale of the observations, the references can
only be improved to be as close to reality as possible in order to reduce the uncertainty in
the comparison between classiﬁcations.
The clearest setback for SAR systems when it comes to separating water and land classes
appears when the land class shows low backscatter similar to the water class. In these
cases, unsupervised algorithms get very low accuracies and even supervised algorithms do
not perform much better. The advantage of MSI with respect to SAR systems in these
cases is very large. An aspect that can improve the results from supervised algorithms is
the precise selection of labelled training data for the model to learn how to diﬀerentiate
between classes. However, in the case of this project, this might also create uncertainty
as pixels are selected from much lower resolution water masks derived from data of the
past decades. A clear limitation thus appears with climate change or the creation of new
reservoirs through dams or infrastructures of the kind, as this data could be obsolete for
some regions and therefore impossible to use.
7.3
Suggestions for further improvements
Building up from the beginning of the project, one aspect that could potentially improve
the results would be to explore diﬀerent pre-processing routines that would highlight fur-
ther the diﬀerences between water and land. Diﬀerent speckle ﬁlters could be tried, aiming
at the maximum possible resolution without compromising the results. Also, further pre-
processing steps such as terrain ﬂattening could be applied, if for instance regions with
more mountains, leading to shadowing in the imagery which could be confused with water,
were to be analysed.
Regarding the methodologies, the baseline Otsu thresholding method could be expanded
with adaptable tile-based methods. The supervised algorithms could be trained in many
labelled images at once in order to be able to analyse then many images without having
to be trained for each one of them. This would allow for diﬀerences in the background

99
==PAGE:116==
of the imagery and the water body itself. Moreover, and seeing how machine learning
techniques in general seem to have the best results, another potential improvement for
these methods would be to obtain a large-scale labelling data set that could be used to train
the algorithms. In general, this is lacking for global-scale low-resolution imagery. However,
in the meantime, other approaches for conﬁdently labelling data should be pursued, as it
has been observed that the algorithms seem to get better results when labels are more
accurate.
If more time was to be used for this project more methodologies of diﬀerent complexities
could be used. A possible approach could be the so-called deep learning, already used
in remote sensing imagery analysis with very good results in comparison to simpler tech-
niques (e.g. Isikdogan et al., 2017). Deep learning techniques such as convolutional neural
networks (CNN) have been observed as very good tools for image segmentation and classi-
ﬁcation in other research areas such as medicine (Litjens et al., 2017), involving gray-scale
images such as the ones obtained from SAR imagery.
Finally, another aspect that would make this project more complete would be to make
an accurate assessment of the weather patterns such as rains, droughts, ﬂoods, winds and
such conditions around the observation dates. This would help in obtaining a clearer view
of the reasons for water extent change, the errors in classiﬁcations, the diﬀerences between
acquisitions and even the diﬀerences between sensors. It has to be noted that the imagery
taken from MSI and SAR can diﬀer in time up to 3 days prior or forward.
