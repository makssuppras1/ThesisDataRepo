Preface
This project was carried out at the department of Civil Engineering at the Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark during the spring-semester in 2018. The thesis corresponds to 30 ECTS
points and is the ﬁnishing project of a MSc-degree in Architectural Engineering.
The topic of this project was chosen because of the authors interest for designing and de-
veloping solutions that can be used to achieve energy-eﬃcient buildings. In a time where
energy eﬃcient and cost-optimal solutions are essential in order to fulﬁll the upcoming
Nearly Zero Energy Buildings (nZEB) standard, prefabricated timber elements can be a
environmental-friendly and energy eﬃcient alternative. It was therefore in the interest of
the author to investigate and analyze diﬀerent alternatives within prefabricated timber ele-
ments in order to ﬁnd optimal solutions that can be used to reach the standard of a nZEB.
The investigations will compare diﬀerent types of timber wall elements in relation to energy
consumption and environmental impact. The diﬀerent types of wall elements will later also
be investigated in combination with several diﬀerent insulation materials in order to ﬁnd
solutions that are both energy-eﬃcient and cost-optimal. The focus on prefabrication was
eventually added because of the believed increase in the use of prefabricated solutions in
the coming years, following the need for a higher productivity within the building industry.
In the end I would like to express my gratitude to my supervisor at the Technical Univer-
sity of Denmark, Svend Svendsen, for sharing his experience and knowledge and for good
guidance. My gratitude also goes to Petter Martin Skjeldrum and Trond Ulriksen from the
Norwegian company Multiconsult, who made time in a busy work-schedule to share their
ideas, knowledge and relevant information whenever I needed it.

Summary
This report compares and investigates three diﬀerent types of prefabricated wall elements,
in relation to the energy and environmental performance. The aim is to investigate prefab-
ricated timber elements as a potential construction method for reaching the standard of a
Nearly Zero Energy Building. In the investigations three diﬀerent types of wall elements
were looked at; A regular timber framed-element, a timber framed-element with I-proﬁles
and a Cross Laminated Timber-element. These were simulated with six diﬀerent insulation
materials, in order to ﬁnd the cost-optimal insulation material. Additionally the method
of prefabrication is discussed as a way to increase the work productivity in the building
industry.
In order to compare the performance of the diﬀerent types of wall elements and insulation
materials, a simulation model room was designed. The design of the model room was de-
signed to be similar to a patient room in a hospital. The model room was designed to be
similar to a patient room in a hospital. This was decided due to the many new hospitals
that are currently planned and built in both Denmark and Norway. A patient room is
also often repetitively designed and is therefore of great interest to optimize. The designed
room consists of three internal walls and one facade wall, with internal concrete slabs above
and below. This was done in order to simulate a situation where the overall impact on
the energy use from the facade wall was thought to be low, which very often is the case in
larger buildings such as hospitals, oﬃce spaces or hotels.
From the investigations the most energy-eﬃcient and environmental-friendly type of wall-
element was found to be the timber framed-element that uses I-proﬁled studs. This solution
achieved the lowest heating consumption of the three and had the lowest environmental im-
pact when the three walls were compared in a Life Cycle Analysis.
An essential requisite for a successful integration of the Building Code 2020 as an energy
standard in Denmark is to develop solutions that are cost-optimal.
Some of the most
commonly used insulation materials were therefore compared in a CCE-analysis, where the
cost of the material and the energy saved were used to calculate the Cost of Conserved
Energy. This investigation showed that the cost-optimal insulation material to use was
Mineral wool class 37. This can be explained by the low price and that the facade wall in
general were found to have a low impact on the overall energy consumption.

Abstract
A report by the Energy Performance of Buildings Directive states that buildings are re-
sponsible for approximately 40% of the total energy consumption and 36% of the total CO2
emissions in the EU [1]. Additionally over 35% of the buildings are older than 50 years, and
of the total building stock 75% of them are deﬁned as energy ineﬃcient [2]. As a response
to the already aging building stock and an increased focus on lowering the overall emissions
of greenhouse gasses and energy usage, the European Performance of Buildings Directive
have therefore introduced a new standard for buildings; Nearly Zero Energy Houses (nZEB).
This new standard requires all new buildings in EU member countries to be Nearly Zero
Energy Houses by the end of year 2020. For public buildings the requirement is by the end
of 2018 [1].
Following the new nZEB-requirements, Denmark has introduced the voluntarily building
class ”Building Class 2020”. This class aims to comply with the requirements set by the
Energy Performance of Buildings Directive. In this report diﬀerent types of timber framed
wall-elements have been investigated and compared with the aim of meeting the energy
frame of the Building class 2020.
Additionally diﬀerent insulation materials have been
analyzed in order to ﬁnd cost-optimal solutions. The aim is to create a report which can
be used as a guideline for choosing the optimal type of prefabricated timber wall-element
and the most cost-optimal insulation material, when designing buildings that are aiming
for fulﬁlling the standard of a Nearly Zero Energy Buildings.
At the same time the productivity of the building industry in northern countries have been
criticized for being low. One solution for improving the productivity and at the same time
meet the stricter energy requirements can be to implement the method of prefabrication.
Within the building industry there seems to be an uniform understanding that some of
the biggest advantages of using prefabrication are the shortened construction time and the
increase of production quality. When using the method of prefabrication the parts are
produced oﬀ-site and usually in a controlled indoor environment. This eliminates potential
inﬂuencing factors like bad weather and the risk for accidents, which can potentially decrease
the productivity on site when using on-site production. In general the number of projects
using prefabrication of modules and elements are expected to increase globally by 6% before
2022 [3].

Introduction
This chapter discusses the background and states the aim of the project
1.1
Background
A report by the Energy Performance of Buildings Directive states that buildings are re-
sponsible for approximately 40% of the total energy consumption and 36% of the total CO2
emissions in the EU [1]. Additionally over 35% of the buildings are older than 50 years and
of the total building stock 75% of them are deﬁned as energy ineﬃcient [2]. Additionally
the EU have committed to minimize the global warming to a 2◦C temperature rise. In order
to ensure that reports have shown that a reduction of 80-95% in CO2 levels are required by
2050. As a response to the already aging building stock and an increased focus on lowering
the overall emissions of greenhouse gasses and energy usage the European Performance of
Buildings Directive have therefore introduced a new standard for buildings; Nearly Zero
Energy Houses (nZEB). This new standard requires all new buildings in EU member coun-
tries to be Nearly Zero Energy Houses by the end of year 2020. For public buildings the
requirement is by the end of 2018 [1].
In 2008 an Energy Policy Agreement was reached in Denmark between the governing par-
ties at that time.
One of the agreed targets was a reduction in the total gross energy
consumption of 2% by 2011 and 4% by 2020 in relation to the energy requirement in 2006.
For new buildings a stricter requirement was decided. For new buildings the energy target
were set to a reduction of at least 25% in 2010, another 25% in 2015 and then another 25%
by 2020. In total the agreed reduction equals a 75% reduction from the 2006 requirements.
In order to reach the target of a 75% reduction by 2020 a new Building Class were intro-
duced in BR10 the 18th of August 2011 as a voluntarily class named ”Building Class 2020”.
The Building Class 2020 aims to comply with the set requirement from the Energy Per-
formance of Buildings Directive of Nearly Zero Energy Buildings by the end of year 2020 [4].
Following the stricter energy requirements the building industry needs to quickly develop
more energy eﬃcient solutions. At the same time the productivity within the Northern
countries needs to be raised, as the building industry have been criticized for a low produc-
tivity in recent years [5]. One measure for increasing the productivity can be prefabrication.
Within the building industry there seems to be an uniform understanding that some of the
biggest advantages of using prefabrication are the shortened construction time and the
increase of production quality. When using the method of prefabrication the parts are
produced oﬀ-site and usually in a controlled indoor environment. This eliminates factors

1.2
Aim
In this report several diﬀerent types of prefabricated wall elements and insulation materials
are investigated in relation to energy performance and environmental impact. The aim is
to ﬁnd the energy-optimal prefabricated timber element to be used in a Nearly Zero Energy
Building, combined with the most cost-optimal insulation material. Additionally the report
discusses strategies to take fully advantage of the potential beneﬁts of using prefabrication.

Nearly Zero Energy Buildings
In this chapter the deﬁnition of a Nearly Zero Energy Building is given, as well as the
background for developing such a building class. The chapter also describe the development
in building requirements in Denmark up until the Building Class 2020.
3.1
Deﬁnition
A report by the EU commission shows that buildings are responsible for approximately 40%
of the total energy consumption and 36% of the total CO2 emissions in the EU [1]. In the
EU over 35% of the buildings are also older than 50 years and of the total building stock
75% of them are deﬁned as energy ineﬃcient [2]. Additionally the EU have committed
to contribute to that the global warming to a 2◦C temperature rise. In order to ensure
that target reports have shown that a reduction of 80-95% in CO2 levels are required by
2050. As a response to the already aging building stock and an increased focus on lowering
the overall emissions of greenhouse gasses and energy usage the European Performance of
Buildings Directive have introduced the new standards for buildings; Nearly Zero Energy
Houses (nZEB). The new standard requires all new buildings in EU member countries to be
Nearly Zero Energy Houses by the end of year 2020. For public buildings the requirement
is by the end of 2018. The deﬁnition of a Nearly Zero Energy Building as given by the
European Performance of Buildings Directive is:
”A building that has a very high energy performance and the nearly zero or very low amount
of energy required should be covered to a very signiﬁcant extent by energy from renewable
sources, including energy from renewable sources produced on-site or nearby [1]”.

3.2
Required level of energy performance
From the 2010 Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD) directions on how each
country should implement and deﬁne the required level of energy performance in a nZEB-
standards is described:
”It is the sole responsibility of Member States to set minimum requirements for the energy
performance of buildings and building elements. Those requirements should be set with a
view to achieving the cost-optimal balance between the investments involved and the energy
costs saved throughout the lifecycle of the building, without prejudice to the right of Mem-
ber States to set minimum requirements which are more energy eﬃcient than cost-optimal
energy eﬃciency levels. Provision should be made for the possibility for Member States to
review regularly their minimum energy performance requirements for buildings in the light
of technical progress[1]”.
Based on the directive from the Energy Performance of Buildings, it is up to the Member
States to deﬁne the minimum required energy performance of buildings. The required level
of energy performance should be deﬁned in relation to achieving a balance between the cost
of investment and cost of energy throughout the life of a building. The required level of
energy performance in each country will therefore vary based on the ﬁnancial state, in order
to ensure solutions that are cost-optimal. To ensure cost-optimal solutions the directive
states that the minimum required energy level should not be more than 15% lower than
the cost-optimal level of performance [16].

4.2.2

As discussed in chapter 3, using cost-optimal solutions when designing nearly Zero Energy
Buildings is essential in order to successfully implement nZEB as a required energy stan-

an increased insulation thickness to ﬁnd the Cost of Conserved Energy.
In this report CCE were used in order to ﬁnd cost-optimal insulation materials. For com-

for all combinations of wall elements/insulation material at thicknesses varying from 20-

∆Eyear

Where n equals the reference period, typically 30 or 100 years and d is the real interest

In the energy simulations diﬀerent insulation thicknesses, types of wall elements and in-

conductivities and speciﬁc heat capacities of the appearing materials. The calculation is
based on and validated against EN ISO 10211 and EN ISO 10077-2 [21].

Prefabrication
This chapter presents the method of prefabrication and potential beneﬁts and limitations
of using prefabrication are discussed. Strategies and tools to help the implementation of
prefabrication into a project are also discussed.
5.1
Product
Prefabrication of building parts is a well-known production method in the building indus-
try and components like stairs, roof trusses, walls, beams or columns are all common to
prefabricate. Common for all of them are that they are produced oﬀ-site, usually in a
factory, before transported to and assembled at the project-location. The report Prefab
Architecture (2010), by Ryan E. Smith, gives the following deﬁnition of oﬀ-site fabrication
or prefabrication .
”Oﬀsite fabrication is the practice of assembling components of a structure in a factory
and transporting complete assemblies or sub-assemblies to the construction site where the
building is to be located [12]”.
Prefabricated wall elements can be prefabricated to diﬀerent levels. Usually it is divided
into two diﬀerent classes; Open and closed elements [23].
– Closed elements: Delivered as a completely ﬁnished part, with all layers from the
outside to the inside mounted before transported to the building site.
– Open elements: Delivered without internal cladding, vapour barrier and in some cases
also without the insulation and wind barrier. This means that mechanical piping and
other installations can be installed on site, before the element is closed from the inside.
Closed elements are usually produced by one fabricator. The fabricator can develop the
entire element or coordinate products from several diﬀerent producers into one closed ele-
ment. Open elements are usually consisting of products from several diﬀerent producers,
which will be coordinated once they reach the building site. This allows the elements to be
more ﬂexible by oﬀering the option to combine products to the wish of the client. Open
elements are therefore thought to be more ﬂexible in terms of the design [12].
In general products can either be counted as a customized or a standardized product. This
is also the case for prefabricated elements. In order to describe to which extent an element
is customized the following four terms are usually used in the production industry: Made-to
stock (MTS), Assembled-to-stock (ATS), Made-to-order (MTO) and Engineered-to-order

(ETO). Depending on the type of product and customization the costs, lead times and
ﬂexibility changes. Of the four terms only ATS, MTO and ETO elements are considered to
be prefabricated. The relationship between customization, ﬂexibility, costs and lead times
for each of the product types can be seen in the ﬁgure below.

Standardized products with reduced complexity.
Typically inventory-products or ware-

5.2
Quality
The term quality can be used about two diﬀerent aspects of prefabrication. It can be quality
in the production or in the design, which is related to the functional and aesthetic aspects
of the building. To achieve a successful building both of them are equally important [12].
Achieving quality in the production is relying on several diﬀerent factors. One method
thought to increase the quality of a production process is standardization. Liker and Meier
(2006) states that standardization is one of the most important methods for reducing pro-
duction errors [24]. According to Anderson and Anderson (2007) standardization is much
easier to apply on a fabric rather than a building site, and the result of such a standard-
ization will lead to increased quality and a reduced error rate [25].
The fact that the
prefabricated elements are produced in a controlled environment and indoor is also stated
to be an important factor for increased quality, as this makes it easier to control and follow
up the work. The number of errors and damages will therefore be reduced, according to
Gibb (2001) [26]. On a building site there will often be conditions that makes it hard to
achieve good working positions, e.g. heights, small construction sites. Working in a factory
with permanent work stations and a set system can ensure better working positions, and
this will eventually result in a better quality of the ﬁnished product [27].
Building the elements inside a factory will also remove the challenge of bad weather. Ac-
cording to Geving and Thue (2002) the dehumidiﬁcation of constructions can be a challenge
when using traditional on-site production, because of uncontrolled weather [28]. In a con-
trolled indoor environment the air humidity and temperature can be controlled, which
reduces the risk of built in moisture. Indoor production gives the opportunity for many
beneﬁts over in-site production, but without deliberately using these opportunities the qual-
ity will not automatically be improved according as Gibb (2001) points out [26].
”Beneﬁts will be realized only if the
traditional site-based practices and
culture are not merely transferred
to the factory but rather changed to
reﬂect the manufacturing culture”

5.4
Cost
The overall cost of a building project can be simpliﬁed to be depending on three factors.
Labour, materials and time. Reducing one of these will potentially also reduce the cost
[12]. Prefabrication have solutions that can potentially reduce all of them. Reducing the
use of material in a project will save money. By using prefabricated elements in a factory
this can be possible. Standardization can lead to better precision in the work performed
by the workers, which can lead to less waste. On-site production means that materials
are purchased and brought to the site in a certain amount, often higher than required in
order to ensure high enough quantity. This can often lead to more waste than in oﬀ-site
production. Here suﬃcient quantity of the materials will most likely be stored on a regu-
lar basis as it is used in several projects. Using prefabricated wall elements will therefore
potentially save material costs, because they are delivered based on a pre-deﬁned need. In
the previous chapter parallel processes and standardization were mentioned as time-saving
aspects of prefabrication. Reduced construction and project time will bring economical
advantages as it will reduce expenses related to the building site, such as electrical power,
barracks, rigs, scaﬀolds and containers for storage. Usually these costs are paid per day
or week and any reduction of the construction time will therefore result in savings. Addi-
tionally prefabrication will reduce the extent of the activities on site. Reducing the extent
of activities will implicate less need for human resources. This again leads to decreased
expenses for wardrobes, toilet facilities and barracks [6, 30] . In order for prefabrication
to be cost-saving, Grimstad (2015) lists the following as important potential cost-saving
aspects [30]:
– Increased productivity when production happens in a factory.
– Shortened project time.
– Less human resources on site.
– Less work to be done on site.
– Reduce the need of transportation.
– Ensure an eﬃcient installation process and optimal use of cranes.
– Reduce the impact of unpredicted remedial work.

5.5
Challenging factors when implementing prefabrication
The implementation of prefabrication brings several beneﬁts to a building project as men-
tioned above. The most commonly talked about are reduced building time, reduced cost,
improved quality, improved working conditions etc. Nevertheless when wanting to imple-
ment prefabricated elements into a project, there are also some challenging factors that
needs to be considered. Tam et.al. 2007 mentions the following factors [32]:
1. Little ﬂexibility related to the design
2. High initial building costs
3. Limited research conducted on using prefabrication
4. Increased time consumption in the planning stage
5. Insuﬃcient storage space on the building site for storing building components
6. Leakage problems during assembling process
7. Lack of knowledge and experience with using prefabrication
8. Monotonically aesthetic expression
9. Missing knowledge about IT-implementation
The impact of the above listed factors should optimally be removed or at least reduced, in
order to eﬃciently implement prefabrication. According to Grimstad (2015) one tool for
minimizing the impact can be to introduce an imminent philosophy into the project [30]. As
potential strategies LEAN Construction or Integrated Project Delivery is often mentioned
(IPD). In short, LEAN construction is a strategy/philosophy with the purpose of minimizing
”waste” in a project, where waste is deﬁned as wasted time, wasted movement or wasted
human potential. Integrated Project Delivery is a relational contract with intention to also
reduce ”waste” and maximize the eﬃciency of all project stages.
5.5.1
Integrated Project Delivery
As given by the American institute of Architects (AIA), one deﬁnition of IPD is ”a project
delivery approach that integrates people, systems, business structures and practices into a
process that collaboratively harnesses the talents and insights of all participants to optimize
project results, increase value to the owner, reduce waste, and maximize eﬃciency through
all phases of design, fabrication, and construction [33]”. The foundation of an IPD-contract

continues until the project is ﬁnished. A diﬀerence between traditional processes is that
in an IPD-process the risk management is equally divided by the actors. In traditional

on to other actors. These projects are characterized by actors wanting maximum proﬁt at

Air-tightness and moisture control
This chapter discusses the importance of an airtight building and presents available studies
related to the performance of prefabricated wall elements. The chapter also discusses critical
areas and strategies to avoid air leakages.
A report from 2010 by Enova conducted for SINTEF Byggforsk showed that in Norway re-
ducing the amount of air leakages could potentially save up to 3.7TWh in energy losses [43].
Assuring air tightness is also one of the stressed measures in the guide that accompanies the
Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) No 244/2012 of 16 January 2012, which presents
some measures and methods as a helping guidance for the Member States to achieve the
target of nZEB. Other mentioned measures are improved framing of doors and windows,
building orientation, sun shading etc. [44].
In addition to the energy eﬃciency of a building, the air tightness is also especially important
for the indoor climate and moisture protection.
In general the air tightness has great
signiﬁcance in regards to moisture protection, thermal comfort, air quality, ﬁre safety and
sound insulation [45]. A good indoor climate is a prerequisite for all measures taken to
ensure a energy eﬃcient building. In order to ensure a building that is both energy eﬃcient
and has a good indoor climate, it is necessary to have good control over the ventilation
system and the air tightness of the building envelope.
The air tightness of a building
cannot be precisely calculated, but have to measured when the building is ﬁnished. The
air tightness of a building is measured as the quantity of air that leaks into or out of
the building at a pressure diﬀerence of 50 Pa [m3/(h.m2)], divided by the internal area
of the building. In general the air-tightness is depending on the thermal buoyancy, wind,
ventilation system and the location and size of potential leak spots in a building envelope.
The pressure diﬀerence is highest when the wind force is high, and will increase with higher
temperature diﬀerences [45]. In this chapter some previously conducted investigations of
the performance of prefabricated wall elements will be presented, along with some strategies
and critical areas that needs extra attention are presented.

7.1
Measures for achieving an airtight building
In general there are many good materials, detail-solution and tools available today for
building airtight. In recent years many tools have been produced with the intention of
improving and simplifying the process of making buildings airtight.
7.1.1
Vapour barriers
One of the most important protective measure is the vapour barrier. In addition to pre-
venting warm and moisture air to go through the envelope it is also airtight to prevent
inﬁltration and exﬁltration of air. Sintef recommends that the air coeﬃcient number for
vapour barrier does not exceed 0.002m3/(m2 hPa) and a vapour permeability value at min-
imum 50x109m2a/kg.
Most vapour barriers on the market today have good performance in relation to air-tightness
and moisture. Therefore the seams around joints, penetrations and transfer between mate-
rials are often where the leaks in the vapour barrier appears. One commonly used solution
for reducing the risk of leakages around penetrations is to retract the vapour barrier into
the wall construction. This allows for all installations to be installed without having to
penetrate the vapour barrier. Here Sintef recommends that a maximum of 1/4 of the over-
all insulation thickness should be on the inside of the vapour barrier to ensure suﬃcient
moisture control. Illustration of this solution can be seen below [45] (NB: Text translated
to English from Norwegian).

7.1.2
Wind barriers
The main object of a wind barrier is to protect the wall against wind and rain/water that
passes the outer cladding. During the construction phase the wind barrier is also an im-
portant measure to close the building so that the insulation and other critical parts are
protected against the weather. For the wind barrier Sintef recommends that the total air
coeﬃcient including a normal amount of joints by does not exceed 0.05m3/(m2hPa). Addi-
tionally a wind barrier should allow moisture through from the inside, and should therefore
have a low vapour resistance. This allows for a faster drying of moisture inside the construc-
tion. Some vapour barriers also have the ability to absorb moisture and store it temporary,
which can be an advantage in critical periods with high moisture levels. Sintef recommends
a vapour resistance of maximum below 0.5m (sd-value, equals the vapour resistance of 0.5m
of air)[45].

– Transition between the wall element and the roof.

7.4
Strategies and focus-points
In general achieving good air-tightness is very much depending on using well-planned and
well-described solutions. As a general guide for designing airtight solutions Sintef gives the
following recommendations in the report ”Bygg Tett”:
– Seams/sealing methods for external/internal air tightening must be planned and de-
scribed.
– Transitions should be drawn in a larger scale (1:10, 1:5).
– Complicated 3D-corners(for instance sealings around windows) should be considered
to be drawn in perspective.
– Work ﬂow for tricky transitions should be described, either on a drawing or by text.
– Use products with documented performance and adherent should be used (tape, ad-
hesive tape, etc.).
– Larger formats when applying the wind and vapour barrier reduces the number of
seams/joints.
– Retracted vapour barrier in the wall and a suspended ceiling for a hidden electrical
installation.
7.4.1
Inﬂuence of having a clear project goal
A research by Yanke Zou (2010) investigated how a clear project goal in regards to air
tightness inﬂuences the outcome. The study investigated the air tightness of 67 buildings
in Sweden in which the buildings were divided into two categories. Energy eﬃcient houses
and non-energy eﬃcient houses. The diﬀerence between them where that the energy eﬃcient
ones had a project goal of building energy eﬃcient, and the other houses was built with
conventional methods according to the applying regulations. The study clearly showed that
by having a clear project goal of building an air tight building improves the outcome [48].
As the illustration below shows, those with a goal of being energy eﬃcient had signiﬁcantly
better air tightness, compared to those without a clear project goal.

9.5.2
Installation
The position of the wall element also inﬂuences the installation-process. When installing
the elements a crane is very often used. In projects where elements are only used as a fa-
cade, the elements are typically installed after all other structural slabs, beams and columns
are installed. In these types of projects, designing a solution with the wall elements posi-
tioned as far ”out” of the building as possible can therefore make the installation process
much more convenient. A solution where the elements are placed partly inside the building
(placement partly between two ﬂoor slabs) rather than entirely on the outside is more dif-
ﬁcult, as the crane obviously are limited to hold elements in an entirely vertical position.
The elements would therefore have to be moved manually the ﬁnal distance, working partly
against the natural position caused by the cable. This can make the installation process
more diﬃcult and time consuming. Especially in windy conditions, when the element be-
comes more diﬃcult to maneuver.
However another aspect of the installation process is the load-carrying system when the
elements have been installed. If the elements are placed entirely or mostly on the outside
of the slab, it requires an additional system to carry the weight of the elements. If the
elements are placed further into the building, the loads can be transferred directly onto the
slab. This can therefore be a much cheaper solution in terms of installation.
Previously the critical area around intermediate ﬂoors were mentioned, as it can be diﬃ-
cult to ensure a continuous vapour barrier in this area. By installing the element in a more
external placed position the installation of the vapour barrier can be much easier. By using
a solution where the wall elements are placed outside the ﬂoor slab the vapour barrier do
not need to be wrapped around the slab, and just run continuously along the wall.

Discussion
In this chapter the results of the literature study and the performance analysis will be dis-
cussed. It presents tools and strategies for implementing prefabrication into a project, as
well as how diﬀerent design choices performs and impacts the overall performance of the
building.
10.1
Implementing prefabrication into a project
Overall the potential beneﬁts of using prefabrication are many, as discussed in chapter 5.
However in order to achieve these beneﬁts the implementation must be eﬃcient. In the
following sub-chapters important factors and beneﬁcial tools to implement prefabricated
solutions in a projects are discussed, based on the literature study presented in the earlier
chapters.
10.1.1
Achieving the beneﬁts of prefabrication
In chapter 5 the overall eﬀects of using prefabricated solutions were discussed. Some of the
beneﬁts mentioned were increased building quality, reduced costs and reduced construction
time. However using prefabricated solutions does not always provide those beneﬁts, if the
process of implementing the prefabricated solutions is not eﬃcient. In the same chapter the
challenging factors were also discussed, where lack of knowledge, high initial building costs
and little ﬂexibility in the design were mentioned as factors that had to be overcome in
order to beneﬁt from using the method of prefabrication. In chapter 5.5 the introduction of
philosophies like LEAN and Integrated Project Delivery were discussed as potential meth-
ods for overcoming the challenges of prefabrication. As Grimstad (2015) stated, the use
of these strategies can minimize the impact of the challenges of prefabrication. Addition-
ally they could also ensure a more eﬃcient process, as well as better ﬂexibility in the design.
The eﬀect of introducing the relational contract of Integrated Project Delivery could in
other words be essential in order to take the full advantage of using prefabricated solutions.
One of the beneﬁts discussed was the earlier involvement of key players because of a more
integrated building process. By opting for a more integrated process, where all key players
have access to and the possibility to inﬂuence the decisions earlier, both the eﬀectiveness
of the process and the ﬂexibility in the design can be improved. As discussed in chapter
5.4, prefabrication will however require more time for planning as the possibility for late
adjustments is reduced and also costly as the cost of change is higher further into a project.
Early integration of key players is also stated to reduce the risk of errors. One important
factor that were mentioned was the opportunity for the designers to discuss solutions and
Page 73 of 112
==PAGE:86==
Master thesis
Ole Fløvik Nyg˚ard
possibilities with the engineers earlier. This can eventually lead to less errors, in addition
to reducing the risk of adjustments. Another discussed aspect of an Integrated Project De-
livery was the diﬀerence in risk management (chapter 5.5.1), where in an IPD-process the
risk is equally divided between the actors. This was stated to result in a more integrated
and eﬃcient process, as the shared risk is said to ensure that all actors works towards the
same target. Seemingly, introducing the relational contract of IPD will beneﬁt the project
in many ways. However as discussed in chapter 5.5.1 the nine key-principles listed by AIA
needs to be addressed and considered in order to have a successful collaboration.
10.1.2
Inﬂuence of a clear project goal
In chapter 7.3 available studies of the performance of prefabricated wall elements were
discussed.
In relation to implementing wall elements, one of the studies presented the
inﬂuence of having a clear project goal. A research project where 67 Swedish buildings
were investigated showed that by a clear project goal of building air tight signiﬁcantly
improved the resulting air tightness.
One of the reasons mentioned was that the clear
focus led to solutions that were developed with the purpose of being airtight. Because of
the higher focus on air tightness more experienced workers were also used to ensure good
craftsmanship. Early implementation when using prefabrication can therefore be a very
important measure to increase the air tightness of the building. Together with IPD a clear
focus on building air tight can signiﬁcantly improve the air tightness as the focus on optimal
solutions is thereby introduced earlier for all actors.
10.1.3
Digital tools
The development of digital tools like BIM and VDC are mentioned in chapter 5.5.2 as tools
that will be beneﬁcial to use in an Integrated Project Delivery-process. Using digital tools
were found to increase the quality of the product, organization and the building process.
The BIM can work as a common database for all parties, and will ensure that everyone have
the same and most recent information available. This will lead to a more open process and
one of the beneﬁts mentioned was the early visualizing of the building, which was found
to reduce the risk of late adjustments. This is possible because everyone will through the
BIM-ﬁle have access to all details and therefore have the possibility to control and inﬂuence
the solutions earlier in the process. Especially the possibility for the engineer to discuss and
evaluate the solutions by the architect earlier were mentioned as an example. For the client
the visualization means a better understanding of the building and thereby the opportunity
to adjust/change the design earlier, before it is constructed.

10.2
Designing an energy optimal facade solution
In the performance simulations diﬀerent types of wall elements and insulation materials
were analyzed and compared in relation to the energy performance, LCA, CCE and daylight
performance. In this chapter the results from those simulations are discussed.
10.2.1
Type of wall elements
When designing the facade there are many decisions to be made. One of the ﬁrst will be
to decide the type of element. In addition to what kind of visual expression the architects
want, the energy performance is also an inﬂuencing factor. In this report three of the most
commonly used timber elements were investigated and compared. In general the results
also showed that because the model room only had one facade wall and were surrounded
by concrete ﬂoor slabs above and below, the impact of changing the insulation material
and thickness was present but not signiﬁcant. However from the simulations of the heating
demand it was found that the I-proﬁled element performed slightly better than the other
two, likely due to the fact that this type achieves lower U-values because of a smaller im-
pact from thermal bridges. Eventually the use of I-proﬁled timber studs also allowed for a
thinner wall construction, compared to the regular timber frame and the CLT-element.
The results also showed that the CLT-element required less insulation than the other two,
even though the heating demand was higher. This can be explained by the fact that increas-
ing the insulation thickness had only a small impact on the heating demand, but caused
the cooling load to increase. This is most likely due to the high thermal capacity of the
CLT-element, which will balance the temperatures better over a day. In general the energy
consumption for cooling was also much higher than the heating demand. In relation to the
total wall thickness the I-proﬁled element still achieved the slimmest wall construction, as
the separation of the insulation and structural layers in the CLT-element adds 100mm to
the overall thickness. This meant that the total wall thickness was found to be lowest for
the I-proﬁled element and that the resulting total thicknesses of the CLT-element and the
regular timber frame-element were about the same.
If the target is to achieve a low wall thickness and high energy performance, the I-proﬁled
timber frame element is found to be the best choice. The reason for a thinner wall can be
many, but often the visual expression of a wall that is very thick is not wished for by the
architect or the client. Extra wall thickness also means extra volume, which could make it
more expensive to transport. Additionally the extra thickness also means that the element
is heavier. This could possibly also make it more diﬃcult to install. Especially to maneuver

elements that are thick and heavy can be diﬃcult in windy conditions. In the worst case
it can require a larger truck for transportation as well as a larger crane on-site, which will
increase the cost. An increased wall thickness was also found to cause a change in the
daylight performance, as seen in Fig. 41
In terms of transportation and installation the wall thickness is therefore important to
consider, but the geometry and dimensions of each element are also important decisions.
In one aspect, larger elements will be beneﬁcial over smaller elements. Larger elements
will require less connections and are therefore typically faster to install. Since the quantity
of elements decreases it can also lead to a reduced assembly time. Less connections will

of the LCA the CLT-element was therefore found to be the least environmental-friendly
choice, with the I-proﬁled element the best choice and the regular timber framed element
slightly behind.

In order to ﬁnd one (or more) optimal insulation material(s), several diﬀerent materials
were analyzed and compared against each other. From the comparisons it could be seen
that the performance of the investigated materials are very diﬀerent, but that the cost of the
diﬀerent materials is very diﬀerent as well. The ﬁrst comparison was the level of insulation
required of each type of insulation to meet the energy frame. If using a regular timber frame

using Vacuum Insulated Panels the needed level of insulation decreased to only be 150mm.
This makes a big diﬀerence to the total wall thickness, but the reduction comes with a much
higher cost. Vacuum Insulated Panels is not a very common insulation material to use, as
it is very expensive to produce (15 times as expensive as the Class 37). It was also found
that the EPS and Mineral Wool class 32 performs quite similar. The energy performance
of the XPS and PUR/PIR materials were also similar to each other, but the two performed
slightly better than the EPS and Mineral Wool Class 32. In relation to cost the diﬀerences
are bigger, with Mineral Wool Class 32 and PUR/PIR being much cheaper than the EPS
and the XPS-materials.
Eventually the cost of the insulation material was also considered, as the importance of
ﬁnding cost-optimal solutions is stressed in the EU-directive. In order to ﬁnd and com-

therefore compared using CCE as a measuring tool. From the CCE-calculations it became
clear that Vacuum Insulated Panels was a very expensive solution. Even though the insu-

PUR/PIR-material. Further the CCE calculations also showed that because of the low
cost of the Mineral Wool Class 32 material, the Cost of Conserved Energy was similar to
the PUR/PIR-material, even though the PUR/PIR performed slightly better in relation
to the energy and required a lower insulation thickness. From the CCE-calculations it was
however clear that the Mineral wool Class 37 was the cheapest choice, mainly because the
entry price was much lower than the other materials and that the overall diﬀerence in the
energy use was low. Therefore this material were found to be the most cost-optimal solution.
In relation to the wall thickness the Mineral wool 37 resulted in the thickest wall construc-

Position of the wall element
From the thermal bridge investigations it become clear that the position of the wall el-
ement is an important decision. Because a position where the element is placed further
”out” of the building allows for a thicker thermal bridge breaker, it could reduce the heat
losses signiﬁcantly. The eﬀect of moving the wall element so that the insulation thickness
of the thermal bridge breaker increases, were found to potentially be saving 0.21kWh/m2.
For a larger buildings such as a hospital that improvement can be quite signiﬁcant just by
adjusting the position of the wall element, as it could mean huge cost savings during the
entire lifetime of a building. Another aspect discussed in relation to the position were the
installation. Because the elements normally are installed by using a crane, the maneuver
of placing an element partly inside a building is much more diﬃcult than just placing it up
until the slab. Especially in windy conditions this can be a great advantage. However such
position can potentially cost more, as it might require a diﬀerent and separate load-bearing
system in order to take the loads from the wall elements and windows.
The installation of the vapour barrier were also a mentioned beneﬁt of a external posi-
tion of the wall element. Because of an easier installation of the vapour barrier there is a
lower risk for air leakages, which also reduces the risk of moisture problems. The easier
installation were justiﬁed with the fact that an external position allows for a continuous
vapour barrier, because the ﬂoor slab will not intersect with the wall element. Any addi-
tional installation other than the seam between the vapour barrier of two connecting wall
elements are therefore not required on site. Since the vapour barrier is usually installed in
the factory before the transporting stage, this will be both time saving and increase the
quality of the joints.

10.3
Minimize the risk of moisture and air leakages
In general using prefabricated wall elements are found to require a longer planning period.
Because the elements are built in a factory and arrives the site in a state where most of
the wall is completed, the possible adjustments that could be made with on-site fabrica-
tion is reduced. This means that the elements have to be designed precisely at an earlier
stage in the project, and the time for changes in the design is therefore reduced. Limited
possibility for adjustments also have an impact on the ﬁnishing of joints and transitions,
as it means that these also needs to be planned early on and the room for errors are reduced.
From the literature study and available research-studies it is however clear that even though
prefabricated wall elements may require additional time in the design period, it is more
than possible to design solutions that ensures both air-tightness and moisture-safety. The
presented studies showed for instance that the air-tightness also depends on the overall
geometry and design, and not necessarily so much on whether the walls are prefabricated
or in-situ built. This is shown by the results from the research-study in Estonia, where pre-
fabricated wall element-buildings performed better than buildings constructed with in-situ
timber frame walls. The studies also showed that one-storey houses performed signiﬁcantly
better than two-storey, where the concluded reason where found to be the intermediate
ﬂoor construction.
However, since comparing projects is diﬃcult because of diﬀerences
in weather conditions, geometry and craftsmanship the better performance can also be of
other reasons. Because the level of craftsmanship and detail-design is stated to have a big
impact, part of the diﬀerences could also be related to a better design, more skilled workers
etc. However, from all the studies it could be seen that designing and building houses and
buildings with suﬃcient air-tightness is possible, no matter the construction method.
The number of available tools and measures on the market today are stated as good.
By using certiﬁed products and solutions the possibility for a good solution increases. Ad-
ditionally the importance of designing joints and details that are easy to construct and
visualize them by large drawings, which are clear and understandable, were found to be
essential. One of the research-studies that were discussed also showed that by having a
clear goal of building airtight from an early stage in a project could aﬀect the outcome, as
everyone are aware of where the critical areas are. Using veriﬁed products and solutions
will also be essential, in order to also ensure that the solution is durable. In addition to
using solutions that are veriﬁed, the importance of clear drawings that are easy to read and
understand was also stated as an essential measure. For tricky transitions or joints it was
suggested by Sintef to make step-by-step guides for the assembling process. In this way it
is possible for workers to execute the detail without speciﬁc experience with an identical

Conclusion
Based on the results from the case study and the literature study the following can be
concluded:
– The timber framed wall element with I-proﬁle studs were in this report found to be the
most energy eﬃcient solution of the three wall types. This type of wall construction
achieved lower U-values resulting in a lower heating consumption. This type of wall
also resulted in the slimmest wall construction at the same level of energy performance.
– Mineral wool class 37 were found to be the cost-optimal insulation material from the
simulated case study. Because of the small impact an increased insulation thickness
had on the energy consumption the low price of the material makes it the most cost-
optimal solution in the simulated situation.
– The position of the element in relation to the internal ﬂoor slabs were found to
potentially save 0.21kWh/m2 as a result of lower heat losses when using a more
external position of the wall-element.
– In the investigation of the environmental impact of the three diﬀerent wall types the
Cross Laminated Timber-element were found to be the least environmental-friendly.
The best performance in relation to the environmental performance was achieved by
the I-proﬁled element.
– The relational contract of Integrated Project Delivery combined with digital tools can
be essential for achieving the beneﬁts of prefabrication.
– Achieving good performance of prefabricated elements in relation to air tightness relies
on the attention for critical details. This can positively aﬀected by a clearly stated
project goal. In general designing clear and simple solutions with good documentation
is also essential.

Further studies
This chapter presents possible investigations for further studies
– As the project were limited to only consider a simple model room placed in the
middle of a building, connections between the wall elements and the external ﬂoor
slab, roof and other wall elements in corners etc. have been excluded. The impact
from including these connections should be investigated further.
– The performance of the tested insulation materials in relation to moisture protection
should also be investigated in order to ﬁnd the actual product which is optimal to
use.
– The eﬀect of using the diﬀerent insulation materials in relation to wind forces and sta-
bility should also be investigated as the materials have diﬀerent compression strength.
This have not been considered in this report.
