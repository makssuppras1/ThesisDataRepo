Summary
The Internet of Things (IoT) revolution promises to make our lives easier by
providing cheap and always connected smart embedded devices, that can inter-
act and exchange useful information in Internet, creating new values for human
needs. But all that glitters is not gold. Indeed, the other side of the coin is
that, this plethora of IoT devices that have been ooding the market are very
badly protected and are easy prey of several dierent families of malwares, that
exploit them to build large-scale malicious networks (dubbed "botnets"). This
(in)security trend has brought back to the top Distributed Denial of Service
(DDoS) attacks, making them more powerful and easier to achieve than ever.
The aim of the thesis is to study DDoS attacks from the IoT perspective, ending
up with a possible solution to prevent or mitigate the phenomenon. In its path
toward the solution, the work oers dierent contributions. First, it provides
a comprehensive taxonomy of DDoS attacks based on previous scientic liter-
ature and upgraded with new data from recent oensives. Using this DDoS
attacks classication as foundation, an analysis of all the recent DDoS capable
IoT malwares is conducted. Besides, a special attention is reserved to Mirai, the
IoT malware that changed the world perception of IoT security with its massive
DDoS attacks, by undertaking the analysis of its source code and ending up with
a detailed exposition of its infrastructure and modus operandi. All the informa-
tion gathered during this path, along with the solid background of knowledge
built, has been nally used to design a possible solution: AntibIoTic. The pro-
posed solution is aimed to both secure IoT devices preventing their recruitment
into DDoS botnets, and create a protable connection with people in order to
increase awareness and cooperation about the IoT security problem.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The Internet of Things (IoT) is rapidly and unavoidably changing our society,
aecting the way we live and work.
The IoT mission is to enable everyday
objects to communicate with each other through the Internet, resulting in a
gurative tsunami of connectivity.
From a business perspective, IoT is all about excitement. Firms are rushing the
development of their IoT products in order to commercialise them as soon as
possible, and stay on the crest of the wave. IoT predictions by several consul-
tancy rms (like Bain & Company, General Electric, IHS Markit, Gartner, to
mention only a few) clearly indicate that the IoT market will be massive in the
coming 10 years [Luc16, MKG15].
From a security perspective, all this excitement goes to the detriment not only
of the IoT security, but also of the security of every single Internet connected
device, causing a potential disaster. Indeed, security still represents the most
overlooked characteristic when quickness is considered of paramount importance
for business, and the consequences of it are easily guessable looking at the IoT
cosmos. More connected and insecure devices mean more attack vectors and
more possibilities for malicious users to target us, access sensitive data and
control personal devices.
As expected, this security disaster in the IoT tsunami of connectivity has made

Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks more and more popular among the

managed to infect a wide range of IoT devices simply through a very basic dic-
tionary attack based on around 60 entries, and especially relying upon the fact
that those devices used default login credentials that many users never change
and which sometimes can not even be changed for technical reasons. All this
highlights an undeniable need to seriously face the IoT security problem.
That is the context from which the thesis comes out.

Theoretical Background
2.1.1
DoS Attacks
A Denial of Service (DoS) attack is a cyber-attack in which the attacker tries
to make the victim unavailable to its legitimate users through a temporary
or indenite interruption of the services that it oers. The victim could be a
network resource (e.g., a router or a link), an entire network, an end host (i.e., a
machine that implements all layers of OSI reference model), an infrastructure,
or any combination or variant of them [Abl11].
Denial of Service attacks are often achieved by ooding the victim with malicious
packets in an attempt to overload its hardware (CPU, memory, bandwidth, etc.)
or to cause a crash of the software (or a part of it) that runs on it. For instance,
a DoS attack addressed to a web server may prevent nal users to access the
website hosted on it. Nevertheless, nowadays the ways in which a DoS attack
can be carried out are countless.
A Denial of Service attack can be basically of two main types [Abl11]:
 Single-source Denial of Service (SDoS) attack - DoS attack in which the
attacker directly interacts with the victim, sending malicious packets from
a single host.
 Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack - DoS attack in which the
attacker indirectly interacts with the victim, sending malicious packets
from multiple third-party hosts which are under his control and that may
be distributed in the network.
Sometimes, the term DoS is used in the literature to refer to SDoS attacks.
However, in this work the term DoS is only used to refer to the category of
attacks, and we will explicitly state DDoS or SDoS when needed.
2.1.2
DDoS Attacks
A Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack is a type of DoS attack imple-
mented in a distributed environment. It takes advantage of a great number
of compromised devices (commonly dubbed bots or agents) to perform an in-
direct DoS attack on the victim. The use of third-party devices provides the
attacker with the opportunity to both execute a much larger and more disrup-
tive attack and remain anonymous due to diculties in tracking down the real
attacker [SL04]. The collection of bots used to perpetrate the DDoS attack is
generally known as botnet, whereas the infrastructure used by the the attacker

to control the bots is referred to as Command-and-Control (CNC or C&C)
infrastructure.
In general, DDoS attacks are more dangerous than SDoS ones, because the
amount of victim resources (CPU, memory, bandwidth, etc.) that can be af-
fected by an attack performed by hundreds or thousands of compromised ma-
chines is greater than the one that can be aicted by an attack carried out by
a single host [Abl11]. In fact, it is an established fact that defending against
DDoS attacks is way more challenging than protecting from SDoS ones.
2.1.3
The reasons behind DDoS Attacks
The reasons behind a DDoS attack could be several. The main one is certainty
to cause disruptions or damage to the victim, but further motivations could also
be suitable. The most common driving forces in the DDoS world include but
are not limited to [MR04]:
 Prestige or Popularity - successful worldwide attacks allow attacker to
gain both a huge visibility and the respect of the hacker community.
 Personal reasons (e.g., revenge purposes) - a signicant number of DDoS
attacks are addressed to personal devices, such as home computers.
 Political reasons (e.g., a country at war could perpetrate an attack against
its war opponent's critical resources).
 Material gain (e.g., corrupting competitors resources)
It is relevant to point out that, regardless the reasons that drove the DDoS
attack, sometimes the real targets of the attack are those who rely upon the
victim services instead of the victim itself.
2.1.4
Why are DDoS Attacks possible?
What makes DDoS attacks possible and extremely powerful is the Internet ar-
chitecture. The Internet was designed thinking about functionality, not security,
and it has been pretty successful in achieving its aim [MR04]. However, the In-
ternet design opens several security issues that can be used for perpetrating a
Distributed Denial of Service attack [MR04, DM04]:
 Internet security is extremely interdependent - It does not matter how
well secured the victim system may be, its vulnerability to DDoS attacks

depends on the security of the rest of the global Internet.
 Internet entities have limited resources - Each Internet entity (such as
hosts, networks, services, etc.) has limited resources that can be saturated
by a given number of users.
 Many is better than a few - Coordinated and concurrent distributed at-
tacks will always be eective if the resources of the attackers are greater
than the resources of the victims.
 Intelligence and resources are not collocated - Most of the intelligence
needed to guarantee online services is located in end hosts. Nevertheless,
the requirement of large throughput brought to design high bandwidth
pathways in the intermediate network. As a result, attackers can exploit
the abundant resources of the intermediate network in order to delivery a
great number of malicious messages to the victim.
 Accountability is not enforced - In IP packets, the source address eld
is assumed to carry the IP address of the host that creates the packet.
However, this is an assumption which is not validated or enforced at all,
therefore there is the opportunity of perpetrating an IP source address
spoong1 attack. This attack provides DDoS attackers with a powerful
mechanism to avoid responsibility for their actions.
 Control is distributed - Internet management is distributed and each net-
work can work with its own local policies dened by its administrator.
Consequently, there is no way to deploy a global security mechanism or
policy and it is often impossible to investigate cross-network trac be-
haviour due to privacy issues.
2.1.5
DDoS Strategy
A Distributed Denial of Service attack is usually perpetrated going through
several phases [MR04, DM04]:
1. Recruitment - The attacker scans for vulnerable machines (named agents
or bots) that will be later used to perpetrate the attack against the real
victim. In the past this process was performed manually, afterwards it
has been automated and today several scanning tools can be used for the
purpose.
2. Exploitation & Infection - Agent machines are added to the botnet by
exploiting their discovered vulnerabilities to inject them with the malicious

3. Communication - The attacker uses the Command-and-Control infrastruc-

2.2
The Internet of Things
Today, one of the most popular ways to deliver DDoS attacks is by using an IoT
botnet (i.e., a botnet composed of IoT devices). The choice is easily explained
by the always higher availability of such devices combined with their intrinsic
insecurity, since they are usually poorly protected by the manufacturer and
poorly maintained by the owners. Therefore, in order to understand what kind
of security problems we are facing and nd a possible solution, a brief analysis
of the current situation of the IoT ecosystem is given in this section.
2.2.1
The Concept
The term "Internet of Things" was originally coined in 1999 by Kevin Ash-
ton, executive director of the Auto-ID Center in Massachusetts Institute of
Technology (MIT), and then it has assumed several slightly dierent mean-
ings. Today, there is no a unique and commonly accepted denition for IoT
and several formalizations can be found on the web and in the literature (such
as [KAKA16, MRT15, FHKJ15, AIM10]). In this work, the denition given by
the International and Telecommunication Union (ITU) is assumed.
According to the International and Telecommunication Union, the Internet of
Things is "a global infrastructure for the information society, enabling ad-
vanced services by interconnecting (physical and virtual) things based on ex-

isting and evolving interoperable information and communication technologies
(ICT)" [IT12]. In this environment, a thing is intended as "an object of the phys-

capable of being identied and integrated into communication networks", while
a device is "a piece of equipment with the mandatory capabilities of communi-

fullled [IT12].
As stated in [IT12], the IoT adds the dimension "any thing
communication" to the information and communication technologies (ICTs),
which already provide the dimensions "any place communication" and "any
time communication". This means that the IoT has the purpose of connecting
(virtual or physical) objects and people through any kind of network (wired or

owners and users of the infected machine are usually not aware that

impacted when the system takes part in an attack.

Distributed Denial of Service attacks exploit dierent vulnerabilities to deny

Force), Resource Depletion.

In Bandwidth Depletion DDoS attacks, a great amount of apparently legitimate
packets are sent to the victim in order to clogs up its communication resources

to send a reply to the victim. The broadcast IP address is used to amplify
and reect the malicious trac in order to reduce the available bandwidth
of the victim machine. The intermediary nodes involved in the attack are
called reectors (refer to Subsection 3.1.1 for further details). In these

3.1.3

Distributed Denial of Service attacks can be perpetrated through protocols
that belong to dierent layers of the TCP/IP model.
Based on the proto-

gories [AMG+12, ZJT13]: Network Level, Application Level.
Network Level - In Network Level DDoS attacks, either Network or Trans-
port layer protocols are used to carry the attack out and to deny the
access to the victim services. Examples of these attacks are the TCP SYN
attack (Subsection 3.2.1), the PUSH & ACK attack (Subsection 3.2.2),
the UDP Flood attack (Subsection 3.2.4) and the ICMP Flood attack
(Subsection 3.2.3).

(e.g., CPU, memory, disk/database, etc.) are exhausted by targeting Ap-

this kind of attacks can be found in [ZJT13, RSUK06, Net11].

In the literature is also possible to nd a more specic classication based on
the exact protocol involved in the attack [GJM09, KJS06], however we will not
consider that taxonomy since we believe it is extremely inaccurate and hard to
use (it is possible to have DDoS attacks which involve more than one protocol).

Manual - In Manual DDoS attacks, the attacker scans by hand remote devices
looking for any vulnerability. Once a vulnerability is found, the attacker
manually breaks into the victim machine, installs the attack code and
then commands the onset of the attack. Only the early DDoS attacks
belong to this category because today most of the phases of the attack are

the one which oers the minimal exposure to the attacker, since he is only
involved in issuing the attack command. Nevertheless, this kind of DDoS
attacks are not exible because all the specications of the attack are hard-
coded thus, if exibility is needed, it has to be designed in advance into
the code (e.g., the propagation mechanism could leave an open backdoor

attack code in the future).

possible to have a DDoS attack in which the recruitment and attack phases are
automated, while the exploitation & infection and the communication ones are
performed manually.
3.1.5
Scanning Strategy
The goal of the scanning strategy, which is part of the recruitment phase along
with the propagation technique, is to locate as many vulnerable machines as
possible while creating a low trac volume to avoid the detection. Based on the

scanning strategy, it is possible to classify DDoS attacks into ve classes [MR04,
AR08]: Random Scanning, Hitlist Scanning, Signpost (or Topological) Scanning,
Permutation Scanning, Local Subnet Scanning.
Random Scanning - In DDoS attacks with Random Scanning, each compro-
mised host uses a dierent seed to probe random addresses in the IP
address space and nd new vulnerable hosts. This scanning strategy po-
tentially creates high trac volume (since many machines could probe the
same addresses) which can lead to attack detection.
Hitlist Scanning - In DDoS attacks with Hitlist Scanning, the scanning ma-
chine probes all addresses from an external list. When a new vulnerable
machine is detected and infected, a portion of the initial hitlist is sent to
it. This scanning strategy allows for great propagation speed and no col-
lisions during the scanning. The drawback is that the hitlist needs to be
assembled in advance. Moreover, if the hitlist is too large, its transmission
might generate a high trac volume and lead to attack detection, while if
it is too small, it generates a small botnet.
Signpost Scanning - In DDoS attacks with Signpost Scanning, some pieces
of information on the compromised machines are used to nd new targets
(e.g., e-mail worms could exploit information form address books of in-
fected machines, a Web-server based worm could spread by infecting each
vulnerable client that access to the server Web page, etc.). This scanning
strategy does not generate a high trac load, hence it reduces the possi-
bility of attack detection. However, the agent mobilization may be slower
and less exhaustive compared to other scanning techniques because the
spreading speed is not under the control of the attacker but it depends on
both the agent machines and the behaviour of their users.
Permutation Scanning - In DDoS attacks with Permutation Scanning, the
Permutation Scanning is preceded by a limited Hitlist Scanning from which
a small initial population of agents is created. Subsequently, all compro-
mised hosts share a common pseudo-random permutation of the IP address
space and each IP address is mapped to an index in this permutation. A
machine infected during the initial phase begins scanning through the per-
mutation by using the index computed from its IP address as a starting
point.
Whenever it sees a machine that has been already infected, it
chooses a new random starting point. A machine infected by Permutation
Scanning always starts from a random point in the permutation. This
scanning strategy maintains the benets of the Random one but it also
has the eect of providing a semi-coordinated and comprehensive scan.
Local Subnet Scanning - The Local Subnet Scanning can be added to each
of the aforementioned strategies to preferentially scan for targets which

are located on the same subnet of the compromised host. This technique
allows a single copy of the scanning code to compromise many vulnerable

3.1.6
Propagation Mechanism
After the recruitment, the agent machine is exploited and infected with the
attack code. Based on the attack code propagation mechanism used during the
exploitation & infection phase, it is possible to classify DDoS attacks into three
dierent categories [MR04, AR08]: Central Source Propagation, Back-chaining
Propagation, Autonomous Propagation.
Central Source Propagation - In DDoS attacks with Central Source Prop-
agation, the attack code is stored on a central server (or a set of servers).
When an agent machine is compromised, the code is downloaded from the
server through a le transfer mechanism (such as wget or tftp). This prop-
agation mechanism leads to a large load on the central server, generating
high trac volume which results in the possibility of attack discovery.
Moreover, the central server is a single point of failure.
Back-chaining Propagation - In DDoS attacks with Back-chaining Propa-
gation, the attack code is downloaded from the machine which was used
to exploit the system. The infected machine then becomes the source for
the next propagation step. This propagation mechanism is more durable
then the Central Source one because it does not have a single point of
failure.
Autonomous Propagation - In DDoS attacks with Autonomous Propaga-
tion, the attack instructions are directly injected into the target host when
infected. This propagation mechanism avoids the le retrieval step and
reduces the frequency of network trac for agent mobilization, hence it
reduces the possibility that the attack is discovered.
Further details about propagation mechanisms of the attack code can be found
in [HW01].

majority of attacks rely on this mechanism.

3.1.12
Victim Type
Distributed Denial of Service attacks need not necessarily be carried out against
a single host machine. According to the type of victim targeted, it is possible to
classify them into four classes [MR04]: Application, Host, Network, Infrastruc-

Application - In Application DDoS attacks, one or more features of a specic

applications which are not addressed by the attack continue their regular

appear atypical. Moreover, attack packets are virtually indistinguishable
from legitimate ones and it is necessary to deeply use the semantic of the

the attack (assumed that malicious packets can be distinguished from

the host can not defend alone against them because its network resources
are exhausted, hence it usually needs the help of some upstream machines
(such as a rewall).
Network - In Network DDoS attacks, the incoming bandwidth of a network
is consumed with attack packets whose destination address can be taken
from the victim network address space. The detection of these attacks is

operations of a sub-network. Examples of this attack are the ones ad-

Distributed Denial of Service attacks can be perpetrated using dierent locations

3.2
DDoS Attacks Description
This section gives a brief overview (based on [SL04, DM04, GJM09, PLR07,
AMG+12, SL03, Rio14]) of some of the most common types of DDoS attacks
that have been carried out in the last years, with the aim of better understanding
the classication proposed in the previous section. Please note that it is not
a comprehensive analysis (for instance, the description of additional types of
DDoS attacks can be found in [GJM09, PLR07, AMG+12, Rio14, ZJT13]) and
the explanations given below are not intended to be exhaustive.
3.2.1
TCP SYN Attack
In a TCP SYN attack, the inherent vulnerability of the TCP three-way hand-
shake is exploited: the server needs to allocate a data structure for each incoming
SYN packet, regardless of its authenticity. Therefore, the attacker uses its agents
to send a large number of TCP SYN packets to the victim system with spoofed
source IP addresses. The reply TCP SYN/ACK packets of the victim are sent
to the spoofed addresses (which may not exist or be not in use) and hence will
not be acknowledged, leaving the target machine waiting indenitely for the
ACK packets. Considering that the victim system has a limited buer queue
for new TCP connections, when a large volume of TCP SYN requests are pro-
cessed and no ACK packets are received, it runs out of resources (i.e., the TCP
connections buer queue gets overloaded) and it is unable to process legitimate
users requests. A deeper analysis of this attack can be found in [CER00b].
3.2.2
PUSH & ACK Attack
In a TCP PUSH & ACK attack, TCP packets with ags PUSH and ACK setted
are sent from the agents to the victim. These ags instruct the victim machine
to unload all data in the incoming TCP buer (regardless of whether it is full or
not) and to send back an ACK when it has been done. If a lot of TCP PUSH &

ACK packets are sent from dierent agents to the victim system, it is overloaded
and it will crash.
3.2.3
ICMP Flood Attack
In an ICMP Flood attack, a large volume of ICMP ECHO REQUEST packets
(also known as "ping") are sent by the agents to the victim. These packets
request a reply from the victim and the combination of ICMP requests and
responses leads to the bandwidth saturation of the victim network. During this
attacks, the source IP address of the ICMP packets is often spoofed, so the
response packets from the victim are not sent back to the agents but to other
unaware hosts.
3.2.4
UDP Flood Attack
In an UDP Flood attack, a lot of UDP packets are sent to either a random or
a specied port of the victim. Once received, the host tries to process them
to identify which application is waiting on the targeted port. If there are no
applications running on that port, the victim machine sends back an ICMP
packet with a "destination port unreachable" message. However, the response
packet usually does not reach the agents (real senders of UDP packets) because
the source IP address is spoofed to hide their identity. The result of the attack
is that the network of the victim is saturated and the available bandwidth for
legitimate service request is depleted. Moreover, if enough UDP packets are
delivered to the victim, its machine will be exhausted.
This kind of attack
often impacts also the connectivity of systems situated near the victim and may
saturate the bandwidth of connections located around the targeted system as
well.
3.2.5
Smurf Attack
The Smurf Attack is a particular kind of ICMP Flood attack in which the
attacker sends ICMP ECHO REQUEST packets ("ping") to a network amplier
(a system supporting broadcast addressing) spoong the source IP addresses
with the victim IP address. The amplier forwards the "ping" packets to all
the machines within the broadcast address range and each of them replies with
an ICMP ECHO REPLY to the victim machine. This type of attack amplies
the original attack packets tens or hundreds of times, depending on the number

and the intermediate broadcast systems. A deeper analysis of this attack can
be found in [CER00a].
3.2.6
Fraggle Attack
The Fraggle Attack is a particular type of UDP Flood attack which is similar
to the Smurf one but the attacker sends UDP ECHO packets to the network
amplier instead of ICMP ECHO ones. There is a variation of this attack in
which the UDP ECHO packets are sent to the port that supports the character
generation protocol (usually port 19), spoong the source port with the victim
echo service protocol port (usually port 7), thus creating an innite attack loop:
UDP ECHO packets target the character generation service of intermediate
broadcast systems, which generate characters that are sent to the echo service
of the victim system, that replies with an echo packet back to the character
generator, and so on so forth. This attack generates even more attack trac
than Smurf one, hence it can create more damages.
3.2.7
DNS Flood Attack
In a DNS Flood attack, a great number of spoofed DNS queries are sent by
agents to the victim name server in order to exhaust its communication and
computational resources. The victim is not able to distinguish the legitimate
requests to the malicious ones, therefore it is overwhelmed while trying to answer
to all of them. This attack is extremely dicult to detect since the malicious
DNS requests are identical to the legitimate ones.
3.2.8
HTTP Flood Attack
In a HTTP Flood attack, a great number of HTTP requests are sent by agents to
the victim server in order to exhaust its resources. These requests are accurately
formulated in order to both maximize the attack power and avoid the detection.
For instance, a single HTTP request that downloads a large le from a server
(e.g., an image) can signicantly consume its resources, but the repetition of
requests for large les can be easily detected and blocked.
Thus, attackers
may simulate legitimate HTTP trac by instructing the bots to send multiple
requests to the target, analysing the replies and following recursively the links.

distinguish malicious trac from legitimate one.
3.2.9
DNS Amplication Attack
In a DNS Amplication attack, the attacker sends a lot of DNS requests to
a name server (used as reector) spoong their source IP address with the
victim one. The name server responds to those requests sending back the DNS

larger DNS response, if the number of requests sent to the reector is suciently
high, it is possible to saturate the victim bandwidth. In this type of attack, the
attacker can send the DNS requests either directly or through the bots in order
to increase the trac attack volume.

4.1
The Story
Mirai is one of the most predominant malware of the last few years. It has been
used to create a botnet of approximately 500,000 compromised IoT devices
later exploited to perpetrate the largest DDoS attacks ever known. The attacks
include the abuse of the French Internet service and hosting provider OVH on
22nd September 2016 [Ang17, Kla16], the attack to KrebsOnSecurity blog on
30th September 2016 [Ang17, Mil16], and the well-known takedown of Dyn DNS
service on 21st October 2016 [Ang17, Yor16, Hil16] that, with a trac peak of
1.2 Tbps, is the biggest Distributed Denial of Service attack ever recorded.
The Mirai worm is designed to infect and control several types of IoT devices,
such as home routers, DVRs, CCTV cameras, and any other "smart" Internet-
connected appliance, mainly manufactured by XiongMai Technology. The mal-
ware is able to run on a wide range of CPU architectures (such as MIPS, ARM,
PPC, etc.) and it uses a brute force authentication attack based on a dictionary
of 62 entries to gain control of vulnerable units. Once exploited, the insecure de-
vices are reported to a control server in order to be used as part of a large-scale
Agent-Handler botnet [MD16]. Afterwards, the botnet can be used to perpe-
trate several types of DDoS attacks, ranging from the basic SYN Flood attack
to the more sophisticated DNS Water Torture, and exploiting a wide range of
protocols as attack vectors (such as GRE, TCP, UDP, DNS and HTTP).
Today, the Mirai source code is available online. It was rst published on the
hacking community forum HackForums.net on 30th September 2016 by a user
named "Anna-Senpai" [Mil17], then it appeared on GitHub [AS16] and other
Internet locations in the early October 2016.
However, if on one hand the
source code leak gave security researchers the chance to analyse it and identify
possible countermeasures, on the other hand, it raised some issues. First, it
has made more dicult to identify the original creator of Mirai since it is no
longer enough to nd a copy of its source code on a system to spot him [Jer17];
secondly, it has led to the birth of a wide variety of new malwares based on
Mirai (such as [Bad16, Kha17]) which often are more sophisticated and with
improved capabilities.

Once seen the basic infrastructure of the Mirai botnet, we are ready to give a

valid admin credentials is required.

It performs several foreground and background tasks which can be neatly
described as follows:
1. Masking: once running, the worm performs some operations in fore-
ground (such as deleting itself from the le system, altering its name
to a random value, etc.)
in order to both avoid to be discovered
and prevent the reboot of the infected device (that would wipe the

2. Killer: subsequently, it tries to protect itself from any competing
malwares by running a background killer process with the aim of
both eradicating possibly other well-known worms in execution on
the same device and preventing anyone else to breaking in using any
other common methods (such us through Telnet, SSH or HTTP). The
purpose of this behaviour is to both maximize the attack potential
of each device ensuring the full availability of all its computational
resources and prevent to be removed from other malwares.
3. Scanner: afterwards, the worm starts a background process which
is in charge of performing a wide-ranging scan of IP addresses look-
ing for possible vulnerable IoT devices. If it is able to successfully
connect to a target, it tries to remotely access the device by carry-

62 common default pairs of username and password (e.g., admin/ad-
min, root/1234). Once a vulnerability is found, its details (i.e., IP
address, port and login credentials) are sent to the Reporting Server

4. Waiting Commands: nally, it enters in the main foreground exe-
cution loop in which it basically establishes the connection with the
CNC Server and keeps it alive waiting for further commands. If an

the attack is performed.

The Solution: AntibIoTic
The 2016 is remembered as the year that showed to the world how danger-
ous Distributed Denial of Service attacks can be [Yor16, Hil16]. Gauge of the
disruptiveness of a DDoS attack is surely the number of bots it involves: the
bigger the botnet, the more powerful the attack. This character, along with
the increasing availability of connected and insecure IoT devices, makes DDoS
and IoT the perfect pair for the malware industry. Accordingly, the aim of this
chapter is to present AntibIoTic: a possible or, at least, a palliative solution
to prevent DDoS attacks perpetrated through IoT devices.
The chapter is organized adopting a top-down approach. First, the AntibIoTic
idea is illustrated, giving a taste of its functionalities and a look at how it be-
haves in common scenarios. Secondly, its infrastructure is presented, providing a
description of its logical components and how they interact between them. Sub-
sequently, some considerations about the implementation of the solution and a
comparison with its existing "twins", are given. Finally, knowing that the white
worm approach could give rise to some ethical and legal issues, a section that
discusses the security and ethic implications of the solution is oered at the end
of the chapter.

5.1
The Idea
Today, it's a matter of fact that IoT devices are extremely poorly secured and
many dierent IoT malwares are exploiting this insecurity trend to spread glob-
ally in the IoT world and build large-scale botnets later used for extremely
powerful cyber-attacks, especially DDoS. Therefore, the main problem that has
to be solved is the low security level of the IoT cosmos, and that is where
AntibIoTic comes in.
What drove us in the design of AntibIoTic is the belief that the intrinsic weak-
ness of IoT devices might be seen as the solution of the problem instead of as
the problem itself. In fact, the idea is to use the vulnerability of IoT units as a
means to grant their security: like an antibiotic that enters in the bloodstream
and travels through human body killing bacteria without damaging human cells,
AntibIoTic is a worm that infects vulnerable devices and creates a white botnet
of safe systems, removing them from the clutches of other potential dangerous
malwares. Basically, it exploits the most ecient spreading capabilities of exist-
ing IoT malwares (such as Mirai) in order to compete with them in exploiting
and infecting weak IoT hosts but, once control is gained, instead of taking ad-
vantage of them, it performs several operations aimed to notify the owner about
the security threats of his device and potentially acting on his behalf to x them.
In our plans, the AntibIoTic solution will raise the IoT environment to a safer
level, making the life way harsher for DDoS capable IoT malwares that should
eventually slowly disappear. Moreover, the whole solution has been designed
including some functionalities aimed at creating a bridge between security ex-
perts, devices manufacturers and users, in order to increase the awareness about
the IoT security problem and potentially pushing all of them to do their duties
for a more secure global Internet.
Similar approaches have been occasionally tried so far [Bal15, Gra17, Cim17b]
but, to the best of our knowledge, they have mostly been rudimentary and not
documented pieces of code referable to crackers (or, as wrongly but commonly
named, hackers) that want to solve the IoT security problem by taking the
law into their own hands, thus poorness or even lack of preventive design and
documentation are the common traits.
Nevertheless, these attempts are the
proof that the proposed solution is feasible and parts of their source code have
been published under OpenGL license [The15], which makes them reusable for
the implementation of AntibIoTic.

5.3
Technical Feasibility and Implementation
The implementation of the solution proposed so far has not been deployed in
this work, due to lack of time. Nevertheless, a study of technical feasibility was
performed before accurately designing AntibIoTic and its results are presented
below. Noteworthy is to highlight that, although the proposed solution imple-
mentation brings with it some technical challenges to face, most of the required
work has been already done in order to implement similar or antagonist solutions
whose source codes have been published online.
First of all, the backbone behind the AntibIoTic worm, which includes the CNC
Server and the Database, has to be built up. It is the core of the botnet and it
has to be implemented taking in consideration the large amount of infected IoT
devices that will be part of the network (let's consider that the Mirai botnet
included around 500,000 IoT devices), hence redundancy and scalability are
a must.
However, as already mentioned, the source code used for building
the Mirai botnet was published online [AS16] and it includes the GO code
that implements the backbone infrastructure which can be mostly reused to
implement the AntibIoTic backbone.
The second challenge that needs to be faced is the actual AntibIoTic worm
implementation. The worm functionalities can be divided in two main parts:
spreading section and security section; the spreading part includes all the func-
tionalities needed to scatter the worm worldwide, whereas the security section
embraces all the actions performed to secure the weak devices.
As regards
the spreading section, our solutions doesn't need advanced propagations func-
tionalities or 0-day exploits since it is a white worm aimed to infect the most
vulnerable IoT devices which can be secured adopting the most common security
best practice. Therefore, the simple as well as eective spreading functionalities
implemented by Mirai are basically enough for the AntibIoTic implementation,
thus again most of the Mirai C code which implements its worm propagation
functions can be reused for our purpose. On the other hand, the security section
includes several functionalities which can not be obviously found in the Mirai
source code, thus some work has to be performed in order to implement and test
them. Nevertheless, some of the functions included in the AntibIoTic worm se-
curity section are also implemented in Linux.Wifatch [Bal15] which source code
has been publicly released under OpenGL license [The15]. Therefore, it can be
used as source of inspiration and standard of comparison for the AntibIoTic
implementation.
Summarising, the AntibIoTic implementation requires time and dedication, and
it is denitely not a trivial task. However, most of the work can be saved by
reusing some public repositories available online. Moreover, similar alternatives

It is undeniable that the proposed solution drags on some ethical and legal

devices, even if it is done for security purposes.
Sometimes the line between ethical and unethical behaviour is a ne one and,

not be exempt from asking ourselves if our proposal goes too far. Even though
AntibIoTic is motivated by the desire of xing a harsh situation created by rms
unforgivable negligence, it requires to break-in third-party devices without the
owners' explicit consent, which is an illegal and prosecutable practice in several
countries. Nevertheless, we can not ignore that, accordingly to various legisla-
tions, also the very action of failing to protect your own device and unwillingly
participating to a malicious action could be considered illegal. This entails that
our solution could be warmly welcomed and tolerated by the less knowledgeable
users worried to incur in possible prosecution, but unable to apply themselves
a more adequate and stronger security policy.
Somehow, we can think about AntibIoTic as a scapegoat that secures IoT de-
vices and impedes them to cause any harm. A scapegoat that accepts the risk
to be accused for the hosts infection, but both increases the IoT security and

we do not pretend to be considered better than others, hence we know that
the resulting white botnet could always being hacked and used for malicious
purposes. That is where the second open-source benet comes in: an open-
source project would attract other white-hat volunteers and companies that
share our willingness to ght the IoT security threats, which would ensure a
more updated, ecient and reliable software.
Truth be told, we are very concerned about users' privacy and we feel that the
path traced by AntibIoTic should not be taken by anyone, because it could un-
expectedly backre and expose the vulnerabilities to malicious users, no matter
if criminal organisations or intelligence agencies, that could exltrate highly-
sensitive personal data. The only reason why we suggest this solution, con-
tinuously stressing about the transparency requirements, is that the current
situation is beyond any control and something has to be done before it gets
even worse.
We are basically in front of the eternal dispute between freedom and security, and
we are aware that the very right answer does not exist. However, to conclude,
since we strongly believe that "my freedom ends where yours begins", we would
like to leave the reader with a nal question: what should we do when your
freedom aects our security?

Chapter 6
Conclusions
This chapter concludes the thesis. It draws the conclusions of the whole study
by pointing out its main contributions, and discussing results and possible future
work.
6.1
Contributions
The aim of the thesis was to study Distributed Denial of Service attacks from
the IoT perspective, possibly ending up with a solution intended to prevent or
mitigate the phenomenon. Accordingly, the main goal of the work can be con-
sidered successfully fullled, even if a working implementation of the proposed
solution was not provided, due to lack of time. Furthermore, all the thesis objec-
tives, stated in Section 1.1, were successfully accomplished and they eciently
drove the work in the desired direction.
The main contributions of the thesis are discussed below.
The Classication of Distributed Denial of Service Attacks. DDoS
attacks have rapidly evolved in the last few years, becoming more complex
and especially more powerful and eective, due to the IoT revolution.
==PAGE:112==
100
Conclusions
Besides, to the best of our knowledge, the last research work discussing
a taxonomy of DDoS attacks dates back to the the early 2008 [AR08],
long before the IoT outburst. That is why, in Section 3.1, we proposed
an up-to-date comprehensive classication of DDoS attacks based on both
previous scientic literature and newly performed attacks, which can be
considered one of the contributions of the thesis.
The Analysis of DDoS Capable IoT Malwares. Using the new DDoS
attacks classication as foundation of our study, we provided in Section 3.3
a detailed analysis of all the DDoS capable IoT malwares that recently
characterized the IoT-DDoS landscape, showing their main traits, their
correlations, and their numeric progression over the last few years. This
is accounted as another contribution of the thesis.
The Investigation of Mirai. Mirai is one of the most predominant IoT mal-
wares of the last few years. Its disruptiveness changed the world perception
of IoT Security and its source code was published in the late 2016. Given
that, we could not miss the opportunity of carefully analysing the Mirai
source code, and understanding in details its infrastructure and mode of
operation. That is where the Chapter 4 comes from and it is considered a
contribution of this work.
The Proposed Solution: AntibIoTic. AntibIoTic, the solution presented
in Chapter 5, is undeniably the main contribution of the thesis. There are
some main ascertainments that inspired us in its design. First of all, during
the analysis of the Mirai source code, we spotted an extremely interesting
functionality that drew our attention: the Mirai capability of eradicating
other potential malwares running on the same hosting device. Secondly,
it is commonly known that IoT manufacturers are struggling against the
IoT security problem by releasing new rmwares and updates that patch
the well-known threats. Nevertheless, most of the device owners are com-
pletely not security aware and absolutely not motivated to secure their
devices, thus security updates are rarely installed and security best prac-
tises are never adopted. These observations, along with the consciousness
that something has to be done to improve the current dramatic situation
of the IoT security, is what inspired us in the design of AntibIoTic: a
solution aimed to both secure vulnerable IoT devices against existing ma-
licious malwares and increase the people awareness about the IoT security
problem.

6.2
Results and Future Work
The thesis contributions summarized in the previous section mainly fall into two
categories: analysis, design.
The analysis contributions include the DDoS attacks classication, the IoT mal-
ware analysis and the Mirai investigation. The aim of these contributions is to
provide a reference to the scientic community in order to better understand
all the latest types of treats related to DDoS attacks and IoT. We believe these
studies represent a key step in order to raise the awareness of the research com-
munity and tackle the IoT security emergency. Future work of extension and
upgrade are encouraged.
The design contribution is represented by the AntibIoTic solution. The aim
of the proposed design is to provide the main idea, the infrastructure and the
most important functionalities of AntibIoTic, in order to validate it with the
scientic community. Nevertheless, we absolutely are aware that a lot of work
has still to be done. Without going too far into details, future work can be
aimed at facing the following high level issues:
 Design - The proposed design has to be validated and potentially improved
(e.g., by replacing the Agent-Handler architecture with a P2P-Based one
or by simply adding new functionalities) in order to result in a solid and
ecient solution.
 Ethical and Legal Issues - The ethical and legal implications carried out by
the solution (discussed in Section 5.5) have to be tackled before converting
the design into implementation.
 Implementation - The solution has eventually to be implemented either
following the advices given in Section 5.3 or starting from scratch.
