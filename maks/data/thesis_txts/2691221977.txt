Abstract
The Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) protocol is getting more popular for every passing day
but as the popularity has grown the focus on privacy and security has not. This thesis
focuses on privacy leakages within the advertising mechanism of BLE found in literature.
The literature for this thesis was selected through a process described in the methodol­
ogy section. It is found that there are several ways to exploit advertisements and that
many smart products poorly implement the privacy features of BLE. These vulnerabilities
and products have had the impact and likeliness assessed for three types of user groups:
average users, tech enthusiasts, and persons of interest. The impact was assessed de­
pending on the consequences a particular user group would suffer from an attack. The
likeliness was assessed as the risk of a particular user group being subject to an attack.
The possibilities for developers and users to mitigate the different attacks are discussed
as well.

Introduction
Today, a great number of consumer devices are Bluetooth enabled thanks to the support
of the technology in smartphones and the rise of Internet­of­Things (IoT). The ability for
any device to seamlessly connect and exchange information via Bluetooth Low Energy
(BLE) protocol promises unlimited room for innovation. However, as these devices grow
in popularity, so should concerns over security and privacy.
To assess privacy and security different attacks and exploits associated with BLE adver­
tisements will be evaluated in this paper. The likeliness and impact of attacks are going
to be assessed for three different kinds of user groups.
• The average users regarded as not technically proficient
• The tech enthusiasts
• Persons of interest who is generally a more attractive target i.e. politician or million­
aire, than the first two groups.
The likeliness of attack scores are: low (unlikely), medium (somewhat likely), high (likely).
The impact of attack scores are: low (little impact), medium (some impact), high (huge
impact).
All papers found throughout the search process which discussed BLE vulnerabilities in
general and smart products based on BLE are included in this literature review. The full
description of the references in this paper can be found in the bibliography or by clicking
on the year in the reference as this is a hyperlink to the paper in the bibliography.
Wherever possible, the assumptions made have been clearly stated in order to reduce
the subjectivity and increase transparency for the reader.
DISCLAIMER: all mac addresses and other potentially sensitive information seen in the
figures throughout this report are randomly generated and not from any real devices.

Background of Bluetooth
Advertisements
In this section a short walk­through of the technology behind BLE advertisements will be
described. This is intended for the reader not familiar with BLE.
BLE is part of Bluetooth Core Specification and is a wireless technology designed to be
used for IoT and was released in 2009. A device only compatible with BLE cannot com­
municate with a Bluetooth Classic device. The content of this section are from the doc­
umentation and writings of: Core Specification Working Group 2019, Gupta 2016, and
Afaneh 2018.
The goal of BLE advertising packets is to convey information to other nearby BLE devices.
To understand the BLE advertisement it is necessary to understand parts of the Bluetooth
Low Energy architecture which can be seen below in figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: Bluetooth Low Energy Protocol Stack. Source: Mendoza et al. 2018

4.1
Generic Access Profile
Generic Access Profile (GAP) provides a framework that dictates how BLE devices in­
teract with one another. This includes: the mode of the device, procedures related to
discovery of devices, connecting to devices, and security. This ensures uniformity be­
tween devices from different vendors. So even though devices are manufactured and
designed by different manufactures they can work together, provided some of the same
profiles are present. There are four main roles in GAP that BLE devices can operate in.
• Central
• Peripheral
• Broadcaster
• Observer
Devices can operate in one or more roles at the same time provided the Link Layer sup­
ports this.
A device in the central role (master) discovers devices in peripheral and broadcaster
mode with the additional capability of connecting to peripherals as the initiator.
A device in the peripheral role (slave) advertises itself with the additional capability of
accepting connections from a device with a central role.
A device in the observer role receives advertisements from broadcasters or peripherals.
A device in the broadcaster role transmits advertising packets.
If two devices are connected this allows for bi­directional data transfer. If a device is in
advertise mode (broadcaster or peripheral without connection support) it cannot receive
packets from a device in central or observer mode, i.e. it is one­directional. These types
of connections can be seen in figure 4.2

A one­directional transmission is also known as a passive scan as the scanner only listens
for advertisements without engaging. The advertiser does not know of the existence of
the scanner in this scenario.

A bi­directional transmission is also known as an active scan because the scanner re­
quests additional information once an advertisement is received. A bi­directional trans­
mission can be used for establishing a pairing or exchanging data between two paired
devices. A scanner can also only request additional information without pairing.
4.2
Radio Frequency Channels
For advertising devices there are 40 Radio Frequency (RF) channels. The three channels
37, 38, and 39 are the primary advertising channels as they have the lowest WiFi activity
(WiFi uses the same channels for its connections). This is done to minimize the amount
of WiFi interference. The 37 remaining channels are the secondary advertising channels.
An overview of these 40 channels can be seen in figure 4.3.
Figure 4.3: BLE advertising channels source: Tosi et al. 2017
To understand secondary channels it must first be known that there are two types of ad­
vertising depending on the version of BLE in the device.
• Legacy, Bluetooth 4.0­4.2
• Extended, Bluetooth 5.0+
Before 4.0 there were only one Bluetooth protocol now known as Bluetooth Classic. BLE
was introduced when Bluetooth Classic 4.0 was released, and they follow the same re­
lease numbering.
Secondary channels are utilized in extended advertising. Their purpose is to enable more
data being advertised. This is done by having a small header packet sent on the primary
channels which points to an auxiliary packet on the secondary channel as seen in fig­
ure 4.4. In addition this also reduces contention, because legacy advertising is sending
the complete payload repeatedly on the main advertising channels.

The options of pairing methods partly depends on the input and output capabilities of the
two devices being paired.
4.3.1
Just Works
From the user’s perspective the devices seemingly pair without any further interaction
needed. This option is the best for user experience but the worst for security. In BLE
Extended it does not protect against man­in­the­middle (MITM) attacks and in BLE legacy
it does not protect against eavesdropping in addition to MITM.
4.3.2
Numeric Comparison
For this pairing method both devices need have a display for a generated 6­digit confir­
mation code to show. The user then visually compares these codes. If they match the
user must confirm on the devices.
4.3.3
Passkey Entry
This method can be used if one device has a display and the other device has a keyboard
or similar way to input numbers. A 6­digit passkey is displayed on the device with a screen
and the user is prompted to type this passkey into the other device.
4.3.4
Out of Band (OOB)
In OOB pairing, the cryptographic values are replaced by an alternative wireless tech­
nology such as Near Field Communication. This requires both devices to support this
feature. This is the most secure option but rarely used due to its complexity.
4.3.5
Elliptical Curve Diffie­Hellman (ECDH) in BLE 4.2
BLE 4.2 introduces Elliptical Curve Diffie­Hellman (ECDH) algorithm for key exchange
which fixed the possibility of passive eavesdropping when using the Just Works pairing
A Survey on Private Information Leakage from BLE Advertisements

==PAGE:21==
method in older versions. If a device uses the Just Works pairing method, a version of
BLE older than 4.2, and if the attacker is listening on communication channels when the
pairing initiates, then the long­term key for encryption can be obtained. This is due to the
short­term key being hard coded which is used for encrypting the long­term key.

4.6
Generic Attribute Profile (GATT)
To understand GATT the underlying framework: the Attribute Protocol (ATT) must be un­
derstood.
4.6.1
ATT
ATT tells how a server exposes its data to a client and how it is structured. Server and
client are defined as:
Server
The device which exposes data. It accepts incoming commands and sends responses,
notifications and indications. For example, a thermometer will be a server when it adver­
tises its data to devices in its proximity.
Client
The device which interacts with the server with the purpose of reading its data or sending
it commands. It sends commands, requests, and receives notifications. An example is a
smartphone which connects to the thermometer.
The data which a server exposes is structured as attributes. An attribute is the generic
term for any data type exposed by the server.
GATT defines the way which two BLE devices transfer data between themselves using
services and characteristics. It makes use of ATT, which is used to store services, char­
acteristics and related data in a simple lookup table using UUIDs for the entries in the
table.

Random Address is a privacy feature of BLE where the device’s Public Address stays
hidden and the device uses a random address that changes periodically to prevent out­

through the different random addresses by an Identity Resolution Key (IRK). When two
devices are paired they exchange this key which the later randomized address can be
resolved by. Essentially it works as the symmetric key algorithm in cryptography. GAP
has two types of random addresses.

it is running. If a device changes its static address another device will not be able
to link it with its previous address.
2. Private Address: the private address has additionally two sub types
(a) Non­resolvable private address: The real address of the device can never be
derived.
(b) Resolvable private address: The real address of the device can be derived by
the means of the random address and some initial connection details.

5.1.1
Why Randomize
MAC randomization for personal devices is an important privacy feature which if not uti­
lized can lead to tracking of an individual through his Bluetooth MAC address. It must
be expected that devices carried around by human users, which are highly susceptible to
tracking, should use Random Resolvable Address or Random Non­Resolvable Address

of type public thereby revealing their globally unique Bluetooth MAC address for any­

indication as not all devices need to have a randomized Bluetooth MAC address. A ther­
mometer can have a Public address as this information is very rarely sensitive and does
not need to be disguised. Some stationary smart products can use a Public Address or
Static Random Address as they are clearly not threatened by tracking. Figure 5.1 visual­

5.1.2
Randomize the Randomization
An important factor of Bluetooth MAC address randomization is that the time for random­
ization also needs to be randomized. This is not a part of the recommendation by the
Bluetooth SIG group but in reality it would be good practice. This is because if a Blue­
tooth MAC address’ randomization happens consistently at the same interval each time
it becomes too predictable which weakens the randomization. An adversary can observe
that when one Bluetooth MAC address stops transmitting, then at the same time, a new
Bluetooth MAC address starts transmitting. Thus, an adversary can therefore deduce that
they are most likely the same device. Even in an area with a high density of devices, if
they all randomize at a fixed time, all devices’ randomization time could be mapped and
thereby tracked. Only those devices which randomization coincidentally happens at the
same time would be hidden. The size of the anonymity set would still be drastically re­
duced. This still means that this attack does not scale well and is most effective in small
environments with a limited number of addresses randomizing.
The issue of randomization intervals is discussed by Martin, Alpuche, et al. 2019 where
they among other findings discover that both Apple and Microsoft products randomize

5.1.3
Timing Attacks
Matte et al. 2016 discuss how WiFi MAC randomization could be defeated by timing at­
tacks. The timing attack is a timing based method which considers inter­frame­arrival
time between frames from the same MAC address. The authors then find patterns in the
timing based distances by an algorithm enabling them to group different MAC addresses
together as originating from the same device. It might be possible to utilize this attack in
BLE by scan requests as it shares many features with probe requests seen in WiFi. This
can neither be confirmed or disproved without further research into the subject which is
outside the scope of this thesis. Assuming this would work in BLE it could be a signifi­
cant flaw in Bluetooth MAC address randomization as the authors of the paper had 77.2%
success rate of grouping MAC addresses together coming from the same device.

other alternatives. If it is feasible to upscale this attack it could be useful to deanonymize
parts of a larger crowd and link randomized MAC addresses together. The attack would
probably be most effective as a tool in a collection of different attacks so the success of

5.1.4
Universal Adoption
For Bluetooth MAC address randomization to be truly effective it should first and foremost
be universally adopted where appropriate. It becomes increasingly more difficult for a
malicious user to diffuse each device’s associated traffic the higher the percentage of
those devices that perform proper randomization is. If an attacker has a problem set of
ten devices but half of them are public it becomes much easier to ’solve’ the rest. So
whether it is a complete lack of any randomization or just easily breakable randomization,
it effectively aids in the reduction of the problem set for an attacker. The universal adoption
issue is discussed by Martin, Mayberry, et al. 2017. This author discusses WiFi MAC
randomization but the principle is the same. Secondly the Bluetooth SIG group should also
make it mandatory to randomize the timing of the randomization so it does not become
predictable as discussed in the beginning of this section.
It could also be considered to lower the minimum amount of time before a randomization
should happen as this will further increase security. The problem of this aspect, compared
to the ones mentioned earlier, is that this affects the user experience. The most secure
thing would be to randomize the Bluetooth MAC address very frequently but this would
drain the battery and lead to a frustrating user experience. As many other aspects of
security it is a balance between usability and security.

5.3.1
Bluetooth Low Energy Botnet
Issoufaly and Tournoux 2017a theorize how to compose a botnet consisting of BLE de­
vices that will be able to track an individual with high precision. Figure 5.6 is from the
paper and shows how the botnet functions.

==PAGE:39==
Figure 5.6: Bluetooth Low Energy Botnet (BLEB). From: Issoufaly and Tournoux 2017a
The botnet relies on Public addresses or Static Random addresses from the advertising
packets of vulnerable fitness, smartwatch, and sensor devices. The addresses are gath­
ered on other users’ smartphones in proximity by a compromised application installed
on them. This malicious application captures the Bluetooth MAC addresses and sends
them to a centralized server where this information is stored. The effectiveness of cap­
turing addresses varies greatly depending on which mobile OS the malicious application
is installed on:
iOS does not allow scan requests for applications running in the background. Additionally,
the Bluetooth MAC address captured by iOS is partly anonymized in sense that the device
only returns the device name and a UUID that is generated for this pair of devices. The
UUID is consistent as long as the Bluetooth MAC address stays the same. This makes
iOS subpar to use as it would be difficult to merge the traces of the UUID from different
iOS devices tracking the same device.
Android allows for scan requests of applications running the in the background but it is
limited and easily detectable by the OS if misused. Android returns the Bluetooth MAC
address of the advertising packet which makes tracking significantly easier compared to
iOS.
The best options are Linux, Ubertooth, and rooted smartphones. These operating
systems allow for unlimited scan requests of an installed application in the background.
Additionally, applications are given access to the raw data obtained from advertising pack­
ets. This essentially means: it can be done stealthy in the background, it can process all

system. The biggest downside for the perpetrator is by far that these operating systems
account for a small fraction of the operating systems on smartphones.
Mitigation ­ user
A user can mitigate being part of the botnet by not downloading malicious applications.
Additionally, owning an iOS device lowers the risk as iOS is subpar for a botnet.
A user can mitigate being tracked by the botnet by ensuring that the smart devices he owns
are using either Random Resolvable Address or Random Non­Resolvable Address.
Mitigation ­ developer
To mitigate devices being part of the botnet all smart phone operating systems should
anonymize the Bluetooth MAC address, as is seen in iOS. It might be possible for the
perpetrator to somehow still link these anonymized addresses but it will be substantially
more difficult.
To mitigate a user being tracked by the botnet, the developers should implement either
Random Resolvable Address or Random Non­Resolvable Address in their devices.

of the advertisement, which means that they are in the same location. Therefore, if the
location data can be compromised by just one of these devices then all other devices’

a user would still be able to find the desired advertisement but this precaution would con­
fuse algorithms with ill intent. Another way to prevent this fingerprinting is already seen
in iOS and is discussed in section 5.3.1. iOS does not allow scan requests for applica­
tions to run in the background and this will often prevent the applications from collecting
advertisements and creating the fingerprint.

A user can mitigate being fingerprinted by scan traces by not downloading malicious ap­

The operating system could inject fake advertisements every time an application would

5.3.3
Cross App Tracking
Korolova and Sharma 2018 hypothesizes on how to create a unique fingerprint based on
the incoming advertising packets of nearby BLE devices. More specifically the fingerprint
is based on two different applications on the same device sharing their BLE scan data
between them. The derivation of such a fairly unique fingerprint is possible because of
the rapidly increasing number and diversity of BLE devices as well as easy access to
the highly detailed BLE data in the Android and iOS operating systems. This fingerprint
would be able to track the user without reliance on shared login or other identifiers such
as Bluetooth MAC address. By analyzing the frequency of various advertisements, one
could deduce which products a user may own and thereby commit targeted advertising.
As discussed in section 5.3.2, it should be plausible for the fingerprint to be used for
location tracking if the perpetrators set up their own beacons. It would, though, be limited
to single targets and not be very scalable.
Creating this fingerprint requires that the user is exposed to enough BLE devices so that
the two applications with a high certainty can create a fingerprint. Subjects living in the
far off countryside with few BLE devices in their daily vicinity would have uncertain finger­
prints.
Korolova and Sharma 2018 ran into the same restrictions regarding background scan­
ning as Issoufaly and Tournoux 2017a, which is discussed in section 5.3.1. Therefore,
Korolova and Sharma 2018 tested the scenario of the users actively engaging with the
application so as not to exclude iOS. The testing took place at a university campus. The
authors were able to achieve 99% correct fingerprinting if the applications were used very
frequently and about a 50% correct fingerprinting if the applications were used only once
daily.
To prevent this form of tracking the operating systems should anonymize incoming ad­
vertisements’ addresses. This anonymization would ensure that each application on the
smartphone could still function correctly without being able to fingerprint the user by shar­
ing the information with each other. The incoming advertisement anonymization seen in
iOS (section 5.3.1) is per device and not per application, meaning two apps on the same
device will receive the same randomized address, thus allowing an attack to succeed.
Mitigation ­ user
A user can mitigate being cross app tracked by not downloading two malicious applica­
tions which interact with each other.
Mitigation ­ developer
To prevent this form of tracking the operating systems should anonymize incoming adver­
tisements’ addresses for every application.

RSSI (Received Signal Strength Indication)
Fafoutis, Marchegiani, et al. 2017 show how to determine the activity and distance of the
victim through the RSSI of eavesdropped packets. The victim’s movement can be de­
termined and a correlation can be made to typical every day activities such as watching
television, working on the computer, cooking etc. These findings are similar to Kalan­
tar, Mohammadi, and Sadrieh 2018 who discover that the user’s motion status can be
specified using RSSI if a user is within a space where four beacons is set up.
An adversary might also sit outside someone’s home and detect whether or not the device
is present. If he surveils someone’s smartphone, which a person is very inclined to always
have on them, and there is no signal, it can be assumed that the target is not home. This
information could be used to determine an optimal point in time for robbing the house.
Fafoutis, Mellios, et al. 2015 created an RSSI­based wall prediction model based on the
WiFi signal received. This model turned out fairly accurate to estimate the number of
walls between the receiver and broadcaster. The concept could be applied to RSSI of
BLE advertisement. If multiple devices are present in the same household, which would
often be the case in a ’smart home’, a map could be created of the room layout in the
house. This could be abused by robbers to plan their break in more precisely.

5.5
Man­In­The­Middle (MITM)
Melamed 2018 demonstrates how to do a Man­In­The­Middle (MITM) attack in BLE. A
perpetrator secretly relays the communication between the master and slave which is
believed to be direct. The intruder captures the advertisements sent out by the slave
and then uses them to simulate a fake device. The intruder will try to lure the user to
connect to the fake device instead of the real device. This attack typically utilizes the
lowest advertising interval possible so the user’s device will see the fake device first. For
devices using a BLE version 5.0 or newer this works for the pairing method Just Works.
For devices still using a BLE version older than 4.2, this attack also works for Passkey
Entry as it can be brute forced on the fly as proven by Ryan 2013.
Fawaz, Kim, and Shin 2016 discuss how to protect products which are paired and have
been identified as vulnerable to various attacks. A concept is designed where an external
Bluetooth radio is attached to the vulnerable product. An accompanying app is installed
on the central (in this paper it is a smartphone, but it could be designed for other centrals
if necessary as long as they are screen or button compatible). The purpose of this design
is to act as a middleman, jamming all outgoing and ingoing signals to the smart prod­
uct unless the user via the accompanying app decides to authorize a connection. The
design requires additional hardware, increases difficulty of use and thereby lowers User
Experience but is one of the few mitigations against MITM.
Mitigation ­ user
Avoid devices with a BLE version older than 4.2. Avoid devices with the Just Works pairing
method.
Mitigation ­ developer
Do not use a BLE version older than 4.2.Avoid using the Just Works pairing method in
products.

5.6
GATT Profile Fingerprinting
Attribute data exposed by an advertising device (server) are shown in a GATT profile
which is a hierarchical structure of these attributes as discussed in section 4.6. The GATT
profile information can be obtained by detecting a connectable Peripheral, connecting to
it, and then requesting the profile. This means that a device cannot passively collect
GATT profiles. The GATT profile contains services and characteristics which contain a
large number of components that are subject to variation depending on the device. For
example if a device have a characteristic by the name ’Cycling_Speed’ it is most likely
some sort of sports watch. In addition to these variations, the services and characteristics
also have a UUID each.
Some of the information in the GATT profile can also be used to intrude on the device
owner’s privacy. Depending on the device, it is possible to gather: user’s name, device
type, device manufacturer, and device model. Martin, Alpuche, et al. 2019 show that an
adversary can query any Apple device for their exact model through the GATT profile, if
they support BLE. If an advertisement can be determined to be a certain product or from
a certain manufacturer then targeted advertisement can be sent to this user.
All of this information can still be randomized or anonymized to an extent where devices
cannot be fingerprintable or privacy leaking. The privacy of the user is at the complete
discretion of the manufacturer.
From the data set by Celosia and Cunche 2019b, 95% of devices with Public Addresses
can also be uniquely identified by by GATT profile fingerprints. In contrast, 86% of iPhones,
which have proper randomization of the Bluetooth MAC address, could only be identified
to anonymity sets of 100 units or more which makes them difficult to identify as unique
units. Of the other non­iPhone devices with Private Addresses, 74% of them were in
anonymity sets of size 10 or less and 32% of them were uniquely identifiable.
Zuo et al. 2019 discover that 94.6% of the devices could be fingerprinted from their ob­
served packets. This number sounds promising but their data is only extracted from An­
droid devices’ companion apps. This data is from a selective group but within this group
the vast majority of devices are exposed to tracking through the GATT profile.
As described by Zuo et al. 2019, it is also possible to extract the UUID from the BLE
IoT mobile apps themselves due to the mobile apps need to know the UUIDs of the de­
vice which is cross­referenced with the advertising packets when a connection is created.
Many UUIDs can also be mapped in a single application on the smartphone which can
A Survey on Private Information Leakage from BLE Advertisements
38
==PAGE:48==
make way for targeted advertising, for example if an area has a high number of UUIDs re­
lated to fitness. Many companies use 16­bit UUIDs because they are more efficient (see
section 4.5) but this means that their UUIDs have a manufacturer specific prefix which
they can be identified by.
Mitigation ­ user
There is no mitigation for a user.
Mitigation ­ developer
An effective way of preventing sniffing the UUID would be to randomize the UUID on the

6.1.1
Fitbit Charge 2
The Fitbit Charge 2 is a smartwatch which is investigated in several papers where multiple
privacy concerns are highlighted. According to Issoufaly and Tournoux 2017a, the Charge
2 uses Random Static Address and it should according to the flag in the PDU header that
tells the receiver what address type it uses. However, Becker, Li, and Starobinski 2019
tried to:
• Drain the battery
• Performing a hard reset
• Factory resetting
and concludes that it in fact uses a Public Address. They conclude this because the Blue­
tooth MAC address still remained the same after these attempts when it should change
at power cycles. Mendoza et al. 2018 also list the Fitbit Charge 2 as a device with Public
Address.
It is bad practice to use both address types in devices meant for users to carry around. A
Random Static Address can be tracked for as long as the power cycle is continuing. This
can be indefinite, if the user never lets the device run out of power. It is slightly better
than using a Public Address but not much. Users would be less likely to be successfully
tracked in a scaled attack as their address might change sometimes but in a targeted
attack it would offer very little protection.
The device also keeps using undirected advertising instead of direct discovery even af­
ter being paired. This means the device accepts connection requests from any unknown
peer device which makes it vulnerable to GATT profiling as discussed in section 5.6. GATT
profiling exposes users to tracking but tracking through the Public Address is significantly
easier as it does not require a connection. Thus, for this device GATT profiling is unnec­
essary.
The Fitbit Charge 2 device does not use LE Secure Connections that was released with

6.4
AirDrop/AWDL
Stute et al. 2019 discuss and test AirDrop/AWDL vulnerabilities. AirDrop is a part of the of
Apple Wireless Direct Link (AWDL) protocol which is an extension of WiFi that integrates
with BLE and is a low latency, high speed, WiFi peer­to­peer connection. AirDrop is a
service which allows sharing of files or images with other Apple products in the proximity.
AWDL implements MAC randomization for the IEEE 802.11 header (WiFi packet) but it
contains several identifiers which disclose sensitive information. After being activated (as
a result of an authorized connection) it broadcasts these identifiers un­encrypted. These
identifiers are:
• The hostname which in many cases are the user’s real name e.g ”Bob­iPhone”
• The un­randomized Bluetooth MAC address
• The device class, which tells which type of Apple OS the device is running (iOS,
macOS, or tvOS)
• The protocol version, which can be used in combination with the device class to
figure out exactly the OS version, e.g. AWDL 2.0 is used in macOS 10.12 but AWDL
3.0 is native to macOS 10.13.
For sharing files AirDrop has three settings built in:
• Everyone
• Contacts only
• Off
If the settings of an Apple device is ’Contacts only’, which is the default, it works by Air­
Drop using a device­specific Apple­signed certificate UUID. This UUID is an Apple­signed,
A Survey on Private Information Leakage from BLE Advertisements
51
==PAGE:61==
record data block which contains the UUID and contact identifiers in hashed format that
is registered in the user’s Apple ID. Essentially this is a list of approved contacts which is
presented for any AirDrop connection when the device is in ’Contacts Only’ mode. AirDrop
uses four contact identifier hashes which are encrypted with SHA­256 as documented by
Celosia and Cunche 2020. Due to the short length, an attacker could, with the appropriate
tools, brute force this in about 30 seconds (the complete calculation of this can be seen
in the paper by Stute et al. 2019). This means that after 30 seconds a ’correct’ contact
identifier can be found and the perpetrator would be able to transfer a file via AirDrop to
a device having the ”Contacts Only” setting enabled.
Because these identifiers can be decrypted (as discussed by Heinrich, Hollick, et al. 2021),
the perpetrator can disclose sensitive information both as a malicious receiver and a ma­
licious sender as follows:
Malicious receiver: a sender discloses their own contact identifiers as part of the initial
discover message; thereby a malicious receiver can learn the hashed contact identifiers
of the sender. This entails that the perpetrator must wait for the user to send a file over
AirDrop, so it is not very effective for single targets, but can be viable for public spaces to
gather a lot of users’ information.
Malicious sender: a receiver responds with all their personal contact identifiers when
receiving an AirDrop request. This attack can impact users with their AirDrop setting set
to ’Everyone’. Users with setting ’Contacts Only’ can also be subject to this attack by the
previously explained exploit of brute forcing the contact identifiers.
The un­randomized Bluetooth MAC address could be used for tracking, but this seems
unreliable to do in reality. To be traceable it requires the victim to either receive or send
files via AirDrop. This means that either the user has to be a frequent user of the protocol
or the perpetrator needs to send the user files to activate it. Is it possible for the perpetrator
to send the user AirDrop files even if set to ’Contacts Only’ but the user would probably be
annoyed or suspicious as he would keep receiving files from someone not in his contacts
and then disable the function. Therefore, unless the user voluntarily and actively uses the
AirDrop functionality, it is not a reliable way of tracking.

The user should disable AirDrop to prevent any perpetrator of learning the operating sys­

Apple Siri
Siri is the virtual assistant in Apple devices which uses voice commands, issued by the
user to perform actions and answer questions. The keyword ”Hey Siri” activates it and
messages are broadcasted to Siri enabled devices. The message is subject to percep­

pronunciation. As discussed by Celosia and Cunche 2020 it would be possible to monitor
BLE traffic for observable events to then be able to cross reference it with the perceptual
hashes. After gathering enough data to assemble a dictionary, the commands could be
decoded.

bypass the need to obtain a user’s explicit permission to access location when the appli­

that the Google Play store does not accept new apps or app updates that targets anything
below API level 295. If the app is already on the Play store it can do whatever it wants
but it cannot be updated unless it starts to target API level 29 or above. Furthermore, as
of Android 10 (API level 29), users see a warning when starting an app for the first time if
the app targets API level 22 or lower. At the moment only about 9% of Android user have
Android 10 or above but this number increases fast with time. Many users would probably
be discouraged to use the application if a warning appears when launching it. Thus, this
vulnerability has been fixed in later API levels and is slowly being phased out.
6.6.1
Mitigation ­ user
If the user confirms that the apps he owns are fairly recently updated, this would prevent
any application targeting a SdkVersion lower than 29.
6.6.2
Mitigation ­ developer
It seems Google are, albeit rather slowly, fixing this issue.
6.6.3
Likeliness of attack
As it requires a relatively old app and new ones with this exploit cannot be published, it
has a low likeliness for all three user groups.

6.8
TrackR
TrackR is a commercial, localization gadget which assists in the tracking of lost belongings
and devices. It works by pairing it to a smartphone. When connected, they can ping each
other after which the user can find the device. It operates on BLE 4.0 and uses the ’Just
Works’ pairing method as seen by their instruction video6. As discussed in section 4.3.5
this entails that the device is susceptible to passive eavesdropping. The information being
exchanged is not that sensitive in itself, but it does allow for tracking.
According to Issoufaly and Tournoux 2017b and Issoufaly and Tournoux 2017a the TrackR
uses Static Random Address, which randomizes the address between power cycles, and
the battery is supposedly able to last up to a year. This essentially makes the device
traceable for very long periods of time.
TrackR uses a service called Immediate Alert Service (IAS) but the permission for this
value in TrackR is set to write without permission. This means that, as TrackR uses ’Just
Works’ for pairing, a malicious user might connect to another user’s TrackR device and
alter the IAS value to activate the ring sound.
6.8.1
Mitigation ­ user
The three vulnerabilities discussed related to the TrackR device are all unavoidable for a
user of the device and therefore the only mitigation is not to use it.
6.8.2
Mitigation ­ developer
The company behind TrackR should use the newest version of BLE to prevent passive
eavesdropping. They should use Random Resolvable Address to prevent tracking. Fi­
nally the the permission for the IAS service should be set to write with permission.
6.8.3
Likeliness of attack
The IAS exploit is most likely not going to used very much as it is mostly useful as a way
to prank your friends and is thus assessed as low likeliness.
As discussed in section 6.1.1 due to the eavesdropping requiring an attacker to already
be listening on the RF channel at the time of pairing, it has a low likeliness for the average
user and tech enthusiast but medium for a person of interest. However, the Random
Static Address is an easy way for a perpetrator to track the device. As there is no way to
prevent this tracking, it will be assessed as high likeliness for all three user groups.

Celosia and Cunche 2019a have determined that these three smart products advertise

somewhat reliably track this device. If the adversary would run into a device with the same
number he would most likely still be able to track the device when comparing additional
factors such as randomization time.
This tracking would not require constant surveillance either. This is because if an ad­
versary would see a device with the same digits after a little while, it would most likely
be the same unit. However, due to the size of the number space it is still not as reliable
as a Public address or a completely unique identifier. The reason is that after a while of
not tracking the device a perpetrator can not be certain if it is the same. For example if
someone is tracking a location such as a mall and if a device is seen with the same digits
after a week of disappearance it might as well be a different device.

Mitigation ­ user

Discussion
In this section I discuss some of the methods which the thesis is based upon, the core
aspects of this thesis, and potential future work.
The method of this thesis is a literature review. The subject could have been approached
in other ways that might have contributed with other relevant findings. A potential bias of
this literature review is that accepted papers might have a tendency to focus on products
with bigger market shares as such papers may attract more interest from the journal’s
readers. As mentioned in the section on Methodology, 50% of the papers were from 2018
or later but even a paper from 2019 can easily be outdated. Technology moves at an
extreme pace and every year major developments are accomplished. This pace means
that there is a possibility for some of the attacks mentioned throughout the thesis to be
outdated and no longer relevant. I have tried to search for official fixes to the identified
vulnerabilities but none have been found.
This thesis has looked into products’ vulnerabilities and intentionally not included the mar­
ket share when assessing likeliness and impact of attacks. However, it would have been
interesting to actually determine how many users are affected by the found vulnerabilities,
as this would give an impression of the risk for society. However, this was not within the
chosen scope of the thesis.
Developers wishing to develop a device with BLE can learn from this thesis about the
mistakes and the tendency to sloppiness of others. One could speculate if there is an
immature understanding of how important security issues are starting to become to the
general public. Developers should be aware of the following:
• Make sure personal device uses a Random Non­Resolvable Address or Random
Resolvable Address
• Use the newest version of BLE
• Implement a variation in the randomization cycle
• Do not implement any identifiers in advertisements that are static or have a pre­
dictable pattern
• Do not expose information such as device type or operating system in advertise­
ments
This list is not a complete fix to all issues seen in this thesis. It is the most common and
general problems which BLE developers should take note of.
Assessing the impact and likeliness of attacks is inherently a subjective and difficult task.
The assessments throughout this thesis are my considerations, and it should be noted
that others might take a different view.
This thesis has found location tracking to be the most common vulnerability seen in BLE
advertisements. If one were to take this work further, different intents of the tracking
could be investigated. This could result in a more precise impact assessment as there is
a big difference of impact depending on the use of location tracking: crowd localization,
targeted advertisements, stalking etc.One could also consider the extensiveness of the
tracking. Is the user e.g. only tracked while inside a limited public area, in his own home,
A Survey on Private Information Leakage from BLE Advertisements

==PAGE:70==
or is the user tracked everywhere etc.
Security and privacy in products are for the manufactures often a case of cost­benefit.
Manufactures weigh the cost of improved security against the potential loss of privacy­
orientated customers. The Fitbit brand have had numerous vulnerabilities pointed out as
far back as 2016. One must assume the company behind Fitbit knows about these vul­
nerabilities. Nonetheless, one of their newest products continues to use Bluetooth 4.0
which gives this device the same vulnerabilities as older products.
Perhaps the Fitbit corporation have assessed that their customers do not care for po­
tential privacy leaks such as tracking. Many consumers already give up their location
data (maybe unknowingly) when using popular free applications such as Google Maps,
Snapchat, Facebook, Instagram etc. Maybe Fitbit has decided that keeping Bluetooth 4.0
outweighs the benefit of making the product more secure as this will make it incompatible
with older products. It might also be that Fitbit merely keeps doing what they have done so
far because no significant backlash has yet occurred related to weak or missing security.
Implementing a variation in the randomization cycle would be a quick and inexpensive
change. Such a variation would not decrease battery time nor increase difficulty of use,
so the user experience would remain unaltered. One might wonder why corporations such
as Microsoft and Apple have not done this. The papers discussing this issue are from 2019
so it might be that Microsoft and Apple has implemented this variation by now. It could be
that Microsoft and Apple were unknowing of this issue when the papers discussing this
vulnerability were released. Perhaps Apple and Microsoft simply do not care because
there is no legislative consequences, the same way most companies did not care for the
handling of personal data before GDPR was introduced.

8
Conclusion
This thesis presents a literature review on privacy leakages in BLE advertisements. Sev­
eral vulnerabilities have been identified through the selected, relevant literature. A number
of smart products using BLE are documented as having privacy vulnerabilities. Some of
the most common vulnerabilities found in this thesis’ literature pool are:
• Missing Bluetooth MAC randomization
• Exposure of device type and operating system in the advertisements
• Use of old BLE versions
• Identifiers that are static or have a predictable pattern
Some of the devices exposed as having privacy vulnerabilities are:
• A number of Fitbit products
• Apple products utilizing Apple Handoff
• Apple products utilizing Apple AirDrop
• Microsoft products utilizing Microsoft CDP
These vulnerabilities and products have had the impact and likeliness assessed for three
types of user groups: average users, tech enthusiasts, and persons of interest. The im­
pact was assessed depending on the consequences a particular user group would suffer
from an attack. The likeliness was assessed as the risk of a particular user group being
subject to an attack. The possibilities for developers and users to mitigate the different
attacks are discussed as well.
