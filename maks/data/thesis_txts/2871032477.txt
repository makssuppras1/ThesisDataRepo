Abstract
The formation of high molecular weight proteins (HMWP) due to disulfide bond shuffling poses a sig-
nificant challenge to the stability of aqueous injectable formulations. This project aimed to investigate
the mechanisms underlying HMWP formation and identify the specific conditions that influence this
shuffling process. Through a series of studies, we assessed the impact of mild oxidative agents, including
hydrogen peroxide and oxygen, and Fe(III), as well as ionic strength from sodium acetate, on HMWP
formation rates. The findings indicate that low concentrations of H2O2 and O2 positively contribute to
reducing HMWP formation rates, while N2 and certain chelating agents exacerbate HMWP levels by
promoting reductive conditions. Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that increasing ionic strength
stabilizes the thiolate ion, enhancing its reactivity with disulfide bonds, which influences HMWP forma-
tion. The findings emphasize the importance of balancing oxidative and reductive conditions to stabilize
proteins in drug formulations. It suggests strategic manipulation of ionic and redox environments to
improve peptide stability, bioavailability, and effectiveness, guiding pharmaceutical development and
therapeutic strategies. Future research should further explore the interactions of chelating agents and
examine the use of other transition metals to deepen the understanding of their roles in HMWP forma-
tion.

1.1
The Global Burden of Diabetes and Obesity
As Novo Nordisk’s initiatives focus on advancing diabetes care, they are directly addressing the intensi-
fying public health challenge posed by the global rise in diabetes mellitus. Diabetes mellitus represents
one of the most pressing public health crises of the 21st century. As of 2021, approximately 537 million
adults worldwide live with diabetes Fig.1.2, a figure projected to rise to 643 million by 2030 and 783
million by 2045, driven largely by the escalating prevalence of Type 2 diabetes (T2D) [2]. T2D accounts
for over 90% of cases and is linked to obesity, lack of physical activity, and poor dietary habits. In
2011, diabetes contributed to 4.6 million deaths globally [3].
Figure 1.2: Estimates of the global prevalence of diabetes in the 20–79 year age group (millions)
(reproduced from [2])
Diabetes can be divided into two main types: Type 1 diabetes (T1D) and Type 2 diabetes (T2D).
Type 1 Diabetes (T1D) is an autoimmune disorder, meaning the body’s immune system mistakenly
identifies its cells as foreign threats. In T1D, the immune system attacks and destroys insulin-producing
beta cells in the pancreas.
Without insulin—a hormone critical for regulating blood sugar levels—
individuals with T1D require lifelong insulin therapy to manage their blood glucose [4]. This form of
diabetes often manifests in childhood or adolescence, which is why it was previously known as juvenile
diabetes, although it can develop at any age. Genetic predispositions, such as variations in genes like
HLA-DQ/DR, contribute to the risk; however, the exact trigger for this autoimmune attack remains
largely unknown. It is suspected that environmental factors, viruses, or other stressors may initiate the
immune response [5].
Conversely, Type 2 Diabetes (T2D) is far more common than T1D and has become a global public
health concern due to its increasing prevalence. T2D results from a combination of genetic and lifestyle
factors. While genetic factors, such as variations in the TCF7L2 gene, can increase susceptibility, T2D
is strongly influenced by modifiable lifestyle factors, particularly obesity, physical inactivity, and poor
diet [6]. These elements lead to insulin resistance, where the body’s cells do not respond effectively
to insulin, and eventual beta-cell dysfunction, where beta cells fail to produce adequate insulin. Once

considered a disease of adulthood, T2D is increasingly diagnosed in younger populations, including
children and adolescents, largely due to rising obesity rates [7].
Obesity, defined as a body mass index (BMI) of 30 kg/m² or higher, triggers inflammation in
adipose tissue. This inflammation releases cytokines like TNF-alpha and IL-6, which interfere with
insulin signaling and speed up beta-cell failure [8]. These changes contribute to other health issues,
such as hypertension. Hypertension, in turn, is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease (CVD),
as persistently high blood pressure damages blood vessels and the heart, leading to serious conditions
that account for 60–80% of diabetes-related deaths [9].
Lifestyle changes characterized by increased caloric intake, reduced physical activity, and rising life
expectancy have led to an aging population facing obesity, diabetes, and related comorbidities Fig.1.3.
This trend has significantly contributed to the escalating healthcare costs observed over recent decades.
The rising rates of T2D and obesity pose considerable risks to cardiovascular health, with poor lifestyle
choices being a major contributor to the development of CVD, which is the leading cause of death
among individuals with diabetes [10].
Obesity causes dysfunction in adipose tissue, resulting in elevated circulating levels of free fatty
acids and leptin, which contribute to endothelial dysfunction, oxidative stress, and the formation of
arterial plaques. Additionally, high blood sugar levels promote the formation of advanced glycation
end-products (AGEs), which result from the glycation of proteins or lipids due to sugar exposure.
This accumulation stiffens blood vessels and impairs heart function, highlighting the interconnected
challenges posed by lifestyle factors, obesity, and diabetes on overall cardiovascular health [11].
Furthermore, hypertension, which affects over 70% of individuals with T2D, increases the risk of
CVD by elevating left ventricular afterload and encouraging myocardial fibrosis [12]. These factors lead
to coronary artery disease (CAD), heart failure, and stroke, together accounting for more than 50% of
deaths related to diabetes [13].

1.2
Liquid Drug Formulations: Addressing Global Health
Challenges
The pervasive increase in diabetes mellitus, obesity, and associated cardiovascular diseases worldwide
has brought the need for effective and adaptable treatment options into sharp focus. As these condi-
tions continue to evolve into significant public health challenges, they demand innovative formulations
that can address the complexities of managing such chronic diseases. Liquid drug formulations have
emerged as critical solutions in this endeavor, offering unique advantages to tackle the intricate needs of
patients affected by these interconnected conditions [14] [15]. This advancement is crucial in ensuring
therapeutic eﬀicacy and improving patient adherence, especially in populations facing diﬀiculties with
==PAGE:12==
1.2 Liquid Drug Formulations: Addressing Global Health Challenges
4
traditional dosage forms, such as tablets and capsules, which often present various challenges to some
patient populations. For instance, elderly patients and young children frequently experience diﬀiculties
with swallowing solid forms of medication, which can hinder adherence to prescribed treatments. Fur-
thermore, tablets and capsules can lead to variable absorption rates, affecting the consistent delivery
of active ingredients to the bloodstream, thus diminishing therapeutic effectiveness. The delayed or
inconsistent onset of action associated with solid forms may also result in inadequate management of
acute symptoms, leaving patients without timely relief when necessary [2]. By developing liquid drug
formulations, pharmaceutical efforts can more effectively mitigate the health implications associated
with diabetes, obesity, and cardiovascular disease, further emphasizing the importance of adaptive and
patient-centered treatment approaches [16].
Liquid dosage forms are versatile pharmaceutical preparations designed to deliver active ingredients
in a fluid state. They can be adapted to various routes of administration, including parenteral, oral,
topical, and rectal.
Depending on the route of administration, liquid drug products offer distinct
advantages such as ease of administration, rapid onset of action, and flexibility in dosing.
These
formulations are broadly classified into monophasic and biphasic systems [17] as shown in Fig. 1.4.
Monophasic liquids, such as solutions, consist of a single homogeneous phase where the drug is fully
dissolved in a solvent. Examples include syrups for oral use, injectable solutions for parenteral delivery,
and gargles for throat treatment. These formulations are particularly effective when quick absorption
or localized action is required, as the dissolved drug is readily available for therapeutic effect [17] [18].
Biphasic liquids, on the other hand, involve two distinct phases. Suspensions contain finely dispersed
solid particles in a liquid medium, while emulsions combine immiscible liquids, such as oil droplets in
water [19]. These systems are ideal for drugs with poor solubility or stability in solution, allowing
for controlled release or targeted delivery. For instance, topical lotions or rectal enemas often utilize
biphasic formulations to enhance drug retention at the application site [17].
Oral solutions and syrups are commonly used for systemic effects, especially in pediatric and geriatric
patients who may struggle with solid dosage forms [20] [21]. Topical applications, such as lotions or
ophthalmic drops, provide localized treatment for skin or eye conditions. Gargles and nasal sprays
target the pharynx or respiratory tract, while rectal or vaginal preparations like enemas offer localized
or systemic effects through mucosal absorption [17].
Dosing devices like oral syringes ensure accuracy in pediatric care. However, challenges include
taste-masking bitter drugs and ensuring excipient safety for sensitive patients. Stability is another key
consideration; liquid formulations often require storage in airtight, light-resistant containers, sometimes
under refrigeration, to maintain eﬀicacy and prevent degradation [17].
The development of liquid drug formulations involves a comprehensive understanding of the physico-
chemical properties of the active pharmaceutical ingredient (API), the selection of appropriate excipients,
and the optimization of the formulation to achieve the desired pK and pharmacodynamic (PD) profiles
[22] [23].
However, formulating a liquid drug product involves addressing several challenges, such as solubility,
stability, viscosity, and compatibility. Excipients play a crucial role in liquid formulations, enhancing
solubility, stability, and bioavailability, improving manufacturability, patient acceptability, and con-
tributing to the product’s osmolality. Common excipients used in liquid formulations include solubilizing
agents, co-solvents, and surfactants that can enhance the solubility of poorly soluble APIs. Stabilizing
agents, antioxidants, preservatives, and appropriate packaging can protect the API from degradation
and microbial contamination [24]. Thickeners and viscosity modifiers can be used to achieve the desired
viscosity, ensuring ease of administration and accurate dosing. Adjusting the pH of the formulation
can enhance the solubility and stability of the API, with buffer systems maintaining the desired pH
throughout the shelf life of the product [23]. The primary objective of liquid drug product formulation is
to develop a stable, effective, and patient-friendly dosage form that delivers the API at the desired rate
and extent, ensuring stability, optimizing bioavailability, improving patient compliance, and facilitating
manufacturing [24] [23].
While these considerations are fundamental to the development of liquid drug formulations, it is es-

2.1
Route of Administration
One category of parenteral drug formulations is injectable formulations, which are integral to the mod-
ern management of diabetes, obesity, and other diseases, offering targeted therapeutic effects that
address complex pathophysiological pathways [17]. Injectable drug formulations are administered via
intravenous (IV), intramuscular (IM), or subcutaneous (SC) routes, each with distinct pK and clinical
profiles as shown in Fig.2.1. The subcutaneous (SC) route is the preferred method for administering
therapies in diabetes and obesity management due to its unique balance of pK, practical, and safety
benefits [25] [26]. Unlike intramuscular (IM) or intravenous (IV) routes, SC administration allows for
slow, steady absorption of medications from the fatty tissue beneath the skin, a process critical for
mimicking physiological hormone release (e.g., insulin’s basal-bolus profile) or maintaining stable drug
levels [27]. This controlled release minimizes risks such as hypoglycemia with insulin or severe gastroin-
testinal side effects with GLP-1 agonists (e.g., semaglutide), which can occur with faster absorption
from IM or immediate systemic exposure via IV [28] [29].
SC administration prioritizes patient autonomy and comfort, as its minimally invasive nature—using
short, thin needles (4mm)—reduces pain and anxiety, enabling self-administration at home without
medical supervision [28] [30]. This is particularly advantageous for chronic therapies like daily insulin
or weekly GLP-1 agonist doses, where adherence is critical [25]. In contrast, IM injections could require
deeper needle penetration, increasing discomfort and the risk of nerve or vascular injury [31], while
IV administration demands skilled healthcare professionals for venous access, making it impractical for
routine care [32].
The SC route is also uniquely compatible with biologics and long-acting formulations.
Large
molecules, such as peptides or monoclonal antibodies (mAB) used in diabetes and obesity therapies,
remain stable in subcutaneous tissue and form depots for gradual release [26]. For example, once-weekly
SC formulations rely on slow absorption from fat tissue to prolong their effects but also depend on the
formulation, a feature not achievable with IM or IV routes due to differences in tissue retention and
clearance [33].
Safety further underscores the preference for SC administration. It avoids complications associated
with IV access, such as infections, thrombosis, or extravasation, and eliminates variability in drug
absorption linked to IM injections in individuals with obesity, where muscle depth and fat distribution
can alter pK [34]. Additionally, SC injections accommodate the small volumes (typically ￿1 mL) required
for high-potency diabetes and obesity drugs, whereas IM injections often require larger volumes, and
IV administration may involve complex dilution protocols [35].

2.2
Pharmacokinetic Profile
With the advantages of SC administration established, it is important to examine how the pK profile
of peptide-based therapeutics influences their overall eﬀicacy and safety, and overall pharmacological
behavior in the body. The pK profile encompasses key parameters such as absorption, distribution,
metabolism, and excretion (ADME) as shown in Fig.2.2 [37]. Absorption refers to the process through
which a drug enters the bloodstream after administration. For peptides, this process can be particularly
challenging due to their susceptibility to enzymatic degradation and poor permeability through biolog-
ical membranes, especially when given orally. Formulation strategies, such as employing permeation
enhancers, nanoemulsions, or alternative delivery methods (e.g., subcutaneous or intravenous admin-
istration), play a critical role in improving absorption rates, leading to higher systemic bioavailability
[38] [39].
Once absorbed, distribution describes how the drug disperses throughout the body. Factors such as
blood flow, tissue permeability, and the molecule’s aﬀinity for various tissues influence the distribution
of peptides. Certain formulations may utilize lipophilic carriers to facilitate better penetration into lipid-
rich environments within the body, thus enhancing tissue distribution. Additionally, the distribution
can be affected by the peptide’s binding to plasma proteins, which can either enhance or hinder its
availability to target tissues [40].
Metabolism refers to the biochemical modification of the drug, primarily occurring in the liver but
also other tissues. For peptides, metabolic stability is a significant concern due to their vulnerability
to proteolytic enzymes that can quickly inactivate them. Formulation strategies that protect peptides
against enzymatic attack or those that involve designing peptides to be less prone to breakdown (e.g.,
through structural modifications) can help prolong their metabolic stability and extend their therapeutic
action in the body [41].
Excretion is the process through which the metabolized drug or its derivatives are eliminated from
the body, primarily via the kidneys or bile. The excretion rate can be influenced by factors including the
molecular weight of the peptides, its polarity, and binding to albumin. Formulations that promote slower
release and reduced clearance from the body can enhance the peptide’s half-life, ensuring therapeutic
levels are maintained over a more extended period [39].

Peptide-Based Therapeutic
The analysis of the pK profile provides essential insights into how drugs behave within the body. This
understanding is particularly crucial for peptide-based therapeutics, which must maintain their chem-
ical and physical stability to achieve optimal therapeutic outcomes. Peptide-based therapeutics are
often the reason for the choice of subcutaneous (SC) injections, as this route is well-suited for their
delivery for the long-term treatment of diabetes, obesity, and cardiovascular disease (CVD) because
they provide stable and patient-friendly administration. Peptide-based therapeutics—a class of drugs
that often require stable, patient-friendly delivery systems to maintain their effectiveness and overcome
challenges. As mentioned previously Peptides like other drugs face inherent limitations, including poor
oral bioavailability due to enzymatic degradation in the gastrointestinal tract (GIT) and rapid renal
clearance [43]. To address this, strategies like subcutaneous delivery or conjugation to albumin-binding
motifs (e.g., fatty acid acylation in semaglutide) prolong half-life [44]. Peptide-based therapeutics drugs

molecules (<500 Da) and large biologics (>10 kDa).
Defined as short chains of 2–50 amino acids,
peptides combine the high specificity and potency of proteins with the synthetic tractability of small

advantages, peptides face significant challenges in maintaining chemical and physical stability, which is
critical for ensuring their eﬀicacy and safety throughout their shelf life [46].
2.3.1
Chemical stability of peptides
Chemical stability of peptides is the ability of these molecules to maintain their structure and func-
tion over time without breaking down or losing effectiveness. Within this framework, addressing the

chemical stability of peptides involves tackling various degradation pathways, deamidation, hydrolysis,
peptide bond cleavage, oxidation, Maillard reaction, elimination, enantiomerization, isomerization and
dimerization [47].
Oxidation stands as one of the predominant degradation pathways affecting the stability of protein
and peptides pharmaceuticals. This degradation process occurs due to the modification of sensitive
side chains that are prone to oxidative damage. Among the amino acids, histidine (His), methionine
(Met), cysteine (Cys), and aromatic residues such as tyrosine (Tyr) and tryptophan (Trp) are particu-

significant structural alterations, ultimately influencing the biological function and therapeutic eﬀicacy
of the protein or peptides [48].
The mechanisms of oxidation vary; for instance, methionine can undergo oxidation to form sulfoxide
and, under harsher conditions, convert to sulfone. Similarly, cysteine oxidation can lead to the for-
mation of inter- and intra-molecular disulfide bonds as shown in Fig.2.3 or further oxidation products
like sulfenic, sulfinic, and even sulfonic acids [49] [50] [51]. These transformations not only alter the
properties of the amino acids but can also change the overall conformation of the protein, potentially
leading to aggregation or loss of biological activity. The oxidation of aromatic amino acids can initiate
the opening of their side chains, leading to a plethora of oxidation products that complicate the under-
standing of their stability [48] [52] [53].
Histidine, often found in active sites of enzymes, can also undergo oxidation. The imidazole ring of histi-
dine may open, resulting in the formation of various oxidation products that can disrupt local structure
and function, leading to potential loss of enzymatic activity. In aromatic amino acids, oxidation can
initiate the cleavage of side chains, generating a range of by-products that can significantly affect the
solubility and aggregation propensity of the molecules [48] [50] [51].
Moreover, the oxidation process can lead to the formation of aggregates, as oxidized residues may par-
ticipate in non-covalent interactions that promote protein aggregation. This aggregation is particularly
concerning in therapeutic applications, as it can result in immunogenicity and reduced eﬀicacy of the
administered protein or peptides [48].

made up of monomers (quaternary structure), inter-molecular disulfide bonds are essential for stabiliza-

Deamidation is a spontaneous, non-enzymatic reaction in which the covalent amide functional group
is transformed into a carboxylic acid. In proteins and peptides, deamidation typically occurs on as-
paragine (Asn) or glutamine (Gln) residues, resulting in the formation of Asp or glutamic acid (Glu)
[58] [59]. The deamidation of Asn occurs through a succinimide intermediate, formed when the side
chain’s carbonyl carbon is attacked by the backbone amine [60]. The succinimide ring is then hydrolyzed
at either the alpha- or Beta-carbonyl groups, yielding isoAsp and Asp in approximately a 3:1 ratio [61].
In acidic conditions, Asn generally undergoes deamidation through direct hydrolysis facilitated by acid
catalysis [60]. Similarly, Gln deamidation occurs via direct hydrolysis under acidic pH and proceeds
through a glutarimide intermediate when in neutral or alkaline conditions [62]. The rates of deamida-
tion for Asn and Gln residues are influenced by the protein’s sequence and conformation [63], as well
as external factors such as buffer type, ionic strength, pH, and temperature [64].
Hydrolysis targets the amide bonds within the peptides backbone, involves the cleaving of peptides
bonds by the addition of water, leading to the breakdown of proteins into smaller peptides or free amino
acids, see Fig.2.5. This degradation pathway is highly sensitive to environmental conditions such as
pH and temperature. Under acidic or alkaline conditions, hydrolysis occurs more rapidly, which can
significantly affect the protein’s biological function. Peptides with Asp residues are especially prone
to acid-catalyzed hydrolysis compared to those lacking these residues [50]. This hydrolysis can occur
at either the N-terminal or C-terminal bond adjacent to the Asp residue. Due to the higher basicity
of the proline (Pro) nitrogen, the peptides bond in an Asp-Pro sequence is particularly unstable, as
protonated Pro acts as an effective leaving group.
Peptides with N-terminal residues next to serine (Ser) and Thr are also susceptible to hydrolysis
[65] [48].

2.3.2
Physical stability of peptides
The physical instability of peptides in injectable products arises from a complex interplay of intrin-
sic molecular properties and extrinsic environmental factors, which can compromise their structural
integrity, solubility, and therapeutic eﬀicacy [66].
After addressing the chemical stability of peptides, which focuses on preventing molecular degrada-
tion pathways like oxidation and hydrolysis, it is vital to also consider their physical stability. Whereas
chemical stability ensures the preservation of molecular composition, physical stability is concerned with
maintaining the protein’s structural integrity and function during storage and use. This includes pre-
venting aggregation, precipitation, denaturation, and adsorption, which can impair therapeutic eﬀicacy.
Hence, physical stability is essential to complement chemical stability, ensuring peptides therapeutics
are safe and effective.
Physical stability in peptides refers to changes in their structure or physical properties that occur
without altering their chemical composition. This type of instability can significantly impact the eﬀicacy,
safety, and shelf life of peptide-based pharmaceuticals. Various factors contribute to physical instability,
including environmental conditions such as temperature, humidity, light exposure, and interactions with
other formulation components [66].
Peptides, composed of amino acid chains with ionizable groups, are highly sensitive to pH fluc-
tuations, particularly near their isoelectric point (pI), where reduced net charge leads to decreased
solubility and increased aggregation or precipitation changes that may manifest as visible particulates
or turbidity in the solution. This risk is exacerbated in formulations with inadequate buffering systems
or during storage if pH shifts occur, such as through CO￿ingress in vials, as seen in insulin formulations
requiring precise pH control. Temperature also plays a critical role: elevated temperatures accelerate
molecular motion, promoting denaturation or aggregation, while freeze-thaw cycles during storage or
transport can induce phase separation or ice crystal formation, destabilizing lyophilized peptides like
glucagon-like peptide-1 analogs. High peptides concentrations, often necessary for subcutaneous deliv-
ery, further amplify intermolecular interactions, such as hydrophobic or electrostatic forces, leading to
self-association, gelation, or aggregation processes detectable through increased viscosity or opalescence,
necessitating stabilizers like polysorbate surfactants to shield exposed hydrophobic regions [66] [67].
A primary manifestation of physical instability is aggregation, where individual peptides molecules
come together to form larger aggregates. This phenomenon may arise from hydrophobic interactions,
especially in peptides with hydrophobic regions that can lead to non-specific attractions. Aggregated
peptides can demonstrate altered pK and reduced bioavailability, ultimately resulting in diminished
therapeutic effectiveness [68] [66]. Another critical aspect of physical instability is precipitation, which
occurs when peptides become insoluble under certain conditions, often due to changes in pH or solvent
composition.
Precipitation not only reduces the available drug substance but can also lead to the
formation of solid particles that may trigger an immune response when administered [66]. Additionally,
adsorption poses a significant challenge; peptides can adhere to surfaces such as vials, syringes, and
filtration membranes, resulting in a considerable loss of active substance and potentially leading to
inadequate dosing. Conformational changes can also compromise peptides activity and functionality,

with fluctuations in temperature and physical stress during storage or manufacturing further influencing
stability [68].
Manufacturing processes introduce additional risks, as shear stress from pumping, filtration, or
mixing can disrupt peptides structure, while interfacial stresses at air-liquid or solid-liquid interfaces—
such as those caused by agitation during shipping or contact with container walls—promote surface-
induced aggregation, mitigated by silicone-coated syringes or surfactants. Excipient interactions further
complicate stability; high ionic strength buffers may induce ”salting out,” reducing solubility, while
impurities or degradants can catalyze instability, potentially altering the product’s color or clarity.
Oxidation of susceptible residues like methionine or cysteine, accelerated by light exposure in clear glass
vials or oxygen-permeable containers, necessitates antioxidants like methionine or inert gas headspaces
to prevent browning or haze formation [69].
Fibrillation is another critical issue, where peptides misfold and aggregate into elongated structures
known as fibrils, primarily involving the formation of beta-sheet structures. Normally, proteins fold into
functional conformations, but under certain conditions—such as changes in pH, temperature, or the
presence of crowding agents—they may misfold and expose aggregation-prone regions [70]. The forma-
tion of small aggregates called nuclei then occurs, which adopt stabilized beta-sheet conformations. As
aggregation continues, more misfolded proteins are recruited, facilitating the elongation of these fibrils
[71] [72].
The beta-sheet-rich structure that characterizes amyloid fibrils contributes to their stability and
resistance to degradation, promoting accumulation that can disrupt cellular function and is associated
with neurodegenerative diseases, such as Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s. Understanding this process and
the role of beta-sheet formation is crucial for developing strategies to prevent or treat amyloid-related
disorders [73]. Moreover, physical instability can be compounded in lyophilized products, as the freeze-
drying process may introduce stresses leading to structural changes upon reconstitution. For instance,
the amorphous nature of freeze-dried peptides can render them more susceptible to crystallization and
aggregation during storage if not managed carefully. Additionally, lyophilized peptides are vulnerable
to moisture uptake, which can alter their physical state, resulting in degradation or loss of activity
[74]. The route of administration also influences stability, as subcutaneous injection exposes peptides
to prolonged high local concentrations and slower absorption, increasing aggregation risks, whereas
intravenous delivery subjects them to shear stress in infusion pumps and rapid dilution in blood [75].
Throughout the product lifecycle, monitoring for physical changes such as particulate formation, shifts
in color, pH variations, or viscosity alteration is critical to detect instability early, as these phenomena
directly correlate with underlying mechanisms like aggregation, oxidation, buffering failure, or excipi-
ent incompatibility. Furthermore, given the critical importance of maintaining the integrity of peptides
pharmaceuticals, thorough characterization and optimization of formulation conditions are essential.
These measures include selecting stabilizing excipients, employing appropriate storage conditions, and
utilizing advanced formulation techniques to minimize physical instability. Understanding and miti-
gating physical instability is vital for the development of effective therapeutic agents, ensuring patient
safety and achieving reliable therapeutic outcomes [76].
To mitigate these challenges, formulation strategies such as pH optimization, lyophilization, and
stabilizer incorporation, combined with robust packaging (amber vials, airtight seals) and controlled
manufacturing processes, are essential to ensure the physical stability, safety, and eﬀicacy of peptide-
based products [77].
Ultimately, while both chemical and physical stability are crucial in ensuring the eﬀicacy of pharmaceuti-
cal formulations, this project will specifically focus on chemical stability, as it is essential for preventing
degradation and ensuring the safety and effectiveness of the therapeutic products under investigation.

Preservative Eﬀicacy in Injectable Formulations
Preservative eﬀicacy is a critical aspect of pharmaceutical formulations, particularly for products suscep-
tible to microbial contamination like multiple-use products. For injectable drugs, maintaining sterility

is crucial, as any microbial contamination can lead to severe health risks, including infections. This
is especially important because injectable products are administered directly into the bloodstream or
tissues, providing a direct pathway for pathogens to enter the body. In contrast, while the sterility of
oral drugs is still important, they typically have a greater margin for error due to the digestive system’s
ability to handle some degree of microbial exposure. Preservatives are added to formulations for multi-
use products to inhibit the growth of microorganisms, ensuring the safety and stability of the product
throughout its shelf life. In the case of single-use products, this may not be necessary. Evaluating
preservative eﬀicacy involves assessing the ability of preservatives to maintain microbial control under
various conditions, which is essential for both product integrity and patient safety [78].
The importance of preservative eﬀicacy cannot be overstated. Effective preservatives prevent the
proliferation of bacteria, fungi, and yeast that can cause product spoilage and pose health risks to
patients. Additionally, preservatives help maintain the integrity of the formulation by preventing mi-
crobial degradation, which can alter the product’s therapeutic properties. Regulatory compliance is
another critical aspect, as pharmaceutical products must meet stringent regulatory requirements for
microbial safety, necessitating robust preservative systems [79].
Several classes of preservatives are commonly used in pharmaceutical formulations, each with specific
mechanisms of action. Antimicrobial agents such as benzalkonium chloride, phenol, and chlorhexidine
inhibit or kill microorganisms directly. Antioxidants like butylated hydroxytoluene (BHT) and ascorbic
acid prevent oxidative degradation that can promote microbial growth. Chelating agents such as EDTA
bind metal ions that can catalyze microbial growth, thereby enhancing the effectiveness of antimicrobial
preservatives [79].
The eﬀicacy of preservatives can be influenced by several factors. The formulation pH can affect
the activity of preservatives; for example, weak acid preservatives like benzoic acid are more effective
at lower pH levels. High ionic strength can reduce the eﬀicacy of preservatives by interfering with their
ability to interact with microbial cells. Additionally, preservatives can interact with other components
of the formulation, potentially reducing their effectiveness.
For instance, surfactants can sequester
preservatives, making them less available to inhibit microbial growth. The concentration of the preser-
vative must also be suﬀicient to provide effective microbial control without compromising the safety
and eﬀicacy of the product [80].
Evaluating preservative eﬀicacy involves several standardized tests. Challenge tests involve inocu-
lating the formulation with specific microorganisms and assessing the preservative’s ability to inhibit
growth over time. The Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC) test determines the lowest concen-
tration of preservative that inhibits visible microbial growth. Real-time stability studies monitor the
formulation over its shelf life to ensure continued microbial control. These tests provide a comprehensive
understanding of how preservatives perform under various conditions [81].
In conclusion, preservative eﬀicacy is crucial for ensuring the microbial safety and stability of pharma-
ceutical formulations. By understanding the factors that influence preservative activity and employing
rigorous testing methods, formulators can design products that meet regulatory requirements and pro-
vide safe, effective therapy to patients. Ensuring robust preservative systems is essential for maintaining
product integrity, patient safety, and regulatory compliance.

2.6
Analytical Methods
To ensure the stability, safety, and eﬀicacy of an injectable drug products, robust analytical methods
are employed to monitor critical quality attributes (CQAs) such as purity, aggregation, and degrada-
tion. There are different analytical techniques for stability assessment, including Reverse-phase high-
performance liquid chromatography (RP-HPLC), size-exclusion chromatography (SEC), mass spectrom-
etry (MS), and spectroscopy methods, such as UV-visible spectroscopy and infrared spectroscopy [85].
RP-HPLC is a widely used technique for determining the purity of compounds. This method sepa-
rates individual components based on their hydrophobic interactions with the stationary phase, typically
a non-polar packing material, while a polar mobile phase is used to elute the compounds. The degree
of hydrophobicity influences the retention time of each component, allowing for effective separation.
RP-HPLC is known for its high resolution and sensitivity, making it ideal for the purity assessment of
small molecules and macromolecules alike. The method is particularly advantageous in pharmaceutical
applications, as it can be utilized to identify impurities, degradation products, and assess overall drug
stability under various conditions [86].
SEC, on the other hand, is a separation that occurs based on the size of the molecules specifically
for peptides separations (molecular weight range 100–7000), where larger molecules are excluded from
entering the pores of the stationary phase and, as a result, elute first. Conversely, smaller molecules
penetrate the porous packing material, resulting in longer retention times. SEC provides valuable in-
sights into the size distribution and aggregation state of molecules in solution, which are critical for
understanding protein stability. This analytical technique is particularly beneficial for biopharmaceu-
ticals, where the presence of aggregates can significantly affect safety and eﬀicacy [86] [87]. SEC is
often divided into two main state conditions: denaturing SEC and native SEC [88]. Under denaturing
conditions, proteins are examined in a way that disrupts non-covalent bonds, resulting in the loss of
their natural three-dimensional structure. This approach is useful for identifying aggregates that may
form under stressed conditions [89].
Conversely, native SEC operates under milder conditions that
preserve proteins’ quaternary structure, allowing for the observation of oligomeric states such as dimers,
trimers, and hexamers [90] [91]. This method is particularly valuable for characterizing proteins that
must remain biologically active, as it maintains inter-subunit interactions crucial for their functionality
[91]

MS is another powerful analytical method that, when coupled with liquid chromatography (LC-
MS), offers a highly sensitive and selective approach for characterizing drugs and their metabolites. In
LC-MS, a sample is first separated by liquid chromatography, where compounds are eluted based on
their chemical properties, such as hydrophobicity and charge. As these separated compounds exit the
liquid chromatograph, they are introduced into the mass spectrometer, where they are ionized (usually
via electrospray ionization or atmospheric pressure chemical ionization). The ions are then detected
based on their mass-to-charge ratio (m/z), providing detailed information about the molecular weight
and structure of the compounds [92]. This method can provide extensive information on molecular
weight, structure, and purity, enabling developers to monitor drug stability over time and under vari-
ous conditions. It is particularly valuable for detecting low-level impurities and degradation products
that may arise during development and storage, thereby ensuring a comprehensive assessment of the
drug’s quality [93] [94].
Other techniques, like differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA),
assess thermal stability, while particle size analysis ensures that particulate matter remains within ac-
ceptable limits. Together, these techniques produce a comprehensive stability profile critical for the
regulatory approval and successful deployment of injectable therapeutics [95].
In this project, we primarily employed RP-HPLC, SEC, in addition to LC-MS to thoroughly assess
the stability of our injectable drug product. RP-HPLC remains the gold standard for assessing chemical
purity in peptides and protein formulations (e.g., insulin, GLP-1 analogs). By leveraging hydrophobic
interactions between analytes and a nonpolar stationary phase, RP-HPLC resolves degradation products
(e.g., deamidation, oxidation) with high sensitivity (detection limits ￿0.1%) [96]. SEC is indispensable for
detecting high molecular weight protein (HMWP) aggregates, which arise from physical instability (e.g.,
misfolding, interfacial stress) and pose immunogenicity risks. SEC separates monomers from aggregates
(dimers, oligomers) based on hydrodynamic radius, with quantification limits as low as 0.01% [97].
LC-MS provides orthogonal characterization of HMWP species identified by SEC, enabling pre-
cise determination of their structural identity (e.g., covalent vs. non-covalent aggregates) and post-
translational modifications (e.g., glycation, crosslinking). For instance, LC-MS has been employed to
confirm dimerization in stressed monoclonal antibody formulations and elucidate aggregation mecha-
nisms (e.g., disulfide scrambling) [98]. Coupled with SEC, LC-MS quantifies low-abundance HMWP
species while mapping degradation pathways, ensuring comprehensive stability profiling [99].
Together, RP-HPLC, SEC, and LC-MS form a robust analytical framework for monitoring injectable
formulation stability, aligning with regulatory requirements and mitigating risks to product quality.

Disulfide Bonds & High Molecular Weight Protein
High molecular weight protein (HMWP) presents significant challenges in drug production and for-
mulation development, such as monoclonal antibodies and therapeutic enzymes. These HMWP can
compromise the safety, eﬀicacy, and stability of biopharmaceuticals. The classification of these HMWP
is multifaceted, encompassing various types based on structural characteristics, size, and solubility.
Building on the challenges of chemical instability in peptides formulations, the structural integrity of
therapeutic proteins and peptides is often underpinned by disulfide bonds, a critical post-translational
modification that covalently links the sulfur atoms of two cysteine residues in close spatial proximity.
These bonds stabilize tertiary and quaternary structures, ensuring proper folding, bioactivity, and
resistance to proteolytic degradation [100]
However, while disulfide bonds are indispensable for maintaining conformational stability, their
dynamic nature under stress conditions can paradoxically drive the formation of HMWP species, a
major concern in therapeutic peptides and protein manufacturing [101].

proteins; they exist in equilibrium with aggregated states. Oligomers—comprised of small clusters of

While certain oligomers might possess biological activity [102], others can elicit toxic effects or provoke
immune responses in patients, making them a significant concern in therapeutic contexts. Fibrillar
HMWP, which frequently arise from misfolded proteins, form elongated, thread-like structures typically
associated with conditions such as amyloidosis and neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimer’s. These
fibrils can induce cellular toxicity and inflammation [102] [103].
Particulate HMWP, on the other hand, can obstruct drug delivery systems and create adverse in-
jection site reactions.
Additional classifications include native HMWP, which can be dynamic and
reversible, and denatured HMWP, arising from significant conformational changes driven by environ-
mental stressors, leading to irreversible loss of biological function [102] [104].
The nature of the interactions that lead to aggregation significantly influences the stability and
characteristics of these HMWP. Covalent HMWP arise when stable covalent bonds form between protein
molecules, often as a result of disulfide bonds. These HMWP are typically more stable and persistent,
formed under oxidative conditions, through chemical cross-linking, or via glycation. The formation of
covalent HMWP can lead to cross-linking of proteins, potentially resulting in aggregative states that
alter therapeutic eﬀicacy. Additionally, chemical modifications during the manufacturing process, such
as pegylation, can induce covalent aggregation if reagents react with multiple protein sites [102].
In contrast, non-covalent HMWP form through weaker, reversible interactions, including hydropho-
bic interactions, hydrogen bonds, electrostatic interactions, and van der Waals forces. Hydrophobic
interactions are particularly critical, as proteins tend to minimize exposure to the aqueous environ-
ment, driving the clumping of non-polar regions from the protein surface. This tendency is exacerbated
by alterations in temperature, pH, or ionic strength, destabilizing the protein structure and triggering
aggregation [102].
2.7.1
HMWP due to Disulfide Bonds Shuffling
For small peptides containing one or more disulfide bonds and have a molecular weight of op to 10000
Da, a common type of HMWP is caused by disulfide bond shuffling. The disulfide bond is cleaved in a
reductive step, leading to the free cysteines. In a subsequent oxidation, the disulfide bond is re-formed
either reversibly, leading to the starting material, or in the case of reaction with a neighboring molecule,
a covalently bound dimer is formed. The reaction can proceed and form a distribution of multimeric
HMWP species [105]. The ability of the peptides to undergo this type of reaction is something inherent
to the molecule and can only be completely mitigated by chemically modifying the peptides, e.g.,
removing disulfide bonds, using alternative chemical functionalities, substituting cysteines with other
amino acids, etc. These modifications are likely to affect the activity of the drug and, therefore, are
not always applicable. Alternatively, the problem must be solved in the development of a liquid drug
product formulation that reduces the formation of these HMWP species due to the shuffling of disulfide
bonds. It is well known that the reaction is highly pH dependent [106] [107]. At lower pH levels, the
reaction rate decreases. However, developing stable liquid drug product formulations often requires
balancing multiple parameters, such as chemical stability, physical stability, preservative eﬀicacy, and
pK profiles. As a result, lowering the pH is not always feasible, as it can negatively impact physical
stability, potentially leading to solubility issues or the formation of sub-visible particles. To address
these challenges effectively, we conducted a deeper mechanistic understanding, which is crucial for
expanding the toolbox available when developing liquid drug products [108] [109].
2.7.2
Mechanism
Disulfide bond shuffling is a critical process in protein chemistry, involving the formation, breakage,
and rearrangement of disulfide bonds (R-S-S-R). The shuffling occurs primarily through two pathways:
the oxidative pathway [110] and the thiol-disulfide exchange pathway [111].

The Oxidative Pathway (Two-step mechanism)
The oxidative pathway, as mentioned previously, involves two key steps: reduction and oxidation. In
the reductive step, disulfide bonds are cleaved, leading to the formation of free thiols. Subsequently, in
the oxidative step, disulfide bonds are re-formed [112]. This reformation can occur either within the
same molecule, leading to the original structure, or between different molecules, resulting in covalently
bound dimers or higher-order multimers [113]. The reaction mechanism is illustrated in the Fig.2.6.

Oxidaizing agent: play a crucial role in the two-step mechanism of disulfide bond formation and
shuffling by facilitating the oxidation of thiols to form disulfide bridges. For instance, molecular oxygen
(O2) acts as a terminal electron acceptor in this process, oxidizing thiols (RSH) to disulfides (RSSR)
while producing water: 2 RSH + 1
2 O2 −−→RSSR + H2O. However, the nature of the oxidizing agent is
paramount in determining the outcomes of these reactions. Over-oxidation by stronger oxidants, such
as hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), can lead to the formation of sulfenic and sulfonic acids, destabilizing
proteins and promoting aggregation, thereby compromising protein function. For example, this study
[114] demonstrated that the presence of H2O2 not only facilitates oxidative folding but can also induce
modification in proteins, affecting their structural integrity and function, particularly under increased
oxidative stress. Additionally, the presence of excess metal ions can complicate the oxidation land-
scape, accelerating radical-mediated crosslinking processes that increase the formation of HMWP. The
effectiveness and specificity of disulfide bond reshuffling are thus intricately linked to the type and con-
centration of oxidizing agents present in the system. Understanding and optimizing these conditions
is vital for enhancing the eﬀiciency of disulfide bond formation and maintaining protein stability in
biochemical contexts [49]
Transition metals (e.g., Cu2+, Fe3+): Transition metals are important in mediating redox reac-
tions, particularly in processes involving the oxidation of thiols during disulfide bond formation. These
metals, including (e.g., Cu2+, Fe3+, and Mg2+) can facilitate electron transfer processes due to their
variable oxidation states. They can act as catalysts, increasing the rate of disulfide bond shuffling. The
catalytic activity of transition metals is attributed to their ability to undergo reversible oxidation and
reduction reactions, allowing them to help stabilize intermediate species formed during the reaction
[49]. Furthermore, transition metals can coordinate with thiolate ions, influencing the geometry and
electronic environment of thiols, which can subsequently affect their reactivity. The concentration and
type of transition metal present in the reaction medium can significantly impact the overall dynamics
of disulfide bond redox reactions, making them crucial components in the regulation of protein folding
and stability [115].
Chelating Agents (e.g., EDTA): Chelating agents, compounds that can form multiple bonds
to a single metal ion, play a crucial role in modulating the availability of metal ions in biochemical
systems. EDTA, a common chelating agent, functions by coordinating with metal ions through its
ligand binding sites, typically forming bidentate complexes. This means that a single EDTA molecule
utilizes two functional groups, primarily nitrogen (N) and oxygen (O) atoms, to bind effectively to a

Stabilizing the thiolate
Stabilizing thiolate ions (RS–) is crucial in protein chemistry and formulation development, as their
reactivity can significantly influence the stability and eﬀicacy of protein-based therapeutics. Both the
well-known factor pH and our hypothesis about ionic strength play key roles in determining the proto-
nation state and electrostatic interactions of thiolate ions, impacting their stability in solution [120].
pH: The pH of a solution is an essential factor affecting the oxidative pathways of disulfide bond
shuffling and the formation of HMWP. At various pH levels, the ionization state of thiol groups signifi-
cantly changes, directly influencing their reactivity and capacity for disulfide bonding. In more alkaline
environments, thiols (RSH) deprotonate into thiolate (RS–), which are highly nucleophilic and more
reactive towards oxidation [121]. This transformation typically occurs at an alkaline pH, as the pKa
for cysteine is around 8.3. Consequently, at pH levels greater than 8.0, rapid thiolate-driven oxidation
predominantly forms native disulfide initially. However, if the exposure is prolonged, it can lead to
over-oxidation and the formation of non-native crosslinks. Conversely, in acidic conditions, increased
protonation of thiol groups diminishes the concentration of reactive thiolate, thereby slowing disulfide
==PAGE:27==

exchange and possibly reducing HMWP aggregation. pH not only affects the chemistry of thiol groups
but also impacts the overall stability and structure of proteins, influencing their interactive capabilities.
Extreme pH levels can lead to protein denaturation, exposing reactive cysteine residues and amplifying
oxidative pathways. Thus, maintaining an optimal pH is crucial for balancing oxidative potential within
a formulation and regulating HMWP formation [122].
Ionic strength: High ionic strength environments, such as solutions with over 150 mM NaCl,
significantly influence the interactions between thiolate and disulfide due to the Debye-Hückel shield-
ing effect [123]. This effect minimizes electrostatic repulsion by effectively screening charged particles
with counterions present in the solution, thus lowering the activation energy necessary for nucleophilic
substitution reactions, akin to the SN2 mechanism [124]. By reducing the electrostatic repulsion and
stabilizing the transition state, high ionic strength environments facilitate this process, allowing thiolate
(RS–) to more readily attack disulfide bonds. Consequently, in high-salt formulations, the facilitated
thiol-disulfide exchange leads to increased rates of disulfide bond shuffling and formation of high molec-
ular weight complexes. This dynamic is evidenced by the reported increase in antibody aggregation
rates, as observed in the study, highlighting a critical formulation challenge. This underscore the im-
portance of carefully controlling ionic strength in formulating protein-based therapeutics, as unchecked
aggregation not only impacts stability and eﬀicacy but can also compromise the safety of these products.
2.7.2.2
Thiol-Disulfide Exchange Pathway (One-step mechanism)
Thiol-disulfide exchange involves nucleophilic attack of a thiolate (–S–) on a disulfide bond (-S-S-),
leading to bond rearrangement. This pathway dominates in reducing or neutral pH environments and
drives HMWP via intermolecular crosslinking [125].
The classical thiol–disulfide interchange mechanism wherein a single reaction step involves the nu-
cleophile, a thiolate ion (R–S–) attacks a disulfide bond (R’-S-S-R”), resulting in the cleavage of the
disulfide bond and the formation of a new disulfide bond, while releasing a new thiolate ion [125]. The
disulfide bonds shuffling mechanism pathways are illustrated in Fig.2.8. However, the tendency for
disulfide bond shuffling and the formation of HMWP species is influenced by several factors, as the
factors will be introduced in the following section.

Stabilizing the thiolate: Since the thiolate is the reactive species, all factors that stabilize the

Redox Buffers (e.g., Glutathione, Cysteine/Cystine) Redox buffers such as glutathione and
the cysteine/cystine pair play a crucial role in maintaining the equilibrium between reduced and ox-
idized states within a formulation. These buffers regulate the balance between reduced glutathione
(GSH) and its oxidized form (GSSG), which can significantly impact the oxidative state of proteins by

influencing disulfide bond formation and reduction. Specifically, when the GSH/GSSG ratio is low, less
than 10:1, the environment favors the reduction of disulfide bonds, leading to an increase in free thiols.
This increase in free thiols enhances the likelihood of disulfide exchange reactions, where thiols can

Thiol Scavengers (e.g., N-Ethylmaleimide, (NEM)) One factor significantly impacting thiol-
disulfide exchange reactions is the use of NEM, a compound known for its ability to irreversibly alkylate
free thiol groups (-SH). By creating a covalent bond with the sulfur atom in thiol groups, NEM effectively
blocks these thiols from engaging in nucleophilic exchange reactions that are essential for disulfide bond
shuffling [127]. Firstly, while maleimides are frequently characterized in the literature as irreversible
thiol-modifying reagents, the adducts are subject to base-catalyzed reverse Michael reactions (Fig.2.9

ring-opening reactions have been used to identify maleimide-labeled peptides [130].
This mechanism can notably reduce the formation of HMWP by preventing the aggregation processes
that arise from such exchanges. In peptides formulations, the application of NEM has been theoretically
proposed to decrease HMWP levels by up to 90%, highlighting its potential utility in maintaining protein
stability and preventing undesired crosslinking. However, this substantial inhibition of thiol-disulfide
interactions comes with the risk of unintended consequences, particularly in therapeutic proteins where
essential thiols play a critical role in maintaining biological function and structural integrity. The irre-
versible nature of NEM’s action could inactivate these proteins, thus compromising their therapeutic
eﬀicacy and safety. According to the research, quantifying the modification of protein thiols is crucial
as it underscores the potential for such interactions to disrupt protein functionality. Therefore, the
concentration of NEM and the specific conditions under which it is employed must be carefully man-
aged to ensure that while thiol-disulfide exchanges are controlled, the essential functional activity of
therapeutic proteins is preserved [131].

Project scope
This project aims to investigate the formation of HMWP due to disulfide bond shuffling and to gain an
understanding of the specific conditions that influence this shuffling process within aqueous injectable
formulations. With the above-mentioned two mechanistic pathways as an offset, different factors that
could have a potential impact were investigated with the overall aim of providing the mechanistic insight
necessary for the optimization of pharmaceutical formulations.
1. The mechanistic steps involving the reductive opening and oxidative formation of disulfide bonds
will be studied to evaluate how the formation rates of HMWP are influenced by conditions affecting
the redox potential of the solution (Study 1-3).
2. The role of nucleophilic thiolate as reactive species in the rate-determining step will be investigated,
focusing on how the stabilization or destabilization of thiolate influences HMWP formation rate
(Study 4-5).

5.1
Study 1 - Effect of oxidative agents (H2O2, O2, N2)
As shuffling of S-S bonds involves both an oxidative and a reductive step, one of these must be the rate-
limiting step. If the rate-limiting step is reductive, it would make sense that increasing the oxidative
conditions might lead to lower HMWP. Therefore, the aim of study 1 is to investigate how oxidative
conditions can affect the HMWP rate, with a hypothesis that mild oxidative additives such as H2O2
are suitable for reducing the HMWP formation rate [133].

API P
As the main aim of this study was to investigate the impact of oxidative conditions on the HMWP
formation rate, the findings indicate the following:
For API (P), the addition of a low concentration of H2O2 (0.33 mM) in batch 1.2 resulted in a
slightly lower HMWP formation rate when compared to the reference batch. This indicates that mild
oxidative conditions contribute positively to the reduction of HMWP. As the concentration of H2O2
was incrementally increased to 1 mM, a corresponding rise in HMWP level was observed, although
remaining slightly lower than the reference batch. The reduction of HMWP rates seems to be more
effective when compared to the reference up to a concentration of 1 mM of H2O2 (batch 1.3). This
indicates that concentrations greater than 1 mM may negatively impact HMWP rates compared to the
reference.
However, increasing the concentration of the H2O2 to 3 mM gives a slight elevation in the HMWP
compared to the reference, this indicates that in case of using H2O2, it needs to be kept being lower
than 3 mM for avoiding a higher HMWP rate, and maintained as low as possible.
It suggests that a small amount of H2O2 appears to be beneficial, but that H2O2 may also cause
other issues. It is possible that it quenches small amounts of reducing species, while excess H2O2 leads
to undesirable effects. Therefore, testing even lower concentrations could be interesting as it might
yield an even lower rate. This reinforces the idea that controlled oxidative conditions are essential for
managing HMWP formation.
Purging the solution with N2 (batch 1.5) gives a slightly positive effect, resulting in a higher HMWP
formation rate compared to the reference batch.
This suggests that N2 has lowered the oxidation
conditions in the formulation appears to worsen the HMWP formation rate. In this context, purging
with N2 indicates that the presence of an oxidative agent instead of the reductive agent is more effective
in reducing the rate of HMWP formation, which aligns well with our hypothesis.

The effects of adding H2O2 to the two APIs are contradictory. The lowest concentration of H2O2
(0.33 mM) has a positive effect on API (P), but it harms API (C). In the case of API (C), H2O2
significantly degrades the API itself.
Additionally, as previously mentioned, the addition of H2O2 for API (P) is beneficial, leading to
the possibility that it quenches small amounts of reducing species in the sample. While, an higher
concentration of H2O2, leads to significant issues that obscure any positive effects for API (C). Therefore,
it may be worthwhile to investigate lower concentrations of H2O2 for API (C), such as 0.0033 mM, to
determine if it can help minimize the HMWP formation rate.
The findings of this study were confirmed by LC-MS analysis, which can be found in Appendix A for
API (P) and Appendix B for API (C). According to a study in the literature [133], utilizing hydrogen
peroxide in controlled concentrations is a promising strategy to prevent disulfide bond reduction during
antibody production. The authors highlight that maintaining an appropriate oxidative environment
can significantly enhance the stability and functionality of antibodies, potentially leading to improved
therapeutic outcomes. They recommend that future studies further investigate the long-term effects
of H2O2 treatment on antibody therapeutic eﬀicacy and the implications for large-scale manufacturing
processes. Our study aligns closely with these findings, strengthening the hypothesis that mild oxidative
conditions are beneficial for reducing HMWP formation and increasing peptide stability, as it was
observed that the application of hydrogen peroxide effectively minimized HMWP rate, supporting
the assertion that controlled oxidative environments can foster better protein stability. Both studies
underscore the critical role of oxidative agents like H2O2 in optimizing protein stability. This article
[133] also describes how H2O2 is effective in quenching HMWP formation, which suggests that something
similar may apply to O2/N2.
Literature typically characterizes oxygen as unfavorable and nitrogen as beneficial for stability.
Specifically, oxygen is known to oxidize peptides, leading to aggregation, a reaction that can be mit-
igated by using nitrogen. For instance, the study by [134] examines the impact of nitrogen-modified
atmosphere storage on lipid oxidation in peanuts through a lipidomic perspective. Their key findings
indicate that a nitrogen-rich storage environment significantly reduces lipid oxidation levels in peanuts
compared to conventional storage conditions. Additionally, lipidomic analysis from their study demon-
strates that certain lipid species, particularly polyunsaturated fatty acids, are less prone to oxidation
when stored in a nitrogen-modified atmosphere. This study highlights the protective role of nitrogen
against oxidative degradation and proposes that this storage technique can enhance both the shelf life
and nutritional quality of peanuts. In contrast to these observations, our findings indicate that nitrogen
leads to increased formation of HMWP, and oxygen reduces the HMWP formation, that align well with
our hypothesis.
Study 1 results indicate that mild oxidative conditions are more effective in reducing the HMWP
formation rate.
Adding an oxidative agent minimizes the reductive step, thereby accelerating the
reaction. Reducing the rate-determining step leads to lower HMWP formation, which confirms our
hypothesis. The effect of N2 worsens the redox environment as expected and leads to more reductive
conditions, confirming the hypothesis that the reductive step is the rate-limiting step in the two-step
mechanism. This means that increasing the reductive step slows the reaction, thereby increasing the
reductive conditions and leading to higher HMWP formation.
In drug development, understanding redox conditions can guide the selection of excipients and
formulation components that either enhance or mitigate oxidative or reductive conditions that lead to
HMWP formation.

5.3.2.1
API P
The primary aim of this study was to investigate further the influence of a mild oxidative agent like
Fe(III) on HMWP formation rate, building on the results from Study 1.
One key finding of our study is that the presence of Fe(III) has an effect on the rate of HMWP
reduction. The small effect of Fe(III) confirmed our thoughts. Fe(III) decreases the HMWP formation
rate, which means that the addition of an oxidative agent increases the oxidation conditions and thereby
reduces the HMWP formation rate. The result aligns with the overall hypothesis mentioned in the effect
of the oxidative agent; the reductive step is the rate-determining step, which, when reduced, leads to
lower HMWP.

The findings revealed that the combination of Fe(III) with O2 resulted in an even greater reduction
in the HMWP formation rate compared to Fe(III) alone for API (P). This observation indicates that the
addition of O2 as an additional oxidative agent to the formulation leads to more oxidative conditions.
This supports the initial hypothesis and confirms the results observed in the effect of oxidative agent
study. The data suggest that the interaction between Fe(III) and O2 plays a critical role in modulating
the HMWP formation rate, highlighting the potential importance of using a similar mild oxidative
agents that can be used in the drug formulation to control the stability of the peptide and minimize
the HMWP formation.
However, the question remains: what is the role of Fe(III)? The expectation was that Fe(III), as an
oxidative agent, would yield a significantly lower HMWP formation rate than observed; however, it did
not clarify how much it contributes to the oxidative conditions. As mentioned previously, the effect is
slightly lower but almost on par with the reference. This indicates that, in the combination of Fe(III)
with O2, the effect is not directly attributable to Fe(III); rather, it is likely due to the addition of O2.
This explanation of Fe(III) performance suggests that Fe(III) preferentially reacted with the disulfide
bonds instead of the oxygen molecules in the solution.
Adding EDTA to the formulation indicated a higher effect on the HMWP formation rate for API
(P), as it caused the HMWP formation rate to double compared to the reference. This suggests that
EDTA may chelate the Fe already present in the drug product formulation. However, as mentioned

not due to the chelation of Fe(III) by EDTA. This explanation is based on what was observed in the
first batch with Fe(III) alone.

reductive conditions, thereby increasing the HMWP formation rate; however, this explanation could
also stem from another reason, such as Fe(III) reacting with the disulfide bond instead of binding to

strength, as it is highly charged. This will be discussed further later in the study.

investigation is necessary.

raises something not easy to explain. Additionally, some observations suggest that when the formula-
tion approaches a stability threshold, reactions may accelerate rapidly, particularly if the reaction is
chain-driven, leading to behaviors that are diﬀicult to understand.

5.5
Study 4 - Effect of ionic strength (Acetate)
Based on previous findings from the effect of Fe(III), EDTA and citrate (Study 2) study, which indicate
that chelating agents are detrimental, it is hypothesized that the reasoning behind its negative impact
could be due to its ability to chelate Fe(III), which shows a slightly higher HMWP rate. However, this
argument may/ may not be enough and requires further examination. Therefore, the hypothesis is
whether chelating agents contribute to ionic strength, thereby increasing the rate of HMWP formation.
The effect of ionic strength is based on the assumption of a thiol-disulfide exchange mechanism where
the thiolate serves as a better nucleophile. In this context, higher ionic strength stabilizes the thiolate
ion compared to the thiol form. This stabilization subsequently leads to increased shuffling of thiol
and disulfide groups, facilitating the exchange reaction. This study aims to investigate the impact of
ionic strength on the rate of HMWP formation using sodium acetate (NaOAc) and examine whether
chelating agents contribute to the ionic strength.

5.5.2
Discussion of study 4 - Effect of ionic strength
5.5.2.1
API P
The primary aim of this study was to investigate the impact of ionic strength on the rate of HMWP
formation using NaOAc. The addition of different concentrations of NaOAc indicated a proportional
correlation with the rate of HMWP formation.
For API (P), the lowest concentration resulted in a slightly higher HMWP rate compared to the
reference, as it elevated the rate. This means that the contribution of ionic strength led to the stabiliza-
tion of the thiolate ion and simultaneously minimized the electrostatic repulsion between the charged
molecules, which resulted in a higher rate of HMWP formation.
Continuing to add NaOAc indicated a further higher HMWP formation rate than 50 mM NaOAc,
as it also increased the rate of HMWP formation. This again highlighted a strong correlation between
ionic strength and the HMWP formation rate. This means that by adding an additional amount of
NaOAc, ionic strength was elevated, which in turn stabilized the thiolate over the thiol and led to more
disulfide bond shuffling, thereby resulting in increased HMWP formation. Furthermore, the increase in
ionic strength, as mentioned before, reduced the electrostatic repulsion between the charged molecules
and brought them closer together, which additionally facilitated a higher HMWP formation rate.
Glycerol was known to some extent for not reacting with the API molecule, as confirmed by resulting
in a low rate of HMWP formation. Therefore, glycerol was used as a control to check if osmolality
contributed to HMWP formation. Fortunately, this showed that it did not have any effect. If the rate
had been high and osmolality had impacted the HMWP formation rate, then there would have been
challenges regarding the ionic strength being the only factor, suggesting that osmolality also influenced
the HMWP formation rate. However, since there was a very low HMWP formation rate when glycerol
was added, this excluded the option of an osmolality effect.
The observations in the HMWP data are confirmed by the purity model for API (P). The trend
in the purity data aligns with that of HMWP, which thereby supports the results discussed. As a
broader measure, purity gives insight into the extent of all impurities formed, not limited to HMWP,
thus providing valuable information.
Fig.5.22 confirms the observed results, indicating a proportional increase between the acetate and
the HMWP rate, and shows the clear effect of NaOAc on the rate. The RMSE for all the formulations
of API (P) shows very minimal RMSE, indicating that our regression model is robust.

CHAPTER 6
General Discussion
The chapter aims to summarize the results of five studies conducted on two APIs, with a focus on
understanding the disulfide bond shuffling mechanism in these compounds. In this section, findings
from each study will be summarized and discussed in the context of existing literature.
Study 1 investigates the effects of oxidative agents (H2O2, O2, N2) on the formation of HMWP. The
findings for API (P) revealed that H2O2 concentrations significantly impacted HMWP rate, with lower
concentrations being more effective in reducing HMWP formation, while higher concentrations resulted
in increased levels. Additionally, purging with N2 showed a higher HMWP rate, whereas purging with
O2 showed a positive effect by reducing HMWP formation effectively. For API (C), the results indicated
that H2O2 was detrimental, causing a significant increase in HMWP levels compared to the reference
batch. Similar to API (P), the introduction of N2 resulted in increased HMWP formation, and O2
exhibited a beneficial effect for API (C).
Upon analyzing the effects of H2O2 on API (P), it was noted that a low concentration (0.33 mM)
yielded a positive effect on HMWP formation rate, with higher concentrations surpassing 1 mM having
a detrimental effect, indicating a threshold for the effective concentration of H2O2. The comparison
between nitrogen and oxygen presented distinct results, where the presence of N2 led to an increase
in HMWP formation, aligning with the hypothesis that reducing oxidative conditions would yield a
beneficial effect. In contrast, O2 purging was found to improve results, suggesting the importance of
mild oxidative conditions in maintaining the integrity of the product. The behavior of API (C) mirrored
that of API (P), as O2 demonstrated positive effects while N2 contributed to increased rates of HMWP
formation.
In the literature [133] described that controlled (H2O2) levels can prevent disulfide bond reduction
during antibody production by maintaining a mild oxidative environment, enhancing antibody stability
and functionality. They suggest further research into the long-term effects of H2O2 on therapeutic
eﬀicacy and large-scale manufacturing. Our findings support this, showing that H2O2 reduces HMWP
formation and improves peptide stability, reinforcing the value of oxidative control in protein production.
Typically, oxygen is considered detrimental due to its role in peptide oxidation and aggregation, whereas
nitrogen is seen as protective. For example, Ma et al. [134] show that nitrogen-enriched storage reduces
lipid oxidation in peanuts, especially in polyunsaturated fatty acids, enhancing shelf life and nutritional
quality. In contrast, our study finds that nitrogen increases HMWP formation, while oxygen reduces it,
supporting our hypothesis that mild oxidative conditions can promote protein stability.
The implications of these findings highlight the critical role of maintaining mild oxidative conditions
to minimize HMWP formation effectively. The results presented in this study challenge some existing
literature, particularly the idea that nitrogen is universally beneficial [133], as it was linked to higher
HMWP levels in this context. These findings contribute to the understanding of how various oxidative
conditions can significantly impact peptide stability, suggesting that established notions regarding the
effects of oxidative and reductive agents may require reevaluation.
Future research should explore lower concentrations of H2O2, especially in relation to API (C), to
find optimal conditions that minimize HMWP formation. Additionally, investigating other oxidative
agents could yield further insights into managing the stability of pharmaceutical formulations. Over-
all, the outcomes from studies on API (P) and (C) underscore the importance of tailored oxidative
conditions in formulation strategies, which could ultimately enhance the eﬀicacy and safety profiles of
pharmaceutical products.

The results challenge traditional notions in the study from the literature [49]

Fe(III) and chelating agent for API (P), For API (C) findings of the combination between transition
metal and chelating agent lead to lower the HMWP, by increasing the oxidative condition that confirmed

Further investigation into whether chelating agent influences ionic strength and impact HMWP
formation is essential for a better understanding of their role in these formulations. Moreover, the
unprecedented reaction between EDTA and Fe(III) raises questions about reactivity and complex for-
mation, which merits further investigation.
Overall, this study indicates that while enhancing oxidative conditions through Fe(III) can reduce
HMWP formation, the addition of chelating agents may inadvertently create reductive conditions that
promote HMWP formation. This contrast to existing literature invites further research to unpack the
intricacies of these relationships. Understanding the specific contributions of various forms of iron and
their interactions with chelating agents could prove pivotal in formulating stable and effective pharma-
ceuticals.

both APIs, while the introduction of Fe(II) resulted in a slight increase in HMWP formation, purging
with O2 subsequently lowered HMWP levels compared to the Fe(II) alone, supporting the premise that
mild oxidative conditions are more beneficial in reducing HMWP. Contrarily, purging with N2 for API
(P) produced negligible differences in HMWP rates when combined with Fe(II), indicating unexpected
outcomes where neither agent appeared to worsen HMWP formation significantly.
Our findings are consistent with prior research [138], which examined the influence of trace metals
on chemical stability in liraglutide formulations with increasing levels of metal ions. Notably, samples
containing Fe(II) exhibited a significantly higher rate of HMWP formation than those with other metal
salts. Conducted at 37°C over 22 days, the study revealed that Fe(II) accelerated HMWP formation.
While our results were obtained at 40°C over 4 weeks, the study supports the hypothesis that Fe(II)
enhances HMWP formation as a reductive agent that reduces oxidative conditions. Additionally, the
study highlights a key difference between Fe(II) and Fe(III), showing that Fe(II) contributes more signif-
icantly to HMWP formation, while Fe(III) promotes higher oxidative conditions, leading to decreased
HMWP formation. These findings strengthen our hypothesis that mild oxidative environments favor
protein stability in the case of disulfide bond shuffling and reduces HMWP formation.
The outcomes from Study 3 highlight the duality of Fe(II) as a reductive agent and its consequential
role in affecting HMWP formation. While the results verify the hypothesis that Fe(II) increases HMWP
rates due to its reductive properties, the favorable response observed with O2 suggests that oxidative
conditions are crucial in mitigating HMWP formation. Therefore, the findings align with the effect of
oxidative agent (study 1), and the effect of transition metals and chelating agent (study 2) indicating
that maintaining a controlled oxidative environment can significantly enhance the stability of drug
formulations by reducing the occurrence of unwanted disulfide bond shuffling.
The unremarkable impact of N2 when paired with Fe(II) calls for further inquiry into inert gases
and their interactions with oxidative conditions. Understanding the balance between oxidative and
reductive forces will be critical as researchers seek to refine formulation practices aimed at minimizing
HMWP formation.
Additionally, the relationship between the purity of APIs and HMWP formation provides a more
comprehensive view of the sample impurities. Thus, this study contributes valuable insights regarding
the optimization of redox conditions in pharmaceutical formulations, focusing on the roles of transi-
tion metals and oxidative agents in minimizing HMWP formation. Future investigations should aim
to elucidate the mechanisms behind the interactions of these agents, particularly assessing the redox
potential’s influence in varying environmental conditions, which could ultimately drive advancements
in the formulation of stable and effective drug products.
Study 4 aims to investigate the impact of ionic strength on the formation rate of HMWP in APIs,
particularly focusing on how NaOAc, as a chelating agent, might influence this process.
Building
on previous findings that identified chelating agents as detrimental to HMWP formation, this study
hypothesizes that the detrimental effects could result from chelation of Fe(III) and an increase in ionic
strength, enhancing the rate of HMWP formation through a thiol-disulfide exchange mechanism. The
study hypothesized that higher ionic strength may stabilize thiolate ions, thereby facilitating greater
disulfide bond shuffling and leading to increased HMWP formation rate.
The results show a clear
trend in HMWP formation rates relative to the concentrations of NaOAc in both APIs.
For API
(P), increasing concentrations of NaOAc resulted in substantially higher HMWP formation rates, with
the highest concentration (500 mM) leading to a 57% increase compared to the reference. Moreover,
glycerol, which maintained the same tonicity as 500 mM NaOAc, exhibited the lowest HMWP formation,

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
In this project, the primary aim was to investigate the formation of high molecular weight protein due
to disulfide bond shuffling and to understand the specific conditions that influence this shuffling process
in aqueous injectable formulations.
Study 1 (Effect of Oxidative Agents) revealed a clear effect of H2O2, O2, and N2. Mild oxidative
agents, such as H2O2 for API (P) and O2 for both APIs, contribute positively by creating mild oxidative
conditions that help reduce the rate of HMWP formation. It was concluded that N2 removes oxygen,
which is unfavorable because it increases reductive conditions that can lead to a higher rate of HMWP
formation. This indicates that the reductive step is the rate-determining step.
Study 2 (Effect of Transition Metals and Chelating Agents) In Study 2, it was concluded that
transition metals have a positive effect on both APIs by contributing to oxidative conditions that
reduce HMWP formation. The addition of O2 showed a clear effect on decreasing HMWP formation,
with this effect becoming more pronounced, consistent with the impact of O2 observed in Study 1. This
concludes that oxidative conditions have a positive impact on the two-step mechanism.
It was also concluded that chelating agents have a significant effect on both APIs by creating more
reductive conditions. Combining an oxidative agent like Fe(III) with a chelating agent was beneficial for
API (C), but could create a reactive complex for API (P). It is crucial to note that these formulations
exist at a stability threshold, introducing variations that complicate our conclusions. For this study,
it was concluded that oxidative conditions again benefit the two-step mechanism by lowering disulfide
bond shuffling.
In Study 3 (Effect of Fe(II) species), it was concluded that reducing agents have an unfavorable
effect on HMWP formation, leading to increased reductive conditions in the solution for the two-step
mechanism. The addition of a mild oxidative agent like O2 led to a more positive effect, resulting in
improved oxidative conditions that reduce HMWP rates. Combining reducing agents and purging with
N2 for API (P) led to negligible effects.
These findings provide essential insights for guiding future research and formulation strategies. Stud-
ies 1-3 align well with the project scope and conclude that the conditions affecting the redox potential
of the solution are crucial, with the addition of reductive agents being the most significant factor influ-
encing the two-step mechanism in controlling HMWP formation.
In Study 4 (Effect of Ionic Strength), it was concluded that ionic strength has a major impact on
stabilizing the thiolate ion, while chelating agents contribute slightly to ionic strength. This study
concluded that the one-step mechanism is highly affected by ionic strength, which stabilizes thiolate as
a reactive species in the rate-determining step, aligning with the project scope.
These findings highlight the importance of carefully managing conditions in drug formulations to
enhance stability and control HMWP formation. By understanding how factors such as oxidative agents
and ionic strength influence HMWP formation, scientists can improve drug formulations, making them
more effective and reducing the risks related to HMWP formation.

CHAPTER 8
Future Perspectives
Moving forward, mild oxidative conditions can be advantageous for high molecular weight protein
formation due to shuffling in certain cases. Ionic strength affects HMWP formation, but it can be
diﬀicult to change aside from removing buffers (if possible) and avoiding di- or trivalent ions. However,
this provides a new understanding that can be further investigated.
Future investigations could focus on exploring different formulations with lower concentrations of
H2O2. Alternatively, the use of other mild oxidative agents comparable to H2O2 may also be considered;
this would facilitate easier observation of the underlying mechanisms and provide a better understanding
of the project scope and how the redox environment affects peptide shuffling.
Conducting additional studies to examine various concentrations of Fe(III) will be essential for
gaining a comprehensive understanding of the mild oxidative agent and its effects on protein stability
and aggregation. Additionally, exploring the use of other transition metals, such as copper or zinc,
could provide further insights. This will yield new knowledge about how different transition metals
affect the two-step mechanism of HMWP formation due to peptide shuffling.
Furthermore, it is crucial to investigate the role of chelating agents and their impact on the HMWP
formation rate in greater depth, to gain knowledge about whether they should be avoided in drug
formulations that face issues with peptide shuffling. Focusing on the reactivity of the EDTA-Fe complex
will provide valuable information regarding the roles of transition metals and chelating agents in HMWP
formation.
Several future directions will be explored regarding the proportionality of ionic strength to the
HMWP rate.
One area of investigation will evaluate whether other salts, such as NaCl at similar
concentrations, fall above or below the NaOAc curve. Further research on other salts that create closer
relationships will enhance our understanding of the contributions of chelating agents to ionic strength
and the role of stabilizing the reactive thiolate in the one-step mechanism.
It would also be interesting to investigate the impact of using divalent salts, such as magnesium,
instead of solely focusing on monovalent salts while maintaining an uncomplicated formulation. This will
provide deeper insights into the contributions of EDTA and citrate to ionic strength, thereby enhancing
our understanding of their roles in disulfide bond shuffling.
If resources and time permit, collaboration with an LC-MS expert would enhance our understanding
of the characterization of HMWP under different conditions and how the HMWP changes due to
excipients. This collaboration will allow for more extensive studies to deepen our comprehension of
both pathways. These future efforts will help refine formulations and improve the overall stability and
eﬀicacy of the products under investigation.
Lastly, if time allows, repeating the studies multiple times will ensure that the results are consistent
and reliable and that our regression model is robust. Additionally, we could investigate whether other
scavengers are more effective than NEM and Fumarate to demonstrate that the thiolate is the reactive
species in the one-step mechanism. Ultimately, expanding the research to include other peptides beyond
just the two specifically studied will provide a more comprehensive understanding of how disulfide bond
shuffling generally affects the rate of HMWP formation. This approach could lead to more effective
strategies for mitigating HMWP formation and achieving a stable active pharmaceutical ingredient with
higher eﬀicacy and stability in drug product formulations.
