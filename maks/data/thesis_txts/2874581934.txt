PREFACE 
 
Vietnam is witnessing rapid growth in the renewable energy sector, especially offshore wind 
power, to meet growing energy demand and its commitment to reducing greenhouse gas emissions. 
According to the “National Power Development Plan 2021 to 2030, with a vision to 2050” (PDP8), 
Vietnam is aiming to reach 91.5 GW of offshore wind power capacity by 2050. This ambitious 
goal is opening up great opportunities for international investors and project developers. However, 
to exploit these opportunities, the investors need to clearly understand the potential and situation 
of wind power development in Vietnam, the legal requirements, as well as the challenges that may 
be encountered. In addition, requirements and challenges related to the environmental and social 
impacts of offshore wind power projects also need to be considered comprehensively and 
accurately. This study aims to provide an overview of the offshore wind development situation in 
Vietnam, focusing on licensing processes, challenges that investors may face, and relevant 
environmental and social factors. 
The report begins by analyzing Vietnam's offshore wind power development potential, thereby 
identifying key opportunities and challenges. Next, the report looks at development process and 
the legal framework to be adhered with while planning a project. Environmental and social impacts 
that have been identified for offshore wind projects elsewhere and nearshore projects in Vietnam 
are reviewed. The environmental impacts include seawater quality, noise emission, biodiversity 
and marine ecosystems whereas social impacts are those effects on community life, economics 
and traditional activities of local people. Afterward, the requirement and processes for regulatory 
EIA and international ESIA are reviewed and compared. The reviews looks to provide some 
insight into the key environmental and social aspects to be considered as per the Vietnamese 
regulations and international standards. 
The report concludes with a case study of the Ba Ria Vung Tau offshore wind power project that 
is being developed by Marubeni and its partners. An analysis of the local project area context and 
a scoping exercise were done to initially identify the potential environmental and social impacts 
of the project.  
Hopefully this report will provide useful information for any individual or organization interested 
in exploring offshore wind power development opportunities in Vietnam.

1.  INTRODUCTION 
1.1. 
Background 
Offshore wind power hold tremendous promise for Vietnam's energy landscape, capitalizing on 
the nation's extensive coastline and favorable wind conditions. It was reported that Vietnam holds 
a theoretical offshore wind potential of up to 600 GW, wherein 261 GW of offshore wind power 
with fixed foundation (at a depth of <50 m) and 338 GW of offshore wind power projects with 
floating foundation (at a depth of >50 m.) (Pham, 2023). This abundance of wind resources 
presents a compelling opportunity for Vietnam to bolster its energy security, mitigate greenhouse 
gas emissions, and drive sustainable economic growth. However, despite the recent wind power 
frenzy in 2019 – 2021 following the attractive Feed-in-Tariff (FIT) policy, Vietnam has yet had 
any offshore wind power project. All on-water wind power projects currently in operation or under 
construction are nearshore, i.e. within six nautical miles from the shoreline. Offshore wind power 
projects are still in a very early conceptual and planning stage.  
Vietnam committed at the 26th session of the Conference of the Parties (COP26) to the United 
Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) in Glasgow, UK to achieve net 
zero by 2050. The country's updated National Determination Contribution (NDC) identifies 
developing of offshore wind power as one of the key measures for the country to reduce its 
greenhouse gas emission to meet the goal (Vietnam Government, 2022). Wind power sources 
including onshore and offshore are projected to make up the largest proportion of the total 
electricity generation (as per the “National Power Development Plan 2021 to 2030, with a vision 
to 2050”, which is commonly known as Power Development Plan VIII or PDP8) (Vietnam 
Government, 2022). Developing offshore wind power not only taps into vast energy potential but 
also aligns with the vision of economic growth. 
However, along with this great potential, the development of offshore wind power projects also 
brings many complex environmental and social challenges (Drewitt & Langston, 2006; Kadellis, 
Apostolou, Kapsali, & Kondil, 2016; Whitehouse, Harris, Sutherland, & Rees, 2011; Ladenburg, 
2009). From disrupting marine ecosystems to resettling coastal communities (Glasson, Durning, 
Welch, & Olorundami, 2022; Abramic, Cordero-Penin, & Haroun, 2022), offshore wind 
development requires a comprehensive understanding of its impact on the environment and 
society. Balancing the need to produce clean energy with the conservation of marine biodiversity,

Against this background, the present study aims to explore the complex relationship between the

1.2. 
Objectives of the study 
This report aims to comprehensively survey the development picture of offshore wind power 
projects in Vietnam. By shedding light on the current state of the industry, regulatory frameworks, 
environmental impacts and socio-economic implications, this study strives to provide stakeholders 
with valuable information. Furthermore, the report also seeks to identify potential opportunities 
and challenges in developing offshore wind power projects in Vietnam. 
1.3. 
Key research questions 
To achieve its objectives, this study addresses several key research questions: 
1. What is the current state of offshore wind power project development in Vietnam? 
2. What are the opportunities and challenges influencing offshore wind power deployment in 
the Vietnamese context? 
3. What are the environmental and social impacts associated with offshore wind power 
projects? 
4. How do Vietnam's regulatory frameworks align with international standards and best 
practices for offshore wind energy development? 
5. How do potential developers proceed with the necessary licensing processes to develop 
offshore wind power projects in Vietnam?

2.  METHODOLOGY  
This section outlines the methodology employed in conducting the study, which includes literature 
review and expert interviews.  
2.1. 
Literature Review and Data analysis 
The main method used for this research is literature review and document analysis. The study 
reviews and analyzes information from a variety of sources such as legal documents, reports of 
international organizations and scientific articles. The literature review has long been recognized 
and widely used as a powerful and effective research method (Snyder, 2019). It brings many 
outstanding advantages, helping research achieve diversity and comprehensiveness when 
synthesizing up-to-date understanding from former studies and resources. This saves time and 
costs, while providing high-quality data from reputable sources. This method is especially 
effective in accessing available and reliable information, necessary when evaluating aspects of 
wind power project development (Chukwuere, 2023; Snyder, 2019). The use of documents from 
multiple reputable sources, helps ensure objectivity and provides a solid foundation for in-depth 
and comprehensive analysis of the research. Furthermore, the triangulation of data increases the 
trustworthiness and validity of the research and helps overcome bias from a single method and 
provides a more balanced view of the research topic (Noble & Heale, 2019). 
Below are the main sources of data and information used in this study: 
- 
Articles from scientific journals: The review of scientific articles aims primarily at 
obtaining quantitative and qualitative insights of the offshore wind energy situation 
worldwide, as well as the range of environmental and social impacts that have been 
identified for offshore wind projects around the world.  
- 
Legal documents issued by the Government of Vietnam, and relevant governmental bodies: 
These documents help determine the regulations, legal requirements that the projects must 
comply with and support mechanisms.  
- 
International standards and guidelines: These guidelines define standards and technical 
requirements for wind power projects in terms of environmental and social impacts. 
- 
Reports of international organizations such as the World Bank, International Renewable 
Energy Organization (IRENA), Global Wind Energy Council (GWEC), German Agency 
for International Cooperation (GIZ), independently or within the framework of friendship 
with the government or agencies in Vietnam. These reports often provide a comprehensive

2.3. 
Limitations 
Although this study attempts to provide a comprehensive view of the research topic, certain 
limitations are inevitable that need to be recognized to ensure a clear understanding of the context 
and scope of the findings. The key limitations that need to be carefully considered when evaluating 
research results and making recommendations are listed below. 
- 
Data unavailability: one of the main limitations is the lack of specific and detailed 
information. In Vietnam, nearshore wind power is generally accounted under the category 
of onshore wind power, thus data is not available for in-depth analysis.  
- 
Inconsistent documentation: existing documents are often inconsistent in content and 
quality, leading to difficulties in synthesizing and comparing data. Differences in approach 
and analysis of documentary sources can cause confusion or distort research results. 
- 
Outdated information: the Vietnam renewable energy market, especially wind power, 
changes rapidly. Therefore, documents and data may be out of date and no longer 
accurately reflect the current situation. Relying on outdated information can affect the 
accuracy and applicability of research results.

3.  OFFSHORE WIND POWER DEVELOPMENT IN VIETNAM 
3.1. 
Vietnam’s potential for offshore wind power  
It should be noted that in Vietnam, wind power projects are classified into three categories, namely 
inland, nearshore and offshore projects, as directly translated from Vietnamese terms. Wherein, 
nearshore and inland projects are generally considered together in Vietnamese legal system as 
onshore projects. Definitions of each of the above project types are presented below. This study 
uses the terms as defined and used in Vietnam to avoid confusion. 
- 
Inland wind power projects are projects with wind power turbines built and operated on 
land and coastal lands with the outer boundary being the average lowest sea level for many 
years (Vietnam Government, 2018). 
- 
Nearshore wind power projects are projects with wind power turbines built and operated 
within the sea area from the average lowest sea level line to a distance of 6 nautical miles 
offshore (The World Bank, 2019). 
- 
Offshore wind power projects are projects with wind power turbines built and operated 
beyond the nearshore region. 
Vietnam, with a coastline of more than 3,000 km, has great potential for wind energy, favorable 
for the development of both nearshore and offshore wind power (Nguyen, 2023; Le, 2021). Studies 
reveal that over 39% of Vietnam's area has an average annual wind speed above 6 m/s at a height 
of 65m, and over 8% of the area has an average annual wind speed above 7 m/s (Vietdata, 2024). 
Vietnam has begun exploiting nearshore wind power since 2013 by putting into operation the first 
nearshore wind power plant in Bac Lieu province with an initial capacity of 16 MW and has 
expanded to 99,2 MW (Power Technology, 2024). Nearshore wind power projects not only 
effectively exploit wind energy but are also easier to build and maintain than offshore projects. 
The central and southern provinces of Vietnam such as Ben Tre, Tra Vinh, and Soc Trang all 
possess favorable wind conditions and have focused on investing in exploiting nearshore wind 
power in recent years. 
Meanwhile, offshore wind power is a new and promising type of wind energy in Vietnam. The 
potential for offshore wind power has been recognized in recent years when studies showed that 
Vietnamese waters have ideal conditions to develop this type of energy, with high and stable 
average wind speeds. The Offshore Wind Roadmap for Vietnam conducted by the World Bank

estimates that the economic and technical potential for offshore wind power of Vietnam can reach

In a study in October 2019, the Energy Sector Management Assistance Program (ESMAP) team 
of World Bank did an analysis of publicly available spatial data to identify Vietnam’s technical 
potential for offshore wind development (ESMAP, 2021). Wind data from the Global Wind Atlas1 
and seabed depth from the General Bathymetric Chart of the Oceans (GEBCO)2 were used for the 
analysis with the focus on the area within 200 km distance from the shoreline. The study reveals 
that Vietnam has a potential of 261 GW for fixed foundation wind and 388 GW for floating 
foundation wind respectively. Figure 3.1 presents Vietnam’s offshore wind potential map 
developed in the study. The map indicates two main regions with highest potential for development 
of offshore wind energy of the country, which are: 
- 
The northern waters in the Gulf of Tonkin, which have relatively consistent mean wind 
speeds of typically 7.5 to 8.5 m/s at 100 m height. Fixed offshore wind farms development 
can be developed around the shallow region nearby Ha Noi and the coastal area. 
- 
The southeastern waters, which are suitable for both fixed and floating wind farms. The 
areas near the coastline of Khanh Hoa, Binh Thuan, and Ninh Thuan provinces, in 
particular, have higher mean wind speeds exceeding 9.5 m/s at 100 m height 
According to the World Bank, offshore wind power can play an important role not only in meeting 
Vietnam's rapidly growing electricity demand but also contribute at least 50 billion USD to the 
Vietnamese economy. Offshore wind development will help develop a strong domestic supply 
chain and create thousands of highly skilled jobs, while expanding export opportunities to other 
global offshore wind markets. This clearly shows that offshore wind power is not only a clean 
energy solution for Vietnam, but also an economic driving force, creating sustainable development 
opportunities for the country (The Straits Time, 2023; World Bank, 2021).

3.2. 
Necessity and importance of offshore wind power development

Typically, a wind power project will go through five main stages during its development, namely 
Preliminary Development, Project Development, Construction (EPC Commissioning), Operation 
& Maintenance, and Decommissioning. Each stage will include certain steps to be taken by the 
developers and certain agreements to be achieved between the developers and  the Vietnamese 
authorities. Detailed focus and key activities of each stage are as follows: 
- 
Preliminary Development: The preliminary development phase of the project begins with 
site selection and ends when the project developer opens an escrow account and receives 
the Investment Certificate. At this stage, it is crucial to determine whether the project site 
falls within the National Power Development Plan (NPDP). Only wind power projects 
accepted in the NPDP can apply for an Investment Certificate. If the proposed project is 
==PAGE:34==

impact assessment of offshore projects in Vietnam 
Mohit Sharma 
 
 
24 
not included in any power development plan, it needs to proceed an application and get 
approval for admission to the NPDP which also include preliminary site study, wind 
measurement and pre-feasibility study. This process can take 1-2 years to complete. Upon 
receiving the Investment Certificate, foreign developer can apply for the Investment 
Registration Certificate (IRC) and open the escrow account. 
- 
Project Development: This phase starts with the Feasibility Study and ending with receipt 
of the power purchase agreement (PPA) and construction permit. During this phase, the 
developer needs to obtain a number of agreements and licenses from authorities, including 
grid connection agreement, SCADA agreement, and so on. The Environmental Impact 
Assessment (EIA) report also needs to be carried out during this phase as part of the 
Feasibility Study and receive approval from MONRE. Land clearance is to be conducted 
and Land use right certificate is to be obtained during this stage. This phase typically lasts 
1 to 2 years. 
- 
Construction: The construction phase starts from drawing design to project acceptance. 
In this phase, the developers need to ensure that the land is available in whole or in part 
according to the construction schedule; valid construction permit has been obtained; and 
construction drawing design has been approved by competent authorities. Once all the 
construction works are done, they will be accepted, and the construction phase concludes. 
The construction of an onshore/nearshore wind power project typical lasts 1-2 years. For 
offshore wind power projects, the construction may last longer. 
- 
Operation and Maintenance: The operation phase includes training and obtaining 
licenses for operation staff, commissioning and testing, commercial operation date (COD), 
operation and maintenance. This phase involves multiple activities and documents that 
require appraisal and approval from govermental bodies such as MOIT, National/Regional 
Load Dispatch Center, Electricity Regulatory Authority of Vietnam (ERAV), and so on. 
- 
Decommissioning: Decommissioning shall occur at the end of the operation period of the 
wind farm. In the event of decommissioning, the site shall be restored to its previous land 
use. 
Further details on the activities to be conducted at each stage as well as other useful notes to 
developers can be found in the handbook (Matrix AIM, 2020).

3.6. 
Opportunities and challenges for offshore wind power project development in 
Vietnam 
Vietnam is facing huge investment demand for offshore wind power, which is a great opportunity 
for international investors and developer to participate in Vietnam's market. The ambitous goal of 
reaching up to 91.5 GW offshore wind power by 2050 creates a great prospect for energy 
expansion and investment. Foreign investors are encouraged to participate in Vietnam's wind 
energy sector without any foreign ownership restrictions. In addition, the Vietnamese Government 
has also launched many attractive incentive programs for wind power project developers. 
Furthermore, offshore wind power has been developed on a large scale in Europe with proven 
technology with many successes and significantly reduced costs in Europe. The deployment of 
offshore wind power in markets near Vietnam such as Taiwan, Japan, China and Korea is leading 
to the development of supply chains and cost reductions in the Asian region, which Vietnam’s 
developers can benefit from. 
However, despite Vietnam’s effort in promoting offshore wind power growth and its ambitious 
goals, tapping into country’s full potential is still a great challenge to the investors. The main 
difficulties are related to the readiness of the legal framework, ill-equipped national grid, 
curtailment risks, and concern over PPA bankability. The key challenges are elaborated further 
below. 
- 
Legal challenges: The implementation of offshore wind power projects in Vietnam is 
facing many complex legal challenges (ASL Law, 2024). These challenges cause 
significant uncertainty and obstacles to the investment and development of offshore wind 
power projects. A major problem is that current regulations are unclear on survey and 
exploration rights in marine areas. There is also a lack of clarity on how the legal

regulations apply to the exploitation of offshore resources, leading to difficulties in 
determining the rules related to the use of subsea land and waters.  
Determining the authority competent to approve investment in offshore wind power 
projects is still unclear between the National Assembly, the Prime Minister and the 
Provincial People's Committees.  
- 
Delayed Marine Spatial Planning (MSP) approval: many wind power investors are 
facing a deadlock and having to wait for a long overdue MSP to be approved (Vietnam 
News, 2023). This planning essentially serves as the legal basis for offshore wind power 
development in conjunction with other marine activities. The MSP has only been approved 
by the Vietnam Assembly on 28 June 2024 with the official copy has yet been issued. The 
delay of the MSP approval not only delays the investment decision-making process, 
leading to many potential projects being left hanging, waiting for clear instructions from 
the government side.  
- 
Poor grid system: Vietnam's national power grid is developed and managed exclusively 
by EVN. The system is not yet capable of receiving large amounts of electricity from 
offshore wind power projects. The majority of renewable energy projects are concentrated 
in the Central and Southern regions, where the demand and growth of electricity are lower 
than in the Northern region, while the infrastructure is insufficient to transmit enough 
electricity from the Central and Southern regions to the Northern region. Upgrading the 
power grid system to solve this problem requires time and large financial resources. The 
World Bank estimates that upgrading the grid to accommodate 5 to 10 GW of new offshore 
wind capacity will take at least five to ten years at a cost of $34.8 to $38.6 billion (Fulcrum, 
2023). There is still debate over whether private enterprises should be allowed to 
participate in the power grid market. 
- 
PPA bankability: according to the Vietnam Wind Investment and Development 
Handbook, there are concerns about the EVN’s PPA bankability wherein several risks are 
highlighted. Curtailment risk is considered the most significant risks wherein EVN has the 
right to acquire only the capacity that the grid system can obtain, rather than the full 
capacity generated. Other risks include currency exchange risk, change in law, and force 
majeure.

4.  KEY ENVIRONMENTAL AND SOCIAL IMPACTS  
Vietnam is standing on the threshold of a renewable energy revolution with extremely promising 
offshore wind power development. However, this exciting prospective development also brings 
many environmental and social challenges. Wind turbine construction and installation activities 
can affect the marine ecosystem, impacting species and coastal communities. Ecological changes, 
noise and vibrations from turbines can cause negative impacts on marine animals such as whales, 
dolphins and coral reefs. In addition, the construction and operation of wind power projects can 
also affect the livelihoods of local people, causing changes in communities and local culture.  
It should be noted again that Vietnam’s offshore wind power development is still in early planning 
stage. The country so far has only operational nearshore wind power projects, most of which are 
located along the shoreline of the Mekong Delta (see section 3.4.1). Nevertheless, the development 
of offshore and nearshore wind power projects often carries similar potential impacts on the 
environment and society. Both types of projects have onshore infrastructure components such as 
substations and power transmission systems, along with subsea components such as turbines and 
submarine cables. These similarities suggest that many of the potential impacts are common to 
both types of projects.  
Offshore wind projects are generally viewed as causing fewer negative environmental and social 
impacts than nearshore projects given the diversity of the nearshore environment and more use of 
water surface by local people. Nevertheless, reviewing environmental and social impacts 
associated with nearshore projects can provide valuable insights into the type and scale of potential 
impacts from offshore projects. Therefore, this section aims to explore the potential impacts of 
offshore wind power development in Vietnam via reviewing both the offshore impacts reported 
worldwide and the impacts that have been identified for the existing nearshore projects in Vietnam.  
4.1. 
Impacts from offshore wind power projects worldwide 
Offshore wind power has become a rapidly growing sector around the world. Advanced countries 
such as Denmark, Germany and the UK have been at the forefront of developing this technology, 
with hundreds of projects already in operation. The development of offshore wind power projects 
has been widely researched, and their impacts on the environment and society are now relatively 
well understood. A summary of key impacts as reported in literature is presented below. 
Environmental impacts:

Air quality can be affected by small emission of air pollutants during construction, 
maintenance and shutdown activities (Kadellis, Apostolou, Kapsali, & Kondil, 2016).  
- 
Hydrography is affected by construction and decommissioning activities. During this 
period, pollutants chemicals can contaminate the seawater causing short-term degradation. 
Flow changes can also occur, so as redistribution of sediment (Cazanave, Torres, & Allen,

- 
Scouring effect: The presence of wind turbine foundations causes changes to flow patterns 
in the surrounding area, leading to increased local sediment transport and thus increased 
scouring tendency (Whitehouse, Harris, Sutherland, & Rees, 2011). Scouring effects are 
defined as the removal of sediment by hydrodynamic forces. This effect is quite likely to 
occur around structures and will be influenced by the type of foundation design. 
Biodiversity impacts: 
- 
Birds and bats are affected by wind turbine operations, including turbine blades, light 
emissions, noise emissions, and boat traffic during installation and maintenance. During 
the construction, operations and decommissioning phases, these factors can have 
significant impacts. Specifically, fatal collisions for birds and bats can occur, or they can 
be attracted to the light from the turbines, leading to increased collisions (WWF, 2014; 
Abramic, Cordero-Penin, & Haroun, 2022). The wind farm can become barriers to 
migration routes, forcing birds and bats to change their flight paths, causing disturbances 
and flight avoidance (Drewitt & Langston, 2006). Noise emitted from turbines can disrupt 
breeding and disrupt roosting areas, leading to disturbances and migration of birds and bats. 
Methods such as limiting turbine activities, using ultrasonic deterrents and painting the 
blades black are current discussed as potential measures to allow co-existence of birds and 
bats with wind farm projects (Zimmer, 2023). 
- 
Fish are affected by underwater sound vibrations, pile driving noises during construction, 
and electromagnetic fields around cables (Kadellis, Apostolou, Kapsali, & Kondil, 2016). 
Loss of habitat due to construction, maintenance or decommissioning is a main threat. 
Habitat size may be permanently reduced by operational noise. Sedimentation during 
construction phase may affect the fish larvae. On the other hand, the formation of artificial 
reefs at the foundations of the wind turbine can have some positive impact on enriching 
the fish community (WWF, 2014).

species diversity and abundance (Abramic, Cordero-Penin, & Haroun, 2022).  
- 
Zoobenthos are affected by construction activities, footprints of structures and noise

Social and community health impacts:

Employment: The development of offshore wind power projects create direct jobs in the 
construction, operations and maintenance phases, along with indirect jobs in the supply 
chain. These developments also contribute to improving skills and education (Glasson, 
Durning, Welch, & Olorundami, 2022).

From onshore components:

pollution but can also negatively impact human health and ecology. 
- 
Freshwater quality: During construction phase, freshwater resources in the area can be 
contaminated by activities such as discharges from construction equipment which can be 
non-hazardous or hazardous. This is especially important for sensitive ecosystems and 
water sources used for domestic or agricultural purposes, requiring close monitoring and 
management to ensure that these impacts are minimized. 
- 
Electromagnetic interference: the transmission lines and transformer stations can create 
electromagnetic interference, causing significant effects on human health. Electromagnetic 
fields (EMF) are formed along power transmission lines, at substations and wind turbines, 
potentially affecting people living near these areas. Long-term exposure to EMFs can lead 
to health problems, including stress, insomnia, and other neurological disorders. 
From nearshore components: 
-

cables encompass a range of activities, which include piling, limited-scale dredging, 
anchoring, and the operation of construction vessels. These operations have the potential 
to yield notable effects on seawater quality due to temporary rises in turbidity and the 
accumulation of suspended solids and chemicals during the construction phase.  
- 
Noise: During the construction and operation of offshore wind power projects, noise can 
cause significant impacts on human health, especially for communities living near the 
project area. Noise generated from construction activities and noise from onshore 
substations can affect quality of life and cause stress for residents.

alter coastal landscapes and may cause undesirable visual impacts to local communities.

4.2.2. Biodiversity Impacts 
The development of wind power projects in Vietnam, especially in the Mekong Delta, is posing 
many risks to the region's biodiversity value. In a presentation in 2022, the experts from IFC 
pointed out the key concerns in terms of biodiversity impacts from nearshore wind power project 
development in Vietnam (IFC, 2022). One of the main impacts is on migratory shorebirds, which 
live in coastal mudflat habitats in the Mekong Delta. These birds face the risk of habitat loss and 
disturbance from wind turbine construction. In addition, waterfowl and high-flying birds, 
including resident forest birds, may also be affected. Offshore wind projects pose a particular risk 
to seabirds due to disturbance from offshore activities. According to the report, Vietnam has about 
70 species of bats, including large fruit bats, which may be affected by wind turbine blades, 
changing their habitat and lifestyle.   
Aquatic habitats, including nearshore and offshore areas, also face risk from wind power projects. 
These environments contain many species of fish, rays and sharks, and can be impacted by 
disturbance and changes in currents (IFC, 2022). Nearshore habitats such as mangroves, coral reefs 
and seagrass beds are also vulnerable. Furthermore, whales and dolphins living in the coastal 
estuaries of the Mekong Delta may be negatively impacted by noise and disturbance from wind 
power project activities.  
The development of offshore wind projects can cause disturbance and displacement of marine 
animals, creating barriers, fragmentation and impacting the boundaries of the marine environment 
(TWPC, 2022). These impacts can affect the activities of marine animals and increase the risk of 
death from collisions with vehicles or equipment during construction or maintenance. In particular, 
noise and heat emissions from pile driving or electromagnetic fields arising from subsea cables 
can disturb the activities of marine animals.

According to the ESIAs conducted by the developers for the nearshore projects in Vietnam, the

- 
Land acquisition: Activities that cause this impact include permanent loss of land, 
temporary appropriation for construction of transmission lines and landing areas, or 
restrictions imposed by safety regulations. The land acquisition process needs to comply

-

clearances zones and restrictions on fishing. Such prohibition will negatively affect the 
livelihood of local people.

Local economy and employment: Wind power projects can bring positive impacts to the

economic development and create more employment opportunities for local people.

causing incremental effects. Some of these impacts can benefit communities, including 
positive economic impacts and the creation of more jobs. However, during the construction

to minimize negative impacts on the community.

5.1. 
Environmental permitting procedure and Regulartory EIA 
The Law on Environmental Protection 2020 (LEP2020) is the most important law on 
environmental protection in Vietnam. This law stipulates the principles, processes and standards 
necessary to protect and improve the living environment, while promoting sustainable 
development. LEP2020 sets out regulations on environmental impact assessment, waste 
management, pollution control, and nature conservation. Importantly, this law classifies 
investment projects into four groups based on the level of environmental impact, thereby 
determining the corresponding environmental licensing and reporting requirements. The law also 
emphasizes the responsibility of state agencies, businesses and individuals in protecting the 
environment, ensuring economic development coupled with protection of natural resources and 
the environment. 
According to LEP2020, investment projects are classified into four groups based on environmental 
criteria: I, II, III and IV. The subgroup will determine which projects are subject to which type of 
environmental permits and licenses. Those include the EIA, Environmental License, 
Environmental Registration. According to Article 28 of the LEP2020, new offshore wind power 
projects can generally be classified as a Group I investment project of large- or medium-scale 
projects using land, land with water surface and marine area with environmentally sensitive factors 
(Vietnam National Assembly, 2020). Accordingly, these projects must first conduct a preliminary 
EIA during the pre-FS phase, then conduct an EIA during the FS phase. After the EIA report has 
been appraised and approved by MONRE, the investor must prepare an application dossier for an 
environmental license before proceeding with the trial operation. The environmental license is 
required for Group I investment projects that discharge wastewater, dust, or exhaust gases into the 
environment that must be treated, or generate hazardous wastes that must be managed in 
accordance with waste management regulations when in operation. Figure 5.1 presents the key 
procedure to obtain the necessary environmental permits in Vietnam for Group I projects.

5.2. 
Environmental and social impact assessment (ESIA)  
5.2.1. Relevant guidelines and standards 
To achieve the planned goal of developing wind power, Vietnam needs to attract large foreign 
investment. This strategic move not only helps provide the necessary financial resources but also 
brings in advanced technology and management experience from developed countries. However, 
attracting investment capital is not simply a financial issue. To truly attract and retain investment, 
the projects must compliance with environmental and social standards according to international 
==PAGE:55==
Wind farm planning and environmental and social  
impact assessment of offshore projects in Vietnam 
Mohit Sharma 
 
 
45 
standards and guidelines. These standards not only protect the natural environment but also ensure 
the rights of local communities, contributing to sustainable and comprehensive development.  
Unlike the case of developed countries, the regulatory EIA conducted as per the Vietnam law is 
usually considered insufficient by foreign financial institutes. It is due to the lack of assurance that 
the risks relating to environmental and social impacts can be adequately addressed. As a result, 
projects in Vietnam are usually required to conduct an internationally complied ESIA study in 
order to secure foreign financing. The ESIAs need to follow various standards and guidelines that 
were developed by the financial institutions. The most commonly used set of standards is the IFC’s 
Performance Standards on Environmental and Social Sustainability (IFC PSs) (IFC, 2012). The 
IFC PSs are supplemented by World Bank General Environmental Health and Safety (EHS) 
Guidelines and the Guideline for relevant sectors. 
Other standards that are also common for project development in Vietnam include ADB’s 
Safeguard Policy Statement (SPS) (ADB, 2009) and the AIIB’s Environmental and Social 
Framework (ESF) (AIIB, 2019). Financial institutions without internal standards typically 
participate in the Equator Principles (EPs), which are based on the IFC PSs. The EPs have been 
applied by 129 financial institutions globally accounting for a large proportion of project financing 
in emerging markets (Equator Principles, 2024). 
The key focuses of the above standards and guidelines are presented briefly below. 
IFC Performance Standards: 
The IFC Performance Standards are a set of international guidelines for managing environmental 
and social risks and impacts in investment projects. With eight standards covering risk 
management, workplace safety, and community protection, IFC PSs help businesses grow 
sustainably and responsibly in emerging markets. The key focuses of the PSs are presented below. 
- 
PS1 (Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts) 
emphasizes the importance of effectively managing environmental and social risks and 
impacts throughout the life cycle of a project. The Environmental and Social Management 
System (ESMS) is a dynamic and continuous process, supported by the participation of 
stakeholders, including customers, employees, local communities affected by project and 
other stakeholders. ESMS is based on the business management process of "plan, do, check 
and act," with the goal of managing risks and impacts in a structured and ongoing manner.

Marubeni Corporation is planning to develop the large-scale Vung Tau offshore wind farm project

clean and sustainable energy sources for the region. The Ba Ria Vung Tau offshore wind farm

-

building infrastructure and starting electricity production. 
-

integrate more wind turbines, while optimizing support systems.

provides guidance on planned capacity of each sea region. The developers will need to do the site 
selection and propose the suitable site themselves. As such, Marubeni and its partners have

as well as other conditions such as loads and infrastructures.

divided in two offshore areas of the Ba Ria – Vung Tau province. Phase 1 and 2 of the Project will

offshore from Vung Tau city. Phase 3 of the project will be developed in Area 2 (VT2), which has

Conclusions 
This study explores the overall picture of the wind power development situation in Vietnam, along 
with an assessment of related environmental and social impacts from nearshore and offshore wind 
power projects. Available studies and information were reviewed and analyzed to construct an 
overall understanding of the situation. Interviews with the experts working closely in the sector 
were conducted to gain further insights and complete the picture. From analyzing the potential for 
offshore wind power development to reviewing current legal frameworks and the process of 
developing new projects, we can clearly see the importance of wind power in sustainable energy 
strategies of Vietnam. Vietnam has great potential for offshore wind power development, which 
not only meets growing energy demand but also helps the country meeting the net-zero target by 
2050 as committed at the COP26.  
Vietnam is facing a large investment need in offshore wind power, opening up great opportunities 
for international investors and developers to participate in the market. The ambitious target of 91.5 
GW of offshore wind capacity by 2050 creates great prospects for energy expansion and 
investment. However, despite Vietnam's efforts to promote offshore wind development, exploiting 
the country's full potential remains a major challenge for investors. The main difficulties relate to 
the readiness of the legal framework, the inadequate national grid system, limited risks, and 
concerns about the bankability of the PPAs. Legal challenges include a lack of clarity on survey 
and exploration rights in maritime areas, as well as regulations on offshore resource exploitation. 
Delays in MSP approval delay investment decisions, leading to many potential projects being put 
on hold. Vietnam's weak power grid system is not capable of receiving large amounts of electricity 
from offshore wind power projects. EVN's declining business performance and PPA policy 
increase concerns about the ability to pay power producers. These issues create financial risks for 
investors. The lack of support measures has hindered Vietnam's transition to a sustainable energy 
future and its ability to attract investment in renewable energy projects. 
Many suggestions have been made by the interviewed experts as well as relevant international 
organizations to promote the development of offshore wind power in Vietnam. These actions 
include improving the legal system, announcing a vision and annual installation targets to 2050, 
establishing development zones through marine spatial planning and a transparent licensing 
process under international standards. It is important to define the approach to seabed leasing and

power purchasing, moving to a competitive system of leasing and PPA contracts by 2026. Other 
additional approaches to be considered could include encouraging financial mechanisms to reduce 
capital costs, strengthen transmission systems and port infrastructure, building supply chains, 
provide skills training, and invest in local businesses. It is also suggested that the ESIA and safety 
framework and technical regulations are established according to international standards. 
Offshore wind power projects can cause many notable environmental and social impacts. 
Construction and demolition activities can pollute sea water and change water flows, affecting 
hydrology. Biodiversity is also affected, with birds and bats at risk of collisions and being attracted 
to light from turbines, causing changes in flight paths. Fish are affected by underwater noise and 
magnetic fields around the cable. Marine animals may lose their habitat and have their 
communication disrupted. Construction activities also affect zoobenthos through sediment 
redistribution. Social impacts include changes in landscape aesthetics, risk of minor collisions 
between ships and turbines, and impacts on socio-economic and emergency activities due to 
limited shipping routes. 
Environmental and social impact assessment is an indispensable part of the project development 
process. Implementing an ESIA from the early stages of a project not only helps identify and 
manage potential risks but also facilitates the participation of stakeholders, ensuring that projects 
are developed in accordance with sustainable and beneficial way for all parties. Although applying 
international standards may increase time and costs during the project preparation and 
development phase, the long-term benefits it brings are significant, including financial savings. 
resources and time during project financing and implementation. 
Marubeni Corporation is developing offshore wind project in Ba Ria Vung Tau province together 
with its local partners. The understanding and insight gained from this study will likely benefit the 
project along with other similar development during its preparation for the development and 
environmental permitting. 
7.2. 
Recommendations  
Vietnam's policy system is developing rapidly and changing continuously, reflecting the 
government's efforts to adapt to new requirements for economic development and environmental 
protection. However, this volatility requires investors and stakeholders to closely monitor and 
promptly analyze policy changes. This helps ensure that project implementation strategies and 
plans are always consistent with current regulations, minimize legal risks and make the most of

66 
preferential opportunities. Flexibility and continuous updates in understanding and applying new 
policies will be important factors in helping projects, especially offshore wind power projects, 
achieve sustainable success. Other recommendations regarding the environmental and social 
impact assessment for offshore wind power projects include: 
- 
The ESIA should be done following international standards right from the beginning to 
achieve the highest efficiency. Results from ESIA studies can be adapted for submission 
to local agencies in Vietnam. Although progress in the project preparation and development 
phase may be delayed due to the need for additional studies, the risk of delay and increased 
cost may be avoided when upgrading to international standards later for financing purpose.  
- 
The ESIA should be done early to help promptly identify environmental and social issues, 
from which the initial design can be adjusted to avoid high incurring costs for changes and 
for mitigation the impacts. 
- 
Investors need to carefully conduct desk-based and field-based studies to ensure 
information about the project area is fully and accurately understood. This process includes 
collecting data on natural conditions, climate, soil, biodiversity, as well as social factors 
such as community life, culture and economy. Collecting detailed and accurate data will 
help investors fully identify factors that can affect the project and, conversely, factors that 
the project can affect. This is important as it allows potential problems to be identified at 
the outset and appropriate mitigation measures proposed.  
- 
Understanding information about the project area also helps investors establish more 
effective management programs. These programs will include long-term monitoring, risk 
management and environmental protection measures, ensuring that the project not only 
complies with legal regulations but also contributes to the sustainable development of area.  
- 
Implementing good governance programs will also help build trust and support from local 
communities and stakeholders.

Vietnam's wind energy market has been experiencing significant growth over the past five 
years, driven by increasing energy demand, government support, and favorable natural 
conditions for wind power generation, particularly along the coast. The installed capacity of 
wind energy in Vietnam has increased significantly over the past five years. Vietnam has 
attracted investment in its wind energy sector from both domestic and international investors. 
Major energy companies and developers have been investing in wind projects across the 
country, contributing to the expansion of the market. The Vietnamese government has been 
actively promoting renewable energy, including wind power, through various policy 
initiatives and incentives. These include feed-in tariffs, tax incentives, and preferential 
treatment for wind energy projects. 
Question: What do you think about the main challenges that a project developer faces in 
wind farm development in Vietnam? 
Despite the rapid growth, Vietnam's wind energy sector still faces challenges such as grid 
integration issues, land acquisition, and regulatory barriers. Developers may face 
bureaucratic hurdles, permitting delays, and evolving regulations that can impact project 
timelines and costs. Securing suitable land for wind farm development, especially along 
coastal areas where wind resources are abundant, can be challenging due to competing land 
uses, land tenure issues, and community concerns. Connecting wind farms to the grid and 
ensuring grid stability are also challenging. Vietnam's grid infrastructure may require
