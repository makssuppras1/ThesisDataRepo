Abstract 
 
Sterol transport proteins (STPs) are important for the cholesterol homeostasis in the cells. 
Therefore, misregulation of STPscan cause several diseases. However, identification of 
selective inhibitors for these proteins have turned out to be difficult. Thus, the Laraia group 
have started working on a testosterone-like library to identify new STP inhibitors. The aims of 
this project are first to develop protection conditions of the Wieland-Miescher ketone (WMK) 
to access new reactions and then to continue the expansion of the pseudo-natural 
testosterone-like library. Thus, a thioketal-protecting group and optimal deprotection 
conditions were developed. Subsequently, a WMK was fused to a series of secondary 
heterocyclic scaffolds where nine testosterone-inspired molecules were prepared for 
screening against STPs.

1 Introduction 
 
The introduction will describe cholesterol in health and disease and more specifically how it 
is regulated and transported within the cells, as well as a description of the sterol transport 
proteins (STPs). Furthermore, a discussion on the current state of STP inhibitors is included 
followed by a final discussion on different ways to design compound libraries and how they 
are combined to create a pseudo-natural compound library. 
 
1.1 Role of cholesterol in health and diseases 
 
Cholesterol is often known as a common cause of cardiovascular diseases where too high 
blood concentrations leads to heart diseases and stroke, which are some of the dominant 
causes of death in the USA. For example,it was shown that 38% of American adults suffer 
from too much cholesterol in their blood from 2015 to 20181. Therefore, a lot of research has 
looked into reducing extracellular cholesterol levels for therapeutic applications. Extracellular 
cholesterol mainly comes from dietary cholesterol, which ends up in the bound to a 
lipoprotein known as low-density lipoprotein (LDL). 
 When the levels of cholesterol are too high, cholesterol can be transported to the 
apolipoprotein A1, which forms high-density lipoproteins (HDL). HDL transports cholesterol 
to the liver where it is excreted. Therefore high levels of LDL and low levels of HDL leads to 
an over aggregation of cholesterol leading to cardiovascular diseases by formation of 
plaques.2,3 Excess intracellular cholesterol can be esterified and stored in organelles known 
as lipid droplets (LDs).2  
 
Despite being the root of several diseases, cholesterol is still essential for our cells to 
function properly. Cells get cholesterol in two ways, either by extracellular uptake from 
dietary cholesterol, or by biosynthesis.4 Cholesterol is predominantly synthesized in the liver 
cells in the endo-plasmic reticulum (ER). The pathway starts with acetyl-CoA which via a 
cascade of enzymatic reactions is transformed into squalene, a 30-carbon terpenoid that is 
oxidized to form 2,3-oxdosqualene which can be cyclized into lanosterol and further reduced 
to cholesterol (Figure 1).5 
==PAGE:13==
2 
 
 
Figur 1 Key intermediates in the biosynthesis of cholesterol. Starting with several units of acetyl-CoA being fused 
followed by several enzymatic steps that leads into the formation of cholesterol. Reproduced from Cortes et al.6 
Dietary uptake can happen in two ways, either by the Niemann-pick type C1-like 1 (NPC1L1) 
protein on enterocytes, or through the LDL receptor (LDLR). When there are low levels of 
cholesterol, NPC1L1 will bind unesterified cholesterol by translocating to the plasma 
membrane on intestinal cells. The receptor bound cholesterol will then undergo endocytosis 
to be transported to the ER via unknown mechanisms.4  
Additionally the LDLR is plasma membrane bound and can bind LDL particles, which 
contains esterified cholesterols, through an extracellular binding domain. The bound LDLR 
will undergo endocytosis which leads to releasing the LDL particles in the lysosome. In the 
lysosomes esterified cholesterol is hydrolyzed into free cholesterol.4 
 
As cholesterol makes up 30% of the cell membrane on average, it is important for regulation 
of the integrity and the fluidity of the lipid bilayer. Additionally cholesterol is used by our cells 
for biosynthesis of different steroid hormones like testosterone and estrogen.7,4 
All the different kinds of sterols are built, from the same scaffold and this scaffold consists of 
a ring system with three cyclohexane rings and one cyclopentane which are assigned the 
letters A to D as shown in figure 2.8 
==PAGE:14==
3 
 
 
Figure 2 The conserved 4 ring structure of sterols that is often assigned the letters A-D and cholesterol along 
with the hormone steroid Estrogen that are both built up from the core sterol scaffold.  
In the cell membrane sterols can interact with a diverse set of transmembrane proteins 
whose active conformations can be both altered and maintained by this interaction. Thus, 
cholesterol can act as both a regulator of signal transduction and trafficking in the cell 
membrane. Specifically, these roles mean that diseases relating to intracellular cholesterol 
can lead to severe consequences such as cancer and improper growth of organs. This also 
means that the amount of cholesterol intracellularly is very important and thereby regulation 
of the cholesterol trafficking within, into and out of the cell is crucial to maintain cell 
homeostasis.9 
 
1.2 Sterol transport    
 
Cholesterol homeostasis is maintained by a tight regulation mediated by the relationship of 
biosynthesis, influx and efflux of cholesterol in the cells. Cholesterol is transported by both 
vesicular and non-vesicular mechanisms. The vesicular transport is mainly mediated 
extracellularly, and the non-vesicular transport is mostly mediated intracellularly. The non-
vesicular transport is centered around the three protein families: the oxysterol-binding 
protein-related protein (ORP), the steroidogenic acute regulatory protein-related lipid 
transfer(START) domain (STARD) family proteins and lastly the Aster-domain containing 
family4. These proteins transport sterols in the cytosol between various organelles and 
additionally aid keeping up the sterol gradient between the ER and the plasma membrane 
(PM) (see figure 3). Though some of the trafficking routes are known for the STPs, the 
mechanisms behind these and their physiological relevance still remain largely unkown.10

1.2.1 The oxysterol-binding protein-related protein  
 
The ORP is a family of proteins consisting of 12 members (ORP1-12) that all contain an 
oxysterol-binding protein (OSBP) related ligand-binding domain (ORD). The ORD contains a 
hydrophobic binding pocket made of a β-barrel where the sterol is oriented with its 
hydrophilic head facing the bottom of the pocket. When the sterol is bound the protein folds 
to a stable conformation with a lid made of α-helices and β-sheets (see figure 4). In 
mammals OSBP, ORP1, ORP2, ORP4 and ORP9 have been identified to bind and transport 
cholesterol.11,10

1.2.2 The steroid acute Regulatory Protein related Lipid Transfer Domain 
STARD is a family of proteins that all contain the START lipid transfer domain. It consists of 
15 members that can be separated into 6 sub-families based on their sequence and their 
binding, herein STARD1-6 are shown to transport sterols, with their trafficking paths shown 
in figure 3.4  
The domain has been resolved for several of the START proteins and is shown to be 
conserved. The binding site is shown to be a hydrophobic cleft made of nine anti-parallel β-
sheets closed by two α-helices along with a third C-terminal α-helix that acts as a lid to the 
cleft (figure 5) .12 
 
 
Figure 5 (1) Structure of the START domain of STARD1 (PDB: 3P0L), highlighting the hydrophobic binding cleft 
made of anti-parallel betasheets (green) gripped by two α-helices (yellow and red). (2) The size of the binding 
cleft is assumed to be defined by the amino acids E169, F184, R188, L199, H220, F267. Reproduced from Depta 
et al.4 
 
 
1.2.3 The Aster protein family 
There are three members of the Aster family: Aster-A, Aster-B and Aster-C. The Asters are 
membrane associated proteins that all contain an N-terminal GRAM domain and a START-
like domain. It is the START-like domain that is used for sterol binding, which consists of a 
curved seven stranded β-sheet grove that can bind the sterols, a C-terminal α-helix and two 
small N-terminal helices that holds the cleft together. Aster-A and Aster-C have their crystal 
structure solved which shows that there can be space for larger molecules than cholesterol. 
The crystal structure of Aster-A has shown that its binding pocket is quite similar to the one 
of the START domain, however the sterol is more rotated because of the amino acids F405, 
Y524 and F525, which are conserved in all three Asters, that is important for the orientation 
of the sterol (Figure 6).4

1.3 Current small molecule inhibitors of sterol transport proteins 
Even though the STPs are very important for cellular processes, only few small molecules 
have been found to bind them. A few natural products (NPs) have been found to inhibit 
OSBP and ORP4L proteins leading to cytotoxicity against cancer cells. The compounds are 
known as ORPphilins, some of them are shown in figure 7.13 However, they still do not 
selectively bind one of OSBP and ORP4L. After the discovery of the ORPphilins as OSBP 
and ORP4L inhibitors, Zhong et al. identified a synthetic analogue of OSW-1 called LYZ-81. 
This compound showed high selectivity towards ORP4L inducing death in leukemia stem 
cells. They also showed it being selective towards Leukemia cells, making it a potential 
starting point for development of drugs towards leukemia treatment.14

OSBP and ORP4L.

AI-1l was identified to bind ASTER-C with higher selectivity over ASTER-A and ASTER-B. 
On the other hand Al-3d was identified as a more potent ASTER protein inhibitor binding all

identified. It was found during a screening for autophagy inducing compounds. It turned out 
to be highly selective towards ASTER-A, it is shown to be selective over the other ASTER 
proteins A and B, as well as other STPs.16

found. Lapillo et al. discovered VS1 (figure 9) a molecule that binds STARD3, however it

is clear that there is room for discovery of new STP inhibitors. Especially for the STARDs

of new inhibitors may help advancing field of modulating STP domains and identify the 
correct sterol transport mechanisms in the cells.4 The next section will discuss different 
strategies to synthesize compound libraries.

1.4 Library design 
 
Target oriented synthesis (TOS) has originally been the go-to method for making libraries of 
compounds where the focus was mainly on making NPs and their derivatives. However, 
TOS is a lengthy and time consuming process that often leads to low yields and in the 
1990’s it was eventually out-phased by high-throughput screening (HTS) of combinatorial 
chemistry derived libraries. Combinatorial chemistry was introduced because it was possible 
to create many compounds in a short time and less complicated way. With combinatorial 
chemistry rising in popularity, NPs were also phased out. It was not until the early 2000’s 
where it was discovered that these HTS-libraries lacked complexity and diversity and 
therefore did not cover a very large chemical space with biological relevance. Instead, in 
recent years, the focus has turned back to NPs as they have a large source of biologically 
active compounds. These bio-active compounds usually contain a larger chemical diversity 
and complexity than combinatorial products. Scientists have worked on developing effective 
strategies to create these natural product-like (NPL) compound libraries. 
 These are strategies like diversity-oriented synthesis (DOS), complexity-to-Diversity (CtD), 
biology-oriented synthesis(BIOS) and pseudo-natural products (PSPs).19,20 
 
1.4.1 Diversity-oriented synthesis (DOS) 
 The chemical space of small molecules is the complete collection of all possible small 
molecule structures. This is an infinite number of molecules and thus it is impossible to 
synthesize all of them. The aim of DOS is to create a collection of small molecules, which 
covers as much of this chemical space as possible. This can be achieved by taking simple 
starting molecules which can act in a diverse set of reactions and thereby turn it into different 
much more structural diverse set of core scaffolds by the use of different reagents.21  
The most used strategy for creating DOS libraries is the build/couple/pair (B/C/P) strategy 
(see figure 10). The build phase consists of building blocks which are then coupled 
intermolecularly. Lastly, in the pairing phase the coupled compounds are paired via 
intramolecular reactions. These phases can lead to stereochemical and skeletal diversity in 
the library.22 
 
The disadvantage with DOS is that even though it may lead to synthesis of a library that 
expands in a huge chemical space it may not lead to any bioactive compounds. This is 
because the strategy is not necessarily based on natural products or other pre-validated 
structures.  
==PAGE:20==
9

Couple: the building blocks are coupled via intermolecular reaction between various functional groups giving 
stereochemical diversity. Pair: The new coupled molecules are paired by intramolecular reaction giving skeletal 
diversity. Reproduced from Nielsen et al.23 
Another strategy related to DOS is the CtD strategy. In CtD, NPs are subjected to different 
ring distortion and modification reactions including ring expansion or ring cleavage reactions. 
This leads to new compounds with increased structural diversity. This strategy has the 
advantage, in comparison to B/C/P, of using NPs, which increases the chance of hitting 
biologically relevant chemical space. 
 
1.4.2 Biology-oriented synthesis (BIOS) 
In BIOS the library synthesis is based upon a specific core scaffold simplified from a natural 
product with biological activity. This is under the assumption that the core scaffold is 
responsible for orientation of the NP in its target binding pocket. Therefore, maintaining the 
core scaffold should keep some of the biological activity and specificity of the parent 
molecule.24  
Waldmann et al. developed a strategy called structural classification of natural products 
(SCONP). SCONP simplifies the structure of the parent molecule in a hierarchical manner, 
which is built up as a tree containing scaffolds. Having the more complex NP-structures on 
the other branches moving inwards along the branches gives more simplified structures by 
removing substituents and outer rings, which immensely reduces the complexity of the NPs 
as one approaches the center of the tree.25 A small sample of their scaffold tree is shown in 
figure 11. 
==PAGE:21==
10 
 
 
Figure 11 Hierarchial tree containing NPs that is simplyfied into scaffolds. Containing 0.2% of the derived 
scaffolds. Reproduced from Karageorgis and Waldmann. 25 
However, BIOS has the disadvantage that it is limited to NPs only and thereby only covers 
the chemical and biological space from the guiding NPs. Instead, following the fragment 
based methods, it is possible to form NP-hybrids, where NP fragments are fused to form 
new products. These are PNPs.26 
 
1.4.3 Pseudo-natural products (PNPs) 
PNPs are as the name states, not entirely NPs, however, they contain fragments and 
scaffolds from NP’s. When chemists create a library of PNPs, they try to combine the use of 
BIOS and DOS. Combination of natural product fragments that retains the biological aspects 
of the NP’s and BIOS derived scaffolds leads to formation new pseudo-natural products, 
which are not produced in biosynthetic pathways. Where the NPs themselves only covers a 
certain portion of the chemical space. It has been shown that PNPs can reach beyond this 
portion into another part of the chemical space while still maintaining a similarity to natural 
product.27  
 
PNP-libraries can be inspired by or derived from NPs, these kinds of libraries are called NP-
inspired libraries and NP-derived library. In the NP-inspired library, the scaffold is very 
similar to the NP it is derived from but not identical. This means other reactive groups may 
==PAGE:22==
11 
 
be introduced to the scaffold and thereby new reaction types can be chosen and this may 
lead to a larger library. On the other hand, the NP-derived library is made from a scaffold 
that is identical to the guiding NP. This means the reaction types used to expand the library 
will be the same and therefore the size of the library will be smaller than the one following 
NP-inspired principle.20 Additionally Alonso et al. used the NP-derived method to create a 
library of steroid derived compounds which could further explore new parts of the chemical 
space by fusing the natural derived steroid scaffold with unnatural compounds that are not 
found in nature, but have shown biological activity in drugs (figure 12). As the chemical 
space filled by NPs and unnatural products are quite different, fusing NP derived scaffolds 
with unnatural fragments may lead to discovery of new biologically relevant chemical space 
that cannot be achieved by basing the library only on NPs.28

3 Aims and Objective 
The aims of the project are to synthesize a compound-library using a scaffold based on 
testosterone. It is a continuous work on a library that has already been started in the group 
using the WMK as the core scaffold molecule. Initially the scaffold will be synthesized in two 
easy steps. As the previous work in the group showed, the scaffold had to be protected and 
this was done via a ketal and a thio ketal. Before any analogues are synthesized, conditions 
for deprotection of the thioketal is found and optimized. After suitable deprotection conditions 
are found for the thioketal, the synthesis pathway will extend to the synthesis of different 
analogues via heterocyclic reactions with the exposed ketone on the protected-WMK 
scaffold. This will be reactions like Fischer-indole synthesis, Friedländer quinolone synthesis 
and 1,3-dipolar [3+2] cycloadditions. The analogues will be thoroughly analyzed using 
various techniques such as nanomagnetic resonance (NMR) and liquid chromatography 
mass spectrometry (LCMS) among others. A post-doc in the group will then later screen the 
compounds against various STPs as potential inhibitors. The work carried out during the 
project will make a basis for future synthesis of new analogues that will be made for the 
library based on a testosterone-like scaffold.

4.1.1 Synthesis of triketone 
The following triketone (3) had to be made as a precursor to make the WMK. Reacting 
methyl vinyl ketone (2) with the diketone (1) in presence of triethyl amine for 20 hours 
resulted in formation of the triketone (3) in high yield. 
 
Figure 19 Synthesis of triketone (3). 
It is a classical Michael addition where the tertiary carbon in the diketone contains a highly 
acidic proton that can lead to the formation of an enolate when reacting with a base. The 
enolate could attack in 1,2-addition by attacking directly onto the carbonyl carbon or in a 1,4 
addition at the β-position. The carbonyl carbon is a hard electrophile and the carbon in the β-
position is a soft electrophile. The enolate alpha-carbon is a soft nucleophile and prefers to 
attack a soft electrophile, thus determines the regioselectivity. Soft-soft interactions are 
directed by orbital interactions and due to better orbital overlap of the highest occupied 
molecular orbital (HOMO) on the enolate α-carbon and the lowest un-occupied molecular 
orbital (LUMO) on the β-carbon in the enone 1,4-addition is preferred.  
The 1,4-addition is the thermodynamic product because it’s the most stable, to avoid the 
kinetic product from 1,2-addition, the reaction was run at room temperature and with a very 
low amount of base. The mechanism for 1,4-addition is shown below in figure 20.

4.2 Protection of the enone 
From previous work in the Laraia group it has been shown that the enone in the WMK is 
more reactive than the ketone. Therefore, the enone had to be protected before any 
analogues could be made. This was done via and ketal and a thioketal. 
 
4.2.1 Ketal protection of enone 
Having made the WMK, the enone was protected following a published procedure. The 
WMK (4) with excess ethylene glycol (5), catalytic amount of 
triethylsilyltrifluoromethanesulfonate (TMSOTf), 4Å mol. sieves in dichloromethane (DCM).31 
In the previous work from Whitmarsh-Everiss et al.29 the ketone was protected in high yield 
using p-TosOH•H2O at rt for 30 minutes. However, the enone was protected was carrying 
the reaction out at -10 OC to selectively protect the enone due to this being the 
thermodynamical product. It successfully provided the ketal protected WMK (6) at a 
moderate yield. 
 
Figure 23 Reaction scheme for ketal protection of WMK. 
Due to some minor mistakes during the purification some fractions containing the pure 
product was lost during the flash column chromatography meaning the yield obtained could 
possibly have been higher than 23%. Furthermore, it was very hard to separate the starting 
material and the product because only one of the carbonyl groups should be protected, the 
reaction could not run to completion as too much of the double protected product would 
form. The reaction was stopped after a reasonable amount of the double protected WMK 
started to show on the LCMS data while monitoring.

4.2.2 Thioketal protection of enone 
As the ketal formation is a reversible reaction and is very acid sensitive, it would not be 
possible to use the ketal-protected WMK in acid catalyzed reactions. To broaden the scope 
of possible reactions a thioketal was used to protect the enone aswel. 
 
The WMK was protected by thioketal following procedure of Bosch et al. 32 Reacting the 
WMK (4) with a slight excess of 1,2-ethanedithiol (7) in the presence of p-TsOH•H2O in 
glacial acetic acid the enone could be rapidly protected in a good yield (8).  
 
Figure 25 Reaction scheme for thioketal protection of WMK. 
The reaction was tested on a small scale, TLC and flash column chromatography with a 
DCM/pentane eluent showed nice separation. However, afterwards when the reaction was 
carried out on full scale, it turned out that the double protected product and the mono-
protected product could not really be separated by this eluent. Therefore, the large scale 
reaction was purified once again with an optimised eluent system, which was able to 
separate the two products better. When the test reaction was carried out there might have 
been only very little to none double protected product formed due to it being on small scale. 
In addition to the thioketal protected WMK was synthesized on large scale a second time 
later in the project. This time it was purified with the optimized eluent system right away and 
gave a yield of 74%.

4.2.3 Thioketal deprotection 
As mentioned in section 1.1.3 the thioketal formation is not reversible like the ethylene ketal. 
The thioketal is therefore more challenging to deprotect. The three most common methods 
for dethioketalisation are strong electrionphiles, oxidation or alkylation. Furthermore, the 
common strong electrophiles are Hg and Tl, but due to safety and health reasons, it could be 
great to find less harsh conditions to deprotect the compound (8).   
Several small-scale test reactions were carried out to find the optimal conditions for 
thedeprotection. These were carried out on the indole-fused thioketal protected WMK (9.1, 
see section 4.3 for synthesis of indole containin analogues). 
The first test was using (bis(trifluoroacetoxy)iodo)benzene (PIFA) as an oxidative agent in a 
DCM/MeOH/H2O solution as used by Carneiro et al.33

5 Conclusions and future work 
 
The project was based on fusing the WMK to different NP-fragments. The WMK (4) was 
synthesized in two easy steps with a great yield of 85%. Having made the WMK a method 
for deprotecting the enone with a thioketal had to be developed. This was done based on 
procedures found in literature to yield the thioketal-protected WMK (8) in good yields (50-
74%). In addition, optimal deprotection conditions had to be developed as well.  Several 
deprotection conditions were tested and optimized to finally discover AgNO3 as the best 
compound for deprotection of the analogues. Along with the thio-keal WMK, a ketal-
protected WMK (8) was also synthesized in moderate yield (23%). Based on these protected 
WMKs, a testosterone-like pseuo-NP compound library consisting of nine diverse analogues 
was synthesized. These were more specifically: five indoles, a spiropyrazole, a 
spiropyrazoline, a quinoline and an imine. Furthermore a tosylhydrazone-fused thioketal 
protected WMK (18) was synthesized in a good yield (60%) to access the spirocyclic 
compounds via 1,3 dipolar [3+2] cycloadditions. All the analogues that were synthesized and 
characterized are shown in the scheme below (figure 62). 
The project works as a base for the synthesis of new substituted quinoline-, spiropyrazole- 
and spiropyrazoline-fused analogues. Furthermore, Azidoboronic acid (31) was synthesized 
in a good yield (58%). It was made as a starting material for planned synthesis of 
spiroisoindolines. However, there was only time for one test reaction that did not work. In the 
future the right conditions to synthesize these could be investigated. Additionally, it was also 
planned to test and synthesize a thiadiazole analogue.  
Moreover, potentially new thioketal-deprotection conditions could be tested since it seemed 
that a lot of yield was lost for some of the compounds during the deprotection. Other 
conditions that were considered, but not tested during the project, were Lewis acid based 
Cu(BF4)252 or oxidation conditions with oxone®.53    
If any compounds are identified as hits towards any STPs the project will focus on these and 
SAR-studies can be carried out on them to develop potent STP inhibitors. Hopefully, these 
would be able to work as tools for better studying of STPs or as drugs.
