Summary
Retirements are a very important stage of life that require careful consideration to-
wards saving. It is achievable through contributing a percentage of wages to a pension
fund, investing in housing equity, and other investment strategies. Pension funds will
generally follow the recommendations of the life-cycle model, in which allocation to-
ward the riskier assets should decrease as one ages, how long one invests in risky
assets is determined using set risk preference profiles of low, medium, and high.
This thesis will consider housing equity in the pension fund model. With contri-
butions reduced to account for the high mortgage rates compared to renting. Where
housing equity is the subtraction of withstanding principal of the mortgage from the
homes market value.
The house prices were calculated using 45 years of interest
rates generated from Monte Carlo simulation. In most cases home equity produces
the greatest returns over the life-cycle. Where the best case environment for interest
rates is the exception. Depositing at least 30,000 EUR or 10% of the homes value
into the pension fund produced higher returns, when the investor had the highest risk.
There were cases where renting performed on par with home equity, which should be
considered promising due to high costs of mortgages that were not covered in this
study. Leveraging on the pension funds was investigated for the renting scenario, but
did not bridge the gap between renting and owning home equity.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Housing equity amounts to the largest financial asset for most households, with the
aging population and phasing out of defined benefit pensions, the need for investors
to save more for a longer life and be more in control of their pensions is on the rise.
For many investors owning a home is a given due to the consumption nature of the
asset. The time to liquidate this asset is different for many households, for some it
is not liquidated for the sole purpose of retirement funds and instead only liquidated
when absolutely necessary.
This project will aim to address how pension funds perform with the addition of
housing equity. To aid in this investigation, it will try to answer, ”how lucrative is
the idea of owning versus renting?” by adding housing equity to the resulting pension
funds. As the nature of mortgages are an act of leveraging, the affect of leveraging
on the pension fund will also be explored.
Finally, asking, ”when and if the risk
preference of an investor affects the motivation for buying or renting”. The basis of
this thesis will use Monte Carlo simulation to generate future interest rates.
1.1
Pension Funds
Pension funds are investment funds that provide retirement commitments to retirees.
The funds are garnered from accumulated contributions from either employers, em-
ployees, or a combination of both (Whiteside 2019). There are two kinds of pension
funds; defined benefit and defined contribution funds.
The defined benefit funds
promise the employee a certain amount of pension when they retire, based on age,
length of service, and so forth. Defined benefit plans have many positives against
their comparative. Mainly, that they allow the employee to have an adequate plan
during retirement, as the amount received is a predetermined amount - or percentage
- of one’s salary. The negatives of such plans are that they put more onus on the
employer, the very definition of accruing money in such a way means that funding
the pensions has an impact on company profits and the companies must predict the
amount of money needed to meet obligations (McWhinney 2019).
On the other hand, defined contribution funds rely on how much the employee
has contributed to the fund and how much interest the money has been able to
accumulate. Therefore, the benefits paid out rely on the performance of the fund,
which can fluctuate between years and funds. Most pension funds are risk-averse and
try to return long term growth with minimal risk (Phipps 2019). Employers have

contribution plans becoming the plan of choice for many employees, with now only

1.2
Leveraging
In finance, leveraging is the act of borrowing capital in order to make more substantial
investments that yield higher returns. The leveraged amount can vary, but a company
or individual having more debt than equity would be classified as highly leveraged.
This ratio of debt to equity can be described using a leverage factor, in equation
1.1. In numerical terms, an investment or portfolio with a leverage factor of 3, has
borrowed 200% of the capital.
LF = equity + debt
equity
(1.1)
Leveraging has a positive relationship with risk; the higher the leverage, the higher
the risk. Leveraging by nature, also comes with the risk of default, i.e., the failure
to meet the obligations of repaying a loan. The leverage factor for this report will
follow work of a previous Master’s student at the Technical University of Denmark
(Kristjánsson 2019), where the findings will be used as a measure. This work was
expanded on by the collaborators of this thesis (Magnússon et al. 2020). In their thesis,
Kristjánsson (2019) analyzed the optimal leveraged asset allocation strategies. The
research concluded that a leverage factor of 3, 4 and 5 was optimal, meaning that
the probability of default increased rapidly with a factor of a higher order than 5.
The thesis also concluded that a leverage factor of 3 had the overall best performance
regarding risk-adjusted returns and other risk characteristics. Figures 1.1 and 1.2
illustrates Kristjánsson (2019) findings regarding the optimal leverage factor.
In their thesis Magnússon et al. (2020) used these findings to investigate the
relationship between human capital and leveraging for pension savings. Which in
turn will be adapted to this study, however the goal is to not test the performance
of leveraging itself, but to instead test if leveraging can provide the same returns as
home equity.

1.3
Salaries and Pension Payments
Salary and wage vary with profession, experience, age, country, gender, inflation rates,
among other variables. They will almost exclusively rise as one is promoted through
a company or career path. For the average person, the most significant increase will
occur when a person is transitioning from a role lacking any experience criteria to

an experienced role. The expected pattern of wages is a gradual increase - that is
steeper early in the life-cycle - that steadily tapers up to retirement, where it will be
on a gradual decrease. The rate of which will be dependent on the pension scheme
and how much was invested by an employee or employer.
In this analysis, the average salary in Denmark was selected. Focusing on the total
for men and women and only fixed salary-earners, the average salary of 2018 for ages
ranging from 25 to 65 can be seen in figure 1.3. The age group of 45-49 years shows
the peak of earning potential; beyond that, the average salary will steadily decrease,
the rate of which slows down with increasing age.
Figure 1.3: Average salary based on age in Denmark (Statistik 2018).
In Denmark and many other countries, it is mandatory to pay into some form

1.4
Human Capital
Human capital has a broad definition; it is the economic implication that is pro-
duced from knowledge, experience, and skills of the employed. With this definition,
investing in human capital would take a more personable approach than investing in
traditional markets, meaning supplying training and education for employees (Goldin
2016). Human capital is essential to both employees and employers. For the employee,
the human capital can be surmised as the accrued lifetime salary. Although human
capital can change throughout the lifetime - as it is dependent on employment status -
it generally starts off considerable and will steadily decrease as it is transferred to real
funds. This is due to a person having a lifetime of earning power early in the life-cycle
and will steadily decrease as it is transferred to real funds. From an employer’s point
of view, human capital is an employee’s potential future earnings.
The human capital that will be the focus of this study is the employees’ human
capital, i.e., the potential future earnings adjusted for inflation. In this context, the
definition will be ”the amount of work people choose to do and are capable of doing.”
To satisfy this definition, human capital was calculated from the sum of all pension
contributions remaining. When considering that human capital is not yet acquired
funds, instead a future projection of what earnings could be, it becomes difficult to
incorporate its financial planning scenarios intuitively. Especially when considering
future projections of human capital will not be obtained all at once, but rather in
contributions of a salary.
In general, life-cycle investing will set to spread consumption from periods of high
consumption to periods of low consumption.
The life-cycle of human capital can
be described by the ebbs and flows of wealth gains and losses, where the goal is to
spread wealth and consumption, from periods where it is high to periods where it
is low. In a simplified life-cycle model, there are two stages to human capital, ac-
cumulation, and de-cumulation (Bovenberg et al. 2008). The de-cumulation phase
would be characterized by retirement and accumulation by working. In the accumu-
lation phase, consumption is assumed to be high and low when in the de-cumulation

The two main types of mortgages, fixed rate and adjustable rate mortgage (ARM),

Literature review
The following section will discuss previous literature on life-cycle investing, the major-
ity of papers considered were published from the early 2000s, but earlier papers were
considered when new literature was not available. The main focus of topics was in
relation to the incorporation of human capital and housing decisions in the life-cycle.
The majority of the literature has a focus on the United States market, which would
indicate a bias. This bias would arise from motivation and reasoning behind saving
for retirement - in the USA there would be a greater emphasis on saving for long or
short term health care needs, whereas this motivation is not as prevalent in Denmark.
This bias will be attempted to be counteracted with literature specific to a European
market.
Life-cycle investing is an important topic but has the primary desire to spread
consumption of a consumer over the life-cycle, so that periods of low consumption
will be supplemented by periods of high consumption.
The most optimal way to
achieve this is by taking a higher risk in youth, and slowly decreasing the proportion
of assets to the risky side as one ages (Merton 1969). A critique of the life-cycle model
in this most simplistic form is that it does not offer advice on optimal asset allocation,
after a one-off selection of the portfolio, where the fraction of risky assets decreases
with age (Bodie et al. 2007). This paper argues that because of human capital, the
proportion of risky assets can vary dramatically.
When labor supply flexibility is considered in life-cycle investing, it can be shown
that those with a risky wage, will be subject to the conflicting advice given in standard
life-cycle models (Bodie et al. 1992). With a risky wage being considered as anyone
whose wages are more uncertain than usual - the most common example given by
Bodie is a stockbroker. Meaning young investors with risky wages or earning po-
tentials, should not invest in stocks. They should instead wait until real wealth is
accumulated, at a later time in life, to start investing in risky assets. Studies using
a model with a mandatory retirement age have been considered (Gomes et al. 2008).
However, mandatory retirement ages are slated to be harmful to investors. Sudden
shocks in the financial market could mean that one is forced to delay retirement
(Mitchell et al. 1983). The case for phased retirement could be considered in this
context (Rappaport 2009), but a criticism of phased retirement is that it can confuse
matters for consumers receiving their retirement benefits. It can also be difficult for
older workers to hone new skills and find work, meaning phased retirement could only
be an option for those who are able to phase into a consultancy role (Munnell 2009).

mean a one percent increase in nominal interest rates as Fishers’ ex-ante equation 3.3

3.2.5
Volatility
Another measurement for an investment strategy is to measure its volatility. While
the returns and return averages have a focus on the central tendency of returns, the
volatility focuses on deviation from the central. Volatility is a measure of uncertainty,
specifically in the change of the returns and, therefore, the risk. The most common
way volatility is measured, is in its standard deviation. A big move up or down leads
to higher volatility. The securities’ value can consequently be spread over a bigger
price range, resulting in a higher risk.
Volatility does not measure the direction
of price changes, and normally, investors are more worried about a negative price
change from the average (Jorion 1995). Consequently, volatility is not always seen as
a perfect way to measure risk.

4.3
Simulation and Scenario Generation
The objective of the simulation is to generate a substantial amount of future scenarios
based on historical data. The generated future scenarios only give an idea of how the
future can look and cannot be taken as gospel.
The scenarios can give a good indication of how the worst, best, base, and average
case scenarios will look. Section 4.3.2 gives a deeper understanding of each case. The
scenario generation is then compared to the historical data to ensure its statistical
significance. The method used for the generation of the scenarios is known as Monte
Carlo simulation. It is a standard method for scenario generation and uses known
parameters from the training set, the historical data, to generate the test set, possible
future scenarios. One of the upsides of using Monte Carlo is that it generates scenarios
that are not in the historical data. It can, therefore, generate some extreme scenarios
which have not occurred but have a possibility of happening in the future. Historical
returns cannot guarantee any future returns. However, due to a lack of reliable data,
an assumption is made that simulation of the historical data can represent future
returns.
4.3.1
Stationary Multivariate Normal Process
The distribution in which the scenario returns are drawn from is called a stationary
multivariate normal distribution. The distribution uses the average, µ, and the co-
variance, Σ, from the historical data. This distribution is an expanded version of the
normal distribution to higher dimensions (Lapidoth 2009).
The data reaches back from July 1998 to February 2019, almost 21 years. The
simulation then generates 45 years of data and generates 1000 different scenarios. The

Pension Funds - Theoretical Scenario
The theoretical scenarios describe the average over 100 different scenarios. It leads
to a good estimation of expected returns, however the volatility becomes negligible,
and thus difficult to measure risk. In section 5.3 a single scenario will be used to test
the risk and volatility of the pension funds.
==PAGE:63==
5.2 Pension Funds - Theoretical Scenario
47
Monthly contributions were discussed in section 1.3. It is assumed that our in-
vestor will be guaranteed to at least be paying rent. So the contributions detailed
there will apply to the scenario of renting. It is assumed that regardless of whether
the investor buys or rents, they will have the capital to fund the down-payment for
the house. So the corresponding down-payment will be added to the initial contri-
bution amount for the renting portfolio. So that the purchase of a house with 10%
down-payment will be mirrored with a pension fund with the first month’s contri-

contribution is added to the pension fund.
For the case of mortgaging, due to the payments being significantly larger, the dif-
ference between the monthly mortgage payment and corresponding rent was deducted
from the wages. This new wage was then used to calculate new pension contributions,
which were taken as 15% of the new wage. Understandably sacrificing pension contri-
bution to account for larger monthly costs could be described as risk averse practice.
So, in the case of renting with no addition of initial deposit (0% rent) will be used to
test for both renting and home equity. When home equity is included this portfolio
will be titled the standard case. Each pension fund is then calculated for the worst,
base, and best case for each housing scenario. The risk profiles will also be included.
Where the bond to stock ratio will alter depending on which framework is used.

explore, especially due to the fact that Danish mortgage lenders are offering negative
interest rates on their loans. Whilst this does not mean that investors will receive,
in pocket, money from their lenders, instead the debt will be reduced at a faster rate
than the interest paid. This scenario shows the true risk of ARM mortgages and how
drastically payments can change. Figure 5.34 shows the monthly mortgage amounts
for the single scenario; at some points the mortgage payments double from one year
to the next.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
House prices will rise with inflation in addition to some premium, in general this
reduces the riskiness of housing as an asset. When considering the loan needed to
afford housing to a purchaser, the asset ends up naturally diversifying ones portfolio.
Although in the worst case, interest rates are low, and thus returns on assets will be
lower, as well as the overall house price. This lower interest rate makes for better
mortgages, as the monthly payments are lower. Although the interest paid on the
principal will be lower and thus home equity will accrue slower.
Monte Carlo simulation was used to generate the aforementioned scenarios, as
detailed in section 4. These scenarios proved useful when analysing the merits and
drawbacks of renting versus buying and the relating pension funds.
In Denmark, monthly rent is considerably cheaper than mortgage payments. This
eliminates the revenue stream of buying to let property, as the homeowner would
end up spending more in mortgage payments than they receive in rent. However,
this amounts to a positive environment for the investor who rents. The price-to-rent
ratio was calculated to be above 21 which indicates an environment where renting is
more beneficial for an investor. Supplemented with the calculated DTI, rent appears
to be a viable option for an investor. Mortgage rates were found to be above the
standard threshold for acceptable DTI values stated by mortgage lenders. Whilst
this study assumes that the mortgage will be accepted regardless of this ratio, it is
still a dangerous position to be in and puts one at risk of defaulting. If this occurred
and the investor defaulted on their mortgage and subsequently lost their housing
equity, then section 5.2 would say that renting will yield higher returns. Especially
when taking into account that the down-payment needed for a home could be invested
elsewhere.
Although, lower rent prices and higher mortgage rates leans in the direction of a
positive renting environment, the total cost tells a different story. Mortgage payments
will only occur for the term of a mortgage, which lasts a maximum of 30 years, after
that the only sure cost, in this study, would be property tax. But one would have
to still be paying rent at least for the next 15 years of the generated life-cycle. This
amounts to, in most cases, renting being more expensive. The difference in total cost
for different mortgage types was explored in figure 5.1.3. Realistically there are more
potential costs related to owning a home. The market value of a home is not always an
obtainable goal for home sales. In this study, no maintenance or remodeling costs were
considered, it is unrealistic that a home that has never had money put into it would
==PAGE:94==

receive market value when it is time to sell. This will be taken into account when
discussing the pension funds pertaining to renting. Time of purchase is also found
to be important for the purchase of home equity. Whilst interest rate environment
has important connotations outside of total cost. Table 5.3, shows that deposit size
might be a stronger determinant. If one could only afford a 5% down-payment on a
mortgage when interest rates are in the favour of housing, then waiting to accrue a
15% down-payment reduces the overall cost of a mortgage in any environment.
Section 5.2 analyses the generated house prices, mortgage payments and rent, in
conjunction with pension funds. A standard equal weight asset allocation strategy
was used in conjunction with 15% of wages being allocated to pension contributions.
In both the worst and base cases the mortgaged properties performed the best, when
home equity was considered. In these cases the home equity was considerable enough
to negate the ignored costs of owning a home outside of mortgage payments and prop-
erty tax. In the best case of returns for the theoretical case, some rent scenarios out
performed all of the mortgage scenarios. The best case also produced an interesting
result that the basic contribution pension fund with high risk strategy out performed
all medium and low risk mortgage structures.
The single scenario highlighted faults in rent price determinations, a way to coun-
teract this would be to use the current price-to-rent ratio to calculate these prices.
However this would make the assumption that this ratio will stay the same for 45
years, which is perhaps as unrealistic as drastic yearly rent price changes. Leveraging
considerations did not result in higher yields for the pension funds with renting. How-
ever NPV is not always the best indication of performance. Various risk measures
were used on the single scenario to test if leveraging was a viable option for renting.
Unfortunately in most cases the leveraged portfolios resulted in lower returns.
In the best cases, with high risk considerations, renting could outperform housing.
It was found that initial deposit in the renting portfolios had more of an affect on
final values than larger down-payments on the pension funds. Whilst securing a home
with a large enough deposit would be important for monthly payments, it does not
have a great effect on pension funds, especially considering that contributions would
be higher for higher down-payments on homes.
6.1
Future Work
Extending this thesis to include more realistic examples and housing decisions would
create a clearer image of how housing decisions would affect the pension funds. Due
to the high DTI ratio for the mortgage payments it is unrealistic that our 25 year
old investor would likely gain access to the mortgage on the average Danish house.
Testing how the equity would be affected by housing purchases made later in the
life-cycle would affect the pension fund would be beneficial. In this study leveraging
is used in conjunction with human capital, exploring ways this leveraging might be
better related to housing could provide more insight.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to connect the pros and cons between renting and buying. Whilst
habitat is a personal choice that has many implications beyond financial gain, home-
equity provided a useful way to boost pension funds. Even out performing the lever-
aged strategy used. This showed that home equity is important to consider in pension
funds as it has a positive affect on returns. Maximizing retirement savings may be
perceived as the goal, saving too much could be problematic. If housing equity wasn’t
considered in the pension fund then our investor may take unnecessary risks or sacri-
fice too much consumption to maximize retirement savings, when the analysis shows
that by contributing less and owning a home will out perform pension funds with no
home equity. This asset shows passive risk management.
Renting came out on top with the best interest rate environments.
A logical
statement to make is that renting can perform, at least on par with home equity
when higher risks are taken. Only under the assumption that the down-payment on a
house is invested elsewhere. Unfortunately the investor that did not choose to invest
their down-payment when renting under-performed in all scenarios.
Whilst home equity performed consistently the best, the DTI of this asset is
hard to ignore.
If our investor defaults and loses their home equity then renting
with deposits would have been a better scenario. Leveraging with pension funds for
renting did not have the desired affect, and resulted in the pension funds having less
value. The interest rate environment had more of an affect for renting, especially for
portfolios with the highest initial contributions.
Under the naive cost assumptions made in the thesis, homes costs less over the
life-cycle. Considering that fixed rate mortgage payments will remain the same for
the duration of its term, one would end up paying less and less of a proportion of their
wage towards their mortgage, when adjusted for inflation. Whereas rent payments
would be roughly consistent percentage-wise.
In the worst and base case interest
rate environments the portfolios with home equity considerations performed best.
However for the most risk tolerant investor, renting performed on par with mortgages
- providing the interest rates were in the 50th - 97.5th percentiles and initial deposit
was sufficient enough. In conclusion, although the housing market would dictate that
there is a preference for renting over purchasing real estate, the renting fell short
in many other ways; including matching returns of their respective pension funds,
diversifying a portfolio, and life-time cost.
