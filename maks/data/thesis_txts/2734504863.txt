Abstract
Thousands of buildings are built every year. As they are getting erected as fast as pos-
sible, structural calculations need to be agile, as changes to the layout often occur due
to architectural decisions, MEP, or other unexpected events. Consequently, force might
need to be redistributed to other parts of the building.
When considering the serviceability limit state (SLS), it is necessary to ensure that cracks
and deformations are within the defined limits. A strut and tie (S.T.) simplification of
the finite element serviceability analysis (FESA) described by Vestergaard is, therefore,
developed to identify areas where strains exceed the limit and stresses, therefore, should
be reduced.
Having determined the problematic area that does not comply with design regulations, it
should be identified where to increase stiffness. However, considering the non-linear be-
haviour of steel and concrete, it can be problematic to identify where to increase stiffness
and remedy the issue. This thesis, therefore, uses consideration from the Maxwell-Betti
theorem to identify where to increase stiffness effectively.
It was found that an S.T. FESA model was a computationally effective way of identi-
fying the problematic area. Using simple expressions to express the properties of a wall
meant that the analysis only took a few seconds. In addition, the S.T. model showed
compliant results when comparing the load-displacement curve of a high beam with an
equivalent analysis performed by Vestergaard.
Having identified the problematic area, it was possible to use the influence factor based
on the Maxwell-Betti theorem to determine where to increase stiffness eﬀiciently. Addi-
tionally, the relation dA = dN/Influence factor could be used to indicate how much the
area should be increased.
From a bigger perspective, this method, therefore, enables a computationally eﬀicient
consideration of the SLS when design changes are made.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Every year, thousands of buildings are commenced [1] for both the private sector and the
industry. One thing they have in common is that both parties want to use the building
as soon as possible.
There are different reasons for this. But for the industry where time pressure is often
highest, it mainly has to do with the fact that the longer it takes to complete the building,
the more money is lost in revenue.
The rush from decision to completion means that construction sometimes gets initiated
before all stakeholders have had time to complete their work. This means that changes
to the building may still occur during the construction phase. These changes sometimes
mean that construction elements such as foundations or walls are subject to more force
than they can obtain. Consequently, the force must be redistributed to other parts of the
building.
When investigating ultimate limit state (ULS) models, eﬀicient finite element methods
(FEM) already exist.
Tools such as finite element limit analysis (FELA) can be used
to determine the minimum amount of material needed for a structure to obtain a certain
load. But it can also be used to determine how much load a structure can take. Therefore,
calling it finite element limit analysis meaning finding the limit.
However, FELA does not consider the cracks and deformation in reinforced concrete (RC)
due to assuming rigid-perfectly plastic material behaviour. When considering the model
in serviceability limit state (SLS), this is a problem due to SLS ensuring that cracks and
deformations are within acceptable limits under the service load.
Instead, finite element serviceability analysis (FESA) can be used. When choosing a bi-
linear material model working under the assumption of linear elastic-plastic behaviour, it
allows strains to occur, and this method is, therefore, suitable for investigating SLS, as
cracks and deformations can be considered.
Engineer in structural mechanics, Ph.D, Daniel Vestergaard has, in his work Design-
Oriented Nonlinear Modeling of Reinforced Concrete Wall Structures for Numerical Limit
State Analysis [2], worked on formulating a FESA framework for modelling RC walls sub-
jected to in-plane loading. This framework is a strong tool, as it can determine the strains
in the element numerically eﬀiciently.
The method considers the bilinear material behaviour of both concrete and reinforcement.
Therefore, this method can be used to imitate RC having a non-linear behaviour with an
elastic and plastic branch.
As has been established, changes to the structure often occur, and the load applied some-
times results in the element’s maximal capacity being exceeded.
In some cases, it is possible with engineering experience to determine where the problem
lies and increase stiffness in that area for remediation.
However, some cases are more complex, and it might not always solve the problem when
adding more stiffness locally.

Instead of relying only on experience, this project will use considerations from the Maxwell-
Betti theorem [3]. The theorem tells what a load in one system gives of deformations in
another system. A development of this connection was made so that it could be used
to determine the influence of a change in one area on another. Thereby systematically
increasing the area in certain areas and reducing the stress in the overutilized areas until
stress and strain levels are within the allowed limits.
When making the Maxwell-Betti analysis, it is necessary to know how much stress and
stiffness each area experiences. Therefore, the FESA framework presented by Vestergaard
is simplified into a bar model, a so-called strut and tie model (S.T.) model. The simplified
S.T. model can be used to estimate the stiffness in the different areas.
Finally, the simplified S.T. FESA model can be used to imitate the non-linear behaviour
of RC and be combined with the Maxwell-Betti theorem to answer the question:
Where is there a problem with overutilized areas in the original case, and where should
more stiffness be introduced to get an acceptable distribution of forces?
1.1
Outline of the thesis
The first step in fulfilling the thesis objective is creating a program that can handle the
principle of minimum complementary energy (MCE) for a bar system.
This step includes setting up a function for applying the principle of MCE to a bar system.
In doing so, it is also necessary to determine the dimensions of the bars when replicating
the properties of a real wall with a S.T. model. Therefore, the thesis will explain how this
simplification from a real wall to a S.T. model is made.
The second step in fulfilling the thesis objective is to create a program that can cal-
culate the influence of a change of dimension in bar j on the normal force in bar i.
Finally, the two programs are combined and applied to an example to show how this
method can be applied to a wall with strains exceeding the ultimate strain limit.

3.2.2
Previous considerations when dimensioning the S.T. bars
Before obtaining the equations summarised in table 3.1, other approaches were initially
used to dimension the S.T. bars.
In the first version, it was assumed that all load applied in pure shear would go into
the diagonal bars and that pure shear would give the same strain in both directions.
Based on the assumption that both diagonal bars would be activated in pure shear, both
vertical and diagonal bars were considered when subjecting the S.T. model to pure tension
and compression.
The dimensions found with these assumptions did not replicate the properties of a real
wall. This was concluded on the basis of the stress-strain curves obtained from the S.T.
model when subjecting the model to pure shear, tension and compression. The test showed
that when applying a load corresponding to what the wall should maximally be able to
obtain, there was more strain in the pure shear and compression case.
Derivation of the formulas and the test performed to conclude this can be found in ap-
pendix D.1.
In the second version of the formulas used to determine the dimension of the bars, it
was still considered that both bars would obtain the load applied in pure shear, but the
strain would vary in x’- and y’-direction.
The reason for this assumption was that stress goes towards areas with the largest stiffness
and that the load applied had the same direction as the diagonal bar.
However, the dimensions found under this assumption showed, that the load applied in
pure shear would not go directly into the diagonal. Instead, there would be tension in all

4.3
General consideration about the program
As have already been discussed, there is a disruption between the results obtained from
the MCE program and the membrane model made by Vestergaard.
However, the goal when using the MCE program is to determine where there is an issue
with overutilization. In this regard, it is a huge advantage that the S.T. MCE program
is incredibly fast. Each iteration with the MCE function only takes a few seconds from
beginning to completion. This is the biggest force with this method.
When knowing from the MCE program where there is a problem with overutilization, the
influence factor from Betti is an easy and effective way of finding where the largest positive
influence of increasing stiffness would be on the overutilized area.
In example 1, two methods were used to increase the areas of bars j. Initially, the di-
mension of bar j was increased until a new bar had the largest positive influence on bar i.
Using this method, the total increase of material was 300 mm2.
In the other method, the relation dA = dN/Influence factor was used to increase the areas
of bar j. With this method, the area was increased with 179 mm2. This method, therefore,
used 40% less material.
The challenge with both methods where that even when dealing with a simple exam-
ple such as the one seen in example 1, multiple interactions were needed to find the area
each bar should be increased with.
However, due to 40% less material being used when the relation dA = dN/Influence factor,
this method is preferred.
Finally, example 2 illustrated that the Betti program also could be performed on a more
complex wall and give an indication of where more stiffness should be introduced.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
Based on this project, it can be concluded that it is possible to set up a simplified S.T.
FESA model that can replicate the properties of a real wall where non-linear behaviour
is taken into consideration for compression and tension. The struts and ties are found on
the basis of the thickness of the wall and the amount of reinforcement that is placed in
the specific wall.
It is found that this way of modelling a wall is very eﬀicient when considering time. When
running the program it is very computational eﬀicient as it only takes a few seconds to
run the MCE function. But also, when calculating the dimensions of the bars, simple
expressions are used to express an RC wall with struts and ties.
This means that this method is a very effective way of getting an initial overview of where
the problem is located and thereby finding a solution to the problem.
It can also be concluded that the influence factor found with the Maxwell-Betti theo-
rem can be used to eﬀiciently determine where an increase in stiffness would have the
largest positive impact on the problematic area. In addition, the influence factor can be
used to determine how much the area, and thereby stiffness, should be increased with.
Lastly, it is worth mentioning that this method is slightly conservative, as the method
outputs a higher deformation than the actual case. Therefore, this method is excellent at
giving an initial qualified guess as to how the stiffness throughout the wall should be. If
the solution is on the edge of being accepted, it would be recommended to input it into a
membrane program.
5.1
Future Works
In this project, simplifications were made in regard to determining the dimensions of the
S.T. bars. In the future, it could be interesting to investigate what effect it would have
on the results if an α factor was applied to the compressive strength, and if more accurate
results could be obtained.
Furthermore, when assessing walls or other structural elements, it is often the case that
multiple bars exceed the ultimate strain simultaneously. Currently, the Betti program
only considers the influence on one bar.
As a future work, it would be interesting to
evolve the Betti program so it could find the overall best influence factor for multiple bars
exceeding the ultimate strain.

For bars 1 and 2, this point is when the stress level reaches fcy in compression. However,
bars 5 and 6 reach this point at half the stress level of bars 1 and 2, which is fcy/2.
For the model in tension, finding the stress level where the bars reach the plastic part is
possible. For bars 1 and 2 this is at fty and in bars 5 and 6 fty/2.
With this knowledge, the ub in the MCE program can be defined and is carried into
the next chapter considering the strength.
