Abstract
This thesis explores the optimization of polarization stabilization and four-wave mixing
(FWM) efficiency in optical communication systems. In the studied highly nonlinear fiber
(HNLF), conversion efficiencies (CE) upwards of -12 dB were realised for a 15 nm signal-pump
wavelength shift bandwidth. In a bidirectional loop experiment, for a similar bandwidth, we
found a -20 dB CE.
Gradient descent and direct descent algorithms are implemented to enhance system stability,
with a focus on overcoming mechanical limitations such as bidirectional repeatability and po-
larization controller saturation. The findings showed a 40% maximal instability during active
polarization control, and highlight the critical role of precise polarization control and robust
error functions in maximizing FWM efficiency and ensuring reliable wavelength conversion.

Introduction
Optical communication stands as one of the most transformative advancements in modern
telecommunications, enabling the transmission of vast amounts of data over considerable
distances with unparalleled speed and minimal loss [1].
The exponential growth of data
demand driven by internet usage, video streaming, and emerging technologies underscores
the critical need for continued innovation in this field.
Central to the efficiency and reliability of optical communication systems is the understand-
ing and management of the state of polarization (SOP) of light within optical fibers. The
SOP at the end of a conventional single-mode fiber is subject to slow but potentially large
changes [2]. Polarization affects not only the transmission quality but also the performance
of various nonlinear optical processes, such as four-wave mixing (FWM), which is pivotal
for wavelength conversion in dense wavelength-division multiplexing systems [3]. To ensure
reliable communication without interruptions the tracking range of the polarization control
system should be endless.
An optical parametric amplifier (OPA) amplifies an optical signal using the nonlinear inter-
action between a pump laser and the signal within a nonlinear medium. It is the mixing of
three waves in a nonlinear crystal, where the interaction of the pump and signal generates an
amplified signal and an idler. This device can be used for signal amplification, wavelength
conversion and generation of new frequencies in optical systems. A fiber optical parametric
amplifier (FOPA) uses an optical fiber as the nonlinear medium to achieve amplification and
wavelength conversion through FWM. This device is used in optical communication systems
for signal amplification and wavelength conversion [4].
This thesis delves into the intricate interplay between polarization stabilization and FWM
processes within highly nonlinear fibers (HNLFs).
Through a combination of theoretical
insights, meticulous experimental setups, and advanced stabilization algorithms, this research
aims to enhance the efficiency and reliability of optical communication systems.
In the optical setup of this thesis, a FOPA configuration is considered. The HNLF medium,
characterized by a third-order nonlinear susceptibility χ(3), is illuminated by two strong
counter-propagating pump waves and by a signal wave. The pump waves are characterized
by having amplitudes that are complex conjugates of one another.
The result is newly
generated light at a frequency governed by the energy conservation relations between the
pump and signal energies and efficiency governed by the quantum mechanically momentum
conservation of the two.

The study is rooted in the foundational principles of electromagnetism, particularly Maxwell’s
equations, which describe the behavior of light as it propagates through different media.
Understanding the SOP of light, represented through Stokes calculus and visualized on the
Poincar´e sphere, is essential for optimizing FWM processes. By manipulating the SOP using
waveplates and precise control mechanisms, we can achieve the desired conditions necessary
for efficient wavelength conversion.
A significant portion of this thesis focuses on the development and implementation of stabi-
lization algorithms designed to maintain optimal polarization states. The standard approach
such an optimization problem is to use gradient-descent-like algorithms [5].

2
Light theory
In this chapter, the essential theory necessary for designing, implementing, and characterizing
an experimental setup for wavelength conversion will be explained. The primary approach
involves classical electromagnetic theory, with propagation described through Maxwell’s equa-
tions. However, some quantum-mechanical descriptions are incorporated to quantify energy
considerations in optical nonlinear processes. The main source of reference throughout this
section is Nonlinear Optics; Principles and Applications by Rottwitt and Tidemand [6].
2.1. Linear optics
Light can be described as electromagnetic radiation consisting of both an electric and a
magnetic field components that self-propagate in space. Light exhibits four basic properties:
frequency, amplitude, phase, and state of polarization. The frequency of the light is how
often the light oscillates per time unit. The amplitude is the magnitude of the oscillations of
the electric and magnetic fields. The phase is a comparative figure of either the temporal or
spatial delay of a harmonic oscillation or a wave, compared to some reference. The phase can,
similarly to an angle, be given in units of degrees or radians. The state of polarization (SOP)
is a property of transverse waves which yield information about the geometrical orientation of
the oscillations. In transverse waves, the direction of the oscillations are perpendicular to the
direction of wave propagation. In the perspective of light, which consists of coupled oscillating
electric and magnetic fields that are always perpendicular to each other, by convention, the
polarization of the electromagnetic waves refers to the direction of the electric field. This
is because the size of the electric field is often a lot larger than the size of the magnetic
field. In linear polarization, the fields oscillate in a single direction. In circular or elliptical
polarization, the fields rotate at a constant rate in a plane as the wave travels, either in a
right-hand or left-hand direction.
2.1.1. Maxwell’s equations
The classical representation of light uses boundary conditions on Maxwell’s equations to
explain light-matter interactions. Maxwell’s equations describe how electric and magnetic
fields interact and propagate.
We use Maxwell’s equations in differential form, which in
general exist in two formulations; the microscopic and the macroscopic form [6, p. 4-7].

When an electric field is applied to a dielectric material, its molecules react by forming
microscopic electric dipoles. This occurs as the atomic nuclei shift slightly in the direction
of the field, while the electrons move a small distance in the opposite direction, and this
separation of charges is the dipole. The dipoles generate a macroscopic bound charge density
within the material, even though all the charges remain bound to individual molecules and
the total charge of the material remains zero. When every molecule in the material responds
uniformly, these slight charge shifts combine to form a layer of positive bound charge on one
side and a layer of negative charge on the opposite side. This bound charge is described in
terms of polarization P within the material. If P is uniform, a macroscopic charge separation
occurs only at the surfaces where P enters and exits the material, because all neighbouring
internal charges average out to zero.
Similarly, the atoms exhibit magnetic moments that are linked to the angular momentum
of their electrons. This suggests the existence of many internal microscopic current loops.
However, outside the material, a collection of such microscopic current loops is no different
from a macroscopic current circulating around the material’s surface. This is true despite
the face that no individual current flows a large distance. The bound current density may
be described using the magnetization M [6]. These bound charges and bound currents can
be represented macroscopically in terms of P and M, which average charges and currents
over a sufficiently large scale to smooth out the individual atoms, yet are small enough to
reflect variations within the material. Consequently, Maxwell’s macroscopic equations ignore
many fine-scale details that are insignificant for understanding matters on a larger scale, by
calculating fields that are averaged over appropriately sized volumes.

2.1.3. Linear effects in optical fibers
The optical fiber is a dielectric waveguide. The simplest form of an optical fiber is a step-
index fiber, which consists of two different glasses in a cylindrical form. The inner part (core)
is doped with a material different from the outer part (cladding) to increase the refractive
index n of the core. The cladding can similarly be doped to have a smaller refractive index
than the core, i.e. ncore > nclad. Optical fibers are often categorized in either single-mode
fibers (SMFs) or multi-mode fibers (MMFs). The SMF supports only the propagation of the
fundamental mode in the fiber, whereas MMF supports the propagation of several modes.
The physical phenomenon which governs the propagation of light in the optical fiber is the
principle of total internal reflection. In the step-index SMF, the higher refractive index of
the core ncore guides the light in the core [11].
The linear properties of an optical fiber are the phenomena that are independent of the
propagating light intensity. Fiber loss, also known as fiber attenuation, is one of the linear
properties. The power loss through a fiber is governed by:
dP(z)
dz
= −α · P(z),
(2.1.19)
where P is the average optical power, z is the propagation distance, and α is the attenuation

Regarding its axes, the Poincar´e sphere is the parametrisation of the last three Stokes’ pa-
rameters in spherical coordinates.
The surface of the sphere represents all possible fully
polarized states. Around the equator of the Poincar´e sphere, the x- and y-components are in
phase, and thus all the polarization states are linearly polarized. At the poles of the sphere,
the states are circular polarization states, which corresponds to the previously mentioned
special case when the components are of equal magnitude but π/2 out of phase.

states and have x- and y components that are out of phase.
Every point on the north-

resents left handed light. Points on opposite sides of the sphere represent orthogonal po-
larization states.

[S0, S1, S2, S3]T = [1, 1, 0, 0]T lies opposite of the vertical polarization state with Stokes
vector S = [1, −1, 0, 0]T because the states are orthogonal, and hence their second Stokes’
parameters have opposite signs.
Unpolarized light is not represented as a single point on the Poincar´e sphere shell. Instead, it
can be thought of as having an equal probability of being in any polarization state. It is thus
represented as the origin of the sphere, indicating no preferred polarization state. Partially
polarized light is represented by points inside the Poincar´e sphere, but not at the center.
The degree of polarization determines how close the point is to the surface. The closer the
point is to the surface, the higher the DOP. Unpolarized light, such as natural light from the
Sun, is characterized by having a random, time-varying polarization caused by uncorrelated
emission.

2.2.3. Waveplates
Birefringence is an optical characteristic of a material where an incoming light wave divides
into two rays, each moving at a different velocity.
This effect arises due to the material
having varying refractive indices based on the light’s polarization and direction of travel. A
waveplate is a linear retarder that introduces a relative phase shift δ between the electric field
components due to birefringence. The phase shifter always has two specified perpendicular
axes, the fast and the slow. If its fast axis is in the x-direction, it advances the electrical field
component Ex by a fixed amount, leaving Ey behind [12, Chap. 8].
In optics, we commonly work with two types of waveplates. The first is the quarter-wave plate
(QWP), which induces a 90◦phase shift and converts between different elliptical polarizations.
The second is the half-wave plate (HWP), which introduces a phase shift of 180◦and changes
the direction of linearly polarized light.

2.2.4. Endless polarization tracking
Endless polarization tracking refers to the ability to continuously and accurately follow the
SOP of a light beam without any limit on the range of polarization states that can be tracked.
Linear retarders, such as waveplates, can be used to achieve this by systematically adjusting
the phase shift between the orthogonal components of the light.
The HWP effectively rotates the polarization direction of linearly polarized light while the
QWP can convert linearly polarized light to circularly polarized light and vice versa. By ro-
tating these waveplates, and thus altering the introduced phase retardation from Eq. (2.2.11),
any desired polarization transformation can be achieved. Using a configuration of a QWP,
followed by a HWP, followed by a QWP, it is possible to convert between any arbitrary
SOP because this configuration decouples the two quarter-wave plates. In the literature,
this configuration goes by the name quarter-half-quarter (QHQ). The suggested idea to track
polarization is thus to use a combination of waveplates to control and adjust the SOP.
To achieve endless tracking, the optical system must dynamically adjust the waveplates’
angles in response to changes in the SOP of the incoming light. This requires a live feedback
mechanism to continuously monitor the SOP and make real-time adjustments.
To make a feedback system, a polarization analyzer of sorts is required to measure the SOP
of the light. This device is designed to provide real-time information about the polarization
state. Then an algorithm processes the feedback from the polarization analyzer and deter-
mines the necessary adjustments to the waveplates. The algorithm must account for the full
Poincar´e sphere, allowing it to track any polarization state seamlessly.

Often, optical waveplates are thought of as free-space rotatable discs of some birefringent
material, where the introduced phase retardation experienced by light propagating through
the material depends on the rotation angle of the disc. Another type of waveplates popularly
used in optical communication systems are the optical fiber-based rotatable waveplates. They
eliminate the requirement of converting fiber-based signals to free-space beam propagation

waveplate can be integrated directly into a fiber optic system.
By systematically adjusting the fiber waveplates based on real-time feedback, endless polar-

tion state of light across the entire Poincar´e sphere.
2.3. Nonlinear optics
Nonlinear optics is a branch of optics that studies how the propagation of electromagnetic
fields interact with materials in ways that depend on the field itself. Thus, nonlinear optics
deals with light-matter interactions. Such an interaction example could be the intensity of
the field, affecting how the field propagates. This contrasts with linear optics, where the
properties of light propagation, such as reflection, refraction and absorption, do not depend
on the light intensity.
Nonlinear optics includes intensity dependent refractive index, intensity dependent absorp-
tion, electro- and magneto-optical effects, scattering phenomena, and mutual interaction
among different frequencies in the propagating electromagnetic field. For this project, we
will mainly consider the latter.
When an intense light field interacts with a material, it induces an electric polarization within
the material. This dipole is what is referred to as the induced polarization, and was briefly
discussed in section 2.1.1. In linear optics, this polarization is directly proportional to the
electric field of the light. However, in nonlinear optics, the polarization becomes a nonlinear
function of the electric field:
P = ε0(χ(1)E + χ(2)EE + χ(3)EEE + ...).
(2.3.1)
Here, P is the induced polarization vector, ε0 is the vacuum permittivity, and E describes
the amplitude of the propagating electric fields. χ(1) is the linear susceptibility, while χ(2)
and χ(3) are the second- and third-order nonlinear susceptibilities, respectively. Note how the
induced polarization scales linearly with the vacuum permittivity and the linear susceptibility
times the electric field, quadratically with the second-order susceptibility, and cubically with
the third-order susceptibility.
Thus, this Eq.
(2.3.1) shows that for small electric fields
propagating in materials, the linear susceptibility will dominate. However, as the electric

field intensity increases sufficiently, the effects of higher-order susceptibilities will become
significant.
The second order nonlinear optical effects require materials that lack inversion symmetry,
i.e. non-centrosymmetric materials. However, optical fibers are made of silica glass, which
is an amorphous material that is an isotropic material, i.e. it possesses inversion symmetry.
Therefore, we do not observe any second order effects in optical fibers. In fact, we do not
observe any even-order nonlinear optical effects in optical fibers [6]. We are therefore left
with first and third order nonlinearities in optical fibers.
If we assume the nonlinear response component from Eq. (2.1.23) to be instantaneous such

In nonlinear optics, the light state of polarization is important because different components

is described through the susceptibility tensors.
2.3.1. Time-domain response for centrosymmetric materials
In many nonlinear effects all frequencies of the applied electric field as well as of the induced
polarization are optical, i.e. well above any of the frequencies of the nuclear response. The
nuclear response is the interaction between the electric field and the nucleus, whereas the
electronics response is the interaction between the electric field and the electrons.
Cubic and isotropic materials are centrosymmetric and are characterized by having linear
optical properties that are directional-invariant. Such materials include glasses and fluids.
In cubic and isotropic materials the electronic part of the induced polarization may for all
practical purposes be considered instantaneous since it responds to field changes within a few
electronics cycles (10−16). Contrary to the electronic susceptibility, the nuclear susceptibility
has a non-instantaneous time dependence. This is because it involves the movement and
reorientation of the atomic nuclei and the associated changes in molecular structure. Nuclei
are much heavier than electrons, and thus require more time to respond to the applied
field. The result is that the nuclear susceptibility is orders of magnitude slower, often in the
picosecond to nanosecond range [6, Chap. 2, 4].

In some communication systems, FWM can be an unwanted side effect of optical frequency

dispersion are well suppressed [7].
2.3.3. Brillouin scattering
Brillouin scattering is a nonlinear optical process characterized by a slight frequency shift
from the original pump frequency. Stimulated Brillouin scattering (SBS) can occur when
optical power is sufficiently concentrated within a material.
In many applications, Brillouin scattering imposes a power limitation. For instance, in high-
power fiber lasers, Brillouin scattering hinders the achievement of high power levels with a
narrow laser line-width. Similarly, in fiber optical parametric amplifiers (FOPA), Brillouin
scattering, especially SBS, prevents the use of high pump power with narrow line-width
alongside a signal [6]. This issue is particularly challenging in optical communication systems.
Consequently, there has been significant effort to develop methods to reduce SBS and miti-
gate its impact. These methods include fiber design strategies, such as doping with suitable
materials, varying parameters along the fiber length (e.g., stress or temperature), and imple-
menting phase modulation or dithering of the optical signal.
When calculating the SBS threshold power, it scales with the reciprocal of the overlap between
the electric and acoustic fields.
Therefore, designing fibers that guide both acoustic and
optical fields, but minimize their overlap, is an effective approach [4].

3.3. The art of finding global minimum
In subjects of mathematical analysis, such as the fields of signal processing and machine
learning, local and global extrema are two important concepts related to optimization. Seek-
ing extrema values refer to optimization problems that require maximization or minimization
of the objective function. Maximization problems, however, can be represented equivalently
in the minimization form, because maximizing f is equivalent to minimizing −f. Therefore,
we can continue our attention to minimization problems without loss of generality.
3.3.1. Local and global extrema
When discussing the optimization of a loss functions f, we distinguish between a local and
the global minimum. The global minimum is defined as the point xm that yields the lowest
loss for every x in the domain, i.e. [23, Chap. 6]
f(xm) ≤f(x) ∀x ∈Domain of f.
(3.3.1)
A local minimum, on the other hand, is defined as a point xm if f(xm) is less than or equal
to the loss function f(x) for all x within the neighborhood of xm [25].
Local extrema can be calculated analytically. If the derivative f′(xm) = 0, the point xm is
either a local minimum or maximum. To assess whether xm corresponds to a local minimum
or maximum, we could either compute the sign of the double derivative f′′(xm) or we could
investigate the sign of f′(x) on the neighboring points of xm. However, the second derivative
test does not always work. In the case where f′′(xm) = 0, the test is inconclusive. Thus it
is more bulletproof to investigate the sign of the single derivative of the neighboring points
when determining local minima analytically.

3.3.2. Gradient descent
The standard approach to solving an optimization problem for finding extremas is to use
gradient descent algorithms [5, 23]. The gradient descent algorithm is a 3-step approach for
converging towards local extrema. The first step consists of measuring the loss function at
two points adjacent to the initial point. The second step is computing a gradient based on
these neighbouring measurement, which yields in which direction the loss function increases.
If the algorithm is used for finding a minimum, the gradient thus yields the desired direc-
tion for minimizing the loss function. Once the direction of adjustments to be performed is
known, the third step can begin. This includes to iteratively move in the opposite direction
from the positive gradient and towards the minimal value of the loss function. Additionally,
the computation of the gradient can be used to determine an adaptive step size to be used in
the iterative convergence process. This can allow the system to catch big differences faster
and navigate towards smaller differences with a higher sensitivity. For a complete schematic

3.4. The art of stabilization
Stabilizing a regulation system involves making the system steady, consistent, and resistant
to disturbances. In the context of this thesis, stabilization is defined as the ability to achieve
and maintain a local minimum of a cost function.
3.4.1. Cost functions
To evaluate the stability of a system, you need some sort of error function that enables you
to quantify degradation in signal quality. This error function should be as instantaneous as
possible with regards to system perturbations, such that any changes can be observed when
they occur. Additionally, the ideal error function should be consistent, reliable and exhibit
low noise.
Multiple examples of loss functions exhibiting these characteristics have been proposed during
this project. They include: sum of oscilloscope spectral components, squared sum of oscillo-
scope spectral components, sum of time trace measurement (both mean value and standard
deviation of traces), and squared sum of time trace measurements. The summed components
are computed as L1 norms, while the squared sums are part of the L2 norm perspective.
More details can be found in later chapters.
Determining local minima analytically is not very computationally difficult. However, this re-
quires a mathematical function or expression that is at least both differentiable and piece-wise
continuous, where the function is continuous within intervals but may have discontinuities
at some points. Achieving such a function in practice can be challenging when measuring
real-time data on optical systems that inherently drift over time, causing the expression to
change. Therefore, it is more feasible to perform optimization numerically. The underlying
challenges of finding extrema for optimization algorithms remain the same.
The proposed algorithm for finding and maintaining a global minimum involves performing
a brute-force sweep of (some of) all possible states of polarization, returning to the smallest
point on the loss function, and using this state as an initial condition. The optical system
may drift due to thermal fluctuations caused by factors such as direct sunlight, changes
to indoor climate control, or the heat generation of nearby electrical devices. The system
may also drift due to phonon vibrations caused by various factors, such as opening and
closing nearby doors. For this reason, a stabilization algorithm is then sequentially run. This
==PAGE:38==
Regulation technique
Summary
Page 30 of 84
algorithm incrementally adjusts parameters to constantly ensure the local minimum even
during polarization drifts or changes in the loss function.
To address these practical challenges, the stabilization process involves the following steps
• Initial Sweep: Perform a brute-force sweep of possible states of polarization to find
the best guess global minimum of the loss function and set this state as the initial
condition.
• Continuous Adjustment: Implement a stabilization algorithm that incrementally
adjusts the parameters to maintain the local minimum, compensating for drifts due to
thermal fluctuations or phonon vibrations.
• Rapid Measurement: Utilize rapid measurements on neighboring points of the cost
function to calculate fitting step sizes and determine the direction of optimization,
avoiding the need for a complete mathematical model of the cost function.
• Adaptive Feedback: Continuously monitor and adapt to changes in the cost function,
ensuring the system remains in an optimal state despite external disturbances.
By employing these strategies, the stabilization algorithm ensures that the regulation system
remains robust and efficient, maintaining optimal performance even under varying conditions.
3.4.2. Erroneous measurements
If the measurable loss function fluctuates due to external factors or noise, small differences
will continually trigger the adjustment loop. Thus, stabilization is only achieved in practice
when the measured value including noise does not exceed the outer boundaries of the power
measurement, in which case a pause is inserted. We therefore include an error margin of
some sort on all the measurable parameters. This could for example be defined as a per-
centage deviation from previous measured value or a moving average over multiple previous
measurements to filter noise. In the case of a stabilization system where the loss function is
within some satisfying boundary, the regulation should be paused to refrain from perturbing
the system.

Gradient descent is discussed for finding global or local minima, and

4.1.3. Polarization diversity loop
From the isolator, the collective signal and pump enters a polarization diversity loop through
the transmittance port T on a polarization beam splitter (PBS). The PBS exploits the dif-
ference in the way that orthogonal linear polarizations of light interact with a specific opti-
cal interface or material to separate these components. This means that light transmitted
through PBS port 2 has an orthogonal polarization to light transmitted through port 1. For
example, if the input polarization on the PBS is collected in horizontal state, the output will
be collected on the same output port in a horizontal state. To separate the input polarization
in an equally distributed configuration with regards to the PBS output ports 1 and 2, the
input SOP could be prepared in the 45◦diagonal SOP. Because this SOP contains an equal
amount of horizontal and vertical components, the output would be a 50/50 configuration
of these states. It of course works both ways, and can thus be used to separate or combine
light based on its polarization state. In this experimental setup, the PBS works as both a
polarization separator and a polarization combiner.
Exiting port 1 on the PBS, the collection of pump and signal is now in a well-defined linear
SOP. The light propagates through a polarization maintaining fiber (PMF) and enters an

pump EPC which was labeled 444. The purpose of this polarization controller is to modify
the polarization to control whether light exits through port T or port R of the PBS. This
can theoretically be achieved using a HWP, as it induces the same retardance for both of

the orthogonal polarization states, effectively turning horizontal light vertical, while turning

Moving from right to left through the EPC, the light now enters a highly nonlinear fiber
(HNLF) with high χ(3)-effects. This is the fiber in which the third-order nonlinear DFWM
process occurs. The high intensity pump induces a polarization in the fiber which mixes with

the pump and signal as explained in Chapter 2. Further characterization of the HNLF will

Exiting the HNLF on the left-hand side, the co-propagating pump, signal and idler now
enters a 20 dB coupler. This coupler is inserted with the purpose of investigating the power
relations inside the loop at all time. It does so by transmitting power through port A to
power meter PM1 and power through port 2 to PM2. The coupler is oriented such that the
low-loss connection 1-B is within the loop, as this configuration preserves pump power inside
the loop. Consequently, the powers extracted on PM1 and PM2 are the attenuated powers,
meaning they each measure roughly 20 dB less than the actual powers inside the loop.
At this point the light components that traveled counter-clockwise through the loop now
is propagating from coupler port B to PBS port 2 through a PMF. Considering the PBS,
horizontally polarized light entering through port T, exits through port 1, and re-enters
through port 2 to exit via port T if unchanged, or via port R if altered by the EPC. The loop
EPC modifies the SOP such that horizontally polarized light is transformed into vertically
polarized light (or vice versa) to control the PBS exit port.
Taking a temporary step back, the BPS serves to make the inherently polarization dependent
four-wave mixing in the HNLF polarization independent, because the intensity is equally split
in both directions. When considering the signal component, this is ideally in an unpolarized
state with DOP = 0. It will therefore exit the PBS in a 50/50 configuration by default.
The purpose of the pump EPC is thus to turn the pump polarization in such a way that it
similarly will be transmitted in a 50/50 configuration through the PBS. It should be noted
that the 50/50 configuration referred to is not the actual output power configuration on port
1 and 2 on the PBS, but rather the input power of the HNLF in each direction should be
equally split. Practically this means that the power measured on the two power meters PM1
and PM2 should not yield the same measurement, but the loop loss-compensated powers
should be equal in a 50/50 configuration.

4.4.1. Electrical polarization controllers
The motorized polarization controllers used in this project are two MPC320 controllers from
Thorlabs. These motorized PCs utilize stress-induced birefringence, to alter the polarization
state of light traveling through a SMF. The insertion loss of these fibers was found to be
about 1 dB. However, depending on the orientation of the three paddles, variations in the
range 0.9 dB - 1.2 dB loss were observed for both EPCs.
The amount of birefringence induced in the fiber is a function of the fiber cladding diameter,
the spool diameter, the number of fiber loops per spool, and the wavelength of the light.
According to Thorlabs, the fast axis of the fiber is in the plane of the spool. Consequently,
rotating the paddles adjusts the orientation of this axis with respect to the transmitted
polarization vector.
Given the wavelength dependency on retardance, the number of fiber loops on the polarization
controller must match what is used in the project. Referring to the MPC320 data sheet, we
see on Figure 4.9 the relation between retardance and wavelength. To obtain a QWP with
retardance of δ = π/2 at λ = 1550 nm we can suffice with a single fiber loop. To obtain a HWP
with retardance of δ = π at the same wavelength, the required number of fiber loops are 2.
Therefore, our QHQ device becomes a 1-2-1 loop configuration. However, it should be noted
that we use multiple different wavelengths in the neighborhood of 1550 nm due to the different
wavelengths of the signal and pump laser and due to the wavelength conversions covered in
Subsection 2.3.2. Because the retarders only become exact waveplates at specific wavelengths,
there will be a slight absolute retardance error on each waveplate. In the data sheet for
the Thorlabs MPC320 polarization controllers, bidirectional repeatability is highlighted as
a crucial parameter. This term refers to the ability of a mechanical system to consistently
return to a specific position setting from different directions, indicating a form of mechanical
hysteresis. This value measures the precision of the system and is critical in stabilization
setups, as it shows how accurately the system can position itself after reversing direction.
For the Thorlabs MPC320, the bidirectional repeatability is specified as 2◦. Practically, this

measurable effect on the system.
In practical terms, this hysteresis means that small changes, especially when reversing direc-

overcome this initial lag before showing any response, which is critical for achieving precise
and reliable control in optical setups. By understanding and accounting for these parame-

accurate and consistent operation.

4.4.2. Polarization scrambler
The polarization scrambler used in the optical setup of 4.1 is the FIBERPRO PS-155-A
All Fiber Optical Polarization Scrambler [27]. According to the data sheet, the scrambler
operates with a DOP of less than 5%. It has a center operating wavelength of 1550 nm and
an operational wavelength range of 40 nm. The modulation frequencies, as specified in the
user manual, range from 600 kHz to 1.2 MHz.
Polarization scrambling can be understood as polarization modulation, where the SOP of light
is modulated by varying the birefringence of the medium through which the light passes.
This scrambler uses a double-stage birefringence modulator to achieve input polarization-
independent operation. The modulation of birefringence randomizes the SOP of the input
light, effectively lowering the DOP in a time-averaged sense. It does not generate a truly
random trajectory, but a predetermined trajectory based on input SOP. The goal is to achieve
conditions where the modulation amplitudes cause the Stokes parameters S1, S2, and S3 to
average to zero, thus achieving DOP = 0. Note that this is not equivalent of causing S0 = 0.
The birefringence modulator in the PS-155-A consists of a SMF and an acousto-optic trans-
ducer driven by a sinusoidal RF signal within 700 kHz - 1 MHz. This setup ensures effective
polarization scrambling, where the RF signals cause the birefringence modulators to oscil-
late, thereby modulating the phase difference between the orthogonal components of the
light. In the context of the polarization scramblers, the signals are used to drive the bire-
fringence modulators, causing them to oscillate and thereby modulate the phase difference
between orthogonal components of the light passing through, leading to the desired polariza-
tion scrambling effect.

Quantization noise arises during the analog-to-digital
conversion process performed by the OSC. Quantization noise is a type of error that occurs
because digital systems can only represent a finite number of levels. When an analog voltage
is converted to a digital signal, the continuous range of the analog signal must be mapped to
discrete levels. This mapping process involves rounding the actual analog value to the nearest
available digital value, introducing a small error known as quantization error. Quantization
noise is an inherent part of digital signal processing that arises from the finite resolution of
ADCs, and will likely be present in all error function measurements, to some degree.

4.5. Summary
This optical setup, with its polarization diversity loop, ensures that the inherently polarization-
dependent four-wave mixing in the HNLF becomes polarization-independent by splitting the
intensity equally in both directions. In practical terms, the polarization scrambler ensures
that the SOP of the light is randomized, achieving a low DOP. The arrangement of control
mechanisms provide a robust framework for managing different polarization states.
It is possible that the observed bandwidth could be further improved by investigating the
spectrum for pump wavelengths closer to and just above the ZDW of the HNLF to ensure
better phase match. However, due to device limitations, this was not tested.

This chapter is dedicated to the documentation of the scientific method employed during the
stabilization algorithm testing and improvement phase. As such, this chapter will include a
detailed analysis supported by an increased amount of graphs displaying measured stability,
and corresponding loop power measurements and electrical polarization controller paddle
configurations.
5.1. Active stabilization system
In this optical experiment, the HNLF is placed inside an open loop. The reason for the
introduction of a loop-scheme is to make the FWM process polarization dependent. This
is accomplished by using a PBS to separate the incoming pump and signal light into two
orthogonal polarizations. The light waves in these two SOPs will therefore counter-propagate
inside the loop. The 20 dB coupler in the loop is introduced to allow for direct in-loop power
measurements in both propagation directions.
5.1.1. Time evolution of the error function
The first proposed error function is the time evolution of the Fourier spectrum of the OSC
time trace from Figure 4.12. Specifically, we consider the L1 norm of all the components of
the spectral domain. This spectral sum will naturally change over time as the optical system
experiences environmental disturbances.
The way the stabilization algorithms work is by continuously converging towards EPC con-
figurations resulting in the minimal measured value of this error function. The purpose is to
counter any polarization drift. The method used to visualize this operation is to display the
time evolution the error function together with the active EPC paddle positions.

calibration can be seen on Figure 5.1. To achieve a calibrated state, we need to sweep the

5.2. Gradient descent approach
Finishing the calibration system, the optical setup now becomes a complex, polarization state-
dependent system with real-time regulation based on sensor probing. Multiple stabilization
algorithms were tested during the experimental work of this thesis. Generally, they can be
divided in gradient decent approach and the direct decent approach. Both script types are
written in Python and involve concurrent operations in the form of multi-threading data
handling and real-time animation updates.
The goal of both algorithms is to continuously minimize the moving average of the summed
oscilloscope frequencies by adaptively adjusting the positions of the polarization controller’s
paddles. The values it tries to converge towards are the positions of the paddles that result in
the lowest possible frequency measurements, indicating a stable and optimized system state.
This is performed either based on the sums of such frequencies or on the sums of the squares
of these frequencies, as was referred to as the L1 and L2 norms, respectively.
The gradient descent algorithm needs to calculate a gradient before it can determine a desired
direction of adjustment. This practically means that the system needs to be perturbed in a
controllable manner in both directions. Such a measurement is performed on the Figure 5.3.
As seen on Figure 5.3, a gradient descent algorithm is run to stabilize the system post an
initial calibration. In case of polarization drift, regular updates to the gradients and step sizes
are required. The gradient perturbations are performed every second hour, as is evident on
the bottom figure that displays absolute paddle positions. This is visible as big perturbations
of the loss function and in the loop power in the shape of sudden spikes. This is an example
measurement of what happens if the perturbations are very large, in this case a result of
rotating the pump EPC paddles 10◦in each direction to measure the desired direction.
This perturbation shift clearly overshot the required perturbation size required to measure

5.3. Direct descent approach
The previous Section dealt with stabilization systems using a gradient decent algorithm.
The measurements in this section are performed using a new type of algorithm. Generally,
it is very similar to the gradient descent algorithm, but it is fundamentally simpler. Instead
of probing neighboring position configurations and determining a gradient, it immediately
picks an adjustment direction. The EPC moves in increments in the given direction while
measuring the error function, and if the moving average of subsequent measurements yield
increased loss function, it reverses direction after some number of iterations. It is therefore
an arguably less sophisticated algorithm because it is more trial and error-based.
For this algorithm, we introduce a tolerance range to avoid the risk of over-correcting or
inducing oscillations in the systems. This range aims to prevent aggressive or too frequent
adjustments, which can lead to large variations in the error function and unstable power
measurements, as observed in previous measurements of the gradient descent algorithm.
The tolerance ensures that the algorithm pauses when neighboring measurements yield error
function values as low as the current state, thereby avoiding unnecessary perturbations that
could occur in non-strict local minima, depicted as x3 in Figure 3.2, where neighboring points
yield equivalent loss function values. Additionally, the algorithm utilizes a moving average
to determine the current power level, smoothing out abrupt fluctuations in readings. This
approach provides a stable and reliable metric for decision-making.
In contrast to gradient descent, the direct descent algorithm incorporates a built-in wait
command upon achieving stability. It sets the initial error function as the optimization goal,
derived from the calibration, to prevent continuous operation near the goal state. This pause
prevents unnecessary instability, contrasting with the continuous perturbations observed in
the gradient descent algorithm.

the initial calibration whenever any paddle saturates. This idea is more robust that the hard
reset of a single paddle, because it eliminates the risk of entering a situation where the paddle
position is repeatedly resetting. However, both of these suggestions are inherently invasive
procedures that lead to system down-time. Other suggestions are to implement additional
phase retarders that can counter-retard this un-winding process of the paddle, such that
during un-winding, the polarization stays constant and the system is unperturbed. However,
implementing additional phase retarders inevitably leads to the delay of the problem instead
of fixing it.

5.3.4. Time trace measurement
Given that we earlier saw significant difference in signal quality of the different error functions,
multiple other methods of assessing idler stability were speculated on. Two such approaches
considers the time trace directly. The first one consists of stabilizing the mean time trace
value, as this is easily measurable. However, minimization of such function would inevitably
put the system in a state where as little as possible idler power exited the loop, as it would
basically minimize the DC term of spectral view. Instead, the algorithm could be tweaked
to search a constant mean value. This would in turn use the power dependent measurements
on the PD as an error function.
Though this was never realized in practice, a similar time-based approach was speculated,
namely the minimization of the standard deviation (SD) of the time trace from the OSC.
The idea is that a small standard of the time trace could lead to a more optimized state with
less intensity perturbation on the OSC, as was also shown on the optimized time trace from
Chapter 4. In addition, this approach was more feasible to implement in the existing Python
script. The results of such a measurement can be seen on Figure 4.12.
The algorithm on Figure 5.14 performs adjustments on the first QWP and the HWP only,
and is therefore directly comparable to Figure 5.12. The measured loop power through this
approach is generally lower than previous approaches. The standard deviation of the time
trace looks to be stable at first glance, but when considering the paddle positions, it appears
that not much drift has been compensated for. This may seem odd, because the loop power
difference decreases steadily over time. The reason for this is that the initial calibration of
the the time trace SD minimization unable to find reliable local or global minima. This
is illustrated by the fact that the minimal SD value can decrease below the initial value.
Intuitively, this makes sense in the case when the mean power entering the DC-coupled PD
also decreases. This makes the SD of the time trace a bad loss function.

5.5. Summary
The measurements in this Chapter showed a strong and consistent calibration method for
creating a reliable error function minimum to be used as initial comparison value. We saw
clear increased performance when regulating both a QWP and a HWP compared to the
case of only adjusting a QWP. The measurement of the QHQ configuration was generally
similar to the quarter-half waveplate configuration, and did yield an improved stability, but

the optical system with the loop power as feedback mechanism may not be possible. However,
further analysis is required to conclude this hypothesis.

Discussion & Outlook
In this chapter, we discuss the experimental results presented in Chapter 5 and the method-
ologies described in Chapter 4.
This discussion aims to highlight key findings, evaluate
the performance of the stabilization algorithms, and propose future directions for improving
polarization stabilization in optical systems.
The initialization algorithm consistently reproduced an optimized polarization state for the
HNLF, establishing a reliable starting point for further stabilization. This initial calibration
process, involving systematic sweeps of the QWP and HWP of both pump EPC and loop
EPC, ensured that the loop output power was maximized before minimizing the polarization
dependency error function. Regular EPC adjustments are crucial to maintaining accuracy,
especially when the system is subject to environmental changes such as temperature fluctu-
ations, dynamic air flow changes, or vibrations.
While effective in theory, the gradient descent algorithm faced practical challenges due to
the bidirectional repeatability of the EPC paddles. Adjusting the perturbation step size to
match this repeatability of 2◦was crucial for effective stabilization. Once this correction was
implemented, the systems proved relatively stable. However, the gradient descent approach
still showed degradation of the loss function over time, indicating that further improvement
is needed in this department.
The direct descent algorithm provided a simpler yet effective alternative to gradient de-
scent.
By immediately selecting an adjustment direction and moving in increments, this
approach avoided the complexities of gradient perturbations and calculations, and the im-
pact of bidirectional repeatability was reduced. Introducing a tolerance range helped prevent
over-correction and oscillations, enhancing system stability. However, the moving average
used for decision-making slowed down the system, as it required recurring increases in the er-
ror function before reversing direction. This resulted in increased overshooting of the desired
minimal error function values, and noisier idler amplitudes.
The primary weakness of the stabilization algorithm emerged when the EPC paddles hit their
saturation limits. The current system lacks a mechanism for automatic resetting without
altering the polarization state, which could disrupt the entire optical setup. Implementing
additional EPCs or other phase retarders could potentially allow for non-invasive unwinding,
thereby achieving continuous polarization tracking.
The observed drift in optimized states throughout the measurements suggests that the chosen
loss function might not be optimal. This drift could be linked to the polarization scrambler,
==PAGE:84==
Discussion & Outlook
Page 76 of 84
optimized for the 1530 nm - 1570 nm range, while the signal laser was tuned to 1570.5
nm. This discrepancy might prevent true wavelength independence, thus affecting the error
function. Future studies could involve swapping the idler and signal frequencies and adjusting
the following filters accordingly to investigate this hypothesis further.
Stabilizing with a single QWP mitigated polarization drift to some extent but was limited in
its effectiveness. Introducing both a QWP and a HWP improved stability by allowing more
flexible adjustments across the Poincar´e sphere.
Utilizing the entire QHQ configuration
showed only minor improvement, and the underlying inconsistent error function problem
persisted.
Exploring the standard deviation of the OSC time trace as an error function
aimed to minimize intensity perturbations. However, this approach showed limitations due
to the influence of the mean power entering the PD. The findings suggest that more complex
or combined error functions may be necessary to achieve optimal stabilization.
Attempts to stabilize the system based on loop output power measurements highlighted
the interplay between different EPC adjustments. The observed challenges due to changes
in bending loss and the interdependence of EPC paddle adjustments indicate that further
refinement and potentially new hardware solutions, such as polarization controllers with
constant insertion loss, are required to make this approach viable.
Future research should focus on addressing the remaining challenges, such as the degradation
of the error function over time and possibly the development of more sophisticated error
functions. One could imagine a refined approach of using the gradient descent algorithm in-
stead of the direct adjustment approach. The perturbation step size for calculating gradients
should be equal to or larger than the bidirectional repeatability of the EPC to avoid futile
perturbation. Furthermore, such an algorithm should utilize the squared sum components of
the spectral image to yield the most promising error function. Lastly, the algorithm should
iteratively change all EPC paddles to allow for arbitrary wavelength conversion. This would
allow for the combination of the most promising stabilization system with the most robust
error function.
Mechanical limitations of the EPC paddles suggest the need to explore new hardware solutions
and further refine the stabilization algorithms for achieving long-term, reliable polarization
stabilization in optical systems. Implementing multiple EPCs or other phase retarders could
allow for unperturbed non-invasive polarization tracking, even during the unwinding process.
An initial step could be to test the hypothesis that changing idler and signal frequencies to
encapsulate the operational range of the polarization scrambler leads to less polarization-
dependent FWM. Combining the multiple EPC paddle configuration approach with the L2
norm error function could maximize the benefits of both existing algorithms.

Conclusion
This thesis has delved into the complexities of the requirements of stable and efficient wave-
length conversion. We considered polarization stabilization in optical communication sys-
tems, particularly focusing on optimizing the nonlinear χ(3) process FWM in a HNLF.
From the FWM process, we observed varying conversion efficiencies and bandwidths. The
most optimal pump wavelength was found to be 1565.5 nm, which was the closest achievable
pump wavelength to the ZDW of the HNLF. In a uni-directional pass of the HNLF, this
pump wavelength resulted in a -12 dB CE between idler and signal for a 15 nm bandwidth.
In the bi-cirectional pass of the HNLF, a corresponding -20 dB CE between idler and signal
was found at a similar signal-pump wavelength shift. The main discrepancy between the two
is caused by the difference in pump power in addition to the idler power cut introduced by the
loop. However, the bi-directional pass was shown to increase bandwidth of the FWM process,
where the 35 nm bandwidth had a measurable -15 dB CE. Furthermore, it was ensured that
the operation of the HNLF was outside of SBS threshold.
The theoretical foundations laid by Maxwell’s equations and wave propagation provided a
crucial understanding of electromagnetic wave behavior in optical fibers, addressing chal-
lenges such as chromatic dispersion and other linear effects. This fundamental knowledge
was essential for optimizing FWM. The use of polarization optics, specifically Stokes calculus
and the Poincar´e sphere, enabled precise visualization and manipulation of the SOP, which
is vital for maximizing the efficiency of FWM.
The characterization of both passive and active components was a critical step in ensuring
optimal system performance. Key components such as the TLS, EDFA, EPC, and PS were
carefully examined. The resilience of the HNLF to SBS under the experimental conditions
confirmed its suitability for high-power applications. In particular, we found that passive
devices may show upwards of 1 dB wavelength dependent loss within the investigated spec-
trum.
The experimental results demonstrated that the initialization and calibration processes are
crucial for achieving a stable starting point for stabilization algorithms. By systematically
adjusting the QWP and HWP, the loop output power was maximized, and polarization
dependency was minimized. This initial calibration ensured a reliable baseline for further
stabilization efforts.

Conclusion

From the experimental stabilization results, we conclude that with the current algorithms and
devices, it is possible to remain below 40% of the maximized normalized spectral sum. This
was the worst-case error function value yielded across the numerous stabilization experiments.
The implementation of stabilization algorithms revealed several key insights. The gradient
descent algorithm, while theoretically effective, faced practical challenges due to the bidirec-
tional repeatability of the EPC paddles. Adjusting the perturbation step size to match this
repeatability was crucial for effective stabilization. Despite these adjustments, the gradient
descent approach still exhibited degradation of the loss function over time, indicating the
need for further refinement.
The direct descent algorithm offered a simpler yet effective alternative.
By immediately
selecting an adjustment direction and moving in increments, this approach avoided the com-
plexities of gradient calculations and was less affected by bidirectional repeatability. However,
the use of a moving average for decision-making slowed down the system, leading to increased
overshooting of the desired minimal error function values and more noisy idler amplitudes.
The primary weakness of the stabilization algorithms was observed when the EPC paddles
reached their saturation limits. The current system lacks a mechanism for automatic re-
setting without altering the polarization state, which could disrupt the entire optical setup.
Implementing additional EPCs or phase retarders could allow for non-invasive unwinding,
achieving continuous polarization tracking.
Furthermore, the observed drift in optimized states suggested that the chosen loss function
might not be optimal. This drift could be related to the polarization scrambler, which is
optimized for the 1530 nm - 1570 nm range, while the signal laser was tuned to 1570.5 nm.
This discrepancy might prevent true wavelength independence, affecting the error function.
Future studies could involve swapping the idler and signal frequencies and adjusting the
following filters accordingly to investigate this hypothesis further.
Future research should focus on addressing the remaining challenges, such as the degradation
of the error function over time and the development of more sophisticated error functions. A
refined approach could involve using the gradient descent algorithm with perturbation step
sizes equal to or larger than the bidirectional repeatability of the EPC. Utilizing the squared
sum components of the spectral image could yield the most promising error function.
An initial step could be to test the hypothesis that changing idler and signal frequencies to
encapsulate the operational range of the polarization scrambler leads to less polarization-
dependent FWM. By combining multiple EPC paddle configurations with the L2 norm error
function, it may be possible to maximize the benefits of existing algorithms.
In conclusion, this thesis has demonstrated significant improvements in polarization stabi-
lization and FWM efficiency through the integration of theoretical principles with practical
experimental setups. By continuing to refine these techniques and explore new solutions, we
can enhance the performance and reliability of optical communication systems, paving the
way for more efficient and robust optical technologies.
