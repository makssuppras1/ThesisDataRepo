Abstract (English)
The aim of this thesis is to determine if a multinomial innite relational model
(MIRM) can be used in combination with classication methods to determine
if a patient suers from amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) or not. The model
nds dierences across networks based on diusion weighted imaging (DWI)
and is designed such that the optimal result is found by maximizing the log-
likelihood for the model.
It is examined if the three methods: Logistic regression, K-nearest neighbours
and support vector machines can use data generated from MIRM to classify the
test subjects. Furthermore it is examined if a forward selection algorithm can
nd regions of interest (ROI), that are especially inuenced by ALS.
Brain scan data in the thesis comes from DWI and originates of a control group
consisting of 19 healthy individuals and 24 ALS patients. The MATLAB pro-
gram "ExploreDTI" is used to process the data, such that a deterministic trac-
tography is performed to locate connections between voxels in the dierent ROI.
This amounts to a network for each of the test subjects brains.
There are two scenarios: One with 90 ROI and one with 500 ROI, and both have
been analysed with regards to classication and selection of signicant features
with a forward selection algorithm.
MIRM has been tested on synthetic data in dierent settings, and it turns out
that the model in general performs well with regards to identifying dierences
across the relatively simple synthetic networks.
==PAGE:4==
ii
The results from DWI data indicates, that MIRM is highly sensitive towards
dierences across networks, and as a result is also able to nd dierences across
the healthy subjects. Hence it is not possible to choose few and consistent ROI,
that can be used for classication.
It is furthermore found that there is a quite high error rate for all classication
methods, which is why it at present time is not feasible to use the models as a
diagnostics tool.
MIRM is reliable for synthetic data, but apparently the brain is too complex a
network to analyse with low error rates for classication and consistent results
for feature selection with DWI data.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In 1865 Pierre Paul Broca described the localization of the articulated language
by means of an autopsy of a patient with a speech impediment, and he became
one of the pioneers in neuroscience. In the beginning, lesion studies was the
main approach in the explorations of the structure of the brain, since there was
no way of accessing the brain within living individuals without great risk. Since
then a much more detailed knowledge on the way our brain works - as well
as several non-invasive methods to investigate the brain - has been developed.
Nevertheless there are still an immense number of processes within the brain,
that we either do not understand or cannot describe satisfactorily.
One of the promising non-invasive methods to investigate the structural dier-
ences and similarities within brains is the Diusion Weighted Imaging (DWI), in
which the diusion on water within the brain tissue is registered. Due to these
highly detailed scans, we can study the neural pathways of the brain. In other
words, DWI can help us determine which regions of the brain are anatomically
connected. [8]. These regions are also called regions of interest (ROI).
The detailed imaging of the brain gives us new possibilities within the research
and understanding the complex network, that the brain indeed is. The techno-
logical progress within brain scans gives way to new approaches on the study of
the brain, and mathematical models play a large part in these studies, since the
amount of data gathered from these methods are massive to say the least. This

means that data can not be fully examined manually, and we need models to
interpret data. One of the models that can possibly be of use is the Multinomial
Innite Relational Model (MIRM), which I will use in this thesis. The model
can be used to infer network dierences - where the brain from each test subject
is considered a single network. With this model we hope to be able to locate dif-

traditional methods usually imposes a similarity measure and cluster data ac-

since it relies on a non-parametric Bayesian generative model that can struc-
ture the given data. This means that the model can use the entire structure of
the networks simultaneously and is not as limited as many other approaches. [3]
In this thesis, I will go through the theory behind MIRM as well as three classi-

tests of the model on synthetic data. Since I produce this data myself, I have

is able to detect network dierences, and also how much dierence is in fact
needed in order for the model to identify the dierences across networks. Fi-
nally I will process DWI data with the MIRM and determine the accuracy of
the classiers. The DWI data used consists of two groups:
• A healthy control group.
• A group consisting of amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) patients on dif-
ferent stages of the disease.
The data from DWI scans is provided by Saber Sami. The ALS patients are at
dierent stages of progress of their disease and are of diering age and cognitive
skills. Regarding age they are approximately matched to the control group.
The main goal is to examine if the MIRM can process data such that a classica-
tion model can distinguish between the two groups. Since ALS is a degenerative
disease, it is possible that the structural changes caused by ALS is detectable
by the MIRM in combination with a classication model. Furthermore I will
use forward feature selection to nd signicant features, and examine if these
results are consistent and as such can determine ROIs that are in particular

2.1
Introduction
In our world we are a part of complex networks all the time. We interact via
social networks, live in cities connected by transportation networks and use wa-
ter and electricity that is distributed through networks. We even have several
very complex network within our own bodies - one of which is the brain. When
studying a network, we need mathematical theory to structure and investigate
these networks, and this is where graph theory comes into play. A network such
as the brain can be translated into a graph of nodes and links between these
nodes. [1]
A graph can be both directed and undirected, where the directed graph de-
nes a network where the links have a direction, such as links on WWW. The
undirected graphs have bidirectional links, and an example would be a social
network, where the links dene a connection between acquaintances - or as we
will examine in this thesis: Connections between regions of the brain. In order
to have a basis for further investigations of networks, we will dene some basic
concepts for graph theory, that will be used throughout the thesis.

When we use the term graph, a very short denition would be, that it is a
mathematical representation of points and connections between them. A more
formal denition would be:
Definition 2.1 A graph, G = (V, E) consists of the two sets V and E where
• Elements of V are called vertices or nodes.
• Elements of E are calles links or edges.
• A loop is an edge that joins a node to itself.
• The degree of a node is the number of edges incident on the node.
• Each edge has a set of one or two nodes associated with it, which is called
its endpoints.[6]

2.3
Innite relational model
The innite relational model is a nonparametric Bayesian network model. A
common method of modelling the structure of networks is through mixture
==PAGE:22==
8
Method
models. The name is used, since the given model is a mixture of probability
distributions. This is widely used to detect similarities in data - also known as
clusters - and it is also the method behind the innite relational model which is
a model that describes graphs/networks. [1]
The innite relational model is a latent variable model, which means that the
network is equipped with a "hidden" structure that can characterize the gener-
ative process of the network. Each node in the model is assigned to a category,
which corresponds to a clustering of nodes in the network. The number of clus-
ters in the model is inferred from the given data.
A generative model is a way of dening, how observed data could have been
generated via a statistical process. The distributions in the generative model
are the components that the model consists of. In this way we generate the
nodes as well as the links of the network.

2.11.1
Forward feature selection
For some of the datasets there is an overwhelming number of variables (fea-
tures) involved. This leads to highly complex models, which naturally leads to
the question: Can we reduce the dimensionality of the model by eliminating re-
dundant features? This could lead to models that are easier to interpret, faster
to train and reduce overtting.
There are numerous approaches to this problem, but here I will implement for-
ward selection, which has the following structure
• Start with no features
• Evaluate cross validation error for
 The feature subset in question
 All feature subsets equal to this with 1 added feature
• The best feature subset is chosen
• This is repeated until no further improvement [4]
With many features in the original set, this is a process that can be quite time
consuming.
2.11.2
Cross validation
Two types of cross validation will be used later on. Standard cross validation is
illustrated below.

We partition the data such that we for each fold have a training set on which the
model is trained, and then a test set that the model has not seen. The model
is then tested on this test set, and in this way we can estimate the accuracy of
the model. In order to reduce variability this is done multiple times, and an
average over the folds of the cross validation is found.
As an expansion to this, we will also use nested cross validation: When we
look at the real world data, we encounter a problem with testing the dierent
classication models, since we only have 37 subjects. If we had a larger dataset,
a natural approach would be to set up a training, test and validation set, since
we need to nd the relevant variables for the models. Since we have a quite
small number of subjects, we will use nested cross validation, as we need an
unbiased method to measure the error rate of the classication method. With
standard cross validation, the data that is held out would not be independent,
since it is used in the parameter selection process. The algorithm we use for
nested cross validation is given below
Split the whole data set into i=5 folds (outer CV);
for 1:i do
Split training set into j=5 more folds (inner CV) ;
for 1:j do
Train the model and perform variable selection on the inner training
set;
Calculate inner CV test error and take the mean of the 5 folds, ej ;
end
Select the set of parameters with minimum ej ;
Train the selected set of parameters on the outer training fold
end
Take the mean test error ei on the outer test folds;

2.11.2.1
Standardizing data
It should be noted that with regards to the DWI scanning data, we will work
on standardized data. In order to keep the test and training set completely
separate, the training data from a cross validation fold is standardized, and the
parameters µ and σ are then used to standardize the corresponding test data.
This means that the test data will in no way inuence the training data.
2.11.3
Logistic regression
Since the matter of classifying a subject as diagnosed with ALS or not is ob-
viously a two-class classication problem, logistic regression seems a natural
initial choice of modelling. In logistic regression, we have a combination of a

Inferring parameters
For the synthetic data, we set up the parameters ourselves, but on real world
data, we know nothing about the parameters of our model, so we need a way to
infer these. The method we will use rst is the Metropolis Hastings algorithm
to infer the parameters α and η.
The Metropolis Hastings algorithm is an MCMC method that can be used to
generate random samples from distributions, and it can also be used for inferring
parameters of a model.
3.1
The Metropolis-Hastings algorithm explained
If a probability density, π - the target distribution - is given on a state space,
X, and π(x) is computable up to a constant, then the Metropolis-Hastings
algorithm is a way to create a Markov chain on X, in which X(t) ∼π(x). In
other words the Markov chain converges to the original distribution, π. The
Markov chain examines the regions of the state space X that are of interest.
The given Markov chain is a sample distribution that is approximated from the
true distribution.
Since an element of a Markov chain is dependent only on the previous element,
we usually use a burn-in period, in order to let the Markov chain be completely

independent on the rst element, X(1). The nature of the Markov chain also
ensures that the whole state space is covered. [7]

The Gibbs sampler is a special case of the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm, in
which the variables are sampled from the conditional distribution. Each node
is considered one at a time, and since we do it iteratively, we approximate the
posterior distribution.
If we have a distribution p(z) = p(z1, ..., zM) that we sample from, then in each
step of the Gibbs sampling we replace the value of one variable by a value drawn
from the distribution of the given variable conditioned by all other variables:
p(zi|z\i). This procedure is then repeated through all variables. [5]

size, and in this case, the sampler works awlessly.
In order to make the sampler more ecient, we need to implement a split-merge
procedure. However this is too time consuming for this thesis, so the code with
split-merge procedure has been provided by Morten Mørup, and I will give a
brief overview of the idea behind the procedure.
3.2.1
Split-merge procedure
The Markov chain of the Gibbs sampler can become trapped in a mode, which
leads to wrong clustering of the data. If two or more components have similar pa-
rameters, it is unlikely that the Gibbs sampler will move a single observation (in
==PAGE:50==
36
Inferring parameters
our case node) to a new component, due to the low probability. Thus the com-
ponents that are in fact dierent, will be merged and be a compromise between
the two. This pattern is also what we see in the simple Gibbs sampler above.
The split-merge procedure can move a group of observations in each update,
which leads to a larger statistical support for the split/merge. The algorithm
tests, if splitting a component is benecial, and assigns the observations to new
components, if this is the case [11]. The implemented algorithm looks as follows:
Result: Splits/merges clusters in Z
initialization: Select two observations: i,j ;
if i, j are in same cluster then
Split cluster;
Reassign observations with Gibbs sampling;
Calculate Metropolis-Hastings ratio;
Split if it is an advantage
else
Merge clusters;
Calculate likelihood of merged cluster ;
Split merged cluster and calculate transition probability ;
Reassign observation with Gibbs sampling
end
Algorithm 3: Split/merge procedure
With the split/merge procedure implemented, the accuracy of the inferred Z is
much better, as we will see in the following chapter, where it is used.

5.1
Introduction
The multinomial IRM seems to be able to nd network dierences across net-

The idea is to use diusion weighted imaging (DWI) data from a control group
consisting of healthy individuals and a group of ALS patients. Both the concept
of ALS and DWI will be introduced below.
5.2
Diusion weighted imaging
The method DWI is a non-invasive neuroimaging technique, that examines the
diusion of extracelluar water molecules in the brain tissue. In the white matter
of a brain, water will move faster in the direction of the neural pathways and
slower across these pathways, and hence the imaging can be used to examine
connections within the human brain.
This property is known as anisotropy,
which means that the diusion is directionally dependent. [8]
The diusion coecient is not measured directly, but within each voxel1 of the

brain scan, the displacement of water molecules are measured. Since there are
several hindrances within the tissue, such as cell membranes, macromolecules
etc., the diusion within the brain is slower than in free water. Because of these
obstacles, we use the term ADC 2, which is the diusion coecient that can be
observed. In some regions of the brain, the ADC is the same for all directions -
which means that it is isotropic, whereas in others it is anisotropic. In general
for the white matter, which is of interest to us, it is anisotropic, and in the grey
matter of the brain, it is isotropic.[9].
One problem with DWI is, that there is a great deal of noise in the imaging,
since the resolution is very high. This means that even breathing in a subject
during a scan will result in noise in the output. Naturally we will correct the
data as best as possible for noise.
Since brains are dierent in a number of matters, we need to make a number
of corrections to the data, in order to make the dierent scans comparable.
For instance brains vary in size, which means that we need to transform our
scanning data to a template of similar size in order to be able to identify the
subsections of the brain in the same fashion for each subject. A short overview
of the steps needed are given below:
1. Correct for distortions (movement and Eddy currents). This is done with
a MATLAB package called ExploreDTI.
2. Generate a subdivision of a standard cortex (the template) into regions of
interest (ROI). The dened regions will be examined in the connectivity
analysis.
3. Coregister the DWI images (which have been adjusted for noise) to the
template. This gives us a transformation eld per subject.
4. Map the ROIs from (1) back to each subject's own DWI space by using
the inverse transformation from (2).
5. Perform whole brain tractography in ExploreDTI using Constrained Spher-
ical Deconvolution for each subject. We will seed in each white matter
voxel and keep streamlines that connect any two target ROIs.
6. Average the connectivities per ROI together per subject (to account for
anatomical variation, noise, etc).
7. Generate the connectivity matrices per subject. The matrices should then
be equivalent, in order for them to be compared directly.
8. Perform the MIRM analysis, looking for group dierences. The results
from the MIRM will be used to analyse via logistic regression, K-nearest
neighbours (KNN) and support vector machines (SVM).

5.3
ALS
ALS is short for Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis and is also known as Lou Gehrig
disease. It is a progressive neurodegenerative disease that aects nerve cells in
the brain and the spinal cord. Motorneurons communicate to the spinal chord,
that controls the muscles of the body, and as the disease progresses, the mo-
torneurons die, resulting in loss of muscle control. Over time the patient will
lose the ability to speak, eat and death is usually caused by respiratory compli-
cations within ve years of initial diagnosis. So far it has been assumed, that
the cognitive and sensory functions are preserved throughout the course of dis-
ease. However recent research suggests that there are also seems to be changes
in regions usually associated with cognitive skills.[12]
At the moment there is no cure for ALS, and only a few medications, that do
not prolong life, but primarily improves the quality of life after diagnosis. [10]
5.4
Hope for the ndings
In order to gain deeper knowledge of the changes induced by ALS, it is our
hope, that the multinomial innite relational model can process the data from
the DWI from dierent subjects, and in this fashion examine which regions of
the brain is aected by the disease. Since we will subset the brain of each subject
into many smaller regions, we hope that we can be quite specic in the areas
aected by ALS. Naturally in this setting, we will primarily determine, if the
model can at all be used to accumulate more knowledge about this disease. If
this should be the case, the model could be of great interest to the research in
ALS.

From the software treatment of the DWI data in each resolution, we get a set of

PASS and END.
Figure 5.1: Illustration of PASS and END through 3 ROIs
The PASS matrix will include all pathways between the ROIs and assign A to
B, B to C and A to C. The END matrix however will only assign A to C, since
this is where the connection terminates. As a result the PASS matrices have a
much higher density than the END matrices. The PASS and END matrices are
analysed separately.
5.5.1
Binary matrices
The types of connectivity matrices, I have chosen to examine are the binary
matrices and the matrices with a count of the number of tracts (connections)
between ROIs.
The binary matrices are logical matrices, that indicate a connection between
ROIs with an entry of 1, and a non-connection with a 0. According to the docu-
mentation and further investigation, there is no threshold in this setting, and all
connections are accounted for. This also means that the density of the matrices
are very dierent. In order to normalize data, the η matrices has been divided
by the total number of connections from the adjacency matrix. Though this is
not optimal, it is the best we can do to make the data as standardized as possible.

Chapter 6
Discussion
6.1
Feature selection
In most of the cases the feature selection process results in a higher error rate
on the classication problem. This is a symptom that the feature selection may
not be suited for this type of data. Two other approaches to feature selection
has been tested: Lasso and backwards feature selection. The results are not
included in the thesis, since they did not improve the classication error com-
pared to forward selection. Furthermore the features that are chosen are not
consistent across cross validation folds or classication methods. In a best case
scenario, naturally the features that are deemed signicant would be more alike
for each data set.
The results show, that the classication models nd dierent regions of interest
and hence dierent brain regions to be inuential on the results. Thus the re-
sults are inconclusive with regards to the areas aected by ALS. However this is
corresponds well with many other studies on ALS, which suggest that many ar-
eas besides the motor regions are aected by the disease - among others regions
responsible for behaviour and cognition. Other recent theories state that ALS
enhances the general ageing process of the brain, which is known to cause atro-
phy of the whole brain with a slight prevalence towards the right hemisphere.
[12] [13] [14]
This general deterioration of the brain with ALS patients would naturally be
==PAGE:98==
84
Discussion
very problematic for the selection of features that correspond to regions of in-
terest, since many of the features have an impact on the aected brain. Thus a
simplied model cannot be recommended for the classication process.
6.2
Classication
Both with and without feature selection we see, that the SVM approach with
a tuning of the boxconstraint generally performs signicantly better than KNN
and logistic regression. However it is still doubtful that it can be used as a
diagnostics tool, since the cross validated error rate is still between 27 % and
50 %. There is however no doubt that this is the most benecial classier for
the datasets in question.
6.3
Possible errors
Though DWI is a promising tool for the study of white matter in humans, the
method is not perfect, and as such has several possible aws due to the acquisi-
tion technique. If the abnormalities in the white matter is severe, the can rather
easily be identied, but for more subtle changes, the scans are more prone to
error.
In the acquisition of data the diusion of water molecules is measured. This
diusion meets dierent natural barriers within the brain of each subject. How-
ever since no two brains are exactly alike, there are natural structural dierences
between all subjects. When the data is used on the MIRM, these natural dif-
ferences could be registered, and as such point towards dierences that are not
necessarily induced by ALS.
The DWI is also very inuenced by motion, so breathing and vascular pulsation
will in fact aect the results from the scan. This leads to the presence of so called
artifacts in the images that are produced - the total elimination of artifacts is
impossible on living subjects. This is why we have used ExploreDTI to reduce
the noise as much as possible, but naturally it can not be totally eliminated by
processing either.
The MIRM has some possible errors as well. Since the model nds dierences
across subjects, it would be inuenced by outliers - i.e. one subject that is dif-
ferent from the others - and this will result in clusters that are not necessarily
useful as a predictive measure. Naturally this is sought reduced by variable
selection, but we can not exclude the possibility for this happening to our data
set.
==PAGE:99==
6.4 Future work
85
Furthermore one cannot dismiss the natural occurring dierences between hu-
man brains. When the MIRM is used only on the control group with 500 ROIs,
there are still found more than 30 clusters, which indicates that there are indeed
dierences across networks even for the healthy control group.
6.4
Future work
In this thesis I have focused on three dierent classication methods and used
them in the same fashion on all the datasets. Generally one should choose the
approach that best suits the problem at hand, and since the SVM has proven
most ecient it would be natural to delve deeper into this approach than it has
been possible for me. There are many ways to optimize the SVM, and it is also
possible to construct ones own kernel function - there may be a kernel function
better suited for this problem. There are also more ecient ways of optimizing
the hyperparameters of the model, than the ones I have used here, such as better
optimization algorithms etc., which all could contribute to a better classication
of the subjects. The optimization of the model should in this case be performed
for each type of dataset, since the way they are constructed are very dierent.
In the dataset provided, the ALS patients and control group are aligned with
regards to age, but the ALS patients have dierent cognitive scores and disease
severity. It may be a good idea to choose patients that are at an early but
similar stage of their disease, since patients at a late stage of their disease are
more prone to an extensive atrophy of the brain - which naturally means that
more regions of interest are deteriorated and hence the feature selection is com-
plicated. Naturally a larger group of subjects would also be useful in order to
improve the modelling and classication. Since the preprocessing of data is very
computational expensive, one would expect this to be a quite time consuming
process for a larger dataset.
Since many regions of the brain are aected by ALS - despite what was thought
in the past - there is a lot of work on the way to the development of a math-
ematical diagnostics tool, as both natural occurrences and many neurological
diseases will aect the dierences across networks.
6.5
Conclusion
In conclusion the accuracy using the multinomial innite relational model and
the three dierent classication methods does not yet discriminate well enough
between ALS patients and the control group to be used as a diagnostics tool.

The feature selection is not consistent and based on the methods used, we cannot
determine a single feature subset that performs as well as the full feature set.
Further research and development of the multinomial innite relational model as
well as classication methods and feature selection is necessary in order to make
neuroimaging in this context a useful diagnostics tool. It is an open question, if
the structure of the brain and naturally occurring dierences is too complex an
entity to describe with this model. However the modelling shows some promise,
and a successful implementation of such a model would be highly benecial for
patients as well as doctors and researchers.
