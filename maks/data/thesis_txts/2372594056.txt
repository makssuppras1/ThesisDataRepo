==PAGE:3==
2
This report contains the master thesis of Nicklas Werge Svendsen finalizing his studies in engineering design at Design and Innovation, Technical University of Denmark. The 
project goal has been to develop a medical device aimed at eye surgeries in developing countries.
Initially a thorough needs analysis was carried out determining pathogenesis, epidemiology and state of the art treatment equipment through a combine literature study etno­
graphic resarch study screening 7 different eye diseases recommended by WHO to develop invetervention aimed at, where corneal opacitites was chosen as focus due to the 
magnitude of the disease and thee soon average age of occurence combined with the multi-channel potential and treatment procedures less developed than these of the other 
major issues in the developing world (cataract and glaucuma) 
Based on the needs analysis’ findings, a concept development phase conducted using bio-inspired design and multiple rounds of prototyping and experimenting with function­
al models, a concept developed through the combination of the most promising rounds of prototyping an experimenting, whereby conceptualized graft containing, washing, 
delivering and controlling functions was validated. 
Finally, a detailed design phase was carried out to see the concept evolve into a theoretical coherent solution capable of addressing the needs of the DMEK surgery in a way fa­
cilitating the earlier division of tasks between the main stakeholders affecting the performance of the DMEK surgery, hospitals and donor cornea banks, providing a foundation 
for significantly increasing the number of patients being treated in the developing world and possibly also in the industrialized world.

Pathogenesis: The specific cellular actions and mechanisms involved in the development and progression of a disease
Epidemiology: The magnitude, frequency and distribution of a disease or a health-related condition among a certain population
Cornea: The outermost transparent layer of the eye protecting the eye’s components and possesing refractive power (‘hornhinde‘ in Danish)
Retina: The innermost layer of the eye globe responsible of receiving incoming light and transmitting this optic signals onwards towards the brain (‘nethinde‘ in Danish) 
Cataract: Opacities within the lens (‘grå stær‘ in Danish)
Glaucoma: Optic neuropathy due to increased intraocular pressure mainly (‘grøn stær‘ in Danish)
Myopia: Light beams are focused before the retina (‘nærsynet‘ in Danish)
Hyperopia: Light beams are after before the retina (‘langsynet‘ in Danish)
Astigatism: Light beams are focused multiple places on/before/after the retina (‘bygningsfejl‘ in Danish)

Since the initiation of the ‘VISION 2020: The right to sight’ in 1999 by World Health Organization (WHO) and The International Agency for the Prevention of Blindness 
(IAPB), the elimination of avoidable blindness and the prevention of the 1999-projected doubling of avoidable visual impairment towards 2020 has been addressed on a global 
basis.  
However, 2010-estimates published by WHO indicates that 285.3 million people are visually impaired, distributed on 246 million people with low vision and 39.4 million 
blind people on a global scale [1]. Thereby, a significant portion of the world’s population are affected with a 3-fold increased likelihood of unemployment and combined with 
eye care expenses resulting in an estimated global bill of 102 billion US dollar [URL1]. 
Furthermore, 90% of the visually impaired live in low-income settings [2], respectively 65% of the visually impaired and 82% of the world’s blind people are 50 years or older 
[1] which globally is expected to increase, but especially for the regions China, India, South‐East Asia and Eastern‐Mediterranean [2], indicating that any development guided 
towards the low-income settings are appreciated. Thereby, efforts to address these issues are still essential to reach the VISION2020 goals.
On the other hand, a multi-channel cannula polymer has been developed by Torben Lenau, an invention which might be applicable to this problem area. The advantages of 
the multi-channel polymer needle are related to the material characteristics, the geometric design and the mechanical properties. 
1. Polymers are non-magnetic and disposable opposed to needles of metal and the specific polymer microstructure provides transparency, allowing instant visual detec­
tion of injected or extracted fluid. 
2. The geometric design with multiple-channels provides the opportunity for injecting and extracting fluid or tissue simultaneously. 
3. The inherent flexibility of the polymer cannula due to its microstructural properties makes it less subject to overloads from unintended use, compared to more stiff 
steel needles.
Thereby, the main hypothesis addressed throughout this Master’s Project is the following: 
A multi-channel polymer cannula can be applied in a 
simplified medical device for eye surgeries in the developing world

1. Introduction
This 1st part of the Master’s Thesis, the needs analysis, contains the assessment of 
following three hypotheses: 
H1. The polymer cannula is likely more applicable to certain eye conditions 
rather than equally applicable to all eye conditions
H2. The magnitude and prevalence of eye conditions are varying with respect 
to geography and ethnicity, whereby certain eye diseases might constitute a 
larger health issue in the developing world.   
H3. State of the art equipment for diagnosis and treatment of eye diseases 
might have requirements incompatible to the context of the developing world. 
Initially, the most common eye diseases was found with the priority eye diseases 
listed by WHO [URL2] as the starting point. Although, by comparing these with 
the updated WHO-figures from 2012 on the most prevalent causes of blind­
ness and visual impairment [1], Trachoma and Onchocerciasis was discarded as 
WHO-leaded prevention programmes aimed at both of these infectious diseases 
have been launched causing the prevalences of these to decline remarkably the 
last 20 years [2].

3.1.1 Cataract

usually acquired, but 0.4% of cataracts are congenital. Acquired or age-related 
cataracts are designated with respect to their physical placement within the lens: 
Cortical, nuclear and sub-posterior subcapsular [3, 4], displayed in figure 6.

3.1.2 Glaucoma
In a normal eye, aqueous humor is produced in the ciliary body, excreted into the 
posterior chamber, flowing by the anterior surface of iris and the anterior surface 
of the lens, entering the anterior chamber through the pupil where it moistens 
the cornea before exiting the anterior chamber through the trabecular meshwork 
at the base of the iris’ anterior side (figure 7).
Glaucoma on the other hand, is most often caused by an increased intraocular 
pressure (IOP), due to a blocked drainage system in the front of the eye. Even­
tually, as fluid is building up in the anterior chamber or posterior chamber, the 
lens is pushed backwards and pushes the vitreous body towards the optic disc, 
resulting in optic neuropathy and irreversible visual field loss [3]. The type of 
glaucoma is determined by the chamber angle between the posterior surface of 
the cornea/limbus intersection and the anterior surface of iris [4], yielding two 
main disease types: Open-angle glaucoma (OAG) and angle-closure glaucoma 
(ACG), displayed in figure 8.

3.1.3 Corneal opacities
The cornea is the anterior transparent element of the eye, protecting the eye 
against structural damage to the internal elements and possessing approximate­
ly ⅔ of the eye’s refractive power due its transparency and the prolate spheroid 
shape with a curvature radius around 8mm [3, URL3]. The cornea consists of five 
main structures: The epithelium, stroma and the endothelium which are divided 
by Bowman’s layer and Descemet’s membrane (figure 9).
The epithelium is the the outer surface with a thickness of 4-6 cell layers, cor­
responding to 40-50 micrometer. The epithelium layer is responsible for the 
production of mucin that aids the hydrophilic nature of the 7 micrometer tear 
film layer, which is essential in order to smoothen the cornea surface and remain 
the refractive properties. The central stroma layer represents 85% of the cornea 
thickness embodied by parallely arranged collagen fibers to give the cornea its 
structural properties.

the cornea and ultimately resulting in corneal blindness are multiple, ranging

3.1.4 Age-related macular degeneration
The macula designates the area of the retina that unfolds circularly (5.5mm) at 
the end of the lights’ entrance axis and is responsible for the well-functioning of 
the central vision. Cross-sectionally, the macula is constituted by the following 
layers: A retinal layer, a layer of retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) cells, Bruch’s 
membrane and the choroid layer (figure 10.1).  
As the retina is responsible of both capturing the incoming light and processing 
this optic communicating onwards, the layer’s physiology is complex and distrib­
uted respectively on  ganglion cells and bipolar cells closest to the vitreous and 
photoreceptors closest to the RPE cells. The ganglion cells and the bipolar cells 
are responsible for receiving the incoming light and transferring it to the pho­
toreceptors, whereby the light sensing cells of the photoreceptors’ outer segment 
(rods and cones) generates the nerve impulses for further transmittance towards 
the optic nerve. These rods and cones are surrounded by square-formed RPE

3.1.5 Diabetic retinopathy
As the designation of the disease suggest, diabetic retinopathy damage the retinal 
imaging and affects diabetics. The disease usually develops from nonprolifera­
tive diabetic retinopathy (NPDR) over advanced NPDR too proliferative diabetic 
retinopathy (PDR) and manageable, but not reversible [3]. 
 
In NPDR, the diabetes-related hyperglycemia damages the retinal pericyte cells, 
when osmotic forces causes water diffusion into the retinal pericyte cells in order 
to decrease glucose levels. Although, retinal pericytes cells are essential to the 
blood flow regulation, whereby the osmotic damage of the pericytes result in 
weakened capillary walls and retinal ischemia [3, URL6].
Weakened capillary walls are linked to the formation of microaneurysm, small 
dilatations of retinal capillary vessels and increased permeability of both,  and 
the capillary vessels results in intraretinal hemorrhage of blood or so-called hard 
exudates (lipids and proteins) of varying sizes. As NPDR progresses, soft exu­
dates or cotton-wool spots which basically is nerve fiber infarcts are formed as a 
result of the decreased oxygen supply (figure 11.2). Depending on the amount of 
soft exudates, hard exudates and hemorrhages, this phase of the disease might 
be asymptomatic or causing symptoms like  blurry vision or floating spots in the 
visual field [3, URL6].

3.1.6 Uncorrected refractive errors

errors. Due to previously mentioned, presbyopia is neglected and focus put on: 
Myopia, hyperopia and astigmatism.

3.1.7 Childhood blindness
Childhood blindness is more a range of conditions rather than one disease, as

3.1.8 Pathogenesis findings
The investigation of the pathogenesis of the seven eye diseases yielded findings 
regarding the diseases’ associated functional problems, prognosis and the fit with 
the advantages of the polymer cannula.
Initially, childhood blindness and uncorrected refractive errors are discarded 
and will not be subject of further investigation i sections 3.2 and 3.3 respective­

congenital conditions and thereby found too comprehensive to address, being an 
area of improvement of WHO rather than a single disease to address. Addition­
ally, uncorrected refractive errors are discarded as the causes of these conditions 
(the eye’s refractive power, the axial length of the eye and the depth of the anteri­
or chamber) are assessed to be incompatible with the solution space provided by 
the advantages of the polymer cannula.

3.2 Epidemiology
The epidemiology of eye diseases are examined to investigate the 2nd hypothesis 
of the needs analysis:
H2. The magnitude and prevalence of eye conditions are varying with respect 
to geography and ethnicity, whereby certain eye diseases might constitute a 
larger health issue in the developing world.   
	
	
	
In continuation of WHO’s 2010-digits outlined previously, uncorrected refractive 
errors and cataract are by far the two biggest contributors to the distribution of 
visual impairment (VI), respectively accountable for 42% and 33% (figure 17).
Cataract are by far the biggest contributor to global blindness, being responsible 
for 51% of the world’s blind people (figure 18). Furthermore, uncorrected re­

3.3 Diagnosis and treatment of eye diseases
State of the art diagnosis and treatment of eye diseases are examined beneath and 
subsequently compared with the development world conditions and status of eye 
care to investigate the 3rd hypothesis of the needs analysis:
H3. State of the art equipment for diagnosis and treatment of eye diseases 
might have requirements incompatible to the context of the developing world.
3.3.1 Cataract
As cataract occurs as opacities within the lens, diagnosis are made through a 
slit-lamp biomicroscopy (figure 25) examination by a medical doctor, while the 
pupil is maximally dilated in order to improve the medical doctor’s vision [3]. 
Although, due to the anatomical placement of the disease, the visibility of the 
disease increases with time whereby non-educated people are able to detect irreg­
ularities compared to normal eyes. 
Treatment is done through surgery, including removal of the crystalline lens and 
insertion of an intraocular lens(IOL) implant of polymer, which is the golden 
standard of cataract treatment. As the obstruction of entering light beams to­
wards the retina is the functional problem combined with the absence of medical 
treatment opportunities available, surgery is the preferred option [3-p. 394].

The removal of the crystalline lens are done in a range of different ways using 
either advanced electronic instruments or manually operated instruments. Of 
the advanced equipment, the  phacoemulsification (PE) surgery technique is 
the golden standard and the femtosecond (FS) laser procedure is increasing in 
popularity, whereas the manual small-incision cataract surgery (MSICS) is the 
manually preferred procedure [A1, 13, 3-p. 465]. Thereby, regular ophthalmology 
instruments are used (figure 26) in addition to more advanced equipment for FS 
laser cataract surgery and PE cataract surgery (figures 27-28).
The PE cataract surgery is a highly reproducible surgical procedure, which is 
usually done in 20 minutes in a setting with an experienced surgeon and the 
presence of 2-3 surgical nurses and the demanded equipment. For this proce­
dure, the curvilinear capsulorhexis is performed manually and subsequently the 
crystalline lens is removed by interacting irrigation and aspiration after the dis­
integration due to the exposure to ultrasound by a phaco-probe [A2]. This allows 
for small incision holes and no sutures whereby minimal trauma to the eye and 
impact to other ocular components are achieved [3-p. 447].
The FS laser cataract surgery follows the abovementioned surgery steps, likewise 
benefitting from the application of ultrasound. Although, the capsulorhexis is 
performed with a laser opposed to the mechanical procedure of phacoemulsifica­
tion, in order to minimize the lens pieces in size and thereby possibly make the 
procedure more gentle and recovery faster [URL7].

3.3.2 Glaucoma 
Diagnosis of glaucoma (both for OAG and ACG) includes multiple steps: Ex­
amination of medical and ocular history as for cataract, although visual field 
testing, optic nerve analysis, in addition to measurements of intraocular pressure 
(IOP) and chamber angle are also performed [3]. However, it is worth to notice 
that the diagnostic procedure serves a twofold purpose: The determination of 
whether glaucoma is present or not in addition to determining the progression of 
the disease, wherefore the glaucoma therapy is usually not initiated after a single 
examination [3-p. 1213].
In visual field testing, the eye’s sensitivity to light is mapped throughout the visu­
al field one eye at a time, whereby accommodation is prevented and vision defects 
of the patient are discovered. This is most often done using equipment for the 
standard automated perimetry method (figure 30), where stimulus of white

3.3.3 Corneal opacities
The diagnosis of corneal conditions are highly related to the condition of the 
specific case, whether it’s related to sclera, conjunctiva or the actual cornea.
Most of the corneal degenerations and dystrophies are diagnosed through slit-
lamp biomicroscopy examinations performed by a medical doctor in addition to 
laboratory tests of corneal scrapings. For corneal keratitis, diagnosis is usually 
done in a combined clinical and historical examination, although slit-lamp bio­
microscopy and confocal microscopes are used to determine some kinds of kera­
titis. Most conjunctival or scleral-mediated conditions are clinically diagnosed by 
moving the eye lid to improve overview. However, diagnosis of some diseases are 
assisted by laboratory tests of corneal scrapings to determine the condition and 
for posterior scleral conditions more advanced equipment like ultrasonography, 
tomography or fluorescein angiography are used [3]. 
Treatment opportunities are many, due to the wide range of causalities affect­
ing the cornea. However, infectious diseases and keratitis are most often treated 
medically [3-part 4], whereas surgeries are done to restore vision or globe integ­

The following section will contain the reflections on the applied methods and the

4.1 Methods
The incorporation of educational videos in addition to medical literature in the 
empirical study definitely accelerated the pace of the learning curve of the diseas­
es’ pathogenesis through animations and visualizations of the diseases’ progres­
sion. Furthermore, handling a couple of medical textbooks, with an engineering 
mindset, as the sole data sources might also be unnecessarily inefficient when 
balancing the development project’s data quality and progression. Likewise, com­
bining more recent data on the diseases’ epidemiology, diagnosis and treatment 
found in medical articles with the facts given in older medical textbooks was 
valuable in order to get a grasp of geographic dependencies and the development 
world’s status on eye care. 
Performing the ethnographic research including surgery observations, interviews 
in person and on telephone was useful to understand clinical requirements of 
eye surgeries in general and the conditions of the developing world, although the 
development project would most likely have benefitted even more from ethno­
graphic research within developing world areas like rural India or many African 
countries. However, due to the financial barriers of a field trip abroad and the

2. Methods
To adress the hypotheses of the concept development phase ethnographic research and 
different problem analysis frameworks was used to specify the product requirements, 
while different advanced design methods was applied for the ideation. This was done in 
an integrated design process ensuring co-evolution of the problem/solution space.  
 
2.1 Problem analysis
The problem analysis was initiated prior to the actual concept development, but also it­
erated throughout the phase in order to ensure alignment between solution and design 
requirements. 
2.2.1 Ethnographic research
In order to specify requirements of the DMEK surgery process, two additional visits 
was conducted at Rigshospitalet in Glostrup observing and interviewing Javier while 
performing DMEK surgeries.

4. Discussion
The below sections will contain reflections on applied methods obtained results 
while investigating the hypotheses H4-H5 in addition to the overall process of the 
concept development phase.
4.1 Methods
Using prototyping and experimenting in an integrated iterative approach prooved 
to be a nice way of making something that actual works on a conceptual basis. 
However, opposed to the throwaway prototyping effort where different concepts 
are tested and validated seperately, the effort within this project was turned 
towards evolutionary prototyping where most of the components was re-used to 
build on top of the different functional models validity. 
While this proved to work and all functions required for the DMEK device has 
been verifyed, the solution space could most likely have been expanded more 
by incorporating more advanced design methods than the bio-inspired design 
framework incorporated at a late stage. However, the required functions tested 
throughout the prototyping sessions were all very concrete and applying to a frag­
ile subject (cornea graft) within a device, whereby chunking up to a systems level 
to expand the solution space or pursue abstract ideas was difficult. 
For the bio-inspired design effort, a top-down approach was followed initially 
==PAGE:75==
74
combining a bunch of generated synonyms with primarily high rank biological 
classification phrasings like ‘bacteria’, ‘plantae’, ‘fungi’ and only unfolding the 
animal phylla, yielding a huge amount of unexact articles. Especially, phrasings 
assembling the very small organisms in combination with synonms like ‘screen’, 
‘monitor’ and ‘control’ provided articles on how these organisms are often used in 
experiments (‘bacteria’, ‘fungi’, etc.), decreasing the value of the initial performed 
searches. Thereby, subsequently apply a bottom-up approach starting at relatively 
known organisms and more accurate keywords actually resembling the physical 
action needed (‘filter’, ‘capture’, etc.), whereby more useful analogies was found. 
For future use of the bioinspired design method, carefull attention should there­
for be paid towards the synonym formulation and starting at a known organism 
coying the keywords describing this organism’s biological phenoma is most likely 
provded faster and more accurate results.
 
4.2 Results
Based on a first-hand source in addition to watching multiple DMEKs, the find­
ings of section 3.1 rejecting the potential of the multi-channel cannula is per­
ceived to be significantly reliable. Although, if a DMEK device can be developed 
enabling a perfect fit of the graft immediately after the injection, the advantages of 
a multi-channel cannula will apply to a DMEK surgery context.
Using different types of cornea donor graft substitutes throught the section 
3.2-3.4, the results here confirming hypothesis H5 are in general perceived to be 
trusthworthy.

For the containing and washing functions, the results can be viewed as very reli­
able being validated showing completely tighthness for the containing function 
and using the actual conserving for the washing function, respectively.  
Furthermore, while the actual thickness of the contact lenses used as substitutes is 
most likely much bigger than the ~0.15µm cornea donor graft, it was still manage­

escape the device if tilted more than intended when diluted contents is released.   
Summarizing, the integrity and microstructural behaviour of the cornea donor 
graft has been assumed to be equal to that of the contact lens, whereby testing 
using the an actual cornea donor graft should be carried out going forward.

However a wide range of aspects could benefit from the performance of a full-circle 
experiment using a  functional prototype of the DMEK device capable of performing 
all the functions. 
Especially, the delivering function being composed by two aligned cones facilitating 
the rolling from a Ø10mm with a 20° angle entering the cone. Although, acommodat­
ing for the perceived necessary wall thickness of 1mm this function might be com­
promized. Thereby, improving the injecton moulding quality for this part might be 
necessary as a 1mm wall thickness is highly constraining to the design. For instance, the 
placement of the O-ring is forced to be 6.5mm above the part’s base while the enter­
ing cone diameter is reduced to ~Ø10mm to avoid wall thickness’ below 1mm, which 
would be found as the perpendicular distance between the internal cone and the O-ring 
track and as the wall thickness at the part’s base.
The inlet plug might likewise be a source of optimizattion. For instance, the trade-off 
between grip and freight has been chosen to favor freight with no outhanging diameter 
to interact with, however this is chosen due to the fact that being a disposable device, 
assembling is only performed once, wherefore detaching the parts is likeley not neces­
sary. Furthermore, decreasing the internal diameter of the blocking cone might also be 
done as an infusion time trade-off.
Finally, uptimizing the rounds’ radius in addition to other geometrical entities should 
be enlightened to accomodate mass production if needed in the future.

Concluding the Master Thesis a range of sub hypothesis - 7 in total - has been formulated as the project’s academic angle, adressed to investigated the Master Thesis’ main 
hypothesis: A multi-channel polymer cannula can be applied in a simplified medical device for eye surgeries in the developing world. 
By initially converging the project’s focus towards corneal opacitites due to the magnitude of people affected by the underlying conditions especially in developing countries, 
the initially perceived multi-channel polymer cannula potential and the lacking amount of equipment available for this surgery.
Subsequently, rejecting the main hypothesis throughout the concept development phase, while simultaneously confirming the sub hypothesis by applying different advanced 
design methods to develop functional models capable of performing all related subfunctions and in the detailing concept phase afterwards synthethis these functions into one 
hole single-use DMEK device supporting the expansion of the surgical procedure to developing countries where cornea specialized ophthalmolgists are in few.
Featuring af system constituted by a transparent PP container and enclosed by two PP plugs with specialized internal dimensionings, the inlet plug facilitates graft controlling, 
washing and dyeing whereas the outlet plug is responsible for delivering the graft to an additional syringe for actual injecton. 
Only being offered by one company, a cornea donor bank, the DMEK device might constitute an inherited value attractive to both hospitals and donor banks being able to 
reduce surgery time while increasing effeciency, degree of specialization within the DMEK procedure and increase the amount of patients being treated as the critical dissection 
of endothelium and descemet’s membrane measuring ~15µm is insourced to the the donor banks.
Ready for final validation through an experiment conducted with a coherent functional prototype using an actual donor graft injected in a chamber similar to the human’s 
eye, thereby the foundation is layed out of achieving the project’s goal: To develop and test a device for eye surgeries aimed at development countries which will enable a dra­
ma2c increase in amount of people treated for their illness.
