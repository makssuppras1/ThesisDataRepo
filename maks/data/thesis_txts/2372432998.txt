Abstract 
This thesis serves as a comprehensive sustainable transport appraisal study for the complex project 
of constructing a new s-train station in Solrød Kommune’s newest neighborhood, Trylleskov Strand. 
The goal of the corresponding research was to evaluate the possible outcomes of this project within 
a framework that can be understood as consultancy to all of the involved stakeholders. The guiding 
research question of this work was thus: under the currently assumed transport, societal, 
environmental and economic characteristics of the Trylleskov Strand Station project, is its 
construction a feasible and sustainable investment? Three sets of analysis methods were used to 
answer this question: traffic analysis, socio-economic analysis and strategic analysis. The traffic 
analysis was performed using the OTM public and road transport models and the Traffic Analyst tool 
in ArcGIS. This analysis served as input into the socio-economic analysis, which was broken into 
three segments: financial affordability analysis, cost-benefit analysis (CBA) and feasibility risk 
assessment (FRA). This socio-economic assessment was then supported by a strategic assessment 
that was carried out in the form of a multi-actor multi-criteria analysis (MAMCA) based on survey 
response data from relevant stakeholders. The combination of these assessments enables a 
comprehensive understanding of the project’s feasibility that extends beyond the traditional 
financial or socio-economic aspects. Although the level of uncertainty in the available data used to 
support this research suggests the need for even further research, the comprehensive and multi-
faceted analysis performed within this thesis recommends that the Trylleskov Strand Station project 
is likely to be a worthwhile investment that will generate both socio-economic and sustainability-
related benefits over the course of its lifetime.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Introduction to project 
The goal of this thesis is to produce a feasibility report that identifies potential outcomes of building 
a new s-train station in Solrød Kommune’s newest neighborhood, Trylleskov Strand, and evaluates 
these outcomes within a framework that can be understood as consultancy to all stakeholders 
involved. The primary stakeholder group that participated continuously through the report process 
was the city planning and administration team from Solrød municipality, Solrød Kommune Teknik og 
Miljø. Their primary goal is to gain approval for the construction of the new station from external 
stakeholders, such as DSB, Banedanmark and the Danish Ministry of Transport. Thus, this report is 
designed to address the evaluation criteria set in place by these external stakeholders, as well as, 
those evaluation criteria proven to be beneficial throughout the literature on sustainable project 
assessment of transport infrastructure projects. The report will also include comprehensive forms of 
analysis that will enable the team in Solrød to understand the actions that must be taken in order to 
promote the sustainable feasibility of the new station. 
The first step in this thesis project is to develop a set of realistic scenarios to reflect the demographic 
changes expected for Solrød and the surrounding areas based on upcoming development projects. 
The base-data, which was provided by Solrød Kommune and the Data and Model Center at DTU, is 
forecasted into relevant scenarios based on the input given by these institutes. This demographic 
data then serves as a guide for modeling the traffic demand to be used as input in the traffic 
assignment model, which is used to gain an understanding of how the travel behavior of the users in 
the network are expected to change with the construction of Trylleskov Strand Station. The traffic 
assignment modeling is implemented in ArcGIS using the Orestad Traffic Model (OTM) and the 
Traffic Analyst (TA) tool. 
The results from the traffic assignment model and the estimated construction and maintenance 
costs of the station (from previous reports produced by Solrød Kommune and DSB) are used to 
formulate a traditional Cost Benefit Analysis (CBA) to assess the feasibility of the project under the 
given traffic demand scenarios. The CBA is a useful tool because it provides a clear, economic 
analysis of how the expected benefits of the project compare to its costs and, thus, of its feasibility. 
It is also the most widely-accepted method of appraisal in transport infrastructure studies and, 
furthermore, the method of appraisal suggested by the Danish Ministry of Transport 
(Transportministeriet 2015). Thus, the CBA is necessary for obtaining socio-economic results which 
will be considered professionally valid. 
Despite its benefits, research in transport assessment has shown that CBA is not always a sufficient 
indicator of the feasibility or expected success of a transport infrastructure project, as there are 
many complex social and environmental impacts of these types of projects that cannot be assessed 
monetarily, as the CBA requires. This is particularly true in situations involving significant 
demographic changes with unknown outcomes. Therefore, the CBA presented in this project is 
expanded with a form of strategic analysis, which follows the widely respected methodology of 
Multi-Actor Multi-Criteria Analysis (MAMCA) (Macharis 2007). This methodology provides a 
framework for assessing the feasibility of the project with respect to sustainability criteria and 
includes relevant stakeholders of the project throughout the assessment process. 
The results of the traffic analysis, the socio-economic analysis and the strategic analysis all 
contribute uniquely to an understanding of the feasibility of the Trylleskov Strand Station project.

Each analysis method cannot capture the complexity and multi-faceted nature of the expected 
success and short-comings of the project alone. However, together, the results of these analysis 
methods can provide wide and fully-encompassing understandings of the project that approach all 
meaningful considerations from all relevant perspectives. This complete understanding of both the 
overall and more specific and internal strengths and complications of the Trylleskov Strand Station 
project, can be used to formulate an assessment of the project’s feasibility, as well as, to frame a 
general consultancy of the steps that can be taken in order to ensure that the project has a 
sustainable future.  
This report showcases the complete transport appraisal analysis preformed throughout the project 
regarding the construction of the new Trylleskov Strand Station. It will include relevant background 
information on Solrød Kommune and the surrounding areas of interest (Section 1.2 and 1.3), as well 
as a literature review regarding relevant research within transport appraisal and, specifically, within 
each of the applied methodologies. The specific methodologies used within the study will be 
explained, along with the data and the techniques used to manipulate and forecast the data to 
reflect appropriate scenarios for investigation (Section 2). The results from the analyses will be 
presented and discussed (Section 3) and then consolidated into a summative recommendation 
(Section 4). 
1.2 Introduction to Trylleskov Strand 
The primary objectives of constructing Trylleskov Strand Station are to provide accessible public 
transport to the residents of Trylleskov Strand and to enhance the overall goals for the community. 
The development of Trylleskov Strand began in 2009, with the first dwellings available for the new 
residents to move-in in 2013. The final homes are planned to be finished in 2019. In total, there will 
be 750 homes once the construction phase is complete: 75 single-family homes, and the rest condos 
or townhouses. The total area of Trylleskov Strand is 377,000 m2 and is divided into parcels or 
“islands” with groups of similar housing structures (Solrød Kommune 2008). The composition of the 
area can be seen in Figure 1.

the already existing natural landscape. Solrød’s planners explain that, “Trylleskov Strand should be a

Another goal for the village is to limit the amount of car traffic passing through central areas, so as 
to ensure the safety and freedom of all residents, particularly the free-playing children. Ideally, the 
residents will rely on modes of transport other than the private car in order to limit the car-traffic 
entering, exiting and traveling through the community. In 2016, an expected share of 75% of 
residents commuted to work outside of Solrød Kommune (Danmarks Statistik 2017) with an average 
commute distance of 22.7 km (Center for Vækstanalyse et al. 2016). It is, therefore, anticipated that 
the travel behavior for the residents of Trylleskov Strand will follow these trends, which means that 
they will likely rely on fast-modes of transport to travel to and from work. Without sufficient access 
to public transport, it is likely that Trylleskov Strand will be a car-reliant society and will not achieve 
this goal of being a low-traffic living environment.

Strand Station (see Figure 4). Although Solrød Strand Station is within a reasonable cycle distance (<

Since Trylleskov Strand Station will be serviced by existing s-train lines, the necessary infrastructure 
costs and impacts are relatively low when compared to other new transport infrastructure projects; 
there is no need for new track infrastructure, only new station infrastructure. Similarly, there will be 
very limited changes to the operational needs and costs of the railway. Although the final design of 
the station has not yet been decided, the expected construction cost is 87 million Danish kroner (in 
2017 prices)(Solrød Kommune 2006).  
A station at Trylleskov Strand was included in the plans for prospective station from the original 
plans for Køge Bugt railway, but it originally did not have the passenger demand to support its 
construction (Roloff 2007). With the development of the Trylleskov Strand area, the feasibility of the 
new station has ground for reevaluation. However, it is important that this feasibility assessment 
include social and environmental components in addition to the traditional economic analysis that 
has been performed in past research (DSB 2006)(Solrød Kommune 2006). 
1.3 Previous research into the feasibility of Trylleskov Strand Station 
The idea of constructing a new s-train station at Trylleskov Strand along the Køge Bugt railway is not 
a new idea within Denmark’s transport planning community. The potential for Trylleskov Strand 
Station was included in the original plans for the Køge Bugt railway in 1966, as well as in Denmark’s 
public transport traffic plan in 2003 and the corresponding regional traffic plans in 2005 (DSB 2006). 
Although the plans for the station have not yet been realized, the potential for doing so has been 
under consideration for many years.

The first true evaluations of the station’s feasibility were carried out by Solrød Kommune’s Teknik og 
Miljø department and DSB’s Traffic Analysis and Planning department in 2006. Two reports were 
produced in this process: one being the product of DSB’s research (DSB 2006) and the other being 
the product of Solrød Kommune’s research (Solrød Kommune 2006)3. Both reports use the 
population prognosis data for the new Trylleskov Strand area in order to compute the expected 
passenger demand for the new station. This passenger demand is combined with calculations of the 
expected costs of the project in order to determine the economic feasibility of the project. The 
calculations used to conduct the cost-benefit analysis in these reports are purely financial in nature, 
as they evaluate the ratio of expected project costs to the expected increase in ticket sales due to 
the new passengers serviced by Trylleskov Strand Station. 
DSB estimated that there will be between 925 and 1,105 daily users of Trylleskov Strand Station 
(dependent on the number of previous passengers lost due to the extended trip time with the new 
stop), where 623 of these are new passengers. This calculation was based on the number of 
passengers expected to use the new station from each zone around the station. This number of 
passengers is a specific share of the total population within that zone, depending on the distance 
from that zone to the station. For example, there are 464 residents in an area that lies within 0.6 to 
1 km from the station. It is therefore expected that 20% of these residents, or 93 passengers, will use 
the station per day. Under the assumptions of a construction cost of approximately 4.5 million dkk, 
an annual operation cost of 800,000 dkk, an annual rent of 6%, an expected daily ticket intake of 24 
dkk per passenger and a 5% uncertainty buffer, the necessary number of new daily passengers 
needed for the annual ticket income to break even with the annual expenditure on the project costs 
is 621. This corresponds to a total of 1,103 passengers using Trylleskov Strand Station (DSB 2006). 
Based on these calculations, it was claimed that there was not adequate demand for the new station 
to support its construction (Roloff 2007). 
In their independent investigation of the potential for Trylleskov Strand Station, Solrød Kommune 
included the construction costs for Banedanmark in addition to those calculated by DSB, which 
resulted in a total construction cost of just under 72 million dkk. Aside from the construction cost, all 
other input parameters for the financial assessment were assumed to be the same as those used by 
DSB. These calculations led to a necessary 889 new passengers per day, or 1,849 total passengers 
using Trylleskov Strand Station per day. Following a similar method for calculating the expected 
number of daily passengers based on resident density and proximity to the station, Solrød Kommune 
found that 5,396 passengers could be expected to use the new station daily. However, this 
calculation was followed by a call for more precise data and calculation methods (Solrød Kommune 
2006). 
Although the precise design of the station is not specified in either report, it is assumed that the 
general design follows that of an “island-platform” configuration, such as the station-design 
examples displayed in Figure 5. The construction costs are then based off of those known for

2 Methodology 
The feasibility analysis in this report consists of two major forms of appraisal: socio-economic 
assessment and strategic assessment. The methodology used for the socio-economic analysis has 
been designed with respect to the Danish Ministry of Transport’s manual for socio-economic 
appraisal of transport infrastructure projects (Transportministeriet 2015) in order to remain 
consistent with the current practice within transport project appraisal in Denmark. This socio-
economic assessment method relies on traffic modeling and analysis to determine the impact that 
the new infrastructure will have on the travel behavior of the users in the transport network. The 
transport model used in this study is the OTM model, which can be used to model road transport 
and public transport in the extended region of Greater Copenhagen. 
The strategic assessment in this study has been designed to follow the methodology of Multi-Actor 
Multi-Criteria Analysis (MAMCA) as presented by (Cornet 2016). This form of assessment is useful 
because it allows for the inclusion of a variety of perspectives on sustainability, in terms of both the 
criteria considered in the assessment, as well as, the diversity of stakeholders contributing 
throughout the process. 
This chapter includes one section for each of the three primary research fields used in the feasibility 
analysis of this report: traffic modeling and analysis, socio-economic analysis and strategic analysis. 
In each section, a literature review of relevant research within the field is presented, followed by an 
explanation of the methodological framework used for that assessment tool. The methodological 
framework includes all of the assumptions made to ensure that the methodology sufficiently models 
the specific attributes and considerations of the Trylleskov Strand Station project.

2.1 Traffic Modeling and Analysis 
The traffic model used in this study is the Orestad Traffic Model (OTM) developed by the Danish 
Transport Research Institute and the Technical University of Denmark. The OTM is a passenger 
traffic demand and assignment model, which can be used to model road traffic or transit traffic 
within the extended greater Copenhagen region. Although the National Transport Model is a more 
extensive model which uses more advanced modeling techniques than the OTM, the OTM includes 
more precise zone modeling, which corresponds to a higher level of precision in the OD trip data at 
the demand level. Since the size of the Trylleskov Strand Station project is quite small relative to the 
transport infrastructure projects that are typically evaluated with these models, it is crucial to use a 
model which allows for more precise modeling at the demand level in order to ensure an acceptable 
level of accuracy in the assignment results. 
The socio-economic analysis in this feasibility study relies on the traffic model output for both the 
public transport users and the road transport users. Although the only infrastructure changes been 
made in this project are contained within the public transport network, it is still expected that a 
number of private road users in the surrounding area will switch modes from private road transport 
to public transport. This expectation is grounded in the assumption that the addition of the new 
station will increase the accessibility to the public transport network (by decreasing the travel time 
of the access/egress portion of the trip) and thus, make public transport more desirable to some 
users than the private car (Givoni & Rietveld 2007; Rietveld 2000; Krygsman et al. 2004). Therefore, 
modeling the effects of Trylleskov Strand Station on the road network is also important in 
comprehensively evaluating its socio-economic value. 
2.1.1 Assignment models 
The OTM road and public transportation assignment models are run in ArcGIS by ESRI, using the 
Traffic Analyst (TA) geoprocessing tools by Rapidis. The input to the models includes a network 
dataset of geospatial data for the relevant transport network being analyzed, trip matrices to 
describe the travel patterns of the users and relevant parameters describing the characteristics of 
the network and the users of the network. These input data are used to calculate the best route 
through the network between each pair of model zones (see Sections 2.1.1.1 and 2.1.1.2 for more 
information about the route choice modeling) and then to assign the appropriate amount of traffic 
between each zone-pair, as dictated by the trip matrices, to the corresponding network features. 
In the road network, each road section is represented by a link and the link-network is connected to 
the model zones by zone connectors. Therefore, the road assignment model uses the assignment of 
traffic to these links and connectors in order to represent the travel behavior of the users through 
the network from their origin zone to their destination zone. The process of the road assignment 
model is outlined in Figure 6. 
The public assignment method uses schedule-based route choice modeling, which requires precise 
schedule data for each line running in the public transport network. Therefore, the public transit 
network is much more complicated than the road network and includes significantly more inter-
related model datasets in order to account for the various features of both the transit infrastructure 
and scheduling data. An overview of the public assignment model process can be seen in Figure 7.

It is important to note that this generalized cost function does not include any congestion effects. 
While congestion plays a large role in route choice in road transport, it is assumed to have a minimal 
impact in public route choice, since the infrastructure capacity is not as strictly defined for public 
transport as it is for road transport. This assumption is based on the observed characteristics of the 
Copenhagen transit network, and is not equally applicable for all public transit networks. 
Due to the relatively small-scale of the infrastructure changes in this project, the level of 
stochasticity that is normally included in the model to account for variations in user preferences was 
found to be masking the actual impacts of the project. Therefore, in the collection of the final 
results, all model parameters were set to be deterministic, including the stochasticity in the timing of 
each launch. The danger with removing the stochasticity on the launch timing, is that it can mean 
that all users arrive to a station with the same amount of waiting time until the next bus or train. 
This waiting time could be a minimal value or, in the worst-case-scenario, it could be almost the 
same as the cycle-time, thus increasing the generalized cost of travel for every traveler at that 
station. In order to compensate for this change, the number of launches was set to 120, meaning 
that a group of users entered the network every minute during the launch period. This ensures that 
all variations in the relation between launch time and run time are modeled, thus preserving the 
robustness of the results.

2.1.5 Induced traffic 
The concept of induced traffic is based on the knowledge that the implementation of new transport 
infrastructure leads to increased transport demand within the network. When users experience time 
savings due to the new infrastructure, a portion of these time savings will be used to make new 
and/or different trips within the network. These impacts are particularly important to consider in the 
analysis of road traffic projects because the induced trips can account for a decrease between 50% 
and 100% of the time-savings initially calculated due to the increased congestion caused by the 
increase in traffic density (Goodwin 1996). However, the induced traffic is also important to consider 
in the analysis of public transport infrastructure projects, because of the expectation that a share of 
road users will shift mode from road transport to public transport in order to make use of the new 
transit infrastructure. This shift can be accounted for through the induced traffic calculations. 
The OTM traffic models calculate the effects of the latent traffic demand by iteratively preforming 
induced traffic calculations based on the assignment results, and then re-running the assignment 
model based on the new traffic demand matrices. The public assignment model only includes one 
induced traffic calculation since congestion effects are assumed to be negligible. This process can be 
seen in Figure 18. The road assignment model, on the other hand, requires a more complex iterative 
process to calculate the actual traffic assignments due to the interdependence of traffic demand, 
density, speed, congestion and route choice. This calculation process is displayed in Figure 19. 
In the OTM, it is assumed that the total traffic demand has an elasticity of 35%, which is divided into 
two categorizations: 25% of the time savings are assumed to be used for induced trips within the 
same zone pairs, while 10% are assumed to be used for trips to or from other zones (5% from the 
original zone to other zones, and 5% to the original zone from other zones). This model is based on 
the findings from British road projects (Goodwin 1996) and Amsterdam’s orbital motorway (Kroes et 
al. 1996) and has been adapted to meet the expected traffic conditions and elasticities of the 
Copenhagen traffic network. 
Although the new infrastructure constructed in this project is not part of the road network 
infrastructure, it is assumed that the same induced traffic calculations should be used in order to 
determine the latent trips that will join the network due to the new public transport infrastructure 
and changing road traffic dynamics due to the shift of users between modes.

2.2 Socio-Economic Analysis 
2.2.1 Financial Affordability Analysis 
A purely financial assessment is included in this project to create a form of analysis that can be 
compared directly with the appraisal calculations performed by Solrød Kommune and DSB in their 
initial assessments of the feasibility of Trylleskov Strand Station. The methodology used for this type 
of assessment thus follows the methodology implemented by these institutions. 
Two methods of this type of financial analysis are used in this report in order to assess the financial 
affordability of the project. The first method follows the simple methodology applied by Roloff 
(2007) in DSB’s original financial analysis for the Trylleskov Strand Station project, while the 
calculations used in the second method follow the more complex methodology of the traditional 
cost-benefit analysis. 
The basis for both methods of analysis is the change in the number of kilometers traveled by the 
public transport users between the base and alternative scenarios. These values can be used to 
compute the total increase in ticket sales expected to be collected due to the construction of 
Trylleskov Strand Station by multiplying the total change in kilometers traveled by the unit-price of 
1.14 dkk/km (Transport DTU Data- og Modelcenteret 2017). This value is given in 2010 prices in 
order to remain consistent with the project costs and other calculation values used in the various 
assessments throughout this report. 
Both methods of analysis are performed for each assumed scenario of traffic growth within the 
Trylleskov Strand area due to the expected population growth scenarios modeled in trip matrices 
OD1, OD2 and OD3 (see Table 5). Furthermore, the analysis is performed for each of the assumed 
shares of weekday traffic occurring between 7-9am: 20%, 25% and 30%. This expansion of the 
analysis helps to provide an understanding of the project’s robustness and enables the consideration 
of how changes to the input parameters impact the financial feasibility of the project. 
2.2.1.1 
Simple financial assessment 
The simple financial appraisal methodology calculates the financial feasibility of the project as the 
sum of the expected annual expenditure required to pay-off the initial construction costs, the 
change in the annual operational costs and the change in the annual income from ticket sales. This 
analysis is performed in order to identify the project’s ability to pay for itself under the given 
economic assumptions of rent and depreciation allowance. It is this methodology that was used by 
Roloff (2007) in DSB’s financial assessment of the Trylleskov Strand Station project. 
2.2.1.2 
Complex financial assessment 
The complex financial appraisal methodology is used in order to evaluate the costs and ticket-
income over the project lifetime of 50 years, including the assumed effects of traffic growth, price 
growth and price discounting. This methodology is the same as that presented in Section 2.2.2, 
however the traditional benefits included in the cost-benefit analysis calculations are replaced by

2.2.2 Cost-Benefit Analysis 
2.2.2.1 
Literature review 
Cost-benefit analysis (CBA) is a widely accepted socio-economic appraisal framework for decision 
makers to use to improve the quality of their investment decisions (Janic 2003). This assessment 
methodology relies on the assumption that all costs and benefits of a project can be calculated into 
==PAGE:30==
  22 | HOLT 
 
monetary values (Ambrasaite et al. 2011). Once transformed into the same scale, these project costs 
and benefits can be aggregated and compared to determine the absolute or relative total benefit of 
the project (Dasgupta & Pearce 1978). The output of the CBA can then be used to assess how a 
project will perform over its lifetime (or the project assessment period), as well as, how it ranks in 
comparison to other projects. The appealing feature of CBA is that projects that differ with respect 
to size, construction cost, location, transport type, or a multitude of other features, can be 
compared objectively. CBA can be used to determine which alternative design of the same project is 
preferred, but it can also be significantly useful when considering investment decisions for projects 
from the local to the international level, where the investment alternatives are not necessarily 
equivalent. 
The CBA goes beyond traditional financial analysis, as it approaches the analysis from a societal 
perspective, rather than a perspective that focuses solely on the net costs and incomes of the 
investor, operator or other financially-minded entity (Wright et al. 2009). This is done by including 
benefits like the consumer surplus and the monetary value of the impacts on externalities such as air 
quality, noise, climate and accidents. The calculation of these externality impacts as financial values 
typically requires unit price indices and traffic scenario analysis. 
Consumer surplus 
The benefit to individuals of the society considered in the analysis, typically referred to as consumer 
surplus, is conceptually grounded in micro-economic welfare-theory and the assumption that all 
individuals are rational consumers, and therefore, will always seek to maximize their individual 
welfare or utility (Dasgupta & Pearce 1978; Gissel 1999). It also assumes that the total welfare of 
society is simply the aggregate sum of the welfare of all individuals in society. This assumption is 
made in order to model societal welfare in a single function, which follows the same assumption of 
rationality: society will always choose to maximize its welfare or utility (Gissel 1999). This 
assumption can be challenged from a truly socio-economic perspective, as it means that the benefit 
to one user is equally beneficial to society, regardless of how well- or poor-off that individual user 
already is. However, this consideration of fairness cannot be accounted for in the CBA. The 
objectivity of the CBA, though, does ensure the consistency and transparency of the results (Barfod 
& Leleur 2012). 
The consumer surplus is measured as the net benefit for the individuals of the society due to a 
change in the price of a good or, in the assessment of transport infrastructure projects, the cost of 
transport. If the total consumer surplus is positive, then the project is beneficial to the users and if it 
is negative, then it is detrimental to the users. The cost of transport includes both the material cost 
of the transport (in road transport this can be the cost due to wear-and-tear, for example), as well 
as, the cost of the time spent traveling. 
The consumer surplus calculations follow an economic principle known as, “the rule-of-the-half”. 
This principle is included in consumer surplus calculations due to the latent demand that is realized 
when the price of a good decreases. In transport, this is called induced traffic and refers to the new 
trips that are made in the network once the network conditions are enhanced due to an 
improvement in the transport infrastructure. Under these conditions, it is assumed that the existing 
users, or those who were originally willing to pay the previous price for the good, will acquire the 
value of the consumer surplus, while the new users will only acquire half of the value. The total

2.2.2.2 
Introduction to methodology: The five-step process 
The socio-economic analysis process suggested by the Danish Ministry of Transport includes five 
major steps, as outlined in Figure 22. 
 
Figure 22 – The five steps of the socio-economic appraisal process (Transportministeriet 2015) 
Step 1 is to define the overall scope and goal of the project. This includes determining the base 
scenario (also known as the business-as-usual scenario), which is used for comparison of the 
alternative scenarios. 
Step 2 is to define all possible project alternatives that should be considered in the appraisal 
process. The alternatives will vary from each other, but should be clearly defined in order to increase 
the precision of the resulting analysis. 
Step 3 involves the quantification and valuation of the expected costs and benefits of each 
alternative over the lifetime of the project. These values are measured as the difference between 
the considered alternative and the base scenario, in order to generate comparative values. The 
primary project cost is the construction cost and is typically given by an initial cost estimate or 
project bid. The benefits of the project (or dis-benefits, if negative) are calculated using unit-prices, 
which typically correspond to a price per kilometer traveled in the network. These calculations, 
therefore, rely on the output data from a traffic prognosis, which is typically calculated using a 
relevant traffic model. The unit prices used in this study are those suggested by the Danish Ministry 
of Transport: “Transportøkonomiske Enhedspriser 1.71” (Transport DTU Data- og Modelcenteret 
2017). The traffic model used in this study is the OTM model, the road and public transport traffic 
model for the greater Copenhagen region presented in Section 2.1.1. 
Step 4 is to aggregate the weighted costs and benefits of each alternative into a final net present 
value (NPV). The weighting of the costs and benefits is also known as discounting, and is applied in 
order to differentiate between the significance of short-term and long-term costs and benefits. 
Additional criteria, such as the BCR and IRR, can also be used to evaluate the feasibility of the 
project. 
Step 5 is used when more than one project alternative is evaluated in order to determine which 
alternative preforms best under the specified considerations.

The measures used for feasibility assessment in this report are: 
 
Net Present Value (NPV) – the sum of the discounted costs and benefits over the project 
evaluation period (here: project years -3 to 49). This value gives an assessment of the 
absolute feasibility of the project. If the NPV is greater than 0, then the project is assumed to 
be feasible. This measure is useful when determining whether a single project will be 
feasible or not, as is the goal in this project. 
 
Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR) – the ratio of the total discounted project benefits to costs. This 
value gives an assessment of the relative feasibility of the project. If the BCR is greater than 
1, then the project is assumed to be feasible. The relative benefit to cost unit can be an 
especially meaningful indicator when deciding which projects to invest in given a certain 
budget constraint. However, it can also be useful in understanding the robustness of a 
project within a single-project feasibility assessment. 
 
Internal Rate of Return (IRR) – the discount rate required to give an NPV of 0. The IRR is also 
a useful measure of robustness, as it shows how applying varying weights to current and 
future values can change the overall feasibility of the project.

2.2.3 Feasibility Risk Assessment 
Feasibility risk assessment is a method of sensitivity analysis which models the robustness of the CBA 
results with respect to possible cost overruns. This is particularly necessary in transport appraisal 
projects due to the tendency for project appraisals to underestimate costs and overestimate 
benefits (Flyvbjerg 2008; Flyvbjerg et al. 2003; Ascher 1970; Banister & Berechman 2000). The risk 
assessment methodology proposed by Flyvbjerg (2008) applies reference class forecasting (RCF), an 
extension of prospect theory, which was developed by Kahneman & Tversky (1979). RCF draws upon 
the analysis of past projects which are similar in nature to the one currently under investigation, and 
identifies uplift factors for various levels of acceptable uncertainty that correspond to the average 
error in the estimation of the projects’ construction costs (Salling 2008). 
Flyvbjerg (2008) presents the relevant uplift factors for projects of types road, rail and fixed-link, 
based on the RCF analysis of similar large-scale transport infrastructure projects. However, he 
explains that there is a lack of historical data and research for station building projects and, thus, 
insufficient evidence from which to generate the complete corresponding probability functions for 
these types of projects. Therefore, only estimates for the minimally and maximally accepted 
uncertainty levels of 50% and 80% (or accepted chances of cost overrun of 50% and 20%, 
respectively) are given for this type of project. 
In order to model the feasibility risk assessment of the station building project evaluated in this 
report, the intermittent uplift factors for uncertainty levels of 60% and 70% are estimated by 
generating a resultant probability function that follows the general shape of those modeled for the 
project types which are specified by Flyvbjerg: road, rail and fixed-link projects. The resultant 
probability function is displayed in Figure 23.

2.3 Strategic Analysis 
2.3.1 Literature review 
Cost-benefit analysis is a useful and widely accepted tool for transport appraisal and decision 
support due to its transparency, objectivity, comparability and systematic design. However, there 
are also many issues surrounding the socio-economic methodology. The central role of unit prices in 
the CBA calculations means that the analysis relies on the assumption that all of the impacts of a 
project can be monetized. This is notably problematic when it comes to social or environmental 
externalities, such as impacts on accessibility, local employment, urban development, transport 
connectivity or biodiversity and nature. These criteria may be difficult to quantify in economic units, 
but are crucial to the assessment of a project’s impact, particularly from a perspective that 
prioritizes sustainability (Engelbrecht 2009; Næss 2006). 
Multi Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) was developed in order to provide a framework for project 
evaluation and analysis that can include various types of strategic analysis criteria and, thus, 
supplement the purely economic analysis that is provided by the CBA (Barfod & Leleur 2014). Much 
like the CBA, the MCDA was designed in order to provide support to decision makers in the process 
of preforming complex decisions. The MCDA does this by breaking the complex decision into smaller, 
more manageable assessments which can later be combined into a meaningful total measure of the 
overall performance of a project and then used for judgement or comparison purposes. 
The central components of the MCDA assessment method are: a set of possible alternatives, a set of 
criteria which are used to assess the characteristics of the alternatives, and the preferential 
weightings assigned by the decision makers to each criteria (Barfod & Leleur 2014). Scores are then 
given by the decision makers for each of the possible alternatives regarding their performance with 
respect to each criterion. The goal for the evaluation process is that all decisions be made objectively 
with reference to indicators and, when possible, by way of data-based research. The criteria weights 
and scores are then combined systematically to compute a value measurement of each alternative’s 
relative performance. The most commonly used additive value function is given by:

Although there are variations to the MCDA methods applied in practice, the method used in this 
report is a normative approach, which assumes that decision makers can express decisive and 
transitive preferences for all features of the decision problem at hand (Lootsma 1999; Barfod & 
Leleur 2014). The specific normative methodology used in this case follows the REMBRANDT 
technique, which implements a multiplicative value function and relies on the use of a geometric 
scale for direct ratings and the geometric mean for the calculation of impact scores (Olson 1996; 
Barzilai et al. 1987; Lootsma 1988; Barfod & Leleur 2012). The specifics of the REMBRANDT

While the MCDA is generally superior to socio-economic appraisal methods, such as the CBA, 
because of its ability to include a wide range of strategic criteria in the analysis process, it is still 
significantly lacking in its inclusion or representation of stakeholders. Stakeholder perspectives can 
be evaluated through sensitivity analysis, where variations in the criteria weightings are investigated 
to model the strategic preferences of the different stakeholder groups (Macharis et al. 2006). 
However, this analysis is typically done by the decision support experts carrying out the MCDA 
research and calculations; the stakeholders are left out of the rational assessment process that 
constitutes the core of the MCDA procedure (Cornet 2016). 
Stakeholder inclusion is particularly valuable in transport appraisal due to the large role that political 
factors contribute to the decision making process for infrastructure investments. De Brucker et al. 
explain that “by bringing together all stakeholders in a carefully designed forum…it may become 
much easier to construct and implement solutions acceptable to the community of stakeholders, 
thereby creating value added for society as a whole” (De Brucker et al. 2011). Belton (2002, p.60) 
suggests that this is especially true when the stakeholders involved in the project have differing 
ideas regarding which aspects of the project should be prioritized.  
Cornet (2016) proposes the approach referred to as “communicative rationality” within planning 
literature (Willson 2001; Sanderson 2001; Healey 1998; Allmendinger 2009; Næss 2006) as a solution 
for transforming the complex and politically-driven decision process into inclusive and interactive 
processes where discussion, deliberation and negotiation between stakeholders are seen as crucial 
aspects of the decision making procedure. The Multi-Actor Multi-Criteria Analysis (MAMCA) method 
developed by Macharis (2005; 2007) applies this idea by systematically incorporating stakeholders 
into the analysis process of the traditional MCDA. In this way, “each stakeholder group can then 
assess the different alternatives in terms of its own objectives/criteria” (De Brucker et al. 2011). The 
MAMCA has been found to be a useful tool within the field of transport appraisal (Macharis et al. 
2012), particularly when dealing with projects that include scientific and engineering complexities, as 
well as, strategic and politic complexities.

Step 3 – Collect stakeholder comparative assessment data 
The third step in the strategic analysis framework constitutes the majority of the process of actually 
assessing the relevant project alternatives. In the traditional MCDA, there are three major steps to 
the analysis, which are typically all carried out by the relevant research group. The research group 
first selects a set of criteria which the project alternatives are to be evaluated with respect to, then 
determines the comparative weights of these criteria and finally preforms the comparative 
assessments of each project alternative with respect to each criterion. The MAMCA follows a similar 
framework, however the entire analysis process is done by a group of relevant actors or 
stakeholders in the project, rather than only by decision support analysis experts. While there are 
different possibilities for how the assessment data can be collect from these actors, the method that 
is used in this research is an online survey constructed and distributed using the free software

Step 4 – Combine data into overall assessment of project alternative performances 
Criteria selection 
The first step in the MAMCA calculation process is to determine the final set of evaluation criteria to 
include in the assessment. In this case, the constraint for inclusion is that the criterion be selected by 
at least 33% of the survey participants. This ensures that there is some consensus across 
stakeholders on the importance of the criterion and that it does not represent the interests 
prioritized by one only possible perspective.

criteria across all of the survey responses. The average rank-score of each criterion is then converted

As this project assessment follows the MAMCA methodology, the final geometric mean value for

The normalized score for each criterion with respect to each alternative, which is the value that is 
used in the final calculation for that alternative’s assessment score, is then its geometric mean 
divided by the sum of the geometric means of all criteria included in the final assessment set, with 
respect to that specific alternative. 
Total assessment scores 
The total assessment score for each of the considered alternatives (in this case, the project scenario 
and the base scenario) is calculated as the sum of the normalized criteria scores multiplied by their 
corresponding normalized weight. These total scores are, thus, also normalized and provide an 
indication of the likelihood that each alternative will be the preferred alternative.

assessment scores and their products, across each category of criteria. The second set of criteria

3.1.1.1 
Discussion 
Imbalance between passengers traveling to/from each direction 
It is expected that the number of trips entering the zone and exiting the zone on any given day are 
relatively equal and that the number of trips between zone pairs is also evenly distributed across 
each direction, since it is assumed that the majority of trips are made in pairs. These pairs of trips, 
which are referred to as tours, include an outgoing trip from an origin zone to a destination zone, 
and a return trip from the original destination zone back to the original origin zone. Since these tours 
are typically completed within the timeframe of a single day, it would be expected that the total 
number of trips out of a zone in a specific direction, with a specific purpose, would be roughly 
equivalent to the number of trips into that zone from the same direction and with the same 
purpose. However, the results presented in Table 21 through Table 23 indicate a significant 
                                                          
16 Assuming 25% share of weekday traffic between 7-9am interval 
==PAGE:56==
  48 | HOLT 
 
difference in the number of trips made by the users of Trylleskov Strand Station in each direction 
along the A and E train lines for each trip category. 
This deviation from the expected results is likely due to the fact that the model time period is the 
average weekday morning peak period of 7-9am. Since the Trylleskov Strand area is primarily 
dominated by residences, the majority of users are expected to make trips from this model zone to 
another model zone, which is more heavily dominated by business or leisure points-of-interest, 
during the period or 7-9am. Since this model time period is scaled to represent the traffic patterns 
over the average weekday, the distribution of trips remains the same as that for the morning peak. 
Therefore, the return trips, which would form the complete tour, or trip pairing, with those trips 
made during the morning peak period, are replaced by duplicates of the outgoing trips made 
between 7-9am. 
Implications of forecasting the traffic demand at the zonal level 
One limitation with the method of modeling the traffic growth due to the population growth in 
Trylleskov Strand by scaling the entire trip matrix for the model zone surrounding the area, is that it 
is not necessarily true that the resulting traffic demand will be representative of the expected traffic 
demand. This is particularly true in relation to the specific distribution patterns of the demand. For 
example, the traffic demand for the original model zone containing Trylleskov Strand is dominated 
by trips with the categorization of “other”. Although it is possible that this will still be the case with 
the effects of the population growth, it is likely that there will be a greater number of commute trips 
than leisure trips once the Trylleskov Strand residents are fully settled within the area. This is due to 
the expectation that the majority of the residents will be commuting to work, many to Copenhagen 
or to Køge, and therefore will be motivated to use public transport, particularly if they are provided 
efficient access to it through the construction of Trylleskov Strand Station. This consideration was 
taken into account and modeled by applying a significantly larger growth factor to the commute 
trips than the other trips. However, it is still not guaranteed that this methodology can account for 
the expected growth in the total traffic demand per trip category. 
It would have been most effective to use a model zone that corresponds directly to the area of 
Trylleskov Strand. However, the model data is not built to include the specific traffic demand in this 
area. Furthermore, it is possible that the method of applying the expected traffic growth factors to 
the total traffic within the zone would not have worked efficiently if this more specific zone had 
been included in the model, as the data associated with the model zones are from 2014. At that 
point, there were essentially no residents living within the corresponding zone, which leads to a 
similarly low traffic demand, despite the growth factors applied to the demand matrices. 
Implications of altering the stochasticity parameters 
The OTM was designed to model large-scale projects such as the Copenhagen Metro (Vuk et al. 
2007), the Copenhagen Harbor Tunnel, a reorganization of the Copenhagen bus network that is 
referred to as the Stambusnet project, and the FORTRIN project, which investigated implications of 
road-pricing schemes within the Greater Copenhagen Region (Jovicic & Hansen 2001). The validation 
of the model and the accepted level of precision in the network design, as well as in the theoretical 
design of the route choice and assignment models, has been defined with specific consideration to 
these types of projects. For example, the utility functions of the route choice models include 
stochastic terms in order to model the uncertainty of the users’ preferences and, thus, their chosen 
routes. The stochasticity at the route choice level is necessary for calculating realistic assignment 
results and the uncertainty or variation between iterations is not significant compared to the 
==PAGE:57==
  49 | HOLT 
 
magnitude of the final results, due to the large scope of the projects. However, when the scope of 
the project is significantly smaller, as is the case with the Trylleskov Strand Station project, the 
variations between iterations of the model due to stochasticity proved to dominate the actual 
change in the output results between project scenarios. Therefore, the stochasticity was removed 
from the model and, instead, the possible variation in the route choice and corresponding cost of 
transport was modeled by setting the number of launches to 120, meaning that one share of 
travelers between each zone pair is launched into the network every minute of the two-hour model 
period.  
Although the choice to remove the stochasticity from the model was made in order to generate 
results that are as detectable and significant, as well as realistic and useful, as possible, this choice 
also has implications for the validity of the results and thus the validity of the subsequent analysis of 
Trylleskov Strand Station. The specific implications of this cannot be identified without further 
analysis and validation through either shared or revealed preference travel data representing the 
relevant users of the system, as was done by Vuk & Hansen (2006) for the original OTM using data 
from Denmark’s National Travel Survey (TU). As the assumptions and data used for the analysis of 
the Trylleskov Strand Station project are completely reliant on the expected development of the 
Trylleskov Strand area, which has yet to be actualized, it is not possible to perform this type of 
validation analysis with the sufficient level of accuracy needed to draw conclusions about the 
accuracy of the model under the re-defined assumptions. 
The resultant discrepancies caused by the changes to the input parameters of the model are 
expected to be absorbed in the general uncertainties of the project, which are largely caused by the 
fact that it has not been researched deeply or defined precisely and, furthermore, is grounded in 
future residential developments that have not yet been actualized. It is still important, though, to 
maintain an awareness of these issues with the base assumptions and modeling specifications, 
which are at the core of the traffic analysis results, and thus, the socio-economic results. 
It could be more effective to conduct the traffic analysis of the Trylleskov Strand Station project with 
a different demand and assignment model that is able to sufficiently handle the scope of the project 
in a more accurate manner. This could be a model with more precise local network design, trip 
demand data or modeling of the multi-modal implications of the new station. However, at this time, 
no such model is known by the author, Solrød Kommune’s administration or the Data and Model 
Center at Transport DTU. Therefore, the OTM was determined to be the most appropriate and 
useful model for the traffic analysis of the Trylleskov Strand Station project.

3.1.2.1 
Discussion 
Unexpected increase in km-driven for some categories 
The increase in the total number of kilometers driven by some of the road user categories in OD 
scenarios 1 and 3 diverge from the expected and overall trend of a decrease in the number of 
kilometers driven between the base and alternative scenarios. However, the absolute values of 
these categories are low in comparison to the categories that make up the largest portion of the 
total kilometers driven. Furthermore, these categories correspond to trips made by vans and trucks, 
and thereby are not impacted by the modal shift between road and rail, as the new transport 
infrastructure is assumed to only be appealing to private car users. Therefore, it may be assumed 
that these variations from the expected results are due to uncertainty factors that are possibly 
further exaggerated by differences in the sign of the change in traffic computed in the induced 
traffic calculations. These deviations from the general pattern of a reduction in km-driven are 
important to note, as they do impact the calculations for the externality impacts. However, due to 
the relative significance of these category values, they are assumed to be satisfactorily absorbed into 
the total overall values for the km-driven per category. 
Comparison of model zone sizes to those used in the public transport model 
The model zones used in the network data for the OTM road transport model are different from 
those used in the OTM public transport model. A comparison of the corresponding zone 
configurations can be seen in Figure 25. This posed some complications in the modeling of the traffic 
growth within Trylleskov Strand due to the new housing developments and resultant population 
growth. This growth was most effectively modeled by scaling the entire trip-demand matrix for the 
model zone containing the Trylleskov Strand area. However, since the model zone used in the road 
transport model is notably larger than that used in the public transport model, the growth factors 
are not completely equivalent. The implications of these variations in the model zones are deemed 
to be insignificant compared to other forms of uncertainty in the model, for example, in the actual 
growth factors themselves. However, these additional uncertainty or imprecision factors could have 
been avoided if it was possible to model the growth using the same model zones for both the public 
transport and road transport assignment models. 
Implications of altering the stochasticity parameters 
As explained in Section 3.1.1.1, the precision of the OTM is not entirely satisfactory for the scope of 
the Trylleskov Strand Station project. Just as the stochasticity was removed from the public transport 
route choice model, it was also removed from the road transport route choice model by setting the 
route choice parameters such that all road users simply choose the path that maximizes their utility. 
This choice to limit the uncertainty modeled in the road traffic assignment model has the same 
==PAGE:60==
  52 | HOLT 
 
implications for the results of the traffic analysis as those described in Section 3.1.1.1 for the similar 
choices made for the public traffic assignment model.

3.2 Socio-Economic Analysis 
The original research performed by DSB to assess the feasibility of Trylleskov Station, included a 
purely economic analysis of its affordability, in terms of the construction and operation costs for DSB 
and Banedanmark and the increase in the total revenue from ticket sales (Roloff 2007). This is a 
worthwhile calculation from the point of view of the operator, as it clarifies the direct rate-of-return 
on the project investment. However, these calculations are purely financial in nature and ignore 
essential social components of what constitutes a socio-economic appraisal, which is the 
cornerstone of most transport appraisal studies. For the benefit of financially-focused stakeholders 
such as DSB and Banedanmark, a similar analytical framework to that found in Roloff (2007) has 
been used to re-evaluate the financial affordability for the Trylleskov Strand Station project based on 
the traffic assignment data collected in this study. This financial affordability analysis has been 
broken down into three methodologies; the results and discussion of these analyses are presented in 
Section 3.2.1. The next sections of this chapter, Section 3.2.2, focuses on the results of the 
comprehensive socio-economic analysis performed using the CBA methodology outlined in Section 
==PAGE:61==
  53 | HOLT 
 
2.2.2. This is followed by the presentation of the FRA results in Section 3.2.3, which can be used to 
methodologically examine the robustness of the CBA results using reference class forecasting (as 
explained in detail in Section 2.2.3).

3.2.1.4 
Discussion 
Comparison of simple and complex financial appraisal methods 
In order to understand the differences between the two methodologies used for the financial 
appraisal, it is interesting to compare the results of the analysis scenarios with similar input 
parameter values. In both appraisal methodologies, the increase in the income from ticket sales 
needed for project feasibility was calculated for all OD scenarios, given the base assumption of a 
25% share of weekday traffic in the 7-9am period. The values calculated using the complex financial 
appraisal method were: 1.61, 1.47 and 1.29 for trips matrices OD1, OD2 and OD3, respectively. 
However, the corresponding values calculated using the simple financial appraisal method were: 
2.20, 2.00 and 1.76, respectively. These values, along with the BCR and the similar ratio of annual 
benefits to write-off payment values, are more meaningful in this context than the absolute 
measurements of the NPV and the sum of all annual costs and incomes. This is due to the drastic 
difference in the magnitude of the absolute measures, which is a result of the differing calculation 
methodologies. 
One conclusion that can be drawn from these results, is that applying an annual depreciation 
allowance of 6% generates a greater annual share of the initial construction cost than that generated 
by evaluating the net-present value of the costs and benefits over the project’s 50-year lifetime, 
when compared with the corresponding annual share of benefits. The net-present value of the 
construction costs is larger in the complex financial appraisal method than the total construction 
cost used in the simple method since the complex method also accounts for the costs of the 
infrastructure with a 20-year lifecycle that are expected to be paid in project-years 19 and 39. 
However, the complex method also accounts for the scrap value of the project infrastructure at the 
end of its lifetime. Furthermore, the higher costs are counter-balanced by the inclusion of the 
positive net benefits realized in each year of the evaluation period. 
These variations in the methodology of each appraisal leads to significant variations in their 
corresponding results. Although each method has its own benefits—the simple method is 
straightforward and only involves easy and transparent calculations, while the complex method 
accounts for various effects to the costs and benefits realized in each year over the project’s lifetime 
that have been deemed crucial to sufficient socio-economic appraisal—and are thereby preferable 
==PAGE:65==
  57 | HOLT 
 
under different circumstances, it is important to note the possibility of obtaining significantly 
different results for the same analysis scenarios through each of the dissimilar methodologies.  
Passenger demand necessary for financial feasibility 
The number of new public transport users deemed necessary for the Trylleskov Strand Station 
project to be financially feasible, and the number of new passengers estimated to enter the public 
transport network due to the construction of the new station, are extremely low in comparison with 
the corresponding figures computed by Solrød Kommune and DSB in the original analysis of the 
project. This indication of inconsistency in the demand estimates suggests the need for a more 
precise modeling of the residential growth within Trylleskov Strand and its corresponding effect on 
the traffic demand. 
If the financial analysis performed in this report and the demand analysis performed in DSB’s original 
report are assumed to be accurate, then the number of potential weekday passengers far exceeds 
the number of weekday passengers needed in order for the project to be financially sustainable. 
There are, of course, inconsistencies and uncertainties in combining the results of differing analyses 
in this way, but it is still a useful practice in order to better understand the implications of each form 
of analysis and, ultimately, the feasibility of the Trylleskov Strand Station project. 
Reality of achieving feasibility 
In both methods of financial analysis, the project costs are assumed to be pre-determined and are 
thereby fixed across scenarios. This decision can be criticized, as it is well-known that the costs of 
transport infrastructure projects are often overestimated (Flyvbjerg 2008). This choice is made under 
the assumption that, due to the relatively small size of the project and the lack of novelty in the 
design of the infrastructure, the uncertainties in the cost are likely to be relatively small. 
Furthermore, it was decided to limit the scope of the analysis by focusing on the possible 
uncertainties in the traffic demand and modeling assumptions throughout the systematic scenario 
analysis, rather than on the uncertainty of the project costs. 
One outcome of this decision is that the variations in the results of the scenarios are exclusively 
dependent on the changes in the traffic demand due to the variations in the assumption parameters. 
This also means that variations in the financial feasibility measures are exclusively dependent on the 
variations in the benefits of the project, as the costs are held fixed across scenarios. In both financial 
appraisal methodologies, the total benefits are composed entirely of the income from ticket sales. 
Therefore, the feasibility of the project relies on an adequate amount of ticket sales, which is 
ultimately determined by the change in the total traffic demand of the public transport network due 
to the construction of Trylleskov Strand Station. 
The results presented in Table 36 and Table 38 illustrate a necessary increase in the ticket revenue 
due to the new station of between 29% and 164%. However, this minimal increase of 29% is for the 
traffic demand scenario, OD3, which already includes a 25% increase in traffic demand compared to 
the trip matrix for the base model scenario, OD1. Therefore, the financial feasibility of the project 
relies on a drastic increase in expected traffic demand due to the construction of Trylleskov Strand 
Station. 
One reason for an increase in the expected traffic demand could be an unexpected growth in the 
population. However, the population growth is generally defined by the residential capacity of the 
area. Another reason for increased traffic demand is a shift in transport mode of the users. Although 
it is likely that new transport users will enter the network due to the new infrastructure, this 
==PAGE:66==
  58 | HOLT 
 
tendency is assumed to be accounted for in the induced traffic calculations included in the traffic 
assignment model. Therefore, it is unlikely that the Trylleskov Strand Station project will be 
financially feasible, from the financially-minded perspective of the operators.  
General relevance of financial appraisal methodology 
Although the total revenue acquired by the operator due to ticket sales is a significant figure for the 
operator to evaluate in order to determine the project’s likelihood of being financially sustainable, it 
is not relevant to the socio-economic analysis calculations. The ticket sales may be a profit to the 
operators, but they are also a deficit to the users of the system so they have a net-benefit of zero for 
society.

Discussion 
Implications of relying on past construction costs estimates 
The construction and operating cost values used in this report follow those estimated by Solrød 
Kommune and DSB in their original assessments of the Trylleskov Strand Station project. These 
values were calculated following the expected station design and with respect to the known costs of 
similar stations within the Greater Copenhagen transport network. For use in this project, these 
original estimates were re-calculated in 2010 prices in order to coincide with the price-levels of the 
other estimates of the cost-benefit analysis. Although these values are assumed to be sufficiently 
accurate, there are expected uncertainties in the values due to the fact that they were not re-
estimated with present-day expertise. 
Consumer surplus calculations 
The consumer surplus values presented in Table 39, show that there is a significant, and possibly 
illogical, increase in number of passengers experiencing an increased travel time due to new station 
across the model scenarios, which further results in a significant decrease in the net-consumer 
surplus across model scenarios. The model scenarios OD2 and OD3 include a 10% and 25% increase 
in the total traffic demand within the Trylleskov Strand area, which is expected to induce a similar 
increase in the number of new users of Trylleskov Strand Station, as the majority of the users from 
the Trylleskov Strand model zone are expected to use the new station. Due to this same reasoning, 
the increase in the total traffic demand of the Trylleskov Strand area, is not expected to elicit an 
increase in the number of other passengers passing through Trylleskov Strand Station. If the increase 
in travel time for these other users was measured in the assignment model, it is likely that the 
results would follow this reasoning. However, because the number of passengers experiencing this 
loss in time savings was calculated externally in the post-analysis as the difference between the 
base-passengers traveling through the station and the passengers transferring to the Ringsted Bane 
==PAGE:72==
  64 | HOLT 
 
or from the surrounding stations to Trylleskov Station, this final calculated passenger count does not 
follow the same proportional scale factor applied in the modeling of the trip matrices across model 
scenarios. 
For example, if model scenario OD2 is compared to the base model scenario, OD1, then it can be 
seen that the total number of base passengers through the new station and the total number of 
passengers transferring from Solrød Strand Station or Karlslunde Station to Trylleskov Strand Station 
is 10% greater for OD2 across all trip categories. This follows the difference in the total traffic 
demand modeled in each scenario. The total number of passengers shifting from the Køge Bugt to 
the Ringsted Bane remains the same in each model scenario. However, the total number of 
passengers with an increased travel time, and thus the total loss in consumer surplus, is 15.6%, 
14.4% and 14.5% greater for commute, business and other passengers, respectively, in model 
scenario OD2. This is logical based on the methodology of the calculation, but it is not logical with 
respect to the reality that these calculations are intended to model. As explained previously, the 
total number of passengers experiencing an increased travel time should not be influenced by the 
scaling of the traffic demand in the Trylleskov Strand model zone across model scenarios. Therefore, 
it can be concluded that the decision to model the time savings lost due to the extended travel time 
by way of the applied post-analysis calculation methodology, rather than within the public traffic 
assignment model, meaningfully impacts the precision of the consumer surplus calculations, and 
thus, the overall socio-economic analysis. Although the significance of this modeling decision is most 
impactful for the results of model scenario OD2 and OD3, it must also be noted that the traffic 
demand modeled in the base model scenario, OD1, was also calculated by scaling the OTM traffic 
demand matrices in order to model the assumed population and traffic growth within the Trylleskov 
Strand area, and thus these modeling decisions are also expected to impact the results of this base 
model scenario. While these impacts are not expected to influence the general conclusions of the 
socio-economic analysis, they are important to take into consideration throughout the analysis 
process. 
Group 1 
The most notable feature of the results presented for analysis group 1, is that the illustrated trend of 
an increase in the share of weekday traffic assumed to be within the 7-9am model period resulting in 
increase in the total net-present benefits of the project, is due to the fact that the net consumer 
surplus for public transport users is negative. If the project had proven to have had a positive impact 
on the consumer surplus for these users, then the opposite trend would be exhibited. This finding is, 
consequently, also a result of the choice to model the consumer surplus as being dependent on this 
parameter, while the values of road driving costs, for example, are initially modeled across the 
average weekday and therefore are independent of this parameter. 
Group 2 
The results of analysis group 2 follow the expected correlations between traffic growth and net-
present benefits (and thus net-present value). An interesting feature of these results, though, is the 
finding that the positive benefits for the road users of the network (measured as the decrease in 
driving costs between base and alternative scenarios) dominate the negative net benefits for the 
public transport users. Since the total consumer surplus proved to grow increasingly negative with 
an increase in the traffic demand of the Trylleskov Strand area (see Section 3.2.2.1), it could have 
been expected that the total net-present benefits of the project would follow the same pattern. 
However, this is not the case. Therefore, it can be concluded that the negative correlation between 
==PAGE:73==
  65 | HOLT 
 
traffic demand and public transport consumer surplus is dominated by the positive correlation 
between traffic demand and the other externality impacts. 
Group 3 
It should be noted that the methods used for understanding the impact of including varying shares 
of the original driving costs, are purely estimation methods and should not be assumed to indicate 
corresponding model assumptions or traffic analysis patterns. For example, when identifying the 
impact on the results for half of the value of the driving costs, it is still indeterminable which traffic 
analysis results would correspond to this driving cost value. This is attributable to the fact that the 
change in the total kilometers driven, and thus, the change in driving costs, is not directly 
proportional to the traffic demand due to the inclusion of congestion and induced traffic effects in 
the traffic assignment model calculations. Similarly, the modal shares expected to correlate with the 
minimum annual reduction in driving costs necessary to reach an NPV of zero in each presented 
scenario, are purely estimates and can only be used for purposes of sensitivity analysis. However, all 
of these measures are still useful in understanding the general robustness of the results and impact 
of the driving costs on the measured socio-economic feasibility of the project, so long as they are 
interpreted as estimates and not as precise forecasts. 
It could be argued that a modal shift of 10%, and even 5%, is an optimistic expectation, particularly 
within a short time frame. Transport behavior is not something that can be changed instantaneously, 
even despite the apparent logic behind the decision to change certain behaviors in order to better 
one’s experience with, or benefit from, the given transport system. Therefore, it can take time to 
induce a high modal shift between transport modes. These modal shift assumption parameters were 
chosen, nevertheless, due to their combined qualities of being small enough to be acceptably 
reasonable in terms or applicability, as well as, being large enough to be capable of providing 
meaningful results in the traffic analysis modeling. However, if one is to still challenge that these 
values are too optimistic, and therefore push the results of the CBA’s to be similarly optimistic, then 
it is useful to identify that the project will prove to be feasible under the given assumptions so long 
as the mode share is greater than the less optimistic values of 2-2.5%. These values, as stated 
previously, are estimations. However, they provide a measure of the impact of this possible 
optimism bias on the final CBA results. If a 2-2.5% modal shift is deemed reasonable, which is very 
likely, then one can assume that the feasibility of the project is robust with respect to the optimism 
bias of the modal shift from road to rail due to the construction of Trylleskov Strand Station. 
Group 4 
The impact of the extended travel time for the passengers passing through Trylleskov Strand Station 
has already been discussed in Section 2.1.3 and Section 3.1.1, with respect to the net consumer 
surplus for the public transport passengers in the network. However, it is also useful to investigate 
this impact on the net-present value of the project under various analysis scenarios. For example, 
following the assumptions of model scenario OD1, the inclusion of driving costs results in difference 
in the NPV of approximately 400 million dkk, while the inclusion of the time savings lost for the 
existing passengers results in a difference of approximately 200 million dkk. 
This finding can be the result of the large difference in the total number of road passengers 
compared to the total number of public transport passengers. For example, although the average 
trip length for road transport passengers traveling to or from the Trylleskov Strand model zone is 
just under 7km, the total number of weekday trips made is approximately 45,000. On the other 
hand, the average public transport trip to or from the Trylleskov Strand model zone is just under 
==PAGE:74==
  66 | HOLT 
 
27km, but the total number of weekday trips is approximately 550. This difference can be due to 
multiple factors. First, there is a notable difference in the size of the model zones used in the two 
models. Second, the road traffic demand matrices used in the OTM include trips made for the 
purpose of transporting goods, while the public traffic demand matrices include passenger transport 
only. Lastly, and likely most significantly, there is a large difference in the modal shares of the private 
car and public transport within the Trylleskov Strand area. Although many of the trips made by 
private car are much shorter than those made by public transport, there is still a significant 
preference for the private car. However, that makes the possibility of generating a general tendency 
to switch modes from private car to public transport due to the increased benefits of the new 
Trylleskov Strand Station even more significant for the conditions of both transport networks. 
Although the impacts of the change in driving costs may be greater than the impact of the lost time 
savings for the existing users of the public network with respect to the magnitude of the net present 
value calculations, it is clear that both input parameters are significant and, therefore, must be 
considered when assessing the socio-economic feasibility of this project.

General discussion of results 
The FRA results indicate that the feasibility of the Trylleskov Strand Station project is sensitive to the 
specific input parameters assumed in the socio-economic analysis. Following the base assumptions 
(model scenario 2 in Table 34), the project is resilient to the expected levels of uncertainty in the 
project cost due to optimism bias and underestimation. However, as discussed in Section 3.2.2.5, it

to a more moderate level of between 2-2.5% (modeled by reducing the driving costs input values by 
half, see model scenario 9 in Table 34), then the project is likely to become infeasible if the 
construction costs are estimated with more than 50% uncertainty. Although this level of uncertainty 
may seem pessimistic, as reported in Section 2.2.3, the corresponding underestimation of costs (and 
overestimation of benefits) due to optimism bias has been proven to be quite common in the 
appraisal of transport infrastructure projects. The results of the FRA for model scenario 9 indicate

Implications of self-manufacturing uplift factors 
As Flyvbjerg’s well-respected and widely applied research on feasibility risk assessment and 
reference class forecasting does not include specific uplift factors for the risk modeling of transport 
station building projects, these uplift factors needed to be estimated (Flyvbjerg 2008). As explained 
in Section 2.2.3, the estimation was based on the three risk probability distributions presented by 
Flyvbjerg for road, rail and fixed-link. These functions have relatively the same shape, so it was 
assumed that the probability distribution for station building projects would also follow suit, but 
would have the specific endpoint values that were estimated by Flyvbjerg. Although this method of 
estimation is assumed to be sufficient for the purpose of this project, it must be noted that there are 
expected uncertainties in the feasibility risk assessment due to the assumptions made in the 
formulation of these uplift factors, as they are not grounded in the evidence of past projects.  
Limitations of only evaluating two model scenarios 
A feasibility risk assessment was only performed for two of the fifteen model scenarios that were 
evaluated using CBA. This, of course, limits the breadth of the risk assessment. However, as stated in 
Section 2.2.3, model scenarios 2 and 9 are considered to be the most relevant and interesting 
scenarios to investigate; model scenario 2 follows the base assumptions of this entire research 
portfolio and, therefore, is crucial to evaluate, while model scenario 9 is possibly even more realistic 
than model scenario 2. The NPV and BCR values of the project following the base assumption of trip 
demand matrix OD1 proved to be lower than those following matrices OD2 and OD3 due to the 
variation in the number of new passengers within the Trylleskov Strand area. Therefore, since there 
was no level of uncertainty in the project costs which could result in an indication of project 
infeasibility based on the corresponding BCR, it follows that the FRA of these additional model 
scenarios (see Group 2 in Table 34) also would not provide any meaningful findings regarding the 
potential infeasibility of the project. The NPV and BCR values of some of the model scenarios of 
Group 4 border the corresponding criteria for feasibility of 0 and 1, so it may be assumed that these 
model scenarios would be interesting to evaluate through FRA. However, the assumptions included 
in these model scenarios (exclusion of the negative time savings for existing passengers through 
Trylleskov Strand Station and the road user driving costs) are useful for understanding the inter-
workings of the socio-economic feasibility of the project and the individual significances of each 
impact included in the analysis, but they are not very realistic in practice. Therefore, it was deemed 
unadvantageous to evaluate these results further using FRA.

3.3.9 Discussion 
Technological failures and the resultant reduction in participation 
The online questionnaire software from Qualtrics was chosen for use in this project due to its 
apparent ease of use for the author in the preparation, distribution and data-collection phases, as 
well as, for the stakeholders in the participation phase. It was anticipated that both the quantity and 
quality of the response data collected would depend on the usability of the software. 
Unfortunately, some of the participants experienced substantial issues with the software; the 
majority of the participants from Solrød Kommune were unable to access the survey successfully. 
Possible solutions were thoroughly investigated. The users tried to open the link in various web 
browsers, but the survey remained inaccessible. One participant was able to complete the survey on 
an iPad instead of a PC, however another also tried this and her results were not registered by the 
software correctly, and therefore not actually collected. One participant finally resulted to 
completing the survey in an offline format, which meant that, although the response data was 
collected, the intended structure, usability and some of the online functionalities were lost. These 
technological issues ultimately meant that the primary project leader for the Trylleskov Strand 
project and the major of Solrød, among others, were not able to participate in the survey. Although 
the total number of participants was within the initial expectations and goals, the level of 
participation and quality of participation could have been drastically improved had these 
technological issues been avoided.  
The power of the MAMCA 
This research project identifies an important conflict within the strategic assessment of small-scale 
transport infrastructure projects, such as the Trylleskov Strand Station project. It is a common 
assumption that the more data that can be used to support a decision, the more likely it is to be 
realistically suitable. It is for this reason that the majority of traditional MCDA research relies on the 
use of indicators in order to perform the pair-wise comparisons. However, the compilation of the 
data needed to quantitatively assess the performance of project alternatives sufficiently is an 
exhaustive process. Small-scale infrastructure projects tend to lack the financial support for such 
thorough investigations into the expected impacts of the project before it has gained a high level of 
support or is deemed likely to be constructed. As explained previously, this trend applies to the 
Trylleskov Strand Station project, as the only impacts that have been investigated to date are those 
relating to financial concerns. This means that the judgements made by the survey participants could 
not necessarily be supported by valid data or research. Instead, the participants used their 
knowledge of the project (from their own previous experience or from the information provided 
within the survey) in order to perform the pair-wise assessments.  
==PAGE:90==
  82 | HOLT 
 
Another important consideration is that the process of transforming sufficiently extensive sets of 
data into corresponding value scores is a task that requires a level of knowledge and skill that tends 
to be possessed only by decision support specialists. It is for this reason that the assessments of 
traditional MCDA research are typically performed by a team of decision-support specialists. As the 
MAMCA ideally involves all relevant stakeholders in the assessment process, it is not practical to 
assume that all analysts will have sufficient knowledge of these decision-support-specific calculation 
methods. However, the main goal of the MAMCA methodology is to provide a framework through 
which stakeholders can effectively participate in the project evaluation process. Therefore, the lack 
of sufficient data is not necessarily a weakness in this assessment. It is assumed that the diversity of 
perspectives will account for inconsistencies caused by any questionable assumptions made by 
participants due to a lack of sufficient information. 
It can thus be concluded that the MAMCA proves to be an especially appropriate assessment tool 
within the realm of small-scale projects which involve complex stakeholder dynamics and 
sustainability concerns, but lack the financial support for extensive impact assessment research in 
the pre-assessment phase. This is an important conclusion, not just for the discussion of this 
assessment of the Trylleskov Strand Station project, but for the discussion of similar projects within 
the field of sustainable appraisal of small-scale transport infrastructure projects. 
Equal weighting of all stakeholder inputs 
The response data collected in the analysis sections of the survey was all weighted the same in the 
post-processing MAMCA calculations, regardless of which stakeholder it was collected from. This 
choice to give each stakeholder the same level of representation in the analysis was made 
deliberately in order to maintain a balanced and non-biased approach to the assessment of the 
project. It can be argued that this equal distribution of influence is not realized in practice. In reality, 
there is an uneven distribution of power that leads to a prioritization of assessment criteria that 
does not necessarily reflect the opinion of all stakeholders, but merely those who hold the most 
influence over the project. However, it is due to this exact circumstance that maintaining this 
equality and diversity in the representation of the stakeholders was considered to be so important in 
the framing of the strategic analysis. The aspects of transport infrastructure projects, such as the 
Trylleskov Strand Station project, that are most associated with sustainability concerns, are often 
overlooked by the most powerful stakeholders due to their self-interested emphasis on purely 
financial or economic concerns. Therefore, if the strategic analysis is to be used to supplement the 
financial and socio-economic analyses, such as it is intended to do in this research, then the diverse 
and complex perspectives of all stakeholders must be represented and considered fairly. 
Diversity in the participants’ levels of familiarity with the project 
Although the diversity across the respondents is beneficial in terms of including a variety of 
perspectives in the strategic analysis, it is not necessarily beneficial to have a variety in the level of 
familiarity with the projects being evaluated. The effects of this variation were targeted through the 
inclusion of an introductory background section in the beginning of the survey. Furthermore, the 
participants were encouraged to base their pair-wise criteria evaluations on the provided 
information, as well as, their own previous knowledge and experience. However, there has been 
limited research into the specific impacts of the project with respect to the relevant assessment 
criteria. While it might be preferred to have all assessments be grounded in relevant data or derived 
from value functions based on relevant scientific research, this was not possible for this project. The 
information provided in the introductory section was thus broad and not specific to the measurable 
impacts of the project. It is also not guaranteed that all participants read the introductory section or 
==PAGE:91==
  83 | HOLT 
 
the criteria descriptions with the same level of completeness and precision. Therefore, the variety in 
the participants’ familiarity with the project, particularly when combined with the generally low 
average level of familiarity with the plans for Trylleskov Strand Station, is likely to have had an 
impact on the precision or accuracy of the project assessment. 
Interaction with optional informational survey sections 
The response data presented in Table 56 illustrates that the majority of users chose to skip over the 
optional MAMCA description section. This section was supplementary to the very brief description of 
the MCDA and MAMCA theory and how it would be applied in this survey. The option to skip over 
the deeper introduction section was intended for those participants who were already familiar with 
MAMCA. However, it appears that many of the participants who were unfamiliar with it also decided 
to skip the section. One could argue that the participants would have been able to more accurately 
perform the criteria evaluations if they had a more complete understanding of the methodology. 
However, the methodology should not necessarily be considered when performing the criteria 
selection, weighting and judgements. It is important that the participants understand the intended 
purpose of each analysis section and the structure of its applied methodology—for example, the 
relevant assessment scales—but it is not important to understand the complete theory of the 
MAMCA assessment calculations. The analysis sections were created with the intention to be able to 
stand alone and a brief outline of the MAMCA was given in the survey introduction, so the choice to 
skip the specific MAMCA section should not have been an issue with respect to the validity of the 
results. However, it is important to note that, when given the choice to pass-over a section, the 
majority of the participants capitalized on that opportunity. This pattern was also detected for the 
option of reading the criteria descriptions during the criteria evaluation processes. These choices 
may have been made in this survey, specifically, because of the length of the survey. It was 
suggested in the introduction that the survey would take approximately 20 minutes to complete, but 
the majority of the participants spent an average of 30 minutes on the survey, even without 
accessing the optional information sections. It could, therefore, be hypothesized that the 
participants’ willingness to engage in the informational and background sections of the survey is 
impacted by both the expected and actual length of the survey.  
Environmental concerns, or lack thereof 
As reported in Section 3.3.2, none of the environmental criteria were selected by a large enough 
share of the participants in order to qualify for inclusion in the final set of assessment criteria. It can 
be argued, according to the sensitivity analysis presented in Section 3.3.8, that the overall results of 
the strategic analysis would not have changed significantly had the environmental criteria been 
selected. This is due to the fact the average performance scores associated with the environmental 
criteria fell within the average of the scores associated with the remaining criteria. However, it is still 
interesting to consider why the environmental criteria were not selected by the participants at a 
qualifying rate. 
The average selection count per environmental criteria category was 2.167, while the average 
selection counts for the societal, transport and institutional criteria were 6.75, 8.5 and 5, 
respectively. Therefore, it cannot be assumed that there were too many environmental criteria 
provided in the initial set for there to be a high selection of any particular criteria. There were eight 
societal criteria given in the initial set and, still, the average selection count was found to be above 
the average value for all criteria.  
==PAGE:92==
  84 | HOLT 
 
A logical explanation for this lack of emphasis on the environmental criteria could be that the survey 
participants were simply, on average, less interested in the environmental aspects of the project 
than they were in the societal, transport, and institutional aspects. However, the response data 
collected in the post-assessment phase of the survey and presented in Figure 31, illustrates a 
contradicting trend. The largest share of participants reported prioritizing the environmental aspects 
of the project over the societal aspects, the economic aspects and the construction and operational 
costs of the project. There is thus an apparent difference between the self-reported prioritization of 
the environmental criteria and the actual selection of the environmental criteria in the evaluation 
process of the survey. It is possible that, while the participants prefer to believe that they prioritize 
the environmental aspects of a project, when it comes to practice, these preferences shift back to a 
perspective that does not prioritize environmental or sustainability concerns in the assessment of 
transport projects. This is a significant finding, as it suggests something about the way that 
stakeholders approach the sustainable assessment of transport projects. 
It is also possible that the environmental criteria were not selected by the participants due to the 
presumption that the environmental impacts of the project will not differ significantly from those of 
the base scenario. This assumption is supported by the few assessment scores that were reported 
for the environmental criteria. Although the specific phrasing of the questions and the structure of 
the survey were developed with the intention of avoiding this issue, it is possible that these issues 
arose regardless.  
A final consideration for the reasoning behind these results is that there could have been a lack of 
clarity or undecipherability of the environmental criteria. One participant reported that it was 
difficult to choose between such similar criteria, while another participant reported that it was 
difficult to choose between such different criteria. These contrasting opinions illustrate that there 
was likely some confusion amongst the participants with respect to the criteria, despite the 
strategically selected criteria names and supplementary descriptions. Although the criteria were also 
developed with the intention that they could be interpreted by all stakeholders, the environmental 
criteria are possibly the criteria which contain the most field-specific formulations. It is possible, 
then, that some participants may have skipped over the environmental criteria all together due to 
the confusion in the formulations or due to their inability to sufficiently assess the relative 
significance of the initial criteria. 
It could have been possible to detect the validity of these aforementioned hypotheses if questions 
were included in the pre- or post-assessment sections to address the users’ intentions or 
considerations in the criteria selection process. However, without this sort of response data, the 
discussion of the possible reasons for the lack of selection of environmental criteria must remain 
hypothetical. 
The power of knowledge 
The survey response data presented in Figure 30 and Figure 34 illustrate a general increase in the 
overall level of approval of the Trylleskov Strand Station project between the pre-assessment 
questions and the post-assessment questions. Furthermore, the only changes across participant 
responses were positive changes; no participant exhibited a lower approval of the project after 
completing the survey. Although it is difficult to say exactly what may have caused this change in the 
level of approval for some of the participants, it appears suggests that the more information about 
the project a stakeholder has, or the more time they spend reflecting on various aspects of the 
project, the greater their appreciation for the benefits of the project, as opposed to the disbenefits. 
This is an interesting finding about the power of strategic assessment and the impact that 
==PAGE:93==
  85 | HOLT 
 
approaching the project from multiple, possibly diverse and previously unconsidered perspectives, 
can have on the mindset of stakeholders, particularly those who are originally critical of a project 
due to the fact that its benefits are largely dominated by considerations related to sustainability, 
rather than financial feasibility. 
 
4 Summative Assessment 
4.1 Summary of significant findings 
4.1.1 Traffic analysis 
The traffic analysis for the Trylleskov Strand Station project was conducted using the OTM and 
Traffic Analyst tools in ArcGIS. The original input data of the model was updated in order to include 
the new network attributes associated with the new station, as well as, the expected changes in the 
traffic demand within the zone containing Trylleskov Strand due to the population growth resulting 
from the ongoing development of the new neighborhood. These changes to the traffic demand were 
modeled for three possible growth scenarios: a base scenario following the expected population 
growth within the area (OD1), a scenario including a 10% increase from the base (OD2) and a final 
scenario including a 25% increase from the base (OD3). Specific adjustments were made to the 
configuration of the model input parameters in order to increase the detectability of the results due 
to the small size of the project. 
The public transport traffic analysis was conducted by comparing a base scenario in which the 
station was not constructed with an alternative scenario where the station was constructed and thus 
represented in the public transport network. The road transport traffic analysis was conducted by 
comparing a base scenario which included the aforementioned traffic demand scenarios and two 
alternative scenarios which included a 5% and a 10% decrease in the total traffic demand within the 
Trylleskov Strand model zone, used to represent the possible modal shift from road to rail due to the 
new station. In the road transport model scenarios, no changes were made to the network data, as 
the only network changes due to the new station occur within the public transport infrastructure. 
The most significant findings of the traffic analysis are18: 
 
The majority of Trylleskov Strand Station passengers use line E, rather than line A. 
 
The majority of Trylleskov Strand Station passengers travel to and from the Copenhagen 
direction, rather than the Solrød Strand/Køge direction. 
 
The expected number of daily passengers using Trylleskov Strand Station is between 4,000 
and 5,000. 
 
The number of weekday passengers expected to shift from Solrød Strand Station or 
Karlslunde Station is 3,801. 
 
The total number of passengers with an increased travel time of two minutes due to the 
construction of Trylleskov Strand Station is 12,840. 
 
The total decrease in kilometers driven by the road users of the system is 28,506 for a 5% 
modal-shift and 57,377 for a 10% modal-shift.

4.1.2 Socio-economic analysis 
The socio-economic analysis performed in this report included three sections. The first analysis was a 
financial analysis which modeled the previous assessments done on the feasibility of Trylleskov 
Strand Station. The second form of analysis was the traditional cost-benefit analysis (CBA), which 
was organized following the methodology suggested by the Danish Ministry of Transport. Due to the 
high levels of uncertainty in the basic assumptions behind the project, extensive sensitivity analysis 
was performed by re-calculating the CBA results under various combinations of the possible 
variations in these basic assumptions. Finally, a feasibility risk assessment (FRA) was performed for 
two of the scenarios modeled in the CBA.  
The most significant findings of the socio-economic analysis are19: 
Financial Analysis 
 
Following all methods of financial assessment, the project is not expected to be financially 
self-sustainable. 
 
According to the simple financial appraisal, the ticket sales needed in order to achieve 
financial feasibility is 2.2 times greater than the actual level of expected ticket sales. This 
value is 1.61 according to the complex financial appraisal. 
 
The total number of new weekday passengers required in order to achieve financial 
feasibility is 231. However, the number of new weekday passengers assumed from the 
results of traffic analysis is 115. 
Cost-Benefit Analysis 
 
The net consumer surplus for the public transport users on an average weekday is -15,960 
dkk. 
 
In order for the net consumer surplus to be non-negative, the total stopping time at 
Trylleskov Strand Station would need to be 42.6 seconds, instead of the assumed value of 
120 seconds. 
 
Following the base assumption profile of the CBA calculations, the project has an NPV of 220 
million dkk, a BCR of 2.94 and an IRR of 3.19%.  
 
The estimated decrease in road-user driving costs due to the mode shift between road and 
public transport induced by the construction of the new station has a significant impact on 
the feasibility of the project; with the change in driving costs, the NPV is 220 million dkk, 
while without it, the NPV is -208 million dkk. 
 
The base estimated modal shift of 5% is likely to be too optimistic. If the change in driving 
costs is reduced by 50% in order to compensate for this optimism bias, then the NPV is 5.6 
million dkk. The minimum reduction of the driving costs in order to reach an NPV of 0, is 
51.3%. This corresponds to an estimated modal shift of 2.4%. 
Feasibility Risk Assessment 
 
The results of the model scenario which follows the base assumption profile for the CBA 
calculations (model scenario 2) are robust for construction cost uncertainties of up to at 
least 80%.

4.1.3 Strategic analysis 
The final form of analysis that was conducted for this research was a strategic analysis with 
methodology grounded in Multi-Actor Multi-Criteria Analysis. This methodology was selected 
because it provides a framework to assess transport infrastructure projects with respect to broader 
sustainability concerns that are incredibly important but cannot be monetized and, therefore, 
cannot be included in the traditional methods of socio-economic analysis. 
A survey was distributed to the relevant stakeholders of the project in order to assess the overall 
performance of the two project alternatives: a base scenario which follows the business-as-usual 
scenario, and an alternative scenario in which Trylleskov Strand Station is constructed. The 
participants were asked to choose eight to ten strategic assessment criteria out of an initial set of 
twenty criteria which were tactfully compiled from relevant references and other similar strategic 
assessment projects. They were then asked to assign relative weights to these criteria and to 
evaluate the relative performance of each project alternative with respect to each criterion. The 
data collected from this section of the survey served as the input for the calculation of an overall 
quantitative assessment score for the performance of each alternative. 
The survey participants were also asked to provide qualitative information regarding their previous 
knowledge of the project, as well as, their general opinions of it. This data was used to qualitatively 
assess the results of the MAMCA calculations and, furthermore, to understand the dynamic 
relationship between the stakeholders and the Trylleskov Strand Station project more 
comprehensively. 
The most significant findings of the strategic analysis are: 
 
A total of 13 stakeholders participated in the survey and thus were included in the strategic 
assessment process. 
 
The eleven criteria that qualified for inclusion in the final set of assessment criteria, listed in 
order of their selection percentage, are: 
1. Mobility and accessibility 
Affordability of transport 
3. Land use and urban development 
Non-automobile travel pattern 
5. Transport integration and connectivity 
6. Journey reliability and system resilience 
7. Livability, amenity and local environmental quality 
8. Automobile travel pattern 
9. Population and health 
Customer satisfaction/agency reputation 
Ensure effective engagement of stakeholders, citizens and communities 
 
Although the majority of participants self-reported that they prioritize the environmental 
aspects of the project, none of the environmental criteria in the initial criteria set obtained a 
high enough selection percentage to be included in the final set of assessment criteria. 
 
The highest-weighted criterion was “mobility and accessibility”, while the lowest-weighted 
criterion was “population and health”. 
==PAGE:96==
  88 | HOLT 
 
 
The results of the pair-wise comparisons of the alternative scenarios illustrated that the 
project alternative outperforms the base alternative with respect to every criterion included 
in the final assessment. The criterion under which the project alternative obtained the best 
relative performance assessment is “land use and urban development”, while the criterion 
under which it achieved the worst relative performance assessment (only slightly 
outperforming the base alternative) is “journey reliability and system resilience”.  
 
The total performance scores suggest that the project alternative is preferred to the base 
alternative at a level of 84.5%. 
 
The results of the MAMCA are highly robust to changes in the weightings of the criteria. 
 
The participants’ overall opinion of the Trylleskov Strand Station project increased, on 
average, throughout the process of completing the survey. 
 
The majority of the participants were interested in the project, but they felt that they have 
little to no influence on the outcome of the project. 
4.2 General recommendation for investment in Trylleskov Strand Station 
The primary goal of this research project was to address the question: is the construction of 
Trylleskov Strand Station a feasible and sustainable investment? The findings of the financial analysis 
are consistent with those made by DSB in 2006; the expected income due to ticket sales is not 
sufficient to overcome the estimated construction and operational costs of the project. However, 
following the more comprehensive and theoretically sound socio-economic analysis methodology of 
the CBA, the results illustrate that the project is likely to be feasible, as the expected socio-economic 
benefits of the project outweigh the project costs over its 50-year lifetime. According to the 
feasibility risk assessment calculations, the robustness of the project’s feasibility depends most 
heavily on the modal shift from car to public transport induced by the construction of the new 
station. Approximately 2-3% of the current trips made by road users in the Trylleskov Strand area 
must be shifted to public transport in order for the project to be socio-economically feasible. 
The traditional socio-economic analysis of this transport infrastructure project was supplemented 
with a strategic analysis in order to address the sustainability concerns which are not able to be 
included in the socio-economic analysis, as well as, to include the project’s stakeholders in the 
assessment process. The results of this strategic assessment, illustrate a high level of stakeholder 
support for the construction of Trylleskov Strand Station. The results of the MAMCA identified that 
the project is expected to outperform the business-as-usual scenario with 84.5% certainty. 
Therefore, following the strategic analysis, the Trylleskov Strand Station project is expected to be 
sustainably feasible. 
In conclusion, the Trylleskov Strand Station project is likely to be a worthwhile investment that will 
generate both socio-economic and sustainability-related benefits over the course of its lifetime. 
However, some of the data used to support this conclusion are associated with notable 
uncertainties. It is, therefore, crucial that further analysis be undertaken with respect to the project 
before this conclusion can be sufficiently validated.  
4.3 General recommendation for future work and research 
The scope of the research and analysis performed in this report has been extensive in order to assess 
the wide and complex set of impacts influencing, and influenced by, the sustainability of the 
Trylleskov Strand Station project. Although the theoretical basis for the methodology used for these 
assessments has been fully grounded in relevant literature and current practice, the data supporting 
these methods could be improved in order to increase the validity of the results. There has not been 
==PAGE:97==
  89 | HOLT 
 
much research done regarding the actual strategic impacts of the project; the past research that has 
been undertaken has focused primarily on financial aspects. 
The first improvement that could be made in order to increase the validity of this sustainable 
transport assessment research is the development of an improved traffic model which could be used 
to model and analyze the specific traffic dynamics within the Trylleskov Strand area. The success of 
this new model would also require a more thorough and validated representation of the expected 
traffic demand matrices following the future developments of the residential area. 
Further research into the potential impacts of the project, particularly those of societal and 
environmental nature, would also increase the validity of the project. This would enable the decision 
makers performing the strategic assessment analysis to make decisions that are objective and 
grounded in data and technical research, rather than assumptions which have the potential to 
involve a high degree of subjectivity. 
Although these aforementioned solutions are possible, they would be very costly. Considering the 
small size of the Trylleskov Strand Station project, it is safe to assume that these solutions would 
need to be backed with overwhelming support by the involved stakeholders in order to be realized. 
If this support can be found and these improvements can be put into action, then they have the 
potential to elevate the validity of the assessment results to a level where they can form the basis 
for a conclusive judgement on the potential impacts of the Trylleskov Strand Station project, and 
thus its feasibility. 
5 Conclusion 
The purpose of this report has been to examine the potential feasibility of a new s-train station in 
the Solrød Kommune’s newest neighborhood, Trylleskov Strand. Although previous studies (DSB 
2006) have concluded that there was not a sufficient passenger demand to financially support the 
construction of the station, the addition of 750 new homes in Trylleskov Strand (between 2009 and 
2019) will generate population growth and thus traffic growth that may sufficiently improve the 
feasibility of the station’s implementation. The primary goal of this research project was thus to 
address the question: under the currently assumed transport, societal, environmental and economic 
characteristics of the Trylleskov Strand Station project, is its construction a feasible and sustainable 
investment? 
Three sets of analysis methods have been used in this research project: traffic analysis, socio-
economic analysis and strategic analysis. The traffic analysis was performed using the OTM public 
and road transport models and the Traffic Analyst tool in ArcGIS. This analysis served as input into 
the socio-economic analysis, which was broken into three segments: financial affordability analysis, 
cost-benefit analysis (CBA) and feasibility risk assessment (FRA). This socio-economic assessment 
was then supported by a strategic assessment that was carried out in the form of a multi-actor 
multi-criteria analysis (MAMCA) based on survey response data from relevant stakeholders. The 
combination of these assessments enabled a comprehensive understanding of the project’s 
feasibility that extends beyond the traditional financial or socio-economic aspects. 
The complete set of results and discussion from this feasibility assessment suggested that the 
Trylleskov Strand Station project is likely to be a worthwhile investment that will generate both 
socio-economic and sustainability-related benefits over the course of its lifetime. However, some of 
the data used to support this conclusion are associated with notable uncertainties. It is, therefore, 
crucial that further analysis be undertaken with respect to the project before this conclusion can be 
sufficiently validated.

7.3.2 Criteria descriptions provided in stakeholder assessment survey (Danish) 
Sociale: 
1. Mobilitet og tilgængelighed 
a. Mobilitet er et udtryk for hvordan, eller hvor effektivt, passagerer (eller varer) kan 
rejse igennem et netværk. Mobilitet kan defineres som: “en karakteristik af folk, 
sociale kategorier og samfundet generelt. [Mobilitet] udtrykker den allerede 
fuldførte bevægelse såvel som den potentielle bevægelse, som personer udfører 
enten alene eller kollektivt”(Ciuffini 1995). Fra et teoretisk perspektiv skal den 
sidstnævnte karakterisering af mobilitet som en potentiel eller mulig bevægelse 
understreges. Imidlertid er den aktuelle bevægelse af passagerer (eller varer) et 
direkte resultat af potentialet for bevægelse igennem netværket (DTU Transport 
2015). Derfor er begge definitioner af mobilitet nyttige at tage i betragtning når man 
evaluerer transport infrastruktur i praksis. 
b. Tilgængelighed er et udtryk for antallet af muligheder eller interessepunkter 
(arbejdspladser, butikker, attraktioner, venner, familie, osv.) som man kan rejse til. 
Dette er typisk givet inden for et rejsetidsinterval (DTU Transport 2015). Frem for at 
angive rejsen i sig selv, angiver tilgængelighed formålet med rejsen og kan derfor 
udtrykke en definition af en ”god” eller ”nyttig” rejse. Selvom mobiliteten til 
Næstved fra Solrød Strand og til København fra Solrød Strand kan være ens (med 
hensyn til rejsetid og omkostninger), er der en stor forskel mellem tilgængeligheden 
til eller fra hvert sted på grund af forskellen i de mulige faciliteter i hvert sted. Derfor 
er det vitalt at inkludere tilgængelighed ud over mobilitet når man evaluerer 
kvaliteten af et transportsystem. 
c. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Alle faciliteter man kunne ønske eller have brug for er lige uden 
for hoveddøren, og der er ikke behov for transport. 
d. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Der er ingenting at finde inden for en acceptabel rejsetid eller 
med acceptable rejsemåder, og man må derfor være selvforsynende. 
2. Transportens omkostninger 
a. Betalingsvillighed for transport er en måde at udtrykke transportomkostningerne 
overfor de rejsendes leveomkostninger og dermed de omkostninger, som de mener 
er acceptable. Prisen af transport er så godt som irrelevant, hvis den ikke er tænkt 
sammen med betalingsvilligheden, da transportformen ikke benyttes, hvis prisen 
ikke er acceptabel for de rejsende. 
b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Transportomkostningerne sættes til et optimalt niveau, som er 
overkommeligt for dem der har brug for det, men som derudover tilskynder folk til 
at evaluere deres transportvaner og prioriterer de rejser, som har den største fordel 
(for individer eller for samfundet). 
c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Transportomkostningerne er så høje at passagerne ikke vil rejse. 
Det at rejse er kun tilgængelig for en lille del af samfundet, hvilket bliver en 
socioøkonomisk katastrofe. 
3. Beskæftigelse 
a. Beskæftigelse udtrykker tilstandene af et arbejdsmiljø i et givet område. Dette kan 
inkludere antallet af mennesker, som er ansatte (eller ikke ansatte), antallet 
jobmuligheder, antallet af jobmuligheder for højtuddannede, osv. Med hensyn til 
dette projekt, skal beskæftigelse forstås fra to perspektiver: beskæftigelse af 
beboere i lokalområdet samt jobmuligheder i lokalområdet på grund af den nye 
station. 
==PAGE:113==
  L | HOLT 
 
b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Alle beboere har optimal tilgængelighed til deres job og antallet af 
muligheder for pendlere til Trylleskov Strand er også optimeret, så alle 
beskæftigelseskvaliteterne giver maksimal fordel for samfundet. 
c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Alle beboere mister deres tilgængelighed til alle arbejdspladser, 
og alle arbejdspladser ved Trylleskov Strand området tabes. 
4. Beboelseskarakteristikker 
a. Beboelseskarakteristikker inkluderer faktorer, som er forbundet til 
boligsammensætning, -kvalitet, -densitet og -værdi. For eksempel er det sandsynligt 
at opbygningen af den nye station vil påvirke ejendomsværdierne i området. 
Beboersammensætningen (familier med små børn vs. gamle og enlige) vil måske 
også ændre sig på grund af ændringerne i mobilitet og tilgængelighed. 
b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Ejendomspriserne i området stiger til et optimalt niveau 
(maksimum investeringsafkast og maksimum potentiale for nye købere), den 
demografiske fordeling af beboere bliver funktionel og optimal. 
c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Voldsomme fald i ejendomspriserne eller voldsomme fald i 
potentialet for at sælge ejendommene, et voldsom fald i antallet af beboere, samt 
en ændring i demografien til et dysfunktionelt samfund. 
5. Befolkning og sundhed 
a. Indvirkninger på befolkningen kan illustreres ved brug af demografiske statistiker 
(ældre, køn, løn, osv.) og livstilskarakteristikker (andelen af tid brugt på rejser, visuel 
æstetik i område, sikkerhedsfølelser, osv.). Sundhedsindvirkninger kan illustreres 
ved eksponering for bl.a. støj, ulykker, emissioner og lys i både anlægs- og 
driftsperioderne. 
b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Det nye anlæg eliminerer alle farlige indvirkninger på befolkning 
og sundhed. 
c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Det nye anlæg genererer voldsomme farlige indvirkninger på 
befolkning og sundhed, som driver folk ud fra området. 
6. Arealanvendelse og byudvikling 
a. Indvirkninger på arealanvendelse og byudvikling forbindes med fordelingen af areal i 
området. Det er vigtigt at tænke over forholdet mellem plads, der benyttes til 
diverse formål (arbejdspladser, boliger, fritidscentre, kulturcentre, osv.) såvel som 
forholdet mellem offentlige rum og plads der benyttes til et enkelt formål. Den 
sidstnævnte karakteristik er særlig vigtig i forbindelse med bæredygtighed. 
b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Der er ingen farlige indvirkninger på kvaliteten af areal og 
byudvikling, og der er kun indvirkninger som påvirker sammensætningen, kvaliteten 
og udviklingen af det lokale område positivt. Yderligere er mængden af offentlige 
rum på et optimalt niveau. 
c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Projektet udsletter alt areal og byudvikling i området, så der 
ingen er, som kan eller vil blive i området. 
7. Livskvalitet, komfort og lokal miljøkvalitet (fx affald, støv, graffiti, offentlige rum, 
vandkvalitet, fri for kriminalitet og hærværk) 
a. Livskvalitet, komfort og lokal miljøkvalitet er meget betydningsfulde, når de 
vurderes med hensyn til hjemmehørende tilfredshed. Livskvalitet refererer til evnen 
til at leve frit inden for området. Komfort refererer til de tjenester eller varer, der er 
tilgængelige i området. Lokal miljøkvalitet refererer til kvaliteten af det offentlige 
rum inden for området, både fra et miljømæssigt og socialt perspektiv.

b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Det lokale miljø er omdannet til et velstående og livligt rum, hvor 
folk elsker at være, leve og interagere. Det er fuld af bæredygtige og æstetisk 
behagelige funktioner. 
c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Det lokale miljø er ødelagt, og fuldstændig ubehageligt og farligt 
for beboere at opholde sig i pga. affald, kriminalitet og hærværk. 
8. Indflydelse holdninger, adfærd og kulturer mod bæredygtighed 
a. Indflydelsen på holdninger, adfærd og kulturer mod bæredygtighed henviser til 
projektets potentiale for at skabe forandring i offentlighedens bevidsthed om eller 
interesse for bæredygtighedsproblemer. Dette omfatter både individuelle og 
kommunale virkninger på bæredygtighed og kan vurderes både på kort og lang sigt.

bæredygtighedsproblemet, og hvordan de kan bidrage til at øge bæredygtighed som 
enkeltpersoner og som fællesskab. Vigtigst er, at de aktivt arbejder for at aktualisere 
disse livsstilsændringer og øge bevidstheden for andre. 
c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Projektet fører beboere til at benægte spørgsmål som 
klimaændringer og behovet for bæredygtig livsstil og fuldstændig se bort fra alle 
miljømæssige eller samfundsmæssigt fordelagtige virkninger, de har på verdensplan. 
Miljømæssig: 
9. Indvirkninger på miljømæssige råvarer 
a. Indvirkninger på miljømæssige råvarer refererer til typen og mængden af jord 
benyttet af den transportmæssige infrastruktur, forstyrrelsen af det lokale (eller 
ikke-lokale) habitat eller økosystem, såvel som strukturen og flow af 
grundvandsvejerne og afløbsnetværkerne. 
b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Der er absolut ikke nogle negative indvirkninger på miljøet. I 
stedet fremhjælper projektet miljøtilstandens tilbagevenden til en naturlig og mere 
bæredygtig tilstand. 
c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: En stor del jord benyttes af projektet og miljøråvarerne er totalt 
opbrugt og uopretteligt ødelagt. 
10. Miljømæssige risikoer og skader 
a. Miljømæssige risikoer og skader inkluderer forurening af afstrømning af regnvand og 
transport, såvel som potentiellet for ulykker såsom oversvømmelse og udslip af 
farligt spildevand. 
b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Det er absolut ikke noget negative indvirkninger på miljøet. I 
stedet reducerer projektet de miljømæssige risikoer og potentielle skader som kan 
forekomme. 
c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Projektet medfører ekstreme stigninger i risikoerne for 
miljømæssig kontamination og skader, som vil hærge de omkringliggende områder. 
11. Landskab og visuel tilstand 
a. Landskab referer primært til fysiske egenskaber af området, såsom topografi, 
bymæssig form, jordtype, osv. Det inkluderer også områdets sociale egenskaber, 
såsom den historiske værdi eller prestige. Visuel tilstand refererer til områdets 
æstetiske egenskaber med hensyn til de naturlige landskaber såvel som 
infrastrukturen og landmærkerne. 
b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Projektet er i sig selv et inspirerende landmærke eller forbedrer 
markantlig det lokale områdets æstetiske egenskaber, og beskyttelsen eller 
bevarelsen af historiske egenskaber stiger.

c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Projektet ødelægger meget vigtige historiske og kulturelle 
landmærker og får en utilstrækkelig struktur som dominerer området på en voldsom 
negativ måde. 
12. Biodiversitet og natur 
a. Virkningerne på biodiversitet og natur relaterer sig til virkningerne på kvaliteten og

b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Projektet indeholder ingen negative konsekvenser for flora og

c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Projektet resulterer i død af al flora og fauna i de 
omkringliggende områder. 
13. Materialefodspor 
a. Projektets materielle fodaftryk omfatter udvinding af råmaterialer, der anvendes i 
bygge- eller driftsfasen, erhvervelse af nye materialer fra eksterne kilder og

b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Projektet omfatter ingen udvinding af råmaterialer og bruger i 
stedet materialer, der tidligere har været brugt eller truer dette område eller et

c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Projektet kræver udvinding af en utrolig stor mængde 
råmaterialer fra lokalområdet eller i udlandet (hvilket kræver massive mængder 
transport). Desuden er de nye materialer helt uforenelige med eller endog 
ødelæggende for den eksisterende infrastruktur. 
14. Vand- og jordforurening 
a. Vandforurening henviser til forskellige virkninger på vandets kvalitet og sundhed 
inden for området. Dette kan måles ved: indholdet af kridt, nitrat, pesticider mv. 
Vandniveauet og infiltrationshastighederne, vandtemperaturerne, 
nedbørsmønstrene, grundvandsstrømmen og dræningsmønstre. På samme måde 
kan jordforurening måles ved indholdet af forurenende stoffer i jorden. 
b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Projektet indeholder ingen negative virkninger for vand og jord i 
det omkringliggende område, og det gavner endog dets sundhed og hjælper med at 
sikre, at det forbliver i en bæredygtig tilstand. 
c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Projektet resulterer i en så stor forurening af alt vand og jord i 
det omkringliggende område, at det bliver truende farligt for beboerne, floraen og 
faunaen.

transitnaboskabet og nærheden af transitmulighederne til arbejdspladser, boliger og 
fritidsaktiviteter, antallet af rejser udføres for diverse formål og med diverse 
transportformer, antallet af kilometer rejst med diverse formål og med diverse 
transportformer, såvel som indvirkningen på de bløde transportmåder (gang og

a. Driftssikkerhed refererer til punktligheden af systemet og kan være evalueret fra

b. BEDST TÆNKELIG: Transportsystemet er fuldstændigt sikkert, punktligt og inkluderer

c. VÆRST TÆNKELIG: Transportsystemet har absolut ingen robusthed og selv det 
mindste problem medfører voldsomme forsinkelser igennem systemet. Dette 
betyder at systemet bliver ekstremt irregulært og ikke punktligt.

transportformer og systemet er totalt ineffektivt og bygget så rejsen fra ethvert 
udgangspunkt til enhver destination involverer en uacceptabel rejsetid.

implementerings- og driftsprocessen og ignorerer fuldstændigt de andres meninger 
og bekymringer. Projektet får ingen støtte fra nogen af interessenterne og bliver

7.3.3 Complete stakeholder assessment survey, with example responses (Danish) 
1. Introduktion og projektformål 
Formålet med dette spørgeskema er for at samle data, som kan benyttes til at evaluere 
gennemførligheden af en ny S-tog station på Køge Bugt jernbanen ved den nye bydel, Trylleskov 
Strand, i Solrød Kommune. Denne del af undersøgelsen fokuserer på den strategiske 
gennemførlighed af projektet og benyttes til at supplere den traditionel socioøkonomiske analyse, 
som typisk benyttes til at evaluere gennemførligheden af transportinfrastruktur projekter. Denne 
type analyse benævnes normalt som Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) eller, på en mere 
præcis måde, som Multi-Actor Multi-Criteria Analysis (MAMCA), da det påtænkes at involvere 
aktuelle interessenter i evalueringen og analyseprocessen. MAMCA (og MCDA) er bevist som 
værende en effektiv fremgangsmåde til at inkludere sociale- og miljøindvirkninger, som kan ikke 
evalueres af den traditionelle socioøkonomiske analyse, da de kan ikke måles vha. økonomiske 
prissætninger (Cornet, 2016). Disse strategiske kvaliteter inkluderer sociale- og miljøindvirkninger 
som ofte er de centrale med hensyn til bæredygtigheden af et transportinfrastrukturprojekt. 
Spørgeskemaet kan forventes at tage ca. 20 minutter at udfylde. Det inkluderer en kort beskrivelse 
om projektet og udviklingen af den ny bydel, Trylleskov Stand. Derefter, præsenteres deltagerne for 
et sæt af 20 evalueringskriterier, som er samlet ved et studie af aktuel litteratur og forskning 
indenfor transportvurdering og MCDA. Hver deltager bedes om at udvælge otte til ti kriterier, som 
de mener skal benyttes til at evaluere gennemførligheden af Trylleskov Strand Station og derefter at 
rangordne disse kriterier med hensyn til deres vigtighed. Derefter, bedes deltageren om at lave en 
komparativ evaluering af situationen med implementeringen af stationen mod situationen uden 
implementeringen af stationen under hvert af de valgte kriterier. Processen for denne evaluering 
præsenteres inden for den specifikke sektion af spørgeskemaet. Til sidst, bedes hver deltager om at 
give basale informationer om involvering eller interesserer i projektet.  
Denne undersøgelse er en del af mit speciale for Kandidaten i Transport og Logistik på Danmarks 
Tekniske Universitet (DTU) og udføres som et samarbejde mellem Solrød Kommune og DTU 
Management Engineering/Transport DTU. Alle svar vil behandles fortroligt og vil kun blive 
præsenteret på en måde som sikrer anonymiteten af deltagerne.

3. Baggrundsinformation om projektet og Trylleskov Strand 
Infrastrukturprojektet som vurderes i dette studie er den mulige anlæggelse af en ny S-tog station på 
Køge Bugt jernbane. Det primære formål med stationen er at betjene beboerne i den nye Trylleskov 
Strand bydel i Solrød Kommune. Udviklingen af Trylleskov Strand begyndte i 2009, og de første 
boliger var klar til indflytning i 2013. De sidste boliger er planlagt til at blive færdige i 2019. Totalt set 
vil der være 750 boliger når udbygningen er færdig: 75 parcelhuse, og resten etage- eller rækkehuse. 
De totale areal af Trylleskov Strand er 377,000 m2 og fordeles imellem parceller eller ”øer” med 
grupper af liggende boliger. (Anlægsplanen af området kan ses i den første nedstående figur.) 
Solrøds byplanlæggere mener at, "Trylleskov Strand skal være en ny type haveby [hvor "øerne"] 
ligger let hævet i det grønne bylandskab. Det grønne bylandskab mod strandvejen skal være 
inspireret af Trylleskoven. Vest for banen skal landskabet være inspireret af mosen" (Solrød 
Kommune, 2008). Trylleskov Strand tilbyder varierende boligmuligheder, luksuriøse behageligheder 
og fællesområder hvor beboerne kan interagere med hinanden og nyde den omliggende natur. 
Et andet formål med området er at minimere biltrafikken i de centrale områder, for at sikre 
sikkerhed og frihed for alle beboere - især de frit legende børn. Optimalt vil beboerne ikke være 
afhængige af bilen og vil have mulighederne for at bruge offentlig transport. Det vil minimere 
biltrafik som kører ind og ud gennem området. I 2016, pendlede 75% af beboerne i Solrød Kommune 
ud fra kommunen (Danmarks Statistik, 2017) med en gennemsnitlig rejseafstand på 22.7 km (Center 
for Vækstanalyse, Væksthus Sjælland, & Vækstforum Sjælland, 2016). Det forventes, at

rejsemønstrene for Trylleskov Strands beboere vil følge med denne tendens, hvilket betyder at de vil 
have brug for hurtige transportformer til at rejse til og fra arbejde. Uden fornuftig tilgængelighed til 
offentlig transport er det sandsynligt, at Trylleskov Strand bliver et bilafhængigt samfund og formålet 
med at have et bilfrit bymiljø vil ikke nås. 
Trylleskoven Station vil ligge ca. 1.4 km fra Solrød Strand Station og 3.4 km fra Karlslunde Station. 
Selvom Solrød Strand Station er inden for en fornuftig cykleafstand (<3 km) fra det meste af 
Trylleskov Strand, er der ingen S-tog station inden for en fornuftig gåafstand (<1 km) (Givoni & 
Rietveld, 2007; Iacono, Krizek, & El-Geneidy, 2008; Nielsen, 2014; Solrød Kommune, 2006). 
Trylleskoven Station vil derfor forbedre tilgængeligheden markant til offentligtransport for alle 
beboerne af Trylleskov Strand. 
En station ved Trylleskov Strand var inkluderet i den originale Fingerplan for jernbanen, men den 
havde opfyldte ikke passagerkravene for at støtte anlægget (Roloff, 2007). Eftersom Trylleskoven 
Station bliver en del af den eksisterende jernbane, er de nødvendige anlægsomkostninger og 
indvirkninger relativt lave i sammenligning med andre nye transportinfrastrukturprojekter, da der 
ikke anlægges ny sporinfrastruktur, kun ny stationsinfrastruktur. Ligeledes, vil der kun skulle udføres 
små ændringer af driftsbehov og -omkostninger. Selvom den endelige udformning af stationen er 
ubekendt på dette tidspunkt, forventes det at anlægsomkostningen bliver på 87 millioner kr. (2017 
prisniveau) (Solrød Kommune, 2006).

4. Information om MCDA/MAMCA (ikke obligatorisk) 
 
Introduktion til MCDA and MAMCA: 
Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) er en evalueringsmetode som er udviklet for at løse 
problemer med traditionel socioøkonomisk analyse og cost-benefit-analyse. MCDA kan benyttes til 
at vurdere transportinfrastrukturprojekter fra et bæredygtighedsperspektiv, som inkluderer sociale, 
miljømæssige og økonomiske aspekter, der ikke kan vurderes med en ren økonomisk metode, som 
transformerer alle indvirkninger til en monetær værdi. MCDA kombinerer kriterievægte og parvise 
sammenligninger af de aktuelle alternativer med hensyn til hvert kriterium for at beregne en samlet 
karakter for hvert alternativ. De parvise sammenligninger udføres typisk vha. en numerisk skala som 
udtrykker forskellige præferencegrader.     Dette projekt benytter en særlig formulering af MCDA, 
som hedder Multi-Actor Multi-Criteria Analysis (MAMCA). MCDA er udføres typisk af eksperter, 
enten indenfor beslutningsstøtte eller ingeniørarbejdet af det aktuelle projekt, og derved

ekskluderes vigtige interessenter fra dele af evalueringsprocessen. For at løse dette problem, 
inkluderer MAMCA interessenter som vigtige bidragydere i processen. Dette sikrer at 
beslutningsanalysen inkluderer et grundigt sæt af aktuelle perspektiver, som ultimativt sikrer dybde 
og præcision i vurderingen og underbygger potentialet for projektets succes. 
For at læse mere om MAMCA, se: Cornet, Y. (2016). Indicators and beyond: Assessing the 
sustainability of transport projects. PhD thesis, Technical University of Denmark
