Abstract
As digital reading becomes increasingly central to communication, education, and in-
formation access, the role of typography in supporting readability has become of crit-
ical importance.
Although accessibility guidelines such as the Web Content Accessi-
bility Guidelines (WCAG) establish minimum technical standards, such as contrast and
text resizability—they do not offer empirical direction on the use of specific font types or
weights. This thesis examines the impact of typographic variations, specifically font type
(Roboto vs. Atkinson Hyperlegible) and font weight (Regular vs. Bold), on the reading
experience of users with and without reading difficulties.
The research is conducted under the ”Reading the Reader” project at the Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark (DTU) and is funded by the Royal Danish Academy. The objective of
this project is to explore both objective indicators, such as reading speed, comprehension
accuracy, and eye tracking data, and subjective experiences, such as perceived comfort,
clarity, and satisfaction. Using a controlled experimental setup, participants read stan-
dardized text passages rendered in different font types and weight combinations. The
study captures real-time data using eye tracking technology, post-task surveys, and com-
prehension tests.
The objectives are fourfold: - Assess the usability challenges of adaptive reading inter-
faces; - Evaluate the effects of font design on efficiency and clarity; - Identify optimal
font-weight combinations; and - Apply user-centered design principles to inform accessi-
ble digital reading. The findings of this work are expected to contribute empirical insights
to the field of inclusive typographic design, provide data-driven recommendations to in-
terface designers and developers, and complement WCAG standards with behavioral
usability evidence.
Ultimately, this research supports the development of more accessible, readable, and
user-centered digital environments for diverse reading populations.

Introduction
1.1
Background
1.1.1
Digital Reading
Over the past two decades, the transition from print to digital media has fundamentally
reshaped how people engage with text. From websites and e-books to academic portals
and social networks, digital reading has become an integral part of modern life. This shift
offers clear advantages, such as portability, instant access to diverse content, and the
ability to search, annotate, and share information dynamically. However, it also introduces
new cognitive and ergonomic demands that are not found in traditional print environments
[1].
One of the key distinctions between digital and print reading is the screen-based nature
of digital interfaces. Backlit displays, varying resolutions, pixelation, and glare all affect
visual comfort and concentration. Readers often experience screen fatigue due to pro-
longed exposure to light-emitting devices, especially when readability is not optimized
through design [2]. The constant influx of stimuli in digital contexts, such as hyperlinks,
notifications, and scrolling content, contributes to the increased cognitive load. These
conditions can compromise sustained attention, comprehension, and information reten-
tion, especially in non-linear hypertext environments [3].
Research comparing visual behaviour and reading performance on paper, computers and
tablets has revealed notable differences in eye movement patterns, reading speed, and
attentional focus. For example, print readers tend to exhibit more consistent fixation du-
rations and fewer regressions, indicative of smoother reading. In contrast, screen reading
is often associated with increased visual effort and fatigue , particularly on devices with
poor typography or inadequate customization options [1, 4].
Despite these drawbacks, digital reading environments offer unprecedented opportunities
for personalization. Readers can adjust font size, type, spacing, and contrast, features
that are particularly beneficial to users with visual or cognitive impairments. Studies have
shown that user-controlled customization can significantly reduce visual fatigue and im-
prove comprehension, especially when the screen size is small or when users are reading
under suboptimal lighting conditions [4]. Personalization tools not only enhance acces-
sibility but also enable users to adapt their reading experience to suit their needs and
preferences.
More recently, digital platforms have begun to explore adaptive interfaces, which adjust
content presentation based on user behaviour, emotion, or cognitive state. These sys-
tems, powered by machine learning and real-time user data, aim to reduce overload and
support focus in dynamic digital environments [5]. Such innovations indicate that digi-
tal reading is not merely a replication of print experiences but a fundamentally different
medium with its own strengths, challenges, and design imperatives.
In short, we can say that digital reading introduces unique challenges in cognitive pro-
cessing and visual ergonomics. While it allows for enhanced personalization and dynamic
engagement, it also demands careful attention to readability and interface design. These
conditions make typographic design, including font type and weight, a critical area for
research, particularly in optimizing readability across diverse user populations.

1.1.2
Importance of Typography in Legibility
In the digital reading era, typography plays a central role in determining how effectively text
is perceived, processed, and understood. Beyond aesthetics, it encompasses a range of
visual attributes, including font style, weight, size, spacing, and line height that directly af-
fect readability and comprehension. In the digital age, where reading increasingly occurs
on screens, typographic design has evolved into a functional interface between content
and cognition [6].
A fundamental differentiation within typographic research pertains to legibility, which refers
to the ease with which individual characters can be identified, and readability, which con-
cerns the comfort and comprehension achievable when reading and interpreting blocks of
text. A well-designed font/typeface improves both. For example, Rello & Baeza-Yates’s
[7] studies show that characteristics such as open letterforms, consistent stroke widths,
and ample spacing increase reading speed and reduce error rates. Additionally, Biliktü’s
study [8] shows that font weight has emerged as a meaningful variable; while heavier
weights can emphasize text, they may also reduce legibility if poorly designed or used
excessively.
This is particularly relevant for readers with dyslexia, who often benefit from specialised
fonts such as OpenDyslexic or Sylexiad, or readers with low vision, who often benefit from
specialised fonts such as Atkinson Hyperlegible. These typefaces incorporate unique
design elements, such as heavier baselines and exaggerated ascenders/descenders, to
reduce letter confusion and improve character recognition [9, 10].
Although Atkinson Hyperlegible is widely cited as a font optimized for legibility, especially
for users with low vision or dyslexia, there is currently limited empirical research directly
comparing it to standard fonts like Roboto in terms of objective reading outcomes. For ex-
ample, Santiago Portas (2021) [11] recommends Atkinson for accessible UI design but ac-
knowledges the need for data-driven validation. Similarly, Greaves (2022) [12] discusses
its design principles in inclusive game development but does not report quantitative find-
ings on reading performance. This highlights the need for controlled studies, such as the
current thesis, to evaluate its actual impact on comprehension and usability.
Font style, such as serif vs. sans-serif, is another long-debated topic. Sans-serif fonts like
Roboto and Helvetica are often preferred in digital interfaces due to their clean, minimal
strokes, especially at smaller sizes or on low-resolution screens. Serif fonts, although his-
torically associated with print readability, may introduce noise in digital settings. This has
led to the adoption of fonts specifically engineered for screen legibility, such as Atkinson
Hyperlegible, which enhances differentiation between similar-looking letters.
The role of typography becomes even more critical when content is viewed on various
devices and under varying conditions. A recent study highlighted that bold weights, when
carefully calibrated, can support low-vision users by improving contrast and stroke clar-
ity without overwhelming the visual field [8]. However, excessive boldness or poor font
rendering can induce visual clutter, especially in dense paragraphs.
Despite the growing body of research, font weight remains underexplored relative to other
typographic variables such as size or spacing. This gap is particularly significant given
the increasing use of digital platforms in education, health and communication domains
where clarity and accessibility are vital. Thus, empirical investigation of how font style and
weight interact to affect readability across user groups is timely and necessary.

Challenges for Readers With and Without Reading Difficulties
Typography affects all readers, but its impact is particularly profound for readers with
reading difficulties, such as dyslexia, low vision or cognitive impairments. They often
experience increased visual discomfort, slower reading speeds, or difficulty maintaining
attention, challenges closely related to the way typographic content is rendered on the
screen [8].
For people with dyslexia, even subtle typographic features such as stroke width, spac-
ing, and letter shapes can significantly affect character recognition and word decoding.
Research by Rello and Baeza-Yates (2017) [13] shows that fonts designed specifically
for dyslexia, such as OpenDyslexic, can support recognition for some users, though the
results vary depending on individual cognitive profiles. Similarly, low-vision users bene-
fit from high-contrast and well-spaced fonts that prevent visual merging of characters or
excessive fixations [8, 12]. However, as Greaves points out, there is no ”one-size-fits-all”
solution, which highlights the need for adaptive and customizable reading interfaces.
Challenges are not limited to users with diagnosed impairments. Neurotypical readers
or those without apparent reading difficulties also face cognitive and ergonomic burdens
from poorly designed type. Visual clutter, insufficient contrast, or overly decorative fonts
can induce eye strain, fatigue, or misinterpretation, even during short reading sessions
[14]. This is especially relevant in digital contexts, where extended screen exposure and
environmental variables such as glare or pixel rendering further amplify visual demand.
The complexity of these issues underscores the importance of inclusive design, which
aims to make interfaces usable for the widest range of people without requiring adaptation.
As Dunne (2024) [15] argues, inclusive typographic strategies, such as larger critical print
sizes, clearer x-heights, and bold differentiation, improve not only accessibility but also
overall usability. These approaches do not focus solely on disabled users; instead, they
embody universal design principles that elevate the experience for everyone, including
older adults, second-language readers, and mobile users under suboptimal conditions.
One of the key challenges in implementing inclusive typography is the lack of empirical
consensus on which specific font traits (such as weight and style) consistently benefit
readers. Although guidelines like WCAG recommend minimum contrast ratios and scal-
able text, they do not offer font-specific evidence for legibility and comprehension gains
across populations. This knowledge gap reinforces the value of experimental studies,
such as the current thesis, in quantifying the effects of typographic variations in controlled
settings and between user groups.
In the end, the interplay between typography and readability is a matter of both accessibil-
ity and user experience. Readers with and without diagnosed impairments are influenced
by design choices that can support or hinder engagement. Understanding these dynam-
ics is essential for developing adaptive reading interfaces that uphold the principles of
user-centred inclusive design.
1.1.3
Role of Digital Accessibility (WCAG, Inclusive Design)
Digital accessibility has become a core principle in interface design, particularly as more
services, education, and media migrate online. The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines
(WCAG), developed by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), serve as the foundational
framework to ensure that content is accessible to users with disabilities. WCAG 2.1 out-
lines specific requirements, including contrast ratios, text scalability, keyboard navigation,
and semantic structure to promote perceivable and operable content for all users [16].
In the context of typography, the WCAG provides minimum technical standards such as

a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text and resizable text up to 200% without loss
of content or functionality. These specifications are critical for users with visual impair-
ments, colour blindness, and low vision [17]. However, WCAG does not prescribe specific
typefaces, font weights, or typographic styles, leaving considerable room for subjective
design choices that may or may not support legibility and readability in practice.
Recent research has raised concerns about this limitation. For example, Coronel et al.
(2024) [18] argue that design conformance with WCAG does not always guarantee us-
ability, particularly for neurodivergent or low-vision users.
Their findings suggest that
even interfaces that meet formal compliance criteria can fall short in terms of actual user
experience, especially when poor typography undermines clarity or engagement. This
discrepancy illustrates a broader issue in digital accessibility: technical compliance does
not necessarily translate into cognitive or perceptual ease.
In response, scholars and practitioners advocate for inclusive design, a broader, user-
centred framework that goes beyond compliance to address real-world usability across
diverse populations. Inclusive design incorporates user feedback, adapts to contextual
needs (such as screen size or lighting), and treats typography as a functional, rather than
merely stylistic, element of digital content. As Sare (2025) [19] emphasizes in her analysis
of digital book covers, typographic legibility, including font selection and weight, must be
prioritized alongside WCAG’s measurable standards to ensure true accessibility.
Moreover, researchers like Biliktü (2023) [8] underscore the role of typographic flexibility,
arguing that customization options (for example, selecting bold weights or hyperlegible
fonts) should be integrated into digital platforms to accommodate individual reading pro-
files. This aligns with the principle of ”design for all”, where content adapts to users, not
the other way around.
Thus, while WCAG remains a crucial baseline, it is increasingly evident that more empir-
ical user-driven research is needed to inform how specific typographic features, such as
font weight and style, affect accessibility outcomes. The current thesis seeks to fill that
gap by testing font legibility across user groups and providing evidence-based guidance
to complement existing standards.
1.2
Problem Statement
Although the role of typography in digital readability has gained growing interest, existing
research has predominantly focused on general typographic variables such as font size,
letter spacing, or contrast ratios. Less attention has been directed towards how font style
(such as geometric vs. humanist typefaces) and font weight (such as regular vs. bold)
influence the actual reading behaviour of users in real-time digital environments. This
oversight leaves a critical gap in understanding how subtle typographic decisions affect
legibility, comprehension, and user comfort, specially when reading occurs on screens
under variable lighting, resolution, and cognitive load.
Furthermore, most existing studies that do consider font types or specialized fonts (such
as OpenDyslexic or Sylexiad) tend to evaluate them in isolated or small-scale contexts,
often focusing on either subjective user feedback or static comprehension tasks. There
is a lack of empirical data that connect font weight and style with objective reading be-
haviour, such as reading speed, eye movement patterns, and fixation counts, all of which
are measurable through technologies such as eye tracking. Without such data, design
decisions are often based on heuristics, preferences, or assumed best practices rather
than evidence-based standards.

This problem is particularly significant in accessibility-driven design. Digital platforms in-
creasingly aim to serve users with a wide spectrum of reading abilities, including those
with dyslexia, visual impairments, or cognitive challenges. However, even typographic
choices intended to improve readability may fail to deliver measurable improvements if not
grounded in rigorous testing. For example, a bold font may theoretically improve contrast,
but in practice it could also create visual crowding or decrease word shape recognition.
Similarly, a humanist typeface like Atkinson Hyperlegible may improve character distinc-
tion, but its impact on reading speed or subjective comfort remains insufficiently studied
across diverse user populations.
At the same time, accessibility standards such as the Web Content Accessibility Guide-
lines (WCAG) provide minimum contrast and layout recommendations but do not pre-
scribe specific font combinations or weight specifications. This regulatory gap highlights
the need for research that translates typographic design principles into quantifiable acces-
sibility outcomes. Bridging this gap requires empirical studies that go beyond compliance
and explore how real users respond to typographic variation in dynamic reading contexts.
Therefore, this thesis focuses on investigating how two commonly used screen-optimized
fonts, Roboto and Atkinson Hyperlegible, perform in both regular and bold weights across
user groups with and without reading difficulties. Using eye tracking data, reading speed
metrics, and user feedback, this study aims to produce evidence-based insights into the
impact of font weight and style on readability, clarity, comfort, and preference. The findings
will contribute to the broader body of inclusive design research and support more informed
typographic decisions on digital platforms geared toward accessibility.
1.3
Research Objectives, Questions & Hypotheses
1.3.1
Research Objectives
The primary goal of this research is to evaluate how typographic variation, specifically font
style and weight, affects digital readability between user groups, with and without reading
difficulties. This study aims to generate empirical data that contribute to evidence-based
inclusive typographic design practices for digital reading environments. Four specific ob-
jectives are outlined:
1. Improve digital readability using eye-tracking metrics
Using real-time eye-tracking tools, this study seeks to capture objective indicators
of reading behaviour (such as fixation duration, saccadic patterns) that reflect visual
effort, attention, and processing fluency across different typographic conditions.
2. Assess how bold fonts enhance clarity and user comfort
One of the focal points is to assess whether increasing font weight (such as Roboto
Bold or Atkinson Hyperlegible Bold) results in measurable gains in perceived com-
fort, legibility, and overall user satisfaction, particularly for users with visual or cog-
nitive reading difficulties.
3. Evaluate how font variations affect reading speed, comprehension, and sub-
jective experience
The study combines quantitative measures (reading speed and comprehension
scores) with qualitative data (subjective ratings of clarity, visual stress, and satisfac-
tion) to determine which font combinations are optimal for inclusive digital reading.

4. Provide actionable design recommendations for accessible digital content
Based on the experimental results, this thesis aims to offer practical, evidence-

1.3.2
Research Questions & Hypotheses
The research is structured around four core research questions (RQs), each paired with
a testable hypothesis (H), grounded in both theoretical insights and practical concerns
around digital accessibility.
Research Question 1 (RQ1): Usability challenges of different reading interfaces
What are the key usability challenges that readers face when interacting with reading
interfaces?
Hypothesis 1 (H1):
Readers will encounter specific usability challenges, such as reduced legibility, increased
cognitive load, or visual discomfort, when interacting with adaptive digital reading environ-
ments. These challenges may be more pronounced among users with or without visual
impairments.
Research Question 2 (RQ2): Effect of font type and weight on reading efficiency,
accuracy, and user experience
How do different typeface (Atkinson Hyperlegible vs. Roboto) and font weights (Regular
vs. Bold) affect reading efficiency, accuracy, and user experience?
Hypothesis 2 (H2):
Font weight and typeface will significantly influence the readability for users in terms of
eye tracking measurements. Specifically, bold weights and accessibility-optimized fonts
(such as Atkinson Hyperlegible which was created with low-vision readers in mind more
broadly — including those with conditions that cause central or peripheral vision loss [20])
will improve subjective satisfaction, particularly in terms of visual clarity and comfort, com-
pared to standard fonts like Roboto Regular.
Research Question 3 (RQ3): Value of user-centered design and subjective feedback
Part 1: Optimal combinations of font style and weight
Which combination of font style and typeface optimizes both objective (reading speed,
accuracy, regression ratio) and subjective (comfort, clarity) visual performance for read-
ers?
Part 2: Performance within preferred font variation and skim reading
For the preferred font weight and typeface, does reading performance differ significantly,
and does skim reading aid comprehension?
Hypothesis 3 (H3):
Part 1: A specific font-weight pairing (for example, Roboto Bold or Atkinson Hyperlegible
Bold) will outperform others across both quantitative measures (such as higher reading
speed and better comprehension) and qualitative feedback (such as reduced visual strain
and improved satisfaction).

Typography and Readability in Digital Contexts
Typography plays a functional role in digital interface design, influencing not only aesthetic
appeal but also legibility, readability, and user performance. In screen-based reading en-
vironments, where glare, resolution, and contrast vary significantly, typographic features
become essential for visual clarity and cognitive processing. Two critical concepts emerge
here: legibility, which refers to the ease of recognizing individual characters and readabil-
ity, which relates to how comfortably blocks of text can be processed and understood.
Digital typography must balance both aspects, especially when catering to diverse pop-
ulations with varied visual and cognitive needs. Neumeier’s research [21] emphasizes
that fonts designed for screen readability must account for pixel clarity, consistent stroke
widths, and spacing characteristics that reduce eye strain and improve recognition speed.
This is particularly relevant in the context of increased screen exposure and screen fa-
tigue.
In digital user interfaces, sans-serif fonts, such as Roboto, are commonly adopted for
their clean geometry and scalability. Roboto, in particular, is widely used on Android and
Google platforms and offers a modern, neutral appearance. However, Roboto was not
specifically optimized for accessibility. On the other hand, Atkinson Hyperlegible, devel-
oped by the Braille Institute, was explicitly designed to improve readability for low-vision
users. Its exaggerated letterforms, disambiguated glyphs, and open counters aim to im-
prove character differentiation, especially for users with visual impairments.
Although Atkinson Hyperlegible is widely recommended in accessibility contexts, empir-
ical studies that validate its performance remain limited. For example, Santiago Portas
[22] recommends it for accessible UI applications but acknowledges that more behavioural
evidence is required to support its widespread use. Similarly, Greaves discusses its adop-
tion in inclusive design projects but does not provide quantifiable data on readability out-
comes [23]. This evidentiary gap highlights the need for controlled research that compares
Atkinson and more mainstream fonts like Roboto between user groups and typographic
weights.
Font weight, such as regular versus bold, is another variable that can significantly influ-
ence digital readability. Biliktü’s [24] research on interface design for low-vision users
found that increased font weight, when applied judiciously, improved stroke clarity and
contrast perception, especially in visually demanding environments. However, excessive
boldness can also introduce visual crowding, particularly in dense paragraphs or small
font sizes, reducing reading fluency. Therefore, the optimal weight must balance clarity
with visual simplicity.

language decoding. For many users, especially those with reading difficulties such as
dyslexia, ADHD, or visual impairments, digital reading introduces additional layers of cog-
nitive and ergonomic strain. Recognizing the diversity of these challenges is essential in
designing inclusive and accessible digital reading experiences.

2.2.1
Dyslexia, ADHD, and Cognitive Load
Dyslexia is characterized by difficulties in accurate or fluent word recognition and de-
coding. It often manifests itself in slow reading, increased fixations, and word-skipping
errors. Typographic elements such as inconsistent letterforms or tight spacing can exac-
erbate these difficulties. Rello and Baeza-Yates (2017) [26] demonstrated that dyslexia
users benefit from typographic designs that include open letter shapes, ample spacing,
and minimal visual noise. Fonts such as OpenDyslexic and Sylexiad were developed to
address these issues through distinct letterforms and adjusted weighting to reduce letter
confusion.
Similarly, people with Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) face challenges in
maintaining visual attention and filtering distractions. Digital reading environments, with
hyperlinks, scrolling, and animations, increase the cognitive load for such users. Although
research is still emerging, studies suggest that clean and consistent typography can re-
duce visual distractions and enhance comprehension for readers with attention difficulties
[27].
2.2.2
Low Vision and Age-Related Vision Decline
Readers with low vision, including conditions such as macular degeneration or glaucoma,
require typographic support that improves contrast, spacing, and character differentiation.
Atkinson Hyperlegible font was specifically designed for this population, offering exagger-
ated letter shapes and enhanced distinguishability between characters [28]. Similarly,
age-related changes, such as reduced contrast sensitivity and slower visual processing,
demand larger font sizes and clearer strokes. Older adults often benefit from bold weights
and high-contrast text, yet WCAG guidelines do not specify font types or optimal weights
for this user group.
Accessibility-driven typography must therefore account for the intersection of ageing and
vision, particularly as the global population ages and relies increasingly on digital inter-
faces for information access and communication.
2.2.3
Typography’s Role in Assistive Readability Tools
A growing body of research has focused on assistive typefaces, including OpenDyslexic,
Sylexiad, and Atkinson Hyperlegible. These fonts are intended to support users with
reading or vision impairments by enhancing character differentiation and visual clarity.
Although the design intentions are clear, the empirical results are mixed. Hillier [29],
who designed Sylexiad based on feedback from adult dyslexic readers, found improved
reading comfort among some users, although not uniformly. Similarly, OpenDyslexic has
gained wide-spread attention but lacks large-scale validation.
These tools illustrate the potential of typography as a low-barrier assistive technology that
does not require additional hardware or screen readers. When integrated into adaptive
interfaces, they allow customization for users with diverse needs, supporting both acces-
sibility and user autonomy.
2.2.4
Neurotypical Users and Screen Fatigue
Importantly, typographic challenges are not limited to those with diagnosed conditions.
Neurotypical users, particularly those who engage in prolonged screen reading, often
experience screen fatigue, blurred vision, and reduced reading speed. These effects
are intensified by poor font rendering, inadequate spacing, or overly stylised typefaces.
Benedetto [30] reported increased visual fatigue in users reading on screens compared
to e-ink devices, particularly when font legibility was compromised.

2.3
Cognitive and Perceptual Mechanisms in Reading
Reading is not a passive visual activity but a dynamic cognitive process involving rapid eye
movements, attentional changes, and linguistic decoding. Understanding how readers
process text at the perceptual level is essential for designing effective reading interfaces,
particularly when employing eye tracking as a primary research method.
2.3.1
Eye Movement Theories: EZ Reader and SWIFT
Two dominant computational models explain how the eyes and brain coordinate during
reading: EZ Reader and SWIFT. The EZ Reader model assumes serial attention allo-
cation, where words are processed one at a time, and eye movements (saccades) are
initiated once lexical processing of the current word is complete [31]. In contrast, SWIFT
proposes a parallel attention gradient, suggesting that multiple words may be processed
simultaneously in a distributed fashion [32]. Both models emphasize how cognition con-
trols eye movements and are widely used to interpret fixation durations, regressions, and
word skipping.
These models form the foundation for interpreting eye tracking metrics, such as average
fixation duration, number of regressions (backward eye movements) and saccadic ampli-
tude. Understanding them helps contextualize how typographic changes might influence
not just visual perception but also cognitive processing during reading.
2.3.2
Fixations, Regressions, and Saccades
Reading involves alternating fixations (pauses to extract information) and saccades (quick
jumps between words). Fixation durations typically range between 200–250 milliseconds,
but can increase with text difficulty, unfamiliar words, or poor typographic clarity. Regres-
sions, or backward saccades, indicate breakdowns in comprehension or recognition and
are often elevated in texts with ambiguous syntax or visually dense layouts.
Typography directly influences these patterns. Fonts with poor character differentiation
or excessive stroke weight can increase fixation times and cause more regressions, es-
pecially in users with dyslexia or low vision. Eye tracking can reveal how such visual
characteristics affect cognitive effort during reading.
2.3.3
Visual Span and Parafoveal Preview
The visual span is the number of characters that a reader can clearly recognize during a
single fixation, typically extending 3 to 4 characters to the left and up to 15 characters to the
right of fixation in skilled readers. Larger visual spans are associated with faster reading
speeds. Parafoveal preview refers to the ability to glean partial information from upcoming
words before directly fixating on them. Both visual span and preview are sensitive to
typographic clarity.
Rayner and colleagues [33] demonstrated that clearer fonts and adequate spacing in-
crease parafoveal processing efficiency, allowing smoother saccadic planning and fewer
regressions. On the other hand, crowded or stylistically ornate fonts can reduce span
size and increase perceptual uncertainty, leading to cognitive overload and slower read-
ing rates.

2.3.4
Reading Fluency and Typographic Modulation
Reading fluency refers to the smooth, accurate and expressive delivery of text. At a
perceptual level, fluency is affected by visual familiarity with font shapes and consistency
in line spacing, character width, and x-height. Research by Slattery and Rayner (2010)
[34] showed that disfluent fonts (such as poorly designed italicized or overly bold text)
slow reading speed and increase fixation time, even among proficient readers.
Typographic modulation, such as changes in font weight or style, can be beneficial when
applied deliberately, for instance, to highlight key content or headings. However, inconsis-
tent or excessive modulation can disrupt rhythmic eye movements and increase saccadic
misalignment. For accessibility-focused design, especially when using eye tracking, such
modulation should be empirically justified.
Thus, integrating these perceptual mechanisms into typographic design allows for a more
evidence-based approach to readability. Understanding how eye behaviour responds to
font changes is not only methodologically sound, but it is also necessary for validating
interface choices grounded in user-centred design.
2.4
Eye-Tracking in UX and Reading Research
Eye tracking has become a powerful methodological tool in user experience (UX) research
and reading studies, offering fine-grained insights into how users visually engage with
interfaces and text. By recording real-time eye movements, researchers can capture
metrics that reveal cognitive load, attention distribution, and reading efficiency, especially
relevant when assessing the impact of typography on readability.
2.4.1
Core Metrics: Fixation Duration, Regression ratio, Saccadic
Amplitude and Pupil Dilation
Modern systems such as Tobii eye trackers record gaze patterns using infrared light and
high-frequency cameras. Three of the most frequently used metrics in reading and UX
studies include:
Fixation duration:
The length of time that the eye pauses to process a word or area. Longer fixations may
indicate difficulty or increased cognitive load. In reading, this can reflect unfamiliar vocab-
ulary, poor typography, or visual confusion.
Regression Ratio:
The number of backward eye movements. High regression rates are associated with
breakdowns in comprehension or word recognition and can signal issues with font legibility
or text flow.
Saccadic amplitude:
The angular distance the eye travels during a saccade (rapid movement between fixa-
tions). Larger amplitudes suggest longer jumps across words or lines, often linked to
fluent reading. Smaller or highly variable amplitudes may indicate difficulty in processing
text, which requires shorter corrective movements.
Pupil dilation:
The change in pupil size during reading. Increased dilation is frequently associated with
higher cognitive effort, mental workload, or emotional arousal. In typography and UX stud-
ies, pupil dilation can reveal subtle signs of visual strain, engagement, or task difficulty,
complementing fixation and saccade-based metrics.

These metrics are particularly valuable for comparing font types, weights, and spacing, as
they can provide objective evidence of how design choices influence reading performance

2.4.2
Applications in Reading and Accessibility Research
Eye tracking has been widely adopted in studies of digital reading, particularly to under-
stand the reading challenges faced by users with dyslexia, ADHD, or visual impairments.
For example, Rello and Ballesteros (2015) [35] used eye tracking to evaluate how dyslexic
readers interact with different fonts and spacing, finding that certain typographic adjust-
ments significantly reduced fixation durations and regressions.
Similarly, Benfatto (2016) [36] demonstrated how eye tracking patterns can be used to
diagnose dyslexia with high precision, based on saccadic behaviour and gaze distributions
[36]. This highlights eye-tracking’s dual role as both a research tool and a diagnostic aid
in accessibility.
In the UX domain, eye tracking is increasingly being used to test web interfaces, e-learning
platforms, and mobile apps. By mapping heat maps and scan paths, designers can iden-
tify areas of user confusion or distraction, enabling data-driven improvements to layout,
typography, and navigation.
2.4.3
Reliability and Limitations in Typography Studies
Despite its strengths, eye tracking comes with several methodological challenges. First,
precision varies by hardware, and lower-cost trackers (such as consumer-level Tobii mod-
els) may suffer from drift or reduced sampling accuracy during head movement. This can
affect the granularity of the fixation data, especially in fine-grained typography studies
where character-level resolution is needed [37].
Second, eye tracking reflects visual attention, but not necessarily cognitive processing.
For example, a fixation on a word does not always indicate comprehension. Therefore,
eye tracking must be combined with comprehension tests and subjective feedback for a
holistic view of reading behaviour.
Finally, environmental factors such as screen glare, lighting conditions, and participant
fatigue can influence the quality of eye tracking data. Researchers must design controlled
experiments and apply statistical filters to ensure reliability.
However, when used with care, eye tracking offers unparalleled insight into the real-time
effects of typographic variation on reading. In this thesis, Tobii-based eye tracking will be
used to assess how font style and weight (Roboto vs. Atkinson Hyperlegible; regular vs.
bold) influence reading patterns across users with and without reading difficulties. Metrics
such as fixation duration and regression count will serve as objective indicators of legibility
and visual effort, complementing subjective comfort ratings and comprehension scores.

or spacing, can have a substantial impact on readability and user comfort.

2.5.1
Overview of WCAG and Typography
The WCAG developed by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) is the global standard
for digital accessibility. WCAG version 2.1 and the newer version 2.2 focus on four foun-
dational principles: Content must be perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust
(POUR). Specific criteria relevant to text include minimum contrast ratios (4.5:1 for nor-
mal text), text resizability up to 200% and the ability to navigate and read content without
relying solely on colour or visual cues [38].
However, WCAG does not specify font style, weight, or typeface recommendations. This
omission is particularly important given that many users, especially those with dyslexia,
low vision, or cognitive impairments, are significantly affected by typographic choices.
While designers can meet WCAG’s contrast and scaling requirements, they may still use
fonts that are confusing, crowded, or visually tiring, undermining the actual usability of the
content.
2.5.2
Gaps in WCAG: Style and Weight Exclusion
Recent research highlights this gap. Coronel [39] argues that the conformity of the WCAG
does not guarantee usability, particularly for readers with neurodiverse profiles or mild
visual challenges. Similarly, Nelson and Cook [40] found that older adults performed
better with clearer and bolder fonts, even when both test versions technically met the
WCAG contrast guidelines.
This regulatory silence on font weight, typeface and spacing leaves design decisions vul-
nerable to subjectivity or aesthetic trends, rather than data-driven accessibility consider-
ations. This study helps to address this by investigating how variations in font style (such
as Roboto vs. Atkinson Hyperlegible) and weight (Regular vs. Bold) affect actual reading
performance across diverse populations.
2.5.3
Inclusive Design and UDL Principles
Inclusive design is a broader framework that emphasizes usability for the widest possi-
ble range of people, regardless of age, ability, or circumstance. It promotes adaptability,
flexibility, and personalization, not just compliance. In typography, this might mean offer-
ing multiple font options, adjustable text spacing, or bold emphasis that can be toggled
according to the reader’s preference.
Closely related is the concept of Universal Design for Learning (UDL), originally devel-
oped for educational settings. UDL advocates for presenting content in multiple ways
and providing learners with flexible means of engagement and expression. When applied
to digital typography, the principle of UDL supports interface designs that allow users to
control visual presentation (such as font type, size, spacing) to suit their perceptual or
cognitive needs [41].
This research directly contributes to this field by testing how different font choices affect
not just aesthetic preference but real-time reading performance. This kind of behavioural
evidence is critical to advance inclusive design standards beyond generalized guidelines.
2.5.4
From Compliance to Usability
A growing critique of current accessibility frameworks is that they emphasize technical
conformity over human experience. Sare [42] observed that even the ’accessible’ inter-
faces under WCAG often did not provide effective, comfortable experiences for readers
with cognitive load issues or attention challenges. This mirrors the broader movement in
UX towards user-centred design, which emphasizes iterative prototyping, feedback loops
and real-world usability testing.

Summary of Research Gaps
Despite increasing attention to digital accessibility, key typographic questions remain un-
derexplored, specially when viewed through the lens of inclusive user-centred design.
Although the literature acknowledges the importance of font characteristics, such as style
and weight, in influencing readability, empirical testing of these variables across different
user populations is notably lacking.
2.6.1
Lack of Comparative Studies on Font Weight and Style
Most existing research focuses on general design heuristics, such as contrast, font size, or
spacing, rather than font weight and style as measurable variables. Although WCAG sets
clear guidelines on contrast ratios and resizability, it remains silent on which font charac-
teristics improve usability for individuals with dyslexia, ADHD, low vision, or neurotypical
users with screen fatigue [38].
Few controlled experiments compare commonly used fonts like Roboto with accessibility-
optimized alternatives such as Atkinson Hyperlegible. Although anecdotal support exists
for fonts such as Atkinson, Sylexiad, or OpenDyslexic, large-scale behavioural validation
remains limited. For example, Santiago Portas (2021) highlights the design intentions
behind Atkinson Hyperlegible but acknowledges the absence of controlled testing with
diverse populations [22].
2.6.2
Limited Research on Bold Font Use in Accessibility
Font weight, particularly the use of bold variants, is another typographic variable with sig-
nificant design implications but minimal empirical analysis. Biliktü [24] notes that while
heavier font weights can increase character distinction for users with low vision, their im-
pact varies depending on context, crowding, and paragraph length. However, few studies
explore these effects using objective performance metrics such as reading speed, fixation
patterns, or comprehension accuracy between user groups.
This is a crucial oversight given the growing use of bold text in responsive design, mobile
interfaces, and accessibility plug-ins. Without empirical grounding, designers are left to
rely on assumptions or limited A/B testing to guide critical typographic decisions.
2.6.3
Insufficient User-Centered Evidence Using Behavioral Metrics
Another major gap is the lack of user-centred design studies based on behavioural data,
such as eye tracking. Although eye tracking tools are increasingly used in UX and mar-
keting, they remain underutilized in accessibility-driven typographic research. Holmqvist
[37] emphasizes the value of eye tracking in understanding visual effort, cognitive load,
and attentional distribution during reading. However, few studies connect specific font
characteristics to real-time visual behaviour in both typical and impaired readers.
This lack of physiological data leads to accessibility guidelines that may be technically
compliant but not experientially optimized. As Sare [42] critiques, many designs labelled
”accessible” fail to address practical usability challenges, particularly for neurodivergent
users or those reading under strain.

2.6.5
Previous Work on This Project
This thesis builds on the foundational research developed within the ’Reading the Reader’
initiative, a collaborative effort at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) funded by
the Royal Danish Academy. The project aims to understand how readers read with digital
reading environments, with a special focus on real-time interface adaptation using eye
tracking and user modelling techniques.
Adaptive Typography Interfaces
Several prior works in this research group have explored typography-aware reading inter-
faces. Palinec developed a reactive front-end capable of dynamically modifying text size,
contrast, and font weight in response to user interaction [43]. Pereira and Andrei extended
this with a CVL-specific prototype, offering graduated interventions such as progressively
bolder fonts or increased spacing based on real-time visual fatigue and performance indi-
cators [44]. Both studies validated their systems using mixed-method evaluation, showing
measurable improvements in reading speed and subjective satisfaction.
Gaze-Based Prediction and Detection
Some research has focused on creating the computational backbone for adaptive fea-
tures. Nedergaard applied a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) approach to classify fixations
and saccades with high temporal accuracy, while Løvgreen introduced predictive models
for fixation duration using low-cost trackers [45, 46]. These works provided foundational
tools to support anticipatory adjustments in user interfaces.
Integration with Font Accessibility Research
Despite this technological groundwork, previous efforts have not explicitly addressed the
perceptual impact of specific fonts on readers. The present study fills that gap by em-
pirically testing two distinct typefaces, Atkinson Hyperlegible and Roboto, in regular and
bold weights. In doing so, it combines adaptive interface thinking with accessible design,
forming a bridge between algorithmic intelligence and visual ergonomics.
Extending this foundation, several recent DTU theses under the ”Reading the Reader”
project have examined how typographic variables such as font size, spacing, and line
height influence reading behaviour using eye-tracking methods. These studies consis-
tently show that subtle adjustments, such as moderate increases in letter spacing, larger
font sizes, or adaptive line height, can improve fixation stability, reduce regressions, and
increase reading fluency, although perceived comfort does not always align with objec-
tive performance. Together, these works highlight the need for controlled, font-specific
investigations, directly motivating the present study’s focus on comparing typefaces and
weights in an evidence-based manner.

Gaps Identified in Previous Work
Although the “Reading the Reader” project has established a strong foundation for the
development of adaptive reading interfaces using gaze-based metrics, several notable
gaps remain in the empirical and perceptual validation of such systems for readers.
• Lack of font-level perceptual evaluation: Most previous studies have focused on
interface-level personalization (such as layout, contrast) without empirically exam-
ining how different font styles or weights affect reading performance.
• Limited focus on font weight: Although bold type is often assumed to improve

• No comparative testing of accessible fonts: Atkinson Hyperlegible and Roboto

• No longitudinal insight into adaptation: Previous research rarely addresses how
readability or user preference may evolve over time with repeated exposure to spe-
cific typographic settings.
• Disconnection between perceptual design and adaptive systems: Although
some work focuses on typography and others on machine learning–based person-
alization, there is limited cross-pollination between these fields.
By addressing these specific gaps, the current study aims to contribute to a more inte-
grated understanding of how accessible font configurations (style and weight) influence
reading behaviour in readers and how such insights can inform adaptive interface design
grounded in perceptual ergonomics.

Methodology
This chapter describes the experimental design, participants, materials, procedures, and
data analysis methods used to evaluate the readability and user experience of different
typefaces (Atkinson Hyperlegible and Roboto, both in regular and bold weights) among
individuals.
3.1
Research Design
This study used an experimental design within subjects using a mixed-method approach
to comprehensively evaluate how font style and weight adaptation affect reading experi-
ences for individuals with central vision loss.
3.1.1
Type of Study and Rationale
A within-subjects (repeated-measures) design was used, in which each participant was
exposed to all experimental conditions. This design controls for interparticipant variability,
which is especially important when working with a small, heterogeneous sample typical
of accessibility research. By having each participant serve as their own control, the study
increases statistical power and ensures that observed differences are more likely to be
attributable to experimental manipulations rather than individual differences in vision or
reading ability.
To capture both measurable performance and subjective experience, a mixed-method
approach was implemented. Quantitative data (such as reading speed, fixation rate, sac-
cade amplitude and pupil dilation) provided objective measures of reading efficiency and
cognitive effort, while qualitative data (such as Likert scale comfort ratings and open-
ended verbal feedback) offered insights into user preferences, comfort, and perceived
usability. The combination of these methods enabled a more nuanced understanding of
how font and weight adaptations influence both the functional and experiential aspects of
reading.
3.1.2
Overview of Font and Weight Conditions
Each participant completed reading tasks under four distinct conditions, representing
combinations of font and weight adaptations:
Roboto - Regular: Standard font and weight
Roboto - Bold: Standard font with increased weight
Atkinson Hyperlegible - Regular: Adaptive font designed for visual clarity, standard weight
Atkinson Hyperlegible - Bold: Adaptive font with increased weight
By systematically varying both the font family (Roboto vs. Atkinson Hyperlegible) and the
font weight (Regular vs. Bold), the study isolated and compared individual and combined
effects of these adaptations on reading performance and user experience.

Procedure
Each participant completed the experimental protocol in a single session, which consisted

was counterbalanced across participants using a Latin square design, ensuring that each

3.5.1
Step-by-step procedure:
Arrival and Setup: Upon arrival, participants were welcomed and informed about the
study objectives and procedures. A written informed consent paper was signed and any
questions were answered at this stage.
Seating and Calibration: Participants sit comfortably in front of the display and the Tobii
Eye Tracker 4C. The eye tracker was calibrated for each individual to ensure accurate
recording of gaze data. Participants were reminded to remain as still as possible and
maintain their focus on the screen during reading tasks.
Experimental Trials: During the experimental trials, participants were presented with
four reading passages. Participants were instructed to read them carefully at their own
pace. After finishing the initial reading, they proceeded to a set of multiple-choice com-
prehension questions, designed to assess their understanding of the text content.
An important feature of the study design was that the participants were not restricted to a
single-pass reading. Instead, they had the option of revisiting the text for skimming before
answering the comprehension questions. This option was provided to mimic realistic dig-
ital reading scenarios, where readers frequently move back and forth between a passage
and related tasks, such as answering questions or searching for specific details. Skim-
ming also helped reduce the artificiality of a strictly linear reading protocol, supporting the
ecological validity of the experiment.
All revisit events were automatically recorded by the experimental software, creating a
clear distinction between the first reading phase and subsequent skimming phases. Eye
tracking and behavioural data collected during these different phases were stored sepa-
rately, enabling a more fine-grained analysis of reading behaviour. This made it possible
to compare first-pass reading, where participants typically read for general comprehen-
sion, against skimming, where the purpose is often targeted information retrieval or con-
firmation of details.

3.6
Collected Measurements
This study is based on a comprehensive set of objective and subjective measures to eval-
uate both the efficiency and the experience of reading under different font and weight con-
ditions for participants. The measures were carefully selected not only for performance
outcomes but also for underlying cognitive effort and user perceptions.
3.6.1
Objective Measures:
Objective measurements were recorded using the Tobii Eye Tracker 4C and the custom
web-based experimental script interface. These include:
Reading Speed (Words Per Minute, WPM):
The reading speed was computed as the number of words read divided by the time (in
minutes) taken to complete each passage. This measure provided a direct indicator of
reading efficiency in all four font conditions, with higher WPM reflecting faster, more fluent
reading.
Fixation Rate (Fixations per Second):
Fixation rate captured how often a participant’s gaze paused per second while reading.
Higher fixation rates can indicate greater cognitive or visual processing demands or in-
creased scanning behaviour when text is difficult to process.
Normalized Fixation Duration (Milliseconds):
The average duration of each fixation, normalized per participant to control for individ-
ual differences. Longer fixation durations are generally interpreted as requiring greater
cognitive effort to decode or comprehend the text.
Mean Saccade Amplitude (Degrees):
This metric reflects the average length of forward eye movements (saccades) as partici-
pants progressed through the text. Shorter saccades may indicate concious and effortful
reading, while longer saccades may reflect smoother or more confident text scanning.

Regression Ratio and Regression Count:
The regression ratio represents the proportion of saccades that were backward (regres-
sive) movements to previously read text, while regression count is the absolute number of
such movements per paragraph. High regression rates typically signal reading difficulties
or the need to re-examine ambiguous or challenging text.
Pupil Dilation (Millimetres):
The average change in pupil size during reading was used as a physiological marker of
cognitive load. Increased pupil dilation has been associated with greater mental effort
and task difficulty in reading research.
Eye-Tracking Visualizations:
To qualitatively supplement numerical data, fixation duration, pupil dilation were generated
for each condition, revealing areas of text that drew the most attention.
3.6.2
Subjective Measures
Subjective measurements were collected through structured rating scales and open-ended
feedback to capture the personal, lived experiences of participants:
Likert-Scale Ratings (Readability, Clarity, Comfort, and Overall Preference):
After each reading passage, participants rated the font condition on four dimensions using
a 5-point Likert scale (1 = very dislike, 5 = very like). These scores allowed quantitative
comparison of perceived legibility, visual comfort, and general satisfaction across condi-
tions.
Preference Rankings:
Upon completing all font conditions, participants ranked the four styles from the most
to the least preferred. This holistic measure helped identify which adaptation(s) users
would actually choose for everyday reading, integrating both conscious and subconscious
preferences.
Open-Ended Comments:
Participants were invited to provide open feedback after each trial and at the end of the
experiment. These qualitative responses captured nuanced insights, such as specific
difficulties (such as “Bold text feels tiring after a while”), positive experiences (such as
“Roboto Regular is easiest on my eyes”), or suggestions for future improvements.

preference, reading phase (first reading vs. skimming), and text readability (as assessed

Data Preparation

• Alignment of log files with paragraph IDs and font conditions.

Comprehension Analysis
Comprehension accuracy was analyzed using two complementary approaches via multiple-

hension performance was examined across preferred vs. non-preferred paragraphs and
between first reading and skimming phases. This allowed evaluation of whether readers
used skimming strategically to improve comprehension, and whether font characteristics
interacted with these behaviours.
To ensure comparability of passages, LIX (Läsbarhetsindex) scores were calculated for all
four texts. These confirmed that passages ranged from “easy” to “difficult” but remained
within a controlled range of readability. LIX scores were descriptively used to contextualise
comprehension performance and rule out systematic bias from text difficulty.

Subjective Ratings
Participants rated each paragraph on comfort, ease of reading, and satisfaction using 5-
point Likert scales. These ordinal responses were treated as interval data for descriptive
purposes and averaged between participants per condition.
Furthermore, subjective preference was directly compared with objective performance:
for each participant, the font condition identified as “most comfortable” was contrasted
with their highest measured WPM and comprehension accuracy. This enabled evaluation
of alignment or mismatch between subjective perception and actual performance.

3.7.5
Preferred vs Non-Preferred Paragraph Analysis
To examine whether individual preferences translated into measurable differences, trials
were categorized as “preferred” (paragraphs displayed in a font-weight combination iden-
tified by the participant as their favourite) or “non-preferred.” Reading speed, comprehen-

Paired samples t-tests assessed within-participant differences, while linear mixed-effects
models tested whether preference was a significant predictor of performance, controlling
for font type, weight, and participant random effects.
3.7.6
First Reading vs Skimming Analysis
The logging of revisit events allowed a direct comparison of the first reading and skim-
ming phases. The descriptive statistics summarized differences in reading speed, fixation
duration, regressions, and comprehension between phases. Paired samples t-tests eval-
uated whether skimming improved comprehension at the cost of slower or more effortful
eye movements. Linear mixed-effects models further examined whether typography in-
teracted with reading phase, testing hypotheses such as whether bold fonts supported
faster skimming or whether Atkinson facilitated comprehension during rereading.
3.7.7
Inferential Statistics
The core inferential analyses addressed font type, font weight, preference, and reading
phase:
• Paired t-tests: Conducted for direct comparisons between Roboto and Atkinson
and between Regular and Bold. Effect sizes (Cohen’s d) were reported, alongside
90% and 95% confidence intervals.
• Linear Mixed-Effects Models (LMMs): For eye-tracking metrics and comprehen-
sion accuracy, LMMs were fitted with fixed effects for font type, weight, reading
phase, and preference, and random intercepts for participants and paragraphs.
Models were evaluated using likelihood ratio tests and information criteria.
3.7.8
Outlier Analysis
Outliers were identified separately for each dependent variable using the ±2.5 SD crite-
rion. The analyses were conducted first with all data included, then repeated with outliers
excluded. This approach ensured that the findings were not driven by a small number of
extreme observations. The results were consistently reported, with a note on whether the
conclusions were robust to outlier exclusion.
3.7.9
Data Visualization
Data visualization was used extensively to aid interpretation. The error bar plots and the

with overlaid individual data points, were employed for first-reading vs. skimming com-
parisons. Annotated plots displayed p-values or significance markers to connect visual-
izations with statistical tests.

By combining descriptive statistics, inferential testing,

of analysis—such as preference alignment, first reading vs skimming, and comprehen-

situated within broader debates in accessible typography.

4.1
Data Cleaning and Preprocessing
The analysis pipeline began with a systematic cleaning and preprocessing of the raw data
obtained from three sources: eye-tracking recordings (gaze coordinates, pupil size, fixa-
tion and saccade events), comprehension task responses, and subjective ratings. After
each experimental session, all datasets were exported and merged into a unified struc-
ture. The primary goal of preprocessing was to ensure that the data set was reliable and
aligned with the experimental context, thus reducing noise and enabling accurate com-
parisons across font and weight conditions.
4.1.1
Preprocessing
The first step involved filtering the raw gaze data to remove unreliable or irrelevant sam-
ples. Each gaze and pupil sample exported from the Tobii system was accompanied
by a validity code. Samples flagged with low validity, typically caused by blinks, signal
dropouts, or brief loss of eye-tracking, were systematically excluded. This procedure re-
duced noise and prevented artefacts from biasing the analysis.
Next, the noise reduction was then applied following established eye tracking practices.
Very short fixations (<60 ms) were considered physiologically implausible for meaningful
reading and therefore removed. Similarly, brief gaps in the data streams were interpolated
if they were short enough to preserve temporal continuity; longer gaps were excluded from
the analysis. Blink-related noise was also handled by filtering gaps shorter than 100 ms
and merging adjacent fixations within a 0.5° visual angle to prevent fragmentation of gaze
events.
Finally, segmentation was performed to align the gaze data with the experimental design.
Using timestamps, each sample was assigned a paragraph identifier and font condition
(Roboto Regular, Roboto Bold, Atkinson Regular, or Atkinson Bold). This ensured that
every portion of the eye-tracking data could be precisely matched to its experimental con-
text, supporting within-subject and between-condition comparisons.
4.1.2
Merging Preparation
Once the gaze data was cleaned and segmented, participant-level merging was per-
formed. Data streams, including gaze points, fixations, saccades, and pupil diameter,
were merged by participant ID, trial condition, and timestamp. This structured merging al-
lowed every recorded event to be traced back to the correct participant and experimental
condition, thus enabling robust statistical modelling.
Synchronization was also implemented to integrate subjective and objective data streams.
The participants’ Likert-scale ratings (comfort, clarity, legibility, and preference) and com-
prehension question scores were aligned with the corresponding eye-tracking segments
for each reading condition. This multimodal dataset facilitated comprehensive analyzes
linking physiological behaviour with subjective reading experiences.

4.1.3
Fixation and Saccade Detection
To extract actionable metrics, fixation and saccade detection were performed using the
I2MC (Identification by Two-Means Clustering) algorithm. Unlike traditional fixed-threshold
methods, I2MC dynamically distinguishes between stable periods (fixations) and rapid eye
movements (saccades) by clustering gaze velocity and position data. Its robustness to
noise and adaptability to individual differences make it especially suitable for real-world
datasets collected from participants who may exhibit irregular gaze patterns.
This algorithmic detection produced core metrics for subsequent analysis, including mean
fixation duration (fix_dur), saccade amplitude (sac_amp) and regression ratio. These
measures provide critical insight into reading performance and cognitive effort under dif-
ferent font and weight conditions. For example, longer fixation durations may reflect in-
creased processing difficulty, whereas higher regression ratios may indicate rereading
behaviour associated with comprehension challenges.
4.1.4
Visit Segmentation
Crucially, re-visit events were used to segment each trial into two reading phases. The
first reading phase referred to the initial continuous pass through the text, while the skim-
ming phase captured revisits occurring before answering comprehension questions. Both
phases were logged separately, allowing fine-grained analysis of reading strategies and
allowing comparisons between initial processing and later skimming behaviours.
4.1.5
Preference Coding and Documentation
The participant preference data were merged into the final dataset, with each trial coded
as “preferred” or “non-preferred” depending on whether it matched the participant’s stated
preference for font combination. This enabled analyzes comparing subjective preference
with objective performance outcomes.
3×IQR Outlier Filtering
Outliers were identified using the interquartile range (IQR) method. Specifically, any val-
ues that are above three times the IQR below the first quartile (Q1 – 3×IQR) or above
the third quartile (Q3 + 3×IQR) were flagged as extreme observations. This conservative
threshold was chosen instead of the more commonly used 1.5×IQR in order to minimize
the risk of excluding valid but naturally variable reading behaviour, which is common in
eye tracking and human performance data. Outlier filtering was applied separately for
each dependent variable and condition to account for within-subject differences. Ana-
lyzes were conducted both with and without these outliers to evaluate the robustness of
results.

Reading Speed and Comprehension

Comprehension accuracy was determined from the participants’ responses to multiple-

ages of correct answers out of the total of answered questions. Reporting accuracy in
percentage terms allowed for easier comparison between participants and conditions,
ensuring interpretability beyond raw counts. Tables highlighted overall comprehension
success, while bar plots displayed the distribution of correct versus incorrect responses.
4.2.2
Subjective Ratings
In addition to objective metrics, participants self-reported comfort, legibility, and overall
satisfaction using a Likert scale format. These subjective evaluations were summarized
by calculating mean values and standard deviations per condition. Comparisons between
font conditions allowed for the preliminary identification of typographic setups perceived
as more or less favorable. Box plots and violin plots were used to visualize the spread
of responses, capturing both central tendency and variability in participants’ subjective
impressions.
4.2.3
Eye Tracking Measures
Eye-tracking metrics offered deeper insight into the cognitive processes underlying read-
ing performance. Three main measures were summarized descriptively:
Fixation duration: The average time (in milliseconds) the eyes remained stable in a given
location. Longer fixations were interpreted as potential indicators of increased processing
difficulty.
Regression ratio: The proportion of backward saccades relative to total saccades, re-
flecting rereading or difficulty in text comprehension.
Saccade amplitude: The angular distance of forward eye movements, reflecting the span
of visual intake per saccade.
For each metric, means and standard deviations were calculated per condition, with outlier
distributions noted for subsequent inferential checks. Summary tables provided numerical
values, while error-bar plots visualized condition-wise differences in central tendency with
confidence intervals.
4.2.4

An additional dimension of the descriptive analysis is participants preference. Since each
participant selected a preferred font condition, all tests were coded as either “preferred”
or “non-preferred.” Descriptive comparisons were then made to evaluate whether partic-
ipants read faster, achieved higher comprehension scores, or rated texts more positively
when reading in their chosen condition. Visual summaries included grouped bar charts
and hybrid box/violin plots, providing intuitive representations of differences across the
preference dimension.

4.3
Inferential Statistics
Inferential analyzes were performed to formally test whether differences between the four
font and weight conditions (Roboto Regular, Roboto Bold, Atkinson Regular, Atkinson
Bold) were statistically meaningful. Two complementary approaches were used: paired
t-tests for direct within-subject comparisons and linear mixed models (LMMs) for model-
based analysis that account for repeated measures and variability at both participants and
paragraph levels.
4.3.1
Paired t-tests across font–weight conditions
Paired t-tests were performed between all six possible pairs of typeface and font weight
combinations: Roboto Bold vs.
Roboto Regular, Roboto Bold vs.
Atkinson Regular,
Roboto Bold vs. Atkinson Bold, Roboto Regular vs. Atkinson Regular, Roboto Regu-
lar vs. Atkinson Bold, and Atkinson Regular vs. Atkinson Bold. with dependent variables
included reading speed (words per minute) and eye tracking metrics (fixation count, mean
fixation duration, fixation rate, saccade amplitude, pupil dilation, regression ratio).
For each comparison, results were reported in terms of the t statistic, degrees of freedom,
and p-value. In addition, confidence intervals (90% and 95%) were provided to quantify
the strength and precision of observed effects. This comprehensive set of comparisons
ensured that differences were not only tested for broad contrasts but also for specific
font–weight pairings.
4.3.2
Linear Mixed-effects Models (LMM)
To complement the t-tests, LMMs were fitted with typeface (Roboto vs. Atkinson) and
weight (Regular vs. Bold) specified as fixed effects and random intercepts for participants
and paragraphs. In some cases, random slopes were also considered to capture differen-
tial sensitivity to conditions. This approach allowed the analysis to retain all usable trials,
even when some participants had missing data due to exclusions during preprocessing.
LMMs produced model-based estimates of mean differences, accompanied by coeffi-
cients (β), standard errors, p-values, and 95% confidence intervals. Pairwise contrasts
between all typeface and font weight conditions were additionally derived from the LMMs,
enabling direct comparison of conditions while accounting for repeated measures.
Marginal and conditional R² values were calculated to indicate the proportion of variance
explained by fixed effects alone and by the full model, respectively.
4.3.3
Integration of methods
In practice, paired t-tests and LMM-based pairwise contrasts were reported. Although
the t-tests provided straightforward comparisons of mean differences, the LMMs offered
a model-based perspective that incorporated random effects at the participant and para-
graph levels. By presenting both sets of results, transparency was ensured and triangu-
lation was allowed: the t-tests highlighted direct within-subject effects, while the LMMs
confirmed whether these differences remained robust under a more flexible model. This
dual strategy provided complementary information and reinforced the reliability of the find-
ings.

4.4
Sub-Analyses
In addition to the primary analyzes that compared performance across font families and
weights, two sub-analyses were conducted to explore factors that may modulate reading
behavior. These included (a) the influence of participant preference and (b) the distinction
between first-reading and skimming phases. Together, these analyzes provided informa-
tion on how subjective factors and reading strategies interact with typographic variation.
4.4.1
Preferred vs. Non-Preferred Conditions
Each participant was asked to identify their preferred font condition (such as Roboto Bold
or Atkinson Regular). Using this information, all trials were categorized as either pre-
ferred or non-preferred, depending on whether the font matched the participant’s stated
preference. This coding allowed a direct test of whether subjective liking translated into
measurable differences in objective performance.
Performance outcomes were evaluated in three dimensions:
Reading speed (WPM): whether participants read more quickly when texts were pre-
sented in their preferred style.
Comprehension accuracy: whether the preferred fonts facilitated more accurate re-
sponses to comprehension questions.
Eye-tracking metrics: including fixation duration, regression ratio, and saccade ampli-
tude, to assess whether preference altered cognitive effort and reading tests.
Paired t-tests compared preferred vs non-preferred conditions within each participant. In
parallel, linear mixed models (LMMs) were fitted with preference status as a fixed effect
while including random intercepts for participants and paragraphs. This allowed for more
robust modelling of repeated measures and potential inter-individual analysis.
These analysis not only tested the hypothesis that preference improves performance but
also examined whether subjective comfort is aligned with measurable efficiency. For ex-
ample, a scenario in which preferred fonts yield higher satisfaction ratings but no mea-
surable differences in comprehension or fixation behaviour would suggest a dissociation
between subjective and objective outcomes.
4.4.2
First Reading vs. Skimming Phases
A second sub-analysis investigated the role of the reading phase. Using visit logs, tests
were segmented into two phases:
First reading: Defined as the participant’s initial continuous pass through the text.
Skimming: Defined as any revisits to the text prior to answering comprehension ques-
tions.
This distinction allowed for a fine-grained examination of how reading strategies influence
comprehension and processing effort.
Paired comparisons were conducted to test whether comprehension improved during
skim reading compared to first reading and whether this improvement came at the cost of
increased cognitive load. Specific hypotheses included: (a) skim reading would lead to
higher comprehension scores, reflecting the opportunity to revisit difficult passages; and
(b) skim reading would be associated with longer fixation durations and higher regression
ratios, reflecting more effortful analysis.

4.4.3
Outlier Treatment
To ensure the robustness of the results, special attention was paid to the identification
and treatment of outliers. Outliers were defined using a conservative statistical rule: any
observation falling more than ±2.5 standard deviations from the mean within a given par-
ticipant and the condition was flagged for review. This approach allowed the detection
of extreme values at the individual level, rather than applying a blank criterion across the
entire dataset, thereby respecting inter-individual differences in reading behaviour.
Outlier checks were applied between all dependent variables, including reading speed,
comprehension accuracy, fixation duration, regression ratio, saccade amplitude, and sub-
jective ratings. The rationale for examining outliers separately within each condition was
that reading times and fixation patterns can vary not only by participant but also by font
style and weight. By isolating extreme values within these strata, the method minimized
the risk of falsely classifying legitimate but condition-specific variations as anomalies.
Analyses were then conducted under two scenarios. The first included the complete data
set with all participants, regardless of the outlier status. The second excluded flagged
values, resulting in a ”cleaned” data set. Running all statistical tests twice, once with and
once without outliers, it was possible to assess whether significant results were driven by
a small number of extreme cases.
In most cases, the results remained consistent between both versions of the data set,
suggesting that the main findings were not influenced unduly by extreme values. When
minor differences emerge, they are explicitly noted in the Results chapter, along with a
justification for whether the full or cleaned data set was prioritized for interpretation. In
general, the inclusion of outliers was retained unless their removal materially improved
model fit or corrected implausible distortions.
This two-stage approach improved confidence in the validity of the findings and demon-
strated a transparent handling of the variability inherent in human reading data.

5.1
Overview
The purpose of this chapter is to present the empirical results of the experimental study
investigating how variations in typeface and font weight influence digital reading perfor-
mance among participants with and without reading difficulties. As detailed in the research
design, the study systematically compared two typefaces, Roboto and Atkinson Hyperleg-
ible, in both Regular and Bold weights. Beyond these font-level comparisons, additional
analyzes examined whether outcomes differed for preferred vs non-preferred paragraphs
and whether performance varied between first reading and skim reading.
To provide a clear and transparent account, the results are structured into four main sec-
tions. The first set of findings focuses on objective performance measures, including
reading speed (words per minute) and comprehension accuracy (percentage of correct
responses). These measures offer direct evidence of efficiency and understanding be-
tween conditions. The second section reports on eye-tracking metrics, which capture
the cognitive and perceptual processes underlying reading. Specifically, fixation dura-
tion, fixation rate, saccade amplitude, regression ratio, and pupil dilation are examined to
evaluate differences in visual effort and reading fluency.
The third section presents subjective ratings, gathered through Likert scales and prefer-
ence rankings. These measures reflect the perceptions of participants’ clarity, comfort and
overall satisfaction, providing information on how font variations influence the lived read-
ing experience. Finally, two sub-analyses are included: one contrasting preferred versus
non-preferred font conditions and the other comparing first-pass reading with subsequent
skimming. These additional layers of analysis enable a more nuanced understanding of
individual differences and strategic reading behaviours.
Throughout the chapter, results are first introduced with descriptive statistics to summarize
central tendencies and variability, followed by inferential statistics, including paired t-tests
and linear mixed-effects models (LMMs) to assess the significance and robustness of
observed differences. Where relevant, results are illustrated with tables and graphical
visualisations to aid interpretation. Importantly, this chapter focuses solely on reporting the
findings, while interpretation and theoretical implications are reserved for the subsequent
Discussion chapter.

Inferential Statistics
To move beyond descriptive patterns, inferential analyses were performed to test whether
observed differences across font style and weight were statistically reliable. Two ap-
proaches were used: paired t-tests, which provide straightforward condition contrasts,
and linear mixed-effects models (LMMs), which account for repeated measures within par-
ticipants. Together, these methods offer both accessible pairwise comparisons and more
robust modelling of individual variability. Unless otherwise noted, all effects are reported
with 95% confidence intervals; exploratory 90% CIs are clearly labelled where applied.
The following subsections present results for the paired t-tests, followed by LMMs, with
significance levels and confidence intervals reported for all metrics.

6.1
Interpretation of Key Findings
6.1.1
Font Weight and Typeface Effects
The findings indicate that font weight exerted a stronger influence than typeface on
eye-movement behaviour and cognitive effort. Bold weights, particularly Roboto Bold,
were associated with shorter fixations, fewer regressions, and smaller pupil dilation val-
ues, reflecting lower cognitive load and smoother reading. While Roboto Bold was not
consistently superior in all objective measures, it received the highest subjective ratings
for clarity and comfort. Differences between Roboto Regular and Atkinson Regular were
relatively minor, indicating that typeface alone did not reliably predict reading efficiency.
Both typefaces share geometric, open-counter letterforms optimised for legibility, which
may account for their comparable baseline performance. However, Atkinson Hyperlegi-
ble’s exaggerated character distinctions did not yield proportional gains in fluency, sug-
gesting that readability benefits depend more on stroke contrast and weight balance than
on accessibility-driven form alone. These findings align with [24], who reported that mod-
erate boldness can enhance stroke clarity for low-vision readers, though excessive weight

Robustness of Results
A critical step in evaluating the reliability of experimental findings is assessing their ro-
bustness in the presence of variability and potential outliers. Eye tracking and behavioural
data are naturally heterogeneous, reflecting differences in participants’ cognitive strate-
gies, visual acuity, and familiarity with digital reading. To address this, this study used
outlier analyses based on a conservative criterion (±2.5 standard deviations from condi-
tion means and an additional 3×IQR filter for sensitivity checks). The results were then
compared across full and cleaned datasets to determine whether the main findings were
dependent on extreme values.
In total, less than five percent of trials were identified as outliers using the 3×IQR criterion,
confirming that the dataset retained the vast majority of observations. Extreme dispersion
was most evident in the Atkinson Bold condition (SD ≈792 WPM), yet the removal of
these high-speed cases did not alter the relative ordering of means across font–weight
combinations (Fig. 5.24). This indicates that the principal effects of font weight and pref-
erence were not artefacts of extreme individual responses but reflected consistent trends
between participants. 4
The analyses showed that the overall trends were largely consistent, regardless of whether
outliers were retained or removed. For reading speed (WPM), for instance, the ordering
of font conditions remained stable: Atkinson Bold and Roboto Regular produced higher
average speeds compared to Roboto Bold and Atkinson Regular. Although individual ex-
treme cases inflated standard deviations, particularly in the Atkinson Bold condition, the
central patterns were preserved after trimming. This suggests that differences in WPM
were not artefacts of a small number of unusually fast or slow readers but reflected sys-
tematic variation across conditions. Specifically, the hierarchy in reading speed (Atkinson
Bold > Roboto Regular > Roboto Bold > Atkinson Regular) persisted across both the
full and trimmed datasets (Fig. 5.24). Likewise, preference-related differences in com-
prehension (approximately 8% higher for preferred texts) and reductions in mean pupil
size remained directionally consistent even when extreme values were excluded. These
parallel trends confirm that the observed effects are robust to data filtering and not driven
by outlier behaviour. 5

and fewer fixations, indicating more efficient processing, whereas non-preferred condi-

values were excluded, reinforcing the robustness of preference-related advantages. Sim-
ilarly, saccade amplitude patterns were largely unaffected by trimming. Both first reading

Practical Implications
The outcomes of this study offer several practical insights for the design of digital reading
interfaces, particularly in contexts where accessibility and inclusion are central concerns.
The evidence demonstrates that font type and weight not only influence objective reading
efficiency and comprehension but also shape subjective comfort and user satisfaction.
This dual impact underscores the importance of integrating both performance data and
user preferences into interface design. Quantitatively, these practical implications derive
from the empirical patterns reported in the results chapter, where bold weights reduced
regressions and pupil dilation while preferred styles improved comprehension by roughly
eight percentage points.
First, the results suggest that no single font–weight combination universally optimizes per-
formance. Atkinson Bold (M = 588.8 WPM; SD = 792.5) consistently improved reading
speed and reduced regressions, making it useful for scenarios that demand quick infor-
mation extraction, such as scanning reports or reviewing large volumes of text. In con-
trast, Roboto Regular produced the highest comprehension accuracy and may therefore
be preferable in tasks requiring careful understanding, such as studying or exam prepa-
ration. Roboto Bold was rated most favourably by participants in terms of comfort and
clarity, suggesting that it may be particularly valuable for everyday reading tasks where
long-term comfort is prioritized. These findings highlight that digital systems should not
prescribe a single setting but instead provide flexible options that users can adapt to their

individual needs and reading contexts.
Second, the study emphasizes the importance of personalization. Readers performed
better in their preferred font condition, both in first reading and in skim reading, sug-
gesting that allowing users to choose their preferred typography can mitigate usability
challenges. Incorporating adaptive or preference-sensitive defaults, where systems re-
member a reader’s chosen style, could further enhance accessibility. This is particularly
important for readers with dyslexia, low vision, or other reading difficulties, who may rely
heavily on customization to optimize their experience. These quantitative and percep-

frameworks.
Third, the findings carry implications for accessibility standards such as the WCAG. Cur-

6.6
Limitations
Although the findings of this study provide valuable insight into the role of typography in
digital reading, several limitations should be acknowledged. These limitations inform the
interpretation of the results and highlight areas for future research.
Small Participant Group
The study involved a relatively small number of participants, most of whom were univer-
sity students or young adults with similar levels of digital literacy. Although this group
provided useful data for controlled experimentation, the sample size restricts the statisti-
cal power of the analyses and may limit the generalisability of the findings. Larger, more
heterogeneous participant groups would allow for more robust conclusions.
Limited Stimuli: Only Two Fonts and Two Weights.
The experimental design compared only two typefaces (Roboto and Atkinson Hyperleg-
ible) in two weights (Regular and Bold). Although these choices were sufficient to test
hypotheses about font weight and accessibility-focused fonts, they do not capture the
wider spectrum of typographic variation encountered in practice. Features such as ital-
ics, semi-bold weights, letter spacing, line spacing or alternative font families (serif vs.
sans-serif) were not included. This limits the breadth of conclusions that can be drawn.
Artificial Laboratory Setting
All tasks were performed in a controlled laboratory environment using standardized para-
graphs and eye tracking technology. Although this ensured precision and repeatability,
it does not fully reflect real-world reading contexts where device variability, multitasking,
lighting conditions, and environmental distractions may play significant roles. The eco-
logical validity of the findings is therefore limited.
Short Exposure Duration
Participants were only exposed to each font condition for a short period during the exper-
iment. Although this enabled the collection of detailed eye tracking data, it does not cap-
ture long-term adaptation or fatigue effects that may arise during extended use. Results
should therefore be interpreted as reflecting immediate responses rather than sustained
reading behaviour.
Cultural and Linguistic Constraints
The study used English-language reading paragraphs and participants were primarily
proficient English readers. Typography effects may vary across languages and writing
systems, particularly those with different orthographies (such as logographic or highly in-
flected scripts). Cultural familiarity with certain fonts may also influence preference and
readability. Thus, the findings may not generalise in linguistic or cultural contexts.
In summary, the limitations of this study is small sample size, restricted font selection,
artificial laboratory conditions, short exposure duration and cultural constraints- mean that
the results should be viewed as indicative rather than definitive. However, they provide a
strong empirical foundation for more comprehensive investigations in future work.

6.7
Future Work
The limitations of this study highlight several avenues for future research that could ex-
pand and refine the understanding of the role of typography in digital reading. Addressing
these directions will strengthen both the empirical foundation and the practical impact of
design recommendations.
Exploration of Additional Fonts and Weights
Future studies should extend beyond Roboto and Atkinson Hyperlegible to include a wider
range of font families (such as serif, sans-serif, monospaced) and font weights (light,
semi-bold, extra-bold). Investigating typographic features such as italics, letter spacing,
and line spacing would provide a more comprehensive view of how visual presentation
interacts with reading behaviour.
Larger and More Diverse Participant Populations
Subsequent research should recruit larger samples that capture greater demographic di-
versity, including age groups beyond young adults, individuals with diagnosed reading
difficulties such as dyslexia, and participants with varying degrees of visual impairment.
Expanding participant diversity will improve the generalisability of results and ensure that
design recommendations address the needs of different user populations.
Longitudinal and Ecological Studies
The short exposure duration in the present study limits conclusions about long-term adap-
tation to font conditions. Future research should adopt longitudinal designs to assess
whether readability and preference patterns change over time, particularly under sus-
tained reading. In addition, ecological studies conducted in naturalistic contexts, such as
the use of everyday digital devices at home, school, or work, would enhance the ecologi-
cal validity of the findings and capture the influence of environmental factors.
Cross-Linguistic and Cross-Cultural Validation
Given that this study was limited to English language paragraphs and primarily English
proficient participants, future work should examine typographic effects across languages
with different orthographic systems, such as logographic (Chinese), syllabic (Japanese
Kana) or highly inflected languages (Finnish, Turkish). Cross-cultural studies would clarify
whether cultural familiarity with certain fonts shapes preferences and performance.
Integration with Adaptive Technologies.
An important avenue for future research is the development and evaluation of adaptive
reading systems that dynamically adjust font type and weight based on user preferences
or performance data. Such systems could employ machine learning to identify optimal
configurations in real time, improving both efficiency and user satisfaction.

7
Conclusion
7.1
Restating the Aim
The aim of this thesis was to investigate how variations in font type (Roboto vs. Atkin-
son Hyperlegible) and font weight (Regular vs. Bold) influence the digital reading expe-
rience of users with and without reading difficulties. The study addressed four research
questions, focusing on usability challenges, the effects of typeface and weight on reading
efficiency and comprehension, the identification of optimal combinations, and the role of
user-centred design in shaping accessible digital reading environments. By combining
eye-tracking data, comprehension measures, and subjective ratings, the thesis sought to
provide a comprehensive understanding of how typographic variation shapes both cogni-
tive processing and user experience.
7.2
Summary of Key Findings
The results demonstrated that font weight had a stronger and more consistent impact
on reading behaviour than typeface. Bold fonts reduced regressions and pupil dilation,
suggesting lower cognitive effort, while Atkinson Bold enabled the fastest speeds. Roboto
Regular supported the highest comprehension accuracy, and Roboto Bold was rated most
favourably in terms of comfort and clarity. Importantly, participants consistently performed
better in their preferred font condition, both in the first reading and in the skim reading
tasks, underscoring the significance of personal alignment. The study also confirmed
a clear speed–accuracy trade-off between first reading and skimming, with preferences
mitigate some of the comprehension loss associated with faster reading. Collectively,
these findings show that boldness does affect reading, but its benefits are nuanced and
context-dependent.
7.3
Theoretical Contributions
This thesis contributes to cognitive psychology by linking typographic variation to core
reading mechanisms such as fixation behaviour, perceptual span, and regression as a re-
pair strategy. Bold fonts improved stroke clarity and reduced uncertainty, enabling faster
lexical access. Preferences were shown to improve perceptual fluency, freeing cognitive
resources for higher-level comprehension. The moderation of skim reading by prefer-
ence extends theories of shallow versus deep processing, showing that typography can
buffer against comprehension loss in time-limited reading. In doing so, the study provides
empirical evidence that typographic design is not a neutral surface feature, but an active
factor in cognitive processing.
7.4
Practical Contributions
On a practical level, the thesis offers evidence-based guidance for designers of digital
reading platforms.
The results demonstrate that no single font–weight pairing is uni-
versally optimal; instead, optimal performance depends on context and user preference.
Atkinson Bold may suit tasks that require rapid scanning, Roboto Regular may be best for
comprehension-focused tasks, and Roboto Bold is valuable for long-term comfort. These
insights support the design of adaptive systems that allow readers to switch between
the “speed” and “comprehension” modes or adjust settings to suit personal needs. The
findings also reveal gaps in current accessibility standards, such as the WCAG, which

overlook the role of typeface and weight. Incorporating empirical evidence on typogra-
phy into such standards would enhance the inclusion of readers with and without reading
difficulties.
7.5
Final Reflection on Bold Typeface
The central theme of this thesis was whether bold typeface impacts the reading experi-
ence. The evidence confirms that it does: bold fonts consistently influenced eye move-
ments, cognitive load, and subjective perception. However, the effects were not uniform.
Boldness improved efficiency in some cases but reduced comfort in others, and its ben-
efits depended on the pairing of typeface and user preference. Thus, bold should not be
regarded as a universal solution, but as a flexible tool within a broader, personalisable
design system. Its true value lies in providing users with choices that align typography
with their goals and needs.
7.6
Limitations and Future Outlook
The scope of this study was limited by several limitations, including a small participant
group, a focus on only two typefaces and two weights, the artificiality of a laboratory
setting, short exposure duration, and linguistic homogeneity. These limitations highlight
opportunities for future work: expanding the typographic scope, recruiting larger and more
diverse populations (including readers with dyslexia and visual impairments), conducting
longitudinal and ecological studies in real world environments and extending research
across languages and cultures. Future efforts should also explore adaptive systems that
use preference data or machine learning to personalise typography in real time.
7.7
Closing Statement
In conclusion, this thesis has shown that typography, particularly bold typeface, plays
a significant and complex role in digital reading. By combining behavioural, cognitive
and subjective perspectives, it provides a nuanced understanding of how font type and
weight shape reading efficiency, comprehension and comfort. The findings advance the-
ory, inform practice, and highlight the importance of user-centred and evidence based
approaches to accessible design. Bold fonts matter, but not in isolation: they are most
effective when embedded within flexible, adaptive systems that respect individual differ-
ences. This study thus contributes to the growing recognition that inclusive design re-
quires balancing universal principles with personal adaptability, ensuring that digital read-
ing environments are not only functional but truly accessible for all.

C
Executive Summary
Typography plays a central role in shaping the way readers perceive and process digi-
tal text. Although conventional design decisions often emphasize aesthetics, there is a
growing need to understand how font weight and typeface influence reading efficiency,
comprehension and comfort, particularly for readers with and without reading difficulties.
This thesis investigates whether bold typeface variations—specifically Roboto and Atkin-
son Hyperlegible in Regular and Bold weights—impact reading performance and user
experience.
Aim and Research Questions
The aim of the study was to evaluate how font type and weight affect reading behaviour
and to provide evidence-based recommendations for accessible digital design. Four re-
search questions guided the work:
(1) What usability challenges do readers face in adaptive reading interfaces?
(2) How do font style and weight affect speed, accuracy, and satisfaction?
(3) Which combinations of type and weight provide optimal performance, and how does
preference influence reading under first and skim conditions?
(4) How can user-centred design principles inform more accessible reading systems?
Methodology
The study recruited participants to read standardised paragraphs in four font conditions:
Roboto Regular, Roboto Bold, Atkinson Regular, and Atkinson Bold. Eye-tracking metrics
(fixations, saccades, regressions, pupil dilation), behavioural outcomes (reading speed
and comprehension), and subjective ratings (comfort, clarity, preference) were collected.
Both statistical tests and mixed-effects modelling were applied to identify reliable effects
across conditions.
Key Findings
The results showed that font weight exerted stronger effects than typeface. Bold condi-
tions reduced regressions and pupil dilation, suggesting a lower cognitive load, although
comfort ratings did not always align with efficiency. Atkinson Bold produced the fastest
reading speeds, while Roboto Regular supported the highest comprehension accuracy.
Roboto Bold was rated most favourably by participants for comfort and clarity, despite
showing signs of higher cognitive effort. Importantly, participants consistently performed
better in their preferred font condition, both in first reading and skimming, indicating that
personal alignment strongly influences outcomes. Comparison of reading strategies con-
firmed a clear speed–accuracy trade-off, with preference mitigating some comprehension
loss during skimming.
Contributions
This thesis makes both theoretical and practical contributions. Theoretically, it links typo-
graphic variation to cognitive models of reading, showing how bold fonts expand percep-
tual span and reduce reliance on regressions and how preference enhances perceptual
fluency. It also extends the understanding of shallow versus deep processing by showing
that typography can buffer against comprehension loss in fast reading. Practically, the

can select typography to match their goals and needs. The findings also highlight gaps

empirical recommendations on typeface and weight.
Conclusion
This thesis demonstrates that bold typeface does impact the reading experience, but not
in a uniform way. Its benefits are context-dependent, shaped by typeface pairing, task
demands, and user preference. Rather than prescribing a universal solution, the study ar-
gues for personalisable design systems that integrate user choice. In doing so, it provides
an evidence-based foundation for advancing accessible digital reading environments, en-
suring inclusivity for both typical readers and those with reading difficulties.
