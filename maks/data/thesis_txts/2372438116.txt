Abstract
With increasing global population and energy demand sustainable alternatives to petroleum
is needed to sustain our living standards without emitting critical amounts of green house
gases. In this thesis a processing network for bioreﬁneries has been developed starting with a
single biomass feedstock and branching into four diﬀerent value-added chemical products. The
network has been formulated as a superstructure based on the framework by Quaglia et al.[1]
and Bertran et al.[2]. To select the biomass feedstock used, a one parameter screening method
has been developed screening biomass specie and location based on harvest ﬁeld yield.
The best candidate resulting from the biomass screening method was found to be wheat straw in
France. The four products produced from the wheat straw were selected to be 1,3-propanediol
(1.76 $/kg), 2,3-butanediol (3 $/kg), succinic acid (3 $/kg) and itacoic acid (2 $/kg) with glucose
as a common intermediate. They were chosen based on their potential as platform chemicals in
a future bioeconomy, their current research interest and current market potential. The wheat
straw is converted to glucose via mechanical grinding, steam explosion with 0.2 wt% [H2SO4
and enzymatic hydrolysis. The glucose was converted to the products via maximum theoretical
conversion based on redox potentials. The four products were produced at broth concentrations
observed in literature. 1,3-propanediol and 2,3-butanediol were recovered at 99.9% purity via a
one step distillation. Succinic acid and itaconic acid were recovered at 99.5% purity via a two-
step recovery process having a ﬂash and a crystalliser. The superstructure and unit operation
speciﬁcations were setup based on literature and simulations. The superstructure was translated
into a mixed-integer linear programming problem via the user-interface Super-O and solved for
two diﬀerent scenarios. In scenario 1 the conversion of glucose to succinic acid is 100% and in
scenario 2 the conversion is 80%. The solution for both scenarios showed the ﬂowsheet producing
succinic acid to be best with an economic objective function. The operating proﬁt of the process
for scenario 1 was found to be 37804.6 $/hr and have a capital cost of 59 m $. This gives a
payback time 65 days. The operating proﬁt and capital cost for scenario 2 was found to be
26733.4 $/hr and 51 m $ respectively. This gave a payback time of 80 days, slightly more than
scenario 1. The second best in both scenarios was production of itaconic acid, third best was
2,3-butanediol and worst was 1,3-propanediol. The sensitivity of the results were investigated
with a sensitivity analysis and the economic evaluation was found to be most dependant on the
product price and second most on the utility cost.
Finally the method for biomass selection was evaluated through a transportation estimate based
on yield and process plant capacity. It was found that the harvest ﬁeld yield has almost no
impact on the ﬁeld-to-gate price of biomass thus selecting the biomass and location based on
this variable alone is not suﬃcient.

1
INTRODUCTION
In order to not exceed the critial amount of emitted CO2 the global use of fossil fuels have to be
reduced, for energy consumption as well as in the petrochemical industry. A promising alterna-
tive to fossil fuels is renewable biomass with a total available amount of 2000 billion tonnes and
a production rate of 150 billion tonnes/year[11]. Biomass can be used for heat and electricity,
but also as an alternative raw material in the chemical industry to produce chemical products
such as polymers, medicine and fuel.
Biomass is categorised as organic matter coming from living or recently living organisms. The
mass is primarily grown from CO2, water and solar energy via the photosynthesis. This makes
biomass a CO2-neutral renewable material, thus if utilised capable of reducing green house gas
emission and act as an alternative renewable raw material for energy and in the chemical industry.
1.1.2
Process synthesis tools
In 2012, Quaglia et al.
[1] proposed an integrated business and engineering framework for
the synthesis and design problem. The framework is developed to avoid the decomposition of
the synthesis and design problem into two layers, the strategical layer and the tactical layer.
The decomposition is a challenge because the problem is not independent across the two layers
but interdependent since calculations of economy indicators are dependent on the performance
indicators that result from the design and optimisation.
The problem this framework seeks to solve is even more relevant when it involves bioreﬁneries
and biomass feedstocks. Here the increased complexity in terms of feedstock composition and
transportation together with the complex biocatalysed reactions makes the decomposition even
more disadvantageous. In 2016 Bertran et al. [12] extended this framework to make it more
suitable for the synthesis problem of bioreﬁneries by including location dependent solutions. In
this thesis the capabilites of the framework are illustrated through a synthesis problem where
transport of the raw material and the bioreﬁneries location proves to be important for the
solution of the synthesis problem.

2.2
Biomass
The supply of biomass is predicted to come mainly from dedicated energy crops, residues from
agriculture and forestry, and waste from animals, manure or municipal waste. This means a
large fraction of the biomass is lignocellulosic biomass. The mass in lignocellulosic bimoass is
primarily the cell wall of plant cells. Therefore the main components are carbon, hydrogen and
oxygen found mostly in three polymers. These polymers, cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin are
highly intermeshed with cellulose and hemicellulose bonded by non-covalent forces and hemi-

cellulose and lignin covalently cross-linked. The largest fraction in lignocellulosic biomass is
cellulose (30-50% DM), an unbranched polymer of glucose with a DP varying between 300 and
10.000 units depending on the origin. The second largest fraction is hemicellulose (20-40 % DM).
Hemicellulose is also a polymer with various C5 sugars as repeating unit. The most dominant
unit is xylose. The third largest fraction is lignin (7-30% DM). Lignin is a complex threedi-
mensional organic molecule where phenylpropane is the main repeating unit. The rest of the
lignocellulosic biomass is various proteins and minerals. [15]
Structurally the plant cell wall can be divided into three main parts, the middle lamella, the
primary wall and the secondary wall. The middle lamella is the outer boundary of the plant cell,
a hydrophilic gel shared by neighbouring cells. It is the ﬁrst thing formed during cell formation.
Next formed is the primary cell wall. The primary cell wall has a sparse distribution of cellulosic
microﬁbrils held together by pectic compounds and hemi-cellulose. With the primary wall in
place the secondary wall is formed. The secondary wall is more structured than the other two
layers and can be attributed to the strength and ﬂexibility of the plant. It is in the secondary
wall the largest diﬀerences from species to species can be found, but common for them are that
the secondary wall has highly structured crystalline cellulosic microﬁbrils. The microﬁbril net-
work is hydrophobic and responsible for the resistance towards enzyme action. The microﬁbrils
are dispersed in a matrix of hemi-cellulose and lignin. The hemi-cellulose is cross-linked to the
lignin and form hydrogen bonds with the cellulose acting as glue between the two. Most of the
cell’s carbohydrate is present in the secondary wall and unlocking the individual substrates is
key for utilising biomass in bioreﬁneries.[16]
2.2.1
Energy crops
Energy crops are crops grown with the only purpose of producing CO2-neutral fuels and chem-
icals. The best energy crops exhibit easy establishment, robustness, life span above 10 years,
low irrigation, fertiliser, pesticide and insecticide requirements. Besides that they should be
non-invasive and preferably native to the area. If implemented correctly energy crops can also
help to increase the global biodiversity by providing good natural habitats for wildlife. The
species most commonly referred to as energy crops include eucalyptus, poplar, willow, pine,
miscanthus, switchgrass, sugar cane, energy cane and sorghum. The production of these energy
crops have been estimated to 60$/t DM. Two of the most promising species are miscanthus and
switchgrass.
Miscanthus is a perennial grass with its origins in the south-east Asia and imported to Europe
in 1935. Three miscanthus species are investigated for use as a future energy crop, Miscanthus
x giganteus, Miscanthus sinensis and Miscanthus sacchariﬂorus with Mischantus x giganteus
receiving most attention. Above ground yields of Miscanthus x giganteus have been to reported

biofuel production.

Forestry residues
Forestry residues refers to residues from sawmills, logging residues and thinning residues. The

To make the biomass ready for fermentation it is pretreated to make the substrate more avail-
able and hydrolysed to break down the polymer chains. When speaking of pretreatment and
following hydrolysis it is especially with a focus on the recalcitrance of cellulose. The recalci-

trance of cellulose can be attributed to the crystallinity in the microﬁbrils and the hydrophobic
nature of lignin. The goal of the pretreatment is thus to remove the lignin and reduce the crys-
tallinity. The pretreatment methods can be divided into diﬀerent categories, physichal, chemical,
physiochemical, biological and solvent-based pretreatment.
2.3.1
Physical
The physical pretreatment methods include but are not limited to size-reduction. The size-
reduction methods include milling, chipping, grinding etc. The goal is to reduce the particle
sizes of the biomass and thus increase the surface area of the biomass. The physical pretreat-
ment reduces the size to 0.2-30 mm depending on method. The process also cleaves some of the
cellulose chains and thus reduces the crystallinity. The size-reduction is often combined with
other pretreatment methods as the size-reduction makes them more eﬀective. [18][17]
2.3.2
Chemical
The chemical pretreatment methods can be divided into three categories, oxidative, acidic and
alkaline.
In the oxidative methods lignin is oxidised to acids thus making the crystalline cellulose available
for the following enzymatic hydrolysis. The oxidation can be performed with dilute hydrogen
peroxide, where studies have shown up to 50% removal of lignin at 30◦C in 2% hydrogen perox-
ide. Yields of up to 90% reducing sugars have been obtained. Another oxidation agent used is
ozone. The results of ozonolysis are worse than the hydrogen peroxide treatment. [18]
In the acidic treatment dilute sulphuric acid, nitric acid, phosphoric acid or hydrochloric acid is
used. Sulphuric acid is the most commonly used showing the best results. During acid pretreat-
ment the hydrocarbons, cellulose and hemi-cellulose are hydrolysed to their sugar components,
with hemi-cellulose showing almost complete conversion at high temperatures. The acidic pre-
treatment is done at 140-215◦C with a residence time varying from a few seconds to a couple
minutes and a sulphuric acid with under 4 wt% acid. Glucose yields of 80% has been reported
using this method. A disadvantage of the method is corrosive resistance needed in the equip-
ment making the capital investment higher and a need for neutralisation before the downstream
processing. [18][19]
In the alkaline pretreatment sodium hydroxide, potassium hydroxide, lime and ammonia has
been used. The alkaline pretreatment works by swelling the biomass increasing the internal
active surface area, disrupting the bonds between lignin and hemi-cellulose and reducing the
crystallinity of cellulose microﬁbrils. The alkaline pretreatment is most eﬀective in low lignin

2.3.3
Physiochemical
The physiochemical methods include steam explosion, liquid hot water pretreatment and am-
monia ﬁbre/freeze explosion.
Steam explosion is one of the most promising and extensively studied methods. In this method
the biomass is exposed to high pressure steam at elevated temperatures for a few second to a
couple of minutes. Then the pressure is reduced rapidly resulting in an explosive decompression
ruining the structure of the biomass.
Hemi-cellulose is also solubilized in the process.
The
process is operated between 0.7 and 4.8 MPa and at 160-240◦C. Steam explosion is commonly
combined with dilute acids that serve as catalyst for the decomposition. Glucose yields in the
following enzymatic hydrolysis has been reported as high as 94%. Steam explosion is currently
run in pilot plants by NREL, SEKAB and the Italian steam explosion programme. [18][17][8].
In Liquid hot water pretreatment the temperatures are the same as for steam explosion, but
here the pressure is kept so high (>5 MPa) that the water is liquid. This method removes the
lignin and hydrolyses the hemi-cellulose. The yields obtained are comparable to those of steam
explosion. [18][17]
Ammonia ﬁbre/freeze explosion (AFEX) works in the same way as steam explosion but uses
ammonia instead of water. An advantage of AFEX is that it operates at ambient temperatures
instead of high temperatures. To be commercial the ammonia has to be recovered and multiple
systems for that have been developed. Observed glucose yields are comparable to that of steam
explosion. [18][17]
2.3.4
Biological
Biological pretreatment methods degrade the biomass by biological processes. Often used micro-
organisms include white-rot fungi, brown-rot fungi, soft-rot fungi and bacteria. For the fungi,
brown-rot and soft-rot fungi mainly attack cellulose and white-rod attacks lignin decomposing
it. Thus white-rot fungi is receiving most attention. The treatment with fungi takes several
weeks, but gives a high selectivity. The low chemical and energy input for the treamtent might
makeup for the long treatment time. It is so far not attractive commercially.[18]

2.3.5
Solvent based
The solvent-based methods utilise cellulose-solvents such as phosphoric acid and ionic liquid.
The idea is that the solvent can completely dissolve the crystalline cellulose microﬁbrils. This
makes hydrolysis much faster resulting in a reduction in sugar degradation, fewer fermentation
inhibitors and a lower capital cost. Especially ionic liquid shows promising results as they are
easily tailored to the speciﬁc process and can be recovered to almost 100 % purity. The ionic
liquids are already used to produce cellulose ﬁbers by Lyocell. The challenges still faced when
using solvents are high solvent cost, generation of inhibitors and regeneration requirements.

2.5
Synthesis and design framework
The goal of the process synthesis and design problem is to ﬁnd the best process ﬂowsheet and
an optimal solution in terms of operating conditions and design decisions. Historically there are
3 ways to solve this problem in a systematic manner, heuristic methods, mathematical program-
ming methods and a hybrid of the methods.
The heuristic approach is based on knowledge, rules-of-thumb and engineering experience. One
of the most well-known heuristics for process synthesis is the hierarchical decision procedure by
Douglas in 1985 [22]. The method decomposes the process synthesis problem into 5 levels. The
levels are: (1) Batch vs. Continuous, (2) Input-Output Structure of the Flow Sheet, (3) Recycle
Structure of the Flow Sheet and Reactor Considerations, (4) Separation System Speciﬁcation
and (5) Heat Exchanger Network. At each level of the structure more detail is added to the
ﬂowsheet and the result is evaluated through appropriate economic analysis. The advantage of
the heuristic method is a feasible process ﬂowsheet with minimal extra work, the disadvantage is
that heuristics cannot guarantee optimality in the solution and can only be applied to problems
with prior knowledge.
The mathematical programming approach is based on network superstructure optimisation
where many alternatives ﬁtting the problem formulation are represented in a superstructure,
translated into a mathematical optimisation problem and solved. The nature of the optimisa-
tion problem is a mixed-integer non-linear programming (MINLP) problem. In 1985 Grossmann
[23] showed the potential of this metyhod. The advantage of the method is that if the solution
algorithm and the nature of the problem allows it, optimality of the problem can be guaranteed,
however many MINLP problems can not be optimised to a guaranteed deﬁnite global optimum.
Another disadvantage is the extensive data collection required to setup the superstructure and

that the true optimal design might not be included in the superstructure. In recent years su-
perstructure optimisation has been applied to bioreﬁneries by Drobez et al.[24] to ﬁnd optimal
integrated biogas processes from waste, and by Zondervan et al. [25] who used superstructure
optimisation to ﬁnd the optimal design of a multi-product bioreﬁnery system. Most recently the
framework used in this thesis has been developed by Quaglia et al. [1] and extended by Bertran
et al. [2].
Hybrid methods are a combination of the two previous methods exploiting the advantages of
each of them without inheriting the disadvantages.

3.1
Superstructure optimisation framework for process synthesis
The superstructure optimisation framework applied in this thesis is often applied in connection
with the 3-stage process synthesis-intensiﬁcation approach proposed by Babi et al. [5] where the
process synthesis, design and intensiﬁcation problem is decomposed into three sub problems,
one at each stage: (1) Synthesis, (2) Design (& analysis) and (3) Innovation Design.
The
framework is illustrated in ﬁgure 4. The goal of stage 1 is to supply stage 2, the design stage,
with one or multiple feasible process ﬂowsheets. The ﬂowsheets can be generated by a number
of diﬀerent ways where one of them is the superstructure optimisation framework developed by
Quaglia et al. [1] in 2012 and extended by Bertran et al. [12] in 2016. The framework was
initially developed in 2012 as an integrated business and engineering framework to eliminate
the iterative steps between strategic and tactical decisions taken in a business. In 2016 Bertran
et al. extended the framework to make it more applicable to bioreﬁneries. The challenge in
doing process synthesis for bioreﬁneries is the diﬀerence in the nature of the raw material. In
the traditional petrochemical industry crude oil is often the raw material. Crude oil has the
advantage that the compostion is simple and it is invariant of location, climate, season etc.
On the other hand biomass as a raw material has a complex composition with many diﬀerent
compounds such as polymers, large organic molecules, water, proteins and minerals. On top of
that the composition can vary depending on the biomass specie, location, climate, season etc.
So to make the framework better suited for bioreﬁneries the framework was extended to handle
location-dependant problems.

The objective function of the optimisation problem C(w, y) is typically an economic or envi-

The problem is either a mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) problem or a MINLP. This
depends of the optimisation function, C(w, y), and the constraints r(w) and s(w). If any of
those are non-linear the whole problem is a non-linear problem and if they are all linear the
whole problem is linear. The solution algorithms for the two are very diﬀerent. The most basic
solution of the MILP problem is via the branch and bound algorithm. First the MILP problem
is relaxed to a linear programming (LP) problem by removing the integrality of the integers
meaning now all variables are continuous. Then the optimal solution is found to this relaxed
problem. If the solution has a non-integer value for integer variables the integer is branched to
be above and below the two nearest integers resulting in two new MILP problems. The two new
problems are relaxed and again solved as LP problems and if the solutions are still not integer
solutions they are again branched continuing until integer solutions are reached. Finally the
reached integer solutions are compared and the optimal solution found. The solution achieved
is guaranteed to be optimal because of the linearity of the problem. The solution strategy is
illustrated in ﬁgure 6. [6] More advanced solvers are available but they work with more or less

3.1.5
Super-O as user-interface
Super-O is a software developed to serve as an interface for setting up the superstructure and
translating it into a mathematical programming problem and solving it.
It works by prompting the user for all information needed. That includes the number of steps,
intervals at each step, the number of chemicals and their molecular weight, number of utilities,
number of reactions, reaction stoichiometries and conversions, speciﬁc interval speciﬁcations
such as mixing ratio µ, waste separation fraction δ, separation fraction ω, and utility consump-
tion ratios β1, β2 and β3, primary and secondary connection speciﬁcations between intervals,
transport expenses between intervals, the raw material ﬂow rate and prices for all raw materials,
products, added chemicals and utilities. Based on all the give information Super-O translates
the optimisation problem into an input ﬁle for GAMS, a mathematical system for mathematical
optimisation, where the problem is solved. Super-O then translates the output ﬁle from GAMS
and gives information about the solution and the way GAMS got to it. GAMS is using the so-
lution algotithm CPLEX 12 for the solution. Finally the result is saved as an excel-ﬁle readable
by the user.

3.3
Biomass selection
When solving the synthesis problem for bioreﬁneries a selection of biomass feedstock and location
alternatives have to be done.
A signiﬁcant diﬀerence between species and from location to
location can be seen in terms of composition, density, price, harvest ﬁeld yield, harvest index
etc. The many variables to consider when choosing the biomass source and location make the
problem diﬃcult and it is a yet unsolved problem. Depending on the objective function for the
synthesis problem biomass and location alternatives can be evaluated in diﬀerent ways, but if
the objective function evaluates the economics or the environmental impact three characteristics
become very important. The three are harvest ﬁeld yield, how much substrate can be grown
per area per year for the given biomass and location, the at-ﬁeld price of biomass, what is
the price of the biomass per tonnes substrate equivalent for the given location and biomass,
and ﬁnally the transportation cost from the ﬁeld to the gate of the plant, what is the price of
transportation per tonnes substrate equivalent per kilometre. An economic evaluation is highly
dependant on the last two, as a low price of the biomass and transportation would be purely
beneﬁcial. The ﬁrst characteristic, the harvest ﬁeld yield, however also inﬂuences the economics
as a high ﬁeld yield means the area from which the biomass is collected can be reduced and
thus the kilometres needed to transport the biomass can be reduced. With an objective function
weighing environmental impact high the ﬁrst parameter is the most important. A high yield
means shorter transportation and less fuel used but also and more importantly less land used.
The amount of land used becomes important as the global demand for energy and chemicals
have to be covered by biomass and other renewable energies. This means the more biomass
produced per area the better. It is especially important when evaluating the potential of energy
crops as their environmental impact is highly aﬀected by indirect land use change.
Based on this, a prime biomass specie and location alternative would have high harvest ﬁeld
yield, low at-ﬁeld price and low transportation cost. This solution is illustrated as the green

No variation in composition and harvest index: This is a necessary assumption, because

climate, soil conditions and season.

chosen because it is a precursor of 1,3-butadiene used to make styrene-butadiene rubbers used in

Discussion
5.1
Theoretical reactions
The reactions implemented in the superstructure are all based on the theoretical redox balance.
Real reactions balance the redox through metabolic side products and biomass formation. The
ideal reaction for industrial processes is the homo fermentative reaction where only the desired
product and biomass is produced, a scenario very close to what is investigated in scenario 2.
These homo fermentative reactions could be achieved through metabolic engineering, however
so far the results show poor reaction activities, low broth concentration or low reaction yield.
This is a challenge to solve in the future but the beneﬁts are noticeable as can be seen from the
results of the superstructure optimisation in the case study.

5.2
Expenses and potential proﬁt not included in the superstructure
5
DISCUSSION
So far though homo fermentative reactions are not a ready for commercialisation, and the
downstream process in bioreﬁneries have to be designed to handle the side products. Without
technological development these downstream processes are too expensive, which is why a lot of
research is done in innovative recovery methods. Methods under intensive research are reactive
extraction, electrodialysis and membrane technologies. These processes suﬀer from high solvent
cost, high utility demand and high capital investment. Besides the side products the downstream
process of such biological reactions are also inﬂuenced by the inevitable solid biomass from the
reaction. This increases the demands on the equipment downstream as they have to handle
and separate the two phases. All these factors mean that the easy and cost eﬀective separation
scheme seen in the generated ﬂowsheet from this thesis is not likely to be seen in commercial
bioreﬁneries for some time and thus the high proﬁt margin observed is not possible with current
technology. However the results can still be used to illustrate the future potential in bioreﬁneries,

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
With increasing global population and energy demand sustainable alternatives to petroleum
is needed to sustain our living standards without emitting critical amounts of green house
gases. In this thesis a processing network for bioreﬁneries has been developed starting with a
single biomass feedstock and branching into four diﬀerent value-added chemical products. The
network has been formulated as a superstructure based on the framework by Quaglia et al.[1]
and Bertran et al.[2]. To select the biomass feedstock used, a one parameter screening method
has been developed screening biomass specie and location based on harvest ﬁeld yield.
The best candidate resulting from the biomass screening method was found to be wheat straw
in France. Other good candidates included corn stover in USA and Canada. The four products
produced from the wheat straw were selected to be 1,3-propanediol (1.76 $/kg), 2,3-butanediol (3
$/kg), succinic acid (3 $/kg) and itacoic acid (2 $/kg). They were chosen based on their potential
as platform chemicals in a future bioeconomy, their current research interest and current market
potential. The four products were all produced from glucose coming from the wheat straw.
The wheat straw is converted to glucose via mechanical grinding, steam explosion with 0.2
wt% [H2SO4 and enzymatic hydrolysis. These three steps produced 33073.8 kg/hr glucose from
85616.4 kg/hr dry biomass. The glucose was converted to the products via maximum theoretical
conversion based on redox potentials. The molar theoretical yields were 3/4, 8/11, 8/7 and 10/9
for 1,3-propanediol, 2,3-butanediol, succinic acid and itaconic acid respectively from glucose.
The four products were converted to the four products at concentrations observed in literature.
1,3-propanediol and 2,3-butanediol were recovered at 99.9% purity via a one step distillation.
Succinic acid and itaconic acid were recovered at 99.5% purity via a two-step recovery process
having a ﬂash and a crystalliser. The superstructure and unit operation speciﬁcations were setup
based on literature and simulations. The superstructure was translated into a mixed-integer
linear programming problem via the user-interface Super-O and solved with the mathematical
optimisation software GAMS for two diﬀerent scenarios. In scenario 1 the conversion of glucose
to succinic acid is 100% and in scenario 2 the conversion is 80%. The solution for both scenarios
showed the ﬂowsheet producing succinic acid to be best with an economic objective function. In
the solution for scenario 1 glucose was converted to succinic acid with a yield of 0.89 g succinic
acid/g glucose and in scenario 2 with a yield of 0.72 g/g. The operating proﬁt of the process for
scenario 1 was found to be 37804.6 $/hr and have a capital cost of 59 m $. This gives a payback
time 65 days. The operating proﬁt and capital cost for scenario 2 was found to be 26733.4 $/hr
and 51 m $ respectively. This gave a payback time of 80 days, slightly more than scenario 1.
The second best in both scenarios was production of itaconic acid, third best was 2,3-butanediol
and worst was 1,3-propanediol. The sensitivity of the results were investigated with a sensitivity
analysis and the economic evaluation was found to be most dependant on the product price and

In the evaluation of the processes several factors were not taken into account including the cost of
adding sulphuric acid, hydrochloric acid and CO2 to the process, the cost of water treatment, the
cost of waste treatment, the cost of supply chain management, the value of the water insoluble

Finally the method for biomass selection was evaluated through a transportation estimate based
on yield and process plant capacity. It as found that the harvest ﬁeld yield has almost no impact
on the ﬁeld-to-gate price of biomass thus selecting the biomass and location based on this variable
alone is not suﬃcient. It was also found that the biomass price in general was of little signiﬁcance
for the process proﬁt.
6.1
Future work
The future work should focus on
– Inclusion of water treatment, value from the water insoluble solids and recycle streams.
These three should be included to see how they inﬂuence the process economics.
– Non-ideal fermentation reactions.
By implementing non-ideal fermentation reactions a
more realistic picture of the current technological potential to produce the four speciﬁed
products can be given. Such an implementation would call for signiﬁcant changes in the
downstream process to remove the biomass and metabolic side products.
– A thorough collection biomass price data from many diﬀerent locations and species. A
collection of biomass data would make selection of biomass specie and location based on
more than one parameter feasible. Such a search could reveal biomass specie and location
alternatives located in the green optimal area and a comparison to other candidates could
reveal how the market and technology have to evolve to make biomass a cheap economic
and environmentally feasible feedstock.
– A detailed design of the optimal processing ﬂowsheet found via superstructure generation.
This would be the next step towards an innovative sustainable process via the process
synthesis and innovation framework by Babi et al.[5].
