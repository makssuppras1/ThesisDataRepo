Abstract
The purpose of this master’s thesis was to investigate the fatigue behaviour of concrete with
special attention paid to the eﬀect of the coarse aggregates and the area surrounding them.
The results from this project have been found by carrying out experimental work on specimens
where a glass rod had been cast into a mortar prism, with the glass rod representing the coarse
aggregates of concrete. During the tests, the behaviour of selected load cycles was documented
using two cameras, and the pictures obtained in these tests were analysed using digital image
correlation.
One of the things that were investigated in the project was the development of fatigue damages.
Using digital image correlation to analyse the test results, it was found that damages occurred
in the interface around the aggregate ﬁrst, and then propagated further into the mortar. It was
found that the behaviour was, to a large extent, dependent on the ingredients in the mortar
mixes used for the specimens, with more pronounced damages occurring in the specimens with-
out silica fume in the mortar. Investigations into the eﬀect of the coarse aggregate diameter did
not show any change in the behaviour, although it was noted that the diﬃculty in obtaining
usable results from the DIC-software increased for the smallest rod size.
Another thing that was investigated in the project was the eﬀect of pre-existing cracks in the
specimens. To investigate this, a set of specimens were cured in air instead of water to induce
cracking from shrinkage. The results have shown that the pre-existing cracks change the be-
haviour of the specimen so that the development of damages in the specimen is slowed down
during the 2005 cycles of the test.
Some of the tests that were carried out in this project did not use digital image correlation to
monitor the behaviour. Instead, extensometers were used to continuously measure the strains
experienced by the specimen during the 2005 load cycles. Based on the strains, the fatigue mod-
ulus development was analysed. The results from these tests have shown that while the fatigue
modulus development in the ﬁrst 2005 cycles could be divided into two stages similar to the
ﬁrst two stages of fatigue behaviour described by several of the sources included in the literature
review, the exact location of the behaviour change in the fatigue life did not correspond to the
theory. This would indicate that the behaviour of the specimens was slightly diﬀerent from that
of normal concrete.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The fatigue properties of concrete describe how concrete as a material behaves when it is sub-
jected to a cyclic load. While several investigations have looked into the relationship between
the load levels and the number of cycles to failure, very few studies have investigated the be-
haviour of concrete on a microstructural level. In this project, the fatigue behaviour of concrete
in uniaxial compression will be investigated. The main focus of the investigation will be on the
coarse aggregates in the concrete, and the part they play in the development of fatigue damages.
A secondary focus will be on the elasticity of the concrete and how this changes during the early
part of the fatigue life. In the project, digital image correlation (DIC) will be used to analyse
the test results. The use of digital image correlation will make it possible to investigate how the
specimens behave when they are subjected to load in further detail than manual measurements
can tell. The project serves as the initial investigation into the fatigue behaviour in preparation
for a future research project into the microstructural behaviour of concrete.
This project has been carried out in cooperation with two other master’s projects that are
investigating similar aspects of concrete elasticity. Results regarding the mix designs and the
mortar have been shared between the three projects, along with the test setup and casting of
the specimens.
1.1
Problem statement
In the project, the following questions will be answered:
• How do fatigue damages develop in concrete around the coarse aggregates?
• What eﬀect does aggregate size have on fatigue damage development?
• How do pre-existing cracks in concrete (from e.g. shrinkage) aﬀect the fatigue damage
development?
1.2
Report structure
The project report has been written using the following structure.
In Chapter 2, relevant
literature will be presented along with a section explaining the expected outcome of the tests.
Chapter 3 describes the practical parts of the project, such as the test setup and the test
procedure. In Chapter 4, the results of the tests are presented and brieﬂy commented on, and
Chapter 5 contains the further analysis and discussion of the results.

2.1
Recap of literature review
The literature review had two main subjects of interest. The ﬁrst subject was factors inﬂuencing
the elasticity of concrete (including factors aﬀecting the measured value of the modulus of
elasticity), and the second subject regarded fatigue of concrete. The main ﬁndings on these two
topics will be presented in the following sections, along with some additional sources that have
been found to be relevant for the project.
2.1.1
Elasticity of concrete
The elasticity of concrete is one of the parameters that are used to describe how concrete is
behaving when it is subjected to load. The relation between the load applied to concrete (the
stress, σ) and the way the concrete deforms (the strain, ε) is described in the constitutive relation
σ = E ε. In this equation, the parameter E signiﬁes the elastic modulus of the material. There
are diﬀerent ways of deﬁning the elastic modulus of concrete since concrete is not behaving
linearly in the elastic region, but in a curvilinear way (Destrebecq, 2013; Soroka, 1979; Neville,
1997). One type of elastic modulus is the secant modulus, which is deﬁned as the slope between
the origin of a stress-strain curve and a point on the curve (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014). Generally
speaking, the secant modulus can be determined from the following equation:
Esec = σmax −σmin
εmax −εmin
(2.1.1)
The maximum and minimum stress levels used to determine the secant modulus depend on
which standard is used. For example, in the European standard, the minimum stress level is
σmin = 0.5MPa and the maximum stress level is σmax = fc/3 (DS/EN 12390-13, 2013), whereas
in the American standard, the minimum stress level is σmin = 0 MPa and the maximum stress
level is σmax = 0.4fc (ASTM Standard C469/C469M, 2014).
Diﬀerent parameters aﬀect the elasticity of concrete. The sources examined in the literature
review have shown that the elasticity of a material is highly dependent on the porosity of it.
According to (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014) among others, an inverse relationship exists between
the porosity of concrete and the elasticity of it, meaning that a decrease in porosity leads to an

increase in stiﬀness.
The stiﬀness of the individual components of the concrete will also inﬂuence the elasticity of it,
and the stiﬀness of the individual components is dependent on their porosity. The porosity of the
matrix is dependent on several diﬀerent factors, with one of them being the water-cement ratio
(Mehta and Monteiro, 2014). With respect to the aggregates, high porosity aggregates have a
lower stiﬀness, just like the matrix. The dependency of the stiﬀness of concrete on the stiﬀness
of its components has led to the development of phase models that can be used to estimate the
stiﬀness of concrete. Among these models, the two simplest are the parallel and series models,
which provide an upper and a lower limit of the elastic modulus (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014, p.
559). The models can not be used for an exact determination of the elastic modulus since they
only take volume fraction and phase stiﬀness into account.
In the literature review, several studies concluded that the areas around the aggregates are
weak zones in the concrete. These zones are called the interfacial transition zones, or the ITZ
for short. In the ITZ there is a higher concentration of microcracks and voids when compared
to the concentration in the matrix (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014, p. 87). This, combined with
the lower degree of hydration around the aggregates due to the packing of the cement particles,
results in a higher porosity of the ITZ, and thereby also a lower stiﬀness (Neville, 1997). Because
of the high porosity of the ITZ, it is considered to be the weak link of normal strength concrete
(Aquino et al., 1995).
2.1.2
Fatigue of concrete
Fatigue is the deterioration of a material due to repeated loading (Destrebecq, 2013).
The
repeated loading of a concrete specimen will lead to development of damages in the concrete
that will lead to failure of the specimen if the number of repetitions is high enough. The number
of repetitions to failure (also known as the fatigue life) is dependent on the size of the load
applied to the specimen. The relation can be expressed using Aas-Jakobsen’s formula, as given
below (Destrebecq, 2013):

In the equation above, β is a material constant, normally equal to β = 0.0685 for ordinary
concrete, and NF is the number of load cycles to failure. Aas-Jakobsen’s formula has also been
presented in (Hsu, 1984). In this article, it is argued that equation (2.1.2) does not take the
rate of loading into account. Studies have shown that rate of loading becomes relevant for the
fatigue behaviour when load levels are above 0.8fc (Destrebecq, 2013).
Fatigue tests on concrete can be divided into diﬀerent categories, depending on the fatigue
life, NF . The categories are deﬁned from the number of cycles to failure, but are also indirectly
dependent on the load levels, as seen from equation (2.1.2). In (Destrebecq, 2013), the following
three categories are being used:
• Low cycle fatigue, NF ≤ca. 103
• Large cycle fatigue, ca. 103 ≤NF ≤ca. 107
• Very large cycle fatigue, ca. 107 ≤NF
The fatigue behaviour of concrete is dependent on which of the three categories above the fatigue
test ﬁts into. A big diﬀerence is found between low cycle fatigue and large cycle fatigue with
regards to the inﬂuence of the loading rate (which was mentioned above). It is also mentioned

One parameter that provides a link between fatigue of concrete and the elasticity of concrete
is the fatigue modulus. The fatigue modulus is deﬁned as the ratio between the stress range
and the strain range of a load cycle (Liu and Zhou, 2017). Written as an equation, the fatigue
modulus is expressed in the following way (Destrebecq, 2013):
Efa = σmax −σmin
εmax −εmin
(2.1.3)
It can be seen that the deﬁnition is identical to the deﬁnition of the secant modulus in equation
(2.1.1). Throughout diﬀerent sources, diﬀerent names and diﬀerent symbols are used for the
fatigue modulus, such as ”fatigue modulus” and ”longitudinal modulus of elasticity”, E, ED
and Efa. In this report, the term ”fatigue modulus” is used along with the symbol Efa in order
to avoid confusion with other types of elastic moduli.
It can be seen from equation (2.1.3) that the fatigue modulus is highly dependent on the develop-
ment of strains in the concrete. According to (Liu and Zhou, 2017), fatigue strain development
can be divided into three stages, namely a fast initial stage, a stable middle stage and a fast
ﬁnal stage. A similar division of the fatigue life into stages can be found in (Destrebecq, 2013)
as well. For each load cycle the specimen is subjected to, the strain amplitude increases. In
the case of a constant amplitude load, the stress range will be the same for all load cycles, and
the fatigue modulus will be inversely proportional with the fatigue strain amplitude (Liu and
Zhou, 2017). Since the strain amplitude increases with the number of load cycles, the fatigue
modulus will decrease with the number of load cycles, making the concrete softer. The fatigue
modulus can be used to determine the parameter called the damage ratio (Destrebecq, 2013)
or the fatigue damage (Gao and Hsu, 1998). This parameter is deﬁned through the following
equation, with E0 being the initial fatigue modulus:
D = 1 −Efa
E0
(2.1.4)
It can be seen from equation (2.1.4) that the fatigue damage will increase with decreasing fa-
tigue modulus, and thereby with an increasing number of load cycles. According to (Destrebecq,
2013) failure will not occur at D = 1, but at a critical value, Dcr, below 1.
Despite the low number of studies into the microstructural damages sustained by concrete under
fatigue loading, one study has been found. In (Zhao et al., 2019), a study regarding low cycle
fatigue on high strength concrete after subjecting it to high temperatures has been presented.
In this study, pore development was used as a way of measuring fatigue damage. The results
of this study show that the pore volume increased with an increasing number of load cycles.
The results from (Zhao et al., 2019) also show that the development of fatigue damages can be
divided into stages. In the ﬁrst stage, from 0% to 25% of the fatigue life, the development of
fatigue damages is rapid, from 25% to 75%, development is linear and from 75% of the fatigue
life until failure, damage development will speed up again. This is similar to the three stages
for fatigue strain development described in (Liu and Zhou, 2017) and in (Destrebecq, 2013),
even though the percentages are not the same. Furthermore, results from the study also show
that in the ﬁrst stage of fatigue damage development, microcracks form in the concrete in the
areas surrounding the aggregates of the concrete (the interfaces) and extend along the aggregate
surfaces.

2.2. EXPECTED RESULTS
2.2
Expected results
The studies that were found during the work with the literature review did not include any
research into what happens in the concrete on a microstructural level when it is subjected to a
fatigue load (the only study that covers this to some extent is (Zhao et al., 2019), which is for
high strength concrete). However, it is possible to use the ﬁndings from the literature review to
make a prediction of the results that are expected from the fatigue tests in this project. The
studies that were found during the work with the literature review suggest that the weak link
in normal strength concrete is the interfacial transition zone (ITZ) around the aggregates. This
zone is characterised by a higher porosity, and thereby a lower stiﬀness. Therefore, it is expected
that the damages that occur in the concrete will originate in the ITZ and then propagate into
the mortar. Furthermore, it is expected that the damages that form in the concrete will increase
in size with an increasing number of load cycles, and, if the number of load cycles reaches high
enough levels, join to form macrocracks.

rapid cement, CEM I 52.5N (LA), the silica fume was Elkem Microsilica Grade 940-U (unden-

3.1.1
Mortar testing
The 12 mortar specimens were tested to determine the strength and the elastic modulus of the
mortar. Of the 12 specimens, four were tested to determine the strength before the elasticity
tests (one from each mould), while the remaining specimens were tested in the elasticity test ﬁrst,
and then tested to failure afterwards (in the case of the stiﬀmix, 6 specimens were tested initially

Specimens
The specimens used for the tests in the project are prisms with the dimensions 60×50×120mm.
In the specimens, a glass rod has been cast into the prism, which will act as a coarse aggregate.
Glass rods have been used instead of regular coarse aggregates in order to make concrete speci-
mens that are approximately 2-dimensional, so that the damages occurring in the specimen are
the same all the way through the specimen. The glass used for the specimens has an elastic
modulus of Eglass = 73 GPa, which is approximately the elastic modulus of coarse aggregates
in concrete (according to (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014, p. 257), the stiﬀness of granite, basalt
and other types of stone aggregates is in the range 70 −90 GPa). The material properties of
the glass can be found in (Schott AG, 2015). The specimens were cast in a 400 × 150 × 60 mm
mould and then cut into the correct size for the specimens at a later time. The mould used for
casting can be seen in Figure 3.4.

3.3
Test setup
The strength tests of the mortar samples and the specimens were carried out in a servohydraulic
TONI machine capable of applying a load of 3000 kN. The load was applied at a constant rate
of 0.6 MPa/s as described in (DS / EN 12390-3, 2007), and the fracture detection was set at
0.15% (meaning a drop in the load of 0.15% would be regarded as a failure).
The elasticity tests and the fatigue tests were carried out in a servohydraulic MTS machine
with a static load capacity of ±500 kN (the load has been restricted by the compression heads,
which can’t be used for loads above 200 kN). During the fatigue tests, two cameras were used
to take pictures of the damages that occurred in the specimens. The cameras were Nikon D800
cameras with a 105 mm lens for the close-up pictures and a 60 mm lens for the pictures of the
entire specimen. In order to avoid imperfections in the top and bottom surfaces to cause prema-
ture failure of the specimen, wooden plates were placed between the specimen and the loading
platens. The wooden plates were 3 mm thick, and had been cut into pieces of 60 mm × 50 mm,
corresponding to the cross-section of the specimen. Furthermore, the top loading platen that
was used is made with a ball bearing to adjust to any skewness in the specimen. A sketch of
the test setup for the fatigue tests can be seen in Figure 3.8.
When specimens were put into the test setup initially, two extensometers were mounted on the
left and right sides of the specimen (seen from one of the cameras). The extensometers were
Instron extensometers with a gage length of 50 mm. The travel of the two extensometers were
±5 mm for the ﬁrst extensometer (called ”Extensometer 1” in this report) and ±2.5 mm for the
second extensometer (called ”Extensometer 2”). Once the specimen had been placed in the test

3.5
Data analysis
The specimens were painted using chalk paint before testing, by ﬁrst painting them with a
white coat layer on the two sides facing the cameras and then painting them with black dots in
a random pattern. After testing, the pictures from the slow load cycles were analysed using the
software GOM Correlate. In the software, plots of the strains at the surface were used to pinpoint
where in the specimen the displacements came from. In reality, these plots do not necessarily
show the strains, so the plots will be called displacement visualisations in the report. The scales
on these plots have been changed in order to be able to see where the displacements are hap-
pening, meaning that the scales are not necessarily the same. The displacement visualisations
have been made in two directions, namely in the load direction (red are small deformations, blue
are big deformations) and perpendicular to the load direction (blue are small deformations, red
are big deformations). Displacement measurements were then made to investigate the overall
behaviour of the specimen during the load cycles both in the load direction and perpendicular
to the load direction (by measuring the displacements of the entire specimen), and to investigate
the behaviour of the individual phases of the specimen, namely the mortar, the aggregate and
the interface. Furthermore, the close-up pictures were used to investigate the behaviour of the
aggregate interface even further by looking at the crack opening at four locations along the
aggregate interface.
In order to see if the aggregate had any inﬂuence on the overall displacements of the spec-
imen, three measurements have been made in each of the two directions - one through the
aggregate, and then one on either side of the aggregate. The displacement of the lines has only
been checked along the line itself, not perpendicular to the direction of the line. The lines were
not drawn all the way to the edge of the picture, but a small distance from the edge, since the
edges seem to be noisier than the rest of the picture. For the investigation of the behaviour
of the diﬀerent phases of the specimen, the measuring lines through the aggregate on the full
specimen pictures have been divided into segments corresponding to the mortar, the interface
and the aggregate. The measuring lines used in the pictures of the full specimen can be seen in
Figure 3.9a, and the measuring lines on the close-up pictures can be seen in Figure 3.9b.
With regards to the data obtained in GOM Correlate from the measuring lines, two diﬀerent
types of measurements are being used. These are displacements and length changes. The dis-
placements are measured in millimetres and show how much closer or further away two points
have moved from each other. These have been called actual displacements in the report, in
contrast to the length change, which is called relative displacements in the report. The length
changes are in per cent and show the displacements relative to the length over which they are
measured. In that sense, the length changes are a form of strain and will be used as such in the
project.

Chapter 5
Discussion
In this section, the test results will be analysed further, and trends in the data will be identiﬁed
in order to answer the questions listed in the problem statements in Chapter 1. Furthermore,
the discussion will be used to identify and comment on the factors that could have inﬂuenced
the results of the tests and to assess the accuracy of the results.
5.1
Development of fatigue damages
The main subject of investigation in the project has been to see how fatigue damages have
developed in the tested specimens. Based on the results that were presented in Chapter 4, some
general trends can be found, as well as some diﬀerences between the stiﬀand the soft specimens.
These will be commented on in the following sections.
5.1.1
General fatigue behaviour
Based on the displacement visualisations that have been found during this project, it seems that
both in the load direction and perpendicular to the load direction, large deformations occur at
the interface of the aggregates. This is in agreement with the theory, which states that the area
surrounding the aggregates is weaker than the remaining concrete. Another factor that could
contribute to the larger displacements at the interface is that the change in stiﬀness between
the mortar and the aggregate will lead to higher stress levels at the interface of the aggregate.
Because the aggregate is stiﬀer than the mortar, stresses will try to move around the aggregate
instead of through the aggregate. Therefore, stresses will concentrate at the interface, and this
would naturally lead to larger deformations.
Looking over the results in the data sheets (see Appendix D), it can be noted that the biggest
changes in the behaviour of the specimens happen between cycle 5 and cycle 1001, while the
changes between cycle 1005 and 2001 are not as prominent. Examples of this can be seen in
Figures 4.25b and 4.28, where the change between cycle 5 and cycle 1001 is clear, while the
change between cycle 1005 and 2001 is more insigniﬁcant. This behaviour can be explained by
looking at the development of the fatigue modulus in Figure 4.40. In this ﬁgure, it can be seen
that for both types of water-cured specimens, the development of the fatigue modulus is rapid
in the ﬁrst 400-600 cycles, but slower in the remaining cycles. The reason for this behaviour is
that in the ﬁrst cycles, damages develop in the specimens rapidly. The development of damages
slows down later in the test procedure, leading to the more linear decrease of the fatigue mod-
ulus. This result is consistent with the theory, which divides the fatigue life into three stages:
a rapid initial stage, a slow, linear second stage, and a rapid ﬁnal stage. Divisions like this are
mentioned in e.g. (Destrebecq, 2013) and (Zhao et al., 2019), although the exact division into
the stages, in terms of percentages of the fatigue life, diﬀer between the studies.

5.1. DEVELOPMENT OF FATIGUE DAMAGES
One of the observations that were made from the displacement of the entire specimen (see
Section 4.3.2) was that in some of the specimens, ”valleys” could be seen in the graphs at un-
loading. Since the load was applied using a sinusoidal displacement curve, the displacement
curves of the specimens in the load direction would have approximately the same shape if the
specimens were loaded in even compression. The observation of the ”valleys” in some of the
plots could therefore indicate that the specimens have not been centred correctly before the
tests were started. It was mentioned in Section 3.3 that the specimens were centred sideways
using the two extensometers and were centred by eye in the direction towards the cameras.
Since the ”valleys” are not seen on both sides of a specimen at the same time, it would suggest
that the centring in the direction towards the cameras was incorrect. However, when looking
at Navier’s equation, σ = N/A ± M/W, it shows that the behaviour is not explained purely
by an eccentrically loaded specimen. If M = N · e is inserted into Navier’s equation, it can
be seen that for a constant eccentricity e, both terms of the equation would be ﬁrst degree
polynomials of N, and because of this, a constant eccentricity can not explain the behaviour
that has been observed in the graphs. This means that the eccentricity has to change during the
early parts of the loading cycles. If there was an eccentricity at the beginning of a load cycle,
but it reached 0 at very low load levels, it would explain why the ”valleys” occur, since this
would allow one side to go into tension at low loads (because of bending) until the eccentricity
reached 0. If the specimen had been loaded in pure compression, the ”valleys” would not have
occurred in the graphs, and it should therefore not be regarded as a part of the fatigue behaviour.
In Section 4.3.2 it was also observed that the three curves showing the displacements in the
load direction did not always reach the same level. While it would be expected that the dis-
placements in the centre might be diﬀerent than the displacements to the left and to the right
of the aggregate (since the aggregate is stiﬀer than the mortar), it does not explain why one
side deforms more and the other side less than the centre line. There are several things that
could explain this. Firstly, it could be caused by a skewness in the specimen, e.g. from when
the specimens have been cut. This would lead to the compression plates possibly losing contact
with a part of the specimen at very low load levels, and therefore not be able to transfer loads to
this part of the specimen. Secondly, it could have been caused by the specimen not being loaded
completely in the centre. Even though the specimen was centred in the left-right-direction using
extensometers, it could be possible that the specimen was slightly oﬀ-centre. In most cases,
the diﬀerence does not exceed one hundredth of a millimetre, so the eccentricity would be very
small. Another possible solution is in-homogeneity of the specimen. In the analysis, it has been
assumed that the specimens have been completely homogeneous, but in reality, concrete is an
in-homogeneous material (because of the aggregates). Therefore, if one side of the specimen was
stiﬀer than the other side, the soft side would deform more than the stiﬀunder the same load.
This could have been caused by the presence of air bubbles in the specimen.
It was also noted during the analysis of the results that some of the curves in the load di-
rection show a jump between cycle 5 and cycle 1001. An example of this is the ﬁgure showing
the overall behaviour of specimen L5-20W-F1 in the load direction (this ﬁgure can be found in
the data sheets in Appendix D). In some graphs, the jump moves downwards, indicating larger
displacements, while in other graphs, the jumps move upwards, indicating smaller displacements.
Often, when one side shows a jump downwards, the other side shows a jump upwards. This is
the case for specimens H5-20W-F1, H5-12W-F5, L5-16W-F2 and L5-20W-LONG1 between cycle
5 and 5001 (graphs from the close-up side of the specimens can be found in the data sheets in
Appendix D). This indicates that the jump originates from an uneven loading of the two sides of
the specimen since both sides should behave in the same way, had the specimen been loaded in
pure compression. Furthermore, if the direction of the jump is compared to which plots include

the ”valleys” that have been discussed above, it is found that the ”valleys” occur on the same
side of the specimen as the downwards jump, and never on the side of the specimen that shows

of load cycles. This does not, however, explain the upwards jumps, and it must therefore be as-

Another observation made when looking at the data from the tests was that when the spec-
imens were unloaded, the curves showing the displacements in the load direction often went
above 0, thereby showing the specimen going into tension. However, this is not true, since the
setup prevented the specimens from going into tension unless it originated from load eccentric-
ity (this has been discussed earlier in this section). The reason for the apparent tension in the
specimen is that GOM Correlate works by deﬁning a reference picture and then determining
the diﬀerence between each picture and the reference picture. The reference pictures were taken
when the preload was on the specimens (i.e. while the specimens were loaded with 10 kN), so
when the graphs show that the displacement in the load direction go into positive numbers (ten-
sion), they are actually showing that the load at the end of each cycle went below the preload.
The fact that the load went under the preload can also be seen in graphs showing the force and
stress in the 15 documented cycles in the data sheets in Appendix D, and in the tables with the
mean load levels shown in Appendix C. These show that the load dropped below the preload
after the ﬁrst cycle, and continued to decrease throughout the tests. This has happened because
the tests have been deformation controlled and not load controlled. Since the load levels went
below the preload, positive deformations in the load direction should not necessarily be inter-
preted as tension. One thing that is worth noting in the unloading parts of the displacement
curves is that some specimens show the interface to reach larger positive values than the mortar
and the aggregate. This is most evident in specimens H5-16W-F3, H5-20W-F2 and L5-12W-F4.
If this observation shows the actual behaviour of the concrete, and is not caused by noise in the
data, then it could indicate that the initial displacements (when the specimens are loaded from
an unloaded state) occur in the interface. This would be in accordance with the theory which
states that the interface is the weakest part of the concrete, and should therefore experience
deformations ﬁrst. However, there is not a suﬃcient amount of data in this project to conﬁrm
this hypothesis.
Earlier in this section, it was mentioned that the curves showing the displacement of the entire
specimen in the load direction sometimes showed ”valleys” at unloading. When looking at the
ﬁgures that show the displacement in the loading direction divided into the diﬀerent phases,
it can be seen that the ”valleys” also occur in some of these curves. When investigating this
further, it can be seen that the ”valleys” only occur in the top half of the specimen, namely
the mortar and the interface above the aggregate and the aggregate itself. This would suggest
that the specimen is not entirely homogeneous. Another indication of this can be found in the
displacement visualisations. From the visualisations of the displacements perpendicular to the
load direction, it can be seen that more damages occur in the top half of the specimen compared
to the bottom half. This has been observed in specimen H5-20W-F2, L5-20W-F1, L5-12W-F4,
L5-20A-F4 and L5-20W-LONG1. The diﬀerence in the mortar is thought to come from the
casting of the specimens. When the specimens were cast, the mortar was poured into the mould

in steps, and the mould was vibrated two or three times during this process. The purpose of

This would make the top half of the specimen weaker, leading to more damages in this part.
5.1.2
Diﬀerence between the two mixes
In addition to the general fatigue behaviour, some of the results also indicate a diﬀerence in
the behaviour of the stiﬀand the soft specimens. One very noticeable diﬀerence between the
specimens can be seen in the graphs showing the behaviour of each of the phases perpendicular
to the load direction. In these graphs, it can be seen that the interface behaves diﬀerently in
the soft specimens compared to the stiﬀspecimens. In the soft specimens, a big change can be
seen in the behaviour after 1000 cycles, where the amplitude of the relative displacements have
increased a lot, whereas the amplitude does not change much in the case of the stiﬀspecimens.
This diﬀerence can be seen by comparing Figures 4.25b and 4.24b, since they show the relative
displacement of the phases perpendicular to the load direction for specimens L5-20W-F1 and
H5-20W-F2, respectively. In these two ﬁgures, a large change in amplitude can be seen in the
case of the soft specimen, while the stiﬀspecimen does not show the same change. Similar
observations can be made for the rest of the specimens. The reason for this diﬀerence in be-
haviour can be found in the ingredients that have been used in the two mixes. In (Aquino et al.,
1995), it was found that adding silica fume to a mix decreased the porosity of the ITZ since the
smaller particle size of the silica fume improved the packing of the particles in the area around
the aggregates. It was also found that the frictional and shear bond strengths increased when
adding silica fume. In this project, silica fume has been included in the stiﬀmix, but not in the
soft. This means that the interface should be stronger and stiﬀer in the stiﬀspecimens compared
to the soft specimens, which the results also indicate. Based on the diﬀerence in the interface
behaviour seen in this project, the observation made in (Aquino et al., 1995) can be conﬁrmed.
It is not only the interface that behaves diﬀerently in the soft specimens. When looking at
the displacement visualisations (in Section 4.3.1 or in Appendix D), it can be seen that more
and larger damages form in the soft specimens compared to the stiﬀspecimens. In the soft
specimens, the damages are very visibly showing as lines above and below the aggregate, as
well as around the aggregate, while in the stiﬀspecimens, there are fewer lines to be seen, and
the ones that can be seen are not equally visible. This could be due to the same reasons as
the diﬀerence in the behaviour of the interface, namely that the stiﬀmix contains silica fume,
which decreases the porosity of the mix. Since the porosity is lower, there are fewer pores that
could develop into ﬁrst microcracks and ultimately cracks in the specimen. It could also have
been caused by a diﬀerence in the workability of the mixes when the specimens were cast. If the
stiﬀmix had been more liquid than the soft mix, it would have been easier to get rid of the air
bubbles in the mortar by vibrating the mould, and thereby get fewer voids in the specimens. A
third possible reason can be found in the a/p-ratio, which is more than three times higher for
the soft mix than for the stiﬀmix. This means that a larger part of the particles in the mix
have large diameters, making less room for the cement particles to form bonds.
In Section 4.2 and Section 4.3.1, the displacement visualisations in the load direction showed
that for specimens H5-20W*-F2, H5-20W*-F3 and H5-16W-F3, lines could be seen going from
the interface of the aggregate and into the mortar. The lines were running almost vertically,
i.e. perpendicular to the direction of the load, and showed areas of the mortar that have been
compressed more than the rest of the mortar. The three specimens mentioned above are all
specimens made with the stiﬀmix, and the lines have not been observed in any of the soft

5.1.3
Eﬀect of aggregate diameter
In the problem statement in Chapter 1, one of the questions regarded the eﬀect of changing the
aggregate size. Based on the results of the tests, it doesn’t seem like there is a diﬀerence in
the way the fatigue damages develop in the specimen when the aggregate diameter is changed.
In spite of this, one observation has been made with regards to the aggregate diameter, which
could indicate a diﬀerence. During the tests, it has been noted that the smaller the aggregate
diameter, the more diﬃcult it has been to obtain some usable results in the DIC software. When
the pictures have been analysed using GOM Correlate, it has been more diﬃcult to identify the
location of the aggregate for the Drod = 12 mm specimens compared to the larger aggregate
sizes. This could be an indication that the size of the interface changes for changing aggregate
size, or it could be an indication that the method used (digital image correlation) is not precise
enough to use on such small aggregates. Which of these reasons is more likely is not possible to
determine without carrying out further tests, so no conclusion can be drawn from the tests in
this project.
5.1.4
Fatigue life
One of the fatigue tests that were carried out lasted 10005 cycles. The results from this test
can give an indication of the further fatigue behaviour of the specimens when loaded more than
2005 times, which has been the focus of the main part of the tests. When looking at the results,
it is evident that the specimen behaved in the same way after 10000 cycles as the specimens
did after 2000 cycles. The diﬀerence between the behaviour after 2000 cycles and after 10000
cycles is that the deformations are bigger after 10000 cycles. The reason why the behaviour has
not changed after 10000 cycles could be that this is not much further along the fatigue life than
2000 cycles is. If the fatigue life is determined using Aas-Jakobsen’s formula, equation (2.1.2),
it is found that with σmin = 0 MPa and σmax = 0.6 fc, the fatigue life is NF ≈700.000 cycles.
If the numbers of load cycles in the tests are compared to this, it would be seen that the 2005
cycle tests are 0.3% of the fatigue life, and the 10005 cycle test is 1.4% of the fatigue life, i.e.
not far along the fatigue life, so the behaviour is assumed to be the same at these stages. The
fatigue life determined from equation (2.1.2) can also be used to reﬂect on the ﬁndings from
the extensometer tests. These tests showed that the fatigue modulus decreases rapidly in the
ﬁrst 400-600 cycles, and decrease at a slower, linear rate in the remaining cycles of the tests.
This is in good agreement with the ﬁndings of several of the studies included in the literature
review. However, when comparing the percentages that were found above with the stages used
in the literature, the test results do not agree with the literature any longer. The stages used in
(Destrebecq, 2013) are 5 −10% for the ﬁrst stage of rapid development, and even though these
are the percentages in the literature that are closest to the test results, it is still very far from
the results. In the case of the stiﬀspecimens, the diﬀerence between test results and literature
is even bigger, since the stiﬀspecimens were only loaded to σmax = 0.5 fc, leading to a fatigue
life of NF = 2.0 · 107 cycles. This means that the 2005 cycles are approximately 0.01% of the
fatigue life (it is worth noting that both the stiﬀand the soft specimens are tested in large
cycle fatigue, and should therefore behave in the same way). So, assuming that equation (2.1.2)
yields the correct fatigue life for concrete, it would seem that the specimens either behave in a
diﬀerent way than described by the theory, or that the ﬁrst stage can be subdivided into smaller
stages, and that two of these sub-stages have been observed in the tests. Since the specimens

5.2
Eﬀect of pre-existing cracks
One of the questions that have been investigated in the project looks into the eﬀect of pre-
existing cracks in the specimen, and their eﬀect on the fatigue behaviour of concrete. In order
to investigate their eﬀect, an air-cured specimen has been tested. The eﬀect of the pre-existing
cracks can be seen by comparing the results of the L5-20A specimen with the results of the
L5-20W specimen since the only diﬀerence between these specimens is the curing conditions.
When looking at the diﬀerent graphs that have been made in the analysis, the greatest diﬀer-
ence between the two specimens can be seen in the graphs showing the relative displacements
of the phases perpendicular to the load direction (these plots can be seen in Figure 4.25b and
Figure 4.27 for specimen L5-20W-F1 and L5-20A-F4, respectively, as well as in the data sheets
in Appendix D). In these graphs, it can be seen that the specimen that was cured in water
showed an increase in the amplitude for the two interfaces after 1000 cycles, whereas the spec-
imen cured in air showed no increase. This observation is substantiated by the displacement
visualisations (presented in Section 4.3.1 and in Appendix D). These visualisations show that
in the water-cured specimen, the crack development is more extensive than in the case of the
air-cured specimen, that shows almost no cracking, especially at the aggregate interface. Fur-
ther support for this interpretation of the results can be found in the investigation of the crack
opening at the interface. These graphs also show a change in amplitude for the water-cured
specimen after 1000 cycles, but not for the air-cured specimen. The displacement visualisations
from the close-up pictures show that while some cracks form at the interface in the air-cured
specimen, the crack formation in the water-cured specimen is signiﬁcantly larger.
One way of explaining why the changes in the water-cured specimen are not seen in the air-cured
specimen is by looking at the development of the fatigue modulus in Figure 4.40. In this ﬁgure,
it can be seen that the water-cured specimen shows a rapid change in the fatigue modulus in the
ﬁrst 400 cycles, while the air-cured specimen does not. Also, when looking at the values from
extensometer 1 (since data from extensometer 2 is too noisy), it can be seen that the water-
cured specimen starts at Efa ≈31GPa and ends at Efa ≈28GPa after 2005 cycles, while the
air-cured specimen goes from Efa ≈35GPa to Efa ≈34GPa. While the values can not be com-
pared between the two specimens (due to the diﬀerent factors that have inﬂuenced the results,
see the previous sections), the change in the values from start to ﬁnish can be compared. The
fatigue modulus development shows that the water-cured specimen experiences a larger change
in stiﬀness during the 2005 cycles compared to the air-cured specimen (approximately 3GPa for
the water-cured specimen, compared to 1 GPa for the air-cured specimen). One parameter that
can be compared between the two specimens is the damage ratio, which has been determined
using the fatigue modulus. Looking at the plots of the damage ratio development in Figure 4.41
shows that the damage ratio reaches a much higher value for the water-cured specimen than for
the air-cured specimen. For the water-cured specimen, the damage ratio reaches approximately
0.06 for extensometer 1, while the air-cured specimen reaches approximately 0.03 for extensome-
ter 1. It therefore suggests that the water-cured specimen is damaged more than the air-cured,
compared to the beginning of the test.
A likely explanation of the diﬀerences between the water- and air-cured specimens is found
in the pre-existing cracks that have formed in the air-cured specimen due to drying shrinkage.
For the water-cured specimen, microcracks will form in the ﬁrst cycles of the test. These cracks
will form relatively fast, which explains the rapid decrease of the fatigue modulus in the ﬁrst

5.3
Accuracy of the results
It has been mentioned in the previous sections that the results indicate that an eccentricity has
been present in the way the load has been applied. In addition to the eﬀects this has had on
the results (and been mentioned in the previous sections), it also has the eﬀect that one of the
assumptions used during the analysis is wrong. This is the assumption that the stress levels
are the same on either side of the specimen. The most evident sign of this can be found when
looking at the fatigue moduli determined using digital image correlation, which are shown in
Figure 4.38 for the stiﬀspecimens and Figure 4.39 for the soft specimens. In these ﬁgures, it
can be seen that there are some specimens where the fatigue moduli from the two sides are very
far from each other. Only one of the specimens (specimen L5-20W-F1) shows approximately
the same values on both sides of the specimen. It should be noted that the measuring lengths
used to determine the length changes (strains) are diﬀerent, with the full specimen pictures
measuring over 10 to 11 centimetres, and the close-up pictures measuring over approximately 3
centimetres. This should theoretically lead to a small diﬀerence in the fatigue modulus. Also,
on the close-up, a larger proportion of the measured distance constitutes the glass aggregate,
which has a higher stiﬀness than the surrounding mortar.
The magnitude of the diﬀerence
between fatigue modulus from the two sides of the specimen cannot be explained by using the
arguments mentioned above since these should not lead to fatigue moduli above the stiﬀness of
the glass aggregate (73 GPa). Other factors have had an inﬂuence that could explain the large
diﬀerence. In some of the cases, the measurements on the close up have been very noisy, making
it impossible to determine the proper values of the strain experienced by the specimen. In other
cases, the diﬀerence in stiﬀness between the two sides come from the assumption that both sides
are loaded with the same stress level. In reality, the stress on the two sides would be diﬀerent
if the specimen was loaded eccentrically. The side showing the large fatigue modulus shows this
because the stress is smaller than what has been assumed. Similarly, on the side showing the
small fatigue modulus, the stress is larger than what has been assumed. If the calculations had
been adjusted to take this into account, the fatigue moduli should be closer together. This has
not been possible, however, since the magnitude of the eccentricity is unknown. In addition to
this, some of the specimens seem to become stiﬀer during the test. A similar observation has
been made in the two extensometer tests that were carried out on the H5-20W specimens. Since
this should not be possible according to the theory (since it would mean that the specimen ﬁxed
the damages in itself), this observation would suggest that some form of error had occurred,
most likely due to an eccentricity in the load or skewness of the specimen.
In order to check the accuracy of the results obtained in GOM Correlate, one test was carried
out using both extensometers and digital image correlation to determine the fatigue modulus.
The results from this test have been presented in Section 4.4.2. In the test, it was found that the
close-up pictures and the full specimen pictures do not result in the same value of the fatigue
modulus for this speciﬁc test. This indicates that the specimen has been loaded eccentrically, as
it was described above. The test also showed that the fatigue moduli determined from the ex-
tensometers were in between the values determined from digital image correlation. Even though
it is not a perfect result (a perfect result would be that both sides and the two extensometers
show the same fatigue modulus), the results can still be used to argue that the results obtained
from GOM Correlate are realistic, and that digital image correlation can be used as a method
for analysing the tests and determining the fatigue modulus.

5.4
Reliability of the results
In addition to a discussion of the results and what they show, the reliability of the results should
also be discussed. The majority of the results that have been found in this project have been
analysed using GOM Correlate. Therefore, it would be relevant to see how precise the results
are. When the pictures were analysed in GOM Correlate, a facet size and a point distance were
chosen to obtain a good surface component (the part of the picture the program analyses). In
the data sheets (Appendix D), the facet size and the point distance have been included for all
the analysed tests, including an approximate facet size in millimetres (the default unit is pixels).
In the pictures of the whole specimen, the facet size has been approximately 0.5 mm for all the
specimens, and in the close up pictures, the facet size has been between 0.11 mm and 0.2 mm.
This means that the software can pick up on rather small deformations. However, according to
(Neville, 1997), the ITZ is approximately 0.05mm wide, i.e. smaller than the facets used for the
analysis. Due to this, it is not certain that the deformations that have been observed around
the aggregates only originate from the ITZ, and it is not possible to see exactly what happens
in the ITZ. Because of this, a distinction has been made in this report between the ITZ and
the interface, with the ITZ being the area with a diﬀerent chemical structure, and the interface
meaning a larger area around the aggregate. The facet size and point distance have been depen-
dent on the quality of the pictures and of the painted pattern. Two reasons for the large number
of tests that could not be used are cameras that were out of focus or that the paint pattern was
not good enough. During the analysis of the results, it has been noted that the smaller the mea-
surement is and the shorter the distance it is measured over, the noisier the data has been. This
could indicate that there is a lower limit, at which the software no longer provides usable results.
Another thing that inﬂuences the reliability of the results is the fact that the tests have been
carried out as deformation controlled tests. Due to this, the load has changed over the course of
the 2005 cycles the tests have lasted. In the literature, fatigue tests are normally carried out as
load controlled tests. According to (Destrebecq, 2013), it has been found that when the number
of cycles increases, irrecoverable strains occur in the specimen, meaning that the specimen would
deform more and more under the same load. Therefore, it would be expected that the specimen
would deform more after 1000 and 2000 cycles and that the deformation in the unloaded state
would be higher as well. This has not been observed in the tests that were carried out in the
project, but it is not known whether this is due to the tests being deformation controlled or if
other factors (such as eccentrical loading) have hidden the phenomenon.
In addition to the things that happened during the tests that could have inﬂuenced the re-
sults, other things earlier in the process could have had an inﬂuence on the results as well.
Firstly, the reason behind having two diﬀerent mixes was to test whether a stiﬀmix and a soft
mix had diﬀerent fatigue behaviour. However, when looking at the data showing the elastic
modulus of the two mixes (in Figure 3.3), it can be seen that the stiﬀnesses of the two mixes
were not that far apart. Because of this, it is not straight forward to see if the diﬀerences in the
specimens are originating from the diﬀerent stiﬀnesses of the mixes. Those diﬀerences that have
been observed are most likely coming from the ingredients used in the mixes, most importantly
the addition of silica fume in the stiﬀmix. It is also worth noting that while the stiﬀnesses of the
two mixes are very close to each other, the strengths of the two mixes are far apart (see Figure
3.2). Secondly, the curing conditions used for curing the specimens in water could also have
had an eﬀect on the results. The specimens were cured in water for 14 days at 40◦C. However,
according to (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014, Figure 3-9a), the specimens should have been cured
for approximately 21 days, if the temperature of the water was 40 −45◦C, and furthermore,
the ﬁnal strength of the specimens would be lower when the specimens were cured in warm
water instead of water at room temperature (this is especially important when comparing to the
air-cured specimen). The temperature in the water tanks where the mortar samples were cured

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this project, the fatigue behaviour of concrete has been investigated with special focus on
the eﬀect of the coarse aggregates and the aggregate interface. The results from the tests in the
project have shown that the interface plays a big part in the behaviour of concrete. Damages
develop at the aggregate interface initially, and then propagate into the mortar. It has been
found that especially in the direction perpendicular to the load direction, the interface deforms
more than the aggregate and the mortar when seen relative to the size of the diﬀerent phases.
In the project, the diﬀerence between a high stiﬀness mix and a low stiﬀness mix has been
investigated. It has been found that the fatigue behaviour is highly dependent on the ingre-
dients used in the mortar mix.
The results have shown that the behaviour of the interface
changes drastically when silica fume is added to the mortar mix. In the specimens without
silica fume, the deformations of the interface perpendicular to the load direction increase after
1000 cycles, while no clear change in the deformations of the interface can be seen during the
2005 load cycles in the specimens with silica fume. The addition of silica fume also seems to
have the eﬀect that crack development is limited compared to the specimens without silica fume.
The results have also indicated that pre-existing cracks (from shrinkage) in the specimens in-
troduce damages in the concrete before testing. A consequence of this is that the development
of the fatigue modulus of the air-cured specimens is slower compared to specimens with fewer
shrinkage cracks (water-cured specimens). Furthermore, it has been found that the behaviour of
the diﬀerent phases of the concrete do not exhibit clear changes in behaviour after 2005 cycles
when the specimen has been cured in air instead of water.
With regards to the eﬀect of the aggregate size, no clear conclusions can be drawn.
From
the collected data, it seems that the fatigue behaviour does not change when the aggregate
diameter is changed. It was noted during the process that it was more diﬃcult to obtain usable
results with the 12 mm rods compared to the two other rod sizes, indicating that the interface
may change according to aggregate size. However, the exact reason for this has not been found.
Investigations into the development of the fatigue modulus have shown that the stiﬀness of
the specimens generally speaking decrease with an increasing number of load cycles. It has been
found that the water-cured specimens initially experience a rapid development in the ﬁrst 400-
600 load cycles, while the fatigue modulus development in the remaining 1400-1600 load cycles
is slower and linear. When the fatigue modulus development and the fatigue life of the specimen
are compared to the fatigue behaviour described in several of the sources from the literature
review, it has been found that the specimens used in this project have behaved diﬀerently. It
seems from the results that the specimens in this project reached the second stage of fatigue
modulus development (the linear stage) faster than it is found in the literature. This diﬀerence

6.1
Further work
It was mentioned in the introduction that this project is the initial investigation in preparation
for a future research project. Because of this, there are many possibilities for further work. In
the following, some of these possibilities have been brieﬂy commented upon.
The most basic form of further work on this project would be to make adjustments to the
test setup, test procedure and the specimens used in this project to reduce the errors that have
occurred. Among the factors that could have had an eﬀect on the results are skewness in the
specimens, deformation controlled tests and eccentricities in the test setup. Fixing these prob-
lems would decrease the amount of error in the tests and therefore make the analysis of the
results more straight forward and more conclusive. Tests should also be carried out to conﬁrm
the observations from GOM Correlate using other ways of measuring. This could be by using
strain gauges on the specimen, both during the normal fatigue tests (using the DIC-software)
and for additional tests, where several strain gauges could be mounted on the specimen to in-
vestigate the behaviour of the diﬀerent phases.
Another thing that could be used for further work is to increase the number of tests. In this
project, diﬀerent specimen types have been tested and analysed. However, the goal of the tests
was to have one good test for each specimen type. In order to be able to make some better
conclusions, the number of tests should be increased so that each specimen type is tested more
than once to make sure that the observations have not been one-time occurrences. Furthermore,
changes could be made in the test procedure to investigate other scenarios. This could include
changes to the load levels in the test or changes to the number of load cycles. These tests could
show whether the load level has any inﬂuence other than the speed at which the damages occur,
and how the damages develop further until the end of the fatigue life since the tests in this
project did not investigate much of the total fatigue life. Expanding the project even further
could be to pin-point when damages occur in the concrete, and exactly how the damages de-
velop. In order to do this, pictures should be taken during all load cycles, not just during 5
cycles every 1000th load cycle. This would also help to identify where certain observations in
the results have come from, e.g. the jumps that were observed when the cameras had not taken
pictures.
Besides the work that is based on the specimens used in this project, further work could include
other types of specimens. The purpose of this would be to move gradually closer and closer to
actual concrete from the specimens that were used in this project. One change in the specimens
could be changing the glass rods to stone cylinders. Another step towards actual concrete would
be to merge this project with the two other projects that were carried out alongside this one,
namely an investigation into the surface curing of the glass rods, and an investigation into the
arrangement of the glass rods. By combining the three projects, it would provide a fuller picture
of the behaviour of concrete in both in fatigue and in general.

1
Introduction
This report contains a literature study into the elasticity of concrete and the parameters that aﬀect it.
The study has been carried out as a preparation for a master project, in which the inﬂuence of fatigue
loading on the modulus of elasticity of concrete will be investigated. Because of this, the study will also
include a section regarding fatigue or cyclic loading of concrete.
In the following sections of the report, aspects that have been investigated in the literature study will
be presented. The ﬁrst part of the report will focus on the eﬀect of diﬀerent parameters on the elasticity
of concrete. This includes the material properties (with special attention towards the properties of the
aggregates) and external parameters such as testing conditions. The second part of the report will present
the literature that has been found regarding fatigue of concrete. In the last part of the report, a conclusion
of the literature study and a project plan for the master project will be presented.
1.1
Deﬁnitions of elastic modulus
Elasticity is one of the main properties used to describe the behaviour of a material. The elasticity is
normally quantiﬁed using the modulus of elasticity, E, which describes the relation between stresses and
strains. The modulus of elasticity is determined as the slope in the elastic region of a stress-strain curve.
For a linear elastic material, the slope (and therefore also the modulus of elasticity) will be constant in
the elastic region. Concrete, however, is not a linear elastic material, but a curvilinear material (since
the stress-strain relationship is curvilinear (Neville, 1997)), which means that the modulus of elasticity is
dependent on the current stress level of the concrete (Soroka, 1979, section 9.4). Since the stress-strain
curve is non-linear, there are diﬀerent ways of deﬁning the slope, and diﬀerent types of elastic modulus.
According to (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014) the following deﬁnitions can be used:
1. Tangent modulus: The slope of the tangent to the curve at any point.
2. Secant modulus: The slope of a line drawn from the origin to the point on the curve where the
stress level is 40% of the stress at failure.
3. Chord modulus: The slope of a line drawn between any two points on the stress-strain curve.
4. Dynamic modulus: Approximately the slope of the tangent to the curve at the origin (also known
as the initial tangent modulus).
In addition to the four diﬀerent deﬁnitions mentioned above, (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014) also mentions
the ﬂexural modulus of elasticity, which is determined from deﬂection tests of beams.
Normally when the elasticity of concrete is needed, it is estimated from empirical expressions often
dependent on the strength of the concrete (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014). These equations diﬀer a lot
depending on the source, with each country having their own standard to describe the relation.
In
(Kockal, 2015), a study is completed to investigate the accuracy of the American and British equations,
and the results show that the empirical expressions yield underestimates of the experimentally determined
values.
2
Inﬂuence of material components
Concrete is a material that consists of diﬀerent phases with diﬀerent properties. Each of these phases
aﬀect the material properties of the concrete. The division of concrete into phases with diﬀerent properties
is most evident when considering the diﬀerent types of phase models that have been developed over the
years. The phase models can be used to describe composite materials in general, but within concrete
technology, they are being used to estimate the modulus of elasticity (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014, p. 556).
The most common models consist of two phases, and normally the two phases used are the cement paste
and the aggregates. Several of these models are described in (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014), (Soroka, 1979)
and (Manns, 1970), with diﬀerent complexities. However, the two simplest models also provide upper
and lower bounds for the modulus of elasticity when used (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014, p. 559). These
models are the parallel model and the series model, also known as the Voigt model and the Reuss model,
respectively. The two models impose a condition of uniform strain and uniform stress, respectively. The
models can only be used for achieving estimates of the modulus of elasticity, since they do not take any
factors except the stiﬀness and volume fraction into account (Soroka, 1979, p. 234), and other factors will
inﬂuence the elasticity of the concrete as well. Some of these factors will be presented in the following
sections.

2.1
Matrix composition
It can be seen from the phase models described above that the modulus of elasticity of concrete is depen-
dent on the stiﬀness of the diﬀerent components that make up the concrete. One of these components
is the cement matrix. In (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014) it is stated that the stiﬀness of the matrix is
dependent on the porosity of the matrix itself, and that if the porosity increases, the stiﬀness will de-
crease. The porosity is dependent of several diﬀerent parameters of the cement mix used, including the
water-cement ratio and the degree of hydration. Some of these parameters and their inﬂuence on the
porosity is described shortly in the following (based on (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014)).
The ﬁrst and most important of the parameters aﬀecting matrix porosity is the water-cement ratio
(Mehta and Monteiro, 2014). When the water-cement ratio of concrete is increased, the porosity of the
cement matrix is increased as well. This could lead to a decrease in the stiﬀness of the concrete, but since
the water-cement ratio also has an eﬀect on the interfacial zone around the aggregates, these two eﬀects
should be considered in their combined state, not separately.
The second factor is the degree of hydration of the cement. The higher the degree of hydration is,
the lower the porosity, and thereby the higher the stiﬀness of the cement matrix. Several factors can
inﬂuence the degree of hydration. One of these is the cement type used. It has been found that the ﬁner
the cement is, the higher the rate of hydration will be (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014, p. 53). This means
that if the cement used for the concrete is a ﬁne cement, the porosity will decrease at a higher rate, and
stiﬀness will increase at a higher rate as well. Other factors that aﬀect the degree of hydration are the
curing conditions of the concrete, such as the temperature and humidity.
Lastly, the addition of admixtures to the concrete mix does also have an eﬀect on cement porosity.
One type of admixture is mineral admixtures. If these are added to the concrete mix, the porosity will
be reduced, since the mineral admixtures take part in chemical reactions in the concrete mix that form
calcium silicate hydrate. However, mineral admixtures also reduce the strength development rate at early
ages, if it is used to replace some of the portland cement. Whether this has a similar eﬀect on the rate
of porosity change at early ages is not mentioned in (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014). A second type of
admixtures is air entraining admixtures, that will result in an increase in porosity (and therefore also a
decrease in stiﬀness) of the matrix if used. Air entrainment due to bad compaction of the concrete has
the same eﬀect.
2.2
Aggregates
2.2.1
Porosity and stiﬀness
In normal concrete, the cement paste has a lower stiﬀness than the aggregates used in the concrete
(Soroka, 1979). This means that the stiﬀness of the concrete will be higher than the stiﬀness of the
cement paste but lower than the stiﬀness of the aggregates, as it can be seen in (Mehta and Monteiro,
2014, Figure 4-2). It also means that when the volume fraction of the aggregates increase, the stiﬀness
of the concrete will increase as well (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014, p. 86). The stiﬀness of concrete is
dependent on the stiﬀness of the aggregates used in the concrete mix. As it has been the case with the
cement paste, one parameter that has a great inﬂuence on the stiﬀness of the aggregate is its porosity
(Mehta and Monteiro, 2014, p. 86). The lower the porosity of the aggregates the higher the stiﬀness of
the aggregates, and thereby also the concrete. The eﬀect that the stiﬀness of the aggregates have on the
stiﬀness of the concrete disappears for certain types of concrete, such as in concrete with light-weight
aggregates, where the porosity of the aggregates is higher, resulting in a lower stiﬀness, and thereby
bringing it closer to the stiﬀness of the cement paste (Neville, 1997). In combined aggregate concrete,
normal and light-weight aggregates are used to get a higher stiﬀness than light-weight aggregate concrete,
but a lower density (and thereby weight) of normal aggregate concrete (Zhang et al., 2012).
2.2.2
Size, shape and surface texture
An aspect regarding the concrete aggregates that could be of high interest for this project is the size and
shape of the aggregates and the eﬀect of these to parameters on the concrete. One study that looks into
the eﬀects of aggregate size on the material parameters of concrete is presented in (Wolinski et al., 1987).
In this study, the inﬂuence of the maximum aggregate size is investigated for several material parameters,
including the initial modulus of elasticity. The tests carried out in the study were tensile tests. The study
found that the inﬂuence of the maximum aggregate size could be divided into two groups (and that there
were no signiﬁcant diﬀerences within the groups). The ﬁrst group had the maximum aggregate sizes
of 2mm and 4mm, and the second had a maximum aggregate size of 8mm or higher. The ﬁrst groups

is called mortars, and the second group concretes. It was found that most of the material properties
acted according to which of the two groups the specimen was classiﬁed in, but that the initial modulus
of elasticity seemed to be independent of whether the specimen was made of mortar or concrete.
(Choubane et al., 1996) investigated the eﬀects of the coarse aggregates on diﬀerent types of elastic

in the magnitude, so the results of this study must be combined with the results of further studies to be
able to conclude anything regarding the inﬂuence of the individual aggregate parameters.
2.3
Interfacial zone
The interfacial zone is the area around the aggregates. In the interfacial zone (also called the transition
zone), microcracks and capillary voids are far more common than in the cement matrix (Mehta and
Monteiro, 2014, p. 87). Also, since the aggregates are so big relative to the cement particles it is more
diﬃcult for the cement particles to be packed closely, meaning that there is less cement to hydrate near
the aggregates (Neville, 1997). These two properties of the interfacial zone results in a higher porosity
of the interfacial zone compared to the hardened cement paste, and due to the link between porosity
and stiﬀness, the stiﬀness of the interfacial zone is lower. According to (Neville, 1997) the interfacial
zone develops microcracks at lower loads than the rest of the concrete, and this is what causes the
curvilinear behaviour of concrete (when compared to the stress-strain behaviour of the cement paste and
the aggregates by themselves, their behaviour is mainly linear according to (Neville, 1997, Fig. 1) and
(Mehta and Monteiro, 2014, Figure 4-2)). The porosity of the interfacial zone is also dependent on many
of the same parameters as the porosity of the cement matrix, namely the water-cement ratio, degree of
hydration and the use of mineral admixtures (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014).
According to (Aquino et al., 1995), the interface zone is considered to be the weak link in concrete with
normal strength because of its high porosity. It is therefore of high interest to determine the properties
of the interfacial zone. In (Aquino et al., 1995), tests are performed in order to investigate the diﬀerent
material properties such as the stiﬀness and the bond strength. In the paper, they do not determine
the elastic modulus of the interfacial zone, but rather a parameter they call the ”stiﬀness parameter”,
ω. However, even if the modulus of elasticity is not determined, the results do indicate how the tested
parameters inﬂuence the stiﬀness of the zone. The test they perform is designed to evaluate the eﬀect of
aggregate surface area and variation in the cement paste, and is carried out by casting a stone cylinder
into a cylinder of cement paste, and then apply a force on the stone to push it out. The results presented in
the paper show that the surface area of the aggregate does not seem to have any inﬂuence on the stiﬀness
of the interfacial zone. It was also found that when placing additional coarse aggregates in the cement
paste, the stiﬀness of the interfacial zone decreased, which has been explained to be caused by cracks
forming in the interfacial zones of the other aggregates and thereby reducing the conﬁning pressure on
the stone cylinder. Addition of admixtures in the cement paste gave mixed results, depending on the type
of admixture. Latex additive showed a decrease in stiﬀness, where as latex additive with an anti-foaming
agent and silica fume additive showed increases in stiﬀness.
3
Inﬂuence of testing
Besides the properties of the concrete and the diﬀerent constituents of the material, other factors can
also inﬂuence the elasticity of the concrete. More speciﬁcally, most of these factors do not inﬂuence the
elasticity of the concrete itself, but rather the measured value of the modulus of elasticity. Some of these
factors are commented on in the following sections.
When determining the modulus of elasticity, the method used should be described in a standard to
assure the same method being used in diﬀerent studies. However, this could prove to be diﬃcult, since
diﬀerent countries have diﬀerent standards, and the methods are not the same in the diﬀerent standards.
In Europe, the standard used is (Dansk Standard, 2013), which describes two diﬀerent methods (these are
explained in further detail in Section 3.1). In Denmark, however, an older standard exists that describes

a diﬀerent method, and this standard is still valid (Dansk Standardiseringsr˚ad, 1984). In the US, a third
standard should be used (ASTM Standard C469/C469M, 2014). In these three standards, the modulus
of elasticity is determined from the stress-strain response of a concrete specimen. The main diﬀerence
between the methods described in the standards are the stress levels used in the cyclic loading. In (ASTM
Standard C469/C469M, 2014), the maximum stress level is 0.4fc and the minimum stress level is 0MPa,
in (Dansk Standardiseringsr˚ad, 1984) the maximum stress level is 0.45fcm in the ﬁrst cycle and then

should be rounded to nearest 200MPa and in (Dansk Standard, 2013), results should be rounded to
nearest 100MPa.
3.1
Test method
As it was mentioned previously, the European standard describes two diﬀerent methods for determining
the secant modulus of concrete (Dansk Standard, 2013). The ﬁrst method (Method A) begins with three
load cycles at low loads and three load cycles at larger loads, where both the initial secant modulus and
the stabilised secant modulus is determined. The second method (Method B) consists of three load cycles
going from a low load to a high load, and is used for determining only the stabilised secant modulus. Since
the standard describes two diﬀerent methods for determining the stabilised secant modulus, (Domaga la
and Dobrowolska, 2018) has investigated the inﬂuence of these two methods on the results to see whether
the two methods provide similar values. In the study, concrete cylinders and concrete cubes where tested
according to the two methods, with each specimen being tested with both methods to rule out the
possibility of material heterogeneity inﬂuencing the results. The tests where carried out right after each
other so specimen placement would not aﬀect results, and the order in which the methods were tested was
changed as to rule out inﬂuence from test order. The results of the study carried out by (Domaga la and
Dobrowolska, 2018) showed that Method A yielded the largest values of the stabilised secant modulus.
According to the study, this could be caused by either the compaction from the initial three load cycles
(at low loads) or from the larger value of lower stress in the three last load cycles (at which the elastic
modulus is determined). The results of the study also show that the diﬀerence between the results of the
two methods is larger for normal-weight concrete than for lightweight aggregate concrete, possibly due
to the fact that lightweight aggregate concrete is more homogeneous in terms of stiﬀness.
Over the recent years, Digital Image Correlation (DIC) has become a more useful tool with regards
to material testing. In the project, DIC will be used for monitoring the response of the concrete during
loading.
It would therefore be good idea to look into studies regarding use of the DIC-method for
determining the modulus of elasticity. One such study is (Santos et al., 2016), which compares moduli
of elasticity determined through strain gauge measurements and through DIC. The results of the study
shows that the values of the modulus of elasticity are similar for the two methods, but that the values
obtained from DIC generally are higher. The study also found that the DIC results are closer to results
obtained from long strain gauges than to those from short strain gauges, and that this could be due to
the fact that the long strain gauges correct for the eﬀect of specimen bending, which the DIC does by
looking at a very small section of the specimen. It should be noted that the measuring method used for
the strains in this study was through a microscope, whereas the tests carried out in this master project
will be using a camera, which could aﬀect the diﬀerence between the strain gauge data and the DIC data
(but whether the diﬀerence will become smaller or larger is unknown).
3.2
Test conditions
The conditions under which the specimens are tested can have an inﬂuence on the stiﬀness of the concrete
sample. According to (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014, p. 88), concrete samples tested in wet conditions
has showed higher elastic modulus compared to tests carried out in dry conditions. This observation is
explained by the apparently diﬀerent eﬀect specimen drying has on the cement paste and on the interfacial
zone. When the specimen is drying, microcracking will occur in the interfacial zone, and this cause the
stiﬀness of the concrete to decrease. Additionally, the rate of load application seems to have an eﬀect
on the elasticity of the concrete according to (Mehta and Monteiro, 2014), where it is claimed that the
faster the load application is, the higher the stiﬀness will be.

A part of the literature study has also looked into the restraining eﬀect of the loading platens on
the test specimen. Since the elasticity of concrete and steel are unequal, the limited lateral expansion
of loading platens will possibly change the behaviour of the concrete (Soroka, 1979, Section 8.6.1.1). In
this study, two articles regarding the end platen eﬀect has been included. In (Kotsovos, 1983), diﬀerent
devices has been tested and compared to reduce the restraining eﬀect. In this study, the eﬀect of the
end platen restraint is quantiﬁed by looking into the diﬀerence in strength of concrete specimens in cube
and cylinder shape, the reason behind this being that the size ratio of the cylinder specimens will have
removed the eﬀect of end platen restraint. The study concludes that the most eﬀective type of anti-friction
media (among the ones tested) is brush platens used of steel.
In (Kumar et al., 2016), diﬀerent materials have been investigated in order to ﬁnd an anti-friction
media that is less diﬃcult to handle (since the brush platens are diﬃcult to manufacture). According to
(Kumar et al., 2016), the failure of the concrete specimen will occur more rapidly for decreasing friction at
the loading platens. Because of this, the study assesses the eﬃciency of the anti-friction media by looking
at the slope of the stress-strain curve in the part after the ultimate stress level has been reached, and
the steeper the slope of the curve, the better the anti-friction media. The results of the study show that
using a layer of grease is the best method for decreasing the end platen restraint, since the stress-strain
curve shows an almost immediate drop after reaching the ultimate stress level.
Both (Kotsovos, 1983) and (Kumar et al., 2016) concludes that friction between the specimen’s loading
surface and the loading platens inﬂuence the behaviour of the concrete after it has reached the ultimate
stress level (although they do not agree on the exact behaviour). Both studies also conclude that the
behaviour of the concrete up until reaching ultimate stress is independent of the friction between the
loading platens and the specimen surface. This would therefore indicate that friction would not inﬂuence
the test results regarding the elastic behaviour of the concrete. When comparing the two studies, it should
be noted, that the two studies used diﬀerent methods for quantifying the eﬀect of end platen restraint.
The results of the two studies can also not be compared, since the stress-strain curves in (Kotsovos,
1983) show a reduction in strains after the ultimate stress level has been reached (it is referred to as
”elastic recovery” in the study). This decrease is explained by (Kumar et al., 2016) as being a result of
the testing machine used. Also, the anti-friction media testeed in (Kumar et al., 2016) does not include
brush platens (which they try to ﬁnd an alternative for). It is therefore not certain whether grease is
better than brush platens.
4
Fatigue of concrete
Fatigue is a term used to describe how repeated loading deteriorates a material (Destrebecq, 2013).
When a load is applied to a material (in this case a concrete specimen), the material suﬀers from damage
induced by the loading, and the sum of the damages will eventually lead to failure of the specimen, even
at loads lower than the static strength of the material (Destrebecq, 2013). In the following sections,
diﬀerent aspects of fatigue with relevance to the project are discussed.
4.1
Crack formation due to fatigue loading
The deterioration of concrete due to fatigue loading is caused by development of microcracks in the
concrete and the propagation and expansion of the microcracks (Destrebecq, 2013). When cracks are
formed in concrete, the area of concrete in front of the crack is aﬀected. This area is referred to as the
fracture process zone, or FPZ for short (Horii et al., 1992; Sain and Kishen, 2007). The fracture process
zone can be divided into two parts, namely the microcracking zone, where microcrack development is
dominating, and a bridging zone, where the macrocrack has propagated through, but the aggregates are
able to transfer stresses across the crack (Horii et al., 1992).
A couple of investigations have been carried out with focus on the crack development in concrete. One
of these studies is mentioned by (Hsu, 1984), in which a specimen is subjected to static loads. According
to the study mentioned by (Hsu, 1984), cracks in concrete exist in the bond between the aggregates and
the cement matrix (the interfacial zone) before loading due to shrinkage. It was further found that the
number, length and width of bond cracks in the concrete increases rapidly between 30% and 70% of the
ultimate load (increase below 30% is said to be negligible), and that between 70% and 90% of the ultimate
load, the cracks in the mortar develop rapidly and start to form continuous cracks. In (Hsu, 1984), the
term discontinuity stress is also described. According to (Hsu, 1984), the discontinuity stress is the point
where mortar cracks begin to form continuous crack networks and where a load that is applied over a
long period will lead to failure. The value of the discontinuity stress varies according to the diﬀerent
Elasticity of Concrete - Literature study
7
==PAGE:86==
studies cited in the paper, but is approximately 75% of the ultimate stress (Hsu, 1984). This places the
discontinuity stress at the beginning of the last stage of crack development (as mentioned above).
The division of crack development into phases is also seen in (Zhao et al., 2019).
In this paper,
high strength concrete has been tested in compressive cyclic loading after it has been subjected to high
temperatures (in order to simulate ﬁre damage on the concrete).
The development of pores (in the
paper, cracks are a subgroup of pores) in the concrete was investigated using diﬀerent methods, including
mercury intrusion porosimetry (MIP) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM). The maximum stress
levels in the tests were between 80% and 90% of the ultimate strength. In the study, they categorised the
pores of the concrete into harmless (dpore < 50nm), harmful (50nm < dpore < 200nm) and very harmful
(200nm < dpore). It was found that the total pore volume of the concrete increased after the specimen
was loaded. According to the study, the development can be divided into a fast phase from 0% to 25% of
the fatigue life, in which development is rapid (the phase is called ”the rapid micro-cracks development
stage”). From 25% to 75%, the development rate decreases and the development itself is linear (the
phase is called ”the micro-crakcs linear development stage” in the paper). From 75% to the fatigue life
(i.e. until failure), the development rate increase again, and the microcracks expand and are joined with
other microcracks to form macrocracks, that will lead to failure of the specimen. It is also found in (Zhao
et al., 2019) that in the range from 0% to 25%, harmless pores develop into harmful pores, from 25% to
50%, harmful pores develop into more harmful pores. Additionally, it is found that when the specimens
were loaded to 50%, the interfacial zone would become looser, meaning that a lot of microcracks form in
the interface of the aggregates at this stage. These cracks would then propagate into the cement matrix.
When comparing the ﬁndings of (Hsu, 1984) and (Zhao et al., 2019) it can be seen that they do not
agree on every aspect of the crack development. One notable diﬀerence is that while (Hsu, 1984) claims
that the initial phase is negligible and the second phase is rapid development, (Zhao et al., 2019) claims
that the ﬁrst phase is rapid development and the second phase is slower. This diﬀerence could be caused
by the fact that the study in (Hsu, 1984) is a static loading, or that the study in (Zhao et al., 2019) is
on high strength concrete. When the two studies are compared to the deterioration development (similar
to the crack development) mentioned in (Destrebecq, 2013), it would seem that the results from (Zhao
et al., 2019) are the best, since the categorisation into phases in (Destrebecq, 2013) is similar, although
the intervals are diﬀerent. In (Destrebecq, 2013), the ﬁrst phase is the ﬁrst 5 −10% of the fatigue life,
and is characterised by rapid deterioration, the second phase is 80 −90% of the total fatigue life, and
sees stabilised deterioration of the concrete, and the ﬁnal phase is characterised by an acceleration of the
deterioration until failure is reached.
4.2
Damage assessment
A parameter that can be used when looking into the fatigue behaviour of concrete is the fatigue modulus,
which is denoted as either E or Efa, depending on the source (in this study, Efa is used to prevent
confusion with the elastic modulus). According to (Liu and Zhou, 2017), the fatigue modulus is deﬁned
as the ratio between the stress range and the strain range, and can be determined through the following
equation:
Efa = σmax −σmin
εmax −εmin
(4.1)
From the equation it can be seen that if the stress range is constant (as it is for constant amplitude
fatigue load), the fatigue modulus will decrease with increasing maximum strain. In the case of constant
amplitude fatigue load, the fatigue modulus development will be inversely proportional with the fatigue
strain amplitude, meaning that if the fatigue strain amplitude increase, the fatigue modulus decrease (Liu
and Zhou, 2017). The fatigue modulus is also used in (Destrebecq, 2013), where it is called the longitudinal
modulus of elasticity, and written as ED. In (Destrebecq, 2013), it is stated that the longitudinal modulus
of elasticity has a value of approximately 60% of the initial value when failure occurs.
The fatigue modulus can be used for assessing the damage sustained by the concrete due to fatigue
loading. A method for doing so is presented by (Gao and Hsu, 1998). In this paper, the fatigue modulus
is used for determining a parameter called fatigue damage, which is deﬁned using the following equation
(Gao and Hsu, 1998):
D = 1 −

E0

(4.2)
In the equation above, D is the fatigue damage, Efa is the fatigue modulus at load cycle N and E0 is the
initial fatigue modulus. The damage parameter is also introduced in (Destrebecq, 2013). In (Destrebecq,

2013), the damage is explained in a physical manner and a mechanical manner. The mechanical expla-
nation is the one used by (Gao and Hsu, 1998), namely the decrease in fatigue modulus (or longitudinal
modulus of elasticity in (Destrebecq, 2013)) with increasing fatigue load. The physical explanation is
the increase in the total crack area compared to the initial crack area. In (Destrebecq, 2013), several
important characteristics regarding the damage D are mentioned. Firstly, since microcracking is an un-
recoverable damage to the concrete, D can only increase. Secondly, failure will not occur at D = 1, but
rather at a critical value Dcr < 1. When D reaches the critical value, the remaining concrete (the part
that is uncracked) is not suﬃcient to carry the load applied to the specimen, and the specimen will fail.
4.3
Fatigue and its eﬀect on the elastic modulus
In addition to the papers mentioning the fatigue modulus of concrete, not much literature has been found
regarding the inﬂuence of fatigue loading on the elastic modulus of concrete. However, in (Nordby, 1958),
a couple of studies are mentioned that include ﬁndings regarding the modulus of elasticity. It should be
kept in mind when studying the results of these studies that they are approximately 100 years old, so the
accuracy of the results may not be that good.
The ﬁrst study mentioned in (Nordby, 1958) has found that the elastic modulus changes in relation to
the number of the load cycle. It was found that when concrete specimens were loaded below the fatigue
limit, the modulus of elasticity was reduced during the ﬁrst load cycles, but then reached a steady level
and remained constant for the remaining part of the tests. The concrete used in these tests were of low
strength (1200psi = 8.3MPa to 1580psi = 10.9MPa), and higher concrete strengths were not tested, so it
is possible that the elastic modulus could change in a diﬀerent way for higher concrete strengths.
The second study mentioned by (Nordby, 1958) looked at concrete beams loaded with very few load
repetitions (at most a few hundred). This study found that the modulus of elasticity changed slightly
and that the stress-strain diagram became a straight line. However, the way the results are presented
in (Nordby, 1958) does not deﬁnitively state whether the change in E is an increase or a decrease. It is
stated in (Nordby, 1958) that the test carried out do not represent the fatigue behaviour of concrete.
5
Conclusion
From the sections above, it can be seen that there has been made a fair amount of research into the
factors that aﬀect the elasticity of concrete, and also a great deal of research about the fatigue behaviour
of concrete. However, it is also evident that very little research has been carried out regarding these
two aspects in combination. A lot of the fatigue research has been carried out with the objective of
determining the fatigue strength, and not the elastic properties.
Also, it seems as if the amount of
research regarding what part of the concrete the damage occurs is minimal. In the following, a project
plan for the master project is presented, including information about what should be examined in the
project.
5.1
Project plan
In the project, the fatigue response of concrete will be examined.
Several diﬀerent aspects will be
investigated, and these are described in the following.
One thing that will be investigated is the change of elasticity of the concrete in relation to the number
of load cycles, the specimen has been subjected to. This could be done by monitoring the stresses and
strains the specimen is subjected to and looking at the stress-strain curves to determine the fatigue
modulus (or longitudinal modulus of elasticity) as shown in (Destrebecq, 2013, Figure 5.4). In relation
to this, the damage sustained by the concrete could be monitored as well, using the equations from
(Destrebecq, 2013) and (Gao and Hsu, 1998).
Another thing that will be included in the project is a microstructural investigation to determine
which part of the concrete suﬀers the most damage and at what stage the damages occur. This part of
the project could be quite similar to the investigation carried out by (Zhao et al., 2019), who investigated
high strength concrete. Instead of using SEM and MIP to look at the deterioration of the concrete, digital
image correlation will be used.
Before the actual testing begins, a series of tests should be carried out to determine some of the
properties of the materials used in the specimens. For example, it would be useful to know the elasticity
and perhaps also the strength of the cement matrix and the glass rod aggregates prior to testing. Also,
the strength of the specimens should be investigated as well.
