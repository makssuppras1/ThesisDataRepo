Abstract
In recent times passwords have become more and more vulnerable to attacks. New meth-
ods of biometric interfaces allow for new authentication procedures in security. This study
searches the boundaries of new ways for low cost biometric authentication. The research
conducts a novel Proof of Concept where images will play a key role. The overall method-
ology of the Proof of Concept will be a method where creating a hypothesis is followed
by an attempt to prove or disprove the claim. In order to make this happen the possi-
bility and usability of authentication through non-invasive electroencephalogram (EEG)
will be studied. The focus is narrowed down to human image stimuli and will accumulate
into performing tests to determine if it is possible to authenticate users either by using
existing data or by collecting new data. The external stimuli towards the user is varied
and tested in order to test multiple possibilities and verify that the authentication is usable.
The research provided key insights to possible EEG solutions and which methods of au-
thentication have been conducted previously. Additionally the experimental work yielded
experience and discovered problems which have to be overcome in order to make EEG
authentication feasible.
Experiments were performed on 20 test persons with 4 diﬀerent image sessions each to
collect data. The data was captured with an Emotiv EPOC headset, and the environment
of the experiments was an oﬃce setting with lights turned oﬀ. The data capture diﬀers
from a clinical setting which is commonly used in EEG experiments.
This Proof of Concept concludes that it is currently not possible to use a low cost EEG de-
vice and images as stimuli for authentication. The future will likely bring new possibilities
in EEG authentication that might expose new opportunities.

Introduction 1
This study will explore the possibility of using ElectroEncephaloGram (EEG) for biometric
authentication. The authentication will focus on the possibility of using brainwaves, and
further expand into the possibility of using an image sequence with repeated images as
stimulus, thus prompting a unique recognition response.
The study will make use of EEG knowledge for analyzing subjects. In this study, sub-
jects are regarded as individuals who are being tested. EEG captures brain waves in a
subject by placing electrodes on the scalp of the subject. Brain waves have a huge plethora
of uses, which can potentially be extracted for authentication purposes. Currently, the ma-
jority of EEG studies are focused on creating Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI) in order to
aid disabled people. Hence authentication through EEG is a relatively novel idea, which
has been theoreticized a few times in the past decades [1].
This study is based on an intuition that people will have a unique brain response to
images of near relatives or friends. Other studies in EEG authentication focus more on
basic stimuli responses, thus the high-level method of showing images with people is a very
novel approach in the ﬁeld. Thorough research must be performed as this is a preliminary
study within the research ﬁeld.
The focus is on the brain wave interface of EEG through non invasive Brain-Computer
Interfaces (BCI). Captured brain waves carry many characterizing features, which can be
used to identify the subject. To create a reproducible action for testing, a test setting of
a sequence of images will be used. This exploits the action of human image recognition
which is also captured by the EEG by having images repeated thus forcing a recognition
response. The topic is inspired by the problem of biometric recognition and authentication
becoming easier to imitate and therefore spoof (e.g. with ﬁngerprints and gummy ﬁngers).
This is further emphazised by the problem of passwords becoming easier to crack due to
technology advances. Searching for more secure authentication mechanism thus leads to
uncovering authentication possibilities by using EEG.
1.1
Biometric authentication principles
In order to evaluate the biometric authentication, a set of principles is needed.
The
authentication principles used in this thesis are as follows [2][3]:
• Universality – all people should have this characteristic.
• Uniqueness – any two people should be distinct from each other based on the
characteristic.

• Permanence – the characteristic should be suﬃciently invariant across time as well
as attempts.
• Collectability – the characteristic can be measured quantatively.
• Acceptance – the extent to which the general public will accept the biometric
authentication method.
• Circumvention – how easily the method can be fooled by a perpetrator.
• Performance – the accuracy of authentication, the speed of execution and the
operational and environmental factors aﬀecting the accuracy and speed.
The most important points being universality and distinctiveness since that is what we seek
to investigate in this study. Following this, permanence quickly becomes a problem due to
the nature of the brain and the aging process. Lastly it is investigated if collectability can
be improved by removing cost restrictions and diﬃculties in setting up EEG equipment
(e.g. gel or saline solutions). This will presumably also improve the acceptance of the
method in the general public, although there seems to be a general consensus against
brain wave biometrics (e.g. fear of intrusion of privacy and control) [4].
1.2
Basic principles of the experiment
In this work the goal is to ﬁnd a simple and robust method, using brainwaves as an input to
a security algorithm which can be used in a generic setting with focus on a low cost solution
for authentication. The idea is to perform a Proof of Concept with 20 persons in order to
retrieve a signiﬁcant volume of data to be processed. The test method shows a sequence
of pictures with target and dummy (ﬁller) images. Data from the EEG device will then be
collected and data will be analysed in order to ﬁnd possible unique connections between
a given proﬁle of measurements and the images shown during the test. The analysis of
the data retrieved will be based on machine learning principles in order to utilize classiﬁ-
cation to authenticate the user. An overview of the Proof of Concept is shown in ﬁgure 1.1.

1.3
Setting direction in the research
Following the initial research, an interview was conducted with Professor Angelo Spognardi
and Professor Tobias Andersen (Cognition, DTU) gaining clarity in the novel approach
and gaining conﬁdence in the intuition in the beginning of the research. The interview
resulted in a change of direction in the study. The initial direction of the study was to
perform experiments with a sequence of images where the user would be able to recognize
a repeat image in the sequence. This resulted in searching for existing datasets for the
study, although none with images were found due to being a novel approach. A dataset
was found and used to gain experience with working in the EEG ﬁeld. The experience
from existing studies yielded a foundation for designing and conducting experiments for
the novel approach.
A dataset from another study [5][6] is used in the thesis. This was found by surveying the
possible datasets that exist which were examined based on a summary website [7]. The
dataset was chosen based on the fact that it was used in an existing study which yielded
good results. The primary intention of this recreation is to gain experience with the data
and learn concepts before conducting the experiments.
The EEG data is diﬃcult to gather from the website since the data ﬁles of the individual
subjects have to be downloaded separately. In order to bulk download the data, a simple
script was created to manage the downloads.

Description and workﬂow
The overall methodology in this study will be a hypothetical-deductive method, where the
hypothesis is created followed by an attempt to prove or disprove the claim. The ﬁrst
part will consist of studying the possibility and usability [9] of authentication through
non-invasive EEG. The focus will be narrowed down to human image recognition and
features which can identify [3]. This will accumulate into performing tests to decide if it
is possible to identify users by either using existing data or by performing new tests. It is
assumed that all humans have a distinct response. The methods will be based on simple
and known test settings as a Proof of Concept and possibly followed by a more speciﬁc
experiment setup. Second part will therefore build upon the ﬁndings of part one, where we
now take steps toward performing the EEG authentication with classiﬁcation. This will
use measures of performance in relation to the needed level of authentication.The workﬂow

Problem deﬁnition
Based on the studies and ﬁndings a problem deﬁnition can be created. The problem area
will be deﬁned as a preliminary study and proof of concept in order to give a framework
for a paper that is under development by Prof. Angelo Spognardi and Prof. Mauro Conti.
This section outlines the problem deﬁnition of the thesis.
It begins with a problem
statement, followed by the hypothesis of the problem statement.
1.6.1
Problem statement
Based on the framing of the EEG research area, the problem deﬁnition will be centered
around a preliminary study in order to establish a way forward for future work around
EEG biometric authentication studies. This path focuses on the generic high-level stimuli
approaches in contrast to existing simple low-level sensorical stimuli approaches. These
will hopefully be easier to use in real world applications and reduce reluctance towards
brain monitoring as a means of authentication.
This leads to the following problem statement:
Can EEG authentication with human object recognition be used as an authentication mea-
sure?
• Can EEG with human object recognition provide a distinct response using low-cost
BCI hardware?
• Can the distinct response be used to authenticate the user?
• How can this approach be applied, and if so are any additional approaches required?
The study thus aims to build a prototype and evaluate a proof of concept in order to
show that an authentication measure can succeed. In relation to this, possible toolkits
can be found in order to create an initial starting point for the proof of concept and the
preparations for a further study.

2.1
Quantiﬁable study
The experiments and research is motivated by quantiﬁable research and the inherited
deduction methodology. It follows the process of creating a hypothesis or a similar loose
problem statement based on existing theory. To prove or disprove the hypothesis a research
setup must be made to ensure that the hypothesis and data collected are aligned and
correct, i.e. decide on measures in the test and on who/where to conduct experiments.
Data collection proceeds such that the data can be processed and analyzed w.r.t to the
hypothesis. Findings can be concluded and documented such that they are added to the
existing knowledge and theory [10]. An illustration of the process can be found in ﬁgure
2.1.

2.1.1
Methods
The study began with an extensive literature study in both cognition, biosecurity and data
processing (signal processing and machine learning). The hypothesis scoped the study to
focus on testing subjects for uniqueness w.r.t. an image sequence with speciﬁc personal
images. The research setup possibilities were then investigated to discover how to best
collect data. The deﬁnitions will ﬁrst be introduced and then later discussed.
2.1.2
Deﬁnitions
Certain deﬁnitions were used to determine what would yield the best results in the
experiment/study.
These deﬁnitions are integral to the quantiﬁable study and hence
provide a means to evaluate the process of the study.
Thus fulﬁlling and establishing
the deﬁnitions increases the quality of the study. These deﬁnitions will now brieﬂy be
introduced and then discussed later.
Causality concerns the ﬁnding of the cause or reason behind science. It is as such the
why of the study. Reliability introduces the concept of consistency to the study, i.e.
will the experimental results be correct in all cases, (e.g.
all people in this particular
case). Validity relates to whether the result can be interpreted as correct, i.e. if a study
determines an object is black, is it really black?
Replication concerns the ability to

Discussion of quantiﬁable research
The most signiﬁcant factor in the experiment will be the causality or correlation. The
diﬀerent aspects of quantiﬁable research will stem from this topic due to complex data
collection.
A degree of uncertainty in the causality is accepted as a premise for the

Causality
One of the main concerns in this study is the causality between the stimuli and response.
Due to the choice of using EEG as a measurement tool, we are bound to have a low
Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR). This problem stems from the inherit complexity of the
brain. In simple terms, it is the many concurrent processes of the brain that tends to
create problems in determining causality. This causality problem is the reason behind the
frequent use of speciﬁc sensorical stimuli and response analysis, since this method tends
to single out speciﬁc brain processes. Furthermore, studies using mental processing (e.g.
mental calculation) can be strongly inﬂuenced by the learned abilities of the subject. This
learning ability clouds the causality of such experiments since the response of the subject
will change over time, i.e. in the case of any previously seen mental exercise.
The study seeks to ﬁnd unique responses to certain images either through recognition
and the feelings associated with objects in the image. It is a top-down approach to EEG
analysis inspired by neuro-engineering. It attempts to advance research in the subject by
taking a new angle on EEG and biosecurity. Emotional responses have been shown to
be detectable by EEG [4] but not in the context of uniqueness and to a speciﬁc personal
image, e.g. of relatives or themselves. The approach causes us to view the brain as a simple
black box rather than a complex system as seen in Figure 2.2, thus allowing the use of
correlation instead of causality. Greater clarity in causality will increase the conﬁdence in
the results of the study signiﬁcantly, thus making it especially important for this particular
study.

2. Methodology
A general methodology for EEG data analysis was found in the research of the topic. This
methodology is summarized in the following enumeration [13]:
1. Preprocessing and denoising
2. Source estimation
3. Time–frequency analysis
4. Statistical testing
5. Estimation of functional connectivity
6. Applying machine learning algorithms
7. Visualization of sensor- and source-space data
The diﬀerent points vary in importance and use, based on the individual experiment. Not
all of the points are necessary but they are usually present in some form.
The preprocessing and denoising step focuses on artifact correction and noise in the data.
The step will perform preparation in data for resampling and ﬁltering followed by the
removal of unwanted artifacts and noise by several data processing methods. Knowing
experiment event timestamps is therefore important for denoising. It is worth noting that
one can also choose to ignore artifacts and noise, and proceed directly to the analysis.
Several methods of artifact removal can be applied which will be explained and discussed
later.
Source estimation relates to discovering the source dipoles of the response in order to
determine the causality through the source. Finding the source relates to ﬁnding the dipoles
in the magnetic ﬁeld generated by the response of the subject [14]. Source estimation is
not used in this study.
Time-frequency analysis is simply studying the time and frequency simultaneously, e.g.
wavelets. The bulletpoint also cover Power Spectral Density (PSD) and spectral coherence
among others, which is used in the study. This relates mostly to the more sensor-level
analysis, where averaging of signals is relevant due to the low Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR).
Statistical testing can also be used to analyze EEG data, by applying diﬀerent well-known
test methods, e.g. t-test, ANOVA [15]. Statistical testing is chosen to not be touched upon
in the study.
Functional connectivity is simply testing if diﬀerent areas of the brain can be connected
to give certain results and in the case of the study, a unique response. It is an attempt at
identifying the underlying brain activity by looking at how connected the diﬀerent areas
of the brain are [16].
Machine learning is a method for learning how data can be handled and classiﬁed either
by supervised or unsupervised learning. This part is crucial to most BCI research since it
deﬁnes whether or not it is possible to recognize certain behaviors of subjects. Machine
learning is often coupled with validation mechanisms to avoid overﬁtting data.
Visualization is the showing of data such that it is human readable. This makes it possible
for the scientist to validate and investigate the data easily. It is used comprehensively in
this study to identify what the data looks like and how it can be analyzed.

3.1.1
Biometric security principles
The experiment outlined in section 1.2 uses EEG to authenticate users and is therefore
considered a biometric system. Biometric systems are widely used in authentication, with
ﬁngerprinting being predominant. The biometrics used in security require a certain degree
of precaution, mostly because of the non-deterministic nature of biometrics, i.e. a password
will be deterministic since there is only one correct password, whereas biometrics will have
to accept a response which is similar to the correct and expected response. This is often
described as the biometric system errors which introduces the concept of allowing a certain
degree of error in a practicial biometric security system. Another aspect of security is the
EEG security princples, which seek to evaluate the applicability of the biometric security
authentication. A general beneﬁt of using biometric security is that it cannot be forgotten
or lost and that it is simply harder for attackers to forge [9][3].
3.1.2
Biometric system errors
In biometrics there must be some sort of error acceptance. This is mostly due to imperfect
and changing conditions for the biometric system, e.g. impedance from the electrodes in
EEG.
Biometric systems introduce two diﬀerent types of errors; True Negative and False Positive.
A True Negative error happens when two measurements from the same person are found
to be non-matching, this is also known as a false nonmatch or False Rejection Rate (FRR)
error. A False Positive error happens when measurements from two diﬀerent persons are
found to be matching, this is also known as a false match or False Acceptance Rate (FAR)

error. When using biometric systems, one must therefore balance the trade-oﬀbetween the
False Non Match Rate (FNMR) and the False Match Rate (FMR) to achieve the desired
biometric security. This is done by introducing a threshold t. If t is decreased, the FMR
decreases and the FNMR increases thus creating a more strict authentication. On the
other hand, if t is increased, the FMR increases and the FNMR decreases thus creating a
more relaxed authentication (this is not necessarily a linear relationship). The principle is

Brain waves
In order to retrieve data for the evaluation of brain signals, it is necessary to use an
EEG, MagnetoEncephaloGraphy (MEG) or functional Magnetic Rsonance Imaging (fMRI)
device. In this study, an EEG device is used. The brain signals measured relate to the
amount of activity in an area of the brain and the interconnection of brain areas.
In EEG signals we distinguish between 5 types of wave ranges. It is known that when the
frequency of the brain waves increases the task complexity also increases [4]. The diﬀerent
types of brain waves can be seen in ﬁgure 3.2 and brain activity in ﬁgure 3.3.
• Delta waves range from 0.5-3Hz, slow wave - often during sleep, relates to increase
in subjects attention to internal processing.
• Theta waves range from 4-7Hz, selectively reﬂecting successful encoding of new
information, increases in response to memory demands.
• Alpha waves range from 8-13Hz, characteristic of a relaxed but wakeful state.
Attenuates with eye opening or mental exertion. Increased activity reﬂects increased
arousal caused by basic processing of visual info. Attentional and semantic memory
tasks may cause selective suppression of these waves.
• Beta waves range from 14-30Hz, is increased/enhanced when subject to conciously
perceived stimuli. Thus reﬂecting alertness and focused attention.
• Gamma waves range from >30Hz, usually does not exceed a 45Hz limit. Primarily
used for active information processing.
These wave types will be used as basic information going forward into the analysis of the
project.

3.2.5
Evoked Potentials
An Evoked Potential (EP) is a brain wave response to an event, often external, which
is usually a stimuli or task used in an experiment. EPs can be related to mental tasks,
memory, attention and so forth.
In the ﬁeld there is diﬀerentiated between stimulated and spontaneous potentials.
Stimulated potentials occur when a stimuli is presented as part of the experiment and
spontaneous potentials are passive unstimulated responses or peaks.
As such EP is a general term for potentials and covers many subgrouped or subtermed
potentials. In this study we look into Sensory Evoked Potentials (SEP), Event-Related
Potentials (ERPs) and to some degree Steady-State Evoked Potentials (SSEP).
Sensory Evoked Potentials (SEPs) are evoked potentials stemming from sensory
stimulation (phase-locked to stimulation).
These can be evoked by the diﬀerent brain
lobes with stimuli, e.g. eyes, ears, speech, motor function and even motor imagery. For
the sake of this study the main focus is on Visual Evoked Potentials (VEPs), in other

3.2.6
Event Related Potentials
Event Related Potentials (ERPs) are timelocked EPs, i.e. EPs bound to a stimuli at a
given point in time. This means that the response is locked directly to the presentation
of the stimulus. ERPs can be the response to any sensory, motor or cognitive stimuli [4].
A popular and well known component in ERP is the P300. The P300 is closer related
to decision making and evaluation of stimulus. Since there are many diﬀerent waveforms
in response to stimuli, the ERP is as such the "summed up" potentials in response to
stimuli (not actual sums). In a time perspective from stimuli to reaction early parts of the
ERP is more related to the sensory part and physical parameters of the stimulus (sensory,
exogenous), whereas the later parts (after 100ms) are more related to stimulus evaluation
(cognitive, endogenous). There exist many diﬀerent ERP waveforms, of which a few rele-
vant to the study will be examined [23]. The ERP components and their location in the
ERP wave can be seen in ﬁgure 3.7.

In this thesis we will select to consider the P300, N100 and N400 components, due to the
belief that these may have the greatest connection to the experiment stimuli.
The P300 component (also known as P3) is the most researched in ERP. It has a la-

as the speed of stimulus classiﬁcation, i.e. discriminating one event from another. P300 is

The N100 (also known as N1) wave is a negative deﬂection peak that lies around 90-
200ms after stimulus (onset). It occurs when the subject observes unexpected stimuli,
hence it is an orientation response of the brain. The brain does this by matching the
new stimuli with previous stimuli, and seeing if anything unexpected has happened. The
maximum amplitude can be observed on the Cz electrode [23].
N400 relates to the context of semantic incongruity at 300-600ms after stimulus. It is
a negative wave which is inversely related expectancy of a given word at the end of a
sentence. This component further indicates that expectation and later mental processes
lie later in the signal [23].
3.2.7
Mu rhythm
Mu rhythms are present in the brain in order to ensure that the neurons function in
synchrony and generate oscillatory activity. The most well-known oscillations lie in the
9-13Hz band and the activity associated with it are known as the ’rolandic mu rhythms’.
These rhythms are known to be used for planning and executing hand and/or ﬁnger
movements by desynchronizing the rhythm and restriction of movement will synchronize
the rhythm (i.e. ceasing movement). It is known that motor imagery also impact these
rhythms, thus making it possible to use the rhythms for realizing an EEG BCI solution
based on motor imagery [27][4].
3.2.8
Temporal dependencies in the brain
The diﬀerent brain regions may work together and exchange information. This can be seen
when multiple areas are active in dependency of each other. It is indicated by correlated
or coherent activity which indicates that there is active exchange of information between
the brain regions.
This can be used to understand how the brain is organized and can thus be analyzed with
mathematical tools to ﬁnd dependence and interdependence in the brain. It is known that
the brain regions are connected homogenously, and that speciﬁc connectivity patterns exist
in wakeful resting state conditions [5].

3.4
Preprocessing EEG signals
In data analysis the data must ﬁrst be preprocessed such that data becomes manageable
and more trustworthy. This is done by reducing datasizes and removing unwanted data.
Additionally the biometric data can be cleaned of noise, in biometric terms often known as
artifacts. Artifacts are remnants of impedance changes or simply unwanted responses from
the subject, e.g. due to blinking. The section will go into methods for reducing the data
and removing artifacts from the data as a preparation for the feature extraction. Firstly,
the Nyquist sampling criterion must be held in all cases when reducing the datasize. The
Nyquist frequency is the minimum frequency that satisﬁes the Nyquist sampling criterion,
B < fs/2 (the sampling rate must be at least twice as large as the highest waveform in
the signal).
In order to ﬁlter out the brain wave frequencies which the study is not interested in a
ﬁlter must be used. In digital signal processing the Butterworth ﬁlter is mostly used and
is often used implicitly in ﬁltering functions, thus the Butterworth ﬁlter is mostly used as
a blackbox tool in the project. A Butterworth ﬁlter is a ﬁlter based on a mathematical
function which is used as a maximally ﬂat ﬁlter magnitude responses. The ﬁlter function
has no amplitude ripple in passband nor stopband, but at the cost of having a wide
transition region for the given ﬁlter orders [38].

5.1
Recreating existing study on EEG authentication
This recreation is based on an existing study [5]. It was used primarily to gain experience
and conﬁdence with EEG analysis methods. This also resulted in the discovery of MNE
and the ﬁle formats associated with EEG.
In the existing study, the resting state of subjects is used to identify the individual subjects.
The study is based on a dataset which contains sequences where the subjects are resting
with eyes closed (EC) or eyes open (EO). The data sample for each subject is 1 minute
long, recorded over 64 channels, placed in the 10-10 montage system [6].
A large dataset of subjects (108 subjects) and tries to ﬁnd spectral coherence connectivity.
They obtained good results in distinctiveness by using the brain connectivity instead
of using external stimuli to create ’distinctive’ artifacts. The approach also makes the
usability of the method better since it becomes easier to control the test environment
(i.e. not having to control unwanted stimuli). The data is taken from the website of the
experiment [6].
The data is ﬁrst lowpass ﬁltered with a cut-oﬀfrequency at 50Hz using a Butterworth
ﬁlter. This is in order to remove unnecessary frequencies since frequencies above 50Hz are
of no interest. Following this, the data is downsampled from 160Hz to 100Hz sampling
rate in order to reduce the size of the dataset and thus the computation time as well.

5.1.2
Feature extraction
For feature extraction Power Spectral Density (PSD) and functional connectivity are used
[5]. The PSD (see section 3.5) computes the power of each frequency in the signal. PSD
is computed for each epoch and each individual channel. This gives an idea of the activity
of the diﬀerent wavelets (frequencies).
Functional connectivity is determined using spectral coherence to compute how much
the diﬀerent channels or features relate to each other (see section 3.5). This yields the
connectivity between all the diﬀerent channels within the epoch, thus yielding a matrix
with the upper triangle computed, to avoid redundant computation since computing the
lower triangle would yield the same result as the top triangle (calculating everything twice).
The coherence is calculated with a hanning window of 1 second to increase precision (the
window is used in the Fourier transform of the spectral coherence).
The size of the PSD feature vector is the amount of channels (56) with 40 elements each
(1Hz frequency bins). The size of the coherence feature vector is the amount of channels
divided into 40 elements (1Hz frequency bins) thus yielding 1540 features in total (56
channels) from the calculation [5].
The two feature vectors consisting of the PSD and the functional connectivity will then be
used in the classiﬁcation of subjects. In ﬁgure 5.3 the PSD of the epochs across a single
session of a single subject is shown. An overview of the full analysis and the two feature
vectors can be seen in ﬁgure 5.2.

5.2.1
Hypothesis
It is believed that the target images will evoke a unique response (ERP), since the emotions
and recognition of people is nested in a deeper level of the brain (frontal lobe and later
stages) which in conjunction will create a unique response.
Firstly, the study focuses on proving that the secret image in the setup receives a diﬀerent
response from the random images in the sequence. Once this has been established, the
study moves on to prove that the subjects response to the secret image is diﬀerent and
therefore unique. Upon the knowledge that the response can be used as a unique identiﬁer,
it can be used in an authentication context and prove that error rates will be adequate for
the given security purposes.
5.2.2
Experiment knowledge
There is already a lot of research in the EEG ﬁeld that can be drawn upon in order to build
an expectation of what will happen. We will start oﬀby explaining the default reaction of
subjects to stimuli and then investigate which kind of reaction will be received from the
subject to the given stimuli of the experiment.
Upon stimulating the subject (with images) it is expected to see Event Related Potentials
(ERPs) as a response (see section 3.2.6). These ERPs are then analyzed w.r.t. the P300
component, which is a known wave type in the brain. The P300 component is a commonly
known wave used in Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI) thus giving good merit for its use
for this speciﬁc experiment. The analysis is chosen to be based on the ERPs instead of
just Visual Evoked Potentials (VEPs) in order to capture a broader reaction signal (a
VEP will most likely exist in the ERP). However it is not known if the P300 wave will
capture the emotional response or even the recognition response of the subject. A study
has shown that emotions can be determined [18], and another has looked into image based
distinctiveness (as mentioned in the theory) [43].
To ensure that the response to each image is unaﬀected by previous stimuli, the duration
between each visual stimuli is chosen to be 10 seconds.
Due to the inherent nature of the experiment, a certain caution must be taken to ad-
dress external noise. It is important to note that any form of stimuli, even outside the

5.2.4
Test concerns
During the creation and execution of the experiment(s), several concerns were raised.
There is a fundamental problem in the way the experiment is setup w.r.t. to the brain’s

may cause changes in the response of the subject over time, which may be solved with
permanence mitigation.
Problems with the test setup became apparent when having to setup the Emotiv EPOC
headset. The sensors were hard to moisten due to tools missing from the kit and the
electrode sensors were questionable and fell oﬀdue to being partially broken. Additionally
the sensors were hard to ﬁt onto the scalp of some subjects, either due to head size or even
wearing glasses.
There were also apparent environmental problems due to not being able to create com-
pletely silent and protected environments.
Data collection was not consistent which meant that a very large amount of reruns was
needed due to problems in reading the usb device. Turning oﬀthe headset and reopen-

of everything would sometimes ﬁx the issue. This led to some experiments taking a very

minutes).
Lastly, the randomization of images sometimes placed the target images back-to-back such
that it would not look like the image would change for 20 seconds, which means that no
response can be expected due to the perception of the image not changing. The target
image would also sometimes be placed ﬁrst which should be doable but it might be less
optimal.
All in all a great deal of challenges exist when performing the experiments.
5.2.5
Applicable security
The experiment seeks to use both the randomness and the biometric authentication of
EEG to create a secure authentication. Since the images can include many diﬀerent objects
and/or people, it will be diﬃcult for any perpetrator to ﬁgure out which image is the target
authentication image. The perpetrator will have to ﬁgure out which image has relevance
(i.e. target image) more or less real-time and then apply the correct response to the image.
Additionally to ensure biometric security, the image recognition process will be repeated
several times to reduce errors in the biometric model (see section 3.1.3).
The biometric security factors of EEG are discussed in the following [3]. The experiment
uses the general EEG principles from section 1.1 and 3.1.3.
Universality is already fulﬁlled from the description in section 3.1.3, since the universality
of brain signals is already naturally high.
Uniqueness (also known as distinctiveness) is what we will try to prove in this experiment
and to ﬁgure out if the stimulus will elicit a unique response for the given subject.
Additionally, a sequence in the experiment displays an image sequence of another subject
thus testing that other subjects will not elicit a similar response.

5.4
Applied analysis
Once the data is collected, the data analysis is applied to prove or disprove the hypothesis
as stated in section 5.2.1. The analysis is thus performed using the methods described
here.
First it will be attempted to prove that the target image is distinct from the random or
dummy images in the session. If this cannot be proven then there is no diﬀerence between
the target and non-target images which disproves the hypothesis. If the ﬁrst concept can
be proven, it is possible to investigate if the target images are unique for the given subject,
such that the response is distinct for the subject, thus making the method viable as an
authentication.
It can also be tested if the response is based on the target image, or the person in the
target image making the method more ﬂexible, if it allows using diﬀerent images of the
same person instead of using one speciﬁc image continuously.
The concept will also be tested on self images, to see if it yields a distinct response as well.
The following statements will be investigated:
• Prove target image of a person is distinguishable from random or dummy images.
• Prove that diﬀerent target images with the same person in the frame yields the same
67
==PAGE:78==
Master’s thesis
5. Empirical Evidence
response across the diﬀerent images.
• Prove that target self images will yield distinct responses compared to random/-
dummy images.
• Prove that subjects do not have similar response when shown other subjects sessions
(being distinct from others).
The starting point of the analysis is to show whether or not the target images are distinct
in the image session.
The data is ﬁltered with a lowpass ﬁlter to 50Hz since the higher frequencies are less
relevant to the study.
The data is resampled to 100Hz to reduce the data size and
computational load.
The data is then split into epochs (time windows) which are 10 seconds long. Normally
these can be found automatically but it requires a stimuli channel to indicate when stimuli
events happen or it has to be visible from the data. Since this was not possible, the data
had to be split based on 10 second (ﬁxed) intervals of each image.
Next, a feature vector is created based on the data. A Power Spectral Density (PSD) is
used to create a feature vector of size; number of channels, N = 14, and the number of
frequencies in the PSD (50Hz due to ﬁlter), Nfreq = 50 and thus the feature vector is size
N = 14 with 50 elements.
To optimize the model and reduce overﬁtting, both cross validation in the form of stratiﬁed
3-fold and forward-backward feature selection is used. 3-fold is chosen since there are only
3 target images in the sessions. 3-fold can be used since the data set is relatively small,
thus computational load is not a problem.
For machine learning SVM with diﬀerent kernels and kNN is performed and the best result
is used.
Lastly the data is scored using accuracy and precision to show how well the classiﬁcation
performs. Once authentication is relevant, correct recognition rate will be used to measure
performance.
Once the data analysis is applied the results are obtained and can be evaluated.

Proof of Concept setup
The test setting was centered around ideas from Prof. Angelo Spognardi and Prof. Mauro
Conti, who are seeking to compose an article on authentication using an image sequence
of people.
The sequence contains target images which contains a person of special signiﬁcation to
the subject who is being authenticated, e.g. wife, child or special friend. The method
sought to ﬁnd any unique responses when seeing the given person. The concept was ex-
panded to test if diﬀerent images of the given person would yield the same response and
even if images of the subject themselves would yield a unique response. Experiments to test
if the response is unique and distinct across humans and all subjects were tested against a

As such the experiment seeks to ﬁnd distinctiveness based on the images through a com-

In the given context it is a novel approach since current studies mostly look into lower
sensorical stimuli, such as ﬂashing screens or imaginary movement. The experiment used

6.2
Insights from experiments
During the thesis the ﬁeld of cognition has been researched extensively to discover the fea-
sibility and plausibility of the experiment succeeding. The research yielded many insights
into how one should proceed when conducting EEG experiments. From the experiment
setup point of view, it was found that the reasoning for using more simple and low-level
stimuli approaches was the low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). This is to isolate the response
to the stimuli adequately in order to ensure that the captured response is related to the
stimuli shown. As such the clarity is sought in order to understand causality, i.e. that the
stimuli directly correlates with a given response and in this case a unique and distinct one.
When introducing more complex stimuli, one faces a more unclear correlation between the
stimuli and the response from the subject. This is due to the brain having many diﬀerent
regions working concurrently, hence more complex stimuli may be riddled with noise from
other regions since the stimuli may cause unexpected responses. In this sense, adding
more complexity more or less widens the margin of error. Thus making the responses of
the brain susceptible to changes in each trial, thus making replication diﬃcult. This may
add additional problems to the authentication.
Addtionally, the research yielded no proof of the assumption that seeing speciﬁc peo-
ple may yield any unique response. The closest ﬁndings is emotionally related, simply
that emotions can be detected in the subject but this is far from distinct in the sense
that is sought in this study. Another study tested a simple visual stimuli with target and
non-target ﬁgures, e.g. with a square, circle, triangle. This study showed that when people
were assigned a target ﬁgure (e.g. circle), the performance was incredibly poor and that
simply using non-target images yielded the same if not better results. This is one of the
closest visual stimuli studies found and it does not strengthen the belief in the current
authentication proposal [43].
6.3
Distinct responses in EEG authentication
Finding distinct responses from subjects when given visual stimuli of diﬀerent objects, be
it human or other images, is an area where more research is needed. In the ﬁeld of au-
thentication and security there is no necessity of understanding the underlying causality,
and attempting new ways of stimuli is certainly an interesting concept, since it may ﬁnd
possibilities that other researchers have missed or never have thought of.
Several alternative approaches can also be considered for authentication. One of the most

In terms of security, biometric authentication principles have been studied. Biometric se-
curity principles to evaluate the authentication method are used. In the theory section,
some of the concepts have already been discovered and evaluated by other studies. The
principles used are universality, uniqueness, permanence, collectability, acceptance and cir-
cumvention.
In the theory it is stated that universality is high since all humans have brain waves, except
for those with pathological conditions.
In this study the uniqueness principle is synonomous with distinctiveness. Distinctiveness
was not found in the study conducted, although many possibilities may still exist. This is
only a preliminary study as stated earlier, meaning that there may still be possibilities.
The problem of permanence exists but there have been found methods of mitigating the
permanence issues which may be developed in future studies. The solution found is to
update the model each time the subject is authenticated in order to help permanence.
Problems may arise if the time period between authentications is too large, which can
eﬀectively make the authentication fail.
There may also exist inherent problems with
updating the model in every authentication since it has to accept some amount of lee-
way/threshold, which could allow a perpetrator to authenticate and retrain the model to
the perpetrator instead. This indicates that the authentication must be coupled with some
other form of authentication. The permanence of the experiment used in the study has
not been tested, but would be a necessity if the distinctiveness in authentication is proven.
Collectability proved very diﬃcult when performing the experiments due to the practical
setup of the experiment. Most data in studies are taken from existing datasets. These
are clinical trials, with well protected and well-deﬁned conditions meaning that noise is
more or less non existing. In an authentication setting this is a rather diﬃcult condition to
achieve since it greatly limits the environments in which the authentication can be used.
As such, a robust method must be found that can mitigate external/exogenous noise and
an analysis which can deal with noisy data without losing too much performance. These
trials also use the full setups of EEG electrodes (often up to 64 or even higher) and gel
to reduce impedance. This greatly reduces the usability of the EEG biometric since it is
simply too diﬃcult to use in normal conditions. A more usable approach with a low-cost
headset is therefore used.
A low-cost Emotiv EPOC headset is used for EEG data capture which has 14 electrodes
moistened with saline (saltwater, 5%). This makes the authentication at least feasible in
terms of usability. However, many problems were encountered when using the headset

to ensure that subjects would not be stressed out.

6.6
Future
For future works in EEG authentication there is a lot to work on. From a practical point of
view, the hardware needs to mature for ease of use. The setup should be simple and easy,
and maybe methods can even be made to compensate for misplacements of electrodes (this
can exist already, but has not been researched). Hardware that works with no inconvience
towards the subject, since saline or gel solutions may not be preferable to many people, is
an absolute must.
From the perspective of security, it becomes apparent that actual feasibility of the biometric
must be split from the security itself. As such there are two elements:
• Proof of biometric distinctiveness.
• Security authentication applied to distinctiveness.
To make a short analogy; ﬁngerprints have been known to be mostly distinct for all people,
and later on it has been used as a method of authentication in biometric systems.
Now it can be discussed that the actual biometric EEG stimuli needs to be well orga-
nized and in a controlled environment due to many sources of noise and errors. Seeking
methods which will be robust to this kind of noise is necessary, until methods of removing
external noise have been developed. It comes down to the choice between removing exter-

Conclusion 7
The intention of this research was to make initial steps towards a completely new approach
towards biometric authentication. The hypothesis and the problem deﬁnition frame a work
that cover intense study of previous work in the area and applying methods and tools that
can be used for biometric datacollection and the succeeding data analysis. Based on the
scoping of the EEG problem area in the problem deﬁnition, the result is a preliminary study
in establishing a way forward for future work within the EEG biometric authentication
area. This path focuses on the generic high-level stimuli approaches in contrast to existing
simple low-level sensorical stimuli approaches.
7.1
Findings
The initial parts of the thesis conducted heavy studying on the area of EEG and biometric
security. Firm knowledge of EEG was needed in order to setup experiments, perform them
and then analyze the outcome. The topic had many facets in terms of understanding the
brain signals and simply how to preprocess and analyze the data in a proper manner.
These ﬁndings can conclude that EEG authentication is not as simple as it was expected
to be and full understandings and tools are not necessarily readily avaible.
During the later stages of the research into EEG, the Proof of Concept was begun. This
including deﬁning an experiment to gather the data as well as conducting the experiment,
including the set up of hardware and software components necessary for the experiments.
Software for the experiment existed albeit having many start-up issues with the software.
The software was expanded for data analysis and ﬁle conversion to facilitate the use of EEG
analysis tools. The goal of the Proof of Concept has been achieved building a datahandling
prototype and an easy to use framework (MNE) has been found and used with success.
During the process of performing the experiments many concerns and problems were
quickly introduced, which have given insights into what should be done in the future. This
ranges from simple things in the experiment to the general future of EEG authentication
ﬁeld.
The ﬁndings were that many problems in the practical aspects of EEG authentication have
to be managed in order to make the method feasible. This quickly becomes a study in
noise tolerance since it depends on the external noise introduced in the experiment and
how it can be dealt with in the analysis.
20 persons have participated in testing the image sequence of 4 times 12 images which
yielded new datasets. As described earlier the completion of experiments gave a lot of
experience in setting the scene and performing the experiment, yielding both positive and
negative insights.

7. Conclusion
The raised questions from the problem deﬁnition will now be answered and elaborated
upon.
7.2
Can EEG authentication with human object recognition
be used as an authentication measure?
In this study there is no evidence that it is possible to use EEG as an authentication
measure. The reason is believed to be the complexity of the brain and the issue of exposing
the user to too many inputs.
The amount of noise in the brain signals is quite high
making it hard to make a distinct connection between non-simple stimuli and the brain
signal associated with it. The idea of using feelings towards images as a unique feature is
questionable since it has never been done in previous studies. Several datasets have been
collected and analyzed in the study but no signiﬁcant results have been found. Studies
using other methods of EEG distinctiveness exist which have provided promising results,
but it has not been further shown in this study. Seen from a security perspective it will be
questionable using such an unstable and immature Emotiv hardware and the developed
software for authentication in general.
7.2.1
Can EEG with human object recognition provide a distinct
response using low-cost BCI hardware?
As a natural consequence of the negative answer on the ﬁrst question it has not been
possible to prove that it is possible to provide a distinct response with low-cost BCI
hardware. Other studies imply that more advanced EEG methods could be used as a
distinct response but it requires quite a lot of research before it is possible to claim that
stable and robust EEG measurements can be achieved, being adequate for providing the
distinct response.
In short, one could say that feelings that are the basic foundation for the EEG
measurements are not distinct.
The distinct response will most likely be provided by
a combination of EEG and ﬁngerprints or other authentication methods in order to reach
a decent security level. Going forward using a low-cost BCI hardware requires a signiﬁcant
amount of technological development in order to create the simplicity that is crucial for
providing low cost hardware on a larger scale.
7.2.2
Can the distinct response be used to authenticate the user?
The results shown in this report indicates that the distinct response can be used to au-
thenticate a user but due to a lot of environmental disturbances, that causes errors in the
measurement, it seems diﬃcult to use it in a visual/image context.

7.2.3
How can this approach, be applied and if so are any additional
approaches required?
The result of the study implies that other approaches have to be developed in order to
realize EEG authentication. Further research is needed to discover ways of reducing noise
from external stimuli. New measures of capturing data is needed to collect more valid data
and improvements to usability are necessary for use in authentication purposes.
7.3
Concluding remarks
It is believed that it is not possible to authenticate users based on high-level and lowcost
EEG authentication using an image sequences of people. Hence the null theory hypothesis
has been proven.
Due to all the constraints with noise and disturbances during the test a clear
recommendation would be to look more into audio as a distinct response.
With eyes
closed and playing an audio sequence it might be a path to follow in order to use EEG as
an authentication method.
All in all this work has outlined a basic foundation for future work in using EEG as an
authentication method with quite an extensive portfolio of possible ways forward.
