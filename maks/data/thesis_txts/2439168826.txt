Abstract 
Sporadic off-flavors, produced during beer fermentation, constitute a major problem 
for the brewing industry. Control of the unwanted butter-testing diketone off-flavor, 
diacetyl, is taking place with traditional methods such as optimization of wort, long 
maturation period and enzymes addition. In this study, CRISPR/Cas9 system for target 
genome editing is used to construct different S. cerevisiae strains (laboratory and 
industrial) with disrupted ILV2 and ILV6 genes. Those genes are responsible for 
encoding the precursor molecule of diacetyl, α-acetolactate. Studies report that 
fermentation with those modified strains resulted in production of 60% less diacetyl in 
the final product. Different strain backgrounds in combination with the gene knock-
outs resulted in total of 17 mutant strains. All newly constructed strains were tested in 
their ability to ferment beer under the classical beer fermentation conditions. All tested 
mutants successful transformed the fermentable sugars included in wort and produced 
ethanol. Differences in physiological characteristics between parental and mutant 
strains observed, indicating that double knock-out of ILV2 and ILV6 genes decrease the 
reproduction of yeast. Moreover, colorimetric assay proved inefficient for 
quantification of diacetyl in beer and samples are under process with GC-MS analysis 
to quantify the final diacetyl concentrations in beer fermentations with the modified 
strains. This study provides a novel way of transformation of industrial strains to 
construct brewing candidates that not produce off-flavors during fermentation.

1. Introduction 
Yeasts have been used for fermentations of food and drink products for thousands of 
years. Different strains are used for each specific fermentation process and produce 
invaluable outcomes [1]. Beer, and alcoholic fermentation, is one of the most well 
established and traditional usages of yeast, dating back to ancient Egypt and 
Mesopotamia [2]. Brewing, along with bread making and winemaking are some of the 
world's oldest biotechnologies. Saccharomyces cerevisiae, top fermenting yeast, and 
Saccharomyces pastorianus, bottom fermenting yeast, are responsible for beer 
production and many different beer styles available today [3]. Together, with the 
production of ethanol in beer, yeast is also responsible for producing many volatile and 
flavor metabolites effecting the body and aromas of the final product. However, some 
of those metabolites are not always desired in beer due to their unpleasant flavors. Beer 
off-flavors can occur spontaneously during fermentation or as a metabolic unwanted 
by-product. Control and elimination of undesired off-flavors in beer, is demanding and 
challenging for the brewers. 
Beer production is a traditional and well-established process but nowadays, however, 
there is an increasing demand on both the quality of the product and the economics of 
production. Fermentation step is crucial for brewers, because it is the most time-
consuming step and, flavors are produced corresponding with the brewing yeast strain 
used. Therefore, enhancing the performance of brewing strains is of high importance 
for the brewing industry [3]. Targeted genetic modification of a yeast strain is one way 
to obtain higher beer quality and to improve the economics of beer production. 
Although, genetically modified organisms are not yet accepted for food utilization 
because of the misconception that exists about hazards occurring from GMO organisms 
in biodiversity and food safety.  
Advances in molecular biology and bioinformatics, enable scientists to do efficient 
deletion of targeted genes. In this perspective, the genes responsible for production of 
unwanted compounds that spoil the flavor of beer can be eliminated. In recent years, 
clustered regularly interspaced palindromic repeats (CRISPR) and its associated 
proteins (Cas) have become a versatile and efficient method of choice for precision 
genome engineering in many organisms, including yeast [4]. In yeast, DiCarlo et al. 
were the first to adopt this technique to introduce a gene knock-out by homologous 
DNA repair with the usage of a double-stranded 90-bp oligonucleotide (dsOligo) as a 
template. This dsOligo was designed in that way that contains homologous ends to the 
target site and a stop codon to achieve the wanted recombination and knock-out of the 
CAN1 gene, a plasma membrane arginine permease, in haploid yeast cells [5]. Also, 
several studies report that the CRISPR/Cas technique is efficient in targeting and 
knocking-out more than one gene simultaneously [6]. Nevertheless, all together the 
examples of studies in field of molecular biology, suggest that gene knock-out illustrate 
the widely successful choice of CRISPR/Cas9 for efficient and definite genome 
engineering in yeast using HDR of Cas9-induced DSBs [4].

1.1 Aim of this project 
The main goal of this study was to eliminate the beer off-flavors that are produced 
through yeast’s metabolism by constructing a mutant strain that will not produce the 
target responsible compound. For this purpose, the CRISPR/Cas9 technique for genome 
editing was used to knock-out the genes associated with the chosen off-flavors. 
Different S. cerevisiae strains were used in this study to test the efficiency of the 
method. Also, the modified strains were examined in their ability to ferment beer 
efficiently after genome editing and compared with their parental unmodified strains 
under the same experimental conditions. Finally, the compound responsible for each 
off-flavor will be determined and quantified. An overview of the experimental approach 
is shown in Figure 1. 
Questions aimed to be answered in this study: Is CRISPR/Cas9 an efficient method for 
genome editing of industrial brewing candidates of S. cerevisiae? Are the mutant strains 
able to be used in the brewing industry with the same performance as their unmodified 
parents? Is the gene knock-out successful on eliminating the produced off-flavor?

2. Theoretical Background 
2.1 Beer brewing 
The production of alcoholic beverages dates back in 4000 BC. Beer is one of the most 
popular beverages with a significant economical contribution in many countries. The 
main ingredients for beer brewing are yeast, malt, hops, and water. Also, other starch 
subordinates like rice and syrup can be used as brewing materials. The steps involved 
in beer brewing include mashing, fermentation, filtration, filling and maturation [7]. 
Hops are vital in beer, as they enrich beer with flavor and aroma compounds and 
antimicrobial resistance. The two main enzymes used in beer brewing are proteases and 
amylases (α-amylase, β-amylase, α-1,4-glucan glucohydrolase). Yeast is also an 
indispensable factor in beer brewing, as it plays a crucial role in taste and quality. Based 
on the yeast used in brewing, there are two main categories of beer ale and lager [8].  
The differences between ale and other styles are the temperature during fermentation, 
yeast strains used, and taste. Ale’s temperature ranges from 15 and 24°C. Yeast used in 
ale fermentation is called top-fermenting yeast, derived from this yeast’s ability to rise 
at the top of the tank during fermentation, contains Saccharomyces cerevisiae species. 
The taste of ale beer is unique. Ales are distinguished with more ester and aromatic 
compounds that characterize the final beer flavor. Lager, on the other hand, is produced 
with the usage of Saccharomyces pastorianus species with the significant difference 
that lager yeasts tend to sink to the bottom of the beer during fermentation. Also, the 
temperature profile is different, with optimal fermentation temperature at 10-12°C. 
Lager taste is a characteristic of the raw materials used. The sweet and light taste of 
malt and freshness of hops are distinct of lager beer compared to the fruity and aromatic 
character of ale, which is not desired in lager [8] 
Apart from yeast, cereal is also very important material for beer brewing. Barley has 
been the major raw material for beer brewing but nowadays cereals such as wheat, oats, 
rye and maize are used extensively. The conversion of cereals into beer is a complex 
procedure. To achieve better beer quality and beer flavors, cereals are malted. The 
cereals must transform in this way that they will contain sufficient quantities of 
fermentable sugars. This conversion is achieved during the malting and mashing steps 
to generate the carbohydrates that yeast can transform into alcohol and CO2. After 
fermentation, the produced beer should be maturated for final flavor development 
before it filtering  and packaging [7] [8]. A description of the brewing process is shown 
in Figure 2.

Malting: The main objective of malting is to produce an adequate quantity of enzymes 
to downgrade starch, proteins, and other components of grain. A medium enhanced 
with α-amylase, β-amylase and proteases is created in this setp. Those produced

enzymes trigger the appropriate changes to the wort sugars needed to support 
fermentation. Malt also is an important factor to the final color determination and taste 
characteristics of the finished beer [9].  
Mashing: During mashing, most of the carbohydrates and proteins are hydrolyzed into 
utilizable materials, by the enzymes of the malt. To achieve this, the malt is mixed with 
water and exposed under specific temperature/time profiles. To intensify α-amylase 
activity for production of dextrins, the temperature of the mixture is maintained around 
50°C. Then, the temperature of the mix is increased to 65 to enhance β-amylase activity. 
To complete the process, all the enzymes should be inactivated, and the temperature is 
increased to at least 75°C. While the amylases are active, they degrade the starch to 
fermentable sugars. Alterations in the color are also taken place (from light to darker 
color) during mashing. Also, to achieve optimum enzyme activity, the pH of the wort 
should be regulated to 5.2 for lager production and to 5 for ale production. The spent 
grains are also removed to form the final composition of the wort [9]. 
Wort Boiling: After the wort is transferred into a heating tank and hops are added in 
this step. The boiling of the wort achieves several points, some of them are:  
I. 
Disinfection from microbes 
II. 
 Inactivation of residual enzymes  
III. 
 Improved extraction of aromas from hops 
IV. 
Color development   
V. 
 Water evaporation and the wort’s sugar concentration is augmenting. The wort 
is boiled for 1–2 h [9]. 
Fermentation: At this point, the selected yeast is introduced to the wort and fermented. 
Wort needs to be cooled down at the desire fermentation temperature. Yeast consumes 
the sugars and produces alcohol, CO2, and some compounds attributed to flavor and 
aromas. It is important during this step, to regulate and keep constant the temperature 
according to the temperature profile of the selected yeast and the final product (ale or 
lager). Also, the fermentation tanks must be cooled till the desired temperature. To 
avoid contamination, the fermentation room should be cleaned and taken care 
respectively. Oxygen in the tank should also be considered as it is not wanted during 
fermentation, but wort aeriation prior to fermentation is necessary to achieve better 
fermentation yields. Regardless of the type of beer, pH during the fermentation is 
rapidly decreased which is beneficial for the stability and potential problems of 
contamination. After fermentation, a final pH to approximately 4.1–4.2 is reached [9].  
Maturation and Conditioning: This step allows the beer to create its final flavor, color, 
and body characteristics. In addition, a short conditioning period after fermentation can 
be implemented. Conditioning occurs at temperatures of approximately 0°C–4°C for at 
least 3 days [9]. 
Finishing: Finally, beer go through several processing steps to get ready for 
commercialization. Filtration and clarification to remove remaining solids, carbonation 
to reach the final CO2 level and packaging vary to cans, bottles, barrels, and kegs. 
Pasteurization increases the shelf life of beer, but it can affect the flavor if the product 
is overheated [9].

2.2 Beer off-flavors 
Beer is a complex alcoholic beverage with numerous compounds that play a significant 
role in its taste and aroma. Many of those compounds have been analyzed and identified 
through-out the years and, a lot of more complex compounds have not been 
characterized completely yet. The final profile of a beer is delicately well balanced and 
a constant challenge to the brewing society to maintain a desire level of all these 
compounds together. Off-flavors can be produced during the beer making process, from 
a range of different factors such as wort making, contamination and spoilage problems, 
storage conditions, type of ingredients, fermentation by-products [9] [10].  
It is thus important to tell, that sporadic off-flavors can occur naturally under different 
circumstances and affect the final product. The most common undesired off-flavors are 
described below  [11][10] [12]  
➢ Acetaldehyde: Is a naturally produced intermediate compound in alcohol 
formation. It gives a flavor of green / rotten apples or pumpkin. It is also caused 
by bacterial contamination. 
➢ Alcoholic off-flavor: A sharp and strong undesirable alcohol flavor. It can be 
cause by production of butanol, propanol, isobutanol, isoamyl alcohol and 
phenolic alcohols through yeast metabolism. 
➢ Astringent: This flavor can be characterized as tarty, bitter and vinegary. It is 
caused by different factors such as presence of tannins and polyphenols, 
bacterial contamination, over-hopping and/or oversparging during mashing 
step. 
➢ Cidery: This off-taste like apple cider or wine, is a fermentation by product from 
different yeast and it is also temperature dependent. Formation of acetaldehyde 
gives also this unwanted off-flavor result. 
➢ Diacetyl: It is a buttery off-flavor. This compound is produced through 
reproduction of yeast during fermentation but also during contamination from 
lactic acid bacteria. In some beers, it is desired in certain levels, but its strong 
threshold requires long maturation times in order to be controlled.  
➢ Dimethyl Sulfides: This onion/ cooked vegetables off-flavor is formatted during 
wort boiling by the reduction of S-methyl-methionine, a compound derived 
from malt. It can also be derived from bacterial contamination and/or yeast’s 
metabolism. Different storage conditions have been reported to enhance this by-
product. 
➢ Fruity: Overproduction of esters during fermentation can result in an unpleasant 
and undesired intense fruity flavor in beer. High fermentation temperatures can 
determine the levels of esters in the final product. 
➢ Grassy: An aroma described as fresh cut grass is the result of compounds of 
fungal and bacterial contamination. It can also be derived from insufficiently 
dried or improperly stored hops.  
➢ Medicinal: Phenols produced by yeast or during mashing/sparging can create 
an unpleasant after taste. Incorrect pH levels, water amount and temperature 
also contribute in the phenol levels in beer. 
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
➢ Metallic: This off-flavor is described as iron. It can be caused by hydrolysis of 
lipids in wrongly stored malts. Also, unprotected metallic equipment used in the 
wort making process is a common explanation for this off flavor.  
➢ Moldy: Fungi can grow in wort and beer due to inappropriate storage conditions. 
Humidity and wrong sanitation are the most common causes. 
➢ Oxidized: Oxidation is a common problem in the brewing industry. When the 
wort or beer is exposed to temperatures above 27C in the presence of oxygen, 
oxidation of variant molecules occurs. Sherry – like flavors produced and in an 
excess level the product is inevitably decayed.  
➢ Solvent-like: Solvent-like flavors occur often due to a combination of a high 
fermentation temperature and oxidation of the beer. Similar flavors can also 
result from using plastic that is not accepted for food usage. 
➢ Yeasty: Yeast autolysis is the primary source of this off-flavor. “Dead” biomass 
can give rise to unpleasant sulfuric aromas.  
 
 
 
2.3 Biochemistry of the target off-flavors 
As described above, there are many off-flavors generated naturally from the yeast’s 
metabolism.  
In this study, two yeast derived off-flavors are examined: diacetyl and dimethyl sulfide 
(DMS). Both off-flavors are occurring during fermentation step, thus they were selected 
because their control is dependent on yeast’s metabolism. The genes associated with 
the production of those compounds and their mechanism of action are described. 
 
2.3.1 Diacetyl 
Diacetyl (IUPAC systematic name 2,3 butanedione), is a compound that creates a flavor 
characterized as butter or butterscotch. It is a common compound found in beer and 
wine [13].  
Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Saccharomyces pastorianus produce diacetyl as a by-
product of valine synthesis. They arise from the impromptu non-enzymatic oxidative 
decarboxylation of α-acetohydroxy acids that are intermediates in the valine 
biosynthesis pathway [14] [15] 
In this pathway (see Figure 3), the reaction between α-acetolactate and 2,3-dihydro-
isovalerate is occurring during fermentation and yeast growth, where α-acetolactate is 
secreted out of the cell [16] [14][15]. The reasons and mechanisms of this secretion 
may rely on the protection of the cell from carbonyl stress[17]. The α-acetolactate is 
then decarboxylated, resulting in formation of the compounds diacetyl or acetoin and 
CO2. It is observed that non-oxidative decarboxylation into acetoin is favored by 
heating in absence of O2 and with relatively  low wort pH [18].  
 
==PAGE:16==
8 
 
 
Figure 3: Diacetyl pathway as shown in [14] 
Increased diacetyl production has reported to be connected with increased valine 
biosynthesis, which is dependent on the cell’s need for higher levels of valine and other 
amino acids. Also, fermentation conditions that enable rapid yeast growth can give rise 
to increased diacetyl production in case wort’s composition is free of amino acids. 
During fermentation, free diacetyl in wort is relatively low and α-acetolactate’s 
concentration is characterized as the ‘total diacetyl’ present[19][20]. “Total diacetyl” 
represents both the concentrations of free diacetyl and α-acetolactate during analysis of 
the compound. 
Concentration of diacetyl in the final product is dependent in numerous factors such as, 
the precursor compound α-acetolactate, decarboxylation of α-acetohydroxy acid to 
diacetyl and removal of diacetyl through the action of various reductase enzymes. In 
low concentrations, diacetyl is acceptable and even wanted in some beer styles such as 
English Ales [14]. Typical concentrations can vary from 0.002 to 0.1 ppm (with an 
average of 0.05 ppm) [21]. Although, the flavor threshold is reported around 0.1-0.2 
ppm in lager and 0.1-0.4 ppm in ales [22].  
As it described before, diacetyl is directly linked with its precursor compound α-
acetolactate. The acetohydroxy acid synthase (AHA) enzyme, is the one responsible for 
formatting α-acetolactate from pyruvate and its encoded from ILV2 gene [14]  
As a consequence, an active enzyme is converting more α-acetolactate from pyruvate 
and thus, more diacetyl. By inhibiting this enzyme, the formation of diacetyl will be 
inhibited.  
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
In yeast’s metabolism, the genes involved in the formation of the AHA enzyme are 
ILV2 and ILV6. Disrupting those genes will affect the total diacetyl formed in the final 
product [23]. Wang et al.(2008) report that diacetyl concentrations was decreased by 
approximately 60% in beer fermentations with modiﬁed yeast strains of those genes, 
compared with fermentations with the strains without any modification. The disruption 
caused lower expression of AHA synthase and eventually less diacetyl was produced. 
Liu et al. [24] also observed similar reduction in the diacetyl production (66%) and 
better maturation times with disrupted ILV2 gene in an industrial strain of S. 
pastorianus.  
 
2.3.2 Dimethyl Trisulfide  
Onion -like off flavor is highly unpleasant and undesirable in beer products. Dimethyl 
trisulfide is the key compound responsible for this garlic like off-flavor that occurs in 
beer. In some cases, it is a desirable hop-derived character of some ales but in high 
concentrations it creates a negative outcome. DMTS is produced from several sources 
in beer –wort boiling, hop oil and oxidization of methanthiol. It is one of a number of 
sulfur flavors and off notes that can occur in beer [25]. DMTS normally occurs during 
fermentation by yeast and also it can be found as a malt / hop derivative.  
The precursor compound of DMTS, 1,2-dihydroxy-5-(methylsulfinyl) pentan-3-one 
(DMTS-P1) is deriving from yeast metabolism during fermentation [26]. The chemical 
structure of DMTS-P1 promoted the involvement of the methionine salvage pathway, 
because of the similarity between compounds involved in this pathway with the 
chemical structure of DMTS-P1 (see Figure 4). The methionine salvage pathway, also 
called 5′-methylthioadenosine (MTA) cycle, utilizes sulfur from MTA, a by-product of 
the biosynthesis of polyamines [26].  
 
==PAGE:18==
10 
 
 
Figure 4: MTA cycle in Saccharomyces cerevisiae and structure of DMTS-P1 as shown in[26] 
 
In S. cerevisiae, 1,2-dihydroxy-5-(methylthio)-1-penten-3-one (the structure of which 
resembles DMTS-P1) [26] is produced by conversion of MTA under the activity of 
different enzymes encoded by genes MDE1, MEU1 and MRI1. 
Thus, in the hypothesis that DMTS-P1 can be generated from MTA cycle, the potential 
involvement of those genes in the formation of DMTS-P1 can be evaluated by using 
MTA cycle gene mutants, to examine its relationship with the formation of DMTS. In 
the same study, [26] a significant 70% decrease in the DMTS-P1 content and DMTS 
potential concentration in sake was observed by the deletion of yeast MTA cycle genes, 
especially MRI1, MDE1. 
 
 
2.4 Evolution of the CRISPR technology 
The Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR) technology 
is the latest development in the field of genomic modification and owes its impressive 
evolution to a research process that took more than a decade to reveal the biological 
function of those repetitive elements, today known as CRISPRs. These small repetitive 
sequences are mainly found in classes of bacteria and archaea.  
CRISPR were officially first mentioned in 1987, due to studies in the Iap enzyme 
(alkaline phosphatase isozyme) in Escherichia Coli. A strange sequence of 29 repeated 
nucleotides downstream of the Iap gene was observed [27]. Between those repetitions 
there were 5 non-repetitive sequences of 32 nucleotides. During the following decade, 
while more microbial genomes were sequencing, a significant number of similar 
==PAGE:19==
11 
 
repetitions was identified [28]. The CRISPR acronym was created in 2002, in order to 
unite all the microbe genome areas that are comprised of repetitive sequences, including 
intervals of non-repetitive sequences, under the same term [29]. At the same time, a 
cluster of particularly preserved CRISPR – associated genes (Cas genes) was 
recognized. The categorization of the CRISPR systems was based on those Cas genes 
and three main systems emerged [30] [31]. Types I and III contain a significant number 
of Cas proteins for generating the double-strand break of the targeted genomic type, in 
relation to type II, which contains a smaller amount of Cas proteins [32]. 
A very important point in the evolution of the CRISPR technology was during 2005 
(see Figure 5), when a theory suggesting that the intervening sequences (spacers) of 
repeated CRISPR parts have a different origin, related to phage genome, was stated 
[33] [34]. Moreover, it was also observed that viruses, possessing parts of their 
sequences, resembling to archaea CRISPR spacers, were not able to infect the archaea 
[33].Today, it is know that the CRISPR areas are characterized by a series of repetitions 
of 20-50 bases, in between which, unique sequences of similar size interfere (spacers). 
Those spacer areas arise from the insertion of foreign gene elements (protospacers). 
 
Figure 5: Key Studies Characterizing and Engineering CRISPR Systems as shown in [35] 
Furthermore, the theory that CRISPR systems act as defense mechanisms was 
confirmed, attributing them the ability to remember bacteriophage invaders [36]. 
Studies  revealed the natural role of the type II CRISPR as a defense mechanism, during 
which, the interfering CRISPR spacer parts regulate the targeting specialization, while 
the Cas enzymes prevent the infection from phages [37]. Studies in the type I CRISPR 
of E. coli showed that the CRISPR strands are transcribed into small crRNAS, which 
include the interfering spacer sequences, that will guide the action of Cas nuclease [32]. 
Also, identification of the role of a motif, always present in the targeted sequence of 
the virus genome, but never on the bacterium genome, was revealed. This motif, known 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
as PAM (Protospacer Adjacent Motif), seemed to ensure the action of Cas nuclease 
only in the host DNA and not in the bacterial one [37]. 
Exploiting of the CRISPR mechanism, initial efforts were made to improve resistance 
in phages of the bacteria used in dairy industry and the phylogenetic classification of 
bacterial strains [37] [38]. The idea of utilizing this mechanism for purposes of genomic 
alteration, through the action of an RNA-regulated DNA nuclease, was quickly 
identified. The nuclease that would perform the process of DNA cleavage, was named 
Cas9 [39]. The CRISPR mechanism became more and more clear, when trans-
activating crRNA (tractRNA) was discovered with the ability to hybridize with crRNA, 
resulting in production of mature crRNA [40]. Therefore, it was possible to construct 
an artificial single guide RNA (sgRNA), which combines and simulates the properties 
of natural crRNA and tracrRNA in vitro  [41]. In eukaryotic cells, the system could be 
used with the purpose of breaking the DNA double strand, process known as double 
strand break (DSB). Those DSBs would in turn be repaired by cell mechanisms in two 
ways: either through a non-homologous end-joining (NHEJ) or through a homology 
directed repair (HDR).  
In 2013, two studies were published, presenting the successful design of type II 
CRISPR, with the purpose of performing genomic alteration in mammalian cells. The 
first one mentions the design of CRISPR from the Streptococcus thermophiles species 
[35], while the second one from the Streptococcus pyrogenes species [35] [42].  
 
2.4.1 Genomic alteration using the CRISPR-Cas9 technology in eukaryotic cells 
Nowadays, the CRISPR-Cas9 type II system has been widely used for the efficient 
genomic alteration in various organisms and cell types, such as human cells, bacteria, 
yeast, mice, rats, insects, grass plants, pigs and monkeys. In most cases, it is used for 
targeting a sequence, an artificial sgRNA molecule, which combines and stimulates the 
properties of natural crRNA and is consisted of approximately 20 nucleotides, 
complementary to the targeted sequence. A necessary requirement for the proper 
function of the system is the presence of the PAM strand downstream of the targeted 
strand [35] [42][41] and the system is used for various genomic alterations. 
 
2.4.2 Off-target alterations of the CRISPR technology 
Bearing in mind that genome processing leads to permanent effects later, it is easy to 
assume that off-target effects are highly important to the system. The identification of 
the target by Cas9 takes place because of complementarity of the RNA-guide to the 
DNA-target. Studies in similar direction suggested that Cas9 allows mismatches in the 
guiding strand, depending on the position, the number and the allocation of those 
mismatches [35] [43]. Moreover, the bases that are located further away from the PAM 
site, appear being less important for the specialty of the RNA-guide. The concentration 
of Cas9 appears to be consisting of another important factor regarding the mutagenesis 
caused by off-target effects. As a matter of fact, a larger concentration of Cas9 leads to 
more mismatches between the guiding and the targeted sequences [44]. 
 
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
2.4.3 Advantages of the CRISPR technology 
The characteristics of the CRIPSR technology are making it an almost ideal tool for 
genetic alterations, superior to the pre-existing and established models. In particular, 
one of its advantages is the facilitated design of targeting, based on ribonucleic 
recognition and not the DNA-protein. gRNAs are designed quite easily, on a small cost 
and can target genomic areas followed by PAM site. In addition, it is also worth 
mentioning the effectiveness of the method. The CRISPR system is based on the simple 
process of transferring RNA that codes gRNAs and the Cas9 protein, leading to an 
increased efficiency. Multiple gRNAs provide also the possibility of multiple and 
simultaneous targeting of various genes. One such case was the simultaneous 
transferring of multiple plasmids that were bearing gRNA, leading to the multiple 
alterations of various genetic targets in the S. cerevisiae [6]. 
It can be safely assumed that the ongoing optimizations of this technology are bound to 
add more advantages to its future applications.

➢ Precise Modifications Using Homology Directed Repair (HDR) 
Homology directed repair (HDR) can be used to produce specific nucleotide changes.  
To utilize HDR for genome editing, a DNA repair template should be introduced to the 
cleavage site containing the desired sequence. This repair template must contain the 
chosen alteration as well as an homologous sequence upstream and downstream of the 
site. The length should be accordingly to the change being introduced, with larger 
insertions requiring longer homology arms.

3.2 USER Cloning 
To create target-specific CRISPR-Cas vectors, a restriction digest and ligation-based 
cloning method moderated by E. coli is used.  
USER (Uracil-Specific Excision Reagent) cloning technique is an efficient and rapid 
method for cloning of PCR products, as described in [45] (commercially available 
from New England Biolabs). 
==PAGE:25==
17 
 
DNA fragments can be amplified with U-(uracil) containing primers, with a tail added 
to the target sequence. These fragments are then treated with uracil DNA glycosylase 
(UDS), removing uracil residues incorporated at the end of the amplified fragment and 
helping dissociation of the single stranded fragments lying upstream the cleavage site 
[46]. This technique results in creation of products containing overhangs that can be 
annealed to a linear vector with complementary overhangs. Also, the treated PCR 
product and vector are able to form a stable circular product that can be transformed 
into E. coli  [45]. 
For constructing appropriate vectors, a cassette needs to be inserted in the cloning site. 
This cassette contains a restriction site and two nicking sites in a specific arrangement. 
After enzyme digestion, small DNA fragments separate from the vector and the 
overhangs rise (see example illustration in figure 10).  
 
Figure 10: Illustration of USER cloning with PacI/Nt.BdvCl digest as shown in [47] 
 
The advantages of the USER cloning method, among others, are [47]:  
• Primers for fragments amplification require just 10 bp tail added to the target 
sequence 
• Introduction of a small and simple cassette is adequate for the vector design 
• High efficiency in generating recombinant DNA molecules from the 
complementary overhangs. 
• Simple and easy to perform method.

3.2.1 USER Cloning approach in this study 
Each guide RNA expression vector is constructed to specifically Cas9 target nuclease 
sequence of interest.  
In this study, each gRNA is expressed under the snoRNA SNR52 promoter and CYC1 
terminator.  
All the six different vectors that were generated in this study, were constructed with the

the linearized vector. 
The plasmid pTS_Cfb68 was used as a template to generate the bio-bricks for the gRNA 
cassette, which was then introduced to the 2micron plasmid pDIV_025 (see figure 11).

be replaced with the corresponding plasmid with the gRNA cassette for the newly

carrying Cas9 expression (which is generating resistance to G418) and discard the 
plasmid with the gRNA cassette (which is generating resistance to NTC). Yeast cells

suspension are platted in YPD-G418 media plates and incubated at 30 °C for 2 days. 
Then, with the usage of the specific sterile velvet cloth, the formatted colonies were 
pressed down lightly on the surface of the plate, and the velvet picked up cells wherever 
there was a colony. With careful handing the velvet cloth with picked up colonies was 
pressed down in different media plates of YPD-G418 and YPD-NTC, imprinting the 
relative positions of the colonies from the original plate [52]. All plates incubated at 30 
°C for 2-3 days. Colonies that appear in YPG-G418 plates but not in the exact same 
position of YPD-NTC plates, were selected for further transformation with another 
plasmid. Figure 13 shows an illustration of the technique.

4.6 Strain screening 
 
▪ Pre-cultures  
Pre-cultures for all cultivation and fermentation experiments were prepared by 
inoculation of a single colony in 50 ml YPD into a 500 ml shake flask. Shake flasks 
were incubated overnight at 30 °C at 200 rpm.  
▪ Yeast strain cultivation 
The mutant yeast strains containing one or two gene knock-outs, had to get tested for 
cell growth and metabolites production. To perform that, a small cultivation system that 
could mimic the conditions in fermentation bioreactors was used. BioLector machine 
(https://www.m2p-labs.com/bioreactors/products/biolector/#!/2 ) is microbioreactor 
for high-throughput fermentations with an online system for monitoring of biomass, 
pH, DO, and fluorescences. The instrument was used for running cultivation of all 
strains used in this study. A flat bottom 48-well flower plate contained 1.3 ml of media 
and 200μl of cell inoculum with OD600 0,1. The selected conditions for the cultivation 
process included: set temperatute 20 °C, set humidity 95 rH, set O2 21 %, set CO2 0%, 
set shake frequency 500 rpm. Also, strains were tested in two different media, YPD and 
brewing Pilsner wort. Two set of experiments took place with the same conditions for 
95h and 72h respectively.  
 
==PAGE:37==
29 
 
▪ Beer fermentation 
To get a better understanding of each strains performance, fermentation experiments in 
1L bioreactor system (Biostat Qplus bioreactors, Sartorius Stedim Biotech) were 
conducted. Bioreactors were equipped with baffles, perforated stainless-steel ring 
sparger, two blade impellers, pH and DO sensors. The selected conditions for those 
experiments tried to mimic the beer brewing fermentation conditions as the examined 
mutant strains are beer brewing strains. For this reason, 600ml of Pilsner wort was used 
and 40ml of cell inoculum with OD600 0,4 was added. 1ml of sterile organic antifoam 
reagent was added to prevent excess foaming. Condition included: stable set 
temperature at 20°C and 30 rpm stirring frequency. Initial pH was 6±0.5 in all batch 
fermentations. pH values and CO2 off-gas emissions were monitored through-out the 
fermentation period. No further O2 was supplied in the reactor, as beer fermentation is 
partially anaerobic. Experiments run for 72 hours. All sensors were calibrated 
accordingly to manufacturer’s standard procedures. The batch cultivations were 
monitored and controlled by a Control Tower (Biostat Qplus Bioreactor System, 
Sartorius Stedim Biotech). 
Samples were taken by discarding 5 ml waste and afterwards withdrawing 5 ml sample 
that used for HPLC analysis and diacetyl determination. Filtered samples were used for 
HPLC analysis for determination of maltose, glucose, ethanol, glycerol, pyruvate, 
succinate and acetate. 1.5 ml filtrated and diluted samples used in HPLC. The system 
is equipped with an ion exclusion column (9 μm particle size, Model Aminex HPX-
87H, Bio-Rad,) with a refractive index detector (Model 1100-1200 Series HPLC 
System, Agilent Technologies). Components were separated by elution at flow rate 0.6 
ml/min at 60 °C with 5mM H2SO4 solution as mobile phase. 
 
4.7 Determination of compounds responsible for off-flavors 
 
Diacetyl production was measured colorimetrically as described in [53]. A 680 μl 
aliquot of sample and 20 μl 0.5% 3,3-diaminobenzidine tetrahydrochloride were 
incubated in the dark for 1 min at room temperature. 200 μl 3M H2SO4 and 100μl H2O 
were added and the tubes were incubated again in the dark for 10 min at room 
temperature before the absorption was measured at 366 nm.

responsible for unwanted flavors in beer. Focus was given on elimination of diacetyl

responsible for production of DMTS were also examined and eliminated with the same 
techniques as a second target off-flavor. Steps from constructing the new plasmids, 
transforming S. cerevisiae strains, validating the mutations and performing 
fermentations were followed, results of which are presented in this chapter.

➢ Replica plating 
The technique was used for negative screening of the validated strains that 
contained both plasmids carrying Cas9 expression (G418 resistance) and gRNA 
expression (NTC resistance). When a plasmid was no longer desired into a cell e.g. 
pKNO_il2, colonies growing in selective media containing NTC, were rejected. 
Plasmid loss will result in cells that do not grow in the selective antibiotic. When 
screening between two antibiotics (G418 and NTC) the cells will not be resistant to 
NTC, but they will still be resistant to G418. Figure 25 illustrates an example.

➢ Colorimetric assay 
Samples from beer fermentations treated for colorimetric determination of diacetyl as 
described in [53]. 
Unfortunately, this method of determination proved inefficient for detection of diacetyl 
as it cannot detect low amounts of diacetyl. An average concentration of diacetyl in 
beer is around 0.05ppm (which is 0.00058 mM). Method’s detection limit was tested to 
0.01mM and therefore lower concentrations were unable to detect. An optimization of 
the method was tested, with initiation of an amount of diacetyl (0.01Mm) to all 
measured samples and trial to detect the differences between them. Again, the method 
was not able to detect the trace amounts that were expected.

6. Discussion and future perspectives 
The aim of the present study was to create S. cerevisiae strains that will not produce an 
undesired off-flavor during beer fermentation. Strains that produced decreased amounts 
of diacetyl were created with the usage of CRISPR/Cas9, a highly promising system 
for simple, flexible and precise genome editing. Plasmids were successfully constructed 
and used for genetic transformation of the target strains. Different variations of gene 
knock-outs were created (strains with single and double gene knock-outs) to determine 
which lead to smaller amounts of diacetyl. The technique was also applied in industrial 
brewing strains to emphasize that CRISPR/Cas 9 system can generate modifications 
even in the demanding brewing candidates because it is considered much easier to 
perform genetic modification in haploid laboratory strains than genetically complex 
industrial strains, which are typically polyploid, aneuploid, and even alloploid 
[56][57][3] 
In particular, the laboratory strain CEN.PK 113-7D was used as a reference haploid 
strain for the genome editing to reflect the efficiency of the CRISPR/Cas9 technique. 
CLIB382 is also a diploid strain used extensively in lab scale experiments but it is a 
Irish’s brewers isolate strain [48]. Strains CBS1171 and W210 are industrial brewing 
top fermenting polyploid strains. 12 Mutant transformants were constructed from the 4 
different parental strains described above with single and double variations of ILV2 and 
ILV6 knock-outs (see also Table 1). Also, GSH1 gene is involved in decreased 
production of diacetyl but it is not a primary involved gene in the pathway [14]. It was 
intended to create transformants with disrupted GSH1 to confirm the necessity of this 
gene, but unfortunately only CLIB382 strain managed to get transformed in a GSH1 
knock-out mutant. It is speculated that design of the sgRNA was the major reason for 
transformation failure. It is concluded, that the CRISPR/Cas9 system is very efficient 
and simple to perform for knock-outs but it is vital to check all the parameters of 
system’s performance carefully before applying it.  
Details of the functioning mechanism of the CRISPR/Cas9 system can be found in 
“Introduction”. Generation of a double strand break and its repair by homologous 
recombination resulted in a loss of two base pairs and created a frame-shift mutation. 
This made an alteration to the protein encoded from each target gene, leading to 
dysfunctionality. This applied to all new yeasts strains that were created with the 
CRISPR/Cas9 system. It is important to consider that the change of a few bp at such a 
site modification, would constitute a genome-edited microorganism (GenEd). If a large 
sequence derived from another species (or the same species) is integrated at site, it 
would be considered a genetically modified organism (GMO) [58]. This should be a 
proposal of rethinking that beer products from these mutant yeasts should or should not 
be classified as a GM beverage. The US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) does 
not require genetically engineered organisms (GEOs) to be identified because it has 
determined that genetically engineered foods would require labeling only if 
modifications altered food composition significantly, changed the nutritional value, or 
introduced an allergen [59]. Furthermore, CRISPR/Cas9 system is approved for usage 
by the US Department of Agriculture as it consists a technique of creating new crops, 
classifies as New Breeding Techniques (NBTs) developed the last years (more 
information on https://www.usda.gov/ ). So far, crops generated with NBTs are not 
==PAGE:54==
46 
 
under the classical GMO legislation and this may possibly apply for more food 
products. In addition, conduction of a risk assessment between conventional beer and 
beer produced from GenEd yeast will reveal any potential hazards to human health and 
the environment.  
Furthermore, diacetyl reduction is a major issue for brewers. Techniques for controlling 
diacetyl production are the usage of natural cross-breeding yeast hybrids, increased 
conditioning periods, addition of α‐acetolactate decarboxylase enzyme at the beginning 
of the fermentation step, valine supplementation (or optimizing free amino acids 
content) in wort, increasing maturation temperature and using better quality and type 
of malt [14]. All those techniques require time, devotion and money to brewers and can 
be avoided by using a GenEd yeast strain with disrupting ILV2 or ILV6 gene expression.  
Additionally, strain cultivations and beer fermentations were conducted. Fermentation 
experiments revealed that all strains are functional, and they can produce beer under 
monitored conditions according to the desired type of beer (in this study, ales). 
Comparing the parental strains and the mutant transformants, in all 4 different S. 
cerevisiae strains it is observed that: there is no significant difference between parental 
and ILV6 disputant strain. Growth rates, growth curves and yield coefficients are similar 
as shown in chapter 5. This signifies that knock-out of gene ILV6 does not essentially 
affect the metabolism of the yeast. However, a significant difference was perceived 
between parental strains and strains with disrupt ILV2 and ILV6 genes simultaneously. 
The modified double knock-outs strains had significantly lower growth rates and 
biomass production but higher yield in ethanol production. Also, a higher yield in 
production of acetate was observed and this may result in cell’s stress response. Yields 
in the rest of measured metabolites (glycerol, pyruvate) were different between the 4 
examined “strain families” and are not conclusive. Also, modified strains with ILV2 
gene knock-out performed differently between industrial and laboratory strains and 
further studies on their metabolic profiles should be done. In total, it was expected that 
the mutant strains will perform differently from their parental strains, as the genes 
involved are part of the significant valine pathway. But, the strains that were produced 
are still able to perform as brewing candidates.  
It is vital to say, that CO2 yield coefficient was not calculated as there are no recorded 
data of CO2 because fermentations were simulated as real-time beer fermentations. In 
order to be able to have a better understanding and physiological characterization of all 
strains’ performance, batch cultivations in synthetic yeast growth media containing 
only glycose as carbon source and monitoring the O2 and CO2 off-gas emissions would 
enable us to calculate precisely all yield coefficients.  
Finally, beer samples were measured colorimetrically to detect diacetyl concentrations. 
The assay for diacetyl determination did not result in any quantification or indication 
of the expected concentration of diacetyl in the different final beer products that were 
produced. By the time of completion of this study, there is no determination of the target 
compound, but further work is under process. A method for the direct determination of 
diacetyl in beer head space by gas liquid chromatography using an electron capture 
detector will be used as described in [60] to make the final estimation and validation 
that all the produced mutant strains have decreased diacetyl production.

Moreover, CRISPR/Cas9 system was also applied for creating modified yeast strains 
that will not produce DMTS during beer fermentation. However, knock-outs on target 
genes associated with DMTS production were not completed due time-limitation of this 
project and, those few mutant strains (CLIB382 mde1∆, CEN.PK 113-7D meu1∆, 
CBS1171 mde1∆, W210 mri1∆) did not proceed on fermentation experiments. 
Plasmids and gRNA regions were designed the same way as in diacetyl modification 
but DMTS elimination experiments were a supplementary work based on the principals 
of the same genome-editing technique. Future knock-out attempts should again revise 
the design of gRNAs and plasmids and, also analyze colonies coming from a single cell 
at a time to overcome possible troubles and drawbacks. Future work on DMTS knock-
outs should include constructs of combinations of double and triple gene knock-outs 
(MEU1, MRI1, MDE1) in one strain and a conclusive study on the beer fermentation 
performance of those transformants.

7. Conclusion 
In this study, it was attempted and succeeded on constructing different laboratory and 
industrial S. cerevisiae strains with disruptive genes for production of diacetyl and/or 
DMTS. 17 new yeast strains were created with the novel technique of CRISPR/Cas9 
and validated by sequencing to confirm the desired genetic alteration. CRISPR/Cas9 
was proved an efficient tool for successful genome manipulation in laboratory and 
industrial strains. New plasmids were constructed expressing different gRNAs for site-
specific genome editing and those plasmids can be used in future studies for using the 
CRISPR/Cas9 system in more industrial strains. This study is a striking example of 
CRISPR/Cas system application to address challenges related to food industry and 
whether these mutants should be regulated under the EU GMO legislation. Abundant 
studies in food bacteria and crops, demonstrate that CRISPR/Cas systems could be used 
to improve nutrient content in foods and this study demonstrates a yeast strain that 
contributes in the production of a beverage of high quality.  
Moreover, all strains were successfully tested on their ability to transform wort sugars 
into beer and all of them proved efficient candidates for beer fermentation highlighting 
that the genetic manipulation did not result in any strain being dysfunctional. In mutant 
strains for decreased production of diacetyl, the parent strain was compared with an 
ILV2 mutant, an ILV6 mutant and an ILV6 and ILV2 double mutant strain. Differences 
in growth rates revealed that double knock-outs mutants tend to reproduce slower but 
produce more ethanol. There were no significant conclusive differences between parent 
strains and single knock-outs mutants.  
Furthermore, samples from the final beer products were analyzed with colorimetric 
assay in order to determine the significant decrease in diacetyl production between 
parent and mutant strains. The detection limit of this method was unfortunately far 
above the typical average concentration of diacetyl in beer, and the colorimetric assay 
did not result in quantifying the produced beer samples. Head space GC-MS analysis 
will determine and quantify diacetyl concentrations and therefore will be able to 
confirm that the newly constructed S. cerevisiae strains are promising candidates for 
production of beer with less diacetyl.
