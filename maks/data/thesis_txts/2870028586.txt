Abstract 
Bifidobacterium species, dominating the gut of breastfed infants, have been shown to produce 
aromatic amino acid metabolites that are involved in early life immune regulation. However, exactly 
how the production of these metabolites is regulated in Bifidobacterium species remains unknown. 
To investigate how environmental factors, such as substrate availability and pH, affect aromatic 
amino acid metabolism, and especially their conversion into aromatic lactic acids, in vitro cultivations 
with Bifidobacterium longum subsp. infantis were established. Liquid chromatography mass 
spectrometry was conducted to identify and quantify aromatic amino acid metabolites. Finally, the 
relative expression of the aldh gene, involved in the production of aromatic lactic acids, was 
determined using RT-qPCR. It was found that the exogenous concentration of aromatic amino acids 
promoted aromatic lactic acid production and that a proportional relationship existed between the 
concentrations of tryptophan and indole-3-lactic acid, tyrosine and 4-hydroxyphenyllactic acid, and 
phenylalanine and phenyllactic acid. Chemostat cultivations at physiological relevant pH levels 
showed that an acidic pH affected both growth and aromatic lactic acid production by Bifidobacterium 
longum subsp. infantis. Overall, aromatic lactic acid concentrations correlated with the bacterial 
abundance in the cultures at all tested pH levels. However, the results also suggested that pH 
contributed to a shift in aromatic lactic acid production, with the production of phenyllactic acid being 
notably enhanced at acidic pH levels. In conclusion, this study shows that aromatic amino acid 
availability and environmental pH affect aromatic amino acid metabolism by Bifidobacterium longum 
subsp. infantis.

Introduction 
The human gastrointestinal (GI) tract is home to trillions of microorganisms, collectively known as 
the gut microbiota. The gut microbiota plays a crucial role in maintaining physiological homeostasis, 
influencing various aspects of host health. The gut microbiota is highly dynamic and evolves 
throughout the lifespan, with the most significant changes occurring in the first three years (Laursen, 
Bahl, et al., 2021; Renz & Skevaki, 2021a). During early life, the gut microbiota is considered critical 
to immunological development. Dysbiosis of the infant gut microbiota is common in modern societies 
and a likely contributing factor to increased incidences of immune-mediated disorders such as asthma, 
allergy and type 1 diabetes (Arrieta et al., 2015; Renz & Skevaki, 2021b; Vatanen et al., 2016). 
Therefore, there is a great interest in identifying microbial factors that can support healthy immune 
system development during infancy (Renz & Skevaki, 2021b). 
Breastfeeding is an important factor that determines the formation of the gut microbiota during early 
life. Breastmilk contains not only nutritious compounds that are absorbed by the intestinal epithelium, 
such as lactose, but also non-digestible carbohydrates. These carbohydrates, classified as human milk 
oligosaccharides (HMOs), are the third-most abundant solid component in breastmilk and are likely 
to have serval functions (Bode, 2012). In the infant gut, HMOs can mimic receptor sites for 
pathogenic microbes and have direct effects on intestinal epithelial cells (Bode, 2012). Furthermore, 
HMOs act as prebiotics, promoting the growth of beneficial commensal bacteria, particularly 
Bifidobacterium species that are equipped with the cellular machinery to breakdown and utilize these 
complex substrates as an energy source (LoCascio et al., 2007). 
The genus Bifidobacterium constitutes Gram-positive, non-motile, non-spore forming, anaerobic, 
saccharolytic microorganisms with a Y-shaped or ‘bifid’ morphology (Ventura et al., 2007). In early 
life, bifidobacteria are vertically transmitted from the mother and are predominant in the gut of 
healthy, breastfed infants (Underwood et al., 2015). With age, the relative abundance of bifidobacteria 
decline, as the host transitions from infancy to adolescence and adulthood. The bifidobacterial 
community then stabilizes and persists until old age, suggesting its relevant role during the entire 
lifespan (Alessandri et al., 2021). At the species level, age also influences the composition of 
bifidobacterial populations. In infancy, breastmilk-associated species such as Bifidobacterium breve, 
B. longum, and B. bifidum are commonly found in the gut. In adulthood, however, the dominant

species shift to B. adolescentis, B. longum, and B. pseudocatenulatum, reflecting changes in diet and 
gut environment over time (Ventura et al., 2007). 
The genus Bifidobacterium has been associated with a range of beneficial health effects, including a 
critical impact on the immune system (Picard et al., 2005). In the infant gut, lack of breastmilk-
promoted Bifidobacterium species has been associated with systemic inflammation and immune 
dysregulation early in life (Henrick et al., 2021a). In addition their presence may decrease the 
susceptibility to infectious diseases (Fukuda et al., 2011).

One way bifidobacteria exert their beneficial effects is through the production of metabolites. Among 
these, aromatic lactic acids (ALAs) produced by breastmilk-associated Bifidobacterium species, have 
been identified as key metabolites involved in early life immune regulation (Henrick et al., 2021a; 
Laursen, Sakanaka, et al., 2021) (Figure 2). 
ALAs are derived from the three aromatic amino acids (AAAs), tryptophan, tyrosine and 
phenylalanine, which are converted to indole-3-lactic acid (ILA), phenyllactic acid (PLA), and 4-
hydroxyphenyllactic acid (OH-PLA), respectively. Transamination is the first step in this metabolic 
pathway, where aromatic aminotransferase (AraT) catalyses the transfer of AAA ammonium groups

to a keto acid acceptor, producing aromatic pyruvic acids. These aromatic pyruvic acids are then 
converted into ALAs through the action of and an aromatic lactate dehydrogenase (ALDH) (Figure 
1) (Laursen, Sakanaka, et al., 2021). 
Mounting evidence indicate the beneficial effects of ALAs. ILA is particularly recognized for its 
ability to activate the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR). AhR is a transcription factor widely expressed 
by cells in the immune system (Roager & Licht, 2018). Serval benefits of ILA-induced AhR activation 
have been described, including enhanced epithelial barrier function, protections against pathogens 
and regulation of host metabolism (Natividad et al., 2018; Zelante et al., 2013). In contrast, PLA and 
OH-PLA are not associated with AhR activation (Sakurai et al., 2021).

ILA and PLA are both potent ligands for the hydroxycarboxylic acid receptor 3 (HCA3), which is 
involved in regulation of immune functions and energy homeostasis (Laursen, Sakanaka, et al., 2021; 
Peters et al., 2019; Sakurai et al., 2021). Also related to energy homeostasis, mouse studies have

shown that PLA protects against metabolic dysfunction caused by early-life exposure to antibiotics 
and a high-fat died. This effect is explained by PLAs ability to regulate peroxisome proliferator-
activated receptor γ (PPAR-γ), a regulator of lipid metabolism, on small intestinal epithelial cells 
(Shelton et al., 2023). At last, ILA, PLA and OH-PLA all exhibits antimicrobial activity (Guimarães 
et al., 2018; Lavermicocca et al., 2003; Ning et al., 2017), thus providing a growth advantage of 
bifidobacteria and host protection, by preventing colonization of pathogens. 
In the GI tract, the gut microbiota is influenced by various abiotic (environmental) factors, including 
substrate availability, antimicrobial compound concentrations, transit time, and pH that collectively 
determine the microbial composition, diversity, and metabolic activity (Lewis & Heaton, 1997; Roager 
et al., 2016). The impact of abiotic factors on the gut microbiota in infants remains largely unknown.  
In the context of AAA metabolism, research on lactic acid bacteria (LAB) and Clostridiales species 
has demonstrated a direct relationship between amino acid availability and ALA production. (Mu et 
al., 2012) and (Valerio et al., 2004) showed that PLA and OH-PLA production could be increased 
during P. acidilactici and L. plantarum fermentation when the fermentation broth was supplemented 
with phenylalanine and tyrosine. Furthermore, a dose dependent relationship between accumulation 
of ILA in the culture supernatant was observed for C. sporogenes and P. anaerobius, upon tryptophan 
supplementation in the medium (Sinha et al., 2024). These effects have yet not been described in 
Bifidobacteirum species, however we hypothesize that similar relationships between AAA and ALA 
exist. 
pH is another abiotic factor, potentially influencing ALA production in the gut. While little is known 
about pH changes in the infant GI tract, the presence of breastmilk-promoted Bifidobacterium species 
is generally linked to lower faecal pH (Laursen, Bahl, et al., 2021). This decrease in pH is primarily 
due to the degradation of HMOs into acidic end products, such as lactate and acetate (Underwood et 
al., 2015). In a cohort of Danish infants, a correlation between high ALA concentration and low pH 
is faeces has been established (unpublished data). Furthermore, an upregulation of genes involved in 
amino acid metabolism in response to acid stress has been reported for B. longum (Wei et al., 2019). 
It can therefore by hypothesised that a low intestinal pH could be contributing to higher metabolism 
of AAA into ALAs in the infant gut. 
Bifidobacterium longum subsp. infantis (B. infantis) is one of the subspecies with the strongest ability 
to metabolize HMOs, and thus one of the predominant species in the intestinal microbiome of 
breastfed infants (Underwood et al., 2015). Furthermore, abundance of this species has been

amino acid metabolism in the infant gut. 
Materials and Methods 
Bacterial strains and culture conditions 
In this study, Bifidobacterium longum subsp. infantis DSM2008 (B. infantis) obtained from DSM 
(Deutsche Sammlung von Mikroorganism, Germany) was used. The strain was stored in glycerol 
stocks at -80 oC until use. Glycerol stocks were streaked on deMan–Rogosa–Sharpe agar (SSI 
Diagnostica, Cat. No.: 50123) supplemented with 0.05% (w/v) L-cysteine hydrochloride (Sigma, Cat. 
No.: C7477) (MRS-cys agar plates) and incubated for 48 h at 37 oC in an anaerobic chamber (Don 
Whitley Scientific Limited) containing 10% H2, 10% CO2 and 80% N2 gases. For overnight cultures, 
single colonies were resuspended in MRS-cys broth (De MAN et al., 1960), and incubated for 20-
24 h at 37°C in the anaerobic chamber. 
The MRS-cys broth contained: 10 g/L Peptone from casein (Merck, Cat. No.: 1.07213.1000); 8.0 g/L 
Meat extract (Oxoid, Cat. No.: LP0029); 4.0 g Yeast extract (Oxoid, Cat. No.: LP0021); 2.0 g/L Di-
potassium hydrogen phosphate (Merck, Cat. No.: 1.05104.1000); 5.0 g/L Sodium acetate (Sigma, 
Cat. No.: S8750), 2.0 g/L Tri-ammonium citrate (Sigma, Cat. No.: A1332); 0.2 g Magnesium sulfate 
heptahydrate (Merck, Cat. No.: 1.05886.0500); 0.05 g/L Manganous sulfate monohydrate (Sigma, 
Cat. No.: M7634) supplemented with 0.05% (w/v) L-cysteine hydrochloride (Sigma, Cat. No.: 
C7477) and 2% (w/v) D-glucose (Merck, Cat. No.: G8270). 
In vitro fermentation with aromatic amino acid supplementation 
To test how ALA production by B. infantis is affected by AAA availability, in vitro fermentations with 
different initial concentrations of AAA were set up. Singles colonies of B. infantis were inoculated 
into MRS-cys broth supplemented with 0.025, 0.05, 0.1 and 0.2% (w/v) L-tryptophan (Sigma, Cat. 
No.: T0254), L-phenylalanine (Sigma, Cat. No.: P2126) or L-tyrosine disodium salt dihydrate 
(Merck, Cat. No.: 1.02413.0100) and incubated in the anaerobic chamber at 37°C. Each AAA 
supplemented culture was evaluated in biological triplicates and cultures in MRS-cys broth without 
supplementation served as control. After 48 h of growth, the optical density at 600 nm (OD600) was

Chemostat cultivations 
To test the influence of pH on aromatic lactic acid production by B. infantis, chemostat cultures were 
set up. Since chemostat fermentations of B. infantis has not been described, a bioreactor setup was 
designed following methods established for culturing other Bifidobacterium species in chemostats 
(Amaretti et al., 2006; Camargo et al., 2023; Manuela et al., 2019). The protocol was developed in 
three stages where initial bioreactor parameters were tested, followed by determining a suitable 
dilution rate and at last testing different pH conditions (See flow chart in Supplementary Figure 1). 
 
 
Figure 3: Final parameters for the chemostat culture setup. The culture vessel was maintained at a temperature of 37°C, 
with three different pH conditions tested (4.5, 5.5, and 6.5). Stirring was set to 100 rpm, and the gas inflow consisted of 
20% CO₂ and 80% N₂ at a flow rate of 50 mL/min, with 10% of the gas released as exhaust. To achieve a steady state of 
cell growth, a dilution rate (D) of 0.06 h⁻¹ was applied and the volume was kept constant by continuously pumping excess 
culture out of the system.

Identification and quantification of aromatic lactic acids by LC-MS 
For quantification of aromatic lactic acids (and other AAA metabolites), liquid chromatography mass 
spectrometry (LC-MS) was performed. 
For the LC-MS, isotopic-labeled L-tryptophan (indole-d5, 98%) (Cambridge Isotope Laboratories) 
was used as internal standard.  To quantify the AAA metabolites, different concentrations of analytes 
(0 μg ml−1, 0.8 μg ml−1, 2 μg ml−1 and 4 μg ml−1) were prepared by mixing analyte mix (see 
Supplementary Table 1), internal standard and sterile water. Furthermore, quality control (QC) 
standards were prepared and processed in the same way as samples to normalize against any loss or 
gain of the analytes during data processing. The QC standards were prepared in triplicates of 80 μL 
by mixing growth media 1:1 with analyte mix (each analyte at 40 μg/mL). 
To prepare samples for LC-MS, culture supernatants were thawed at 4 °C and then centrifuged at 
16,000g at 4 °C for 10 min. 80 µl supernatant was then transferred to a new tube. To each sample and 
QC standard, 20 µl internal standard (40 µg ml−1) and 300 µl acetonitrile (Sigma, Cat. No.: 
1003363276) was added. The samples were vortexed for 10 seconds and placed at −20 °C for 10 min 
to precipitate proteins. Afterwards, the samples were centrifuged at 16,000g, at 4 °C for 10 min. 50 µl 
of supernatant of each sample followed by 50 µl of sterile water was transferred to a liquid 
chromatography vial (resulting in a 1:10 dilution of the sample and an internal standard concentration 
of 1 µg ml−1).

The isolated RNA samples were reverse transcribed into cDNA using the reverse transcription

Statistics 
All statistical analyses were carried out in GraphPad Prism software (v. 10.4.1). Normal distributions 
were evaluated by the Shapiro–Wilk test. For the AAA availability test, statistical significance 
between groups was determined by one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey's 
multiple comparisons test. For the chemostat experiments, cell densities were compared using 
repeated measures two-way ANOVA followed by Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons 
test. Metabolite differences in the chemostat experiments were compared using two-way ANOVA. 
Relative aldh expression between groups were compared using nested one-way ANOVA followed by 
Tukey's multiple comparisons test. All correlations were Pearson correlations. For all statistical 
analysis a p-value < 0.05 was considered as significant.

Bioreactor cultivation of B. infantis require CO2 for growth 
Since ALA production has been linked to a decrease in faecal pH in breastfed infants (unpublished 
data), bioreactor chemostat cultures were established to investigate this relationship. The chemostat 
cultivation system was selected due to its ability to maintain steady-state microbial growth by 
continuously supplying fresh nutrients and removing waste. This approach minimizes fluctuations 
observed in batch cultures due to different growth phases and thereby facilitates a more accurate 
comparison of microbial metabolite production under varying conditions. 
Different approaches have been reported to maintain anaerobic conditions for cultivation of 
Bifidobacterium species in bioreactors, including 100% N2 (Kawasaki et al., 2006), 80% N2 and 20% 
CO2 (Ninomiya et al., 2009a) and 100% CO2 (González et al., 2004). Initially, it was attempted to 
cultivate B. infantis in bioreactors using 100% N2 gas. However, in these cultures, very limited growth 
occurred (OD600 < 0.1 after 24 h). Since B. infantis grew well in the anaerobic chamber containing 
a mixture of gases, bioreactor cultures with both N2 and CO2 was tested. 
During a 24 h period, growth (OD600) was monitored in bioreactor batch fermentations with three 
different gas ratios (20% CO2 and 80% N2, 50% CO2 and 50% N2, 100% CO2) (Supplementary 
Materials Figure 8). At the end of the fermentations, all cultures had reached an OD600 of 8.6 ± 0.5, 
indicating that CO2 was required for B. infantis growth, while the specific N2-to-CO2 ratio had a 
minimal impact under these conditions.

from each bioreactor were examined under an inverted microscope. All samples displayed bacteria

ALA production follows growth of B. infantis 
To investigate the impact of pH on ALA production, B. infantis was cultivated in chemostat cultures 
maintained at pH 6.5, 5.5, and 4.5, reflecting the pH range of infant faecal samples associated with 
higher ALA concentrations.

time. Additionally, these cultures grew with almost the same growth rate (Figure 9.B). At the 24 h

Growth appeared to be inhibited in pH 4.5 conditions since both the growth rate (Figure 9.B) and cell 
densities over time were significantly lower than for the less acidic pH conditions (Figure 9.C). pH

and 1.9 h, respectively (Figure 9.B). pH 4.5 cultures didn’t show a stabilization in in cell density when 
applying chemostat mode (Figure 9.C). For the first 24 h of chemostat mode, the pH 4.5 culture 
continued to increase and peaked at the 48 h timepoint. Hereafter a constant decrease in cell density 
was observed.

Across pH conditions, ILA, OH-PLA and PLA showed a positive correlation with the cell density

negatively correlated with the concentration of aromatic amino acids, indicating that more cells

densities. 
ALA production is regulated by pH 
Since growth had a significant impact on ALA production and varied across different pH levels, ALA 
concentrations were normalized to OD600 values to account for differences in cell density and ensure 
accurate comparisons between conditions. 
Normalized ALA concentrations demonstrated a significant influence of pH on their production 
(Figure 12). Specifically, PLA production was significantly higher in cultures at pH 4.5 and pH 5.5 
compared to pH 6.5 (Figure 12.C). In contrast, ILA exhibited the highest production at pH 6.5, though 
this increase was only statistically significant relative to pH 5.5 (Figure 12.A). Additionally, OH-PLA 
production was elevated at pH 4.5 compared to pH 6.5 (Figure 12.B). The total accumulation of ALAs 
was significantly higher at pH 4.5 than at the other tested pH conditions, likely driven by the elevated 
PLA production (Figure 12.D). It worth mentioning that beyond the effect of pH, time and the 
interaction between time and pH also significantly influenced differences in ALA production. This 
aligns with the fluctuations in biomass, especially at pH 4.5, and suggests that the observed trends 
may change over the course of cultivation. Therefore, pH did not show a significant effect on ALA 
production at all timepoints as shown by the multiple comparisons in Supplementary Materials Figure 
11. 
The higher PLA production at pH 5.5 compared with pH 6.5 was not significantly affected by time 
(Figure 12.C). Furthermore, PLA was overall the most abundant metabolite and appeared to be the 
most influenced by pH. PLA was not correlated with phenylalanine detected in the chemostat cultures 
(Table 4), indicating that the concentration of the precursor was not reflected in the corresponding 
metabolite production. Finally, PLA also showed a weaker correlation with cell density and CFU mL-
1 than ILA and OH-PLA (Table 3), supporting the effects of pH rather than only number of cells.

The relative expression of aldh in comparison to the pH 6.5 culture varied over time (Figure 14). At 
the 48 time point, a significant increase in aldh expression was observed for both pH 4.5 and 5.5. At 
72 h, only expression in pH 4.5 was significantly higher than at pH 6.5. Finally, at 96 h no difference 
in aldh expression between the pH conditions was observed. 
Overall, these findings suggest that a decrease in pH may lead to increased aldh expression. However, 
additional validation is required to confirm this conclusion. In this study, three bifidobacterial 
housekeeping genes were used for qPCR normalization across different pH conditions. Although 
these genes had been previously validated for various experimental settings, including acid stress 
(Stenico et al., 2014; Turroni et al., 2011), their expression levels fluctuated with changing pH 
(Supplementary Materials Table 4). Therefore, selecting and validating new reference genes that 
remain stable across pH variations is essential to accurately assess the effect of pH on aldh expression. 
Discussion 
Bifidobacterium species are abundant in the infant gut, where they support early life immune 
development through the production of beneficial metabolites such as ALAs (Henrick et al., 2021b; 
Laursen, Sakanaka, et al., 2021). While the presence of ALAs and their positive correlation with 
breast-milk promoted bifidobacterium species in the infant gut has been established, little is known 
about how abiotic factors in the gut affects ALA production. In this project, we aimed to investigate 
in vitro how abiotic factors, including the availability of amino acids and intestinal pH affects ALA 
production by B. infantis. 
In vitro fermentation of B. infantis in MRS-cys medium showed production of all three ALAs. In 
agreement with (Laursen, Sakanaka, et al., 2021), PLA was the most abundant, followed by OH-PLA 
and ILA. None of the potential downstream products (aromatic propionic acids) of the three ALA 
were detected, likewise observed by (Laursen, Sakanaka, et al., 2021). Furthermore, this also aligns 
with (Sakurai et al., 2019) that investigated tryptophan metabolism in 19 different Bifidobacterium 
strains and observed no production of indole propionic acid. 
Since ILA, OH-PLA and PLA are metabolites of tryptophan, tyrosine and phenylalanine, respectively, 
we tested whether increased availability of these amino acids would affect their production. The 
concentration of each metabolite was directly proportional to the supplemented concentration of the 
corresponding metabolic precursor. Furthermore, when supplementing with one AAA, the

Thilde Garbøl Plenge 
Page | 31  
 
corresponding ALA was negatively correlated with the other two ALAs, consistent with the fact that 
the three AAAs compete for the same enzymatic machinery (Laursen, Sakanaka, et al., 2021). 
In addition to changes in substrate availability, pH is another abiotic factor that affects the viability 
and metabolic activity of gut bacteria (Yamamura et al., 2023). Bifidobacteria are typically associated 
with acidic environments due to their production of acetate and lactate (Underwood et al., 2015; 
Yamamura et al., 2023), yet little is known about how pH directly impacts their production of 
metabolites, including ALAs. In breastfed infants, a correlation between high ALA concentrations 
and low faecal pH has been observed (unpublished data), implying that low pH could stimulate 
increased ALA production. To investigate this, B. infantis was cultured in chemostats at neutral and 
acidic pH levels.  
To successfully cultivate B. infantis in bioreactor chemostat cultures, it was discovered that not only 
anaerobic conditions but also CO2 supplementation was required for growth. This observation aligns 
with (Kawasaki et al., 2007), who reported that external CO2 stimulated colony formation of serval 
Bifidobacterium species. In addition, (Ninomiya et al., 2009b) observed maximum growth capacity 
of B. longum with > 20% CO₂ in a CO2-N2 mixture. CO2 is essential for the growth of bifidobacteria 
since they have CO2-anabolic pathways that involve serval enzymes with a requirement for 
bicarbonate which is produced from CO2 (Ninomiya et al., 2009b). 
After establishing the chemostat parameters, B. infantis was cultivated at pH 4.5, 5.5 and 6.5. The 
highest growth rate and cell density were observed at pH 6.5, closely followed by pH 5.5. However, 
at pH 4.5, growth was significantly reduced, consistent with the weak acid tolerance previously 
reported for B. longum (Sánchez et al., 2007). During the chemostat fermentations in this study, 
cultures at pH 5.5 and 6.5 exhibited steady biomass accumulation between 48 and 96 hours. In 
contrast, cultures at pH 4.5 exhibited a peak in cell density at 48 hours, followed by a gradual decline. 
It remains uncertain whether the decline at pH 4.5 would ultimately lead to washout or if the culture 
could stabilize beyond 96 hours. 
In the chemostat cultures at different pH levels, the production of ALAs was strongly associated with 
the growth of B. infantis. This aligns with earlier findings in the cohort of Danish infants, where a 
positive correlation was established between faecal concentrations of ALAs and the abundance of 
breastmilk-promoted Bifidobacterium species (Laursen, Sakanaka, et al., 2021). Moreover, the 
association between these Bifidobacterium species and lower faecal pH raises the possibility that both 
low pH and increased ALA levels are merely byproducts of higher cell density, rather than low pH

actively enhancing ALA production. This explanation would be in agreement with ALA production 
following growth in Lactobacillus (Li et al., 2007; Mu et al., 2009). However, the results in the study 
also suggests that a low pH increases PLA production which ultimately contribute to higher overall 
ALA production by B. infantis. Oppositely, a pH closer to neutral resulted in higher ILA production. 
A previous study on ALDH activity show, that this enzyme exhibits the highest catalytic efficiency 
for indolepyruvic acid followed by hydroxyphenylpyruvic acid and phenylpyruvic acid (Laursen, 
Sakanaka, et al., 2021) at the pH optimum of 8-8.5. With the results obtained in this project, it can be 
hypothesised that ALDH (or AraT) may change shift its preference or have altered activity, and in 
term alter the ILA/PLA ratio. However, this requires elucidation by further studies. 
It has been shown that B. longum adapts to acidic conditions by altering its transcriptional and 
physiological responses, including upregulation of genes involved in carbohydrate and amino acid 
metabolism (Sánchez et al., 2007; Wei et al., 2019). While these genes are not directly involved in 
aromatic amino acid metabolism, it can be speculated whether their upregulation may indirectly affect 
ALA production. In the bacterial metabolism of AAA into ALAs, the transamination reaction is 
generally considered as the rate-limiting step (Li et al., 2007; Vermeulen et al., 2006). During 
transamination, ammonium on the R-amino group is transferred to amino group acceptor, potentially 
α-Ketoglutarate (α‐KG). Evidence suggests, that increased availability of α‐KG leads to enhanced 
production of ALAs (especially PLA) by B. infantis (Kasperek et al., 2024). In LAB, α‐KG is 
produced by glutamate dehydrogenase (GDH), an enzyme that catalyses the reversible oxidative 
deamination of glutamate (Glu) to α‐KG and ammonium, with NAD(P)+ as a cofactor (Dallagnol et 
al., 2011). During acid stress, a reduction in intracellular pH is likely to occur, as observed for B. 
longum at pH 4.8 (Sánchez et al., 2007). This pH drop could, in turn, alter the redox state of the cell. 
Since GDH activity is strongly influenced by the redox state of the cell (Dallagnol et al., 2011), it can 
be speculated whether a low pH increases the activity of this enzyme and thus lead to higher ALA 
production in bifidobacteria. However, this remains a speculation, as the experiments in this project 
do not provide sufficient evidence to draw definitive conclusions about the underlying cellular 
mechanisms. 
Another possible explanation for the enhanced ALA  production at pH 4.5 is an upregulation of genes 
involved in the relevant metabolic pathway. While qPCR analysis indicated an increase in aldh 
expression at pH 4.5 compared to pH 6.5, further experiments with new reference genes are needed 
to confirm this finding.

Limitations 
While the findings of this study provide valuable insights on ALA production, there are some 
limitations to consider when interpreting the results. One notable issue occurred during the LC-MS 
analysis, where tyrosine was not detected as expected, and no increase in tyrosine levels was observed 
in the supplemented cultures. Tyrosine has low solubility in aqueous solutions (0.45 mg/ml in water 
at neutral pH) and may have crystallized during freezing, potentially affecting the LC-MS analysis. 
This highlights the challenge of ensuring accurate representation of sample contents in LC-MS 
analyses. However, it is important to note that other amino acids and metabolites appeared unaffected 
in the analysis, suggesting that the method remains reliable for these compounds. 
Another limitation of the LC-MS analysis was the concentration range used to generate standard 
curves for each analyte in the chemostat cultures. In the pH 5.5 and 6.5 cultures, all ALAs were 
detected at concentrations above the lowest point on the standard curve starting at 24 hours. However, 
in the pH 4.5 cultures, only metabolite PLA reached this threshold, and extrapolated concentrations 
for ILA and OH-PLA were used for the analysis in this project. Additionally, the LC-MS 
chromatograms indicated that the ALAs could be detected earlier than 24 hours, but these data were 
excluded from the analysis due to the signal not reaching the minimum concentration on the standard 
curve, and the lack of a media control to determine the precise timing of the signal. If lower 
concentrations had been used to generate the standard curves and a MRS-cys control had been 
included in the analysis, we could have gained more insight into the timing of ALA production. 
Finally, when performing chemostat fermentations, the most reliable comparisons between conditions 
are made during steady state, where microbial responses to the environmental conditions has 
stabilized. In this study, B. infantis appeared to reach steady state after 48 hours at pH 5.5 and 6.5, 
but this was not observed at pH 4.5, making time a significant factor in the metabolite comparisons. 
Only four residence times were achieved with the dilution rate of 0.06 h-1 and cultivation time used 
in this study. By extending the cultivation period, more samples could potentially be collected during 
steady state for pH 5.5 and 6.5, and this may also allow pH 4.5 to stabilize. This would help reduce 
the time-dependent variation in metabolite profiles.

Conclusion 
This study demonstrates that aromatic amino acid metabolism in B. infantis is influenced by both 
substrate availability (AAAs) and environmental pH. While AAA availability directly enhances the 
production of aromatic lactic acids, pH appears to have a more sophisticated regulatory effect, 
potentially shifting metabolic preferences for aromatic amino acids. This in vitro investigation offers 
insight into how aromatic amino acid metabolism may be regulated in the infant gut.
