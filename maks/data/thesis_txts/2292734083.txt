Abstract
This thesis investigates the modelling of formation and condensation of sulphuric acid in large
two-stroke marine diesel engines by the use of a commercial CFD package. The validity of
the condensation model is investigated by comparing the results produced by the numerical
model against the benchmark experiments of condensation of sulphuric acid and water vapour
conducted by Wilson. Furthermore a parametric study of the model is conducted. A deeper
parametric study is to be carried out to fully understand the inﬂuence of each parameter.
The results show that the model needs modiﬁcations in the modelling of the coupling between
condensation of sulphuric acid and water vapour. The condensation model is applied to the
numerical model of the 4T50MX test engine in order to give an quantitative analysis. Results
are analysed and it is concluded that further work is to be conducted before the results from
the numerical engine model are applicable.

Introduction
Maritime transport is essential to the world economy as over 90 % of the world trade is carried
by sea and it is, by far, the most cost-eﬀective way to move goods and raw materials en masse
around the world [18].
The standard propulsion technology for large commercial vessels (container ships, bulk carriers
and tankers)is the marine low speed two-stroke Diesel engine. The two-stroke engine provides
the required power, up to megawatt in magnitude and oﬀers thermal eﬃciencies above 50 %.
This eﬃciency is obtained despite the fact that most engines use HFO (Heavy Fuel Oil ) due
to cost concerns, but which is of very low quality. The main part of HFO are remains from oil
reﬁneries and basically a waste product which has limited use in most applications consuming
fossil fuel. Maritime transport accounts for more than 2 % of CO2 emissions [13], which is a
main contributor to global warming. In addition to CO2 the ships emit large levels of nitrogen
oxides, NOx, particulate matter/soot, and sulphur oxides, SOx. HFO contains a considerable
amount of sulphur that is oxidized during the combustion and forms SO2 or SO3.
The allowable amount of sulphur in HFO is dependent on the area the ship is operating in
and how the exhaust gas is cleaned. The International Maritime Organization (IMO) sets the
allowed amount of sulphur in the fuel. In 2010 the limit was 1 wt % in emission controlled
areas (ECA’s) and 3.5wt % outside these areas. These limits decreased to 0.1 wt % in 2015 for
ECA’s and will decrease to 0.5 wt% in 2020 for non- ECA’s [13]. If the exhaust gas is cleaned
on-board using scrubbers these limits do not apply.
During the last years increasing fuel prices and stricter emission standards imply that engine
designers make large eﬀorts to reduce fuel consumption and improve engine eﬃciency at low
engine load. Another consequence of these changes is that the operation of the ship engines will
be changed as well, running at lower loads the majority of the time. One of the disadvantages
with low load operation is increased cold corrosion, which has proved to be a bigger problem
than expected. Cold corrosion reduces the life time of some components by a factor of 5 to 10
[12] , which raises the cost of maintenance. Cold corrosion is caused by mechanical wear and
corrosive wear, originating from SO3 which forms sulfuric acid in the presence of water, that
condensates onto the cylinder wall; also known as the liner, in the engine. The condensation
of sulphuric acid on the liner will be investigated in this paper by simulating this complicated
process in the cylinder of the engine by the use of a commercial Computational Fluid Dynamics
(CFD) package which features modelling of condensation. CFD is a powerful tool to study
complex phenomena, such as the combustion inside an engine and is capably of producing data
which would be hard to obtain from physical experiments.

Theory
In maritime transportation most commercial vessels are powered by large two-stroke diesel
engines. The two-stroke principle is preferred when power and eﬃciency is of highest priority.
The two-stroke term arises from the combustion cycle which consists of two strokes. Fig. 1
shows the engine parts of interest. The two-stroke cycle is explained in the following way.
Starting with the piston in the bottom position, the piston will uncover the scavenging ports
and air will be blown into the cylinder. The piston moves upward compressing the air and fuel
is injected slightly before the piston reaches the top dead center. Here the gas mixture will
self ignite due to the large pressures and the expanding gases will force the piston down. As
the piston moves towards the bottom the exhaust valve will open letting the gas out and the
piston will uncover the scavenging ports letting fresh air in and the cycle repeats.
The majority of large commercial vessels powered by two-stroke marine diesel engines uses
HFO as fuel. In 2010 around 80 % of fuel used in this category was HFO. Stricter emissions
have put focus on alternative fuels and reduced the amount of sulphur content in fuels gradu-
ally, but it is expected that HFO will still account for more than 40 % of the fuel used in total
[11].
One of the issues with high sulphur content in HFO is the formation of sulphur oxides dur-
ing the combustion which is one of the main contributors to acid rain which is causing large
environmental problems. Another issue is that the sulphur oxides reacts with water vapour
in the combustion chamber which forms corrosive species, which is believed to increase wear
signiﬁcantly. This is especially pronounced at low engine loads when vessels are "slow stream-
ing". The lower engine loads result in a lower liner temperature which causes sulphuric acid
vapour to condense on the cooled liner. The sulphuric acid increases the acidity in the lube
oil which would lead to enhanced corrosion. Base additives is therefore mixed into the engine
oil to neutralize it. The right amount of additives is crucial to the performance of the engine
oil since an insuﬃcient dosing will increase the degradation of lube oil and a too large dosing
may build up calcium compounds which contribute to linear wear [21].
2.1
Chemical reactions
It should be noted that the sulphur reaction mechanism during combustion in the cylinder con-
sists of more than 100 elementary reactions. The major reactions mechanism will be presented
but the details will not be covered. A more detailed description of the sulphur chemistry in
found in the description of the engine simulation. During the combustion of sulphur containing
fuel the majority of the sulphur is transformed into sulphur oxides, where SO2 represent the
majority of the sulphur compounds.The SO2 is further oxidized to SO3 where reactions with
O and OH radicals at high temperatures play a key role [7].
In general a few percent of the fuel sulphur are converted into SO3 [16], which reacts with
water vapour to form sulphuric acid H2SO4:
SO3(g) + H2O(g) →H2SO4(g)
(1)
The formed H2SO4 then condenses on the liner when the temperature of the liner is below
the acid dew point.

2.2
Condensation mechanisms
Sulphuric acid condenses at a higher temperature than pure water. In the case of a local liner
temperature being below the sulphuric acid dew point temperature, but above the water dew
point temperature, gaseous sulphuric acid and water will condense on the liner surface. The
gaseous sulphuric acid condenses on the liner and will absorb water vapour, which is considered
as condensation of water vapour [6]. This means in principle that the boundary conditions for
the species is not independent but coupled.
In cases where the local liner temperature is below both the sulphuric acid and water dew
point temperature both sulphuric acid and water will condense. The higher concentration rate
of water in this case means that the mass fraction of sulphuric acid of the condensate will be
low, but the condensate will still be highly corrosive.
No matter the surface temperature of the liner, the condensable species moves towards the
gas-liquid interfase due to partial pressure gradients [2]. In some cases it has been shown that
sulphuric acid will condense into droplets, or fog, in the gaseous boundary layer close to the
gas-liquid interfase if the gas is abruptly cooled e.g. by a cold surface [2]. The formation of
fog is believed to reduce the sulphuric acid condensation rate but to the authors knowledge it
has not been shown in experiments.

Validation of ﬂuid ﬁlm model
In order to validate the ﬂuid ﬁlm model a benchmark case is set up. The numerical model
resembles the test section of the works by Wilson [22]. Wilson measured the condensation
rates in a circular pipe for a ternary gas consisting of air, water vapour and sulphuric acid
vapour. The pipe is 0.3175 m long, Lpipe with a diameter of 0.01 m, Dpipe and is visualized
in ﬁg. 4. The gas enters at the inlet and exits at the outlet. The hot gas will be cooled down
by the cold pipe surface and the condensable species will condensate if the wall temperature
is below the respective dew point temperatures. The following assumptions are made:
• The temperature of the wall of the pipe is constant
• The density of the gas has a constant density and is incompressible
• The species concentration is uniformly distributed in the incoming gas
• The velocity proﬁle in the z-direction is fully developed and the ﬂow is laminar and steady
• There is no condensation in the gas phase itself
• The ﬁlm does not occupy any volume in the neighbour cell to the shell region since the
thickness of the ﬁlm is small

the liquid ﬁlm, from the cold walls is balanced out with the heat transfer from the warmer
inlet gas, the heat of vaporization from the condensing species and the motion of the ﬂuid ﬁlm.
This will result in a constant condensation rate of the condensable species.

3.3
Discussion
To validate the ﬂuid ﬁlm model the experiments of condensation of sulphuric acid and wa-
ter vapour performed by Wilson were modelled. The reference conﬁguration were executed
which produced a condensation rate distribution for sulphuric acid which corresponds with
those obtained by Wilson. A parametric study was conducted to investigate the inﬂuence of
each parameter. It was seen that the condensation rate distribution was transient and varied
as function of the solution time leading to a steady solution. The overall condensation rate
showed dependency of time step size in the cases where the initial ﬁlm thickness was zero.
In simulations with a small initial ﬁlm thickness the condensation rates did not seem to be
independent of the time step size. In general it was seen that increased concentrations of the
condensable species induced larger condensation rates. The water did only condense in one
case , which was when the mole fractions of water vapour was increased signiﬁcantly. The
choice of conﬁguration of the reference case was chosen since the parameters were close to
Han’s conﬁguration. However it was seen that the reference case conﬁguration was at the limit
of what the physics in the ﬂuid ﬁlm model could produce. This resulted in a solution which
required a long solution time in order to reach a steady state.
The initial ﬂuid ﬁlm thickness appeared to have an inﬂuence on whether the solution was
dependent on the time step size or not. The cause of this could be related to how the ﬁlm is
formed initially on a dry wall by the growth of droplet on the wall. A deeper parametric study
is to be carried out before it can be concluded that an small initial ﬁlm thickness produces a
solution which is independent of the time step size.
Compared to the results obtained by Wilson the ﬂuid ﬁlm model lacked to model the the
coupling between the condensation of sulphuric acid and water, where sulphuric acid is believed
to absorb the water or due to sulphuric acid inﬂuence on the water vapour pressure. Wilson’s
experiments did show condensation of water in the form of a solution of sulphuric acid and
water, but this could not be estimated by the ﬂuid ﬁlm model. This indicates that the ﬂuid
ﬁlm model lacks within the interaction of the condensable species, which should be coupled.

4.2.2
Fluid ﬁlm assumptions
The properties of the ﬂuid ﬁlm on the liner is not simply estimated since the spatial distribution,
composition etc. is very complex and dependent on engine load, fuel type, lubrication oil etc.
The typical oil ﬁlm thickness in a large two-stroke engine is the order of µm [15] and several
times less around TDC [16]. It is therefore assumed that the ﬁlm is uniformly distributed on
the liner and hf = 5 · 10−6. The composition of the ﬁlm is simpliﬁed since the ﬂuid ﬁlm can
only consist of the species which is condensing, which in this case is sulphuric acid and water.
The chemical reactions between acid, water and the lube oil are therefore neglected.
At the top of the cylinder a wall boundary condition prevails which prevents the ﬂuid from
leaving the ﬁlm regime. At the edge around the piston a ﬂow exit boundary conditions prevail
which enables the ﬂuid to leave the ﬁlm domain. It is also assumed that the ﬂuid ﬁlm stays
attached to the liner. The properties of the ﬂuid ﬁlm is identical to the Wilson benchmark
case and is found in Appendix A.

Engine simulation results
The results from the 4T50MX engine are shown in this section. The added complexity of
the engine model compared to the Wilson case resulted in large computational time. The
combustion models combined with phase coupling and the ﬂuid meant that the computa-

the model. Instead of running a simulation with all models switched on a compromise was
made. The simulation was initialized with the combustion chemistry activated and results

stopped at 20◦CAD ATDC and ran until 40◦CAD ATDC etc. The results presented are for

60◦CAD ATDC the showed results are at the last iteration before 60◦CAD ATDC are reached.

then be given. Since the lube oil is not modelled itself as mentioned in section 4.2.2, the well

Discussion
The results obtained from the engine simulation arises from assumptions which are rough, and
one should therefore discuss the trustworthiness of the results. It was assumed that the ﬂuid
ﬁlm could be applied to the simulations at certain time levels and freeze the chemical reactions
in order to obtain a quantitative analysis of the condensation rates. In reality the conden-
sation rate would be dependent on the initial conditions, e.g. one would expect a diﬀerent
condensation rate at 40◦CAD ATDC in the case where the chemical reactions are deactivated
and the ﬂuid ﬁlm is activated to a case where the ﬂuid ﬁlm would develop from the start and
inﬂuence the composition of the gas during the combustion and thereby the condensation rate.
The eﬀect on the results of the diﬀerent approaches would have to be investigated further to
give an indication of the error.
From the results it was seen that some proportional relationship between the concentration
of the condensable species and the condensation rate of these species was visible. However a
parametric study has to be conducted before these trends can be ﬁnally conﬁrmed.
It was seen that results tended to become more noisefull as the mesh was extended in the axial
direction, due to the morphing of the mesh. The morphing extends the cells in the axial direc-
tion which reduces the accuracy in this direction, and means that the gradients are resolved
more and more inaccurately as the cells are extended. The morphing does also seem to cause
60
==PAGE:67==
a stretching eﬀect on the ﬂuid ﬁlm which, due to mass conservation, is getting thinner as the
cells are extended, since the shell mesh is regenerated and adapted to the new surface in the
bulk region mesh. This is reducing the ﬁlm thickness which increases the rate at which the
bulk gas is cooled.
In addition to this a general mesh convergence study is missing which has to be performed be-
fore any conclusions can be reached. The obtained solution is considered as mesh independent
when reﬁnement of the mesh does not contribute to a change in the solution.
It should be considered whether the further reactions of the sulphuric acid with water should
be added to the reaction schemes. The protonation reaction of sulphuric acid with water could
mean that more water vapour would the absorbed from the bulk gas leading to increased con-
densation rates, due to the additionally corrosive species which could be formed. Analysing
the tendencies from the simulations gave an indication of the ability of the ﬂuid ﬁlm model
to estimate the condensation rates. From these simulations an observed tendency was that
sulphuric acid and water did condense on the liner and that increased mass fractions resulted
in increased condensation rates. These tendencies should be veriﬁed by additional simulation
conﬁgurations.

5
Comparison of model to reference work
As discussed in earlier chapters the ﬂuid ﬁlm model in STAR-CCM+ is too generic to model
the physics of the coupled condensation process. Comparison of the ﬂuid ﬁlm model to the
current state of the art condensation model developed by Han [6] shows that the ﬂuid ﬁlm
model has some incorrect assumptions. The assumption in eq. 19 of γi = 1, but this is not the
case for a trinary system of sulphuric acid, water and air. This leads to incorrect vapour pres-
sures of sulphuric acid and water which gives incorrect condensation rates. The condensation
rate is calculated from Ygs which is determined from eq. 19 which rely on the knowledge of
Xls which is a user-input in STAR-CCM+. However the coupling between sulphuric acid and
water condensation is never introduced. An example of this is the lack of water condensation
in the simulation of the Wilson’s case, where Wilson’s experiments showed condensation of
water.
It has for many years been known that the presence of sulphuric acid greatly aﬀects the vapour
pressures for water and sulphuric acid, and experiments have shown that the vapour pressures
for sulphuric acid can vary more than two orders depending on the concentration of sulfuric
acid in the solution [5]. It is diﬃcult to give a complete mathematical description of the inter-
faces between ﬂuid phases themselves and solids which is why vapour-liquid equilibrium (VLE)
models are used to describe the boundary condition at interfaces. Several models have been
proposed and have been implemented in condensation models in commercial CFD programs [6]
[14]. In Han’s work the vapour pressures of water and sulphuric acid are determined from the
VLE by Gmitro and Vermeulen [4] which is dependent on a series of coeﬃcients where some are
dependent of solution composition. Furthermore Han model accounts for the dissociation of
H2SO4 into H20 and is validated through experiments[5]. The ﬂuid ﬁlm model could possibly
be improved signiﬁcantly by creating a ﬁeld function for the vapour pressures which took some
activity coeﬃcients into account depending on the the sulphuric acid concentration.
A disadvantage with most of the cases which involve modelling of condensation is that one
only solves for a steady solution by neglecting the formation of the ﬂuid ﬁlm which means that
these models have limited applicability in a transient domain, such as in an engine, since the
time level to reach the equilibrium is not known. In the engine case one cannot solve for a
steady solution since the combustion and the following condensation happens within a very
small time interval.
The diﬀusion coeﬃcient for each species has in this thesis been treated as a constant and it
has been assumed that diﬀusion is given by Fick’s law, which is true when the concentration of
the main species in a ﬂuid is signiﬁcantly larger than the remaining species. If the remaining
species make up for a large fraction , the diﬀusion coeﬃcients can no longer be treated as
constant. The interaction of the diﬀerent species with each other will be dominant and inﬂuence
the diﬀusion coeﬃcients which will become a tensor [8].
In literature where condensation
is modelled using CFD [14] diﬀusion coeﬃcients are typically calculated by mole weighting
binary diﬀusion coeﬃcients which is found from empirical correlations [6] or the Maxwell-
Stefan equations [14]. In STAR-CCM+ it is possible to specify binary diﬀusion coeﬃcients,
which assumably would lead to a more accurate model.
In general one should also look further into the reactions of not only how H2SO4 is formed
but also how H2SO4 reacts afterwards with H20 to form H3O+, Hydronium, by protonation,
and HSO−
4 , bisulfate anion, which again can react with H20 to form H3O+ and SO−2
4 , sulfate
62
==PAGE:69==
anion. This is also known as the dehydration eﬀect of H2SO4, which is quite eﬀective since the
hydration reactions are favoured due to the negative reaction enthalpy ∆H. The formation
of the previously mentioned species could also aﬀect other species during the combustion cycle.
6
Conclusion
The validity of the ﬂuid ﬁlm model in STAR-CCM+ were investigated by comparing the
numerical model against the benchmark experiments of condensation of sulphuric acid and
water vapour conducted by Wilson. It was seen that the condensation rate distribution were
transient and varied as function of the solution time until a steady solution was reached. A
parametric study was conducted to investigate the inﬂuence of the parameters. It was seen
that some parameters had an inﬂuence on the solution time to obtain a steady condensation
rate. A deeper parametric study is to be carried out to fully understand the inﬂuence of each
parameter. A mesh convergence study were conducted, but it could not be concluded that a
mesh independent solution was obtained.
The ﬂuid ﬁlm model were not able to evaluate the coupling between the condensation of
sulphuric acid and water vapour. Modiﬁcations have to be implemented in order to obtain a
coupling of the condensation physics between the condensable species.
The ﬂuid ﬁlm model were applied to the numerical model of the 4T50MX test engine in order
to give an quantitative analysis. The model estimated the condensation rate and behaviour
of the condensation of sulphuric acid and water. However the accuracy of these results can
not be estimated before a parametric study is performed together with an mesh convergence
analysis. Furthermore additional reactions for the corrosive species should be considered to be
implemented to improve the numerical engine model. Afterwards the results should be veriﬁed
by real engine measurements.
