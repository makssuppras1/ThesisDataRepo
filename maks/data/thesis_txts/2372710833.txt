Abstract
This thesis reports on the work on the DTU fusor, an inertial electrostatic conﬁnement device, used
for fusion reactions. The goal of the thesis was to ﬁnd the conditions for breakdown voltage, as well
as make the fusor ready for fusion reactions.
Bremsstrahlung radiation levels were calculated and measured, in order to establish a safe working
environment around the fusor. By shielding the fusor with lead, it was possible to lower the radiation
level to background levels. At the same time, the bremsstrahlung spectrum was measured, where
it showed some interesting peaks, as well as clear indications, that the x-rays did indeed come from
electron-iron interactions.
Finally breakdown conditions were measured, where there were some diﬃculties. Because breakdown
voltage is a function of pressure times distance, both these had to be known. Unfortunately the fusor
chamber has previously been used as a spotter chamber, and therefore has a number of inlets in the
bottom of the chamber, making it almost impossible to tell, what the distance for the breakdown
voltage was.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The world of today faces many challenges, and two of those challenges are the ever rising demand for
energy combined with the climate change challenge. These two are connected such that more energy
creates more climate challenges. As we burn oil, gas and coal for energy production, we also produce
CO2 in great amounts. But the changes are slow as the natural buﬀer system is trying to compensate
for these changes. The CO2 gets absorbed in the oceans, and stored in the plants and the atmosphere.
But as the amount of produced CO2 keeps climbing, and the fact that no CO2 from former years is
removed, the natural buﬀer system can not keep up, and slowly the level in the oceans and atmosphere
rises. The eﬀects diﬀerent levels of CO2 amounts in the atmosphere has on the planets ecosystem is
still being discussed but there is no doubt that the changes will be plentiful and must not for the
better.
In order to combat these rising levels, there are multiple solutions at hand, where they all have diﬀerent
advantages and disadvantages. The main problem is that we have an increasing population on our
planet and this population consumes more and more energy per person every year. This means, that
there are three obvious ways to reduce our total CO2 output, and that is by reducing our amount of
energy used per person, thus making our energy use more eﬃcient, reduce the amount of CO2 output
per unit energy, or reduce the number of people on the planet. It should be clear to everyone that
the last way is not an option, which means we need to think about how we produce energy and how
much we consume. There is no easy or obvious way to do so but there are elements which we can
focus on, especially in the part of the world with more privileges. This paper will focus on the energy
production and only mention, that the consumption part is equally important.
Today most energy comes from burning coal, oil and gas to make electricity and from gasoline and
diesel for transportation [8]. This is part due to the fact of the availability, the easiness to transform
these energy carries to the needed energy form, the fact of how easy it is to store these energy carriers,
and the energy density of the carriers. Other energy carriers simply do not have all these advantages
and are not as attractive to use, if it was not for the fact, that oil, coal and gas produce a lot of
greenhouse gases as they are transformed into mechanical and electrical energy. We are therefore
looking for other ways of producing energy. But when we do so, we also need to specify for what
the energy is used, is it electricity or is it transportation?
It is important between those two as
they have vastly diﬀerent needs. Energy for transportation really depends on an energy carrier that
is compact, light, and easy to recharge and a compact and light device to transform this carrier to
mechanical energy. Electric energy production does not have the same needs for the energy carrier or
transformation device as it is mainly stationary power plants that produce electrical energy. These
diﬀerences also means that we face two diﬀerent set of problems. We will focus on the electrical energy
production, and therefore not take into account diﬀerent promising energy carriers for transportation
use, such as batteries and fuel cells.
Wind, water, sun, and nuclear ﬁssion are the four main competitors for taking over as the main

electrical energy producers [15]. These four have all been proved to work, they are all well established
and they produce no greenhouse gases as they produce energy. They do, however, all have diﬀerent
problems, which is why they are all still competing for being the main producer.
Water power has the problem, that it is hard to scale it up, partly because all the great places for
hydro plants have already been used, and partly because it needs a huge area to ﬂood with water as
a reservoir. This last requirement means that massive areas of nature are getting destroyed, which is

Wind power faces the issues that it is hard to control the power output (as we can not control the

and that wind mills take up large areas. The last problem can be solved by placing wind mills in the
ocean, but this raises production and maintenance cost.
Sun, or solar, power faces problems with scaling and eﬃciency. Si-based solar panels have fairly low
eﬃciency and high production cost but has the advantage of high amounts of Si in the world. Other
solar panels with higher eﬃciency either have issues with durability or simply uses rare elements,
meaning it will be diﬃcult or impossible to scale up the production to needed levels.
Nuclear ﬁssion, the last competitor, only has two problems, which both are hard to solve. The ﬁrst
problem is the waste products produced. The waste is long lasting and hard to get rid of, making
a long time solution hard to come by, and even storing it under ground can at some point become
a problem. The second problem is a political one, as the general public opinion is against nuclear
ﬁssion power. A lot of people are afraid of a possible melt down or another kind of catastrophe with a
nuclear ﬁssion reactor involved. Whether this is a true concern or not is hard to say but the problems
stands that the public is concerned and therefore does not want nuclear ﬁssion energy to be the energy
producer of the future.
1.1
Fusion
There is a ﬁfth competitor but it is not as far developed as the other four. Nuclear fusion energy
might be the future energy source. It has not been proven that a surplus of energy production can be
achieved, and many sceptics believe it will never happen. It has, however, the beneﬁt that the fuel is
easily transported, easily stored and is available all over the world, as it uses a hydrogen isotope as
fuel. The two main fusion reactions are: Deuterium-deuterium (D-D) fusion, and deuterium-tritium
(D-T) [12], where D-D fusion is the long term end goal of nuclear fusion power, as deuterium is so
abundant that with the energy consumption of today, we have enough deuterium for the next one
billion years, making it one of the longest lasting sustainable energy source. Only wind and solar
power has the same time frame for how long they can last, and a billion years is so much time, that
we can not even imagine what humanity will have to go through to make it that long.
At the moment D-D fusion is not the goal, as there is a a fusion process that is easier to make happen.
When using tritium (3
1H or T) as the one component and deuterium as the other (D-T fusion), the
fusion process should in theory go easier.
This is due to the higher reactivity at lower energies,
meaning it should be easier to achieve. There are two problems with this process though. First,
tritium is highly toxic, and therefore has to be dealt with carefully. Secondly, tritium is not natural
occurring (as it is radioactive and hence decaying fast), which means it has to be made out of lithium.
And lithium is simply not as abundant as deuterium, making the supposedly never ending source of
energy to last much shorter. Fortunately D-T fusion is not the end goal, it is simply a way to get to
the end goal faster. As soon as D-T fusion is accomplished and we can make that run as long as we
want to while getting a surplus of power, there will be tries to shift to D-D fusion. The D-D fusion has
all the advantages of a sustainable energy source and at the same time has an abundant fuel source
that is distributed evenly around the world. The fuel can be stored and transported relative easily.
The fusion power plant will not take up a huge area, and the waste products are limited, making

fusion power the ultimate future energy source.
The reason why fusion power has yet not been achieved, is because it is notoriously diﬃcult to make
happen and get a surplus of energy out of it. This is due to Coulomb forces, where the atoms repel each
other, and only with very high kinetic energies and with the help of quantum mechanical tunnelling
can fusion occur. The energy needed for D-T fusion is around 100 keV, making it diﬃcult at best to
control the atoms. In order to have high enough part of the atoms to fuse, some sort of conﬁnement is
needed, as the loses from lost high energy ions and atoms otherwise will surpass the energy gained by
fusion. Today there are two main ways to conﬁne the fuel long enough for it to initiate fusion, one is by
magnetic conﬁnement and the other is by inertial conﬁnement. Magnetic conﬁnement uses powerful
magnets to control the shape of the plasma and how it moves. There has been many ideas and tries
within magnetic conﬁnement, some of which have been more successful than others, where today the
leading magnetic conﬁnements are the tokamak and the stellarator. The tokamak is a donut shaped
machine, using a toroidal and a poloidal magnetic ﬁeld to control the hot plasma made of deuterium.
The stellerator has a much more complex magnetic ﬁeld, that removes some of the instabilities from
which the tokamak suﬀers under. Both types are showing promising results, and are the leading ways
today to reach surplus energy production.
Inertial conﬁnement compress and heat up the fuel in order for it to initiate fusion. This can be
done by applying powerful laser to shoot at the fuel, thereby compressing it immensely. By doing
so, the fuel reaches conditions where fusion is possible. Another way to inertial conﬁne the fuel is by
inertial electrostatic conﬁnement. Here a voltage is applied to a cathode, creating a plasma between
the cathode and the anode. An electric ﬁeld is then used to conﬁne the ions in the plasma, making it
possible to have fusion occur.
1.2
Fusor
The fusor is an inertial electrostatic conﬁnement device, making use of an electric ﬁeld to heat and
conﬁne the plasma. It is made of a closed chamber, in which an almost transparent grid is suspended.
The grid works as a cathode, as a large negative voltage is applied to it, and the chamber walls works
as an anode. Inside the chamber the pressure is lowered, such that when the voltage is applied, a
current will start to run, and a stable plasma will form (see Fig. 1.1 [5]). If the conditions are right,
there will be a visible plasma inside the grid. Here the ions have accelerated from outside the grid, and
are now colliding with each other and the background gas, that has not been turned into a plasma.
It is also inside the grid that potential fusion will mostly happen, and thus where the most of the
neutron production will come from.

The reason why the fusor is even considered as a fusion device, is the simplicity of the design. It does
not take up much space (as it is relative small), it can be build and run with relative few pieces of
equipment, and the working principles are relative simple. Even though at ﬁrst the fusor seems simple,
it brings a lot of physics that has to be understood in order to optimize the design. Unfortunately for
the fusor, it has been proven, that no surplus energy can be gained by using this design, rendering
it obsolete as a design for a possible fusion power plant. It does, however, provide an easy accessible
platform for a lot of fusion relevant physics, which makes it perfect for smaller projects. It can be
used to test measurements methods, provide experimental plasma and fusion physics, and give more
physic students the opportunity to perform plasma and fusion experiments, without the need to plan
the form of the experiments for months. As such, the fusor is an excellent tool.
The goal of this thesis is to get a deeper understanding on how the fusor works, and get an under-
standing of the relevant physics behind the design. I will ﬁnd the conditions for breakdown voltage,
and look at how electrons in the fusor behave. Futhermore is the goal, to make the fusor ready for
fusion reactions.

Background and Theory
The simplicity of an inertial electrostatic conﬁnement (IEC) device, or a fusor, is amazing from an
engineering view, but it can also be misleading. In all its simplicity, the fusor works by applying
a negative voltage to an cathode, accelerating ions towards it and repelling electrons to an outer
anode [16] [2]. In Fig. 2.1 [9] we see how the ions are moving toward the grid in the center, and
how the electrons are moving toward the outer anode. By forming the cathode as a transparent grid,
it is possible to condense the ions in the middle, and thus creating high densities. If the ions are
accelerated enough, and they obtain high enough energies, fusion relevant conditions will have been
established. The ions will move towards the grid, accelerating all the way. As soon as they reach the
grid, they will hopefully, due to the high transparency of the grid, pass through and fuse inside the
grid. Those that do not fuse, will then again pass the grid, whereas they will start to slow down, and
ﬁnally turn around towards the grid, just before they hit the outer electrode, the anode. As long as
they do not fuse, hit the grid, or are lost due to interactions with the surrounding gas, they will stay
trapped and make an oscillatory motion, essentially increasing the number of possible passes inside
the grid, and thus increasing the number of possible fusion events.
This simple view of the fusor is, however, not the full view, as there are many factors limiting the
fusion process. First of all the grid is not perfectly transparent, there will be loses due to particle
interactions, and the potential of the grid will be perturbed as the ions and lack of electrons will
interfere.
In order to try to understand what eﬀects the fusion process in the fusor, some of the
underlying physics will be presented.

2.1
Fusion physics
The reason we are even considering nuclear fusion, as well as nuclear ﬁssion, are the massive amounts
of energy stored in the atoms as binding energy [27]. This energy describes how much energy the atom
has put into holding itself together, essentially meaning how stable the atoms are. As the atoms are
growing bigger, more and more energy is needed to hold it together, so in order to be able to compare
the energies, we look at how much binding energy there is per nucleon. If this is plotted vs. the mass
number (as in Fig. 2.2 [1]), we get a two fold curve. The left side is extremely steep, showing the need
for a lot more energy as the atom grow in size. Then the right side with a gradual decreasing slope,
stating that these atoms are less stable. It is also at higher mass numbers we see the highest number
of radioactive isotopes, telling the same stability story as the binding energy curve.
The binding energy also has to be understood, as the amount of energy needed to add to the atom
to move down one of the slopes, or the amount of energy we gain from the atom when it moves up
one of the slopes. We see, that breaking apart uranium (on the far right), thus moving to the left, we
gain around 1 MeV per ﬁssion reaction. [7] Fusing together deuterium and tritium, on the left side of
the curve, we can gain 17.6 MeV per fusion reaction [12], which is almost 20 times as much energy
as with ﬁssion. Both reaction types, however, are much more energy advantageous, as we are talking
in the MeV range. All chemical reactions are in the eV range, making ﬁssion and fusion a million
times more eﬃcient per reaction, and therefore very attractive. This combined with the lack of CO2
emission, nuclear fusion is very much a solution we have to look into.

The fusion reaction and the fusion reaction rate are coupled, as diﬀerent fusions have diﬀerent reaction
rates at the same conditions. The rate depends on which two particles that fuse, as these will have
diﬀerent cross sections. The cross section is how big the particles see each other and how likely they
are to interact, and it is dependent on what particles they are, what the energy is. At the same time,
the quantum states of the particles also plays a role, and as diﬀerent particles interact, they will have
resonances at certain energies, making it even harder to predict the diﬀerent cross sections. The most
common way, is to determine them experimentally [7], and then later look them up.

ping. This is partly because they have the largest cross sections, making the easiest to fuse, but also
because of the availability of the fuel, making them a long term solution.

Ions are the important particles in the fusor, as they are the ones making fusion possible which is the
goal. By applying a large negative voltage to the cathode in the middle, it is possible to create ions.
In any gas with a temperature above 0 kelvin, there is a possibility that electrons gain enough energy
to be free of the atom, and thus creating a free electron and an ion. The higher the temperature,
the higher the possibility and thus higher amount of free electrons and ions. These ions and electrons
are accelerated in the high electrical potential, and will at some point collide with another particle,
depending on the mean free path in the gas [11]:
λ = 1
σn
(2.6)
where σ is the average cross section of the gas, and n is the number density of the medium (this only
applies to fast moving particles in a slow medium, as is in our case). When the high energy particles
interact with a slow moving atom, there is a possibility that an electron is knocked oﬀ, thus creating
a new ion and free electron. If enough of these ions are created, a cascade of ions and electrons is
made. This avalanche of electrons and ions is essentially a current, and the moment the cascade is
established, breakdown voltage has been reached. This voltage is dependent on a number of criteria,
where pressure plays a large part. If the mean free path is too small, the electrons and ions will
move too short a distance before interacting with other particles, which means they do not reach the
required energies to knock oﬀfurther electrons. At the same time, if the mean free path is too large,
the particles will not interact with other particles before reaching either the anode or the cathode,
ensuring no cascade will happen [14]. Of course this also depends on the cross section of the gas,
but this is of the same order of magnitude (except hydrogen, which is one order of magnitude lower),
around 1 × 10−16 cm2, meaning the only thing we can vary is the pressure. In atmospheric air at
normal pressure, the mean free path is around 68 nm REF. This mean free path is too large for a
cascade to happen, unless extreme voltages is being used, at which we are not interested. On the
other hand, if we make the mean free path too large, the particles will never collide and all be lost as

2.3
Dominant fusion mechanism
We will now look at what mechanism is biggest contributor to the fusion reaction. Here we have a
number of possibilities: Fast ion - fast ion, fast ion - fast neutral, fast neutral - fast neutral, fast ion
- background, fast neutral - background, and background - background. The fast ions are the ions
generated between the anode and the cathode, and then accelerated to high energies. The background
is all the gas molecules that have not been aﬀected by the electrical potential in any way, neither by
being accelerated directly or through interactions with the fast ions. As a last kind of species, we
have the fast neutrals. These are non-charged particles, that have been accelerated to high energies,
by ﬁrst being born as fast ions, and then through charge exchange reverted back to being a neutral.
As they went back to being a neutral, they kept their energy, meaning a fast neutral is born. These
three species can interact and inﬂuence the fusion reaction rate in the following ways [10]:
 The background - background reaction rate is almost non existing, as they simply lack the
needed energies for fusion reactions to occur. Even if there are a few of the background particles
that do indeed fuse, the reaction rate is so small that it can be completely neglected compared
to some of the other reaction rates.
 The fast neutral - fast neutral reaction rate can be roughly estimated. If we assume that all
the fast ions have the maximum potential energy, and the all make a charge exchange, turning
them in to fast neutrals, then all the fast neutrals will converge towards the center of the fusor.
But the number density of the fast neutrals will be much smaller than that of the fast ions, as
they will only transverse the grid once, whereas the fast ions will recirculate and thus pass a
number of times, essentially increasing the number density of the ions. And as the fusion rate
scales with the number density squared, the fast neutral - fast neutral reaction rate is much
lower than that of fast ion - fast ion, and we therefore ignore this contribution.
 The fast ion - fast neutral reaction rate can be estimated the same way as the fast neutral -
fast neutral reaction rate, only with the slight change, that it now scales with the product of the

Chamber set-up and fusor equipment
The fusor chamber was originally a spotter chamber, in which surface physic experiments were carried
out. As so, the fusor chamber is not originally designed as a fusor chamber, meaning some of the
decisions regarding the design has been made on non-fusor related problems and requirements. For-
tunately a spotter chamber has some of the same requirements as a fusor chamber, as they both need
to be able to handle low pressures, they have to have ports in the chamber, where it is possible to
mount viewports, pumps or other equipment. And both types of chambers are of similar size, making
it easy to adopt the spotter chamber. There are, however, also a series of diﬀerences. First thing is,
that with the fusor chamber, we want as clean a chamber as possible, so we can make sure what is
going on inside. We also want to known which materials the chamber walls are made of, as diﬀerent
materials will have diﬀerent impact on the breakdown voltage. Without knowing the materials, it can
be diﬃcult to predict when breakdown will happen. Secondly, the spotter chamber also has a number
of inlets in the bottom, that have no use in the fusor chamber. This means, that when the breakdown
voltage is established, it might be diﬃcult to determine from where to where the breakdown occurs,
and thus it might be diﬃcult to exclude the distance as a variable.
The whole fusor set-up can be seen in Fig. 3.1 [6], where a single thing needs to be pointed out. As
we can see, all the instruments are on one side of the wall, and the operator PC is on the other side.
The decision to make it so is part due to safety for the operator, and part a wish to, at some point,
move all the instrument controls to the side with the PC. However this has not happened yet, as it
is not needed in order to run the fusor or to make experiments. It is more an inconvenience and it is
time consuming to make adjustments while the fusor is running.

3.1.2
Vacuum Pumps
The vacuum pumps has one purpose only, and that is to create a suﬃcient high enough vacuum
in the chamber. The needed pressure is around 1 × 10−3 mbar. But in order to have a clean gas
in the chamber too, the pumps must be able to reach a pressure a 100 times lower, which means
1 × 10−5 mbar.
The lower pressure ensures a very clean chamber and when the pressure later is
raised, it is only one percent of the gas in the chamber that is atmospheric air, while the rest will
be the desired gas. When the purity of the gas is suﬃcient high, it is possible with out big errors to
assume, that the desired gas is the only gas in the chamber, making it possible to make more precise
measurements.
As the desired pressure is far below atmospheric pressure, a single pump is not capable to reach
the desired pressure.
Therefore we use two pumps in a series combination to lower the pressure
suﬃciently. First a roughing pump brings the pressure down to 1 × 102 mbar. Thereafter a turbo
pump takes over, making it possible to reach the desired pressure. It is important that the turbo
pump is powerful enough to counter act the diﬀerent sources that try to bring the pressure back up.
This includes possible leaks, diﬀusion through the rubber membranes and out-gassing from the steel

At ﬁrst the mounted turbo pump was too small and too weak to achieve this, meaning the lowest

this pressure, eﬀectively making changes on the chamber a time costly aﬀair. The pump was therefore

3.1.3
Mass ﬂow controller
To control the gas input and at the same time the pressure in the chamber, a mass ﬂow controller
is used. It controls a small valve with a sensor on, making it possible to control the amount of gas
being put into the chamber. It is manually controlled and has a display to tell how much gas is
put into the chamber, which works best between 10 % and 90 % of its maximum. Even within this
range, the controller is imprecise at best, and acts more as an indicator rather than an exact value.
In combination to this, it reacts slow, making it time consuming to control the ﬂow. Because of this
it is diﬃcult to change it to the wanted value, and it often take some tries before it sets on the desired
value. It is of less concern than what one might think, as it is more important to be able to control
the pressure than the actual gas ﬂow. It does, however, impact the ability to control the pressure, as
actual controlling the ﬂow is a matter of ﬁne feeling more than anything.
A way to improve the set-up, would be to make it remote controlled, as at the moment one has to
walk past the fusor in order to reach the controller, which makes it diﬃcult to control the pressure
during or in-between runs. This is time consuming as well as a potential risk hazard, as there might
be a far higher dose of radiation close to the fusor during operation.
3.1.4
Pressuremeter
The pressuremeter ensures us, that we are in the desired operating conditions, and at the same time
deliver valuable data when performing measurements. These two criteria demands two diﬀerent preci-
sion levels. If we are only interested in what the pressure approximately is and not too small changes,
the pressuremeter does not need to be very precise. This is ideal in the situation, as the fusor is
brought back to atmospheric pressure, or as the pressure is lowered to the desired levels. But this is
not what we want, as one of the only parameters we can change in the fusor, and also one of the easy
measurable parameters, is the pressure, and we therefore very much want a precise pressuremeter,
both to show exact pressures and to register and measure small changes.
On the fusor we use two diﬀerent pressuremeters, one for high pressures (atmospheric) and one for low
pressures [17]. The one for high pressures is a convection pressure gauge and the one for low pressures
is a cold cathode gauge. The reason that we need two diﬀerent pressuremeters is because measuring
pressure has to be done diﬀerently depending on how much gas interacts with the meter. At high
pressures, there are enough gas molecules and low enough mean free paths for the sensor to work on
principles based on high number of gas molecules. But as the pressure drops, the amount of gas goes
down and the mean free path becomes greater, and it gets harder to get reliable readings from those
21
==PAGE:29==
CHAPTER 3. CHAMBER SET-UP AND FUSOR EQUIPMENT
same principles. For example, at atmospheric pressure, it is possible to use convection as an indirect
way of measuring the pressure, but at low pressures, there simply is not enough interactions between
the gas molecules, rendering convection useless as method of measuring pressure. At low pressures
it is possible to ionise the molecules with a hot wire, and by counting the number of ionisations the
measurement can be translated into a pressure. This does not work at high pressures, as the hot wire
would start to burn and get ruined.
 The convection gauge works by using a thermocouple. Whenever a gas molecule hits a heated
ﬁlament and carries away some energy. As the meter feeds a constant current through the ﬁla-
ment, the temperature of the ﬁlament will change and hence the voltage across the thermocouple
changes. This change is then measured, where after the pressure can be calculated. At low pres-
sures the amount of energy removed from the ﬁlament can be calculated by the number of gas
molecules, but at higher pressures one has to take convection terms into account. By doing so,
the pressuremeter has a range from atmospheric pressure down to 1 × 10−2 mbar. The disad-
vantage of this type of pressuremeter is the gas dependency, as each gas has diﬀerent thermal
conductivities. It is therefore best at noting repeating pressure events but has diﬃculties mea-
suring absolute pressures.
 The cold cathode gauge works by ionizing gas molecules. It uses an anode and a cathode
to ionize gas molecules and capture ions. By having a discharge between the anode and the
cathode electrons are produced, and then kept conﬁned by a small magnetic ﬁeld surrounding
the gauge. As the electrons hit the gas molecules, they ionize some of them, which then starts
moving towards the anode, creating a current. This current is measured and then converted
to a pressure. The cold cathode gauge only works at low pressures, when the amount of gas
molecules is low enough such that convection terms does not play a signiﬁcant role. At the same
time we want to turn on the gauge at fairly high pressures, as it makes use of initial electrons to
create the starting current, and at low pressures, this can take relative long. The cold cathode
gauge has the same drawback as the convection gauge, as it is highly gas dependent. Diﬀerent
gas molecules will have diﬀerent ionization properties and one therefore needs to make recent
calibration for the exact speciﬁed gas in order to receive precise measurements. It is also very
sensitive to ﬂow changes, as high ﬂows will result in lower amount of ions, which the gauge
will see as a lower pressure than what actually is. These two ﬂaws can make it diﬃcult to get
absolute pressure measurements.
For the experiments the pressuremeters were both used to set the needed pressure for operation
conditions, and as an adjustable parameter. When simply used to setting the operation conditions,
only the cold cathode was used, as it range spans the operation conditions mostly used in the fusor.
When they were used to measure the pressure exact as it was one of the parameters adjusted, both
pressuremeters were used. This meant an overlapping area, in which there was some non-agreement
between the two pressuremeters. This was expected, as this area was in the outer range of what
was possible for both meters, and it was adjusted for, by letting each meter start in a more suitable
working area and then slowly going to the outer range.

X-ray spectroscope
To determine the x-ray spectrum coming from the fusor a x-ray spectroscope was used. The model

3.1.7
The grid
The grid is one of the essential parts of the fusor, as it acts as the cathode. As the cathode is applied
up to 60 kV, it has to be made of a high conducting material, as it would otherwise start to melt. At
the same time, the material has to be very durable with a high melting point, as it will start to heat
up once the fusor is running. This happens because of the ions hitting the grid, and thus transferring
their energy to the grid, which means that even great conductors will start to heat up. The ion heating
also shows the necessity of a highly transparent grid, as every time an ion hits the grid, it is lost and
can not make a fusion reactoin, essentially lowering the potential output.
As work on the fusor began, the grid was suspended from a two part hook, one part made of tungsten,
and one part made of copper. During initial runs on the fusor, the grid and the hook started to glow
red hot as a result of ions hitting them, and thus heating them. Looking at Fig. 3.4, we notice both
how the grid glows red, and how it lights up, especially around connections, indicating that the ions
hit here more. Because copper starts to melt far sooner than tungsten, and because it would start to
get soft even sooner, the copper hook was exchanged with a new one made of tungsten.
After a further series of runs, another problem with the grid became clear. As the voltage feed through
comes down the top of the fusor, there is a small length where the distance between the hook and the
chamber walls is shorter than the distance between the grid and the walls. This problem became clear
the moment we started to go to high voltages (higher than 40 kV). When this voltage and higher was
applied, arching started to happen inside the fusor. It was not possible to observe where the arching
exactly took place, but it was clear that it was happening between some part of the grid/hook system
and the wall. This conclusion was drawn after it was noticed, that the grid started to oscillate the
moment arching started to occur, and it was roughly the same frequency as that of the arching. In
order to combat this, and thus making it possible to reach even higher voltages, a quartz tube was
put in as an isolator. It covered the feed through wire from where it enters the fusor and all the
way down to the grid. With this modiﬁcation, the arching stopped, but another issue showed up.
Apparently the wire had function as part of the cathode, and as such, because of the shorter distance
between chamber and cathode, it had acted as a starting point for the plasma. Without it, it now
became diﬃcult to form a stable plasma at lower pressures, which almost cancelled out the eﬀect of
the isolator. The only reason why the isolator was not removed as this problem emerged was, that
it was possible to create high voltages plasmas, it simply took a long time to form. We remember
that breakdown voltage happens as soon as a cascade of electrons and ions are produced, and they
form a current between anode and cathode. When high voltages were applied on the fusor after the
insulator was installed, only very few arching events was happening. If the voltage was kept on for
a suﬃcient amount of time, the frequency of the arching would become higher and higher, and then
suddenly form the plasma. This could happen, because the arching produced more electrons and ions,
essentially helping the breakdown voltage on its way.
Before the insulator was installed, unusually high radiation levels was measured with the Geiger-M¨uller
counter. In order to determine where these radiation levels came from, the x-ray spectroscope was
used to measure x-ray radiation levels during arching conditions.

Safety
Throughout the project, safety has played a big part. It has been set at a high priority from the
beginning, making sure that no one will get hurt, neither those who carry out experiments, but
also not those sitting in rooms adjacent to the fusor room. At the same time, safety has also been
determining how soon experiments could be carried out, as radiation levels from x-rays had to be
under a certain level, before neutron production could even start.
Everybody who wants to control the fusor has to be approved to do so, and has to be around a number
of times when the fusor is started to see how it is done, and try it out under supervision. This is to
make sure, that everyone operating the fusor knows what they are doing, and further limits the risk
of accidents.
4.1
High voltage
High voltage safety is probably the most important of all the safety measures, and should not be taken
lightly even though it might seem trivial. The reason for this is, that high voltage can potentially kill
someone very quickly if they do something very wrong or act carelessly This means, that high voltage
safety is highest priority. Luckily, it is perhaps also the easiest to take safety measures against.
The ﬁrst thing one needs to make sure, is that no one can touch the fusor when it is running. This
can be done either by having it in a locked room by itself or having a safety zone around it, in which
no one may or can walk into. We have chosen the latter, and put up a cage around the fusor (see Fig.
4.1), such that it is not possible to touch the fusor directly. This solution was made, as it makes it
easier to get to the fusor when it is not running, and also it brings the option to visually observe the
fusor while running, making it possible to observe any potential dangers. The cage is also far enough
away from the fusor such that no arching between the cage and the fusor can happen. In order to make
sure, that no one accidentally enters the cage while the fusor is running, the door has been connected
to a switch lock. This is making sure that there can be no voltage on the fusor as long as the door is
not closed, and also shutting down the voltage supply the moment someone opens the door.
Right when the voltage is shut oﬀ, the voltage on the fusor does not drop to zero right away, as this
takes some time. To make sure there is no voltage left on the fusor before touching it, a ground pole
is used to touch the fusor beforehand, and thereafter connecting the ground pole to the wire going
from the voltage supply to the fusor, making sure no voltage can be applied while work is done on the
fusor. The ground pole also works when the cage is closed, making it important to remember, as the
fusor otherwise will not be able to run.
The fusor itself also needs to be protected from eventual voltage spikes, and a resistor bank is hence
connected in series to the voltage supply for the fusor. This is also makes it easier to control the voltage
inside the fusor, as some of the applied voltage will go to the resistor bank. The only small downside
of this, is we have to remember to subtract the voltage across the resistor bank when determining the
26
==PAGE:34==

voltage inside the fusor.
The last extra safety feature applied to the high voltage is to make sure the voltage is never accidentally
turned on. This is ensured by several steps, that have to be done in turn before it is possible to turn the
fusor on. First the power plug to the voltage controller has to be inserted, and then turned on. Then
the voltage controller itself has to be turned on. When this is done, the switch lock has to be turned
on and activated. We are now ready to start the computer program that controls the voltage, where a
button in the program needs to be switch on. As this is done, a last button on the voltage controller
has to be turned on, and ﬁnally we can start applying voltage to the fusor. The long procedure is
part that long in order to avoid accidents. The more steps one has to run through before being able
to apply voltage, the less risk there is to accidentally apply voltage at a wrong time, making the risk
of accidents occurring as small as possible.

4.3
Neutrons
Neutron radiation coming from the fusor is potentially the hardest to protect us from. This is due to
the very nature of neutrons. As they are not charged particles they rarely interact with matter, which
means they have a very small cross section when interacting with most materials. Some materials even
work as a multiplicator, eﬀectively increasing the radiation level beyond that material. We are looking
for a moderator that can satisfy all our needs, both in how good it slow down or absorb neutrons, but
also how easy the material is to handle.
The two most eﬀective ways to get protection from neutrons is either by using a material with a high
neutron cross section or to be far away (using the inverse square law as a safety). Both scenarios
have pros and cons. If distance to the fusor is chosen as the only safety measure, it becomes nearly
impossible to have the fusor in a building where people work in, as the distance simply become too
great. This means, that the fusor would have to be put in a building by itself, limiting the interaction
with the fusor. By using a material it is possible to limit the radiation level to a suitable level even
when we are close to the fusor.
The problem here is choosing the right material, which depends
on a series of factors. It has to be easy to handle and install, not toxic (or if so, easy to protect
against), how likely are the neutrons to interact with the given matter, and how much will they slow
down after each interaction. We will look at the last two factors, as these will narrow down our ﬁeld

helium and tritium. The He3 produced causes no worries, but the tritium has to be treated with care,

into the human body, potentially harming it. Even outside the body it can be harmful, meaning care
has to be taken when handling tritium. The tritium produced in the fusor chamber can go two places.
It can stick to the inside of the fusor, potentially making the inside chamber highly radioactive. The
other place it can go is into the pump and out of the chamber, where it will be pumped into the
outside air. This is the best outcome, as the amount of tritium produced is relative low, meaning it
will be of no harm when mixed with the air outside. No matter where the tritium does ends up, the
outcome has to be investigated when fusion is occurring, making sure the chamber does not become
a radioactive safety hazard.

Summery

fusor, especially the x-ray safety. Because most of the high voltage safety was already set up before
starting on the thesis, the safety regarding high voltages was more focussed on maintaining a high
safety standard, as high voltages potentially can be life threatening. Therefore, some eﬀort has been
put into learning to handle the high voltages. As there were no surprises, a certain routine as soon
made, making it possible to do without much thought.
While this was carried out, work on the x-ray safety had begun, and it was underestimated how big

5.1
Break-down voltage
The ﬁrst thing we wanted to measure, was at which conditions breakdown voltage occurred in the
chamber, so we could predict the pressure and voltage needed for future experiments. It also lets us
see how well the Paschen curve ﬁts with our measurements, and how well our assumptions in this
regard are, as we are far from having to plate electrodes with a symmetric electrical ﬁeld between
them. We will also look at how the distance of the breakdown voltage impact our results, as the
distance is unknown. By looking at the measured Paschen curve, it might be possible to ﬁnd the
distance at which the breakdown inside the fusor occurs.
The measurements were made in two parts. First the pressure was lowered below the value at which

Discussion
Looking at the results, some of them are matching the theory more than others, which was to be
expected. We also got some results that do not match the theory at all, making it diﬃcult at best to
explain what is going on, or what might be the cause.
6.1
Measured Paschen curve
When looking at Fig. 5.1 and at Fig. 5.2, there certainly is a discrepancy between the measured
curve and the theoretical curve. One explanation for this, is that the theoretical one is based on two
plate electrodes close to each other, and taking advantage of a high level of symmetry, regarding both
electric potential and shape of the electrodes. The fusor is nothing like that. First, the grid dictates
a lower level of symmetry both regarding shape and potential. Because of the shape of the grid, the
electrical potential can not be fully symmetrical, as there is a gradient in the ﬁeld between the rings
of the grid. This could explain the oﬀshape of the curve.
The need for a larger voltage for breakdown conditions at the minimum of the measured curve can
be also be explained by the form of the grid. It can also have something to due with the thin grid,
as a smaller surface area means a smaller area where ions interact with the grid, and thus producing
fewer electrons. By having fewer electrons available, a higher voltage has to be applied for breakdown
conditions to occur, and thereby raising the entire curve.
This could also explain why the right side of the curve is as ﬂat as it is. Because of the lower amount
of electrons available, a higher pressure would mean more free electrons. Even though the electrons
are lost more rapidly as the mean free path get larger, it seems like the eﬀect of more free electrons
cancel this out.
The impact of the distance on the measurements is, that we do not know the true distance at which
breakdown occurs. And by looking at our results, we can not determine it either. The four plotted
distances all describe a possible solution. The distances of 16 cm and 20 cm matches the distance
from the side of the grid to the side of the, and from the top of the grid to the top of the chamber,
respectively (assuming the grid is perfectly in the center of the chamber).
The problem here lies
in determining which one is the correct distance, or are we even talking about a single distance?
Furthermore, the chamber has some old inlets at the bottom, making it possible for the distance to
be even shorter than the 16 cm.
On the positive side, we can see, that the measurements follow the trends of the theoretical Paschen
curve. This means, that the theory can still be used to roughly estimate the breakdown voltage at
certain pressures, making it easier to start the fusor at the right settings from the start.
A last thing making the Paschen curve showed, was how much the fusor relies on its pressure gauges.
As the measurements where made, the pressure gauges had to take turns in two diﬀerent pressure
regimes in order to make the Paschen curve. That means, that no matter how good the results might

6.2
Bremsstrahlung spectrum
Bremsstrahlung coming from the fusor is mostly radiated out through the window, which is why we
chose to place the detector there. This also made it possible to measure the lower energy photons,
as the window has a higher transmission for low energy photons than the iron. Looking ﬁrst at Fig.
5.3a, we see clear indications, that it is indeed a bremsstrahlung spectrum we are looking at. First of
all, the highest measured photon energy matches exactly the applied voltage. This means, that the
highest energy the electrons could have had also matches the applied voltage. Because they have been
accelerated to the highest energy they most have moved along the full potential, making it very likely,
that they have interacted with the chamber wall.
The second thing that indicates a bremsstrahlung spectrum, is that all the spectra follow the same
trend, regardless what the applied voltage is. We would expect this trend, because as the maximum
energy goes up, so does the average energy, which is exactly what we observe.
Looking at Fig. 5.3b, we see that the adjusted x-ray spectrum even more looks like a bremsstrahlung
spectrum. A bremsstrahlung spectrum where there is no absorption, will be a straight line moving
down from left to right, meaning that most interactions between electrons and the nuclei only slow
the electrons a small amount, and full stop happens much more rarely.
The problem we are facing with these measurements is, that we have a hard time saying anything about
the energy distribution of the electrons before they hit the chamber wall. A normal bremsstrahlung
spectrum is made from a single energy beam of electrons, but in our case we have a continuous energy
distribution of the electrons before they even start to interact with the iron nuclei, which applies a new
distribution on top of the new one. However, if it is possible to model the bremsstrahlung spectrum,
ﬁrst as a function of a single energy beam, and then model the spectrum as a function of multiple
energies, it might be possible to calculate the energy distribution of the electrons inside the chamber.
It does rely on a lot of ”ifs”, but it could be a way to use the x-ray spectrum.
We now look at Fig. 5.4, where we see a peculiar peak. If only a single applied voltage would make
a small peak here, it would be disregarded as noise. But with eight diﬀerent applied voltages having
a peak at the same energy, something is going on. The problem is, I do not know what it could be.
Neither iron nor the borosilicate of the window has energy lines at this energy. It could be due to
some resonances in the equipment, causing it to register x-rays. Another possibility is, that it could
be a small source outside the chamber, and only when nothing else is measured does it show up, as
it is very weak. This is very unlikely, but can not be fully disregarded, before it has been determined
where the peak comes from.
We now look at Fig. 5.5, where we wanted to see, whether there were any high energy x-rays created
as arching in the fusor occured. This was not the case, and was even not possible to measure any
x-rays through the lead shielding (except for the unexplained peak around 19.5 keV). This could mean,
that the early measurement that showed much more x-ray radiation when arching occurred, was a
result of a changing electrical ﬁeld. As the arc in the fusor comes and goes at a rapid rate, it will also
change the electrical ﬁeld at the same rate. These ﬂuctuation can interfere with the electronics in the
equipment, and make it believe a lot of x-rays are measured. This is a possible explanation, as we can
exclude high energy x-rays, that penetrate the lead.
What we can also see, is that even by having a tiny amount of plasma in short periods of time, the
amount of x-rays increases very rapidly. This tells us, that there is a clear correlation between the
current in the chamber and the radiation. This was expected, as the electrons are the current carriers
as well as the producers of the x-rays.
The last ﬁgure, Fig. 5.6, shows how the x-rays are emitted through the window. The two ﬁrst things
we notice, is how the radiation level gets lower as the angle grows larger, and how wide the measure-
ments are scattered. It is almost impossible to predict a real trend other than the radiation gets lower

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The goal of the thesis was to determine the conditions for which breakdown occurs, and to look at
how electrons in the fusor behave. By using Paschen’s law, it has been possible to get a sense of, what
the conditions for breakdown are. Because of the odd geometry of the grid and the chamber, while
also being limited by pressuremeters working at the limit of their range, the conditions for breakdown
has not been fully established. In order to do so, a pressuremeter that spans the full pressure range
is needed, as well as having to clear out the bottom of the chamber, making sure there are no shorter
distance than from the side of the grid to the side of the chamber.
The electrons are heavily inﬂuenced by both the voltage on the grid, but also the pressure in the
chamber. As the mean free path grows smaller, the electrons also increase in number, making the
Paschen curve ﬂattens out as higher pressures are reached. The electrons are also determining the
x-ray radiation, from which the highest energies of the electrons can be found. With more work, it
might be possible to determine the electron energy distribution.
The fusor has almost made it to the state, where fusion reactions can begin. X-ray radiation is no
longer a concern, which means only some neutron shielding is needed before it can start to do fusion.
7.1
Future work and Outlook
Several improvements for the fusor can be made, starting by installing shielding against neutrons.
After that, work can either begin with making it easier and smoother to operate the fusor, or by
making improvements to the chamber itself. Removing the inlet at the bottom of the chamber would
help when determining the breakdown voltage.
A way to get rid of arching and at the same time not have to apply huge voltages for the plasma to
start, would be to install a small tungsten rod, that can be heated. By doing so, more free electrons
would be available, thus making it easier to obtain plasma.
Another project for the future might be, to see if the plasma density can be determined. By using
reﬂectometry or interferometry it might be possible.
The fusor is an excellent tool to teach and learn about fusion energy. It is so simple, that with only a
basic understanding, it is possible to carry out experiments, and see diﬀerent phenomenon take place.
At the same time is it possible to spend huge amount of time on a single problem, which makes the fusor
great for student projects, bachelor and master thesis’s, and possibly use it for testing complicated or
expensive equipment, before putting it to use on a large fusion reactor. The possibilities are many,
and the fusor might be able to handle it all.
