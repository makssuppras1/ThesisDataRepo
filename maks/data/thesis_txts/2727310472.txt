Abstract
Capacitive micromachined ultrasound transducers (CMUTs) are a developing technology
with many applications, especially within medical imaging. Work is progressing to construct
large 2D arrays, which will allow for high-resolution 3D-imaging within the human body.
One method to realize the required large arrays is row-column addressed (RCA) arrays,
which require highly conducting electrodes to maintain the signal across the long elements.
Gold is therefore a suitable material for these electrodes.
Several challenges have been encountered when developing gold electrodes, including gold
ribbons formed from ion beam etching (IBE) and highly disruptive vertical short-circuits.
In this project, these two issues are investigated.
It was found that replacing the IBE with a KI-based wet etch was not feasible, but that
ribbons could be effectively removed using a 30 second dip in a 10% solution of Transene
GE-8110, a commercial KI-based etchant.
The defects related to the vertical short-circuits were investigated, and found to be
significantly reduced in locations with a protective nitride layer. Additionally, these defects
were not present when no voltage was applied during an anodic bond, though Au-Si eutectic
formations with a similar presentation appeared when temperatures exceeded the eutectic
point of 363 ◦C. A potential mechanism is proposed that could explain the short-circuit
defects through localized joule heating at gold protrusions during anodic bonding, causing
a localized eutectic to form.

1
Introduction
A capacitive micromachined ultrasound transducer (CMUT) is a type of ultrasound transducer
that relies on a plate-capacitor structure. When a voltage is applied across the two plates, a
force will be induced. If the top plate is sufficiently thin, it will be flexible enough to deflect.
Applying the correct electrical signal on a device with the right resonance frequency can allow
operation at ultrasonic frequencies, causing emission of ultrasound, as seen in Fig. 1.
Such devices can be used to both transmit and receive ultrasound, and are usually designed for
the purpose of imaging, especially medical imaging. When used for imaging, the transmitted
ultrasound waves will be reflected from the samples, and these reflected waves then cause the
flexible top plate of the CMUT to deflect.
This causes a change in capacitance, which can
be measured with external electronics [11][10]. Reflection of the incident ultrasound waves are
caused by a mismatch in acoustic impedance, and different e.g. organs in the body can all cause
an ultrasound echo. Medical ultrasound imaging has become widely used, due to its fast, cheap
and non-invasive nature.
CMUTs were developed as an alternative to earlier ultrasound transducers, generally based on
piezo-electric thick-film transducers. These utilize the voltage-induced deformation that occurs
in piezo-electric materials to transmit ultrasound. Compared to these, CMUTs can have greater
bandwidths and higher sensitivity, allowing for better imaging[11]. Additionally, CMUT fab-
rication is based on conventional microfabrication techniques, creating the possibility of more
complicated designs, such as linear or 2D arrays.
Figure 1: A diagram of a simplified anodically bonded CMUT design. The gold bottom electrodes
are separated from the polysilicon top electrodes, which also composes the bulk of the flexible
top plate. Separating the two plates is a vacuum gap, with borofloat (pyrex) as a dielectric
structural material. Applying an alternating current across the two electrodes causes the top
plate to flex, emitting ultrasound.
Such arrays allow for more complicated imaging regimes, with 2D arrays enabling 3D-imaging
[11][10]. While 3D imaging does come with potential advantages for medical diagnostics, the 2D
arrays also unsurprisingly present fabrication challenges. One challenge comes from the large
arrays necessary in order to achieve high resolution. If each cell in an N × N array is addressed
individually, with electrical connections to the top and bottom electrodes, 2N 2 connectors would
be necessary. One example of a large array could be 190 × 190 cells, which requires over 72000
connections. The necessary number of connections for individually addressed large arrays would
be infeasible to fabricate. Instead, the top and bottom electrodes can be addressed in rows and
columns separately, effectively constructing the 2D array as a series of orthogonal linear arrays,
illustrated in Fig. 2. Such a structure would be called a row-column addressed (RCA) array, and
requires only N + N electrical connections, a huge reduction for large arrays. A RCA-array has
been found to always have a better resolution than fully populated arrays with the same number
of elements. This means a 128 + 128 = 256 RCA-array has 256 elements and better resolution
than 16 × 16 = 256 fully populated array, which also has 256 elements [10][5].
An issue that is then presented with the advent of large row-column addressed arrays, is that
of electrode resistance. High frequencies are necessary to achieve high resolution imaging, and

these frequencies place constraints upon the geometries of the device. This limits the possible
size of the CMUT electrodes, which in turn means that the electrical connections that span
the length of each row or column in an array are long and narrow, creating the opportunity
for significant resistance if the electrode material is not conductive enough. If the resistance is
elevated, the long elements may behave as low-pass filters, due to their capacitive connection
to the other electrodes. This causes the pressure of the emitted sound waves to drop along
the length of the elements, which causes lower quality images [8][10]. The primary reason for
this issue, is that many CMUTs are constructed using doped silicon as the conductive electrode
material. With a resistivity that can go down to 10−4 Ωcm depending on doping, doped silicon
is usually good enough, but metal electrodes on the order of 10−6 Ωcm would be preferable.
However, metal electrodes are not compatible with high-temperature processes, such as fusion
bonding, which requires a temperature on the order of 1000 ◦C, and is necessary to bond the top
plate to the bottom for many CMUT designs. For this reason, anodically bonded CMUTs have
been investigated, as anodic bonding does not require as high temperatures as fusion bonding.
It also has the added advantage of being less sensitive to particles and contaminants than fusion
bonding, in order to achieve a good bond [8][10].
This project is primarily based upon the earlier work completed by the MEMS Applied Sensors
group at DTU, which has been focused on realizing high quality RCA anodically bonded CMUTs.
During fabrication of these, other challenges have been encountered. Among these are issues
involving the creation of ”ribbons” during patterning of the gold electrodes, as well as short-
circuits between the top and bottom electrodes [10]. The primary purpose of this project has
been to investigate these issues, and attempt to solve them.
In the following report, a description of the fabrication process of row-column addressed CMUT
arrays can be found in Section 2. In Section 3, the etching of gold is investigated and optimized
for removal of ribbons.
Finally, the different defect types found on the gold electrodes are
investigated in Section 4, including the defect type associated with short circuits between the
top and bottom electrodes. A summary of the conclusions of this report is laid out in Section 5,
along with suggestions for design and process changes, as well as additional investigations that
may be performed.

2
CMUT design
The CMUT designs used in this project were based on previous designs developed and iterated
upon in the MEMS group. This means that the most important parameters for the design of
a CMUT were the same as those chosen for the TH1 project [10]. These parameters influence
the behaviour of the CMUT during operation, and include things like the vacuum gap between
the electrode and the plate, the width of the cavity, and the thickness of the plate. The cavity
width and plate thickness are both important geometrical parameters that - together with the
material parameters of the plate - define the mechanical resonance frequency of the CMUT. They
also influence how much the membrane flexes, which in turn influences the emitted pressure.
Combining this with the vacuum gap, the pull-in voltage is defined as the voltage across the
capacitor at which the electrostatic attraction of the bottom electrode surpasses the possible
mechanical restoring force of the plate, causing the plate to be pulled into contact with the
bottom electrode. The selected dimensions have been a 37 µm cell width and a 33 µm electrode,
with a 135 nm vacuum gap and a plate thickness of 2.5 µm. Additionally, an oxynitride layer
beneath the polysilicon prevents a short-circuit between the polysilicon plate and gold electrode
during pull-in. This nitride layer is 200 nm thick. These dimensions correspond to a CMUT
that would operate around 8 MHz and with a pull-in voltage of 187 V [10]. The geometry of the
device is illustrated on figure Fig. 3.
Though the bulk of the CMUT and array designs were identical to previous work, some minor
design changes were implemented in this project. These include wider interconnects between the
bottom electrodes in each element, replacing the square shape of some CMUTs with a circular
shape, and adding larger contact pads for wire bonding. Beyond this, minor cosmetic changes
such as additional identification marks and fiducials were also added. These changes have little
influence on the actual investigations performed during the project, as these were primarily
concerned with the behaviour of the gold electrodes.
2.1
Fabrication
Much like the design itself was based on previous designs, the planned process flow for CMUT
fabrication was also based on the process flow that had been developed for anodically bonded

RCA CMUT arrays by the MEMS group. An overview of the most important steps involved in
the fabrication can be seen on Fig. 4.
Summarized, the fabrication begins with a borofloat wafer, where lithography is used to define
the areas in which buffered hydrofluoric acid (BHF) is used to etch the cavities. After this, gold
is deposited upon the wafer, and patterned so that it only remains within the cavities, making
up the bottom electrodes and interconnects. The wafer is then aligned with the top wafer, a
polysilicon on insulator (PSOI) wafer with an additional protective oxynitride layer. This wafer
is fabricated separately, and the oxynitride has been patterned with holes where the contact
pads to the bottom electrodes are located. After alignment, the wafers are anodically bonded
together. During anodic bonding, the wafers are forced together at an elevated temperature (300
- 500 ◦C), and a bias voltage is applied across the wafer stack. In this project, a bias voltage
of 400 V has been used. The elevated temperature increases the mobility of the ions in the
borofloat, and primarily Na+ ions leave the interface, creating a negatively charged depletion
region. This creates a strong electrostatic force between the conducting silicon an the depletion
region, adhering the wafers together. This then facilitats the forming of a permanent bond [10].
After the anodic bonding, the top wafer is thinned down through several different etching steps to
remove the different layers, and the final polysilicon layer is then used as the plate. Aluminium
is deposited and patterned, and used as a mask to define where the polysilicon is opened to
access the contact pads for the bottom electrode. Note that while it is not depicted on Fig. 4,
there is a cavity between the electrode and contact pads, depicted on the left and right of the
figure respectively. This cavity includes a gold interconnect, which is how electrical connection
is established between the contact pads and the electrodes. Since it is desired that the CMUT
cavities are in vacuum, a thick aluminium layer is deposited, plugging the hole into the inter-
connect cavity that leads to the primary CMUT cavity. Since this is done within the vacuum of
the E-beam deposition, the desired vacuum in the cavities is achieved. Finally, the aluminium
and underlying polysilicon is patterned, separating the top electrodes and the contact pads.
While all these steps are necessary for a functioning device, processing for this project did not
proceed beyond the anodic bonding, as the behaviour of the gold electrodes was investigated
instead. All fabrication and cleanroom work for this project was performed in DTU Nanolab.
3
Etching Gold
Previous work in the MEMS group encountered difficulties with gold ”ribbons”. These have a
thickness on the order of a few nanometers, and are formed during the ion beam etching (IBE)
of gold, when the gold particles removed by the ion beam are redeposited on the sides of the
photoresist [10]. After the photoresist is removed, the gold remains as a thin fence around the
edges of the remaining gold. As these are fragile, they can easily be knocked off, creating a
ribbon of gold that is not attached to anything. These long, conductive particles could present
many potential problems, depending on where they end up.
To limit the adverse impacts of these particles, previous projects used a short dip in the standard
gold etchant available in the DTU cleanroom, a potassium-iodide based etchant. This etching
solution, however, has a nominative etch rate of 100 nm/min [16], meaning the time required
to etch through the nanometer-thick ribbons is on the order of a few seconds, making the etch
difficult to control. Additionally, this solution also etches the contacts themselves.
To solve this issue, a slower and more controlled etch is desired. If a sufficiently well-controlled
etchant is found, it could even be feasible to etch the gold contacts using a wet etch rather than
the ion beam, avoiding entirely the formation of ribbons. For this purpose, different options for
wet gold etchants are investigated, along with their advantages and disadvantages.

3.1
Investigation of Gold Etchants
KI-etch
Potassium-Iodide etch is the most commonly used gold etchant, as well as the standard used
in the DTU cleanroom. Chemically, it is composed of KI:I2:H2O, where the ratios between the
KI and I2 is usually 4:1 [1], with different amounts of water to dilute it to the desired strength.
The DTU standard etchant uses a 100g:25g:500ml formulation, resulting in an etch rate of 100
nm/min, but both faster and slower etch rates are often achieved.
There is good reason for KI-etch being popular - it is easy and safe to work with, achieves a rea-
sonable etch rate, and results in an acceptable finish. However, it can have a significant problem
with undercutting the gold layers it etches. A poorly understood electrochemical interaction
between the gold, the metallic adhesion layer that is commonly used for gold, and the etchant
itself can cause the gold to become etched significantly faster near the adhesion layer. This
means that if the gold layer is etched all the way through, the etchant starts aggressively eating
away at the bottom of the layer, where both the gold and the adhesion layer are in contact with
the solution [6].
Though this problem can be significant, many different commercial gold etchants advertise ”re-
duced undercutting” on their KI etchants [14].
This is due to additives that can alter the
characteristics of the etch, which are commonly used to e.g. eliminate etching of certain metals
[6].
Aqua Regia
Aqua Regia is mixture famous for its ability to etch gold. It is composed of hydrochloric and
nitric acid (HCl:HNO3), usually in a ratio of 3:1. This etchant is also available as a standard
in DTU Nanolab, though it is not commonly used for patterning purposes, due to its extremely
fast etch and difficulty masking. Instead, it is a good choice if the simple removal of all gold is
desired. In general, Aqua Regia has a very fast etch, which is not always very consistent [6][16].
HCN
Cyanide based etchants also exist, and seem to have excellent etch characteristics. Operating at
a similar etch rate as KI-etchants, they have no undercutting issue and also leave a smoother
surface. The main drawback of this etchant is the extreme toxicity of the cyanide, which can
escape through usage. For this reason, this etchant has only niche use cases [6].
Boron based etchants
Some work has been done with boron based etchants, showing etch characteristics extremely
similar to KI-etch, seemingly with the same undercutting issue. Additionally, it is significantly
more toxic [6].
Non-aqueous etchants
Most etching is done in aqueous solutions, but some non-aqueous etchants have been developed.
These are also fairly niche, and though they can have good etch characteristics, the fact that
they use a solvent such as acetone makes the usage of photoresist-based etch masks impossible
[6].
3.2
Gold etch optimization
Based on the information gathered from literature, it was determined that a KI-based etchant was
likely the best option. The main advantages considered were the reasonable etch characteristics,
the safe nature of the etch, and the etch being a known quantity, already approved for use in
Nanolab. The commercial etchant Au Etch 200 made by Microchemicals [13] was also considered,
but we were unable to get the necessary details for approval in Nanolab.
The primary challenges to the use of this etchant were perceived to be the relatively high etch
rate of Nanolab’s standard mixture, as well as the potential for undercutting due to the elec-
trochemical interaction with the adhesion layer. To combat these issues, a commercial KI-based

3.2.3

diluted, the etch rates were still extremely high, with the 10% mixture of GE-8110 having an

then accelerating the etch in the center once the edges had been etched all the way down. Finally,
a significant undercut of the photoresist was observed in many situations, and crucially the poor

4.1
Wafer Fabrication
The fabrication for the tests performed was based on the RCA CMUT array fabrication methods
presented in Section 2. Since it was the defects being investigated, and these defects were visible
through the bottom wafer immediately after bonding, there was no need to thin down the
PSOI, which would otherwise have been necessary for functioning CMUT arrays. Additionally,
while the bottom wafers utilized designs made for this project and were fabricated for it, the
PSOIs were fabricated as part of another project. While parameters such as oxynitride and
polysilicon thickness were as intended, the openings in the oxynitride on the PSOI were not
located appropriately for the structures on the bottom wafer. As these are not being finished as
proper CMUTs, this is acceptable.
To explain more fully the final few fabrication steps before the anodic bonding, a wafer immedi-
ately after ion-beam etching of the gold is considered. Such a wafer then has the cavities in the
borofloat, with the gold electrodes defined. The ribbon-removal step introduced in subsubsec-
tion 3.2.4 is then used, and the photoresist is stripped. Then, immediately prior to bonding, the
PSOI is subjected to an RCA1 clean to remove organic contaminants, and the bottom wafer is
subjected to a piranha cleaning for the same reason. This is intended to reduce the amount of
particles between the wafers and allow for a better bond. Since the gold layer uses a titanium
adhesion layer, the piranha clean is only of a few minutes, as solution etches away the titanium,
undercutting the gold. Some aspects of the design were not created with this in mind, as gold
interconnects existed that were sufficiently thin that the piranha undercut it completely, allow-
ing it to peel off. Examples of this can be seen on Fig. 25. Future designs should either use an
alternate cleaning method, an alternate adhesion layer, or ensure all structures are wide enough
to not risk total undercutting and delamination.

defect counts both with and without oxynitride originating from the same defect rate can be
found.
Here, the Poisson E-test is used to evaluate the probability of the defect rate being the same
within the cells with nitride as the cells without nitride, making it a coincidence that more
defects were found where there was no nitride.

Since it seems that temperature may be a factor in the formation of defects, a series of ”fake”
anodic bonds were performed, where no voltage was applied across the wafer stack. In this
way, it can be determined whether the voltage is necessary for the defects to occur. As only
the voltage is eliminated, any potential influence of e.g. the piston forcing the wafers together
remains. Additionally, the lack of voltage means that the wafers were not truly bonded, making
it possible to separate them afterwards for further investigations. In this fashion, 0 V bonds
were performed at 300 ◦C, 350 ◦C and 400 ◦C.
The first 0 V bond was performed at 350 ◦C, and microscope investigations revealed the presence
of several objects that resembled type 3 defects. An example is depicted on Fig. 29(b), where
a large black dot surrounded by a green aura is clearly visible. However, in contrast to the
type 3 defects that had previously been observed, these defects were mostly localized to a small
region of the wafer, and their central dark dots were much larger. Additionally, they were often
correlated with large amounts of particles beside them, as seen on Fig. 30.
Since the wafers were not truly bonded, they could easily be separated. This allowed images to
be taken of the surface of the gold electrodes, both using optical microscopy and SEM. With

Pinholes in a physical vapour deposition (PVD) thin film can occur for multiple reasons, and
one likely mechanism is a small particle or surface irregularity on the bottom of the deposited
layer, which creates a defect that propagates up through the layer. This has the potential to
create nodular defects, which form surface structures of the deposited metal that are much larger
than the original seed particle. If metal deposited on the side of the particles block metal from
being deposited in the sidewalls surrounding it, pinholes can also be created around the metal
structure growing from the seed particle [9].
That this is the origin of type 2 and 4 defects also suggests that comparison between the different
wafers was not as useful as desired.
Previous statistics showed that there was a significant
deviation between the defect rates of wafers that had been bonded at different temperatures, but
these defects were already present before the bonding. This means that the observed differences
in defect rate were not due to the temperature differences at the bonding, but rather just
differences in the wafers themselves.

As type 1, 2 and 4 defects all have proposed origins now, type 3 defects are investigated once
more. It had been considered whether these defects were due to eutectic formation between the
gold from the electrodes and the polysilicon in the plate. This eutectic refers to the fact that the
gold-silicon system shows a reduced melting point when mixed in a proportion of 19.5% gold. At
this proportion, the system melts at 363 ◦C [3]. Eutectic formation can cause voids and craters
similar to what was observed on Fig. 35, making it a possible candidate for the origin of the
defects [4].
To investigate what a eutectic forming would look like, a bond was attempted at 0 V and 400
◦C. The most significant result from this was a single type 3 defect being observed, displayed
on Fig. 41. To investigate whether that defect was eutectic in origin, a more extreme test was
performed. Using a PSOI wafer that had no nitride whatsoever, and keeping the wafer stack at
400 ◦C for longer, eutectic formation was very likely to happen.
Upon investigation, this more extreme experiment had defects that very much resembled type 3
defects, and also areas where eutectic formation had clearly occurred. The regions with eutectic
formation also presented with brighter green auras, consistent with the idea that this color
originates from a disruption to the gold structure as found earlier. Both large and small defects
can be found, with the smaller defects resembling type 3 defects more. The large defects show
clear signs of eutectic formations, with the fractal-like dendritic growths visible on the surface
being a common feature of Au-Si eutectics [7]. All these features can be seen on Fig. 42 and
Fig. 43.
It is very clear that all the observed features are due to eutectic formation. However, it is less
clear that this is the same effect as has created the usual type 3 defects found when doing proper
anodic bonds. For this reason, the defects that are certain to be eutectic are defined to be type
5 defects. Type 5 defects can present similarly to type 3 defects, but can also cover large areas
and create dendritic growths that have never been observed with type 3 defects. Determining
whether type 3 and type 5 defects are, in fact, of the same nature is desired.

Additionally, since Au-Si eutectics melt together at 363 ◦C, it should not occur when bonding
happens below these temperatures. So if type 3 defects are eutectic, there must be a reason that
this formation can occur.
One potential mechanism is that a lump on the gold electrodes caused by e.g. a nodular defect
causes the gold to be in contact with the layer above, and the voltage that is applied during
anodic bonding causes small current to run through this gold defect.
As the current runs
through, localized joule heating of the surroundings brings the area above the 363 ◦C eutectic
point, causing a very localized eutectic to form. As only a small region is heated sufficiently to
form the eutectic, it does not spread as much, and does form the dendritic growths.
That mechanism only functions where there is no protective nitride layer, as this would prevent
contact between the gold and polysilicon. In these regions, the same conditions would cause the
gold lump to be in contact with the nitride, causing a high voltage to lie across that layer, which
could potentially be sufficient to cause electrical breakdown. During electrical breakdown, a
filament joining the anode and cathode is often formed, which would allow the same conduction
of current, localized joule heating, and finally eutectic formation as in the nitrideless case.
One indication that the eutectic-based type 5 defects are the same as type 3 defects are the SEM
pictures of the formed eutectics (seen on Fig. 44 and Fig. 45), that resemble the type 3 defects
that had previously been investigated with FIB-SEM (seen on Fig. 35).

5
Conclusion
The original two problems with gold electrodes that had been encountered in previous work have
been investigated. These problems both presented their own difficulties in investigations, and
had little to do with each other, beyond their relation to gold and to the fabrication of CMUTs.
5.1
Ribbon removal
With regards to the gold ribbons, the removal method of simply performing a quick dip in a 10%
solution of GE-8110 is effective and simple. However, the KI-based etches that were investigated
had etch characteristics too fast and not sufficiently predictable to be able to replace the IBE
with a wet etch. While etchants based on other chemistry may potentially show superior results,
these are likely to be significantly more niche. Luckily, the ease of simply removing the ribbons
renders it unnecessary to find an option for the wet etch.
There is certainly more to learn about the investigated etchants. The anisotropic etch profile
that was consistently observed, where it seemed the etchant started out slow, before rapidly
tunnelling through the layer at the edges of the cavities, and then swiftly removing the rest
of the gold in the center of the cavities, was not found when searching literature. This could
certainly be investigated, especially a test of an etch with no adhesion layer could be interesting.
This could then determine for certain whether the etch profile is due to any sort of galvanic
interaction, or if something else is at play. No matter what, the non-uniformity combined with
the undercutting of the mask are sufficient that it seems a bad idea to use KI-based etchants for
the purposes of CMUT electrode patterning, even if the etch profile were better understood.
5.2
Short-circuiting defects
The defects that were correlated with the short-circuits that had previously been encountered
were investigated, along with other types of defects discovered. The 5 defect types that were
discovered were:
Type 1 A dark spot
Type 2 A dark spot surrounded by a gold outline
Type 3 A dark spot with a green aura
Type 4 A golden spot
Type 5 A eutectic formation, has a green aura
Of these, type 3 was the type correlated with short-circuits. It was determined that type 2 and
4 are types on pinhole defects, that allow the cleaning piranha to flow through the gold layer
and etch away at the titanium adhesion layer, creating the golden outline. The green aura was
instead found to be caused by a disruptions in the gold structure, where even a surface disruption
can cause a color change when observed from below.
It was also found that a protective oxynitride layer drastically reduces the number of type 3
defects that occur, though they are not eliminated completely. Type 5 defects were observed to
resemble type 3, with a similar brighter, green aura surrounding them. However, no type 3 defects
have been as large as the type 5 defects, nor have they displayed any of the dendritic growths
that would characterize it as eutectic. Though the evidence is not conclusive, it is my proposal
that type 3 and 5 defects are in fact both the same, with the more localized type 3 defects being
caused by localized joule heating during the anodic bonding. This could occur if small growth
defects in the gold causes gold protrusions in contact with the top plate, potentially allowing

an electrical connection. This mechanism could also explain why the nitride layer reduces the
type 3 defect rate so much, as the defect would additionally require a breakdown of the nitride,
before it could form. This is also somewhat supported by SEM images showing structures in the
eutectic formed on a newly created sample that are similar to the cross-sectional images taken
of type 3 defects in previous work. However, this resemblance is purely structural, and says
nothing about how the defects were formed. Even if type 3 defects are eutectic in nature, their
formation mechanism could be different than the proposed one, which is purely hypothetical.
There are many more investigations that could be performed to attempt to resolve more of these
uncertainties. Initially, investigating the bottom of a gold film deposited on a newly purchased,
very clean wafer could help determine the origin of the particles that potentially cause the
protrusions on the surface. This could help determine whether it is earlier fabrication processes
that need to be cleaner, or if it is particles deposited during the gold deposition itself that are
the issue. Further steps could be taken to investigate the composition of the true short-circuiting
type 3 defects, as a composition of around 19.5 % gold to silicon would be a very good indication
that the defects are indeed eutectic.
If the proposed mechanism does adequately describe what is happening, multiple steps could be
taken to reduce the severity of the problem. If a thicker nitride layer was used, breakdown would
be less likely, preventing some defects. If another metal was used, that does not have form a
eutectic with silicon at temperatures that low, some of the problems could also be circumvented.
While the small conductive filaments that can form during dielectric breakdown would still
cause short-circuits, these would no longer induce the large-scale mixing of the bottom electrode
and the polysilicon. This could potentially then allow localized removal of the short through
”burning” with a high current, which is not feasible when the gold is completely mixed into the
polysilicon.
