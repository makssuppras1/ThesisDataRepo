Summary (English)
This thesis attempts to narrow the gap between high- and low-resource languages within speech
emotion recognition (SER) and propose techniques to mitigate the problem of data scarcity. One of
the main contributions is the creation of EmoTale; a corpus comprising 450 Danish and 350 English
speech recordings with associated emotion annotations. We investigate three research questions re-
lating to SER in the low-resource domain.
First, an investigation of input features to a linear support vector classiﬁer shows that Self-Super-
vised Speech Model (SSLM) embeddings beat hand-crafted features. The best model achieves an un-
weighted average recall (UAR) of 94.7% ± 3.9% on the Emo-DB corpus using leave-one-speaker-out
cross-validation. Furthermore, training on SSLM embeddings generally leads to better transferability
with cross-corpus UARs of up to 79%.
Secondly, we explore the use of Explainable Artiﬁcial Intelligence (XAI) methods in SER. We propose
two diﬀerent approaches to explain SER model predictions including an adaption of the Local Inter-
pretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME) algorithm. The method oﬀers great potential for easily
interpretable local explanations of black-box SER models.
Lastly, the EmoTale corpus was used to develop Danish SER models using state-of-the-art machine
learning. Recent advances in transformer-based architectures have shown promise in several ma-
chine learning tasks including SER. We investigate how the model performance depends on the
amount of training data to test the feasibility of diﬀerent ﬁne-tuning techniques in low-resource do-
main. We show that a 60% recognition rate can be achieved with just 330 training instances.

Resumé (Danish)
Denne afhandling forsøger at mindske kløften mellem sprog med høj og lav ressource inden for
følelsesgenkendelse fra tale (SER) og foreslå teknikker til at afhjælpe problemet med dataknaphed. Et
af hovedbidragene er skabelsen af EmoTale; et korpus, som omfatter 450 danske og 350 engelske op-
tagelser af tale med tilhørende følelsesannotationer. Vi undersøger tre forskningsspørgsmål ve-
drørende SER i lavressourcedomænet.
For det første viser en sammenligning af forskellige input variable til en lineær support vector classi-
ﬁer, at repræsentationer fra Self-Supervised Speech modeller (SSLM) overgår menneskeskabte attrib-
uter. Den bedste model opnår en vægtet prædikstionsrate (UAR) på 94,7% ± 3,9% for Emo-DB
datasættet ved brug af leave-one-speaker-out krydsvalidering. Derudover fører SSLM repræsenta-
tioner generelt til bedre modeloverførbarhed, og vi opnår op til 79% UAR på tværs af datasæt.
For det andet udforsker vi metoder for Explainable Artiﬁcial Intelligence (XAI) i SER. Vi foreslår to
forskellige tilgange til at forklare SER modellers forudsigelser, herunder en tilpasning af algoritmen
Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME). Metoden har potentiale til at producere let-
forståelige lokale forklaringer for black-box SER modeller.
Endelig bliver EmoTale brugt til at udvikle danske SER modeller ved hjælp af state-of-the-art
maskinlæring. Nylige fremskridt inden for transformer-baserede arkitekturer har vist lovende resul-
tater i ﬂere maskinlæringsopgaver, herunder SER. Vi undersøger, hvordan modellens ydeevne
afhænger af mængden af træningsdata for at teste brugbarheden af forskellige ﬁne-tuning teknikker
i lav-ressource SER. Vi viser, at en 60% nøjagtighed kan opnås med kun 330 træningseksempler.

1.1   Motivation and scope
This project is initiated with the broad objective of narrowing the gap between high- and low-resource
languages within speech emotion recognition (SER). The project will take inspiration from the recent
developments in SER and focus on how pre-trained models can be leveraged in the low-resource
domain to exploit the generic features learned from the source language. While multiple emotional
speech corpora exist for commonly spoken languages, there is a lack of functional datasets for a large
number of low-resource languages (LRLs), such as Danish. To our knowledge, the 30-year-old Danish
Emotional Speech (DES) corpus was the only available database of Danish emotional speech before
starting the project. Therefore, a Danish speech aﬀect corpus, named EmoTale, is developed comprising
Danish and English speech recordings and the associated aﬀect annotations, as described in the ethical
approval [2]. Apart from EmoTale, existing datasets for low-resource languages will be investigated.
Following the successful creation and retrieval of relevant corpora, state-of-the-art machine learning,
and signal processing methods are applied to develop models for SER. These will be evaluated across
the diﬀerent corpora including investigation of inherent biases and other diﬀerences.
The desired outcomes of the project are:
1. The creation of a Danish emotional speech corpus (EmoTale).
2. To develop Danish SER models using state-of-the-art machine learning.
3. To compare techniques and propose methods to mitigate the problem of data scarcity.
Three research questions will form the backbone of the project. They have all been investigated by
previous research for diﬀerent emotional databases than the ones used in this thesis. Hence, related
studies serve as a reference, and the hypothesis in most cases will be to conﬁrm the ﬁndings in liter-
ature. Low-resource is interpreted in the context of Danish SER primarily utilizing the data available
in the DES and EmoTale corpora. These two databases, and the other corpora used, contain limited
amounts of labeled data (< 1 hour of raw speech). Therefore, the analysis results are expected to gen-
eralize well to other low-resource language settings.
1.2   Research questions
The three main questions that will form the basis of analysis are:
RQ 1) How do hand-crafted features compare to pre-trained model embeddings when applied
to SER models in the low-resource domain?
RQ 2) Which Explainable AI methods are applicable in SER, and how can they explain the emo-
tional representations learned by black box models?
RQ 3) How much training data is needed to achieve a human-level recognition rate by ﬁne-
tuning a pre-trained transformer model?

1.3   Literature review
Speech Emotion Recognition (SER) refers to the discipline of automatically recognizing human emo-
tions and aﬀective states through speech. For instance, in the detection of mental disorders in health-
care, risk assessment within the criminal justice system, and commercial applications in call centers.
SER has its roots in psychology and is mainly based on signal processing, machine learning, and deep
learning. It lies within Human-Computer Interaction and Aﬀective Computing and has been an ongo-
ing ﬁeld of research for the past decades. Cultural and ethnic diﬀerences exist in how emotions are
expressed, for instance, the portrayal and perception of neutral speech can vary between people with
diﬀerent cultural heritage and across languages. Studies have found that the human emotion recog-
nition rate is 60-70% in cross-lingual tests [3]. Due to the subjectivity of emotions, reliable labels are
necessary to support aﬀective speech technologies. Hence, one of the main challenges of SER research
is the scarcity of data labeled by emotion. Other challenges include promoting robustness and relia-
bility, personalizing the User Experience, and preserving privacy and security [4]. Ideally, the model
should be robust against environmental noises, varying languages, or even cultural inﬂuences [5]. The
following sections provide the context of the research problem and cover the following aspects of SER
technology:
1. Emotion representation
2. Data preparation and feature extraction
3. Commonly used databases
4. Machine learning approaches
5. Evaluation metrics and benchmarks
6. State-of-the-art SER and research gaps
1.3.1   Emotion representation
Vocal expression of diﬀerent aﬀect states has been a topic of interest in psychology for a long time.
From a physiological perspective, numerous organs and muscles are involved in speech production,
e.g. the tongue, vocal folds, and cheek muscles. Studies have focused on how a speaker’s emotional state
inﬂuences the neuromuscular control of these vocal organs which further inﬂuences the prosodic¹and
¹Prosodic features relate to the rhythm and intonation of language.
spectral features of speech [6]. For example, the pitch average tends to increase for highly activated
emotions, such as anger, happiness, or fear, when compared to neutral emotions [7].
Emotions are inherently subjective and this can lead to discrepancies in labeling emotional data. Over-
all, two diﬀerent labeling schemes are adopted in literature: categorical class labels which are discrete,
and dimensional labels which are continuous. The former often follows the Basic Emotion Theory
developed by Paul Ekman [8] which assumes the existence of 6 basic and universal emotions that
transcend language, cultural, and ethnic diﬀerences. The emotions, also known as The Big 6, are anger,
disgust, fear, happiness, neutral, and sadness. Following the dimensional scheme, emotions can be de-
scribed numerically in the two dimensions activation/arousal and valence, or in three dimensions by
including dominance. For example, happiness is positive valence, high activation, and neutral domi-
nance, i.e. neither dominant nor submissive. Figure 1 illustrates the relative score on the dimensional
scale for happy, sad, and angry emotions.
Natural speech in everyday situations mostly contains neutral aﬀect, therefore, data for emotional
corpora is often collected from acted or induced speech. Acted speech data are typically annotated at
an utterance level with the discrete, predeﬁned emotion the speaker was instructed to enact. To verify
the reliability of the labels, prior work has obtained perceptual ratings from an independent group of
listeners. The labels are considered valid only when the raters agree with the intended emotion. Many
other labeling techniques have been proposed, such as large-scale crowdsourcing, self-assessment, and

soft labeling. The latter is a technique that utilizes listener ratings to account for diﬀerences in emo-
tional perception. For example, if a speech recording is perceived as happy by two raters, angry by one
rater, and sad by one rater, the model is trained with the vector [0.5, 0.25, 0.25, 0] [9]. To completely
bypass the issue of labeling, unlabelled data can be utilized for unsupervised models if the emotion

1.3.2   Data preparation and feature extraction
Speech as a data source contains a great amount of information, both linguistic in the form of sentences
and words and paralinguistic denoting mood and aﬀective state. In addition, the speech signal carries
information about multiple speaker traits, such as age, gender, and nationality. From a technical point
of view, speech data is acoustic signals recorded with a microphone converting the modulations in
air pressure to digital signals. Hence, it becomes a digital signal processing task to discard redundant
aspects of the data and quantify the emotions expressed. Several types of features can be extracted
from each frame of the signal, therefore, a subﬁeld of SER is devoted to designing features that best
reﬂect the emotional content.
Researchers have proposed a minimalistic acoustic parameter set for various areas of automatic voice
analysis, and their implementation is openly available with the openSMILE toolkit [12]. Commonly
used feature sets include eGeMAPS [13] and COVAREP [14], or subsets of the two, e.g. Mel-frequency
cepstral coeﬃcients (MFCCs). Before feature extraction, the audio can be pre-processed by diﬀerent
techniques, such as re-sampling, noise-reduction, or Voice Activation Detection (VAD) which discrim-
inates between speech and silence. With the advancement of deep learning methods, feature extraction
has moved away from conventional signal processing techniques and towards learned feature em-

the speech signal leading to a loss of information. In contrast, learning feature embeddings through
neural network architectures is a data-driven approach, enabling end-to-end frameworks that directly
map the raw speech signal to a classiﬁcation output [16]. Although MFCCs and spectrograms provide
spectral representations of audio, they lose some temporal information when the phase information is

1.3.4   Machine learning approaches
Classiﬁcation methods for SER have evolved from signal processing and conventional machine learn-
ing to more advanced deep learning. Historically, Support Vector Machines are frequently used clas-
siﬁers for SER tasks due to their ability to handle high-dimensional feature spaces and noisy or sparse
features [14], [30], [31]. Other traditional classiﬁers include K-nearest Neighbours (KNN) and Linear
Discriminant Analysis (LDA) [31], [32]. SVMs perform static modeling where a single vector of pa-
rameters represents a segment of audio. In contrast, dynamic modeling takes a sequence of feature
vectors as input and maps to a label or a sequence of labels. Examples of a dynamic model are Hid-
den Markov Models (HMM), which are also commonly used for SER to model the temporal nature of
speech [33], [34]. Other dynamic modeling approaches include Deep Neural Networks (DNN), such
as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), and Long Short-Term
Memory Recurrent Neural Networks (LSTM-RNN), to mention a few. DNNs have the advantage that
they can learn complex patterns and perform feature extraction directly from raw data but also have

limitations, such as the need for large datasets and computational resources. In comparison, traditional
classiﬁers are less scalable, and feature engineering is time-consuming. However, they are more inter-
pretable and the explainability is better when compared to DNN classiﬁers.
1.3.5   State-of-the-art SER and research gaps
Most state-of-the-art results in modern SER research are obtained using diﬀerent transformer-based
architectures of pre-trained speech representations. General large-scale language models such as
Whisper from OpenAI [35], HuBERT from Google AI [36], and wav2vec 2.0 from Facebook AI [37]
are trained on vast amounts of unlabeled speech data to learn a robust representation that general-
izes well across languages and tasks. The pre-training of these models can be continued on speech
data from a diﬀerent target language or domain, for example, the Danish start-up company Alvenir
recently launched a wav2vec 2.0 model trained on 1300 hours of Danish podcasts and audiobooks [38].
In addition, the foundational² language models can be adapted to speciﬁc downstream tasks through
²Foundational, base, and pre-trained are all used in this thesis to describe large-scale general speech models.
ﬁne-tuning. Beavski et al. [37] showed that using just 10 minutes of labeled data for ﬁne-tuning a
pre-trained model obtains high classiﬁcation performance for speech recognition tasks. This leads to
a paradigm where shared models are pre-trained on large volumes of unlabeled data and achieve high
performance for various tasks with minimal adaption [39]. Fine-tuning lightweight prediction heads
for emotion recognition has also signiﬁcantly enhanced performance in SER [40].
Speech processing Universal PERformance Benchmark (SUPERB) is a benchmarking framework de-
signed to evaluate the performance of shared models across a wide range of speech processing tasks
[39]. The tasks consist of learning application-speciﬁc prediction heads on top of a frozen shared
model, for example, Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR), Speaker Identiﬁcation (SID), and Emotion
Recognition (ER). In the case of ER, the widely used IEMOCAP dataset is adopted and its conventional
evaluation protocol is followed. The authors of the SUPERB paper achieved an accuracy of 67.6% for
Emotion Recognition (ER) on the IEMOCAP test set achieved using HuBERT Large. The current high-
est score on the SUPERB leaderboard [41] achieves an accuracy of 70.6% by using a WavLM pre-trained
model [42]. Table 2 lists some of the general speech models that have been used for developing mod-
els for emotion recognition and provides an overview of the scale, language, and highest prediction
accuracy obtained on the IEMOCAP dataset. Of these models, wav2vec 2.0, HuBERT, and WavLM use
Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) and train exclusively on speech, while models from the Whisper family
use Weakly Supervised Learning (WSL) and train on speech paired with transcripts [43].
Wu et al. [15] recently proposed EMO-SUPERB as an extension to the SUPERB benchmark for SER and
identiﬁed several research gaps in the ﬁeld. They highlight reproducibility as a critical issue in SER
where more than 80% of research cannot be reproduced [44]. EMO-SUPERB attempts to address this
issue together with other fundamental problems, such as hard labels for classiﬁcation and the absence
of a standard data partition. Autoniou et al. [44] propose to follow an identical protocol for future
work on SER using IEMOCAP to ensure a comparable baseline which involves performing 10-fold
speaker-independent cross-validation with one speaker as test, eight as training, and one as validation
[44]. Edward B. Kang [45] criticized the use of discrete emotion theory in SER applications for being
problematic and founded on tenuous assumptions. Labeling emotions by categories is practical for ML
frameworks, however, it prioritizes utility over scientiﬁc depth. Hence, Kang calls for a reevaluation
of the limitations underlying the premise of SER. Deterministic emotion labels are overly restrictive,
hence new annotation strategies are needed in future research. Lee et al. [4] point to other important
problems to solve in SER including improving the robustness and generalizability, and moving towards
real-world “in-the-wild” settings. Furthermore, inclusiveness and interpretability should be ensured.
For example, by implementing performance metrics that consider bias, fairness, and explainability.

2.1.1   Low-Level Descriptors
In time domain, the speech signal contains information about amplitude, etc., and in frequency do-
main, the spectrum contains information such as pitch. All these attributes change over time, hence
the signal must be split into short, overlapping segments instead of performing a global analysis of
the whole signal or spectrum. With relatively slow changes over time, the signal becomes close to
stationary in time windows of 5-20 milliseconds, hence, it can be assumed quasi-stationary concern-
ing its characteristics. Low-level descriptors (LLDs) are parameters that can be computed for a short
time frame of an audio signal where the length of the time frame should satisfy quasi-stationarity and
contain enough data for the LLD to be computed [52]. Typical frame lengths range from 20 to 60 ms
depending on the temporal accuracy required. For some LLDs, a windowing function is applied to the
frame before computing the LLD, also known as short-time analysis. LLDs can be split into several
subcategories such as Time domain descriptors, Energy, Spectrum, and Cepstral features. Examples of
time domain descriptors include minimum and maximum signal amplitudes, and examples of energy
descriptors include signal energy, normalized, and RMS energy. The signal energy, 𝐸, for a discrete
signal, 𝑥, is given by the sum of the squared amplitudes:

Convolutional Neural Networks (CCNs) are a group of deep learning models widely used for computer
vision tasks, such as image recognition. CCNs use three basic ideas: (1) local receptive ﬁelds, (2) weight
sharing, and (3) pooling [59]. If we consider the speciﬁc task of recognizing hand-written digits, a
simple approach would be to treat the image as the input to a fully connected neural network like the
one from Figure 4b. However, this approach ignores the key property of images that nearby pixels are
more strongly correlated than distant pixels. Instead, we only connect every hidden neuron to small,
localized regions called local receptive ﬁelds. We then slide the local receptive ﬁeld across the entire
input image. The same weights and bias are used for each of the hidden neurons deﬁning a ﬁlter or
kernel, and this greatly reduces the number of parameters involved in a convolutional neural network.

2.2.3   Transformers
Transformers are a family of deep learning models proposed by Vaswani et al. [62] that process input
sequences in parallel and use the attention mechanism to increase training speed. Traditionally, trans-
formers consist of an encoder and a decoder stack which are layered structures, however, the encoder
and decoder component have also been used independently to create models on their own, such as
GPT-3 and BERT. The encoder and decoder sub-layers are visualized in Figure 5. The encoder block
receives a sequence of embeddings and its main role is to update them and produce representations
that encode some contextual information in the input sequence. It consists of two sub-layers: a multi-
head self-attention layer, which we will return to, and a fully connected FFNN. The fully connected
FFNN sublayer is a so-called position-wise feed-forward layer, which processes each embedding in-
dependently instead of processing the whole embedding sequence as a single vector [63]. The main
diﬀerence between the encoder and the decoder is that the decoder has two attention sublayers; a
masked multi-head attention layer and an encoder-decoder attention layer. Masking is applied to pre-
vent later inputs from inﬂuencing earlier ones.
Figure 5: Schematic of encoder and decoder sublayers of a transformer adapted from [64].
One weakness of the encoder-decoder architecture is that the ﬁnal hidden state of the encoder creates
an information bottleneck. This is especially challenging for long sequences, where information at the
start of the sequence might be lost when compressed to a single, ﬁxed representation [63]. The atten-
tion mechanism is a way to avoid this bottleneck, and it is a key component in transformers. Attention
weighs the input sequences according to their importance to enable the model to learn how sequences
are typically structured. In other words, this mechanism lets the model know how much focus to place
on other parts of the input sentence when one is being processed. Self-attention focuses on the rela-
tionships within a single input sequence. The main idea is that instead of using one ﬁxed embedding
for each position in the input, we can use the whole sequence to compute a weighted average of each
embedding. The weights are normalized and known as attention weights.

2.2.4   Pre-Trained Models
Transfer learning is an approach to adapt or ﬁne-tune a transformer-based model to a new task, and
it is widely used in computer vision and Natural Language Processing (NLP). It involves splitting the
model into a task-independent body and a task-speciﬁc head, as illustrated in Figure 6. The body is
transferred to the new domain, and the head is a task-speciﬁc network. During the training of the body,
called pre-training, it learns broad features from the source domain which are used to initialize a new
model in the target domain. Applying a task-speciﬁc head to the pre-trained model and training on
data from the source domain is called ﬁne-tuning. In addition, partial ﬁne-tuning refers to freezing the
neural network layers in the model’s body and exclusively training the head. Compared to traditional
supervised learning, transfer learning typically produces high-quality models that can be trained much
more eﬃciently on various tasks, and with less labeled data [63, Ch. 1].
Figure 6: Comparison of supervised learning (left) and transfer learning (right), adapted from [63].
In supervised learning, model training and evaluation happen on the same task/domain. In transfer
learning, knowledge from the source task is extracted and applied to the target task.
HuBERT [36] and wav2vec 2.0 [37] are examples of transformer-based models pre-trained on large
amounts of unlabeled data. The network architectures of these models are very similar. As input, the

consisting of 7 convolutional layers. The CNN layers extract feature vectors over time with a dimen-

fed to the encoder. To inject temporal information, the output of a convolutional layer is added to the
input of the encoder. The only diﬀerence between the main variants of these models is the way they

representation learning, which utilizes an ofﬂine clustering step to provide aligned target labels for
a BERT-like prediction loss. Before training the HuBERT, a separate unsupervised step is performed
using K-means clustering of MFCCs. During training, a cross-entropy loss between the predicted and
the pre-assigned cluster labels is minimized.
2.3   XAI methods for audio classiﬁcation
Explainable AI methods aim to make models more interpretable, increase transparency, and improve
trustworthiness for the end user. With advancements in hardware technologies and model architec-
tures, state-of-the-art models become increasingly complex with billions of parameters (Table 2). The
advantage of large transformer-based models, such as GPT-3 [65] and wav2vec 2.0 [37], is their versa-
tility across diﬀerent domains, however, one of the main drawbacks is the diﬃculty of understanding
the decision-making process behind these black box models. Especially in sectors where decisions have
serious consequences, such as healthcare, law, and autonomous vehicles. In a recent review article
by Akman et al. [17], the authors summarize existing XAI methods for audio models. These methods
cover diﬀerent techniques, including rule-based explanations, feature importance analysis, and local
surrogate models. Common to them is that they aim to explain complex audio signals and leverage
human adeptness at interpreting harmonies, rhythm, and other high-level concepts through listening.
XAI methods are often classiﬁed by the stage of application (before, during or after model training),
the scope (local or global), and the input data format [66]. The explanations can also have diﬀerent
formats including numerical, logical, visual, and textual. Depending on the input audio representation
(waveform, spectrogram, etc.) diﬀerent XAI methods are applicable.
2.3.1   Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations
Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME) is an algorithm that can explain the predic-
tions of any classiﬁer or regressor, by treating it as a black box and approximating it locally with
an interpretable model. Explanations are generated by perturbing the input and training a surrogate
model which assigns weights to each input component. LIME is applied after model training and has
local scope, meaning that explanations are produced for each prediction individually and not for the
model as a whole. Compared to gradient-based XAI techniques, LIME has an advantage in explain-
ing waveform-fed models by directly assigning importance to decomposed audio patches rather than
single time points. This makes LIME explanations easier to interpret since we can relate diﬀerent ele-
ments or segments of the audio to the prediction. A crucial part of generating LIME explanations for
audio is how the input is decomposed. The approach adopted in this thesis uses temporal and spectral
decompositions, which decompose the audio into equally sized temporal and spectral components,
respectively. This leads to spectral and temporal masking in the training of the surrogate model.

3.1   Emotional speech corpora
3.1.1   Danish Emotional Speech Database (DES)
The DES database [18] was created by Engberg et al. at Aalborg University in 1996 and contains four
speakers (two male and two female) expressing the ﬁve emotions; neutral, surprise, happiness, sadness,
and anger. All utterances are equally balanced for each gender and actor. The database was developed
as a part of the VAESS project [71], which aimed to develop improved quality and range of synthetic
voices, including a range of emotions. Some utterances belong to the target class instead of being la-
beled by an emotion. DES contains utterances of diﬀerent lengths including two single words, nine
sentences, and two passages of ﬂuent speech. The single words are “Ja” (Yes) and “Nej” (No). The sen-
tences vary in length from three up to nine words and some sentences are questions, for instance “Hvor
skal du hen?” (Where are you going?). The recordings took place in an acoustically damped studio with
a high-quality microphone. 20 listeners performed four listening tests, one for each of the actors, to
guarantee the unambiguous emotional content of the recorded utterances. On average, the emotions
were correctly classiﬁed in 67% of the tests, ranging from 55% to 80%. Surprise and happiness were
often confused and so were neutral and sadness. The type of utterance was important for the recogni-
tion rate with passages being the easiest to identify emotions from.
3.1.2   Berlin Database of Emotional Speech (Emo-DB)
Emo-DB [19] was created by the Institute of Communication Science, Technical University, Berlin. Ten
speakers (ﬁve male and ﬁve female) participated in the study producing ten German utterances that
were a mix of short and longer sentences. The utterances were pre-deﬁned to be emotionally neutral,
for example, the sentence “Der Lappen liegt auf den Eisshrank” (The cloth is lying on the fridge). Overall,
the database contains 535 utterances and the seven emotions; anger, boredom, fear, happiness, sadness,
disgust, and neutral. The recordings took place in an anechoic chamber with high-quality recording
equipment. The complete database was evaluated in perception tests to quantify the recognisability
of emotions and their naturalness. The mean emotion recognition rate was 84.3%, ranging between
79.6-96.9%. Utterances recognized better than 80% and judged as natural by more than 60% of the lis-
teners were kept in the database.
3.1.3   Urdu Speech Emotion Database (Urdu)
The Urdu Speech Emotion Database [27] was created to study the problem of cross-lingual emotion
recognition between Western languages and Urdu. The corpus consists of audio excerpts from natural
and unscripted discussions between guests in Urdu TV talk shows. Overall, there are 400 utterances
expressing four diﬀerent emotions; happy, angry, sad, and neutral, and the corpus includes 38 speakers
(27 male and 11 female). The recordings are collected from YouTube and annotated with emotional
labels by four students. Final labels were given to each utterance when at least two annotators assigned
them the same emotion.
3.1.4   Acted Emotional Speech Dynamic Database (AESDD)
The AESDD corpus [72] consists of Greek utterances acted by six professional actors (three male and
three female). The recordings took place in the sound studio of the Laboratory of Electronic Media,
Aristotle University of Thessaloniki in Greece. 19 utterances were chosen from diﬀerent theatrical

3.1.5   MSP-Podcast

3.2   Creation of the EmoTale corpus
A Danish speech aﬀect corpus was developed as part of this thesis comprising Danish speech record-
ings and the associated aﬀect annotations, as described in the ethical approval [2]. While the primary
focus is adult speech, a few children are included to enable the evaluation and validation of existing
methods on children. The data collection procedure was inspired by the one followed by Burkhardt et
al. [19] when creating the German Emo-DB database.
3.2.1   Recruitment
Participants with acting experience and Danish and English language skills were recruited through
physical ﬂyers and posts on social media, and theatre schools in the Greater Copenhagen area were
contacted by email and phone. An online registration form was available in Google Forms where
participants signed up by providing their contact information and choosing their desired experiment
date from a list of options. The exclusion criteria included being under 7 years old or not speaking
Danish. Before the experiment, we obtained written consent from the participant or the guardian of
participants younger than 18 years old, and information about gender and age was recorded. All par-
ticipants were reimbursed with a 150 DKK gift card to the cinema for their participation in the study.
See Appedix D for the recruitment ﬂyer and consent form in Danish.
3.2.2   Data protection and ethical considerations
The recorded speech signals and the participant information has been pseudo-anonymized by gener-
ating a random identiﬁer for each participant. Since the emotions are enacted and the speech sentences
are pre-deﬁned to minimize subjective inﬂuences, the data collection should not reﬂect the state of the
individual. Hence, any potential negative impact on the individual is minimized.

3.4   Hand-crafted features vs. PTM embeddings
The ﬁrst research question concerns how hand-crafted features compare to pre-trained model embed-
dings, also known as deep features, when applied to SER models in the low-resource domain. As dis-
cussed in Chapter 1, hand-crafted features derived through digital signal processing techniques might
lead to a loss of information due to mathematical transformations, etc. In contrast, directly using the
waveform representation is favorable for capturing ﬁne-grained temporal details without requiring
any feature extraction method. Therefore, using the raw speech signal is theoretically the best approach
since the model learns the best features without being inﬂuenced by a potentially biased human prior.
This leads to the following research question:
RQ 1: How do hand-crafted features compare to pre-trained model embeddings when ap-
plied to SER models in the low-resource domain?
The feature embeddings are extracted as the last hidden states of the pre-trained model, i.e. the last
layer before any task-speciﬁc head is applied, and it is assumed that model embeddings provide a
compact representation of the emotional content in a speech signal. In transformer models, this is the
output from the ﬁnal transformer block. The experimental procedure is adapted from the one outlined
by Wagner et al. in the publicly available Colab notebook¹⁰ and makes use of the Python libraries pro-
¹⁰https://github.com/audeering/w2v2-how-to/blob/main/notebook.ipynb
vided by audEERING®. The DES, EmoTale, Emo-DB, Urdu, and AESDD datasets are used for analysis
with minimal data pre-processing where all audio ﬁles are downsampled to 16 kHz as the pre-trained
models require, and stereo audio ﬁles were converted to mono by averaging to a single channel.
Figure 7 illustrates the model pipelines for hand-crafted and deep features. The eGeMAPS (extended
Geneva Minimalistic Acoustic Parameter Set) [13] and the ComParE (Computational Paralinguistics
Challenge) [55] feature sets were extracted using the openSMILE toolkit [12] and serve as two separate
baselines. Both are widely used in the ﬁeld of speech analysis, and designed for aﬀective computing and
paralinguistic analysis tasks. eGeMAPS focuses on a minimalistic approach, providing a compact set
of 88 acoustic features. ComParE is more comprehensive allowing for more ﬁne-grained analysis and
it consists of 6,373 features. These were tested against embeddings from the wav2vec2 base model [37]
(w2v2-b) as well as a wav2vec2 model ﬁne-tuned for SER on the RAVDESS corpus [25] (w2v2-FT-cat)
and one ﬁne-tuned on MSP-Podcast [76] (w2v2-FT-dim). The latter is ﬁne-tuned on dimensional scores
and not categorical labels, therefore, the output of hidden states is necessary to access the latent space
of the model. Model embeddings are extracted by applying average pooling over the hidden states
of the last transformer layer. Subsequently, the features are fed to a Support Vector Classiﬁer with a
linear kernel from scikit-learn, and Leave-One-Speaker-Out (LOSO) cross-validation is applied. In
each fold, features were standardized to the mean and standard deviation of the respective training
set. A linear kernel was chosen since it yielded good results compared to a radial basis function, and
it resembles the method proposed by Schuller et al. [55]. Furthermore, in the case of a linear kernel,
the SVM weights can be used to interpret the feature importance. The default strategy for multi-class
classiﬁcation in scikit-learn is the One-versus-One (OvO) approach where a separate binary classi-
ﬁer is trained for every pair of classes. Hence, for 𝐶 classes the number of classiﬁers would be 𝐶(𝐶−
1)/2. To perform inference, each classiﬁer votes on a class and the class with the most votes becomes
the prediction. Occasionally, the abbreviation ComParE-SVC will be used to refer to the SVC trained on
ComParE features, and similar for the other models.
Applying LOSO cross-validation introduces variability in the performance metric. The aggregated
unweighted average recall (UAR) across cross-validation folds is commonly used in SER research to
provide a holistic measure of model performance. However, it may overlook performance diﬀerences

3.5   Explainable SER
To gain a deeper understanding of the models and explain their predictions it is necessary to explore
feature attribution. This leads to the second research question, which can be considered an extension
of RQ1, since the same model pipelines (Figure 7) are applied.
RQ 2: Which Explainable AI methods are applicable in SER, and how can they explain
the emotional representations learned by black box models?
Several considerations come into play when applying XAI methods to an audio classiﬁcation task such
as emotion recognition from speech. The models we consider here, take a raw waveform input. Subse-
quently, feature representations are produced either via digital signal processing with the openSMILE
toolkit or by feeding the input to a pre-trained black box model. The former represents physical char-
acteristics of the audio such as pitch, loudness, and frequency, and are easy to interpret. However, the
latter does not oﬀer such a straightforward interpretation. To account for this, two feature attribution
methods are proposed in the following section:
1. Feature importance through SVM coeﬃcients

2. Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME)
Feature importances are computed as the average SVM coeﬃcients for classiﬁcation models trained
on the eGeMAPS feature set. Inspired by Das et al. [80], the 88 features are further divided into 27
feature groups, such as Pitch, MFCC for voiced (V) segments, and Alpha Ratio for unvoiced (UV) seg-
ments. The scikit-learn implementation of SVC uses the OvO strategy as default for multi-class
classiﬁcation, hence, 21 binary classiﬁers are trained for seven emotion classes. However, to simplify
the interpretation of the feature attributions, an OvR strategy is adopted instead. This way, the linear
SVC coeﬃcients will have the shape (n_classes, n_features) and each row corresponds to feature
attributions for one of the classiﬁers. The next step includes grouping SVM coeﬃcients into feature
groups by average aggregation. For visualization purposes, we assume the mean coeﬃcients within a
feature group for a speciﬁc dataset follow a normal distribution, and coeﬃcients less than the standard
error are set to zero. The SVM coeﬃcients for each emotion are drawn from this distribution and are
assumed to be independent and representative samples.
Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME) [67] is an algorithm that can explain the pre-
dictions of any classiﬁer or regressor by treating it as a black box and approximating it locally with
an interpretable model. As presented in Section 2.3.1, explanations are generated by perturbing the
input and training a surrogate model that assigns weights to each input component. The advantage of
LIME is that it explains waveform-fed models by directly assigning importance to decomposed audio
patches through decomposition weights. The implementation of LIME for explaining COVID-cough
classiﬁcations by Wullenweber et al. [68] was modiﬁed to interpret SER models. CoughLIME¹¹ builds

on the LIME Python module [81], and it requires the prediction function to output logits rather than

probability output is not supported for scikit-learn’s SVC with OvO strategy, it was changed to One-
vs-Rest (OvR). To accommodate multiple classes, separate prediction functions were deﬁned for each

included in the analysis were all correctly classiﬁed by the models, hence, the positive class is the cor-

on the model explanation. Our analysis focuses on spectral decompositions, however, examples of
temporal decompositions are included in Appedix F.

3.6   Transfer learning in low-resource domain
Most state-of-the-art SER research focuses on English or other high-resource languages while low-
resource languages, such as Danish, have been given little attention. This section describes how Danish
SER models were developed using the EmoTale corpus. An experiment was designed to investigate
how model performance depends on the amount of training data and address the research question:
RQ 3: How much training data is needed to achieve a human-level recognition rate by
ﬁne-tuning a pre-trained transformer model?
As mentioned in Chapter 1, studies have shown that the human emotion recognition rate is 60-70% in
cross-lingual tests [3]. In listening tests for the DES corpus, emotions were correctly classiﬁed in 67.3%
on average [18]. During the creation of Emo-DB, the complete database was evaluated in a perception
test, and only utterances recognized more than 80% of the time were included in the ﬁnal corpus [82].
These facts indicate how well a SER model should perform to match human listeners.
In a setting with data scarcity, a trade-oﬀ exists between having a large enough training set to enhance
model robustness and a suﬃciently sized test set to give a fair estimation of model performance. Fig-
ure 8 illustrates the cross-validation (CV) strategy adopted in the experiment, designed to balance this
trade-oﬀ. The full EmoTale dataset contains a total of 450 Danish utterances with emotion labels and is
split into cross-validation with ﬁve folds. Every CV fold contains one validation and one test set with
45 utterances each, and 16 models are trained on diﬀerent fractions of the training set. This ensures
that all data has been used either during the validation or test phase of training and that the models
in each fold are evaluated similarly. Every data split is stratiﬁed to have an equal representation of
emotions and speakers, which in turn ensures an equal representation of gender. Where a stratiﬁed
split based on speakers was not possible (when the splits were too small), gender was used instead.
The advantage of stratiﬁcation is that it reduces the experimental variance making it easier to identify
the best classiﬁcation model.

which other samples are already in the test set. Hence, summing the TP, FP, and FN across all folds,
and then computing the F1 provides the least biased estimates and the smallest variance, especially in
the presence of class imbalance. The same goes for the unweighted average recall. Hence, F1 and UAR
scores are computed using the results obtained from the aggregated TP and FN values.

The UAR scores are computed as the sum of class-wise recall divided by the number of emotion classes
(Equation 27), and the overall score is the average UAR across all datasets. To provide a comprehensive
view of model performance, we report both the aggregated results (highlighted in Table 6) and the
mean results across speakers. The former combines the predictions of all folds into a single confusion
matrix and calculates the UAR. Once SVM parameters are ﬁxed, changing the random seed does not
aﬀect results, hence the standard deviation is zero. The latter calculates the UAR for each LOSO cross-
validation fold individually and takes the mean to consider how well the model generalizes across
speakers. Speaker and Sentence UARs are found by ﬁrst grouping prediction results by speaker and
sentence, respectively, calculating the UAR per group, and then taking a simple average across the
groups (Equation 28). This way, all speakers and sentences are given equal weight. Standard deviations
are reported to provide insights into the variability of the UAR scores across speakers, sentences, and
datasets. Sentence UARs are not included for the Urdu corpus since it contains natural utterances,
hence no sentences are repeated. In the following, Table 6 and Figure 10 results are discussed to iden-
tify overall trends and give an intuition about the datasets. Further analysis is required to conﬁrm if
the results generalize to other languages and feature extraction methods.
We ﬁrst turn our attention to the aggregated UAR scores treating the models’ performance as if they
were tested on all data at once. For Emo-DB, the results reported in Table 6 using ComParE and w2v2-
FT-dim embeddings are reproduced from the work by Wagner et al. [40]. The UAR scores for the three
left-most datasets follow the same trend with model performance in descending order using the fea-
tures: w2v2-FT-dim, w2v2-FT-cat, ComParE, eGeMAPS, and w2v2-b. Interestingly, Urdu deviates from
the trend with eGeMAPS features outperforming both ComParE and the embeddings from the PTM
ﬁne-tuned on categorical labels, w2v2-FT-cat. In all cases, deep features from the ﬁne-tuned models
yield the highest UARs while embeddings from the wav2vec2 model without ﬁne-tuning perform the
worst. Furthermore, the PTM ﬁne-tuned on dimensional labels leads to the highest mean UAR across
datasets (74.1 ± 13.6). This points to the necessity and beneﬁt of ﬁne-tuning. Embeddings from w2v2-
FT-dim outperform those from w2v2-FT-cat. This could indicate that the dimensional labels allow the
PTM to learn a more robust emotion representation. Here, it is worth mentioning that the pre-trained
models are diﬀerent variants of wav2vec2. One is wav2vec2-Large-Robust (ﬁne-tuned on dimensional
labels). The other is wav2vec2-Large-xlsr-53-English (ﬁne-tuned on categorical labels).

performances in-domain, we get a diﬀerent perspective on the results. For example, ﬁne-tuning on

Figure 15 presents the feature attributions interpreted as SVM coeﬃcients across datasets and the four
emotions: happy, sad, angry, and neutral. The goal of this analysis is to ﬁnd feature groups with high
attribution scores. Additionally, the feature groups should diﬀer between the emotion classes. Detailed
deﬁnitions of the hand-crafted features can be found in the openSMILE documentation [52]. The most
signiﬁcant features are described below.
SpecSlope1 and SpecSlope2 are the spectral slopes from 0 to 500 Hz and 500 to 1500 Hz, respectively.
They indicate the rate at which the amplitude of the frequency components in a sound signal decreases
with increasing frequency. Seglen (V), (UV) is computed as the length of voiced and unvoiced seg-
ments. Jitter and Shimmer measure variation in the fundamental frequency and amplitude, respec-
tively. F1, F2, and F3 are the formant frequencies including their characteristics, such as bandwidth,
logarithmic amplitude, and average value. These are the resonant frequencies of the vocal tract, which
include the throat, mouth, and nasal passages. SoundLevel represents the overall loudness level of
the audio and is computed as the average RMS energy converted to decibels. Lastly, a-Ratio is the
Alpha Ratio representing the spectral balance between energy in the low-frequency region and the
high-frequency region. It is deﬁned as the ratio between the summed energy from 50 to 1000 Hz and
from 1 to 5 kHz.
Happiness and anger are considered high arousal emotions, while sadness and neutral are considered
low arousal. Some general trends can be seen in Figure 15:
i) High (vs) Low arousal: SpecSlope2 (V) and Seglen (UV) seem to have higher absolute attribution
scores for high arousal emotions compared to low arousal emotions.
ii) Happiness: Jitter is given a high positive attribution across most datasets. Similarly, Shimmer
has high absolute (positive or negative) attribution scores.
iii) Sadness: Formant frequencies (F1, F2, F3) all seem to be given less attribution compared to the
other emotions.
iv) Anger: Relatively high absolute attribution to Seglen (UV) and SoundLevel sets anger apart from
the other emotions.
v) Neutral: seems to be more domain-dependent than the rest of the emotions and there is no clear
trends in how the feature attributions are distributed.
Apart from these overall trends, there are some corpus-dependent diﬀerences in the feature attribu-
tions. A-ratio (UV) is very dataset-dependent with large positive weights for angry and neutral
emotions in EmoTale and Urdu, and negative weights for happy and neutral emotions in Emo-DB.
SoundLevel has a signiﬁcantly higher attribution for anger in AESDD compared to the other corpora.
There does not seem to be a correlation between feature attributions for EmoTale and DES. This in-
dicates that variations in attribution are better explained by the diﬀerences between the individual
datasets than diﬀerences in language.
Now, we turn our attention to the deep features extracted from pre-trained model embeddings. In con-
trast to hand-crafted features, deep features do not represent any physical characteristics of the audio
and require alternative XAI techniques for interpretation. As described in Section 3.5, we apply LIME
to explain the predictions of an SVC trained on hand-crafted and deep features, respectively, by treat-
ing it as black box models and approximating it locally with an interpretable model. Explanations are
generated by perturbing the input and training a linear sparse surrogate model which assigns weights
to each input component. Decomposing the input audio into equally sized temporal components re-
veals that the two models weigh the audio segments diﬀerently. Examples of temporal decompositions
are included in Appedix F. The analysis below focuses on spectral decompositions that decompose the
audio into eight equally sized spectral components in the frequency range between 0 to 8 kHz. Only
true predictions are included in the visualizations, hence positive weights correspond to components
that tend the model towards predicting the true class. One could hypothesize that low-pitch speech

5   Discussion
This chapter analyzes the choices made during the experimental design and the limitations of the
methods adopted. We assess the extent to which the research questions were answered and the exper-
imental results are put into context with related SER research. Furthermore, future work and further
directions of SER in the low-resource domain are discussed.
5.1   Research questions and limitations
In RQ1, the results outline several advantages of using pre-trained model embeddings compared to
hand-crafted features. Deep features led to higher classiﬁcation performance with statistical signiﬁ-
cance for three out of ﬁve corpora (Emo-DB, EmoTale, and AESDD). For the two remaining corpora
(DES and Urdu), aggregated UAR scores lead to the same conclusion, but are not supported by statis-
tical tests when taking the variance across LOSO folds into account. We explain this by the nature of
the two datasets; DES was not created for SER purposes and Urdu contains many speakers and few
utterances per speaker leading to high variance across speakers. Training on Emo-DB consistently
yielded higher UAR scores regardless of the feature extraction method, and we hypothesize, that this
is because utterances recognized in less than 80% of perception tests were discarded from the corpus
when it was created. All in all, these observations highlight the importance of interpreting SER classi-
ﬁers in the light of which dataset they were trained on and the method used for evaluating the model.
Compared to related SER studies, we achieved a notable model performance using a relatively simple
SVC head combined with pre-trained model embeddings. For instance, Bandela et al. [31] achieved a
classiﬁcation accuracy of 90.1% and 89.3% for Emo-DB using SVM and k-Nearest Neighbor classiﬁers,
respectively, in combination with their proposed type of feature optimization. We compare this with
the aggregated UAR score of 96.1% obtained with w2v2-FT-dim-SVC in Chapter 4. Other studies report
accuracies ranging between 69.3% to 92.7% training on Emo-DB with diﬀerent model architectures,
feature extraction, and evaluation methods [32], [46], [91]. Vryzas et al. [26] report a classiﬁcation
accuracy of 60.8% for Greek SER using AESDD compared to our results of 83.2% UAR. An in-depth
comparison should take design choices, such as the number of emotion classes, cross-validation tech-
nique, and performance metric, into account. However, transferring pre-trained speech models to SER
in the low-resource domain shows great potential.
Some limitations to the experiment performed in the investigation of RQ1 can be mentioned. For ex-
ample, the inﬂuence of the SVC regularization factor has not been investigated but was instead set to
the default value in the scikit-learn implementation of SVC. When extracting feature embeddings
from the last hidden layer of pre-trained transformer models, a pooling strategy is chosen to decrease
the dimensionality of the embeddings. Mean-pooling was used in the experiment, however, other pool-
ing techniques could be explored to investigate how this inﬂuences the results. Additionally, there are
some limitations to the statistical tests performed to compare model performances. In pairwise t-test
among ﬁve models there are (5
2) = 10 pairwise comparisons, hence the test does not directly compare
all models to each other simultaneously leading to possible inconsistencies. Other statistical methods
could allow for a simultaneous comparison, such as ANOVA analysis of variance to determine if there
are any signiﬁcant diﬀerences between groups of models or a type of ranking to adjust for multiple
comparisons.
We wish to achieve SER models with good generalization ability that perform well on new, unseen
data, especially in real-world applications. Furthermore, a model that transfers well is less likely to
be overﬁtted on the training data. We expect the PTM embeddings to provide more robust speech
representations across corpora compared to hand-crafted features since they are pre-trained on vast
amounts of speech. However, with 6373-dimensional ComParE features and relatively few training

samples (∼ 500), one might speculate if the poor generalizability could be accredited to either over- or
underﬁtting. Hyperparameter tuning to ﬁnd the optimal regularization might lead to better classiﬁca-
tion results by adjusting the SVC margin and improving model generalization. Furthermore, a diﬀerent
SVC kernel could reduce any tendency to under- or overﬁt.
In RQ2, explainability in SER is a complex topic and many aspects must be considered when choosing
between diﬀerent XAI techniques. For instance, the desired input and output formats of the explana-
tion and whether the explanation should have a local or global scope. As a result, the analysis per-
formed in RQ2 is less structured and more exploratory compared to RQ1 and RQ3. Feature attribution
was analyzed via linear eGeMAPS-SVC coeﬃcients in Figure 15, and qualitative conclusions were drawn
across diﬀerent datasets. Here, we assumed that feature importance can be explained by the emotion
class. In reality, such an assumption is fairly crude and feature attributions are expected to depend
highly on the dataset’s language, speakers, and type of utterances. Such inﬂuence should be further
investigated to determine if feature importance can be attributed to the emotion expressed, or if other
information in the speech signal plays a greater role. In RQ2, the low-resource corpora from RQ1 were
used again for consistency. However, feature importance analysis would beneﬁt from including high-
resource datasets to minimize the inﬂuence of speaker and sentence diﬀerences. A limitation of the
method based on SVC coeﬃcients is that it can only be applied to models with hand-crafted or other
human-interpretable input features.
Das et al. [80] analyzed feature importance when training varying autoencoder (AE) architectures on
eGeMAPS features across multiple datasets. A linear SVC was trained on the latent embeddings of the
AE models before the DeepLIFT (Deep Learning Important FeaTures) algorithm [92] was applied to
compute feature importances. Even though the group followed a diﬀerent approach to obtain feature
attribution, some ﬁndings are common to the analysis of linear SVC coeﬃcients in RQ2. For example,
formant frequencies seem to be important when telling apart sadness from the other emotions. Fur-
thermore, the sound level has higher attribution scores for anger, which is expected based on the most
common expression of angry emotions.
LIME has the potential to produce local explanations for SER models, but it also has some disadvan-
tages. The technique is best suited for binary classiﬁcation and does not oﬀer explanations that are
as easily interpretable for multiple classes. As proposed in [67], one way to adapt the method to the
multi-class case is to explain each class separately, thus 𝑓 in Equation 20 is the prediction of the rele-
vant class. LIME is used in RQ2 to explain true predictions of happy and angry emotions, respectively.
However, an exhaustive analysis using LIME would require looking at several explanations from all
possible positions in the confusion matrix. That is, one would need to explore why the classiﬁer makes
true and false predictions of both the positive and negative classes, and in multi-class classiﬁcation,
this further complicates the matter. Supplementary XAI techniques could be used in addition to LIME
to provide more general insights into model predictions, such as global explanations.
In RQ3, diﬀerent models were trained on the EmoTale corpus to create Danish speech emotion recog-
nition models. We varied the amount of training data to investigate how much data is necessary to
achieve a recognition rate on level with human listeners. To compare diﬀerent ﬁne-tuning techniques,
four models were suggested including simple zero- and few-shot models, and a ﬁne-tuned wav2vec 2.0
model pre-trained on Danish speech. The linear SVC seen in RQ1 and RQ2 was also included for con-
sistency. The models were evaluated using a cross-validation setup, designed to balance the trade-oﬀ
between the number of training instances and a suﬃciently sized test set. Additionally, SVC and few-
shot models were developed using both w2v2-FT-dim and xlsr-danish embeddings, resulting in quite
a large number of models to navigate between. The best-performing models were presented in Table 7,
where the SVC and ﬁne-tuned models outperformed the few- and zero-shot models with statistical
signiﬁcance. Based on the standard deviation of performance metrics across CV-folds, the ﬁne-tuned

been ﬁne-tuned for SER, hence ﬁne-tuning for the downstream task might explain the diﬀerence in
performance. Further investigation is needed to conﬁrm such a hypothesis.
The cross-domain performance of the best ﬁne-tuned models was assessed by transferring them to
DES and Emo-DB (Table 8 and Table 9). The SVC trained on w2v2-FT-dim transfers best to Emo-DB
obtaining a UAR score of (62 ± 4)% which beats the ﬁne-tuned model with UAR of (47 ± 8)%. How-
ever, when transferring to DES, the ﬁne-tuned model outperforms the other models achieving a UAR
of (60 ± 8)%. This could suggest that the ﬁne-tuned model has captured some language-speciﬁc emo-
tional representations. An alternative explanation is that language-speciﬁc information is transferred
to the ﬁne-tuned prediction head from the base model, xlsr-danish, which was pre-trained in Danish
speech. A minor detail in the training pipeline for the ﬁne-tuned model is that the evaluation metric
is set to accuracy, while the ﬁnal performance metric is unweighted average recall (UAR). Then again,
stratiﬁcation ensures class balance in the data splits, so changing the evaluation metric to UAR should
not inﬂuence the results signiﬁcantly. A limitation of the models developed in RQ3 is that they are
speaker-dependent, which is impractical in most real-world applications.
5.2   Choice of model architectures
This thesis only explores a small number of model architectures including Support Vector Classiﬁers
and K-means clustering. It has been a conscious choice to stick with some simple model architectures
and spend limited time on hyperparameter-tuning to not lose focus on the research aims, i.e. investi-
gating diﬀerent input features (RQ1), model explanations (RQ2), and other training and performance
aspects (RQ3). A more rigorous investigation of alternative model architectures is left for future work.
The foundational models applied in this thesis (Table 5) are all from the wav2vec 2.0 family and share
the same CNN structure. PTMs from this family are frequently used in literature for transfer learning
and there are plenty of open-source resources available when setting out to ﬁne-tune on a custom
dataset. Hence, the foundational models were selected based on what was most practical and not on
their suitability for low-resource ﬁne-tuning. A conscious choice could have been made in terms of the
base models’ pre-training, continued pre-training, and ﬁne-tuning to keep more parameters controlled
during the experiments. For example, in the comparison of input embeddings in RQ1, the w2v2-FT-dim
and w2v2-FT-cat were ﬁne-tuned versions of two diﬀerent base wav2vec 2.0 models (w2v2-L-robust

from the same base model ﬁne-tuned on diﬀerent datasets.
The zero- and few-shot model architectures adopted in RQ3 are naive implementations of zero- and

5.3   Future work
Research has suggested that dimensional emotion labels lead to more accurate ground truths and are
more transferable to new domains [75], [93]. Hence, future directions in both high- and low-resource
SER could focus on building models based on continuous labels from the dimensional emotion model.
In the context of this thesis, dimensional labels are available for all databases used in RQ1, hence the
experiment could be repeated using Support Vector Regression (SVR) instead of SVC. To compare clas-
siﬁcation and regression model performances, however, is not straightforward. The SVC performance
was measured as the class-wise recall (UAR) score, while the continuous predictions from an SVR
would be evaluated by CCC score. A suggested approach could be to transfer both models to another
speech corpora and compare the relative drop in the performance metric moving from in- to cross-
domain. Likewise, the ﬁne-tuning technique applied in RQ3 could be adapted to dimensional emotion
48
5 Discussion
==PAGE:59==
labels by incorporating a CCC loss function. Using DES and EmoTale with dimensional labels, it would
be interesting to develop a dimensional SER model in Danish.
Insuﬃcient resources and the lack of labeled data are critical issues in SER making supervised learn-
ing inapplicable for languages beyond the most commonly spoken ones. Recently, researchers have
investigated the use of foundational models to assist in automating SER annotation [94]. They found
that combining limited human annotations with Self-Supervised Language Model (SSLM) output sub-
stantially improves SER training. Relying on the response from one speech model could yield biased
language reasoning, hence Feng et al. [94] propose assessing the agreement between the SSLM anno-
tations and one human feedback for quality control. The PTM ﬁne-tuned on MSP-Podcast with dimen-
sional annotations (w2v2-FT-dim) outputs activation, valence, and dominance scores from 0 to 1. In an
exploratory analysis (Section 4.4), the distribution of scaled PTM labels is compared to manual labels
for DES. Similar comparisons can be found in Appedix I for EmoTale, Emo-DB, Urdu, and AESDD. We
see that the distributions for activation and dominance are well-aligned, but not for valence. Automat-
ing the labeling of emotional speech could be a big step toward narrowing the gap between high- and
low-resource SER.
The EmoTale corpora include both Danish and English utterances spoken by the same speakers and
recorded under the same conditions, which is rare among SER corpora. Thus, the corpora can be used
for multi-lingual analysis with several variables kept constant. For example, models trained in English
or another high-resource language could be transferred to EmoTale to test if the model transfers better
to utterances from a high-resource language (English) or a low-resource language (Danish). In RQ3,
ﬁne-tuning on any other model than the one pre-trained in Danish did not work. This could indicate
that pre-training in the target language could be necessary for learning the downstream task. Hence,
a ﬁeld for exploration is how the pre-training language of shared speech models aﬀects the perfor-
mance of the downstream classiﬁers. Building on the experiment from RQ1, one could also investigate
if combining hand-crafted and deep features would lead to performance enhancement. Lastly, future
directions in low-resource SER could be inspired by the methods adopted in Automatic Speech Recog-
nition in scarce data settings, e.g. by using data augmentation.

6   Conclusion
This chapter summarizes the main results obtained throughout the thesis and presents the conclusions
to the research questions. The main outcomes of this thesis include the creation of a Danish emotional
speech corpus, the development of SER models in the low-resource domain, and an investigation of
techniques to mitigate the problem of data scarcity.
RQ 1: How do hand-crafted features compare to pre-trained model embeddings when applied
to SER models in the low-resource domain?
Linear SVC classiﬁers were trained on hand-crafted and deep features using LOSO cross-validation
for ﬁve diﬀerent corpora. Several feature sets were compared including ComParE and eGeMAPS from
the openSMILE toolkit and feature embeddings from pre-trained speech models. The best performance
was obtained with deep features from the w2v2-FT-dim model achieving an average UAR score of (74.1
± 13.6)% across the diﬀerent corpora. Training on base wav2vec 2.0 embeddings leads to a UAR of
less than 50% for all datasets except Emo-DB (58.9%). Hence, the importance of ﬁne-tuning for the
downstream SER tasks is evident. Both model robustness and inherent variability of the corpora play a
role in explaining the performance diﬀerences across speakers and sentences. It is a general trend that
the models tend to confuse emotions with low arousal (neutral, boredom, and sadness) and those with
high arousal (happiness, anger, and surprise). Training on deep features generally leads to better cross-
corpus transferability than hand-crafted ones and achieves cross-domain UAR scores of up to 79%.
RQ 2: Which Explainable AI methods are applicable in SER, and how can they explain the
emotional representations learned by black box models?
Expressing and interpreting emotions is a highly subjective process, and investigating XAI methods for
SER forces us to reﬂect on how we perceive emotions through speech ourselves. Two approaches are
suggested to explain the models developed in RQ1, i.e. SVM feature attributions and LIME. Analyzing
linear SVM coeﬃcients gave way for a qualitative analysis. Even though some trends were observed,
the results highly depend on the corpus. Using LIME, an exploration of average spectral decomposition
weights for models based on hand-crafted and deep features was undertaken. For the deep-features
model, low-pitch components (< 3 kHz) contribute most towards predicting angry and neutral emo-
tions, while very high-pitch components (6-8 kHz) lead to happy and neutral predictions. Thus, the
emotional representations learned by the pre-trained model align well with the intuitive connection
between pitch and high vs. low valence emotions.
RQ3: How much training data is needed to achieve a human-level recognition rate by ﬁne-
tuning a pre-trained transformer model?
The EmoTale corpus contains 450 Danish utterances and was used to develop Danish SER models using
four diﬀerent model architectures. A cross-validation setup was designed to investigate how the model
performance depends on the amount of training data, and the results reveal that zero-shot, few-shot,
and SVC performance already saturates at around 150 training instances. However, ﬁne-tuned model
performance increases with the amount of training data, and including more data is expected to lead
to further performance gains. The best models achieved UAR of (61 ± 4)% on the test set. There was
no statistically signiﬁcant diﬀerence in performance between an SVC and the model obtained through
partial ﬁne-tuning. Hence, we conﬁrm the observation from RQ1 that adding an SVC classiﬁcation head
to pre-trained model embeddings is a powerful approach to low-resource SER. Assessing the cross-
domain performance by transferring to DES and Emo-DB, contradicting results were observed. The
SVC trained on w2v2-FT-dim embeddings obtained the highest UAR for Emo-DB (62 ± 4)%, whereas
the ﬁne-tuned model is superior for DES and obtains (60 ± 8)%. Depending on the deﬁnition of human-
level recognition rate, the models do not surpass, but almost match, human-listeners.
