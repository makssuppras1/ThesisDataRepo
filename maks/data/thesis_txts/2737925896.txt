Abstract
This thesis investigates the current capabilities, limitations, and potential of generative AI, focusing
on advanced agent architectures and frameworks in complex data analysis tasks within the consulting
industry. Through a comprehensive evaluation of multiple agent frameworks (LangGraph, CrewAI,
AutoGen, and TaskWeaver), the study provides insights into both the broader landscape of generative
AI and the specific capabilities of recent agent-based approaches. Employing a qualitative methodol-
ogy, the research combines an extensive literature review with hands-on testing of LLM-based agent
frameworks in collaboration with Beecon, a Danish strategy consultancy. The study develops and
refines a use-case matrix as a versatile tool for evaluating generative AI readiness across various
business functions, incorporating dimensions of data type (text-based to numerical) and task nature
(creative to precise).
Key findings reveal significant gaps between the theoretical capabilities of generative AI systems,
including agent architectures, and their practical performance in real-world data analysis scenarios.
The research highlights the current limitations of these technologies in handling complex datasets
and maintaining context across multi-step analyses, particularly for tasks requiring precision and
heavily based on numerical data.
While showing promise in basic data exploration and simple
analytical tasks, the evaluated frameworks struggled with the complexity and nuance often required
in consulting-level data analysis.
This research addresses a significant gap between theoretical advancements in LLM-based agent
frameworks and their practical application in complex data analysis tasks. By empirically testing
these recently developed agent frameworks in real-world scenarios, the study bridges the divide be-
tween promising theoretical capabilities and limitations identified in recent literature. This approach
provides one of the first practical evaluations of emerging agent frameworks in data analysis contexts,
contributing to a more nuanced understanding of the current state of AI in this field.
The thesis proposes a cautious approach to generative AI and agent framework integration in data
analysis, suggesting their use for initial data exploration and routine tasks involving simple, well-
structured datasets, while emphasizing the critical role of human expertise in strategic interpretation
and complex, precision-critical analyses.
This research underscores the need for consulting firms
to carefully consider the current limitations of AI technologies in data analysis, particularly when
dealing with complex datasets.
This study offers valuable insights for both practitioners and researchers across various industries,
providing a nuanced view of the current state of generative AI and agent architectures and their
practical applicability in consulting and data analysis contexts. The findings have implications be-
yond consulting, offering insights into the potential and limitations of generative AI in data analysis
across various sectors, and emphasizing the importance of a balanced approach that leverages both
AI capabilities and human expertise.
Generative AI tools were used in the writing process of this thesis, in accordance with university
guidelines and academic standards, as discussed in the methodology section 3.8.

Introduction
1.1
Background
1.1.1
Definition and Evolution of Generative AI
Generative AI refers to a class of artificial intelligence systems capable of creating new content, rather
than simply analyzing or categorizing existing data (Bandi et al., 2023). The origins of generative AI
can be traced back to early concepts such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), introduced
in 2014 (Goodfellow et al., 2014). However, the field has experienced rapid advancements in recent
years, particularly in the domain of natural language processing (NLP). A significant milestone in
the evolution of generative AI was the development of large language models (LLMs). These models,
trained on vast amounts of text data, have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in understanding
and generating human-like text. The GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) series, developed
by OpenAI, has been at the forefront of this revolution. Each iteration, from GPT-1 to GPT-4,
has shown significant improvements in language understanding and generation capabilities (Brown
et al., 2020). The transition from narrow AI, designed for specific tasks, to more general capabil-
ities has been a key characteristic of this evolution. Modern LLMs can perform a wide range of
language-related tasks, from translation and summarization to creative writing and code generation,
often with minimal task-specific training (Bommasani et al., 2021). Recent surveys by McKinsey
(McKinsey & Company, 2024) indicate that the adoption of generative AI has accelerated rapidly,
with organizations increasingly recognizing its potential to generate value across various sectors. This
rapid evolution has set the stage for more advanced applications and integrations of generative AI
technologies.
1.1.2
Emergence of Advanced Agent Architectures
As generative AI capabilities have expanded, there has been a growing interest in developing more
sophisticated AI systems that can operate autonomously or semi-autonomously to accomplish com-
plex tasks. This has led to the emergence of advanced agent architectures, which aim to combine the
power of LLMs with other AI components to create more versatile and capable systems (Xi et al.,
2023). Agent architectures in the world of AI and LLMs refer to frameworks that enable AI systems
to perceive their environment, reason about their observations, and take actions to achieve specific
goals. These architectures have evolved from single-agent systems, focused on individual tasks, to
multi-agent systems with the potential for collaboration and tackling more complex problems (Zhao
et al., 2024). Key components of LLM-powered autonomous agent systems typically include (Weng,
2023):
1. Planning: The ability to break down large tasks into smaller, manageable subgoals, and refine
actions through self-reflection.
2. Memory: Both short-term memory (utilizing in-context learning) and long-term memory
(often implemented through external vector stores for information retention and retrieval).
3. Tool use: The capability to interact with external APIs and tools to access information or
perform actions beyond the model’s training data.

1.1.3
Potential for Transformation of Complex Workflows
The integration of generative AI and the development of advanced agent architectures hold signif-
icant potential for transforming complex workflows across various industries. While many of these
applications are still in early stages or theoretical, the possibilities include:
1. Automation of routine tasks: LLM-based agent systems could potentially handle repetitive,
time-consuming tasks, freeing up human workers to focus on higher-value activities (Zhao et al.,
2024)).
2. Augmentation of human capabilities: By providing rapid information synthesis and gen-
erating preliminary insights, AI agents could support human decision-making in areas such as
strategic planning and problem-solving (Weng, 2023).
3. Enhanced collaboration: Advanced agent architectures might facilitate new forms of human-
AI collaboration, where AI systems work alongside humans in complex problem-solving scenar-
ios (Weng, 2023).
4. Personalization at scale: The ability of generative AI to produce customized content and
solutions could lead to more personalized services across various industries.
However, it’s important to note that many of these potential applications face significant challenges
in practical implementation. These challenges include:

1. Finite context length: The restricted context capacity of current LLMs limits the inclusion
of historical information, detailed instructions, and responses, affecting the agent’s ability to
maintain long-term coherence (Weng, 2023).
2. Challenges in long-term planning and task decomposition: LLMs still struggle with
adjusting plans when faced with unexpected errors, making them less robust compared to
humans who can learn from trial and error (Weng, 2023).
3. Reliability of natural language interface: The current reliance on natural language as an
interface between LLMs and external components can lead to formatting errors and occasional
unexpected behaviors (Weng, 2023).
4. Technical integration issues and the need for workforce reskilling (McKinsey & Com-
pany, 2024).
5. Ethical concerns related to AI use, including privacy, bias, and accountability.
As generative AI and agent architectures continue to evolve, their potential impact on industries
and workflows is significant. However, realizing the full potential of these technologies will require
addressing both technical and organizational challenges, as well as careful consideration of ethical
implications.
1.2
Significance
The emergence of generative AI and advanced agent architectures represents a pivotal moment in
the evolution of technology, with far-reaching implications across various industries. This section
explores the significance of these developments, focusing on their potential to transform the consulting
industry, their impact on data analysis applications, and their broader implications for other sectors.
1.2.1
Potential of generative AI in transforming the consulting industry
The consulting industry stands at the cusp of a significant transformation driven by generative AI
technologies.
According to a recent report by Deloitte (Deloitte, 2023a), generative AI has the
potential to revolutionize how consultants work, enhancing their capabilities and enabling them to
deliver more value to clients. This transformation is manifested in several key areas:
1. Enhancing data analysis capabilities: Generative AI can process and analyze vast amounts
of data at unprecedented speeds, allowing consultants to derive insights more quickly and
accurately.
2. Automating report generation and presentation creation: AI-powered tools can assist in creating
comprehensive reports and visually appealing presentations, freeing up consultants’ time for
higher-value tasks.
3. Augmenting strategic decision-making processes: By providing rapid scenario analysis and
predictive modeling, generative AI can support consultants in formulating more robust strategic
recommendations.
4. Enabling new service offerings: The integration of AI technologies opens up possibilities for
novel consulting services, such as AI implementation strategies and AI-driven process optimiza-
tion.

1.3
Problem Statement
The rapid advancement of generative AI technologies has sparked significant interest across vari-
ous industries, particularly in the realm of management consulting. As these technologies continue
to evolve, it becomes increasingly important to critically examine their practical applications and
limitations, especially in complex, real-world scenarios.
This thesis, titled ’Generative AI and Agent Architectures: From Toy Examples to Solving Real
Tasks,’ aims to move beyond simplified demonstrations of AI capabilities. The term ’toy examples’
refers critically to often oversimplified demonstrations used to showcase generative AI capabilities,
particularly in demonstrations of theoretical advances that may not yet be practically realizable. This
research seeks to evaluate these technologies in real-world, complex consulting scenarios, acknowl-
edging that current capabilities may fall short of the impressiveness suggested by such simplified
demonstrations.
1.3.1
Thesis Evolution
This thesis initially set out to explore the broad topic of ”Enhancing Consultancy Practices in the
Digital Age: The Strategic Integration of Generative AI”. The original problem statement focused
on analyzing the evolving role of AI in management consulting, with a particular emphasis on the
strategic use of generative AI to enhance consulting practices. However, as the research progressed, it
became apparent that a more focused approach was necessary to address the most pressing challenges
and opportunities in this field. Through an iterative learning process, involving extensive literature
review and engagement with industry developments, the thesis focus evolved.
The investigation
into capabilities and supporting use-cases for smaller consultancies, specifically Beecon, a Danish
strategy consultancy, led to a narrower focus on data analysis as a key use case.
Concurrently,
exploration of the most recent developments in generative AI revealed the emerging potential of
state-of-the-art LLM-based agent frameworks. This evolution in understanding and focus is reflected
in the revised title and problem statement, which now more accurately capture the intersection of
generative AI, advanced agent architectures, and their practical applications in complex, real-world
tasks, particularly within the consulting industry.

1.3.2
Current Problem Statement
Generative AI technologies hold significant potential for transforming various industries through
advanced agent architectures capable of handling complex tasks autonomously.
However, the gap
between theoretical capabilities demonstrated in controlled environments and practical applications in
real-world scenarios remains significant. This thesis explores the current state and future potential
of generative AI and agent architectures in performing comprehensive, complex tasks. By evaluating
multiple agent frameworks, including those that extend LLMs with external memory and multiagent
frameworks for planning, execution, and observation, the research aims to identify the strengths and
limitations of existing technologies and outline a vision for their future development.
The thesis
will also provide a practical implementation strategy for integrating generative AI into consulting
workflows, addressing the challenges faced in realizing the full potential of these technologies.
This refined problem statement addresses several key issues at the forefront of generative AI and its
application in consulting:
Gap between theoretical capabilities and practical applications
This thesis addresses a

2.1
Generative AI
Generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) represents a significant advancement in the field of AI, charac-
terized by its ability to create new content. This section explores the fundamental concepts, historical
development, key technologies, and core capabilities of generative AI, laying the groundwork for sub-
sequent discussions on its applications and implications in consulting and data analysis.
2.1.1
Definition and Core Concepts
At the heart of generative AI lie several core concepts:
1. Data Distribution Modeling: Generative AI fundamentally aims to learn and model the un-
derlying probability distribution of the training data. This enables the model to generate new
samples that follow similar statistical patterns to the original data (Goodfellow et al., 2014).
2. Neural Networks: The backbone of modern generative AI systems are artificial neural net-
works, computational models inspired by biological neural networks. These networks consist of
interconnected nodes (neurons) organized in layers, capable of learning complex patterns from
data through a process called training (Katariya, 2023).
3. Latent Space Representation: Many generative models learn to represent data in a compressed,
abstract form known as latent space. This representation captures the essential features of the
data, allowing for eﬀicient manipulation and generation of new samples Emami, 2023.
4. Self-Attention Mechanisms: A key innovation in modern generative AI, particularly in language
models, is the self-attention mechanism. This allows the model to weigh the importance of
different parts of the input when generating each part of the output, enabling more coherent
and context-aware generation (Vaswani et al., 2017).
2.1.2
Historical Development
The evolution of generative AI has been marked by several key milestones, each building upon
previous advancements to expand the capabilities and applications of these systems:
1. Early Probabilistic Models: The roots of generative AI can be traced back to probabilistic
models such as Hidden Markov Models and Gaussian Mixture Models. These early approaches
laid the groundwork for modeling data distributions but were limited in their ability to handle
complex, high-dimensional data (Katariya, 2023).
2. Neural Network Renaissance: The resurgence of neural networks, driven by advancements in
computing power, the availability of large datasets, and breakthroughs like backpropagation
and deep learning techniques, paved the way for more sophisticated generative models C. Gupta,
2024.
3. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs): Introduced in 2014, GANs represented a break-
through in generative modeling. The GAN architecture, consisting of a generator and a dis-
criminator network competing against each other, enabled the creation of more realistic and
diverse outputs, particularly in image generation tasks (Goodfellow et al., 2014).

4. Transformer Architecture: The introduction of the Transformer architecture in 2017 marked
a pivotal moment in the development of generative AI, particularly for natural language pro-
cessing tasks. The self-attention mechanism introduced in this work became a cornerstone of
modern language models, enabling more effective processing of sequential data (Vaswani et al.,
2017).
5. Large Language Models (LLMs): Building upon the Transformer architecture, the development
of increasingly large and sophisticated language models, such as the GPT (Generative Pre-
trained Transformer) series, BERT, and their successors, has pushed the boundaries of what’s
possible in text generation and understanding. These models have demonstrated remarkable
capabilities in various language tasks, from translation to creative writing (Brown et al., 2020).
This historical progression reflects a shift from simple statistical models to increasingly complex
neural network architectures, each advancement expanding the capabilities and potential applications
of generative AI.
2.1.3
Fundamental Capabilities and Limitations
Generative AI models exhibit several fundamental capabilities that set them apart from traditional
AI systems:
1. Content Generation: The primary capability of generative AI is to create new, original content
that mimics the patterns and structures of its training data.
This extends beyond text to
include images, audio, and even 3D models (Bandi et al., 2023).
2. Pattern Recognition and Completion: These models can recognize complex patterns in data
and complete partial inputs in a context-aware manner. This capability is particularly evident
in tasks like text completion and image inpainting (Brown et al., 2020).
3. Transfer Learning: Advanced generative models can apply knowledge from one domain to
related tasks or domains, demonstrating a form of generalization. This capability has been
crucial in developing models that can perform well across a wide range of tasks with minimal
task-specific training (Bommasani et al., 2021)).
4. Few-shot and Zero-shot Learning: Some models, particularly large language models, can per-
form new tasks with minimal or no specific training examples. This flexibility allows them to
adapt to new scenarios and tasks quickly (Brown et al., 2020).
However, despite these impressive capabilities, generative AI also faces several important limitations:
1. Lack of True Understanding: Despite their impressive outputs, current generative AI models do
not possess true comprehension or reasoning abilities comparable to human intelligence. They
operate based on statistical patterns rather than semantic understanding (Fraser, 2024).
2. Hallucinations: Generative models, especially in text generation, can sometimes produce plausible-
sounding but factually incorrect information. This phenomenon, often referred to as ”halluci-
nation,” poses challenges in applications where accuracy is crucial (Fraser, 2024).
3. Bias and Fairness Issues: These models can inadvertently learn and amplify biases present
in their training data, raising concerns about fairness and ethical use in various applications
(Bommasani et al., 2021)).

2.3.1
LLM-based Agents
As introduced in section 1.1.2, we will go further in-depth on LLM-based agents and advanced agent
architectures here.
LLM-based agents are AI systems that leverage the powerful language understanding and generation
capabilities of large language models as their core cognitive engine. These agents are designed to
autonomously perform complex tasks by combining the LLM’s capabilities with additional compo-
nents that enable interaction with the environment (Weng, 2023). The architecture of these agents
typically comprises three fundamental components: planning, memory, and tool use.

The planning component allows agents to decompose complex tasks into manageable steps and create
strategies for achieving goals. This often involves sophisticated task decomposition techniques and the
ability to adjust plans based on new information or unexpected outcomes. Weng (2023) highlights
that effective planning is crucial for agents to handle real-world tasks where trial and error are

Memory systems in LLM-based agents usually consist of both short-term and long-term compo-

while long-term memory may involve external vector stores for eﬀicient information retention and

past experiences (Weng, 2023).
The tool use component enables agents to interact with external APIs, databases, or other resources,
significantly expanding their capabilities beyond the inherent knowledge of the LLM (Weng, 2023).
This ability to leverage external tools is particularly valuable in domains like consulting, where access
to up-to-date information and specialized tools is often crucial.
LLM-based agents can be deployed in either single-agent or multi-agent configurations. Single-agent
setups involve a single LLM-powered agent tackling a task or problem, while multi-agent systems
involve multiple agents working collaboratively. Multi-agent systems have shown promising results in
various applications, from scientific discovery to complex problem-solving scenarios (Li et al., 2024).
In multi-agent systems, effective collaboration between agents is crucial for successful problem-
solving. This collaboration can take various forms, including role-based cooperation, task decom-
position and distribution, hierarchical structures, and consensus-based decision making (Zhao et al.,
2024). For instance, in a data analysis scenario, one agent might specialize in data cleaning, another
in statistical analysis, and a third in data visualization.
The flexibility of LLM-based agents allows for dynamic assignment of roles and tasks, adapting to
the specific requirements of each problem or scenario. This adaptability is particularly valuable in
complex, open-ended domains such as consulting, where problems often lack clear-cut solutions and
require a combination of analytical, creative, and strategic thinking.

2.3.2
Popular Agent Frameworks
The growing interest in LLM-based agents has led to the development of various frameworks designed
to facilitate the creation and deployment of these intelligent systems. While numerous frameworks
exist, including ChatDev, MetaGPT, AutoGPT, and LlamaIndex, this section focuses on four promi-
nent frameworks: LangGraph, CrewAI, AutoGen, and TaskWeaver.
LangGraph, developed as part of the LangChain ecosystem, provides a flexible framework for building
multi-agent systems.
It emphasizes the creation of conversational agents that can interact with
each other and external tools.
LangGraph’s design philosophy centers on the idea of creating a
graph of language models and tools, allowing for complex workflows and decision-making processes
(LangChain, 2024b).
CrewAI is an emerging framework that focuses on creating collaborative multi-agent systems. It
emphasizes the creation of specialized agents that can work together to solve complex tasks. CrewAI’s
architecture is designed to facilitate easy integration making it suitable for less advanced developers.
(CrewAI, 2024).
AutoGen, developed by Microsoft, is designed to enable the creation of next-generation LLM ap-
plications through multi-agent conversations. Wu et al. (2023) describe AutoGen as a modular and
programmable framework that simplifies the development of complex workflows and enhances perfor-
mance across various tasks. It supports dynamic group chats and multi-agent interactions, making
it versatile for different scenarios.
TaskWeaver, also developed by Microsoft, presents itself as a code-first agent framework. Qiao et al.
(2023) explain that TaskWeaver addresses the limitations of existing frameworks in handling rich
data structures and incorporating domain knowledge. It converts user requests into executable code,
treating user-defined plugins as callable functions. This approach allows for seamless integration
with custom code and supports complex data structures, enabling sophisticated and domain-specific
applications.
Each of these frameworks offers unique features and functionalities tailored to different use cases and
applications. In the context of consulting and data analysis, these frameworks can be applied to tasks
such as complex data analysis pipelines, automated report generation, collaborative problem-solving
in strategic consulting scenarios, and integration of domain-specific tools and knowledge bases into
AI-driven workflows.
2.3.3
Advanced Agent Techniques and Patterns
The development of LLM-based agents has been accompanied by the emergence of advanced tech-
niques and patterns designed to enhance their reasoning capabilities, improve task performance,
and enable more sophisticated problem-solving approaches. This section explores some of the key
techniques that have significantly impacted the field of AI agents.
Chain of Thought (CoT) and Tree of Thoughts (ToT) are two related techniques that aim to improve
the reasoning capabilities of language models. Chain of Thought, introduced by Wei et al. (2022) is
a prompting technique that enhances model performance on complex tasks by instructing the model
to ”think step by step.” This approach breaks down complex problems into smaller, more manageable
steps, providing an interpretable sequence of the model’s reasoning process.
Building upon CoT, Tree of Thoughts (ToT), proposed by Yao et al. (2023b), extends this concept
by exploring multiple reasoning possibilities at each step. ToT creates a tree structure of thoughts,
allowing for a more comprehensive exploration of potential solutions. This method involves decom-
posing problems into multiple thought steps, generating multiple thoughts per step, and using search

processes like breadth-first search (BFS) or depth-first search (DFS) to evaluate different reasoning

The ReAct framework, introduced by Yao et al. (2023a), integrates reasoning and acting within
LLMs. ReAct extends the action space to include both task-specific discrete actions and language
generation. This approach enables LLMs to interact with the environment (e.g., using external APIs)
while generating reasoning traces in natural language. The ReAct prompt template incorporates
explicit steps for the LLM to reason, then act, creating a more structured approach to problem-
solving.
Reflexion, proposed by Shinn and Labash (2023), is a framework designed to enhance language-based
agents through linguistic feedback and self-reflection. Reflexion functions by converting environmen-
tal feedback—either in free-form language or scalar form—into self-reflective context for the agent.
This feedback, utilized in subsequent interactions, enables the agent to rapidly learn from previous
errors, thereby improving performance on complex tasks. By leveraging this dynamic memory and
self-reflection mechanism, Reflexion equips agents with the ability to refine their reasoning skills and
strategies over time..
The Buffer of Thoughts (BoT) technique, recently introduced by Yang et al. (2024), aims to enhance
the exploration of ideas in LLMs by providing a structured method to explore different lines of
reasoning. BoT introduces a meta-buffer to store high-level thoughts (thought-templates) distilled
from problem-solving processes across various tasks. These templates are retrieved and instantiated
with specific reasoning structures for each problem, allowing for eﬀicient reasoning. A buffer-manager
dynamically updates the meta-buffer, enhancing its capacity as more tasks are solved.
These advanced techniques and patterns represent significant strides in improving the reasoning and
problem-solving capabilities of LLM-based agents. Their development reflects the ongoing efforts to
create more robust, eﬀicient, and adaptable AI systems capable of handling increasingly complex
tasks in real-world scenarios.
As we continue to explore the landscape of LLM-based agents, it becomes evident that these tech-

analysis. The following sections will delve deeper into the comparison of agent frameworks, their
capabilities in handling complex tasks, and the challenges and future trends in this rapidly evolving
field.
2.3.4
Challenges, Limitations, and Future Trends
Despite the significant advancements in LLM-based agent technologies, several challenges and limi-
tations persist, particularly in handling complex, real-world tasks. Understanding these limitations
is crucial for setting realistic expectations and identifying areas for future development.
One of the primary challenges in current agent frameworks is the handling of long-term dependencies
and maintaining coherence over extended task sequences. Weng (2023) points out that the finite
context length of LLMs poses a significant constraint, limiting the amount of historical information
and detailed instructions that can be included in a single interaction. This limitation can impact
the agent’s ability to maintain consistency and context awareness in complex, multi-step tasks often
encountered in consulting projects.
Another critical limitation lies in the reliability of natural language interfaces between LLMs and
external components.
As Weng (2023) notes, the current reliance on natural language for tool
interactions can lead to formatting errors and occasional unexpected behaviors. This unreliability
can be particularly problematic in data analysis scenarios where precision is paramount.
The challenge of long-term planning and effective task decomposition also remains significant. While
techniques like Tree of Thoughts (Yao et al., 2023b) have shown promise in improving planning

Looking towards future trends, several promising directions emerge:
1. Enhanced Integration of Traditional AI: Future developments may focus on combining the
strengths of LLM-based agents with traditional AI approaches. This integration could lead to

2. Advanced Multi-Modal Capabilities: As highlighted by Y. Cheng et al. (2024), future agent

4. Domain-Specific Specialization: As the field matures, we may see the emergence of agent frame-

The implications of these developments for consulting and data analysis applications are profound.

Methodology
3.1
Research Approach and Design
This study adopted an exploratory qualitative research methodology to investigate the potential of
generative AI and agent architectures in performing complex data analysis tasks within the consulting
domain. Further, the choice of an exploratory approach was driven by the rapidly evolving nature of
AI technologies and the relative novelty of agent architectures in practical applications. The research
design incorporated an iterative process that combined extensive literature review with hands-on,
practical exploration of various agent frameworks. This approach allowed for a dynamic investigation
that could adapt to new insights and technological developments as they emerged during the research
process. The justification for this methodology lies in its ability to provide rich, contextual insights
into a field where quantitative metrics alone may not capture the full complexity of AI capabilities
and limitations. By focusing on qualitative assessment, the study aimed to uncover nuanced aspects
of AI performance in real-world consulting scenarios, which are often characterized by ambiguity and
complexity.
3.2
Research Process and Evolution
The research process began with a broad exploration of generative AI applications in consulting. This
initial phase involved an extensive literature review, covering academic papers, industry reports, and
technical documentation. Key sources included publications from major consulting firms like McKin-
sey & Company and Deloitte, as well as academic research on generative AI and its applications. As
the research progressed, a collaboration was established with Beecon, a Danish strategy consultancy.
Through discussions with Beecon and a lightweight use-case analysis, data analysis was selected as
the primary use-case for investigation. This focus on data analysis aligned well with the research
objectives, as it represented a complex, multifaceted task that could potentially benefit significantly
from AI augmentation. The research then transitioned to a more focused exploration of LLM-based
agent frameworks. This shift was motivated by several factors:
1. Initial consideration of single-model approaches like ChatGPT, custom GPTs, and OpenAI’s
Assistants revealed limitations in addressing the complex, multi-step nature of data analysis
tasks in consulting.
2. The exploration of agent frameworks presented an opportunity to address identified research
gaps, particularly in handling more sophisticated, real-world data analysis scenarios.
3. Agent architectures offered the potential to push the boundaries of AI capabilities in data
analysis, moving beyond the ’toy examples’ often seen in AI demonstrations.
This evolution in focus reflects the iterative nature of the research process, allowing for adjustment
based on emerging insights and technological developments.

1. Workflow Initiation: LangGraph initiates the workflow by sending the initial state (e.g., con-

TaskWeaver: Comprehensive Testing and Evaluation
TaskWeaver, developed by Microsoft, is a code-first agent framework designed for seamless planning
and execution of data analytics tasks. According to Microsoft’s documentation (Microsoft, 2024),
TaskWeaver’s architecture consists of three core components:
1. Planner: Serves as the system’s entry point and interacts with the user.
It breaks down
user requests into subtasks, manages the execution process with self-reflection, and transforms
execution results into human-readable responses.
2. Code Generator: Generates code for given subtasks from the Planner, considering existing
plugins and domain-specific task examples.
3. Code Executor: Responsible for executing the generated code and maintaining the execution
state throughout the entire session.
This architecture allows TaskWeaver to convert natural language user requests into executable code,
with support for rich data structures, dynamic plugin selection, and domain-adapted planning pro-
cesses. The framework aims to handle complex data structures prevalent in data analytics tasks and
domain-specific scenarios.

2. Technical Integration and Usability:
• Challenge: Framework setup and configuration often required significant technical ex-

• Future Priority: Design more user-friendly interfaces and streamline integration pro-

3. Error Handling and Reliability:
• Challenge: Agents frequently became stuck in error loops or failed to recognize errors in

• Future Priority: Implement sophisticated self-monitoring and error-correction mecha-

• Challenge: Current frameworks present barriers to effective integration into human-led
analytical teams.
• Future Priority: Create seamless interfaces for human-AI collaboration, focusing on
intuitive handoffs between AI and human analysts in complex workflows.
5. Visualization and Reporting Capabilities:
• Challenge: AI-generated visualizations and reports often lacked the sophistication re-
quired for professional consulting contexts.

5.5
Limitations of the Study
While our research provides valuable insights into the current state and potential of AI agent frame-
works in data analysis, it’s important to acknowledge several limitations of our study:
1. Scope of Frameworks Tested: Due to time and resource constraints, we were only able to
evaluate a limited number of agent frameworks (LangGraph, CrewAI, AutoGen, and TaskWeaver).
While these represent some of the leading technologies in the field, our findings may not be
fully generalizable to all existing or emerging AI agent frameworks.
2. Focus on Data Analysis in an Consulting Context: Our study primarily focused on
the application of AI in consulting data analysis scenarios, particularly in collaboration with
Beecon. While we believe many of our findings are applicable to data analysis in other in-
dustries, the specific needs and constraints of the consulting industry may have influenced our
results.
3. Simplified Test Scenarios: To manage the complexity of our evaluation, we often used sim-
plified datasets and analytical tasks. While this was necessary given the current limitations of
the technologies, it may not fully represent the complexity of real-world consulting engagements.
4. Rapid Pace of AI Development: The field of AI is evolving rapidly, with new advancements
emerging frequently. Our findings represent a snapshot of the technology’s capabilities at the
time of our study and may not reflect subsequent improvements or new approaches that have
since been developed.
5. Qualitative Nature of Evaluation: While we used a structured evaluation framework, much
of our assessment was qualitative in nature. This approach allowed for nuanced insights but
may lack the statistical rigor of more quantitative studies.
6. Limited Size, Depth, and Repetitions: Our insights beyond the literature review stems
from the researcher’s integration and testing of the agent frameworks and strengthened by
Beecon’s involvement in TaskWeaver and ChatGPT testing. However, a larger group of partic-
ipants and various other tests might have provided additional insights or perspectives.
These limitations suggest several avenues for future research, including broader evaluations of AI
frameworks, longer-term studies of AI impact on consulting practices, and more quantitative assess-
ments of AI performance in complex, real-world analytical tasks.

The integration of AI technologies in data analysis raises several important ethical considerations
that must be addressed for responsible adoption:
1. Data Privacy and Security: As AI systems process vast amounts of data, often including
sensitive client information, ensuring robust data protection measures is crucial. Organizations
must implement stringent data governance policies, including data anonymization techniques
and secure data storage practices. For instance, consulting firms working with government
clients and personal data of individuals, must ensure their AI systems comply with regulations
like GDPR in EU.

6
Conclusion
This thesis set out to explore the current state and future potential of generative AI and agent
architectures in performing complex data analysis tasks, with a particular focus on applications
within the consulting domain. Through a comprehensive evaluation of multiple agent frameworks,
including LangGraph, CrewAI, AutoGen, and TaskWeaver, we have gained valuable insights into
the capabilities, limitations, and challenges of implementing these technologies for data analysis in
real-world scenarios.
Revisiting our research questions, we can draw several key conclusions:
1. Current Capabilities, Limitations, and Risks in Data Analysis: Our investigation
revealed that while generative AI and agent architectures show promise in certain areas of
data analysis, they face significant challenges when dealing with complex, real-world tasks,
particularly those involving numerical data and requiring precision. The capabilities of these
systems are currently limited to basic data exploration, simple analysis tasks, and the generation
of initial insights from well-structured, simple datasets. They also demonstrate some ability to
create basic visualizations and potentially automate simple, routine reporting tasks.
However, the limitations of these technologies in data analysis are substantial. We observed
poor performance with complex, multi-dimensional datasets, inconsistent code execution and
error handling, and a lack of depth and creativity in analysis compared to human experts. The
AI systems struggled to maintain context across multi-step analyses and showed limited ability
to incorporate domain-specific knowledge and contextual understanding, which are crucial for
precise, number-based analyses.
The potential risks associated with the use of these technologies in data analysis include the
generation of inaccurate or misleading insights due to misinterpretation of complex data, over-
reliance on AI-generated analyses without proper human oversight, and privacy and security
concerns when handling sensitive data. There is also a risk of reinforcing biases present in
training data or algorithms, which can be particularly problematic in numerical analyses where
precision is paramount.
2. Performance of Agent Architectures vs. Single-Agent Approaches in Data Anal-
ysis: Our evaluation revealed that while agent architectures like LangGraph and TaskWeaver
offer potential advantages over single-agent approaches like ChatGPT in data analysis tasks,
their practical implementation faces several challenges. The advantages include the potential
for more structured workflows and task decomposition, possibly more robust error-handling ca-
pabilities, and flexibility in defining specialized agent roles for different aspects of data analysis.
However, these advantages were often outweighed by implementation challenges and current
capability limitations in complex data analysis scenarios. We observed diﬀiculties in main-
taining context and coherence across multi-agent interactions, increased complexity in setup
and configuration compared to single-agent systems, and inconsistent performance, with some
multi-agent setups performing worse than simpler approaches when dealing with numerical data
and precision-based tasks.
A key finding was the significant gap between the theoretical capabilities of agent architec-
tures, as described in recent literature, and their practical performance in our real-world data
analysis testing scenarios. This highlights the need for caution when interpreting theoretical
advancements and underscores the importance of rigorous, real-world testing in assessing the
practical applicability of these technologies for data analysis tasks.
3. Augmentation of Human Capabilities in Data Analysis: Our findings suggest that while
current AI technologies cannot replace human expertise in complex data analysis tasks, they

precision and heavily based on numerical data. The most promising scenarios for AI augmenta-
