Abstract
The daily physical activities of blue-collar workers are in this project investigated
through their relation to various health indicators, where especially lower back pain
plays a prominent role. The physical activities are deﬁned on a 24-hour basis, which
imply an interdependency of the daily activities since any time spent in one activity
eﬀects the time available for other activities.
The compositional nature of the data makes a multinomial logistic regression model
a ﬁtting choice, as this model allows us to connect several health indicators to the
daily physical activities. Due to the heterogeneity of the data and consequently large
variance from worker to worker, a layer is added to the Multinomial model in the form
of Dirichlet distributed parameters.
Finally, a logistic regression model with a variable describing a form of acute lower
back pain as the dependent variable is considered. Through this model, it is possible
to predict the probability of experiencing lower back pain given a distribution of daily
physical activity. The distributions associated with low risk of back pain could then
be searched for, while considering the practical limitations of a blue-collar worker. It
was found that Moderate to Vigorous Physical Activity at work exhibited a quadratic
relation with lower back pain, where both a very small and large amount increased
the risk of lower back pain. At work a general increase in Sedentary Behaviour lead
to a decrease in the predicted probability of pain, likely due to the more strenuous
alternative tasks performed as a blue-collar worker. For leisure, a general increase in
Moderate to Vigorous Physical Activity was beneﬁcial for decreasing the risk of lower
back pain. These results were found to be congruent with previous studies.

Introduction
1
Introduction
This thesis is a collaborative project with the National Centre for the Working Environ-
ment (NFA). This external inﬂuence has played a signiﬁcant role in the choices made in
the project, which in the end has made the project more diverse. The project concerns
itself with the analysis of the distributions of daily physical activities (such as Standing
or Light Physical Activity) on a 24-hour basis.
It will seek to answer the questions of optimality in regards to the amount of time one
should allocate to each activity. In order to search purposefully for answers to these
questions, the daily physical activities are linked to various health indicators.
The
project will especially focus on lower back pain, as an indicator of health.
The data considered in this project is part of a large data set collected among danish
workers in the manufacturing, transportation and cleaning industry. Due to the labo-
rious nature of the tasks performed in these sectors, it is of great societal importance
to be able to positively adjust the distribution of daily physical activities in order to
minimize the risk of developing acute and chronic injuries.
Studies concerned with the distribution of our daily physical activity, have often been
grounded in self-reported data and thus introducing the error of human estimation [22].
For this project, we will consider objective measurements of blue-collar workers mea-
sured throughout multiple days with wearable accelerometers. The accelerometer is a
device worn, in this case, on the thigh where it constantly measures the acceleration
and angle. This data will form the basis of which the subsequent statistical models will
be deﬁned. The daily physical activities are generally negatively correlated with one
another, which will inﬂuence the assumptions imposed on the models used throughout
the analysis. This is due to the fact that all activities take place within 24 hours, which
means that more time spent in one activity will lead to less time being available for
other activities. This interdependence of the variables is the subject of focus in the ﬁeld
of compositional data analysis, which will play a signiﬁcant role in the models of the
analysis.
The ﬁrst part of the project (section 4 to 6) aims to explore the relations in the data
without a speciﬁc relation in mind. This part is the result of a modelling approach free
from requirements later imposed on the modelling. First, the basis of the assumptions
regarding compositional data analysis are presented as well as an overview of previous
studies using this method. A generalized linear model, using a Multinomial distribu-
tion as a link to the compositional data analysis, is then deﬁned. This models seeks to

extend the use of compositional data analysis to a higher dimensional problem. The

in the form of Dirichlet distributed parameters. By deﬁning a hierarchical model we

The second part (section 7 to 10) incorporates the expectations and requirements of
the external partner (NFA) and focuses the modelling on speciﬁc variables/relations in

Environment. Speciﬁcally, one health indicator is chosen as the model output which
reﬂects the level of complexity found in similar studies. The main model considered
in the second part is a logistic regression model used to predict the probability of
experiencing acute lower back pain, given a daily physical activity pattern. The relation
between lower back pain and daily physical activity is especially interesting, as the
problem of lower back pain prevails as one of the main work injuries observed. Since the
modelling approach is greatly inﬂuenced by practical aspects, constraints on the logistic
regression model must be imposed as well. It is important to deﬁne an interpretable
model and bound the solutions to a realistic space, such that we can infer meaningful
results for possible future use in a practical setting.
The data considered in this project is cross-sectional, meaning that all data is collected
at the same point in time. This limits the analysis to a cross-sectional study where any
causality can not be determined. Rather the prevalence or existence of relations can be
investigated. The analysis carried out in this project can thus form the basis of a future
causality study of the connection between lower back pain and daily physical activity
patterns.
The ﬁrst part has the more interesting mathematical complexity, which was desired as
a part of this project. Whereas the second part is more focused on the interpretation
of the results.
A preliminary explorative analysis of the data (e.g. a correlation analysis), indicated a
weak signal in the data. Due to the heterogeneity of the population considered in the
data, a large amount of noise in the data is expected. Generally in public health studies,
the resulting eﬀect sizes are small due to the heterogeneous data. It is therefore expected
that the weak signal will aﬀect the performance of the selected models. Consequently
the eﬀect sizes of the models should be considered in the context of similar studies.

2
Data
The data set used for this project is supplied by the National Research Centre for
the Working Environment. Most importantly the data consists of daily physical activ-
ity measurements. These measurements describe how a person’s daily time-budget is
distributed among various activities.
2.1
Data Collection
In 2013 a large study involving hundreds of danish workers was instigated in order
to gather daily physical activity measurements (Jørgensen et al.
(2013) [14]).
The
previously available research were primarily based on self-reported data, making it easy
to question the validity of any ﬁndings.
A technological advancement had however
made more objective measurements possible and a study measuring how (primarily)
blue-collar workers distributed their daily time budget was carried out. The workers
were also asked to ﬁll out a baseline questionnaire regarding demographics, dietary
habits, education and so fourth. Physical tests, pain indications and measurable health
indicators, such as BMI, were also measured. For the physical activity measurements,
the workers were asked to wear accelerometers for up to ﬁve workdays, with as little
non-wear time as possible.
The accelerometers are able to measure the tilt angles as well as acceleration. These
measurements can then be used to determine whether the wearer is standing, sitting,
running etc, based on certain threshold criteria of the accelerations and angles. The
accelerometry data is transformed using the software Acti4 [15], which categorizes the
raw data into minutes spent sitting, standing, doing physical activity etc. for each day.
Furthermore the participants of the study denoted when they were working in order to
separate leisure and work time in the data set. Any days with less than 4 hours of work
or leisure time of accelerometry measurements were excluded from the data set.
The accelerometry data was measured across multiple days (the amount of days varying
from person to person) and the mean time spent standing, sitting etc. on a daily basis
was computed and are represented as single data points in the data set. The amount of
time in bed was likewise measured, but here the median of the measurements was taken
as the representative data point. The distribution of bedtime was skewed in a way that
made the median the preferred option over the mean.
The transformed accelerometry data describing daily activity patterns are compositional
in nature, as activities such as sleeping, walking and sedentary behaviour are measured
as being executed in the same 24 hours. Time spent in one activity will naturally aﬀect
the time spent in the rest, as their is an unchangeable amount of hours in a day.

The following analysis of this data will have to consider the underlying compositional
nature of the data and the implied codependency of these variables.
2.2
Variables of Interest
The data set containing the averaged activities over the measured days contains 1746
observations with over 500 variables. Many of the variables are however derivations or
transformations of other variables, such as variables encoding the number of times spent
doing an activity instead of time spent in the activity. For this project, only a fraction
of the total number of variables is relevant. All the variables are missing observations
in some degree. In fact, if all rows containing a missing observation are removed, the
data set is reduced to nothing. This is likely due to logistic diﬃculties in the gathering
of a data set involving many participants, such as faulty measurements. These missing
values results in a smaller data set once the relevant variables are extracted for further
analysis.
The accelerometry data is divided into many diﬀerent categories, but for this project
they will be combined into ﬁve composite variables consisting of:
Sedentary Behaviour, which consists of the time spent Lying Down and Sitting during
the waking day.
Light Physical Activity (LPA), which consists of time spent Moving and Walking Slowly.
Standing, which merely consists of time spent standing upright and not walking.
Moderate to Vigorous Physical Activity (MVPA), which consists of time spent Running,
walking on Stairs and Walking Fast.
Lastly Sleep consists purely of time spent sleeping.

3
Compositional Data
The distribution of activities throughout the day are assumed to be compositional in
nature, as time spent in sleeping naturally aﬀects the time available for other activ-
ities. It is assumed that the diﬀerent activities are fractions of a whole day and the
summation of them all constitutes 24 hours. This assumption turns out to be slightly
violated for the considered data as many observations do not sum to exactly 24 hours.
For the purpose of this project we will assume that the activity variables represents a
fair estimate of the fraction of the day spent in that activity for all observations. By
this assumption, the activities can be normalized, i.e. divided by their sum in order to
sum to one, and thus represented as proportions of a day.
A data-vector is said to be compositional when the elements of the vector sum to a
known whole. Consider a vector of positive elements x = [x1, x2, .., xD] ∈RD , where

of the constant C, which is some real positive number, can be any arbitrary number but
is for simplicity sake often scaled to one. This expresses x as proportions or percentages,
which is more interpretable in some cases. There are thus D−1 degrees of freedom, as the
last element of x can be determined from the constraint. The space where x is deﬁned
can be represented by the D-part simplex (SD), which is a D −1 dimensional subset
of RD. The standard euclidean rules are not valid on the simplex, as values beyond the
simplex are not part of the space. The normal operations, multiplication and addition,
are therefore replaced by closed operations on the simplex. These are called perturbation
and powering, which are closed versions of multiplication and powering. Under these
operations, the simplex forms an D −1 dimensional euclidean space. Due to the lack

3.2
Methods in the Literature
The transformations of compositional data can generally be used to move the data into
a euclidean space where traditional statistical methods can be used. When the statisti-
cal analysis has been carried out, the resulting inference can be transformed back into
compositional space and interpreted in its original context. The log-ratio transforma-
tions have readily available inverse functions, that maps back onto the simplex.
Using compositional data without a transformation in models that assume the data to
be normally distributed can lead to problems in estimation, as the normal distribution
can assume values over the full real axis, (−∞to ∞). Therefore the transformations are
needed to meet the requirements of statistical models and make inference meaningful.
Compositional physical activity data spanning 24 hours has previously been analysed
using log-ratio transformations ([10], [8], [9]). The compositional data was transformed
and a multiple linear regression was made, with a continuous variable as the dependent
variable.
Previous examples of the use of log-ratio transformation on compositional activity data
in relation to health indicators are primarily centered around a linear regression analysis.
How the compositional data is treated for the linear regression varies from study to
study. A concrete example hereof is found in Dumuid et al. [10].

3.2.2
Limitations and Further Applications
In traditional compositional data analysis all elements are assumed to be strictly greater
than zero. One of the limitations in compositional data analysis is dealing with elements
being zero. Especially when the number of categories or compositional elements in-
crease, the risk of elements being zero increases. Consider for example data derived from
counting observations, when the number of categories an observation can be counted as
increases the probability of seeing no observations for a given category must increase.
When applying the log-ratio transformations, a compositional element of zero leads to
an undeﬁned log-ratio. This is due to either applying the logarithm to zero or dividing
by zero, which in both cases is not well deﬁned. Diﬀerent imputation strategies can
be applied to overcome this problem, as presented in Martín-Fernández et al.[13]. One
is the multiplicative replacement strategy, which can be used to substitute these zero
elements with a small value (less than a given threshold) and in the process alter the re-
maining compositional elements such that the total sum constraint is maintained. This
small adjustment was shown to not introduce any major distortions to the data, as the
ratios between the compositional elements are maintained. In the daily physical activ-
ity data there are no compositional elements of completely zero, as the method of data
gathering and the implicit noise of the accelerometers naturally leads to some, albeit
sometimes very small, nonzero value for all elements of the compositions. The methods
for dealing with zeros in compositional data will therefore not be applied in this project.
The log-ratio transformation approach is not necessarily conﬁned to multiple linear re-
gression analysis and are relevant in many uses containing compositional data. The ﬁrst
model to be considered in this project is the Multinomial Logistic Regression model,
which considers the compositional nature of the activity patterns using a link function
[2] that is equivalent to the alr-transformation (see equation (2)). This connection is
an example of the shared properties of models considering compositional data.

Y ∼N(µ, σ2),
(8)

Instead a link function, g(·), is introduced
g(µ) = xβ
(9)
Such that the expected value of Y in the linear model is a function of the inverse link
function

4.3.1
Estimation of Parameters
By ﬁnding the parameters β that maximizes the log-likelihood (23), we ﬁnd the model
that is the best representative given the data. As a consequence of the log function’s
strictly increasing property, the parameters that maximizes the likelihood function also
maximizes the log-likelihood function. It can be shown that the log-likelihood is con-
cave, given that the multinomial distribution is a member of the exponential family.
An optimization routine, such as the Newton method or Fisher’s scoring algorithm
which is normally used, will therefore converge on the maximum likelihood estimator.
Both aforementioned methods involves computing the ﬁrst and second derivatives of
the log-likelihood function and iteratively move from a starting guess to a minimum or
maximum.
Once the parameters that maximizes the log-likelihood function are found, they are
used as coeﬃcients in the Multinomial model.
The coeﬃcients of the multinomial regression model, β, is interpretable through the
reference category. Each coeﬃcient expresses the eﬀect on the log odds of being in
a certain category as opposed to the reference category. As an example, for β12 the
odds of the response variable being in category 1 (max(p) = p1) compared to the ref-
erence category increases with eβ12 for each increase in x2, when all else is held constant.
We wish to investigate the importance of the independent variables. This can be done
by comparing the respective coeﬃcients of an independent variable to zero. If there
are no signiﬁcant diﬀerence, then the coeﬃcient might in fact be zero. Each coeﬃcient
can be evaluated through a standard t-test, but is is also possible to test all coeﬃcients
related to one variable in a pooled test.
For example can either a Wald test or a likelihood-ratio test be applied [2]. Where the
Wald test measures the distance between β and β0 in parameter space, the likelihood-
ratio test measures the distance in the likelihood value of β and β0. Where β0 in most
cases will be zero, since this value will determine if the independent variable belonging
to the parameter in question is statistically signiﬁcant. It has been shown that the Wald
test and likelihood-ratio test are asymptotically equivalent [2], i.e. they are equal for
an inﬁnite amount of observations. The test statistics of both tests follow a chi-squared
distribution for the multivariate case.
The value of the likelihood function on a test set can also be inspected as a preliminary
analysis, as the models are developed under the assumption that a larger value indicates
a better ﬁt of the data. This assumption also applies when comparing diﬀerent models,

4.3.2
Selection of Variables
In the pursuit of a parsimonious model a feature selection algorithm can be applied.
Selecting the variables of your model can be done in many diﬀerent ways. We will
consider the method of sequential forward (or backwards) feature selection. This method
iteratively considers a model, where you have either added or removed a variable, and
using cross-validation computes if the model improved. The evaluation of improvement
using cross-validation is carried out in regards to a speciﬁed cost function. In each
iteration, for forward selection, all remaining variables are tried one at a time and
the variable that corresponds to the largest reduction in error is added to the model.
The starting model is the constant function, or the absence of all variables, and in
each iteration the best variable is added until a predetermined error tolerance is met
and the algorithm terminates. The cost function can be deﬁned in numerous ways,
depending on the type of problem. For likelihood based models the natural choice is to
use the negative log-likelihood. By selecting the variables that minimizes the negative
log-likelihood, the likelihood function will in turn be maximized. Since the log function
is a strictly growing function, the likelihood and log-likelihood function will have their
optimum at the same parameter values.
The forward selection uses a k-fold cross-validation approach for each iteration, in which
the model is ﬁtted to the training data set and then, using the estimated coeﬃcients,
computes the negative log-likelihood (see equation (23)) of the test set. This method
randomly divides the data set into k folds and uses one fold as the test set and the
rest as the training set. The fold used as the test set is then cycled such that all folds
are used as test set. The value of the cost function is then the averaged negative log-
likelihood value of the test sets.
A well documented Matlab implementation of the multinomial logistic regression model
was made by Zhou and Zhang [5]. It was implemented to be used for regression of
multivariate count data [6]. This implementation, with some adjustments, will be used
to evaluate the Multinomial model on the daily activity patterns data set.

A Dirichlet-Multinomial Model
The actual variation or dispersion of the compositional activity data might be larger
than what is predicted by the Multinomial model. This section seeks to incorporate
more variation, by allowing a worker-to-worker variation.
6.1
Hierarchical Models
The Multinomial model assumes that there are ﬁxed true underlying probabilities to the
data, which is perhaps a simpliﬁed assumption. Due to the heterogeneity of the workers
in the data set, the underlying distribution of activities might vary a lot from worker
to worker. This can be implemented in the model by considering a hierarchical model.
A hierarchical model is a two-stage model, with the ﬁrst stage selecting a parameter
which is used in the modelling of the outcome in the second stage [2]. The ﬁrst stage
can thereby be seen as a model of the variation from worker-to-worker. A hierarchical
model can be written as
Y | ϕ ∼f1(ϕ)
ϕ ∼f2(θ).
Thus f1 is the distribution for Y (activity patterns) given the parameter ϕ (corre-
sponding to p in the Multinomial model), which is in turn distributed according to
f2. f1 should naturally be the considered multinomial distribution. A natural choice
when extending a Multinomial model is to choose f2 as a Dirichlet distribution [6], as
the properties of the Dirichlet distribution reﬂect the assumptions of the parameters
of the multinomial distribution. By using a Dirichlet distribution, we make sure that
the proportion parameters, p, are positive and satisfy the constraint of summing to one.
When the parameters of the multinomial distribution are assumed to be distributed as a
Dirichlet, the resulting compound distribution is the Dirichlet-Multinomial distribution.
This distribution contains a more ﬂexible variance-covariance structure, which might be
able to better ﬁt the data. The activity patterns are suspected to contain more variance
than the measured covariates and parameters of the Multinomial model can explain.
This is likely due to a more complex nature of the data that is not captured solely by the
independent variables and sampling variability. This becomes evident as the activity
patterns of subjects sharing many of the same characteristics can have widely diﬀering
activity patterns.

The Akaike and Bayesian Information Criterion (AIC and BIC) are tools for such
a comparison. The AIC and BIC are goodness-of-ﬁt measures, which gives a more
nuanced insight in the ﬁts of the models. The criteria considers the degrees of freedom
of the models and penalizes the goodness-of-ﬁt hereafter. By doing so, a model with
less degrees of freedom is favored if two models equally well ﬁt the data (the value of
the likelihood function is equivalent) in order to prevent overﬁtting. The AIC and BIC
are deﬁned as
AIC = 2 · df −2 ln(ˆL)
(32)
BIC = ln(n) · df −2 ln(ˆL)
(33)
where df is the number of degrees of freedom or the amount of parameters estimated
in the model. ˆL is the value of the maximized likelihood function and n the number of
observations. When comparing models, the model with the lowest value in both criteria
is the preferred one. Both the AIC and BIC seeks a trade-oﬀbetween a well-ﬁtting
model and a parsimonious model. The BIC penalizes with ln(n) multiplied with the
number of estimated parameters, the AIC simply penalizes with 2 multiplied with the
number of estimated parameters. This leads to, in most cases, that the BIC penalizes
overﬁtting more than the AIC. An increase in value of the maximized likelihood function
as a consequence of adding more parameters to the model is penalized more in BIC than
AIC, as a result of incorporating the number of observations in the criterion.
For the two models considered so far, the AIC and BIC can be computed as the
likelihood functions are readily available for both models. Since the models are ﬁtted
to the same data, the AIC and BIC are comparable as the value of the maximized
likelihood function is the sum over data points, and each term is a probability mass
function describing how ’likely’ it is that the observation comes from the distribution
deﬁned by the parameters found in the maximum likelihood estimation. The actual
value of the AIC and BIC are arbitrary and can only convey relative information used
to compare diﬀerent models.

8
Analysis of Lower Back Pain
In the analysis of the Multinomial model and speciﬁcally in the feature selection pro-
cess, the variable encoding back pain were included as a signiﬁcant term of the model
in relation to daily physical activities. As so, it provides an incentive to analyse the
relation between daily physical activity patterns and back pain. We will speciﬁcally
focus on lower back pain, as this generally is the more troublesome and prevalent work
injury seen. We will now model lower back pain as the dependent variable, as the main
interest is the eﬀect various physical activity patterns have on the risk of experiencing
lower back pain.
The data considered in this project includes measures of lower back pain. Speciﬁcally
there are three variables describing diﬀerent measure of lower back pain. The data was
collected through a questionnaire, as pain is a cumbersome thing to measure. As such
the variables are expected to be more inﬂuenced by the participants tolerance for pain,
which is subjective from person to person. The questions corresponding to the three
variables were:
Have you had pain/discomfort in the last 7 days? Yes/No.
In the last 12 months, how many days in all have you had diﬃculty (pain or discomfort)
in: Lower back? (1=0 days 2=1-7 days 3=8-30 days 4=31-90 days 5=More than 90
days 6=Every day)
In the last 3 months, state your worst pain in: Lower back? (0=No pain 10=Worst pain)
These encode slightly diﬀerent measures of lower back pain. The ﬁrst corresponds to a
more acute and perhaps less reliable as the timeframe is shorter than the other questions.
These on the other hand give a better indication of more chronic pains. However, when
the timeframe of the question is extended there will be more bias and uncertainty related
to these variables due to the limitations of the memory of the participants. There is
assumed to be a certain overlap between the variables, as a chronic form of lower back
pain could also be caught by the ﬁrst question.
It is of great interest to model the eﬀect of daily physical activity patterns on pain and
especially lower back pain which is by far the most widespread work related injury [19].
Being able to give an indication of what particular pattern of daily physical activity
gives rise to lower back pain, would be an interesting and useful result.

8.1

The model we will consider is a logistic regression applied to the binary variable cor-

pain, which is experienced in roughly the same timeframe as the daily physical activ-

whether it is possible to describe the relationship between the daily physical activity
patterns and the simpliﬁed question of whether or not a person has experienced lower
back pain in the last week. Approximately 43% of the workers answered ’yes’ to the
question. The fact that this proportion is relatively high, indicates that lower back pain
is a widespread problem. It further indicates that the question manages to capture
both weak and strong pain, but can not distinguish between them. The model must
therefore assume that the same types of activity pattern leads to both weak and severe
lower back pain.

8.1.1
Choosing the Best Model
In order to ﬁnd the model that is the best ﬁt to the data and by extension the model that
is most suitable for prediction of lower back pain, the variables of the model must be
examined for importance. The complete model includes all terms, both ilr-coordinates
and squared ilr-coordinates as well as confounding variables. This model is likely to
overﬁt due to unnecessary complexity, as it has more degrees of freedom which allows
it to ﬁt to the noise and outliers of the data rather than the true underlying patterns
of the populations.
Ideally we wish to have a model with predictive power, as the goal of the analysis is to be
able to predict the probability of experiencing lower back pain given a certain activity
pattern. If the model is able to give conﬁdent predictions, it will then be possible to
ﬁnd the activity patterns associated with low predicted probabilities.

In order to ﬁnd the optimal model, we must ﬁrst deﬁne which error we wish to minimize.
There are a few natural candidates: the classiﬁcation error on a test set (i.e. the total
number of correctly classiﬁed observations divided by the total number of observations)
and an error measure which takes overﬁtting into account such as AIC and BIC. Finally,
the log-likelihood of a test set is considered, which corresponds exactly to the selection
procedure applied for the Multinomial model.
When considering classiﬁcation problems, the accuracy of the predictions on a test set
are often used (alongside the precision and recall, dependent on the problem at hand)
to measure the performance of a model.
The model that maximizes the test classiﬁcation accuracy is a natural choice as the
goal is to correctly predict the probability of lower back pain given a certain activity
pattern.
By maximizing the accuracy on a test set, the predictive ability will be maximized
as well.
However, due to the classiﬁcation method of the logistic regression model;
if the predicted probabilities are close to 0.5, the conﬁdence in the classiﬁcations are
weakened. When the predicted probability is close to 0.5, a small change in the input
of the model could change the predicted class, making the classiﬁcation accuracy an
unreliable measurement.
In this case, whether an observation is classiﬁed as experiencing lower back pain or not
is perhaps not an important measure.
Classically, AIC and BIC (see equation (32) and (33)) are useful tools for model se-
lection, as they penalize the likelihood value proportionally to the amount of degrees
of freedom. A model minimizing AIC and BIC, will therefore be a suitable candidate
for the optimal model in the case where many of the predicted probabilities lie close to
0.5. By minimizing AIC and BIC, the resulting model will minimize overﬁtting while
maintaining a good ﬁt. Similar to the feature selection in the Multinomial model, a
forward selection approach can be applied where instead of minimizing the negative
likelihood, BIC or AIC is minimized. However, it is a possibility that the models that
minimizes BIC and AIC have too few variables, resulting in models that encode very
little information and thus making interpretation impossible. In practice AIC and es-
pecially BIC tend to favour overly simple models [21]. In that case, a trade-oﬀbetween
classiﬁcation error and information criteria should be considered in order to deﬁne a
model with a high enough complexity while minimizing overﬁtting.

By using the log-likelihood of a test set as an error measure, we are able to estimate
the generalization error of the model with a more nuanced perspective than the classi-
ﬁcation error. The likelihood function of the logistic regression model is able to weigh
how much a misclassiﬁed observation is penalized, where as the classiﬁcation error is a
binary function.
When selecting the best model, there are also some constraints that must be considered.
Since we are investigating the relationship between daily physical activity patterns and
the risk of lower back pain, a model not containing the activity patterns will be irrel-
evant. Due to this prior requirement, the model should be deﬁned as the best model
which includes the ﬁrst order term of the ilr-transformed activity patterns. Henceforth
this requirement is denoted as the interpretability requirement. The second order terms
should not necessarily be included, as we wish to investigate the importance and validity
of the terms through the model selection process.

8.1.3
Finding the Optimal Composition
Once the best model has been built and trained on the entire available data set, the
search for the optimal condition can begin.
We wish to ﬁnd the composition that
minimizes the probability of lower back pain, i.e. equation (37), under the constraint
that the sum of the activities must sum to one. Further constraints can be imposed, in
order to ﬁnd a realistic optimal activity pattern (e.g. sleeping 24 hours is a theoretical
valid activity pattern, but not a useful one).
In Dumuid, Simm et al. [20], the optimal composition of 4 activities, in relation to
diﬀerent skeletal-health indicators in children, is searched for. It is estimated through a
gridsearch of the activity space and the range of the 5% best hypothetical compositions
were further interpreted as the optimal composition for skeletal-health.
A gridsearch in 9 dimensions is not as feasible as in 4, due to the curse of dimensionality.
When the dimensionality is increased the amount of observations needed to gain the
same density increases exponentially. A random sampling approach is more suited as a
pseudo-gridsearch in high dimensional spaces, in order to reduce the needed amount of
samples.
Another approach to ﬁnding the optimal composition is to apply a constrained opti-
mization routine on the model function. This would seem like the natural choice as it
would eliminate any randomness associated with a pseudo-gridsearch approach. It is
however likely that a gradient-based optimization will converge in a local minimum, if
there are many diﬀerent activity patterns associated with a very low predicted probabil-
49
==PAGE:54==
Analysis of Lower Back Pain
ity of lower back pain. Due to the constraints imposed on the solutions of the model, it
is not expected that the function behaves smoothly. It is furthermore worth considering
if there is an acceptable threshold of probability of experiencing lower back pain and if
activity patterns falling below this threshold are equally good. Due to the large variance
and inherent noise of the data, as seen in the previous modelling, any predictions from
the model will necessarily carry some noise. For example a predicted probability of
0.003 and 0.0003 are low enough that both should be accepted as minimizing the risk
of back pain, even though one is lower by a factor of 10. Rather than ﬁnding the global
minimum of the function, we are interested in mapping out the regions associated with
low predicted probabilities.
A constrained optimization routine is more likely to ﬁnd a local minimum with a lower
predicted probability than the pseudo-gridsearch will ﬁnd. The pseudo-gridsearch is
however able to ﬁnd many ’good’ candidates which enables an analysis of the range of
activity patterns that minimizes the predicted probability of lower back pain.
The constrained optimization routine that will be used to minimize the logistic model
is a Sequential Quadratic Programming routine which applies a BFGS update. This
optimization routine is gradient based and relies on estimates of the Hessian at each
update. It allows for constraints, which is necessary in order to ﬁnd a valid optimum. In
order to ﬁnd the activity pattern that minimizes the probability of experiencing lower
back pain, we can construct the function used as input in the optimization routine
in such a way that the activity patterns are the input of the function. Alternatively,
the inverse ilr-transformation can be applied to the ilr-coordinates that minimizes the
model. For interpretation purposes, the former method is chosen.
8.1.4
Imposing Bound Constraints
In order to ﬁnd an optimum that is also valid as a realistic activity pattern, certain
bounds of the activity space must be imposed. It is not possible to change the require-
ments employers impose on the workers, i.e. a workday is 8 hours long and the type
of work might demand a certain amount of time spent standing or doing light physi-
cal activity (which is in the state most of the work of a manufacturer or cleaner takes
place). Other bounds should be imposed on leisure time, in order to avoid unrealistic
compositions in the form of one dominant activity (such as MV PAL taking a value of
8 hours). A natural choice of bound is to search inside the range of the data set, as
these must necessarily be realistic values. The extreme values of the data set are rare
cases, but valid ones nonetheless.

==PAGE:55==

By limiting the parameter space, the predicted probabilities will increase as the bound-
ary is restricted.
Due to the inherent linearity of the logistic regression, given the framework of generalized
linear models, the values of the independent variables that minimizes the unconstrained
function will be located on the boundary of the parameter space . By looking at equation
(37), it is clear that in order to minimize P(C1|x), xβ must be minimized. However,
due to the introduction of squared terms and the ilr-transformation that takes place, it
is not necessarily the boundary of the activity pattern space that minimizes the model.
When analysing the model, the activity patterns will be the variables considered and the
optimization routine will seek an optimum in this space. The unconstrained optimum is
not of interest, as the activity patterns must respect the assumptions of compositional
data, i.e. summing to 1 and deﬁned in the interval (0, 1). When the constraints are
added to the problem, the compositional properties play an important role and the
optimization routine is further forced to seek a trade-oﬀbetween the variables. When
limiting the space of the activity variables (to avoid unrealistic compositions), the local
minimum the optimization routine converges on will have a higher function value than
in the unconstrained case (or equal value if they converge on the same minimum).
Furthermore, limiting the space will likely lead to the same solutions being found more
often, as the number of local minima will likewise be limited.

9.4
Extending the Range of Acceptable Solutions
The optimization routine is able to ﬁnd local minima, where the distribution of activi-
ties are very diﬀerent from each other. All found solutions have a predicted probability
of lower back pain of less than 0.02.
Due to the inherent noise and relatively high
classiﬁcation error of the model, it is not a certainty that a diﬀerence in a few percent-
age points between solutions is an accurate indication of the goodness of the solution.
Furthermore, we wish to interpret the results in a context relevant to practical appli-
cations. If the predicted probabilities were completely accurate (due to a large amount
of unknown factors, they can never be completely accurate), we would like to examine
solutions with higher probabilities in case these distributions are more realistic. A 5%
risk of back pain is perhaps still acceptable in a practical aspect if the solution distribu-
tions are more easily achieved. An optimization routine can not be used in this regard,
as it will seek towards the local minima and disregards other ’good’ solutions.
In order to ﬁnd the solutions with an acceptable level of probability, a bootstrapping
approach can be applied. This method is comparable to a gridsearch in lower dimen-
sions, where candidate solutions from the activity pattern space is randomly sampled
and their respective probability is predicted. The advantages of a sampling approach
versus a gridsearch is outlined in section 8.1.3.

9.5
Constraining Light Physical Activity and Standing at Work
The activity variable LPAW is a composed variable consisting of time spent moving
and walking slowly during work time. The model seeks to minimize this variable in
most of the proposed solutions, but it is likely when moving and walking slowly that a
signiﬁcant proportion of the work of a blue-collar worker is carried out. It is therefore
a natural next step to impose constraints on Light Physical Activity at work. As the
large majority of the considered data is represented by the Cleaning and Manufacturing
sector (see Table 1), it seems safe to assume that the workers are not allowed to spend
a majority of their time in Sedentary Behaviour. Likewise should StandW be subject to
similar assumptions, as this activity seems equally impossible to avoid as a blue-collar
worker.

10.4
Interpretation in an Epidemiological Context
The ﬁndings presented in the simplices seen in Figure 33 and 34 are the result of a
logistic regression model applied to the daily physical activities in relation to acute
lower back pain.
The observable tendencies in the simplices are congruent with the main aspects of the
extended solutions presented in section 9, despite the simpliﬁcation of ﬁxing either the
leisure or work related variables. The extended solutions go into detail of the exact
compositions that minimizes the predicted probability of lower back pain, whereas the
simplices provide an overview of what type of compositions are associated with either
a higher or lower risk of lower back pain. The actual prediction values are not directly
readable from the simplices, as these are dependent on which values the remaining vari-
ables are ﬁxed at.
In Heneweer, Vanhees & Picavet [23] the relation between physical activity (in the form
of exercise) and lower back pain is postulated to be U-shaped. It was found that there
indeed was a basis for this postulation. As such when the amount of physical activity
is increased, at a certain point the positives eﬀects on lower back pain are negated. We
have observed the same relationship for Moderate to Vigorous Physical Activity at work,
in the analysis carried out in this project. This eﬀect is both visible in Figure 19 and
33, where a very small amount of MV PAW is associated with a high risk of lower back
pain and a high amount increases the risk as well. The subject of analysis in this part
of the project was described as a form of acute lower back pain, based on an evaluation
of lower back pain in the past week. In Heneweer, Vanhees & Picavet [23], the sub-
ject of analysis is described as a chronic form of lower back pain similar to question 2
and 3 mentioned in section 8. It is natural to assume an overlap between the two forms
of pain, as a chronic form of lower back pain will likely also be present ’in the past week’.
In Korshøj et al. [24] and in Gupta et al. [25], the eﬀect of sitting (Sedentary Behaviour)
on lower back pain is investigated among blue-collar workers. While a beneﬁcial eﬀect of
increased sitting time on lower back pain was concluded in Gupta et al. [25], there was
no eﬀect found in Korshøj et al. [24]. In many of the best solutions presented for the
logistic regression model on lower back pain, a large share of the daily physical activity
distribution was allocated to Sedentary Behaviour for both work and leisure. Further-
more, both the work and leisure simplex (Figure 33 and 34) show the same tendencies
within the bounds of the data. Generally an increase in sitting leads to a decrease in the
predicted probabilities. This relation is more pronounced in the work simplex, which
82
==PAGE:87==
A Simpler Logistic Regression Model
makes intuitive sense as the alternative activities blue-collar workers might engage in,
would likely involve strenuous tasks.
The relations uncovered in the data through the logistic regression model used in this
project are thus comparable to previous studies.

Discussion
This project has explored the relation between daily physical activity patterns and se-
lected health indicators. First, the basis of compositional data analysis was deﬁned,
which proved a necessary tool when analysing physical activity patterns on a 24-hour
scale. The relations to multiple health indicators were then investigated through the
Multinomial and later the Dirichlet-Multinomial model. The models ﬁt to the data were
not particular persuasive in their goodness, as only a few clear relations could be seen
between the selected health indicators and the daily physical activity patterns. While
the theoretical ﬁt was largely improved by adding the Dirichlet part to the model (as
seen by the comparison in information criteria), the general relations uncovered in the
data remained the same. This indicates that while the ﬁt to the data improved, the
predictive power of the model was still poor. The complexity and number of variables
is likely too high for the amount of data available, resulting in imprecise models. These
models contributed to the overall understanding of the diﬃculties of deﬁning ’good’
models using the considered data.
The focus was narrowed on speciﬁc health indicators and their relation to the daily
physical activities. The ﬁrst analysis hereof (i.e. the pivot-ilr regression analysis), was
inspired by an article (Dumuid et al. [10]) using this model on similar data. While the
resulting eﬀect sizes of this analysis were small, it was an important step in the overall
analysis, as the relations seen in the Multinomial and Dirichlet-Multinomial were also
present here.
By suggestion from the external partner, the focus was further narrowed on variables
describing lower back pain as this had particular interest for practical applications. A
logistic regression model was deﬁned using a variable encoding the existence of dis-
comfort/pain in the lower back in the past week. The problem of model selection was
considered in detail and especially what error measure should guide the selection of the
best model. The ﬁrst error measure considered was the classiﬁcation accuracy on a test
set, whereas the other measures were grounded in the likelihood function. It was argued,
that the classiﬁcation accuracy alone could provide an unstable model since most of the
prediction values lay near the middle of the prediction spectrum. Thus, if an observa-
tion was misclassiﬁed with a predictive value for lower back pain of, for example, 0.49
it would be weighed as heavily as a grossly misclassiﬁed observation. The other error
measures were introduced to compensate for this bias, where the log-likelihood function
(equation (38)) is used to provide a more balanced weighing in the model selection pro-
84
==PAGE:89==
Discussion
cess. The model selected in the end, resembled the one chosen through the classiﬁcation
accuracy as the values of the predicted probabilities were deemed to be a minor source
of error and the requirement of interpretability had to be taken into account as well.
The p-values were not used in the model selection process, since they are highly depen-
dent on which variables are included in the model. Furthermore, due to the generally
weak signal, the signiﬁcance threshold used to determine if a variable should be included
or not would be diﬃcult to choose. The selection process was also complicated by the
interpretability requirement, which made it necessary to include some variables which
otherwise would have been discarded. The inspection of p-values was therefore reserved
for evaluation of the model after it had been selected.
The selected variables were inspected for signiﬁcance (see Table 9), where around half
of the included variables were seen to be statistical signiﬁcant on a 95% conﬁdence level.
This is another symptom of a generally weak signal in the data, which was also visible
in the previous models. This is further supported by Figure A4, where the observations
experiencing lower back pain are diﬀerentiated from those without pain, shown on the
work simplex. Here we see an overlapping distribution without any obvious patterns,
where even the few outliers are represented in both classes. It is no surprise that a clas-
siﬁcation algorithm struggles to correctly predict if an observation experiences lower
back pain or not. The weak signal in the data is furthermore indicated by the relatively
low classiﬁcation accuracy (∼58%) on a test set, which is only 8 percentage points
better than a random assignment.
We have generally seen small eﬀect sizes in the models used in this project. This is
not unprecedented in studies using data from the ﬁeld of public health, where the data
is often very heterogeneous and inﬂuenced by many unknown factors. For example in
Dumuid et al. [10], an adjusted R-squared of 0.11 is reported for a linear regression
analysis. The data used in the Dumuid study is similar to the data used in this project,
where the eﬀect on adiposity was investigated as a function of daily physical activities.
In the logistic regression model connecting lower back pain to daily physical activities,
the ilr-coordinates of the activity variables are squared in order to capture the possible
non-linear relations in the data. Since the ilr-coordinates are in fact composite variables
consisting of more than one activity, it is unclear whether is is meaningful to square the
coordinates after transformation. On the other hand, if the activities were squared be-
85
==PAGE:90==
Discussion
fore transformation they would violate the basic assumption of being compositional and
summing to one. The ilr-coordinates are purposefully never interpreted in the analysis,
rather the original activity variables are changed and the eﬀects are traced, through the
ilr-transformation, to the model predictions.
The limitations of the data introduces a source of error in the models, as many aspects
of the often complex origins of the data are not represented. The daily physical activity
variables describe the daily average amount of time spent in the associated activity,
where it would likely have been advantageous to be able to diﬀerentiate between, for
example, sitting down for 2 hours continuously and in four 30 minute intervals. It seems
intuitively beneﬁcial in terms of general health to stand up and stretch your legs once
in a while, so to speak.
The matter of inconsistent total sum of activities in the observations was treated brieﬂy
in section 5. The problem was solved by adjusting all observations such that they sum
to the same whole. This implies that all missing measured hours are assumed to be
distributed equal to the measured hours. This is naturally a simpliﬁed approach and
it seems likely that the missing hours are not distributed as the measured hours but
rather related to the time of day. It is likely possible to impute the missing values in a
way that provides a more realistic complete composition.
It was further noticed that some of the observations had a total sum above 24 hours.
These were also adjusted to sum to 24 hours, while retaining the proportional value
of the activities. It remains unclear why some observations had a total average above
24 hours. It is possible that by taking the median of the reported Sleep durations,
instead of the mean, the total sum was skewed past 24 hours. These adjustments and
assumptions regarding the missing hours introduce a bias to the hour estimates pre-
sented throughout the project. It is further assumed that a work day is 8 hours long,
whereas the empirical average was slightly less. For this reason the proportion/ratio
between activity variables are more reliable units than hour estimates. Thus the results
of this project are not suitable for suggestions regarding the total amount of hours, but
rather how you should allocate time in one activity compared to other activities.
Only the data consisting of the average daily physical activity was used in this project.
The origins of the average data, namely the data describing physical activity from day
to day would also have been interesting to explore. It is unclear how much variance
would be explained by having the days as separate data points. The distribution of
86

work time is probably similar from day to day, while leisure could be more varied. If
the data is separated on a daily basis, it would be possible to create a mixed eﬀects
model, where the day and worker is modelled as a random eﬀect. This would allow us
to better model the variance, which in turn could provide a stronger model. However,
due to a shortage of time this was not possible.
It would also be interesting to include the remaining variables describing a more chronic
form of lower back pain in an analysis. During the analysis of the logistic regression
model, the three variables were temporarily combined to a composite binary pain vari-
able in an attempt to increase the performance of the model. This was not successful.
In fact the performance decreased when using this crudely deﬁned composite variable.
One could either experiment further with the deﬁnition of a composite binary variable
or choose a model that is better suited for a combination of the variables.
It was further considered to explore the physical activity patterns through unsupervised
learning, for example cluster analysis, in order to ﬁnd patterns in the physical activi-
ties and consequently similarities or dissimilarities between the workers sharing related
activity patterns.
The search for an optimal distribution of physical activities on a 24-hour basis has been
the goal of which the analysis throughout the project has steered towards. While this
search has deﬁnitely not been fruitless, it has, unsurprisingly, not provided a deﬁnitive
answer either. The analysis proposed a number of distributions as possible solutions
and while the variance among these solutions was quite high, they were all ’good’ in
the eyes of the model. To further diﬀerentiate between these solutions would require
a deeper knowledge of the practical implications of the diﬀerent physical activities and
likely further studies.
The data used in the project is cross-sectional, which implies that it is impossible to
interpret the causality of the data. Due to the cross-sectionality, it is unclear whether a
given activity pattern leads to lower back pain or vice versa. The models are naturally
a consequence of the data, which is both cross-sectional and with a large variance from
worker to worker. It is therefore likely that an activity deemed beneﬁcial by the models,
is actually dominated by healthy workers that are yet to experience any drawbacks of
the given activity. For example, an increase in Standing at work is associated with
a decrease in the risk of lower back pain (see Figure 19), where we would expect the
opposite to be true. It is possible that workers with lower back pain are not able to
stand for a prolonged period of time, and thus the observations associated with longer

durations of Standing at work do not experience lower back pain. The model is then
fooled into associating a large amount of standing with a decreasing risk of lower back
pain.
Any causal relationship that might exist is furthermore interfered by the underlying
existing relations between general health (including lower back pain) and environmental
conditions and personal attributes. None of these unknown variables were included in
the data analysis. Even within the variables included in the data analysis, there exists
a number of unknown factors (e.g.

perspectives, a causality study should be considered where participants are assigned
speciﬁc activity patterns followed by a later measurement to ensure a causal relationship.
