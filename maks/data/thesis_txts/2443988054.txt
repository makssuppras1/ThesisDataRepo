Abstract
The present master’s thesis considers laboratory scale hydrodynamic and morphodynamic modelling
of a solitary plunging breaker, as well as laboratory scale hydrodynamic modelling of regular plunging
breakers. The considered numerical model is a fully coupled (hydrodynamic and morphological) CFD-
model available in the open source CFD-software OpenFOAM. The hydrodynamic model solves the
incompressible Reynolds Averaged Navier Stokes (RANS) equations making use of the stabilized two
equation k −ω turbulence model of (Larsen and Fuhrman, 2018b). In that connection the model applies
the Volume of Fluid (VOF) method for surface tracking. The morphological model considers both bed
and suspended load in the solution of the Exner equation for the bed level change.
The hydrodynamic study of the solitary wave case is evaluated based on the experimental results of
(Sumer et al., 2011) in terms of: surface elevation, mean bed shear stress and near bed turbulent kinetic
energy density. These quantities are evaluated during shoaling and breaking of the wave, during run-up
and run-down of the wave, and during the evolution of a hydraulic jump as well as a trailing wave. It
is shown that the model performance is generally quite good as larger discrepancies are only seen in
relation to the complex processes of the hydraulic jump and in the very shallow waters of the swash zone.
The overall good model performance is reproduced in the morphodynamic study of the case. Here the
only signiﬁcant issue is related to the underestimated bed shear stress in the swash zone giving rise to an
underestimated level of erosion. The stabilizing effect of the turbulence model has a negligible effect on
the morphological response, which is in full accordance with its working principle: to reduce turbulence
in regions of near potential ﬂow, i.e. outside the littoral zone. The inclusion of buoyancy production in the
calculation of the turbulent kinetic energy density is on the other hand seen to make the response of the
bed more irregular.
The hydrodynamic study of the regular wave case is evaluated similar to the solitary case but based
on the results of (Sumer et al., 2013) considering shoaling and breaking of the wave as well as run-up
and run-down of the wave. The breaking and run-up process is shown to be much more complex than
the solitary case as a number of vortices are generated and thus more mixing takes place. Pre-breaking
the model is shown to perform quite well, whereas post-breaking, i.e. during run-up and run-down in
the surf and swash zone, the model performance is much poorer. This is attributed to the model having
difﬁculty modelling the complexity of the mixed vortex inﬂuenced ﬂow in the surf and swash zone. Vast
improvement of particularly the mean bed shear stress is seen when applying the isoAdvector-principle of
(Roenby et al., 2016) for surface tracking instead of the VOF-method. This model, however, displays
excessively high turbulence levels. Part of this is likely attributed to the lack of a diffusive balance in the
numerical schemes, (Larsen et al., 2018), but also the inclusion of buoyancy production in the turbulent
kinetic energy production may play a role meriting further research.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The sea has, during history, been a source of food, home of global trading routes and its shores are
widely used for recreational purposes. As a result many densely populated cities are located near the sea
and connected to it through great harbours. Over the past couple of decades awareness and knowledge
regarding the rising mean sea level has grown and increased the need for protection against the forces of
the sea. Thus the ability to predict the evolution of the coastline and the coastline response to structural
interventions is becoming increasingly important, which raises the demand for morphological modelling
of good quality.
The evolution of a shoreline is based on the coupled effect of hydrodynamic forcing and sediment
transport. This interaction gives rise to morphological changes in the seabed and thus in the layout of the
shoreline. Any protective measure will change the natural layout of a near-shore area to some degree,
which will lead to changes in the natural layout of the forcing affecting the original shoreline response.
Protective measures are generally divided into soft and hard measures, (Bosboom and Stive, 2015, ch.
10). Soft measures often constitute nourishments, which reinforce the natural coastal protection of the
near-shore area by adding sand to e.g. the dune system and/or the foreshore. Hard measures are seen
as structural reinforcement of the near-shore in the shape of e.g. groynes, breakwaters or revetments.
Nourishments are a quite ﬂexible solution whereas structural reinforcements are less ﬂexible. Only
through an assessment of the shoreline response and evolution can an informed choice between the various
protective and preventive measures available be made.
To assess the annual and long term evolution in a near-shore area numerical models are used. Such
models couple modelling of the hydrodynamic forcing from currents and waves with modelling of
sediment transport to form a description of the shoreline evolution. This depends on sound understanding
of the physics involved why research in terms of experimental work and numerical studies are continuously
being done to increase understanding of the physical processes and the limitations of the employed models.
In numerical models the hydrodynamics are described by the Navier Stokes equations. These equa-
tions are solved numerically using a range of different modelling approaches. The most common ones
are based on either the concept of Large Eddy Simulation (LES) or Reynolds Averaged Navier Stokes
(RANS) equations. The ﬁrst method resolves the turbulence length scales that are larger than the subgrid
scale and parametrizes the turbulent length scales smaller than the subgrid scale. The RANS-method,
considered in the present study, uses a turbulence model to parametrize all the turbulent length scales.
RANS-models consider the velocity signal from currents and waves u = uc + uw to consist of a mean
value u plus a ﬂuctuating component accounting for the turbulent ﬂow u′. Hence u = u+u′ contains both
current and wave contribution. The decomposition of the velocity into a mean and ﬂuctuating component
gives rise to the so-called Reynolds stresses, which must be parametrized to close the equation system.
The parametrization of the Reynolds stresses is contained in a turbulence model.
(Mayer and Madsen, 2000) showed that turbulence models used in RANS-based models are condi-

tionally unstable. The consequence of this instability is that the model generates turbulence in regions of
near potential ﬂow, i.e. in regions where the ﬂow is considered irrotational. Hence the turbulence model
pollutes the results before the wave has even broken, i.e. before the main region of interest: the surf zone.
Recently (Larsen and Fuhrman, 2018b) have extended on the work of (Mayer and Madsen, 2000) showing
that the instability of the turbulence models is in fact not conditional but unconditional. Additionally an
implementable solution to the problem resulted from their work in the shape of a stabilized turbulence
model.
The present report studies the modelling of breaking plunging waves and associated sediment transport
using the stabilized turbulence model of (Larsen and Fuhrman, 2018b). The conducted simulations are
founded on a RANS-based fully coupled (hydrodynamic and morphological) model in the open source
CFD software OpenFOAM. Focus is payed to solitary and periodic plunging waves propagating over beds
of constant slope. In the ﬁrst case both a ﬁxed and a live bed is considered, whereas in the latter case only
a ﬁxed bed is considered. The model set-ups are based on (Sumer et al., 2011) and (Sumer et al., 2013).
These articles present the results of two coupled experiments each. (Sumer et al., 2011) consider a solitary
plunging wave on a constantly sloping bed. The bed condition in the ﬁrst experiment was impermeable
and ﬁxed allowing direct hot ﬁlm measurements of the bed shear stress. In the second experiment the
bed was a permeable sediment bed making it possible to study the pore pressure evolution in the bed and
also the morphological development. Since the ﬂow conditions between the experiments were identical
the measured bed shear stresses from the ﬁrst experiment are linked to the observed morphology of the
second experiment. Similar experiments were made in (Sumer et al., 2013) only here the waves were
periodic plunging breakers. The experimental data of these studies will be used to assess the performance
of the considered numerical model.
1.1
Report structure
The report is structured as follows:
Chapter 2 describes the hydrodynamic model and the sediment transport model in terms of the
governing equations.
Chapter 3 presents the considered computational domains and the applied spatial resolutions. Hereafter
the considered wave forcing, the applied boundary conditions and initial conditions are outlined.
Chapter 4 presents the analysis of the solitary plunging breaker propagating up a ﬁxed bed of constant
slope. The case is analysed by comparing the modelled surface elevation, mean bed shear stress and near
bed turbulent kinetic energy to the measured results of (Sumer et al., 2011).
Chapter 5 outlines the analysis of the regular plunging breakers propagating up a ﬁxed bed of constant
slope. The case is analysed by comparing the modelled phase averaged surface elevation, mean bed shear
stress and near bed turbulent kinetic energy to the measured results of (Sumer et al., 2013).
Chapter 6 considers the analysis of the solitary plunging breaker propagating up a live bed of constant
slope. The assessment compares the modelled bed level after 1-4 waves to the experimental results of
(Sumer et al., 2011) and the simulated results of (Li et al., 2018).
Finally chapter 7 summarizes the presented results in a conclusion.
The remainder of the present chapter will look into three components: hydrodynamics, turbulence
and sediment transport. All of which need quantiﬁcation in a fully coupled CFD model concerned with
hydrodynamic and morphological evolution of a near-shore area. First some of the physical processes
governing the cross-shore morphological evolution will be introduced, then some of the principles applied
in the modelling of sediment transport will be outlined. This is done to illustrate the complexity of the
involved physics to underline the relevance of morphodynamic models in quantifying the near-shore
morphological evolution.

1.2. THE MORPHOLOGICAL MODEL’S THREE COMPONENTS
3
1.2
The morphological model’s three components
Numerical modelling of the morphological evolution in the near-shore region involves three major
contributors: the hydrodynamics in the shape of waves and currents, the turbulence generated by the
hydrodynamic forcing and the sediment transport. The following sub-sections will provide a brief view
into some of the important aspects behind these three components making it possible to see how the
processes interact.
1.2.1
Hydrodynamics and turbulence
In the near-shore region most of the sediment transport shaping our coasts take place. The region is
characterized by very dynamic hydrodynamics due to e.g. waves, tides, wave generated currents and tidal
currents. Waves and currents move sediment both in the along-shore and cross-shore direction. As the
present report focuses on waves and uses a 2DV-model, i.e. a two dimensional vertical model, which only
resolves the cross-shore effects, the present section will likewise only focus on the cross-shore variation
in hydrodynamics resulting from surface waves.
The cross-shore variation in hydrodynamics contains a large number of onshore and offshore directed
contributions making it very difﬁcult to get the size and direction of the cross-shore sediment transport
right. This has been studied by e.g. (Fuhrman et al., 2013) who performed a study of cross-shore sediment
transport using a RANS-based model coupled with a k −ω turbulence model. The model considered (1)
hindered settling caused by high concentrations of suspended sediment, (2) turbulence damping as a result
of density gradients in the water-sand mixture, (3) boundary layer streaming resulting from including the
convective terms in the Navier Stokes equation and (4) converging-diverging effects due to sloping beds.
The set-up of the model allowed switching components on or off enabling investigation of the effect of
each contributor.
The present section presents some of the important aspects of cross-shore variations in hydrodynamics,
for instance the above mentioned point 3 and 4 of (Fuhrman et al., 2013), along with sources of turbulence.
The hydrodynamics in an area will vary over the year. Often shores erode during winter and accrete
during summer giving rise to an inter-annual variability in the cross-shore beach proﬁle. This variability
is linked to the variation in wave forcing and wave breaking. A typical and well known breaking criteria
for linear waves is described by the wave height to depth ratio as H/h ≈0.8, (Holthuijsen, 2007). During
summer the forcing from wind is relatively mild and thus the waves are relatively small. Following the
breaking criteria waves break relatively close to shore during summer. During fall and winter waves are
larger and thus break further offshore. The signiﬁcance of this is for instance seen in the point of initiation
of one of the very important and striking features associated with the hydrodynamic forcing of breaking
waves: the undertow. The undertow is an offshore directed current located near the bed. It is generated as
a response to the near surface onshore directed Stokes drift induced by the breaking waves along with the
wave induced set-up seen in the mean sea level going from the point of breaking towards shore. Thus the
undertow is tightly linked to the point of breaking and as a result the cross-shore position of the initiation
of the undertow will vary over the year in accordance with the wave forcing. Since sediment is mainly
positioned close to the bed movement of the initiation ’point’ of the undertow will cause a cross-shore
variability in sediment transport over the year contributing to the difference in summer and winter beach
proﬁles.
Another source of variability is the waves themselves in terms of size and form. The size relative to
the slope of the bed will determine, which breaker type the wave will form. This is quantiﬁed in terms of
the so-called Iribarren parameter, ξ, which relates the bed slope β to the wave steepness, i.e. ratio between
wave height and wave length, in terms of deep water quantities H0/L0. Three different breaker types are

Wave breaking is often modelled through a surface roller. This roller is formed as the wave breaks and
inside it high levels of rotation and turbulence is produced. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 1.1 showing various
sources of turbulence beneath a wave. Here it is seen that turbulence is produced in the surface roller and
spreads through a sort of shear layer into the water column, where it dissipates. The importance of the
roller depends on the breaker type. Following (Jacobsen, 2011) the spilling breaker forms as the crest
becomes unstable forming the surface roller. Plunging breakers on the other hand breaks as the wave curls
over forming a jet. This happens when the particle velocity exceeds the wave speed. Depending on the
strength of the jet it may reﬂect on the surface or even impinge onto the bed. A surging breaker forms as
the toe of the wave becomes unstable. Figure 1.2 provides an illustration of turbulence beneath spilling
and plunging breakers.
Figure 1.1: Illustration of various sources of turbulence beneath a wave, taken from (Christensen et al.,
2002).
The signiﬁcance of the breaker type in terms of sediment transport is linked to the turbulence
distribution beneath the wave. In ﬁgure 1.2 it is seen that the turbulence beneath a plunging breaker
is highly bound to the area beneath the breaking wave crest. In contrast beneath spilling breakers the
turbulence is spread over a much wider region. This results in turbulence levels of similar magnitude
throughout the wave cycle beneath the spilling breakers according to (Christensen et al., 2002). The
turbulence distribution beneath the spilling/plunging breaker affects the sediment transport differently.
Due to the turbulence travelling with the wave crest under plunging breakers the sediment transport is
very often directed onshore, (Christensen et al., 2002). For spilling breakers having a nearly constant
turbulence level over the wave cycle, the sediment transport is connected to the mean ﬂow and thus
dominated by the undertow, i.e. directed offshore.
The wave generated turbulence can also inﬂuence the bed shear stress as strong breaking induced
vortices impinge onto the bed. This has been studied by (Zhou et al., 2014) using an LES-model. Their
study showed that these bursts of turbulence into the boundary layer from an external source promotes an
increase in bed shear stress, i.e. will also increase sediment transport. However, these bursts are difﬁcult
to model through parametrization.
As mentioned also the form of the wave-signal is important for sediment transport and this signal
varies across the near-shore region as waves propagate across the shoaling region, the breaking region(s)
and ﬁnally enters the swash zone, see ﬁgure 1.3. Note that the deformation zone corresponds to the
shoaling region.
Following (Christensen et al., 2002) the wave height in the shoaling region increases as the waves
propagate towards shore and the front of the wave becomes steeper. This process is known as shoaling.

due to its near-bed location. One of the hydrodynamic features of the boundary layer is known as
streaming. This feature arise from the non-linear convective terms in the Navier Stokes equations and
is thus associated with the non-uniformity of the wave boundary layer in the horizontal direction. This
non-uniformity results in a period averaged onshore directed velocity in the boundary layer and thus
causes an onshore transport of sediment, (Fuhrman et al., 2013). In the study of (Fuhrman et al., 2013)
inclusion of streaming is shown to increase the onshore directed transport of medium coarse sand up to a
factor of two, and considering ﬁne sand subjected to a velocity skewed signal the sign of the cross-shore
sediment transport changed from offshore to onshore when including streaming. An additional source
of streaming is the converging-diverging effects of a slope, studied experimentally by e.g. (Sumer et al.,
1993) and numerically by e.g. (Fuhrman et al., 2009a) and (Fuhrman et al., 2009b) using a RANS-based
CFD-model with a k −ω model for closure. The converging-diverging geometry of such a slope gives
rise to an adverse pressure gradient when the ﬂow is in the divergent direction, i.e. down slope. This
adverse pressure gradient causes a near bed onshore directed ﬂow to develop in the boundary layer. When
the ﬂow is in the convergent direction the pressure gradient is on the other hand favourable, why the near
bed ﬂow is in the same direction as the main ﬂow, i.e. onshore. Thus in a period-averaged sense there will
be a net onshore ﬂow adhering from the effect of the slope.

Modelling of sediment transport involves a quantiﬁcation of when sediment begins to move. Such a
quantiﬁcation is complex and thus simpliﬁcations have to be made. One of the methods used is based on
the so-called critical Shields parameter, which will be elaborated upon in the following.

As a steady ﬂow passes over the bed, it exerts two types of driving forces on the grains: a drag force, FD,
and a lift force, FL, see ﬁgure 1.5. The drag force is associated with two concepts, skin friction and form
drag. Skin friction is directly associated with the friction on the surface of the grains whereas form drag

excess of natural buoyancy partly arises from the curvature of the streamlines as the ﬂow passes over the
grains. The curvature of the streamlines decreases the pressure locally compared to hydrostatic conditions
resulting in a lift force. Additionally the ﬂow separation involved in the creation of the drag force also
induce a lift force. The stabilizing forces, FS, are related to the frictional resistance between the grains
and their weight. These forces are illustrated in ﬁgure 1.5.

The overestimated levels of turbulence following the use of non-stabilized turbulence models is a
problem that has been known for quite some time. For instance, section 3.3.3 in the review article of

instability of the turbulence model. The fact that (Larsen and Fuhrman, 2018b) now have shown that the
instability is unconditional thus provides an answer to a long lasting problem. Due to the signiﬁcance
of turbulence within cross-shore sediment transport combined with the extensive use of RANS-based
models, the evaluation and improvement of turbulence models is a very important part of hydrodynamic
research. The present report is seen in line with this scope.

2.3.4
Sand slide model
As the bed level is updated, it must be ensured that the slope of the bed is not unphysically steep. This is
done by incorporating a sand slide model as described in (Jacobsen et al., 2014). This model accounts for
slopes exceeding the angle of repose of the sediment. The model is described in (Niemann et al., 2011)
and the working principle is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.3. Here it is shown how the model in a stepwise manner
ensures that the slope is lowered to a speciﬁed value below the angle of repose at each relevant location.
This is done starting from the top-most point of relevance. By lowering this point to achieve the allowed
slope, some mass is removed. To achieve mass conservation this mass is added at the next point, which
results in a local increase in slope. Thus the slope at this point may also need to be readjusted to meet the
criteria. Hence the sand slide model produces a stepwise redistribution of mass resulting in an overall
allowable slope. In the present case the sand slide mechanism is activated when the slope exceeds 32◦and
switched off when the slope reaches 31.9◦.

CHAPTER 3
Numerical speciﬁcs
This chapter outlines the numerical speciﬁcs of the model in terms of: the spatial resolution of the domain,
the used boundary and initial conditions and a description of the implementation of the wave forcing.
The governing equations for the two phase ﬂow outlined in chapter 2 are solved in the open source
CFD software OpenFOAM. The hydrodynamic simulations consider foam-extend 3.1 whereas the mor-
phological simulations consider foam-extend 1.6 as well as foam-extend 3.1. For the implementation of
surface waves the waves2Foam-toolbox described in (Jacobsen et al., 2012) is applied making use of the
interFoam-solver with the VOF-method. For the implementation of sediment transport the sediMorphCore
developed by Niels Gjøl Jacobsen has been invoked. A detailed description of this model is given in
(Jacobsen, 2011) and (Jacobsen et al., 2014).
The time derivatives in the governing equations, eq. (2.3) (RANS), (2.8) (k), (2.9) (ω) and (2.6) (γ)
are all discretized using a ﬁrst order implicit Euler scheme. The convective terms applies the Gaussian
discretization scheme with a variety of second order interpolation schemes. The gradients also make
use of the Gaussian discretization scheme but apply linear interpolation. The time derivative of the
advection-diffusion equation for c eq. (2.26) is discretized using the ﬁrst order Euler scheme and the
convective terms follow a Gaussian discretization scheme with an upwind interpolation scheme. Gradients
in the concentration also follow a Gaussian discretization scheme but with a linear interpolation scheme.
The model uses an adjustable time step, which ensures a maximum Courant number of Co =
|ui|∆t/∆xi = 0.15 for the hydrodynamic simulations. The maximum Courant number for the γ-ﬁeld
is also 0.15 and cγ = 1 ensures that the ’compression velocity’ in eq. (2.6) is the same size as the ﬂow
velocity at the interface, (Larsen et al., 2018). In the morphological simulations the Courant number has
been decreased to 0.05 matching (Li et al., 2018).

3.3.2
Inlet
The inlet boundary conditions for the velocity and the indicator ﬁeld γ follow the speciﬁed wave theory.
To do this the initial conditions are updated using the utility setWaveField. Hence instead of having a
domain, which is initially ﬁlled with air, i.e. a γ-ﬁeld of uniform zero, setWaveField ensures that the
γ-ﬁeld matches the still water level (or the speciﬁed initial condition) used in the model from the beginning
of the simulation.
The pressure ﬁeld and the volumetric sediment concentration, as well as the turbulence quantities,
follow Neumann boundary conditions.
Note that the model distinguishes between wet, dry and semi-dry faces in accordance with (Jacobsen
et al., 2012) when evaluating the wave boundary conditions of the ﬂow ﬁeld up, the surface elevation ηp
and the pressure gradient ∂p/∂n on the so-called boundary faces. These faces refer to cell faces, which
the considered cells have in common with the boundary, and the calculation follows the chosen potential
theory (indicated by subscript p). For the semi-dry faces the surface elevation and the pressure gradient is
evaluated at the wet center of mass and then assigned to the geometric center of the boundary face, see
ﬁgure 2 in (Jacobsen et al., 2012). The value of γ is set as the ratio between the area of the wet face and
the total area of the face. For dry faces all properties are set equal to zero and for the totally wet faces the
chosen potential theory is applied at the face centres.
3.3.3
Outlet
Since the run-up of the wave never reaches the outlet, the boundary conditions of this patch are somewhat
simplistic. The velocity is set to a ﬁxed value of uniform zero and for both the turbulence quantities ﬁxed
value boundaries following the initial conditions speciﬁed in table 3.9 are applied.
The pressure, volumetric sediment concentration and γ-ﬁeld follow Neumann boundary conditions.
3.3.4
Atmosphere
The atmosphere is modelled as an inletOutlet. Meaning that Neumann boundary conditions are generally
applied as long as the ﬂow is directed out of the domain, but if the ﬂow in some areas is directed into
the domain the following values are applied: ui = 0 m/s, γ = 0, the values for k and ω follow the initial
conditions. Note that suspended sediment transport in principle is allowed to leave the domain through
the atmospheric boundary, but that it cannot return as the inﬂux is set to occur with c = 0. Thus the
atmospheric boundary should be located sufﬁciently far from the free surface of the ﬂow. The pressure
ﬁeld on the other hand follows the Neumann boundary condition.
3.3.5
Sides
The sides of the domain are in all cases modelled as empty, which is a simple way to create a 2-dimensional
case.

This chapter will study the simulated results of the ﬁxed bed case with the solitary plunger moving up a

As the solitary wave propagates through the domain, ﬁve important hydrodynamic processes will
according to (Sumer et al., 2011) take place: 1) shoaling of the wave evolving to breaking of the wave,
2) run-up as the ﬂow from the broken wave propagates up the slope, 3) run-down evolving into 4) a
hydraulic jump and 5) a trailing wave. The hydrodynamics behind these processes will be investigated
in the following. Initially a qualitative visualization of the ﬁve processes is given as a series of ﬁgures
illustrating each of the processes. Then direct quantitative comparisons will be made with the experimental
data of (Sumer et al., 2011) at selected measuring stations. This comparison considers: ﬁrstly the surface
elevation, secondly the mean bed shear stress and thirdly the turbulent kinetic energy density.

Another possible cause or enhancing effect behind the overestimated run-up reported in (Larsen and
Fuhrman, 2018a) is spurious velocities at the interface. These velocities can arise in models considering
two phase ﬂow with a large density ratio between the two phases, i.e. water and air. At the tip of the
run-up in the swash zone very few cells are ﬁlled with water. If momentum is transferred from the heavier
ﬂuid (water) to the lighter ﬂuid (air) then, due to the large density ratio between these ﬂuids, even a
small transfer of momentum from the heavy ﬂuid will have a very large effect on the light ﬂuid. This can
result in so-called spurious velocities. If these spurious velocities in the air phase are diffused across the
interface back into the water phase the ﬂow can be driven unnaturally onwards when the layer of water is
very thin as is the case in the swash zone.

5.4
waveFoam versus waveFlow solver
The above results are founded on the waveFoam-solver. This solver propagates the air-water interface
using the VOF-method. Recently however a new method has become available. This method is named
isoAdvector and has been developed by (Roenby et al., 2016). As implied by the name the interface
is here deﬁned as an iso-surface, which ensures that the interface will only span one grid cell. Once
the iso-surface representing the interface is identiﬁed, it is propagated not as a volume of ﬂuid but as
an iso-surface in a geometric manner based on the local velocity ﬁeld. Once the interface evolution in
time is deﬁned, the change in ﬂuid volumes resulting from the interface movement can be calculated and
redistributed. For details the reader is referred to (Roenby et al., 2016).
One of the main advantages of the isoAdvector-principle is the limited extend of the interface. Using
the standard VOF-method the interface can take up a number of cells providing a much more smeared
interface description. Especially in the swash zone, where the phases mix and the layer of water is
generally thin, an adequate representation of the interface can become problematic using the VOF-method
as it will require high spatial resolution. A possible explanation for the poor performance considering the
regular plunging wave case inside the swash zone may thus be inadequate interface representation.
To investigate whether an isoAdvector-representation of the interface is capable of improving the
results of the regular plunging wave case in the swash zone, the isoAdvector-solver named interIsoFoam
in OpenFOAM-v1712 has been coupled with the waveFoam-solver of the waves2Foam-toolbox to form
the waveFlow-solver. It is noted that to conduct a completely identical run the turbulence model along
with the wall functions mentioned in chapter 3 would have to be re-coded to be readable in this version of
OpenFOAM. However, as this investigation initiated quite late in the project, it was chosen to conduct
the investigation using standard OpenFOAM wall functions for k, ω and νT instead of the wall functions
mentioned in chapter 3, see appendix I. This was chosen since the previously used wall functions were
not easily convertable to the code-format used in OpenFOAM-v1712. The turbulence model described in
chapter 2 however has been re-coded using the kOmegaStab-model from the stabRAS-setup of (Larsen,
2018) combined with the OpenFOAM-extend-3.1 turbulence model as a starting point. Apart from the
change in wall functions the boundary conditions and initial conditions described in chapter 3 are main-
tained.
In the following ﬁrst an initial investigation of the implemented turbulence model is performed. Then the
results of the waveFlow simulation of the regular plunging wave case will be discussed.

5.5
Evaluation of case B
Overall the regular plunging case has proven a more complicated case to simulate. The fact that none
of the tested wave theories are completely optimum, and that some uncertainty in the slope is present,
complicates the analysis and thus makes it difﬁcult to assess the model.
Considering the waveFoam-simulation the breaking point appears to be well captured but the manner
in which the wave tumbles over differs somewhat from the experimental results. From ﬁgure 3 in (Sumer
et al., 2013) the plunge strikes almost directly down on station D, whereas in the model this happens
slightly onshore of D. Also the increase in crest height leading up to the plunge looks slightly larger in
the model than on the drawing, though this may simply be a matter of scaling of the plot. It is, however,
certain that the modelled surface elevation at station D ﬁts the live bed experiment better than the ﬁxed
bed experiment. In the live bed experiment this station had been shifted 8 cm offshore amounting to
an increase in depth of 0.57 cm. Thus to achieve a behaviour more similar to the ﬁxed bed experiment,
the plunge should have been further in its development, possibly indicating a slightly steeper slope than
1:14. But the very good agreement in surface elevation at station B muddles this picture and thus the
problems at D may also simply be linked to the non-linear effects of the stream function wave or lack
of 3D effects during the collapse of the plunging induced vortex. Similar problems are seen with the
waveFlow-simulation, though here the surface elevation at both A and B is slightly underestimated. Inside

the surf zone moving towards the swash zone issues with overestimation of the surface elevation is seen

reproducing the very diminutive trough at station G and generally ﬁts the high degree of asymmetry in the

that the bed shear stresses of the experiments are generally highest during run-up, it will likely be difﬁcult
to achieve good morphological results with any of the present models since they both underestimate the
mean bed shear stress during run-up considerably in the main morphological region, i.e. station D-G.
The waveFoam-model simulates the near bed turbulence during run-down quite well in direct accor-
dance with the bed shear stress. During run-up in the inner surf and swash zone the model has large
difﬁculty in modelling the near bed turbulence. This is linked to the fact that the applied wall functions
couple turbulence to Uf, which is calculated from the near bed velocity. Since the model has large
problems modelling this ﬂow, hardly any turbulence is seen near the bed. This is likely caused by the
quite irregular looking waterfront. Looking at the spatial variation of νT /ν however the turbulence model
appears to generate reasonable results: Turbulence is only present near the bed in the shoaling region,
whereas during breaking and run-up turbulence is also seen across the water column in accordance with the
mixing vortices induced by the breaking process. As for the waveFlow-model the opposite picture is seen
from D to G, especially during run-up: The near bed turbulence levels are excessive. The wall function
used in this model is a zero gradient condition for k. This means that the near bed turbulence is affected
by the level of turbulence in the water column, which in this case is exaggerated. This exaggeration is
likely linked to two issues. First of all, the model cannot model pure potential ﬂow correctly with the
present set-up, meaning that the stabilizing effect of the turbulence model doesn’t work. Second, the
inclusion of buoyancy will likely give very large contributions during breaking and run-up due to the very
tight interface. Considering the much better performance in modelling the run-up ﬂow, the potential of
this model is good, but further investigations regarding the high turbulence levels are needed.

6.3
Difference between extend-1.6 and extend-3.1
There is a number of differences between the extend-1.6 model and the extend-3.1 model. Firstly, the
slope of the extend-1.6 domain is 1:14, whereas it is 1:13.525 considering the original extend-3.1 model.
Secondly, the sand slide models differ. In extend-1.6 a sand slide model named bedLoadDriven is
implemented. This model is also part of the extend-3.1 set-up, but is not compilable. Instead a slide
model named geometric3D is implemented. Thirdly, the diffusivity of the sediment used in the advection-
diffusion equation for c differs. In extend-1.6 the diffusivity is based on νT , as indicated in eq. (2.26),
hence it includes the stress limiting feature associated with λ1 along with the stabilizing feature associated
with λ2 of the turbulence model. This is similar to the diffusion term for u. In extend-3.1 the diffusivity is
based on k/ω, similar to the diffusion terms for k and ω. Since νT = k/˜ω ≤k/ω following eq. (2.14),
then the sediment diffusivity in extend-3.1 will either be larger than or equal to that used in extend-1.6.
From a modelling point of view it may seem more consistent that the diffusivity of all scalar quantities
(c, k and ω) is modelled in a similar fashion, i.e. as being proportional to k/ω. Fourthly, the resolution
in the non-sloping part of the domain of the extend-3.1 model considers aspect ratios a factor two larger
than those of the extend-1.6 model, as mentioned in chapter 3. Considering that the bed only starts to
change from x > 3 m looking in ﬁgure 6.1, the ﬂow has quite some time to adjust and it does not seem
likely that the morphological differences displayed should be primarily caused by this. Hence to reduce
the computation time and also to be consistent with the initial results in ﬁgure 6.1, the coarser resolution
in the non-sloping part of the domain has been maintained.
It is possible to set the diffusivity in extend-3.1 as turbulenceModelBased, which is equivalent to
extend-1.6, and it has thus been chosen to focus on the difference in diffusivity and slope in the following.

it will inﬂuence the location and the way in which the hydraulic jump forms. Comparing C.3 to C.4 the
stretched interface-peak is seen to be directly linked to the inclusion of buoyancy, which is also seen to
inﬂuence the turbulence level quite markedly. Including buoyancy the turbulence is seen to decrease, i.e.
the low levels of turbulence seen in the hydraulic jump of case A, case C.2 and C.3 are not caused by the
stabilizing limiter λ2, but are instead linked to the buoyancy production, which dampens the turbulence.
The dampened turbulence levels appear to be a likely reason for the slightly lower levels of erosion and
deposition comparing C.3 to C.4, since lower levels of turbulence will reduce the suspended load.
Returning to ﬁgure 6.6b. The fact that so little change in the morphological response is seen comparing
C.4 to C.0 ﬁts very well with the fact that λ2 ought to have very little effect, if any, in regions where the
ﬂow is not potential, i.e. in regions where the majority of the sediment transport takes place: the surf
and swash zone. The present case is quite simple since it only considers a solitary wave. Thus any build
up of turbulence, which might take place without the stabilized turbulence model over time considering
wave trains, is not seen in this case. Thus hardly any effect of the stabilization in the turbulence model
is expected in terms of the morphodynamics for this case. It is thus very reassuring that the results also
show this. For morphodynamic modelling in the swash zone though, it seems advantageous to exclude
buoyancy from the turbulence model since this improves the stability of the bed response signiﬁcantly
and for the present case also shows a better ﬁt with the measurements of (Sumer et al., 2011).

Further research
No morphological simulation of case B has been made. Hence the inﬂuence of the stabilized turbulence
model over a long period of time with more complex turbulent processes during the breaking process

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This thesis has considered the numerical simulation of two types of plunging breaking waves: a solitary
plunging wave and regular plunging waves both moving up a ﬁxed bed with constant slope. Additionally
a morphodynamic study of the solitary wave case has been made. The used numerical model is a fully
coupled morphodynamic RANS-based model developed by Niels Gjøl Jacobsen that combined with the
stabilized two-equation k −ω model of (Larsen and Fuhrman, 2018b) solves the incompressible Reynolds
Averaged Navier Stokes equations for the hydrodynamics and attains the morphological evolution by
solving the Exner equation. The model is set up in the open source CFD-software OpenFOAM and
accounts for both bed load as well as suspended load.
As the solitary wave moves up the slope a number of hydrodynamic processes occur: shoaling evolving to
breaking of the wave, post-breaking run-up of the wave, run-down of the wave evolving to a hydraulic
jump and ﬁnally the evolution of a trailing wave as the hydraulic jump breaks up. The hydrodynamic
model performance is evaluated by comparing the surface elevation, the mean bead shear stress and the
near bed turbulence to the results of (Sumer et al., 2011). The model generally performs very well in
terms of these three quantities during more or less all the hydrodynamic processes. But the run-down ﬂow,
taking place in a very thin layer of water in the swash zone, and the complex processes happening in the
hydraulic jump show larger discrepancies in terms of all three quantities.
The discrepancies seen in the swash zone hydrodynamics are reproduced in the morphodynamic
simulation of the case in the shape of an underestimated level of erosion compared to (Sumer et al., 2011).
However, the deposition pattern matches the quite sparse experimental data very well and the overall
shape of the bed after four waves seem quite natural. The model set-up matches that of (Li et al., 2018),
who reached very similar results in the modelling of this case. The stable and natural response in the
swash zone is particularly impressive since the morphodynamics of the swash zone is inﬂuenced by a
complex ﬂow of mixed ﬂuids, which easily evolve into a quite irregular bed response. To achieve stable
results in this region, a coarse horizontal resolution proved important. This opposes the importance of
small aspect ratios for the modelling of the hydrodynamics as reported by (Jacobsen et al., 2012). It is thus
emphasized that a balance between these two interests has to be struck when considering morphodynamic
modelling that include the swash zone. The inﬂuence of the stabilized turbulence model is seen to be
practically non-existent as expected, since the stabilizing effect is only active in regions governed by
potential ﬂow hence outside the littoral zone. The buoyancy production implemented in the stabilized
turbulence model of (Larsen and Fuhrman, 2018b) on the other hand generally makes the response of the
bed more irregular and thus has a quite distinct effect on the morphological results.
As the regular waves propagate up the slope a slightly smaller number of hydrodynamic processes
occur: shoaling evolving to breaking of the wave, post-breaking run-up of the wave and ﬁnally run-down
of the wave. The breaking of the wave in contrast to the solitary wave is now not only caused by the

decreasing water depth but also affected by the offshore directed ﬂow of the run-down. This gives rise
to a more complex breaking and run-up process during which a number of vortices form. Again, the
hydrodynamic model performance is evaluated by comparing the surface elevation, the mean bed shear
stress and the near bed turbulence to the results of (Sumer et al., 2013). The model appears to work
quite well in the pre-breaking region both in terms of run-up and run-down, though some uncertainty
regarding the run-up measurements at station A remains. In the post-breaking region the model has large
difﬁculty modelling the mean bed shear stress and near bed turbulence during run-up, which are both
underestimated considerably and lags behind the experimental results. During run-down the performance
is better but in the very shallow region of the inner surf and swash zone the model shows a tendency to
overestimate the mean bed shear stress. Both of these discrepancies relate to difﬁculty in modelling the
near bed ﬂow, which due to the generation of vortices contains a lot more mixing than the solitary wave
case, especially during run-up, and thus is much more difﬁcult to model. Improvement of these results
can be made through an increase in resolution but a solution with much greater potential is seen in the
replacement of the VOF interface modelling method with that of the isoAdvector-principle developed
by (Roenby et al., 2016). With this method the modelling of the near bed ﬂow during run-up improved
considerably and hence much better results in terms of the mean bed shear stress was reached. This model
however produces much higher levels of turbulence than the VOF-method. Part of this is attributed to
the fact that this method has difﬁculty modelling pure potential ﬂow when applying numerical schemes
adequate for the VOF-method, (Larsen et al., 2018). Hence inside the potential ﬂow region the stabilizing
effect of the turbulence model is likely not active. Outside the potential ﬂow region the overestimated
turbulence levels possibly adhere from the inclusion of buoyancy production, though a further study
hereof is relevant.
