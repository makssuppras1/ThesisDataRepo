Abstract
The human immune system comprises a highly complex network of cells and pathways within
diverse tissues that all serve unique purposes. In vitro models such as primary cell cultures and
organoids are used for studying the immune system and offer valuable insights into immune cell
behavior. The ability to target and induce knockouts in specific cell types can be used to study
the functions of single cells and has been developed using lentivirus carrying the Cas9 nuclease,
however, targeted knock-ins remain elusive. This thesis explores the development of a lentivirus-
based platform for precise genomic insertions by implementing the Programmable Addition via
Site-specific Targeting Elements (PASTE) editing system into a lentiviral vector. This thesis covers
methods used in lentiviral production, cell culturing, molecular biology and protein analysis for the
creation of PASTE encoding lentiviral vectors.
This report covers the testing of the baseline PASTE editing system, followed by the design
and assembly of two plasmids for the production of lentiviral PASTE vectors, called pSJB1 and
pSJB2. Furthermore, the report follows the process of verifying the constructed plasmids, assessing
protein production in the lentiviral vectors and attempting lentiviral PASTE editing in HEK cells.
Unfortunately, a setback occured late in the project when whole plasmid sequencing revealed that
pSJB1 was not successfully cloned, contrary to initial assumptions. Consequently, numerous results
were invalidated, necessitating a restart of the cloning process.

1. Introduction
The human immune system consists of a diverse network of cells all engaged in complex interactions
within tissues throughout the body. While significant strides have been made in understanding its
fundamental mechanisms, a substantial portion remains unexplored. Understanding the human
immune system is crucial for understanding disease causes, evaluating treatment efficacy, and
comprehending immune responses. Platforms that enable precise gene editing in specific cells are
essential for clarifying cellular functions and interactions within the immune system [1; 2]. Lentiviral
vectors offer a promising method for targeted payload delivery, such as therapeutic proteins, due to
their adaptable tropism. Despite achieving successful cellular gene knockouts with CRISPR-Cas9
through lentiviral vectors [3], the challenge persists in achieving large, targeted insertions.
Programmable addition via site-specific targeting elements (PASTE) editing [4], a novel gene editing
platform, is showing to be a promising method for easily achieving large insertions, however the
system is inherently incapable of targeting specific cells. This thesis embarks on an exploration into
the integration of PASTE into the already established lentiviral platform for CRISPR-Cas9 editing,
possibly allowing for targeted cell-specific precise genomic insertions in complex heterogeneous
cell systems.
1.1 Mapping the human adaptive immune system
Immortalized cell lines offer a cost-effective and readily accessible platform for scientific research,
providing valuable insights into the dynamics of specific cell types. While serving as excellent
models for individual cell behaviors, these cell lines are limited in their ability to replicate the
intricate in vivo interactions observed within more complex biological systems [5]. Furthermore,
immortalized cell lines are prone to accumulate mutations, ultimately changing cell behaviour and
making them less reliable models [6]. In contrast, primary immune cells such as tissue or blood cells,
obtained directly from humans, present a closer approximation to the human immune system when
cultured in vitro [7]. An example of a primary immune cell culture is peripheral mononucleated
blood cells (PBMCs) harvested from blood samples, wherein various cell types found in blood can
be co-cultured to investigate their functions and interactions. In spite of more closely resembling
their tissue of origin, primary cell cultures are simple models incapable of emulating the intricacies
inherent to organs and tissues.
A significant portion of our knowledge about tissues, adaptive immunity and immune responses has
been acquired through studying animals, commonly used as model organisms. However, animal
models lack many essential features of the human immune system, making them unsuitable for
accurately simulating human immune responses. For instance, differences in organ structures,
such as the spleen, can lead to variations in how immune cells interact between mice and humans.
==PAGE:10==

Additionally, genes may exhibit different expression levels and functions across species. For example,
mice lacking the CD28 gene may experience severe immune dysfunctions, while humans with the
same mutation are generally unaffected [8]. Differences between animal models and humans are
particularly notable in vaccine development, where a successful immunization outcome in an animal
model does not reliably predict success in humans. The differences between animal and human
models, as well as ethical concerns associated with using animals in scientific studies have prompted
the development of organoids as an promising alternative model system [9; 10].
Tonsil organoids
Organoids are complex 3D tissue cell culture systems engineered through the proliferation of stem
cells or cells obtained from tissue samples such as tumors or surgically extracted healthy tissue.
Cultivating and engineering functional organoids from diverse tissues provides the opportunity to
observe tissue interactions, typically reserved to in vivo settings, within an in vitro environment [11].
Various organoid systems have been created to cater to specific research objectives, encompassing
an array of tissues such as brain tissue [12], liver cells [13] and oral mucosal cells [14]. Of particular
significance to this study are tonsil organoids [9].
Tonsil organoids were developed as an alternative to murine models for research in immunology.
Tonsillectomies, the surgical excision of tonsils, are a common procedure that yields abundant tonsil
tissue samples. These samples serve as starting material for the generation of tonsil organoids.
Given the lymphoid nature of tonsils, these organs encompass all essential components required to
facilitate the formation of germinal centers (GCs). Organoids derived from tonsils exhibit the same
properties as their tissue of origin, supporting affinity maturation and class switching of B cells, all
while encompassing the cell types integral to adaptive immunity [9]. This makes tonsil organoids a
valuable model in the understanding of human immunology and an interesting target for genetic
engineering.
1.2 Genetic engineering of mammalian cells
The transfer of DNA encoding genes for genetic modification of a recipient cell is a fundamental step
in understanding the functionalities of complex biological systems, including the human immune
system. Transfection, a process involving the introduction of nucleotides into eukaryotic cells, is
pivotal for these studies and is broadly classified into viral and non-viral methods. [15].
Among non-viral strategies, which predominantly center on augmenting cell membrane permeability
or employing chemical compounds for nucleotide delivery [15], a noteworthy methodology is
electroporation. This technique involves the application of short, high-intensity pulses of current
to the cell, inducing the formation of pores that facilitate the entry of foreign genetic material
[16]. Alternatively, compounds such as polyethylenimine (PEI) or Lipofectamine, through their

interactions with nucleotides, form complexes capable of traversing the cell membrane allowing for
transfection [17; 18]. A major drawback in the use of these transfection methods resides in the fact
that they all lack the capability to target specific cell types in a heterogeneous cell population, such
organoids or tissue samples.
Lentivirus
To overcome the issue of non-specific transfection, viruses such as lentivirus and adenovirus have
been developed to be used as gene delivery platforms, due to their intrinsic ability to target and
deliver their genetic material to cells [19]. Transduction, the term for virus-mediated transfection,
allows for precise cellular editing. It offers the capacity to establish stable gene expression in the case
of viral vectors that integrate their genomes into host cells, such as lentiviral vectors, or transiently
introducing genes using vectors like adenovirus vectors.
The Lentivirus genus belongs to the Orthoretrovirinae subfamily of the Retroviridae family, which is
also commonly referred to as retroviruses [20]. Some well-known viruses belonging to the Lentivirus
genus include several immunodeficiency causing viruses, with the most well known and studied
being human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) [21]. Lentivirus relies on three genes to function: gag,
pol and env. Gag encodes structural proteins, including matrix, capsid, nucleocapsid, and p6. Pol
encodes the protease, reverse transcriptase, and integrase proteins necessary for genomic integration
into the target cell. Meanwhile, env is responsible for encoding the viral envelope [19]. Once the
virus enters the host cell through direct membrane fusion or receptor-mediated endocytosis, the
virus uncoats, leading to the dissociation of viral proteins from its core. The viral RNA genome is
converted into DNA through reverse transcription, after which the viral DNA is transported into the
cell nucleus and integrated into the host cells genome. The newly infected host cell now produces
viral RNA, which is transported out into the cytoplasm, where viral proteins are translated, and the
viral particle is assembled. The virion is finally mature once it buds from the cell, creating a new
infectious particle, as depicted in Figure 1 [22].

1.3 CRISPR-Cas9
In 1987, Ishino Y., Shinagawa H., Makino K., Amemura M. and Nakata A. discovered the presence
of clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) in Escherichia coli, which
were series of short direct repeats, interspaced with short variable sequences called spacers, in the
bacterial genome [26]. It was later found that the spacers between the palindomic repeats were of
extrachromosomal origin, and that the spacers were transcribed in tandem with the palindromic
repeats as CRISPR RNA (crRNA) [27; 28]. This, coupled with the discovery of CRISPR-associated
(Cas) proteins with various nuclease and helicase domains, led to the identification of the CRISPR-
Cas system being the bacterial version of a adaptive immune system [29; 30]. Many Cas proteins
exist in the CRISPR system, having varying functions such as inserting spacers into the genome, or
cleaving DNA or RNA targeted by crRNA [31].
==PAGE:14==
Engineering Novel Lentivirus for Transduction of Human Immune Cells Using PASTE – 14/61
CRISPR-Cas9 is one of these Cas proteins, being a nuclease capable of creating a double-strand
break (DSB) in a target sequence utilizing a trans-activating crRNA and the spacer crRNA. The
transactivating crRNA was found to be necessary for crRNA maturation, with the Cas9 utilizing
both to direct DNA cleavage [32]. The crRNA system was further engineered by linking the two
crRNAs together using a linker sequence, creating a single guide RNA (gRNA) that can be used
for Cas9 mediated DSBs [33]. The gRNA must contain a 20 nucleotide sequence at the 5’ end of
the gRNA, which is used by the Cas9 protein as a guide. The gRNA target also needs to be directly
proceeded by a protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) site, a specific nucleotide sequence that varies in
length depending on the specific Cas protein being used. In the case for one of the most frequently
used Cas9 proteins derived from Streptococcus pyogenes, spCas9, the PAM sequence is a 5’-NGG
sequence [34].

DNA ligase to ligate the two broken ends together, often resulting in indels at the DSB site. Homology
directed repair (HDR) is employed by a cell if a repair template with homologous regions is available,
resulting in a precise repair of the DSB. Additionally, HDR may give rise to insertions if the repair template
harbors additional genetic elements. The selection between these pathways depends upon various factors,
such as the cell cycle phase and the availability of homologous templates. Figure taken from [34].
CRISPR-Cas9 has become a widely utilized protein for the genomic modification of cells, as the
DSB created by the protein induces the activation of repair pathways in the cell. This can be utilized
to either create insertions or knockouts of genes, depending on the pathway utilized by the cell. The
two repair pathways to repair a DSB are nonhomologous end joining (NHEJ) and homology directed
repair (HDR), as depicted in Figure 3. NHEJ is a very error-prone DNA repair pathway, often
resulting in insertions and/or deletions (Indels), making it a great option for inducing knockouts of

genes [34]. HDR as a means for genomic engineering is available for cells in the S and G2 phases
of the cell cycle and relies on a repair template in the form of a double-stranded DNA template
or single-stranded DNA being introduced to the cell, making the cell repair the DSB to match the
repair template. This can be used as a method for creating knock-ins of genes [34; 35; 36]. The
ability to make precise edits in a cells genome has made CRISPR based systems an invaluable asset
for use in synthetic biology and molecular biology. Knock-ins are however still very difficult to
achieve in non-dividing cells, as they lack the mechanisms for HDR due to the S and G2 phases
being unavailable.
CRISPR-Cas9 delivery and editing via lentiviral vectors
By inserting sequences encoding gRNA and CRISPR-Cas9 into lentiviral vectors it is possible to
transduce cells with a stable production of a functional CRISPR-Cas9 system, capable of creating
genetic knockouts in human cells [37]. While effective at generating knockouts, the genomic
integration of both the gRNA and CRISPR-Cas9 encoding genes greatly increases the risk of
unintended off-target knockouts or indels [38]. Hamilton et al. presented a method to circumvent
this issue, demonstrating the feasibility of packaging preformed Cas9 proteins into a lentiviral vector
by fusing it to the structural Gag protein via a HIV protease-cleavable linker which is cleaved upon
viral infection. This construct is then delivered to a host cell, enabling Cas9-mediated knockouts [3].
Notably, the Cas9 protein is only transiently present post-transduction, subject to degradation by
the host cells proteases. This still leaves enough time for the Cas9 protein to induce knockouts, but
significantly decreases the risk of off-target effects.
1.4 Prime editing
Expanding upon the possibilities created by CRISPR-directed genomic editing, prime editing is
a method that allows for the rewriting of specific DNA sequences without creating any DSBs or
needing donor DNA [39]. Prime editing utilizes a modified Cas9 protein, known as a Cas9 nickase,
which has been engineered to induce a break solely in the DNA strand containing the PAM sequence.
The Cas9 nickase has been fused to a reverse transcriptase, enabling prime editing. In contrast to
Cas9 gRNA, prime editing guide RNA (pegRNA) also contains a primer binding site (PBS) as well
as a repair template in the 3’ end of the RNA. Following the recognition and nicking of a DNA site
targeted by the Cas9 nickase and the Cas9 gRNA domain of the pegRNA, an exposed 3’-hydroxyl
group is generated in the targeted DNA sequence. The PBS domain of the pegRNA is designed to
bind to the recently nicked DNA strand, allowing for the reverse transcriptase domain of a prime
editor to initiate reverse transcription. The reverse transcription facilitates the incorporation of
edits from the repair template supplied by the pegRNA into the nicked DNA strand. These edits
encompass deletions, insertions of nucleotides, or single-nucleotide changes. Upon completion of
reverse transcription, cellular mismatch repair mechanisms resolve the heteroduplex, often resulting
==PAGE:16==
Engineering Novel Lentivirus for Transduction of Human Immune Cells Using PASTE – 16/61
in the integration of the edit in the nicked strand, as seen in Figure 4. To enhance the efficiency of
prime editing, a second gRNA, known as a nicking guide, is frequently incorporated. This guide,
resembling a typical Cas9 gRNA, is designed to create a nick on the complementary DNA strand
within 100 nucleotides of the prime-edited sequence. By nicking the non-edited strand, the cellular
mismatch repair system tends to favor the incorporation of the edited sequence, thereby boosting
prime editing efficiency. Prime editing can be used to make insertions of up to ≈50 base pairs, and
deletions of up to ≈80 base pairs [39; 40; 4].
Editing small sections of DNA without inducing DSBs makes prime editing a efficient method for
modifying non-dividing cells [41]. However, the limitation arises in the inability to achieve large
insertions exceeding 50 base pairs using prime editing. Consequently, the incorporation of sizable
genes into non-dividing cells is unattainable through this method.

1.5 Serine integrases
Site-specific recombinases, a class of enzymes derived from phages, represent a robust alternative to
homologous recombination for genetic modification. Diverging from homology-based approaches,
site-specific recombinases eliminate the need for extended homologous DNA sequences and operate
independently of endogenous repair pathways for the insertion of foreign genetic material [43].
Serine integrases, a sub-class of site-specific recombinases, excel in generating large genomic
insertions. During viral infection of a cell, integrases recognize enzyme-specific attachment site
sequences found in the phage (referred to as attP) and the infected bacteria (referred to as attB).
Once recognized, the serine integrase catalyzes the integration of the phage genome into the bacterial
genome through recombination of the attP and attB sites into attL and attR sites [43; 44]. These
properties can be utilized in genomic engineering, as simply introducing a serine integrase and
exogenous DNA with an attP site to a attB site will enable recombination between the sites.
Serine integrases do not require additional factors to function, making them excellent tools for use
in synthetic biology. If necessary, introducing a recombination directionality factor (RDF) enables
the reverse reaction of the serine integrase. This further opens up many different possibilities for
DNA assembly, circuit designs and overall genomic engineering, as illustrated in Figure 5.
Figure 5. Schematic representation of serine integrase modes of action. The positional arrangement of att
sites dictates diverse outcomes during serine integrase activity. Illustrative examples include DNA insertion
(a), excision (b), recombination between distinct DNA sequences (c), and intramolecular recombination
within a single DNA strand (d). These mechanisms exemplify the synthetic biology applications of serine
integrases. Figure adapted from [43].
1.6 PASTE
The PASTE [4] system employs a chimeric protein to achieve substantial DNA insertions into a
genome without requiring active double-strand break (DSB) repair pathways in the target cell. The
PASTE editor consists of a CRISPR-Cas9 nickase, a reverse transcriptase and a serine integrase
==PAGE:18==
Engineering Novel Lentivirus for Transduction of Human Immune Cells Using PASTE – 18/61
(BxbI) fused together, resulting in a ≈303 kDa protein. Expanding upon the foundations of prime
editing, the PASTE editor is guided via a attachment site-containing guide RNA (atgRNA) to a
location in the genome, inserting a attB site via prime editing. Addition of a nicking guide targeting
the opposite DNA strand of the atgRNA increases the chance of successfully integrating the attB
site. Recognized by the serine integrase domain of the PASTE editor, the attB site serves as an
insertion site for a cargo plasmid containing an attP site, which is supplied during PASTE editing
transfections. The serine integrase in PASTE is capable of facilitating DNA insertions up to 36
kilobases, making PASTE an incredibly effective system for achieving genomic integrations [4].
To facilitate PASTE editing, a 4-plasmid methodology is employed during cellular transfection.
Successful PASTE editing necessitates the uptake of all four plasmids, encompassing the following
(as is also illustrated in Figure 6):
• atgRNA encoding plasmid
• Nickase guide encoding plasmid
• PASTE editor encoding plasmid
• Minicircle plasmid

Insertion rates when utilizing PASTE editing range from ≈50-60% in cell lines to ≈4-5% in human
T-cells and hepatocytes, and are affected by the payload size and specific cell type being edited. As
PASTE utilizes a serine integrase for insertions, a scar is created at the insertion site in the form
of attL and attR sites. These sites can be utilized as linker sequences, allowing for the tagging of
proteins with the delivered cargo, such as green fluorescent protein (GFP) onto proteins encoded by
genes of interest as depicted in Figure 7.
PASTE editing has demonstrated its capacity to delete and replace genes, denoted as PASTE-Replace
by the authors. This involves the utilization of a paired atgRNA system to initially induce the deletion
of a genomic sequence, followed by the integration of a transgene. Furthermore, PASTE editing
exhibits multiplexing capabilities, showcasing the integration of up to three different transgenes at
three distinct genomic sites through the engineering of attB and attP sites recognized by the serine
integrase of the PASTE editor.

LentiPASTE
The aim of this thesis is to investigate if it is possible to create a lentiviral vector based delivery
system for PASTE editing. Packaging the PASTE editor into the lentiviral particles, with both the
atgRNA and nicking gRNA being expressed from the integrated viral genome would allow for
transient PASTE editing in infected cells. Adhering to the design principles outlined by Hamilton et
al. [3], the PASTE editor will be fused to the lentiviral Gag protein via an HIV protease-cleavable
linker sequence. This fusion facilitates the release of the PASTE editor into the cytosol during viral
infection. Subsequently, nuclear localization signals on the protein guide the PASTE editor to the
nucleus, where the necessary gRNAs are expressed through the recently integrated lentiviral genome.
During LentiPASTE transduction, transfection will be employed to introduce the minicircle plasmids
containing the transgenes for PASTE editing, with the hope of facilitating uptake and subsequent
insertion of the transgene through PASTE editing.

Multiplexed PASTE
For the optimization of PASTE editing efficiency, the co-expression of both a nickase gRNA and
an atgRNA is essential. Within the existing PASTE editing framework, a dual-plasmid system is
utilized, where two distinct plasmids encoding gRNAs are co-transfected along with the PASTE
editor-encoding plasmid and the cargo plasmid. The shift of the PASTE system to a lentiviral
vector-based platform requires the integration of both gRNAs into the lentiviral genome. To this end
a U6 promoter-based approach will be employed.
1.7 Western Blot
In the context of this project, an imperative task is the production and identification of the PASTE
editor protein. Western blotting will be employed for the verification of its expression within
lentiviral vectors. The methodology involves the use of sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide
gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) to separate proteins within a sample based on their respective
molecular weights. After separation, the proteins are transferred onto a solid membrane, commonly
composed of nitrocellulose or polyvinylidene diflouride (PVDF). The membrane is subsequently
probed with primary antibodies selected for their specificity to the protein of interest. Detection and
quantification of the target protein are facilitated through the introduction of secondary antibodies,
which exhibit affinity towards the primary antibodies. These secondary antibodies are conjugated
with either fluorescent molecules or enzymes with the capacity to generate a quantifiable signal,
such as horseradish peroxidase, thereby enabling the detection of the protein of interest [46].
1.8 Fluorescence microscopy
Fluorescence microscopy is a technique to visualize and study the dynamics of biological structures
at the cellular and subcellular levels. Leveraging the intrinsic property of fluorophores to emit light
upon excitation, fluorescence microscopy enables the visualization of specific cellular components
and processes. As the current minicircle plasmids in the PASTE system encode GFP, fluorescence
microscopy will be used to determine if PASTE transfections and future transductions have been
succesful.
Fluorescence microscopes work by directing a light source through a excitation filter onto a dichroic
mirror which redirects the light onto the specimen being examined. The emitted light is then passed
through a barrier filter to filter out excess light, before being split into two beams by a second
dichroic mirror. Both of these beams are captured by detector cameras, thus enabling a visualization
of fluorescence. Changing the excitation filter allows for selection of different wavelengths, allowing
visualization of different light spectra. Emission filters are placed before both detector cameras, to
further filter out unwanted light [47].

2. Project aim
The aim of this thesis is to establish a lentiviral vector based delivery system for the PASTE editing
technology developed by Yarnall, M.T.N., Ioannidi, E.I., Schmitt-Ulms, C. et al. [4]. The primary
objective is to investigate the feasibility of producing and packaging the PASTE editor into lentiviral
particles, with both gRNAs being expressed from the viral genome. This innovative approach
aims to enable transient PASTE editing in infected cells, providing a powerful tool for precise and
large-scale genomic insertions in heterogeneous cell populations.
Key objectives
1. Verifying PASTE functionality in our laboratory
2. Assembling a lentiviral packaging plasmid encoding the Gag-PASTE editor fusion protein
3. Assembling a lentiviral transfer plasmid encoding gRNAs for PASTE editing
4. Confirming PASTE editor production in lentiviral vectors via western blotting
5. Testing LentiPASTE editing in various cell types
Initial testing of the LentiPASTE system will be done in HEK293T cells, as these are a reliable and
easier to perform editing in, compared to primary cells. If successful, the research outcome will
not only contribute to the development of an advanced Lentivirus-based genomic editing platform
but also pave the way for potential applications in complex cell systems such as organoids. The
ability to precisely modify specific cells can significantly enhance our understanding of intricate cell
interactions within the immune system.

4. Results
4.1 Testing baseline PASTE editing
To verify the functionality of the PASTE system in our laboratory, HEK293T cells were transfected
with all the plasmids needed for PASTE editing. The plasmids available allowed for PASTE
editing targeting the β-actin (ACTB) gene of human cells. Initial transfections following the method
presented by Yarnall, M.T.N., Ioannidi, E.I., Schmitt-Ulms, C. et al. showed no signs of fluorescence,
leading to the testing of various plasmid concentrations for PASTE editing in our laboratory. High
amounts of fluorescence was obtained when utilizing the plasmid ratios described in the materials
and methods section of this report. Plasmids pDY181 and pDY423 carrying a GFP encoding gene
were used in both their respective minicircle and full parent plasmid forms during testing, where no
measurable signal was obtained from HEK cells transfected with pDY423, as seen in Figure 9. Cells
transfected with pDY181 plasmids showed a higher amount of cells expressing fluorescence when
transfected with the mini-circularized version of pDY181 (≈1000 kilobases), as compared to the
full parent plasmid (≈4500 kilobases), as seen in Figure 10. This is in line with the observations
made by Yarnall, M.T.N., Ioannidi, E.I., Schmitt-Ulms, C. et al., as larger plasmids yield lower
insertion rates compared to smaller cargos. Due to the lack of measurable GFP signal from cells
transfected with pDY423, it was decided that future PASTE editing experiments would be conducted
using the pDY181 plasmid.

5. Discussion
Many results in this project were invalidated upon discovering that the pSJB01 plasmid had not been
successfully cloned, instead being a self-ligation of the pJRH029 plasmid. The initial sequencing
result seen in Figure A.1 had seemed to confirm the ligation, although the absence of an annotated
nuclear localization sequence was odd. Initially it was assumed to be due to an annotation error, as
these are not uncommon on platforms like Addgene from which the pJRH029 plasmid map was
acquired, as the maps are user-generated. In hindsight, the sequencing result indicated that it was the
pJRH029 plasmid in the sample, which is also be further supported when aligning the sequencing
result with the pJRH029 sequence, revealing a perfect match as seen in Figure 28.
Figure 28. Alignment of the sequencing results of the suspected pSJB1 plasmid and the pJRH029 plasmid
(Gag-Cas9). The sequencing result aligns perfectly with the pJRH029 sequence.
This highlights the importance of verifying constructs before continuing with down-stream ex-
periments. As seen in the SDS-PAGE and western blots of lysed lentiPASTE vectors in Figures
20a and 20b, there were bands that were misinterpreted as being the PASTE editor protein. This
further highlighting the importance of ensuring the accuracy of constructs before their utilization,
as a significant amount of time was expended during this project under the assumption that the
correct constructs had been assembled. In future SDS-PAGE and western blotting experiments,
some controls should also be included, such as a lentiviral vector carrying Cas9 and a standard
lentiviral vector. This would offer a better benchmark and aid in eliminating false positives from the
findings.

The cloning attempts of the pSJB1 plasmid and pSJB2 gRNA insertions have been restarted. Given
that all the components needed for the gRNA insertions into pSJB2 were already in-house, new
restriction digests and insertions were performed. Unfortunately, the sequencing results from 12
different transformed E. coli colonies were not delivered in time for inclusion in this report. The PCR
amplifications of the PASTE gene and pJRH029 without Cas9 have proven to be more challenging
than initially anticipated, which may have contributed to the unsuccessful initial attempt at creating
the pSJB1 plasmid. If the new primers fail to amplify the correct portion of pJRH029, alternative
methods such as a nested PCR may be worth considering to eliminate potential off-target primer
binding sites from the template. Otherwise, performing a gel extraction of bands that correspond to
the expected size of the pJRH029 amplicon could also be conducted, in the hopes that these would
contain the correct amplicon for cloning.
The standard PASTE system seems to be less efficient in our laboratory compared to the original
article. A significant portion of transfected HEK293T cells die during the transfection when
following the instructions given by the authors, with the surviving cells taking a long time to
recover. The only difference in methodology lies in our use of PEI instead of Lipofectamine 2000
as the transfection reagent. As depicted Figure 23, cells transfected with the DNA concentrations
outlined in the PASTE editing article are much less healthy three days post-transfection compared
to the adjusted PASTE editing. Moving the cells over to larger wells with fresh media to allow
for cell expansion led to increased growth in the cells treated with the adjusted PASTE editing
approach, while growth seemed stagnant in the standard PASTE edited cells. Unfortunately, the PCR
amplifications for confirming PASTE insertions have not been successful yet and as such it is still
uncertain if any GFP insertions have actually taken place during the attempted PASTE editing. Some
troubleshooting is needed to ensure that the PCR amplification works as intended, only amplifying
the part of the ACTB gene targeted by the current PASTE editing system. New primers have also
been designed, one set amplifying across the ACTB gene into the 5’ end of the GFP gene and the
other amplifying from 3’ end of the GFP gene into the ACTB gene. These amplifications will serve
as backup methods for verifying PASTE editing insertions. More testing of the original PASTE
system is needed, as it serves as a reference point for future lentiPASTE experiments. Future PASTE
experiments should also be conducted other cell lines and primary cells, to investigate how cells are
impacted and if we are capable of achieving similar editing efficiencies as mentioned in the PASTE
article.
The finding that GFP is spuriously expressed from the minicircles was surprising, as we initially
assumed that fluorescence could be used to distinguish between PASTE edited cells and non-edited
cells. The original PASTE article does not at any point mention that spurious gene expression is
to be expected from the minicircles, instead prompting the use digital droplet PCR (ddPCR) for
==PAGE:47==
Engineering Novel Lentivirus for Transduction of Human Immune Cells Using PASTE – 47/61
determining integration efficiency. The spurious GFP expression highlights an issue with how we
would like to use the lentiPASTE system. We hypothesized that the simultaneous introduction of
lentiPASTE vectors and minicircles would result in GFP expression only in cells infected by the
viral vector, as the GFP gene would then be tagged onto the ACTB gene, leading to expression. The
rest of the cells would uptake the minicircle without being able to express the GFP gene due to a lack
of a promoter sequence, as such not fluorescing. The prolonged GFP signal observed over several
days as cells gradually lose the minicircle plasmid (unless integrated by PASTE editing) is not an
issue when working with cell lines, as the cells can be maintained for extended periods of time.
However, for the intended use of the lentiPASTE system in organoid systems and primary cells,
where the cell culture lifespan is shorter, the need to wait several days to confirm successful editing
could hinder experiments due to the shortened working timeframe. It may be worth exploring if
it is feasible to engineer the GFP encoding miniplasmid to prevent spurious GFP expression. This
could involve altering the sequences upstream of the GFP gene to exclude potential transcriptional
start sites or implementing a mechanism to inhibit GFP expression from the miniplasmid. In its
current state, when evaluating future PASTE and lentiPASTE experiments, ddPCR should be the
go-to method for quantifying PASTE editing efficiency, as it is utilized in the original paper.

Both the eVLP system, as well as the antibody fragment directed tropism seem to be obvious future
improvements to implement in the lentiPASTE system once it is proven to functional. However the
question still remains if the system will even be able to function. A second article released during
this project ”Engineered virus-like particles for transient delivery of prime editor ribonucleoprotein
complexes in vivo” [50] shows that eVLPs can package a functional prime editing system. The
Gag-Prime editor fusion protein in this article has a size of 309 kDa, showing that it is possible
to package large proteins similar to the PASTE editor in eVLPs. Furthermore, the use of eVLPs
compared to lentiviral vectors may be beneficial for the lentiPASTE system, as the removal of the
genome and capsid in the vectors may create more space for other proteins such as the PASTE editor
or gRNA binding proteins. Switching to an eVLP system should be relatively straightforward, as the
packaging and envelope plasmids needed can be ordered online. However, it is essential to note that
the gRNA encoding sequences would need to be expressed from the Gag-PASTE encoding plasmid,
as the transfer plasmid encoding the lentiviral genome is not utilized for eVLP production.
In the case of the PASTE editor being too big to package into either lentiviral vectors or eVLPs, an
alternative would be to develop a Gag-BxbI integrase fusion essentially splitting the PASTE editor
system into several parts. The Gag-BxbI protein could be packaged into the same eVLP or lentiviral
vector along with a prime editor, and would thus allow for PASTE editing.

6. Conclusion
This project aimed to create a lentiviral vector (coined lentiPASTE) that would enable transient viral
PASTE editing, to achieve editing of specific cells in heterogeneous cell populations. Initially, the
base PASTE system was tested in HEK cells to verify functionality, before moving on to the creation
of the lentiPASTE system. Clonings were conducted to create a new transfer plasmid and a plasmid
encoding a Gag-PASTE editor fusion protein. The plasmids were used to produce viral vectors,
which were used in transduction experiments to verify lentiPASTE functionality. Here it was found
out that GFP could not be used as a reliable marker for PASTE editing efficiency, due to spurious
gene expression from the minicircle plasmids. SDS-PAGE and western blotting was conducted on
lysed viral lentiPASTE vectors to verify the presence of the PASTE editor. Towards the end of this
project, sequencing results showed that the Gag-PASTE plasmid had not been correctly assembled,
and all lentiPASTE related results were invalidated. New cloning attempts have been started for the
missing plasmids, to create a starting platform for the eventual continuation of this project. Once
confirmed, these plasmids can be used to verify lentiPASTE functionality. If successful, future
aspects would include testing in organoid systems and possibly moving the platform to engineered
virus-like particles for PASTE delivery.
