Abstract
This project aims to explore the ionic conductivity within twisted 2D heterostructures.
Specifically, percolation paths and their corresponding energy barriers are calculated
across a range of twist angles, spanning from 0◦to 60◦, for bilayer graphene, bilayer
hexagonal boron nitride, and bilayer transition metal dichalcogenides comprising molyb-
denum, and various configurations of selenium and sulfur.
Utilizing a workflow, we computed the percolation paths within these structures. In our
approach, potential energy calculations are employed as a surrogate for the Nudge Elastic
Band (NEB) method, allowing for efficient estimation of energy barriers along the migration
paths. To assess the accuracy of the computed barriers, comparisons are made with
Climbing Image Nudge Elastic Band calculations for selected structures. Our findings
reveal that the accuracy of percolation path determination is highest for graphene, while
challenges arise in accurately determining percolation paths for hBN.
Furthermore, qualitative analyses shed light on how the atomic arrangements influence
the electronic charge density and migration paths of lithium ions within graphene struc-
tures. Despite extensive investigations into potential correlations between atomic struc-
ture and ionic conductivity for graphene, our findings do not provide conclusive explana-
tions for the observed ionic conductivity. In this analysis, we employed the radial distribu-
tion function to examine the atomic structure along the migration path.

Introduction
Technological improvements in rechargeable solid-state batteries have been driven by
the demand for portable electronic devices. litium-ion batteries (LIBs) are favored for
their high energy density, lightweight design, and extended lifespan compared to other
battery technologies. However, the ever-increasing demand for higher energy density,
faster charging rates, and prolonged cycle life necessitates the exploration of novel mate-
rials and designs. Among the critical components of LIBs, the anode plays a pivotal role
in determining overall performance.
LIBs consist of an anode (negative electrode), cathode (positive electrode), electrolyte,
separator, and two current collectors (positive and negative) as illustrated in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Schematic of a LIB
The anode is typically made of graphite, while the cathode is typically made of lithium
metal oxide. When the battery is fully charged, lithium (Li) ions are intercalated between
the layers of graphite material in the anode. During discharge, Li ions travel through the
electrolyte from the anode to the cathode. The separator blocks the flow of electrons,
directing them through an external circuit to the cathode, providing power to the device
(such as a cellphone or computer). When charging the battery, the opposite flow of Li ions

occurs, with them moving from the cathode back to the anode [1, 2].
In recent years, two-dimensional (2D) materials have emerged as promising candidates
for anode materials due to their unique electronic, mechanical, and structural properties.
These materials offer potential advantages over traditional graphite anodes, including
higher surface area and tunable properties [3, 4].
One critical aspect influencing the efficiency of anode materials in LIBs is the ionic conduc-
tivity, which dictates the rate of Li-ion diffusion within the electrode. Higher ionic conduc-
tivity correlates with faster charging and discharging rates, ultimately leading to enhanced
battery performance. Understanding and optimizing the ionic conductivity of 2D materials
hold significant promise for advancing the state-of-the-art in battery technology [5].
This master thesis project aims to investigate the ionic conductivity in twisted bilayer (TB)
configurations of graphene, hexagonal boron nitrite (hBN), and transition metal dichalco-
genides (TMDs) with molybdenum (Mo), sulfur (S), and selenium (Se). Twisting these
2D materials introduces moiré patterns altering their physical and mechanical properties.
Through this research, we aim to deepen the understanding of ionic conductivity in these
twisted bilayer 2D materials, potentially paving the way for the development of more effi-
cient anode materials for batteries

2.1
2D-heterostructures
2D materials represent crystalline solids composed of a single layer of atoms, exhibiting
remarkable mechanical, electronic and optical properties otherwise absent in their bulk
counterparts. Additionally, the combination of 2D materials in vertical stacks, referred
to as heterostructures, introduces a wealth of property opportunities. Among the exten-
sively studied 2D materials are the semiconductors graphene and TMDs, along with the
insulator hBN. Both graphene and hBN consist of a single layer of atoms arranged in a
2-dimensional hexagonal lattice [6].
Figure 2.1: Side view of two distinct hBN materials: BB and BN structure. Blue atoms
represents N and pink atoms represents B.
When stacking 2D materials of the same or different crystals on top of each other, the
resulting material exhibits the potential for novel properties. In the same layer, atoms are
bound together by strong covalent bonds, while atoms in different layers are connected

by weak van der Waals (vdW) bonds. Van der Waals forces, characterized by distance-
dependent interactions between atoms or molecules, arise from various electromagnetic
interactions. The inherent weakness of vdW forces can lead to stacking faults, manifesting
as different sliding or twisted angles between layers, giving rise to moiré patterns [7, 8].
In this project we examine two distinct hBN material displayed in Figure 2.1, one where B
and N atoms are situated on top atoms of the same kind before a twist angle is introduced
into the structure. We will call this material BB. The second is where B and N atoms
are situated on top of atoms of another kind before a twist angle is introduced into the
structure. We will call this material BN.
TMDs are atomically thin materials with the chemical formula MX2, where M represents
a transition metal atom (e.g., Mo, W, Nb), sandwiched between two chalcogen atoms (X),
such as S, Se, and Te. Another category of 2D materials includes Janus TMDs (JTMDs)
with the chemical formula MXY , where Y is a distinct chalcogen atom, signifying the
transition metal is situated between two different chalcogen atoms [9]. In this project, the
TMDs investigated are MoS2-MoS2, MoS2-MoSe2 and MoSe2-MoSe2 and the JTMD are
MoSeS-MoSSe, MoSSe-MoSeS and MoSSe-MoSSe. The different stacking orders can
be seen in figure 2.2. Throughout the project we will refer to both kinds as TMDs.

2.2
Twisted bilayer heterostructures and moire patterns
Moiré patterns in heterostructures present a captivating optical phenomenon that emerges
when two or more overlapping crystalline layers are twisted or exhibit lattice mismatch,
resulting in interference patterns. TB heterostructures’ moiré patterns encompass various
stacking orders, including AA, AB, and two distinct saddle points (SP), namely SPhomo and
SPhetero, illustrated in Figure 2.4. In AA stacking, all atoms in the top layer align directly
with their counterparts in the bottom layer. AB stacking involves half of the atoms in the top
layer directly aligning with atoms in the bottom layer, while the other half lie in the middle
of the bottom layer’s hexagonal arrangement. SPhomo occurs during the transition from
AA to AA stacking and from AB to AB stacking, whereas SPhetero arises in the transition
between AA and AB stacking.
Figure 2.4: Top view visualization of AA, AB, SPhomo and SPhetero stacking order of 2D-
hexagonal bilayer structures.

==PAGE:12==
Distinct stacking orders exhibit varying interlayer energy, resulting in a complex energy
landscape. For bilayer graphene (BG), AA stacking has the highest interlayer energy,
while AB/BA stacking has the lowest, resulting in AA stacking having a higher free energy
than AB stacking. This difference in interlayer energy contributes to out-of-plane deforma-
tion that follows the formation of AA, AB, and SP regions. In the ground state AB/BA con-
figuration, the atom displacement is minimal. Conversely, atoms in the AA configuration
exhibits maximum out-of-plane displacement. The maximum and minimum out-of-plane
displacement are angle-dependent, with the angle dependency symmetrically centered
around a 30◦twist angle for BG. The fraction and width of the various stacking orders also
depend on the angle [7, 10].
Twisted 2D heterostructures have gained significant interest in the scientific field due to
the ability to alter their physical and chemical properties. Moiré patterns can impact elec-
tronic, optical, and mechanical properties, as well as interlayer coupling, defects, impu-
rities, and more. Additionally, variations in the twist angle influences the stability of the
structure. Understanding these interactions becomes crucial for predicting the stability of
these heterostructures under different environmental conditions [11, 12].
Ionic conductivity is a materials ability to conduct electric current through the movement
of ions within it in response to an applied electric field [13]. The varying interlayer spaces
within the twisted structure offer opportunities for controlling the intercalation of foreign
atoms or ions, thereby impacting the overall ionic conductivity of a material
2.3
Lithium intercalation in twisted bilayer heterostructures
In previous studies, a consistent trend has indicated that lithium atoms exhibit a pro-
found preference for intercalating within AA-stacked BG. This phenomenon extends to
TBs, where the lithium atoms tend to intercalate in the AA stacking regions while actively
avoiding the AB regions. This behavior is caused by difference in energy landscape. As
mentioned in Section 2.2, the AA stacking order has the highest interlayer distance, re-
sulting in the lowest electronic charge density, creating an energetically favorable site for
the lithium ions to intercalate. In contrast, AB has the lowest interlayer distance, resulting
in the highest electronic charge density. This behavior has led to the expectation that
any surplus lithium ions will navigate preferential ”highway” paths formed within the SP
regions. Previous studies have also found intercalated ions to induce structural changes
to the bilayer such as increasing the average interlayer distance. This increase is largest
in the AB regions. Additionally, the intercalation binding energy is dependant on the local
environment. [14]
2.4
Computing ionic movements within bilayer structures
This project utilizes a computational workflow to determine the optimal migration path and
charge density barriers for intercalated Li ions within both twisted and non-twisted 2D het-
erostructures. The charge density barrier represents the change in electronic charge den-
sity during this transition. Traditionally, this migration path is computed using the Nudge
Elastic Band (NEB) method to estimate energy barriers. The energy barriers represents
the energy needed to transition a lithium ion from one minima to another via a transi-
tion state. NEB techniques involve multiple iterations of Density Functional Theory (DFT)
simulations to compute energy and forces, necessitating optimization of several internal
images between fixed endpoints over numerous cycles to identify an appropriate saddle
point. However, conventional DFT-based NEB calculations are not suitable for large-scale
screenings or when dealing with larger unit cells.
6

==PAGE:13==
In contrast, the workflow utilized in this project employs NEB procedures on the static
electron charge density between each pair of charge density minima points of relaxed
structures to ascertain the transition state of the static structure. Additionally, the differ-
ence in potential energy of a lithium ion intercalated at the minima versus at the transition
state can serve as a surrogate NEB energy barrier [15]. Therefore, in this project, we also
calculate potential energy barriers on each percolation path segments computed by the
workflow.
To validate the percolation paths and corresponding charge density barriers found by the
workflow, as well as potential energy barriers, we conduct Climbing Image NEB (CI-NEB)
calculations for various twist angles using the percolation path segments provided by the
workflow. Here, we anticipate the CI-NEB energy barriers to scale linearly with the charge
density and potential energy barriers. The climbing image is a slight modification of the
NEB method where the highest energy image is driven up to the saddle point. Unlike
the other images, this image does not experience the spring force. Instead, by reversing
the true force on this image along the tangent, the image aims at maximize its energy
along the band and minimize it in all other directions. Consequently, upon convergence,
it settles at the exact saddle point [16, 17].

Computational Methods
3.1
Creation of twisted bilayer graphene and hexagonal
boron nitrite
The TBG and twisted bilayer hBN (TBhBN) structures were generated using the Python
module flatgraphene. When creating structures with this module, various parameters
can be specified. In our case, we used a hexagonal cell type with 2 layers, a nearest
neighbor distance of 1.5Å, an interlayer separation of 3.5Å, and a vacuum layer height
of 3Å above and below the outer layers. These specific parameters were not critical
as the structures will undergo relaxation before analysis. The twist angle is not directly
specified as an input parameter. Instead, one must provide the corresponding q and
p values for the desired twist angle, as explained in ”Electronic structure of turbostratic
graphene” by Shallcross [18]. The p and q values can be found using a function. The twist
angles are selected based on the criterion of minimizing the number of atoms within that
range of angles. Additionally, an upper limit of 900 atoms per unit cell is imposed. The
TBhBN structures were derived from the TBG structures by replacing the carbon atoms
with alternating boron and nitrogen atoms.
3.2
Creation of twisted TMD structures
The twisted structures of MoS2-MoS2, MoSe2-MoSe2, MoS2-MoSe2, MoSeS-MoSSe, MoSSe-
MoSeS, and MoSSe-MoSSe with AA stacking are constructed using the twister package
workflow developed by Nalik et al. This workflow is tailored to fabricate moiré superlattices
of any 2D homo- or hetero-bilayer materials.
The initial step of the workflow involves determining the commensurate twist angle using
get_angle.py, with the input file get_ang.inp. This input file allows the user to specify
parameters such as the lattice vectors of the top and bottom layers. Here set to 3.231 Å,
which is the mid-value of the lattice vectors of MoS2 and MoSe2. Since the structures will
undergo relaxation, the lattice vectors will change during this process. Additional param-
eters specified is the strain, which is applied to both layers with a value of 1.0. The angle
ranges are chosen, with initial and final mismatch thresholds set to 0.3 Å and 0.00001 Å,
respectively. The initial mismatch threshold specifies the maximum tolerated mismatch
for the twist angles provided, while the final threshold is utilized with the DeepSearch

function, automatically refining the input range to search for narrower angle ranges until
the mismatch is below the specified final threshold. The output is a twist.inp file con-
straining the solution of angle and lattice parameters. Commensurate twist angles are

Subsequently, the twister.py script generates the atomic coordinates within the moiré

The script tovasp.py is then used to convert the DAT file into a VAPS file [19].
3.3
Workflow for determining percolation paths
Within this study, a computational workflow is employed to identify percolation paths in
twisted heterostructures. The workflow encompasses three main components: interpola-
tion, lithium diffusion, and percolation.
3.3.1
Interpolation
The interpolation step performed by the script interpolation_3d.py involves several
stages, including grid search, interpolation, and optimization aimed at identifying the min-
ima points of the charge density within the given atomic structure. Here the input file is
a CUBE file from which the atomic structure, cell and charge density is loaded. These
quantities are used to create an evenly spaced 3D grid of the charge density within the
two layers, from which the coarse minima points are found.
Using multiprocessing, the script refines the position of each coarse minima points, uti-
lizing a RegularGridInterpolator function. This interpolator employs cubic polynomial
fitting between neighboring data points in a wrapped 3D grid, allowing for the estimation
of charge density at arbitrary points within the grid. The wrapped 3D grid mimics periodic
boundary conditions and is created expanding the grid by one half in the x-y direction
by wrapping the values around. Additionally, the code checks the uniqueness of each
minima point by testing if any other minima points are located within a given threshold,
returning an NPY output file containing the refined charge density minima points.
3.3.2
Lithium diffusion
The second phase, performed by the script Li_diff.py, focuses on computing the migra-
tion graph between all the minima. This is achieved by running NEB calculations on the
static charge density between each pair of minima. The input files for this script are the
CUBE file containing the atomic structure and charge density, and the NPY file containing
the coordinates of the minima points.
The script iterates over all pairs of minima, sorted by the longest Euclidean distance, and
applies NEB to the static charge density, effectively finding the migration path between
each pair. In this context, the static charge density serves as a surrogate for the potential
energy surface. NEB constructs a direct path between the minima points, initializing with
64 pivot points along the path, each connected by an infinite spring force constant k. A
tri-linear interpolation between grid points determines the gradient of each pivot point, es-
sential for NEB calculations on the potential. To identify the lowest energy path between
the two minima points, the pivot points are redistributed using torch.optim.SGD (Stochas-
tic Gradient Descent) along the path after each iteration, following the gradient towards
lower charge density. The script runs with 1500 iterations operating with a learning rate
of 0.5 and a momentum term of 0.1 [15].

The NEB path is then divided into segment (two minima connected by a transition state)
and correlated to known minima points in the charge density. This is done by smooth-
ing the charge density values along the NEB path using Savitzky-Golay filters [20] to
determine coordinates of peaks. These peaks are connected to known minima using
minimum image convention (MIC) distance, which accounts for periodic boundary condi-
tions. These identified connected minima are then skipped in the loop, resulting in faster
calculations.
Additional NEB calculations are run on each segment to calculate the forward and reverse
charge density barriers, transition state position, and any crossing of unit cell boundaries.
These pieces of information are stored in the JSON output file in addition to a plot showing
the connected minima points in a 2D charge density map, sliced in the middle index of
the minima points, alongside a connectivity graph showing the NEB energy between the
connected minima.
3.3.3
Percolation
The final stage of the workflow is carried out by the percolation.py script, dedicated to
identifying the percolation path within the charge density of a specified structure. Input
files include the CUBE file for extracting the atomic structure, the NPY file containing the
minima points, and the JSON file generated by the Li_diff.py script.
The script iterates over all minima points, determining the smallest cost path from one
minimum to all others using a modified Dijkstra algorithm. These modifications involves
searching for periodic images of the start node instead of requiring a specified endpoint.
The algorithm only seeks the periodic starting node in neighboring unit cells. If a starting
node can only be reached by moving into the second nearest unit cells, it is not found
by the algorithm. The offset, a coordinate indicating the location of the minimum in the
periodic images, is used to identify equivalent minimum points along the path within the
unit cell, thereby only illustrating the migration path within the unit cell. After identifying
percolation paths, each path undergoes filtration to remove duplicate paths.
The output of this script is a JSON file containing the maximum barrier along the path, the
path indices, as well as the positions of minimum points and transition states for the three
lowest cost paths.
3.4
Density functional theory calculations
All density-functional theory (DFT) calculations were carried out using DFT-D3 in conjunc-
tion with a DFT calculator. DFT-D3 provides dispersion-corrected energies, forces, and
stresses, enhancing van der Waals interactions in the calculations [21]. GPAW, a DFT
Python code based on the projector-augmented wave (PAW) method, incorporates the
Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) functional [22] to work as the DFT calculator. The atomic
simulation environment (ASE) is utilized for structure reading.
3.4.1
Climbing image nudge elastic band barriers
In order to compute the CI-NEB of a given structure, a script was developed to determine
the relaxed structure for a Li atom intercalated at each minimum point along the percola-
tion path. The relaxation of each minimum point must be conducted in separate folders.
Subsequently, these relaxed structures were utilized to compute the energy barrier for
the lithium atom’s movement from one minimum point to the next through the transition
state. The initial image was set as the relaxed starting minima point, and the final image
as the relaxed end minima point. In these calculations, only a single internal image was
employed, representing the atomic structure with a Li atom appended to the transition

state. This single internal image sufficed, as the transition state position offers a reliable
indication of the saddle point’s location in the workflow. The CI-NEB algorithm was ap-
plied to these images, and the script generated a TRAJ file containing information such

3.4.2
Potential energy barriers
As a surrogate NEB barrier we computed the potential energy barrier for Li to transition be-
tween minimum points through the transition state for all structures. The potential energy
was calculated for a lI atom intercalated in the minimum point, the associated transition
state, and the subsequent minimum point, respectively. The potential energy barrier was
determined as the largest potential energy difference between the transition state and the
two minimum points.

final transition image. If the difference in atomic positions exceeds one-third of the bond
length, the CI-NEB energy barrier and the corresponding charge density and potential
energy barrier are filtered out, as the CI-NEB calculation is deemed invalid, rendering the

From the CI-NEB calculations, the forward barriers can be extracted. Thus, to ensure
a correct comparison, the script plots these barriers against the forward charge density
barriers and forward potential energy barriers. The forward barrier represents the activa-
tion energy required to move the lithium atoms from the preceding minimum point to the
transition point.
The script generates plots of the CI-NEB energy barriers against the charge density barri-

plots are produced with CI-NEB energy barriers and the potential energy barriers. The
color bar indicates the twist angle of data points.

Further work
Further development of this project involves optimizing the workflow to enhance its perfor-
mance across different platforms. Currently, the workflow is primarily tailored for graphene,
and some parameters may not be fully optimized for other materials such as hBN and
TMDs.
This observation was evident in the results, where graphene exhibited better
results compared to hBN and TMDs when comparing the outcomes obtained from the
workflow with those from CI-NEB calculations. Once optimized, the workflow can be ap-
plied to analyze more materials, providing a broader understanding of potential causes
and correlations. Comparative studies across multiple materials enable the identification
of similarities and differences, offering insights into factors influencing ionic conductivity.
Additionally, investigating various material properties may shed light on the differences
observed in ionic conductivity among these materials.
In the NEB calculations, we observed movements of atoms and layers, particularly notice-
able in smaller unit cells. However, conducting calculations for large unit cells demands
significant computational resources. As an alternative approach, constraining the layers
in the x-y direction could be considered to prevent changes in twist angles during optimiza-
tions. This strategy will help reduce computational demands while still capturing essential
structural dynamics. Moreover, we observed that the excessive movements were not ex-
clusively confined to smaller unit cells. Hence, it would be intriguing to explore additional
material properties such as absorption energy and bulk modulus to investigate how or if
these properties change with the twist angle, and determining whether they are correlated
with the magnitude of movements observed during CI-NEB calculations.
The alternating arrangement of B and N atoms in hBN could potentially influence its ionic
conductivity. Our analysis revealed differences in charge density and accuracy in com-
puted barriers between the two distinct materials, TB BB and BN. To delve deeper into this
phenomenon, future investigations could explore how the distinct stacking arrangements
in these materials affect their material properties such as electronic properties, strength
and flexibility. Additionally, a more thorough analysis of these material properties may
shed light on the challenges encountered when calculating percolation paths for hBN.
As discussed in Section 2.1, TMDs possess distinct spatial geometries compared to graphene
and hBN. Thus, exploring potential correlations between the atomic structures of these

materials and the differences in barrier height could be valuable. It would be intriguing to
investigate how the selection and arrangement of chalcogenides influence the movement
of Li ion. Calculations of the radial distribution function could serve as a suitable method

In the examination of radial distribution in Section 4.3.1, we discovered a correlation be-

working at lower computational cost. As twist angles requires larger unit cells, it could

Conclusion
In this project, we have constructed twisted and non-twisted structures of graphene, hBN,
and TMDs. Our primary focus has been on analyzing the percolation path and energy
barriers within these structures to investigate their ionic conductivity. Comparing the com-
puted CI-NEB barriers with those obtained from the workflow and DFT-D3, we observed
that for graphene and TMDs, the workflow effectively identified the percolation path, with
potential energy calculations acting as a surrogate NEB. The relative mean error (RME)
for graphene was found to be 30.33% when comparing potential energy barriers to CI-
NEB energy barriers, while for TMDs, it ranged between 36.73% and 55.09%. However,
for hBN, significant inconsistencies were observed among the barriers computed with dif-
ferent methods, rendering the analysis of the percolation path invalid.
We explored various potential correlations with ionic conductivity in TBG but found no
significant relationship between the maximum energy barrier and factors such as aver-
age interlayer distance, atomic density around the transition point, or distance traveled in
the maximum barrier segment. Additionally, we employed the radial distribution function
to investigate potential correlations with atomic structure for all barriers within structures
and maximum barriers across structures. While we observed a correlation between bar-
rier height and distance to surrounding atoms within structures, this correlation did not
hold across structures. No correlation was found between barrier height and average or
maximum distance between atoms.
Qualitative analysis revealed how atomic arrangements affect the charge density and the
migration path of Li ions. However, we were unable to identify a correlation with ionic
conductivity or develop a quantitative understanding thereof. Further research is needed
to explore additional factors and mechanisms underlying ionic conductivity in these mate-
rials.
