Chapter 1
Introduction
In the last 10 years gas sensors based on a broad variety of techniques have
been developed for sensing a wide range of gasses. Many of these sensors are
cheap and easy to fabrication due to the advances in silicon fabricate technology.
The technology now allows for fabrication of large arrays of sensors with ease
at nanoscale and with an exceptional high yield. Futhermore have a demand
for small, inexpensive sensors which has a high sensitivity of sensing certain
Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) or chemical compounds emerged. VOCs
are dened as organic compounds that have a vapour pressure of 10 Pa or
more at 20 oC. Organic compounds are dened as a compound that contains
at least one carbon atom and one or more hydrogen, halogens, oxygen, sulphur,
phosphorous, silicon or nitrogen atoms. Some VOCs and chemical compounds
can be dangerous to humans and a further danger is that concentrations of
VOCs and chemicals can be up to 10 times higher indoor than outdoor[1].
This thesis will work with one detection method, namely gravimetric gas sensing.
The sensor, this thesis works with, is developed from the existing Capacitive
Micromachined Ultrasound Transducer technology, for detection of ammonia,
NH3, that is a chemical compound.
In the following chapter a description of the Capacitive Micromachined Ul-
trasound Transducer will be given. This will be followed by a description of
biological and gas sensors and a further elaboration of gravimetric gas sensors.

1.1
The Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasound
Transducer
The Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasound Transducer (CMUT) consists of two
electrodes with a distance between them where one electrode is xed and the
other is clamped at the edges. In between are a number of insulating layers and
a vacuum gap. A sketch of the cross section of a CMUT can be seen in Figure
1.1.
The vacuum gap creates a cavity within the CMUT. The clamped plate, called
the top plate, is bendable and can therefore bend towards the xed electrode,
which is called the bottom electrode. The insulating layers are typically made
of thermally grown silicon oxide, SiO2, and silicon nitride, Si3N4, and function
as layers that prevents the electrodes to come in electrical contact.
If an DC voltage is applied, VDC, between the two electrodes an electrostatic
force is induced that attracts the two electrodes towards each other. This at-
tractive force will make the top plate deect towards the bottom electrode. It
will bend until the electrostatic attractive force is balanced with a mechanical
==PAGE:11==
1.2 The Gas Sensor
3
force induced by the stiness of the plate, that pulls it back to the undeected
position. If the applied voltage exceeds a certain value the attractive force will
overcome the mechanical force and the top plate will snap into pull-in position.
This is when the plate is touching the insulating layers in the bottom of the
cavity. The voltage where the pull-in occurs is called the pull-in voltage, VPi.
If the voltage is lowered from VPi, another certain voltage is reached where the
plate jumps out from the pull-in position. This is called the pull-out voltage,
VPo, and is occurring at a lower voltage than the pull-in voltage, Vpi > Vpo.
This is later elaborated.
If a DC voltage is applied that is lower than the pull-in voltage, V < Vpi, and
an AC voltage is applied as well, then the top plate will begin to oscillate. At a
certain frequency of the oscillation the amplitude will be greater than of other
frequencies. This frequency is called the resonance frequency and is the basis of
measurements in the use of the CMUT as a gas sensor.
Described above is what is called a single CMUT cell. When fabricating a func-
tioning CMUT sensor multiple CMUT cells are produced on the same chip and
connected in parallel. The individual cells are then placed in certain congura-
tion e.g. in a square lattice or in closed packed conguration. The connection of
the cells will reduce the motional impedance and the thermal noise. Especially
the noise reduction is important in sensor applications since the noise level de-
termines the minimum level of detection. [3] Single CMUT sensors have been
fabricated and a signal is measured.

The Gas Sensor
The function of the CMUT fabricated in this project is as a gas sensor. The
gas sensor is fabricated to sense for example ammonia, NH3.
A gas sensor,
and biosensors, consists of a number of elements as seen in Figure 1.2. The

The sensitivity for the gravimetric sensor is a measure of the change in the
resonance frequency due to a change in mass on the sensor. This is then how
large a change in resonance frequency due to a change in mass of the analyte
on the sensor. Describing the sensitivity in this way means that the sensitivity
should be maximized. The sensitivity is typically normalized to the area of a
cell.
The limit of detection is the smallest change in mass detectable due to noise.
A noise level will always be present and small signals, due to small masses, can
be impossible to detect due to the noise. The limit of detection is determined
from the Allan deviation that is a statistical tool to describe noise.
The quality factor is a measure of how much energy that is kept stored in the
oscillating sensor. A high quality factor means that the sensor will oscillate for
longer time since the damping is then small.
The coupling coecient is a quantity that describes how much of the electrical
energy used to actuate the resonating plate. In other words it is how much
of the electrical energy that is transferred to mechanical energy. The paracitic
capacitance, that is the capacitance originating from other elements than the
resonating plate, decreases the coupling coecient. The LOCOS design reduces
the paracitic capacitance and the increases the coupling coecient.
The gures of merit will be elaborated further later in the thesis and used to
evaluate the performance of the nished devices.

The nal sensor method described is the CMUT which is a MEMS. The CMUT

The CMUT shows three advantages compared to earlier described sensor meth-
ods. First is the thin resonating plate backed by a vacuum cavity making only
the top side of the plate interact with the surroundings. This reduces the over-
all energy loss which results in a higher quality factor compared to cantilevers.
The second advantage is that CMUT sensors are made of as many as 10000
individual resonators connected in parallel. This induces a wide range of elec-
trical impedances to match to electronics and an enhanced reliability compared
to single resonator systems. Another advantage is that the fabrication process
is reliable and repeatable which results in a single resonance peak with a high
quality factor[5]. The fact that the cavity is sealed beneath the plate gives an
advantage in the process of functionalization. For functionalization of other sen-
sor types certain areas has to be protected e.g. the area between the resonating
structure and the electrodes. Since the cavity is sealed this is not an issue.
The better performance and the advantages of the fabrication makes the CMUT
an attractive device for mass sensing compared to the MEMS cantilevers and
NEMS structures and the piezoelectric based devices. [1]

1.5
Ammonia
In this section ammonia will be presented. Following is a description of ammonia
and reasons that it can be vital to detect.
Ammonia, NH3, is a colorless gas with a characteristic pungent smell. Although
it is common in nature it is both caustic and hazardous in concentrated form. In
==PAGE:19==
1.5 Ammonia
11
the U.S. Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act (42 U.S.C.
11002) ammonia is classied as extremely hazardous.
Today most ammonia in the atmosphere is emitted directly or indirectly by
human activity. There are three major sources of this ammonia emission. These
are nitrogen xation, called nitrication, that is used in agriculture as fertilizer.
Even though around 80 % of the atmosphere consist of nitrogen is gas phase
nitrogen not usable for plants. The nitrogen has to be in solid form that then can
be dissolved in water to be obtained by plants. However if too much ammonia
is added to the eld as fertilizer some will evaporate into the atmosphere. The
second source originates from ammonication. This process is decomposition
of organic nitrogen like manure from agriculture and wildlife and leaves. The
decomposition is done by bacteria and fungi. The decomposition will through
chemical processes release ammonium gas into the atmosphere. The third source
comes from combustion from both chemical plants or motorized vehicles. In 1980
the world wide emission of ammonia per year was estimated to 20-30 Tg[11].
Ammonia is easy to detect in high concentration since the human sense of smell
is very sensitive to ammonia. It is due to the characteristic odour of the ammonia
that is easily recognizable if one is passing through farmland. The concentrations
of ammonia in the farmland and especially in the stables can make a serious
heath threat to the farmer and the animals. Sensors placed at the farm to warn
the farmer when the concentrations are getting dangerously high is a possible
use of ammonia sensors. Another application of ammonia gas sensors is in the
automotive industry. The air conditioning systems in cars often takes air from
outside the car into the cockpit.
If the car is driving through an area with

1.6
Historical Review of CMUTs
The technology of using acoustic devices for underwater imaging goes back to
the rst world war and the use of ultrasound used for medical purposes is re-
ported back to the 1930s[4]. Back then and now piezoelectric materials were
primarily used. Further through the history of ultrasound imaging has technolo-
gies as piezoelectric crystals, ceramics, polymers and piezocomposite materials
been used but not capacitive ultrasound transducers even though the idea of the
concept is as old as the piezoelectric[4]. The reason for not utilizing capacitive
ultrasound transducers is that a eld strength of the order of 106
V
cm2 is nec-
essary which has been a challenge. However the advances in microfabrication
technology has made it possible to fabricate functioning CMUTs for ultrasound
imaging as it was reported by Khuri-Yakub and his group from Stanford Uni-
versity in 1994[12]. In this rst fabricated CMUT they made a timed etch of
silicon oxide beneath a silicon nitride layer using hydrouoric acid. To etch the
silicon oxide they made a lithography step to etch holes in the silicon nitride that
ends up being the plate. The next step in the development of the CMUTs came
in 1996 where Eccardt et. al[13] from Siemens and Ladabaum et. al.[14] from
Khuri-Yakubs group at Stanford University simultaneously presented a CMUT
with a vacuum-sealed cavity beneath a silicon nitride plate. In the following
years the development of the vacuum-sealed cavity continued through the work
of Jin et. al.[15] of the Khuri-Yakub group and theoretical work has been done
by Caronti et. al.[16].
The development lead to a brand new way of fabricating the vacuum gap. In
2003 it was done by the wafer bonding technique by Huang et. al.[17]. Here
the cavity is fabricated on one wafer and the transducer plate is dened on a

Silicon-On-Insulator (SOI) wafer. These two wafers are then bonded together
in a vacuum to make the vacuum cavities. Using this method they achieved
better control of the uniformity and the gap height by eliminating several thin
lm steps.[17]
The next leap in the development was in 2011 by Park et. al.[18]. They used the
wafer bonding technique, earlier done by Huang et. al., and the LOCal Oxidized
Silicon (LOCOS) fabrication technique to dene the cavities. They utilized the
partially diusion blocking property of silicon oxide to dene a bump in the

a diusion inhibiting layer to perform a second oxidation around the bump the
create the cavity. After the formation of the cavity with the bump in they used
the wafer bonding technique to bond a SOI wafer to the substrate. Using this
technique they achieved precise gap height control which eliminated the need
for high DC voltage.[18]
Today CMUTs are used for ultrasonic imaging and in the developing eld of
CMUT based gravimetric gas sensors.

The LOCal Oxidaion of Silicon (LOCOS) process is an essential process in the
fabrication of the CMUTs in this project. The LOCOS process was originally
used as a process for making an isolation area in Complementary Metal Ox-
ide Semiconductor (CMOS)-technology. Today the process is used for several
purposes, for example in the CMUT fabrication. [21]

The basic idea of the LOCOS process is that certain areas of the wafer is cov-
ered with a diusion-inhibiting layer during a following oxidation process the
uncovered areas will be oxidized an the covered will not. The most common
diusion-inhibition layer is made of silicon nitride, Si3N4, since this has a low
diusion of oxygen trough it and the oxidation of the silicon nitride layer is slow
it self [19].
The deposition of silicon nitride is done using a Low Pressure Chemical Deposi-
tion (LPCVD) furnace. This process introduces a very high tensile stress in the
nitride layer which further introduces a high compressive stress in the underly-
ing wafer. The solution to this feature is to grow a small oxide before depositing
the nitride. The oxide is called the pad oxide and has a built in compressive
strength which will balance out some of the tensile stress in the nitride.
In Figure 3.2a a picture of the result of the LOCOS process is shown. In the
picture is a area called to Birds beak due to its resemblance to a actual birds
beak. This area is a result of the oxidation also happens to some extend under
the edges of the nitride layer. Due to the eect of the oxidation where the oxide
occupies more space vertically than the original silicon the nitride layer will
bend upwards.

Prior to the thermal oxidation of the wafers a cleaning process has to be per-
formed. The reason for this cleaning is to avoid contamination of the wafers and
the furnaces. Furthermore the quality and performance of the grown oxide will
be increased. The quality is essential in step 7 since this oxide will, together
with the silicon nitride layer, act as an insulating layer. If this layer breaks down
due to the applied voltage the CMUT is destroyed. Furthermore is it mandatory
due to cleanroom rules to maintain a clean as possible furnace.
The cleaning process performed is a RCA clean. The RCA clean consists of two
main solutions and a diluted hydrogenouride, HF, submerging steps. The rst
solution, which is called the RCA-1, consists of a mixture of water, Ammonium
hydroxide, NH4OH, and Hydrogen peroxide, H2O2, mixed at the ratio 5:1:1.
The purpose of the RCA-1 is to remove light organic molecules, particles and
metals. The solutions function by slowly dissolving the thin native oxide on
the wafer and then grow a new oxide by oxidation. This continuous process
of removing and growing oxide dislocates and removes particles from the wafer
surface.
The second solution, RCA-2, consists of water, hydrochloric acid, HCL, and
hydrogen peroxide, H2O2, mixed at the ratio 5:1:1. The RCA-2 solution is used
to remove alkalis, metal hydroxides and some residual metals and particles. The
alkalis and some cations forms insoluble hydroxides in the RCA-1 that settles
on the surface of the wafer. The RCA-2 solution is then used to form soluble
complexes of these hydroxides that are then removed from the wafer surface.
After each of the RCA dips the wafers should by submerged in a diluted HF bath.
The HF removes the oxide that is grown in the two RCA baths. Following the
RCA-1, RCA-2 and the HF submerging wafer cleaning is done. The full process
of the RCA cleaning is as follows:

After the holes has been etched in the plate and the post oxide for the back
contact, a layer of metal has to be deposited. This is done in the machine called
Alcatel. This is an electron beam evaporation system which is also called a
physical vapor deposition machine where a focused beam of electrons is shot
onto a source of metal, which in this project is aluminium. The electron beam
will make aluminium atoms evaporate from the source and the atoms will then
travel to the surface of the wafer. A layer of 100 nm aluminium is deposited
onto the wafer. The wafer now has to be baked on a hot plate. The wafers
has to be heated to around 110 oC for a couple of minutes. This improves the
strength of the aluminium to stick to the silicon nitride.

After the lithography is done the metal layer can be patterned. This is done in

is done using a magnet stirrer and a stirring paddle. When the two phases are
mixed the wafer can be immersed into the solution. The solution can be heated
to 50 oC but to decrease rate this was not done.
The etching of the aluminium creates hydrogen bubbles on the surface of the
aluminium. The bubbles will stop the etching locally where the bubble is po-

of hydrogen the wafers have to be lifted out of the solution every three to ve

The etch rate is dicult to predict since the wafer is lifted out of

Bias Sweep
The next measurement performed is a bias sweep. The bias sweep is essentially
the same as the impedance spectrum measurements as described but in the bias
sweep the biased voltage is stepwise increased (or decreased) to measure the
impedance at several bias voltages.
The voltage was typically swept from 0
V to 50 V in steps of 1 V. An example of four of these spectra with dierent
applied voltages to a chip can be seen in Figure 5.3. Here the phase angle as
a function of frequency for applied voltages of 19 V, 24 V, 26 V and 39 V are
shown.
For the 19 V (blue curve) in Figure 5.3 a resonance peak around 18 MHz is
visible. The peak is actually split into two with a small peak around 15 MHz.
As the biased voltage is increased to 24 V (red curve) the peak increases in
amplitude and shifts to a lower frequency, here around 14 MHz. As the biased
voltage is increased even further (26 V, yellow curve) the peak decreases to lower
frequency but now the peak is broadened and of lower magnitude. Furthermore
a new 'peak' has risen around 40 MHz. As the bias voltage is increased further to
39 V (purple curve) the resonance peak has disappeared at the lower frequency
but the new peak has increased in amplitude and split into two peaks and has
increased its position to a higher frequency.
A number of phenomena are happening as the bias voltage is increased. First it
is seen that the amplitude of the resonance peak is increasing. The reason for the
increase in phase angle amplitude is due to an increased coupling. As the bias
voltage is increased the plate gets pulled further and further towards the bottom
electrode.
This induces that the coupling from the electrical domain to the
mechanical domain becomes increasing. A description of the coupling coecient
measurements and a graph showing the coupling coecient as a function of time
is shown later.
The second phenomenon is that the resonance frequency is decreasing in fre-
quency as the bias voltage is increased. This eect is due to spring softening
which is happening as the plate is attracted to the bottom electrode. From this
the eective spring of the plate is stretched and by that the eective spring con-
stant is lowered. Looking at Equation 2.54 it is seen that for a lowered eective
spring constant the resonance frequency is lowered as well.
Next phenomenon seen is that the resonance peak gets lower and broader and
that a new peak appears at a higher frequency. The reason for these eects is
that the CMUT is in pull-in, in other words the pull-in voltage, Vpi is reached.
In this situation it is not the full plate that is oscillating anymore. As part of
the plate now is touching the bottom on the cavity, this part is not oscillating.

The pull-in voltage is another quantity that is determined for the dierent grids.

the bottom electrode. As described earlier there is a certain voltage where the
plate has been attracted all the way down to the insulating layers, namely
the pull-in voltage, Vpi. The pull-in voltage can be determined using dierent
methods. One method is to observe as the resonance peak drops in magnitude,
as described above in the Bias sweep section. Another method is to consider the
capacitance. As the vacuum gap, gvac is decreased, as the voltage is increased,
the capacitance will increase as described in Equation 2.5. This will happen
until the pull-in situation where the capacitance will drop to a constant value,
for the parallel plate model, given by the thickness and permittivity of the
insulating layers. The pull-in voltage can then be determined by determining
the bias voltage where the capacitance drops.

in Figure 5.5. Here it can be seen that the capacitance is increasing as the bias
voltage is increased until 27 V where the capacitance drops to just below 3 pF.
The pull in voltage for this grid is then 27 V.

Sensitivity
One of the gures of merit for the gravimetric gas sensor is the sensitivity of
the sensor.
The denition of the sensitivity is described in Section 2.6 and
the denition of the relative Sensitivity that is given by Equation 2.61 will be
elaborated in this section.
The method for determining the sensitivity of the sensors fabricated in this
project was to rst locate the resonance peak in the impedance spectrum as
described in Section 5.1. After this the sensor was sputtered with gold for a
short time adding mass on top of the plates. With the gold coated chip the
resonance frequency was determined. The chip was then sputtered a second
time to add another layer of gold on top of the cells. With the new thickness of
the gold layer the resonance frequency was determined.
It was intended that the grids made in the bottom of the wafers, the ones
shown in zoom-in 5 in Figure 4.1, was supposed to be used for the sensitivity
measurement. The reason for this was that the spacing between the grids were
designed larger than for the normal chips in the center of the wafer. The reason
for this was that prior to sputtering gold blue-tape was sticked on all other places
that the grid it self. This was done to ensure that only the area where the grid
were was sputtered with gold to ensure that the chip was not short circuited or
electrically connected to other grids or the top electrode. But as the edge of
the wafer was incomplete the grids intended for sensitivity measurements were
not usable and a normal chip was used instead. This introduced a challenge in
placing the blue-tape properly. But due to steady hands and patience it was
done perfectly.
The sputtering system used to deposit a layer of gold was a Cressington 208HR
sputter coater. The chip was in both depositions sputtered for 5 seconds with
a sputter current of 40 mA and a pressure of 0.01 mbar. The system is working
automatically and the only operation to do is to start the machine when the
vacuum is reached. When the sputtering was performed on the chip two other
wafers was deposited with gold as well, using the same parameters as for the
chip.

Conclusion
The main aim of this project was to successfully fabricate a double LOCOS
CMUT sensor with a Silicon nitride plate. The fabricated CMUT sensor should
then be characterized to determine the gures of merit for gravimetric sensors.
The sensor should be the best gravimetric CMUT based sensor fabricated with
respect to sensitivity.
Through this thesis the principle of operation of the CMUT is presented as well
as how a gravimetric sensor is functioning. A presentation of other methods
of gravimetric sensing is described where the dierent methods are compared.
The sensor fabricated in this project could be used as a ammonia sensor and
the chemical compound ammonia is then described. A historical review of the
CMUT as an ultrasound transducer and later as a gravimetric sensor is con-
ducted and the state of art of gravimetric CMUT sensors are presented.
The theoretical behaviour of the CMUT is described in the thesis, rst in the
model of a parallel plate capacitor. Here the pull-in and pull-out voltages are
described and derived. The descriptive model is expanded to include the deec-
tion behaviour of the plate which is described by the Isotropic plate equation.
For this model the pull-in voltage is derived and the capacitance due to de-
ection is compared to the parallel plate model. As the plate in this project
is fabricated in silicon nitride, which experiences residual stress, the model is
further expanded to include this residual stress. The theory chapter continues

In the third chapter important fabrication processes for the fabrication process
