Abstract 
Molten salt reactors have re-emerged as promising candidates for next-generation nuclear 
reactors due to their inherent safety features and high-temperature operation. However, the 
use of molten fluoride salts such as LiF-NaF-KF (FLiNaK) as coolants and fuel carriers presents 
significant corrosion challenges for structural alloys. This thesis investigates the corrosion 
behaviour of wrought and Laser Powder Bed Fusion (LPBF) processed 316L stainless steel in 
molten FLiNaK at 700˚C, with a focus on the role of microstructural differences and their 
influence on corrosion progression during static exposure. A combination of characterization 
techniques including light optical microscopy, laser scanning confocal microscopy, scanning 
electron microscopy, energy dispersive spectroscopy, electron backscatter diffraction, X-ray 
diffraction, and X-ray computed tomography was employed to evaluate corrosion morphology 
and surface degradation. Both material variants exhibited intergranular corrosion initiated by 
selective leaching of chromium and iron. In wrought samples, corrosion propagated along grain 
boundaries, leading to surface grain detachment and continuous material loss. In contrast, 
LPBF-processed samples showed signs of slowed corrosion progression, potentially due to 
their distinct grain structure, which may inhibit grain detachment and promote accumulation 
of corrosion products on the sample surface. Volumetric CT analysis revealed increased 
subsurface void formation in LPBF-processed samples following long-term FLiNaK exposure, 
likely originating from a combination of Kirkendall-type voids and 1D wormhole corrosion. The 
unique LPBF microstructure appears to influence the relative contribution of these 
mechanisms. This study highlights the critical role of microstructure in governing corrosion 
behaviour in molten salt environments, and the results underscore the importance of continued 
research into how different manufacturing methods and microstructures affect corrosion in 
environments relevant to nuclear energy.

1. Introduction  
In recent years, Molten Salt Reactors (MSRs) have re-emerged as a promising technology for 
next-generation nuclear energy systems.  MSRs use liquid fluoride or chloride salts as both 
coolant and fuel carrier and can operate at higher temperatures and lower pressures than 
conventional nuclear power plants. Although originally developed and tested at Oak Ridge 
National Laboratory (ORNL) in the 1960s (Serp et al., 2014), MSRs were largely forgotten for 
decades. Renewed interest, driven by Kirk Sørensen’s publications of ORNL research findings, 
has reignited global efforts to explore and realize the potential of the MSR reactor concept. This 
revival has inspired the establishment of companies like Saltfoss Energy and Copenhagen 
Atomics in Denmark, both of which are focused on developing modular MSR systems for mass 
production (Tracey, 2024). 
Since the MSR technology never reached commercial deployment, many unknowns must be 
addressed to ensure functional and safe designs. One of the most pressing concerns is the 
corrosion behaviour of structural materials when exposed to molten fluoride salts such as LiF-
NaF-KF (FLiNaK), which is one of the salts proposed for modern MSRs. Current research, 
together with the original ORNL experiments, shows that nickel-based alloys such as Hastelloy 
and Inconel exhibit superior corrosion resistance in FLiNaK environments compared to 
conventional stainless steels (SS) like 316 SS (Guo et al., 2018). However, widespread adoption 
of these alloys is limited by cost, as Inconel and Hastelloy can be up to 20 times more expensive 
per kilogram than 316 SS (MachineMFG, 2025). Additionally, some studies suggest that nickel-
rich alloys may be more susceptible to degradation under the irradiation conditions typical for 
MSRs (Guo et al., 2018). 
Given the high cost and potential irradiation issues associated with nickel-based alloys, there is 
growing interest in re-examining the potential of the more affordable option 316L SS. From a 
materials science perspective, 316L SS might seem like an unconventional choice. In 
environments like air or aqueous solutions, its corrosion resistance relies on the formation of 
a stable chromium oxide (Cr2O3) surface layer. However, the aggressive fluoride ions in molten 
FLiNaK salts prevent the formation of this protective layer. Instead, chromium (Cr) forms 
soluble fluorides that offer little or no corrosion protection (Guo et al 2018).  
To unlock the full potential of MSR technology, Copenhagen Atomics is adopting a "product-
first" approach. This strategy focuses on designing reactor systems that are not only 
technologically advanced but also cheap and scalable for mass production. They are 
approaching this by shifting away from traditional designs that are built to endure harsh 
conditions for many decades. Instead, they are exploring modular reactor units with a shorter 
operational lifespan of 4–10 years (Tracey 2024). These units are designed to be replaced 
periodically, after which they undergo decontamination, and, whenever feasible, refurbishment 
for continued use. This approach could allow for a more flexible and cost-effective reactor 
design while maintaining high safety and efficiency.

Under this model, 316 SS may emerge as the most suitable structural material, offering an 
optimal balance between cost, irradiation resistance, and corrosion durability over the 
intended shorter operational periods (Schulenberg 2022; Zinkle 2019). 
Achieving a design that can be introduced to the market requires balancing technical innovation 
with manufacturing feasibility. One potential avenue for innovation is the use of additive 
manufacturing (AM), which offers advantages such as design flexibility, material efficiency, and 
the potential for reducing production costs. Specifically, Laser Powder Bed Fusion (LPBF) is an 
increasingly popular AM method capable of printing for example 316L SS, a low-carbon variant 
of 316 SS. Recognizing these opportunities, researchers at ORNL are already exploring the 
development of a fully 3D-printed micro light water reactor (LWR), aiming to leverage AM to 
streamline production, reduce lead times, and enhance design adaptability for next-generation 
nuclear systems (Betzler et al., 2022). 
However, the role of LPBF in MSR development remains unexplored. To the best of the authors’ 
knowledge, no studies have investigated the corrosion mechanisms of LPBF 316L SS in molten 
FLiNaK salt. Understanding how LPBF components perform compared to their wrought 
counterparts is critical for evaluating whether this manufacturing method could contribute to 
the realization of safer, more efficient, and cost-effective nuclear energy systems. Given the 
fundamental differences in grain structure, residual stresses, and phase distributions, a 
comparative assessment is necessary to determine whether LPBF-processing affects material 
degradation in molten FLiNaK environments. Thus, this study focuses on how microstructural 
differences between wrought and LPBF-processed 316L SS influence corrosion behaviour. 
Samples will be exposed to molten FLiNaK at 700˚C, replicating realistic temperature 
conditions. This study is limited to coolant salt without active radioactive fuel components, 
meaning that potential effects of fission products and radiation on corrosion behaviour are not 
considered. Also, while a more complete simulation of real-life conditions would involve 
exposing samples to flowing FLiNaK, as thermal gradients and flow dynamics are known to 
influence corrosion behaviour, flow studies are beyond the scope of this study. To evaluate the 
progression of corrosion, a combination of conventional and advanced characterization 
techniques will be employed. Vickers hardness testing will be used to assess changes in 
mechanical properties resulting from exposure to molten FLiNaK. Surface roughness will be 
characterized using laser scanning confocal microscopy (LSCM), while light optical microscopy 
(LOM), scanning electron microscopy (SEM) with energy-dispersive spectroscopy (SEM-EDS), 
and electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) will be used to examine microstructure, surface 
degradation, and elemental distribution. X-ray diffraction (XRD) will be applied to investigate 
crystallographic changes and detect the formation of corrosion products. Lastly, to complement 
these established methods, X-ray computed tomography (X-ray CT) will be employed to enable 
a three-dimensional assessment of material degradation. Parts of the work presented in this 
study have served as the basis for a paper submitted to NOLAMP20 (DTU, 2025). The paper has 
passed the initial review stage and is included in Appendix A.

2. Theoretical Background 
2.1. Molten Salt Reactor Technology 
MSRs are one of the six reactor concepts considered under the Generation IV International 
Forum (GIF). The goal of this forum is to improve safety, sustainability, and efficiency in nuclear 
energy production (LeBlanc, 2010). Unlike conventional solid-fuel reactors, MSRs offer 
advantages such as better thermal efficiency and inherent safety features.  
The concept of MSRs dates back to the 1950s with the Aircraft Reactor Experiment (ARE) and 
the Molten Salt Reactor Experiment (MSRE) at ORNL (Rosenthal et al., 1970). The MSRE was 
on-going from 1965 to 1969 and demonstrated the feasibility of using molten salt as both fuel 
carrier and coolant. An example of an MSR system using both coolant and fuel salt is shown in 
Figure 2.1. In recent years, there has been renewed interest in MSRs due to rising green energy 
demands globally, with projects such as the Molten Salt Fast Reactor (MSFR) in Europe, China’s 
Thorium Molten Salt Reactor (TMSR) program, and the TerraPower Molten Chloride Fast 
Reactor (MCFR) in the US (Serp et al., 2014).  
 
Figure 2.1. Schematic showing the parts of a standard MSR system (DOENE, 2002). 
In most MSR designs, fissile materials such as U-233, U-235, and Pu-239 are dissolved in a 
molten salt mixture. Molten salts suitable for MSRs are either chloride- or fluoride-based 
(LeBlanc, 2010). MSRs typically operate at temperatures ranging from 600–900 °C, significantly 
higher than those of conventional LWRs (Serp et al., 2014).

This enables a higher thermodynamic efficiency and opens opportunities for direct heat 
applications, such as hydrogen production and industrial process heat. 
Some of the most compelling aspects of MSRs, however, are their inherent safety mechanisms: 
• Low-pressure operation: Unlike pressurized water reactors, MSRs operate at near-
atmospheric pressure. This reduces the risk of steam explosion accidents significantly 
(LeBlanc, 2010). 
• Negative temperature coefficient: As temperatures rise, the thermal expansion of the 
molten salt reduces fuel density. This passively decreases the fission rate (LeBlanc, 
2010). 
• Freeze plug technology: If the system overheats, a freeze plug at the bottom of the 
reactor would melt, thereby allowing the molten fuel to drain into cooling tanks 
(LeBlanc, 2010). 
Another advantage of MSRs is the use of liquid fuel, which offers better control over the fuel 
source compared to conventional solid-fueled reactors. In solid fuel systems, gases produced 
during fission accumulate inside the fuel rods, interfering with reactor performance and 
requiring replacement of fuel rods, every 12–24 months. In contrast, MSRs allow for continuous 
circulation and in-line processing of the liquid fuel (World Nuclear Association, 2024).  
Electrochemical separation techniques proposed for MSRs enhance fuel management by 
allowing selective removal of unwanted fission products, including noble gases, actinides, and 
long-lived radioactive isotopes (Guo et al., 2018). This not only improves reactor efficiency but 
also significantly reduces the long-term radiotoxicity of nuclear waste. Copenhagen Atomics 
claims that through continuous recycling and processing, the required storage time for nuclear 
waste can be reduced from over 100,000 years to approximately 300 years (Copenhagen 
Atomics, 2025).

2.2. 316 Stainless Steel

2.2.3. Microstructure of Wrought 316L Stainless Steel 
The properties of 316L SS, especially its microstructure, corrosion resistance, and mechanical 
performance, are influenced not only by chemical composition but also by the chosen 
manufacturing method. Traditional manufacturing techniques, such as rolling combined with 
heat treatments, result in a relatively homogeneous grain structure with equiaxed austenitic 
grains. The grain size is primarily influenced by the degree of deformation and the parameters 
of subsequent annealing treatments (Dolzhenko, 2022). 
High-angle grain boundaries (HAGBs), defined by misorientation angles greater than 15˚, are 
prevalent throughout wrought 316L SS. These boundaries are of interest because of their 
higher energy states compared to low-angle grain boundaries (LAGBs), which means that they 
can act as preferential diffusion paths and are often associated with increased susceptibility to 
corrosion. Another characteristic feature of 316L SS is the occurrence of annealing twins, which 
form during recrystallization and grain growth. These boundaries have the potential to disrupt 
the connectivity of HAGBs and thereby they may improve corrosion resistance (Dolzhenko, 
2022). The microstructure of 316L SS is highly dependent on the manufacturing route, and AM 
techniques such as LPBF produce fundamentally different grain structures and defect 
distributions. These features will be the focus of the following chapter. 
 
2.3. Laser Powder Bed Fusion: Microstructure and Corrosion Behaviour 
LPBF is an AM process that enables near-net-shape production of complex geometries. The 
LPBF process involves the selective melting of metal powder layers using a high-energy laser 
beam. When the laser scans across the powder bed, it produces and drags a melt pool of 
material, similar to what happens in a welding process. The intense laser result in rapid 
solidification and the formation of unique microstructures. For example, Oliveira et al. (2020) 
discussed how key processing parameters, such as laser power, scanning speed, hatch distance 
and layer thickness influence porosity, residual stresses and final part density.  
While optimized LPBF parameters can result in nearly fully dense parts, they also introduce 
significant residual stresses and microstructural heterogeneities that must be considered in 
relation to corrosion behaviour.  
2.3.1 Defects in LPBF-Processed 316L Stainless Steel 
The LPBF process introduces defects that influence the microstructure evolution, mechanical 
properties, and corrosion behaviour of the produced part. Defects such as keyhole porosities 
and lack-of-fusion porosities can arise from variations in laser energy input. Gas porosities are 
another type of defect caused by entrapped gas in the melt pool. Surface roughness is also 
expected due to the layer-by-layer fabrication method of LPBF. All these defects can affect the 
corrosion behaviour of the final part (Vukkum and Gupta, 2022).

A defining feature of LPBF-processed 316L SS is its cellular dislocation network, which Wang 
et al. (2024) characterized as having a polygonal prism morphology. The individual cells, 
aligned along <001> directions, are shaped by a combination of liquid-solid transformation and 
thermal stress-induced deformation. The dislocation density is significantly higher at the cell 
walls, forming a bamboo fibre-like dislocation network, which improves the mechanical 
properties by impeding dislocation motion. Furthermore, side-branching can be observed in 
regions with altered thermal gradients, particularly at melt pool boundaries. This behaviour 
leads to variations in microstructural features at different locations within a single print (Wang 
et al., 2024). The grain- and cell structure of LPBF-processed 316L SS leads to distinct grain 
boundary characteristics. Compared to wrought 316L SS, which typically has a high fraction 
and interconnectivity of HAGBs, LPBF-processed 316L contains a considerable proportion of 
LAGBs due to the cells formed within the columnar grains (Luo et al., 2025). Although HAGBs 
are still present at grain interfaces, their connectivity may be reduced because of the elongated 
nature of the microstructure. 
2.3.3 Effect of LPBF Processing on Corrosion Behaviour  
The unique microstructural features of LPBF-processed 316L SS influence its corrosion 
behaviour, particularly due to surface roughness, microsegregation and residual stresses. 
Bedmar et al. (2022) found that as-built LPBF parts exhibit higher corrosion susceptibility at 
800˚C due to increased surface roughness, which promotes pitting and oxidation. In contrast, 
ground specimens showed improved corrosion resistance by reducing surface defects that act 
as initiation sites for corrosion while also forming a more uniform protective oxide layer.  
Microsegregation of alloying elements, such as Mo and Cr, is commonly observed in LPBF-
processed 316L SS. Wang et al. (2024) reported that segregation at cell walls can enhance 
passivation but may also lead to localized corrosion if elemental depletion occurs in adjacent 
regions. Similarly, Barode et al. (2025) found that post-processing at 800°C dissolves the 
cellular structure and precipitates σ-phase, which may also affect the corrosion behaviour. The 
steep thermal gradients inherent in LPBF also induce significant tensile residual stresses in 
certain directions, which can increase stress corrosion cracking (SCC) susceptibility. Oliveria et 
al. (2020) emphasized that these residual stresses require post-processing treatments to 
mitigate potential failure mechanisms.  
As outlined, LPBF-processed 316L SS may have the potential for improved corrosion resistance 
compared to conventional counterparts due to its refined microstructure and enhanced 
passivation kinetics. However, its corrosion performance is likely to be highly dependent on 
process parameters, surface finish and post-processing. The discussion so far has focused on 
corrosion behaviour in a normal air atmosphere, where passivation plays a key role in 
determining corrosion resistance. However, in high-temperature molten salt environments 
such as FLiNaK at 700 ˚C, the corrosion mechanisms may be fundamentally different, as 
discussed in the following chapter.

2.4. Corrosion of 316L Stainless Steel in Molten FLiNaK 
2.4.1 Thermochemical Properties of FLiNaK  
FLiNaK is a eutectic mixture of LiF (46,5 mol%), NaF (11,5 mol%), and KF (42 mol%). It has 
been studied for its application as a fuel carrier and coolant in MSRs due to its low melting point 
(454˚C) and high thermal stability. It has a low viscosity, moderate density, and high thermal 
conductivity, which makes it ideal for heat transfer in high-temperature nuclear and non-
nuclear systems. FLiNaK is often preferred over chloride-based salts (such as LiCl-KCl) for 
MSRs, as these salts have a worse chemical stability and a lower thermal conductivity than 
FLiNaK (Guo et al., 2018). 
2.4.2 Corrosion Mechanisms of 316L Stainless Steel in Molten FLiNaK 
In typical air or aqueous environments, stainless steels such as 316L resist corrosion by 
forming a passive chromium oxide (𝐶𝑟!𝑂") layer. However, in molten FLiNaK at high 
temperatures (such as 700˚C), this oxide layer becomes unstable when impurities such as 
oxygen and moisture raise the oxidizing potential of the salt, thereby promoting the dissolution 
of alloying elements. In these environments, Cr reacts with fluoride ions to form soluble 
chromium fluoride (mainly 𝐶𝑟𝐹"), which then dissolves into the molten salt (Fig. 2.4). This 
dissolution leads to continuous Cr depletion and material degradation (Doniger et al., 2023). 
The thermodynamic principles governing these reactions are outlined in the following section.

Doniger et al. (2023) confirmed the thermodynamic predictions experimentally by measuring 
Cr concentrations in FLiNaK after 500 hours of exposure. For 316L SS, the Cr concentration 
increased from ~0 μg/g to 225 μg/g, whereas 316H SS increased to 130 μg/g. One proposed 
explanation for the lower Cr dissolution in 316H SS is sensitization, where Cr reacts with carbon 
to form chromium carbides (Cr₂₃C₆) at grain boundaries. In most environments, sensitization 
reduces corrosion resistance by depleting Cr from the matrix, preventing the formation of a 
protective Cr₂O₃ layer. However, in molten salts, where oxide passivation is irrelevant, it may 
slow Cr diffusion and thereby reduce uniform corrosion.A consequence of sensitization, 
however, is the increased susceptibility to intergranular corrosion (IGC). Cr-depleted zones 
that form around 𝐶𝑟!"𝐶/ precipitates can become anodic and act as initiation sites for localized 
attack. Hong et al. (2024) and Doniger et al. (2023) found that sensitized 316H SS exhibited 
deeper IGC than 316L due to these effects. This behaviour means that while sensitization may 
slow uniform Cr loss, it can also promote localized damage along grain boundaries. 
2.4.4 Microstructural and Compositional Evolution During Exposure  
Section 2.4.2 described how corrosion of 316L SS in FLiNaK is primarily driven by selective 
dissolution of the least stable alloying elements (Cr and Fe). This section focuses on how this 
process alters the alloy’s composition and microstructure over time. 
As Cr and Fe is depleted from the matrix, the near-surface regions of 316L SS become enriched 
in more stable elements like Ni and Mo. This enrichment can lead to the formation of a Ni-rich 
outer layer, which may slow further Cr and Fe loss but can also contribute to embrittlement if 
phase segregation occurs. Mo-rich precipitates may also form within the matrix, though their 
exact role in corrosion resistance remains unclear (Guo et al. 2018). Extended exposure to 
molten FLiNaK also results in surface roughening, and void formation, especially near grain 
boundaries due to diffusion of Cr. Doniger et al. (2023) and Hong et al. (2024) both observed 
subsurface voids. 
A recent study by Yang et al. (2022) highlights a corrosion mode termed “1D wormhole 
corrosion” as the mechanism leading to voids in materials exposed to molten FLiNaK, which is 
characterized by highly localized, penetrating void structures aligned along grain boundaries. 
These voids might appear discrete in cross-sections but are shown to be interconnected tunnels 
that facilitate ingress of molten salt into the structural material. The mechanism originates from 
aggressive Cr depletion and even grain boundary migration, which produces zones 
supersaturated with vacancies, thus leading to void nucleation.  
Another explanation for subsurface voids observed in FLiNaK-exposed alloys is the Kirkendall 
effect, which arises from unequal diffusion rates between alloying elements during selective 
leaching. As Cr diffuses out more rapidly than Ni and Mo can compensate, a flux imbalance 
arises, resulting in the accumulation of excess vacancies. These vacancies can coalesce into 
voids, particularly at grain boundaries or dislocation-rich zones (Kiosidou et al., 2023).

The evolution of microstructure during corrosion is strongly influenced by grain size and 
dislocation density due to the grain boundaries acting as diffusion pathways. Hong et al. (2024) 
studied cold-forged 316H SS and found that increased cold work, which refines grain size and 
raises dislocation density, affects corrosion behaviour. Specifically, finer grains redistributed 
Cr more uniformly throughout the matrix, which decreased the penetration depth of 
intergranular corrosion. However, this came at the cost of accelerated uniform corrosion. Thus, 
grain refinement may reduce localized attack while promoting more homogeneous and faster 
material loss.  
2.4.5 Influence of Impurities and Redox Conditions on Corrosion Behaviour 
Impurities affect the redox environment of FLiNaK, thereby directly impacting the corrosion 
behaviour of exposed structural alloys. Even trace amounts of oxygen and moisture can shift 
the redox balance of the salt toward more oxidizing conditions. This enhances the solubility and 
reactivity of otherwise stable alloying elements.  
When oxygen is introduced, it can form reactive oxyfluoride species, further promoting Cr 
dissolution from the alloy surface. Moisture impurities can react with fluoride ions to generate 
hydrogen fluoride (HF), which is a highly aggressive etchant that can oxidize Cr and Fe, and in 
certain conditions even Ni and molybdenum (Mo).  
Guo et al. (2018) showed that the presence of oxygen and moisture significantly increased Cr 
and Fe dissolution rates. Doniger et al. (2023) further showed that corrosion was accelerated 
in FLiNaK environments with higher redox potentials, which confirms that oxidizing impurities 
destabilize Cr and accelerate corrosion by allowing Cr to preferentially dissolve as 𝐶𝑟𝐹! and 
𝐶𝑟𝐹". The presence of fission products (uranium and plutonium) in nuclear applications can 
further increase the oxidizing potential, accelerating corrosion processes. This is, however, 
beyond the scope of our study. 
 
2.5. Corrosion Mitigation Strategies 
2.5.1 Alloy Design and Optimization 
As shown in Chapter 2.4, selecting the right alloy for MSRs is crucial due to the aggressive 
corrosion environment of fluoride salts. Early MSR development efforts used Inconel 600 as 
seen in the ARE (Bettis et al., 1957). However, subsequent studies revealed severe corrosion 
due to the selective dissolution of Cr, leading to the development of Hastelloy N, which 
demonstrated better corrosion resistance (Guo et al., 2018). Recent research has explored 
modifying Ni-based alloys with minor additions of titanium, niobium, and aluminium to 
improve corrosion resistance and mitigate tellurium-induced intergranular cracking (Zhu et al., 
2018).

Despite the historical preference for Ni-based alloys, 316L SS still seems to be of most interest 
today due to its commercial availability and compliance with ASME standards. But as 316L SS 
is more susceptible to Cr dissolution in FLiNaK due to its higher Cr content, this makes other 
mitigation strategies such as environmental control even more relevant (Guo et al., 2018).  
2.5.2 Salt Purification and Environmental Control 
The stability of molten FLiNaK depends on minimizing impurities and maintaining a controlled 
environment. Regarding salt purification, Guo et al. (2018) emphasized that oxide and 
hydroxide impurities in FLiNaK increase its corrosivity, leading to faster material degradation. 
To prevent this, they found HF/H2 sparging as one of the most effective purification methods, 
reducing oxide content and improving stability. Another method, carbochlorination, removes 
metallic impurities by converting them into volatile chlorides that can be eliminated from the 
system. Doniger et al. (2023) found that even trace amounts of oxygen and moisture introduced 
during salt handling can lead to unwanted oxidation reactions, increasing corrosion rates. For 
this reason, purified salt must be stored and handled under controlled conditions to prevent 
recontamination.  
Beyond salt purification, controlling the surrounding atmosphere plays an important role in 
limiting corrosion. Guo et al. (2018) recommended using an inert gas atmosphere, such as 
argon or helium. These gasses should be used both during salt purification and system 
operation to prevent oxidation. The gasses help reduce oxygen and moisture ingress, lowering 
the risk of Cr leaching and other types of degradation. It should also be noted that adding 
reductants such as zirconium or magnesium may limit oxidation, but this is secondary to proper 
salt purification and environmental control (Guo et al., 2018).  
2.5.3 Surface Coatings and Heat Treatments 
Despite not being the primary corrosion mitigation strategy, surface coatings might help 
improve corrosion resistance of structural materials such as LPBF 316L SS in molten FLiNaK. 
Zhu et al. (2018) investigated laser-cladded coatings on Hastelloy N and found that pure Ni and 
cobalt (Co) coatings significantly reduced corrosion rates. They found that pure metal coatings 
act as a barrier, slowing Cr depletion and reducing intergranular attack. Their study showed 
that Co coatings were slightly more effective than Ni coatings due to the higher thermodynamic 
stability of Co in fluoride salts. However, some diffusion of the base material into the coatings 
was observed, which could limit protection in the long-term.  
Heat treatments, such as annealing, can reduce residual stresses and affect grain structure in 
LPBF-processed 316L SS. This could potentially improve the corrosion resistance of the final 
part. High-temperature annealing can homogenize the distribution of Cr and Mo (Nadimpalli 
and Yu, 2024), which would likely reduce localized depletion zones and risk of SCC, but further 
research is needed to evaluate how annealing and resulting homogenization affects Cr 
depletion in molten salt environments.

2.6. Knowledge Gaps and Future Research Directions 
The following sections will identify some of the knowledge gaps within the field of corrosion of 
structural materials in molten FLiNaK. Some of the gaps will be addressed in this study, while 
others will be beyond the scope of this work. The gaps that will be addressed are highlighted in 
bold. 
2.6.1 No Prior Research on Corrosion of LPBF 316L SS in Molten FLiNaK 
As previously noted, there is, to the best of our knowledge, no published research investigating 
the corrosion behaviour of LPBF-processed 316L SS in molten FLiNaK salt. The lack of studies 
on this specific combination creates a gap in the understanding of the material variant’s 
suitability for high-temperature molten salt applications, particularly in nuclear energy 
systems. The differences in microstructure between conventionally manufactured and LPBF-
processed 316L SS may significantly influence the corrosion behaviour of the material.  
To address this knowledge gap, we have chosen to investigate and compare the 
corrosion behaviour of LPBF-processed 316L SS versus wrought 316L SS in molten 
FLiNaK. Understanding these differences is essential for developing guidelines for 
selecting structural materials and manufacturing methods for high-temperature molten 
salt applications. 
2.6.2 Limited 3D Investigation of Corrosion Progression of Structural Alloys in Molten Salts 
Three-dimensional imaging techniques, such as X-ray CT, have been successfully applied to 
study corrosion mechanisms in various environments, including reinforced concrete (Fang et 
al., 2019) and lead-bismuth eutectic systems (Huang et al., 2024). However, there is limited 
research on the application of 3D imaging techniques for studying corrosion in molten salt 
environments. The ability to non-destructively monitor corrosion progression using X-ray CT 
could provide valuable insights into the changes occurring in structural materials exposed to 
molten salts.  
We have chosen to apply X-ray CT in our work to complement traditional 
characterization techniques, providing a more in-depth understanding of corrosion 
progression in 316L SS during FLiNaK exposure. This approach enables volumetric 
reconstructions that reveal both external surface degradation and internal corrosion 
features. 
2.6.3 Influence of Post-Processing on Corrosion Resistance 
As mentioned earlier, pure metal laser-cladding coatings have been proven to improve the 
corrosion resistance of Hastelloy N in molten FLiNaK (Zhu et al., 2018). Post-processing 
techniques such as coatings, grinding, and annealing have also been widely used to enhance the 
corrosion resistance of LPBF-processed 316L SS (Vukkum and Gupta, 2022).

molten FLiNaK remains unexplored, and future work should focus on addressing this

worth noting that post-processing of certain geometries relevant for nuclear reactors, such as

3. Methods  
To characterize the corrosion behaviour of wrought and LPBF-processed 316L SS exposed to 
molten FLiNaK at 700˚C, a range of complementary techniques was used. These included 
hardness testing, microscopy, diffraction, spectroscopy, and tomography, enabling 
investigation of both surface and subsurface degradation. Each method was selected to analyse 
different aspects of the corrosion process. The following sections detail the principles and 
purpose of each technique.  
 
3.1. Vickers Hardness Test

measured. The Vickers hardness number, or VHN (normally expressed as HV), is calculated

3.2. Light Optical Microscopy  
LOM was used to evaluate surface conditions and to characterize the extent and morphology of 
corrosion attacks in the samples. LOM provides an accessible and versatile method for 
characterizing corrosion morphology, grain structure, and surface features. Proper sample 
preparation was carried out prior to imaging, as this is crucial for obtaining meaningful results 
(see Chapter 4.4). 
All imaging was done using a Zeiss Axio Vert A1 optical microscope equipped with Zen2Core 
software. Bright-field (BF) imaging was the primary mode used to assess surface degradation 
and evaluate cross-sectional corrosion features. In addition, Differential Interference Contrast 
(DIC) imaging was employed to enhance grain boundary visibility within the samples. DIC 
works by exploiting differences in optical path length, highlighting surface features by creating 
a pseudo-3D effect. Lastly, polarized light imaging was used, which involves the use of a 
polarizing filter. This enables the identification of certain corrosion products, such as iron 
oxides, due to characteristic coloration. 
 
3.3. Laser Scanning Confocal Microscopy  
LSCM is a high-resolution imaging technique used to analyse surface topography and 
microstructural features with enhanced depth resolution. In this study, LSCM was used to 
examine the topographical features and depth variations in samples after FLiNaK exposure (a 
LEXT OLS5000 system was employed). Unlike conventional optical microscopy, LSCM employs 
a laser as a light source. The laser scans across the sample using two moving mirrors, and 
reflected light is collected through a pinhole, ensuring that only in-focus signals contribute to 
the final image (Goudelis, 2022). This process enables the construction of high-resolution 3D 
surface maps, which provide data on surface roughness. 
 
3.4. X-ray Diffraction 
XRD works by measuring the diffraction of X-rays. By measuring the angles and intensities of 
the diffracted X-rays illuminating the sample, it is possible to determine crystalline phases 
present in materials (Britannica, 2025). In relation to the investigation of corrosion in FLiNaK, 
XRD can be used to track which corrosion products are formed when the alloying elements of 
316L SS react with the fluoride rich environment. We conducted our measurements using a 
Bruker D8 diffractometer and processed the resulting data using the EVA software. The 
measurements were performed with a step size of 0.02° and a step time of 1 second and each 
scan took approximately 60 minutes.

In EVA, we utilized phase databases to match observed diffraction peaks with those 
characteristic of known crystallographic phases. Minor deviations between expected and 
observed peak positions were anticipated due to the presence of defects and variations in 
chemical composition and residual stresses, which can alter the d-spacing of the crystal lattice 
and thus affect peak position and shape. Imperfections in the crystal structure can lead to peak 
broadening and asymmetries (sometimes referred to as “shoulders”), while internal stresses 
may cause peak shifts (Balzar, 1993).  
 
3.5. Scanning Electron Microscopy  
SEM utilizes a focused electron beam that interacts with the target material, generating various 
signals that provide information about the sample’s topography, crystallography, and 
composition. In backscattered electron (BSE) imaging, contrast arises primarily from 
differences in atomic number, as heavier elements backscatter more electrons. These 
backscattered electrons are detected by a BSE detector, enabling compositional contrast 
imaging (Agrawal et al., 2024). 
Due to the short wavelength of electrons, SEM imaging offers significantly higher resolution 
than LOM, making it well-suited for mapping the morphology of metal samples at the nano- and 
microscale. In this project, a SUPRA SEM from Zeiss was employed to analyse cross-sections of 
our samples following exposure to molten FLiNaK salt. 
3.3.1 Energy Dispersive Spectroscopy 
SEM-EDS was utilized to enable quantitative elemental analysis of samples. The SUPRA SEM 
was used with an EDS sensor inserted manually. When high-energy electrons interact with a 
material, they can eject inner-shell electrons from the atoms. The vacancy left behind by the 
electron will be filled by a higher-energy electron from a shell further from the nucleus of the 
atom. As the electron transitions to a lower energy state, the excess energy is released in the 
form of an X-ray photon. EDS works by detecting the characteristic wavelengths of these X-rays, 
allowing for detection of specific elements (Agrawal et al., 2024). 
3.3.2 Electron Backscatter Diffraction 
EBSD is a SEM-based technique which is used to analyse the crystallographic structure of 
materials. To employ the technique, the investigated sample is tilted, typically ~70˚, relative to 
the electron beam. Backscattered electrons then interact with the crystal lattice, generating 
diffraction patterns (Agrawal et al., 2024). The resulting patterns can be processed to 
determine the crystallographic orientation of grains within the material. In this study, the 
method was utilized to locate HAGBs within both wrought and LPBF-processed samples.

3.6. X-ray Computed Tomography  
X-ray CT is a non-destructive imaging technique that utilizes an X-ray source to generate three-
dimensional representations of an object. By acquiring multiple X-ray projections at incremental 
angular steps, CT reconstructs a volumetric dataset, allowing for detailed mapping of porosity, 
and internal defects (Marie and Withers, 2014). This technique is interesting for studying AM 
components, such as those produced via LPBF, where porosity and microstructural 
heterogeneities can significantly impact mechanical properties and corrosion resistance. 
In this study, CT was employed (using a Zeiss Xradia 520 Versa) to analyse 1×1mm LPBF-
processed matchstick samples. The small 1×1mm cross-section was chosen to ensure sufficient 
X-ray penetration allowing for reconstructions with a smaller effective voxel size. This made a 
relatively detailed volumetric reconstruction possible which included the detection of voids 
down to 2,6µm in diameter. The unexposed LPBF matchstick was scanned and compared with 
the LPBF matchstick that had been exposed to FLiNaK for 500 hours. The approach enables 
tracking of microstructural degradation, on the surface as well as internally, over time, 
providing critical insights into how molten salt environments affect the material.

4.3 Experimental Setup ③ 
4.3.1 Furnace Set-up 
For exposure tests, an inert atmosphere was desired around the furnace to prevent moisture 
and oxygen from entering the system. As no inert atmosphere furnace was available at KU, an 
enclosed environment was constructed using a 134L PP storage box (Figure 4.8a) around a 
Nabertherm LE 2/11 muffle furnace. Nitrogen was chosen as the inert atmosphere as it was 
available in large quantities at the site.  
Four holes were melted into the box using a soldering iron: two for gas flow, one for the gas 
inlet line, and one for a gas bubbler used to indicate overpressure within the system (Fig. 4.8b). 
An additional hole was made in the back for the power line, while a hole in the front allowed 
the furnace to be turned on and off using a rod to minimize contamination. All openings were 
sealed with thermal grease and packaging tape to create an almost fully enclosed environment.  
    
 
Figure 4.8. a) The nitrogen environment setup and b) the installed gas bubbler. 
For every round of exposure tests, the system was re-sealed, and the furnace was turned on 
from the outside after making sure the system had been fully flushed with nitrogen. The exact 
nitrogen flow rate used during the experiment was not recorded, as the gas bubbler was relied 
on to verify whether the flow was sufficient after resealing.

the furnace and kept under a fume hood, where the surrounding glass containers were 
shattered, leaving behind solid pieces of FLiNaK. The solid FLiNaK pieces were then tightly 
wrapped in Parafilm and stored in a closed container until use. 
4.3.3 Molten FLiNaK Exposure Tests 
For the exposure tests, the 316L SS containers and sample trees described in Chapter 4.1.1 were

5. Results 
5.1 Vickers Hardness Test 
To evaluate whether the nitrogen atmosphere used during the exposure tests induced nitriding 
effects or annealing temperature effects in the 316L SS samples, Vickers hardness tests were 
conducted on both unexposed samples and samples exposed to FLiNaK for 500 hours. Both 
wrought and LPBF coupons were tested. Figure 5.1 presents the average hardness and spread 
for each condition. 
 
Figure 5.1. Scatter plot showing Vickers hardness test results, including spread and the average 
hardness for each sample. Arrows only indicate hardness changes. 
From the plot it is apparent that the unexposed LPBF sample has a higher hardness (239,5 HV) 
than its wrought counterpart (228 HV), which is expected due to its distinct microstructure. 
After 500 hours of FLiNaK exposure at 700 °C, the two material variants showed opposing 
trends: the wrought sample softened by ~15%, while the LPBF-processed sample experienced 
a ~10% increase in hardness.  
To investigate whether nitriding had occurred, hardness gradients were examined from the 
surface to the bulk of the samples (Fig. 5.2). If significant nitrogen diffusion had taken place, a 
higher hardness near the surface would typically be expected (Yang et al., 2016). However, as 
shown in Figure 5.2, no such gradient was observed after 500 hours of FLiNaK exposure, 
suggesting that if nitriding did occur, it may have affected the entire bulk, though this would 
generally be unexpected under the present conditions, as prior studies show that such nitrogen 
uptake require higher temperatures and active nitriding atmospheres (Valente et al., 2018). 
Still, the molten FLiNaK environment is complex, leaving room for uncertainty.

5.2.4 Surface recession 
Surface recession was quantified by comparing the relative cross-sectional areas of embedded 
matchstick samples before and after exposure to molten FLiNaK. LOM images were vectorized 
in Adobe Illustrator, and the cross-sectional areas were calculated using SolidWorks. All values 
are reported as a percentage of the original unexposed area, which was normalized to 100%, 
as shown in Figure 5.8. The results for the LPBF-processed samples carry an estimated 
uncertainty of ±2.5% based on the variation observed across different cross sections of an 
unexposed LPBF-processed matchstick.  
For the LPBF samples, the entire perimeter of the cross section was used to quantify surface 
area loss. In contrast, only the two as-received (uncut) sides were included for the wrought 
samples, as the two remaining sides had been manually cut and displayed inconsistent 
thicknesses. To estimate total surface recession in the wrought samples, it was assumed that 
material loss was uniform on all sides. The measured recession of the two as-received sides was 
doubled, effectively treating the initial cross section as a square with an area variance of ±2% 
as calculated in Table 4.2. The 24-hour wrought matchstick sample was excluded from the 
comparison, as it was not cut from a 1mm sheet. 
The LPBF-processed samples exhibit a pronounced nominal surface area loss of approximately

6. Analysis 
The purpose of this chapter is to interpret the experimental results presented in Chapter 5 to 
provide insight into the corrosion behaviour of both wrought and LPBF-processed 316L SS in 
molten FLiNaK. The discussion seeks to combine observations from multiple characterization 
techniques to explain the mechanisms driving corrosion, and to enable a comparative 
evaluation of the corrosion performance of both material variants.  
Observations that remain unresolved or beyond the scope of this study will be addressed in 
Chapter 7, where possible directions for future work are proposed.  
 
6.1 Selective Leaching of Cr and Fe Through HAGBs 
In molten fluoride salts such as FLiNaK, corrosion in 316L SS involves the preferential 
dissolution of less noble alloying elements. Recent studies by Maric et al. (2018) and Danon et 
al. (2020) suggest that microstructural features, particularly grain boundary character, can 
influence corrosion behaviour, and HAGBs have been proposed as possible pathways for 
accelerated diffusion. In the following, the electrochemical basis for selective leaching will be 
introduced, followed by an investigation of the microstructural factors that may influence this 
process. 
6.1.1 Electrochemical Basis for Selective Leaching 
Corrosion in molten FLiNaK is an electrochemical process controlled by redox potential 
differences between metallic alloying elements and oxidizing species present in the salt (Guo et 
al., 2018). The corrosion process requires the presence of oxidizing species, which act as sinks 
for electrons released during metal dissolution. Even small amounts of impurities, such as 
moisture, can be enough to initiate corrosion by reacting with fluorine ions in the molten salt:

The hydrogen fluoride (HF) formed by this reaction acts as an oxidant, accepting the electrons 
released by metal alloying elements, thus being reduced at the metal-salt interface:

Fe2+). This oxidation occurs spontaneously because the redox potentials of the Cr3+/Cr and

mixed manually in a fume hood. Also, the nitrogen environment around the furnace might not 
have been perfectly sealed, meaning that small amounts of impurities would be able to enter 
the system throughout the exposure tests.  
6.1.2 HAGBs as Diffusion Pathways  
The microstructure of the wrought 316L SS samples examined in this study is characterized by 
equiaxed grains with clearly defined HAGBs, as confirmed by the LOM images of the etched 
cross-section of an as-received wrought coupon (Fig. 4.5). These HAGBs act as diffusion 
pathways due to their high-energy interfaces, leading to aggressive intergranular attack, and 
eventually, grain detachment in the wrought samples exposed to FLiNaK at 700˚C. The 
depletion of Cr and Fe along HAGBs is supported by the EDS map presented in Figure 5.16a, 
which also shows that the depletion front extends roughly 15-25µm into the material after 500 
hours of exposure.  
In LPBF-processed 316L SS, a similar phenomenon is observed, as indicated by the EDS map in 
Figure 5.16b. Notably, Cr depletion appears more pronounced than that of Fe. This could 
suggest that the corrosion process is still in an early stage, where selective Cr leaching 
dominates. However, due to the unknown interplay between composition, microstructure, and 
salt chemistry, such interpretations should be made with caution. 
The morphology of the attacks appears more irregular and discontinuous than in the wrought 
counterpart, likely reflecting the influence of the distinct melt pool-induced microstructure. 
The EBSD IPF map of the exposed LPBF-processed sample supports this interpretation, 
showing degradation in salt-adjacent HAGBs. (Fig. 5.19). The non-uniform nature of the 
degradation, where some HAGBs are seemingly not being attacked, suggests that relative grain 
boundary misorientations and grain boundary character as well as local heterogeneities such 
as dislocation density, and residual stresses might play a significant role in determining which 
HAGBs are attacked.

This is supported by studies showing that not all HAGBs are equally susceptible to corrosion. 
For example, coincidence site lattice (CSL) boundaries such as S3 twins have been shown to 
exhibit superior resistance to Cr depletion, where random HAGBs with misorientations beyond 
CSL criteria are generally more vulnerable (Bettayeb et al., 2018). Consequently, a more 
detailed grain boundary classification is essential for accurately predicting corrosion pathways 
in both wrought and LPBF-processed 316L SS exposed to molten FLiNaK. 
 
6.2 Grain Detachment Sustains Corrosion  
Smaller grain sizes are generally believed to increase IGC susceptibility, as a higher density of 
boundaries enhances Cr diffusion. Grain detachment has also previously been proposed as a 
mechanism that accelerates material loss during FLiNaK corrosion (Doniger et al., 2023). 
However, the influence of overall grain morphology on the progression of IGC in FLiNaK 
remains underexplored. Our findings suggest that corrosion progression is not solely governed 
by the density of diffusion pathways.  
6.2.1 Different Corrosion Progressions 
As discussed, IGC via Cr and Fe depletion is the dominant corrosion mechanism in both wrought 
and LPBF-processed 316L SS exposed to molten FLiNaK. However, long-term corrosion 
progression differs significantly between the two. In the wrought samples, corrosion 
encapsulates the equiaxed grains, leading to complete grain detachment, thus exposing 
underlying material to FLiNaK, and accelerating corrosion (Fig. 6.1).

6.2.2 Reconciling Surface Recession Measurements 
While both LOM and CT results were utilized in an attempt to quantify surface recession for 
LPBF-processed samples, they report notably different estimates. CT analysis shows a total 
volumetric loss of only 2.7%, whereas comparison of cross-sectional areas obtained from LOM 
images indicates a 16% reduction in cross-sectional area going from the unexposed LPBF 
sample to the one exposed for 500 hours. These differences make it impossible to determine 
the actual extent of surface recession. CT provides a more reliable estimate of total material 
loss, as it captures the entire sample volume, and the CT results also align well with the EBDS 
results for the exposed sample (Fig. 5.19) which reveal that the finer contour layer is still 
present, indicating modest surface recession.  
LOM, however, offers complementary insight by revealing the initial rapid material removal 
after 24 hours of exposure, followed by a stagnation in total cross-sectional area all the way up 
to 500 hours of exposure. Combining CT and LOM results supports rapid initial material loss 
due to the removal of loosely bonded surface features followed by stabilization.  
For the wrought samples, surface recession was only quantified using LOM. However, since 
every sample was cut from the same sheet and the thickness variation is minimal, the calculated 
recession based on LOM cross-sections is considered relatively reliable. At present, no CT scans 
have been performed on the wrought samples. Such data would offer valuable volumetric 
insight that could complement existing LOM results and help validate the proposed mechanism 
of continuous grain detachment driven by IGC. 
 
6.3 Subsurface Voids Observed in Volumetric Reconstructions 
While LPBF-processed 316L SS seemingly resists aggressive surface recession, CT analysis of 
the LPBF matchstick exposed to FLiNaK for 500 hours reveal a dramatic increase in the number 
of fine subsurface porosities as compared to the unexposed sample (Fig. 5.13 & Fig. 5.14). 
Notably, the porosities exhibit a mixture of irregular and spherical shapes, suggesting the 
possibility of multiple contributing corrosion mechanisms. The underlying corrosion 
mechanisms responsible for the voids may be attributed to either Kirkendall-type porosity or 
the recently proposed mechanism termed 1D wormhole corrosion (Yang et al., 2022).  
6.3.1 The Kirkendall Effect and 1D Wormhole Corrosion 
The Kirkendall effect arises when unequal diffusion rates of alloying elements lead to vacancy 
supersaturation and thus void nucleation. This will typically occur along diffusion pathways 
such as HAGBs and will leave behind an Ni-enriched matrix (Fig. 6.3). This mechanism has been 
speculated to be an important contributing factor in molten salt corrosion of steels (Kiosidou 
et al., 2023). Importantly, FLiNaK ingress introduces a strong external sink for Cr and Fe in 
wrought 316L SS, which has been found to possibly limit the formation of Kirkendall-type voids 
in wrought metals (Yang et al., 2022).  
==PAGE:61==
Oliver J. Schollert & Mads Salbæk 
 
Master’s Thesis – DTU Construct

Figure 6.3. Illustration showing a) cross-section of grains exposed to molten FLiNaK, resulting in Cr 
depletion and the formation of Kirkendall-type voids and b) a 3D perspective of a grain showing how 
the voids nucleate and grow along HAGBs without FLiNaK ingress into the bulk. 
1D wormhole corrosion is driven by ingress of molten salt into HAGBs, where it facilitates 
dissolution of Cr. As Cr is leached into the salt, grain boundary regions become destabilized, and 
can migrate through diffusion-induced grain boundary migration (DIGM), resulting in the 
formation of narrow, tunnel-like voids that propagate along the grain boundaries and leave 
behind Ni-rich regions that are directly exposed to FLiNaK (Fig. 6.4) (Yang et al., 2022).

6.3.2 Competing Void Formation Mechanisms  
Though void formation in wrought 316L SS remains unexplored in this study, it can be 
speculated that the equiaxed grain structure and high degree of interconnection between 
HAGBs in the wrought material may promote more homogeneous salt ingress, enabling uniform 
activation of wormhole corrosion and leaving little opportunity for Kirkendall-type voids to 
develop due to continuous grain detachment.  
It is speculated that the elongated grain morphology typical of LPBF-processed 316L SS may 
influence the relative contribution of the two mechanisms. The interwoven and elongated 
grains might retard salt ingress along HAGBs, thus slowing the advance of wormhole corrosion. 
Such a delay may allow for more extensive solid-state diffusion of Cr and Fe, potentially leading 
to vacancy accumulation and localized Kirkendall-type void formation in subsurface zones not 
yet reached by FLiNaK.  
This is further supported by XRD analysis, which reveals increased intensity of metallic Ni 
peaks in LPBF-processed samples relative to the wrought counterparts (Fig. 5.11). This 
confirms selective dissolution of Cr and Fe, leaving behind a Ni-enriched matrix. This is 
consistent with Kirkendall-type degradation, where the bulk structure remains largely intact. 
In contrast, wormhole corrosion may involve more extensive material degradation along grain 
boundaries, which potentially limits the retention of Ni-enriched regions and thus contributes 
less to XRD signals.  
Distinguishing between these two mechanisms relies on whether salt ingress into voids has 
occurred. However, none of the characterization techniques employed in this study confidently 
confirm the presence or absence of salt within the porosities. Based on combined results from 
CT, EDS, and XRD, it is possible that both wormhole corrosion and Kirkendall-type voids 
contribute to the observed degradation of 316L SS, and that the relative impact of each 
mechanism is influenced by microstructural features.

7. Conclusion 
This study has provided a comparative analysis of the corrosion behaviour of wrought and 
LPBF-processed 316L SS in molten FLiNaK salt at 700˚C. This environment is highly relevant 
for the development of structural materials for MSRs, and the work represents, to the best of 
the authors’ knowledge, the first study investigating the corrosion behaviour of LPBF-
processed 316L SS in these conditions.  
Corrosion in both materials was found to proceed mainly by selective leaching of Cr and Fe 
along HAGBs. In wrought 316L SS, the equiaxed grain structure facilitated IGC and progressive 
grain detachment, resulting in continuous material loss. In contrast, the complex 
microstructure of LPBF-processed 316L SS appeared to inhibit grain detachment, instead 
promoting the accumulation of corrosion products on the sample surface. The initially rapid 
corrosion rate observed in LPBF-processed samples is likely due to the removal of partially 
melted powder particles. After this initial phase, the corrosion rate appeared to stabilize, which 
contrasts the continuous degradation observed in wrought 316L SS. This suggests that the LPBF 
microstructure may offer some protective effects, at least under static exposure conditions. 
XRD revealed the formation of oxide phases and Ni enrichment on the corroded surfaces, while 
SEM-EDS mapping confirmed extensive Cr and Fe depletion near the salt-metal interface. 
Volumetric X-ray CT reconstructions enabled quantification of surface recession and internal 
porosity, revealing increased subsurface void formation in LPBF-processed samples following 
500 hours of exposure to FLiNaK at 700˚C. These voids are proposed to result from a 
combination of Kirkendall-type porosity and 1D wormhole corrosion, with the distinct LPBF 
grain structure influencing their relative contribution.  
The findings in this study support the potential value of further investigation into LPBF-
processed 316L SS as a viable structural material in MSRs despite the observed degree of 
material degradation. Considering current industry trends, emphasizing manufacturability and 
modular reactor units with reduced lifespans, the presence of predictable corrosion may be 
tolerable and even expected. Ultimately, a deeper understanding of corrosion mechanisms and 
mitigation strategies is essential for enabling the safe and cost-effective deployment of MSRs as 
part of future energy systems.

8. Outlook 
8.1 Experimental Improvements 
Methodological limitations affect the depth of the present findings. In the following section, 
possible improvements to the experimental setup are identified with the goal of enabling more 
detailed analyses in future studies.  
8.1.1 Improvements of Experimental Setup  
The use of a muffle furnace with suboptimal sealing capabilities may have resulted in some 
contamination from the surrounding moisture-containing atmosphere, which can influence the 
corrosion behaviour of materials in molten fluoride salts. Switching to a sealed furnace with 
integrated inert gas handling would enable better environmental control. This would improve 
the validity of corrosion data, especially for the long-term exposure experiments.  
Also, the corrosion behaviour of materials exposed to FLiNaK is highly sensitive to impurities 
in the salt. In this study, FLiNaK was synthesized manually from constituent salts, which likely 
introduced some degree of contamination to the salt mix. Prior to raising the temperature to 
700˚C for the exposure tests conducted in this study, a drying step at 300˚C was implemented 
to reduce the moisture content of the FLiNaK salt. However, it is unlikely that this step removed 
all impurities. The use of pre-purified FLiNaK salts, handled in gloveboxes, would lead to more 
consistent test conditions, and thus improve the quality of corrosion data.  
8.1.2 Implementation of Flow Systems 
The setup used in this study utilizes static immersion of the 316L SS samples. In real MSR 
systems, however, the salt is in motion, which influences local chemical conditions and thus 
affects the overall corrosion behaviour of exposed materials (Guo et al., 2018). Employing a 
flow loop for circulation of the molten salt would enable more realistic simulations of MSR 
conditions, and thus enhance the relevance of the results. 
Because of present static corrosion experiments, the observed stabilization of corrosion in the 
LPBF-processed samples may be a result of the accumulation of corrosion products on the 
surface. The lack of FLiNaK flow limits the ability to determine whether this behaviour reflects 
an intrinsic material response or is merely a result of insufficient removal of corrosion products 
under static conditions. A flowing system would more closely replicate the continuous exposure 
to fresh salt characteristic of MSR environments, thus enabling a more detailed comparison of 
the corrosion behaviour of wrought and LPBF-processed 316L SS.

8.1.3 Improvements of Post-Exposure Analyses 
Due to the lack of a standardized post-exposure sample cleaning method in this study, reliable 
quantification of mass loss was not feasible. This led to the use of several alternative mass-loss 
estimation methods, with varying degrees of uncertainty. CT scanning showed a lot of potential, 
and this will be discussed further in Chapter 8.2. Future studies should define standardized 
protocols for salt removal that avoid etching or mechanical damage. This would enable kinetic 
studies and better quantification of corrosion rates which could supplement post-exposure 
microstructural observations.   
To further improve the understanding of corrosion kinetics, it is recommended that future 
studies integrate methods such as operando Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) 
into the exposure test system. This would make it possible to capture transient processes such 
as selective leaching in real time. Recent literature demonstrates that EIS can in fact be 
implemented at high temperatures in molten fluoride salts (Chan et al., 2024). 
Another limitation of the current study is the lack of post-exposure salt analysis due to lack of 
access to adequate equipment. Techniques such as magnetic-sector inductively coupled plasma 
mass spectroscopy (ICP-MS) can be deployed to quantify the concentration of dissolved species 
such as Cr and Fe in the salt (Doniger et al, 2023). Such data would provide valuable insight into 
the leaching behaviour of the studied material.  
Lastly, an increase in hardness was observed in the LPBF-processed 316L SS samples, in 
contrast to the softening exhibited by their wrought counterparts (Fig. 5.1). While the 
mechanism causing this hardening effect remains unclear within the scope of the present study, 
the findings highlight the need for better control of environmental variables and post-exposure 
handling to enable a systematic investigation of contributing factors. Understanding this 
phenomenon may be critical for assessing the mechanical performance of LPBF-processed 
components exposed to molten salt environments. 
 
8.2 Unlocking the Potential of CT Scanning  
8.2.1 CT Scanning of Wrought Specimens to Enable Comparative Analysis 
An important limitation of the present study was the restriction of CT characterization to only 
the LPBF-processed matchstick samples due to limited equipment availability. Inclusion of the 
wrought samples in the CT study would have significantly improved the comparative analysis 
by enabling evaluation of surface recession and internal void formation between the two 
material variants. In particular, CT analysis of the wrought matchsticks would have contributed 
to the discussion regarding the origin of the internal voids observed in the LPBF-processed 
sample. Such data would enable a more robust understanding of corrosion progression and 
help clarify to what degree the observed degradation is affected by processing history.

8.2.2 Ex-Situ CT Scanning 
Ex-situ CT scanning, where a sample is cyclically scanned, exposed, and then re-scanned under 
identical parameters would introduce a novel way to track the temporal evolution of corrosion 
in molten FLiNaK salt systems. Additionally, CT scanning may offer a framework for tracking 
and quantifying surface recession with high spatial resolution. The volumetric reconstructions 
of scanned specimens enable precise estimations of material loss without relying on destructive 
cross-sectioning and rigorous post-exposure cleaning procedures that might affect the sample 
surface, influencing subsequent investigations. 
8.2.3 Ultra-High Resolution CT Scanning and LabDCT 
Advanced lab-based CT systems offer sub-micron resolutions, while synchrotron-based CT 
systems can go even further into the nanometre range (Ganesan et al., 2025). This makes it 
possible to characterize and distinguish between different subsurface degradation processes 
such as wormholes and Kirkendall-type voids. Combining ultra-high resolution CT scanning 
with techniques such as EBSD could enable the construction of 3D maps tracking attack 
pathways in relation to grain orientations, twin boundaries, or perhaps LPBF-induced features.  
In this context, lab-based diffraction contrast tomography (labDCT) could be a promising 
complementary technique. LabDCT enables non-destructive reconstruction of a material 
volume, including grain morphologies, boundaries, and orientations (Fang et al., 2023).  
Integrating labDCT could unlock an even more detailed understanding of the corrosion 
mechanisms governing material degradation in molten salts, possibly even enabling the 
detection of DIGM to provide new insights into the formation and propagation of wormholes. 
Also, labDCT investigations could enable an understanding of which types of HAGBs are most 
prone to corrosion in both wrought and LPBF-processed specimens, providing very valuable 
insights that could help with mitigating corrosion through grain boundary engineering. This 
may be particularly important for LPBF-processed materials, as corrosion attacks appear to 
localize more aggressively at specific HAGBs compared to wrought counterparts. 
 
8.3 Post-Processing and Surface Modifications 
The corrosion behaviour of structural materials in molten FLiNaK is strongly tied to their 
microstructure and surface condition. Thus, there is a clear opportunity to explore the impact 
of post-processing and microstructural modifications on material performance in molten 
FLiNaK environments.  
As described in this work, the as-built condition of LPBF-processed 316L SS exhibits a high 
surface roughness and a textured microstructure with a fine-grained outer layer due to 
contouring. These features are likely to influence both short- and long-term corrosion 
behaviour in molten FLiNaK.  
==PAGE:67==
Oliver J. Schollert & Mads Salbæk 
 
Master’s Thesis – DTU Construct 
 
67 
 
To optimize corrosion behaviour, surface treatments such as shot peening may be considered 
for surface smoothening, but this will also introduce a lot of deformation induced dislocation 
boundaries, many of which will likely be HAGBs (Zhang et al., 2012). Thus, these boundaries 
may counteract the benefits of a smoother surface by increasing the risk of localized attack or 
potentially facilitating SCC.  
In addition, the fine-grained outer layer observed throughout this study could be avoided by 
not implementing contouring during printing. This would result in a lower HAGB-density at the 
surface but may also affect the near-surface microstructure by introducing more voids. In short, 
there are many interesting investigations ahead, relating the microstructure of LPBF-processed 
materials to corrosion performance in aggressive environments such as molten FLiNaK.  
Coating treatments, such as laser cladding with Ni-based materials, offer the potential to form 
corrosion-resistant surface layers. Prior studies have shown that such coatings can mitigate Cr 
leaching in FLiNaK, but that there are still challenges which must be addressed (Zhu et al., 
2018). The feasibility of implementing post-processing treatments in structural materials used 
in MSRs must be considered along with the potential benefits of such treatments. The cost, 
scalability, and geometrical accessibility of surface treatments may limit their applicability, 
especially for complex printed parts. This suggests that surface modifications integrated into 
the LPBF print process, such as tailored scan strategies or re-melting strategies, may be worth 
investigating further in future studies.  
 
8.4 Nuclear Industry Perspectives 
8.4.1 The Next Step: Adding Fuel 
This study focused on the corrosion behaviour of wrought and LPBF-processed 316L SS in 
FLiNaK coolant salt. Future work should investigate the effects of exposure to fuel salts, where 
dissolved fission products lead to a much more aggressive environment. In reactor 
environments, neutron irradiation also presents significant challenges. One key issue identified 
at ORNL is helium (He) embrittlement, where He accumulates at grain boundaries, particularly 
in Ni-based alloys, leading to mechanical degradation (Guo et al., 2018). Mitigating this has 
required costly alloy modifications. These considerations underscore the need to evaluate 
whether LPBF processing can enhance the radiation tolerance of more cost-effective materials 
like 316L SS. Combining corrosion and irradiation studies will be essential to assess the viability 
of LPBF steels for structural use in fuel salt environments.

8.4.2 The Future of Additive Manufacturing 
The Transformational Challenge Reactor (TCR) at ORNL is a strong example of how AM can be 
explored and studied to better understand its potential applications in the nuclear field. The 
project integrates 3D printing of core components, embedded sensing, and digital control 
systems in a compact helium-cooled microreactor. Its goal is not only to demonstrate the 
feasibility of printed reactor cores but also to shorten design-to-deployment timelines by 
combining advanced manufacturing, real-time monitoring, and automation (Cetiner, 2019). 
This approach offers valuable insights for MSR development, which similarly faces challenges 
related to design complexity, material durability, and economic scalability. While the broader 
potential of AM is increasingly recognized, its specific role in MSR deployment remains 
underexplored, which was the initial motivation for the work presented in this report. Future 
research should focus on identifying which specific MSR components stand to benefit most 
from AM in terms of performance, cost, or manufacturability. Clarifying this scope is essential 
for translating AM from a promising concept into a design strategy for next-generation reactor 
systems.

Abstract. As molten salt reactors gain traction, the demand for structural 
materials that combine good corrosion resistance with cost-effective 
manufacturability grows. This study investigates the corrosion behaviour of Laser 
Powder Bed Fusion processed AISI 316L stainless steel in molten FLiNaK salt at 
700˚C and compares it with that of wrought counterparts. Samples were exposed 
to the salt for up to 500 hours in a nitrogen environment and were characterized 
with light optical microscopy post exposure. The wrought samples exhibited 
intergranular corrosion and progressive grain detachment with increasing 
exposure time. In contrast, Laser Powder Bed Fusion samples experienced more 
localized surface degradation, potentially linked to their more complex 
microstructure. These Oindings suggest that additive manufacturing introduces 
corrosion mechanisms different from those in wrought steel when exposed to 
molten Oluoride salts. 
 
1. Introduction 
Additive manufacturing, particularly Laser Powder Bed Fusion (LPBF, also often referred to as 
PBF-LM/M), is increasingly being explored for advanced energy applications due to its ability to 
produce complex near-net shape components with minimal material waste. In molten salt 
reactors (MSRs), where high temperature corrosion is a design constraint, LPBF offers potential, 
not only for component optimization, but also on-location manufacturing and repair of critical 
parts. 
While AISI 316L stainless steel (316L SS) remains a viable structural alloy, the corrosion 
resistance of wrought 316L SS in molten Oluoride salts such as the eutectic Oluoride salt FliNaK 
(LiF-NaF-KF) is limited by aggressive chromium depletion [1]. LPBF-processed 316L SS is known 
to produce more complex, hierarchical microstructures with high dislocation densities and 
cellular substructures (Fig. 1) [2-3], but the inOluence of these features on corrosion behaviour in 
molten salts remains largely unexplored. To address this knowledge gap, the present study 
compares the corrosion performance of LPBF and wrought 316L SS exposed to molten FLiNaK at 
700˚C. The focus of the study is to investigate the impact of microstructure on corrosion 
behaviour.

2. Materials and Methods 
2.1 Sample Fabrication 
Two material variants were investigated: cold-rolled and annealed wrought 316L SS, and LPBF-
processed 316L SS. The wrought samples were provided in coupon form from the University of 
Copenhagen, and the dimensions were 15x40x2mm. LPBF samples were fabricated using an 
OpenAM system[4], and their dimensions matched those of the wrought coupons. An inner offset, 
or contour, of 50µm was applied for geometrical accuracy. The printing parameters were 
optimized to minimize porosity and are summarized in Table 1. All prints were performed on a 
316L SS build plate using powder with a composition conforming to ASTM A240. 
No post-processing was conducted in order to preserve the as-built surface features.  
 
2.2 Salt Preparation and Exposure Conditions 
FLiNaK salt (LiF-NaF-KF, 46.5-11.5-42 mol%) was mixed from high-purity constituents, melted, 
and subsequently solidiOied and crushed into powder form for uniform contact with samples.  
Corrosion exposures were carried out in cylindrical 316L SS containers containing 
approximately 80g of FLiNaK. In each container, two samples (one LPBF coupon and one wrought 
coupon) were mounted on a 316L SS “sample tree”. The containers holding the samples were 
placed in a Nabertherm LE2/11/R7 furnace and pre-dried at 300˚C for 2 hours before raising the 
temperature to 700˚C for durations of 24h, 96h, and 500h.  In addition to these intervals, a 24h 
test at 700˚C was also conducted without FLiNaK to isolate temperature effects. In total, eight 
samples were tested across the various conditions (four LPBF coupons and four wrought 
coupons). All exposures tests were conducted in a nitrogen atmosphere to minimize the inOluence 
of oxygen and moisture.

2.3 Microstructural and Surface Characterization 
After exposure, samples were removed from the container at 700˚C and reinserted into the 
furnace while hanging freely from the sample tree. This was done to prevent solidiOication of 
FLiNaK around the samples during cooling. After reinserting the samples, the furnace was 
switched off and allowed to cool to room temperature. The samples were then removed, rinsed in 
ethanol, sectioned, and embedded in epoxy for cross-sectional analysis. Grinding and polishing 
was conducted using 320, 500, 1000 and 4000 grid SiC grinding paper followed by 3µm and lastly 
1µm polishing.  
Light optical microscopy (LOM) was employed for assessment of surface and subsurface 
degradation. Bright-Oield and Differential Interference Contrast (DIC) imaging was used to 
investigate corrosion morphology, and polarized light was applied to identify iron oxide products. 
For consistency, regions along the long sides of the samples were chosen for analysis, with 
representative areas selected based on the predominant surface and corrosion features. 
 
2.4 Preliminary Microstructure Characterization  
The microstructure of both the wrought and LPBF-processed 316L SS samples were characterized 
prior to exposure tests. An as-received wrought coupon was prepared using the steps described 
above and was subsequently etched in aqua regia for a duration of 18 seconds to enhance grain 
boundaries (Fig. 2a). The image reveals a rather equiaxed grain structure with an average grain 
size of about 20µm. An electron backscattering map of an as-printed LPBF coupon reveals a 
characteristic, printed microstructure with grains elongated along the build direction and a Oiner 
substructure near the sample surface (Fig. 2b). 
 
3. Results and Discussion 
3.1 Surface Evolution and Initial Observations  
LOM reveals distinct corrosion morphologies in both LPBF and wrought 316L SS following 
exposure (see Fig. 3). Both LPBF and wrought samples exhibit surface degradation, but with 
seemingly different progression mechanics. In the absence of salt, minor changes in surface 
morphology were observed after 24h at 700˚C.

An iridescent oxide layer, characteristic of thermally grown oxides [5], had formed, which was 
especially clear on the surface of the LPBF sample (Fig. 3). When FLiNaK was introduced, both 
wrought and LPBF samples exhibited surface coloration, indicating oxide formation. The wrought 
surfaces appear to degrade more uniformly, while the LPBF surfaces seem to show a progressive 
reduction in surface roughness.  
3.2 Intergranular Corrosion in Wrought Samples 
The wrought samples exhibited progressive intergranular corrosion (IGC) (Fig. 4) consistent with 
previous studies revealing Cr-depleting effects. After 24 hours of FLiNaK exposure, network-like 
features penetrating 20-30µm beneath the sample surface along grain boundaries are apparent. 
This is consistent with previous Oindings of early-stage intergranular corrosion [1]. 
At 96 hours, the total depth of these features is harder to assess due to surface disontinuities 
likely caused by material loss. DIC imaging reveals faint grain boundaries aligned with corrosion 
fronts, further suggesting intergranular attack (Fig. 5).

The IGC morphology seems consistent with Cr depletion at grain boundaries, which would 
enable preferential dissolution and eventual grain detachment, though complete detachment can 
not be conOirmed from optical evidence alone. Following 500 hours of exposure, surface 
degradation appears more uniform, and the observation of subtle grain boundaries within the 
bulk of the material suggests further subsurface damage. 
Polarized light microscopy images indicate iron oxide coloration (Fig. 6A). These iron oxides 
are potentially formed during exposure to air after the FLiNaK tests rather than during salt 
exposure, since the nitrogen environment around the furnace is expected to prevent oxidation. 
3.3 Corrosion Behaviour of LPBF-processed samples 
Compared to the wrought material, the LPBF samples exhibit more irregular and localized 
degradation, with no clear intergranular corrosion pattern. After 24 hours of FLiNaK exposure, 
corrosion depths of approximately 10µm were observed, and the attacks appear in patches rather 
than uniformly across the sample surface.  
The LPBF sample exposed to FLiNaK for 96 hours display more severe but still non-uniform 
degradation. The surface topography of this sample appears smoother than the 24h LPBF sample, 
based on the lack of reOlective surface features (Fig. 3), suggesting selective dissolution of peaks. 
Following 500 hours of exposure, irregular degradation seems to have progressed.  
No evidence of large-scale grain detachment, such as what was observed for the wrought 
samples, was observed in either of the LPBF samples. This is suggested to be due to the elongated 
grain structure with many low-angle boundaries and thus reduced interconnectivity of high-angle 
grain boundaries, which has been reported to limit the progression of classical IGC [6].

As shown in Fig. 6b, the LPBF sample which had been exposed to FLiNaK for 96 hours also

3.4 Implications for Use of LPBF Components in Molten Salt Environments 
These Oindings suggest that LPBF introduces microstructures with different corrosion 
mechanisms in molten Oluoride environments. While the wrought material seems to corrode 
predominantly through IGC, likely due to Cr-depletion at grain boundaries, the LPBF samples 
appear to degrade via more localized mechanisms that do not rely on grain boundary diffusion to 
the same extent.  
The preferential attack of surface peaks along with localized corrosion in the LPBF-processed 
samples could, over time, compromise the structural integrity of the material even more severely 
than the uniform grain boundary attacks observed in the wrought material. Further investigation, 
including weight loss estimation, is therefore required to evaluate whether the distinct corrosion 
morphology in LPBF components is an advantage for long-term performance or rather a 
limitation. 
 
4. Conclusion 
This study presents a comparative investigation of the corrosion behaviour of LPBF and wrought 
316L SS in molten FLiNaK at 700˚C. Light optical microscopy revealed that while wrought material 
exhibited classical intergranular corrosion with progressive grain boundary attack and 
detachment, LPBF samples experienced localized corrosion attacks and some degree of 
topographical smoothing. These differences are attributed to the complex, textured 
microstructure and high dislocation density inherent to LPBF processing. 
Despite the absence of widespread IGC, LPBF samples were far from immune to material 
loss, and the irregular corrosion observed raises concerns about long-term reliability. Whether 
LPBF offers improved corrosion resistance or just a change in failure mode remains unknown. 
The Oindings of this study underscore the need for a deeper understanding of the relevant 
corrosion mechanisms before LPBF can potentially be deployed in MSRs.
