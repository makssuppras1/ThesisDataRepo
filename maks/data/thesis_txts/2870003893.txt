Abstract
Biopharmaceutical manufacturing has traditionally relied on centralized production mod┬¡
els, which are efficient for mass production, but pose challenges in responding to local┬¡
ized health crises and increasing environmental concerns. The COVID┬¡19 pandemic ex┬¡
posed the rigidity of centralized supply chains, emphasizing the need for more flexible
and sustainable approaches. This thesis explores the implementation of Mobile On De┬¡
mand (MOD) biopharmaceutical manufacturing as an alternative decentralized production
model, focusing on the logistical optimization and environmental assessment of MOD de┬¡
ployment for COVID┬¡19 vaccine production in Berlin.
A two┬¡stage stochastic programming model was developed to optimize the placement
and routing of MOD units, incorporating Monte Carlo simulations to handle demand un┬¡
certainty. The model was converted into a Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) formula┬¡
tion and solved using an L┬¡shaped decomposition algorithm, leveraging real hospital data
from Berlin to ensure practical feasibility. Demand estimation evolved across multiple iter┬¡
ations, transitioning from hospitalization estimates to real vaccination rates, significantly
impacting deployment strategies and associated costs.
A comparative Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) was conducted to evaluate the environmen┬¡
tal impact of MOD manufacturing versus centralized production. The results indicate that
while MOD units reduce the transportation carbon emissions and enhance accessibility,
they also generate 33% more waste per dose due to reliance on single┬¡use materials.
However, MOD units show lower overall Global Warming Potential (GWP), reducing CO2
emissions by 25┬¡33%, primarily due to reduced cold chain logistics and localized produc┬¡
tion.
The findings suggest that MOD manufacturing can enhance vaccine accessibility, reduce
emissions, and improve supply chain resilience, but material efficiency improvements are
needed to mitigate increased waste generation. This research offers a scalable framework
for integrating decentralized biopharmaceutical production into healthcare logistics, with
the potential to revolutionize emergency response strategies.

Background
Pharmaceutical manufacturing is a significant portion of the global economy, with a sub┬¡

has traditionally relied on centralized manufacturing, a production model where all pro┬¡

oversight of the entire production process, economies of scale leading to lower unit costs,
and quality control and regulatory compliance.
The economic significance of the pharmaceutical industry is evident in the combined mar┬¡
ket of the 50 largest drugmakers, which totals $ 4.7 trillion, with the American manufac┬¡
tures dominating the global market share. The industryÔÇÖs revenue has grown by over half
a trillion dollars since 2012. [2]
This growth come with substantial environmental costs. The healthcare sector, including
pharmaceutical production and transport, accounts for 4.4% of global carbon emissions.
Approximately 71% of these emissions come from the supply chain.[3]

The answer lies in the limitations of the centralized production model. Firstly, it lacks of

The pandemic saw numerous instances of supply chain breakdowns, from shortages of

Literature Review
Unlike many other manufacturing sectors, the biopharmaceutical industry faces a different
cost structure, where the expenses associated with product development far prevail those
of production. This characteristic comes from the intensive research and development
(R&D) processes that are required to bring a new drug or biological product to the market,
involving years of laboratory research and studies.
The market structure of the biopharmaceutical industry is reflected essentially into two
primary segments. On one side are the innovative drugs, typically produced by a relatively
small number of companies with substantial R&D capabilities.
These products, protected by patents, often target specific conditions or patient popula┬¡
tions and it is meant to replace an already successful existing product. On the other side
lies the generic drug market, characterized by a larger number of producers manufactur┬¡
ing off┬¡patent drugs for mass markets at lower prices.
The development process for new biopharmaceutical products is both long and with risks.
Studies indicate that the journey from initial research to market approval can span from
10 to 12 years, with only about 1┬¡2% of researched compounds successfully making it
to the market.[8] This low success rate shows the high┬¡risk nature of drug development,
where millions of dollars may be invested in research that ultimately fails to produce a
viable product that make profit. To mitigate this risk and incentivize continued innovation,
the industry relies heavily on patent protection.
This success rate is primarily because these drugs are certain to be profitable and are
meant for large markets. For example, in the case of COVID┬¡19, with ModernaÔÇÖs vaccine
costing $37 per dose, while Pfizer and BioNTechÔÇÖs vaccine cost around $20 per dose for a
global market.[9] In the case of niche markets or rare diseases affecting small populations,
these companies are less likely to invest in production, regardless of the success in finding
a cure, because itÔÇÖs not cost┬¡effective for them. So while this kind of production model is
the best option from a profit perspective, it raises questions about the diversity of new
drugs. Considering the diversity of the worldwide population and the limited treatments
currently available, centralized production may no longer be the most efficient approach.
Patents allow companies a period of market exclusivity, during which they can regain their
substantial R&D investments and generate profits to fund future research.
Once a drugÔÇÖs patent expires, the market dynamics shift significantly. The production
and distribution of the drug become less risky and more accessible to a broader range of
manufacturers, particularly those specializing in generic drugs. This transition often leads
to a rapid decrease in the drugÔÇÖs price as manufacturing costs decline but the market
competition intensifies.
In recent years, the biopharmaceutical industry has increasingly focused on environmen┬¡
tal sustainability and energy efficiency in its manufacturing processes. This shift reflects
a growing in the regulatory pressures and a recognition of the potential cost savings and
also for public relations benefits associated with more sustainable practices. Companies
are trying to reduce the immense energy and water consumption throughout their produc┬¡
tion processes, as well as minimize emissions of CO2 and volatile organic compounds
(VOCs).

The industry has seen notable improvements in energy efficiency, with many companies

The industryÔÇÖs carbon footprint remains a significant concern. According to recent studies,
the global Biotechnology and Pharmaceutical industry plays a crucial role in advancing

3.9% in 2021 to 5% in 2022.
When considering the broader healthcare sector, which includes pharmaceutical produc┬¡

carbon emissions, with approximately 71% of these emissions coming from the supply

Considering all these challenges that a pharmaceutical company has to face, the concept

production units, container┬¡sized units, that can be rapidly relocated to meet fluctuating
demand across different geographical areas. This approach has numerous advantages,
including reduced transportation costs, improved responsiveness to local needs, and po┬¡
tentially lower energy consumption compared to centralized manufacturing and distribu┬¡
tion models.
The COVID┬¡19 pandemic has highlighted the critical importance of agile and resilient sup┬¡
ply chains capable of responding quickly to global health crises. MOD manufacturing in
the biopharmaceuticals market could potentially address many of the challenges faced

To address these challenges of uncertainty and optimize the potential of decentralized

a crucial need for MOD systems.

two┬¡stage stochastic programming problems. This algorithm decomposes the problem

local demand fluctuations can lead to reduced waste and improved product availability,

3
Logistic Network Optimization
Logistics is fundamental to the effective operation of the Mobile on Demand (MOD) man┬¡
ufacturing system. While it is similar in function to the traditional manufacturing facilities,
MOD units differ in their mobility and smaller scale, allowing them to dynamically adjust
their locations and production schedules in response to fluctuating demand.
In this chapter, the logistic optimization of MOD units is explored for the production of
COVID┬¡19 vaccines in Berlin.The objective is to ensure an efficient supply of vaccines to
hospitals across the city by strategically deploying these units while minimizing costs and
effectively meeting vaccine demand at various hospitals, which are treated as customer
nodes in our model.
This work extends the multicut L┬¡shaped decomposition approach proposed by Lei et al.
(2014) [14] to decentralized biopharmaceutical logistics, specifically for vaccine produc┬¡
tion in Berlin. Their approach, originally developed for mobile facility routing, this approach
has been adapted to our case to facilitate robust decision┬¡making under uncertainty. By
incorporating stochastic demand variations, the model ensures that MOD units respond
effectively to dynamic demand patterns while optimizing cost efficiency.

L┬¡Shaped Decomposition
The model for optimizing MOD vaccine production units in Berlin presents computational
challenges due to the large number of scenarios required to represent demand uncertainty.
To address this, the L┬¡shaped decomposition method [14] is implemented, which breaks
down the problem into more manageable parts. The L┬¡shaped method is a decomposition
technique used for solving large┬¡scale two┬¡stage stochastic programming problems. It
separates the problem into a master problem (first┬¡stage decisions) and subproblems
(second┬¡stage recourse actions).
The standard L┬¡shaped method aggregates all second┬¡stage scenarios into a single opti┬¡
mality cut in each iteration. However, this can lead to slow convergence, as the master
problem does not get sufficient information about scenario┬¡specific behavior. The mul┬¡
ticut approach instead generates separate optimality cuts for each scenario, improving
the lower bound approximation and reducing the number of iterations needed to reach
optimality. [16]
This decomposition is optimal for our case as the MOD deployment decisions must be
made before the actual demand is realized, while production quantities ca be adjust once
the demand is known and the iteration goes on until it founds the optimal solution.

Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) of MOD
mRNA Vaccine Production
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) has become an essential tool for evaluating the environ┬¡
mental impacts of products and processes in various industries, including the biophar┬¡
maceutical sector. Unlike other manufacturing sectors, biopharmaceutical production is
characterized by high energy consumption, cleanroom requirements, and the need for
ultra┬¡cold storage and transport, particularly in the case of products like vaccines. The
healthcare sector, including pharmaceutical production and logistics, accounts for approx┬¡
imately 4.4% of global carbon emissions, underscoring the importance of understanding
and minimizing its environmental footprint [3].
The information in the following chapter is taken from the supporting information provided
in the paper ÔÇÖMobile On Demand COVID┬¡19 Vaccine Production Units for Developing
CountriesÔÇÖ [7] and ÔÇÖThe Ecological Footprint of COVID┬¡19 mRNA Vaccines: Estimating
Greenhouse Gas Emissions in GermanyÔÇÖ [17].
Recent global health crises, such as the COVID┬¡19 pandemic, have exposed the lim┬¡
itations of traditional centralized manufacturing models.
Centralized production offers
economies of scale but often lacks the flexibility to respond quickly to surges in demand
or reach underserved populations in remote areas. In contrast, MOD units are container┬¡
ized production systems that can be rapidly deployed to different locations, providing local
production capacity and reducing the need for extensive supply chains.
mRNA vaccines like the one developed by BioNTech/Pfizer and Moderna present a par┬¡
ticularly interesting case for LCA due to their unique production processes and storage
requirements. While they offer groundbreaking medical benefits, their production and
distribution involve significant energy use, carbon emissions, and resource consumption
[17]. This study aims to conduct a comparative LCA of MOD manufacturing and cen┬¡
tralized production for mRNA COVID┬¡19 vaccines rather than a full┬¡scale cradle┬¡to┬¡grave
assessment.
4.1
Goal and Scope Definition
4.1.1
Goal of the Study
The primary goal of this Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is to compare the environmental
impacts of Mobile On Demand (MOD) manufacturing and centralized manufacturing of
mRNA COVID┬¡19 vaccines. This study will assess key indicators such as Global Warming
Potential (GWP), Cumulative Energy Demand (CED), and waste generation to determine
which production model offers a more sustainable approach.
The results of this LCA will help identify key processes and stages with the highest environ┬¡
mental impact, highlighting areas for potential improvement. It aims to provide practical
recommendations on how both centralized and MOD production models can enhance
energy efficiency and reduce waste generation.

4.2.1
Raw Material Input
The production of mRNA vaccines requires a complex set of raw materials, each play┬¡
ing a critical role in the synthesis and formulation of the final product. Key components
include nucleotides, enzymes, lipids, and buffer solutions. The raw material section fo┬¡
cuses on sourcing these essential components, which significantly influence the overall
environmental impact.
For mRNA synthesis, nucleotides (adenosine, cytidine, guanosine, and uridine triphos┬¡
phates) serve as the building blocks for constructing the RNA. Enzymes such as RNA
polymerase are required to catalyze the transcription process, converting the DNA tem┬¡
plate into the mRNA sequence. These materials are typically synthesized in high┬¡purity
conditions, demanding substantial energy input and generating chemical waste during
their production [7].
A critical component in mRNA vaccine formulation is the lipid nanoparticle (LNP) delivery
system, which encapsulates and protects the mRNA from degradation. The primary lipids
used include ionizable lipids, phospholipids, cholesterol, and polyethylene glycol (PEG)┬¡
linked lipids. These lipids are crucial for ensuring the stability and delivery of the mRNA
within the human body. However, their production is resource┬¡intensive, involving multiple
chemical synthesis steps and the use of solvents [17].
Buffer solutions are also required throughout the production process to maintain opti┬¡
mal pH and stability. These solutions, often prepared on┬¡site, rely on purified water and
reagents, which contribute to the overall environmental footprint.
In the centralized production model, raw materials are often procured in bulk and stored
at dedicated facilities, reducing the frequency of shipments but requiring extensive cold
storage capacity. In contrast, MOD units rely on just┬¡in┬¡time supply chains to minimize
storage needs, which reduces inventory emissions but increases the complexity of logis┬¡
tics.

The feasibility and sustainability of MOD deployment depend not only on cost and logis┬¡
tical efficiency but also on energy availability at each deployment site. Since MOD units
require a continuous power supply for vaccine production, refrigeration, and operational
processes, the energy infrastructure of hospitals and super nodes directly impacts the
deployment feasibility and the environmental footprint.
Hospitals vary significantly in energy availability. Some are fully connected to the electrical
grid which ensure a stable power, while others rely on diesel generators, particularly in
emergency settings or areas with unreliable grid connections. Power based on diesel
generation significantly increases the carbon footprint of MOD operations, as each liter of
diesel burned emits 2.7 kg of CO2. [21]
MOD operations require power for: vaccine production, including fermentation, purifica┬¡
tion, and formulation steps. Cold chain storage which is crucial for maintaining mRNA
vaccines at a low temperature, which accounts for up to 50% of MOD energy use. 4.2.3
Transportation emissions depend on the transport mode, with 0.2 kg CO2/km for trucks
and 0.1 kg CO2/km for trains. 4.2.4
Since MOD operate continuously during 60┬¡day deployment periods, reliance on diesel
generators at certain hospitals increases both operational costs and environmental impact.
Future optimization models should factor in energy constraints when selecting deployment
locations to minimize emissions and energy inefficiencies.

Conclusion
This research explores the feasibility of Mobile On┬¡Demand (MOD) biopharmaceutical
manufacturing as an alternative to traditional centralized production, specifically for COVID┬¡
19 vaccine distribution in Berlin. The findings demonstrate that MOD units can reduce
transportation emissions by 25ÔÇô33% and improve supply chain resilience, particularly in
crisis scenarios. However, they also increase waste generation by 33%, primarily due to
their reliance on single┬¡use materials.
One of the major challenges lies in material efficiency. While MOD units optimize logis┬¡
tics and reduce emissions, their reliance on disposable single┬¡use materilas increases
environmental impact. Addressing this issue through the adoption of biodegradable or
reusable materials could significantly improve sustainability. Furthermore, energy infras┬¡
tructure is a critical concern. While MOD units operate efficiently when connected to stable
grids, they may depend on diesel generators in off┬¡grid locations, increasing carbon emis┬¡
sions. Expanding renewable energy integration, such as solar┬¡powered MOD units with
battery storage, could further reduce environmental footprint. Additionally, logistical coor┬¡
dination plays a crucial role in MOD deployment. Real┬¡time tracking and adaptive demand
prediction models are necessary to optimize unit placement and movement, preventing
supply gaps in high┬¡demand regions.
To facilitate the transition to MOD manufacturing, policy interventions should be consid┬¡
ered. Governments and regulatory agencies must establish clear guidelines for decen┬¡
tralized biopharmaceutical production, ensuring both safety and efficiency. Financial in┬¡
centives and tax benefits for pharmaceutical companies adopting MOD units with sustain┬¡
able materials could drive innovation while mitigating waste concerns. Moreover, inte┬¡
grating MOD production into smart logistics networks, with real┬¡time demand monitoring
and AI┬¡driven resource allocation, would enhance deployment effectiveness and minimize
redundancy. Additionally, promoting renewable energy adoption in MOD operations, par┬¡
ticularly for units deployed in resource limited settings would ensure that decentralization
does not lead to increased fossil fuel dependency.
While this research provides a strong foundation for understanding the logistical and en┬¡
vironmental impacts of MOD manufacturing, several areas need further exploration. A
comprehensive cost analysis comparing the economic viability of MOD and centralized
production models could provide deeper insight into its financial feasibility. Future stud┬¡
ies should also expand the stochastic optimization framework to model supply chain re┬¡
silience under pandemic surge scenarios, improving preparedness for future global health
crises. Further investigation into sustainable material alternatives for MOD production
could address waste management challenges, ensuring that the environmental benefits
of decentralization are not nullified by increased single┬¡use consumption. Finally, global
scalability assessments should be conducted to evaluate the potential of MOD produc┬¡
tion in developing countries, where access to essential vaccines and medicines is often
constrained by infrastructure limitations.
Ultimately, MOD manufacturing represents a transformative shift in pharmaceutical logis┬¡
tics, offering enhanced flexibility, reduced emissions, and improved crisis response ca┬¡
pacity. However, for this model to be truly sustainable and scalable, it must be supported
by efficient regulatory policies, advancements in sustainable materials, and strategic en┬¡
ergy integration. By addressing these challenges and leveraging innovative supply chain
