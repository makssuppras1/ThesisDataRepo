Abstract
Copenhagen Suborbitals, an amateur spaceflight program, is building a new rocket for a
manned mission, therefore a new crew capsule design is needed. As the current design
is only in its initial stage, the geometry and the most suitable hatch shape need to be
determined.
Firstly, the loading conditions throughout the capsule’s flight are investigated which
is followed by a Finite Element Analysis of the initial capsule design. Subsequently, Topol-
ogy Optimization methods are used to find an alternative stiffener pattern with the purpose
of minimizing the capsule’s mass while increasing its stiffness. In addition, three different
hatch shapes are considered and their influence on the optimized structure is evaluated for
a chosen load-case. In the course of the project the Abaqus software is used for the pur-
pose of Finite Element Analysis. The Topology Optimization of the capsule is performed
with Tosca software implemented in the Abaqus optimization module.
Alternative stiffener patterns are found for the three considered hatch shapes. Fur-
thermore, the comparison between the initial design and the optimized models directly
demonstrates the structural benefits introduced by the Topology Optimization methods.
Based on the obtained results, the most suitable hatch shape cannot be clearly indicated;
however, further steps necessary for reaching the goal are suggested.
Keywords: Finite Element Methods (FEM); Finite Element Analysis (FEA); Topology
Optimization (TO); capsule design; spaceflight; spacecraft design

Introduction
Since the early space exploration period a lot of aspects of the space industry have changed.
Until the early 2000s space missions and research were run mostly by governmental space
agencies; however, as the significance of the space race decreased, most of governmental
space research funding was cut back resulting in space missions being canceled. That gave
way to the growth of a private space sector with companies like Blue Origin (founded in
2000), SpaceX (2002) or Boeing IDS (2002) and many more.
Since the 2000s the industry has not stopped growing, and with constant techno-
logical progress and easily available international research regarding space flight, space
becomes even more accessible to the public than it ever has been before. Back in the
2000s the space industry was focused on space exploration and research; however, during
recent years as the rocket launch price decreases with time, new perspectives for the in-
dustry start appearing like space tourism or mining on extra-terrestrial bodies.
Although plenty of companies take effort to reduce the price of launching a rocket,
the involved technologies are usually out of technical and financial reach for smaller organ-
isations. Therefore, in order to make launching rockets into space even more accessible,
further studies into cost reduction is needed with techniques approachable for smaller bud-
gets. One of the interest areas for improvement is the structure of the spacecraft itself,
where main costs are connected with materials and used manufacturing technologies.
1.1
Copenhagen Suborbitals Overview
Copenhagen Suborbitals (CS) is a crowd-funded, non-profit organisation with a goal of
flying an astronaut to space. To be more specific, the goal is to fly the astronaut beyond
the Kármán line which is 100 km above the sea level, and which defines a boundary
between the Earth’s atmosphere and the outer space.
Figure 1.1: Heat-1X rocket [1].
So far CS has designed and built six rock-
ets and capsules, all of which were launched from
a floating launch pad on the Baltic sea. The first
rocket HEAT-1X was launched in 2011. At lift-off
it weighed 1630 kg and was 9.38 m tall which was a
significant breakthrough in amateur rocketry when
considering the scale of the project. Although the
rocket launched properly, due to mid-flight problems
it just reached a maximum altitude of 1400 m and
crashed into water [1].
This did not stop CS from designing and build-
ing the next rocket called Smaragd. The design was
smaller as it had just 572 cm of height; however, this
time the rocket used two stages and had plenty of
flight sensors and a GPS unit. Unfortunately this trial did not succeed either and after
lift-off the connection with the rocket was lost, after which it crashed into the sea and
sunk [1].
Afterwards, ’Tycho Deep Space’ Apollo-like capsule was designed and built together
with a Launch Escape System (LES) on top. The objective of this mission was to build a
working crew capsule and testing all necessary subsystems for sending a person to space
and returning them back, while sending it to 1km of altitude. During the test flight all of

has built so far. In comparison to its previous version, Nexø II had a Dynamic Pressure
Regulation (DPR) for the fuel tanks, a new vent valve for cryogenic conditions, and a
Liquid Oxygen (LOX) tank filling monitoring. It was also 1.1 m taller than Nexø I (6.7 m
tall) and weighed around 292 kg during lift-off. This time the mission was successful as
the whole flight went according to the plan and all used systems worked properly, allowing
the rocket to reach 6500 m above the sea level and safely land back in the water. Nexø
II was an incredibly important milestone in CS rocketry, as the used systems have been
proven to work and now they can be scaled up to use in the much bigger ’Spica’ rocket

1.2
Overview of the Spica Rocket
The experience and knowledge gained during developing the six previous spacecrafts, now
lets CS to build a rocket which is meant to have an actual astronaut onboard, and send
them above the Karman line. This rocket is called ’Spica’. In terms of numbers, Spica
is going to be around 13 m tall with 955 mm diameter, which is going to make it the
biggest rocket built by an amateur organisation. It is going to be powered by an engine
with 100 kN of thrust, which uses liquid oxygen (LOX) and ethanol as its propellants. It
is estimated that Spica is going to weigh around 4000 kg at the point of lift-off, out of
which 2600 kg is going to be the propellants.
At the time of writing of this thesis, the organisation has already started building
the rocket and made significant progress since the early works in 2017. As for the current
status, the LOX and ethanol tanks are built together with an inter-tank section. Apart
from these, significant amount of research and testing is being performed for all necessary

1.2.1
Existing capsule design
The structure of the capsule so far consists of a simple pressure vessel and connector rings
which are used for connecting the capsule to the rest of the rocket body (Figure 1.3).
The pressure vessel and the connectors are made out of stainless steel 304 sheet metal.
The connectors have thickness of 3 mm, and the pressure vessel of 1 mm. For purposes
of a later analysis the pressure vessel’s thickness is assumed also as 3 mm. Lastly, the
vessel has diameter of 955 mm and height of 2230 mm. For the end-caps a standardized
thorispherical DIN28011 shape is used. A hatch shape has been proposed (Figure 1.4) in
[2], and is going to be used in the preliminary design analysis. The astronaut seat is going
to be mounted on two 30 mm x 1500 mm rails with 550 mm spacing in between them. In
total, the structure’s mass is around 213 kg.
The goal of considering and analysing the already existing design together with the
proposed hatch is to investigate critical regions of the structure, and to use it as a starting
point for the topology optimization. Afterwards, it is meant to be used as a reference for
comparison and evaluation of the optimized capsule topology.

Figure 1.4: Hatch shape proposed in [2].
1.2.2
Technical requirements
There are several requirements that have to be met by the final capsule design. One should
note that the listed requirements relate to the structural part of the vessel, and do not
contain requirements specific to other aspects of the design.
The technical requirements are following:
1. The capsule diameter has to be equal to 955 mm. The height is to be determined.
2. The total mass of the capsule (i.e. including the astronaut and equipment) cannot
exceed 350 kg.
3. Capsule’s structure has to withstand loads experienced during its entire flight.
4. The hatch geometry has to be functional while not impairing the structural strength
of the vessel.

1.3
Goals of the thesis
The goal of this master thesis is to investigate if the topology optimization methods (avail-
able in Abaqus/Tosca FEM codes) can help to improve the structural properties of a crew
capsule while reducing its mass and keeping the structure manufacturable in the local
machine shop conditions.
It is also an ambition to answer the question if it is possible to make a lightweight
spacecraft structure without using sandwich panels or expensive exotic materials, which
might be financially unreachable for amateur rocket builders.
In order to meet these goals several steps are to be taken. Firstly, the loading condi-
tions throughout the capsule flight are going to be investigated and interpreted into a set
of applicable boundary conditions for a Finite Element model. Then, the initial capsule
design is going to be structurally evaluated by means of a FE Analysis. Subsequently,
the topology of the capsule is going to be optimized for three different hatch shapes in
terms of their stiffness and mass. It is going to be followed with a comparison between the

1.4
Previous work on spacecraft topology optimization
As the Topology Optimization is still a relatively new topic in the field of mechanical
engineering, there is still plenty of engineering areas that are yet to use these numerical
methods. For instance for the aerospace industry, the TO techniques are clearly attractive
because of their mass reduction possibilities. On the other hand the industry is also filled
with strict standards which are not easy to change. Nevertheless, researchers from across
the world continue to work on improving the aircraft/spacecraft structures with the use
of TO, which as for now is for certain taking a step into the future of structural design.
All instances of using TO in aerospace industry mainly stem from the fact that
the classical methods of structure design (e.g., wing structures, fuselage stiffeners and air-
frames in general) have been somewhat perfected, and it is unlikely to get further major
mass and cost reduction while using them. Therefore more and more focus is paid to the
TO methods as they offer a new perspective of structure design.
Rocket payload adapter
The first example of using TO in aerospace industry is the ’Topology optimization of the
lattice payload adapter for carrier rocket’ [3]. The goal of the project was to decrease the
mass of a composite adapter which connects the rocket to a payload, while preserving the
structure’s strength, and therefore to design a more optimal structure.
Firstly the structural performance of the adapter is evaluated with Finite Element
Methods for relevant load cases for reference purposes. Afterwards, its topology is opti-
mized and the resulting geometry with many present imperfections is cleaned up. That
way it is possible to create a 3D model that can be once again evaluated with a FEA.
By means of TO the mass of the adapter has been decreased by 45%, and the struc-
tural performance of the optimized model was proven in a numerical analysis.
It has
to be noted that although the mass has been reduced, the maximum compression stress
in the ribs of the adapter increased by approximately 21%. Nevertheless, the stress re-
sults remained in the safety margins, and the topology optimization provided the needed
structural improvements.

2.1
Governing equations of linear FEM
The Finite Element Method (FEM) is a numerical approach of solving differential equa-
tions, which is widely used in various fields of engineering. In solid mechanics it is used
to evaluate the structural behaviour of mechanical systems which geometries might be
too complex to evaluate with analytical methods. The Finite Element method divides
the analysed domain into a set of discrete elements, which are interconnected with nodes.
Each node in an element carries information about its displacement, which in turn is used
to find the field quantities of an element e.g. strains or stresses. It is important to note
that FE Method does not provide exact solutions, as the field quantities are interpolated
from the nodal values. Nevertheless, one can ensure a high quality of said approximation
by appropriate discretization of the domain.

tion 2.1). The principle states that for an infinitely small (virtual) displacement {δu} in
an arbitrary system, an increment of the internal strain energy is equal to the increment
of the external work done in this system. The external work consists of work done by
body forces {F} in volume V, surface tractions {Φ} on surface S, and concentrated nodal
loads {p} on associated virtual displacements {δu}i [6].

2.2
Mathematics behind Topology Optimization
Topology optimization (TO) consists of mathematical and numerical methods that aim to
determine an optimal material distribution in a given design space for a given set of loads
and constraints. In other words, TO can influence the shape, number, and locations of
holes in a structure where the material is not needed, which makes it distinct from size
and shape optimization (Figure 2.2).

Filtering
The material distribution based topology optimization is prone to two major issues which
has a direct effect on the obtained solutions, namely checkerboard patterns and mesh-
dependency.
Checkerboard patterns consist of fields of solid and void elements connected only
at their corners, and they are a common problem in Topology Optimization (Figure 2.4).
The patterns appear in the optimization results since the FE approximations tend to over-
estimate stiffness of such structures, which in turn reduce the compliance of the optimized
domain, which then makes them a favourable outcome of the optimization. As these pat-
terns are nonphysical, it is required to prevent them from forming during the optimization
process, and this is where the filtering methods are used [8].

3.1.4
Splashdown
The capsule is expected to splashdown with a vertical velocity of 8-10 m/s with a 30◦angle
of attack with respect to the surface of the water level. The capsule orientation during the
splashdown is an important topic in itself as it has significant influence on the experienced
g-loads and the safety of the astronaut. Knowing that the rate of descent is rather high
(max 10 m/s = 36 km/h), and could be easily compared to a car crash, it is the designers
responsibility to decrease the risk of the astronaut’s injury. Considering that the capsule
descent in a purely vertical orientation would result in serious injuries to the astronaut’s
spine, the most optimal capsule orientation is just horizontal. However, in that case the
splashdown g-loads majorly increase due to the increased surface area hitting the water
surface. One can see that the problem of the vessel orientation during splashdown is not
so obvious and several variables have influence on what the desirable orientation might
be.
For that reason CS performed a comparative experiment where scaled-down cylindri-
cal capsules with spherical and conical bottoms were splashed into the water with different
angles of attack, and the experienced decelerations were measured. The study has found
that the lowest g-loads were measured for intermediate angles between 0◦and 90◦of angle
of attack, and that 30◦inclination in respect to the water level is a good compromise be-
tween keeping the deceleration low and keeping the capsule horizontal enough to protect
the astronaut’s spine. As the performed study was just a comparative one, it did not

3.3
Convergence study approach
Finite element methods approximate the results, which is their main difference from the
analytical methods which give exact results. One of the main influences on the quality of
the solution is the discretization of the analysed domain, also called meshing. In order to
ensure a good precision of the result a mesh convergence study has to be made. The study
is based on running the analysis suﬀicient number of times with decreasing element sizes
until the result stops being sensitive to the change of the mesh density and is assumed
converged. On the contrary, it has to be remembered that an increased number of elements
results in increased amount of needed CPU power, and therefore increased analysis time.
That is the reason why each convergence study is a compromise between the precision of
the result and amount of time used for the analysis. In addition, one has to be aware that
as long as a converged mesh provides precise results it does not necessarily indicate that
the result is accurate and true to reality. In short: a convergence study is a way to ensure
precision of the result, but not the accuracy.
As the capsule FE model is expected to be substantially big and have several areas
with stress concentrations and most probably singularities, the mesh convergence study
becomes a more complicated task. The approach in performing the convergence studies

Analysis of the initial capsule design
In this chapter the process of the capsule model development is presented, which refers to
the already existing capsule with implemented additional features as: a thickened shell,
a hatch, and parachute/seat mountings. Afterwards, the results of the FE analyses are

4.1
FEA model setup
For the purpose of the structural analysis of the available design, four FE models are made.
The idea is to make separate FE analyses for launch, ballute descent, parachute descent
and splashdown load-cases to have isolated structure performance characteristics.
The capsule model consists only of one part which is the pressure vessel merged
together with two connector rings. It is done this way mainly to reduce number of incre-
ment convergence errors during the FE analysis, which result from a problematic contact
surface between the pressure vessel and the connector rings. Surely, the contact regions
of the pressure vessel and the connector rings look suspicious with the sharp corners (can
be seen in Figure 4.9), nevertheless they do not result in any major numerical problems.
The material used is S355 steel with yield strength equal to 355 MPa, tensile strength of
470 – 630 MPa, and elastic modulus of 200 GPa.
As it is expected to observe the stress concentrations along the edges of the hatch
and the seat mounting surfaces, the model is partitioned accordingly, visible in Figure 4.1.
What is more, an additional partitioning is done for the parachute load-case since one has
to simulate the connections with the parachute lines (Figure 4.1b). It is assumed that the
parachute connecting brackets are going to have a 80 mm diameter base, and are going to
be attached strictly to the pressure vessel.

Note on post-processing
During the post-processing a shell element can be evaluated at its top and bottom sides,
which might experience different stresses. To make sure the maximal stress values are
presented in the results, the envelope type of field data representation is used. It means
that the software compares the stress results for the two sides of a shell element at its
integration points, and outputs the maximum value which is going to be visible on both
sides of the shell. In addition, averaging is used for the contour plotting, which means
that all extrapolated adjacent nodal stress results which relative difference is lower than
75% is averaged into a single nodal stress result. If the relative difference is higher than
75% between the nodes, then the results remain unaveraged.

5
Topology Optimization
The main goal of the topology optimization in the capsule design process is to find a
feasible stiffener pattern which is going to carry the loads during the flight. As a result,
the mass of the capsule should be reduced, and the shell itself should be less stressed,
which in turn should decrease the hatch opening deformations. The second goal of the
topology optimization process is to investigate the influence of the hatch shape on the
structural characteristics of the capsule.
5.1
Optimization domain
Topology optimization domain indicates physical boundaries where the material can and
cannot be distributed. As the goal is to obtain a stiffening pattern, the shell of the capsule
is going to be disregarded. Not only is it going to help the optimization algorithm to
’generate’ the stiffeners, but also it is going to put focus on the stiffeners being responsible
for carrying the loads instead of the capsule shell. Of course in reality the capsule shell
also carries the loads; however, as it has been shown by the obtained FEA results in
chapter 4, even the 3 mm shell is highly prone to deformation and possibly failure. What
is more, due to the time constraint of the project only the parachute load-case is going to
be evaluated. It is because this flight phase demonstrated substantial amounts of stress
in the structure when compared with launch and ballute phases. Additionally, the nature
of the parachute load-case is understood considerably better than the splash-down, thus
more meaningful and well-understood results can be expected.

Before proceeding to the topology optimization of all three hatch shapes, an appropriate set
of process parameters has to be found to obtain good quality results. Evaluated parameters
include: the average element size, density move limit, minimum element density, initial

and this is why the mesh-dependency is also evaluated. The described parameter study is
going to be performed on the capsule model with the hatch proposed by DTU students in

challenge in this optimization process is the critically low volume constraint (5% of the
initial volume), which has direct influence on its numerical performance. For that reason,
the initial volume parameter is set to 10% of the initial volume. It means that initially all
elements start with 10% of the initial volume instead of 5%. It is expected to make the
initial phase of the optimization smoother and allow for easier transition into the lower

low volume constraint. For that reason the delta stop criteria for the objective function
is set to 0.001, and the delta stop for the constraint function is set to 0.005 (Table 5.3).

5.2.5
Optimization parameter study conclusions
Four Topology Optimizations have been performed to test and find a suitable set of pa-
rameters which would provide satisfactory result quality for the subsequent hatch shape
evaluation. Each case was performed with different parameters and following result charac-
teristics were evaluated: mesh-dependence, minimal feature size, connectivity, behaviour
of objective/constraint functions and convergence. With each attempt certain improve-
ments in the mentioned characteristics are achieved.
The first case presents unacceptable results in most of the aspects, as not only does
it have substantial amount of floating elements, but also it has poor numerical qualities
such as: critical oscillations of the constraint function, high peaks of the objective function,
and no sign of convergence despite 569 performed design cycles.
It is recognized that the rather coarse mesh might be responsible for such process
behaviour and therefore, in the second case the average global element size is reduced from
13 mm to 10 mm and the density move limit is reduced to 0.1 from 0.15 with expectation
of reducing the oscillations. These changes result with more distinct and thinner stiffening
features, and with different placement of floating elements elements. Namely, while in the
first case the floating elements are mainly located in the top and bottom connector rings,
in the second case they can be also found in the cylinder part of the capsule. This possibly
indicates that the TO algorithm tried to ’create’ more stiffening structures in the cylinder
part, but did not have enough available volume to fully connect them. When numerical
characteristics are considered, the second case presents a significant improvement when
compared to the first case. Initially, the volume constraint function shows better stabil-
ity and decent available volume usage without any major oscillations. Nevertheless, with
time it starts decreasing and starts to oscillate, however with less intensity then in the
previous case. In addition, the strain energy seems to be less sensitive to the objective
function oscillations, which indicates that it is only the structurally insignificant elements
that experience the oscillating volume change. Nevertheless, the second TO process does
not converge and is also rejected.
As the high sensitivity of the TO has been observed to the used element size, it has
been further reduced to 9 mm in the third optimization case. The density move limit has
remained the same, as a lower value would significantly increase the optimization time.
In addition, the possible influence of the remaining tested parameters has been also iden-
tified. Hence the convergence criteria were made less demanding by increasing them by
a factor of 10 (back to the default values), the minimum density has been increased to
0.001, and finally the initial density was increased to 10%. These parameters resulted in
better quality topology with minor amounts of floating elements and better connectivity,
especially when the hatch region is concerned. What is more, this optimization process
not only did fully converge, but also it took only 56 design cycles to do so, which is a
significant improvement when compared to the first 2 runs. The only problem were two
minor dips in the constraint function, which were the reason for taking another attempt
and trying to remove them.
In the last case only the average element size has been reduced from 9 mm to 7 mm.
This change more than doubled the number of elements in the optimization domain, which
resulted in even more numerous stiffener patterns, decreased amount of residual elements,
and significantly improved the connectivity. It has to be noted that each optimization case
generated more and more shell like structures, which is highly pronounced in the last case,
especially for connections between parachute mountings and the rest of the capsule. What

6
Summary
This report presents the methodology and execution of several steps during the capsule
design for the Spica rocket. Firstly, the boundary conditions during the different stages of
the rocket flight are evaluated, which is followed by an FEA of the initial capsule design
with an addition of a previously proposed hatch design by DTU students in [2]. Displace-
ment and Von Mises stress results are evaluated in regions of interest throughout the
capsule structure for each flight stage.
Afterwards, Topology Optimization methods are used to obtain a stiffening struc-
ture for the capsule. Due to the time constraint of the project only one load-case could be
evaluated, which was chosen to be the parachute load case. The choice was based on the
fact that the loads present during this flight stage are more critical than during the launch
or ballute descent phases. Although the splashdown load-case also presents considerably
high loads, it is assessed that the load-case is not suﬀiciently understood and it requires
more research.
Firstly, an appropriate set of optimization parameters is evaluated which provides
acceptable results in terms of topological qualities and numerical performance. Conse-
quently, the TO parameters are used to perform a hatch shape study in an attempt to
evaluate the influence of the hatch shape on the optimal structure of the capsule. For this
purpose 3 different hatch shapes are considered, and the obtained TO results are not only
evaluated in terms of their topological and numerical performance, but also in terms of
manufacturing feasibility.
6.1
Initial and optimized designs comparison
Having the original and optimized capsule designs, it is possible to compare them and eval-
uate the changes introduced by the Topology Optimization. Both designs are compared
in terms of total strain energy and Von Mises stress. For the purpose of stress evaluation
the results of linear FEA included in the TO process are going to be used.
It is important to point out and be aware that such a comparison has several short-
comings. The first one stems from a fact that the initial design is modeled with shell
elements, and the topology optimization is performed with continuum elements. These
different element formulations can be expected to introduce a certain amount of errors.
The next flaw considers the optimized models, and is a consequence of the models
still having elements with intermediate densities, which makes these elements less stiff.
The non-uniform properties throughout the elements in the model can be especially ob-
served in the exaggerated maximum deflections of the low stiffness elements, because of
which the displacements are not considered in the final comparison. However, due to the
low stiffness they do not experience any high stresses and therefore do not hinder the
readability of the stress results. On the other hand, the elements which stiffness is not
severely penalized by the SIMP algorithm, present more accurate behaviour.
The last deficiency comes from the fact that the structure is not smoothed and rein-
terpreted, that is, it is not totally physical and there are still some floating elements, or
unconnected features. The comparison of the designs could be improved by smoothing
the stiffener structure and performing another FEA with uniform element properties; how-
ever, the required Tosca license for this function is unavailable at the time of the project
execution.

Conclusions
It can be concluded that the Topology Optimization methods prove to be a feasible tool for
decreasing the structure mass, and are able to provide an alternative stiffener pattern even
Structural Design and Analysis of a Crew Capsule for the Spica Rocket
51
==PAGE:63==
for a critically low volume constraint. However, specifically in the case of the parachute
descent load-case and the used TO modelling approach, the most suitable hatch shape
cannot be directly determined. It is because, in the current model setup where the shell
of the capsule is disregarded, the hatch does not participate in carrying the loads in any
significant way. Nonetheless, it is not a bad thing, as due to that modelling approach the
stiffener pattern is obtained which should reduce the amount of stress and deformation in
the hatch when the cylinder shell is reintroduced.
It is likely that more clear differences between the evaluated hatches performance
could be noticed by using the launch or ballute load-cases. It is due to fact that in these
load-cases the hatch would be in the middle of the load path, and thus have a more direct
influence on the stiffness of the structure when the outer shell is disregarded. What is
more, the introduction of the load carrying stiffeners decreases the sensitivity of the capsule
structure stiffness to the hatch shape itself. It is highly visible in the tested load-case as
the different evaluated hatch shapes do not introduce any significant changes neither to
the overall stiffener layout, nor to the total strain energies.
In case of the optimized parachute descent load-case, it can be concluded that the
hatch shape does not have a major influence on the capsule stiffness, and the choice of the
hatch shape should be mainly driven by the functionality and manufacturing feasibility.
Nevertheless, it is not necessarily the case for the rest of the load-cases, and therefore they
need to be evaluated separately. It is also important to point out that this conclusion
might change after evaluating the structure together with the capsule shell.
6.3
Further work
This project has shown several steps in the capsule design for the Spica rocket; however,
many more steps are still to be taken. Firstly, it is necessary to perform the Topology
Optimization for the rest of the load-cases in question.
Preferably with even further
decreased element size if possible. After all load-cases are evaluated, it should become
more clear which hatch shape is the most suitable; however, as discussed before, the
new global stiffener system should reduce the dependency of the capsule strength on the
hatch shape.
After obtaining a stiffener pattern for each load case, they need to be
joined together into one model with a hatch shape of choice. It can be done by model
parametrization, which means interpreting the optimized models into a new Finite Element
model, preferably made out of shell elements with accordingly assigned shell thicknesses.
The interpretation has to take into account the manufacturability, thus the stiffener shapes
have to be modified accordingly to make them possible to manufacture and assemble.
Afterwards, a size optimization process should be performed in order to find the optimal
thicknesses of separate ribs in the global stiffener layout. Finally, an FEA of the final
model should be carried out and its performance should be compared to the initial FEA
model in terms of mass, Von Mises stress, deflections, and compliance (or total strain
energy). Alternatively, it might be beneficial to investigate the influence of the density
filter in the TO process together with the MIMP interpolation available in the Abaqus
optimization module. Also importantly, pressure vessel calculations should be done for the
capsule to determine the minimum wall thickness. It also might be interesting to evaluate
using a softer material for the shell of the capsule, and a stiffer one for the rib structure.
Finally, the splashdown load-case should be studied in more depth.
This paper
shows a short overview of the available knowledge and literature in this topic; nevertheless,
far more detail is needed about this loading in order to correctly set up the boundary
conditions in the FE models. The importance of such study is amplified by the critically
high loads being present during splashdown which potentially lead to high deformations
and structural failures.
