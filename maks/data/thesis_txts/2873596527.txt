Abstract
In the field of business process management, a significant gap exists between the imper-
ative paradigm of BPMN and the declarative paradigm of DCR Graphs. To bridge this
gap, this thesis presents a formal, behavior-preserving translation from BPMN to DCR.
The core contribution is a systematic encoding that not only maps BPMN objects to
DCR events but also introduces a novel method using auxiliary state events to precisely
translate the complex synchronization logic of BPMN gateways. This ensures the result-
ing DCR model is behaviorally equivalent to the source BPMN process. To validate the
framework, a web-based tool, bpmn2dcr-js, was developed for real-time transformation
and interactive simulation. Analysis of execution traces confirms the translation’s effec-
tiveness in preserving the original process behavior, enabling the application of DCR’s
powerful analytical capabilities to existing BPMN models.
Keywords: BPMN, DCR Graphs, Model Transformation, Formal Semantics, Declarative
Process, Imperative Process

Introduction
In business process management, a fundamental distinction exists between imperative and
declarative modeling paradigms. The imperative approach, standardized by the Business
Process Model and Notation (BPMN), explicitly defines every possible execution path [1].
While this flow-oriented method is effective for structured, predictable workflows and has
become an industry standard [2], its rigidity makes it cumbersome for modeling flexible,
knowledge-intensive processes, often leading to overly complex diagrams that are diﬀicult
to maintain [3].
In contrast, the declarative paradigm, represented by formalisms like Dynamic Condition
Response (DCR) Graphs, specifies a process through a set of constraints that must be
respected, rather than defining a step-by-step procedure [4]. This approach provides the
necessary flexibility to handle unpredictable and dynamic scenarios, making it highly
suitable for domains like adaptive case management and regulatory compliance [5, 6].
The vast industry investment in BPMN models creates a compelling need for a reliable
bridge to the declarative world. A formal, behavior-preserving translation from BPMN
to DCR would allow organizations to apply the powerful formal analysis and verification
capabilities of DCR to their existing process repositories. However, this translation is non-
trivial. The core challenge is to faithfully map the explicit synchronization logic of BPMN
gateways—particularly the AND and OR gateways—into a set of declarative constraints
without losing or altering the original process behavior.
This thesis addresses this challenge by presenting a formal and provably behavior-preserving
translation from a well-formed subset of BPMN to DCR Graphs. The main contribution
is a systematic encoding that not only maps BPMN objects to DCR events but, crucially,
uses a novel approach involving auxiliary state-holding events to precisely translate the
control-flow semantics of all gateway types. This method ensures that the resulting DCR
model is a faithful and analyzable representation of the source BPMN process.
To validate this framework and provide a practical implementation, a web-based tool,
bpmn2dcr-js, was developed. This tool offers a seamless environment for real-time BPMN-
to-DCR transformation and, more importantly, allows for interactive trace simulations.
This empirical validation confirms that the translation effectively preserves the original
process behavior, thereby demonstrating the practical applicability of applying DCR’s
powerful analytical capabilities to the vast repository of existing BPMN models.
The remainder of this thesis is structured as follows. Chapter 2 reviews the relevant liter-
ature. Chapters 3 and 4 establish the formal syntax and operational semantics for BPMN
and DCR Graphs, respectively. Chapter 5 presents the core translation methodology and
its proof. Chapter 6 provides an introduction of bpmn2dcr-js and a practical analysis of
the translation with examples. Finally, Chapter 7 summarizes the findings and discusses
future research directions.

Related Work
This research builds upon three key areas: the formalization of BPMN and DCR, the
theory of process modeling, and methods for model transformation. This chapter surveys
the foundational work in these areas to contextualize the contributions of this thesis.
The oﬀicial BPMN specification lacks a formal execution semantics, which can lead to
ambiguities and prevent rigorous automated analysis . To address this, a significant body
of research has focused on formalizing BPMN, often through translation to well-defined
mathematical models. The most common approach is to map BPMN models to various
classes of Petri Nets [7]. This allows for the formal verification of properties like soundness
and the absence of deadlocks [8]. However, these translations can produce very large and
complex nets, especially for BPMN models with intricate gateway logic, complicating
analysis. Other works, such as [9], have defined the operational semantics of BPMN using
process-algebraic approaches. Our work leverages the principles from these formalizations
to ensure our translation to DCR is semantically sound.
As an alternative to imperative modeling, the declarative paradigm has gained traction
for its ability to handle flexibility and variability in business processes [10].
Dynamic
Condition Response (DCR) Graphs are a prominent declarative formalism, distinguished
by a minimal set of constraints (condition, response, inclusion, exclusion) that yield high
formal expressive power [4]. The formal properties and computational complexity of DCR
Graphs have been well-studied, including reachability analysis [11] and extensions for
nested processes [12], establishing DCR as a mature and robust modeling language suitable
for formal verification.
Bridging the imperative-declarative divide is an active area of research. Efforts include
translating declarative models to imperative ones for execution [13] and techniques for
mining declarative specifications from process logs [14]. More specifically related to our
work, some research has focused on enriching BPMN with declarative constraints [15],
allowing for a hybrid approach. This differs from our goal of a complete translation. The
translation of DCR graphs into Petri nets has also been explored to leverage existing
analysis tools [16].
Our work distinguishes itself by providing the first, to our knowledge, direct and formal
behavior-preserving translation from a significant subset of BPMN into DCR Graphs.
Unlike translations to Petri nets, our approach is designed to maintain a clear structural
correspondence while using a novel method of auxiliary events to precisely encode the
complex synchronization logic of BPMN gateways within the declarative framework of
DCR.

This section introduces the formal syntax and operational semantics for BPMN. It’s pri-
marily inspired by [9], but with the difference that we have omitted all logic related
to ”Collaboration” and ”Pools” to concentrate on the core execution logic internal to a
BPMN process and add the operational semantics of OR-Join Gateway. This is also the
scope involved in the vast majority of usage scenarios. The oﬀicial BPMN 2.0 specification
[1] presents the language’s syntax through a UML-style metamodel. For the purpose of
establishing a formal operational semantics, we introduce an alternative syntax using a
BNF style, which is more amenable to a formal semantics.
This article does not aim to discuss the complete BPMN 2.0 specification [1], but rather
its core workflow concepts, which are illustrated in fig. 3.1. Furthermore, as pointed out
in paper [2], these elements alone cover 90% of use cases.

3.1
Syntax
A complete BPMN process model, written as λP, is represented in our syntax as a collec-
tion of nodes P and a labelling function λ, as detailed in Figure 3.2. An object O could be
a task T, an event E or a gateway G. Each node is defined with an object O along with
its respective incoming and/or outgoing sequence edges as a tuple. For the definitions,
we utilize the set of sequence edge names, ranged over by e. So each sequence edge is
conceptually divided into two parts: an outgoing component from its source node and an
incoming component to its target node. The constituent nodes of a process are organized
as a collection, and we identify processes up to the commutativity and associativity of
this collection. Consequently, a process defined as (P1 || P2) || P3 is considered identical
to P1 || (P2 || P3) due to associativity. We also define the set of all objects appearing
in a process P is called its free object, denoted fo(P), and the set of all sequence flows
appearing in a process P is called its free sequence flow, denoted fsf(P).
Our syntax extends the original BPMN definition with labels with respect to the original
presentation [9]. The labelling function λ is a total function mapping objects to labels.
For simplicity, we often omit λ when it is clear from context, writing P instead of λP. We
assume event labels are unique: if O1, O2 ∈fo(P), then λ(O1) ̸= λ(O2) or O1 = O2. This
uniqueness guarantees that λ has a well-defined inverse, λ−1.
3.2
Semantics
Defining a formal operational semantics for BPMN requires a rigorous model of its funda-
mental workflow aspects. The main objective is to formally capture the synchronization
of various process elements and the progression of execution from a defined start point
to a valid end point. The challenge lies in creating a formalization that can handle com-
plex control-flow constructs, such as gateways, while remaining clear and exploitable for
analysis. Our approach addresses this by defining the semantics directly at the process
level.
We provide the semantics in terms of marked processes, where a process defined by the
syntax in previous section is equipped with a marking. A marking is a distribution of
tokens over the elements of a process, such as its sequence edges, events, and tasks. This
concept is analogous to the token-based semantics of Petri Nets, a formalism that heavily
inspired BPMN’s workflow constructs. In our approach, tokens act as program counters,
and their movement along the syntactic constructs of a process dictates its execution
state.
Therefore, the operational semantics is defined over an enriched version of the
syntax, where elements are labeled by zero or more tokens.

the framework of Structural Operational Semantics. This approach defines the behavior of
a process in terms of atomic transitions between states. A transition, denoted by P

The semantic rules governing events are defined as axioms, as they do not have any
premises. The rule for a start event initiates the process flow. It consumes the token
marking the start event node and produces a new token on its single outgoing sequence
edge, denoted as e. This action is captured by the transition label +e. The propagation
of this token to the corresponding incoming edge of the subsequent node is managed by
the interleaving rules, which will be detailed later.

execution commences. Conversely, the rule for an end event governs the conclusion of an
execution path. It consumes a token from its single incoming sequence edge, e, which
is represented by the transition label −e. It is important to note that this rule simply
consumes the incoming token; the end event itself never becomes marked. This behavior
ensures that the execution along that particular path formally terminates at the event and
does not proceed any further.

A XOR-Split takes one incoming token and activates exactly one outgoing path. A XOR-

The term T defines the structure of a process model, specifying the set of events (repre-

Visually, these correspond to the nodes and arcs of a DCR graph. The marking M cap-

• Condition e→•f: This relation establishes a prerequisite. Event f is prohibited
from executing until event e has happened.
• Response e•→f: This relation creates an obligation.
The execution of event e

(unless it becomes excluded).
• Inclusion e→+f: This relation controls the availability of an event. Executing e
makes event f included, adding it to the set of currently possible actions. This does
not, however, obligate its execution.
• Exclusion e→%f: This relation models irrelevance. When e is executed, event f is
excluded and can no longer be performed. Any conditions that depend on f (e.g.,
f→•g) are consequently invalidated until f is re-included.

5
Translation Approach
This chapter formally defines the translation from the previously introduced subset of
BPMN in chapter 3 to DCR Graphs in chapter 4. Following established practices in formal
language comparison[20], we define this translation as an encoding. An encoding of a source
language LBP into a target language LBP is a pair ([[·]], φ[[ ]]), where [[·]] : P BP →P DCR is
the translation function and φ[[ ]] : N BP →N DCR is the renaming policy. The translation
function maps terms from the source language to terms in the target language, while the
renaming policy handles the mapping of names or identifiers.
In our context, the translation, denoted [[·]], maps a well-formed BPMN process P BP to its
corresponding DCR process P DCR. The renaming policy, φ[[ ]], ensures that each unique
object O ∈fo(P BP) is mapped to a unique event eO ∈fe(P DCR) in the DCR graph,
preserving its semantic identity via the labelling function. Formally:
∀Oi, Oj ∈fo(P BP), φ[[ ]](Oi) = φ[[ ]](Oj) ⇐⇒Oi = Oj
This policy guarantees that distinct BPMN objects are translated into distinct DCR
events, preventing unintended fusion of process logic. For simplicity, we will leave the
renaming policy φ[[ ]] implicit and focus on the definition of the translation function [[·]].
The primary objective of this translation is to be behavioral-preserving[21]. This means
that for any well-formed BPMN process, the resulting DCR process must exhibit the exact
same observable behavior. The translation strategy is twofold. First, we establish a direct
mapping from BPMN’s graphical objects to their corresponding event counterparts in a
DCR graph. Second, we define a set of rules for converting BPMN’s control-flow logic—
represented by sequence flows—into a corresponding set of DCR relations that govern the
execution of the DCR events.
5.1

The foundational step of our translation is the mapping of each BPMN object to a unique
event within a DCR process term. For every object O ∈fo(P BP), where O is a task, event,
or gateway, we introduce a unique corresponding DCR event, which we can denote as eO,
into the set of free events of the target DCR process term T DCR.
Furthermore, given the inherent differences in the core mechanisms of the two modeling
languages, ensuring semantic equivalence necessitates the introduction of auxiliary ele-
ments within the DCR model. These elements have no direct counterpart in the source
BPMN specification; rather, they exist as a mechanism to preserve the strict execution
semantics of specific structures, particularly complex gateway combinations, after trans-
lation. As defined by the translation rules, these auxiliary elements accompany specific
BPMN patterns and are typically set to be non-triggerable in the normal execution flow of
the process. We will elaborate on the design, function, and specific implementation rules
for these auxiliary elements in a subsequent chapter.

Our primary concern is to establish a rigorous correspondence between the operational
semantics of BPMN and DCR. This is fundamental to formally demonstrating that our
translation is behavior-preserving, ensuring the resulting DCR model is a faithful and

To provide a practical implementation and validation of the translation framework pre-

panels, enabling users to dynamically adjust the display ratio to focus on either the
source BPMN model or the target DCR graph.

translation, users can perform interactive trace simulations directly on the generated
DCR graph. By clicking on enabled events, users can observe the process execution
step-by-step and verify that its behavior aligns with the source model.

ability to detect whether the current process marking constitutes an accepting
state. This is crucial for verifying that a process can terminate correctly without
any pending obligations.

The primary motivation for this work has been to bridge the gap between the widely
adopted, imperative paradigm of BPMN and the flexible, declarative approach of DCR,
thereby enabling the powerful formal analysis and verification capabilities of DCR to be
applied to the vast repository of existing BPMN models.
The core contribution of this research is a systematic encoding that not only maps BPMN
objects to DCR events but, crucially, uses a novel approach involving auxiliary state-
holding events to precisely translate the control-flow semantics of all gateway types. This
method ensures that the resulting DCR model is a faithful and analyzable representation
of the source BPMN process. The translation was presented through the development of
a web-based tool, bpmn2dcr-js, which provides real-time transformation and interactive
trace simulation, empirically demonstrating the behavioral equivalence between the source
BPMN models and the generated DCR graphs.
While this thesis has established a solid foundation for BPMN-to-DCR translation, several
avenues for future work have been identified that could further enhance the practical
applicability and scope of this research. Based on feedback and analysis, the following
areas are proposed for future research and development:
• Relaxing Constraints on Gateway Pairing: Further investigation into the con-
straints of the translation model could lead to a more flexible and powerful frame-
work. For example, the current model requires XOR gateways to be strictly paired.
However, it may be possible to relax this constraint with a few modifications to the
translation rules. This would not only make the translation more robust but could
also allow for the validation of a wider range of BPMN models, including those that
might not strictly adhere to the well-formedness conditions defined in this thesis.
This could potentially allow the initial example presented in the thesis to be judged
as valid, further expanding the scope of the translation.
• External Validation with Standard Process Models: To further validate the
practical applicability of the proposed encoding, future work should involve applying
the translation to a wider range of BPMN models from reputable, external sources. A
good starting point would be to use examples from well-known textbooks on business
process management. This would not only serve as a robust test of the translation’s
correctness but also demonstrate its “external validity” by showing its applicability
to real-world, industry-standard process models, rather than just custom-designed
examples.
• Support for Lanes within a Process Pool: A significant extension to the cur-
rent work would be to incorporate support for BPMN’s ”Lane” construct within a
single process pool. Currently, the translation focuses on the overall control flow of
a process, without distinguishing between different roles or departments involved.
By extending the model to support Lanes, the translation could also capture how
tasks are allocated to various participants. This would add a crucial layer of organi-
zational detail, allowing the resulting DCR graph to represent not only the sequence

==PAGE:52==
of activities but also who is responsible for executing them. This would require a
method for mapping Lane information to DCR event attributes or roles, thereby
enriching the analyzable output of the translation.
• Enhanced User Experience and Model Alignment: A key area for improve-
ment is the user interface of the bpmn2dcr-js tool. While the current implemen-
tation provides a real-time translation, the user experience could be significantly
enhanced by providing a more intuitive link between the source and target models.
For instance, implementing a feature where hovering over a DCR element highlights
the corresponding BPMN element (and vice-versa) would provide a much clearer and
more interactive way for users to understand the translation. Additionally, the cur-
rent label generation for gateway-events, which uses numerical identifiers not present
in the original BPMN model, can be confusing. Future work should explore methods
for generating more descriptive and context-aware labels, perhaps by incorporating
user-defined gateway names or other metadata from the BPMN model.
In conclusion, the work presented in this thesis provides a strong foundation for the auto-
mated, behavior-preserving translation of BPMN to DCR. By addressing the future work
outlined above, this research can be extended to create a more powerful, user-friendly, and
comprehensive tool for bridging the imperative and declarative worlds of business process
modeling.
