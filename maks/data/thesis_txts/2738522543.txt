Abstract
The current thesis examines the return spillover effects and connectedness under the
umbrella of the nexus ãCarbonôÙEnergyôÙFinanceã. The investigating variables are the EuôÙ
ropean Union Allowances on behalf of European carbon markets, the Brent crude oil
market accounts for the ãEnergyã, the European stock index, and four imbalance counôÙ
tries, namely, Greece, Portugal, Italy, and Spain with their national stock market being
the main variables, during from 2014 to 2023. The innovation of the project lies in the
gap in the existing literature, based on two branches. First, the absence of sufficient paôÙ
pers inspecting the nexus of ãCarbonôÙEnergyôÙFianceã and second the analysis of return
spillover and linkages among these markets under this system for the particular Southern
European countries is nonôÙexistent. Using the methodologies of the VAR model and its
diagnosing tests for further analysis of four different systems, one for each country, and
diagnosing tests for the robustness of the model to identify potential issues and deliver
accurate inferences. Utilizing an enhanced form of Diebold and Yilmazãs approach for dyôÙ
namic connectedness, based on the paper [1], it is crucial for directional connectedness
amongst the examining variables. The results show that the connectedness at the nexus,
of each country, is not that considerable and lies between 20%ôÙ35%, thus the rest of the
influence stems from exogenous events or the selfôÙinfluence of each market itself. All
the countries behave similarly. Italy is the one with the lowest percentage of information
transmission among the markets and Spain shows the most reliable and highest percentôÙ
age of connectedness at the nexus. For investors and policymakers, some implications
are defined so as to be ready for extreme events and future uncertainties.

Introduction
In recent years, an emerging necessity for investigating the connectedness of carbon
markets to other markets has been raised. Prior studies have proved the existence of
return and volatility spillover effects amongst carbon, energy, and stock markets in EuôÙ
rope and, to a great extent, worldwide [2, 3, 4]. Numerous studies have already been
conducted, with the ultimate goal of researching how the different markets interact and
how the information is transmitted among them [5, 6, 7].
In the present thesis project, the main objective is to examine the return spillovers and
the connectedness dynamics among the European Union Emission Trading System (EU
ETS), Brent oil markets, European stock index (STOXX600), and stock markets of the
four troubled members of the Eurozone known as the PIGS (Portugal, Italy, Greece, and
Spain). The project adds to the broader literature on the ãCarbonôÙEnergyôÙFinanceã nexus
by assessing the return spillovers across the three markets. Although many studies conôÙ
sider biôÙmarket links, the empirical literature on the connectedness of the three markets
simultaneously is sparse. The thesis studies these effects for the selected Southern EuôÙ
ropean countries from 2014 to 2023. Very few studies have examined the spillover efôÙ
fects in PIGS, particularly on the system of the three: carbon, energy, and finance. A
recent study on volatility spillover between oil and equity markets was conducted by [8],
which extensively examined this effect for vulnerable E.U. countries, including the PIGS
and another similar economy, Ireland. The study focused on the Brent crude oil and
stock markets under the umbrella of energy and finance, respectively. They found that
the spillover effect between these markets is not only timeôÙvarying, crisisôÙsensitive, and
frequencyôÙdependent but also that the larger economies, such as Italy and Spain, are net
risk transmitters. In contrast, smaller economies, such as Greece and Portugal, are net
receivers of risk. To the extent of our knowledge, no other paper has considered these
countries regarding the ãCarbonôÙEnergyôÙFinanceã nexus.

1.2
Relation of return spillovers among the markets
The linkages among the three markets have been analyzed by former papers from the
initialization of the EU ETS market. Interestingly, [11] have shown that the main drivers
are economic activities and policy issues. Also, energy prices and temperature events will
not be taken into consideration in the thesis. Another, significant result which is caused
by the previous research is not only that the carbon market has been shown to be highly
influenced by the oil market, but also that the ETS market might influence the conventional
energy market by improving its structure and as an extension reducing carbon emissions.
Later on, [12] have examined the relationship between the carbon market and crude oil
market through linear and nonôÙlinear Granger causality tests. The most relevant outcome
attributed to this project is the apparent linear relationship between the two. It is also
indicated that there has been a biôÙdirectional linear and nonôÙlinear relationship between
the two during a medium time scale, in the range of one week to one year, caused by
significant events and policy amendments. The research of [10] has highlighted there is
no significant volatility spillover between the carbon market and the Brent oil market as well
as there is a positive correlation between the two in less significant levels than coal and
natural gas. Also, there is an asymmetric impact of carbon price surge and diminish on the
Brent oil market. In addition, [13] have come up with evidence of linkages between carbonôÙ
finance markets demonstrating that return (information) spillovers prevail over volatility
(risk) spillovers as well as correlation depended on market uncertainty conditions, which
justifies the linkage due to common macroeconomic shocks.

Diagnostic tests for VAR models

Autocorrelation
To determine whether there are significant autocorrelations in the VAR models, based
on R packages, the Portmanteau test (asymptotic) where used. More specifically, the
null hypothesis tests that the autocorrelation up to a certain lag is zero, indicating that the
residuals from the models are white noise (have no autocorrelation). The rationale behind
the testing is governed by the sum of the squared autocorrelations of the residuals. The
problem of autocorrelation can be reduced by adding lags. Practically, in our case, if there
is evidence of rejecting the null hypothesis, provided that the estimated pôÙvalue is less
than the significance level (typically 0.05), then the main inference could be that there
are significant autocorrelations in the residual of the VAR models. Therefore, potential
reasoning could be translated as the inadequacy of the structure of our models [39].

Conclusion and Future work
In light of the current results, the existing literature in terms of investigating the nexus
of ãCarbonôÙEnergyôÙFinanceã for Southern European countries and given that research
for pairwise relation between carbonôÙoil, oilôÙfinance, and carbonôÙfinance have been previôÙ

combined. Therefore, further focus on this topic is needed.
Using VAR models for each country separately in order to diagnose their behavior in the
nexus of ãCarbonôÙEnergyôÙFinanceã. There are pieces of evidence of autocorrelation, but
this issue could be dealt with by adding more lags in the VAR models. The issue of staôÙ
tionarity is solved due to the transformation of raw data to log returns. Then, the RESET
test implies misspecification errors and underlying nonôÙlinearity in the VAR models, but
scatter plots in the Appendix do not prove it. Subsequently, the OLSôÙCUSUM test does
not provide any structural break for any of the examining countries. The generalized imôÙ
pulse response function results are not significant for all four systems providing inferences
for the scenario of random shocks from the three different markets to the inspecting counôÙ
tries. None of the results are strong enough to justify any significant response to any of
their shocks neither shortôÙterm nor longôÙterm.
Moreover, to examine the directional connectedness and influence of each market to anôÙ
other, the usage of an enhanced model of Diebold and Yilmazãs approach [1] is impleôÙ
mented. Using as a main scenario a window size of 200 trading days, due to the daily
data curation for the thesisãs objectives, the interconnectedness and the total connectôÙ
edness index of variables are revealed. Then, a robust analysis occurs by altering the
window size, to foster the inferences of the study. Finally, some implications are given
to investors and policymakers for ameliorating the current situation and applying decisive
strategies for further understanding all the crucial relations inside the nexus.
A significant observation might be the movements of the variable STOXX600, as it repôÙ
resents the overall economic conditions in Europe. Thus, the results of the current study
confirm that economic conditions are the main drivers of country stock returns. What is
more, the low connectedness of PIGS returns to the oil market indicates that investors
who want to hedge their portfolios against energy price risks can invest in these assets.
Finally, the marginal connectedness of PIGS returns to EUA returns implies that country
stock markets are not affected much by climate policy uncertainties.
As a general outcome from the thesis towards investors and policyôÙmakers is described
as follows. For the investors, the best strategy to be followed is the diversification of portôÙ
folios as there is considerable uncertainty for the investigating nexus due to changes in
carbon markets and opposite results in account for the Brent oil market towards the stock
markets based on the literature. Another reason is that given the current result of the
sharpest peak during COVIDôÙ19, which is translated to intense interconnections among
markets during extreme events, investors should pay attention to where they ought to inôÙ
vest based on their calculated risks and the macroeconomic imbalances of the countries
under examination. For policymakers, the deployment and upgrade of carbon markets
is an important step given the fact that they do not provide sufficient information comôÙ
pared to the rest of the examined variables under the umbrella of the nexus. Eventually,
because carbon prices are determined by their own factors, as all the variables, policyôÙ
makers should pay great attention to past information on carbon prices combined with the
amendments in the regulation as well.
