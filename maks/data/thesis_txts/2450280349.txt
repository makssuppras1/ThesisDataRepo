Summary
As one of rapidly developing technologies of today, the Internet of Things is
as relevant as ever, allowing diﬀerent kinds of devices gather data, communi-
cate with one another, and contribute to the world where computing can be
everywhere and anytime. This makes the challenges concerning IoT security
indubitably important.
One of the solutions addressing this critical issue is AntibIoTic, which utilizes
the novel concept of Fog computing in order to defend the IoT in a secure and
eﬃcient manner.
This thesis analyses the latest version of AntibIoTic, aiming to relax one of the
assumptions stated in its design. As a result, a list of problems is comprised,
one of which - software protection - is selected to focus on. This is followed by
conducting a systematic literature review in order to obtain potential solutions
to the problem. The review results in obtaining a list of scientiﬁc articles which
are studied, systematized, and summarized, and from which relevant approaches
are extracted. The identiﬁed approaches are then analyzed with respect to their
strengths and weaknesses, followed by proposed countermeasures to the latter,
and ﬁnally concluded by recommendations of combinations of approaches which
are believed to be both feasible and reasonable improvements of the system.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The Internet of Things (IoT) enables diﬀerent types of devices to gather data,
communicate with each other, and provide various services contributing to mak-
ing computing as omnipresent as ever. As relevance and reliance on this technol-
ogy grows, so do the risks of it being exploited. The fact that many IoT devices
lack reasonable levels of security resulted in some serious attacks in the past,
such as a notorious series of Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks caused
by Mirai malware [KKS17] which had been able to create a botnet capable of
bringing down large DNS providers [Jer17].
This and many other attacks motivated development of security measures to pro-
tect inherently vulnerable IoT devices. Gaining inspiration from the concept of
Mirai, one project had proposed a "white worm" called AntibIoTic [DDDGM18]
which aims to secure IoT devices by the means of exploiting their lack of security
in order to infect and protect them.
Since this approach has been shown to have some legal issues due to inﬁltrating
and aﬀecting devices without their consent, another project was carried out
[DF18, DDD19] which uses the novel paradigm of Fog computing to facilitate
security of IoT devices in a legal and eﬃcient manner. Although the new design
increases the feasibility of the solution, there are certain questions that remain
unanswered. One of those relates to the assumption related to the security of
the communications within the system: it is assumed that all interactions within
==PAGE:14==

the system are trusted, as well as that the AntibIoTic software installed on IoT
devices is not compromised; however, to implement a system like that in the
real world this assumption would need to be relaxed, and potentially removed.
1.1
Goals
The goal of this work is to ﬁnd a way to relax or, if possible, remove the as-
sumption regarding the security of the communications within AntibIoTic.
The subgoals can thus be deﬁned as follows:
• Study and analyze the latest version of AntibIoTic to determine the exact
nature of the problem we are trying to solve and identify issues which
must be dealt with in order to alleviate reliance on the assumption. Select
one or multiple of the identiﬁed issues to focus on.
• Conduct a systematic literature review in order to obtain approaches which
could potentially be used to solve the selected problems.
• Study, analyze, and compare the obtained approaches to determine their
relevance and applicability to AntibIoTic.
• Potentially propose a set of recommendations aimed at enhancing design
of AntibIoTic using the results of the analysis.

Chapter 2
Background
In this chapter some relevant concepts used throughout the thesis are explained
in order to enable understanding of the discussions to come.
Since this work revolves around the Internet of Things (IoT), we start by describ-
ing its deﬁnition, features, applications, and challenges. We proceed by giving
an overview of the paradigm of Fog computing, which is an essential part of the
idea behind AntibIoTic. Finally, we present the latest version of AntibIoTic,
including the main idea, applications, design, and assumptions made.
2.1
The Internet of Things
The goal of AntibIoTic is securing Internet of Things (IoT) devices. In order to
understand why this task is relevant, we deﬁne the concept of IoT and highlight
some relevant features and challenges it faces.
==PAGE:18==
6
Background
2.1.1
Deﬁnition
Internet of Things can be deﬁned in many diﬀerent ways, and various attempts
to do so can be traced in literature. For example, RFID group deﬁned IoT as
"a world-wide network of interconnected objects uniquely addressable, based on
standard communication protocols" [BH08]. Atzori et al. [AIM10] discuss that
IoT can be approached from "Internet oriented", "Thing oriented" or "Semantic
oriented" perspective, where the ﬁrst one pushes toward a network oriented
vision, while the second one moves the focus on generic "objects", or "things",
and the third one - on the technologies enabling processing and working with
data obtained from IoT devices. Gubbi et al. [GBMP13] argue that the full
usefulness of IoT can be utilized only when all three perspectives intersect.
Roman et al. [RZL13] give the following deﬁnition of IoT: "A worldwide network
of interconnected entities".
They then elaborate that, in most cases, those
entities, also called "things" (humans, computers, appliances, and so on) have an
addressable counterpart on the Internet, and are able to "open a communication
channel with any other entity, providing and receiving services at any time, any
place, and in any way".
Combining several deﬁnitions [BH08,RZL13,Int12], we can summarize that the
Internet of Things refers to interconnecting physical and virtual things in order
to enable various services using existing and evolving communication technolo-
gies.
2.1.2
Features
Features of IoT can vary depending on the source, but are generally agreed
[Bor14,AIM17,RZL13,VDL+16] to include the following items:
• Heterogeneity. The wide variety of technologies, environments, services
and devices should be able to coexist within the Internet of Things.
• Scalability. As the number of devices and data generated and processed
within IoT grows quickly, scalability is a crucial feature to ensure.
• Cost minimization. Large number of devices and the frequency of com-
munication between them calls for optimization of their maintenance and
operational costs.
• Everyday objects as Things.
Everyday objects are participants of
the Internet of Things, and not only classical computing/communication
==PAGE:19==

devices.
• Constrained resources.
Many things within IoT tend to be of lim-
ited power and memory. This has several implications for their security
capabilities.
2.1.3
Applications
There is a vast number of application domains for Internet of Things. This is
largely thanks to its ability to both collect relevant information (e.g. about the
environment) and oﬀer corresponding tailored services (e.g. regulating bright-
ness of lights or air temperature in a smart home) [Bor14]. The sheer scale and
diversity of applications for IoT can be observed in ﬁgure 2.1 [Bor14]. We will
highlight some of them.
• Product lifetime management (industrial domain). An RFID can
be placed on a product in order to facilitate tracking during its life-
time [VVM+12].
This can be used to, for example, improve inventory
management in a warehouse or a retail store, or to trace a product through-
out its supply-chain. Smart shelves in supermarkets in combination with
products with RFID tags can be used to collect and analyze data on de-
mand of a particular item, which they can then use to predict which goods
need to be ordered at which times [Bor14].
• Smart mobility (smart city domain). IoT can guide processes lead-
ing to transforming our cities into smart cities.
A combination of IoT
technologies, such as sensors, cameras, and many others, will process the
information which can be used to manage various parts of the city. Ex-
amples include smart parking systems guiding drivers towards the closest
available parking spot; mobile sensors attached to vehicles, which could
collect data about the roads and traﬃc conditions [Bor14]; and sensors
built into infrastructure to monitor its status and estimate need for main-
tenance [GBMP13].
• Medical and healthcare (medical domain). Using body area sensors
combined with data infrastructure makes IoT the platform suitable for
implementing ubiquitous healthcare [AIM10,GBMP13]. Parameters such
as temperature and blood pressure can be tracked real-time and trans-
mitted to doctors for diagnosis and health monitoring. Sensors situated
around hospitals can streamline their energy consumption and safety man-
agement systems. Material identiﬁcation is another example of applying
IoT to medical domain. Smart RFID labels placed on medical equipment

can help to ensure that the equipment is not lost or even forgotten inside
a patient during an operation [Bor14].

2.1.4
Challenges
It is natural that with myriad of beneﬁts brought to us by IoT, there are some
challenges and open issues that need to be taken into account. Some of these,
as outlined in various sources [Dav14,CZ16], are:
• Internet availability. Clearly, the global success of IoT relies on Internet
being available everywhere. This implies not only eﬃcient coverage, but
also low cost of Internet connection [Dav14].
• Power consumption.
The large numbers of IoT devices around the
world imply the increased demand for power. It would thus be of great
==PAGE:21==
2.2 Fog computing
9
beneﬁt to take into account several factors when designing both IoT de-
vices and their sources of energy, such as environmental impact, life span,
battery types used, and so on [Dav14].
• Resource-constrained devices. Many IoT devices, such as sensors, cer-
tain medical devices, drones, and others, will have a limited computational
ability. This severely limits their ability to fulﬁll certain computationally-
heavy tasks, such as performing cryptographic operations in order to fetch
updates from cloud services [CZ16].
• Latency requirements. Various IoT applications require very low la-
tencies for the information ﬂowing from and to the IoT device. Examples
include smart grids and goods packaging systems with latency require-
ments within a few milliseconds [WJSN14]; and vehicle-to-vehicle/vehicle-
to-roadside communications and drone ﬂight control with potential latency
requirements below a few tens of milliseconds [CZ16].
• Security issues. The security solutions aimed at protecting today’s In-
ternet are not well suited for defending IoT devices. Examples include:
– Keeping software and credentials up to date on a large number of
devices
– Protecting devices whose lack of resources makes it infeasible for them
to secure themselves
– Ensuring security of large number of devices simultaneously, some of
which operate within physically unprotected environments and can
thus be compromised
– Responding to incidents without shutting down the entire system,
but instead maintaining its safe operation [CZ16].
2.2
Fog computing
The latest version of AntibIoTic relies on Fog computing in order to facilitate
securing IoT devices. We thus provide the deﬁnition of Fog computing, as well
as discuss its features, challenges, applications, and relation to IoT.
2.2.1
Deﬁnition
Fog computing is a rather new concept closely related to Cloud and IoT. In
fact, it is often deﬁned utilizing those concepts.
Authors of one of the ﬁrst
==PAGE:22==
10
Background
papers mentioning the term "Fog computing" [BMZA12] say that Fog makes
new types of applications and services possible by extending the paradigm of
Cloud computing to the edge of the network. They proceed to provide the fol-
lowing deﬁnition: "Fog Computing is a highly virtualized platform that provides
compute, storage, and networking services between end devices and traditional
Cloud Computing Data Centers, typically, but not exclusively located at the edge
of network.".
Another paper, published 2 years later [VRMR14] deﬁnes it as "..a scenario
where a huge number of heterogeneous (wireless and sometimes autonomous)
ubiquitous and decentralized devices communicate and potentially cooperate among
them and with the network to perform storage and processing tasks without the
intervention of third parties. These tasks can be for supporting basic network
functions or new services and applications that run in a sandboxed environment.
Users leasing part of their devices to host these services get incentives for doing
so." The authors argue that this covers key characteristics of the Fog, such as
ubiquity, support for cooperation between devices, and improved network ca-
pabilities, and add that some could consider the Fog just an "extension" of the
Cloud.
Chiang et al. [CZ16] deﬁne Fog as "an architecture that distributes computation,
communication, control and storage closer to the end users along the cloud-to-
things continuum", reasoning that both the inadequacy of the traditional Cloud
and new opportunities for IoT justify the relevance of Fog. They proceed to
argue that although the Cloud has been playing an important role over the past
decade, it now faces diﬃculties in meeting many of the requirements posed by
IoT. This in combination with growing number of devices operating on the edge
of the network, such as smartphones, smart home appliances, connected vehicles,
and others, motivated ﬁnding a solution which would bring some features of the
Cloud "closer" to end-users, to enable low latency, mobility, local awareness,
and other properties.
Combining all of those deﬁnitions [BMZA12,VRMR14,CZ16] we can summarize
that the Fog is a platform located between the Cloud and the end devices, at
or close to the edge of the network, with the aim of providing various services
to those devices. A simplistic diagram of the Fog can be observed in ﬁgure
2.2. While the ﬁgure depicts only one layer of Fog devices, an architectures
has been proposed [C+17] which structures that layer with several tiers, where
Fog devices (also called nodes) located closer to the IoT layer are less powerful
and work with collecting data from IoT devices, while those located closer to
the Cloud are more powerful, operate in closer interaction with Cloud servers,
and are aimed at aggregating and interpreting data received from lower tiers.
Fog nodes can thus communicate both horizontally (within the same tier) to
provide additional features (e.g. load balancing), and vertically (with higher or
==PAGE:23==
2.2 Fog computing
11
lower tiers) to pass information in both directions along the Cloud to Things
continuum [DDD19].
Figure 2.2: A simple representation of Fog
2.2.2
Features
Features of Fog are generally agreed to be addressing certain problems of the
traditional Cloud with regards to IoT requirements, and include the following
[BMZA12,CZ16] :
• Low latency. Many IoT applications, such as the ones described in 2.1.4,
demand lower latencies than available with the use of the Cloud. By being
located close to the end users, Fog aims to solve this problem.
• Geographical distribution. It is an expectation that Fog devices have
a wide geographical distribution in order to be able to satisfy the demand
for low latency and support applications such as moving vehicles.
• Very large number of nodes. A wide geographical distribution implies
having a very large number of nodes.
• Mobility support.
As many IoT applications include mobile devices
(moving vehicles, etc.), Fog is naturally expected to support them.
• Real-time interactions. As opposed to the Cloud, which tends to pro-
cess large amounts of information, thus taking more time, Fog nodes are
focused on applications involving real-time interactions.
==PAGE:24==
12
Background
• Integration with the Cloud. Being located between the Cloud and
the Things, Fog should be able to decide in which case the service (e.g.
a computation) must be addressed by Fog devices, and in which sent for
execution to the Cloud.
2.2.3
Applications
Applications of Fog tend to have the aim of solving the problems of IoT which
are diﬃcult to address with other computing models, such as the Cloud. Some of
these problems were described in 2.1.4. More speciﬁc examples of applications,
as described in literature [BMZA12,YLL15,YHQL16], include:
• Connected vehicle. Many properties of the Fog enable it to be a plat-
form of choice when it comes to supporting smart vehicles with the demand
of connectivity and interactions with other vehicles and devices such as
smart traﬃc lights. The latter is an example in the context of a smart
city, where a smart traﬃc light system would analyze the world around it
with the help of sensors and gather information on approaching vehicles,
bikers, and people. This information would then be shared with other
smart traﬃc lights, as well as vehicles, in order to enable a smooth and

traﬃc light timings, thus enabling real-time interaction.
• Augmented reality. AR-based applications typically need to analyze
video in real time and produce corresponding output with next to no

satisfy those requirements. Fog nodes could be delegated video processing
tasks, and being located close to the devices would ensure low latency.
• Smart home. Fog computing can be used to combine smart devices from
diﬀerent manufacturers. For instance, a smart security system may consist
of various sensors, cameras, locks, robots, and other components. Even
when those devices come from diﬀerent vendors, they should be able to
operate together in a seamless fashion. Thanks to Fog, it is possible to
provide those devices with uniﬁed interface, ﬂexible resources and real-

devices within a smart home, where the data collected by them would be
analyzed by the Fog node and used to undertake the most logical actions.

in the living room, the Fog node could initiate live video processing of

2.2.4
Challenges
Some of the open issues of Fog computing, as highlighted in several sources
[VRMR14,CZ16], include:
• Task allocation. With the introduction of Fog computing, it becomes
necessary to determine which tasks and processes must be tackled by the
Fog, Cloud, and IoT devices, as well as how all of these should interact
with each other.
• Security. Since Fog nodes are expected to have a wide geographical dis-
tribution and be located closer to the end users, it makes them more vul-
nerable than traditional Cloud servers, which are usually heavily guarded.
• Decentralized management. Applications and devices running with
the help of Fog computing need to be setup and conﬁgured, which is a
challenge due to a large number of devices. This means that some kind
of decentralized management mechanisms must be utilized to make this
possible.
• Programmability. Considering the scale of the Fog, it becomes impor-
tant to provide suitable abstractions in order to allow developers to focus
on their tasks instead of having to deal with the complexity of the Fog.

Background
2.3.1
The idea
The basic goal of AntibIoTic is ensuring security of IoT devices. The original
design has been described in [DDDGM18] and focused on identifying vulnerable
IoT devices on the Internet and utilizing their vulnerabilities as a means to
secure them. In particular, it was envisioned as a white worm which would scan
the Internet for vulnerable IoT devices, followed by infecting and securing them.
While this idea had merit, it was shown that it exhibited certain legal issues
due to inﬁltrating and potentially altering conﬁguration of devices without their
consent.
Based on the concept of Fog computing brieﬂy described above it was possible
to design a version of AntibIoTic which utilizes beneﬁts of the Fog in order
to protect IoT devices from malware and other dangers of the Internet while
avoiding the legal issues present in the original design. This has been the primary
goal of the thesis by Juan Manuel Donaire Felipe [DF18] and has led to the latest
version of the system.
The main idea behind the latest design of AntibIoTic is using Fog nodes in order
to make sure that only secure devices are allowed to connect to the Internet.
It is accomplished by equipping Fog nodes with an antivirus (aka AntibIoTic
bot), which is installed on the IoT devices connecting to the node with the goal
of scanning them, detecting malware, and ﬁxing vulnerabilities before allowing
the devices to connect to the Internet. The IoT devices thus have to allow being
scanned and sanitized in order to establish and maintain Internet connection.
[DF18]
2.3.2
Features
The main features of AntibIoTic, as described in [DF18,DDD19], are as follows:
• Relevant data collection. The AntibIoTic bot is expected to run on
many diﬀerent IoT devices and report back, thus gathering valuable data,
such as most vulnerable devices, their technical speciﬁcations, common
attacks, secure ﬁrmware versions, new types of malware signatures, and
so on.
• User transparency. AntibIoTic is meant to operate without user inter-
action except in certain scenarios.
• Ease of setup. It is possible to deploy AntibIoTic both on those devices
which were designed to run the solution and on those which were not.

• Device sanitation. The AntibIoTic bot is running in all devices con-

• Vulnerable device protection. The AntibIoTic bot is performing vari-

• Solution ﬂexibility. AntibIoTic is designed to protect devices with dif-

• Collect information which could be of use to the bot, such as new types

• Provide the system administrator the ability to keep track of the network

2.3.4.5
Operation modes
AntibIoTic supports a number of operation modes allowing adjusting the system
based on the requirements of the environment in which it is setup. The motiva-
tion here is that diﬀerent situations call for diﬀerent security and connectivity
needs. The modes include the following, as described by [DF18,DDD19]:
• Lockdown. This is the most restrictive operation mode. When it is set,
only the devices completely secured by the AntibIoTic bot are allowed to
access the Internet. It can be used when it is desired to have the highest
security level, and when it is considered more acceptable to mistakenly
block a secure device from accessing the Internet rather than grant it to
an insecure device.
• Moderate. In this mode, the bot attempts to secure the device, and even
if it does not fully succeed or is uncertain of its success, the device is still
allowed to access the Internet. Only those devices which are not able to
run the bot or have been shown to possess some severe vulnerabilities are
blocked from accessing the Internet. This mode can be used when security
is desired but not crucial.
• Lenient. This is the least restrictive mode. When it is set, all the devices
running the AntibIoTic bot are able to access the Internet, even if they
are not fully secure. This mode can be used when the connectivity of the
device is of utmost importance.
These operation modes are the ones suggested in the original design; however,
it is expected that more will be added to the system as it continues to grow.

2.3.5
Assumptions
Certain assumptions had to be made when designing the system [DF18,DDD19]:
1. The Fog node is trusted. Fog node concerns itself with granting/denying
the devices access to the Internet, as well as with making sure they have
the latest version of AntibIoTic installed. It is thus of utmost importance
that it is not compromised, as that may imply the collapse of the entire
system.
2. Communication between the Fog node and the AntibIoTic bot
is secure. The Fog node must communicate with the bot on a regular
basis in order to assess security level of the device, update the bot, and
grant/deny the device Internet access. It is thus assumed that the inter-
actions between the node and the bot are trusted, as well as that the bot
is not compromised.
3. Fog node is the only gateway. Since the solution is built on the idea
of using the Fog to control device access to the Internet, it makes sense to
assume that the only way the devices can access the Internet is through
the Fog node.
As mentioned earlier, in this project we concern ourselves with relaxing the
second assumption. In the next chapter we analyze the system, determining the
problems occurring when the assumption no longer holds, and come up with a
list of objectives required to solve those problems.

Problem deﬁnition
In this chapter we aim to grasp what achieving secure communication within

3.1
Relaxing the assumption
Relaxing an assumption typically involves determining the consequences of it
ceasing to be valid. (For instance, [Uri13] relaxes some linear regression model
related assumptions by examining the problems posed by their failure.) To this
aim, this section presents some examples of various kinds of malicious behavior
which becomes possible when the assumption about the security of communica-
tion within AntibIoTic no longer holds. Note, that we do not aim to provide an
exhaustive list of all possible risks, but rather highlight some of them in order
to demonstrate the need for a solution.
We begin by analyzing the dangers introduced to the communication channel
itself, referring to the interactions between the AntibIoTic bot and the Fog node.

Problem deﬁnition
We proceed with taking a look at the AntibIoTic software and its behavior when
installed and active on an IoT device. Finally, we describe other issues surfacing

3.1.1
Interactions
We analyze both ﬂows/communication directions (IoT device/AntibIoTic bot
to Fog node and Fog node to IoT device/AntibIoTic bot) separately, due to the
fact that diﬀerent types of information are being passed in each direction. For
each piece of information transmitted within the ﬂow (e.g. keep-alive messages
sent from the AntibIoTic bot to the Fog node) we describe its role and potential
value to an attacker in case he was able to conduct a man-in-the-middle attack,
resulting in him being able to observe and possibly modify the data.
3.1.1.1
IoT device/AntibIoTic bot to the Fog node
First of all, we consider the communication ﬂow from an IoT device to the Fog
node. The functions of this ﬂow are: sending vulnerability reports, keep-alive
messages, and status messages. Each of those pieces of data have diﬀerent role
and value to an attacker:
• Keep-alive messages
Role: being sent by the AntibIoTic bot in order to conﬁrm that it is func-
tional.
Value to an attacker: ﬁnding out that a device has an instance of An-
tibIoTic bot running on it does not pose a danger in itself. However, an
attacker could be interested in re-transmitting keep-alive messages, which
would be useful if he e.g. wanted to kill the AntibIoTic bot without alert-
ing the Fog node.
• Status messages
Role: being sent by the AntibIoTic bot in order to inform the Fog node
of the safety status of the device.
Value to an attacker: obtaining the safety status of a device would give
the attacker an advantage of knowing whether the device (according to
the AntibIoTic bot) is safe, unsafe, or of uncertain safety. This could be
abused to evaluate potential for further attacks: for instance, knowing
that the device is of uncertain safety and guessing that the Fog node is
in moderate operation mode (described in 2.3.4.5) would make it an
==PAGE:39==
3.1 Relaxing the assumption
27
attractive target for an attack, since in this case a potentially unsecure
device is connected to the Internet. The attacker could also modify status
messages to make the Fog node mistakenly believe that a safe device is
unsafe, or vice versa. These attacks could be combined by e.g. discovering
that a device is considered unsafe and modifying the status message to
contain "safe" instead, which would result in an unsecure device being
connected to the Internet.
• Reports
Role: being sent by the AntibIoTic bot in order to inform the Fog node
of the information collected over a certain period of time, such as vulner-
abilities found and actions performed.
Value to an attacker: the contents of the report may contain information
on vulnerabilities found on the device, as well as security actions per-
formed by the bot. This would give the attacker an easy access to device
vulnerabilities. This could be directly abused in case the IoT device being
analyzed is connected to the Internet while having some unﬁxed vulner-
abilities present, which could be possible if the device must always be
online (e.g. an alarm system) and lenient operation mode is used [DF18].
And just like with status messages, the attacker could modify the existing
or craft a new wrongful report, resulting in the Fog node being mistaken
about the status of the device, which could result in the device being
allowed on the Internet while having vulnerabilities regardless of the op-
eration mode used.

• Observing status messages, reports, and potentially special actions;

• Re-transmitting keep-alive messages, status messages and reports.
As a consequence, at least the following dangers are associated with having an

• Data interception. An adversary might be interested in intercepting
data exchanged between the entities. The motivation here could be that
if an adversary was able to see the status of the device, it would give him
the information regarding whether the bot is functional or not (keep-alive
messages) and the current status of the device, as well as the contents of

the device).
• Data modiﬁcation. A malicious entity might want to modify data ex-

sary was able to modify the messages (keep-alive/status) or the reports, it
would lead to her being able to falsify the information contained in those,
which could lead to the Fog node either mistakenly accepting a vulnerable
device as secure, and thus granting it access to the Internet, which would
mean that a vulnerable device is online; or denying a secure device access
to the Internet, which would mean that a perfectly safe device is not able
to go online.
• Data falsiﬁcation. An attacker might send her own information to the
Fog node pretending to be a certain IoT device, essentially performing a
spooﬁng attack. This could have similar implications to "data modiﬁca-
tion" issue described above.
• Data re-transmission. An attacker might be interested in re-transmitting
certain messages exchanged between the Fog node and the AntibIoTic bot.
The motivation could be that if an attacker was able to re-transmit keep-
alive/status messages sent from the bot to the Fog node, he could kill the
AntibIoTic bot while convincing the Fog node that the device is still being
secured.

associated with the risk of being able to tamper with the AntibIoTic code at

• Software tampering. A skilled attacker might be capable of tampering

3.1.3
Reports
One of important actions performed by the AntibIoTic bot, as mentioned in
2.3.4.4, is generating and updating reports containing information on sanitizing
and securing actions performed. We have discussed how such reports (when sent
to the Fog node to make a decision regarding allowing or denying the IoT device
access to the Internet) could be in danger of being viewed and modiﬁed by an
attacker conducting a man-in-the-middle attack, given that the communication
channel is not protected.
However, it is also important to emphasize that reports are not sent to the Fog
node right after being generated. As described in 2.3.4.4, the Fog node only
requests such a report whenever safety status of the device has been changed.
This implies that, for an uncertain period of time, the report remains stored on
the IoT device. (In the current implementation, the report is stored as a simple
text ﬁle on the device.) Therefore, it is possible that someone having obtained
access to the device could read and/or modify its contents.

4.1.1
Overview
Relevant sources [SB12,KMVOV96,BRST16] were studied to write the rest of
this section, aiming to provide an overview and highlight the key diﬀerences of

4.1.1.1
Conﬁdentiality
Commonly encryption techniques are utilized in order to ensure the property
of conﬁdentiality. In the context of AntibIoTic, we are interested in ﬁnding an
encryption scheme which would not only be secure, but also lightweight enough,
due to the fact that we are dealing with IoT devices, many of which may be of
limited memory capacity and computational power.
There exist symmetric cryptography techniques, where a single key is used
to both encrypt and decrypt information sent from one entity to another; and
asymmetric cryptography techniques, where the so-called public key is used
to encrypt the information, and the private key to decrypt it. The clear advan-
tage of asymmetric cryptography techniques is the fact that the public key can
be known by everyone as deriving the corresponding private key from it is con-
sidered infeasible. To achieve this property, various mathematical problems are
used, such as diﬃculty of factorization of large integers, solving discrete loga-
rithm problem, and some others. One disadvantage of asymmetric cryptography
is that keys typically have to be of longer size in order to achieve security lev-
els similar to symmetric encryption. Moreover, the encryption process is much
slower than that of symmetric encryption.
There exist diﬀerent types of symmetric encryption algorithms (ciphers), some
of which are classiﬁed as block ciphers, and others as stream ciphers. Block
ciphers, as the name suggests, operate on blocks of bits, while stream ciphers
operate on single digits, often bits, by combining them with the corresponding
digits of the keystream (a sequence of random digits, generated from a key).
Since data size on which block ciphers operate is often larger than the size of
one block, it often has to be divided into multiple blocks. There are multiple
techniques tackling this, the simplest of which (Electronic Code Book, or ECB)
simply separates the plaintext into a number of blocks and encrypts each of them
with the same key. The disadvantage of this is that encrypting same plaintext
blocks results in the same ciphertext blocks, making analysis easier, especially
in applications whose data is highly structured, such as images. To address this,
various modes of operation are used.
==PAGE:51==
4.1 Secure communication channel
39
Stream ciphers are typically faster than block ciphers. They are most often used
for applications which demand encryption of streams of data, such as browser
and video streaming.
Block ciphers are more often utilized for applications
dealing with blocks of data, such as emails and databases.
They are more
common than stream ciphers.
While stream ciphers are typically faster than block ciphers, they do not pro-
vide properties of integrity and authentication. Although block ciphers do not
provide those properties by default either, there exist operating modes using
block ciphers in combination with other techniques, which provide both of these
properties. This means that it could be advantageous to pick a block cipher for
ensuring conﬁdentiality within AntibIoTic if we intended to use this approach.
An example would be CCM and GCM block cipher modes, both of which provide
encryption and authentication and are approved by NIST [MBTM17].
Some block ciphers designed with the intention of being lightweight include:
PRESENT [BKL+07], Hummingbird [EXG+10], CLEFIA [SSA+], and Simon/Speck
[BSS+15]. Out of those, PRESENT and CLEFIA are approved by NIST in
ISO/IEC 29192-2, while Simon and Speck are both proposed to be included for
the approval in the next revision of the standard [MBTM17].
4.1.1.2
Integrity and mutual-authentication
Various types of techniques exist which can be used to ensure integrity and
mutual-authentication.
Hash functions are able to map data of arbitrary
length to data of some ﬁxed size, called hash, checksum, or digest.
Special
kinds of hash functions adhering to certain properties (e.g. infeasibility to ﬁnd
another message resulting in the same hash) are used in cryptography. To check
integrity of a piece of data, its hash is computed at some point in time, and
later recomputed to conﬁrm that the piece of data has not been altered. This,
however, does not provide authentication. One of techniques providing both
integrity and authentication is Message Authentication Codes(MACs). In
this case, a special key must be shared by the entities desiring to communicate,
which is used to generate and check MACs - special blocks of data - for each
message sent. Whenever an entity wants to send a message, it calculates the
MAC of the message using the shared key. The message together with the MAC
is then sent to the recipient. Since the recipient possesses the same key as the
sender, it is able to compute the MAC of the received message and compare it
with the one received, ensuring that the message has not been modiﬁed. Here,
both the integrity and authentication is ensured, as long as the secret key used
to calculate MACs is only known by the communicating parties.
==PAGE:52==
40
Preliminary analysis
Another technique is digital signatures, which utilize asymmetric cryptog-
raphy to allow entities to generate signatures which can be attached to the
sent messages in order to provide properties of integrity, authenticity, and non-
repudiation (not being able to deny having sent a message). Private keys are
used to sign messages, and public keys can be used to check the validity of the
signatures.
In general, the diﬀerence between those two techniques is that in MACs all the
entities which possess the key are able to both generate and verify the validity
of the messages, while in digital signatures only one entity is able to generate
signatures, and an unlimited number of entities are able to verify their validity.
The additional property of non-repudiation provided by digital signatures is not
an immediate requirement within this project. For ensuring a secure channel
between an IoT device and a Fog node, we do not require every entity being able
to verify the validity of the messages exchanged on the channel. This should
only be accomplished by the entities participating in the communication, which
in our case are the AntibIoTic bot running on the device and the Fog node.
Based on those factors only, using MACs seems to be an optimal choice for en-
suring integrity and mutual-authentication for AntibIoTic. However, the ability
to sign messages could be useful for ensuring, for instance, authentication of en-
tities during the initial key exchange. Moreover, although outside the scope of
this thesis, it could be desired to provide an authentication mechanism between
multiple devices, where the non-repudiation property provided by digital signa-
tures could prove useful. Clearly, the trade-oﬀbetween higher computational
costs of using asymmetric cryptography and the need for it must be taken into
account.
Various types of MAC algorithms exist, such as, for instance, CBC-MAC and
OMAC constructed from block cipher algorithms, as well as HMAC, constructed
with the use of hash functions; and UMAC, which makes use of universal hash-
ing, where a random hash function is used to calculate the MAC.
If we decided to choose a block cipher to implement within AntibIoTic, it could
potentially make sense to make use of block cipher based MAC algorithms, or,
even better, and as mentioned at the end of 4.1.1.1, of block cipher modes of op-
eration which incorporate MAC in order to provide integrity and authentication,
such as CCM (Counter with CBC-MAC) mode.

4.1.1.3
Replay protection
There are multiple ways commonly used to achieve replay protection.
They
include attaching one of the following to the transmitted messages:
• Nonces, which are unique values generated by the sender. The receiver
can keep a history of all received nonces, thus being able to identify a
replayed message.
• Counters.
Each message transmitted within a session can include a
counter increasing by a predeﬁned value with each message sent. A new
message is then only accepted if its counter is greater than that of the last
valid message.
• Timestamps. If both participants have synchronized clocks, timestamps
can provide a natural way of detecting replayed messages.
Clearly, there are advantages and disadvantages to each of these options. Keep-
ing track of nonces implies having to store them in memory, which might be
rather limited in case of an embedded device. Moreover, while nonces provide
replay protection, messages can be reordered or delayed without consequence
(as nonce only shows whether the message has already been processed and does
not provide any information on the order of the messages).
Counters, on the other hand, also have to be kept in memory, although clearly
smaller amount of it is required when compared with nonces. They provide
protection against both replay and reorder attacks, albeit delayed messages
cannot be detected.
Finally, timestamps allow detecting replay, reorder, and delay attacks. However,
the demand for having synchronized clocks on the communicating parties is not
always possible to satisfy.

• Choosing whether to use a block or a stream cipher;

4.2
Secure key distribution
In order to make use of techniques enabling encryption and authentication,
as listed in the previous section, we inevitably need to utilize one or multiple
secret keys. The question then is: how do we obtain a secret key and make
sure it is available both to the IoT device and the Fog node participating in the
communication?
Several sources [NLO15, EG02, DH76] were studied in order to compose an
overview of this problem.
4.2.1
Overview
A good overview on secure communication protocols for IoT has been composed
by K.T. Nguyen et al. [NLO15], with the focus on recent security solutions
addressing a secure bootstrapping phase, which refers to any tasks which need
to be performed before the network can begin to function - mainly transfer of
security parameters to the devices. In their survey, they classify the existing
security solutions into two main types:
• Asymmetric key scheme solutions.
These refer to schemes based
on asymmetric cryptography, and are generally used either to securely
transport the key (by using encryption), or to agree on a secret key.
==PAGE:55==
4.2 Secure key distribution
43
• Symmetric key pre-distribution solutions. These refer to schemes
based on symmetric cryptography, and are either probabilistic, where se-
curity parameters are picked at random from a pool of keys to constrained
nodes, and where each node pair can, with a certain probability, obtain a
common key; or deterministic, where the process of creating the key pool
and distributing it is deterministic, thereby ensuring that each node pair
shares a common key.
The idea behind key distribution is that keys are generated by some trusted
entity and are then distributed among interested parties. This kind of solutions
typically aim at eﬃcient and secure key distribution. Some schemes provide
probabilistic distribution, where a large key pool is generated and used to
randomly obtain keys for each interested party, resulting in every node pair
sharing a key with a certain probability. In case a pair of nodes is discovered
which does not share any common keys, other nodes are used as the secure path
for one of these nodes to pass the newly generated key to the other. Deter-
ministic distribution, on the other hand, relies on deterministic processes to
generate and distribute the keys. One such method is oﬄine key distribu-
tion - here it is assumed that each pair of nodes shares a preinstalled key which
is used to generate session keys. Another is server-assisted key distribu-
tion, where some trusted entity is assumed to share a long-term key with each
communicating entity.
Key agreement implies that both communicating parties establish a secret
(either the key itself or some intermediate value from which the key is derived)
together. A classical example of such agreement is the Diﬃe-Hellman proto-
col [DH76]. The original protocol relies on the diﬃculty of discrete logarithm
problem in order to derive a key over an insecure channel - a passive adversary
(only eavesdropping on the conversation) is not able to derive any information
on the resultant key. However, this protocol does not provide authentication,
meaning that a man-in-the-middle attack can be used to hijack the communica-
tion. In contrast, Station-to-Station(STS) protocol [DVOW92] enhances Diﬃe-
Hellman with authentication by using digital signatures. The basic protocol is
illustrated in ﬁgure 4.1 and works as follows:
0. Public system parameters α and p are set in advance.
1. Alice generates a secret parameter x and sends the parameters and the
exponential αx mod p to Bob.
2. Bob generates a secret parameter y and uses the exponential received
from Alice to derive a shared key K = αxy mod p. He then concatenates
both exponentials, signs them with his private key B, and encrypts the
signature with the derived shared key K. This gets sent to Alice.
==PAGE:56==
44
Preliminary analysis
Figure 4.1: Station-to-Station protocol (adapted from [DVOW92])
3. Alice computes K = αyx mod p. She then decrypts the signature received
from Bob and veriﬁes it with his public key. Finally, she concatenates both
exponentials, signs them with her private key A, encrypt it and sends the
message to Bob.
4. Bob decrypts the received message and veriﬁes Alice’s signature using her
public key.
Within this scheme, it is assumed that both Alice and Bob know each other’s
public keys. A secure private/public key pair can be generated by each device,
and the private keys do not need to be revealed. Advantages of the scheme
include perfect forward secrecy, meaning that even if an attacker manages
to compromise private keys of Alice and Bob (A and B), it does not leak any
information on the keys used in previous sessions (as long as diﬀerent values are
being generated at every run of the protocol).
In case the participants do not know each other’s public keys, they can be e.g.
exchanged during the protocol along with certiﬁcates proving that they are valid
and belong to the expected owner.
Moreover, while the original protocol uses discrete logarithm problem to ensure
its security, more eﬃcient techniques (such as elliptic curve cryptography) can
be utilized instead.

4.3
Software protection
The problem of software protection can be approached diﬀerently depending on
the underlying goals. In the context of this project, we would like to protect
the AntibIoTic software running on an IoT device connected to a Fog node. As
mentioned in objectives, one of our goals is as follows:
Ensuring software protection. There must be a mechanism in
place ensuring the possibility to check that the code of the AntibIoTic
bot running on the IoT device has not been compromised by some
malicious entity.
What makes achieving this goal especially challenging is the fact that we can-
not guarantee that an IoT device we aim to protect is located in a safe envi-
ronment, inaccessible by potentially malevolent users. For example, in a large
oﬃce building devices located in public spaces would be more easily accessible
than those in private rooms [ISTZ16]. This makes the problem similar in na-
ture to the problem of protecting against Man-At-The-End (MATE) attacks,
where the adversary controls the device and is able to modify its hardware or
software [ASA+15]. The attacker is able to use tools such as debuggers, emula-
tors, disassemblers, and so on, in order to analyze and attack the device. The
defending party has to rely on various techniques aiming to protect the device.
This has also been called a "malicious host" problem [CTPM09,SPVK04].

4.3.1
Overview
There exist various types of techniques related to software protection. Relevant
sources [BT06, MKBS07, NZ04] were studied to identify some of those. They
include:
• Hardware based solutions. These are often based on using some kind
of secure, tamper-resistant hardware, which is typically embedded into the
device being protected and is responsible for verifying software running on
the system. An example of such secure coprocessor is Trusted Platform
Module (TPM) [Mor11]. Other solutions involve using mobile hardware
devices, such as dongles or smart cards, which contain some information
required by the software to operate.
• Checksum based solutions. These rely on calculating the checksum
of a program in order to evaluate its integrity.
If the checksum being
calculated matches the expected one (the checksum of the original soft-
ware), the code is deemed to be unaltered. Diﬀerent solutions based on
this technique exist.
These include those based on guards or testers
(e.g. [HMST02]), which are code pieces calculating checksums of diﬀerent
parts of the program. Often they are placed in the code in such a way as
to protect each other and form complicated networks, making it harder
for an attacker to disable them all. Some guard-based techniques, such
as [CA02], not only compute checksums, but also attempt to correct the
code in case it has been compromised.
• Obfuscation based solutions. The general idea of obfuscation is con-
verting a program into an equivalent one, but which is more diﬃcult to
understand. This is meant to make analysis and reverse engineering of the
program more challenging. Diﬀerent solutions based on this technique use
various ways to transform a program, such as modifying the source code
itself (lexical transformation), control ﬂow of the program, and program
data (which involves rearranging data structures within the program). An
example of such solution is [WHKD00], where the authors attempt mak-
ing static analysis of binaries more complicated. To achieve that, they
modify the control-ﬂow graph of the program in such a way as to make
control-ﬂow and data-ﬂow analysis depend on each other.
• Control ﬂow monitoring. The idea behind this approach is to trace the
execution of the program as it is being executed. A program is separated
into blocks with the help of control ﬂow graph, and code responsible for
keeping track of the trace of the software is inserted into those blocks.
The trace is then compared to the expected trace, determined at com-
pile time. An example work using this technique is [BT06]. The authors
==PAGE:59==
4.3 Software protection
47
suggest separating software into two processes, Monitor(M-Process) and
Program(P-Process), where M-Process possesses the knowledge of the ex-
pected control ﬂow of the P-Process and monitors its execution.
• Encryption based solutions. These rely on encrypting the code to make
its modiﬁcation infeasible. An example include solutions for preventing
decompilation of Java class ﬁles, such as [Lam09], where only Java Virtual
Machine could read and process them. The idea is to encrypt the ﬁles
and only decrypt them right before their execution.
The problems of
the approach include the necessity to have the encryption key embedded
into the application in order to facilitate decrypting the ﬁles, as well as
being able to decompile (and subsequently modify) the class responsible
for decrypting the other class ﬁles, so that instead of passing the decrypted
ﬁles’ content to JVM it would simply provide them to the attacker.
• Watermarking. Watermarking techniques aim at ensuring that creators
of the software can prove their ownership of the code. A "watermark" is
typically placed in the code in a way as to make its removal diﬃcult. There
exist both dynamic and static watermarking techniques: the diﬀerence is
whether the watermark is created with (dynamic) or without (static) the
software being executed. This technique is not directly aimed at providing
tamper resistance, but rather at proof of ownership. An example of such
solution for Java is [CHC03].
• Remote attestation. Remote attestation is a technique used within the
context of trusted computing [MoEE05], where a software authenticates
itself to remote parties, allowing the latter to make certain assumptions
about the software’s behavior [HCF04].
Commonly remote attestation
is mentioned in the context of trusted computing.
Here whenever an
application needs to be attested, the OS computes and signs a hash of
its executable using a TPM. The verifying party then checks whether the
hash matches the expected one. One issue of this approach is that what
is being veriﬁed is what executable is running, and not the behavior of
the program. A technique addressing this problem is semantic remote
attestation, which aims to attest high-level properties of the system,
often with the help of virtual environment. In other words, what is being
attested is the behavior of software being run, and not the fact that a
certain binary is running. An example of such solution is [HCF04].
Some works are attempting to implement software-based remote attes-
tation, which, as the name implies, aims to facilitate remote attestation
using only software methods. Those are often used on devices for which
hardware-based techniques are either unavailable or considered to be too
expensive, such as, for instance, embedded devices. Here the veriﬁer (a
trusted party, similar to a Fog node in AntibIoTic) is typically physically
==PAGE:60==

Whenever attestation occurs, some kind of challenge (often computation

key being sent with every attestation request. One problem of this ap-

running on the device, but also the attestation routine run by the prover,

Discussion
Our of those techniques, remote attestation, checksumming, as well as
control ﬂow monitoring seem promising for AntibIoTic. All of those could
aid in making it possible to detect whenever the code has been tampered with.
And since we have a Fog node at our disposal, it would make sense to use it to
facilitate code security - and, as long as we ensure that the node is protected, it
could, for example, act as a veriﬁer to whom the AntibIoTic bot software would
attest to.
The issue of techniques not relying on having a remote trusted party like a Fog
node is that as long as an attacker is able to ﬁgure out the idea behind one
of such solutions, he should be able to modify the code in such a way as to
disable the tamper resistant functionality (unless the code responsible for it is
prevented from being modiﬁed, e.g. by being stored on Read-Only Memory,
if it is available).
A similar problem is with code obfuscation - a dedicated
adversary could ﬁgure out the trick and reverse-engineer the system. It has
also been shown that perfect obfuscation is not possible [BGI+01]. Moreover,
making it more diﬃcult to reverse-engineer AntibIoTic software is not among
our immediate goals. However, these techniques could still be used in order to
make it more challenging for an attacker to compromise the system, even if not
used as the main protection mechanism.
One of the techniques seeming to be less viable for our problem is hardware
based solutions, the drawback of which is an additional requirement of having a
trusted hardware embedded in the devices in order to ensure security. Another
one is watermarking, as its goal of proof of ownership is not something we are
aiming to address. Finally, encryption based techniques do not seem to be a
viable option due to the problems of this approach, such as unavoidable exposing
of the code at some point before execution.
4.4
Key protection
Given our aim to secure the communication between IoT devices and Fog nodes,
it is clear that we not only need to generate/exchange the secret keys required
to ensure the communication safety, but also to somehow store them on the IoT
devices. Due to the fact that those devices might be publicly accessible and do
not necessarily possess the hardware which could make this task easier, we have
to look into alternative ways of achieving this goal.
==PAGE:62==
50
Preliminary analysis
4.4.1
Overview
There exist many ways to approach this problem. In the original AntibIoTic
2.0 report [DF18] it was proposed to have a unique key for each device which
would be embedded into the AntibIoTic binary and renewed in case of a device
restart. It is argued that the binary could be stripped (deprived of debugging
information), which would make extracting the key more diﬃcult; moreover,
even in case of success, the key would only be useful to attack one device.
The goal of white-box cryptography, as described in [SW08], is to pre-
vent extraction of secret keys in a software implementation. The "white-box"
here means that, as opposed to a traditional security model where the at-
tacker is only able to access the inputs and outputs of a cryptographic al-
gorithm ("black-box"), he is also able to control the execution platform, as
well as the software implementation of the cryptographic algorithm. Various
cryptographic algorithms such as AES have been implemented using this tech-
nique [CEJVO02]. It relies on integrating the key into the encryption algorithm,
which is transformed to use lookup tables instead of a classic key. However,
white-box techniques like [CEJVO02] and many others have been successfully
attacked [BGEC04, DLPR13]. Moreover, it has been noted that this approach
lacks theoretical foundations [SWP09].
Another interesting approach to this problem is physically uncloneable func-
tions (PUFs). They are functions generating a unique hardware-speciﬁc out-
put, called response, to each input, called challenge, by exploiting certain side-
eﬀects of chip manufacturing [AAD+16b]. PUFs utilize tools called fuzzy ex-
tractors in order to mitigate the eﬀect of noise (e.g. thermal noise) aﬀecting
certain hardware properties of the device. Certain types of PUFs have been
categorized as weak PUFs: these are PUFs having a very few number of chal-
lenge/response pairs. The main application of such PUFs is deriving secret keys
inside lightweight hardware. Most commonly, PUF manufacturer is able to learn
the key during the setup phase. A clear advantage is that since PUF only derives
its response when requested, it remains present on the system for a very short
time period, in contrast with regular keys which remain stored in non-volatile
memory of the device indeﬁnitely. Common implementations include SRAM
(static RAM) PUFs, which utilize unique properties of SRAM cells [RH14].
While PUFs provide devices with digital ﬁngerprint, another way to go about
this problem could be to generate random key by the device and only keep
it in the application’s random memory.
To avoid side-channel attacks, such
as power analysis, the key could be renegotiated from time to time.
Public
key cryptography could be used to share the generated key with the parties of
interest (e.g. Fog node).
==PAGE:63==

51
4.4.2
Discussion
It is clear that diﬀerent approaches dedicated to solving this problem have been
proposed. Utilizing PUFs seems like a potentially useful approach, as it mini-
mizes the opportunity for an attacker to extract the key from the device. How-
ever, its disadvantages include the static nature of the key, a consequence of
which is that if it somehow gets compromised it cannot simply be replaced.
Key renegotiation is an alternative way to approach this issue. Further research
and experimentation is necessary to determine the most optimal solution.
4.5
Report protection
Vulnerability reports are generated by the AntibIoTic bot during its interactions
with the host device. We are interested in protecting those reports from being
viewed and/or modiﬁed by potential attackers.
4.5.1
Overview
While the problem seems somewhat similar in nature to that of key protection,
we could utilize some of the cryptographic techniques mentioned in 4.1 to address
it.
Integrity and authenticity of reports could be ensured by using MACs. If the
bot and the Fog node share a key, every time a report is generated by the bot,
it could include MAC calculation of that report. Since the attacker is assumed
not to have access to the key, this should make it unfeasible for him to forge or
modify reports. A similar approach is used by [CRT18b], who use this method
to store some data of interest in the regular, unprotected memory of the device.
Using MAC would prevent the report from being modiﬁed, as this would be
detected by the Fog node who is in possession of the shared key.
Moreover, it could be possible to encrypt reports before storing them on the
device. Just as in case of MACs, as long as the adversary cannot access the
key, he should not be able to view any encrypted data. By combining the two
approaches, both authenticity and conﬁdentiality of reports could be ensured.
==PAGE:64==
52
Preliminary analysis
4.5.2
Discussion
While this problem seems to be similar in nature to key protection, it appears to
be possible to approach it from a diﬀerent angle. If we can rely on a secure key,
it can be used to protect the reports by using MACs and encryption techniques.
One positive side-eﬀect of this approach is that the reports would not have
to be additionally encrypted and authenticated when being sent to the Fog
node. However, care must be taken in ensuring the security of the method: e.g.
depending on the particular MAC and encryption schemes used, it might be
required to use a diﬀerent key in each of those schemes.
4.6
Discussion
Having provided an overview on the sub-problems which would need to be ad-
dressed in order to ensure communication security within AntibIoTic, it is clear
that the diﬃculty of solving each of them varies signiﬁcantly. While problems
like achieving secure channel and key distribution seem more straightforward,
and there are various schemes available in order to facilitate those properties,
it appears to be more challenging to ﬁnd solutions to protecting the software
and secret keys on an IoT device having limited processing power and lacking
dedicated security hardware. Due to this, it has been decided to focus on one
of those problems in the rest of the report.
The main problem we decided to focus on is software protection. As described
in 3.1.2, it is a rather important issue: as the goal of AntibIoTic is protecting
vulnerable devices, it would be highly undesirable if the bot responsible for
protecting the device, collecting relevant data and exchanging information with
the Fog node, got compromised and bent to an attacker’s will.
It is clear that there is a need for a ﬁtting solution. The preliminary research
on the problem in 4.3 gave an overview on some of the techniques available to
solve this issue. In order to dig deeper into the problem we need to conduct a
systematic literature review, identifying most relevant techniques, ana-
lyzing their strengths and weaknesses, and ﬁguring out a way to apply them
to AntibIoTic. This is addressed in the rest of the thesis.
Note, that while we do not aim to provide solutions to the other problems, we
acknowledge that they are relevant issues which must be dealt with. The main
reason for deciding to focus on one challenging problem was lack of time. Thus,
although the end goal is to relax the assumption about communication security,

The goal of this chapter is to establish which relevant software protection tech-

To this aim, we conduct a systematic literature review in order to identify

In order to conduct a systematic literature review, we adopt the method used by
Petersen et al. [PVK15]. We thus decide on research questions, search strategy,
search process, and study selection.
5.1
Research questions
The goal of the rest of the thesis is to identify the solution to the problem
of software protection in the context of AntibIoTic. We would like to focus on
which software protection solutions there are available, as well as which ones are

feasible to use in our case. Therefore, we will formulate our research questions
as follows:
• RQ1: what software protection techniques could be most relevant in the
context of AntibIoTic?
• RQ2: what are the key diﬀerences between those techniques?
The goal of RQ1 is to obtain an overview of relevant software protection tech-

Finding the answers to these questions should aid us in solving the problem of
software protection in AntibIoTic, as well as in arguing why the solution we
obtained is the most appropriate one.
5.2
Search strategy
We use the PICOC [Kit07] criteria for software engineering to determine the
appropriate keywords to aid our search.
• Population: we are interested in works studying software protection.
• Intervention: we are interested in works that address tools and technolo-
gies having the goal of software protection. Since "software protection" is
quite a general term, we need to determine which techniques are the most
relevant within AntibIoTic. Techniques which could be of interest include,
as described in 4.3, remote attestation, a technique used within the con-
text of trusted computing [MoEE05], where a software authenticates itself
to remote parties, allowing the latter to make certain assumptions about
the software’s behavior [HCF04]; checksumming, where the checksum of a
program is calculated in order to evaluate its integrity; and control ﬂow
monitoring, the idea of which is tracing the execution of a program as it
is being executed [MKBS07].

• Comparison: we compare the approaches suggested by diﬀerent works
by considering such factors as their performance impact, feasibility, and
relevance in the context of our system.
• Outcomes: we present the identiﬁed approaches in order to facilitate the
discussions of which of them ﬁt our goals, and how can they be applied to
AntibIoTic.
• Context: we are interested in all the works aimed at solving the problem

5.5.2
Potentially relevant software protection techniques
(RQ1)
Software-based attestation
Only 4 out of studied papers propose a tech-
nique based on pure software-based attestation, meaning one having no
hardware requirements. Srinivasan et al. [SDG11] propose a scheme based on
integrity measurement code (C) being sent from server to client. Upon receiv-
ing the code, client injects it into the application being veriﬁed (P), which then
transfers control to it. The measurement code then analyzes P and reports the
results back to the server.
In order to prevent replay attacks, the exact contents of C diﬀer every time it
is sent, which is achieved e.g. by placing random constants into the code and
changing memory region over which the checksum is being taken. To make C
harder to analyze, authors apply obfuscation techniques such as changing vari-
able placement on the stack, inserting dummy instructions, changing execution
ﬂow, and so on. The code gets restored to the runnable state prior to being
executed on the client side.
This solution is oriented at more powerful machines with x86 instruction set,
as mentioned in the full version of their paper [SDGB10], which means that it
might not be as suitable for weaker IoT devices. In particular, and as mentioned
by DeCastro et al. [DCCB18], not all platforms support on-the-ﬂy code injection
used in the scheme.

Another studied paper by Preschern et al. [PHKK13] describes an application
of remote attestation in the area of safety-critical systems. These are systems
which, in case of failure, could lead to loss of life, signiﬁcant property damage,
or damage to the environment [Kni02]. The problem considered by [PHKK13] is
rather industry-speciﬁc, as their focus is on integrating remote attestation within
safety-critical systems domain without increasing safety certiﬁcation eﬀort and,
correspondingly, costs. In particular, they suggest a solution for the case where
a programmable logic controller (PLC) (which is a type of industrial computer,
often designed to operate in harsh environments) needs to be protected against
tampering attempts on program updates and commands sent to the controller
from the dedicated engineering software. Their problem is somewhat similar
to ours: they mention that the integrity of the engineering software must be
veriﬁed in order to avoid the scenario where maliciously altered program updates
or commands are sent to the controller.
Their solution utilizes the black channel principle, which in simple terms means
that as long as certain requirements with regards to the safety of communication
between two end nodes are met, then the communication link between the end-
points of the safety layer can be viewed as a black channel, meaning it does
not have to be safety certiﬁed.
This allows them to implement attestation
using techniques which do not have a certiﬁed implementation, such as, for
example, white-box cryptography. The way they measure the integrity of the
software is by simply hashing the program code. White-box cryptography is then
used to protect the key used to ensure authenticity and conﬁdentiality of the
measurements. In order to prevent the attacker from disabling the attestation
mechanism they obfuscate the code.
While this approach (using uncertiﬁed
techniques like white-box cryptography and code obfuscation) makes sense in
the scenario the authors are working in and is aimed at motivating the use and
development of novel security techniques in the safety domain, the fact that it
relies on methods which are known to be of uncertain security (as described in
4.4) motivates looking for more robust solutions.
Authors of another studied software-based attestation paper [SDI+10] discuss
multiple problems, one of which is related to software-based attestation; how-
ever, upon closer inspection it has become clear that their solution is almost
identical to the one presented in [SDG11] (utilizing code injection and oriented
at powerful machines).
Finally, [SSP+11] propose a solution based on a prover device (in their case, a
smart meter) computing a checksum of random memory locations. The check-
sum is then sent in the direction from the prover to the veriﬁer. Their idea
is that if the communication between the prover and the veriﬁer is one-way, it
becomes more diﬃcult for an attacker to perform certain types of man-in-the-
middle attacks (such as, for instance, creating a challenge-response dictionary

by sending fake challenges to the prover and then using the computed values
to deceive the attestation procedure). Thus, instead of relying on challenges
being sent from the veriﬁer to the prover to avoid replay and pre-calculation at-
tacks, their scheme utilizes a unique serial number stored on each smart meter
combined with the current time as the input to their seed generation function
(used for determining memory addresses to verify). They assume that the se-

This assumption is one of the disadvantages of their approach, as it essentially
makes it necessary to have a protected memory location on each device. Other
problems they address are typically solved with the help of authentication tech-
niques (i.e. authenticating challenge requests in order to prevent an attacker
from calculating potential challenge-response pairs).
Overall, it has become clear from the research that pure software-based attes-
tation methods have some evident disadvantages. Intuitively, if that was not
the case and there existed a perfect attestation scheme relying purely on soft-
ware, then the existence and development of new techniques possessing certain
hardware requirements would not make as much sense. And most importantly,
it is something that is pointed out by many authors of the papers studied. In
particular, [AAD+16a, AAD+16b, IST18, CRT18b, MAB+18] and many other
studied articles mention that software-based attestation schemes are typically
based on strong assumptions that are hard to achieve in practice.
In fact,
classical software-based attestation schemes (including those aimed at embed-
ded devices) such as SWATT [SPVK04] and some others have been analyzed
and placed under scrutiny by researchers aiming to showcase the diﬃculty and
shortcomings of such techniques. One of such works [CFPS09] has demonstrated
some attacks against a variety of attestation schemes. They begin by describing
the protocols, followed by observations, general, and then speciﬁc attacks.
Note, that neither [CFPS09] nor [SPVK04] have initially been identiﬁed as pa-
pers considered for full-text reading; however, it was decided to study them thor-
oughly due to them being heavily referenced by other studied works: [CFPS09]
has been referred to by 9 papers, while [SPVK04] by 20.
SWATT [SPVK04] is one of the protocols covered by [CFPS09]. Its high level
depiction can be viewed in ﬁgure 5.2. It works in the following fashion: the
veriﬁer (V ) generates a nonce (n) and sends it to the prover (P). P uses n as
a seed to the pseudorandom number generator (PRNG) in order to generate
memory addresses to verify.
This is followed by a memory traversal of the
generated addresses, over which a checksum (C′) is generated in an iterative
fashion. C′ is then sent to V , who knows the expected memory contents of
P and uses this knowledge to perform the same computations, resulting in the
expected checksum C. P then proceeds to compare the values of C and C′.
Attestation is considered successful if 1) those values are the same and 2) the
==PAGE:79==
5.5 Results
67
time it took P to compute C′ is lower than or equal to the time expected by
V . The operations of generating memory addresses and computing a checksum
over them can be performed by V either prior to or after sending n to P.

Since the code responsible for generating memory addresses and computing the
checksum is located on P, it is clear that a skilled adversary could attempt
modifying it as well. In fact, if the checksum was always computed over the
same memory region, the attacker could simply replace the veriﬁcation code
with his modiﬁed routine and copy the original memory contents being attested
into some empty memory on the device in order to pass the attestation proce-

dure (as has been illustrated in ﬁgure 4.2). This is the reason for the iterative
pseudorandom memory traversal performed by SWATT. Since the memory
is traversed in a pseudorandom fashion depending on n, the adversary cannot
predict which memory locations are being checked. This makes it more diﬃ-
cult for him to cheat. In particular, his modiﬁed routine would have to check
whether the memory being accessed contains his malicious code, and if that is
the case, redirect the reading operation to the location containing the original
code. Authors of [SPVK04] note that such action would require the attacker to
add an if statement for each modiﬁed memory location. They also claim that
adding even one such statement would increase running time of the veriﬁcation
procedure signiﬁcantly due to it being very eﬃcient and performing many iter-
ations. This is the reason for the second success condition - if it takes longer
time for the prover to perform the attestation procedure than anticipated, it is
a sign of the device being compromised.
Castelluccia et al. [CFPS09] show that the eﬀectiveness of the time-based veriﬁ-
cation is tricky to ensure. In particular, they managed to implement a malicious
(i.e. verifying and redirecting memory accesses) attestation function having only
a 7.4% overhead compared to the original one. While still enough to detect the
tampering, it is a faster implementation than that expected by the authors of
SWATT (13% overhead). [CFPS09] emphasize that it shows just how diﬃcult it
is for protocol designers to assess the best attack against their protocols.
They also add that part of the reason for this is due to diﬀerent functionalities
available in the instruction sets of diﬀerent micro-controllers - in other words,
even better attacks might be possible on other devices than those used by the
authors. They demonstrate it by porting SWATT to a diﬀerent micro-controller
and noting that it 1) required a heavy redesign of the protocol and 2) resulted in
even more eﬃcient implementation of the malicious attestation function. They
conclude that porting SWATT to a device with a diﬀerent memory size and/or
instruction set is tricky and can undermine the security of the scheme.
To make sure that the issues shown by Castelluccia et al. apply to other attesta-
tion schemes as well, we have studied an article by Armknecht et al. [ASSW13]
(referenced by 8 of the initially studied papers), who have created a frame-
work outlining requirements for designing a software-based attestation proce-
dure. In their work, they present a generic attestation scheme which covers
most schemes proposed in the literature (including SWATT, as well as schemes
described in [SSP+11,ANN09,DCCB18] and other works). They show that the
basic structure used by SWATT is present (with some minor diﬀerences) also in
other pure software-based schemes. This includes sending a random seed to the
prover, which is then used to generate random memory addresses and compute
checksum over them in an iterative fashion, followed by sending the result to the
veriﬁer, who uses expected calculation time in order to determine whether the
protocol run was legitimate. [ASSW13] then analyze the security of their generic

procedure, resulting in a set of requirements for provably secure schemes. Their
results imply that the attacks presented by [CFPS09] apply to other software-
based attestation schemes relying on time measurements to detect an attack as
well.
We do not describe other attacks shown by [CFPS09] to be possible against
some other attestation schemes; but one of the key conclusions is that design
of secure time-based attestation schemes is an extremely diﬃcult and error-
prone process due to having to rely on tight timing bounds and the most
eﬃcient implementation possible.
One of the studied works by Francillon et al. [FNRT15] which could be con-
sidered a follow-up on [CFPS09] proceeds to derive certain properties required
to achieve secure remote attestation. These properties are then converted into
architectural requirements which are mapped into minimal hardware features.
This contributes to more works on attestation being based on hybrid schemes
(i.e. those having some minimal hardware requirements), which are described
further.
Hybrid attestation
Looking at the table 5.2, 18 papers propose schemes rely-
ing on hybrid attestation techniques, suggesting that those types of solutions
are proposed rather often. Most of those techniques are either an implemen-
tation of or an improvement on security architectures SMART [ETFP12] and
TrustLite [KSSV14]. (SMART has been referenced by 19 of the studied papers,
while TrustLite by 15, motivating inspecting them further.) SMART utilizes
basic hardware components in order to ensure security of remote attestation.
These are: Read-only Memory (ROM) and a Memory Protection Unit (MPU).
ROM is used to store the attestation routine and (optionally) the secret key,
while MPU ensures that only the attestation routine is able to access the key.
This combination of components achieves the following: since the attestation
code is stored in ROM, it cannot be modiﬁed by e.g. malicious software running
on the device. Thus, integrity of the attestation code is ensured. MPU, on the
other hand, is used to make sure that only the attestation code can access the
key. To achieve that, MPU is set up in a way to only allow access to the key
whenever the program counter is in the ROM code memory region. An exam-
ple of such control can be seen in ﬁgure 5.3, as implemented within DARPA
attestation scheme [ISTZ16]. In their solution, certain parts of ROM and RAM
memory must be protected.
To achieve that, they set up their MPU corre-
spondingly. E.g. the ﬁrst rule ensures that only the code located at addresses
a0 to a3 (which includes their attestation procedure) can read the data stored
at addresses a4 to a5 (the keys used for their scheme).

Dynamic attestation
Just as in case with software-based attestation, only 4
of the studied papers propose a technique with the goal of achieving dynamic

attestation. All of them are based on either hybrid or hardware attestation,
meaning that they all possess at least some hardware requirements.
As mentioned by the authors of the dynamic attestation scheme ATRIUM
[ZDA+17], most typical attestation schemes are static, meaning that the object
getting attested is static in nature - usually binary code of some application. Re-
gardless of the measurement mechanism used (typically some sort of checksum),
it can only ensure the integrity of the application, but not of its execution. In
other words, this kind of attestation falls short of protecting against runtime
attacks, which do not alter the binary, but rather change the application’s
control ﬂow. An example of such attacks is return-oriented programming
(ROP), which is based on connecting pieces of the original machine code (called
"gadgets") in order to generate new, malicious instructions [SPWS13]. This
way, an attacker avoids having to modify the existing code or to add his own
instructions, deceiving static attestation methods.
Aside from ROP, various other types of control-ﬂow attacks exist and moti-
vate development of advanced dynamic attestation schemes.
These include
C-Flat [AAD+16a] which measures control-ﬂow of the program during its exe-
cution. In their solution, branch instructions within the program are analyzed
and then processed by the measurement engine, which computes a hash over
them, thus facilitating creation of an attestation report. This report can be then
veriﬁed for any discrepancies. However, the approach used by C-Flat has been
agreed [ATN18, ZDA+17] to have a high performance overhead on the prover
due to incorporating heavy code monitoring and frequent checksum computa-
tions. Authors of C-Flat addressed this problem by proposing another scheme
called LO-FAT [DZN+17] which utilizes secure hardware to lessen the burden
of those operations.
Another dynamic attestation scheme called ATRIUM [ZDA+17] has been pro-
posed in order to protect against some of the attacks which appeared to be
possible against C-Flat and LO-FAT. In particular, the authors of ATRIUM
have demonstrated that neither C-Flat nor LO-FAT consider physical attacks
involving an attacker capable of manipulating the code memory directly. They
showed that an adversary could manipulate the instructions being fetched by di-
recting them to either the original code in case the attestation scheme is active,
or to malicious code otherwise. This requires replacing the original memory
interface of the device by an interface to a piece of hardware called "memory
controller", which is responsible for directing data ﬂowing to and from the com-
puter’s main memory. This results in performing a type of time of check to
time of use (TOCTOU) attack, where the code being executed is not the
same as the code being attested. Inspired by C-Flat and LO-FAT, ATRIUM
performs runtime code attestation as well. In addition, it measures every exe-
cuted instruction in order to avoid TOCTOU attacks. To achieve this, ATRIUM

is tightly integrated with a processor, implying that its requirements are higher
than those of typical hybrid attestation schemes (like those based on SMART

Yet another paper on the subject is [ATN18], who aim to attest both memory

Finally, [LW14] is aiming to achieve dynamic attestation by collecting execution

Swarm attestation
Multiple protocols have been suggested with the focus on
swarm attestation, referring to ensuring integrity of large networks (swarms)
of embedded devices. It can be observed in table 5.2 that quite many of such
solutions seem to have become available in the recent years, which is possibly
due to the realization that there is a high need for scalable and eﬃcient attes-
tation schemes with the advent of Internet of Things. One of such protocols
is SEDA [ABI+15], where a veriﬁer sends a challenge to a so-called initiator
device which then disseminates it across the network, forming a tree.
Each
device runs the attestation protocol and reports the results to its parent. In
the end, the full report containing information on swarm security is returned
to the veriﬁer by the initiator device. Another swarm attestation scheme called
SANA [ACI+16] has been proposed, which improves on SEDA by implementing
DoS attack protection within the swarm. Yet another scheme with the name
of DARPA [ISTZ16] focuses on detecting both software and physical attacks on
the network. It implements a protocol aimed at detecting and reporting device
absence within the swarm. This has been improved upon by US-AID [IST18]
which supports autonomous and dynamic networks and boasts better eﬃciency
of their protocol than that of DARPA. Another work focused on supporting
dynamic networks, as well as identifying the exact devices which have been
compromised has been proposed by [MJG18].
SCAPI [KBGK17] is yet another swarm attestation protocol, which is in many
ways similar to DARPA; however, it utilizes special session keys which are shared
among all healthy devices in the swarm and periodically updated in such a
way as to make it infeasible for them to be obtained by physically compro-
mised devices (those which were missing when the new session key was shared).
WISE [AWC18] is another scheme, in which attestation of a swarm is distributed
over a large time window, which can be variable for each device or subset of de-
vices. The scheme utilizes the history of previous attestation periods in order

Physical attack detection
Although many papers covering software attes-
tation have an attacker model assuming his inability to physically tamper with
devices (e.g. [SPVK04, AAD+16a]), it is clear that such a scenario can and
does happen in reality.
This is especially the case with embedded and IoT
devices which are often rather accessible.
There are three types of physical
attacks considered in the literature (as mentioned by two of the reviewed pa-
pers [ISTZ16,IST18]):
1. Invasive attacks. In this case, an adversary attempts to obtain some
information of interest from a device by accessing its internal components.
These attacks typically require expensive and elaborate lab equipment to
execute.
2. Semi-invasive attacks. These attacks require cheaper and less compli-
cated equipment compared to invasive attacks. However, they still imply
having to physically tamper with the device.
3. Non-invasive attacks. These include e.g. side-channel attacks and have
the goal of extracting secrets from the device without disturbing its op-
eration. Tools required for such attacks are normally much cheaper than
those for the ﬁrst two attack types.
Typically, it is acknowledged that mitigating all types of physical attacks is
rather diﬃcult. However, multiple works attempt detecting both invasive and
semi-invasive attacks. They are based on the premise that those kind of attacks
require the adversary to make the device unaccessible for a considerable amount
of time: from hours to weeks [Sko11,Sko12]. This has been shown to be true by
e.g. Becher et al. [BBD06] (as referenced by [KBGK17] and [ISTZ16]). (Clearly,
this does not apply to non-invasive attacks, meaning that they require a diﬀer-
ent kind of treatment.) One of the studies tackling physical attacks by absence
detection is DARPA swarm attestation scheme by Ibrahim et al. [ISTZ16]. In
DARPA, device absence in the network is discovered by a collective absence de-
tection protocol called heartbeat. The protocol is executed at regular time in-
tervals, where each device proves its presence by broadcasting a secure heartbeat
==PAGE:86==

to its neighbors, who proceed to forward the received signal to their neighbors,
and so on. In such recursive fashion, the presence of every single device in the
network is veriﬁed. Similar solutions were proposed by other swarm attestation
schemes, e.g. US-AID [IST18].
"Not on-demand" attestation
One interesting solution which diﬀers from
most remote attestation schemes has been proposed by Carpent et al. [CRT18b].
In their work, they discuss existing remote attestation protocols and refer to
them as "on-demand" - meaning that attestation of the prover occurs whenever
it is required by the veriﬁer. The authors note that this approach has a few
disadvantages:
1. It does not address so-called "mobile malware" - the type of malware which
is able to come and go as it pleases.
2. Requiring the device being veriﬁed to perform a potentially time-consuming
task (attestation) may cause it to pause performing its main function,
which is especially undesirable for devices providing critical services.
To this aim, they propose a scheme they call "Eﬃcient Remote Attestation via
Self-Measurement for Unattended Settings (ERASMUS)". The concept behind
it is as follows: prover device measures and records its state at scheduled times.
Those measurements are then stored by the prover and occasionally collected by
the veriﬁer who makes sure they are valid. Aside from addressing the disadvan-
tages of on-demand attestation listed above, this approach mitigates one more
problem: computational DoS attacks on the prover. Since veriﬁcation routines
are often computationally-heavy, many on-demand attestation schemes require
prover to validate each attestation request it receives. Since in ERASMUS ver-
iﬁer simply asks prover to send measurements which have already been made,
computational DoS attacks are no longer feasible. Authors note that ERASMUS
is not a replacement for on-demand attestation, but rather an enhancement aim-
ing to address certain problems of the approach. They proceed to describe their
scheme, including the ways to integrate it with on-demand attestation in case
it is required (e.g. right after software update). An example of how ERASMUS
handles mobile malware can be observed in ﬁgure 5.4 [CRT18b].
Since in ERASMUS devices are responsible for conducting measurements on
their own, the protocol has certain hardware requirements.
In particular, it
is built on SMART security architecture with the addition of reliable read-

SMART+.

Although not proposing a new attestation type

expected hardware. This is aimed at protecting against delegating attestation
procedure calculation to another, more powerful device; it is expected that only
the intended prover device should be able to generate the correct hardware ﬁn-

the veriﬁer’s side: one option is using PUFs based on a mathematical model,

solution is somewhat heavy, as it requires the device to have suﬃcient memory

5.5.4
Feasibility of implementation of diﬀerent techniques
within AntibIoTic
The discussion in this section is based on the answers to RQ1 and RQ2 described
above.
Software-based techniques initially seemed to be the easiest to implement thanks
to their lack of any additional hardware requirements. However, being faced
with having to accept certain assumptions, such as adversary not being able
to change hardware conﬁguration of the device, as well as having to perform
a diﬃcult task of designing the most eﬃcient attestation procedure across all
possible architectures makes it clear that this task would not be as easy as
originally thought.
All things considered, hybrid attestation and schemes based on it (like ERAS-
MUS) seem to be an interesting alternative to the software-based attestation due
to solving some of its problems. In particular, the minimal hardware require-
ments of the hybrid approach alleviate reliance on assumptions on adversarial
capabilities and device architecture. Somewhat ironically, if a software-based
attestation scheme can only be applied to a certain architecture, it becomes
an implicit requirement, while in case of hybrid attestation, the requirements
are clear. However, since focus of AntibIoTic is on protecting as many IoT de-
vices as possible regardless of their hardware conﬁguration, this would not be a
perfect solution.
Dynamic attestation, although the only technique protecting against runtime
attacks, has high hardware requirements and signiﬁcant overhead, which implies
that using that solution might not be the best approach.
Swarm attestation is a technique aimed at ensuring integrity of large networks
of embedded devices. Protocols utilizing this technique strive for achieving the
most scalable and eﬃcient solutions, and are typically based on hybrid attes-
tation schemes.
While current design of AntibIoTic does not consider large

networks like those addressed by swarm attestation, it might seem like a useful
property for ensuring scalability of the system. The basis of the studied swarm
attestation schemes is that IoT or other embedded devices can communicate
with one another, facilitating a scalable, tree-like attestation. While the current
design of AntibIoTic states that all IoT devices in the network are naturally
assumed to maintain connection with the Fog node, if the number of devices

support a network of heterogeneous devices. While existing swarm attestation
protocols do address this problem, they set certain assumptions: for instance,
authors of US-AID [IST18] specify that devices can possess various types of
software and hardware; however, they all must support a lightweight remote

reliable read-only clock. And since AntibIoTic is focused on protecting as many
devices as possible, this too would have to be addressed.

6.1.1
Software-based attestation
Software-based attestation is the type of attestation having no explicit hard-
ware requirements.
What we mean by "explicit" essentially has to do with
diﬃculty of adapting software-based attestation scheme to diﬀerent platforms:
as described earlier, if a software-based attestation scheme can only be safely
used on a certain architecture, it becomes an implicit requirement. Multiple
sources [SMKK05, SLP+06, AAD+16b, VL19] (including those studied during
systematic literature review) have been analyzed in order to provide concise
and precise overview of this technique in the rest of this section.
6.1.1.1
Advantages
Advantages of software-based attestation are as follows:
1. Lack of explicit hardware requirements. Clearly, this is the main
idea behind software-based attestation and its biggest advantage.
Not
having to depend on devices having a certain hardware conﬁguration is a
clear beneﬁt for AntibIoTic.
6.1.1.2
Disadvantages
This type of attestation has, as determined by the literature review, quite many
disadvantages:
1. Need for optimal (best possible) implementation.
As described
earlier, software-based attestation relies on implementing its attestation
routine as eﬃciently as possible and then keeping an eye on time discrep-
ancies in order to detect an attack. If an attacker manages to ﬁnd a more
eﬃcient implementation than that of the protocol creators, he might be
able to use the time diﬀerence in order to insert malicious instructions in
the attestation code, e.g. to divert memory accesses to original code.
==PAGE:95==
6.1 Analysis of relevant approaches
83
2. Complicated portability. Since the previous property must be ensured
on all the platforms the attestation scheme is supposed to protect, it means
that the attestation protocol implementation must be optimal regardless
of the underlying hardware. It has been demonstrated by [CFPS09] that
porting one of software-based attestation schemes to another platform
resulted in lower attack overhead. They also discuss that instruction sets
of some devices may have functionality available which was not expected
by the protocol creators. Due to those reasons, porting a software-based
attestation scheme is a challenging task.
3. No resistance against hardware modiﬁcation attacks. If an attacker
is able to modify hardware of the device by e.g. changing the memory
access timings or increasing processor clock speed, he is potentially able
to enhance device’s ability to execute the attestation protocol, resulting
in having a more eﬃcient implementation, which must be avoided.
4. No resistance against collusion attack. If an attacker is able to set
up one or multiple malicious devices which could help the prover (in our
case, some IoT device) perform the attestation, it would, once again, be
possible to perform the computation faster than anticipated, allowing the
attacker to insert malicious modiﬁcations in the routine code.
5. No resistance against proxy attack. Similar to the previous point,
an attacker could either obtain a device identical to the original one, or
emulate it with the help of some powerful machine. Once a challenge is
received from the veriﬁer (in our case, the Fog node), it could be forwarded
to the attacker’s device, which would calculate the correct response in
faster time than the expected time.
6. Diﬃculty of verifying only a speciﬁc part of memory. Software-
based attestation schemes follow a speciﬁed structure of the attestation
procedure, where memory locations used for response computation are
generated in a pseudorandom fashion. The reason for this is that if only
a speciﬁed part of the memory was attested every time, an attacker could
store the original memory contents in the empty memory region of the
device and redirect the required computation (e.g. a checksum or a MAC)
to that memory region. Thus, it is not possible to simply always compute
the challenge over e.g. the same executable, or, in our case, the AntibIoTic
binary.
7. Need for direct communication between the prover and the ver-
iﬁer. Since most software-based attestation schemes rely on measuring
time it takes the prover to calculate the response to the attestation chal-
lenge, the technique is not adapted to work well for situations when multi-
hop paths are being used (i.e. when a device being attested has to com-
==PAGE:96==
84
Selection of software protection technique for AntibIoTic
municate with the veriﬁer through some intermediary devices), as those
can aﬀect the time measurement.
8. Need for authenticated channel. Software-based attestation schemes
do not ensure authenticity of the communication channel, which results
in certain attacks becoming possible. Examples include sending fake chal-
lenges to the prover and collecting the responses, resulting in a challenge-
response table; and forcing execution of the attestation protocol to perform
a computational DoS attack.
6.1.1.3
Potential countermeasures
Fortunately, there exist potential remedies/countermeasures to these disadvan-
tages:
1. Use tools to ensure that the code for the attestation procedure
cannot be optimized further. As described by [SLP+06], there exist
automated tools to exhaustively look for the most eﬃcient implementation,
e.g. superopt1; as well as theorem proving techniques proving that a piece
of code is optimal [JNR02].
2. A direct way to deal with this limitation would be having a diﬀerent
version of the protocol for every kind of architecture available.
This would certainly be challenging due to diﬀerences between them and
their sheer amount, as well as having limited support of various architec-
tures by the optimization tools mentioned above. It could be argued that
this is similar in diﬃculty to having to support compiling AntibIoTic bot
for diﬀerent devices. Additionally, AntibIoTic bot and AntibIoTic server
(running on the Fog node) would have to be enhanced with functional-
ity allowing them to detect device architecture and use a corresponding
procedure based on it.
3. Use absence detection. As described in 5.5.2, most invasive and semi-
invasive attacks require the adversary to make the device unaccessible for
a considerable amount of time. Using some kind of heartbeat-like mech-
anism we could detect when a device has been absent, and assume that
the reason for it is a hardware modiﬁcation related attack. In AntibIoTic,
keep alive messages could be used to this aim, or a special kind of "not
captured" message could be added. Another way to solve the issue would
be to authenticate hardware used to compute the attestation response
by using e.g. PUFs as proposed by [SSW11]. Change of hardware would

It can be seen that this technique has plenty of issues which must be taken

no software protection at all.
6.1.2
Hybrid attestation
Hybrid attestation is a type of attestation combining software and hardware
in order to solve some of the issues of pure software-based attestation. Most
protocols using this type of attestation are based on lightweight architectures
such as SMART [ETFP12], which require the device to possess minimal hard-
ware components, such as Read-Only Memory(ROM) and Memory Protection
Unit(MPU). [AAD+16b,ETFP12] as well as other sources from the systematic
literature review have been considered to comprise the overview of this tech-
nique.
6.1.2.1
Advantages
Advantages of hybrid attestation are as follows:
==PAGE:99==
6.1 Analysis of relevant approaches
87
1. Immutable attestation code. An essential problem of pure software-
based attestation is that the code used to measure integrity of the device
has to be located on that device, which means that it can be altered by
an adversary as well (hence the need for using side-channel techniques like
time measurement to detect an attack). By forcing the attestation code to
be located in ROM, hybrid attestation ensures its immutability and thus
resistance against modiﬁcations.
2. Inbuilt key management. In pure software-based techniques no secret
values are typically used, as the adversary is assumed to be able to access
them, which calls for alternative authentication methods, as otherwise an
adversary could attempt to e.g. force the challenge response to prevent
the prover from correctly responding to the veriﬁer. Hybrid attestation
solves the issue by using an MPU, which is set up in such a way to prevent
anything (and anyone) except the attestation code from accessing the key
stored on the device. Attestation routines on hybrid architecture require
a key in order to authenticate the code. Correspondingly, the attestation
routine can only be executed successfully by the original code, as only that
code has access to the key. In essence, this simpliﬁes the solution to the
key protection problem in AntibIoTic, as described in 4.4.
3. Multi-hop network support. As opposed to pure software-based attes-
tation, hybrid schemes do not rely on time measurements to evaluate the
trust in the prover. Therefore, they provide support of multi-hop paths.
4. Customizability of attestation region. Since the way hybrid attesta-
tion is handled is very diﬀerent from software-based schemes, it is possible
to measure a speciﬁc memory region (e.g. where the AntibIoTic binary is
located) - since there is no need to detect whether the attacker has modi-
ﬁed the attestation routine, random memory traversal is not necessary.
6.1.2.2
Disadvantages
Disadvantages of hybrid attestation include:
1. Explicit hardware requirements. Clearly, a need for ROM and MPU
is an explicit hardware requirement which cannot be avoided, and makes
this solution inherently less ﬂexible if used by AntibIoTic.
2. Read-only attestation procedure. Since the attestation procedure is
supposed to be located on ROM, it is clear that it must be recorded on it
in advance, which might cause a problem e.g. in case it must be updated.
Moreover, it also implies that new devices must be manufactured with the
attestation technique in mind.

6.1.2.3
Potential countermeasures
The following remedies could be applied to the disadvantages:
1. The hardware requirements is something that is inherent to the method
and cannot be removed.
2. A partial solution could be ensuring that the attestation procedure
is secure and ﬁnal. While it may seem extremely simple, it is actually
more realistic in this scenario compared to software-based attestation, as
we do not strive for the most optimal procedure; we just require one that
is secure and functional. With regards to devices being manufactured with
this technique in mind, it is essentially unavoidable, and depends heavily
on the market of IoT devices and how widespread our solution (as well as
hybrid attestation schemes in general) gets.
6.1.2.4
Summary
It can be observed that hybrid attestation possesses certain advantages over pure
software-based techniques, which mostly come down to not having to rely on
side-channel information like timing of attestation procedure in order to
assess trust in the device being attested. Inbuilt key management could be
useful for both authentication and potentially encryption (e.g. for secure channel
within AntibIoTic), and multi-hop network support enables use of swarm
attestation techniques, as described further in this section. However, explicit
hardware requirements and the necessity of attestation routine being preset
on each device using this attestation type in advance make it not as ﬂexible as
software-based techniques.
6.1.3
Swarm attestation
Swarm attestation refers to attestation protocols allowing verifying integrity of
large networks (swarms) of embedded devices.
Those protocols are built on
hybrid attestation architectures, such as SMART, due to the fact that pure
software-based attestation, as described earlier, does not support indirect com-
munication between the prover and the veriﬁer, which forms the basis for swarm
attestation. [MAB+18,IST18,ISTZ16] and other sources from the systematic lit-
erature review have been studied to form the basis for the following discussion
of this technique.
==PAGE:101==
6.1 Analysis of relevant approaches
89
6.1.3.1
Advantages
Since swarm attestation protocols are built on top of hybrid attestation tech-
niques, it inherently possesses the same advantages as the ones described in
6.1.2.1. New advantages brought by swarm attestation are as follows:
1. Scalability and eﬃciency. Naturally, swarm attestation protocols aim
at scalable attestation of swarms of devices. While plain software-based
and hybrid attestation protocols consider the scenario where there is a
limited number of provers (often only handling one prover setting), swarm
attestation concerns itself with eﬃciently attesting large networks.
In
context of AntibIoTic, if a large amount of devices are connected to a Fog
node, this feature could prove extremely useful.
6.1.3.2
Disadvantages
Disadvantages of using swarm attestation include those described in 6.1.2.2, as
well as the following ones:
1. Necessity to support a network of heterogeneous devices. As men-
tioned in 5.5.2, swarm attestation protocols tend to support heterogeneous
devices to an extent, i.e. as long as they adhere to some minimal require-
ments. In fact, most schemes, such as e.g. [ABI+15,ISTZ16,IST18] require
prover devices to support SMART-like or similar hybrid attestation archi-
tecture. This implies that if some devices comprising the network do not
support those features, they cannot be trusted to perform the attestation
procedure, thus being excluded from the protocol, which is highly unde-
sirable considering that AntibIoTic should be able to protect most IoT
devices, regardless of their architecture.
2. Fog integration and mobility support. None of the studied swarm
attestation schemes seem to take into account Fog computing, which is
the basis for AntibIoTic. Mobile IoT devices which are constantly on the
move (e.g. smart vehicles) pose challenges with regards to ﬁguring out the
most eﬃcient way to attest such devices. One example of a problematic
scenario would be a smart car which receives an attestation request while
being connected to one Fog node, and immediately proceeds its movement,
thereby connecting to a new node.

Potential countermeasures
The following countermeasures could be applied to alleviate the eﬀect of the
disadvantages:
1. One way to address this issue would be to utilize an alternative attes-

posed by the swarm attestation scheme. For example, if a device is not
built on a hybrid architecture, we could utilize software-based attestation
instead, where the veriﬁer (in our case, the Fog node) attests that de-

This way unsupported devices would still get attested, albeit not in the
same fashion as others.
2. This problem is essentially a combination of challenges of Fog computing
and swarm attestation. A research would need to be done in order to
determine the best way to manage mobile devices. For example, if
a device has left the network in which the attestation protocol has been
initiated, the initiator Fog node could convey this information to the next
Fog node the device connects to, which could attempt to e.g. attest that
device directly. In any case, it would be required to determine an eﬃcient
way to ensure integrity of devices which are constantly on the move.

Summary
Swarm attestation is an application of hybrid attestation architectures, which
allows for scalable and eﬃcient attestation of swarms of devices. Examples
include e.g.
large factories with many smart devices in need of attestation.
Challenges include integrating Fog paradigm with swarm attestation

6.1.4
"Not on-demand" attestation
This attestation type is based on hybrid architecture and provides certain en-
hancements to it. The only paper giving a proposal of such technique discovered
during literature review was the one by Carpent et al. [CRT18b], who address the

issues of mobile ("come-and-go") malware and DoS attacks against the prover.
This paper has been studied and used to create an overview of this technique.
Its more detailed (yet succinct) description has been given in 5.5.2.
6.1.4.1
Advantages
Just as swarm attestation, ERASMUS (the protocol proposed by [CRT18b]) is
built on top of hybrid attestation techniques, which is why it inherently possesses
the same advantages as the ones described in 6.1.2.1.
New advantages this
technique brings are as follows:
1. Protection against mobile malware. Previous static attestation schemes
can be classiﬁed as "on-demand", meaning that the attestation occurs
whenever it is requested.
This approach does not address the issue of
mobile malware, which is able to "come and go", thus avoiding classi-
cal methods of attestation.
ERASMUS is based on self-measurements
which are collected and validated by the veriﬁer at certain points in time.
By making the device frequently measure its state it becomes possible
to detect mobile malware without having to initiate a challenge-response
protocol.
2. Protection against computational DoS attacks on the prover.
Other attestation schemes have to address the fact that attestation is typ-
ically a computationally heavy operation, especially for low-power devices,
which means that the prover (in our case, an IoT device) has to be pro-
tected against a "malicious veriﬁer" (in our case, an attacker aiming to
imitate the Fog node) - a problem addressed by e.g. [BRST16]. Such at-
tacks could, for example, target a smart lock in order to make it perform
an attestation routine over and over, hindering it from opening the door.
This is typically solved by authenticating attestation requests in order to
ensure that they indeed were sent by the veriﬁer. ERASMUS protects
against this kind of attacks by design; as the prover measures its own
state, the veriﬁer can only request the measurements which had already
been taken; in other words, it cannot force the prover to perform a heavy
computation.
3. Avoiding forcing the prover to attest. Computationally-heavy at-
testation may mean that the device needs to stop performing its original
task in order to compute a response to the challenge sent by the veriﬁer.
By changing the veriﬁer’s role to collecting measurements from the prover
rather than forcing it to attest, the attestation procedure becomes more

sensitive to the prover’s original task, as custom scheduling can be im-
plemented, allowing the prover to conduct self-measurements when it is
free from performing other tasks. Referring to the example from above,
AntibIoTic bot could be used in order to enable the smart lock to schedule
its attestation based on its usage patterns, avoiding ending up in a situ-
ation where the user has to wait before the attestation procedure is over
in order to open the door.
6.1.4.2
Disadvantages
Disadvantages of this method include those described in 6.1.2.2, as well as the
following ones:
1. Participating devices must be of hybrid architecture. The ERAS-
MUS scheme requires the devices to adhere to SMART+ hybrid archi-
tecture, which means that they have to have ROM, MPU, and a reliable
read-only clock (RROC). It thus does not support e.g. pure software-based
attestation, as that would make self-measurements performed by ERAS-
MUS untrustworthy (as they could not be relied upon). Once again, this
makes this approach not as ﬂexible for use in AntibIoTic, as not all devices
would be able to perform the routine, and, correspondingly, get attested.
6.1.4.3
Potential countermeasures
The following countermeasures could be applied to remedy the disadvantages:
1. Similarly to swarm attestation, it could be possible to utilize an alter-
native attestation method for the devices which do not adhere to the
SMART+ architecture required by the scheme. For example, software-
based attestation could be used for devices unsupported by the ERAS-
MUS scheme. That would require enhancing the AntibIoTic bot and the
Fog node code with functionality allowing them to distinguish between
diﬀerent types of devices and use the appropriate attestation method.
6.1.4.4
Summary
"Not on-demand" attestation is an application of hybrid attestation architec-
tures which aims at enhancing them by ensuring protection against mobile

malware and computational DoS attacks on the prover. Moreover, it

high degree of ﬂexibility and security. While the scheme demands for minimal
hardware requirements, it could be addressed by using alternative attestation
methods when those are not fulﬁlled.

6.1.5.1
Advantages

Disadvantages of all of the described attestation types include the following:
1. No runtime attack protection. All of the described techniques are
based on static attestation, which, as described in 5.5.2, only measures
integrity of some static object (such as memory, an executable, or, in our
case, AntibIoTic binary or memory of an IoT device), and thus does not
protect against runtime attacks, which do not modify the code, but rather
hijack control ﬂow of the application.
2. Diﬃculty of maintaining and updating prover conﬁgurations by
the veriﬁer. In order to validate the response sent from the prover to the
veriﬁer, the latter must maintain memory and hardware conﬁgurations of
the prover. This can become complicated when the amount of provers
which must be veriﬁed is large; also, when a software update is due, the
corresponding information must be sent to the veriﬁer. In AntibIoTic, if
==PAGE:106==
94
Selection of software protection technique for AntibIoTic
a device is setup to always be connected to the same Fog node, this might
not be a problem; however, if the device is mobile, all the Fog nodes to
which it connects must obtain its memory and hardware conﬁguration,
which poses a challenge.
6.1.5.3
Potential countermeasures
Remedies which could be applied to the disadvantages include the following:
1. This is a property which is the consequence of using static attestation.
Dynamic attestation techniques address this problem, but they possess
higher hardware requirements and signiﬁcant overhead.
2. It must be ensured that whenever an update occurs (which is assumed
to originate from the veriﬁer), the updated software conﬁguration is
recorded on the veriﬁer side. With regards to multiple provers, the
problem depends on the amount of available storage of the veriﬁer. In
AntibIoTic, once a new device connects to a Fog node, the node should
have its conﬁguration. This could be provided during initial setup or by
sending the conﬁguration information over a secure channel from the Fog
node the device was connected to previously. Further research would be
required to determine the best way to accomplish this. With regards to
updates, a potential solution would be checking the update integrity and
storing it on the Fog node’s side before passing it over to the device. Once
again, further research is needed to evaluate feasibility of this approach.
6.1.5.4
Summary
While all of the described techniques adhere to the initial goal of detecting
software modiﬁcations, none of them protect against runtime attacks.
Even though dynamic attestation techniques concern themselves with that very
problem, their hardware requirements and performance overhead tend to be
quite high. Finally, an important issue to resolve is maintaining and updat-
ing prover conﬁgurations by the veriﬁer, which is further complicated in
case of AntibIoTic due to supporting mobility, and calls for further research in
order to establish the best way to achieve this.

6.2
Evaluation and discussion
In the previous section we have outlined advantages, disadvantages, and poten-
tial counter-measures to the disadvantages of the selected techniques. As it has
been shown, all of the techniques support detecting software modiﬁcation,
which is the main goal we aim to achieve. However, they all have issues which
must be dealt with. In order to propose the most appropriate techniques, we
take into account more detailed requirements we would like the chosen solution
to support.
6.2.1
Ideal scenario
In the best case scenario, we would like the solution to have the following fea-
tures:
• Heterogeneity.
Just as the current version of AntibIoTic, it should
be applicable to a wide range of IoT devices, regardless of their role or
architecture.
• Low requirements. Many IoT devices are designed to perform quite
speciﬁc tasks, which is one of the reasons why their processing capabilities
tend to be quite low. This is why we would like the proposed solution to
have correspondingly low requirements when it comes to e.g. hardware
conﬁguration of the device.
• High security. Clearly, it is desired to ensure the highest level of secu-
rity for the devices protected by the solution, including protection against
advanced techniques like mobile (come-and-go) malware and runtime at-
tacks, as well as hardware modiﬁcation attacks.
• Scalability.
Considering potentially large networks of IoT devices, it
would be desired to have a method of ensuring their integrity as eﬃciently
as possible.
6.2.2
Realistic scenario
In realistic scenario, we have seen that none of the chosen techniques can sup-
port all of the desired features. Heterogeneity is supported to an extent by
software-based attestation, as it can be applied to most devices; however, it
has been shown that it is more tricky than anticipated, and challenging to port

96

to various architectures to ensure highest security levels. Moreover, it has
some inherent weaknesses. Low requirements are provided by software-based
attestation as well, considering its lack of explicit hardware requirements; this
contrasts with hybrid attestation, which provides higher security levels while
possessing some lightweight hardware requirements. Scalability is addressed
by swarm attestation, which builds on top of hybrid architectures, thus hav-
ing corresponding requirements and advantages. Some of such schemes even
support absence detection, contributing to higher security. "Not on-demand"
attestation provides some extra security features compared to basic hybrid
attestation, such as protection against mobile malware and computational DoS
attacks, at a cost of adding another piece of hardware - RROC - as a requirement
to the scheme. Overall, and as mentioned before, we observe that in order to
achieve better security guarantees, it is often necessary to introduce additional
hardware requirements to the system. Figure 6.1 provides a concise illustration

6.3
Recommendations
Having provided an analysis and evaluation of the approaches which have been
considered relevant in the context of AntibIoTic, we aim to provide a set of
recommendations with respect to which solution (or multiple) could be imple-
mented in order to enhance the system with software protection. Note, that
those recommendations do not intend to provide a perfect solution (fulﬁlling all
of the properties outlined in 6.2.1), as this has been shown to be unrealistic;
but rather aim at improving the system as it is today by making it more secure,
with the possibility of adjusting and implementing more features in the future.
6.3.1
An immediate solution: software-based attestation
with physical attack mitigation
Conducting a literature review, studying relevant works and analyzing them, it
has become clear that software-based attestation is far from being the perfect
solution; its disadvantages include strong assumptions and diﬃcult requirements
to the design of the attestation procedure. However, based on what we have seen,
it is the only type of attestation lacking explicit hardware requirements. This
makes it applicable to a wide range of IoT devices, ensuring heterogeneity,
which is one of the desired features of AntibIoTic. While this attestation type
does not provide strongest security guarantees, many of its problems can be
addressed with the corresponding countermeasures, as described in 6.1.1.3. In
particular, the focus would have to be made on:
• Design and portability of the attestation procedure. As described
earlier, software-based attestation relies on having the most eﬃcient im-
plementation of the attestation procedure in order to detect attempts to
alter the routine. This property ideally has to apply across diﬀerent archi-
tectures. While this is, without a doubt, a challenging task, compromises
could be made to make it more realistic. As described in 6.1.1.3, there
exist tools helping to ensure that code cannot be optimized further, which
could be used to design the attestation procedure. With regards to ensur-
ing security across multiple platforms, it could be approached by ﬁguring
out the most popular platforms for IoT devices (either by research or
empirically, using the information gathered by the AntibIoTic bot) and
ensuring that they are supported ﬁrst; in other words, secure procedures
would have to be written for each of them, taking into account individual
platform properties and capabilities. The scheme could be designed using
the framework introduced in [ASSW13], the authors of which outline for-
==PAGE:110==

mal requirements for an attestation procedure, including speciﬁc features
which must be supported by random number generators and checksum
functions used within such a scheme.
• Resistance against collusion and proxy attacks. The ﬁrst issue can
be avoided by the correct design of the attestation procedure, ensuring it
can only be executed sequentially, as done by e.g. [SPVK04]. The second
issue, as described earlier, can be partially mitigated by utilizing the Fog
node capabilities to block all packets arriving from outside the network
directed to a speciﬁc IoT device during attestation, ensuring that it cannot
delegate the computation to anyone else but self.
• Resistance against hardware modiﬁcation attacks. This issue can
be mitigated with the help of absence detection.
The current version
of AntibIoTic already utilizes keep-alive messages to ensure the Fog
node that the bot is active.
If such message is not received within a
certain time-period, the device gets blocked from accessing the Internet.
However, the Fog node begins to trust the device again once it resumes
message transmission.
This could be modiﬁed in the following fashion: whenever a keep-alive
message is missing (i.e. has not been received within a certain predeﬁned
time period), the device could be suspected of being compromised. Just as
before, the device would be blocked. However, even if keep-alive message
transmission continued afterwards, the device would remain blocked until
it has been conﬁrmed to be uncompromised.
Clearly, such approach would introduce a certain number of false posi-
tives into the system. The challenge is then determining a way to conﬁrm
whether the device has indeed been compromised or if it was just a net-
work failure. It would be thus required to ﬁgure out the time period device
should have in order to send a keep-alive message, taking into account how
much time an attacker would need to tamper with the device, which some
works have shown could be from hours to weeks, depending on the at-
tack [Sko11,Sko12]. While not perfect, this is arguably better than having
false negatives, where no detection of hardware modiﬁcation attacks is
supported at all.

6.3.2
A long-term solution: ﬂexible swarm attestation
Hybrid attestation has been shown to be a technique addressing many problems
of software-based attestation.
However, since it possesses minimal hardware
requirements, it does not seem to be an optimal solution for AntibIoTic, as
some devices would have to be left unsupported. Moreover, it relies on attes-
tation code being written on ROM in advance, meaning that devices have to
be manufactured with that technique in mind. Nevertheless, many attestation
protocols rely on hybrid architectures in order to function, as they have some
==PAGE:113==

undisputed advantages. In particular, both swarm and "not on-demand" at-
testation techniques are based on it.
The former ensures scalability of the
attestation protocol in large networks, which could potentially be a relevant ex-
tension of AntibIoTic. The latter provides additional security measures, such
as protection against mobile malware and inherent prevention of computational
DoS attacks.
Based on this, one way to further strengthen and enhance AntibIoTic would
be to make it support those techniques without giving up supporting weaker
(non-hybrid) devices. Due to this, a long-term recommendation would be to
implement a ﬂexible swarm attestation. This idea is based on the following
points:
• Implementation of heterogeneous swarm attestation. Current swarm
attestation schemes, such as [ABI+15, ISTZ16, IST18] work on networks
of devices adhering to some kind of hybrid architecture. In the context of
AntibIoTic, we want to support a wide range of devices, including those
not adhering to those requirements. As described in 6.1.3.3, this could be
achieved by using software-based attestation (as in the ﬁrst recommen-
dation) on devices not supporting the minimal hardware requirements.
This would mean having to make the bot and the server distinguish which
devices support this approach, and act correspondingly.
• Supporting "not on-demand" attestation for eﬃciency and ex-
tra security. As mentioned in 6.1.4.1, not on-demand techniques include
beneﬁts such as extra protection against mobile malware, as well as inbuilt
prevention of DoS attacks on the prover, resulting in improved security.
Moreover, this type of attestation allows devices to schedule the times
their state gets measured (as they perform it themselves), which makes
it possible for them to continue performing their main function when-
ever required, and only proceed with the attestation when they have a
window of opportunity - this could e.g. be controlled by the AntibIoTic
bot to optimize device usage. The approach is based on hybrid architec-
ture SMART+, which requires devices to possess the lightweight hardware
components ROM, MPU, and RROC.
Both of these points contribute to both scalability and security. Authors of
the "not on-demand" attestation protocol (ERASMUS) [CRT18b] shortly men-
tion the idea of ERASMUS being applied to swarm attestation schemes, noting
that it would be both faster, as well as more easily implementable in the con-
text of dynamic networks. Using this as motivation, and to make the solution
as ﬂexible as possible, we could combine the two techniques in order to reap
the beneﬁts of both approaches. This could work in the following way: a net-

tasks to perform. Each device supporting self-measurement (i.e. adhering to the
requirements of "not on-demand" attestation) performs the measurements at a
certain schedule, which could be set either by the network administrator, the
AntibIoTic bot, or the device itself, depending on its role, privileges, and the cir-

and implement it in a way which would be beneﬁcial in most scenarios. For that
reason, this proposal is considered a more "future oriented" approach.

devices by the Fog nodes; but rather to ensuring security of the Fog nodes

would need to be adjusted depending on the attestation routine selected (if
recommendation 1 is followed).
The second change was adding a message changing the timing interval of keep-
alive messages in AntibIoTic server code. This allows modifying this interval
when a corresponding command is received from the Fog node. This was done
to facilitate potential experiments with the interval in order to enhance the bot
with capture detection (detection of invasive and semi-invasive attacks requiring
disconnecting device from the network for extended periods of time). In general,
a new message type could be added instead, which would be sent with frequency
deemed reasonable depending on various factors.
As mentioned before, the amount of changes is minimal; however, they could
be used as a base in order to implement some of the suggested improvements to
the system.
Some further steps which were not made due to lack of time but could be
natural at this point in order to test the ideas of the thesis (related to the ﬁrst
recommendation) could be:
==PAGE:125==
113
• Implement an attestation procedure within the attester module, which
could either make use of the added SHA-256 functionality, or some other
hash function depending on the procedure needs. In all likelihood, some
kind of PRNG function would have to be implemented as well. In partic-
ular, [ASSW13] outline speciﬁc requirements the hash and PRNG func-
tions must uphold to in order to be considered secure for attestation. Note,
that the same procedure would have to be implemented on the AntibIoTic
server side as well. Moreover, the two will have to be bound together. Pos-
sibly, a new message with ATTEST command would have to be added
to this aim.
• Add the code to the AntibIoTic server to facilitate protection against
proxy attacks. This could utilize already implemented functions working
with iptables to disallow IoT devices sending data to and receiving data
from new (non-established) connections while the attestation procedure is
running.
