Abstract 
One of the major challenges urban communities are facing is congestion and pollution. From a traffic 
engineering perspective, congestion and pollution are interdependent. This is especially true when 
sudden incidents take place, and traffic management becomes extremely difficult for transport 
authorities. Incidents can be variable in regards to location and severity. The degree of disruption 
depends on many aspects, including the number of lane closures, the severity, and the geometry of 
the location. These incidents can cause great disruption to the traffic and create a polluted 
environment through the higher CO2 emissions. As the focus increases on intelligent transport 
systems, various Traffic Management Centres (TMCs) are trying to incorporate new technologies to 
predict real-time travel times, speeds and delays. However, in order to accomplish this, it is necessity 
to analyse the network through different possible scenarios in order to gain a better understanding of 
the location; however, an analysis of various incident functions in the field is impossible. Nonetheless, 
microsimulation tools enable us to perform various operational functionalities on networks in order 
to verify different scenarios. This study uses the microsimulation tool VISSIM to implement incident 
scenarios. The VISSIM model was created for one of the most congested highways in Denmark, 
Hillerød Motorway, and calibrated against travel times and flows. Incident characteristics such as 
incident duration, severity in terms of lane closures and different demand levels were given as inputs 
to the system. The potential output expected from the simulation system is travel time, vehicle 
throughput, delays and network performance. Some of the MOEs obtained from the simulation can 
be used to identify the bottlenecks and mitigate the congestion through effective incident 
management. In addition to this, some regression models were developed in order to study the 
characteristics of speed during the period between reduced speed state caused by incident and traffic 
flows returning to their normal state.

Chapter 1 
1. Introduction 
1.1. Traffic Management 
To mitigate traffic jams on motorways, it is very important to manage traffic capacity efficiently and 
implement better traffic management strategies in order to ensure that the flows will move further 
and to avoid jams. Traffic Management (Helman 2004) involves multiple strategies and should work 
together with different organisations such as the police, media and rescue operation teams, etc. Some 
of the important tasks of TM are the maintenance of traffic during peak hours, using extra lanes 
according to travel demand, the establishment of sensors, loop detectors, variable message signs and 
many other managing tasks. Managing ramps and adaptive signals at the exits and entrances of the 
highway are also very crucial to the traffic system. 
The main objectives of TM are: 
1) Minimise the time for incident detection and verification 
2) Minimise the response time (this includes dispatching rescue personal and teams) 
3) Exercise proper TM strategy and optimise the management of people and different equipment, 
use an appropriate lane closure strategy) 
4) Convey accurate information to the managers so that they can make proper route choices 
As mentioned above, TM is multi-disciplinary field where different organisations work 
collaboratively to ensure an efficient response system. It is very important that these organisations 
collectively perform their specific role at the incident location in order to minimise the impact of the 
incident. 
One important aspect of intelligent TM is to predict the speed on freeways for shorter time intervals 
for the links in a traffic network, with the help of observed speeds of that link and adjacent links. 
These predictions will be used to predict speeds during induced traffic periods such as incidents and 
sudden changes in flow. 
Why is this important? 
TM is very important during incidents because the congestion will spread so fast that it can have a 
huge impact on the network, resulting in delays and a huge impact on the environment. Moreover, 
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
 
 
 
there is a high possibility of a second crash if incident detection is not prompt and delays in exercising 
the key management strategies to ensure the efficient movement of the vehicles. An efficient TIM 
strategy will help to reduce congestion, delay, fuel consumption and environmental impacts. Society 
will therefore benefit from improved TIM implementation. In order to ensure sustainable transport, 
it is vital that transport authorities should come up with new innovative TIM strategies and planning. 
Several transport authorities around the world are currently working on optimising the TM process 
under incident conditions so that the subsequent impact will be mitigated. Many researchers have 
shown that effective ramp management, traffic diversion, lane propagations and variable speed limits 
could save a considerable amount of travel time and reduce the impact of emissions as well at the 
possibility of secondary crashes.  
1.2. Problem Statement 
Non-recurrent traffic incidents are a major source of congestion in urban environments. Studies have 
shown that their impacts on emissions, secondary crashes and people’s health are enormous. 
Continuous increasing congestion and its impacts have made transportation engineers around the 
world look for solutions that will mitigate the current levels of traffic and manage future traffic 
efficiently. We frequently see commuters encountering significant problems on major roads and 
arterials around major cities. The effective implementation of traffic operation to curb the delays is 
the most important aspect of Traffic Management Centres (TMCs). TIM has existed for many years 
but the advancement of technology and increased processing speed of computers has given it a new 
dimension and various technologies have been invented to tackle congestion issues. Building various 
scenarios and making a set of decision-making tools are essential in ensuring the mobility, safety and 
health of the citizens. The task of managing traffic has become a major challenge for all cities. 
Accordingly, traffic organisations need to develop different models and verify the kinds of response 
they should make when an incident takes place. The major roads across the cities tend to encounter 
sudden increases in traffic during peak hours, which causes delays. This problem is exacerbated by 
factors such as breakdowns, accidents, and holidays and sporting events. These occurrences can, 
however, be effectively handled if the TM organisations analyse the causes and implement an 
effective response system. An effective response system refers to the different route choice strategies 
and message conveyance systems such as VMS and VSL along with the appropriate lane closure/lane 
opening. All these tasks need trained, experienced people who can implement pre-prepared strategies. 
Developing various planning responses and using those at the appropriate time will be crucial for an 
effective and efficient response to the incident. Most organisations focus on the isolation of vehicles 
==PAGE:12==
 
11 
 
 
 
from the incident location and improving the nearby flows. There are huge impacts resulting from 
such incidents on the network during the peak hours. These will spread congestion to the arterials and 
adjacent roads, causing widespread negative impacts upon society. Most TM research consists of 
analysing the impact of incidents the resulting flows, and it is crucial for these organisations to pre-
assess critical locations and develop preplanning methodologies to bring back traffic network to a 
normal situation. This study will focus on quantifying the impacts of an incident in terms of delays 
in travel time, along with a reduction of speed, and will also attempt to reveal the relation between 
speeds of the link with the help of regression models. 
1.3. Research Goal 
The main goal of this research is to quantify the delays due to the occurrence of non-recurrent 
incidents in motorway. This entails developing different incident scenarios and analysing the impacts 
of them on the network. These scenarios can give a deep insight of traffic situations and help to 
develop different strategies that will aid TMCs in conducting their operations efficiently during the 
period. Speed and travel time estimations are very important to communicate to commuters and 
various organisations need to send out reliable travel times to them. Microsimulation models facilitate 
the testing of some of these conditions in advance in order to identify incident characteristics during 
the period to gain a better understanding of the situation. This will help traffic managers to take an 
appropriate decision to mitigate the impact of the incident and thus reduce the congestion and 
pollution. 
This study gives us an opportunity to study the flow characteristics, travel patterns and fluctuations 
in demand. It is vital for any transport analysis project to test the network with different traffic 
assignments and evaluate its performance in terms of travel time, delay, speed and overall network 
performance.  
In order to achieve this goal, the following objectives have been devised: 
 Build a network for incident modelling and identify key locations 
 Calibrate the model to replicate field conditions 
 Validate the model 
 Analyse the network performance in base scenario 
 Model an incident with specific time duration, lane closure and give different demand levels 
as an input 
==PAGE:13==
 
12 
 
 
 
 Analyse the impacts of different demand levels 
 build regression models for speed with and without incident 
This kind of study facilitates defining to what extent the network will tolerate a reduced capacity 
during the incident period. VISSIM simulation outputs give us an insight of network performance 
under different circumstances, but are very unrealistic to test in the real world. These results can be 
useful in order for TMCs to adopt various planning strategies to reduce congestion in the main 
network and adjacent arterials due to the spillover. This study is organised into 7 chapters. In the 
following chapter, a literature review on various topics related to incident modelling is offered. 
Following the literature review, the methodology chapter documents model building, calibration and 
validation and experiment. In the results chapter, performance of the network in terms of delay, travel 
time, speed and vehicle flows are presented analysed. In the discussion chapter, findings from the 
research are discussed. In the conclusion chapter, the conclusion and proposals for future research are 
presented. In the last chapter, Appendices and references are presented.

Chapter 2 
2. Literature review 
The papers reviewed in this chapter related to the topics such as incident modelling, traffic operations 
management, microscopic simulation tools and traffic incident impacts on traffic networks. There are 
many models developed to predict incident duration from historical data and its impact on travel times 
and delays in the network. Microscopic simulation models allow us to replicate the real traffic 
conditions and perform various operational analyses on it to verify the real impact on the network, as 
it is often difficult to test these situations in the field. Calibrating these models in order to replicate 
the field statics is often tedious and cumbersome due to the various parameters involved as well as 
being time consuming. Since the major research in this paper regards the impact of incidents on the 
network, it is necessary to have a well-calibrated model to provide accurate results.    
2.1. Traffic Incident Management 
TIM is a multi-disciplinary process which involves the different stages of detecting an accident, 
responding, dispatching the workforce and clearing the incident location in order to restore the traffic 
flows to normal levels in and provide a safe and quick journey for the commuters.  
The United States Department of Transportation - Federal Highway Administration (Science 
Applications International Corporation & American Transportation Research Institute 2010) has 
prescribed several guidelines for TIM strategies with the help of modelling, simulation and analysis. 
The manual describes many topics related to TIM, including identifying the key factors for secondary 
crashes, using simulations for ‘what if’ scenarios, capacity loss studies during the incident, 
progressive lane openings, estimating total delays and cost/benefits analysis on secondary impacts. 
Using a Simulation to Test Traffic Incident Management Strategies, the Benefits of Preplanning 
(Wirtz et al. 2005) et al performed a study on a dynamic traffic assignment tool to analyse TIM 
strategies on a freeway. Incidents of various scales were modelled and simulated in a VISSIM model 
to envisage the severity of incidents and their effect on congestion. The study focuses on impacts of 
TM and formulates different scenarios with the aim of making the entire process more effective. 
Different lane closure levels were tested to envisage how the congestion spreads to other parts of the 
network. The majority of TIM strategies revolve around clearing the directly impacted area due to 
==PAGE:15==
 
14 
 
 
 
the incident, but here great focus has been placed on spillover effects to adjacent links which are 
indirectly impacted by the traffic incident. Preplanning response strategies offer more time for traffic 
control implementation and also allow the authorities to implement them faster. In the end, regression 
models were developed, including congestion as a dependent variable and scale, duration, signal 
retiming as. 
(Baykal-Gürsoy et al. 2006) conducted research on Evaluation of Incident Management Strategies 
and Technologies using an integrated traffic/incident management simulation to test the impacts of 
various strategies for traffic incident management. The process involved traffic simulation, incident 
generation and incident response simulation. The incident response simulation process has different 
scenarios where the effects of VMS, cellular phone usage, and patrolling services were taken into 
consideration. The software is based on cellular transmission model proposed by (Daganzo n.d.). 
2.2. Incident Modelling 
Yaser e Hawas, (Hawas 2007) developed a sim-s external module to replicate an accident and used a 
NETSIM simulation model to conduct the experiment. Descriptions of various car-following models 
and their limitations were discussed. The validation was based on averages of traffic variables; 
however, the study has several shortcomings. For instance, one could demonstrate that for the same 
aggregate measure (e.g. average link travel time), the spatial and temporal distribution might vary 
significantly. 
(Hadi et al. 2007) conducted research on different microscopic simulation tools to model an accident 
in order to analyse the correlation between the capacity and car-following parameters. 
Microsimulation tools such as AIMSUN, VISSIM and CORSIM were used to perform this task. The 
paper also described the difference between three different simulation models, namely Macro, Micro 
and Mesco. Incident impacts on capacity in accordance to (HCM 2010) were discussed and compared 
with the experiment. The main output of this paper is an analysis of capacity reduction in three-lane 
highways due to incidents as well as capacity prevails during no-incident situations. 
(Dia et al. 2006) conducted an experiment on an Australian highway and calibrated the model to 
replicate the field conditions. Two sets of data were used to calibrate and validate the model and later 
emission/fuel consumption models were developed to calculate different scenarios. SIDRA, a tool for 
emission calculations, was used to conduct the analysis. How a reduction in incident duration benefits 
society in terms of pollution, congestion and delay was discussed. Transport network performance 
==PAGE:16==
 
15 
 
 
 
such as speed, queues and travel times were analysed and compared in No Incident and Incident 
situations. 
Freeway Incidents: Modelling and Simulation (Martin 2011) is a paper which has performed research 
on highways in Utah and conducted an analysis of VMS performance, delay statistics, network 
performance and queue lengths in different locations under different circumstances. Different VMS 
levels and lane closures constituted variables. A pre-calibrated VISSIM model was used to replicate 
an incident and perform an analysis of travel times, average network level performance, vehicle 
throughput and delays. Different strategies were developed to support the decision-support system in 
different scenarios. The main goal of this research is to envisage the impact of VMS level as an input 
and check the traffic diversion rate, which also includes an analysis of alternative routes taken by the 
drivers according to VMS display levels. 
Delay Estimation for Traffic Flow Interrupted by Incidents (Baykal-Gürsoy et al. 2006) - presented 
a report on delay estimation during an incident based on queuing theory and validated it with recent 
simulation-based model INTEGRATION. The results from a queueing theory- and simulation-based 
model offered a very close estimation of incident delays for link performance analysis. The research 
recommends queueing based- over simulation-based models as an alternative. 
2.3. Calibration of Microscopic Simulation Models 
Calibration of VISSIM for a Congested Freeway (Gomes et al. 2004)- this document describes the 
methodology to build and calibrate the network in VISSIM and outlines the strategy to implement it. 
This also explores gathering and processing data from PeMS system and the estimation of OD 
matrices. The model includes HOV lanes, ramps and several bottlenecks. The research revealed that 
one bottleneck was due to the geometric design and another due to the weaving behaviour of the 
drivers. The calibration process involves the observation of driving behaviour in complicated areas 
and modifying driving behaviour parameters in accordance to them, with the VISSIM model being 
able to produce field conditions. The authors conclude that the VISSIM simulation tool is well-suited 
for freeway analysis which involves complex interactions. Research continued with different ramp 
strategies on highway during peak-hour traffic. 
 Methodology for the Calibration of VISSIM in Mixed Traffic (Manjunatha et al. 2013) outlines the 
methodology for calibrating a VISSIM model in mixed traffic conditions. Normally, driving 
behaviour studies in mixed traffic are a fairly complex procedure. Two intersections were chosen in 
==PAGE:17==
 
16 
 
 
 
order to implement the calibration process. An initial process consists of simulating a model with 
default parameters and performing a sensitivity analysis in order to select a suitable range for the 
subsequent process. A two-way ANOVA process is carried out to find the significance of the 
parameters as well as the interaction between them. Following this, a GA algorithm is used with 
VISSIM COM interface to tune the parameters which can produce minimum error when respectively 
observed and simulated. 
Calibration of Microsimulation Traffic model Using Neural Networks (Ištoka Otković et al. 2013) 
discusses the process of comparing and minimising differences between observed data and simulated 
data using neural networks concepts. The developed model compares the actual travel times and 
queues with simulated travel times and queues to validate the model. A prediction function is 
developed in neural networks with the help of MATLAB and VISSIM COM environments for each 
combination of input parameters with a specified range for each. The condition for comparison of 
modelled and measured values of travel time is set with a 5% error margin. The validation process 
was performed through two different datasets at the same location on a different day and with a 
different roundabout. Both results have shown greater correlation between observed and measured 
values. 
This is another approach followed by (Park & Won 2006) et al to calibrate a VISSIM model. The 
calibration process consists of identifying the suitable parameters and defining appropriate ranges for 
each. An LH sampling technique is used to identify different combinations of parameters and run the 
simulation model with a random seed of 5 multiplied by 124 combinations. An average of five 
simulation runs were taken as the performance for each of the 124 parameters set. Excel Solver was 
used to find a suitable set of parameters out of those available by specifying target travel time value. 
Simulation runs were performed with parameter sets and a t-test was performed on travel time 
outputs. Based on suitable P-values and visualisations, a suitable candidate set was finalised and a 
validation test conducted with a different set of data. 
Virginia Transport Authority (Park, B.; Schneeberger 2003) has set rules and regulations on how to 
conduct a calibration process on microscopic simulation models. The process involves network 
building and initial simulation with default parameters. A feasibility analysis will be conducted on 
default parameters to check which parameter was more influential on traffic network performance. In 
addition, a histogram will be constructed on travel time measurements to see whether the model will 
fit a new travel time from a different day. If the default parameters provide acceptable results, then 
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
 
 
 
the model will be used for further analysis. If not, a genetic algorithm will be used as an optimisation 
algorithm to reveal the suitable parameter set for the model. Finally, a fitness function will be used 
to highlight what could be the margin of error for simulation models with respective field 
measurements.

Chapter 3 
3. Methodology 
The methodology in this chapter proposes to develop a base network model and perform calibration 
and validation to replicate real-time field measurements. After obtaining a well-calibrated model in 
terms of various operational MSEs, it will develop and simulate several incident locations to analyse 
the possible effects on networks. The critical location for incidents was chosen based on accident data 
which is mapped along the freeway. This map is obtained from vejman.dk, Danish Road Directorate. 
The results obtained from incident modelling will be used to analyse the traffic characteristics of the 
network. Several scenarios were designed with respective lane closures and varying demand levels 
in order to envisage the delay statistics. Multiple scenarios were developed for different incident 
locations to compare the importance of location across varying results. In the end, several regression 
models were developed to compare non-incident and incident scenario speeds, the following process 
can be seen in Figure 1.

3.1. Infrastructure Description 
The network chosen for this experiment was Hillerød Motorway, which is located in Sjælland region 
in the Greater Copenhagen Area. The test network was constructed from Farum N to Værebrovej 
towards Copenhagen City, as shown in Figure 1, since the experiment will simulate peak-hour traffic 
from 7 am - 8 am in the morning, only N-S running traffic was considered. The network consists of 
five exit points defined as (TSA 10 to TSA 5). The location of the network was chosen since it is one 
of the busiest motorways in Denmark and faces routine congestion problems due to the capacity 
problems and non-recurrent incidents on the motorway. The availability of traffic data is also very 
important for the calibration process: since the Danish Road Directorate has traffic counts along the 
stretch of motorway on a daily basis, this part of network was well-suited for the experiment. 
The test network is, in total, 11 km, and consists of signalised ramps and right-of-way ramp 
intersections which encounter minimum to peak congestion levels during the morning traffic. Traffic 
volumes, speeds, road geometry, demand data, reduced speed areas, and hard shoulder utilisation had 
been taken into consideration before starting the network-building process. The motorway is 
predominately two-lane and some parts of the network utilise the hard shoulder during the morning 
peak hours. These locations are critical during an incident, since there is no availability for emergency 
parking. These factors were also considered in order to replicate real incident characteristics. 
The traffic on Hillerød Motorway has been directly affected by the frequent changes in the 
infrastructure along with the newly expanded road network. The Hillerød area has implemented a 
number of building projects and is conducting a massive development drive which can have an 
adverse effect in the form of ever-increasing traffic. The cities along the Hillerød Motorway corridor, 
Farum, Værløse and Gladsaxe, are all municipalities involved in several major infrastructure projects 
which will put tremendous pressure on the network by 2020 (Transport- 2016).

There is also a proposal from the Danish Transport Ministry that an expansion of Hillerød Motorway 
will be carried out between Hillerød and Allerød to alleviate traffic congestion in one of the busiest 
networks in Sjælland (13). All these developments will increase congestion in Ring 4 (Bagsværd) and 
Gladsaxe (Ring 3). 
3.2. Data Collection 
The data collection process is carried out with the help of the system developed by the Danish Road 
Directorate for traffic analysis. Mastra and Hastrid were used to collect vehicle counts, speeds and 
travel times. The data collection process consisted of building a matrix with vehicle flows from 7 am 
- 8 am. In addition, different set data was collected to validate the model outputs. There were a number 
of data collection points on the motorway and the model also involved data collection points at the 
same locations, which makes it easier to compare both results. There were, however, a number of 
constraints in terms of kinds of data available so the utmost importance was given to utilising as many 
different sources of data to validate the model. The most important MOEs on the freeway include: 
==PAGE:22==
 
21 
 
 
 
 Travel Time 
 Vehicle Flows 
 Speed 
 Delays 
It was also very important to collect field operational data such as speed limits, signal timings and 
hard shoulder areas used. This study required visiting the location in order to obtain a better 
geographical knowledge and consequently improve the reality of the model. The online systems 
consist of data regarding travel times from one stretch to the next and speeds between those two 
stretches. 
The data collection process also involved the classification of vehicles. In this model, only cars and

3.3. VISSIM Model Development 
VISSIM was the microscopic or traffic simulation model used to build the Hillerød Motorway 
network. It is been an effective tool for modelling public transportation systems as well as 
reproducing freeway driving conditions. More recently, it is being widely used for traffic operations 
analysis. The model works on two different kinds of traffic model, namely Wiedemann 74 and 
Wiedemann 99. The driving behavioural model consists of four stages: free drive, approaching, 
following and breaking. The driving behavioural model works on the principles of certain kinds of 
response to the relative speed and distances with respect to preceding vehicles. Wiedemann 99 is 
generally used to model freeway corridors. The driving behaviour model contains various parameters 
such as desired speed, acceleration, deceleration, lane-changing behaviour, and safety distance factors 
for different vehicle types according to different sections of the road. Drivers adjust their behaviour 
according to the preceding vehicle’s speed and distance in order to match the abovementioned speeds 
and perform according to their capabilities. Vehicles can change their lanes either through forced 
route choices such as intersections or exits and to reach their destinations faster. Network coding is 
performed with the help of the open street map which is available with the VISSIM software. Links 
and connectors were used to construct the stretch and Google Earth views were used to model recent 
changes in the infrastructure. Utmost care was taken to reproduce the original traffic conditions in 
terms of speed limits and hard shoulder utilisation (Gomes et al. 2004). 
VISSIM provides different kinds of signal programming to facilitate traffic signal systems at the 
intersections. This has been very useful for constructing from a very simple traffic control with fixed 
time to a complex one with signal stage performance using a specific programme called the VISVAP 
interface, which provided loop detectors in order to accommodate peak-hour traffic in the system. 
The loop detectors recorded the vehicle data and generated a file used for further analysis of green 
times and vehicle throughput to provide free passage the drivers. A detailed description of VISVAP 
can be found in PTV manual (PTV 2013)  
3.3.1. Lane change behaviour 
Lane changing behaviour is another quite important aspect of a microsimulation model under DTA 
conditions. Since vehicles know their destination prior to their journey, they can decide when to 
change lane in order to reach their destination. VISSIM has multiple lane change parameters which 
play an important role in the calibration process. Lane changes are necessary in order to improve 
==PAGE:26==
 
25 
 
 
 
vehicle performance, or sometimes it is necessary to change lanes under circumstances such as off-
ramps or lane drops and aggressive behaviour in order to reach destinations on time. 
Look back distance: This specifies how far the driver should look beyond before manoeuvring 
towards desired lines. The default value specified by VISSIM is 200 m. This distance can varies from 
(0, ∞) 
Emergency stop distance: Distance before the bifurcation where the driver will stop if he or she has 
not reached the desired lane. Range = (0, ∞). Default = 5 m. 
 Waiting time before diffusion: A vehicle that has come to a halt at the emergency stop position will 
wait, at most, this amount of time for a gap to appear in the adjacent lane. After the waiting time, has 
elapsed, the vehicle is removed from the simulation. Range = (0, ∞), Default = 60 s. (PTV 2013). 
3.3.2. Car following behaviour 
 
Figure 4-Car-following behaviour of the VISSIM model (PTV VISSIM 7) (15) 
VISSIM uses psychophysical behaviour developed by Widemann (1974). It has two kinds of model, 
namely Widemann (74) and Widemann (99). The first is primarily used for urban roads and the latter 
for freeways. The concept behind this model is that, whenever a lead vehicle slows down, then the 
==PAGE:27==
 
26 
 
 
 
following vehicle adjusts its speed in accordance to it. When the following vehicle’s speed becomes 
less than the lead vehicle due to an imperfect estimation of speed, it adjusts its speed again to match 
the leading vehicle. This process is an iterative one with acceleration and deceleration by following 
and leading vehicles. The Figure 4 shows the logic explained in this paragraph. The entire process 
revolves around free moving, approaching, following and breaking modes. 
 AX: Describes the distance between two vehicles 
 BX: Specifies the minimum distance considered as a safety distance by the drivers 
 CLDV: This is the point where the following driver perceives that he or she is driving faster 
than the leading vehicle. 
 SDV: This is the point where the following driver perceives the difference between the speeds 
with respect to the leading vehicle 
 SDX: Describes the maximum following distance indicating the upper limit of the car 
following process 
 OPDV: This is the point where following driver perceives that he or she is driving more slowly 
than the leading vehicle (Aghabayk et al. 2013)

3.3.3.  Dynamic Assignment 
In a dynamic assignment, we specify travel demand in the form of OD matrices which contain data 
regarding origin and destinations. In traffic assignments, we also need to specify the number of 
vehicles and from which zone to which zone the traffic is moving. Route choice in VISSIM is an 
equilibrium assignment where path choices will be taken into consideration. All drivers have prior 
information on path choices and travel times on each path (whether they are using it or not) obtained 
in previous iterations. Therefore, the solution here is that all drivers try to minimise their travel times, 
and the DTA algorithm performs an iterative procedure to obtain convergence in these conditions 
(Florian et al. 2008). 
Basically, VISSIM takes the following factors into consideration, including travel times on the links, 
cost of the links, and length of the journey. Dynamic assignment is thus quite useful, since it is quite 
a tedious job to manually allocate vehicle routing for all destinations with static assignments. In 
particular, when the size of network increases, it is harder to assign traffic for all destinations. 
In VISSIM, dynamic assignment travel time from one edge the other will be reported along with how 
much time is being spent on that particular edge. In heavy congestion situations, vehicles may 
encompass more than one evaluation period. This problem is handled by also reporting dwelling time. 
VISSIM also allows the supplying of trip chain files which contain very detailed information 
regarding trip zones travelled, purpose of journey and time being spent in that zone. However, this 
procedure requires extra efforts to code. 
VISSIM uses the Method of Successive Averages to set the relative weight of the respective iteration. 
After the measurement of new travel time in the current iteration, the following algorithm uses a 
smoothing factor to compute the next iteration. Travel time will be computed from one edge to the 
other.

The dynamic assignment in VISSIM is an iterative process whereby the simulation is run multiple 
times until the driver finds the best path available based on the experience from previous iterations. 
In the VISSIM simulation, not every driver chooses the best path all the time and some choose less 
attractive paths. In the end, k number of best paths will be used. In the course of the iterations, the 
number of paths will be increased for each iteration and also the situation for traffic changes. An 
evaluation should be performed for all these paths in order to choose the best one. This is achieved 
by calculating the cost of the paths which includes travel distance, travel time and extra costs such as 
toll pricing and extra costs. Travel time will only be available through the simulation performance, 
whereas the costs and distances will be available through the network. 
The selection of a path based on cost functions is a discrete choice problem. The most widely used 
mathematical function, logit function, is used in the VISSIM model. Travel times are measured 
through an evaluation of edges during the evaluation interval. Vehicles report the duration of stay in 
a particular edge during the specific time interval; thus, through collecting the time spent by all 
vehicles, an average travel time will be calculated.  
3.4. Model calibration and validation 
Proper calibration depends on degree of closeness between the fields’ measured data and calibrated 
data. One of the major issues with calibration is the availability of quality data from the field. The 
calibrating process involves the assessment of simulation errors in replicating field statistics. The 
major process consists of observing field conditions and adjusting parameters according to it. This is 
an iterative process which requires checking and adjusting so that the model offers an accurate 
representation for further analysis. Generally, there will be set conditions and targets to reduce the 
process. The calibration process in VISSIM is not generalised, and different organisations follow 
different methods. The full flow diagram of the calibration process is available in Fig 21 in 
Appendices. 
3.4.1. Multi-parameter sensitivity analysis 
The approach followed here is to identify suitable ranges for parameters through literature studies 
and the Danish Road Manual for Microsimulation and prepare a multiple set of parameters which 
will be given as an input to the network. The VISSIM calibration process involves studying the 
performance of the network through a default parameter set and analysing different measures such as 
flows, travel times and speed in the network. The visualisation of traffic flows provides a good idea 
of the locations of bottlenecks and abnormal driving behaviour. If the results are in the acceptable 
==PAGE:31==
 
30 
 
 
 
range, then one can continue to the analysis. In contrast, if the results are not satisfactory, then it is 
necessary to adjust the driving behaviour parameters in order to replicate the field measurements. 
This is an iterative process which involves checking different parameter values, analysing their 
impact, and changing to a different value if necessary. It is important to check one parameter at a time 
to identify the impact on the traffic performance. It is also necessary to classify parameters into two 
categories, i.e. the ones which have more impact and, as the second category, parameters which do 
not have significant impact on the model.  
Weaving and merge behaviours of the links need to be addressed separately to freeway lane selection 
behaviour. The manual selection of parameters causes the adjustment of one MOE, which will deviate 
the target values of the other MOEs. The appropriate solution for this is to tune the parameters in a 
way that makes both MOEs produce results very close to the field description. The relaxation of target 
goals also plays an important role. Another way of calibrating VISSIM parameters is through 
optimisation algorithms, which will minimise the error between modelled and measured values. 
VISSIM allows users to communicate with parameters directly through a COM interface which 
enables us to find the optimal set of parameters. Initially for this study, the optimisation of parameters 
through search algorithms was considered, but it is a complex environment to find the suitable ranges 
for all parameters and then implement algorithms. In the process of finding a suitable range, the 
network performance matched location measures very closely. Therefore, it was decided that the 
model will be used for further analysis. There are some statistical methods which were used to 
measure the difference between field observations and calibrated model measurements.

3.5.1. Incident modelling and evaluating post incidents scenarios 
The experiment is designed to model different incident scenarios and simulate them in order to 
quantify the delays with respect to different demand levels. The simulation output values should be 
analysed in order to estimate the impact of the incident which can be used for further analysis to 
develop different strategies. This study is developed to test the different demand levels during the 
incident duration and predict different MOEs of the network. 
In the VISSIM model, each incident is specified with different attributes such as location, incident

compared to the original. The rubbernecking factor results in added loss of capacity since many 
drivers have a tendency to look at the incident location while driving and thus do not speed up when 
passing through the incident location. 
Another attribute is incident duration, which specifies how long the incident lasted. In the literature 
study, it was depicted as the whole duration of incident management; that is, from identifying the 
incident to total recovery and normalising the flows. Incident duration is influenced by incident type, 
number of vehicles involved, rescue team’s arrival and traffic flows. An experienced TM team will 
usually estimate the clearance time based on incident severity and time period of the incident. In this 
model, incident duration is defined as the time when the lanes are closed to the incoming traffic and 
when the lanes are open again. Based on the literature study, the incident duration for the simulation 
are considered to be 30 minutes and 60 minutes. The simulation period for this study is, however, 60 
minutes, thus the chosen incident durations are within the limits of simulation period.

3.6. Limitations 
1) There are several assumptions and limitations which exist in this research. The first main 
important assumption is signal time duration. A signal programme cycle was created based 
on general experience rather than on any official programme run by the municipality. Since 
the model consists of traffic flows from north to south, and most of the signal programs were 
set at what was considered a reasonable time to ensure vehicles reach their destination zone, 
two minutes’ cycle time was given to the signals, which is very reasonable during the peak-
hour traffic.  
2) The traffic between Frederiksborgvej-Bagsværd (ring 4) traffic was not considered in this 
experiment since there were no vehicle in counts available. The traffic this road generally 
exits at Ring 4 and merges on the freeway; this makes whole process difficult since there are 
no vehicle count on arterials and some ambiguity exists in this location. To be more realistic, 
including this traffic in the network will be a good idea.

5. Discussion 
5.1. Network building 
VISSIM facilitates an effective graphical user interphase in order to build the network. This process 
is efficient and user-friendly. Network building is a time-consuming process where one needs to make 
sure there are no errors in the model. Connectors are used in order to make a connection between 
links and it is crucial for the network to have appropriate connectors between links that they should 
not be overlapped. This process also involves building intersections, barriers, nodes and zones. The 
process entails collecting field measurements for all these and implementing them in a model to 
replicate a real network. VISSIM uses speed distributions rather using a specific speed for all vehicles. 
Vehicle composition comprises 94% cars and 6% HGVs. Each category’s vehicles sometimes need 
different driving behaviour modelling.  
5.2. Data collection 
There was quality data available for flows, travel time and speed measurements. This has helped me 
to calibrate the model to replicate a field situation. However, with vehicle flows alone, it is very 
difficult process an OD matrix if the user doesn’t have access to macroscopic tools such as VISUM. 
A well-calibrated OD matrix is of crucial importance before the traffic assignment process starts, and 
the Danish Road Directorate maintains traffic data in MASTRA and HASTRID which contains flows, 
travel times and speed. These data are used by various organisations in order to evaluate traffic 
systems in their respective municipalities. However, there were no data regarding queues and delays 
to compare to the calibrated model with field measurement. The major problem with data collection 
was the unavailability of counts for arterial roads and real-time incident data. An accurate information 
of traffic diversions near Bagsværd (Ring 4) would have yielded even better calibration results. 
5.3. Traffic assignment 
A Dynamic Traffic Assignment (DTA) is used in this research, DTA is preferred over static 
assignments since it will represent reality more effectively. In DTA, drivers know their journey before 
they start; consequently, in this sense, the model more accurately represents lane change behaviour. 
Moreover, when the network is relatively bigger, static routing will be extremely difficult. However, 
the DTA process requires OD matrices for the respective zones, and the process of making OD 
==PAGE:53==
 
52 
 
 
 
matrices is not available in VISSIM. The model uses three periods in simulation and three different 
OD matrices were given as an input. The first 15 minutes are given as a warm-up period, 1 hour is 
the main evaluation period, and the last 15 minutes are considered as the cooling period. Generally, 
it is preferred that OD matrices are given for smaller time intervals, since it is a function for balancing 
route choices based on the previous time interval. In this research, a one-hour time interval was used 
in OD matrices. Some of the results show that the real-time field speed and model speed for every 
five-minute interval varies slightly but the average speeds of both measures are almost identical.  
5.4. Calibration 
The calibration process in VISSIM is crucial in order to use the network for further analysis. 
Calibration was performed by manually tuning the parameters one by one in order to verify which of 
these parameters have a significant impact on the network and then change it with a different value 
from a range of values. Systematically changing these parameters is called multi-parameter sensitivity 
analysis. This process has been quite tedious since VISSIM has so many parameters that need to be 
tested in order to reveal their significance. The disadvantage of this simulation model is there is no 
standardisation of the calibration process: different people follow different processes. One of the key 
observations during this process is that having very reliable flow data is highly important. The driving 
parameters generally used in VISSIM are the average values obtained from the observations from the 
real world, given that each location is different and driving behaviour varies, and thus these values 
are never going to be the same for all models. The scope and framework of the project have a greater 
impact on calibration and simulation - if we are unsure about our goals, then calibration certainly 
going to be a difficult process. It is recommended that, at the very least, travel time and flows data is 
necessary in order to calibrate a freeway model. Error checking the network is the first step in 
calibration, in order to ensure that the network represents all characteristics of traffic such as reduced 
speed areas, proper traffic signal junctions, speed distributions and that there are no errors in 
connectors. Once this process is complete, it is necessary to check for deficiencies in the default 
model and distinguish areas where further changes need be made. This is the period where actual 
calibration process starts, revisiting our goals and objectives for calibration and verifying if we are 
able to meet our goals. This process can be performed by graphically plotting speed diagrams and 
finding out where are bottlenecks coming from and whether the model matches the actual volume 
demand. For this, it is necessary to visit the site and find out where the actual bottlenecks are along 
with any changes in infrastructure and check whether this is taking place in the model. The next step 
is to analyse the reasons for congestion or bottlenecks and their locations, which may be because of 
==PAGE:54==
 
53 
 
 
 
the merging and weaving behaviour of the drivers. The main problem in this calibration is that we do 
not directly calibrate the input parameters, often we use travel times or flows to calibrate the model. 
Some literature has described the actual measure of the input parameters observed on the field and 
suggested using them as input parameters may give better and more efficient results. However, the 
majority of the research papers do not discuss calibrating acceleration and deceleration functions in 
VISSIM, which are very important parameters for calibration. In this research lane, changing 
parameters was revealed to be crucial for calibration. Some research papers describe the calibration 
process using non-linear optimisation search methods in order to reveal the optimal parameters to 
minimise the error value between observed and model predicted values. This process may yield better 
and more scientifically accurate results, but replicating the real field measure in spatio-temporal 
manner is extremely difficult, although we can replicate in terms of aggregation. One of the key 
observations is the use of the hard shoulder between Værløse-Bagsværd (Ring 4), calibrating this 
place - which consists of difficult weaving behaviour - was crucial in order to calibrate the model. 
Therefore, an infrastructure study before starting the modelling process is necessary. 
5.5. Incident Modelling 
Incident modelling is currently a topic of frequent discussion in the traffic management field. It can 
be used for many different purposes to evaluate how to effectively mitigate the impacts of incidents. 
These impacts vary from reducing congestion, limiting delays, and communicating reliable travel 
times with commuters. Incidents during peak hours have the potential to spread congestion so rapidly 
that the whole system will come to a standstill. This study was carried out in order to explore how an 
incident may impact upon morning peak hour traffic on Hillerød Motorway. One of the drawbacks 
of this research is it will not reveal any concrete solutions for incidents, rather it focuses on the 
subsequent impact and delay statistics. Another important area of analysis for incidents is capacity 
reduction. These were relatively close to (HCM 2010) guidance and were consistent with respect to 
different lane closures and demand levels. Many simulation models do not have any direct features 
to model an incident, and the general practice in creating an incident is to create a temporary obstacle 
in the form of parking a vehicle or, if there is only one lane, then we can place a stop signal. In an 
incident scenario, it is very important to generate the proper merging behaviour at the incident’s 
location. VISSIM has, however, a problem with the visualisation of merging when the traffic quite 
high. Incident duration was chosen as 30 min/60 min depending upon location. The main problem 
with 60-min closing lanes is that this will create huge queues and many vehicles cannot reach the 
destination zone during the simulation period. VISSIM only calculates the travel time for those 
==PAGE:55==
 
54 
 
 
 
vehicles which have reached the destination zone, since only few will reach it, and the result is 
inappropriate travel times. The simulation period and evaluation period needs to be increased to also 
obtain the travel times of these vehicles. One of the major drawbacks in this process is not having any 
real-time incident data to compare the difference between the model and field observations.

6. Conclusions 
This chapter presents the most important aspects of the process and obtained results. Following this, 
it discusses the contributions of this research to evaluate freeways with the help of microsimulation. 
This research documents the usage of the microsimulation tool VISSIM to evaluate incidents on 
freeways, and analyses the relevant MOEs. Rather than solving any specific problem, this study 
instead attempts to try to model incidents and analyse their subsequent impact on the network. The 
methodology presented here is to develop a network model and calibrate it to match field 
measurements in order to perform a valid alternative analysis of the infrastructure. The main findings 
of this research concern identifying the bottlenecks in the network which are more prone to sudden 
incidents. The entrances to the highway at Værløse and exit and entrance at Bagsværd are critical 
locations in this network. Any incidents near these locations will increase travel time significantly. It 
is also evident that different locations have different impacts during the incident. One of the most 
significant features of this study is in finding the appropriate reduction of capacity during the incident 
period. The Highway Capacity Manual’s (2010) capacity availability during the incident has been 
compared to sections of the Danish highway, and it has been found that the available capacity is 
slightly higher than prescribed. Since there was no available data, incident results were unable to be 
compared to real-time incidents. The reduced speeds at incident locations, which are vital for the 
capacity, remained. 
The incident severity, number of lanes closed and travel demand play an important role in predicting 
travel time. Two different locations were reviewed in order to estimate the characteristics of traffic, 
and both differ significantly. A 60-min blocking period was unsuitable for the study of a 1-hour 
simulation period. Consequently, the majority of the evaluations were performed for a 30-minute 
period, which was more practical. An hour of traffic disruption will affect the network quite 
significantly and will take considerable time to recover. Even though the study has some limitations, 
it nonetheless performed reasonably and replicated a real system. As accident severity has, however, 
a bigger magnitude than we can estimate, it is simply impossible to predict the exact nature of the 
accident. This said, these tools help us to pre-analyse the network in order to obtain a better 
understanding of traffic operations. It has also been observed that the better replication of arterials 
will yield considerably greater results. The main objective of the research study is achieved by 
==PAGE:57==
 
56 
 
 
 
demonstrating how to use traffic modelling tools in order to evaluate infrastructure when non-
recurrent incidents take place. 
The second part of this research is to evaluate regression models in order to predict the accuracy of 
speeds with the help of link data. It has been shown that the model tested with incident data has not 
demonstrated a significant correlation with the non-incident dataset. The model’s prediction for speed 
is also dependent on the location. Both locations with incidents have no commonality between them 
with respect to correlation and margin of error. The study also demonstrates the use of simulated data 
to build regression models in order to predict traffic characteristics as well as to identify which of the 
variables are more critical. 
6.1. Future Research 
The methodology followed in this research project consists of network building, calibration and 
incident modelling. Microsimulation models need to be calibrated before they can be used for the 
further analysis of the different scenarios. This model shows a very close resemblance with field 
measurements and can be further used to evaluate different metric of the traffic flows. Some of the 
main future implementations on this network may be modelling emissions for Incident and Non-
incident scenarios, in order to predict the extra emissions from the delay in traffic due to non-recurrent 
congestion. Another major concept which can be built on this is ramp metering. In many major cities 
in the USA, optimising flows through ramp metering is a highly focused topic, especially in regard 
to the peak hours. The methodology described in this model is very convenient to include in different 
topics related to ramp metering, and can also be used for the effective closure of ramps through 
evaluating ramp closures during the incident period, which could optimise the flows on the freeway 
by evaluating flows, density and travel times. 
The possibility for further development of this model can be achieved by incorporating variable speed 
limits (VSL). This can prevent the shockwave downstream and reduce the possibility of second 
crashes. It is also possible to develop regression models for speed based on predictor variables such 
as flows, density, geometry, and travel times. This is very useful in the evaluation of new 
infrastructure and trying ascertain which of these variables play an important role in deciding the 
speed of the network. 
Overall, this concept will give us an idea on how an infrastructure reacts to the sudden incidents, 
where the bottlenecks can be found, and how we can create strategies to mitigate the congestion.
