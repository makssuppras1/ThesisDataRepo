Driving factors for creative facilitation in industry

The use of facilitators, especially for creative processes, has increased its popu-

Abstract
The focus on creativity and innovation is as high as it has ever been. One way com-
panies improve their creative processes is through the use of facilitators. However, 
research to support facilitator training and practice is missing.
The purpose of this study, was to investigate how facilitation is influencing creative 
processes in teams. Understanding this relationship will allow for better facilitation 
and thereby a higher creative performance. Using a Protocol Analysis, this study 
explores six workshops at Technolution and LEO Innovation Lab facilitated by an 
employee from Technolution. The used coding schema is developed specifically for 
this study and is based on a comprehensive literature review on the main factors of 
facilitation. This research design allows for an in-depth analysis of the contrasting 
cases of internal versus external facilitated workshops.
The results of this study contribute support and elaboration to the current research. 
Specifically in respect to responsibility, neutrality, and structuring of sharing pro-
cesses. What we found was a shifting role of the participants towards a co-facilita-
tor role, consequently moving the responsibilities of the facilitator and participants 
from a distinct division to more shared and loosely defined. Further, we found that 
the facilitators content involvement would go from neutral to involved. Lastly, we 
saw two distinct approaches to facilitating periods of sharing going from a more 
formal one-by-one presentation in the less cohesive group, to an informal collective 
discussion in the more cohesive group.
The study has significant implication for both research and practice. We contribute 
to existing research and thereby extend the current theoretical understanding of 
facilitation, enabling a higher quality of both facilitation training and practice. Con-
sequently, this will allow organizations to enhance their creative performance. 
Keywords: Facilitation, creativity, innovation, neutrality, inspiration, sharing pro-
cess, protocol analysis, case study,

Foreword
By Kasper Friis
Technolution - Partner and Head of UX & Design
As the number of filed patent applications per year continue to rise1, the opportu-
nity spaces for new product development are increasingly smaller and more shat-
tered. Therefore, creative thinking is at the heart of creating new products within 
both the MedTech and Pharmaceutical industries. Facilitating the creative work has 
the potential to drive the product creation to new heights, resulting in more innova-
tive solutions that meet deeper market needs and paves the way for sustainable 
businesses.
With the research presented here, light is shed upon the correlation between the 
role of the facilitator and the outcome of creative sessions. Most designers and 
facilitators are familiar with IDEO’s brainstorming rules: defer judgement, encourage 
wild ideas, build on the ideas of others, stay focused on the topic, one conversation 
at a time, be visual, and go for quantity2. The common denominator for these seven 
rules is that they are centered around the ideas themselves. The preliminary impli-
cations in this new research suggest a slight shift of focus. From focusing on the 
ideas, focus should be on the relationship between all participants in the creative 
session (including the facilitator), as this could drive even more ideas and spark 
even better solutions. Also, the facilitator should be focused on the fact that he 
or she is the driving source of inspiration and the one person that drives how par-
ticipants can and will build on the ideas of others – especially in workshop where 
participants do not know each other well beforehand.
Therefore, the results of this new research are relevant for designers and facilita-
tors across industries.

1.2  AIM OF THE THESIS
Most researchers agree that facilitation has a pos-
itive effect on team processes; however, how facil-
itation achieves this effect is less well understood 
(Geschka, 1986; Isaksen and Gaulin, 2005; Oxley, 
Dzindolet and Paulus, 1996). Together with Techno-
lution, we seek to investigate and understand the 
effect of facilitation in relation to creative process-
es. By understanding the mechanisms at play, we will 
be able to better utilize the effect of the facilitator 
and therefore the creative performance of the team.

2.2.1 Intrinsic motivation
Every person has intrinsically motivated characteris-
tics and behaviors that, by having a curious and mas-
tery-oriented mind, tends to make them explore and 
understand their surrounding world (Deci, Ryan, and 
Williams, 1996). When a team member finds them-
selves with high amounts of autonomy, competence

task or project, Grouzet et al. (2004 - p.340) sug-

an activity for its own sake are intrinsically motivat-
ed.” Intrinsic motivation can be further divided into 
subtypes of motivation. This project will encompass 
three generally accepted categories of intrinsic mo-
tivation: (i) Autonomy, (ii) Competence and (iii) Relat-
edness, largely based on SDT's definition.
(i) AUTONOMY
Giving the responsibility and choice of own fate to 
employees creates a feeling of ownership and em-
powerment (Gaya and Tanaka, 2011). This motivates 
employees or groups to perform (Schwarz, 2002). 
When it comes to motivation for being creative in 
an everyday work setting, freedom (autonomy) is a 
driver of the performance (Amabile, 2008). When 
employees can decide and choose their own path 
or way of working, the creative yield increase (Am-
abile et al., 1996; Liu et al., 2016). Herzberg (1968) 
tested several types of motivation related to auton-
omy in a study which suggests that motivators, e.i. 
given responsibility, will lead to higher performance. 
Through a comprehensive empirical study, Hoyle 
(2010 - p.185) found that the perception of autono-
my is crucial for the “maintenance and enhancement 
of intrinsic motivation, the internalization of extrin-
sic motivation, and the development of a healthy 
personality and system of values.” In the same study, 
it was found that the effects of autonomy are not 
moderated by social classes, genders, and cultures. 
Overall, no studies conclude a direct link between 
autonomy and creative facilitation; however, many 
suggest that autonomy is an intrinsic driver of mo-
tivation for being creative which is useful knowledge 
for a facilitator.
(ii) COMPETENCE
Being given an impossible challenge motivates no-
one to face it. Having the competence, or knowing 
how to obtain the competence or knowledge to 
solve a challenge can itself, be intrinsically moti-
vating (Schwarz, 2002). Perception of competence 
relates to self-determined motivation which in turn 
leads to concentration and intentions to persist 
(Grouzet et al., 2004). Facing and overcoming dif-
ficult challenges strengthens the self-efficacy of 
an employee and enables growth and incentives to 
learn further (Herzberg, 1968). Self-efficacy de-
scribes the perception of personal competences, 
both the “what about the world” and the “how to do 
in the world” (Hoyle, 2010). The perceived knowl-
edge and knowhow includes “the quality and range 
of the cognitive constructions and behavioral en-
actments of which the individual is capable” (Hoyle, 
2010 - p.266). Self-efficacy has been positively re-
lated to creativity in a study by Prabhu, Sutton, and

This is also seen in research on goal setting where a

might be subject to change over time (Hoyle, 2010).

This section aims to provide a brief overview of ex-

extrinsic motivation is directly affected by external

stick” (Amabile, 2008 - p.3). Based on the widely ac-
cepted definitions from Ryan and Deci (2000b), ex-
trinsic motivation has been split up to four catego-

(iii) Identification and (iv) Integrated regulation. 
(i) EXTERNAL REGULATION
Behaviors based purely on external factors such as 
a monetary reward (Amabile, 2008) or the threat of 
punishment is called external regulation. Such be-
haviors often feel controlled (Ryan and Deci, 2000b). 
According to a study, extrinsic reward did not have 
any effect related to employee performance where-
as intrinsic reward did (Jung Yoon and Nam Choi, 
2015).

(ii) INTROJECTED REGULATION
Introjected regulation is closely connected to (i)

regulation that is based on fear of guilt or anxiety or 
trying to obtain an ego-boost. Ryan and Deci (2000b 
- p.62) suggest that “introjection represents regu-

individuals pursue this ego enhancement in sports. 
Derived from this, any competition is motivational 
(Pensgaard and Roberts, 2002). Introjected regu-

recognition - where the recognition and appraisal of 
achievements are of importance (Herzberg, 1968).
(iii) IDENTIFICATION
Once the person has identified the importance of 
behavior and behaves accordingly, the motivation is 
said to be regulated through identification. This can 
be associated with a life goal or similar aspiration 
(Ryan and Deci, 2000b). E.g. “students who do their 
homework because they personally grasp its value 
for their chosen career are extrinsically motivated” 
(Ryan and Deci, 2000c - p.71).
(iv) INTEGRATED REGULATION
Once a behavior has been fully identified and inte-
grated through self-examination, a behavior can be 
said to be self-determined (Ryan and Deci, 2000b).

3.2 NEUTRALITY IN FACILITATION
Managing neutrality as a facilitator is a complex task 
influencing many different situations during the fa-
cilitation process. As the theory of facilitation and 
especially neutrality in facilitation is still relatively 
new as a research area (Wardale, 2013), we look 
to the related field of mediation for a definition. 
The complexity of neutrality is discussed by Cohen, 
Dattner and Luxenburg (1999 - p.342) stating that 
“Neutrality is not singular in nature...” but instead as 
a combination of several aspects such as (i) fair-
ness and respect, (ii) impartiality, and (iii) equidis-
tance (Luxenburg, 1999; Korsgaard, Schweiger, and 
Sapienza, 1995; Cohen et al., 1999). 
(i) FAIRNESS AND RESPECT
Furthermore, we see that when team members feel, 
that if their input is being considered, they will per-
ceive the process as fairer which will lead to greater 
commitment to the decision, greater attachment 
to the team and greater trust (Korsgaard, Schwei-
ger, and Sapienza, 1995). Along with fairness, the 
concepts of impartiality and equidistance are at the 
center of neutrality. 
(ii) IMPARTIALITY
Impartiality is maintaining an unbiased relationship 
with the team members (Cohen et al., 1999; Astor, 
2007) and in the context of creative facilitation, the 
content of the workshop. Furthermore, “impartiali-
ty implies a commitment to aid all the parties (..) in 
reaching a mutually satisfactory agreement.”(Cooks 
and Hala, 1994 - p.63-64) which indicates the need 
for partiality in order to achieve the mutual agree-
ment among participants. 
(iii) EQUIDISTANCE
Equidistance should be understood as the ability 
to assist each person equally. Equally in terms of 
having the same opportunity to present one's idea 
or opinion, not necessarily related to giving the par-
ticipants the same amount of time but instead sup-
porting equal disclosure of information (Cohen et al., 
1999). We adopt the definition “equidistance is the 
active process by which partiality is used to create 
symmetry” (Kolb, 1985) in our understanding of equi-
distance. 
The two latter concepts are somewhat contradict-
ing as, in many cases, one would need to be partial 
in order to be equidistant and to reach a collective 
agreement. Griffith, Fuller, and Northcraft (1998 - 
p.27) describe it as “The paradox of facilitation is 
that facilitators are supposed to influence without 
being influential.” The facilitator should seek only 
to influence the process and thereby constrain the 
influence on the content. However, influencing the 
process will be influential to the outcome of that 
process. One example is to summarize members in-
put in a group discussion, where the facilitator would 
emphasize someone's content input over others 
and as a result of this influence the scoping of the 
problem and thus the participants. Therefore, the 
facilitator should be aware of the intention of the 
actions and interventions during the facilitation. 
Some authors suggest that the facilitator is able to 
share insights on the content as long as they make 
it clear that they during that time does not act as 
a facilitator (Stewart, 2006). The facilitator should 
see neutrality as an aspiration (Astor, 2007), and 
use it to guide their behavior while facilitating. The 
shape and nature of neutrality are challenging to de-
fine. Schwarz (2002), suggest that the facilitator is 
substantively neutral, meaning that your actions but 
not your thoughts are neutral.
Conclusively, the element of neutrality is central for 
facilitation. Nevertheless, there remains a lack of 
agreement among scholars of the extent and defi-
nition of neutrality. In essence, the facilitator should 
aspire to be neutral towards people and content 
by being fair, respectful, impartial and equidistant 
when needed. However, the facilitator should not 
be neutral in regards to process. In fact, the facili-
tator should be seen as responsible for the process 
(Stewart, 2006). In order to properly control the pro-
cess, the facilitator needs a certain amount of au-
thority.

fectively lead a process, the facilitator should have 
enough legitimate authority to assign tasks, give 
responsibility and maintain high credibility (Stewart,

should act effectively, meaning minimizing the nega-
tive effect of outside factors (e.g., the culture of the 
organization, physical environment, etc.) influencing 
the facilitation as well as intervening when a process 
shows not to be working (Stewart, 2006). However, 
we notice that facilitating effectively does not nec-
essarily mean that the team will be effective. This is 
due to the teams own involvement in process deci-
sions (Schwarz, 2002). Geschka (1986) believes in 
contrary that the quality of the outcome from these 
processes is very much related to the quality of the 
process. Therefore, the responsibility of the facilita-
tor's effective behavior can only be assessed from 
the actions of the facilitator and not from the reac-
tion of the participants. 
When working as an external facilitator, there is 
no organizational power to support one's authority 
and power. One way to strengthen the authority of 
the facilitator is through respect (Schwarz, 2002). 
I.e., this could be done by expressing a title, prior 
achievements or special abilities.
To sum up, the facilitator should seek to give each 
team member equality of opportunity to participate 
but cannot be held responsible for the equal out-
come of every team member. This is seen as being 
responsible for utilizing the people in the process. 
The facilitator is responsible for managing the pro-
cess effectively but not for the quality of the de-
rived product. To do so, we see the need for a clearly 
defined authority towards the team. Furthermore, 
we see different perceptions of the facilitator's re-
sponsibility in the areas of people, product and pro-
cess.

3.3 FACILITATING 
EFFECTIVE TEAMS
Before exploring the details of facilitation process-
es, we would like to define the context in which the 
facilitator is working and what he/she is affecting. 
As with the facilitator's responsibility, we see the 
context of the work divided into the areas of peo-
ple, product and process; however, in reality, they are 
very intertwined.
As facilitators are always working in a team context, 
we look in the theory of teamwork processes and 
emergent states to identify what can/will be affect-
ed by the facilitator. We follow the Affective, Be-
havioral and Cognitive (ABC) heuristic for describing 
these areas (Grossman, Friedman, and Kalra, 2017), 
see Table 1 on page 17.
The facilitator will most often be facilitating a team 
of people working on the same project towards a 
certain output (Schwarz, 2002). However, we see 
different levels of teams that will affect the actions 
and potential of the facilitator. Low-level teams 
are characterized by a very task-oriented approach 
while a high-level team is characterized by higher 
attention to the teams' wellbeing and feelings (Mc-
Fadzean, 2002). As the level change from low to 
high, it seems that the style of facilitation moves 
from basic to developmental (Schwarz, 2002). The 
facilitator needs to be aware of the team level he/
she is facilitating, and thereby the approach taken 
to the team.
3.3.1 Effective and 
interpersonal processes
Influencing the affective state and effectiveness 
of the team requires interventions and actions from 
the facilitator or other team members. As both af-
fective and cognitive mechanisms are “hidden” with-
in feelings and thoughts of the individual team mem-
bers, we will focus on the behavioral mechanism and 
its influence on the team and their affect.
(i) EFFECTIVE PROCESSES
According to literature, the action phase is where 
the team progress towards their goals and do actual 
taskwork (Grossman et al., 2017; Marks, Mathieu, 
and Zaccaro, 2001). During this phase, high-perform-
ing teams are more likely to share their thoughts, ask 
for input, speak up, provide assistance and monitor 
their progress (Grossman et al., 2017). Furthermore, 
for facilitating an actual brainstorming session, 
Isaksen and Gaulin (2005) suggest an increased fo-
cus on utilizing the many benefits of an interactive 
group, such as a higher coordinated effort in prob-
lem-solving and clearer common understanding. All 
of the traits and benefits mentioned of a high-per-
forming team are closely related to the processes 
managed by the facilitator. However, for developing 
long-term effectiveness of a team, the interven-
tions of the facilitator should seek to decrease the 
group's dependence on the facilitator (Schwarz, 
2002). In other words, the “perfect team” would not 
need a facilitator to act effectively. However, this is 
rarely the case. 
In the following sections, we elaborate on the key 
action processes performed by the facilitator. 
Structuring the process
One way the facilitator is increasing the effective-
ness of the team is by bringing structure to the pro-
cess. In Bens (2012) definition of the facilitator role, 
contributing structure is one of the key elements. 
The facilitator can use a lot of different methods for 
structuring the workshop; i.e., ideation and evalua-
tion exercises, design games and simply managing 
time. However, the facilitator should make sure that 
the structure does not hinder creativity. 
Schwarz (2002) argues that procedural ground rules 
can be helpful, but adds that the link between clear-
ly stated rules and effective group behavior is not 
found. Additionally proposed by Kramer et al. (2001), 
is the benefits of a tight procedural structure that 
could lead to a decrease in an unwanted evaluation 
of ideas, more efficient communication and clear 
timekeeping. Another advantage of a clear procedur-
al structure is a feeling of fairness of the process 
resulting in a greater commitment from the team 
which is useful for effective facilitation (Korsgaard 
et al., 1995). However, not evaluating and assessing 
the team´s input at all may lead to rash and unre-
alistic decision-making, caused by group euphoria 
(Geschka, 1986).

Correspondingly not considering input has shown 
to significantly decrease the feeling of attachment, 
trust, and acceptance and thereby the commitment 
to the decisions taking (Korsgaard et al., 1995). 
Structuring the process as a facilitator is a delicate 
balancing act that requires the facilitator to be ob-
servant and agile with respect to the input, to se-
cure a commitment from the team while obtaining 
realistic and valuable decisions. Today it seems that 
most facilitation is based on experience, which is 
also valuable, however, research to support the fa-
cilitator could further qualify the structuring.
Involving participants
Involving the participants is a central task for the fa-
cilitator and a key element for an effective process. 
However, involving participants can be a complex 
task. 
The facilitator helps the participants share their 
thoughts and contributions to the team while as-
suring that there is an appropriate weighting of in-
put from each team member. Making sure everybody 
gets a saying is essential for the group to be suc-
cessful and to experience a positive synergy and 
greater commitment (Kramer et al., 2001, Korsgaard 
et al., 1995). One must note that with an aim to be an 
equidistant facilitator, it does not mean that all par-
ticipants have equal time but rather that all are able 
to express their thoughts and ideas equally; even if 
this might mean that one participant has more time 
than another. Or as stated by Storvang, Mortensen, 
and Clarke (2018 - p.166): "the focus of the facili-
tator is getting the participants' knowledge put into 
play." We see that the facilitator should seek to cre-
ate a shared understanding throughout the group by 
summarizing, without evaluating, the inputs from all 
participants. 
While involving the participants, the facilitator must 
secure the right focus of the participants on the 
task (Kramer et al., 2001). If the focus of the task 
is wrong, the team will not progress towards their 
goal, and thereby not be effective, even though the 
process facilitated might be working. Rasmussen 
(2011, p.399) states that “facilitators are supposed 
to guide the process but not be involved in the con-
tent.” However, it seems that the facilitator would 
need some knowledge about the content in order to 
guide the process effectively. So even though the 
facilitator is content-neutral, he/she should not be 
content ignorant.
(ii) MANAGING INTERPERSONAL PROCESSES
Managing the interpersonal processes is another of 
the main responsibilities and skills of the facilitator 
(McFadzean, 2002). The benefits of well managed 
interpersonal processes are many, including a higher 
feeling of involvement, commitment, team cohesion 
and satisfaction with the process, consequent-
ly leading to a general improvement of the team 
performance (Grossman et al., 2017; Isaksen and 
Gaulin, 2005). In the following, we elaborate on the 
key interpersonal processes affecting motivation.
Managing motivation
Motivating the participants has proved to improve 
team performance, including creativity, in the facil-
itation context (Liu et al., 2016). The motivational 
aspect of creative facilitation has been an element 
since Osborn (1957) introduced the brainstorming 
method. Offner, Kramer, and Winter (1996 - p.285) 
include in their description of the facilitator the mo-
tivational role and include statements as “Just ten 
more ideas” as an example of motivating behavior. 
One study mentions the motivation as the one thing 
enhancing the team performance overall (Kramer et 
al., 2001). They state that their questionnaire data 
did not indicate an improvement of performance 
based on equal member participation or general pro-
cess structure, but motivation alone. However, how 
the interventions of the facilitator are perceived by 
the participants might be different from the actu-
al effect they cause. Further, one study indicates 
that the decline in performance usually found in 
brainstorming groups, do not appear when using a 
trained facilitator (Oxley et al., 1996). However, if 
this is purely due to the correct use of motivation, 
is unclear.
Motivation is connected with evaluation apprehen-
sion: the fear of being judged (Park-Gates, 2001). 
With positive comments, the facilitator is able to 
empower specific team members and their ideas, 
while the comments simultaneously create a social 
acceptance of the given idea within the team (Kram-
er et al., 2001). Encouraging and motivating mem-

==PAGE:34==
bers to share and clearly state the importance of 
each participants contribution may reduce free-rid-
ing within the team (Oxley et al., 1996). However, in 
these situations, the facilitator will face a dilemma 
of neutrality and must be as neutral as possible 
while supporting a specific team member. Further-
more, we see the importance of the facilitator cre-
ating a climate where participants are able to share 
ideas freely (Oxley et al., 1996). 
Another useful source of motivation for the facili-
tator is transferring ownership, responsibility, and 
accountability to the participants (Clawson and Bo-
strom, 1996; Nelson and McFadzean, 1998) known 
as autonomy. If a manager (or for the focus of this 
study; the facilitator) wish to induce a lasting com-
mitment within the team and consequently a higher 
performance, promoting ownership and respon-
sibility might be one of the most powerful ways of 
accomplishing this (Schwarz, 2002). However, it is 
only possible to the extent that the team members 
share, and are aware, of the responsibility across the 
team (Schwarz, 2002). How and when the facilitator 
actually transfers ownership to the participants is 
not clearly explained in the literature.
In our project scoping, in Section 2.2 - Motivation 
(page 8), we divide motivation into intrinsic 
and extrinsic motivation. By using the definition 
and knowledge of the three types of intrinsic mo-
tivation, it is possible for the facilitator to control 
and manipulate the participants' engagement and 
creativity. However, researchers suggest that con-
tinuous reinforcement of an intrinsically motivating 
context is needed; otherwise the level of motivation 
declines over time. Creating a context that supports 
autonomy, competence and relatedness are found 
to sustain the internalization of the subtypes of in-
trinsic motivation. Ryan and Deci (2000c - p.76) be-
lieve that this is “of great significance for individuals 
who wish to motivate others in a way that engenders 
commitment, effort, and high-quality performance.” 
Providing performance feedback to participants is a 
way to increase intrinsic motivation, however, in an 
individual context, research suggests that it is ab-
sorbed dependent on perceptions of competence 
and autonomy (Grouzet et al., 2004). In addition, oth-
er research shows that collective efficacy influenc-
es collective motivation, planning, use of resources 
and goal persistence (Hoyle, 2010). This suggests 
that, if used correctly, the three types of intrinsic 
motivation overall induce engagement and higher 
creativity in team members. However, the current 
literature is limited with regards to how or when such 
interventions should happen.
The outcome of using extrinsic motivation is con-
sidered to vary depending on context. Gregersen 
(2017) argues that extrinsic motivation is utilized 
best when it comes to repetitive work. In a study of a 
facilitators competencies, it was found that reward-
ing and recognizing achievements was not particu-
larly important compared to the other interpersonal 
competencies tested in the study when it comes to 
facilitator performance (Stewart, 2006).
In an internalization process, extrinsic motivation 
can become intrinsic. However, the more an event 
is experienced as controlling, the less it provides 
internalized values (Selart et al., 2008). Based on 
this, we determine that a controlling behavior from 
the facilitator will decrease the intrinsic motivation 
and move the participants to a more external locus 
of causality.
Even though the benefits of a motivated team seem 
clear, the importance might not be evident for the 
facilitator. Stewart (2006) found that 23% of work-
shops participants (including facilitators) did not 
consider motivating others to achieve their goals 
as important. In fact, most of these answers came 
from the facilitators. She suggests that the reason 
might be that the facilitators expect the team to be 
self-motivated and therefore discard the responsi-
bility of motivating. 
The act of motivating others and the possible ef-
fects are more complex than expressing a positive 
statement about an idea. It requires a high degree 
of intellectual and emotional awareness of both the 
process and the team (Rasmussen, Sanne, and Ja-
cobsen, 2013). If managed correctly the facilitator 
will not only provide motivation but also increase the 
trust within the team and the team's interpersonal 
skills (Nelson and McFadzean, 1998).
In summary, we see that the facilitator's task re-
volves around two central areas. One area consist-

methods and task-oriented interventions. The oth-

3.4 FACILITATION 
DRIVEN CREATIVITY
The good facilitator is one that catalyzes the group's 
creative energy and is releasing all the potential 
knowledge of the team (Nelson and McFadzean, 
1998). Research shows that some factors hinder or 
limit one's ability to think creatively, while others en-
hance and increase the creative output of a creative 
process. The following sections describe such fac-
tors in respect to the consequence of having them 
present in a workshop setting under the influence of 
a facilitator.
3.4.1 Knowledge helps and hinders
Two perspectives have been found in regards to 
prior knowledge and creativity in literature. Argu-
ments have been made that it can either impede 
the creative process by creating anchors to existing 
solutions or positively increase the creative yield 
by domain-specific knowledge (Weisberg, 1999, 
Gonçalves, 2016). These studies extrapolate that 
knowledge can both hinder and help creativity and 
innovation as one's ability to understand the impli-
cations of implementation requires some amount 
of knowledge, while too much knowledge on a given 
area leads to less novel ideas. It merely depends on 
the balance and level of knowledge and abstraction 
from a given subject, see Figure 3 on page 23. 
While it is useful for the facilitator to understand 
the domain-specific knowledge level of each par-
ticipant, no suggestions of a process to control this 
have been found for this study. Deductively, only 
logical solution for the facilitator is to provide the 
context and needed knowledge to the participants 
themselves.
3.4.2 Fixation inhibits exploration
Summarized by Gonçalves (2016), a hinder of cre-
ativity can be mental fixation and mental set. Men-
tal fixation is where e.g., participants of a workshop 
cannot think of new and innovative implementations 
of familiar objects or constructs. The mind is sim-
ply fixed on that specific way of using that specif-
ic object. Mental set is much similar to fixation, but 
it involves how participants use of strategies and 
processes previously successful when facing a 
new problem. It often leads to a longer path around 
a problem that could have been solved with more 
straightforward steps. Some research suggests 
that positive affect helps to overcome these types 
of fixations, as it leads to greater cognitive flexibility 
(Ashby, Isen, and Turken, 1999). Another type of fix-
ation is the fixation of early-stage ideas (Gonçalves 
2016). Although focusing on one idea would limit 
the cognitive effort needed, it impedes the creative 
thinking process as it does not allow much thought 
for further exploration of the solution space; similar 
to mental fixation. This is experienced by many de-
signers and is seen as part of the facilitator's man-
agement of structure to control (Bens, 2012). 
3.4.3 Intrinsic motivation 
incentives creativity 
Amabile et al. (1996) suggest that more intrinsic 
motivation yields more creativity in businesses. 
While intrinsic motivation can be challenging to give, 
from a facilitator to participants, it would be natural 
to think of extrinsic motivation - a simple reward for 
a creative job. Extrinsic motivation can, however, be 
seen as controlling, and would in some cases lead 
to less intrinsic motivation and further less creative 
output (Gregersen, 2017). At least, the participant

hind extrinsic motivators like rewards. If they are 
given on a base of novelty, then research show that 
creative output might increase. Given based on 
performance, creativity might decrease (Selart et 
al., 2008). Research has further indicated that pro-
social motivation and intrinsic motivation together 
increases creativity (Grant and Berry, 2011). Both 
intrinsic and extrinsic can seemingly be used to in-
crease the creative output, however, using it proper-
ly, extrinsic motivation requires more careful consid-

enables inspiration
It is imperative that the team climate invites to 
share ideas in order for any creative process to oc-
cur beyond one's individual contribution; however, 
disagreements in literature are seen. Logically, a 
team that does not share does not gain anything be-
ing a team. This is also posed by Anderson and West 
(1998), where three out of four factors of innovation 
support specifically this. The four factors, of which 
the latter three concerns trust and support in team 
climate are:

critical, scrutinizing boss, or co-worker, can impair 
the creative environment. It would make the team 
less likely to share. This is also described as eval-

judgments (Offner et al., 1996). Vice versa, having 
a positive environment and mood would positively 
contribute to team creativity (Madjar, Oldham and 
Pratt, 2002). Kramer et al. (2001) partially agree 
that a team with a good team climate helps on the 
performance, herein creativity, however, mentions 
that most research prior to 1996 has been focused 
on recently formed groups of participants, rather 
than cohering teams who have been working togeth-

evolves over time (Andrew, Pirola-Merlo, and Mann, 
2004). Arguing against group creativity, verbally in-
teractive groups are less creative due to, e.g., eval-

blocking; however, the difference is not significant 
(Park-Gates, 2001; Russ and Dillon, 2011). Despite 
research arguing for individuals being more efficient, 
the same study indicates a preference towards 
group brainstorming and a perception that it is more 
effective (Park-Gates, 2001). The facilitator might 
hinder the creative performance by summarizing 
ideas (Offner et al. 1996). This is, in itself, produc-
tion blocking. This overall indicates a relationship 
between team climate and sharing ideas and cre-
ativity, however, the nature of this relationship is not 
fully comprehended; hence being the aim of the next 
section.

3.5 EXPERTISE AND TRAITS 
OF THE FACILITATOR
Motivation has shown to be an essential element 
of facilitation. However, one study claims that it is 
merely the expertise of the facilitator that affects 
the performance (Oxley et al., 1996). It is clear that 
the actual expertise of the facilitator influences the 
performance, but the description of expertise areas 
is often limited to recognizing ideas, keeping partici-
pants focused and reintroducing topics (Oxley et al., 
1996). Furthermore, how the skills are developed 
remains unclear (Harvey et al., 2002). In the follow-
ing sections, we wish to generate a holistic view of 
facilitator expertise and competencies and dive into 
core traits of the skilled facilitator.

are those of human relations and communication 
together with high emotional intelligence and aware-
ness and simply practical experience running the dif-
ferent methods (McFadzean, 2002; Stewart, 2006;

Today professional facilitator training programs are 
often 2-8 days of length after which one becomes 
a certified facilitator. This may create a foundation 
of facilitation methods and skills, however, without 
the experience, one gain while in the field, one will 
not develop the needed competencies of a skilled

linear way of developing facilitation skills, the pro-

Studies often differentiate facilitator skills and 
competencies. Studies of the effect of facilitation 
mention differentiation of highly trained/skilled/
performing, skilled/threshold and basic/student fa-

times two hours training and only facilitating a hand-
ful of real workshops (Oxley et al., 1996) while oth-

cipline of facilitation (Nelson and McFadzean, 1998). 
It is clear that there is a misalignment in the per-
ception and understanding of facilitator expertise 
and competencies. This statement is supported by 
Stewart (2006), emphasizing the missing research 
in the field of facilitator competencies. Further, she 
highlights the possible damages caused by incom-
petent facilitators which validates the importance 
of a facilitator competency framework. To create a 
general overview of facilitator expertise we combine 
key elements of existing research with departure in 
a general set of values.
(ii) FACILITATOR VALUES
Robson and Beary (1995) mention the need for fa-
cilitators to understand the true origin and purpose 
of their job. One way to understand the origin or core 
of facilitating is through the mutual learning model 
adopted and proposed by Schwarz (2002). He sug-
gests that 
»
» Valid information, 
»
» Free and informed choice, 
»
» Internal commitment and 
»
» Compassion 
should be seen as the four core values of facilita-
tion. Where many studies often suggest more spe-
cific tasks and traits, the mutual learning model in-
stead emphasizes the importance of implementing 
the core values from which one takes action. By 
using this approach to developing facilitation skills 
one does not learn what to do in a given situation, 
one learns how to change one's thinking pattern 
and react on any given situation based on the four 
core values. Consequently, there is not one correct 
way to facilitate and every facilitator will have their 
own approach to the job. However, changing thinking 
pattern will require the facilitator to be very aware, 
self-critical and self-reflecting. As facilitation is a 
cognitively and emotionally demanding job, even 
more so is the development of expertise (Schwarz, 
2002).
We see that adopting the core values of facilita-
tion creates highly skilled facilitators; however, the 
process can be long and requires much effort. Even 
though there is no one correct way of facilitating, 
we still see some generic traits of the facilitator. As 
the thoughts of the facilitator are not observable, 
we still need to look into the derived behaviors and 
traits of the skilled facilitator. Many of the traits will 
be linked to one of the four core values.

the skilled facilitator is near impossible. The litera-

and traits but merely that it seems that a mix of 
different skills and traits is beneficial (Harvey et

(i) PARTICIPANT OR FACILITATOR?
One thing we see is that team members can use fa-
cilitation principles and techniques to improve the 
process of a group (Schwarz, 2002). We see that a 
team member can use facilitative techniques with-
out being a facilitator. So what are the actual differ-
ences between being a facilitator and being facilita-
tive?
The derived effects of a facilitator being external to 
the team seem to explain some of the differences. 
This relationship fosters a higher degree of impartial-
ity, no bias towards the desired outcome or people 
nor any personal issues with team members (Griffith 
et al., 1998). It seems that the most notable differ-
ences between the team and non-team members 
are their neutrality towards people and product. This 
is supported by Schwarz (2002 - p.42) stating that 
“a facilitator needs to be a third party because it is 
difficult to act neutrally in your own group.” Howev-
er, one can facilitate while being a member or leader 
of a team as long as one is aware of their role and 
clearly communicate it to the team. If the facilitator 
fails to do so, the team might reject the support and 
nitive flexibility will enable more flexible thinking as 
well as creative problem solving (Isen, Daubman 
and Nowicki, 1987). Therefore positive affectivity 
should be sought, not only for the facilitator but also 
for the team. Managing affect in teams has been 
mentioned as a team process but not as a task for 
the facilitator (Marks et al., 2001). 
Due to the clear overlapping of the effectiveness 
of the facilitator and positive affectivity, we add to 
the tasks and skills of the facilitator affect manage-
ment: the aim of attaining positive affectivity within 
the group.
In summary
Investigating the development of expertise and 
skills of the facilitator, we found that it is near im-
possible to define one specific set of skills, traits 
nor training program. However, we generally see the 
development of the facilitator moving from a task 
orientation towards a more holistic and enabling role 
through experiential and experimental work. Further-
more, it seems that the expertise and skills needed 
are very depended on the purpose and context of 
the situation and team. This would also explain the 
current misalignment in definitions of facilitator ex-
pertise and skills.

3.7 KEY TAKEAWAYS AND 
THEORETICAL FOUNDATION
The literature study set out to investigate the cur-
rent state of knowledge within the use and role of 
facilitators in the context of creative workshops. 
The key takeaways are presented below and rep-
resent the theoretical foundation for the coming 
phases of the project.
We see a general agreement of the overall beneficial 
impact of using a facilitator in creative workshops. 
We identified central elements of the facilitator 
and the facilitation process. However, a deep un-
derstanding of the effective mechanisms relations 
seems to be missing. 
We found that the role of the facilitator is defined 
around elements of neutrality, action and interper-
sonal processes, improved effectiveness, creativi-
ty and team cohesion (Section 3.1 - The role of the 
facilitator). The facilitator should be neutral towards 
people and content and see neutrality as an aspira-
tion guiding their behavior. We see neutrality as a 
combination of fairness, respectfulness, impartiali-
ty and equidistant (Section 3.2 - Neutrality in facil-
itation). Furthermore, we found that despite the fa-
cilitator being content-neutral he/she should not be 
content ignorant. In addition, the facilitator should 
not be neutral in regards to the process. We see the 
facilitator as responsible for acting effectively with-
in the team, but not for the quality of the outcome 
(Section 3.2.1 - Power and responsibility). 
The facilitator mainly affects action processes, in-
cluding structuring and involvement of participants, 
and interpersonal process of which motivation is 
seen as a central element. We have found different 
drivers affecting the intrinsic and extrinsic motiva-
tion of the participants. It is clear that it is the in-
trinsic motivation that leads to the most substantial 
improvement in creativity and overall performance 
(Section 3.3 - Facilitating effective teams). We are 
still left with a missing understanding of the dynam-
ic relations between the structural and motivational 
interventions and their effect on creativity.
Looking specifically into improved creativity we 
found that the determining factors seem to be the 
provided inspiration (sharing within the team), state 
of the team climate, the intrinsic motivation and the 
prevention of fixation (Section 3.4 - Facilitation driv-
en creativity).
In order to utilize the identified mechanisms, we 
found that the experience and expertise of the fa-
cilitator are of great importance, and often present 
as tacit knowledge or intuition. We found that the 
four core values Valid information, Free and informed 
choice, Internal commitment and Compassion, 
should guide the facilitator's behavior and inter-
ventions. In addition, we saw a correlation between 
cognitive flexibility and creativity with positive af-
fectivity (Section 3.5 - Expertise and traits of the 
facilitator). Generally, we see the development of 
the facilitator expertise moving from a task orienta-
tion towards a more holistic and enabling role. Finally, 
we saw the importance of clearly defining the facili-
tator's position to the team and that different roles 
and contexts exist requiring different strategies for 
facilitating (3.6 - Position of the facilitator). 
We have now established a theoretical foundation 
for the central aspects of facilitation driven creativ-
ity. However, the dynamic relationship of the differ-
ent factors is not well supported. With the identified 
elements in mind, it would be relevant to investigate 
the relation of the facilitator and participants be-
havior over time, in a workshop setting. This would 
strengthen the existing understanding of facilitation 
and allow for a deeper understanding of the dynamic 
relationships.

4.2 PROTOCOL ANALYSIS
Protocol analysis is a method developed by Ericsson 
and Simon (1993), where verbal communication is 
thoroughly studied. It provides an increased tempo-
ral to the studies of behavior. Using this tool, we can 
represent a design process in a sequence of events 
for different behaviors by observing the participants 
and facilitator. The sequence can be categorized 
and analyzed in various ways, which yields insight 
into the dynamic relationship of behaviors. Looking 
at the behavior and order of different events over 
time allows us to obtain time-dependent results, 
central for studying team processes and avoid as-
sumptions of homogeneity across time (Leenders, 
Contractor and DeChurch, 2016). In recent years the 
use of protocol analysis for studying design activity 
has gained a wide popularity as it allows researchers 
to study the cognitive abilities of the participants 
(Jiang and Yen, 2009).
However, some researchers have expressed doubt 
about using verbal data, especially asking partici-
pants to introspectively “think aloud.” These reflec-
tions will bias the participant and make the data 
unusable. Other conditions and considerations also 
need to be met to reach valid data (Austin and Del-
aney, 1998). Often protocol studies use existing 
and tested coding schemas, as an example the FBS 
(Function, Behavior, Structure) framework. However, 
for the scope of this study, the existing protocols 
become either to general or to specific to draw in-
spiration from. Due to the gap in suitable protocol 
schemas in prior research, a new coding schema was 
developed specifically for the sake of this study. The 
new protocol is designed based on the theoretical 
foundation and will be used with video recordings 
which enables the inclusion of both verbal and phys-
ical actions to form a more holistic assessment of 
the behavior. The coding schema is presented in 
Section 4.4.2 (page 40).

cases with three workshops each. As the chosen 
method is somewhat resource-intensive, six work-
shops allow for in-depth coding and analysis while

Cohen´s Kappa
Cohen's kappa is a commonly used method for mea-
suring inter-rater reliability (McHugh, 2012). Prior 
to the introduction of Cohen´s kappa, researchers 
would use a percentage of agreement for measur-
ing their inter-rater reliability; however, this meth-
od does not take into account random guessing or 
chance agreement (McHugh, 2012). 
Cohen´s kappa range from -1 to +1, even though 
negative values is rarely obtained. A Kappa value of 
1 represents a perfect correlation between coders 
but is highly unlikely and difficult to obtain as some 
disagreement must be expected. An interpretation 
of Cohen's kappa from McHugh (2012) suggests an 
almost perfect agreement for Kappa values above 
0.81. Due to the somewhat limited dataset of this 
study, we interpreted a slightly higher kappa value 
of 0.85 as acceptable for our inter-rater reliability 
based on McHugh (2012) suggested values.

cus.

ferently, the participants will receive and embody 
information, ideas, and perspectives in various or-
ders and pace. Consequently, the participants will 
react differently to new ideas, in, e.g., idea-sharing, 
which could lead to variations in creative perfor-
mance. One exercise which the facilitator primarily 
uses in Technolution is the silent brainstorm, marked

5.6 KEY-TAKEAWAYS
In the comparison of the two contrasting cases, 
of an internally versus externally facilitated team, 
several elements were uncovered. As we found the 
sharing process to be essential for the creative pro-
cess and output of the workshop, this became the 
central perspective of our investigation. Here we 
saw two dominant patterns explaining the facilitator 
approach to facilitating periods of sharing. We ex-
plain these patterns through three sub-themes; The 
facilitator's relationship with content, Participant or 
co-facilitator, and Impact of split-team exercise.
5.6.1 The facilitator's 
relationship with content
A major difference between the patterns is the fa-
cilitator's involvement in the actual content of the 
workshop. In the pattern dominant for the internally 
facilitated team, we saw a greater continuously in-
volvement through POIs contributions to the actual 
creation of ideas at the same level as the partici-
pants. In addition, we see the facilitator evaluating, 
summarizing and elaborating on participants ideas. 
In contrast, we saw the dominant pattern for the 
externally facilitated team as more neutral towards 
content, focused more on providing alternative per-
spectives to the presented ideas. Furthermore, the 
“spitballing” occurring in the two cases, supports 
the finding of a more content involved facilitator 
during the internal facilitation. However, we saw the 
facilitators content relationship developing over 
time in the external group, towards that found in the 
internal group.
One thing also affecting the participants' approach 
to the sharing process, is the state of the project. 
Moving towards a more finished concept, the partic-
ipants seem less willing to intervene, i.e., share new 
thoughts based on presentations. Interestingly, this 
seems to happen sooner with the externally facili-
tated team. 
Summing up, the relationship or cohesion between 
facilitator and participants will affect the content in-
volvement and consequently the neutrality towards 
content and output. Furthermore, the cohesion of 
the facilitator and team seems to develop with time 
hence changing the content involvement.
5.6.2 Participants or co-facilitators
In addition to the contrasting and evolving content 
involvement from the facilitator, we also see a 
changed approach from the participants. In general, 
we see that the internal participants are taking on a 
co-facilitator role and seemingly more responsibility 
of the process compared to the external team. 
One effect of this is seen in the structure of the 
conversations during sharing. First of all, we see an 
increased spitballing between the facilitator and 
the participants in the internal group. Looking at 
the structure of the discussion between both par-
ticipants and the facilitator, it was clear that a more 
natural, fast-paced discussion occurred at the in-
ternal group. In the external workshops, the sharing 
took the form of a presentation remaining quite for-
mal. As a consequence, more additional ideas were 
created based on the given inspiration during the 
internally facilitated workshop than in the externally.
One reason for the difference in structure seems 
again to be the cohesion between the facilitator and 
the team. The more cohesive, the more involvement 
is seen from the participants during periods of shar-
ing. Through a correlation of the PA, we saw that the 
internal team, in general, had a more mirrored occur-
rence of PA compared to the external team, indicat-
ing a higher cohesion. However, looking at the use of 
IM we saw no significant difference or effect; even 
though motivation is found to play a significant role 
in creative processes. It is clear that the creativi-
ty-boosting motivation is something one brings into

During the final workshop at LEOilab, we saw that

5.6.3 Impact of split-team exercise 
Derived from our other themes, we found that the 
use of split-team exercises seems to have a dis-
tinct effect on the facilitation observed. However, 
the results are based on a few qualitative results 
and cannot be derived from the coding schema used 
for this study alone; thus this key takeaway is of a 
more speculative character. 
Using split-team, the facilitator would distance 
himself from the teams, decreasing the involve-

the cohesion, based on PA correlations, is lowered 
during these sessions. However, the codes show in-

that the cohesiveness between the two teams was 
lowered, as almost no comments or additions were 
made towards the other subteam (externally). This 
could indicate that using split-teams could, in fact,

6.1 STUDY FOCUS 
Our study utilized the protocol analysis for an in-
depth qualitative analysis explaining not only static 
data but also the dynamic relationship of the coded 
parameters. In addition, we are able to look at chang-
es over time. Generally, studies of facilitation rely on 
interviews (Stewart, 2006; Wardale, 2013), ques-
tionnaires (Offner et al., 1996; Oxley et al., 1996), 
observations (Stewart, 2006) or static data explain-
ing output parameter fx ideas created (Kramer et al., 
2001; Offner et al., 1996; Oxley et al., 1996; Isaksen 
and Gaulin, 2005). Furthermore, many studies utilize 
students and use artificial design tasks, which could 
limit the generalizability of these studies (Offner et 
al., 1996; Oxley et al., 1996; Kramer et al., 2001). Our 
explorative study relies on two real-life design tasks, 
two professional design teams and a facilitator us-
ing facilitation professionally, supporting the gener-
alizability and validity of our results.
(i) PERIODS OF SHARING IN FOCUS
The main focus of our analysis is the periods of shar-
ing during a creative workshop. This period is essen-
tial for utilizing the benefits of interactive groups by 
sharing created ideas and viewpoints (Dugosh et al., 
2000; Brown and Paulus, 2002; Isaksen and Gaulin, 
2005) and therefore relevant for research. Further, 
scholars acknowledge the impact and importance of 
providing inspiration during a creative process (Du-
gosh et al., 2000; D´souza and Dastmalchi, 2016; 
Gonçalves, Cardoso and Badke-Schaub, 2014) 
which, in our results, happens during periods of 
sharing. The facilitator shapes the sharing process 
and thereby shapes the provided inspiration. Conse-
quently, he affects the quality of the creative output 
which validates the choice of focus on periods of 
sharing. Through an improved understanding of the

difference in conversation structure between both 
facilitator and team and within the team, moderat-

conversation with the less cohesive group, meaning 
that fewer participants initiated conversation them-

feeling of fairness or unwanted evaluation of ideas

to literature, we add that the natural conversation 
will vary depending on the group's cohesiveness and 
thereby will affect the structure and consequently 
the output of the discussion.

observed distinction of patterns. Our study showed 
that when externally facilitated with low cohesion, 
the facilitator tends to distance himself leaving the 
participants to contribute content focusing on pro-

ers”. A similar division of designers, researchers, and 
users in design practice and research is presented 
by Sanders (2006) describing the co-creation land-
scape with the contrasting mindsets Expert and 
Participatory. In this study, the facilitator is perceiv-

(ii) THE ROLE AFFECTS SHARING
Adding to literature, we found that the participants' 
undertaken role affects the structure of sharing, 
which further impacts the outcome of facilitation. 
The literature argues that obtaining the benefits of 
an interacting group is central to the success of the 
facilitator. The facilitator has a central role in unlock-
ing the possible benefits as a coordinated effort 
in problem-solving, shared understanding and in-
creased commitment (Dugosh et al., 2000; Isaksen 
and Gaulin, 2005). Dugosh et al. (2000) found that 
exposure to others idea positively affects both the 
quantity and quality of the idea generation, support-
ing the importance of utilizing interactive groups. In 
addition, discussing ideas would also show a great-
er consideration of the idea which would lead to a 
greater commitment (Korsgaard, 1995). In our study, 
we saw that the sharing process seemed to be af-
fected by the participants' perceived role and stage 
of the project. 
On one hand, we saw a presentation like sharing with 
few interactions between participants, indicating 
lower attention or involvement in the shared ideas. 
However, this structure allows the facilitator to 
control the sharing, keeping participants on the top-
ic which has shown to be one of the major barriers 
to productivity (Dugosh et al., 2000). On the other 
hand, a discussion-like sharing process indicates a 
high degree of attention and involvement in the idea, 
which consequently leads to the creation of new 
ideas or solutions during the process. However, the 
facilitator has less control and needs to be highly 
aware of the process to avoid a decreased produc-
tivity. 
By elucidating the benefits and disadvantages in 
presentation-like and discussion-like sharing, this 
finding adds to the current understanding of the fa-
cilitator's approach to structuring sharing allowing 
for more control of both process and outcome. 
(iii) BENEFITS OF DISTRIBUTED 
CONSIDERATIONS OF CO-PARTICIPANTS
Linking the literature, we suggest that to improve the 
group's creativity, the facilitator should ensure that 
participants are considerate of the other team mem-
bers input. In line with Dugosh et al. (2000), studies 
show that it is possible to increase the individual's 
creative performance through a well-orchestrated 
sharing process and that the group interaction is 
an important factor for the groups creative quality 
(Taggar, 2002; Woodman, Sawyer and Griffin, 1993). 
Specifically, informative or constructive feedback or 
evaluation that shows consideration has shown to 
improve the creative process (Taggar, 2002). 
Further, Taggar (2002) emphasizes that these pro-
cesses are typically believed important for group 
leaders; however, one should consider the impor-
tance for group members as well. Our study showed 
that when participants moved towards a co-facilita-
tor role, they were, in fact, more recognizing and con-
siderable towards the shared ideas which according 
to literature should improve the creative output.
(iv) THE STAGE OF THE DESIGN

cohesiveness, we saw that the stage of the design 
process would affect the sharing process. The later 
and more defined stage of the process yielded few-
er additions and critical viewpoints from the team. 
This is no surprise, as a more defined stage and fin-
ished concept invites to less divergent thinking and 
alternatives. Being aware of these constraints and 
how they are perceived is central to the creative 
process and balancing the constraints is part of 
creating structure in the workshop, where too little

We suggest that the change happens based on both 
relationship of facilitator and team, as seen in the 
externally facilitated workshops, and the change in 
uncertainty and restriction moving towards a more 
defined stage of the design process. As the facili-

Summing up, our results show two approaches to 
the sharing process depending on the group's cohe-

of the project. This explains how the facilitation of 
sharing will vary depending on the state of the giv-
en team. Awareness of the different approaches in 
regards to structure, team support and use of inspi-

effects for ideation derived from facilitating the pro-
cess of sharing in interactive groups. This way, our 
results suggest a way to better catalyze the cre-
ative energy and knowledge from the team (Nelson 
and McFadzean, 1998).
6.4 IMPACT OF SPLIT-
TEAM EXERCISE
In facilitation, different exercises yield different re-
sults. Sometimes, the goal is a particular content 
related outcome, other times it is purely with the 
intention of improving the interpersonal relationship 
between the team itself or between team and fa-
cilitator (Geschka 1986, Christensen, Ball, and Hal-
skov, 2017). In split-team exercises observed in the 
workshops, we found the facilitator to disengage 
and lower his cohesion to the group. While this might 
be intentional, it is crucial to understand the derived 
consequence.
We observed that in the detachment from the 
group, the facilitator takes on a different role, based 
almost purely on process control. We see this to af-
fect the team's perception of his responsibility and 
authority. If executed wrongly, we expect to see a 
gap between the facilitators “orders” and the partic-
ipant's actions. On the other hand, if executed right, 
we expect to see more sharing in the subteams as 
sharing in large groups can be frightening for some 
and studies shows a negative correlation between 
individual contribution and team-size (Gorla and Lam, 
2004). Further, the facilitator must decide if the 
benefits of interactive groups are lost while split-
ting the team. Conclusively, a split-team exercise 
impacts the team trust and cohesion, both within 
team and group, however, it must be carefully con-
sidered with the desired effects in mind. 
The split-team exercise does not only detach the 
facilitator from the group but also the content. The 
discussion of content neutrality, Section 6.2 (page 
80), is equally valid here. Based on our observa-
tions, the ramification of this is a neutral facilitator, 
not only in the exercise itself but in the following ex-
ercises as well. We observed that in the subsequent 
presentation, the facilitator does not contribute

6.5 MOTIVATION AND 
POSITIVE AFFECT
(i) MOTIVATION
Examining the literature, we found motivation to be 
a central component of creativity ( Amabile, 2008). 
However, coding for motivation turned out to be 
difficult due to the many possible natures and vari-
ations of the concept. Especially in the context of 
facilitation, it was difficult to pinpoint the actions af-
fecting the participants' intrinsic motivation related 
to autonomy, competences, and relatedness. These 
elements of motivation are difficult to measure over 
the short time frame of our workshops and due to 
the motivational effect of the task, organizational 
culture, etc., itself. However, what we did see was 
small appraisals or encouragements used to struc-
ture the process but also indicate the value of the 
participant's contributions. Examples could be ver-
bally expression the value of the idea (“Good idea,” 
“I think that is a very interesting idea”) or asking 
participants to write down specific ideas after shar-
ing them, indicating that the idea is valuable to the 
group. As discussed in section 6.1.1, these apprais-
als will affect the content neutrality of the facilitator 
as the nature of the appraisal is evaluating. However, 
we saw no indication of participants feeling that the 
appraisal was unfair. In some cases, the appraisal 
showed to boost the more quiet participants to con-
tribute more. Despite the current literature view on 
complete neutrality towards content, we see that 
sacrificing the neutrality of the facilitator through 
appraisals and positive evaluations could be a pow-
erful tool for the facilitator.
(ii) POSITIVE AFFECT AS COHESION INDICATOR
Further, we found the atmosphere of the group to be 
relevant for the performance. However, coding for 
atmosphere was a challenge due to the subjectivity 
of the concept versus the need for a defined frame 
for the coding. However, using the coded PA, we did 
see a higher correlation between the facilitator and 
participants in the more cohesive team indicating 
a difference in atmosphere. Diving into the obser-
vation, we saw that the use of personal stories for 
explaining and arguing was dominantly found in the 
internal and cohesive team. Using personal stories 
is seen as a sign of a trustful atmosphere increasing 
the relatedness of the team which has shown to in-
crease motivation and consequently creativity (Deci 
and Ryan,  2008). Our study suggests that fostering 
a trustful atmosphere as a facilitator is important 
and could be done by promoting the sharing of per-
sonal experiences.

7.1 IMPLICATIONS 
FOR RESEARCH
The field of facilitation is complex and dependent 
on the given context. Hence, research is needed to 
support and improve the growing use of facilitation 
in the context of creativity. Using the protocol anal-
ysis on a real-life case, we contribute to a deeper 
understanding of the use of facilitation for fostering 
creativity, and as a result of this, extend the current 
theory on facilitation. Specifically, our study explains 
the impact of using an external or internal facilita-
tor and the derived influence of group cohesion, es-
pecially during the fundamental task of facilitating 
sharing.
7.1.1 Role evolvement over time
Our research extends the theoretical understanding 
of facilitation by suggesting a new, multi-dimension-
al definition describing the facilitator's actions and 
behaviors with respect to group cohesiveness. In 
particular, our study adds to, and elaborate, on the 
current understanding of facilitators responsibili-
ties, neutrality, and process structuring. 
Adding to the current literature is a new under-
standing of the facilitator's responsibility for both 
process and outcome. In contrast to the static and 
well-defined responsibility suggested in current lit-
erature (Schwarz, 2002; Stewart, 2006), we see 
the responsibility as multi-dimensional, shifting to-
wards a shared and dynamic responsibility between 
the facilitator and participants for both process and 
outcome, moderated by the cohesion of the group. 
These shared responsibilities entail that the facili-
tator can no longer be content neutral. In addition, 
we see this as an evolvement of the facilitator and 
participants roles, converging towards a common 
co-facilitator role.
Our study suggests a new approach and dimen-
sion to the existing view on facilitators neutrality 
(Schwarz, 2002; Rasmussen, 2011; Kramer et al., 
2001). We found that the facilitator moderates 
their neutrality towards content, with respect to the 
group's cohesion. This proposes that the facilitator, 
in some cases, could engage in discussions with 
content, while still effectively controlling and sup-
porting the participants.
Lastly, we see that the facilitators structuring of 
sharing process differs between a fast-paced, in-
formal discussion and a less interactive formal 
presentation. We suggest that this structuring is 
moderated by the group's cohesiveness. Our study 
adds to the current understanding of the facilitator 
driven sharing process, found to be essential for 
the creative process. Further, this could also add to 
the understanding of the more general approach to 
the facilitator and team interactions; however, this 
would require a more specific study to validate.

As our study is explorative, we are unable to quantify 
the ramifications of our findings into practice with-
in the timeframe of this project. We can, however, 
make suggestions and share the preliminary impli-
cations we see, which will help practitioners qualify 
their behavioral decisions during facilitation. How-

7.2.1 Content non-neutrality
Our proposed results change the current view on 
the facilitator's neutrality, which will imply changes

8.1.1 The exploratory 
phase of research
This study is inductive and exploratory within applied 
disciplines. In behavior science, theory-building, be-
ing both inductive and deductive, is a challenge as 
the number of contingencies and circumstances 
are endless. Further, as the research and validation 
evolve, all theories are bound to be “rejected, ex-
plained, adopted, or improved.” (Swanson and Cher-
mack, 2013).
Throughout the report - literature review, research 
design, results, discussion and implications - we 
have been discussing, connecting and summarizing 
known concepts and elements from theory and logic 
in an effort to find explanations and build theories 
that enabled us to explain the identified gaps of the 
initial theoretical foundation. This does not come 
without a challenge and thereof limitations of the 
study. Although we went through 6 workshops, in 
total 28240 seconds of video data encoded with 
identified behavior, our study has a limitation in the 
fact that we are building theory based on qualitative 
observations. 
There are several limitations to this study. The first section describes the global limitations that influence the 
contributions of this study allowing us to summarize areas for further work. The three main limitations are struc-
tured in the sections The exploratory phase of research, Research design and The use of protocol analysis.
The second section describes the further work and investigation. Throughout the process of this study, we

8.1.2 Research design: 
Contrasting cases 
Qualitative studies of cases are widely used in 
theory building research (Eisenhardt and Graebner, 
2007); however, it has its limitations as well. Build-
ing a theory based on a relatively small amount of 
data, with insufficient amounts of verification and 
recurrence of patterns has its apparent limitations. 
Adhering to Reynolds (1971) approach to concep-
tual development and theory building, we have mit-
igated the limitations by following a well tested “re-
search-to-theory” strategy. This is carried out by the 
overall structure of this report and the underlying 
process of the study, including the initial literature 
review, theoretical foundation, experiment design, 
data capturing, data analysis and results, and lastly 
the discussion to current literature and theory. 
In summary, we have been exploring trends based 
on patterns, identified first purely by pattern, later 
verified with transcriptions and video review. Based 
on this, we have come up with themes and proposed 
theories. So the real challenge is to build viable theo-
ries on limited qualitative data. However, single case 
studies can be valuable for scientific development 
(Flyvbjerg, 2006) as well as uncovering areas for fu-

Chapter 9
Conclusion
==PAGE:112==
This study investigates the use of a facilitator in creative processes. This contrib-
utes to understanding the varying role and interactions of the facilitator and the 
effect on the team. As a guide for the investigation, the study answers the research 
question:
In order to answer this question, an explorative study using the protocol analysis 
is used. Prior to the protocol analysis, a comprehensive literature review was car-
ried out to provide the theoretical foundation for the study and the self-developed 
coding schema used for the protocol analysis. Two contrasting cases, selected as 
the basis for data collection, elucidate the results of the study. Specifically, three 
internally and three externally facilitated workshops is used for the data collections 
at Technolution and LEO Innovation Lab respectively. 
This study investigates several central factors of facilitation, especially in regards 
to responsibility, neutrality, and structuring of sharing processes. Our study links 
these core aspects of facilitation to the cohesiveness of the facilitator and team. 
In contrast to existing literature, our study suggests an evolution of the responsi-
bility from, very distinct towards a shared responsibility for both process and con-
tent. Further, we suggest a new view on the facilitator's neutrality, arguing that one 
should not necessarily strive for complete neutrality. Lastly, we find that the sharing 
process is moderated by the group's cohesiveness influencing the structure and in-
terventions of the facilitator, going from formal to informal. Overall we see this as a 
development of both participants and facilitator towards a co-facilitator role. 
Despite a number of limitations, the contributions of this study has significant im-
plication for both research and practice. By supporting and extending existing re-
search, we contribute to the theoretical understanding of facilitation, enabling a 
higher quality of facilitation training and practice.

A  Initial project plan
This is the initial project plan for the master thesis project: “Driving factors for creative facilitation in the industry” 
during fall 2018 at the Technological University of Denmark. The content of this document presents the initial 
considerations on the project structure, milestones, and phases. A short description of the project management 
process is included. The project is divided into three main phases. The scope and expected output of each of 
the phases is presented below. In the Gantt diagram, you see the overlapping tasks and phases, allowing time 
for sudden changes in the process (Wallace, 1922). Duration and milestone deadlines can be found in the Gantt 
chart. As we are using a weekly SCRUM/Sprint approach, dedicated hours for each task is not documented in the 
Gantt chart as you otherwise would.
Phase 1 includes the overall project planning. As part of the project planning a SCRUM inspired project manage-
ment system has been set up describing tasks and goals for the coming week (Pope-Ruark, 2012). Using this 
approach, we are able to break down assignments to more manageable tasks that can be divided between us and 
keep the project on track by securing that the right goals for each week are met. These plans will be updated and 
revised as the project evolves. The main activity of phase 1 will be the literature review resulting in the concep-
tual framework and knowledge base of the project. 
Phase 2 is the main and most comprehensive phase of the project, containing observations and the protocol 
analysis of the workshops. Leading to the protocol analysis is deciding on a focus together with Technolution. 
The focus will be based on the findings of the literature review, interest and opportunities. Our final scoping and 
research question will be defined during this process. The outcome of phase 2 is a set of findings related to the 
conceptual framework that we will utilize in phase 3. We allow for a large overlap between the observation/filming 
and the actual coding/analysis as we expect challenges in the coordination of the observations due to the many 
actors involved. If we fail to observe the necessary workshops with Technolution, we have several backup plans 
to secure a valuable outcome of the project. 
Phase 3 will focus on the development of recommendations based on the findings from the analysis. If possible, 
the recommendation (depending on the format and time constraints hereof) will be tested and validated. By the 
end of phase 3, the recommendations will be presented to Technolution. January is focused on finalizing the re-
port and will work as a buffer for unforeseen events.

G  Rules for coding schema
#
Rules - General
G01
When speech is undefinable, both in turn of what that is being said and and who is saying it, then it is not coded
G02
If the same behavior is observed in two sections with less than 5 seconds of pause in between, then the sections is coded together as one, unless 
another rule specifically says otherwise
G03
“Eeehhmm” is considered blank space and therefore not coded for. Even if used in between two sentences - then the rule of the 5 seconds will 
determine whether the section is abrupt or not
G04
Singular and confirming “ja”, “yes” or “no” is not coded. Similarly, if participants or facilitator says “I don’t know”, “maybe” or “yes, exactly” it is not 
coded when alone
G05
The first part of the workshops is often people settling in, before the “officiel” program is started. However this part often contains situations that 
will affect the atmosphere. We here code for positive/Negative Affect, but not the remaining.
#
Rules - Facilitator and team coding
FT01
If one or more participant from the team does a behaviour of interest, then the whole team will be coded as such unless other team members have 
a direct opposite behaviour. This might result in double coding, where both intrinsically motivation is occuring while extrinsically motivation
FT02
The facilitator is always coded as an individual. Cannot be combined with team. In the case where both team and facilitator are e.g. laughing, both 
will be coded as such
FT03
If the behaviour of the facilitator is timewise indistinguishable from the behaviour of the team and vice versa, then both groups will be marked as 
state: “on”
G04
Singular and confirming “ja”, “yes” or “no” is not coded. Similarly, if participants or facilitator says “I don’t know”, “maybe” or “yes, exactly” it is not 
coded when alone
G05
The first part of the workshops is often people settling in, before the “officiel” program is started. However this part often contains situations that 
will affect the atmosphere. We here code for positive/Negative Affect, but not the remaining.
#
Rules - Creativity states coding
CS01
Create State: When participants hands or other view-obstructing element are covering up the sketch and it is unclear whether sketching is occur-
ring, it is only counted as Create State if the participant looks directly at the post-it
CS02
Rule: How to count prototyping? As long as the prototype is manipulated or holded it is considered to be a Create State
CS03
One Inspiration State section includes one person's idea presentations. The start is counted as when the person starts talking on the actual ideas; 
ending when person ends or if 5 second rule apply. The “meta” of the ideas is not inspirational state
CS04
Suggesting to combine with other ideas, or use ideas in other ways are considered inspirational as it requires the participant or team to think of the 
same idea in a new way
CS05
“What about x material/product/etc.” is a statement of inspiration
CS06
Speaking about a mentioned idea, to hereafter ask a critical/provocative/deepening question is all considered to be and coded as inspirational state. 
I.e: “For what do you intend to use this?” “How would you do that?”
CS07
Rhetorical questions are not considered to be of inspirational character. Questions like: “Do you agree?” “Did I understand it correctly?” “Does that 
cover everything?”
CS08
While giving the initial problem/project/context of a workshop, this could be inspirational in its nature, however, it is not counted as inspirational 
state. Reason for this is that it is about setting the frame and it is necessary information for the participants.
#
Rules - Points of creativity coding
CP01
Counting Point of Creationss: Creation of an idea is the Point of Creations however, when counting the ideas for the study, we identify the point 
where post-its taken of the pile and put to the side as a “completed idea” to be the creative point . This allows for easy counting, while the same 
amount of ideas is counted, just with a delay of the actual drawing
CP02
Objects or suggestions to look at objects are considered points of inspiration when done so in context of inspiration - not if it is in setting the frame. 
The inspirational point is set to be the specific word or in the specific process of handing over documents of inspiration
CP03
When an idea is left on the post-it until presentation or use, the Point of Creations will be coded as the last add on to the idea.
CP04
Repetitions within the same presentation/sharing section from one participant are not considered new inspirational points - same goes for other 
phrases that describe the same feature unless significantly different. An exception to this rule is if another participant share the same idea, feature, 
function etc - this is counted as a new point of inspiration
CP05
"The inspiration point it set to be the beginning of a new idea during the inspirational state. It might also be set as the beginning of a critical/inspi-
rational question.

#
Rules - Motivation rules
M01
Statements that relate problem to participants are considered to be intrinsically motivating. “Imagine if your grandma was in the center of this 
problem” is considered Intrinsic Motivation.
M02
Statements that gives responsibility and ownership of tasks are considered to be intrinsically motivating under the concept of autonomy
M03
“Cool”, “Great” and “Nice” is considered intrinsically motivating when said towards a contribution, idea or suggestion of any kinds when talking about 
a contribution, as it talk to the self-efficacy. “I think Franks ziplocks idea was really great - or just that way of fastening something...”
M04
Statements of promises of rewards such as monetary prized or appraisals are considered to be extrinsically motivating
M05
Statements that includes threat of punishment such as “if you don’t do x, you’ll be kicked out” or negative comments are considered to be extrin-
sically motivating
M06
Speaking about ideas or comments towards the whole group is considered to be below the threshold of Intrinsic Motivation. If he mentions specific 
competencies, skills or contributions that apply for the whole group or specific persons - then it is.
M07
Intrinsic Motivation is mainly focused at individuals, meaning that praisal towards one person has a lower threshold of “on” while the same sentence 
towards the whole group would be “off” due to higher threshold.
M08
Using “me” and relating problems to “me” as an example is considered relatedness, however, not above threshold, therefore not coded as Intrinsic 
Motivation
M09
“That’s awesome” is moreover considered to be acknowledging and increase the self-efficacy, hereby making the statement intrinsically motivating
M10
Motivating related to increased team cohesion fx setting goals, statements about group identity etc. is considered Intrinsic Motivation.
#
Rules - Content coding
CO01
More than one active state can be seen at the same time. E.g if multiple persons speak on various subjects
CO02
Facilitating the team members capabilities can be seen as both process and people content. The right code is chosen based on the purpose of 
the content, e.g: “Morten, you are such a good drawer. Would you like to draw your idea for us?”First part is people while the second part is process
CO03
Process is described as when the facilitator presents (orders) a method, activity etc. that is common for all or for specific persons
CO04
“Great”, “Thanks” or “Thank you for your contribution” is considered to be above the threshold of process content if it indicates a shift in focus or 
“next participant”
CO05
Comments, questions and answers on assumptions on problem and solutions space etc are product content “Can we assume that the user is 65 
years old?” = product
CO06
Comments, questions and answers on assumptions on process are process content “Can we use more time?” or “Can i use the red pen?” = process 
“Where should I place this one?” or “I don’t know if this is a new one, should they be categorized? = Process (and longer answers than yes is also 
process)
CO07
“Cool”, “fine”, “yes”, “good”, etc. and answers of questions related to process are to be coded if they indicate a change of method or controls the 
participants. Often seen with another question as “cool, who’s next?” - however, even one word is enough to be coded as process.
CO08
Translations and suggestions to alternative phrases are not considered product content unless the suggestion significantly differs from the original 
statement
CO09
"When summarizing and subsequently asking a critical question - the two sections are respectively product content and Inspiration State.
CO09
When summarizing and subsequently asking a critical question - the two sections are respectively product content and Inspiration State. “How 
might we assure a high enough information level - for what?
CO10
Process will interrupt almost anything hereby overruling the 5s rule
#
Rules - Affect coding
A01
Telling jokes, both on and off the subject, and mixing in humor is considered Positive Affect, including gestures before or after the verbal part of the 
joke
A02
If one or more people from the team are laughing, or energetic, then it’s coded as Positive Affect
A03
A04
Nodding is not considered Positive Affect, unless multiple team members consensually nodding intensely at the same time
A05
Curiousity is considered Positive Affect. I.e. “I wonder why no t-shirt have been experimented on before“ or “I think it was surprising that there was 
so many ideas to this problem” while smiling and in an amazed sense is therefore considered Positive Affect
A06
When the team is clearly and enthusiastically agreeing, it is considered Positive Affect. This includes when the team says “Yes” more or less at the 
same time.
A07
A person is considered energetic when one is bodily active with fast movements, speaking fast or speaking significantly louder. This is often linked 
and perceived in correlation with enthusiasm, see A08
A08
A person is considered enthusiastic when one is smiling, clapping, laughing, cheering, excited, or speaking in significantly higher pitch voices
