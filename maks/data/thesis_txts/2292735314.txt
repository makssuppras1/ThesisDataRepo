Abstract 
A genome-scale metabolic reconstruction of the thermophilic bacterium R. marinus, DSM 
4252T is presented in this thesis. The reconstruction contains 422 genes, 491 reactions and 432 
metabolites. A biomass objective function, describing all the components and their corresponding 
molar contributions of the overall biomass, was formulated. A special focus in this study was on 
carotenoid production and various saccharide utilizing pathways. A carotenoid biosynthetic pathway 
for R. marinus was proposed and a search for potential genes performed. In order to model all the 
different saccharide utilizing pathways, a thorough literature- and gene search was carried out. The 
reconstruction process is described in detail, from obtaining a draft reconstruction from the Model 
SEED, to extensive network curation and model validation. In silico growth predictions on different 
carbon sources were performed and compared to in vivo experiments. Gene essentiality predictions 
were performed with single gene deletion analysis and the distribution of essential genes between 
subsystems examined. Finally, predictions involving carotenoids were performed in attempt to 
increase the yield. These predictions involved investigating flux distributions of reactions showing 
high correlation to lycopene flux and adding an alternative terpenoid pathway and investigating the 
resulting lycopene flux. An idea for an Icelandic neologism was formed describing a genome-scale 
metabolic model, ‘tölvera’, which in short is derived from the Icelandic words for computer and being 
(as in ‘human being’).

Chapter 1. Introduction 
Biotechnology as a field uses organisms to produce desirable biological products and metabolic 
engineering is a tool that can be used to optimize this production, by genetically modifying the cellular 
functions. Over the past decades, great technological improvements in experimental high-throughput 
measurements have been made, resulting in large amount of data. Computational tools have been 
developed to analyze large datasets, design strains and predict phenotypes for metabolic engineering 
[1].  
The aim of this thesis was to reconstruct a genome-scale metabolic network for the type strain 
of the thermophilic bacterium Rhodothermus marinus, DSM 4252T. During the reconstruction process 
knowledge about the metabolism of the target organism was collected and the resulting model gives 
an overview of what is known, and not known, about the organism’s metabolism. The model was 
meant to improve the understanding of the bacterium’s metabolic network as well as to optimize and 
guide genetic and metabolic engineering efforts. A special focus was on carotenoid biosynthesis and 
saccharide utilizing pathways and corresponding enzymes as they can have beneficial biotechnological 
applications.  
1.1 Rhodothermus marinus 
1.1.1 Physiology and phylogeny 
In 1988, a team of researchers in Iceland discovered a bacterium of a new genus. This bacterium 
is thermophilic and slightly halophilic, and was isolated from submarine alkaline freshwater hot 
springs in Isafjardardjup, NW Iceland. It was named Rhodothermus marinus and the type strain is 
identified as R-10 or DSM 4252T [2]. According to the current taxonomy, R. marinus is placed within 
the phylum Bacteroidetes and the family Crenotrichaceae, based on sequence analysis of the 16S 
rRNA gene [3], [4]. R. marinus is closely related to the extremely halophilic bacterium Salinibacter 
ruber [5]. The position of Rhodothermus in a phylogenetic tree taken from [6] is shown in Fig. 1. R. 
marinus is a gram-negative, red-pigmented rod with optimum growth conditions at 65°C, pH 7 and 
about 1 - 2% NaCl. It is a heterotroph, obligatory aerobic and moderately halophilic. Since it was first 
discovered, R. marinus has been found in similar geothermal environments, both in different locations 
in Iceland and around the world [6]. It was found at Praia de Ribeira Quente [7] and Ferraria [8], on 
==PAGE:12==
2 
 
São Miguel in the Azores, Portugal, in Stufe di Nerone, Italy [9], in Monserrat in the Caribbean Sea 
[8] as well as in Oxarfjordur, NE Iceland and in two additional locations in SW Iceland [10].

1.1.2 Carotenoids 
Most strains of R. marinus, including the type strain, are red-colored. The color comes from 
carotenoids, produced by the organism [2]. Carotenoids are polyisoprenoids produced by many 
different species of fungi, bacteria, algae and plants, for instance in response to environmental stresses 
==PAGE:13==
3 
 
of various kinds and to help with photosynthesis [11]. In the case of R. marinus the carotenoids are 
most likely produced to stabilize the cell membrane in response to high temperature and protect the 
cell from sunlight [12]. Animals do not produce carotenoids, but consume them through food, where 
they have multiple functional roles. They can act as a precursors for vitamin A, visual pigment and 
even contribute to prevent human diseases such as cancer. Carotenoids have many different industrial 
applications, for instance in food, medical, cosmetic and various other biotechnological fields [11].  
1.1.3 Biotechnology and genetic tools 
As a thermophile, R. marinus possesses enzymes that are stable and active at high temperatures. 
The currently characterized polysaccharide degrading enzymes, which are often secreted, are 
particularly thermostable, some with optimum activity at 80 - 100°C [6]. This enzyme quality is often 
desired in the biotechnological industry. In addition to the enzymatic thermostability, R. marinus 
possesses many different enzymes that can degrade a variety of carbon sources. Many industrially 
important metabolites are also produced in R. marinus, including terpenoids, carotenoids, and 
polyamines. Availability of such variety of enzymes and metabolites makes the bacterium of potential 
interest in biotechnology. Being able to genetically- and metabolically engineer such an organism can 
prove beneficial, which is why genetic tools have been developed for R. marinus. Gene transfer and 
genetic selection can be performed, where vectors based on the plasmid pRM21 (2935 bps) from R. 
marinus were used to optimize gene transfer and achieve efficient transformation. Several methods for 
gene alterations have been described [13]. 
1.2 Genome-scale metabolic models 
1.2.1 Systems biology and metabolic reconstructions 
Systems biology seeks to explain complex interactions within organisms by looking at a system 
as a whole instead of its individual parts. Systems biology combines experimental and computational 
biology. With increased number of available genome sequences along with improved computational 
tools, genome scale metabolic models are a growing field within systems biology. A metabolic 
network reconstruction describes the biochemical reactions involved in the metabolism of a given 
organism, in a structured way. Besides metabolic reactions, transport- and exchange reactions also 
need to be included in a reconstruction. Transport reactions move metabolites between cellular 
compartments. In bacteria this is relatively simple since they do not have as many cellular 
compartments as eukaryotes. Transport reactions in bacterial models mainly include transport between 
the environment and cytosol. Exchange reactions allow metabolites to flow in and out of the model 
==PAGE:14==
4 
 
environment. These reactions represent which metabolites are present in the organism’s environment. 
A biomass function is usually included in metabolic reconstructions. The function includes all the 
components making up the biomass along with their fractional composition. Converting the 
reconstruction to a computational model gives the opportunity to compute with the network, generating 
and testing hypotheses, analyzing phenotypes and guiding genetic- and metabolic engineering [14]. 
The reconstruction process includes several steps that need to be followed if the model is to reflect the 
real-life network. Protocols, describing each step in the process, are available [14], [15] and were used 
to guide the current reconstruction. The main steps are shown in Fig. 2, but for a more comprehensive 
list the previously mentioned protocols should be consulted.  
Fig. 2 Overview of the main steps in the reconstruction process, based on [14]. The arrows indicate how the 
curation and the validation steps are an iterative process. 
When reconstructing a metabolic model the goal is to reflect the entire metabolic system of the 
target organism, ending up with a model that can correctly predict various phenotypic features. The 
reality, however, is that our knowledge of metabolism is incomplete. This may lead to incorrect varies 
predictions which in turn can lead to improved knowledge. False predictions may indicate errors or 
misinterpretations of knowledge or lack of knowledge. If experiments do not show growth but the 
model predicts it, the corresponding pathway might potentially be interesting. Overexpressing the 
relevant genes might result in growth. Regulatory systems, that the model does not cover, might be the 
==PAGE:15==
5 
 
cause for no growth. If a gene addition resulted in predicted growth, the real life cell might need time 
to adapt to the genetic change (adaptive laboratory evolution). The reason could also be that the 
reconstructed pathway is simply wrong and can subsequently be corrected. When experiments show 
growth but the model does not, it can often be explained by lack of knowledge. This is how the model 
can be used to guide the researcher towards unexplored research opportunities [18]. 
1.2.2 Flux balance analysis 
Flux balance analysis (FBA) is a mathematical approach commonly used to predict flow rates 
(fluxes) through the reactions in metabolic networks. This approach is often used to predict certain 
abilities of the model, such as the production rate of given metabolites or growth rate. In order to 
perform these calculations, each reaction in the network needs to be mathematically defined. A 
stoichiometric matrix (S-matrix) contains information on all the reactions and metabolites in a given 
network. Each row in the matrix represents one metabolite and each column represents one reaction. 
For each column, or reaction, a stoichiometric coefficient is given for each row, or metabolite. For 
every reaction each metabolite is either being consumed or produced, resulting in a negative or positive 
coefficient, respectively, or it is not involved in the reaction, resulting in a coefficient of zero. Fig. 3 
shows an example of how network reactions (A) are represented in an S-matrix (B).  
The S-matrix defines the so called solution space, which contains all possible flux values. To 
narrow down the solution space, constraints are imposed on the flow of metabolites through the 
network. The assumption that the system exists in a steady state is an important constraint. This means 
that the amount of every metabolite produced must be equal to the amount being consumed, so there 
is no accumulation taking place in the system. The S-matrix and the steady state assumption form a 
system of linear equations, where the equations represent the mass balance for every metabolite and 
the variables represent flux values. The flux for the entire system is represented by v = (v1, v2,…,vn), 
in a system with n reactions. The mass balance equations for the system at steady state can be written 
in matrix form as,

further narrow down the solution space, include lower and upper bounds that are assigned to each 
reaction. These bounds describe the minimum (lower bound) and maximum (upper bound) allowable

where l (lower bound) and u (upper bound) are vectors with n elements. To identify a single 
flux distribution, Flux Balance Analysis (FBA) is frequently used. FBA uses linear programming to 
find flux distributions that fall within the allowed solution space, by fulfilling the constraints 
represented in (1) and (2) while simultaneously optimizing a pre-specified objective. 
In order to do this a linear function representing the objective needs to be formulated. As an

rate, this is not a problem. If, however, the flux distribution itself is being analyzed, for instance 
between subsystems under different conditions, this can lead to difficulties. The COBRA toolbox [17], 
which can be used within the Matlab environment, is frequently used to carry out FBA analysis.

Chapter 2. Building the model 
In this chapter the first two major steps in the reconstruction process are described (see Fig. 2), 
with focus on the literature about metabolic reconstructions, individual metabolic subsystems and the 
corresponding organism specific literature, where available. 
2.1 Draft reconstruction 
The first step in the reconstruction process is to obtain a draft reconstruction based on the 
annotated genome and biochemical databases [14]. Several software platforms that can automatically 
generate a draft reconstruction are available, such as the Model SEED [19], Pathway tools [20], the 
RAVEN toolbox [21], the SuBliMinaL toolbox [22] and metaSHARK [23]. Hamilton and Reed [24] 
provide a comparison of many of those platforms and discuss the pros and cons of each. After 
considering and researching these different tools, it was decided to look further at the Model SEED 
and the RAVEN toolbox. 
2.1.1 The RAVEN toolbox 
The RAVEN toolbox is a platform that works within Matlab. An annotated genome is 
submitted and the RAVEN toolbox uses the KEGG database [25] to identify the KEGG Orthology 
(KO) ID for each gene. This platform also gives the option to use one or more genome-scale metabolic 
model as templates, where protein homology between the template- and the target genome is used to 
identify and add reactions from the template model to the reconstruction. This toolbox contains many 
useful functions that help the user to refine and evaluate the reconstruction, which is always necessary 
but can be very time consuming if no template model is used [21], [24]. S. ruber is a well-known 
relative of R. marinus but a model for that organism has not yet been published. Since R. marinus and 
S. ruber show significant physiological differences to other well-known relatives within the phylum 
Bacteroidetes [26], no suitable template model exists to use in the reconstruction process using the 
RAVEN toolbox and because of that the resulting reconstruction would probably need extensive 
refinement.

2.1.2 The Model SEED 
When automatically reconstructing a draft genome-scale metabolic model using the Model 
SEED platform, the process can be divided into several main steps. First a genome sequence is 
submitted to RAST (Rapid Annotation using Subsystem Technology) [27] for annotation. This process 
involves identifying protein-encoding genes (PEGs) using Glimmer [28], [29] and assigning functions 
to them using annotated reference genomes, that have been curated, for as many PEGs as possible and 
then using BLAST (Basic Local Alignment Search Tool) homology search for the rest.  After an 
automatic annotation, review and curation is needed. The annotated genome is then submitted to the 
Model SEED [30] where a core metabolic model is reconstructed, a draft of the biomass composition 
reaction is generated and the model is auto-completed. The Model SEED uses a comprehensive 
database of biochemistry containing over 13000 reactions and over 16000 compounds from the KEGG 
database [25] and many published genome-scale metabolic models as reference. This database is used 
to translate the gene annotation into a list of metabolic reactions and assign gene-protein-reaction 
association rules that explains how gene activity impacts reaction activity [19]. The Model SEED 
focuses mostly on prokaryotic systems so transport reactions between compartments are not added. 
RAST bases its annotations on other curated annotations and the Model SEED uses its own manually 
curated reaction database to reconstruct models [24]. The Model SEED has a database of draft 
reconstructions for many organisms, including R. marinus DSM 4252, which is publically available. 
The output from the Model SEED can be imported into Matlab where, theoretically, the COBRA 
toolbox can be used to analyze it. However, the Model SEED draft needs to be manually curated in 
order to obtain an accurate genome-scale metabolic model. 
2.1.3 Model SEED and RAVEN comparison 
Draft reconstructions from both Model SEED and RAVEN were obtained and compared. Two 
different comparisons were performed to evaluate the difference between them. First, all EC numbers 
from both platforms were listed. For some reactions, more than one EC number was assigned, and 
program written in Python was used to ‘flatten out’ the numbers resulting in one number per row. This 
was done to make it easier to compare EC numbers between the platforms. The two lists were 
evaluated, where some numbers were thrown out (for instance ‘EC 5.-.-.-‘) or modified (for instance 
‘EC 5.1.1.0’ changed to ‘EC 5.1.1.-‘). The Model SEED draft contained reactions with TC (Transport 
Classification) numbers and since transport reactions were manually added to the curated 
reconstruction, these reactions were not used and therefore discarded during this comparison. Venny 
[31], an online tool to compare lists, was used to compare the slightly modified EC numbers of the 
Model SEED and RAVEN draft reconstructions, and the resulting diagram can be seen in Fig. 4.

2.2 Network curation 
2.2.1 Automatic tools and manual curation 
Curating and refining the draft reconstruction is without a doubt the most time-consuming step 
in the process, but at the same time crucial in order to obtain a reliable model [15]. This process 
includes going through every metabolic reaction in the draft reconstruction to determine if it is indeed 
a part of the metabolism of the target organism and, if so, checking if it is correct in regards to 
substrates, cofactors, etc. The draft reconstruction is mostly based on organism-unspecific biochemical 
databases where enzyme functions common in various organisms can be found. This can give a good 
overview of the metabolic structure of the organism of study but some of these common functions are 
not necessarily present in the organism and some functions might be missing. When curating the draft 
reconstruction, organism-specific databases, textbooks, publications, articles and knowledge from 
experts are all important sources of information. Bioinformatics tools like BLAST and databases such 
as BRENDA and MetaCyc also play an important role in this step [14], [15], [32].

Genome-scale metabolic reconstructions for several organisms have been published and many 
of them are listed on the web page of the systems biology research group at the University of 
California1. The quality between models varies a lot which can be caused by numerous things such as 
lack of biological knowledge or scope, lack of phylogenetic coverage, lack of curation and lack of 
adherence to standards [33]. Protocols describing the reconstruction process in detail are available 
[14], [15] and following them should increase the quality. Tools for automatically performing some 
steps of the curation are available. The previously mentioned tools used to automatically reconstruct a 
draft model offer some additional functions. Other tools include SMILEY [34], GapFind/GapFill [35] 
and PathoLogic [36] to help filling in missing reactions and tools predicting reaction directionality 
based on thermodynamics [37], [38]. Despite all these available tools, manual curation is still always 
needed. Transport reactions, electron carriers and cofactor utilization, reaction localization, organism-
specific pathways and integration of physiological data are all steps that are often hard or even 
impossible to automatically generate.  
2.2.2 Reference models 
In this study it was decided to spend more time on manually going through the entire network 
using automatic tools as guidelines, rather than blindly trusting them. Other curated metabolic models 
were used as references in the manual curation. A model for the human gut bacterium Bacteroides 
thetaiotaomircon (iBth1201) [39] is available and since B.thetaiotaomicron belongs to the same 
phylum as R. marinus it was used as a reference.  A model for Synechocystis sp. PCC6803 (iJN678) 
[40] was used as a reference as well. This bacterium belongs to the phylum Cyanobacteria and is 
obviously quite distinct from R. marinus. This model has been thoroughly curated where charged 
formulas for all metabolites are listed and reactions are balanced. This model was used a lot in this 
reconstruction, mainly to obtain balanced reactions and metabolites. A genome-scale metabolic model 
for the model organism, Escherichia coli, was first assembled in 2000 [41] and has since been updated 
and improved several times [42]. Being one of the most popular model organisms, E. coli is extremely 
well characterized and its reconstruction is the most detailed and complete model that has been made 
until now. The newest version, iJO1366, was used as a reference to help guide the current 
reconstruction effort. Thermotoga maritima is a thermophilic bacteria and can use variety of carbon 
sources, similarly to R. marinus. An available model for T. maritima [43] was also used.

2.2.3 Gene-protein-reaction 
Gene-protein-reaction (GPR) association is important in the reconstruction as it describes the 
relationship between the genes and the corresponding protein functions. A gene can encode a protein 
that is a heteromeric enzyme complex, where it needs at least one more subunit, encoded by another 
gene, to form a functioning protein complex. A gene can also encode for a protein that can perform 
more than one reaction and in some cases, a reactions can be carried out by more than one protein. 
These different GPR associations need to be correctly assigned to each reaction in the reconstruction 
in order to describe the network correctly. The genome annotation often includes this information, but 
in some cases additional literature and database search is needed [14]. Examples of the GPR rules are 
shown in part C of Fig. 3 where reactions from the citric cycle (TCA) are considered.  
GPR associations have great impact on gene essentiality analysis. In a single gene deletion the 
impact of deleting every gene in the system, one at a time, is analyzed in respect to growth. The GPR 
association tells whether deleting a certain gene will affect the corresponding enzyme and therefore if 
it effects the flux of the corresponding reaction(s) [17]. If gene ‘peg.1413’, in part C of Fig. 3, were to 
be deleted the GPR rule of the PDH complex indicates that the complex would not be functional. In 
this case the upper and lower bounds of reaction ‘PDH’ would be set to zero, resulting in zero flux, 
hence inactivation of the reaction. If the gene ‘peg.1418’ were to be deleted, however, it would not 
have any functional effect on the ICD complex according to the GPR rule, and the flux of reaction 
‘ICD’ would be unaffected. FBA is used, as described in subsection 1.2.2, to simulate growth for each 
case of deletion. 
2.2.4 Confidence scoring system 
The draft reconstruction is built on annotated genome information. During manual curation, 
additional information about the pathways is added. In order to assess the amount of information 
known about each reaction, a confidence score is assigned to it. This scoring system ranges from 1 - 
4. One is the lowest and indicates that there is no evidence for the reaction but it is required for the 
model to function. If there is evidence for gene function, such as gene annotation, the reaction gets the 
score of two. The same score is assigned when there is indirect physiological evidence, such as the 
presence or absence of secretion products or medium components. Additional genetic data, such as 
knockout, knock-in or overexpression characterization, gives a score of three. The highest score, four, 
is given when there is direct evidence of the gene product function associated with a given reaction 
[14]. Most reactions in this study end up with confidence score of two. The central metabolism of most 
aerobic bacteria is very similar and generally it is not necessary to study it in every single species. This 
is why there is usually ‘only’ genomic evidence for these reactions. Here, most reactions obtaining a 
==PAGE:23==
13 
 
higher confidence score usually involve enzymes which are biotechnologically interesting and have 
therefore been studied. References are always provided for reactions receiving higher score than 2. 
Several metabolic pathways in R. marinus have been well characterized and include the respiratory 
chain, stress responses, carotenoid production and many different sugar utilizing pathways. These 
pathways will be described in more detail below. 
2.2.5 Substrates, cofactors and directionality 
Enzymes may have different substrate and cofactor specificities between organisms and since 
the draft reconstruction is obtained from generic enzyme information, this often needs additional 
organism specific data. Enzyme databases such as Brenda [44] and KEGG [25] contain information 
about enzymes along with their known functions and properties. If an enzyme catalyzes only one 
reaction, organism specific information is less likely to be needed than in the case of multi-functional 
enzymes. This additional information can sometimes be found in biochemical studies or in organism-
specific databases [14]. 
Reaction directionality determines if reactions are reversible or irreversible. Sometimes, 
organism specific-biochemical data for directionality of certain enzymes is available, but this is usually 
not the case. Other approaches to determine this include using standard Gibbs free energy estimations 
and thermodynamic information. Enzyme databases sometimes have these estimations included. 
Reaction directionality affects the network significantly and if in doubt reverse directionality should 
be applied. If a reversible reaction is defined irreversible, it can result in lack of biological function. 
As an example from the current reconstruction, if the reactions in glycolysis were irreversible, 
gluconeogenesis would not be possible which would lead to a model unable to grow on pyruvate as a 
carbon source. Wrongly applying reversible reactions to the network can also have negative effects, as 
the FBA result can include thermodynamically infeasible loops [14].

reactions from the draft- and/or reference reconstructions and collect them in an Excel sheet. For every 
reaction there is a reaction name, reaction description, formula, gene-reaction association, genes, 
proteins, subsystem, reaction directionality and the lower and upper bounds for the rate constants, 
objective, confidence score, EC number, ID of corresponding reaction in the draft reconstruction and 
notes and references as appropriate (see electronic supplementary information, S1). The metabolites 
were abbreviated the same way as in the reference reconstructions when possible. Metabolites specific 
to R. marinus were abbreviated in a similar style. All metabolites and their abbreviations are listed in

2.2.7 Central metabolism 
The first subsystems to be curated were pathways in connection with the central metabolism, 
including glycolysis, pyruvate metabolism, the pentose phosphate pathway and the citrate cycle 
(TCA). Most reactions in these pathways are the same in R. marinus as in many other bacteria and the 
genes are usually found in the annotated genome. One exception to this is the first step of glycolysis, 
where glucose is turned into glucose-6-phosphate by a glucokinase. Originally there was a gene 
annotated as a ‘glucokinase’ in the genome, but it has since been re-annotated as a ‘transcriptional 
regulator’. This change was due to results from a study on two potential glucokinase genes in 
Synechosyctis sp. PCC 6803 [45]. The results showed that the protein encoded by one of the genes did 
not act as a glucokinase, despite amino acid sequence similarities to glucokinase genes. This gene is 
homologous to the formally annotated glucokinase in R. marinus. The protein encoded by the other 
gene from Synechosyctis did show a glucokinase activity, but no homologous gene can be found in the 
genome of R. marinus. The gene next to the transcriptional regulator is annotated as a hypothetical 
protein. When the sequence was submitted to BLAST and searched for homologous sequences most 
results were ‘hypothetical proteins’ as well. Few hits, however, were annotated as a ‘kinase’, one of 
which is from the bacterium Bacteroides stercoris, belonging to the same phylum as R. marinus. Since 
no evidence of the roles of this gene exists, this is currently just a hypothesis, but can be tested with 
simple knock-out experiments. In the meanwhile, no genes are assigned to this reaction in the 
reconstruction. 
R. marinus is modeled to produce pyruvate from glucose through the Embden-Meyerhof (EM) 
pathway based on the fact that the genes needed for the pathway appear to be present in the genome. 
However, two genes encoding for key enzymes of the Entner-Doudoroff (ED) pathway, are present in 
the genome as well. Upon closer inspection, R. marinus does not seem to have genes encoding for all 
the enzymes of this pathway. This is why, when grown on glucose it most likely does not use the ED 
pathway. The reason for the presence of these two genes remained unclear to the author, until pathways 
for algal sugar degradation were investigated. These pathways are further discussed in subsection

The respiratory chain in aerobic bacteria consists of four complexes, all located in the cell 
membrane. The first complex, NADH dehydrogenase, oxidizes NADH and transfers the electrons to 
quinones [46], which are of the menaquinone-7 type in R. marinus [47]. During this process, protons 
are transported from the cytosol to the periplasm. Complex I from R. marinus has been partially 
purified and characterized [48]. The second complex, succinate dehydrogenase, oxidizes succinate into 
fumarate resulting in electron transfer to quinones [46]. Complex II has also been purified and 
characterized in R. marinus [49]. The first two complexes in R. marinus are similar to those found in 
other bacteria. R. marinus contains an unusual third complex, cytochrome dehydrogenase. Usually the 
third complex removes electrons from reduced quinones and transfers them to cytochrome c, resulting 
in proton translocation from cytosol to periplasm. Typically this is performed by the cytochrome bc1 
complex. In R. marinus there is an alternative complex with a completely different structure which 
carries out the same function as the cytochrome bc1 complex. In this alternative complex, reduced 
quinones are oxidized and High Potential iron-sulfur Protein (HiPIP) and cytochrome c are reduced 
[50]. Oxygen reductase is the last of the four complexes in the respiratory chain. This complex oxidizes 
the periplasmic electron donors, in this case HiPIP and cytochrome c, and reduces oxygen to water. At 
the same time protons are transported from cytosol to perisplam [46]. R. marinus contains three 
different oxygen reductases: cbb3 [51], caa3 [52] and ba3 [53]. A proton gradient, formed during proton

2.2.9 Carotenoid production 
Since there is a special focus on carotenoid production in this study, a thorough literature- and 
gene search was performed with respect to this pathway. A study characterizing the carotenoid content 
of R. marinus strain DSM 4253 is available [12]. The investigated strain is however not the same as 
the type strain, DSM 4252T, studied here. No literature describing the carotenoid content of the type 
strain was found. While this information is missing, carotenoid content on strain DSM 4253 is used 
for the reconstruction. 
2.2.10 Terpenoid pathway 
Before turning to the carotenoid biosynthetic pathway it is important to investigate terpenoid 
biosynthesis. Terpenoids can be produced through two different pathways, the mevalonate and the 
non-mevalonate pathways. In R. marinus, biosynthesis of terpenoids is carried out by the non-
mevalonate pathway where geranylgeranyl diphosphate (GGDP) is produced from pyruvate and 
glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate. GGDP is then taken into the carotenoid pathway. Genes encoding for all 
enzymes in the pathway are found in the annotated genome, except for one. The enzyme usually 
responsible for the first step in the pathway is 1-deoxy-D-xylulose-5-phosphate synthase (EC 2.2.1.7). 
The gene for this enzyme was neither identified in the annotated genome nor after manual homology 
search using BLAST. It seems to be missing from the genome. Since it is well known that the terpenoid 
pathway is present in R. marinus, this first step must take place. A study, where the non-mevalonate 
pathway was proposed as a target for antimicrobial agents, discusses spontaneous mutations in E. coli 
that rescue the otherwise lethal loss of function in the first two steps of the pathway [54]. The study 
showed that a single missense point mutation (E636Q) in the gene encoding for E1 subunit of the 
pyruvate dehydrogenase complex can cause the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex to replace the first 
two enzymes of the pathway. This is, of course, only a hypothesis for R. marinus at the moment, which 
is why no gene was assigned to this reaction in the reconstruction. Since pyruvate dehydrogenase is 
responsible for other essential reactions in the cell, it would not be feasible to knock out the 
corresponding gene and check for color. This gene deletions would most likely result in no growth. It 
would, however, be possible to knock out this gene and insert a gene encoding for pyruvate 
dehydrogenase from another thermophilic organism. If the hypothesis is correct this might result in 
growth but no terpenoid production and therefore no red carotenoid production. If some terpenoids are 
==PAGE:27==
17 
 
essential for growth this experiment would also result in no growth. In that case, it would be possible 
to insert the gene coding for 1-deoxy-D-xylulose-5-phosphate synthase from another thermophilic 
bacteria. If this would result in growth and red color while only pyruvate dehydrogenase from another 
organism would result in no color or no growth, it would strongly strengthen the hypothesis. Another 
way would be to isolate the pyruvate dehydrogenase from R. marinus and measure its substrate 
specificity and activity. Fourteen reactions were added to the reconstruction belonging to the terpenoid 
pathway, with no gene assigned to the first one. 
2.2.11 Carotenoid biosynthesis 
The carotenoid content of R. marinus DSM 4253 was found to be 0.04% of dry cell weight 
[12]. It consists of (all-E,2’S)-2’-hydroxy-1’-(β-ᴅ-glucopyranosyloxy)-3’,4’-didehydro-1’,2’-dihydro-
β,ψ-caroten-4-one (3.3% of total carotenoids) and its C-6’’ acyl derivative (55%), and (all-E)-1’-(β-ᴅ-
glucopyranosyloxy)-3’,4’-didehydro-1’,2’-dihydro-β,ψ-caroten-4-one (2.5%) and its C-6’’ acyl 
derivative (39%). The acyl profiles of both carotenoids were identical and comparable with the lipid 
composition of the cell. Since the lipid pathways have not been extensively modeled yet, an average 
formula for the acyl groups was calculated and used in the carotenoid modeling. This results in two 
different carotenoids and their acyl derivatives being a part of the biomass composition in the 
reconstruction. The major carotenoid is structurally highly similar to the major carotenoid from S. 
ruber, salinxanthin [55]. Given that S. ruber and R. marinus are close relatives, the similarities do not 
come as a surprise. The minor carotenoid from R. marinus shows high resemblance to the carotenoid 
keto-myxocoxanthin from the thermophilic phototrophic bacterium Roseiflexus castenholzii. The 
pathway of this carotenoid has been described in literature [56], and was used here to build a putative 
pathway for carotenoid production in R. marinus. The study on salinxanthin does not characterize the 
biosynthetic pathway. A study on sioxanthin, a carotenoid produced by Salinispora tropica, describes 
the corresponding pathway in detail along with enzymes and genes [57]. Sioxanthin is relatively 
similar in structure to salinxanthin and therefore also used to build a putative pathway. Fig. 6 shows 
the proposed pathway for carotenoid production in R. marinus, based on the pathways for sioxanthin 
and keto-myxocoxanthin. Seventeen reactions belonging to the carotenoid pathway were added to the 
reconstruction and are further described below. 
2.2.12 Proposed carotenoid pathway 
GGDP (Fig. 6, part 1) is obtained from the terpenoid pathway. Phytoene synthase (CrtB), 
catalyzes two units of GGDP to phytoene (Fig. 6, part 2) in two steps and phytoene desaturase (CrtI), 
catalyzes the conversion of phytoene to lycopene (Fig. 6, part 3) in several steps. These first steps in 
the carotenoid pathway are well characterized and highly similar in different species. Genes for both

18 
 
of these enzymes were found in the annotated genome. Experimental data shows that if these genes 
are knocked out, the resulting colonies are colorless (S. Bjornsdottir, personal communication, 
February 2015), confirming the roles of these genes in the pathway. The fate of lycopene varies a lot 
between carotenoid producing organisms. In R. marinus, lycopene is converted to γ-carotene (Fig. 6, 
part 4) by lycopene cyclase (CrtY). A ketolase (CrtO) turns γ-carotene into 4-keto-γ-carotene (Fig. 6, 
part 5). Genes for these enzymes are also found in the annotated genome. The next two enzymes 
required (Fig. 6, parts 5-7) for the biosynthesis are 1’,2’-hydratase and carotenoid 3’,4’-desaturase.  In 
the sioxanthin pathway the 1’,2’-hydratase, CruF, hydrates the double bond in question. CrtC is also 
known to catalyze this reaction. When searching for these genes in R. marinus, BLAST was used since 
no gene was annotated as CruF nor CrtC in the genome. Comparing genes encoding for CruF from 
several organisms to the R. marinus genome, showed homology with 35-40% identity to the gene 
annotated as phytoene synthase (CrtB). No other candidate for this step was found and CrtB therefore 
assigned to it. Carotenoid 3’,4’-desaturase, or CrtC, which adds a double bond, is not found in the 
annotated genome either. CrtC from other organisms showed homology with 30% identity to the gene 
annotated as phytoene desaturase (CrtI) in R. marinus and since no other candidate was found for this 
reaction, CrtI was assigned. Up until now both carotenoids have followed the same pathway (Fig. 6, 
parts 1-7). For the major carotenoid, a hydroxy group is added after the desaturation (Fig. 6, part 8b). 
In the sioxanthin pathway, this is performed by a spheroidene monooxygenase, or CrtA. One gene is 
annotated as monooxygenase in the genome of R. marinus, but this gene does not show homology to 
CrtA genes from other organisms. Because of this and the fact that no other gene showed high 
homology to CrtA, no gene was assigned to this step in the reconstruction. After the addition of a 
hydroxy group to the major carotenoid, both carotenoids follow the same steps. Glycosyltransferase, 
or CruC, adds glucose to the molecules (Fig. 6, parts 8a and 9b). This results in the two carotenoids 
found in R. marinus. Acyl derivatives (Fig. 6, parts 9a and 10b) of these carotenoids are, however, 
much more common. The acetyltransferase, CruD, is responsible for the acyl addition. Genes for both 
CruC and CruD can be found in the annotated genome [12], [56], [57]. The entire proposed pathway 
including molecular structures of the carotenoids being formed, enzyme names and genes are presented 
in Fig. 6. In many well characterized carotenoid pathways, the corresponding genes are all found 
clustered together in the genome. In the study of sioxanthin from Salinispora tropica, a gene search 
for all the enzymes in the pathway was performed. The search revealed that four regions, two gene 
clusters and two independent genes, were found in the genome contributing to the pathway [57]. While 
proposing the carotenoid pathway for R. marinus, possible gene candidates were also proposed. The 
genes assigned to the pathway are distributed over three regions in the genome, one gene cluster and 
two independent genes.

2.2.13 Carbon sources 
R. marinus can grow on many different carbon sources with some variation between strains. 
Most strains can grow on sugars such as glucose, galactose, lactose, maltose, raffinose, sucrose and 
starch [6]. Other carbon sources include the amino acids L-aspartate and L-glutamate along with 
gelatin, acetate and pyruvate [2]. The bacterium’s ability to use a wide variety of carbon sources 
indicates that many different genes encoding for thermostable polysaccharide degrading enzymes can 
be found in its genome. These enzymes can have a great biotechnological potential and in R. marinus, 
many of them have already been characterized and the corresponding genes identified. Since these 
enzymes are usually found extracellularly, they are extremely thermostable in R. marinus case because 
of its hostile environment. 
2.2.14 Starch 
Starch is one of the polysaccharides R. marinus can break down and use. Starch mainly consists 
of amylose and amylopectin. The ratio of amylose and amylopectin depends on the origin of the starch, 
but normally amylose is about 20-35% of the starch composition. Amylose is a linear α-glucan with 
majority of the glucose units linked with (1 → 4)-α linkages. Amylopectin is a larger molecule than 
amylose and heavily branched. Around 95% of the glucose units are linked with (1 → 4)-α linkages, 
but at branch points the units are linked with (1 → 6)-α linkages (c.a. 5%) [58]. Different enzymes are 
involved in starch degradation. α-amylase (EC 3.2.1.1) hydrolyses internal (1 → 4)-α linkages 
randomly in starch, resulting in linear and branched oligosaccharides. α-glucosidase (EC 3.2.1.20) and 
glucoamylase (EC 3.2.1.3) convert oligosaccharides further into glucose by hydrolysing (1 → 4)-α 
linkages from the non-reducing end. α-glucosidase produces glucose with α-anomeric configuration 
while glucoamylase produces glucose with β-anomeric configuration. Enzymes that are able to 
hydolyse (1 → 6)-α linkages are called pullulanases. Two different types of pullulanases exist. Type I 
(EC 3.2.1.41), which is also called debranching enzyme, hydrolyses pullulan into maltotriose and 
branched oligosaccharides into linear oligosaccharides. Type II pullulanase (EC 3.2.1.135 and EC 
3.2.1.57) can hydrolyse both (1 → 4)-α- and (1 → 6)-α- linkages and results in the production of small 
sugars such as glucose, maltose and maltotriose [59]. Trehalose can be produced from starch by two 
enzymes. Maltooligosyltrehalose synthase (EC 5.4.99.15) converts the (1 → 4)-α linkage at the 
reducing end of maltooligosaccharides into a (1 → 1)-α-α bond and maltooligosyltrehalose 
trehalohydrolase (EC 3.2.1.141) releases trehalose during hydrolysis of (1 → 4)-α linkage between the 
second and third glucose units [60]. 
A search for genes coding for these different starch degrading enzymes in the genome of R. 
marinus was performed. Four different genes for α-amylase were found, three different genes for α-
==PAGE:31==

glucosidase and one for glucoamylase. One gene was annotated as debranching enzyme and one as 
neopullulanase. Production and partial characterization of amylase and pullulanase in R. marinus has 
been described [61]. An amylolytic enzyme from R. marinus was purified and characterized. This 
enzyme showed both glucan branching activity and neopullulanase activity [62], which is why this 
gene was also assigned to the glycogen branching reaction. Genes for the two enzymes required for

2.2.15 Trehalose and glucose transport 
Even though the genes for producing trehalose from starch were not found in R. marinus, the 
organism does have a trehalose uptake system and a specific enzyme that converts trehalose into 
glucose. The transport of trehalose has been thoroughly characterized in R. marinus, where a 
mathematical model was used to help interpret the experimental data [63]. It was proposed that R. 
marinus possesses two transport systems for trehalose. One of the systems is a high-affinity, low-
capacity threhalose transporter. It transports trehalose from the periplasm, where it diffused from the 
environment, into the cytosol. In the cytosol the trehalose is then converted into two units of glucose 
which goes into the glycolysis pathway. The other system involves a periplasmic trehalase that 
converts trehalose into glucose in the periplasm and the glucose is then transported into the cytosol by 
the low-affinity, low-capacity glucose transporter. The authors proposed that in a low concentration of 
trehalose, the trehalose transporter is primarily used. Once the concentration is increased, the trehalose 
transporter can’t keep up due to its low-capacity, which is why the periplasmic trehalase activity 
increases and the glucose transporter is used as well. The glucose transporter in R. marinus has low –
affinity and –capacity. The authors discuss a possible reason for its poor activity. They point out that 
R. marinus lives in an extreme environment were monosaccharides are in short supply, so it does not 
need the glucose transporter as much as many other bacteria. The nature of the trehalose transporter 
has not been determined completely, but in the study being discussed it is proposed to be an ABC 
transport system. The authors point out that thermophilic and hyperthermophilic organisms usually do 
not possess the PTS system, which is a carbohydrate phosphotransferase system. They also base their 
hypothesis on a homology search of trehalose transporter genes from other thermophilic organisms, in 
the R. marinus genome. Four genes in the genome are associated with transport on oligosaccharides 
such as trehalose, maltose and sucrose. One gene is annotated as ‘maltose/maltodextrin ATP 
transporter (MalK)/multiple sugar ABC tranporter’ and the other three are annotated together as a 
‘multiple sugar ABC trasnporter’. Another gene is annotated as a ‘sodium-glucose/galactose 
==PAGE:32==
22 
 
cotransporter’. Since no published characterization of these transporters exist for R. marinus, these 
genes are assigned to the relevant transport reactions in the reconstruction. 
2.2.16 Mono- and disaccharides 
R. marinus has been shown to grow on medium with raffinose, sucrose [2] and fructose (S. 
Bjornsdottir, personal communication, February-July 2015) as individual sole carbon sources. The 
genes encoding for the enzymes needed to utilize these carbon sources are found in the genome and 
the reactions were easy to model. R. marinus can also use lactose and galactose as carbon sources [2]. 
Lactose is a disaccharide, made of glucose and galactose. The gene encoding for β-galactosidase (EC 
3.2.1.23), which can hydrolyze lactose into glucose and galactose, was found in the genome. Three 
steps are needed to convert galactose into glucose-6-phosphate, which is then taken into the glycolysis 
pathway. The genes encoding for the first two steps (galactose -> galactose-1-phosphate -> glucose-
1-phosphate) were found in the genome. The third step, where glucose-1-phosphate is turned into 
glucose-6-phosphate, is usually performed by the enzyme phosphoglucomutase (EC 5.4.2.2). The gene 
for this enzyme was not found in the genome, but a search using BLAST showed good homology to 
two genes annotated as phosphoglucosamine mutase (EC 5.4.2.10) in the genome of R. marinus. A 
study investigated properties of the enzyme phosphoglucosamine mutase in E. coli [64]. This enzyme 
usually catalyzes the interconversion of glucosamine-6-phosphate and glucosamine-1-phosphate, but 
can also catalyze the interconversion of glucose-6-phosphate and glucose-1-phosphate, only at much 
lower rate. The site of phosphorylation was identified as Ser102. It was discovered that a mutational 
change in Ser100 to a threonine residue increased the phosphoglucomutase activity significantly. The 
two genes annotated as phosphoglucosamine mutases in the genome of R. marinus were investigated 
in respect to the amino acid residues described in the study. The serine residue responsible for the 
phosphorylation was found to be residue number 103 in the first gene and 106 in the second. The 
corresponding residue to Ser102 from E. coli was found to be a threonine residue in the first gene and 
a serine residue in the second gene of R. marinus. This indicates that the first gene might well be 
responsible for the interconversion of glucose-6-phosphate and glucose-1-phosphate in R. marinus. 
This could be confirmed with a single gene knockout where the mutant should show decreased or no 
growth on galactose, if this hypothesis is correct. Since this is still just a hypothesis, no gene was 
assigned to this reaction in the reconstruction. 
2.2.17 Laminarin 
Laminarin is a polysaccharide made of glucose units that are mostly linked with (1 → 3)-β 
linkages. It is an energy storage glucan in brown algae. Brown algae are very fast growing, can grow 
up to 30 cm per day, and can get quite large. Because of the wide availability of brown algae, it has a 
==PAGE:33==

great potential as an energy source. In order to use laminarin as an energy source, the (1 → 3)-β 
linkages need to be hydrolyzed. The enzyme laminarinase or endo-1,3(4)-β-glucanase (EC 3.2.1.6) 
can perform this reaction and the gene encoding for this enzyme is present in the genome of R. marinus. 
The enzyme from R. marinus has been purified and thoroughly characterized [65]–[67]. Two reactions 
describing extracellular degradation of laminarin into glucose were added to the reconstruction.

phosphogluconate dehydratase (EC 4.2.1.12), being missing (Reaction A in Fig. 7). This missing 
enzyme is not needed to convert 2-keto-3-deoxygluconate to pyruvate. The genes coding for the two 
enzymes that are needed, 2-dehydro-3-deoxyphosphogluconate aldolase (EC 4.1.2.14) and 2-dehydro-
3-deoxygluconokinase (EC 2.7.1.45), are however present in the genome (Reactions B and C in Fig. 
7, respectively). These steps are shown in Fig. 7 which presents a simplified network of the EM- and 
ED- pathways.  
2.2.19 Xylan and cellulose 
Xylan is a polysaccharide made of β-ᴅ-xylose units linked with (1 → 4) linkages. Xylan, along 
with glucans and mannans, is a hemicellulose, which is one of the three major polymeric components 
of the plant cell wall. β-1,4-Xylanases (EC 3.2.1.8) is an enzyme capable of hydrolyzing the (1 → 4)-
β linkages in xylan [70]. A gene encoding for β-1,4-Xylanase can be found in the genome of R. marinus 
and the enzyme has been purified and thoroughly characterized [71]–[74]. Five reactions were added 
to the reconstruction describing the degradation of xylan into xylose. 
Cellulose is another major polymeric component of the plant cell wall. It is made of ᴅ-glucose 
units linked with (1 → 4)-β linkages. Three different enzymes are used to degrade cellulose into 
glucose units. First the internal O-glycosidic bonds are randomly hydrolyzed by an endo-(1,4)-β-ᴅ-
glucanase or cellulase (EC 3.2.1.4) resulting in differently sized glucans. An exo-(1,4)-β-ᴅ-glucanase 
(EC 3.2.1.91) acts on the ends of the glucans resulting in β-cellobioses, which are disaccharides. The 
β-cellobioses are transported into the cell where β-glycosidases (EC 3.2.1.21) hydrolyze them into β-
glucose units [75]. A cellulase has been purified and characterized in R. marinus [76], [77] and four 
different genes in the genome are annotated as β-glycosidases. A BLAST search for exo-(1,4)-β-ᴅ-
glucanase showed no good homology in R. marinus genome. The absence of exo-(1,4)-β-ᴅ-glucanase 
might result in poor cellulose degradation, but this is discussed in detail in section 4.2. Four reactions 
show cellulose degradation into glucose in the reconstruction. 
2.2.20 Stress responses 
Because of the extreme environment R. marinus lives in, it needs to be able to respond to 
sudden osmotic changes due to changes in temperature and/or salinity, in order to survive. The cell 
responds to this stress by accumulating compatible solutes (Nunes, Manaia, DaCosta, & Santos, 1995). 
Compatible solutes are compounds that do not have negative effects on metabolism or enzyme activity 
at high levels. In aerobic heterotrophic bacteria they include amino acids, monosaccharides such as 
glucose, disaccharides such as trehalose and sucrose, small peptides and sugar derivatives. The sugar 
==PAGE:35==
25 
 
derivative, mannosylglycerate, is the major compatible solute in R. marinus, produced via two 
pathways [79]. These pathways, enzymes and corresponding genes in R. marinus have been studied in 
detail [78]–[80]. The mannosylglycerate pathways were included in the reconstruction, but since 
mannosylglycerate is produced in response to stress, these pathways are usually not active in the model 
since a normal stage is assumed there.  
2.2.21 Polyamines 
Polyamines are alkaline organic compounds with at least two primary amino groups. They can 
be found in most forms of live and have diverse functions [81]. Some polyamines are 
biotechnologically interesting because they can be used to produce plastics and as curing agents in 
polymer applications. In thermophilic bacteria unusual, long and branched polyamines have been 
observed. They are thought to have protective effect on nucleic acids and proteins under high 
temperature conditions [81]. Seven different polyamines have been characterized in R. marinus, 
putrescine, spermidine, cadaverine, spermine, thermopentamine, N4-aminopropylspermidine and N4-
bis(aminopropyl)spermidine [82]. Cadaverine is derived from the amino acid L-lysine but the others 
from L-arginine. Different polyamine transporters have been characterized in many bacteria. In the 
genome of R. marinus no gene is annotated as a polyamine specific transporter. In S. ruber, a gene 
annotated as ‘polyamine ABC transporter’, was found. When the amino acid sequence of this gene is 
compared to R. marinus genome using BLAST, several good hits were obtained (32-42% identity), 
most of which annotated as ‘ABC transporters’. Eleven reactions in the reconstruction describe the 
biosynthesis of polyamines. 
2.2.22 Amino acids and lipids 
R. marinus can grow on a minimal medium without any amino acids, suggesting it can synthesis 
all amino acids. These pathways have therefore all been included in the reconstruction, resulting in 
113 reactions belonging to these subsystems. R. marinus has also been reported to use aspartate and 
glutamate as carbon sources [2], which is why transport reactions for these amino acids were added to 
the reconstruction.  
The fatty acid and lipid composition of R. marinus has been characterized and the dominating 
fatty acids are iso- and anteiso-C15 and iso- and anteiso-C17, with iso-C16 and iso-C18 as minor 
components. The major polar lipids are phosphatydilethanolamine, diphosphatydilglycerol and one 
unidentified lipid and phosphatidylglycerol was identified as a minor lipid [47], [83]. The fatty acid 
and lipid pathways are not included in the reconstruction at this time, due to time-constraints.

2.2.23 Biomass objective function 
A biomass objective function (BOF) describes all the components and their corresponding 
molar contributions of the overall biomass. The biomass function is frequently used as an objective in 
flux balance analysis. It is therefore important that the fractional composition of all biomass 
components are correctly formulated, for the model predictions to be accurate. In reality, the biomass 
composition varies depending on different conditions such as growth rate and environmental factors. 
In model reconstructions, this is usually simplified to a single reaction.  
Experimental determination of the biomass composition is done by analyzing cells in the 
logarithmic growth phase under known conditions.  If there is a lack of such information, experimental 
values from related organisms or computational estimates can be used [15]. The BOF can be described 
in different levels of detail. The basic level accounts for the macromolecular content of the cell 
(proteins, DNA, RNA, lipids, etc.) and all metabolites included in each macromolecular group (amino 
acids, nucleotide triphosphates, etc.). At the intermediate level, the energy necessary to synthesis the 
macromolecules is also included. At an advanced level of detail, vitamins, elements and cofactors, 
necessary for growth are included as well [84].  
Fractional macromolecular content is usually obtained experimentally. At the moment, no such 
data exists for R. marinus and therefore data from E. coli was used [85]. The amino acid ratio of 
proteins and nucleic acid ratio of RNA and DNA were obtained from the genome sequence of R. 
marinus. The nucleotide sequence of the entire genome was used to calculate the ratio of nucleotides 
in DNA. The nucleotide sequence of the protein coding part of the genome was used to calculate the 
ratio of nucleotides in RNA. The amino acid sequence was used to calculate the amino acid ratio. 
These calculations were carried out using a program written in Python. Calculating the nucleic acid 
ratio of DNA from the genome sequence gives a good estimate of the true values. The calculations of 
the nucleic acid ratio of RNA and amino acids of proteins will probably not give as good estimates 
since genes are expressed at different levels in the cell and this information cannot be obtained from 
the genome sequence alone. Transcriptomic- and proteomic data could be integrated here, if available. 
Amino acid composition can also be measured in the cell. Presently, this data does not exist for R. 
marinus which is why genomic information was used.  
Experimental measurements for lipid composition in R. marinus can be found in literature [47], 
[83]. As mentioned above the lipid metabolism was not included in the reconstruction and therefore 
not described in detail in the BOF either. These pathways will be added later on and the BOF updated 
accordingly. Values for polyamines [82] and carotenoids [12] were obtained from organism specific 
==PAGE:37==
27 
 
literature. The relative fraction of glycogen, peptidoglycan, lipopolysaccharide, soluble pool and 
inorganic ions were adopted from E. coli [86].  
The energy maintenance refers to all the energy needed in the cell, in the form of ATP. The 
growth associated maintenance cost (GAM) should be experimentally measured, if possible [14]. At 
the moment this data is not available for R. marinus. A part of GAM can be calculated from the 
theoretical energy requirements for macromolecular synthesis [86] but this estimate will always be too 
low and experimental values should be obtained as well. The calculated value for R. marinus was just 
over 20 mmol ATP per gDW when the true value is expected to be closer to 40-50 mmol ATP per 
gDW [14]. NGAM represents the non-growth associated maintenance and is represented in the 
reconstruction with the reaction ATP + H2O  ADP + Pi + H+. The value for NGAM should be 
measured experimentally. 
The fractional calculations for the biomass components can be found in the electronic 
supplementary information, S2. The values presented there will be updated once experimental values 
are available and the necessary measurements and calculations are further discussed in section 6.1.

Chapter 3. Evaluating the model 
In this chapter the next two major steps in the reconstruction process are described (See Fig. 
2), where the reconstruction is represented in a mathematical manner and the network is validated and 
tested.  
3.1 Conversion to a mathematical model 
The Excel sheet was read into Matlab and the COBRA toolbox was used to analyze the 
reconstruction. The network was represented in an S-matrix and objective function and simulation 
constraints of the model were set [14]. The S-matrix, objective function and constraints were described 
in subsection 1.2.2 An illustration showing how network reactions are converted to an S-matix is 
shown in Fig. 3. 
3.2 Network validation 
Once the reconstruction has been converted to a mathematical model, tests and validation of 
the model can begin. This step in the process can be thought of as a ‘debugging stage’. The COBRA 
toolbox contains several functions to help with this process and those used here are described below.  
3.2.1 Mass and charge balance 
All reactions in the network were checked for mass and charge balance and unbalanced 
reactions were fixed [14]. The most common reasons for unbalanced reactions were missing protons, 
water and/or cofactors, the metabolic formulas were wrong or metabolites incorrectly charged. Every 
time new reactions or pathways were added to the network this test was performed and unbalanced 
reactions fixed. 
3.2.2 Testing of individual pathways 
Since the reconstruction was still unfinished when the network validation was performed for 
the first time, individual pathways in the network were tested. This was done to help complete the 
pathways one at a time. To begin with, short pathways were tested. Glucose was used as a carbon 
source and the model asked to produce key metabolites, such as pyruvate and lactate, consequently 
==PAGE:39==
29 
 
producing flux through the glycolysis pathway. At first these tests failed and an optimization procedure 
was used to help figure out why. The procedure is a COBRA add-on (unpublished) that finds the 
smallest set of additional demand/sink reactions that need to be added for the test to work. This can 
help to find the reactions that do not work or are missing from the network. More tests were gradually 
included where production of different metabolites in different ‘corners’ of the network were tested, 
resulting in an expanding functional network. 
3.2.3 Production of biomass 
In order to simulate growth, the model needs to be able to produce all biomass components. A 
test was performed were the lower bound of the biomass reaction was set to a portion of the glucose 
uptake. This is done to force production of biomass but keeping it low enough for the available glucose. 
This experiment tested the model in respect to production of every biomass component and for those 
that could not be produced, the optimizer described above was used the same way as before to find the 
smallest set of additional demand/sink reactions for the tests to work. During this process, many 
reactions were added to the reconstruction, including exchange and transport reactions.   
3.2.4 Dead-end metabolites, blocked reactions and gap filling 
During the tests of individual pathways and biomass components many gaps in the network 
were filled and incomplete pathways fixed. Sometimes when filling gaps, reactions with no evidence 
of taking place in the target organism, need to be added to ensure a complete network and a functioning 
model. These ‘modeling’ reactions always receive a confidence score of one. The current 
reconstruction is still not free of dead-end metabolites or blocked reactions, containing 19 dead-end 
metabolites and 54 blocked reactions. This can partially be explained by the fact that the reconstruction 
is still unfinished, and some pathways are to be added at the end of these blocked reactions and dead-
end metabolites. Another reason can also be that some of the dead-end metabolites are further used in 
cellular mechanisms that are not included in the metabolic network [14].   
3.2.5 Typographical errors and duplications 
If two reactions are given the same name, Matlab cannot distinguish between them which can 
cause trouble when using the model. In some cases, the bounds were incorrectly assigned in respect to 
the directional sign in the formula itself. Programs written in Matlab were used to find these errors and 
they were fixed accordingly.

Both in vivo and in silico experiments showed growth on all mono- and disaccharides tested, 
except for ribulose. If transport of ribulose is assumed, however, the model predicts growth. The same 
holds for the metabolites from the TCA cycle (malate, fumarate, succinate, citrate and 2-oxoglutarate) 
and the amino acid proline. These results suggest that if transporters for these metabolites were present, 
R. marinus could use them.  
R. marinus is able to grow on pyruvate and acetate as sole carbon sources both in vivo and in 
silico. Interestingly, the colonies obtained in vivo were always white in color. This indicates that 
carotenoids are not being produced. As described in subsection 2.2.9, the carotenoid precursor GGDP 
is produced by the non-mevalonate terpenoid pathway. This pathway takes in pyruvate and 
glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate and, in several steps, produces GGDP. Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate is a 
metabolite produced in glycolysis and is used in many different pathways, for instance in the pentose 
phosphate pathway where 5-phospho-alpha-D-ribose 1-diphosphate (PRPP) is produced and used in 
essential pathways. With glucose as a carbon source it is beneficial for the cell to go through glycolysis 
(ATP is produced). With pyruvate or acetate as carbon sources it is necessary to go through 
gluconeogenesis, which is basically going the other way in glycolysis, in order to produce 
glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate. This costs the cell energy and perhaps the glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate 
produced is only used in growth dependent reactions. Although carotenoids have beneficial roles in 
the cell the bacteria can survive without it, but it is probably more fragile than usual.  
When grown on starch, weak growth was observed in vivo and the colonies were colorless. In 
silico predictions show growth on starch. As discussed in subsection 2.2.14, many different enzymes 
and transporters are involved in starch degradation. Some of these enzymes and/or transporters could 
possibly have low-capacity and/or low-affinity. It would be very interesting to compare genomes of 
multiple R. marinus strains and investigate the presence of the genes encoding for these proteins 
between strains and then compare the results to growth of the strains on starch. It is likely that if the 
cell is not able to degrade and transport sufficient amount of usable sugars into the cell to maintain full 
growth, pathways such as carotenoid biosynthesis will be sacrificed for more essential pathways.  
When L-glutamate or L-aspartate are used as sole carbon source in the model, no growth is 
predict, while experiments in vivo show growth. After investigating this further, it was noticed that in 
these cases the amino acid L-arginine seems to build up and needs to be exported out for the model to 
grow. A BLAST search showed a gene in R. marinus genome annotated as ‘Methylmalonyl-CoA 
mutase’ with high homology to ‘Lysine/arginine/ornithine (LAO/AO) transport ABC system (ArgK)’ 
from several organisms. Majority of the polyamines produced in R. marinus are derived from L-
==PAGE:44==
34 
 
arginine. It is also possible that the L-arginine is converted to polyamines which are then transported 
out of the cell. As mentioned in subsection 2.2.21, no gene is annotated as a polyamine transporter. 
Several ABC transporters show, however, good homology to a polyamine ABC transporter from S. 
ruber. It would be very interesting to analyze if any polyamines or L-arginine are present in the 
medium after growing R. marinus on a glutamate/aspartate medium. 
Experimental data has shown that when cellulose is added to a rich medium, R. marinus can 
break it down (O.H. Fridjonsson, personal communication, June 2015) R. marinus does however not 
grow on minimal medium with cellulose as the sole carbon source in vivo. As discussed in subsection 
2.2.19, the gene encoding for exo-(1,4)-β-ᴅ-glucanase does not appear to be present in the genome. It 
is possible that R. marinus can use a portion of the cellulose as a carbon source, by breaking it down 
with the cellulase. The cellulase breaks the cellulose into very differently sized glucans and the small 
cellobiose units are probably the only ones transported into the cell and used. This could explain why 
cellulose is not usable as a sole carbon source, the cellulase simply can´t provide enough cellobiose 
units to maintain growth. It would be interesting to search for a gene encoding for exo-(1,4)-β-ᴅ-
glucanase in another thermophilic bacterium and clone it into R. marinus and check for growth on 
cellulose. This enzyme has been isolated from Thermotoga sp. [87] and the corresponding gene could 
potentially be used for this purpose. If another R. marinus strain is able to grow on cellulose medium, 
it would also be interesting to compare the two genomes with respect to these genes.  
Growing R. marinus on a medium with the monosaccharide xylose as the sole carbon source, 
did not show dense growth but single colonies. As discussed in subsection 2.2.19, R. marinus can break 
down the polysaccharide xylan into smaller xylo-oligomers by secreting the enzyme xylanase. It is 
possible that the small xylo-oligomers are transported into the cell where they are further degraded 
into xylose monomers. A gene annotated as ‘lipopolysaccharide ABC transporter’, was assigned to the 
transport of xylo-oligomers in the reconstruction. If the oligomers are in fact transported instead of 
monomers, it might explain the dispersed growth on xylose. Maybe the transport of xylose is not active 
enough. It would be interesting to try growth on xylan instead.  
Weak growth has also been reported on alanine and on ornithine, but in silico experiments did 
not predict growth. Alanine could potentially be converted to pyruvate by the enzyme alanine 
transaminase (EC 2.6.1.2). No gene is annotated as this enzyme in R. marinus, but BLAST results, 
comparing this gene from other organisms to the genome of R. marinus, showed 30-50% identity to 
genes annotated as ‘aromatic amino acid aminotransferase’ and ‘aspartate aminotransferase’. Ornithine 
could be converted to citruline by the enzyme ornithine carbamoyltransferase (EC 2.1.3.3), but no gene 
==PAGE:45==
35 
 
has such an annotation in R. marinus. A gene annotated as ‘acetylornithine carbamoyltransferase’ in 
R. marinus, showed 60% identity to the ornithine carbamoyltransferase gene from S. ruber. Gene 
knockout experiments could be performed to test these hypotheses.  
4.3 Gene essentiality 
Gene essentiality was determined by estimating the 
effects of single gene knockouts on the growth rate. Single 
gene deletion and the importance of the GPR rules in that 
respect, are described in subsection 2.2.3. In short, growth 
simulations are performed with FBA, after deleting a single 
gene in the reconstruction, and compared to growth rate of 
a wild-type (WT) strain. The same is repeated for all the 
genes. If a gene deletion caused the growth rate ratio 
between the WT and the mutant to be close to zero (rateWT 
/ ratemutant < 0.001), the gene was defined essential for 
growth. This analysis showed that 40% of the total genes 
in the current reconstruction are essential, when grown on 
minimal medium with glucose. For comparison, the same 
analysis was performed for the T. maritima and E. coli 
models, with resulting gene essentiality ratios of 36% and 15%, respectively (Fig. 8). As mentioned in 
section 4.1, T. maritima has a small genome and, in theory, might therefore contain a higher fraction 
of essential genes than bacteria with larger genomes. The current R. marinus reconstruction mainly 
contains pathways known to be essential for biomass production and well-characterized pathways. By 
including more pathways in the reconstruction, the essentiality ratio is expected to decrease. The 
reason it will probably not end up with lower ratio than the E. coli model is that the latter is extremely 
detailed and the organism has a larger genome than R. marinus.

alanine, aspartate and glutamate pathway does not. Glutamate and aspartate appear in many different 
pathways so alternative ways of synthesizing these amino acids could explain the low essentiality of

One would expect the genes encoding for the TCA cycle and oxidative phosphorylation to be 
highly essential in an aerobic bacterium such as R. marinus, since these pathways are used to produce 
energy. Surprisingly, this is not the case according to this analysis. According to the GPR rules, this 
cannot all be explained by several enzymes with the same functions (isozymes). Genes for two 
enzymes are assigned to the oxygen reductase reaction, explaining why they are not assessed essential 
during a single gene deletion analysis. The other reactions belonging to oxidative phosphorylation do 
not contain isozymes. The growth rate of the mutants with deleted genes in these reactions were 
investigated. The growth rate of these mutants is lower than the wild type (ratio: 0.18-0.89) but is not 
low enough to meet the previously set growth rate ratio criteria (ratio: 0.001). It is possible that the 
model is providing ATP by other routes such as glycolysis and nucleotide pathways, but the ATP 
requirement is high and should require the ATP synthase. Presently, the GAM (see subsection 2.2.23) 
is too low and the value for NGAM has not been obtained, which could contribute to these results. In 
the case of the TCA cycle, the metabolites can often be produced by alternative reactions. For instance, 
oxaloacetate and succinate can be produced from the amino acids L-aspartate and L-glutamate,

Chapter 5. Conclusions 
A genome-scale metabolic reconstruction of the thermophilic bacterium R. marinus, DSM 
4252T was reconstructed and currently contains 422 genes, 491 reactions and 432 metabolites. A 
special focus was on carotenoid production and various saccharide utilizing pathways. Carotenoid 
biosynthetic pathway for R. marinus was proposed and a search for potential genes performed. In order 
to model all the different saccharide utilizing pathways, a thorough literature- and gene search was 
needed. Literature- and gene search for other organism specific pathways was carried out as well, for 
instance for the respiratory chain, central metabolism, polyamines and mannosylglycerate. Due to time 
constraints the fatty acid and lipid pathways were not included in the reconstruction. A biomass 
objective function, where all components of the biomass are included along with their fractional 
compositions, was formulated. The reconstruction process was described in detail, from obtaining a 
draft reconstruction from the Model SEED, to an extensive network curation and model validation.  
The model was used to perform in silico predictions. Qualitative growth predictions on 
different carbon sources were performed and compared to in vivo experimental measurements.  Most 
in silico predictions were in agreement with experimental growth measurements. The model did not 
predict growth on L-aspartate and L-glutamate, which according to experimental data, it should have. 
These results were attributed to accumulation of either L-arginine or polyamines in the model.  The 
model predicted growth on cellulose which was not observed experimentally. This was likely due to a 
missing gene encoding for the enzyme exo-(1,4)-β-ᴅ-glucanase. A gene encoding for cellulase is 
present in the genome and was assigned to a cellulose degradation reaction in the model, which caused 
it to grow on cellulose. Weak growth was observed in vivo on ornithine and L-alanine, but was not 
observed in silico. Possible gene candidates for enzymes that could result in utilization of these carbon 
sources were proposed. 
The model was used to predict gene essentiality. This was performed with a single gene 
deletion analysis, where one gene was deleted at a time and the corresponding effects on growth rate 
observed. This analysis showed that 40% of genes in the model are essential, were deleting them would 
result in, essentially, no growth. The distribution of essential genes between subsystems was also 
considered. Gene essentiality of most subsystems could be explained, except for the TCA cycle and 
oxidative phosphorylation. Most genes in these two subsystems were predicted to be non-essential. 
The growth rate of most of the corresponding mutants was lower than the wild types. Several 
==PAGE:52==
42 
 
explanations for these results were provided, including alternative routes for ATP synthesis and too 
low ATP requirements of the BOF. 
The final in silico experiment performed here, focused on carotenoids. Flux distributions of 
individual reactions in the network were obtained via random sampling and those with high correlation 
to lycopene flux, which is a carotenoid precursor, were analyzed. The results of the analysis indicated 
that no reaction could be altered in attempt to obtain higher yield of lycopene, except for the reactions 
of the terpenoid- and carotenoid pathways. An alternative terpenoid pathway was also added to the 
model, in attempt to obtain higher yield of lycopene. This prediction resulted in a slight increase in 
lycopene flux due to decreased CO2 release.  
During the reconstruction process an idea for an Icelandic neologism, describing a genome-
scale metabolic model, was formed. The author felt that the phrase ‘model’ was not descriptive enough 
for the newly formed in silico R. marinus. Using the model to predict growth and produce metabolites, 
using different kinds of carbon sources and necessary nutrients is essentially a computational reflection 
of (the metabolism of) the organism itself. In Icelandic, the English word ‘organism’ is ‘lífvera’. The 
word is composed of two words, ‘líf’ meaning ‘life’ and ‘vera’ meaning ‘being’ (as in ‘human being’). 
The neologism ‘tölvera’ was formed from the two words ‘töl’ and previously described ‘vera’. ‘Töl’ 
is derived from the word ‘tölva’ meaning ‘computer’, where ‘töl’ refers to the word ‘tala’, which means 
‘number’. Unfortunately, no good English translation of ‘tölvera’ could be proposed.

Chapter 6. Future perspectives 
6.1 ‘Complete’ the reconstruction 
Before considering the prediction possibilities of the metabolic model, it is important to 
complete certain pathways that still remain and to obtain necessary experimental data. The most 
essential pathways that have not yet been added to the reconstruction are the fatty acid and lipid 
pathways. These pathways include a large number of reactions and they can vary a lot between 
organisms, which is why the reconstruction of these pathways should not be rushed. The fatty acid- 
and lipid composition has been described in R. marinus, as discussed in subsection 2.2.23. The 
reconstruction of these pathways was started, but could not be finished in time. Other pathways that 
should be included as well are the lipopolysaccharide-, propanoate-, thiamine-, biotin and aminoacyl 
tRNA pathways.  
The BOF needs to be updated as soon as experimental data for the fractional biomass 
composition becomes available. GAM is a large portion of the BOF and it is therefore very important 
that it reflects the real GAM value of the cell. In the present model the GAM estimate has been obtained 
by computational means and is likely to be too low. By measuring growth rates and uptake rates of a 
well-defined carbon source, and therefore the ATP hydrolysis, it is possible to obtain a better estimate 
of the GAM. The relationship between the growth rate and ATP hydrolysis in the exponential phase is 
linear and the slope represents the GAM. NGAM is represented by an ATP hydrolysis reaction in the 
reconstruction and once NGAM has been estimated, the flux through the reaction should be fixed 
accordingly. The value of NGAM can be calculated from the linear relationship between growth rate 
and ATP hydrolysis and is represented by the intercept. Once these measurements become available 
the GAM and NGAM should be updated. 
Experimental data on the uptake rates of individual carbon sources can be represented in the 
reconstruction by changing the lower bound of the corresponding exchange reactions. Once the 
reconstruction has been updated as described above, quantitative in silico predictions of the growth 
rate on different carbon sources can be performed and compared to experimental data, which is already 
available (B. Reynisson, personal communication, June 2015). Our understanding of the metabolism 
of R. marinus is not complete and the reconstruction consequently needs to be continuously updated 
in line with new experimental data.

6.2 Gene deletion predictions 
A single gene deletion analysis was performed and is described in section 4.3. As mentioned 
there, most genes belonging to the oxidative phosphorylation and the TCA cycle were assessed to be 
non-essential. These results are surprising and should be further investigated. 
Since the gene deletion predictions are based on the BOF it might be interesting to construct 
different versions of it. The function that is currently used includes all cellular components present in 
the cell in a logarithmic phase under known conditions. This includes metabolites that are not necessary 
essential for maintaining growth, for instance carotenoids. A ‘core’ BOF, including only essential 
components, could be formulated and gene deletion analysis performed and the results using core BOF 
and regular BOF compared. Another interesting aspect would be to formulate a BOF reflecting the cell 
under stress and compare the corresponding gene deletions to the others.  
6.3 Adding reactions and testing hypotheses 
In silico predictions using an alternative terpenoid pathway are presented in section 4.4. Adding 
the pathway resulted in a slightly increased flux of the downstream metabolites due to improved carbon 
utilization. More predictions, where heterologous pathways or reactions are added, in order to obtain 
biotechnologically desirable metabolites could be performed. One example of such desirable 
metabolites is isoprenoid-based C2-C5 alcohols which can potentially be used as biofuels. E. coli has 
for instance been metabolically engineered for high-yield production of such an alcohol [90]. After 
designing these pathways and predicting the outcomes, this could be tested in the laboratory.  
Many hypotheses were formed during the reconstruction process as described in chapter 2. In 
short, they included a putative gene responsible for the first step in glycolysis, an alternative enzyme 
responsible for the conversion of glucose-1-phosphate to glucose-6-phosphate, an alternative enzyme 
responsible for the first step in the non-mevalonate terpenoid pathway, a proposed carotenoid pathway 
and the corresponding genes and the accumulation of arginine or polyamines when grown on glutamate 
and aspartate. Some of these hypotheses can be tested fairly easily in the laboratory. Forming and 
testing these kind of hypotheses can contribute to a better annotated genome and our growing 
knowledge of R. marinus.
