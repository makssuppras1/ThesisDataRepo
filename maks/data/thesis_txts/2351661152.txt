Abstract
Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) is the most common form of dementia and exists in
two forms; sporadic and familial. The cause is still unknown for the sporadic
form. The familial form is inherited and is caused by a genetic mutation
in the amyloid precursor protein (APP), presenilin 1 or 2 (PSEN1 or 2), or
an enzyme called β-secretase. The focus of this thesis was to assess neurons
derived from patient-speciﬁc induced pluripotent stem cells containing the
PSEN1 mutation A79V as a disease model for familial AD. Its isogenic con-
trol was used as a negative control.
The pluripotency of the isogenic control was veriﬁed by immunocytochem-
istry (ICC) and NPCs were cultured for both the patient and isogenic control
cell lines. It was found that the NPCs had a high co-expression of SOX1 and
NESTIN from the ﬂow cytometry, but lost the co-expression of the NPC
markers as they were passaged. The same tendency appeared when labelling
PAX6 and OTX2 by ICC. The NPCs were terminally diﬀerentiated into neu-
rons and these were characterised using ICC and qPCR. It was found that
most of the neurons were glutamatergic neurons as expected. Based on the
appearance of neuronal rosettes in the ICC results, it appeared that the iso-
genic control was less mature than the patient cell line. This could however
not be resolved with the qPCR results. Endpoint analysis of the neurons
showed an increase in both Aβ40 and Aβ42 compared to the isogenic con-
trol, indicating a hyperactive γ-secretase, where PSEN1 is a subunit. No
diﬀerence in mitochondrial function or localisation between A79V and its
isogenic control was present. Based on the initial results it was assessed that
A79V is a suitable disease model but further optimisations are required to
establish a reliable and fully mature cell line as a disease model.

Introduction
Dementia is a world wide problem, and is not a single disease, but an overall
term describing speciﬁc symptoms including a decline in memory, language,
reasoning, planning, recognition, and identiﬁcation of people or obejcts [Ko-
rolev, 2014]. Several types of dementia exist, here amongst are, frontotempo-
ral dementia, vascular dementia, and Alzheimer’s Disease (AD), which can
be caused by infections, intoxications, tumors, and head trauma, or neuro-
generation [Fago, 2001]. Some of these causes are treatable, but neurode-
generation, the most common cause for dementia is non-treatable [Korolev,
2014].
According to Alzheimer’s Association [2015], AD is responsible for the vast
majority of all dementia cases accounting for approximately 60- 80% of all
cases. An estimated 46.8 million people were worldwide aﬀected by AD in
2015, which is expected to almost triple by 2050 according to Alzheimer’s
Disease International [Prince et al., 2015].
Two diﬀerent types of Alzheimer’s Disease exist; sporadic and familial, where
the sporadic form is the most common. The sporadic form of AD is usually
recognized as having a late onset (> 65 years) and is the cause of approx-
imately 95 % of the cases [Korolev, 2014]. The familial form is inherited
and is caused by a genetic mutation in the amyloid precursor protein (APP),
presenillin 1 or 2 (PSEN1 or 2), or an enzyme called the β-secretase [Wiley
et al., 2005]. These are all involved in the production of Aβ. Regarding the

Alzheimer’s Disease
Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder ﬁrst
described in 1907 by dr. Alois Alzheimer, where he described amyloid β
(Aβ) plaques and neuro ﬁbillary tangles (NFTs) in a post mortem brain
from the dementia diseased patient Auguste Deter [Kumar and Singh, 2015].
In ﬁgure 1.1, the plaques and NFTs can be seen in a silverstained brain slice.
Neuroﬁbrilary tangles are composed of hyperphosphorylated Tau (P-Tau)

When examining the post mortem brain a clear loss of weight and volume,
due to atrophy, is visible in the diseased brain [Rusinek et al., 1991]. Early
in the disease, the areas ﬁrst aﬀected are the the entorhinal cortex and hip-
pocampus, which are responsible for memory and other cognitive functions
[Mann, 1996; Korolev, 2014].

1.1.1
Diagnosis and Treatment Options
Today, the only deﬁnitive way of diagnosing a patient with AD is by visu-
ally examining the post mortem brain [Norfray and Provenzale, 2004], but
recently MRI and PET scans are also being used to measure the degree of
atrophy in the brain to help diagnose the disease [Hwang et al., 2015]. A
downside with these methods is however, that the disease has to be in an ad-
vanced stage before it can be detected in the scans [Kumar and Reddy, 2016].
Several studies have been conducted to determine early disease biomarkers
in cerebrospinal ﬂuid (CSF), such as Aβ42 and P-Tau, but there is still not a
clear correlation between disease progression and biomarkers present in the
CSF [Kumar and Reddy, 2016; Forlenza et al., 2015; Leung et al., 2015].
Currently there is no cure or preventive treatment for the disease, and the
only treatment available is symptom management, such as acetylcholinesterase
inhibitors and glutamate receptor antagonists [Howard et al., 2012; Veronese
et al., 2016; Korolev, 2014]. Acetyalcholinesterase inhibitors help to increase
memory function by decreasing the breakdown of acetylcholine, an impor-
tant neurotransmitter for memory [Korolev, 2014; McGleenon et al., 1999].
Glutamate receptor antagonsits functions by decreasing the levels of the neu-
rotransmitter glutamate at the synapses, which is important for learning and
memory [Reisberg et al., 2003].

1.1.2
Alzheimer’s Disease Mechanism
AD is a multifactorial disease where the root cause is still unknown [Huang
and Mucke, 2012].
Diﬀerent hypotheses for the pathology of the disease
although exist. Amongst the most recognised are the amyloid cascade hy-
pothesis and the tau hypothesis, but Aβ plaques and NFTs are not present
until late in the disease progression, and therefore other hypotheses, which
can lead to the culmination of Aβ plaques and NFT pathology, are gaining
popularity [Lee et al., 2010b; Mufson et al., 2016]. In the following some of
the diﬀerent hypotheses will be investigated.
The Tau Hypothesis
states that protein Tau (tubulin-associated unit)
gets hyperphosphorylated in AD. Tau is an abundant protein mainly found
in the neurons and has been shown to promote microtubule assembly [Bukar
Maina et al., 2016; Weingarten et al., 1975]. Lindwall and Cole [1984] found
that the phosphorylation of Tau aﬀects its ability to promote microtubule
assembly, thereby causing the microtubules to destabilise. Microtubules are
important for axonal transport of diﬀerent organelles and proteins in the cell.
Since the hyperphosphorylation of Tau has been shown to precede the tangles
in AD, it seems to be the hyperphosphorylation that leads to the disassembly
of the microtubules and not the protein aggregation [K¨opke et al., 1993].
Furthermore, Aβ42 has been shown to augment Tau hyperphosphorylation
and decreases levels of microtubule-bound Tau further connecting the two
mechanisms [Ando et al., 2016].

Amyloid Cascade Hypothesis
concerns the accumulation and aggrega-
tion of Aβ in the brain. This accumulation is due to an imbalance between
the activity of α-secretase and BACE1 (β-secretase), see Figure 1.2. In AD
the β-secretase is favored leading to an overproduction of Aβ, especially Aβ42
[Kumar and Singh, 2015]. As mentioned earlier, the cause is not known for
the sporadic forms of AD, but over 20 genetic loci have been implicated in
late onset AD by genome wide association studies [Ridge et al., 2016]. In
the familial form several mutations are known in three distinct genes; the
APP, γ-secretase, or β-secretase [Mufson et al., 2016]. Both β-secretase and
γ-secretase are enzymes important for the production of Aβ. APP can be
processed in two distinct pathways; the non-amyloidogenic pathway and the
amyloidogenic pathway, see ﬁgure 1.2 [Tan et al., 1998].

in AD diseased people there is a skewed ratio between the production and clearance
of Aβ. Figure adapted from [Zolezzi et al., 2014].
The non-amyloidogenic pathway leads to the production of a p3 peptide and
occurs when APP is cleaved by α-secretase and subsequently by γ-secretase
[Mohamed et al., 2016]. In the amyloidogenic pathway, APP is cleaved by
β-secretase and subsequently γ-secretase, and it is in this process that an

The role of γ-secretase in Early Onset Familial Alzheimer’s
Disease
γ-secretase is an aspartyl-protease and consists of four subunits; PSEN1
or PSEN2, Nicastrin (Nct), Presenilin Enhancer 2 (Pen-2), and anterior
pharynx-defective 1 (APH-1) [Li et al., 2013]. Nct is involved in substrate
recognition, APH-1 helps form a scaﬀold, and Pen-2 is important for en-
zyme maturation [Carroll and Li, 2016]. These proteins are less studied, and
therefore the exact role of these subunits is not known [Carroll and Li, 2016].
PSEN1/2 contains the catalytic center of the enzyme [Li et al., 2013], but in
the following only PSEN1 will be considered due to the mutations available
in the project. PSEN1 contains nine transmembrane helixes and the catalyt-
ical aspartyl residues lie between the transmembranal domain 6 and 7, see
ﬁgure 1.3 [Carroll and Li, 2016].
Diﬀerent mutations can occur in this protein, some have been identiﬁed as
pathogenic or non-pathogenic and for some mutations it is still unclear which
eﬀect they have, as seen in ﬁgure 1.3 [alzforum.org]. The focus in this report
will be in the mutation A79V. A79V is a missense mutation and patients
with this mutation have been shown to have a mean onset of 59.3 years of
age [Kumar-Singh et al., 2006; Kauwe et al., 2007]. As seen from Figure 1.3,
the mutation lies outside the membrane in the cytosol. In one study the
carrier of this mutation was still undiseased, but showed increased levels of
Aβ42 and an increased Aβ42/Aβ40 ratio when examining cerebral spinal
ﬂuid from the patient [Kauwe et al., 2007].

Neurons
Neurons are the information processing cells in the brain, and are responsi-
ble for thoughts, feelings, sensations, and actions that mammals experience
every day [Stiles and Jernigan, 2010]. They are highly polarized structures
comprised of a cell body, dendrites and axons, see ﬁgure 1.4 [Stiles and Jerni-
gan, 2010; Hirokawa et al., 2010].
Figure 1.4: The structure of a neuron. Figure from [Stiles and Jernigan, 2010]
The precursor of all cells in the central nervous system (CNS) are neural pro-
genitor cells (NPCs), which are derived from stem cells, and these comprise
a pool of proliferative cells in the brain. Later in the developmental process
they will stop proliferating and diﬀerentiate into either astrocytes, oligoden-
drocytes, or neurons [Okabe et al., 1996]. In the following the development
of neurons will be described and what aﬀect AD has on the mature neurons.

Neural Development in the Human Brain
The process of going from stem cell to NPC is dependent on many diﬀerent
factors. Certain pathways are essential for the neural induction of stem cells
to produce NPCs and later to develop into mature neurons, which will be
discussed in the following.
The production of neurons is called neurogenesis and the most widely used
hypothesis for neurogenesis is the “Default Model”, see ﬁgure 1.5 [Mu˜noz-
Sanju´an and Brivanlou, 2002].

with a neuronal fate - the BMP-pathway and the TGF-β-pathway. In the ﬁgure it
can be seen how the binding of BMP to its receptor can initiate a signalling cascade
leading to the transcription of epidermal genes. In a similar manner when TGF-β
binds to its receptor the transcription of mesodermal genes occurs. The transcrip-
tion of these genes inhibits the transcription of neuronal genes, and therefore it is
essential to inhibit the BMP- and TGF-β-pathways. Figure adapted from [Mu˜noz-

are a family of signal transducers and transcriptional modulators and me-
diates the signalling of both the TGF-β- and the BMP-pathway [Rahman
et al., 2015].
1.2.2
Inﬂuence by Alzheimer’s Disease on Neurons
As mentioned in Section 1.1 AD is a neurodegenerative disease, which causes
neuronal death and damage causing a severe decrease of neuronal number in
the cortex [Arendt et al., 2015]. The neurons mainly aﬀected in the early
stages of AD are the hippocampal and entorhinal neurons and at a later stage
the cortical neurons, see Section 1.1. Diﬀerent cellular hallmarks are known
for the disease such as an increased Aβ42:40 ratio, leading to Aβ plaques,
and the hyperphosphorylation of Tau, leading to NFTs, see Section 1.1. But
other disease related phenotypes are also present in the neurons especially
mitochondrial dysfunction and impaired axonal transport [Ye et al., 2012].
Mitochondria are essential organelles for the neurons to obtain the energy
needed to sustain synaptic activity and neuronal health [Piaceri et al., 2012].
Therefore mitochondrial dysfunction can lead to neuronal damage and death.

1.3
Stem cells
Two types of stem cells exist; totipotent stem cells and pluripotent stem
cells. The totipotent stem cell can develop into all cell types of the organ-
ism including extra-embryonic tissues (the tissue that will form the placenta)
[Dang-Nguyen and Torres-Padilla, 2015]. Totipotent stem cells are found in
the developing zygote, but as this develops into a blastocyte, the stem cells
lose their totipotent status and instead become pluripotent, see ﬁgure 1.7
[Dang-Nguyen and Torres-Padilla, 2015].
Pluripotent stem cells have the
ability to develop into all three germ layers, the endo-, meso-, and ectoderm,
and thereby form all tissues of the developing embryo [Dang-Nguyen and
Torres-Padilla, 2015].
Diﬀerent transcription factors are required to maintain pluripotency in both
early embryos and embryonic stem cells [Takahashi and Yamanaka, 2006].
Here amongst are Oct3/4, Sox2, and NANOG [Loh et al., 2006; Fong et al.,
2008; Masui et al., 2007]. Pluripotent stem cells were ﬁrst isolated in 1981
by Evans and Kaufman [1981] and Martin [1981] from the inner cell mass
of a murine blastocyst, see Figure 1.7. Since the ﬁrst isolation of stem cells
it has been tried to generate pluripotent stem cells from somatic cells, and
it has been shown that by transferring the nucleus from a somatic cell into
an enucleated oocyte, a technique called somatic cell nuclear transfer, it is
possible to generate cells of embryonic stem cell nature [Campbell et al.,
1996].
Another way of producing stem cell-like cells are by fusion of so-
matic ﬁbroblasts with embryonic stem cells [Cowan et al., 2005]. Based on

the so called Yamanaka factors: Oct4, Sox2, c-Myc, and Klf4.

group showed in 2007 that adult human ﬁbroblasts can be reprogrammed by
the same method [Takahashi et al., 2007], which have further increased the

1.3.3
Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells as Patient De-
rived Disease Model for AD
The current disease models used are mainly transgenic animal models. To
be able to see any of the phenotypic traits of AD it is necessary to intro-
duce several diﬀerent human mutations and still the mice do not exhibit all
phenotypic traits such as NFTs even though they have hyperphosphorylated
Tau [LaFerla and Green, 2012].
In humans only one mutation is necessary to give rise to AD. Therefore, it
is advantageous to ﬁnd a more suitable disease model.
As mentioned, it is possible to reprogram somatic cells into iPS cells. This has
given the opportunity to achieve patient-speciﬁc disease models and thereby
the ability to study speciﬁc mutations. A downside for using iPS cell derived

has been generated by the gene-editing technology CRISPR/Cas9. In this

mutated cell line, can be obtained. By utilising CRISPR/Cas9 it is possible
to correct the mutations in the patient-derived cells, and thereby achieving
patient-speciﬁc wild type cells. This gives the possibility to see disease rele-

the genetic and epigenetic background noise resulting from diﬀerent individ-

Creation of Isogenic Controls Using CRISPR/Cas9
Clustered Regular Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats/CRISPR associ-
ated 9 (CRISPR/Cas9) is part of the adaptive immune system of bacteria
but was adapted to the eukaryotic cell, where the most utilised type is the
type II A CRISPR/Cas9 system from Streptococcus Pyrogenes [Jinek et al.,
2013; Shui et al., 2016].
In bacteria, CRISPR loci obtain short pieces of
foreign DNA (i.e. viral DNA), called spacers, and integrate them into the
bacterial genome. This repeated sequence is then transcribed into a single
CRISPR RNA strand (crRNA), which can be bound by Cas9. Cas9 is an

endonuclease, which by the help of the crRNA can introduce doublestranded
breaks (DSBs) into the genome. Furthermore, the CRISPR locus contains
a trans-activating crRNA (tracrRNA). crRNA and tracrRNA function as
guide RNA for the Cas9 [Zhang et al., 2014]. In genome editing however,
the mechanism has been simpliﬁed so the crRNA and the tracrRNA have

containing the HDR template DNA can be utilised to obtain a wild type
sequence [Grobarczyk et al., 2015].

Culturing of iPSCs, NPCs, and neurons
Cells can be used to study disease phenotypes and mechanisms in vitro, but
it is necessary to culture them under the correct conditions. For mammalian
cells it is necessary to make the conditions similar to the conditions in the
organism. Therefore, it is important to maintain a temperature of 37 ◦C
and a CO2 level of 5% [Shi et al., 2012a; Takahashi and Yamanaka, 2006].

such as bacteria and mycoplasma [Small et al., 1986; Todaro and Green,

growing cells such as iPS cells, NPCs and neurons. It has been shown that
components such as selenium, transferrin, L-ascorbic acid, insulin, FGF2,
TGFβ and NaHCO3 are important for the maintenance of pluripotency, pro-
liferation, and sustained culture expansion of iPS cells [Chen et al., 2011].

3.2
Immunocytochemistry
Immunocytochemistry (ICC) is a method used to label diﬀerent proteins.
These can be intracellular, e.g. transcription factors, or extracellular, e.g.
membrane bound proteins, and can be speciﬁc for e.g. pluripotency or cel-
lular identity. The principle behind the method is that there is a primary
capture antibody, which binds to the target and hereafter the primary an-
tibody is visualized with a secondary detection antibody conjugated to a
ﬂuorochrome speciﬁc to the primary antibody. When performing ICC it is
important to ensure that the antibodies are used at the proper concentra-
tion to obtain an optimal signal with little background. Furthermore, it is
important to use antibodies, which are speciﬁc towards the target proteins.
Furthermore, it is important when staining for intracellular markers to per-
meabilize the cells to enable the antibodies to enter the nucleus. ICC can
be used to determine whether the reprogrammed ﬁbroblasts are pluripotent,
where known pluripotent markers, such as NANOG, OCT4, SSEA3, SSEA4,
Tra-1-60, and Tra-1-81, can be labelled [Brimble et al., 2007; Schopperle and
DeWolf, 2006; Loh et al., 2006]. ICC can furthermore be used to characterise
NPCs and neurons. For NPCs the relevant markers are PAX6, PAX6, and
NESTIN. OTX2 and PAX6 are transcriptions factors (TFs) and are speciﬁc
forebrain markers, where NESTIN, is a marker for undiﬀerentiated neural
progenitor cells [Osumi, 2001; Suzuki et al., 2010; Larsen et al., 2010]. Rel-
evant markers for neurons are the neuron specifc markers TUJ1 (class III
β-tubulin), MAP2, and Tau. Moreover speciﬁc neuron-type markers could
be labelled to determine the proportion of diﬀerent neurons. Of these could
be vGlut1 (glutamatergic neurons), GFAP (astrocytes), GABA (GABAergic
neurons), or TBR1 (inner cortical layer) [Wojcik et al., 2004; T¨urkmen et al.,
2008; Chaudhry et al., 1998; Shi et al., 2012b; Gusel’nikova and Korzhevskiy,
2015].

3.3
Determination of NPC Quality using Flow
Cytometry
When diﬀerentiating iPS cells to obtain NPCs there will always be a propor-
tion of the cells that have obtained a diﬀerent fate than neural. The amount
of cells which have obtained the neural fate can be determined by labelling
known NPC markers and count the positive cells. An easy way to do this is
by ﬂow cytometry (FC).
FC is a well-established method for characterising cell populations and is
based on the scattering of light. The sample is injected into the ﬂow cytome-
ter through a nozzle, which directs the particles in the sample to go into the
ﬂow cell [Brown and Wittwer, 2000]. The light scatter is then measured, and
the cells can be separated based on forward scatter (FSC), which is propor-
tional to cell size, and side scatter (SSC), which is proportional to internal
complexity or granularity [Brown and Wittwer, 2000]. It is also possible to
characterize the cells based on intracellular or membrane-bound proteins. By
labelling the target proteins with ﬂourochrome-conjugated antibodies, it is
possible to determine how large a proportion of the cells that have the desired
proﬁle. When characterising NPCs it could be favorable to label Nestin and
Sox1 since these are known NPC markers [Venere et al., 2012]. By labelling
these two markers it will be possible to determine how large of a proportion
of the cells are suitable for terminal diﬀerentiation into neurons. To ensure
an even distribution of neurons the proportion of double positive cells should
be above 80 %.

3.4
Neuronal Characterisation Using qPCR
When characterising the neurons, it can be done by labelling, as mentioned
in Section 3.2, but this can be inaccurate as the labelling antibodies only
detect the expressed proteins and are sensitive towards ﬁxation and perme-
abilisation. Therefore, it can be advantageous to look at the gene expression
by investigating the messenger RNA (mRNA) in the cells. This can be ob-
tained through quantitative Polymerase Chain Reaction (qPCR). qPCR is a
method routinely used to study gene expression in a sample. It is based on
the same principle as PCR, but in qPCR, the ampliﬁed DNA is detected as
the reaction progresses in real time due to nucleoside triphosphates conju-
gated to a ﬂuorescent dye such as SYBR Green [Schmittgen and Livak, 2008].
mRNA is transcribed into cDNA using reverse transcriptase, and the cDNA
is used for the PCR reaction. It is important to eliminate any genomic DNA
from the sample to ensure only cDNA is transcribed. As with classical PCR,
the qPCR is performed in three steps; denaturing, annealing, and elongation.
Pre-designed primers are used to target speciﬁc genes.
The transcript is detected and a Ct value is obtained. The Ct value is the
PCR cycle number in which the ﬂuorescence exceeds the limit of quantiﬁca-
tion [Schmittgen and Livak, 2008]. Therefore, the higher Ct value the lower
amount of transcript is present. The relative amount of transcript can be
quantitﬁed, see equation 3.1.
Relative Quantity = 2Ct, highest−Ct, sample
(3.1)
Target genes could be the neuron speciﬁc MAP2, TUBB3 (class III β-
tubulin), and BLBP [Feng et al., 1994]. Other genes could be AQP4 (as-
trocyte speciﬁc), GFAP (astrocytes speciﬁc), vGLUT1, VGAT (GABAergic
neurons), CHAT (cholinergic neurons), or the NPC speciﬁc genes such as
FOXG1 (forebrain marker), NESTIN, SOX2, PAX6, and TBR2 (cortical
layer) [Satoh et al., 2007; Manuel et al., 2010; Chaudhry et al., 1998; En-

3.5
Using Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent As-
say for investigating Aβ42 and Aβ40
As described in Section 1.1.2 an increase in the Aβ42:Aβ40 ratio has been
shown in this mutation earlier [Kauwe et al., 2007]. To test if this increase is
also present in the cell lines used in this project a way to detect and quantify
Aβ40 and Aβ42 is needed.
For this the Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent
Assay (ELISA) can be used.
An ELISA is a good way of detecting and quantifying a speciﬁc antigen.
Diﬀerent ELISAs exist, and a sandwich ELISA is used in this study.
In
a sandwich ELISA the antigen is captured by an antibody conjugated to
the well and thereafter visualised by a detection antibody. The detection
antibody can be conjugated to horseradish peroxidase (HRP), which cleaves
the substrate 3,3’,5,5’-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) to create a blue colour.
Addition of the stop reagent will stabilise the colour development and turn
the reaction mix yellow. This can be quantiﬁed using a spectrophotometer.
The colour development depends on the HRP-level in the sample. Due to
this correlation between colour development and sample antigen it is possible
to calculate the sample antigen concentration by using a standard curve
created from a known standard dilution. An important notion which has
to be considered when utilising the ELISA for a sample is the sensitivity of
the assay. As it is expected for the Aβ42 concentration to be low in the cell
culture (in the pg/mL range [Kauwe et al., 2007]) it is necessary to have a
high sensitivity assay to ensure all true positives are found. Selectivity is
also important to ensure only true positives are detected.

3.6
Detection of autophagy-marker LC3 us-
ing Western Blotting
To detect autophagy markers in a sample, it is possible to use Western Blot.
Western Blotting is a widely used technique within molecular biology and
can be used as a semi-quantitative assay as it is possible to compare protein
content within diﬀerent samples [Mahmood and Yang, 2012]. The principle
behind Western Blotting is to separate proteins by molecular weight by gel
electrophoresis, e.g. SDS-PAGE, and thereafter make a print of the protein
bands on a membrane ﬁlter, where the proteins of interest can be labelled.
First, the membrane ﬁlter is incubated with primary antibodies against the
target protein, whereafter the membrane is incubated with a secondary an-
tibody targetting the isotype and species of the primary antibody. The sec-
ondary antibody can be conjugated to a ﬂuorochrome, which can be detected
using ﬂuorescence. Western Blot is a simple and quick way to asses autophagy
in the cells and can be a helpful tool to see the momentarily autophagy level
of the cells. As mention earlier, LC3-I and LC3-II are autophagy markers.
LC3-I has a molecular weight of approximately 18 kDa whereas LC3-II has
a molecular weight of approximately 16 kDa [G´omez-S´anchez et al., 2015].
Therefore, it is possible to separate these by electrophoresis and visualise
these. Since it is only the momentarily autophagy level that is measured it is
important to ensure that the neurons are mature to have a high protein level.
To further stress the cells and induce autophagy, the cells can be treated with
rapamycin, an autophagy inducer, and chloroquine, an autophagy inhibitor,
which results in the accumulation of autophagolysosomes in the cells [Sarkar
et al., 2009; Geng et al., 2010].

In AD there can be mitochondrial dysfunction, as mentioned in Section 1.2.2.
Therefore, it would be advantageous to assess the mitochondrial function in
the patient-derived neurons and isogenic control. This is possible by studying
the respiratory function of the mitochondria. A way to do this is by utilising
a Seahorse Bioscience XF Cell Mito Stress Test. By measuring the oxygen
and the pH in the culture it is possible to determine the basal respiration of
the cells, and thereby the function of the mitochondria. By adding diﬀerent
compounds, it is possible to asses the maximum respiration of the mitochon-
dria and the ATP production, see Figure 3.1.

membrane. Lastly, two inhibitors are added, rotenone, a complex I inhibitor, and
antimycin A, a complex III inhibitor. Figure from agilent.com

3.8
Localisation of Mitochondria using Mito-
tracker
R
⃝
As described in Section 1.1.2, the microtubules in the cells of AD patients
are disassembled due to the aggregation of p-Tau. Therefore, it would be
interesting to see if there is a diﬀerence between the localisation of the mito-
chondria in the patient cells compared to the isogenic control. This is done
by Mitotracker R
⃝.
Mitotracker R
⃝is a staining method where the mitochondria of live cells can
be stained and thereby localised.
Mitotracker R
⃝Red CMXRos is a cell-
permeable probe that can diﬀuse through the plasma membrane of the cells
and can accumulate in the mitochondria.
The probe accumulates in the
mitochondria via irreversible binding to the mitochondrial membrane by a
thiol-reactive chloromethyl moiety [Jogdand et al., 2012]. The cells can af-
terwards be ﬁxed and permeabilised if necessary and further stainings can
be performed [thermoﬁsher.com].
3.8.1
Materials
Cells were grown and matured on 0.17 µm coverslips. Cells were washed
once with RT DMEM/F12 and incubated for 15 min at 37 ◦C with 100
nM Mitotracker R
⃝Red CMXRos in DMEM/F12. The cells were ﬁxed by
incubating for 20 min at 37 ◦C in 4 % PFA in PBS. Cells were permeabilised
using 0.5 % Triton-X in PBS and incubated for 15 min at RT. Hereafter, the
nuclei were stained using 10 µg/mL Hoechst solution. In between steps the
cells were washed 3 times with PBS. Hereafter, the coverlips were mounted
onto glass slides using Dako Fluorescent Mounting Medium and sealed with
see-through nail polish.
Mitochondria were visualised with a laser scanning confocal microscope (LSM
710, Imager.Z2, Zeiss, Germany).

CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
Chapter 5
Discussion
The aim of this project was to assess the suitability of neurons diﬀerenti-
ated from patient-derived iPS cells containing the mutation A79V in PSEN1
causing familial Alzheimer’s Disease.
An increase in Aβ42:Aβ40 ratio in the cerebrospinal ﬂuid of an AD patient
with this speciﬁc mutation has been shown previously [Kauwe et al., 2007],
therefore, an increase in the ratio between the two amyloid species was ex-
pected. The cell line A79V showed an increase in both Aβ40 and Aβ42,
but there was no diﬀerence in the ratios between the patient line and the
isogenic control. This could be an indication of the PSEN1 mutation leading
to a hyperactive γ-secretase. The mutation is not located in the catalytic
center of the protein, but in the cytosolic tail, see Section 1.1.2 Figure 1.3.
The overactivation of γ-secretase could be caused by diﬀerent things such
as an hyperactive catalytic center or a higher aﬃnity towards the amylodi-
ogenic pathway. Since A79V is not located near the catalytic center of the
protein, my hypothesis is that the mutation might change the conformation
of the protein and thereby causing a higher aﬃnity towards the amyloido-
genic pathway due to a change in the interaction between the subunits of
γ-secretase. The subunit Nicastrin is believed to be involved in substrate
recognition, as mentioned in Section 1.1.2, and if PSEN1 aﬀects Nicastrin
due to the mutation, this could cause a higher aﬃnity towards the substrate.
It has however not been possible to ﬁnd research supporting this hypothesis,

and it should therefore be further tested. Other familial AD mutations have
been shown to cause a conformational change of PSEN1 to yield a change
in the interaction between APP and PSEN1 [Berezovska et al., 2005]. These
were however located nearer the catalytic center of the protein (D257A and
D385A).
It has been shown in mice that the exretion of inﬂammatory cytokines, due to
neuroinﬂammation, lead to an increase of β-secretase and APP in activated
astrocytes, causing these to produce more Aβ [Zhao et al., 2011]. Since more
astrocytes were present in the patient line, based on the qPCR results, it
would be interesting to determine the amount of Aβ produced by the astro-
cytes. Moreover, it would be interesting to determine if more inﬂammatory
cytokines were excreted by the patient line compared to its isogenic control.
As mentioned in Section 4.3, it was not possible to obtain a consistent con-
centration between dilutions, which was caused by the matrix eﬀect. This
can occur if the sample is complex and the target protein binds to other
components in the sample. A way to circumvent this is by further diluting
the samples, but this can however be problematic since only small amounts
of Aβ42 are produced by the cells. Therefore, it could be advantageous to
ﬁnd another platform with higher speciﬁcity.
Since an increase in Aβ compared to the isogenic control was detected, it was
believed that this would have an inﬂuence on the mitochondrial function, Aβ
has shown to accumulate in mitochondria, see Section 1.2. But as described
in Section 4.5.1, this was not detected.
The neurons used for the Seahorse analysis were 31 days of age, whereas the
neurons used for the ELISA were 49 days of age. Since there was an age
diﬀerence, it is not possible to directly compare the results, and therefore
it would be very interesting to repeat the analysis to see if the phenotype
occurs at a later timepoint.

disassembly of microtubules. The disassembly of microtubules are caused by
the aggregation of hyperphosphorylated Tau. This was believed to result in

transported to the lysosomes, these fail to acidify and therefore the substrate
cannot be degraded. This will lead to lysosome aggregation in the cells [Lee
et al., 2010a]. It was attempted to investigate this in this project, but the
autophagy induction of the cells failed. It however seemed that chloroquine
induced more cell death in the neurons, which have been shown previously
[Geng et al., 2010]. Due to the lack of time for another terminal diﬀerentia-

interesting to try to induce autophagy in a new batch of mature neurons to
see if they show this phenotype. For the repetition it could be advantageous
to try conduct a titration experiment of both component to ﬁnd the optimal
concentrations.
A general obstacle was, that it was diﬃcult to maintain the neurons in cul-
ture for a longer period of time. They were prone to detach from the culture
plate due to continued proliferation. This was especially a concern with the
isogenic control, since this retained its proliferative property for longer. This
made it diﬃcult to obtain fully mature neurons. The culture protocol used
was directly transferred from another cell line containing a mutation which
causes frontotemporal dementia. Therefore, the culture protocol will need to
be further optimised for the Alzheimer’s lines.
Though the A79V GC cell line seemed more immature when analysing the
immunocytochemistry data, this was not clear from the qPCR data. Here
there was no diﬀerence in expression of the NPC markers.

CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
Chapter 6
Conclusion
In conclusion, it is assessed that the cell line A79V is a suitable disease model
for familial Alzheimer’s Disease. It was possible to obtain high quality neural
progenitor cells from the patient cell line A79V and from the isogenic control
A79V GC, and it was possible to obtain neurons from these cells, conﬁrmed
by immunocytochemistry and qPCR. The culture protocol however needs to
be optimised to ensure a similar maturation stage between the two cell lines.
From the endpoint analyses it can be concluded that there was no diﬀerence
in mitochondrial function nor localisation between A79V and A79V GC.
This could both be due to diﬀerent maturation stages of the cells or that
the mutation A79V does not aﬀect the mitochondria. It was not possible
to assess the autophagic state of the neurons, since the autophagy inducing
treatment did not work.
Furthermore, it can be concluded that there was a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in
Aβ40 and Aβ42 level in the patient line compared to the isogenic control but
no diﬀerence in Aβ42:Aβ40 ratios.
It can be concluded that the isogenic control is a suitable negative control,
since it is possible to distinguish between the diﬀerences in phenotypes caused
by the mutation.

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is a progressive and irreversible neurodegenerative disease causing neural cell degen-
eration and brain atrophy and is considered to be the most common form of dementia. We previously generated
an induced pluripotent stem cell (iPSC) line from an AD patient carrying an A79V mutation in PSEN1 as an in vitro
disease model. Here we generated a gene-corrected version from this hiPSC line by substituting the point muta-
tion with the wild-type sequence. The reported A79V-GC-iPSCs line is a very useful resource in combination with
the A79V-iPSC line in order to study pathological cellular phenotypes related to this particular mutation.

The study was approved by the “De Videnskabsetiske Komiteer for
Region Hovedstaden” (protocol number H-4-2011-157), Copenhagen,
Denmark and written informed consent was obtained in all cases. To
protect the patient family privacy, no personal patient information is
presented here. Mutations in presenilin 1 (PSEN1) gene are the most
common known causes of inherited Alzheimer's disease (AD).
A human induced pluripotent stem cell (hiPSC) line was previously
generated from a skin biopsy obtained from a 48-year-old woman car-
rying a heterozygous mutation in exon 4 of the PSEN1 gene, which
causes a change in amino acid A79V. Episomal plasmids carrying gene
sequences for hOCT4, hSOX2, hKLF4, hL-MYC, hLIN28 and a short hairpin
against TP53 (Okita et al., 2011) were used to reprogram the ﬁbroblasts
into iPSCs, successfully establishing integration and feeder-free iPSCs.
This cell line has been described previously as a bona ﬁde iPSC line
with normal karyotype (Li et al., 2016).
The A79V-hiPSC (c.236 C N T) was gene-corrected with the CRISPR/
Cas9 system (Ran et al., 2013) by replacing the point mutation “T”
with the wild-type nucleotide “C” (Fig. 1A). The nucleotide substitu-
tion/correction was validated by sequencing (Fig. 1B). Gene-integrity
was also checked by conﬁrming that the DNA sequence surrounding
the mutation and the CRISPR cutting site were not altered, with no
frameshifts or other mutation(s) being generated upon gene editing,
conﬁrming successful gene correction of A79V-hiPSC, named A79V-
GC-hiPSC (“GC” = gene corrected). The sequencing of the isogenic
line conﬁrmed the c.236 T N C replacement in exon 4 of the PSEN1
