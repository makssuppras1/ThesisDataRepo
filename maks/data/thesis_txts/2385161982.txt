Abstract
Whereas much attention has been devoted to the description of the wind and tur-
bulence conditions over forested areas, few studies have focused on how such highly
turbulent wind ﬁelds aﬀect a wind turbine. This study couples observed wind and
turbulence gradients to the performance of a 2.3MW wind turbine. Multi-level wind
measurements from tall met masts at two forested sites in Sweden, Ryningsnäs and
the conﬁdential Site 2, are analyzed and interpreted. The measured data are used
for deﬁning three turbulence categories observed at the two sites: Category 1, which
describes nearly uniform high turbulence along the vertical direction of the rotor
and Categories 2a and 2b which introduce high turbulence gradients, with the latter
combining also high mean wind shear. The Mann turbulence model parameters,
which have been previously estimated for the Ryningsnäs site, are used for recon-
struction of the observed mean and turbulence wind ﬁeld. A tuning is applied on
the Mann model for reﬂecting the in-homogeneity of shear and turbulence in the
vertical direction. Validation of resulting inﬂow conditions at one of the sites, shows
a good agreement between the modelled wind ﬁeld and the observations. Using a
ﬁctitious turbine model, which imitates the hub-height and rotor diameter of the
wind turbine located at Ryningsnäs, the dynamic response is calculated with HAWC2
aerolastic code and is then used for fatigue assessment of the three turbulence cate-
gories and the observed turbulence gradients. The IEC class A and IEC site speciﬁc
wind models are used as references. The comparison reveals that fatigue loads on
the turbine structure exceed the levels speciﬁed by IEC-A. Further investigation
focused on the lifetime equivalent load given by the probability combination of all
the turbulence gradients observed in the measurements gives up to 35% higher loads
for some of the load signals than IEC-A. It is in general concluded that increasing
hub-height from 80 to 100m, reduces up to 20% the deviation between the combined
turbulence gradient life equivalent load and IEC-A life equivalent load.
Keywords: Loads, forests, canopy, inhomogeneous turbulence, turbulence split,
Ryningsnäs, Mann turbulence model, HAWC2

Learning objectives
The objectives of this Master’s thesis are to:
• Critically evaluate and compare statistical output from wind data analysis for
chosen sites.
• Select and interpret high frequency (20Hz) time series from blocked averaged
time series (10 minute averages).
• Apply model parameters for the Mann model documented in the scientiﬁc
literature Chougule et al. [1]) and use them in the aerolastic HAWC2 code.
• Modify wind input according to data analysis so that the Mann model output
resembles the selected observations.
• Modify HAWC2 model (tower) so that it imitates the wind turbines on site.
• Critically assess the output of HAWC2 load predictions.
• Be familiar with the methods known from the IEC standards ([11], [12]) for
simulation and post-processing of the loads on wind turbine’s structure .
• Create a load map for various combinations of turbulence over the wind tur-
bine’s rotor.
• Provide new insights concerning how turbulence gradients over the rotor aﬀect
the wind turbine loads using HAWC2 and associated tools.

Introduction
As wind energy industry is growing and the size of the wind turbines increased,
developers are exploiting opportunities available onshore, by developing on sites that
were previously considered as not ideal for siting. Public acceptance can inﬂuence
the ﬁnancial perceived risk for a wind farm project. The adverse eﬀects of visual
impact, noise emissions and shadow ﬂickering typically aﬀect fewer people when
wind turbines are located in forested sites. This minimizes ﬁnancial risks by reducing
compensation costs and therefore installation costs.
Except low local impact, wind farms in forested sites are common in countries were
land cover is mainly dominated by forests. In the case of Sweden for example, 53%
of the land is covered by forests.
However, the placement of wind turbines with their tip just above the canopy has
an important impact on the loading applied over the structure. Whereas several
studies have focused on how a typical surface layer at forested sites is modiﬁed
in situations where the atmospheric boundary layer is high and a turbine would
experience nearly homogeneous turbulence conditions over the rotor (Kaimal et al.
[6], Stull [18], Mahrt et al. [17], Moncrieﬀet al. [16]), few – if any - studies have
addressed the situations when the surface layer is shallow and there is a large shear-
and turbulence gradient over the rotor (Figure 1.1).
Previous data analysis has
shown large variations of wind speed and turbulence intensity for increasing height
above the forest (Arnqvist et al.[3]). It is of high interest to examine the response of
wind turbines which undergo conditions where their lower and upper tip experience
diﬀerent levels of turbulence cases. Abnormal behavior of the two wind turbines
sited at one of the sites examined here was observed, with unexpected shutdowns
and high occurrences of pitch were observed.
This project aims to investigate the impact of forest’s boundary layer on wind tur-
bine’s fatigue and lifetime, using observed wind and turbulence gradients. Data
mining in Chapter 2 will introduce the forested sites that are used for exploiting
wind data. Also the ﬁltering of the data for bad and wake aﬀected signal is ap-
pended in the same Chapter. In Chapter 3 the wind climate at the two sites will
be described, by focusing at the turbulence proﬁles observed. Three main loading
categories will be introduced depending on their turbulence proﬁle. During HAWC2
Implementation Chapter, the wind turbine models to be used for the load analysis
will be described. In addition, the generated wind ﬁeld using Mann turbulence box
parameters calculated in Chougule et al. [1] will be tested against the observed wind
ﬁeld for the three loading categories introduced before. Chapter 5, will utilize the
knowledge received about forest boundary layer wind and turbulence properties as

Data Mining
2.1
Sites
The project is focused on the analysis of the load conditions at forested sites. There-
fore, measurement sites with landscape dominated by forest are chosen. This crite-
rion ensures the full adaptation of the boundary layer to the rough forest surface.
Simple orography is the second criterion and is applied in order to ensure that
the results are solely depending on the forested character of the site. Wind ﬂow over
hilly terrains is accompanied with large vertical and lateral wind components.
Finally, a tall mast with multiple measurement heights above the canopy is
required. The utilization of the wind data from a tall mast can lead to the un-
derstanding of the wind vertical proﬁle above dense vegetation, extending from the
canopy height to a modern wind turbine’s high tip.
These encountered, two sites in Sweden, Ryningsnäs and Site 2 were selected for data
analysis. Site 2 will remain anonymous since the owner preferred to not publish the

2.1.1
Ryningsnäs
An 138 m tall tower is located in a clearing at a relatively ﬂat, forested terrain

and West of the tower. The average tree height close to the mast during the cam-
paign period was approximately 20m. The forest cannot be assumed homogeneous,
since the height of the trees is in general varying and is regularly interrupted by
lakes, farmlands and lower roughness clearings (Figure 2.1). Near the mast, the ter-

in Arnqvist et al. report [3].
Two test turbines, Nordex N90, are in the vicinity of the mast.

2.2.1
Data Selection
The aim is to analyze the turbulence conditions at the sites by looking at well de-
ﬁned wind ﬂow data-sets free from any instrument failures. Consequently, the same
selection criteria documented in Arnqvist J. et al. [3], are utilized in the current
project.
Upon initial inspection, clear instrumental errors were detected. A despiking al-
gorithm was applied on both datasets, in order to remove data which could aﬀect
the mean and the standard deviation of the time series. The same algorithm was
responsible for the removal of data associated to instrumental failure during which
the longitudinal component of wind speed was identical for more than ten consecu-
tive recordings. Constant signals were common below freezing conditions or during
windless periods.
Flatness criterion, is also applied to eliminate the signal intermittency and non
stationary data ignored by the despiking algorithm. Data for which Fw =
w′4
w′22 > 10,
are therefore rejected.
To ensure a well deﬁned wind ﬂow a wind speed threshold is used for the sonic
located at h/hc = 2. Data for which the wind speed at this sonic is below the
threshold Uc < 3, are rejected.
The ﬁltered data from the meteorological masts are disturbed when wind is
blowing from certain directions. The disturbed wind ﬂow can be caused either by
the wake of the met mast, provided the boom orientation, or by nearby obstacles,
like the two NORDEX wind turbines at Ryniningsnäs. In order to remove wake
eﬀects, the data were divided into 12 directional sectors, starting with center 0◦,
with an opening of 30◦. Applying the information provided in Table 2.2 which lists
the disturbed wind directions, the respective directional sectors are rejected [11].

3.1.3
Turbulence gradients
One more common characteristic in high roughness sites and shallow boundary layer
cases, is a ﬂow dominated by high turbulence gradients. There are many situations
were the low altitudes experience fully turbulent ﬂow, whereas, far above the forest
the ﬂow is much less turbulent (Figure 3.4). Figure 3.9, illustrates the frequency
of large turbulence gradient occurrences versus height. With increasing distance
from the lower sonic, the occurrences are also increased. Site2, which in general
experiences high gradients more often, approximately 10% of the time experiences
gradients above the ratio of 3.
Another way attempted to examine the turbulence gradients is seen in the density
plots in Figures 3.10, which reveal the most probable turbulence gradients, by com-
paring σhmax and σ40m, for diﬀerent wind speeds of the sonic at h/hc=5. Starting
from U=7ms−1, two diﬀerent clusters of points can be observed. The upper cluster
portrays cases for which turbulence is approximately more that 20% of the mean
wind in the ﬂow for the whole span of the met mast, corresponding to the height
range of RYN_100’s rotor. The lower cluster gathers points with relatively low tur-
bulence. A similar observation was made in Figures 3.6 and 3.7 where turbulence

4
HAWC2 Implementation
Prior to the installation of wind turbines at speciﬁc sites, a site assessment study
should be held. As part of this study, structural analysis questions the agreement
of the structural loads at the site with the IEC standards. Although, in-situ instru-
mentation is available at Ryningsnäs (RYN_80 and RYN_100), the aerolastic code
HAWC2 is used in order to investigate the forest impact on the structural loads. The
code is intended for calculating wind turbine response to external forcing conditions,
which depend mainly on the wind ﬁeld at site [14].
The ﬁrst part of this chapter describes the wind turbine models used for the dynamic
analysis. In the second part, the procedure followed for the wind ﬁeld generation is
explained. Finally the generated wind ﬁeld is compared to the observations on site.
4.1
Wind turbine model
The wind turbine models used in this analysis, imitate the hub-heights and rotor di-
ameter of the two Nordex N-90 turbines located at Ryningsnäs site. As it was stated
also in the Introduction, these turbines were not optimized for forested conditions.
A 2.3MW ﬁctitious wind turbine model is used in this analysis. The characteristics
of the model and original wind turbines are listed in Table 4.1.
It has to be noted that the initial model had a hub-height of 70m and therefore
it was up-scaled using the methodology developed from UPWIND project by P.K.
Chaviaropoulos [15]. The disagreement of the original wind turbine with the model’s
rotor speed range and rated power is expected to introduce diﬀerences between the
magnitude of the original and the simulated loads. Although, the analysis is not
presumed to reﬂect the exact response of the original turbines, it is required to
eliminate further departures introduced by the performance of the model’s controller.
Therefore, a simple wind step condition was investigated in order to verify the
controller performance for the two models and it can be found in the Appendix
(Figure A.4). Modal analysis of the two models can be found in Table A.1.
A performance test of the two models was made with the utilization of the
turbine response, for a 10 minute interval within which the observed load response
was provided by Nordex. The statistics within the 10-min are used in HAWC2 for
simulation of the load.
A close agreement between the simulated and observed
loads was seen. Some vibrations in side-side for tower bottom and edgewise blade
moments in the simulated signal are expected to introduce uncertainties. The load
measurements granted by Nordex are conﬁdential and therefore are presented only

4.2
Generated wind ﬁeld
The statistics of the extracted cases presented in Section 3.2 are used in the aerolastic
code HAWC2 for generating the respective wind ﬁelds. The goal is a close agreement
between simulated wind ﬁeld and observations for trust-able analysis of loads which
will be seen in the next Chapter.
Precise description of the structure of atmospheric turbulence is important for
the calculation of the dynamic loads at the sites. HAWC2 uses Mann turbulent model
for the generation of the turbulence ﬁeld. Mann model calculates the spectral ten-
sor for the atmospheric surface layer turbulence at high wind speeds and simulates
the three dimensional ﬁeld of the wind velocity ﬂuctuations[2]. The model assumes
isotropic turbulent ﬂow meaning that the variance and length scale of turbulence is
identical for all the three directions, u,v,w. In addition the velocity ﬁeld is assumed
to be Gaussian. The modelled spectral tensor is valid in the neutral surface layer
with the assumption of uniform shear dU/dz.
The Mann turbulence box parameters were calculated for three stability classes
from single-point measurements of wind speed for Ryningsnäs by Chougule et al.
[1]. Using the same period analyzed in this project, Chougule used data taken at
sonic h/hc = 4 for multiple wind speed bins. The author of the aforementioned
paper concluded that although the three parameters varied in similar pattern for
height, there was no signiﬁcant variation for diﬀerent wind speed bins.
Mann model parameters, dissipation rate of speciﬁc turbulent kinetic energy,
turbulence length scale and the turbulence anisotropy, which were calculated for
NNS, NNU and N stability are presented in Table 4.2 for the two heights which are
the same as RYN_80 and RYN_100 hub heights. The stability intervals of Monin-
Obukhov length used by Chougule, are focusing only to the near neutral classiﬁcation
due to the neutral surface layer limitation of Mann model. The stability intervals
are the same as before (Table 3.1).

In order to mirror the exact behavior of the observed cases, a tuning is ap-
plied on the Mann turbulent box using user _defined _shear and user _defined
_shear _turbulence option in HAWC2. These options force the vertical variations
of the mean wind ﬁeld and the turbulent ﬂuctuations to match the respective ob-
servations. The tuning equation for mean wind speed is
Ux = Uhub · SFU
and for turbulence
σx = σhub · SFσ
, where x denotes the vertical displacement and hub the sonic lying at hub-
height for the two diﬀerent turbines. The input to HAWC2 are the scaling factors
which are given by the ratios,
SFU = Ux
Uhub
SFσ = σx
σhub
Additionally, for limitation of the stochastic variations of the turbulent box, six
seeds are utilized for each of the cases. The number of points in the Mann box was
set to 16384, for higher resolution.
The simulated wind ﬁelds are cited next to the observed ﬁelds in Figures 3.12 to
3.15. High frequencies are not reﬂected by the simulations. This is due to the coun-
terbalance between computational time and resolution of the turbulence box grid,
which makes it impossible to maintain a ﬁne representation of the high frequencies.
However the high frequencies have limited importance for the turbine load since are
associated with small vortex structures.

with a higher variability for cases taken from Category 1. The small deviations
between observed and simulated wind ﬁeld are introduced by the remaining model’s
stochastic variations.

Results
5.1
Loads
In this chapter, the output of the aerolastic code is presented. The turbine’s load
response is simulated for the three turbulence ﬁeld categories of Table
3.2. The
output frequency of the modelled signals is set to 32Hz, the same frequency as the
measured signal of all the sensors on RYN_80 and RYN_100 [10].
For dynamic characterization of the wind turbines in forested sites, load cases from
the three turbulence categories are extracted for power production operation for
wind speeds 5ms−1 to 13ms−1. Wind speed bins of 1ms−1 are chosen according
to IEC 61400-13 [12]. During steady-state operation, simulations between cut-in
and cut-out wind speeds should be performed. However due to limited availability
above 13ms−1 and bad operation of the turbine model close to cut-in conditions, is
preferred to not evaluate dynamic analysis for these wind speeds. In this project
transient events and parked wind turbine loads are not processed due to poor per-
formance of the model. Six turbulent seeds are used for realizing each 10 minute
ﬁle, for minimization of the stochastic errors.
An algorithm was created for the selection of the cases to be simulated for each
category and wind speed bin (Figure 5.1).
This algorithm is able to locate the
occurrences of the three turbulence categories for each wind speed bin, using the
method provided in Table 3.2. The mean turbulence gradient of each category is
used as the center of a circle of radius 0.27 ms−1. Only occurrences lying within
this circle are considered for each category, because since they are lying closer to the
mean, they are more representative. A case with the highest turbulence at h/hc=4
is extracted per category and wind speed and its characteristics are used for load
simulation. Tables A.2a,A.2b, A.3a list the characteristics of the extracted cases
for each category for the wind speed bins 5ms−1 to 13ms−1. Figure 5.1 shows the
extracted cases for U=7ms−1 at h/hc = 4. The analogous Figures for the rest of the
wind speed bins can be found in the Appendix in Figure A.5.
The three turbulence categories are compared to standard IEC- Class A case
for which TIref = 16% and IEC site speciﬁc turbulence according to [13] for the
same wind speed bins. The IEC turbulence models are listed in Table 5.1 and their
statistics for the chosen wind speed range can be found in Tables A.3b and A.4.

models, it is clear that IEC class A turbulence model underestimates the lifetime
collective load on wind turbines which are subjected to rough turbulence gradient
conditions, as in the case of a forested site like Ryningsnäs. Up to 35% diﬀerence is
observed in the tower bottom fore-aft moment for RYN_80. Trying to describe this
site with an IEC-A wind turbine class could lead to disastrous consequences for the
turbine. However, since nothing is known about the strengths of the wind turbine,
no conclusions regarding the failure of the structure can be made.
The lifetime
equivalent load estimated using the site speciﬁc IEC model for RYN_80, deviates
also from the TGLM model, yet is closer than IEC-A. Although the site speciﬁc IEC
turbulence is used to estimate the 90% quantile of the turbulence standard deviation
at all values of Uhub, it does not encounter the vertical in-homogeneity of turbulence
due to increased roughness.
For increased hub height by 20 m the life collective load for all the signals
is also increased, except the moments associated with tilt and yaw. Higher above
the canopy Uhub = 10ms−1 is more common and therefore the contribution to the
collective load is increased. On the other hand, tilt and yaw depend on wind ﬁeld
variations over the rotor. Since the turbulence gradients are reduced in frequency
and strength by moving away from the forest, RYN_100 will experience weaker
tilt and yaw moments.
Increasing the hub-height of the turbine, results also to
signiﬁcant reduction of the deviations of the IEC-A model from the TGLM model.
Interestingly, the tilt and yaw sensors on the shaft, experience the strongest decrease
of IEC-A and TGLM diﬀerences. The goal is to increase the wind turbine’s hub
height up to the level where the IEC-A model would predict similar loading on the
structure as the TGLM model. For IEC-site the increased hub-height has adverse
eﬀects, since the collective load is higher than TGLM due to the increased turbulence
with height. The bottom line of this analysis, is that increasing the hub-height of the
wind turbine will minimize the extra loading on the structure which is introduced
solely by the turbulence gradients.

6
Conclusion
This study aims to investigate the load response and fatigue on wind turbine struc-
tures located at forested sites, using measurements from tall masts.
Multi-level measurements of the atmospheric wind and turbulence provided
valuable insights in the vertical proﬁle of mean wind speed, turbulence and skew-
ness above forested areas. Despite the signiﬁcant stability dependence seen on the
proﬁles, important vertical variability is seen for all the stability classes. Strong
shear and decay of turbulence with height are some of the over forest wind ﬂow
characteristics.
An interesting behavior of turbulence "splits" is observed in the
ﬂow, as is seen in the turbulence gradient maps, but also in the 20Hz extracted
cases. This indicates the high variability of the surface layer depth in forested sites.
The measurements are classiﬁed into three categories depending on the mean
wind shear and turbulence gradients levels. Interestingly uniform turbulence occurs
only 50% of the time, while an 18% is accredited to turbulence "splits".
Detailed reconstruction of the observed wind and turbulence ﬁeld is done using
Mann turbulence model. The parameters calculated by Chougule et al. are used in
the spectral tensor and reveal the good performance of Mann model above forested
landscape.
Results for the fatigue analysis support that IEC-A class turbines are not de-
signed for forest wind ﬂow conditions. This class underestimates the loads on the
structure of the WT for all the examined signals. More precisely, for tower bottom
fore-aft moment, IEC-A predicts 11% lower lifetime equivalent load than Category 1
and 18% than IEC-site speciﬁc.
Similar results are observed in the utilization of the lifetime equivalent fatigue
load given by the combined probabilities of turbulence gradients observed at Ryn-
ingnäs site. The comparison of the load maps for 10ms−1 of individual signals, to the
lifetime equivalent fatigue load given by IEC-A and IEC- site speciﬁc wind models
for the same signals, demonstrates interesting deviations of the three models. Esti-
mated value for lifetime equivalent load of tower bottom for-aft bending moment, as
is calculated using the turbulence gradient maps, is 9% larger than IEC-site speciﬁc
and 35% larger than IEC-A. The aforementioned deviations decrease to 1% and 17%
respectively, as hub-height increases. These are results for only one wind speed-bin
and therefore, it is not possible to generalize the conclusions for whole wind turbine’s
operational range.
This initial study in this project indicates that a wind turbine in the Scandinavian
pine forest terrain may experience fatigue loading more severe than what is covered
by the current IEC61400-1 wind turbine classes.

It has to be emphasized that the magnitude of the load results contains uncertainties

cases, an algorithm is deﬁned in this project for this purpose. Therefore it is un-
certain whether another methodology would give more representative loads for each
turbulence category.
To gain a more general view of the canopy turbulence and the induced loads on
WT structures, it is necessary to evaluate forested sites with higher wind resource
potential in the future. In addition the Mann model should be validated against
more complex forested terrains.
As it was seen in this project the inhomogeneous turbulence, referred as tur-
bulence "split" has an important impact on wind turbine components which can
also be more severe than the homogeneous turbulence case, especially for higher
wind speeds. Inhomoegeneous turbulence should therefore receive more focus when
estimating the performance of future turbines in forested areas. Consequently it is
essential to develop reference wind models which will be included in the IEC stan-
dards and will encounter turbulence gradients, for more accurate load analysis in
forested sites.
