Introduction and theoretical background
InSAR, or Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar, is a remote sensing technique used to
measure and monitor changes in Earth’s surface with high precision. It combines the princi-
ples of interferometry and synthetic aperture radar (SAR) imaging. By analyzing the phase
differences of radar signals received from the same location at different times, InSAR can
detect subtle deformations and create detailed maps of terrain elevation. It has applications
in Earth sciences, geodesy, and environmental monitoring, helping to study phenomena such
as earthquakes, volcanic activity, land subsidence, and glacier movements. The availability
of satellite-based SAR systems, like the European Space Agency’s (ESA) Sentinel-1, has
greatly expanded the use of InSAR, providing global coverage and frequent data updates for
comprehensive monitoring.
The growing utilization of InSAR has led to increased attention and concern regarding poten-
tial biases associated with its use. As the accuracy and reliability of InSAR results become
more crucial, there is a heightened focus on identifying and understanding systematic er-
rors or inaccuracies that may affect the measurements and interpretations derived from its
analysis. These biases encompass atmospheric phase delays due to fluctuating atmospheric
conditions (water vapour content, ionospheric electron content, pressure, temperature...),
topographic phase delays caused by spatial terrain variations (abrupt shifts in elevation or
slope), and temporal decorrelation resulting from changes in the target area over time. Mis-
measures can also arise from radar system calibration errors, inaccuracies in satellite orbits
or instrument parameters, and geometric distortions in SAR images (such as foreshorten-
ing, layover or shadowing). Decisions on the data processing chain, such as the choice of
unwrapping algorithms, baseline estimation errors, or filtering techniques too can become a
source of distortion. Effectively managing and mitigating these biases is vital to improve the
precision and dependability of InSAR results. Ongoing research is focused on enhancing our
understanding and developing correction techniques for these deviations, thereby enabling
more precise monitoring and analysis of Earth’s surface processes.
In this thesis, our main objective is to investigate the biases exposed in [1] and [2], which
revealed variations in deformation results associated with the use of different maximum tem-
poral baselines (i.e., the maximum time-span considered when computing interferograms).
Both publications partly attribute these inconsistencies to fluctuations in soil moisture con-
tent within the study areas. In general, one would anticipate obtaining similar results when
deriving ground movement, regardless of the chosen temporal baseline, but, as we will expose
in the following sections, some physical processes can break the expected phase closure, an
interferometric observable used to measure consistency between a triplet of SAR acquisitions.
In order to evaluate the magnitude of the influence that this type of bias can have when
studying deformation in rural areas of Denmark, several zones were chosen and examined.
In certain applications, measuring ground deformation in areas characterized by distributed
scatterers (e.g. rural areas), as opposed to permanent ones (e.g. urban areas), is particularly

Finally, the model described in the article authored by De Zan et al., [3], was selected to
provide a benchmark for the expected phase misclosures.

Introduction to InSAR
This section serves to concisely present the principles of SAR, with a particular focus on
InSAR, for those readers unfamiliar with the topic. Rather than exploring the complexities
of SAR processing in depth, a general synopsis will be given, emphasizing the foundational
aspects that enable this method to measure ground movement accurately and dependably.
Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) is a high-definition imaging radar. To attain this enhanced
resolution, it relies on a mobile platform to operate, typically a satellite or aircraft, scanning
while the system traverses its trajectory. Being a coherent radar, SAR requires a highly
stable oscillator to quantify the delay between the transmission and receipt of the signal, so
it is possible to keep track of the received backscatter phase with respect to the emitted signal.

1.2
Concept of phase closure
By combining three coherent synthetic aperture radar (SAR) images (n,k and h), three in-
terferograms can be generated (In,k, In,h and Ik,h) with their corresponding phases φn,k, φn,h
and φk,h. However, it is possible that the modulo 2π (here denoted as ∣x∣2π) phases of the
averaged interferograms exhibit significant inconsistencies, indicating a deviation from the
expected triangular relationship (φn,k,h = ∣φn,k −φn,h + φk,h∣2π = 0). From a purely math-
ematical point of view there is no need for the three phases to be consistent, but most
interferometric models often assume consistency, although they do not always postulate it
explicitly [7], [8]. Overseeing this assumption can result in the presence of the biases men-
tioned earlier in the final deformation estimates.
The reason behind this premise being that a potential lack of triangularity (φn,k,h ≠0)
can challenge simple interpretations of interferometric phase. Specifically, in cases where
the phases lack consistency, extracting a phase vector or phase history is not trivial, as
the phase difference between two images can generally vary depending on the chosen path.
For instance, φnk+φkh could differ from φnh (an alternative approach to formulate φn,k,h ≠0).
This also elucidates the connection between the biases documented in [1] and [2] concerning
varying maximum temporal baselines, and phase misclosures. Let us set a clarifying example,
where we have a triplet of consecutive SAR images (labelled 1, 2 and 3 in sensing order).
Each of the images is detected 6 days apart from the previous one. If we consider a maximum
temporal baseline of 6 days we would solely deal with phases φ1,2 and φ2,3. Therefore, in
order to estimate the relative phase change between acquisition 1 and 3, one could only
combine the phase change between 1 and 2 (φ1,2) with the phase change between 2 and 3
(φ2,3). However, when the maximum temporal baseline is extended to 12 days, φ1,3 is already
supplied from the processed SAR data. In normal conditions one would expect both paths
to yield similar results, but it is straightforward to prove that this equality does not hold
when triangularity is not conserved:
φ1,2,3 ≠0 ⇒φ1,2 + φ2,3 −φ1,3 ≠0 ⇒φ1,2 + φ2,3 ≠φ1,3
opening up the possibility of obtaining different phases when considering interferograms 6
or 12 days apart. As the maximum temporal baseline increases and the number of interfer-
ograms expands, the relationships between them become more complex, offering numerous
potential paths to derive a phase between acquisitions.
On the one hand, using longer
temporal baselines can enhance the robustness of deformation estimates by providing more
paths to compute a common reference phase.
However, the increased intricacy of these
computations can also increase the likelihood of introducing inconsistent triplets, which are
more prone to lead to biases. In particular, according to [8], Small Baseline Subset (SBAS)
techniques are more susceptible to mismodeling when consistency is not valid. The study
suggests that these techniques could be more vulnerable to errors due to the limited num-
ber of interferograms utilized. This observation seems to align with the findings shown in [1].

triangularity are not of special interest for the purposes of this thesis, as we are primarily
concerned by those related to physical properties, particularly soil moisture.
1.3
Soil moisture modelling from InSAR observables
Comprehending the relationship between soil moisture variation and its interference with
phase closure, and subsequently its impact on InSAR measurements, is a topic of interest.
This phenomena was first reported in 1989, when scientists at JPL (Jet Propulsion Labo-
ratory) using SEASAT data reported motions of a few centimeters in interferograms, which
were initially attributed to watering [10]. The idea being that an increase of water content
in soil may have led to a vertical expansion of the fields, sufficient to cause a differential
effect in the radar line of sight. This explanation has been repeatedly challenged by various
researchers in recent years, as the phase change observed can sometimes be too significant to
be attributed to a realistic deformation, let alone irrigation or natural ground water content
change. Current understanding of this phenomena is that the differential propagation of
the electromagnetic waves into the wet soil causes interferometric effects. Therefore, more
satisfactory models for soil moisture retrieval are required, in order to compensate for this
effect when using interferometric SAR (InSAR) data for other purposes.
Thus far, soil moisture estimation with SAR has been primarily focused on amplitude prop-
erties, which can be limited by surface roughness backscatter interference. However, due
to the aforementioned phenomenon, interest has lately arisen to investigate the potential of
interferometric models, either from a purely coherent approach ([3]) or using a hybrid model
(combining both phase and amplitude datum) [11]. Said perspective is not only interesting
in terms of retrieving soil moisture information, but can also be helpful when investigating
its effects on the interferograms and coherence maps used to estimate ground movement.
Being able to decouple the phase change caused by soil moisture from the one that can be
attributed to deformation would be optimal. However, making this distinction is not trivial.
In this report we are going to try to focus on the latter case, under the educated guess that in
the chosen areas of interest no physical process besides soil moisture change and deformation
has contributed to alter the recorded interferometric measurements. Under this assumption
all observed phase misclosure can then be directly linked to soil moisture content change.
Our ultimate solution would then be only applicable in areas where phase inconsistencies
caused by phenomena beyond soil moisture change (for instance: volume scattering, vegeta-

tion moisture or statistical variation) are known to be minimal and hence, negligible.
Fluctuations in soil water content can cause variations in its dielectric constant between
radar acquisitions. These changes result in differential propagation of electromagnetic waves
within the soil, leading to interferometric effects. Specifically, these can affect the verti-
cal wavenumber (kz) in a shallow layer near the surface, depending on the moisture level,
which can in turn, lead to phase misclosure occurrences. According to [12]: Soil moisture
changes can typically correspond to spurious displacement of around 10-20% of the radar
wavelength (λ). For much larger movements, the soil moisture effect is, hence, negligible.
Conversely, for irregular, sub-wavelength displacements results may be substantially affected.
In the analysis conducted by Zwieback et al. [12], the objective was to explore the pos-
sibility of estimating soil moisture using three InSAR observables (phase, coherence and
closure), without relying on any assumptions about its complex spatiotemporal dynamics.
The primary aim of said investigation was to eliminate any potential influence of surface
soil moisture (SSM) on displacement estimates. Their research indicates that phase holds
potential as an effective method for estimating volumetric moisture time series. Nonetheless,
phase referencing is only feasible when there is no motion (including atmospheric delays) or
when such phenomena can be reliably accounted for, as demonstrated in [13]. Consequently,
it falls short in its capability to correct for biases when quantifying deformation. In contrast,
closure and coherence are sensitive to SSM, but remain unaffected by displacements. This
attribute renders them a more suitable choice for such applications.
Yet, the studies carried out as per [12], illustrate that neither method offers adequate infor-
mation as to uniquely and robustly estimate moisture content through inversion. Hence, the
correction of displacement estimates in terms of soil moisture also becomes ambiguous. The
usability of these techniques is further diminished due to their susceptibility to inaccuracies
in model specifications and a propensity for decorrelation. More concretely, regarding phase
closure it concludes that: "These limitations are closely related to the small size of φi,j,k,
as both noise and errors due to model misspecifications can quickly dominate this observable".
Taking everything into account, [12] discourages the application of either closure, coher-
ence, or a combination thereof for the purpose of quantifying soil moisture biases. Instead,
they advocate their use for detecting shifts in soil moisture, especially in cases dealing with
low frequencies and baselines. They deem these methods susceptible to interference from
noise and unmodelled influences, such as alterations in surface roughness. The main infer-
ence being that in order to dissociate the effects of soil moisture from actual deformation,
supplementary information external to InSAR observables is needed.

After identifying methods to analytically derive the coherence and interferometric phase

2.1
Data and methodology
Interferometric Post Processing Chain (IPP) is a set of tools developed by researchers at DTU
for generating offset-tracking and/or interferometric displacement maps, starting from SAR
SLC products [18]. The IPP can natively ingest focused single-look-complex (SLC) radar
data from several different satellite SAR sensors, including Sentinel-1, provided that the SLCs
are focused to a zero-Doppler geometry. Sentinel-1 focused IW (Interferometric Wide) swath
SLCs are provided by ESA in SAFE format, and can be downloaded from the Alaska SAR
Facility (ASF) website (https://search.asf.alaska.edu/). Each SLC covers approximately an
area of 250 km × 250 km at 5 m by 20 m spatial resolution. The IPP generates multilooked
interferograms for a given master geometry and temporal baseline, then a second module
performs flattening, phase unwrapping, inversion to velocity, error estimation and geocoding.
The final output is a geocoded slant range deformation map in latitude-longitude coordinates.
Processing of Sentinel-1 data can be carried out using the state vectors provided in the
SAFE product annotation, but more precise alternatives are the restituted orbits (typically
available at the same time as the SAFE products) or the precise orbits (available 21 days
after acquisition). Both types can be downloaded from the Copernicus Open Access Hub
https://scihub.copernicus.eu/gnss/ for the dates in question.
Unless stated otherwise, a
multilooking factor of 8×2 (range times azimuth) has been used throughout the different
sites studied. 90 m resolution DEMs from the Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM)
covering Denmark and Namibia were employed. A list of the SAFE products utilized to
perform the analysis of each one of the studied areas is included in Appendix B.

2.3.4
Lille Vildmose
Lille Vildmose is a protected area that stretches between the municipalities of Aalborg and
Mariagerfjord in northeastern Himmerland, approximately 28 km southeast of the city of
Aalborg. It is a vast peatland complex renowned for hosting one of the largest areas of
active raised bog in lowland northwest Europe, with approximately 21 km2 of remaining
raised bog habitat, representing over one-third of its original size. Around 2500 years ago,
this area was part of a strait linked to the sea. However, due to the gradual rise in land
level as a result of post-glacial uplifting, the strait was cut off, which led to the creation of
a brackish lagoon blanketed by a reed swamp poor in nutrients. This swamp environment
eventually transformed into a forest bog, before becoming a treeless bog primarily populated
by sphagnum mosses. When these mosses became detached from the groundwater source,
their decayed material over time resulted in the formation of a raised bog, a feature that has
partially endured until our days.
Before human intervention, Lille Vildmose was a mosaic of bogs, forests, lakes, and mead-
ows. Nonetheless, from 1760 to 1769, the lakes underwent drainage and their lakebeds were
repurposed for agricultural use. However, two of these lakes were subsequently restored, one
in 1927 and another in 1973. A third lake was recovered in 2019. The bog stayed mostly
undisturbed until between 1937 and 1939, when the Danish government purchased 23 square
kilometers in its central region with the aim of developing farmland for smallholder farmers.
To achieve this, 200 kilometers of ditches were excavated to enhance drainage, marking the
start of cultivation. Other operations involved the mining of marling, a crumbly earthen
substance composed of clay and calcium carbonate, used to enrich soils lacking in lime. Peat
extraction was also carried out to obtain energy through its combustion, which included
usage in the local cement industry. In the aftermath of World War II, the cultivated plots
were generally perceived as unappealing by agrarians. As a result, a significant portion of
the area transitioned into grassland, serving as a summer grazing site for cattle. Moreover,
the state began leasing land for extensive peat extraction. While this initially catered to fuel
needs, it eventually evolved into an industrial-scale extraction of sphagnum moss for private
and commercial horticulture. Said activities were almost fully discontinued by 2011.
Between 2011 and 2020, restoration efforts saw an investment of about 50 million Danish
kroner financed by the EU LIFE funds, the Aage V. Jensen Nature Foundation (a private
non-profit nature conservation organization which has become the major landowner in Lille
Vildmose), the Danish Nature Agency, and the municipality of Aalborg. The primary aim of
the project has been to safeguard the existing raised bog areas, to facilitate the recovery of
degraded and excavated areas by reestablishing wet conditions, and enhance the connectivity
of different hydrological units within the region. In order to achieve this, numerous ditches
and canals have been sealed, along with the completion of dikes, furnished with waterproof
membranes, and dams designed to hold water within the bog (see Appendix E). In parallel,

processes and favour the conditions for sphagnum moss to thrive. Lake Birkesø has also
been subjected to repletion.

Seasonal deformation in Store and Lille Vildmose
This chapter will focus on examining the various ground deformations observed in Store
Vildmose and Lille Vildmose for different seasons using InSAR techniques and, if feasible,
attempting to separate any potential influences related to non-zero phase closures on our
measurements. Based on the findings from the previous chapter, it appears that soil moisture
changes, or any other factors for that matter, do not significantly and consistently disrupt
the presumed triangularity, thereby not raising concerns about the suitability of commonly
employed InSAR algorithms. Consequently, it seems reasonable to anticipate that our mea-
surements represent the actual line of sight motion, within an acceptable margin of error.
3.1
Geopartner corner reflector data (PSInSAR)
Our examination will begin with Store Vildmose, where we have been granted access to
the PSInSAR analysis performed by Geopartner Landinspektører on the corner reflectors
they installed in the Ringfenner fields on December 2021 (refer to Figure 11 d)). This
section will not yet present their findings, which will be discussed later in 3.3, but will offer
a brief overview of their SAR data processing and how it deviates from the approach we
have adopted in this project, which will be elaborated on in the following section.
First introduced in [24], PSInSAR is a technique that relies on the identification and utiliza-
tion of persistent scatterers (PS). These are unique, temporarily consistent reflectors that are
identifiable within an extensive series of interferograms. Most InSAR processing methods
are limited by a critical spatial and temporal baseline between acquisitions, limits which,
once exceeded, might result in coherence loss, thereby reducing the reliability of the ob-
tained results. PSInSAR however, overcomes this constraint through its ability to recognize
highly stable reference points, whose coherence endures across interferograms, even when
said threshold is surpassed. In essence, it allows for the extraction of valuable deforma-
tion estimates even when the spatial and temporal gaps between images exceed the critical
baseline. The pixels representing these persistent scatterers are harnessed to carry out re-
markably accurate differential measurements. Ultimately, PSInSAR can robustly monitor
changes in the radar signal’s phase that these scatterers reflect over a long period of time,
enabling it to detect subtle changes in the Earth’s surface with exceptional precision.
In the current situation, the PS used to calculate motion originate from corner reflectors,
which produce a backscatter signal stronger than the surrounding areas, making them easily
distinguishable. Once their corresponding pixels have been identified, one of these corner
reflectors, ideally the one known to undergo minimal motion, will serve as the reference
point for measuring the relative ground deformation experienced by the rest. Furthermore,
as measurements are performed on the corner reflectors and not directly on the ground, any
biases originating in soil or vegetation properties are nullified. This implies that variations
in soil moisture do not have any impact, ensuring that any estimates obtained can be at-
tributed solely to land movement and no other factors. Additionally, as measurements are
not influenced by decorrelation due to vegetation changes, it is possible to gather relevant

information even during growth season, which typically occurs between June and September.
Then again, the trade-off for achieving these unbiased and very precise results is that the
estimates obtained will only be for the number of persistent scatterers identified minus one,
which is used for reference. Thus, only specific localized information from a select number
of pixels will ultimately be obtained. If the motion is anticipated or known to be relatively
uniform across the analyzed area, this is not a significant limitation, since the retrieved
results can serve as a proxy measure for the rest. Conversely, if the movement is notably
heterogeneous, we can only estimate the deformation in the remaining pixels by making
assumptions about their relationship with the PS. Having said that, it is important to note
that, for the purposes of this thesis, the primary limitation of PSInSAR is not its pixel-based
output, but rather the scarcity of natural persistent scatterers in rural regions. To address
this challenge, it is necessary to deploy corner reflectors in the areas of interest, which ensues
a large consumption of resources both financially and time-wise.
3.2
Hybrid SBAS (HSBAS) analysis
Hybrid Small BAseline Subset (HSBAS) analysis is a ground deformation algorithm, which
merges the principles of the Small BAseline Subset (SBAS) and Intermittent Small BAse-
line Subset (ISBAS) methodologies. Thus, to fully grasp HSBAS, we will initially provide a
succinct summary of SBAS and ISBAS, individually. For this purpose, the notation used in
[25] will be introduced and momentarily adopted throughout this section. It should be noted
that while the term HSBAS is not found in the literature, it is utilized here to distinguish
the processing employed from the aforementioned alternatives.
The Small BAseline Subset (SBAS, [26]) approach is a method that utilizes stacks of InSAR
observations from the same location to accurately determine slow, both linear and non-linear,
land surface movements with subcentimetric accuracy. Starting with a stack of coregistered
SAR images, SBAS produces differential interferograms between any two pairs, provided
that the orbital and temporal baselines do not exceed a given threshold. The reason be-
hind this being that smaller baselines disfavour low coherences, which in turn minimize the
probability of an inadequate inversion of deformation estimates. The components of the line-
of-sight motion are only constructed for those pixels that maintain consistent high coherence
across all considered interferograms. The utilization of the singular value decomposition
(SVD) method makes it possible to seamlessly "connect" independent SAR acquisition sub-
sets, even those separated by large baselines, thereby enhancing the frequency of observation
times, and ultimately yielding a deformation time-series for each of the surviving pixels.
When dealing with rural areas subject to numerous seasonal changes like land cover, soil
moisture, weather conditions, and agricultural activities, there is a potential for sudden
shifts in surface physical characteristics between observations, resulting in loss of coherence.
The volatile dynamics in these regions suggest that these targets may display intermittent co-
herence - exhibiting high coherence in some interferograms, but not in others. Consequently,
there could be highly coherent interferometric measurements within the accumulated SAR
data that might be overlooked in an SBAS analysis. In such situations, the ISBAS method-

3.4
Peatlands and bog breathing
Collaboration with Aarhus University’s department for Agroecology, through a partnership
with SDFI (Styrelsen for Dataforsyning og Infrastruktur, The Danish Agency for Data Sup-
ply and Infrastructure), proposed bog breathing as a possible explication for our findings.
Bog breathing, also known as the rhythmic rise and fall of the bog surface, is a phenomenon
that occurs in certain types of peatlands. It is caused by the expansion and contraction
of the peat layer in response to changes in moisture levels. During wet periods, the peat
absorbs water and swells, causing the bog surface to rise. This can lead to the saturation of
the surrounding areas and an increase in water levels. As the peat layer expands, it com-
presses the underlying layers, creating an upward movement of the bog surface. Conversely,
during dry periods, the peat releases water and shrinks, causing the bog surface to sink.
This can result in lowered water levels and the exposure of previously submerged areas. As
the peat layer contracts, it creates a downward movement of the bog surface. This natural
process plays a crucial role in regulating water levels within the peatland ecosystem. It also
affects the overall structure and functioning of the bog, influencing the distribution of plant
species and nutrient availability. This mechanism would align with the observed movement,
while providing an explanation for its apparent association with variations in ground water
content. [27][28]
One of the main aspects determining the magnitude of bog breathing, beyond moisture, is
peat thickness. Said variable denotes the vertical extent or depth of a peat layer or accumu-
lation. Its value can range significantly, from a mere few centimeters to multiple meters, and
is determined by several factors, including the pace of plant growth and decay, the water
levels, and the duration of peat formation. Gauging peat thickness plays a pivotal role in re-
search focused on carbon storage, hydrology, and the historical progression of the landscape.
Essentially, peat behaves like a sponge due to its high porosity, meaning that the greater
the amount of peat, the higher the capacity for holding moisture or water, assuming all

peat surface either contracts or expands based on changes in the water table, leading to the
seasonal sinusoidal-like deformation patterns. During summer, the water tables decrease and
the peat contracts, whereas in winter the process is reversed. According to the perspectives
offered by experts consulted on this matter, it appears likely that the extent of deformation
is linked to the thickness of peat. Although the ground movement is typically confined to
the uppermost millimeters or centimeters, it seems to be more conspicuous in areas where

4
Discussion and conclusions
Having assessed the phase misclosures in various rural areas, it can be concluded that their
magnitude in C-band is generally not significant, at least for the coherent periods observed.
More concretely, for the case of Tokkerup (where the Hallikainen model should be applicable),
they appear to be less prominent than the ones projected when using De Zan’s approach,
[3], with the inferred monthly moisture values provided by DHI’s mapping product (1.3.3).
Nonetheless, since these estimates are not validated with onsite measurements, it is not pos-
sible to determine if the fields actually underwent any surface soil moisture (SSM) changes
that could potentially provoke breaches in triangularity. In the remaining sites (that is Ve-
jrumbro, Store and Lille Vildmose), consisting of peat fields, no suitable electromagnetic
mixing model was found to compare with the obtained results. For the former example,
however, in situ soil moisture records were available displaying substantial seasonal fluctua-
tions, with mean moisture values around 75%. Unfortunately, no test areas were found where
in-situ moisture measurements and an appropriate mixing model matching soil texture and
frequency range were available simultaneously.
Overall, the studied areas in Denmark have consistently maintained triangularity over time
across multiple acquisitions, irrespective of documented or anticipated shifts in soil moisture.
The only spatially-correlated anomaly to this trend are certain fields in Lille Vildmose that
exhibit low-coherence, short-lived phase inconsistencies in a few image triplets. SSM fluc-
tuations were discarded as the source of these events and, after closer inspection with data
recorded in-situ (2.3.4.1), changes in the water level coupled with variations in the spatial
baseline were also dismissed. The cause behind these inconsistencies remains unclear and
further investigation is advised before taking any action to mitigate their effects. The sole
conclusion we can draw at this juncture is that these events coincide with areas undergoing
peatland restoration.
This does not negate the possibility of misclosures being relevant and/or regularly present
in other regions, as exemplified by the validation case in Namibia. A possible interpretation,
backed by simulations using De Zan’s approach, [3], in conjunction with Hallikainen mixing
model (1.3.3), might be that arid regions with lower SSM values, like the Namib Desert, are
more prone to exhibiting a lack of triangularity due to slight variations in their volumetric
soil moisture content.
Regarding the potential impact of misclosures when estimating motion, as cautioned in [1]
and [2], a comparison between the PSInSAR analysis performed by Geopartner Landinspek-
tører A/S, using corner reflectors deployed in the area, and the HSBAS approach adopted in
this thesis, was conducted for the Ringfenner fields in Store Vildmose. Both methodologies
yielded relative deformation estimates that were in strong agreement. Thus, suggesting that
any potential moisture biases are negligible (due to the inherent immunity of PSInSAR to
soil moisture influences). As a result, the recorded displacements cannot be attributed to
inaccuracies induced by SSM variations but are more likely due to a genuine ground motion

No inconsistencies were found when performing the HSBAS analysis in Store Vildmose for
multiple temporal baselines (i.e. 12, 24, 36, 48 and 60 days). Conversely, in Lille Vildmose,
a remarkable discrepancy was noted around the areas where misclosure patterns had spo-
radically arisen. This reinforces the idea that the lack of triangularity, regardless of its root
of origin, can indeed cause divergent results across the considered baselines. Hence, even
though they seem to be infrequent, it would be recommended to examine for misclosures in
advance to preclude any potential inconsistencies.
Ultimately, it was deduced that the non-linear seasonal deformation patterns observed in
Store and Lille Vildmose were not due to biases related to soil moisture change, but rather to
actual ground displacements produced by the effects of bog breathing; a natural phenomena
that comes as a consequence of the expansion and contraction of peat in the presence/absence
of water. Additionally, in the case of Store Vildmose, a link could be established between
the areas undergoing a more conspicuous movement and those with a greater peat thickness
extent.
4.1
Future work and applications
For future research on misclosures, it would be compelling to investigate whether other fre-
quency bands, such as the L-band, are more susceptible to their occurrence. An effort to
examine this using ALOS-PALSAR data was made during this thesis, but the results were
not conclusive. Currently, there are no open data satellites providing coverage of Denmark
on par with Sentinel-1 (even considering Sentinel 1-B being out of service as of December
2021). One possibility could be to submit a research application for ALOS-PALSAR or
ALOS-PALSAR 2 data.
Another potential avenue is to leverage the forthcoming ESA’s
Biomass mission, scheduled for launch in 2024, which will be equipped with a SAR system
operating at P-band.
A more detailed investigation of the Lille Vildmose area to decipher the source of the ob-
served punctual misclosures would be a logical next step. The alignment of these phase
inconsistencies with the areas undergoing rewetting suggests that there might be distinct
physical properties that differentiate these fields - specifically, the Mou and Høstemark fen-
ners - from the rest of Lille Vildmose, and can instigate a lack of triangularity between
acquisitions. A deeper comprehension of the rehabilitation process and landcover in these
regions, coupled with some fieldwork, might help to shed light on the factors contributing
to these events.
As an unforeseen outcome of this thesis, it has been demonstrated that HSBAS can serve as a
valuable tool for monitoring the extent of bog breathing. Clearly, there is an increasing global
interest in understanding the behavior and conditions of peatlands, including in Denmark,
as proper management of these areas can significantly contribute to carbon sequestration
strategies. Hence, initiatives like those undertaken in Lille Vildmose or the Ringfenner fields

Derivation of I (ε1r,ε2r) and γ (ε1r,ε2r)
Please be aware that the following explanation is strongly based in the one provided in the
original paper, [3].
The proposed model consists of two separate components: the transmission of the incom-
ing wave through the soil and the subsequent backscatter. This separation simplifies the
analysis and is based on the assumption that backscatter is only caused by small particles
or variations in the soil. Applying the first-order Born approximation, the incident field is
calculated as if the soil discontinuities do not exist; then the backscatter is estimated as a
function of the incident field. Without this approximation, the problem would be non-linear
as the backscatter computation would depend on the total field.
The soil is modeled as a uniform lossy dielectric, with a complex refraction index (n). The
dielectric constant (ε) is a measure of a material’s ability to store energy when in an elec-
tric field, in the case at hand, soil’s ε (also complex) is directly influenced by the water
content and composition of the ground. The relationship between both magnitudes can be
determined through various empirical models (discussed in later sections), which may be
more or less appropriate depending on the frequency of the emitted radar signal and, to a
lesser extent, the composition of the soil. The imaginary component of the dielectric con-
stant, ε, is associated with the amount of energy that is lost or dissipated within the material.

conform the plane of incidence, with the direction of y being perpendicular to it.
