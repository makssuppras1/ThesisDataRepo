ABSTRACT
Designing efficient NNs for resource-constrained systems is a challenging task. Unlike
traditional NNs, where accuracy is the primary performance metric, models tailored
for such systems must prioritize low inference time and minimal energy consumption
as well.
The field of Hardware-Aware Neural Architecture Search (HW-NAS),
which has gained significant attention in the last decades, aims to automate the
design process of Machine Learning (ML) algorithms. The conventional methods
for obtaining hardware cost metrics exhibit several limitations, often resulting in
suboptimal models.
The solution proposed in this paper addresses the limitations associated with using
the number of FLOPs or specific hardware devices to derive cost metrics. This thesis
explores the development of an inference time estimation model for various NN
architectures. Essential characteristics of the system include flexibility in supporting
diverse hardware configurations and minimal deviation from actual measurements.
Additionally, it is crucial to develop a software interface capable of interpreting
multiple ML formats.
The scalar implementation results demonstrate a deviation of approximately 7%
for a full Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) inference, primarily due to the
high complexity of the convolutional layer. Experiments conducted with the vector
implementation indicate enhanced performance, with the model providing estimates
roughly identical to those of RISC-V2.
However, the proposed solution has limitations concerning the number of layers for
which estimates can be obtained, thereby restricting the range of NNs that can be
evaluated. Future work includes extending functionality by incorporating additional
layers and modeling a broader range of mechanisms and techniques present in modern
DSPs.
Keywords— Hardware-Aware Neural Architecture Search, Neural Networks, FLOP, DSP

INTRODUCTION
In recent years, the field of embedded ML has witnessed notable progress based on the
advancements in both hardware and software. The primary focus was enabling Artificial
Intelligence (AI) in resource-constrained devices. Despite these advancements, one of the
bottlenecks is represented by the manual design of ML models that are both accurate
and efficient for target applications and hardware platforms. This process involves a
collaboration between researchers from the fields of AI and hardware design. To address
this challenge, the HW-NAS, which is a subdomain of the Automated Machine Learning
(AutoML), aims to automate the entire procedure by exploring diverse architectures using
computational resources and methods. However, most current approaches to HW-NAS
rely on finding an accurate and low amount of FLOPs models. Recent research [7], [8],
[9] has revealed that the number of FLOPs as well as the model size can be misleading
proxies, in estimating the inference time accurately since they do not take into account
the memory latency or the level of parallelism. This represents one of the major challenges
that the HW-NAS approach has yet to solve.
This master’s thesis aims to develop a highly configurable system capable of estimating
the number of clock cycles required to run a ML model on a given hardware platform.
The proposed estimator will incorporate a series of configurable parameters, enabling it to
model and reproduce the behaviour of a real processor with high fidelity. In addition, the
system will analyze the input NN, decompose it into instructions specific to the configured
architecture, and subsequently execute these instructions on the hardware platform. It is
important to assess the quality of the estimations, which must have a minimum deviation.
The interference time can then be determined by multiplying the output of the model
(clock cycles) by the frequency of target processor/DSP. Furthermore, the estimator will be
designed to be parametric and support typical hardware accelerator configurations such as
VLIW and SIMD architectures. This approach ensures that the system can accommodate a
wide range of hardware configurations, being a viable option in evaluating the performance
of NN across different target platforms.
1.1
Problem statement
As previously mentioned, using the number of FLOPs is not an effective metric for
determining hardware costs such as energy consumption, memory footprint, or inference
time.
Several approaches can be used as an alternative to this method. The first strategy involves
using actual hardware to obtain real measurements for a neural model. From the point
of view of accuracy, this is the most reliable method, because it gives results in a way

similar to the cases used in practice. The development of a cycle-accurate processor model
is complex and resource-intensive. The flexibility of such models is significantly limited, as
they are designed to implement a specific Instruction Set Architecture (ISA). This tight
coupling to a fixed architecture often results in varying hardware cost metrics when the
system undergoes certain modifications. In addition, the cost of production or acquisition

Another emerging approach is the use of predictive models to obtain approximate cost
metrics. These models can be further categorized into several subtypes. One subcategory
involves the development of ML models that can predict the total inference time for a
specific architecture. To achieve accurate estimates, the model must be trained on a large
dataset. Once trained, however, the model offers the advantage of providing cost metric
measurements much faster than if hardware systems were used. However, this method is
closely linked to a unique architecture, and the model must be retrained every time the
architecture is changed.
Therefore, it is highly desirable to design a parametric system that would allow users to
configure a specific architecture, similar to the processor or computing unit integrated
within an embedded system. This system should provide a fast and precise estimation of
the clock cycles required for a NN to run on various hardware platforms. By examining
and exploiting the general characteristics of a NN, such as vector operations, the simulator
should support parametrization with different hardware accelerators such as SIMD or
VLIW.
The purpose of this research is to address the following key questions:
• Is it possible to implement a generic cost estimator model that provides fast and
precise estimates for different target platforms?
• How does the architecture together with the hardware parameters of a processor
influence both the interference time or any other cost metrics?
• How does the processor’s width influence the interference time of a ML algorithm?

In the broadest sense, the goal of the project is to design a parametric system capable of
running and estimating the interference time of different NNs when they are deployed on
different hardware platforms. The system will leverage the benefits of different techniques
and mechanisms presented in modern processors to provide a close estimate of the number
of clock cycles and to mimic the behavior of real-world processors. Moreover, it is desirable
that the software interface of the system offers a high degree of handling different ML
model formats, and thus increases the system’s flexibility.
Thus the goals of the project are:
• Determine an efficient software interface that is capable of handling diverse ML
formats and transforming their architectures into inputs accepted by the estimator

THEORETICAL BACKGROUND
This chapter will explore general background information related to ML, and DSP modeling.
Firstly, a brief introduction to different layers presented in many of ML algorithms will be
covered. Next, different mechanisms and parameters presented in modern DSPs will be
presented.
2.1
Machine Learning
ML is a subfield of AI that is defined by the ability of systems to learn how to solve a
task without being explicitly programmed in this sense [10]. One of the most fundamental
pillars of a ML algorithm is the input data set, which can dramatically influence the
performance of a model. The variance of the dataset as well as its size are characteristics
of the input volume which will require meticulous research from the developer. In addition,
the pre-processing operation is essential in building an efficient solution from the point of
view of accuracy. The algorithm aims to find correlations and patterns to make the best
decision in solving the problem.
2.1.1
Dense
A dense layer is a fundamental element of an AI network. As can be seen in Figure 2.1, in
this type of layer, nodes are interconnected, resulting in a total number of input_size *
output_size connections. The main focus is to discover and learn patterns from the input
volume.
Figure 2.1. An example of a dense layer with three input features and four output
features. Each dense unit from the previous layer is connected to a neuron from the output
layer, resulting in a total number of 12 weights and 3 bias terms. Source: [1]
This type of layer can be mathematically modelled as a vector multiplication, where the
first one contains the values of the neurons located on the previous layer. The second

2. Theoretical Background
2.1.3
MaxPool
Pooling layers are used in neural networks to reduce the number of parameters and
implicitly the computational load. MaxPooling is among the most frequently used pooling
layers. This is usually placed after a convolutional layer and is intended to undersample
the feature map and allow making assumptions about high-level features. As with a
convolutional layer, a window (for instance 2x2) is defined that is slid over the input and
saves only the largest value in the region of interest covered by the window. Consequently,
the result of such a layer is given by a matrix in which only the most significant features
are saved, and the spatial dimension reduction increases exponentially with the size of the
pooling window.
Figure 2.3 illustrates an example of a MaxPool2D layer applied over an input whose size
is 4x4. Regarding the size of the window, it is 2x2, resulting in an output matrix whose
dimensions are 2x2. Depending on the machine learning framework used, the layer also
accepts a series of arguments for stride, padding, dilation, or ceil mode that can influence
both the values and the size of the output matrix.
In addition to the advantages offered by this technique, there are also a number of
disadvantages associated with the use of such a layer, such as over-emphasis of the most

2. Theoretical Background
2.2
Digital Signal Processor
DSPs are hardware equipment specialized in performing mathematical operations in a
short amount of time. Thereby, their field of applicability is extensive, representing an
optimal solution for a varied range of real-time signal processing applications.
In response to the demands and challenges posed by the fourth industrial revolution, there
has been a significant increase in the design of systems that must ensure both enhanced
performance and efficient energy use. To achieve this objective, hardware designers employ
mathematical modeling of the entire process and its interaction with the environment.
This approach aims to improve the quality of the input signal and optimize the process in
terms of time and energy. Consequently, their overall architecture has evolved substantially,
resulting in a variety of models tailored to specific requirements.
In comparison with a general-purpose processor, a DSP presents a series of advantages
and disadvantages, and the choice of use must be in strict correlation with the sphere of
application and its requirements.
Considering that they shape the process in which they are to be integrated, they require
high interdisciplinary technical expertise to be able to develop an efficient system in terms
of hardware and software. For example, strong knowledge in acoustics and biotechnology
may be required in the design of a DSP that is to be integrated into a marine fauna
monitoring system.
The entire system has a unique architecture in which specialized hardware units, as well as
different optimization techniques of the software component, are brought. In consequence,
both the production and operating costs can be increased drastically. Moreover, the unique
architecture specific to a certain use case is at the same time one of the main disadvantages
that such systems have. The performance can decrease considerably if they are used in
different tasks from those for which they were designed.
The system presents the best performance concerning execution time for the problem
it was designed to solve. DSP models generally follow a Harvard-type architecture in
which instructions and data are separated into two distinct memory areas to increase the
throughput. In the case of DSPs that offer multiple processing capabilities, e.g. SIMD, the
data memory is divided into multiple distinct address spaces.
2.2.1
Pipeline
Pipelining is a fundamental mechanism of modern computing system architecture. The
introduction of the pipeline technique represents an important milestone in improving the
processing speed of computing systems. The execution process of an instruction is divided
into several small steps called pipeline stages, which can be executed in parallel. One of
the most prominent pipeline architectures is the 5-stage one:
1. Instruction Fetch (IF): This is the first stage, in which the processor fetches an
instruction from memory and stores it in a special register called Instruction Register
Processor Simulator for Neural Architecture Search
9
==PAGE:26==
2. Theoretical Background
(IR). As part of this process, another special register called Program Counter (PC)
is involved, which indicates the memory address where the respective instruction
can be found.
2. Instruction Decode (ID): In this stage, the content of the IR register is decoded so
that it can be understood by the central processing unit. At the end of this stage,
both the instruction and the associated operands are precisely determined.
3. Execute (EX): It is the central stage, in which the arithmetic-logic unit executes the
instruction and stores the result in the destination register. Also, the status and
control registers are updated during this stage.
4. Memory Access (MEM): This stage is concerned with all memory interactions with
memory: fetching data from memory and storing them in registers or storing data
from registers in memory.
5. Write Back (WB): The last stage in which the result of the instruction produced in
stage EX is written in the registers or the desired memory area.
As previously stated, the main advantage of this mechanism is the increase in throughput,
which is defined as the number of instructions that can be executed by the processor per
unit time.
It should be mentioned that the level of parallelism is closely related to the number
of pipeline stages. As a result, a processor with 5 pipeline stages can execute up to 5
instructions in parallel, whereas in an architecture with 7 pipeline stages, the maximum
limit is 7.
On the other hand, successive instructions generally have dependency relationships in terms
of control and/or operands. This phenomenon is called hazards (control or data), and its
solution involves delaying the execution of instructions, reducing or even eliminating the
total benefit brought by the pipeline. Therefore, a comparative analysis of the trade-off
between the number of pipeline stages, as well as the delays caused by data and control
hazards is essential.
2.2.2
Data fowarding
One of the main disadvantages of the pipeline technique is the introduction of hazards
into the system. They are divided into three large categories and describe situations that
prevent a pipelined CPU from working properly. Understanding and managing them is
essential in achieving good performance.
A data hazard describes a dependency relationship between two instructions. If the operand
of an instruction is the output of a previous instruction that has not yet been completed,
then the system presents a data hazard.
One of the techniques to mitigate a data hazard is blocking dependent instructions. Thus,
by introducing a delay in the execution of the dependent instruction, the final value of
the operand can be determined and written to memory accordingly. Most of the time,
the compiler is the entity responsible for identifying and correcting a data hazard. It

introduces the delay in the execution by sending an No-operation (NOP) instruction to
the execution.
An improved solution for solving a data hazard is to execute an instruction for which the
operands have already been calculated and are available. In this case, the performance of
the system is improved, because the execution time of the entire program is reduced for
each instruction executed in advance. It is imperative to emphasize that the rearrangement
of the instructions must not affect the final result, and the anticipated execution of an
instruction that alters the execution of a program is not allowed.
The process of optimizing the order of execution is a challenging task. Another technical
solution proposed by the researchers and widely used in the current computing system
is represented by the data forwarding technique. As previously mentioned in the section
2.2.1, the result of an instruction is available at the end of the EX stage. This mechanism
involves the elimination of a data hazard by bypassing the final pipeline stages and sending
the data through dedicated hardware to the subsequent instruction. The main advantage of
this solution consists in eliminating the necessity of introducing delay in pipeline execution,
as well as the modification of the execution order.
Although the operand forwarding technique can significantly improve the performance of a
pipeline system, it should be mentioned that it also presents a series of drawbacks. The
complexity of the entire system is increased because it needs additional logic for controlling
and transferring data. In addition, besides the increased complexity, the system requires
additional resources (multiplexers, and additional data paths for bypassing). Finally, this
translates into an increased area as well as a higher energy consumption for the introduced
auxiliary hardware. As a consequence, it may not apply to some application domains, and
a comparative analysis regarding the advantages and limitations of this mechanism as well
as the requirements that the final system must satisfy must be carried out before.

Loop unrolling

2.2.5
Architectures
Addressing tasks that involve large datasets can be a prolonged and resource-intensive
process for scalar architectures. To enhance computational efficiency and achieve superior
overall performance, various models and standards have been proposed and developed by
the academic community. These efforts are primarily focused on two major directions:
• increasing the level of parallelism for the instruction, known in the literature as
Instruction-Level Parallelism (ILP)
• increasing the level of parallelism for the data, referred as Data-Level Parallelism
(DLP)
The first category encompasses VLIW processors, which can execute multiple operations
within a single instruction cycle, in contrast to scalar models where instructions are
executed sequentially [17]. Therefore, multiple independent operations are grouped into
one long instruction word. The primary application domain for VLIW architectures is
DSP tasks, which demand high computational power. The DLP strategy is described
by SIMD architectures, where the computing unit comprises multiple processing units.
These execute the same instruction on different sets of input data. SIMD architectures
are particularly effective in applications such as multimedia processing (image and sound
processing).
In the upper part of Figure 2.7, a general VLIW architecture is depicted, while the lower
part illustrates a high-level diagram of a SIMD architecture.
According to Flynn’s taxonomy [18], vector processors fall under the SIMD category. Unlike
scalar processors, which operate on a single data element at a time, vector processors are
designed to efficiently execute operations on entire one-dimensional arrays of data in a
single instruction. This design offers significant efficiency in handling tasks involving large
datasets.
Similar to scalar architectures, where data is fetched from memory into registers, processed,
and then stored back into memory, vector architectures employ the same technique. Vector
instructions operate on vectors of data elements, known as vector registers. The primary
advantage of vector registers is their flexibility regarding vector length, which can be
adjusted to suit the specific tasks being executed.

LITERATURE REVIEW
The research landscape for processors and NAS methods is rapidly evolving. In the realm
of processors, key trends include the development of multicore and parallel processing
units to enhance computational efficiency, alongside a strong emphasis on energy-efficient
designs to meet sustainability goals. The domain of NAS aims to automate the design
of NN models with high accuracy. This process involves exploring large search spaces,
and there is an increasing focus on developing NAS algorithms that can quickly explore
these spaces and efficiently assess model performances. In recent decades, researchers have
concentrated their efforts on designing NNs for resource-constrained systems. This subfield,
known as HW-NAS has been intensively researched. In addition to a traditional NAS
method, HW-NAS incorporates factors such as execution time and energy consumption in
the search for optimal neural architectures.
3.1
Scalar processors
The study [19] proposes a 32-bit soft processor that can be used in Field Programmable
Gate Arrays (FPGA) implementations. A soft processor is a CPU design written in a
Hardware Description Language (HDL) and that can be customized for a given application
and synthesized for an Application Specific Integrated Circuit (ASIC) or FPGA target [20].
In addition to high performance, the authors are interested in obtaining a model that also
has reduced resource consumption. The model is based on RISC-V ISA, supporting the
RV32IM standard [21]. Among the changes made to a classic RISC-V model, one can notice
the adoption of hierarchical decoding through the development of a pre-decode module
placed immediately after the fetch unit. Additionally, an optimization of the operating
frequency is proposed by enhancing the generation of control signals. The number of
pipeline stages can be changed from 5 to 3 by removing the MEM and WB stages.
The soft model was implemented on Xilinx Zedboard and compared with other RISC-V
processors such as MicroBlaze [22] and Taiga [23]. It achieved a performance 3.75 times
higher than MicroBlaze, with only a 7% increase in consumption. On the other hand,
the results obtained in comparison with the Taiga model are slightly weaker (9%) for
approximately the same energy consumption.

was performed. This investigation provided valuable insights into defining the principal
configuration knobs such as the number of vector units or processor width. The goal was to

the time dedicated to interactions with the memory system. The obtained estimations

4.6.3
Pooling layers
The functional modeling of a pooling layer, whether MaxPool2D or AvgPool2D, adheres to
a consistent general structure. The primary distinction between these two types lies in the
vector instructions applied to the data stored within the VRFs.
Similar to the convolutional layer, it is presumed that the data is optimally organized to
facilitate fast computation. Figure 4.6 provides an overview of the data organization and
its subsequent processing accomplished by vector lanes.
The hardware configuration used in this example is identical to the one used for the
convolution example. Initially, four VRFs are involved in fetching the input data. The
contents of these vectors are then partitioned and dispatched to one of the 9 vector lanes
for execution. The outcome of the logic-arithmetic instruction (element-wise comparison
for MaxPool and element-wise summation for AvgPool) is subsequently stored in vector
v1. This procedure is repeated until all VRFs have been utilized. The size of the pooling
windows determines the number of iterations required for completion. For MaxPool, this
involves kernel_size - 1 vector comparison instructions, whereas AvgPool is characterized
by kernel_size - 1 addition instructions followed by a division instruction.

5.3
Vector technical setup
5.3.1
Hardware configuration
RISC-V2 [52] is a scalable, high-performance, energy-efficient vector processor based on
RISC-V. The proposed architecture uses a superscalar core as the main control processor.
Vector instructions are fetched and decoded in the superscalar pipeline. This approach was
also found in other works introduced in the Literature Review section [27], [31]. Among
the most important contributions made in this study are:
44
Processor Simulator for Neural Architecture Search
==PAGE:61==
5. Experiments and Results
• A new register remapping mechanism, which unrolled dynamically in hardware the
vector loops.
• An alternative decoupled execution between the computation instructions and
memory accesses instructions streams without using synchronization queues.
• Support for vector reduction operation using a scalable tree method
The vector micro-architecture proposed consists of a four-stage pipeline: Register Remap,
Instruction Issue, Execution, and Memory Access. After the Register Remap stage, memory
interaction, and computing instructions follow different datapaths. The VRF is sliced into
configurable N lanes, each corresponding to a separate execution lane. The scoreboard
entity is responsible for hazard handling. The authors also mention the execution latencies
of the main categories of instructions:
• arithmetic-logic: 1
• multiplication: 3
• division: 4
• reduction: log2 vector_length
The register remapping technique allows an approximately 1.5x increase in throughput
(Elements Per Cycle (EPC)) compared to a dual-issue vector processor [53] for CNN
and Deep Convolutional Neural Network (DCNN). The vector reduction instruction also
leads to a throughput growth of 1.89 for CNN. On the other hand, for CNN or DCNN
applications, the proposed micro-architecture does not scale with the number of vector
lanes. This happened only in linear algebra applications, where the complexity of data
organization in memory is much lower.
To analyze any layers, it was necessary to develop machine code programs to model their
behavior in terms of vector instructions allowed by the processor.
5.3.2

the necessary machine code. Key arguments required by the script include the number of
vector lanes and the number of tensor elements. It is essential to thoroughly review and
comprehend these parameters to accurately establish the entire simulation context.

DISCUSSION
This chapter aims to comprehensively analyze the challenges and findings related to the
current research. The applications that can benefit from using the HCEM will be presented.
A brief comparison with other methods and tools used in HW-NAS frameworks will be
presented. Furthermore, the limitations of this system and several directions for future
work will be proposed.
6.1
Challanges and findings
Building a DSP is a complex task that requires high-level technical expertise. To satisfy
general characteristics such as low power consumption, real-time constraints, or small
memory footprint, digital designers must have a strong knowledge of hardware logic and
its optimization mechanisms. Moreover, for improved performance, the development of
modern DSPs also involves strong software optimization. In this thesis, the functional
modeling of a computing system was carried out from the perspective of scalar and vector
architecture.
One of the primary challenges encountered in this study was identifying suitable DSP
models for testing and validating the proposed solution. The entire process can be described
as long and laborious which implies solving numerous issues. Despite the substantial number
of published research works, the availability of open-source projects remains limited. A
particularly demanding task involves the installation and execution of one simulation using
these tools. In the solutions analyzed, there were instances where prerequisites were either
omitted from the documentation or relied on libraries that are no longer supported, thereby
increasing the time required for debugging.
Python is one of the most popular programming languages for developing NNs, primarily
due to the large number of libraries and frameworks available. However, these models must
go through a conversion process to be understood and executed by a DSP. Generating the
machine code equivalent for a layer or an entire NN can present a significant challenge.
For a wide range of DSP models, such as the Ibex core, the source program can be written
in a high-level programming language like C, which is then passed through a compilation
process. A file containing machine code instructions that can be executed directly by the
computing system is generated. As discussed in this work, the conversion method chosen
by the user heavily affects the performance of a NN. Additionally, the compiler and the
optimization techniques employed are crucial in minimizing control instruction overhead
and reducing execution time.

6. Discussion
6.2
Applications
The primary application domain for the model proposed in this study is HW-NAS
frameworks. This approach seeks to address the limitations inherent in methods such as
counting FLOPs or utilizing hardware equipment to measure latency or energy consumption,
by offering an alternative solution.
Utilizing the number of FLOPs as the primary cost metric within a HW-NAS system
presents several limitations. One significant drawback is that it fails to account for the
dependency relationships that may exist between successive instructions. These can lead
to hazards and the only viable solution sometimes involves introducing delays. Thus,
even having an identical number of FLOPs, the execution time of programs can vary
significantly due to pipeline stalls. Furthermore, the instructions a processor supports can
be categorized based on various criteria, one being the execution latency. For instance,
addition is a simple operation that is typically executed in a single clock cycle by the ALU.
On the other hand, the division is a more complex operation that may require a separate
data path and a higher number of clock cycles (e.g. 20 clock cycles). Based on this, the
program on the right side of Listing 12 requires more execution time, even though it has
the same number of FLOPs. Therefore, relying solely on the FLOPs method has several
limitations and may not provide sufficient information to identify the most efficient NN
within a search space.

To overcome the issues associated with using FLOPs as a proxy metric, the real hardware
can be used for determining the cost metrics of a NN. By employing hardware devices,
one can obtain more accurate latency and energy consumption measurements which are
critical for evaluating the efficiency of NNs. This approach provides a deep understanding
of the model’s performance and eases the finding process of the most efficient algorithm
within the search space.
However, the utilization of hardware equipment introduces
several limitations. Setting up experiments and running candidate models on physical
devices can be a resource-intensive and time-consuming task. Another important drawback
of employing physical equipment within HW-NAS frameworks is that cost metrics are
calculated for a fixed architecture, which is immutable. These cannot be generalized or
transferred from one architecture to another. As a consequence, exploring the search space
for a new hardware configuration requires repeating the entire process.

6. Discussion
Using a ML algorithm to predict inference time represents another method applied in
HW-NAS. The model must be trained on a large dataset to ensure accurate estimations.
However, generating such a dataset is a difficult process because it requires labeled data
consisting of models, plus values for the performance metrics. These can be obtained
through real measurements on hardware devices, which inherently ties the algorithm to
a specific architecture. For a new architecture, other measurements are needed, and the
model must be trained again.
The cost estimator proposed in this research aims to address and mitigate some of the
limitations inherent in previously presented methods. It models system hazards and the
mechanisms to counteract them. Additionally, the estimation of inference time incorporates
the latencies of individual instructions for increased precision. Compared to hardware
devices or predictive ML algorithms, the proposed system offers a higher degree of flexibility,
facilitating the exploration of a broader range of hardware configurations. Furthermore,
the time required to provide an estimate is significantly reduced compared to physical
devices. For instance, the simulation time for a dense layer with 3200 input features and 10
output features using the RISC-V2 simulator was 113 seconds, whereas the proposed model
provided an estimate in less than one second, with zero deviation. Unlike predictive ML
models, this approach does not necessitate the development of a large volume of training
data.
6.3
Limitations and future works
This research has provided valuable insights into the potential capabilities of the proposed
method. However, the solution presented in this paper has several limitations, and a list
of future directions for improvement is presented.
One significant limitation of the project is its focus on modeling and estimating only
the primary specific layers of a NN. Although an increased efficiency in modeling these
layers has been demonstrated, both in scalar and vector implementation, the model is not
yet prepared to be intensively used in practice. Modern NNs benefit from regularization
techniques such as Dropout or other functional layers (e.g. Flatten), which are designed to
enhance overall system performance. Extending the functionality to include a wider range
of layers in cost analysis is a potential direction for future research.
The model proposed in this paper offers a substantial variety of configurable hardware
architectures. However, it is crucial to acknowledge that this study may not have explored
and validated all possible combinations of hardware configurations. While the selected
configurations in the experiments generally demonstrated enhanced performance and
efficiency, there remains a possibility that certain hardware configurations may yield
suboptimal performance, necessitating further optimization.
Future research should
prioritize the systematic validation of all hardware configurations supported by the system.
A potential new research direction involves comparing the system’s performance with other
methods proposed and utilized within HW-NAS tools. In addition to the measurements
provided by the simulators, this study attempted to develop a linear regression model to

CONCLUSION
Embedded systems have become indispensable in nearly every aspect of modern life,
seamlessly integrating into a broad spectrum of applications, including telecommunications,
household appliances, medical devices, and the automotive sector. The development of
optimized ML algorithms for these devices can significantly extend their functionality and
enhance overall performance. The HW-NAS domain plays a crucial role in achieving this
objective by efficiently exploring a search space to identify the optimal algorithm that
meets system requirements.
This thesis explored the possibility of developing a configurable cost estimator system
capable of estimating the inference time of NNs, for a given hardware device. The proposed
approach addresses the limitations of traditional methods used in HW-NAS tools, such
as fixed architecture, long setup and estimation time, or misleading metrics. The system
developed supports both scalar and vector architectures. A comprehensive analysis of the
primary techniques and mechanisms involved in modern DSP architectures was conducted
before the modeling phase. Additionally, some of the most widely used architectures
were studied to establish a solid foundation for the system.
The scalar architecture
implementation provided valuable insights into how the layers of a CNN are decomposed
into ISA instructions and how the system manages the hazards.
The results obtained from the empirical experiments were promising, with a normalized
deviation of 7% approximately observed in the case of a full CNN inference for the scalar
model. The accuracy of the vector implementation was even more impressive, as the
model made estimates with minimal deviation in many of the tested scenarios regarding
individual layers. It is essential to continue the validation process and to experiment with
a broader range of networks and computing architectures.
In conclusion, this thesis demonstrates the feasibility of developing a configurable hardware
cost estimator model. Such a system not only facilitates the acquisition of cost metrics
for a diverse range of architectures but also increases the number of models that can be
explored by providing rapid estimates. Therefore, the approach proposed in this study
exhibits notable performance in terms of throughput, and flexibility.
