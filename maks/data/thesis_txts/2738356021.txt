Abstract
The biomass supply chain faces significant challenges due to fluctuating market demands
and production costs, which are influenced by economic activity, environmental condi-
tions, transportation, and raw material prices. This thesis aims to propose a systematic
approach to enhance the decision-making process within the biomass supply chain.
To achieve economic optimization by considering all costs, including biomass purchase,
transportation, pre-treatment, storage, conversion process, and other related expenses
to meet dynamic regional demands. An optimization model is designed to improve profits
for stakeholders and allocate resources more effectively in supply chain management.
From the perspective of factories, the goal is to maximize profit while satisfying regional
demands.
In the process of establishing the mathematical model, sampling from continuous and dis-
crete distributions techniques are employed within the framework of stochastic simulation.
Multistage stochastic programming policy supports the optimization under uncertainty to
address the biomass supply chain problem on an ongoing basis. Risk assessment is
considered to improve the robustness of the model and conditional value at risk analysis
is integrated. After the model development, a case study with simulated data is applied
and the result is evaluated against an expected value model and an optimal in hindsight
model. Consequently, the multistage stochastic programming model enhances decision-
making under uncertainty, providing a reliable method for optimizing the biomass supply
chain. Different risk parameters are applied to evaluate the expected profit and Condi-
tional Value-at-Risk under varying degrees of risk aversion.

1.1
Bio-energy and Biomass Supply Chain
Biomass is constituted from organic materials derived from plant and animal sources. It
is commonly sourced from wood, agricultural byproducts, animal manure, and organic
municipal waste, biomass can be transformed into various energy forms such as heat,
electricity, and liquid biofuels through multiple processes. However, excessive manure
can result in the runoff of nutrients, notably nitrogen and phosphorus, into adjacent aquatic
systems. Inadequate management or over-application of manure on agricultural lands can
degrade soil quality. Elevated concentrations of nitrogen and phosphorus might alter soil
chemistry, diminish fertility, and intensify erosion.
Nevertheless, when effectively harnessed as a new form of energy, biomass offers consid-
erable advantages. It is a versatile and sustainable energy source, notable for its carbon
neutrality. Through technologies such as anaerobic digestion and controlled compost-
ing, biomass can be efficiently converted into biofuels and biogas. This conversion not
only provides a stable energy supply but also reduces the emissions of methane, further
aligning with environmental sustainability goals.
Biofuel is a renewable energy source derived from biomass, which can include plant ma-
terials and animal waste. Common types of biofuels include ethanol, produced from crops
like corn and sugarcane, and bio-diesel, derived from vegetable oils or animal fats. Biofu-
els can be used in place of traditional fossil fuels in vehicles and machinery, contributing
to the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions and reliance on non-renewable resources.
Biogas is a type of biofuel generated through the anaerobic digestion of organic matter,
such as agricultural waste, manure, municipal waste, and plant material. This process
occurs in the absence of oxygen, producing a mixture of gases primarily composed of
methane and carbon dioxide. Biogas can be used for heating, electricity generation, and
as a fuel for vehicles. It provides a sustainable way to manage waste and produce energy,
while also reducing methane emissions from decomposing organic matter in landfills. Bio-
gas and biofuels are increasingly pivotal in today’s energy markets, largely due to their
contributions to economic development, sustainability, and technological innovation.
The economic viability of biomass as an energy source indeed hinges significantly on the
logistics involved in its collection, transportation, and processing. While biomass materials
themselves are relatively inexpensive, the overall cost-effectiveness of using biomass

for energy production can be compromised by high logistical expenses. These costs
include the collection of dispersed biomass sources, transportation to processing facilities,

A biomass supply chain includes various activities, such as cultivation, harvesting, pre-
processing, transportation, handling, and storage[1]. It begins with the sourcing of raw
biomass materials from agricultural residues, forestry by-products, energy crops, indus-
trial waste, municipal solid waste, and animal manure. These materials then undergo
pre-processing, which includes drying to reduce moisture content, size reduction for uni-
formity, and pelletizing or briquetting to increase density and improve transport and stor-
age efficiency. Transportation is associated with distribution, which is a critical step in
production. Biomass is then converted into energy, fuels, or other products through pro-
cesses such as combustion, gasification, pyrolysis, anaerobic digestion, and fermenta-
tion. The final products are utilized in various ways, including direct use of heat or steam
for heating or industrial processes, electricity generation, production of transport fuels
like biodiesel and bioethanol, and the manufacturing of chemicals and materials from
by-products. Additionally, the supply chain also manages waste and recycling aspects,
handling by-products and waste from biomass conversion processes to recycle nutrients
back to the soil or further process waste into new materials or energy sources.
Denmark has a substantial livestock industry, particularly known for its pork production.
According to the research [2], Denmark has been a significant exporter of pork products,
including live pigs, sows, carcasses, and processed food since 2001. It provides signifi-
cant potential for the development of the biomass and bio-energy market. Given the coun-
try’s focus on sustainability and renewable energy, the utilization of livestock manure for
energy production through processes like anaerobic digestion presents a valuable oppor-
tunity. In this article, pig manure is specifically concentrated on as the primary substrate
to optimize the biomass supply chain. By delving into the intricacies of processing pig
manure, we aim to uncover and refine strategies that enhance the efficiency and effec-
tiveness of biomass utilization. This focused approach allows for a detailed examination
of the challenges and opportunities within this niche, providing valuable insights into the
sustainable management of livestock waste and its conversion into renewable energy.

Literature Review
According to the bio-energy technologies study [3], two main approaches used in bioen-
ergy conversion process are thermo-chemical conversion and bio-chemical conversion.
Thermo-chemical conversion processes involve the use of heat to transform biomass into
energy, with several key methods outlined in the provided diagram. Combustion directly
burns biomass to produce hot gases, which generate steam, process heat, and electricity.
Bio-chemical conversion processes utilize biological mechanisms to convert biomass into
energy, with the main processes being fermentation and anaerobic digestion. Quantifying
all procedures and integrating them into a single model is challenging. Consequently, ex-
isting literature offers various approaches to simplify the mathematical modeling of these
processes. Typical biomass supply chain models and their advantages are reviewed in
this study.
In the study of manure and municipal sewage biomass supply chains [4], a comprehensive
system encompassing production and collection from farms and waste treatment plants,

1.3
Significance of This Study
The importance of optimizing the biomass and bio-energy products supply chain is dis-
cussed in Section 1.1. Existing studies have contributed significant knowledge and meth-
ods to improve the decision-making process. However, this thesis proposes a novel and
simplified biomass supply chain system aimed at optimizing economic performance. This
system combines stochastic simulation, decision-making under uncertainty with stochas-
tic programming, and risk assessment.
Stochastic simulation is rarely applied to biomass or bio-energy products supply chain
optimization. In this thesis, it is used to test model robustness and enhance randomness.
Additionally, prices and demands are treated as independent uncertain variables in the
stochastic programming model, which is another innovative aspect of this study. Further-
more, risk management and model evaluation processes are incorporated to complete
the system design.
Finally, the proposed model was compared with benchmark models to ensure its account-
ability. The inclusion of risk aversion considerations for stakeholders adds further flexibility
to the proposed model. In summary, this thesis presents an innovative and comprehen-
sive solution for the continuous optimization of the biomass supply chain.

2.1
Challenges in Biomass Supply Chain
2.1.1
Challenges in Fluctuating Prices and Market Demands in the
Bio-Energy Market
The consumption of fertilizer, biogas, and biofuel fluctuates due to various factors that
impact market demand and production costs, creating significant challenges in the bio-
energy market. Economic activity is a major influence; increased industrial activity during
periods of economic growth boosts energy use, enhancing demand for biogas and bio-
fuels. Conversely, economic downturns lead to reduced industrial activity and energy
consumption, diminishing demand. Weather conditions play a critical role by affecting en-
ergy needs and agricultural productivity. For instance, colder weather increases the need
for heating fuels, while optimal growing conditions can raise the demand for fertilizers dur-
ing planting seasons. Raw material prices further complicate these markets, particularly
impacting the cost structures of fertilizer and biofuel production. These interconnected
factors contribute to a complex and often volatile market environment, posing substan-
tial challenges for stakeholders in the bio-energy sector to navigate and predict market
demands effectively.
2.1.2
Challenges in Optimal Biomass Transportation
Optimizing transportation for biofuel, biogas, and fertilizer presents several challenges,
even when using advanced operations research and mathematical modeling techniques.
These challenges include minimizing costs, reducing transit times, and enhancing overall
efficiency. Real-time decision-making and dynamic routing are complicated by uncer-
tainties in demand, supply, and transit conditions, which necessitate the use of stochastic
modeling and simulation models. These models must account for a range of variables and
potential disruptions, making it difficult to consistently make informed and robust trans-
portation decisions. Ensuring that the biomass supply chain remains resilient to disrup-
tions and responsive to market changes requires continuous adaptation and sophisticated
modeling to manage these inherent uncertainties effectively.

2.1.3
Risks in the Biomass Supply Chain
The biomass supply chain is fraught with several risks that can disrupt the flow of mate-
rials and impact the overall efficiency of the system. Key risks include supply variability
due to seasonal changes, unpredictable weather patterns, and logistical challenges such
as transportation delays and storage issues. Additionally, market price volatility and regu-
latory uncertainties can further complicate supply chain management. Given these com-
plexities, it is crucial to employ robust risk assessment and management tools. A com-
prehensive assessment by focusing on the tail end of the distribution of potential losses,
offering insights into the worst-case scenarios is needed. This enables decision-makers
to not only predict but also quantify extreme risks, ensuring more resilient and informed
strategies for mitigating adverse impacts on the biomass supply chain.

3.1
Stochastic Simulation
Stochastic simulation is a powerful technique employed to model and analyze systems
influenced by randomness or uncertainty. This method involves generating random vari-
ables to simulate the behavior of a system over time. In the context of a stochastic pro-
gramming model, the initial price and demand for stage one can be considered random
variables. Stochastic simulation allows the model to generate price and demand data that
adhere to historical distributions. In this model, price is treated as a continuous parame-
ter, while demands for bio-energy unit is treated as a discrete parameter. The subsequent
sections illustrate both stochastic sampling methods in detail.
3.1.1
Sampling from Discrete Distribution
The prices of raw biomass materials sourced from farmers exhibit relative stability. Each
unit of biomass has a discrete price sample. Empirical data suggests that these prices
do not conform to a specific statistical distribution. Therefore, it is crucial to sample from

Here we use rejection-method to sample from a discrete distribution.
Rejection Sampling Technique: This randomness in sampling from historical distribu-

The process starts by defining a constant that acts as an upper limit for all probabilities in
the target distribution. In a general case, it involves three steps:
1. Proposal Distribution g(x): The proposal distribution g(x) could be a uniform dis-
tribution or another distribution that covers the range of historical prices.
2. Determine c: The constant c is chosen such that c·g(x) bounds the historical prices
distribution from above. In the histogram, the red dashed line indicates this constant.
3. Sampling Process:
• Draw a sample X from g(x).
• Draw a uniform random sample U from U(0, 1).
• Accept sample X if U ≤
f(X)
c·g(X), where f(X) represents the empirical frequency
from the histogram.
By ensuring c is appropriately chosen, the rejection method can effectively sample from
the historical prices distribution. It uses a bounding limit to efficiently decide on acceptance
or rejection, thereby producing samples that match the desired distribution.
3.1.2
Sampling from Continuous Distribution
Sampling from continuous distributions is a critical technique employed in the proposed
model. Given that the prices of biogas, biofuel, and fertilizer can fluctuate by varying
percentages, the demands for these three products in a region are also represented by
continuous values. While the Acceptance-Rejection method [9] is suitable for complex
distributions, we assume that the prices and demands for final products follow a normal
distribution over short periods. Therefore, the Box-Muller transform method is utilized to
sample continuous parameters from this known distribution. Empirical evidence suggests
that prices and demands are primarily influenced by the preceding time period, making
direct sampling challenging due to their complexity. Consequently, Monte Carlo methods
and Markov Chain techniques are introduced to generate a sequence of samples that
accurately represent the target distribution.
Box-Muller Transform
The Box-Muller Transform is a mathematical technique for generating pairs of indepen-
dent, standard, normally distributed random numbers from uniformly distributed random
numbers [10]. The Box-Muller Transform generates two independent standard normal
random variables Z0 and Z1 from two independent uniform random variables U0 and U1
over the interval from 0 to 1. This method generates two normal variables for the cost of
two uniform variables, making it quite efficient.
The transformation is derived from the fact that if U0 and U1 are uniformly distributed, then

3.2.1
Stochastic Programming
Stochastic programming is a mathematical optimization framework that deals with decision-
making under uncertainty [12]. Unlike deterministic models, where all parameters are
known with certainty, stochastic programming incorporates randomness by including prob-
abilistic constraints and objectives. This methodology is particularly useful in fields where
uncertainty is inherent. In finance and supply chain management, SP aims to find opti-
mal decisions by accounting for the probability distributions of uncertain parameters. The
objective in this thesis is to optimize the profit in the proposed supply chain, where inven-
tory control, production planning, and transportation are modeled to achieve optimization
under uncertainty of market prices and regional demands.

3.3.1
Scenario Reduction Technique
K-means algorithm: It is a fundamental clustering technique. Its objective is to partition
a dataset into K clusters based on similarity of data points [15]. The algorithm iteratively
assigns each data point to the nearest cluster centroid, which represents the center of
the cluster. This assignment is based on the Euclidean distance between the data point
and the centroid. After all data points are assigned, the centroids are recalculated as the
mean of the data points belonging to each cluster. This process repeats until convergence,
typically when the assignments no longer change significantly or a predefined number of
iterations is reached.
In the context of scenario reduction for multistage stochastic programming models, K-
means is computationally efficient and scalable, making it suitable for handling large sets
of scenarios. By clustering scenarios, K-means can effectively reduce the dimension-
ality of the stochastic programming model while preserve the key statistical properties
and variability present in the original scenario set. Nevertheless, K-means algorithm is
non-deterministic algorithm. Because these initial positions influence the subsequent as-
signments of data points to clusters and the final cluster centroids, the outcome of the
algorithm can vary between different runs, even when applied to the same dataset.
Figure 3.4: K-mean Scenario Reduction Method
K-medoids algorithm: it is a clustering technique closely related to the K-means algo-
rithm, differing primarily in its method for determining the central points of clusters. Unlike
K-means, which uses the mean to represent the center of a cluster, K-medoids selects
actual data points as centers, referred to as medoids.
By using actual data points as cluster centers, K-medoids demonstrates greater robust-
ness to noise and outliers compared to K-means, which can be significantly influenced by
extreme values [16]. This robustness arises because medoids, being actual data points,
are less susceptible to the distortions caused by outliers. Furthermore, medoids offer en-
hanced interpretability over centroids, as they are real data points rather than hypothetical
averages within the feature space.

3.4.1
Benchmark Models
The Expected Value Model and the Optimal-in-Hindsight Model are two primary approaches
used to make informed decisions under uncertainty. The Expected Value Model optimizes
decisions based on the average expected outcomes of uncertain parameters. Conversely,
the Optimal-in-Hindsight Model provides a benchmark by considering the best possible
decisions with perfect knowledge of future outcomes.
In the Expected Value Model, decisions are made based on the expected values of the
uncertain parameters. The objective is to optimize the expected outcome of the decision-
making process, thereby providing a decision that is optimal on average. This approach
is particularly useful for risk-neutral decision-makers who aim to maximize the average
performance of their decisions.
On the other hand, the Optimal-in-Hindsight Model evaluates the performance of deci-
sions by considering the actual realization of the uncertain parameters. The objective is
to find the optimal decision for each possible realization of uncertainty and compare it to
the decision made without knowledge of the future. This model provides a benchmark for
evaluating the performance of other models by showing the best possible outcome if the
future were known perfectly. However, since perfect knowledge of the future is unrealis-
tic in practical scenarios, the Optimal-in-Hindsight Model is primarily used for comparison
rather than for direct decision-making.
In summary, while the Expected Value Model focuses on optimizing decisions based on
average expected outcomes, the Optimal-in-Hindsight Model serves as a benchmark by
evaluating the best possible decisions with perfect foresight. Both models offer valuable
insights, but they serve different purposes in the context of decision-making under uncer-
tainty.

4.3
Stochastic Programming Policy
Figure 4.2: Stochastic Programming Policy
The optimization of the biomass supply chain should be a continuous process.
The
stochastic programming policy establishes a framework that enables decision-makers to
make optimal decisions on a monthly basis in this case study. The mechanism of the
policy is visualized in Figure 4.2. In this policy, each ”here and now” decision is made af-
ter relevant information, including prices and consumption, is revealed. The look-ahead
period for each decision-making process is 2 months.
Specifically, each month when deciding how many products each plant should produce,
decision-makers gather previous market information and predict the market conditions
for the following 2 months. They then make a decision that aims to maximize profits over
the next 2 months. Once the ”here and now” decision is implemented, the associated
uncertainties are revealed. The new information replaces the previous data and serves
as the basis for predicting the subsequent 2 months. A new ”here and now” decision
is then made. This policy continuously optimizes the decision-making process, ensuring
adaptive and responsive management of the biomass supply chain.

Conclusion
Uncertainty Modeling
The biomass and bio-energy market faces significant challenges in optimizing its supply
chain due to inherent uncertainties in both prices and consumption. This complexity is
further exacerbated by the involvement of numerous stakeholders and the diversity of
product types within the market. Additionally, the asynchrony between production and
sales introduces another layer of unpredictability, complicating the uncertainty modeling
process.
One of the primary methodologies employed to address these challenges in the biomass
supply chain is multistage stochastic programming policy. This advanced approach pro-
vides a robust framework for supporting decision-making under conditions of uncertainty.
Multistage stochastic programming allows for dynamic and sequential decision-making,
which is crucial in adapting to the evolving conditions of the biomass market. It enables
the formulation of strategies that consider a range of possible future scenarios, thereby
improving the resilience and robustness of the supply chain. By incorporating risk assess-
ment into the objective functions, this methodology not only manages the risk associated
with extremely low profit scenarios but also facilitates the alignment of the supply chain
optimization model with the risk aversion preferences of decision-makers.
The policy establishment follows the following three steps of a decision making process:
1. Modeling the System: In this thesis, a comprehensive mathematical model is de-
veloped to represent the real-world biomass supply chain. The primary objective of
this model is to simplify the complexities of the actual supply scenario and to quan-
tify profit performance within an optimization framework. The proposed model en-
compasses various critical components of the supply chain, including biomass pro-
curement, transportation, external warehouse storage, distribution, production, and
internal sales planning. The model is designed to incorporate both product prices
and regional demands for different biomass products as independent sources of un-
certainty. By doing so, it captures the dynamic nature of the market and provides a
realistic representation of the challenges faced by the biomass supply chain. This
approach enables the quantification of the supply chain’s performance under vary-
ing conditions, facilitating more informed and effective decision-making processes.
2. Formulating a Solvable Model: Uncertainties are addressed within the framework
of stochastic programming, which provides an efficient and cost-effective approach
to optimizing profits from the perspective of biomass plants. The initial stage em-
ploys stochastic simulation to address the lack of initial parameters. Both the second
and third stages function as look-ahead periods, allowing for dynamic and adaptive
decision-making. To model the stochastic nature of the simplified supply chain, 2500
potential future scenarios are generated. To manage this extensive dataset, the K-
means clustering technique is employed to reduce the number of scenarios to a
smaller, more manageable set of representative scenarios. This reduction process
ensures that the computational complexity remains tractable while preserving the
diversity of possible outcomes. The discretization method is then utilized to com-
bine these discrete scenarios into a coherent and manageable scenario tree. This
Optimal Operation Strategy of Supply Chain of Biomass
42
==PAGE:52==
scenario tree effectively simulates the uncertain conditions encountered in real-life
supply chain operations, providing a robust tool for decision-making under uncer-
tainty.
3. Solving the Problem: A case study was conducted to evaluate the performance
of the MSP model. The author provided artificial data specifically designed for this
study. This data facilitates numerical testing of the policy and risk assessment. The
results demonstrate a significant variation in outcomes when the risk parameter is
adjusted.
Limitations and Future Work
This study has presented optimization model including risk assessment under stochastic
programming field. Despite these contributions, it is important to acknowledge certain
limitations that may impact the interpretation and generalizability of the results.
1. Mathematical model design: In this thesis, various financial factors are incorpo-
rated into the proposed model. However, due to the complexity of real supply chain
processes and bio-energy production, the model simplifies several aspects, includ-
ing transportation processes, uncertainties in production, storage environments for
different products, and other potential business behaviors within the supply chain.
Addressing these complexities requires extensive industrial research and business
model analysis, which necessitates further study. The mathematical model holds
potential for further development to better align with real business environments.
2. Model optimization: Parameters are critical for the optimization model. Due to
the lack of real data and historical records of biomass supply chain operations, the
stochastic model cannot adjust parameters and compare the results to real-life sce-
narios. Testing different parameters, such as the number of generated scenarios,
the number of reduced scenarios, and the length of the look-ahead period, could
optimize the model and align the outcomes more closely with reality. Furthermore,
different scenario reduction methods should be applied using real data. The choice
of clustering method directly affects the representative scenarios and their probabil-
ities, which form the foundation of stochastic programming modeling.
3. Stochastic simulation: This method is employed to generate initial parameters
from a historical database. Simulation allows for testing the robustness of the pro-
posed MSP model. However, the stochastic sampling treats all parameters as inde-
pendent, disregarding the potential interdependencies among them. Future studies
should consider these internal interrelationships to enhance the accuracy and reli-
ability of the model for robustness analysis.
This thesis aims to provide a feasible solution for optimizing the biomass supply chain and
establish a foundation for future research. Subsequent analyses could expand the model
to include multiple stakeholders and utilize real data to enhance model performance. Ad-
ditionally, the model could be extended to incorporate municipal facilities, such as biogas
pipeline installations and investments in sustainability, to further improve its flexibility and
applicability.
