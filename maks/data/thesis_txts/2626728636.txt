Abstract
The work of this thesis focuses on heart rate data collected from wrist worn activity track-
ers from almost 100,00 users around the world. The work is highly novel as it explores
an unprecedented large-scale dataset that includes not only heart rate measures and
user demographics including age, gender, body mass index and home country, but also
measures of behavior like sleep, exercise and smartphone usage. We found significantly
higher heart rate for females compared to males along with a significant decrease in heart
rate with increasing age. Our results also suggest that users who exercise more and users
who sleep seven to nine hours have a decreased average heart rate. We analyzed the
impact of smartphone usage on the heart rate and our results suggest that higher daily
screen time decreases the heart rate. We acknowledge, however, that future work should
study if this effect is a confounding factor of sedentary behavior.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The human heart rate is one of the most important vital signs and show how well the
body is functioning. The heart rate can be thought of as a measure of the demand and
workload the heart is exposed to. It is important to know how the heart rate naturally
differs between individuals in order to understand how a certain disease or behavior can
be reflected in the heart rate. It is already known that a long-term increase in heart rate
can cause severe cardiovascular effects [83] and increase mortality [38]. Studies show
that stressful work days or poor sleep can cause this long-term increase to the heart rate
[86, 76, 10]. However, a high heart rate can also acts as a natural mechanism for staying
alert and managing difficult situations [52]. During exercise a high heart rate ensures that
the cells of the body are provided with the needed oxygen. Monitoring the heart rate can
be non-invasive, low cost and simple [25] and is therefore a strong tool to improve the
overall well-being and in best case prevent more severe health complications.
For these reasons, researchers have studied the heart rate under different conditions.
There are, among others, studies on exercise [39, 65], sleep [76, 10] and mental dis-
orders [78, 86, 14, 75, 41, 69]. Common for all these studies is that data have been
collected under highly controlled settings, on a small subject group, and often performed
in unnatural environments like laboratories. This can not be considered a true estimate
of the real world’s heterogeneities and do not allow researchers to study how everyday
behavior influences the heart rate.
The technology of consumable wristbands that among others continuously measure heart
rate during the entire day overcome the limitations that controlled studies face. However,
there are still very few studies which take advantages of this kind of data. Data from the
Fitbit system, a wrist worn activity tracker, have been explored in recent studies [62, 67,
60]. These studies focus mainly on the differences in heart rate between gender, age,
BMI and to some extent sleep duration.
As the wristband technology and data collecting advances it is important to uncover the po-
tential that the available data provides. First, to support that the already existing research
conducted under laboratory conditions applies to the greater population and finally, to dis-
cover if this large-scale data provides new information about behavior that can improve
health and well-being.

novel unprecedented dataset. Compared to already existing studies, this dataset offers
the opportunity to study the heart rate not only in relation to gender and age, but also in
relation to behavioral measures like sleep, smartphone usage and exercise on a large
and diverse population. The dataset includes data collect by wristbands between 2016
and 2019 from almost 100,000 users from all over the world. Prior to the work, which is

2.1
The Cardiac Cycle
In this section follows a description of the basic function of the heart and the cycle that
defines the heart rate. The following sections are mainly inspired by the work of F. Shaffer
et al. [73] if nothing else is indicated. The reader is encouraged to review the excellent
work of F. Shaffer et al. [73] for more information.
The heart is a muscle that contracts and pumps blood to the entire body. The heart consist
of two atria and two ventricles. Deoxygenated blood enters the right atria and is pumped
to the right ventricle. The right ventricle now pumps the blood to the lungs were blood is
oxygenated. The blood returns to the left atria and is then pumped to the left ventricle and
from here pumped to the body to deliver oxygen to cells.
The cardiac cycle of the hearts contractions consist of the systole and the diastole. The
systole is defined as the period in which the ventricles contract to eject blood to the body
and the lungs, while the diastole includes the period when the ventricles are relaxed and
filled with blood. During the systole the pressure of the heart and the arteries in the body
is increased due to the increased blood flow.
2.1.1
Heart Rate
The heart pumps blood to the entire body with a rate from 60 to 100 Beats Per Minute
(bpm). The rate at which the heart beats can be used as a measure of balance between
the different players in the nervous system. Many bodily functions, including the heart
rate, works without our awareness and are controlled by the autonomic nervous system
which has two distinct divisions; the sympathetic nervous system and the parasympathetic
nervous system. The Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS) controls the ”fight-or-flight”
response which is responsible for controlling stress-full or energetic situations. While the
Parasympathetic Nervous System (PNS) controls the ”rest and digest” functions, which
is the basic bodily functions like breathing [52]. The SNS will typically increase the heart
rate while the PNS will decrease the heart rate. The PNS innervates the heart through
the vagus nerve, which projects its cardiac control onto the SA node in the heart. The
response of the vagal nerve is almost instantaneous compared to the slower response

from the sympathetic nerves which have a delay of up to 5 seconds. Sudden changes
in heart rate is therefore mainly mediated by either an activation or inhibition of the PNS.
The heart rate at all times can be thought of as the total net effect from the vagus nerve
and the sympathetic nerves. At rest the parasympathetic control through the vagus nerve
is dominant, which is why our heart rate at rest always is lower [73]. According to the
American Heart Association [7] the Resting Heart Rate (RHR) is the rate of which the
heart pumps the lowest amount of blood the body needs at rest. The RHR is the measure
that is reported most often in studies of the heart rate.
2.1.2
Heart Rate Variability
Heart Rate Variability (HRV) was defined in 1996 by the Task Force of The European So-
ciety of Cardiology and the Northern American Society of Pacing and Electrophysiology
[51]. The Task Force defined standards for nomenclature, terms, measurements, cor-
relates to physiology and phatophysiology and clinical applications. They defined Heart
Rate Variability (HRV) as the ”oscillation in the interval between consecutive heart beats
as well as the oscillations between consecutive instantaneous heart rates”. This means
the variation or changes in the time intervals between continuous heart beats, also termed
Interbeat Interval (IBI) [72].
The term HRV is mostly used when derived from the heart cycle, i.e. the ECG signal.
Some literature uses the term Pulse Rate Variability (PRV) when the same measurement
is derived from the pulse cycle, i.e. the PPG signal [71, 15, 93], while others again uses
the term PPG Variability [47]. In this project the term HRV will be used in all cases.
2.2
Measuring Techniques
The heart rate can be recorded either using Electrocardiography (ECG) or Photoplethys-
mography (PPG). The 12-lead ECG is considered the golden standard for monitoring the
heart [42]. ECG is a measure of the electrical activity of the cells in the muscles of the
heart that causes contraction of the heart [82], while PPG is a measure of the variation in
blood flow in a peripheral area [82]. The ECG signal consist of the P wave, the T wave
and the QRS complex, see Figure 2.1. The systole roughly runs from the R peak to the
end of the T wave, while the diastole runs from the end of the T wave to the R peak.
In the ECG the heart rate is determined by the time between the R peaks. While in
the PPG signal the heart rate is determined by the period between the peaks [82]. The
relationship between an ECG, a PPG signal and the Arterial Blood Pressure (ABP) is seen
in Figure 2.1. When the blood pressure drops a peak in PPG signal is observed which
coincides with the start of diastole where the heart is filled with blood. Disagreement
between the RR-interval and PP-interval can be caused by inaccurate detection of peaks
due to artifacts or noise in the signals [71].
In the early software algorithms ECG were considered more accurate, however newer al-
gorithms have improved performance when using PPG [73]. The complexity of the cardiac
cycle can actually result in errors, when deriving the heart rate from the QRS complex; for
example in cases where it is difficult to distinguish between high P and T waves [11]. PPG
has been validated against ECG in multiple studies [11, 15, 47]. Results found excellent
agreement between HRV derived from ECG and PPG [11] and high significant correlation
(p>0.001, r>0.86) between HRV from ECG and PPG under resting conditions in clinical
settings [15]. The validity of PPG in wearable devices is described later in Section 2.3.

2.2.1
Photoplethysmography
Photoplethysmography (PPG) is a well known technique used to monitor and record dif-
ferent physiological measurements including the heart rate [3]. The technique was first
introduces by Hertzman in 1937 [30, 31, 32]. The use of PPG clinically was triggered
partly by the invention of the LED technology 1962 and the development of pulse oxime-
try in 1972 [46]. In the recent years PPG has been used in many consumer devices like
the Fitbit and the Apple Watch.
The principle of PPG includes a light source and a photodetector to record the amount of
backscattered light. The preferred body location for PPG is the fingertip, see Figure 2.2,
other location like the wrist can also be used. The photodetector can either be placed in
transmission-mode on the other side of the finger than the light source or in reflectance-
mode beside the light source [80]. The reflectance-mode PPG is the method used in wrist
worn monitors. PPG can also be conducted using non-contact PPG by using a camera
in the classical way to film the face [17].
The amount of light that is reflected back to the photodetector is proportional to the vol-
ume of the blood [19], see Figure 2.3. The greater the pressure, and thereby volume,
in the capillaries the less light is reflected back to the photodetector. This results in an
downstroke in the PPG signal and an upstroke when the capillaries relaxes again [80].
The pressure and its relation to the cardiac cycle is described above and in Figure 2.1.

Consumer heart rate monitors are available as both wrist worn devices and chest strap
devices. The wrist worn devices use the PPG technique, while the chest strap devices
use ECG. Wrist worn devices have been studied to determine the reliability of their Heart
Rate (HR) measures and the following will outline some of these studies.
In 2008 Lu et al. [47] explored the impact of upright and supine position. They found strong

conditions, though highest in supine position. They suggest that this higher correlation in

et al. [57] compared a commercial available wrist worn and forearm worn HR monitor with

by Schherbina et al. from 2017 [74] the reliability of heart rate from seven wrist-worn
devices when compared to a golden standard measured by ECG under different activities
was tested. They found that most devices performed within acceptable limits of a 0.05

and darker skin tone. Likewise in 2017 Gillinov et al. [26] compared heart rate from ECG,
a chest strap monitor and four wrist worn devices under four different exercises. They

Treadmill exercise gave best results while elliptical trainer gave the worst.
Validation of heart rate recordings using PPG from recording sites other than the wrist has
also been explored. In 2012 Gregoski et al. [28] compared heart rate from contact PPG
using smartphone camera with ECG and pulse oximeter readings at conditions of rest.
They found strong correlation between all three readings and that the reading from the
PPG and the ECG fulfilled the 95% limit of agreement on a Bland Altman plot. Mitchell
et al. [53] investigated in 2016 a smartphone application on both Android and IOS for
measuring fingertip HR using the camera and compared it to a chest strap HR monitor.
They found high degree of test-rest reliability with intraclass correlation values >0.80 for
the Android device and the chest strap. Both the Android and IOS devices showed high
degree of validity (>0.90) to the chest strap on Bland-Altman plots. Similarly in 2017 Van-

HR application and two FDA-declared HR measuring devices in terms of both heart rate
and heart rate variability. Furthermore, in 2017 Coppetti et al. [17] tested four commer-

using non-contact PPG) and compared to standard ECG heart rate recordings. They did
find substantial difference between the applications and reported a mean absolute error
between 2.0% and 8.1% and a Pearson correlation from 0.61 to 0.96. Fingertip/contact

2.4
Heart Rate as a Biomarker
A biomarker is a measure that reflect some biological state or condition [77]. The develop-
ment of wearable heart rate monitors provide the potential for easy every day monitoring
of the hear rate that might contribute to early detection of unhealthy conditions or dis-
eases. The following sections will outline some of the findings that are correlated with
the Heart Rate (HR) and Heart Rate Variability (HRV). Firstly the approximated normal
metrics are reviewed.
According to the American Heart Association the normal resting heart rate lies between
60 bpm and 100 bpm [7]. Highly active people usually have a lower RHR because their
heart muscle often is in better condition. Non medical factors that can elevate the heart
rate includes air temperature, stress, anxious emotions and obesity or high weight.
The heart rate variability is often used to access the physiological and pathological state of
the heart. Different metrics of HRV from the time and frequency domain is used. The sim-
plest time domain metrics includes the mean (NN) and the standard deviation (SDNN) of
the Interbeat Interval (IBI). These are however affected by the measuring context includ-
ing the length of recording, the age and gender of the subject and the subject’s baseline
HRV [72, 73]. A general thumb rule states that a healthy heart is chaotic, which means
that high variability between the IBI is considered better. However, the literature clearly
states that caution should be taken since some unhealthy pathology also produce higher
HRV. A study by Nunan et al. [55] did a meta analysis of 21438 healthy adults to suggest
standardized values of HRV. They found a mean HRV value (NN) of 926±90 ms and a
SDNN of 50±16 ms.
2.4.1
Cardiovascular Disorders (CVD)
Prolonged elevated heart rate can cause stress and inflammation to the heart that might
result in severe cardiovascular effects [83]. However, changes to both HR and HRV can
also be a result of cardiovascular dysfunction and can therefore act as an early predictor
to certain diseases.
Hsia et al. [35] found that higher resting heart rate in females was associated with coronary
events like myocardial infarction and coronary death. Another 18 years study of 6,518
healthy subjects by Jensen et al. [38] suggests that increased RHR is an independent
risk factor for both cardiovascular and all cause mortality. This is supported by the work
of Kristal et al. [45].
2.4.2
Psychological/Mental Disorders
The autonomic nervous system plays a key role in the control of our heart rate [52], but
it also interacts with our cognition and emotions [18]. It is therefor relevant to review the
relation between heart rate and cognition and emotions. An extensive review by G. Forte
et al. [23] underlines the relation between heart rate variability and multiple cognitive func-
tions including memory, language and attention. They found that lower HRV is associated
with poor performance in all domains.
2.4.2.1
Stress
Mental stress is related to activity in the Autonomic Nervous Systems (ANS) [40]. As
described earlier the sympathetic part of the nervous system is responsible for our ”fight-
or-flight” response. This is an important primal feature designed to act its response in a

fast and brief manner, which enables us to escape danger and ensure survival. However,
in the modern society work conditions or personal relation, which in theory do not seem
life threatening, can trigger the same ”fight-or-flight” response over a prolonged period of
time. When this happens sever impacts can affect the body and mind including memory
loss and increased vulnerability to virus [40].
Tealman et al. [78] studied subjects at rest and during mental tasks and found an increase
in HR and a decrease in HRV during the metal task compared to rest. Vrijkotte et al. [86]
performed 24 hours monitoring of 109 male subjects during both work days and non-work
days. They divided the subjects into two groups of high and low overall stress based on an
Effort-Reward Imbalance questionnaire. They found that the high-stress group showed
a higher heart rate both on work and non-work days. However HR during sleep did not
differ between the two groups. Another study by Choi et al. [14] found that wearable
heart rate monitors can be used to detect stressful event during the day using features of
heart rate variability. A similar study [75] also used wearable devices and smartphones to
assess physiological measures and self-reported stress of 1,002 healthy subjects. They
found significantly lower HR on days with self-reported low stress compared to high stress.
Additionally, Geisler et al. [24] hypothesized and confirmed that higher HRV is related to
better subjective well-being.
2.4.2.2
Depression
A study by Kemp et al. [41] compared HRV between depressed and healthy subjects and
found a decrease in depressed subjects related to the severity of depression. Another
study by Saad et al. [69] also found that heart rate during sleep could be used to differ-
entiate between subjects with depression and healthy controls. They suggest that heart
rate measures during sleep can be used as a biomarker for depression. However, they
found strongest evidence when HR is used along with measures of sleep disturbances.
2.4.3
Sleep
Sleep is an significant health factor often understudied by researchers. Sleep is an es-
sential neurophysiological state that plays an important role for restoring brain and body
functions [37]. The two most common sleep disorders includes obstructive sleep apnea
(OSA) and insomnia [37]. Lack of sleep and poor sleep quality is linked to a number of
diseases and disorders. It is found to have an effect on the severity of cardiovascular dis-
eases, future obesity, hypertension, risk of diabetes, risk of cancer, bidirectional i terms
of psychiatric disorders, cognitive performance and overall higher mortality risk [37]. The
influence of sleep on cardiovascular diseases is found to be linked to daily patterns of
blood pressure and heart rate. Some sleep disorders like OSA also have higher preva-
lence and severity related to increasing age and is higher for men compared to women
[37].
A study by Barnett et al. from 2008 [10] found that poor sleep (defined as less than six
hours) on a single night in healthy subjects resulted in higher scores of depression, anxiety
and stress along with reports of poorer well-being. Furthermore heart rate at rest was also
increased.
A review by Stein et al. from 2012 [76] outlines the relation between heart rate, sleep and
sleep disorders. They found evidence that HR and HRV decreases in non-REM sleep
stages and increases in REM sleep. They also suggest that age might affect nighttime
cardiac activity, as studies showed higher HR in older subjects during non-REM sleep.

2.4.4
Nutrition and Lifestyle
Weise et al. [91] studied in 1986 the cardiovascular state during intake of alcohol. They
found that heart rate was slightly increase and heart rate variability was clearly reduced
shortly after the intake of alcohol. Ryan et al. [68] reports similar finding in 2002 but
suggest that the decrease in HRV is a secondary cause from the increase in HR rather
than the alcohol affecting the vagal nerve activity. A review from 2011 on the effects of
acute alcohol intake by Romanowicz et al. [66] supports the claim of decreased HRV in
healthy nonalcoholic subject. Five out the eight studies regarding HR changes reported
significantly increased HR after alcohol consumption while the remaining three reported
no significant change. The increase in heart rate is also supported in the later work of
Piano et al. in 2017 [58] who describes these findings as a results of a weakening of the
heart’s ability to contract due to the alcohol’s toxic effect on the muscles of the heart.
Daily activity and exercise is also an important factor to consider. In 1993 Sandvik et
al. [70] showed that high physical fitness was associated with lower mortality in men.
A 16-year follow-up study of males by Jensen et al. in 2013 [39] found that increased
RHR also increased the risk of mortality by up to 50%. Furthermore, they found that a
higher level of physical activity was related to a lower RHR. However, increased RHR also
increased mortality independent of physical activity, while smoking was found to contribute
as a borderline interaction that further increased the risk of mortality by 20% per 10 bpm
increase in RHR. A systematic review from 2018 by Reimers et al. [65] of 181 studies
found that exercise especially endurance training and yoga decreased RHR in both men
and women. They suggest that yoga might contribute to an enhanced parasympathetic
control which could decrease the RHR. Weight also plays a role for both the HR and HRV.
Weight loss is found to be related to decreased HR and increased HRV [95].
Finally, a study [94] from 2013 suggest that outside temperature affects our HRV. They
found a decrease HRV with increasing temperature. Another study [50] from 2016 found
an increase in heart rate for both warmer and colder outside temperature. They found
lowest heart rate for outside temperature between 22 and 28 ◦C.
2.4.5
Demographics
Umetani et al. [81] analyzed in 1998 260 healthy subject and found that heart rate vari-
ability decreases with age and the difference between gender also decreases with age.
Females under the age of 30 showed a lower HRV compared to males of the same age.
They also found that HR decreased with age, but with a slower rate. HR in females under
the age of 50 was higher compare to males at the same age. Higher HRV in males com-
pared to females and a decline with age is supported by the work of Agelink et al. from
2001 [2] and Voss et al. [85] in 2015. Agelink et al. [2] also reports a higher resting heart
rate in females compared to men up to the age of 55.
Another study from 2014 by Itao et al. [36] analyzed heart rate from wearable heart rate
sensors over a period of more than ten hours from 128 subject. Data collection included a

2.5
Large-Scale Analysis of Health Data
The literature and research regarding long-term HR is relatively limited, especially when
considering large-scale datasets. However, some studies have been conducted and will
be presented in the following sections.
2.5.1
Fitbit - An Extensive Heart Rate Dataset
Fitbit is a wrist worn activity tracking, that measures HR using photoplethysmography.
According to Fitbit they have the most extensive database of heart rate metrics in the
world and a press-release from 2018 [62] revealed RHR correlations to gender and age
based on up to 25 million users from a period of 18 month prior to the press-release. They
state that their female users on average had three bpm higher RHR than their male users.
Both male and female users had the highest RHR at the ages 40-49, with an increase
from the 20’s followed by a decrease. Furthermore their users in the United Sates and
Singapore had the highest RHR.
Resting heart rate measures from the Fitbit system have been validated and daily mea-
surements have been analyzed in a study from 2019 by Russel et al. [67]. The study
first validated the Fitbit system in relation to ECG from a chest strap, which showed good
agreement with an average error of 0.89 bpm. The study then included a total of 169
volunteers who were instructed to measure their HR using the Fitbit system for four days,
seven times a day, under different predefined awake and resting conditions. The results
showed a significant variation in the resting heart rate depending on the time of day. The
lowest RHR was found in the morning, just after waking up, while the highest was found
at midday before lunch.
2.5.1.1

89 years and a BMI from 15 to 50.

Linear interpolation was used for weeks that did not include any data. For each user and

2.5.2
Additional Large-Scale Data Studies
A study from 2017 by Althoff et al. [5] investigated a dataset of physical activity based
on smartphone accelerometry from more than 700,000 subjects in 46 countries. The
data included minute-by-minute step recordings from a smartphone application. They
found that the average user recorded 4,961 steps per day. The study aimed at analyzing
the inequality of activity, based on the step recording, using the Gini coefficient of the
distribution of the activity of a country’s population. They found that the activity inequality
is a better predictor of obesity in a country than the average recorded steps. They also
found that females activity was reduced in countries with high activity inequality compared
to the men. They therefor observed that high activity inequality can be driven by a gender
activity gap. Furthermore they found that cities with more walkable environments was
associated with lower activity inequality.
A study from 2018 by Kim et al. [44] analyzed data from more than 56,000 subjects who
self-measured there blood pressure over a period of minimum one month. They analyzed
the Blood Pressure Variability (BPV) and found a pattern that is influenced by season of
the year, day of the week and time of the day. E.g. they found significant higher BPV in
the weekdays than the weekend. They also found that BPV is higher for females than
men, even when adjusting for higher life expectancy for women and that BPV increases
with age.
2.6
Summary
The heart rate is controlled by the autonomic nervous system but physiological and men-
tal effects plays a role too [52]. Besides demographics like gender and age [81, 2, 84],
stress and sleep seem to have an effect on both heart rate and heart rate variability [37,
10, 78, 86, 14, 75, 24]. Stress and poor sleep increases the heart rate but is also found to
be related to more severe conditions like depression and anxiety [10]. Modern technology
uses photoplethysmography in consumer wearable devices and now enables these mea-
surements to be implemented in users’ every day life. Studies [57, 87, 88, 74, 26] confirm
that these recordings have potential to provided the same quality data as clinical electro-
cardiography recordings. A few studies [62, 67, 60] have investigated these recordings
in a large scale manner including thousands of users. These studies found higher HR in
females compared to males along with a decrease in HR with age.

3.2.2
Body Effort Data
The Body Effort data subset is a metric used in the user application claiming to assess
how well the user manages stress throughout the day [90]. The Body Effort recordings
are a measure between 0 and 100 and the value should be interpreted as outlined in Ta-
ble 3.2. The method for deriving these values are bases on a technique developed by
Firstbeat Technologies Ltd. [22]. The method uses the raw HR data to derive the HRV
and its following continuous variables; the root mean square of successive RR-intervals,
the high frequency power, the low frequency power and the amplitude of the respira-
tory sinus arrhythmia. The method then applies neural network to obtain respiration rate,
oxygen consumption and excess post-exercise oxygen consumption. The data are then
segmented into periods and categorized to one of the following states; physical activity,
recovery, stress or unrecognized. Physical activity is detected based on high oxygen
consumption and movement from accelerometer data. The method enables to further
distinguish between exercise physical activity, daily physical activity and recovery from
physical activity. Non-exercise segments are detected as recovery based on low HR and
great, uniform HRV. Stress is detected based on elevated HR, decreased HRV and low
respiration. A segment is categorized as unrecognized if non of the above applies. Recov-
ery will often occur and will be detected during sleep, relaxation, rest and peaceful work.
The Body Effort metric translates this categorization into a value as seen in Table 3.2.

5.2
Deﬁning Resting Heart Rate
The heart rate varies a lot in relation to a user’s activity. Therefore, the average heart rate
might be a misleading metric to use for comparing difference between groups of users.
According to the American Heart Association [8] the resting heart rate is measured at rest
and not at sleep. The best time to measure RHR is in the morning before you get out of
bed. It is, however, not possible to identify the exact heart rate measurements from our
dataset that matches this condition. Some different approaches were investigated and
are described in the following in order to best estimate the resting heart rate from the the
data in this thesis.
The sleep dataset claims to provide information on the user’s sleep in terms of both
onset and end time of the sleep. This information is used to include heart rate recording
for each user only in the time interval during sleep. The end time of sleep is further used
as an attempt to meet the guideline from the American Heart Association, by including
”wake-up” heart rate samples of each user only in the time interval of 15 minutes before
and after the sleep ends.
The Body Effort dataset claims to distinguish between the user’s activity levels. This
enables another approach to include only heart rate recordings of each user from around
timestamps labeled as recovery and low stress which should reflect the user at rest. See
Section 3.2.2 on how this is defined. As each sample from the Body Effort dataset is
distributed with 10 minutes a part, all timestamps of the samples from the Heart Rate
and Body Effort subset were rounded to the nearest ten minute interval. Hereafter, the
samples were joined to only include heart rate samples corresponding to the user at rest;
i.e. either recovery or low stress. However, as stated in the documentation, most of a
users’ recovery occur during sleep. Therefore, another approach to meet the definition of
resting heart rate is to only include heart rate from recovery and low stress but not include
any samples during sleep.

5.6.3
Mann-Whitney U Test
The Mann-Whitney U test (also called the Wilcoxon rank-sum test) is a non-parametric
test that can be used to compare independent groups of continuous data [9]. The null hy-
pothesis of the Mann-Whitney U test is that two samples come from the same distribution.
I.e. low P-values will reject the null hypothesis meaning the two distribution are signifi-
cantly different. The test will be used to compare difference between groups of users
assuming independence between observations which means that no subject can appear
in both groups.
5.6.4
Wilcoxon Signed Rank Test
The Wilcoxon signed rank test is the dependent equivalent to the Mann-Whitney U test.
It is also a non-parametric test used to compare continuous data [48]. However, the
Wilcoxon signed rank test do not assume independence between the groups. The null
hypothesis of the Wilcoxon signed rank test is that two samples come from the same
distribution. I.e. low P-values will reject the null hypothesis meaning the two distribution
are significantly different. This test will be used to compare difference between groups of
data were a subject appears in both groups.
5.6.5
Pearson's Correlation Coefficient
The Pearson’s Correlation Coefficient is a measure of the linear relationship between two
variables [63]. The coefficient is a value between -1 and 1. A positive correlation coef-
ficient indicates a relationship of increase or decrease in both variables while a negative
correlation coefficient indicates an increase in one variable and a decrease in the other
variable. The closer to zero the coefficient is the weaker the relation between the two
variables. A problem with the correlation coefficient is causality meaning that it is not pos-
sible to determine which of the two variables is responsible for the effect and the coefficient
should therefore be interpreted in context of the data. Before calculation of the correlation
the data can be standardized in order to eliminated the effect of different scales. In the
current project the data were standardized by subtracting the mean and dividing by the
standard deviation.
5.6.6
Spearman's Rank Correlations Coefficient
The Pearson’s Correlation Coefficient assumes a linear relation between the variables.
However, if this is not the case the Spearman’s Rank Correlation Coefficient can be used,
which is a non-parametric version of the Pearson’s Coefficient [63]. The Spearman’s
Coefficient is based on ranked and ordered data instead of raw data and will describe
the monotonic relationship between two variables. A positive correlation will, like for the
Person’s Correlation, indicate an increase or decrease in both variables while a negative
correlation will indicate the inverse relation. The Spearman’s Correlation, however, does
not assume a linear relation and thereby not a constant rate of change in the two variables.
The same causality problem as described for the Pearson’s Correlation Coefficient follows
the Spearman’s correlation.
The library scipy and pandas from Python is used to compute the Pearson’s and Spear-
man’s correlation. scipy takes care of both ordering and ranking the data before returning
the correlation coefficient.

5.7
Panel Regression: Fixed Effects Ordinary Least Squares
Regression
The Heart Rate subset included data from multiple users over time. Panel data are the
term used within econometrics about data with this kind of temporal dimension [1]. Fixed
Effects Regression models can be a strong tool to analyze and estimate the effect of
different time-varying independent variables on the dependent variable of interest - in our
case the heart rate. The variables of a simple fixed effects regression model includes the
dependent variable (yi), the independent variables (xi), the fixed effects (αi) and an error
term (ϵ):
Yi = βXi + αi + ϵi
When dealing with panel data, like in our case, an additional temporal dimension is added
to the model variables. Both time fixed effects and entity fixed effects can take part in the
model. Entity fixed effects are constant over time but vary between individuals, while time
fixed effects are constant across individuals but varies over time. The fixed effects term
can therefore be thought of as the sum of the time and entity fixed effects:
Yit = βXit + ... + αi + ζt + γcym + ϵit
In our case we analyze variation from user (i), date (t), country (c), year (y) and month
(m). By including these fixed effects we account for any confounding factors that could
be caused by user-level or seasonal effects.
The objective of our panel regression modeling strategy is to assess whether within-
person variation in device use is associated with changes in nighttime heart rate. This
i done by estimating the coefficients of the model in order to determine if a change our
dependent variably Y over time is correlated with the one or more of the independent
variables X. The coefficients are evaluated by computing standard errors, t-statistics and
confidence intervals.
The error term should be uncorrelated with all observations. However, autocorrelation
often appear within entities - meaning a variable might be correlated with a delayed version
of itself. This is allowed for both the independent variables and the error term. If this is the
case, clustered standard error can be used instead of the simple standard error, which
will account for the autocorrelation. Clustered standard errors will also account for any
heteroskedasticity, which is the occurrence of varying spread of the residuals related to a
change in one or more variables. E.g. we might expect geographical and yearly trends
will cause a systematic variance in the spread of the residuals.
5.7.1
Ordinary Least Square regression
The coefficients of our model will be estimated using the Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)
method. The method works by minimizing the sum of squares of the difference between
the dependent variable and the values predicted by the model, i.e. the residuals. RStudio
is used to execute this part of the analysis using the package fixest and the function feols.

5.7.2
Model Variables
Our regression model will aim at explaining changes is heart rate due to sleep quality and
smartphone screen time along with a number of fixed effects. In this context the effect
of smartphone screen time on sleep duration is also estimated. The implemented model
are briefly described below.
Data are prepared in Python and imported to RStudio. The data were prepared as csv
files with rows of observations from each user and each day. Each column of the csv files
present a variable for the regression model.
Effect of Sleep Duration on Next Day Heart Rate
The first model estimates next day heart rate (Y) from sleep duration (X), including the
fixed effects; user ID (α), date (ζ) and a concatenated variable representing the year,
month and country (γ).
Yit = βXit + αi + ζt + γycm + ϵit
(5.2)
Effect of Daily Screen Time on Sleep Duration
The second model estimates sleep duration (Y) from daily screen time (X), including again
the fixed effects: user ID (α), date (ζ) and a concatenated variable representing the year,
month and country (γ).
Yit = βXit + αi + ζt + γycm + ϵit
(5.3)
As robustness check the same day heart rate is included as an independents variable.
Additionally the daily screen time is substituted with nightly screen time to test if this is a
better predictor than the daily screen time.
Effect of Daily Screen Time on Heart Rate at Sleep
The third model estimates the heart rate at sleep (Y) from daily screen time (X), including
again the fixed effects: user ID (α), date (ζ) and a concatenated variable representing the
year, month and country (γ).
Yit = βXit + αi + ζt + γycm + ϵit
(5.4)
Like above the same day heart rate and the screen time at night is used for robustness
checks.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
In this chapter I will review and summarize the results presented in Chapter 6 and Chap-
ter 7. In the first section the approach of estimating the resting heart rate from the data
used in this thesis will be discussed. Then follows two sections outlining and discussing
the results found for heart rate in relation to user demographics and seasonal trends. A
section then follows presenting the results of the explorative analysis on how behavior like
exercise, sleep and smartphone usage can impact the heart rate. Finally, a discussion
on large-scale data, a summary and an outlook for future work follows.
8.1
Measuring Resting Heart Rate
The resting heart rate can be a valuable measure to evaluate overall health and mental
state [73]. The heart rate varies during a day, it goes up when your are physically active
and drops when your are at rest [67, 73]. An increase in heart rate can also be a natural
reaction to stressful situations [40]. However, a trigger for alert can be when the heart rate
still shows elevated values during periods of rest. It can be a sign of unhealthy lifestyle
[70], mental stress [75], depression [41, 69] and cardiovascular dysfunction [38, 45].
Most literature on heart rate focus on the resting heart rate [65, 35, 38, 67, 60] and the
measures are conducted under controlled conditions to ensure data consistency. How-
ever, the data used in the work of this thesis did not allow for this control of the users’ data
collection. A main factor that could interfere with the heart rate data are physical activity.
Exercising on a regular basis is in general considered more healthy [20], but a high level
of exercise would be reflected as an increase in a user’s average heart rate. Using the
entire dataset of heart rate can therefor lead to the risk of falsely concluding that users
have poorer health due to their increased heart rate, when it in fact is caused by an active
lifestyle.
Section 5.2 describes different attempts for estimating the resting heart rate. As seen in
Figure 5.1, which show the average heart rate over time, the methods yield very different
results. In order to best estimate the resting heart rate, for this project, the data subset
Body Effort was utilized. According to the documentation this metric provides information
about the users’ level of activity based on heart rate and accelerometer recordings among
others. By matching timestamps of low activity with timestamps in the heart rate data a
collection of heart rate at rest was isolated. However, there is a risk associated with this
method of falsely detecting periods of rest as physical activity due to increased heart rate,
caused by a user’s poor health and not physical activity. This would misleadingly drive
down the resting heart rate as the resting periods with higher heart rate is excluded.
Sleep is important for restitution [37] and the heart rate during sleep was therefore thought
to be a good measure to include. The sleep data subset allowed for this by providing onset
and duration of the users’ sleep, assuming that sleep was recorded accurately. Periods
falsely recorded as sleep could lead to a risk of increasing the average heart rate at sleep.

8.2
Demographics and Heart Rate
The analysis of this project showed significant differences in heart rate between gender,
age groups and BMI. Females consistently have a higher heart rate than males. The
results seen in Figure 6.1 show a difference of two bpm between males and females.
Males have a median resting heart rate of 67 bpm and females have a median resting
heart rate of 69 bpm. This is in agreement with results of other studies [2, 62, 60]. An
explanation could be that females have a smaller heart than males and therefore the heart
needs to pump blood at a faster rate to provide blood to the body. The smaller heart size
in females is more likely caused by hormonal differences rather than body size [59].
Age is highly related to heart rate as suggested both in the literature [81, 2, 85] and ind
the analysis of this project. As seen in Figure 6.2 heart rate decreases with age. Heart
rate during sleep differs from the average heart rate and the resting heart rate. Heart rate
during sleep indicates an increase in heart rate until the age of 40 followed by a decrease.
This trend seen in the heart rate during sleep is supported by other studies [76]. A reason
that this trend is not as clear in the resting heart rate could be that our method for extracting
periods of rest falsely includes physical activity. This risk would be highest among highly
active users, which can be assumed are the case for the younger users. It is therefore
difficult to state whether the results are caused by the difference between generations or
by physical activty.
Overall the demographic results match well with existing literature [60, 67]. This validates
our dataset and indicates realistic findings in the current thesis.
8.3
Geography, Seasons and Heart Rate
Users in the dataset were located in countries all around the world. Most users were
located in Japan, Germany and Sweden. Warmer climate countries showed the highest
median heart rate, as seen in Figures 6.6a, 6.7a and 6.8a. This could be due to warmer
body temperature which lead to dilated blood vessels [13]. When the vessels dilate the
blood pressure decrease if the heart rate is unchanged so the heart rate must increase to
sustain a normal blood pressure.
The time of the year, day of the week and even hour of the day is also reflected in the
heart rate. As seen in Figure 6.16a the average heart rate is lowest during the winter. This
is also seen in similar studies of the Fitbit data [60]. Furthermore, the daily trend seen
in Figure 6.17 mostly agrees with prior studies using the Fitbit system [67]. The heart
rate in my work is found to be lowest in the morning and highest in the afternoon, while
the results of the Fitbit analysis showed highest heart rate at midday. Weekly trends are
also evident from our analysis and show highest heart rate on the weekends. This could
be due to alcohol consumption, which studies have found to have an increasing effect
on heart rate [91, 58, 66, 68]. Alcohol could also explain the peaks in heart rate seen at
new year’s. This peak is seen in the resting heart rate and in the heart rate during sleep,
see Figure 5.1. From Figure 6.19 we see that this peak at new year’s only appears on
the European continent, which, according to the World Health Organization (WHO), is the
union with the highest alcohol consumption [92]. Since these peaks are most evident in
the heart rate at sleep it could also be an effect of data collection and falsely classifying
data collected at the late nights at new year’s as sleep.

8.4
Behavioral Effects
You can not change your biological gender, the country you are born in or your age.
However, there are other parts of our every day live we control that can influence our
health which might be reflected in our heart rate. The dataset used in this project provided
the opportunity of exploring aspects of a user’s behavior and its relation to heart rate.
Three behavioral metrics were studied including; sleep, exercise, and smartphone screen
time. A correlation analysis using Spearman’s correlation coefficient suggest a positive
correlation between resting heart rate and exercise, and a negative correlation between
resting heart rate and sleep. Smartphone screen-time showed no correlation with heart
rate in this analysis. The results are seen in Figure 7.7d which shows the distribution of
Spearman’s correlation coefficients from each user.
8.4.1
Exercising
Exercising is considered to improve health. Daily exercise can strengthen the muscles
of the heart [20] and thereby increase the amount of blood pumped to the body at every
contraction. This could explain why studies suggest that exercising lowers your heart rate
[70, 39, 65].
Lower heart rate in relation to high exercise levels is found in the analysis of this project.
Figures 6.9 to 6.11 show the average heart rate for different measures of exercise in terms
of daily active time, number of daily steps and distance moved. The figures are the result
of a cross-sectional analysis by computing overall averages for each user. The figures
clearly show that users with an overall higher average level of exercise have a lower heart
rate. The greatest difference is observed in the heart rate during sleep, which drops up
to seven beats per minute for users averagely exercising more than 90 minutes a day.
An issues of causality can not be ruled out; does exercising lower your heart rate or do
people with a lower heart rate just exercise more. The Spearman’s correlation analysis
seen in Figure 7.7d indicates a small positive correlation on a user level, which actually
yields that exercising more will increase your heart rate and exercising less will decrease
your heart rate.
In order to address this question further a subset of users were extracted; users with the
highest increase and highest decrease in resting heart rate from the first to last day of data
collection. Figure 7.1 shows the resting heart rate for each day for this subset of users and
the increasing and decreasing trend is very clear. However, Figure 7.2 does not show a
clear increase in exercise level for users with a decreasing heart rate or a clear decrease
in exercise level for users with an increase in heart rate, as we expected based on the
literature [70, 39, 65]. This analysis correlated the entire time series of heart rate samples
with the entire time series of exercise samples. However, we expected that the change
in heart rate is not instantaneous but occurs with some delay after a consistent change
in exercise routine and that day-to-day fluctuations might occur. These fluctuations can
impact the correlation. To obtain a more smooth collecting of heart rate and exercise
samples, where day-to-day fluctuations are suppressed, an analysis of an optimal moving
average window size was performed. The results are seen in Figure 7.6 and show that
the highest correlation between resting heart rate and exercise, in terms of daily steps, is
obtained using exercise data from the previous 12 weeks.
It can be argued that only users with a change in exercise pattern is of interest in order
to analyze how changes in exercise impact the heart rate. Therefore, a subset of users
with either decrease or increase in exercise levels were extracted. By only considering

8.4.2
Screen Time and Smartphone Usage
Studies suggest that blue light and smartphone use impact the circadian rhythm [79, 49],
which is the body’s natural sleep-wake clock. Bedtime smartphone usage can also in-
crease the risk of having poor sleep quality [4], while restricted bedtime screen use can
increase sleep duration [29]. A study from 2017 also found higher heart rate when ex-
posed to blue light than orange light [96].
The initial cross-sectional analysis, seen in Figure 6.12, showed a significant reduction
in the resting heart rate up to two beats per minute for users having low smartphone
screen time. Similar tendency is seen for the smartphone screen time in the night, seen
in Figure 6.14, with a reduction of up to one beat per minute. However, one obvious
confounding factor must be included; age. It is commonly know that the elder generation
spend less time on their smartphone compared to the younger generation. It is very clear
in Figures 6.13 and 6.15 that the older generation drive down the total average for users
with low screen time.

8.4.3
The Effect of a Good Night's Sleep
During sleep the body and brain restores and bad sleep quality is linked to a number
of diseases and disorders, including cardiovascular and psychiatric disorders [37]. The
heart rate is lower during sleep and can be increased by certain sleep disturbing disorders
[76]. One study also found that the heart rate increased when sleeping less than six hours
[10].
Our cross-sectional analysis showed significantly lower resting heart rate for users sleep-
ing between seven and nine hours on average as seen in Figure 6.5a. This agrees with
existing literature [10]. If this was only an effect of insufficient classification of sleep in the
dataset a low heart rate should also be observed for shorter sleep duration. Furthermore,
age could be a confounding factor as the elder generation could be better at achieving
the recommended eight hours of sleep [54]. But the effect is still evident when adjusting
for age as seen in Figure 6.5c.
Our regression analysis, however, shows conflicting results. The panel regression model,
seen in Figure 7.12, shows the effect of sleep on the next day’s heart rate on a user level.
This analysis found that sleeping less than seven hours or sleeping more than ten hours
decreases the heart rate, while sleeping between seven and ten hours slightly increases
the heart rate. As this is an analysis of changes on a subject level the results could
be explained by the fact that sleeping less could result in more sedentary behavior the
following day and that this lack of physical activity is reflected as a decrease in heart rate.
This could also be the case when sleeping more than ten hours.
These results indicate that consistently sleeping seven to nine hours lowers your heart
rate, independent of gender and age, and having nights with too little or too much sleep
will not subsequently increase your next day’s heart rate but rather decreases it. Likely
due do to more sedentary behavior and thereby a reduced cardiac demand.
8.5
The Power of Big Data
The dataset used in this project allowed for unique opportunity to explore the physiological
measure of heart rate on a large scale. Most of the studies conducted by others use
relatively few subjects and short term measures of the heart rate [39, 65, 76, 10, 78, 86,
14, 75, 41, 69]. The disadvantage of these studies is that the results can be hard to
project on to the broader population. The advantage of using large scale dataset is that
the diverse subject population and uncontrolled conditions can ease the discussion on
whether the results are valid in a general sense. The diversity of the dataset population
can, however, be argued to be skewed toward subjects of high socioeconomic status [33].
Another disadvantage of large scale dataset is that the data collection is performed under
uncontrolled conditions, which complicates the procedure of explaining findings and rule
out that no other confounding factors are responsible for the findings.

8.5.1
A Discussion on p-values
Statistical significance along with p-values are terms often used in the world of medical
and biomedical science [33]. However recent discussions [33, 89, 64, 43] emphasize
the importance of caution when using p-values for final conclusions. Ranstam et al. [64]
argued in 2012 that confidence intervals are a better alternative to p-values. Confidence
intervals better reflect what effects are likely to exist rather than focusing on a single effect,
which can fall subject to the lack of adjusting for multiple hypothesis testing. Kim et al.
[43] outlined in 2016 three common misuses of p-values. They also mention the issue of
not adjusting for multiple testing, along with using the quantity of p-values alone to state
the level of significant differences.
Many of the issues with p-values are a consequence of large sample sizes. A large sample
size will always yield a smaller p-value [43]. It is, therefore, important to review the results
in context of the sample size and to consider the term ”meaningful effect sizes” preferably
before the analysis.
8.6
Summary and Outlook
The analysis in this project showed good agreement with already existing literature [62,
67, 60] on large-scale dataset in terms of heart rate in relation to demographics like gender
and age. This validates others new finding not supported by the literature. It is of common
social believe that exercising is healthy and that smartphone usage is considered bad.
The analysis do suggest that users who exercise more have a lower resting heart rate.
However, the causality of exercising leading to a reduction in the resting heart rate is not
clear.
The panel regressions model was implemented in order to exploit the available informa-
tion from the temporal dimension. The model explored the effect of sleep duration on next
day’s heart rate and the effect of smartphone usage on sleep duration and heart rate at
sleep. The analysis found some unexpected results, that might be explained by sedentary
behavior. However, the effects were relatively small. It can be argued that an increase
of three minutes in sleep duration caused by smartphone usage is relatively small, along
with heart rate changes smaller than one beat per minute. The simple models were imple-
mented as an addition to the preceding analysis. Some robustness checks were included,
however, this analysis could benefit from further exploration, e.g. by including a measure
of physical activity to investigate if this could be the main effect for describing the changes
seen in the heart rate.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
This thesis explored heart rate from a large-scale dataset measured by wrist worn de-
vices. The dataset included data from almost 100,000 users around the world over a
period from 2016 to 2019. Methods for handling and cleaning a dataset of this size was
utilized in order to prepare the data for analysis. The analysis focused on two aspects.
First, the demographic differences in heart rate, which showed good agreement with ex-
isting literature. Females have a significantly higher heart rate than males and the heart
rate decreases significantly with age. The analysis also showed geographic and seasonal
differences between the users. Second, the impact of behavior in terms of sleep, exer-
cise and smartphone usage was studied. The results showed decrease in heart rate for
users who averagely exercise more and users who sleep between seven and nine hours.
The effect of smartphone usage suggest a decrease in heart rate for high smartphone
usage. Further work should explore if this is an effect of smartphone usage or an effect
of sedentary behavior.

Abstract
The work of this thesis focuses on heart rate data collected from wrist worn activity trackers from
almost 100,00 users around the world. The work is highly novel as it explores an unprecedented
large-scale dataset that includes not only heart rate measures and user demographics including age,
gender, body mass index and home country, but also measures of behavior like sleep, exercise and
smartphone usage. We found significantly higher heart rate for females compared to males along
with a significant decrease in heart rate with increasing age. Our results also suggest that users who
exercise more and users who sleep seven to nine hours have a decreased average heart rate. We
analyzed the impact of smartphone usage on the heart rate and our results suggest that higher daily
screen time decreases the heart rate. We acknowledge, however, that future work should study if
this effect is a confounding factor of sedentary behavior.
