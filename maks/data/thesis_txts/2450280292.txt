Abstract
Wastewater treatment plants are notable contributors in the emissions of ni-
trous oxide, a potent greenhouse gas. In order curb nitrous oxide emissions from
wastewater treatment plants, control strategies has been developed by manip-
ulating the aeration of the activated sludge reactor. Two control strategies are
for the mitigation of nitrous oxide emissions from wastewater treatment plants
by the manipulation of the external carbon ﬂowrate. An extensive literature
study and evaluation of the current control strategies used to mitigate nitrous
oxide emissions is carried out to obtain greater understand of the important
processes and process parameters inﬂuencing nitrous oxide emissions. A control
strategy is proposed to minimise nitrous oxide production in the anoxic zone of
the activated sludge reactor without inducing oxygen competition in the aerobic
zone for predenitriﬁcation activated sludge reactor conﬁgurations. This is as-
sured by using nitrate removal eﬃciency as the controlled variable, and by using
the oxygen concentration to adapt the setpoint by a fuzzy-logic module. The
control strategy is able reduce the nitrous oxide emissions by 17.2%. Another
control strategy is proposed to complementing the current control strategies in
the mitigation of nitrous oxide emissions for postdenitriﬁcation conﬁgurations
by preventing total nitrogen violations. Total nitrogen is prevented by using the
both a proportional-integral controller and feedforward controller to control to-
tal nitrogen eﬄuent concentration. The control strategy reduces total nitrogen
violations from 98.5% to 0.23%.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Nitrous oxide (N2O) is a potent greenhouse gas with about a greenhous eﬀect
300 times greater than carbon dioxide (CO2) [1]. Moreover, N2O is also impli-
cated in the thinning of the ozone layer [2]. As of 2010, it is estimated that
29.5 million tonnes of N2O is emitted per year. 36% of the N2O emissions are
estimated to be caused by human activity [3]. Wastewater treatment plants
(WWTPs) are known to be a notable contributor to man-made N2O emissions
[4, 5, 6, 7]. N2O emissions only contribute to about 6-8% of the global green-
house eﬀect [8], however a continuous increase in N2O emissions is expected,
due to the ongoing growth of the human population and the increasing pace of
urbanisation. The control of N2O emissions from WWTPs is therefore consid-
ered in the eﬀorts to curb greenhouse gas emissions.
N2O is produced in WWTPs during nitrogen removal by several biological pro-
cesses.
Denitriﬁcation and nitriﬁcation are known to be the main processes
in the production of N2O in WWTPs. During aerobic conditions ammonium
(NH+
4 ) in the wastewater is converted to nitrite (NO−
2 ) and subsequently to ni-
trate (NO−
3 ) by nitriﬁcation. During anoxic conditions NO−
3 reduce to nitrogen
(N2) with NO−
2 as an intermediate by denitriﬁcation. The source and magni-
tude of N2O emissions from WWTPs are nevertheless still not fully understood
and subject for debate. N2O emissions also depend on several factors and have
been found to be highly variable in both laboratory (0-95%) and plant (0-14.6%)
Page 1
==PAGE:13==
scale [9]. Further research in the development of models that can accurately pre-
dicts N2O emissions is therefore still needed for the eﬀective design of control
strategies to minimise N2O emissions from WWTPs. Current control strategies
for the mitigation of N2O emissions from WWTPs are mainly based on manip-
ulating the aeration in the aerobic zone of the activated sludge reactor, which
have shown satisfying results [10, 11]. Another possible option in the control of
N2O emissions from WWTPs is to adjust external organic substrate added to
the anoxic zone of the activated sludge reactor on top of the manipulation of
the aeration in the aerated zone.
Chapter 2 introduces the current research associated with N2O production
and emissions during nitrogen removal in WWTPs.
Chapter 3 presents the
benchmark simulation models used to evaluate control strategies developed for
WWTPs. Chapter 4 presents and evaluates current control strategies used in
WWTPs, while Chapter 5 proposes two control strategies for the mitigation of
N2O emissions from WWTPs.

Chapter 2
Literature overview
2.1
Nitrogen removal in WWTPs
The most widely used process for the removal of nitrogen from the wastewater
is activated sludge treatment [12, 13]: The process uses a microbial community,
also known as the activated sludge, suspended in the wastewater to metabolise
biodegradable components.
Nitrogen present in the wastewater is mainly in
the form of NH+
4 with negligible amounts of NO−
2 and NO−
3 . Other nitrogen
fractions, like amino acids, amino sugars and proteins, are readily microbially
degraded to NH+
4 . Inﬂuent wastewater is treated in an activated sludge reactor.
An activated sludge system conﬁguration is illustrated in Fig.
2.1.
Spatial
separation is achieved in the activated sludge system for the creation of an
aerobic zone and an anoxic zone in order to induce autotrophic nitriﬁcation and
heterotrophic denitriﬁcation, respectively. The activated sludge treatment uses
autotrophic nitriﬁcation and heterotrophic denitriﬁcation to remove nitrogen
from the wastewater. During the activated sludge treatment N2O can be formed
and stripped to the atmosphere.
After the activated sludge treatment, the
activated sludge is separated from the treated liquor by a clariﬁer. Most of the
activated sludge is recycled back to the activated sludge reactor compartments
to keep constant biomass concentration in the activated sludge reactor. A small
fraction of the activated sludge is disposed of due to cell growth. The activated
Page 3
==PAGE:15==
sludge system also includes an internal recycle of treated liquor from the last
activated sludge compartment.

2.2
N2O production in WWTPs
2.2.1
N2O production by ammonium-oxidising bacteria
The major N2O production pathways during activated sludge treatment are
depicted in Fig. 2.2. Autotrophic nitriﬁcation is a biological process that oxidise
NH+
4 to NO−
2 and subsequently to NO−
3 with oxygen as the electron acceptor.
The oxidation of NH+
4 to NO−
2 is mediated by ammonium-oxidising bacteria
(AOB), while the subsequent oxidation of NO−
2 to NO−
3 is mediated by nitrite-
oxidising bacteria (NOB) [9]. The oxidation of NH+
4 to NO−
2 by AOB involves
hydroxylamine (NH2OH) as an intermediate [14, 15]. While N2O is not a direct
intermediate in the oxidation of NH+
4 to NO−
2 by AOB, N2O can form through
2 major pathways according to current research:
• AOB denitriﬁcation
• NH2OH oxidation pathway
Page 4
==PAGE:16==
Under oxygen limiting or complete anoxic conditions, NH2OH can act as an
electron donor in the reduction of NO−
2 and the ensuing reduction of NO to
N2O. The reduction of NO−
2 and nitrogen oxide (NO) to N2O is known as AOB
denitriﬁcation [16, 17, 18]. The reduction of NO−
2 and NO is catalysed by NO−
2
and NO reductase, respectively. N2O reductase is not been found in AOB, which
makes N2O the end product of AOB denitriﬁcation [19].
NH2OH oxidation pathway refers to the production of N2O as a byproduct in
the incomplete oxidation of NH2OH to NO−
2 [12, 16, 20, 21]. Extended studies
show that the oxidation of NH2OH can also occur with nitroxyl radical (NOH)
[22, 23, 24, 25] and nitric oxide (NO) [26, 27, 28] as intermediates. The unstable
breakdown of NOH can yield N2O. In addition to the unstable breakdown of
NOH, the reduction of NO formed during the oxidation of NH2OH is another
potential cause of N2O. The relevance of the NH2OH pathway in the production
of N2O in WWTPs is currently not fully conﬁrmed. Evidence has found N2O
production by the NH2OH oxidation pathway, when NH+
4 oxidation rates are
high [29].
The uncertainty in how N2O is produced by AOB is one of the greatest hurdles
in modelling N2O production, and thereby also in the control of N2O emissions
from WWTPs.
2.2.2
N2O production by heterotrophic bacteria
As depicted in Fig. 2.2, heterotrophic denitriﬁcation is the biological reduction
of NO−
3 to N2 by heterotrophic bacteria (HB) [30, 31, 32]. Heterotrophic denitri-
ﬁcation consist of 4 sequential steps with NO−
2 , NO and N2O as intermediates.
Under anoxic conditions organic substrate donates the necessary electrons for
the reduction of the nitrogen oxides. The maximum reduction rate of N2O has
also been estimated to be almost 4 times faster than the oxidation of NO−
3 and
NO−
2 under anoxic conditions [33]. NO−
3 and NO−
2 are therefore completely
Page 5
==PAGE:17==
reduced to N2 under typical anoxic conditions without N2O accumulation or
emissions. Changes in process conditions such as insuﬃcient organic substrate
or exposure to oxygen can lead N2O accumulation and emissions, due to electron
competition and oxygen inhibition, respectively.

2.3
Process parameters inﬂuencing N2O emissions
from WWTPs
2.3.1
Inﬂuence of dissolved oxygen on N2O emissions
Dissolved oxygen (DO) is a key process parameter inﬂuencing emissions of N2O
from WWTPs. N2O production from both AOB denitriﬁcation and the NH2OH
pathway are observed at low DO levels [34, 35, 36]. Furthermore, NOB have a
lower aﬃnity to oxygen than AOB, which leads to accumulation of NO−
2 and
an increase in the potential for N2O emissions.
Exposure to oxygen during
HB denitriﬁcation inhibits both synthesis and activity of N2O reductase, while
NO−
3 , NO−
2 and NO reductase are relatively tolerant to oxygen [37, 38, 39]. An
exposure to oxygen during HB denitriﬁcation therefore leads to a decrease in
N2O consumption and an increase in N2O accumulation. The active stripping
in the aerobic zone is also a major cause of N2O emissions. N2O is relatively
soluble in water. Negligible N2O was emitted during non-aerated periods. [40],
while gas-phase N2O emission data from WWTPs show that N2O emissions
from aerated zones are 2 to 3 magnitudes higher than non-aerated zones [5].
The N2O produced during aeration or accumulated under anoxic conditions in
the anoxic zone of the activated sludge reactor is therefore easily stripped in
aerobic zone.
2.3.2
Inﬂuence of NO−
2 on N2O emissions
NO−
2 has been observed to promote N2O production during both nitriﬁcation
and denitriﬁcation. NO−
2 promotes AOB denitriﬁcation, which therefore causes
increased N2O emissions. High NO−
2 concentration also leads to lower denitri-
ﬁcation rate, and thus possible accumulation of NO and N2O [41, 42, 43]. The
Page 7
==PAGE:19==
presence of NO−
2 has been found to inhibit N2O reductase [44]. The relationship
between NO−
2 and N2O emission is however not so simple. An increased NO−
2
concentration in a nitritation system has been observed to decrease rather than
increase N2O emissions [42]. NO rather than NO2 has been suggested to be the
true inhibitor of N2O reductase [45]. It has also been shown that free nitrous
acid (HNO2) is possibly the real inhibitor of N2O reductase [46].
2.3.3
Inﬂuence of organic substrate on N2O emissions
Limited availability of organic substrate, which is measured as chemical oxygen
demand (COD), during HB denitriﬁcation is an important parameter govern-
ing N2O production [47, 48]. Under limited organic substrate conditions, the
various enzymes that induce the reduction steps during denitriﬁcation compete
for electrons. Due to a lower aﬃnity to electron donors, N2O reductase is often
the weakest competitor. Hence, limited availability of organic substrate leads
to greater N2O production during HB denitriﬁcation.
A 30% N2O emission
originating from the anoxic zone has been reported for a continuous sequencing
batch reactor treating high strength wastewater with a COD/N below 3.5 [44].
However, excess organic substrate carried from the anoxic zone to the aerobic
zone induces aerobic growth of HB. HB competes with NOB for oxygen, which
leads to NO−
2 accumulation and therefore increases N2O production by AOB
denitriﬁcation. Moreover, excess organic substrate in the aerobic zone might
cause an oxygen limited condition, which can induce both AOB denitriﬁcation
and the NH2OH oxidation pathway.

Chapter 3
Benchmark simulation
model
The benchmark simulation models (BSMs) are benchmark tools for simulation-
based evaluation of control strategies for activated sludge WWTPs. In order to
standardise the evaluation of control strategies, the BSMs deﬁnes plant layouts,
simulation models, inﬂuent loads, test procedures and evaluation criteria.
The plant layout for Benchmark simulation model no. 1 (BSM1) [49] is depicted
in Fig.
3.1.
The BSM1 plant layout includes a ﬁve-compartment activated
sludge reactor, which consists of two anoxic compartments followed by three
aerobic compartments. The reactor compartments are followed by a secondary
clariﬁer. Two recycle streams to the ﬁrst compartment are included; an internal
recycle of treated liquor from the last compartment to allow the HB mediated
reduction of nitrogen oxides, and an external recycle of activated sludge from
the secondary clariﬁer to keep a constant concentration of biomass in the acti-
vated sludge reactor. A small fraction is disposed of as waste sludge, due to cell
growth.
Benchmark simulation model no. 2 (BSM2) is an extension of BSM1 by includ-
ing sludge treatment [50]. The BSM2 plant conﬁguration is depicted in Fig.
3.2. The BSM2 adds a primary clariﬁer, a thickener for activated sludge waste
from the secondary clariﬁer, a dewatering unit and anaerobic digester.
The
thickener separates water from the wastage sludge from the secondary clariﬁer,
Page 9
==PAGE:21==
which is recycled to the primary clariﬁer. The thickened sludge is decomposed
in the anaerobic digester together with wastage sludge from the primary clar-
iﬁer, which is followed by dewatering in the dewatering unit. The dewatering
unit yields 2 streams; a sludge stream for disposal and a nitrogen rich reject
water stream, which is recycled to the primary clariﬁer.

3.1
Benchmark simulation model with N2O pro-
duction
The benchmark simulation model with N2O production (BSM2N) is a further
extension of BSM2 by including N2O production [51]. Due to the N2O produc-
tion model uncertainty and in order to evaluate robustness of proposed control
strategies, BSM2N is found in 3 diﬀerent variants.
The 3 variants diﬀer by
the utilised N2O production models, which are the activated sludge model for
greenhouse gases no. 1 (ASMG1) [52], the two-pathway model no. 1 (2PM1)
[53] and the two-pathway model no. 2 (2PM2) [54]. The 3 diﬀerent BSMN has
the following nomenclature:
• BSM2Na with ASMG1
• BSM2Nb with 2PM1
• BSM2Nb with 2PM2
All BSM2N models include HB denitriﬁcation and the autotrophic nitriﬁcation.
HB denitriﬁcation is modelled as a four-step process from NO−
3 to N2 with
NO−
2 , NO and N2O as intermediates. All BSM2N models include autotrophic
nitriﬁcation and has NO−
2 as an intermediate in oxidation of NH−
4 to NO−
3 . The
models diﬀer in the inclusion of NH2OH and NO as intermediates as well as the
N2O produced from AOB.
The pathways modelled in BSM2Na is depicted in Fig. 3.3. BSM2Na only in-
cludes N2O production by AOB denitriﬁcation. AOB denitriﬁcation is modelled
as a two-step process where N2O is produced as a result of reduction of NO−
2
with NO as intermediate during the oxidation of NH+
4 . Autotrophic nitriﬁca-
tion is also modelled as a two-step process where NO−
3 is produced as a result
of oxidation of NH+
4 with NO−
2 as the only intermediate.
Page 11
==PAGE:23==
Figure 3.3: BSMN2a pathways.
The BSM2Nb pathways are depicted in Fig. 3.4. BSM2Nb includes both AOB
denitriﬁcation and the NH2OH oxidation pathway in the production of N2O
by AOB. NH2OH, NO and NO−
2 are intermediates in autotrophic nitriﬁcation.
AOB denitriﬁcation is modelled as a one-step process where NO−
2 is directly re-
duced to N2O during the oxidation of NH2OH. The NH2OH oxidation pathway
is modelled as a one-step reduction of NO to N2O.
The BSM2Nc pathways are depicted in Fig. 3.5. BSM2Nc also includes both
AOB denitriﬁcation and the NH2OH oxidation pathway in the production of
N2O by AOB, but NO is not included in autotrophic nitriﬁcation as an inter-
mediate in the oxidation of NH2OH to NO−
2 . NO is instead an another end
product in the oxidation of NH2OH. NH2OH oxidation pathway also includes
N2O production due to incomplete oxidation. Otherwise, BSM2Nc models AOB
denitriﬁcation and the NH2OH oxidation pathway in the same way as BSM2Nb.

Chapter 4
Evaluation of current
control strategies
Several control strategies have been developed and implemented for the eﬃcient
removal of nitrogen during activated sludge treatment. However, few control
strategies have been developed with the main purpose of mitigating N2O emis-
sions. This chapter presents and evaluates two control strategies currently used
during activated sludge treatment in order obtain further understanding of the
process parameters leading to N2O emissions. The two presented and evaluated
control strategies are the default BSM2 control strategy [50] and the fuzzy-logic
control (FLC) strategy for minimising N2O emissions [10].
The two control
strategies are referred as CS0 and CS1, respectively.
4.1
Default BSM2 control strategy
BSM2 [50] proposes a default control strategy (CS0), which is commonly used as
a reference control strategy. The CS0 conﬁguration consists of a Proportional-
Integral (PI) controller. The PI controller manipulates KLa in the fourth com-
partment in order to control SO at a set point of 2 mg -COD/L. The KLa in the
third is likewise set to the KLa value in the fourth compartment, while the ﬁfth
compartment is set to half of the KLa value in the fourth compartment. The
manipulated KLa is limited to values between 0 and 360 d−1. The PI control

strategy conﬁguration is depicted in Fig. 4.1

4.2
FLC strategy for minimising N2O emissions
Boiocchi et al. proposed [10] a FLC strategy with the main goal of minimising
N2O emissions during activated sludge treatment in WWTPs (CS1). The main
objective of CS1 is to slow down AOB denitriﬁcation in the aerobic zone by
achieving complete nitriﬁcation. Complete nitriﬁcation is achieved by ensuring
dissolved oxygen availability, while preventing excess dissolved oxygen carried
to the anoxic zone. Dissolved oxygen inhibits HB mediated reduction of NO−
3 to
N2. In order to ensure optimal dissolved oxygen availability, CS1 uses the ratio
between NO−
3 produced and NH+
4 consumed in the aerobic zone as the controlled
variable:
RNatAmm = SNO3,aer,out −SNO3,aer,in
SNH,aer,in −SNH,aer,out
(4.1)
Eq. 4.1 represents the microbial balance between NOB and AOB. A value of 1
theoretically indicates complete nitriﬁcation. In practice complete nitriﬁcation
corresponds to a value greater than 1, because of biomass decay and additional
organic nitrogen release.

temperature T, a fuzzy-logic module adapts the RNatAmm setpoint by using the

4.2.1
Fuzzy-logic control
FLC is a knowledge-based control system that utilises qualitative information,
rather than quantitative information, through a linguistic ruleset of the if-then
form. A generic FLC system architecture consist of the Fuzziﬁer, the Inference
engine and the Defuzziﬁer. Fig. 4.3 depicts a generic FLC conﬁguration.
Figure 4.3: Generic FLC conﬁguration [55].
The Fuzziﬁer converts crisp inputs, for instance the error signal e(t), into fuzzy
inputs represented by predeﬁned membership functions. Membership functions
are often deﬁned by rigid triangle or trapezoid shapes, but curved membership
functions is also used. Given the crisp inputs, membership grades are computed
for each membership function. The membership grade represents the degree of
membership to the associated membership function. An membership grade of 0
represents no membership, a membership grade of 1 represents full membership
and a membership grade between 0 and 1 represents a fuzzy membership. Based
on the fuzzy inputs and a linguistic ruleset deﬁned by the Fuzzy rule base,
the fuzzy output is inferred.
The fuzzy outputs, like the fuzzy inputs, are
represented by predeﬁned output membership functions. Lastly, the Defuzziﬁer
translate the fuzzy outputs to real numerical values.
Page 23
==PAGE:35==
The most simple and common Inference engine model is the Mamdani inference
system. The Mamdani inference system has the following form:
Rule k : If x1 is A1ka and x2 is A2k, ...,
and xN is ANk, Then yk is Bk
(4.2)
where Ank and Bk represents membership functions, xN and yk are fuzzy inputs
and outputs, respectively.
An advantage of FLC is the ability to control highly non-linear processes. FLC
also incorporates practical knowledge and experience from process operators.
The main disadvantage with FLC is the lack of a systematic tuning procedure
and suboptimal control due to insuﬃcient process knowledge.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and future work
6.1
Conclusion
An extensive literature study was carried out in order in the presented work,
in order to obtain greater understanding of the processes and process parame-
ters inﬂuencing N2O emissions from wastewater treatment plants. Furthermore,
current control strategies was evaluated with the help of benchmark simulation
models and used as reference for the newly proposed control strategies. Con-
trol strategies to complement the current control strategies in mitigating N2O
emissions from predenitriﬁcation and postdenitriﬁcation activated sludge reac-
tor conﬁgurations was proposed. The proposed control strategy for predenitri-
ﬁcation conﬁgurations aimed to mitigate N2O emissions, while that for post-
denitriﬁcation conﬁgurations tried to prevent eﬄuent quality violations. The
proposed control strategy for predenitriﬁcation conﬁgurations reduced the N2O
emission by 17.2%, while the proposed control strategy for postdenitriﬁcation
conﬁgurations reduced eﬄuent violations from 98.5% to 0.23%.
Page 44
==PAGE:56==
6.2
Future work
As noted in Chapter 1, the further research in the development of models that
can predicts N2O emissions is needed for the eﬀective design of control strate-
gies, since N2O production is still not fully understood. In order to cope with
the model uncertainly, the proposed control strategies can be validated with dif-
ferent N2O production models. The ﬁnal validation has to be done in practice.
Another topic for future work is the use of more advanced control technique,
like model predictive control or artiﬁcial neural networks.
The development of an overall performance index, which incorporates cost, eﬄu-
ent and emissions, is desirable, in order to more easily evaluate proposed control
strategies. A possible option is to translate eﬄuent quality and emissions to a
total cost.
Finally, improvements of the plant design and the treatment technologies are
another options in the mitigation of N2O emissions from wastewater treatment
plants.
