Abstract
The decomposition and formation of H2SO4 (H2SO4 −−⇀
↽−−SO3 + H2O) has an im-
portant role in high temperature industrial processes, but little is known of either
decomposition and formation of gaseous H2SO4 at these conditions. Therefore, this
thesis puts focus on investigating decomposition of H2SO4 by use of infrared spec-
troscopy in the temperature range 150-500◦C in an existing high temperature ﬂow
gas cell (HGC) setup. This includes test of the setup in general and test of the
feeding method of H2SO4 to the HGC.
It was found that the decomposition of H2SO4 is surprisingly fast and happens
already at temperatures slightly larger than 150◦C. Consequently, the decomposi-
tion of H2SO4 is very fast at higher temperatures. An H2SO4 feeding temperature
of 170◦C gave a workable compromise between having a high absorption signal
and minimization of H2SO4 decomposition. This gave a limited temperature range
where decomposition is feasible to investigate due to a high residence time in the
setup.
The traditional method of estimating H2SO4 concentrations in absorption spectra
showed to be infeasible with the available setup. An alternative method was sug-
gested, which proved to be useful in determining H2SO4 concentrations from the
absorption spectra.
Reaction rate constants in the temperature range between 170-200◦C were esti-
mated by interpretation of the experimental results by simulations in CHEMKIN
and an analysis of the uncertainties showed that large deviations occurred, espe-
cially at temperatures of 190◦C and 200◦C. At these temperatures, the average
concentrations in the HGC of H2SO4 and SO3 approach the equilibrium concentra-
tions, thus leading to higher uncertainties.
A band of upper and lower values of the kinetic parameters (pre-exponential factor
A and activation energy Ea) for both the decomposition and formation of H2SO4
were calculated. Since the temperature range is too narrow (30◦C), it is infeasible
to determine a trustworthy value of Ea for the decomposition of H2SO4. Therefore,
a value of 43 kcal mol−1 was estimated from literature, which gave a resulting A
factor of 4 · 1019 s−1 (Ab = 6.3 · 1017 cm3 mol−1 s−1 and Eab = 21.5 kcal mol−1 for
the formation of H2SO4). To estimate more exact values of the kinetic parameters,
the setup has to be improved to investigate decomposition of H2SO4 in a broader
temperature range than 170-200◦C.

Background
Formation and decomposition of sulfuric acid (H2SO4) plays an important role in
many industrial processes. Sulfuric acid is an important bulk chemical, used in the
fertilizer industry and other industrial processes. In combustion, oxidation of or-
ganically bound sulfur in the fuel leads to formation of sulfur oxides. These oxides
(mainly SO2) can oxidize to SO3 and, in the presence of water, form sulfuric acid,
which acts as a corrosive agent. The oxidation of SO2 to SO3 takes place through a
sequence of gas-phase reactions which are comparatively well established, but the
formation of gaseous H2SO4 has only been studied at low temperature relevant for
atmospheric chemistry. Here the mechanism of formation involves an SO3·H2O in-
termediate that in turn reacts with another SO3 or H2O molecule to form H2SO4.
This low temperature mechanism cannot be extrapolated to higher temperatures
where the SO3·H2O intermediate is unlikely to be stable, and little is known of
either formation or decomposition of gaseous sulfuric acid at these conditions. A
determination of the mechanism and rate constant for the reaction SO3 + H2O
(+M) = H2SO4 (+M) at elevated temperature would have important implications
for the ability to predict formation, as well as thermal decomposition, of sulfuric
acid in high-temperature systems.
xv
==PAGE:16==
Objective
The aim of this project is to characterize experimentally the thermal dissociation of
gaseous sulfuric acid as a function of temperature. Experiments will be conducted
in a laboratory reactor, using IR-based detection of SO2, SO3, and H2SO4 in the
gas-phase. Based on the measurements, the mechanism of reaction and the rate
constant will be determined.
Content
A literature study of formation and decomposition of gaseous H2SO4 will be con-
ducted. The kinetics of thermal dissociation of H2SO4 will be studied in a specially
designed reactor. The reactor is constructed to obtain a long optical pathway, al-
lowing IR-based detection of SO2, SO3, and H2SO4 in the gas-phase. Based on the
experimental results, the mechanism and rate constant of H2SO4 dissociation will
be identiﬁed. From the equilibrium constant, also the rate constant of the reverse
reaction, formation of H2SO4 from reaction of SO3 with H2O, will be calculated.

1. Introduction
Sulfuric acid (H2SO4) is a highly important commodity chemical widely used in
industry, among these for making phosphate fertilizers (60 % of total consump-
tion) as the far largest constituent [King et al., 2013]. Besides being an important
chemical, H2SO4 has long been recognized as an important constituent of atmo-
spheric aerosols and formation of ”acid rain” [Chen and Plummer, 1985], whereby
formation of H2SO4 is comparatively well established at these conditions, i.e. low
temperature. For instance, Lovejoy et al. [1996] have investigated the formation
of H2SO4 in the temperature range between 283 K and 370 K. However, the for-
mation/decomposition of H2SO4 has also a signiﬁcant role in industrial processes
carried out at elevated/high temperatures. In this report, the term ”elevated tem-
perature” is deﬁned as a temperature in the range 100-500◦C, whereas ”high tem-
perature” is deﬁned as a temperature exceeding 500◦C.
The starting point of the literature survey is to cover examples of some of the im-
portant uses of H2SO4 at these conditions. Thus leading to available information
concerning formation and decomposition of H2SO4 in scientiﬁc literature, ﬁnalized
by a discussion.
1.1
Interests in H2SO4 for Industrial Use
1.1.1
Corrosion in Waste-to-Energy Plants
In recent years the utilization of energy in waste through combustion technolo-
gies, Waste-to-Energy, has become increasingly important due to the demand for
reducing emissions to the atmosphere from the industry, driven by the legislation.
Instead of landﬁll the waste, it is possible to recover the energy content in waste by
incineration [European Commission, 2006]. One of the main technical challenges
for waste incineration plants is formation of deposits with high alkali and chlorine
content on the superheaters. At higher metal temperatures in the superheaters,
the deposited alkali chlorides constitute a signiﬁcant corrosion problem, especially
if the temperature exceeds 520◦C [Frandsen, 2005]. In order to reduce corrosion the
metal temperature of the superheater tubes is kept low, usually below 450◦C (cor-
responding to a steam temperature of around 400◦C) [Nielsen et al., 2000]. Thus
limiting the eﬃciency of electricity production in Waste-to-Energy plants, because
the maximum achievable temperature is governed by not having an impractically
high corrosion rate. Therefore, it is desired to increase the temperature, and there-

1.1.2
Hydrogen Production
The world energy demand is continuously growing together with the urgency of
reducing greenhouse gas emissions. In this hunt, the most attractive alternative to
fossil fuels nowadays is hydrogen (H2) from the perspective of pollution-free energy
and zero emission energy production system.
Hydrogen production from water is the most interesting route because water is the
most renewable raw material. However, direct dissociation of water is infeasible,
owing the small value of the equilibrium constant towards H2, even at high temper-
atures. Thus indirect thermochemical cycles are necessary. More than 200 diﬀerent
cycles of water splitting have been proposed, among these the sulfur-iodine (S-I) cy-
cle is seen as the most promising one [Zhang et al., 2014]. The three main reactions
in the cycle are:
SO2(g) + I2(l) + 2 H2O(l)→2 HI(aq) + H2SO4(aq)
(293 - 373 K)

With three consecutive sub-reactions for the decomposition of H2SO4 (aq), viz.,
(1) sublimation of H2SO4, (2) gaseous dissociation of H2SO4 (g) and (3) gaseous
dissociation of SO3 (g) [Zhang et al., 2014, Brutti et al., 2007, O’Keefe et al., 1980,
Dokiya et al., 1977, Barbarossa et al., 2006, Brutti et al., 2006].
As seen from the temperatures for each of the reactions, the decomposition of
H2SO4 is the most energy demanding reaction step of the cycle, more precisely the
dissociation step from SO3 to SO2. Further insight in the decomposition of H2SO4
is necessary in order to evaluate the possibility to lower the energy demand, thus
making the S-I cycle beneﬁcial.
1.1.3
Diesel Engines
In large marine two stroke diesel engines the combustion of sulfur containing fu-
els result in sulfur release. The sulfur oxidizes further to SO2 primarily, but also
substantial amounts of SO3 and H2SO4 form. The two latter species may cause
corrosion on the cylinder liner. This is a serious issue for the diesel engine manu-
facturer MAN Diesel & Turbo A/S especially when ”slow-steaming”, i.e. sailing at
reduced speed. Andreasen and Mayer [2010] from MAN Diesel & Turbo A/S report
that corrosion on the cylinder liner may be caused by condensation of H2SO4 if the
temperature drops below the dew point, or by direct transport of the acidic gas
phase species, e.g. SO3, into the cylinder liner lube oil ﬁlm, diﬀusing through, and
reaching the metal surface leading to corrosion [van Helden et al., 1989]. However,

Aim and Methodology
In the above, three diﬀerent processes are described, where formation and decom-
position of H2SO4 are of great importance. In order to improve the diﬀerent cases,
exact data for formation and decomposition of H2SO4 are necessary, inter alia, the
mechanism and rate constants at the speciﬁc temperature and pressure required in
the cases. The aim of this report is to determine the rate constant of the decom-
position of H2SO4.
This thesis include a literature review to establish the available kinetic data for
formation and decomposition of H2SO4 at high temperature. The decomposition
of H2SO4 is investigated in an existing setup, capable of measuring reactive gases.
An experimental section describes the gas ﬂow cell setup. To ensure that the setup
is suited for H2SO4 decomposition experiments, preliminary experiments are per-
formed to ﬁnd the optimal feeding conditions of H2SO4.
Diﬀerent methods for
quantifying H2SO4 and SO3 in the gas ﬂow cell are also assess and discussed. The
data are processed and reaction rate constants are calculated by use of simulation
software. Uncertainty and sensitivity analyses are performed to investigate the ac-
curacy of the rate constants. Finally, kinetic parameters for the decomposition and
formation of H2SO4 are calculated. The ﬁndings of the project and the assumptions
made are discussed, leading to a suggestion for the future work.

Formation of H2SO4 from SO3
Mechanism
The gas-phase reaction of SO3 and H2O to give H2SO4 was ﬁrst experimentally
investigated by Goodeve et al. [1934], claiming that the reaction of SO3 and H2O is
very fast. Since then, a large number of studies have been published, aiming at iden-
tifying the mechanism. Castleman et al. [1975] reported a rather fast bimolecular
reaction rate constant of 5.5 · 1011 cm3 mol−1 s−1, measured at low pressure (1.0 to
1.3 Torr) and room temperature. Subsequent investigations of the gas-phase reac-
tion by Holland and Castleman [1978], suggested that an adduct is formed between
SO3 and H2O, following the reaction:
SO3 + H2O + M →H2O · SO3 + M
(2.12)
Followed by rearrangement into the stable H2SO4 molecule:
H2O · SO3 (+M) →H2SO4 (+M)
(2.13)
With a corresponding energy barrier for adduct rearrangement of only about 3.3
kcal/mol. However, a later study by Chen and Plummer [1985] contradicts these
ﬁndings. They conclude that a relatively stable adduct (H2O · SO3) is formed (which
has been observed by microwave detection by Phillips et al. [1995]), but the rear-
rangement to H2SO4 is inhibited by a large transition-state energy barrier of ∼
23.2 kcal/mol for the highest level of calculation, indicating that the direct reaction
between SO3 and H2O to H2SO4 is unlikely [Hofmann and Schleyer, 1994], and the
adduct will more likely just dissociate back to reactants. The diﬀerences between
the energy proﬁles for the studies by Holland and Castleman [1978] and Chen and
Plummer [1985] are shown in Figure 2.3.

Reaction Rate Constant
As mentioned above, Castleman et al. [1975] were the ﬁrst to establish a rate con-
stant for the reaction between SO3 and H2O to form H2SO4 of 5.5·1011 cm3 mol−1 s−1
by use of fast-ﬂow experiments with mass spectrometric detection of the SO3 decay.
After the ﬁndings of Chen and Plummer [1985] for the energy barrier from adduct
to H2SO4 not being 3.3 kcal/mol [Holland and Castleman, 1978], but instead in the
range of 23-31 kcal/mol, the high value of the reaction rate constant from Castle-
man et al. [1975] was also questioned. Therefore, Wang et al. [1988] investigated the
determination of the rate constant again for H2SO4 formation, because of the less
credible results from earlier [Castleman et al., 1975, Holland and Castleman, 1978].
They point out that the kinetic measurements could be aﬀected by heterogeneous
reactions with SO3 and H2O on walls of the reaction tube. To minimize surface ef-
fects they coat the tube with halocarbon wax, giving a bimolecular rate constant of
3.4 · 109 cm3 mol−1 s−1 at 1-10 Torr and room temperature (no signiﬁcant pressure
dependency).
In earlier studies of the reaction, the rate constant is deﬁned as a bimolecular
rate constant (kII, unit: cm3 mol−1 s−1) with a ﬁrst order dependence on [H2O]. In
later studies, by Lovejoy et al. [1996] and Jayne et al. [1997], the rate constant is
given as a third order rate constant (kIII, unit: cm6 mol−2 s−1) with a quadratic
dependence on [H2O], dependent on temperature, derived based on the Arrhenius
expression.
The diﬀerent rate constants and conditions in literature are compared to each other
in the next section, in order to establish the degree of agreement between the data
and conditions.

2.1.2
Decomposition of H2SO4
The decomposition of H2SO4 consists of the two reverse reactions from the above
section; the reaction from H2SO4 to SO3 and the reaction from SO3 to SO2.
Dissociation of H2SO4 to SO3
Mechanism and reaction rate constant data for the dissociation of H2SO4 to SO3
are scanty in the literature. The study of Zhang et al. [2014] presents a detailed
kinetic mechanism for the decomposition of H2SO4. However, they just state that
H2SO4 is rapidly converted into SO3 and H2O at 773 K [Zhang et al., 2015, Takai
et al., 2011], thus only focusing on the decomposition of SO3. Brutti et al. [2007]
investigate the decomposition of H2SO4 by direct solar radiation experiments and
modeling. The model used is based on two postulates, viz., (1) the dissociation
of H2SO4 (g) to SO3 (g) is instantaneous and does not aﬀect the kinetics of the
process and (2) the kinetic path includes a single reversible step, i.e. SO3 (g) =
SO2 (g) + 1/2 O2 (g). Schwartz et al. [2000] studied the decomposition of spent
H2SO4 by carrying out experiments with and without catalysts. Once again, only

2.2
Test Methods for SO3 and H2SO4 Detection
In order to establish the mechanism and reaction rate constants for the decomposi-
tion and formation of H2SO4, a measurement technique has to be used to measure
the concentrations of the species in the gas-phase. This section investigates diﬀerent
analytical techniques used in literature for SO3 and H2SO4 determination.
2.2.1
SO3 Measurement Techniques
Measurement of SO3 is especially interesting within the power plant industry, where
the presence of SO3 can have some adverse eﬀects. Therefore, some extractive mea-
surements have been constructed in order to analyze the SO3 concentration.
However, SO3 is diﬃcult to measure for diﬀerent reasons: (1) typically low con-
centrations are present under combustion conditions, (2) high SO2 concentrations
are interfering the measurements, (3) surface reactions, SO3 tends to absorb onto
surfaces, resulting in loss of SO3 and H2SO4, and (4) SO3 starts to form H2SO4
at decreasing temperatures, below 500◦C, when H2O is present [EPRI, 2004, Fleig
et al., 2012].
The diﬀerent analytical techniques for SO3 concentration determination are de-
scribed shortly and their performances are discussed, mainly based on the compar-
ative studies by Jaworowski and Mack [1979], Cooper and Ferm [1994], Cooper and
Andersson [1997], and Fleig et al. [2012].
Controlled Condensation Method
The controlled condensation method is based on complete condensation of H2SO4
above the H2O dew point, followed by sulfate analysis. It means that the extracted
(ﬂue) gas is cooled down between its acid dew point temperature and the H2O
dew point temperature. This is achieved in a cooling spiral, followed by a ﬁlter,
catching H2SO4 aerosols, as shown in Figure 2.12. The cooler is then ﬂushed with
an isopropanol solution and bromophenol blue is used as an indicator afterwards.
The solution is then analyzed with ion chromatography, determining the amount of
SO2–
4 and thereby the concentration of H2SO4.

The just described techniques for SO3 determination are based on the determina-
tion of H2SO4. It is achieved by cooling of the gas stream, thereby converting SO3
to H2SO4. Consequently, the techniques are only useful for processes where the
SO3 concentration is of interest in a gas stream, with no considerable amount of
H2SO4. The methods are extractive, only representing point measurements of SO3,
assuming that the distribution of SO3 is homogeneous in the gas stream, which may
not be the case. For our purpose, it is interesting to determine the concentration
of SO3 as well as the concentration of H2SO4 in a gas stream. These techniques
are described in this section. There are diﬀerent spectroscopy techniques capable
of measuring both SO3 and H2SO4 simultaneously and continuously. Numerous

One of the main problems of this technique is to collect reference spectra of the
species. The analysis of the FTIR data depends on calibrating the observed spec-
tra against reference spectra. The reference spectra diﬀer at varying temperatures,
thereby giving errors, if the temperature between the reference spectra and the ob-
served spectra is too signiﬁcant. EPRI [2006] reports that if the reference spectra
are more than 50 to 100◦C diﬀerent than the collected spectra, the resulting errors
will be unacceptable. In their measurements, the reference spectra were available at
185◦C, and the temperature of the duct was 371◦C, yielding a diﬀerence of 186◦C.
Since only limited reference spectra are available, it is diﬃcult to assess the magni-
tude of the errors. In order to make the technique more accurate, reference spectra
of SO3 and H2SO4 have to be established at higher temperatures.
A patent by Baasner et al. [2012a] from Sick AG (also published as Baasner et al.
[2012b, 2014]) describes an apparatus, using a photometer, capable of measuring
both SO3 and H2SO4 concentrations simultaneously. Not much information is given
in the patent, inter alia, the performance of the apparatus, and nothing is found
about the apparatus in the available literature.

The FTIR spectroscopy technique generates an infrared spectrum, whereas DOAS
is a UV optical measurement technique. EPRI [2004] postulates that it should be
possible to use the DOAS technique to measure SO3 and H2SO4 concentrations.
However, modiﬁcations and UV reference spectra for H2SO4 have to be established,

2.2. TEST METHODS FOR SO3 AND H2SO4 DETECTION
The mechanism for formation of SO3 from SO2 is rather well established. Some
possible reaction pathways have been proposed with corresponding kinetic data
at elevated and high temperatures with good agreement between the data from
diﬀerent research groups. The reverse reaction, decomposition of SO3 to SO2, is
also quite well established, both with respect to possible mechanisms and reaction
rate constants at conditions of interest.
Diﬀerent reaction pathways have been
proposed in literature, and the most important ones are reported in the preceding.
When analyzing the formation and dissociation of H2SO4, the data of inter-
est are scanty.
The formation of H2SO4 from SO3 is only described at low
temperatures, mainly relevant for atmospheric chemistry.
There is agreement
in the literature that the mechanism of H2SO4 formation involves at least two
H2O molecules, giving rise to diﬀerent intermediate species, which are assumed
to be rather stable at low temperatures. However, it is unknown whether these
intermediate species are stable at higher temperatures (probably not).
Lovejoy
et al. [1996] report a rate constant expression valid for temperatures up to 370 K.
It is unknown whether this expression can be extrapolated to higher temperatures.
For the dissociation reaction, H2SO4 to SO3, no useful data are available in
literature. It is only stated that H2SO4 dissociates rapidly at high temperatures to
SO3 and H2O.
The reaction H2SO4 (+M) ⇌SO3 + H2O (+M) is suﬀering from extensive
lack of data in the literature. The determination of the mechanism and rate con-
stants at elevated/high temperatures could be useful in some industrial processes.
The scope of this project is therefore to investigate and determine the mechanism
and rate constant for the reaction at these conditions in order to cope for the lack
of data in the literature.
Diﬀerent methods for H2SO4 and SO3 detection have been described.
IR
spectroscopy is capable of identifying H2SO4, SO3, SO2, and H2O. Therefore this
detection method is used in this project for identifying the diﬀerent compounds
present in the gas-phase.

IR
The IR detection equipment consist of a Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) spec-
trometer (Cary 660 from Agilent Technologies) with a non-linear MCT detector
and an external light source (1385 ◦C), shown in Figure 3.1. During measurements,
the MCT detector has to be cooled, which is done by supplying the spectrometer
with liquid nitrogen. It is possible to achieve a spectral resolution of 0.25 cm−1.
Before running experiments, it is important to make sure that the light source,
HGC, and FTIR are in line. This gives the highest intensity and more accurate
measurements. The light from the light source goes through the HGC and into the
FTIR spectrometer, which generates an interferogram.
The interferogram is collected by the software Resolutions Pro.
Since an inter-
ferogram is raw data, it is then transformed into a single beam spectrum (Ii) by
Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) by the software Essential FTIR. For each absorption
spectrum, four interferograms have to be measured, i.e. two for the background
spectrum and two for the sample spectrum. For each spectrum, an interferogram is
ﬁrst collected with the net emission from the HGC + sample. The second interfer-
ogram is then collected, only with the emission from the HGC (the light source is
blocked). The emission only from the HGC is then subtracted from the net emission
from the HGC + sample. By subtracting the emission from the HGC, a more exact
spectrum is obtained, only containing the emission from the sample.
During the background (reference) measurements the HGC is only ﬁlled N2, and
during the sample measurements the HGC is ﬁlled with sample (H2SO4/SO3/H2O
dependent on the temperature in the HGC) + N2. As mentioned, Essential FTIR is
used to process the raw data to absorbance. The conversion from interferograms to
single beams are ﬁrst carried out, followed by subtraction. Lastly, the ratio between

the single beam background spectrum and the single beam sample spectrum is per-
formed, followed by conversion to an absorbance spectrum. The cross-sections or
concentrations can then be calculated from the absorbance spectrum, cf. Lambert-
Beer law. If having the cross-sections of a compound, an absorption spectrum can
be measured. Eq. 3.4 can then be applied to calculate the concentration, N, of
the compound present in the absorption spectrum. Consequently, an absorption
spectrum can be converted to cross-sections, when the concentration is known (also

Diﬀerent conﬁgurations can be chosen in Resolutions Pro in order to obtain in-

The spectral
resolution is a measure of how close two spectral lines can be distinguished. It
means that at a spectral resolution of 0.25 cm−1 compared to a lower, e.g. 2 cm−1,
more information is gained, i.e. it is possible to distinguish between two spectral
tops that are very close together, e.g. 1 cm−1. At higher spectral resolution (e.g.
0.25 cm−1) more noise in the spectrum is encountered (than for e.g. 2 cm−1). In
order to reduce the noise, more scans are required. The FTIR equipment obtains
a spectrum by performing a speciﬁc number of measurements, called scans. The
resulting spectrum is therefore an average of all the scans. When the number of
scans is increased, the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is increased as well, but the mea-
surement time is increased. It is therefore a trade-oﬀbetween resolution, SNR, and
measurement time. However, it is an advantage to increase number of scans, when
having weak absorbances. The SNR is proportional to the square root of the mea-
surement time, i.e. to the number of scans. If the number of scans (measurement
time) is doubled, then the SNR is only increased by a factor of
√
2. To double the
SNR, then the measurement time has to be four times longer.

3.3
Experimental Protocol
When performing experiments at the setup, the following experimental protocol is
used:
• Before experiments can start, the temperatures in the HGC, mixing reactor
and heated teﬂon line have to be at a desired temperature.
• The FTIR spectrometer is ﬁlled with liquid nitrogen for the MCT detector
which gives an operating time of around 8 hours.
• The light source is switched on.
• After 1 hour, the light source and MCT detector is stable. The N2 ﬂow to
the setup is changed in this stabilisation period to a desired ﬂow rate (∼15
minutes after switching on the light source).
• After minimum 15 minutes of N2 ﬂow to the setup, a reference spectrum is
obtained. Two interferograms are measured: one with light source on and one
with light source blocked.
• The sample container and lid are weighed, sample is poured into the container
and weighed again.

4.4
Development of the Feeding Procedure
The feeding procedure of H2SO4 to the HGC is a rather complex situation since the
vapor pressure of H2SO4 is quite low [Lide, 2015, chapter 6 p. 91] (Tb = 337◦C) and
it decomposes at temperatures below its boiling point (cf. the equilibrium graphs,
Figure 2.1). It means that in order to enhance the concentration of H2SO4 in the
gas phase, the temperature could be increased (also seen for the Stefan Problem
calculations), but an increase in temperature leads to decomposition of H2SO4.
There are diﬀerent requirements to the optimal feeding procedure: get as much
H2SO4 in the gas phase as possible to maximize the intensity of the absorption
spectra, but without decomposition of H2SO4. Another important parameter is
that the feeding concentration of H2SO4 to the HGC has to be constant in order to
obtain steady state absorption spectra.
4.4.1
Optimization of Sensitivity
The sensitivity has to be optimized, i.e. increase the signal compared to the noise
(SNR) without decomposing H2SO4.
From the decomposition screening experi-
ments it was, surprisingly, found that a signiﬁcant part of the H2SO4 is decomposed
at 200◦C. Hintze et al. [2003] use fuming H2SO4 (consists of SO3 and H2SO4) which
they titrate with H2O to get H2SO4 in the gas phase. By this approach it is not
possible to determine the feeding concentration. They state that at a feeding tem-
perature of 150◦C, the feeding rate of H2SO4 is maximized.
Diﬀerent approaches have been used to maximize the feeding rate of H2SO4. The
N2 ﬂow through the system is decreased to 1 Nl min−1 to maximize the signal. The
sample container is placed 24 cm inside the mixing reactor every time in order to

Another Procedure
In order to quantify the amount of H2SO4 in the HGC, measured by the FTIR,
it is important to establish the feeding concentration of H2SO4 from the mixing

experiments at 500◦C in the HGC is carried out with 170◦C in the mixing reactor.

A custom-made sample container for

the equilibrium calculations found in literature (Figure 2.1). The diﬀerence may

reaction is fast, and some unknown phenomena was found in the spectra at 500◦C.

rate of H2SO4 is too low, resulting in a weak signal, of H2SO4 absorption, in the
obtained absorption sample spectra. At slightly higher temperatures, the H2SO4
starts to decompose signiﬁcantly. This gives a limited temperature range where
decomposition is feasible to investigate, mainly due to a relative high residence in
the setup (∼15 seconds in the mixing reactor and ∼9 seconds in the HGC).
Diﬀerent experiments were performed in order to enhance the evaporation rate.

signal of H2SO4, but some SO3 is also formed.

Measurements at this temperature showed good repeatability.

time to reach steady state is around 100 minutes.
Here the sample container
and sample heats up and the evaporation of H2SO4 ﬁnds a steady level. It was
also found that H2SO4 deposits inside the teﬂon tubes. These ﬁndings make the
determination of the feeding rate by the weighing procedure very uncertain.
The intensity of the H2SO4 signal is estimated to be satisfying in order to

5.2
Reaction Rate Constant Simulations
The CHEMKIN 10131 release software is used to simulate the system in order
to establish reaction rate constants (k) for each temperature. The ﬁrst step is to
evaluate, which reactor type that has to be used for the simulations and the initial
H2O concentration in the system. Hereafter the simulation procedure is presented.
It is rather complex, since the decomposition of H2SO4 already starts in the mixing
reactor. By use of CHEMKIN, reaction rate constants (k) at each temperature are
estimated.
5.2.1
Evaluation of Reactor Type
To evaluate whether the HGC and mixing reactor can be thought as a plug ﬂow
reactor (PFR) or not, the Bodenstein Number (Bo) is calculated in Appendix F. Bo
is calculated for a temperature range of 170-200◦C for the HGC and at 170◦C for
the mixing reactor. Only half of the HGC is considered in the calculations, because
both sides of the gas inlet are identical. A total ﬂow rate of 1 Nl min−1 is used for
the calculations. The resulting Bo lies in the range between 22.2 and 23.3 for the
HGC and equals 31.3 for the mixing reactor. It means that the HGC and mixing
reactor can be considered as a PFR, as discussed in Appenix F [Levenspiel, 1993,
chapter 64 p. 16].
It is assumed that the decomposition of H2SO4 is a pseudo-ﬁrst-order, because
N2 is in great excess. It means that the position in an ideal PFR is equivalent to
the time in a batch reactor. It is therefore possible to simulate the PFR as a batch
in CHEMKIN. Consequently, the concentration proﬁles are as a function of time in
the reactor.

were carried out. However, some amount of H2O is present in the system during
the experiments.
The simulations of the system is dependent of the amount of
H2O. It is not possible to determine the H2O concentration in the system by the

Several diﬀerent sources of error exist for the described setup and analysis method.
Each error is responsible for deviations with varying inﬂuence. In this section, the
uncertainties associated with the experiments and the analysis method are therefore
examined.

5.3.1
Experimental Uncertainties
The temperature proﬁle in the HGC is not completely isothermal, particularly when
the temperature in the HGC is higher than the inlet gas stream. It could inﬂuence
the decomposition rate of H2SO4 and the determination of the concentrations from
the spectra. The temperature proﬁles for the HGC in presented in Appendix A.
The standard deviation increases with increasing temperature diﬀerence between
the HGC and the mixing reactor/gas inlet.
At 150◦C in the entire system the
temperature in the HGC deviates with only ± 0.22◦C. At 300◦C in the HGC and
200◦C in the gas inlet and mixing reactor, the deviation is ± 1.14◦C. In this project,
the maximum temperature diﬀerence between the inlet gas and the HGC is 30◦C.
It is therefore estimated that the temperature deviates ± 1◦C in the HGC. Some
temperature measurements in the mixing reactor were obtained, giving a deviation
of ± 2◦C for a set point temperature of 190◦C.
To supply the setup with N2 two Bronkhost mass ﬂow controllers (MFC) are used.
These have an accuracy of ± 0.1%. The MFC supplying the mixing reactor with N2
is calibrated for Ar, meaning that the MFC is corrected to N2 by use of a conversion
factor, C. The accuracy of a conversion factor is given as: 2%
C for C <1 (C = 0.716).
It means that the accuracy is ± 2.8%. This gives a total accuracy of ± 2.9% for
the MFC supplying the mixing reactor with N2 and ± 0.1% for the MFC supplying
the HGC with N2 (to establish the ﬂow windows). To maintain the ﬂow windows,
the diﬀerence between the two ﬂows has to be less than 6% (at 150◦C) according
to Figure 3.7, which is fulﬁlled with the determined deviations.
In order to minimize surface reactions, the reactor is made out of quartz. How-
ever, some parts, such as the ﬂexible gas inlet tube and the ﬂanges of the mixing
reactor, are made out of steel. At these metal surfaces, surface reactions of SO3
and H2SO4 can happen. These surfaces can promote the decomposition of H2SO4,
however, it is assumed that the eﬀect is insigniﬁcant.

The next step is to evaluate the impact of the uncertainties related to the way
of analysing the spectra. Two major uncertainty factors have been addressed: the
way of determining the H2SO4 absorption integrals and the determination of the
SO3 concentrations. The following deviations were estimated for the H2SO4 band
areas: 170◦C: ± 5%, 180◦C: ± 7%, 190◦C: ± 12%, and 200◦C: ± 17%. When the
area of the H2SO4 band changes, it aﬀects the conversion, X, which then aﬀects
the calculation of Ctot and CH2SO4 (for CSO3 = constant). In order to investigate
the limits of k, a combination of the deviations on the H2SO4 absorption integrals,
which gives the lowest and highest Ctot, are used. When Ctot decreases, k increases,
because the conversion is increasing, and vice versa. The methods described in
Section 5.1.1 and 5.1.2 are used for the analysis.

Implications of the kinetic parameters
The determined kinetic parameters for the decomposition and formation of H2SO4
can be used in diﬀerent (industrial) processes. MAN Diesel demands more reliable

kinetic parameters for the decomposition/formation of H2SO4 in order to build
reliable models for the sulfur species in the oil ﬁlm. The kinetics of the decomposi-
tion/formation of H2SO4 have only been investigated at low temperatures relevant
for atmospheric chemistry. This study is therefore a step towards covering kinetics
relevant to high temperature systems.
There is a general agreement in literature that the decomposition of H2SO4 to
SO2 passes through the formation of SO3: (R1) H2SO4 (+M) = SO3 + H2O (+M).
The kinetics of this study can be used to investigate other possible routes from
H2SO4 to SO2 by simulations in CHEMKIN. Rasmussen et al. [2007] established
a comprehensive reaction mechanism, including the most important reactions from
the H/S/O subset. This mechanism include the reaction: HOSO2 (+M) = SO2
+ OH (+M). A possible reaction pathway from H2SO4 to SO2 could include this
reaction and the reaction from H2SO4 to HOSO2: (R2) H2SO4 (+M) = HOSO2 +
OH (+M). A few simulations in CHEMKIN were performed by incorporating R1
and R2, yielding that the reaction pathway through the HOSO2 specie could have
some importance at high temperatures (1000-1500 K). However, more simulations
have to be performed in order to investigate the possibility of the importance of
this reaction pathway in the decomposition of H2SO4 to SO2 at high temperatures.
Uncertainties
In order to investigate the accuracy of the determined k values, a parameter un-
certainty assessment were performed, taking the most important issues into con-
sideration. The way of extracting the k values are associated with some diﬀerent
assumptions, which are rather diﬃcult to quantify the accuracy of. If an assumption
is not valid or inaccurate, it will aﬀect the resulting k value. These unaccounted
uncertainties and assumptions are discussed here.
Average concentration in HGC
The resulting sample spectrum, measured by the FTIR, represents an averaged
spectrum along the cell and consequently an averaged concentration along the cell.
It is assumed that the averaged concentrations measured by the FTIR can be rep-
resented by simulations in CHEMKIN of the concentration proﬁles, which are then
integrated and divided by the residence time. If the simulated concentration proﬁle
is not similar to the real concentration proﬁle in the HGC, deviations occur. If
the assumption about representing the averaged concentration by the integrated
method is not valid either, this also aﬀects the resulting k value.
Diﬀerent assumptions are also taken in order to predict the course of the con-
centration proﬁle in the HGC by simulations. It is assumed that the decompo-
sition of H2SO4 is a pseudo-ﬁrst-order reaction. If this is not the case, the con-
centration proﬁle in the HGC changes.
Since the initial H2O concentration is
low, it is likely that the decomposition of H2SO4 is not ﬁrst order with respect
to H2O. Another possibility could be that the reaction is second order in H2SO4:
H2SO4 + H2SO4 = SO3 + H2O + H2SO4. This would aﬀect the course of the simu-

[H2SO4] is low, it would result in very high A factors, which may not be physical

Activation energy and pre-exponential factor
The temperature range of the experiments is too narrow (only 30◦C) in order to
determine the activation energies accurately. Therefore, an activation energy for
the decomposition of H2SO4 was estimated to be 43 kcal mol−1. To investigate the

Sensitivity Analysis
In the sensitivity analysis, worst-case-scenarios were set up in order to investigate
how changes in the system aﬀected the k values due to limitations in the available
software. Worst-case-scenarios were set up for deviations in ﬂow and temperature
happening at the same time (U1) and also for the integrated H2SO4 band deter-
minations (U2). By doing this, the number of simulations needed in CHEMKIN
were reduced signiﬁcantly. It probably gives too high uncertainties. A more precise
determination of the uncertainties would have been obtained by determining the
uncertainties by only varying one parameter at a time and then use Eq. 5.17 to
calculate a total uncertainty or by performing e.g. Monte Carlo simulations. To do
this, a probability distribution for each parameter has to be assumed. If not using
the correct one, the resulting uncertainties are not accurate. Therefore, worst-case
scenarios were estimated to give a suitable representation for the uncertainties on
the k values.
Only upper limits for uncertainties in the [SO3] determination from cross-sections
were established (U3), but uncertainties related to uncertainties in the lower limit
probably exist.
It was assumed that the temperature only deviates with ± 1◦C in the HGC. The
temperature proﬁles in Appendix A show that a signiﬁcant temperature drop is
occurring at the entrance to the HGC. Since the concentration proﬁle in the HGC
is exponential, it means that the temperature aﬀects the concentration exponen-
tially. The slope of the concentration proﬁles are steepest at the entrance to the
HGC, which means that a lower temperature in this area aﬀects the course of the
concentration proﬁles signiﬁcantly. To include this eﬀect into CHEMKIN, the HGC
could e.g. be simulated as multiple PFR in series.

Suitability of the experimental setup
After a lot of experiments performed at the setup, it was found that some major
issues limited the suitability of the setup to investigate decomposition of H2SO4.
At lower temperatures in the mixing reactor, 150◦C, the feeding rate of H2SO4 is
low leading to a low signal from H2SO4 in the resulting absorption spectra which is
strongly aﬀected by noise also. It was also found that the weighing procedure was
not capable of determining the feeding rate of H2SO4. This made the determination
of H2SO4 cross-sections impossible.
At 170◦C in the mixing reactor, the H2SO4 signal is strongly increased (with a
factor of around 3 compared to the signal at 150◦C), but some of the H2SO4 is

6. Future Work
This project has focused on investigating the decomposition of H2SO4 in the gas-
phase at higher temperatures in an existing hot gas ﬂow cell with IR based detection.
The most time-consuming part was to identify how to get the existing setup to
work for decomposition investigations of H2SO4. During the project a number of
diﬀerent improvements to the setup, experimental procedure, and analysis method
of the data have been observed.
6.1
Improvements to the HGC Setup
The decomposition of H2SO4 was limited to a very narrow temperature range be-
tween 170◦C and 200◦C due to limitations of the setup.
The decomposition of
H2SO4 is limited at higher temperatures because the reaction is fast compared to
the residence time in the HGC. In order to investigate the decomposition at higher
temperatures, a new gas cell has to be constructed which gives the possibility of a
much lower residence time. This can be achieved by decreasing the diameter of the
gas cell. The length of the gas cell should be as long as possible, since it aﬀects the
intensity of the signal in the resulting absorption sample spectra. The reaction rate
constants found in this project can be used to simulate an optimal design for the
new cell.
It is of high priority to determine the cross-sections of H2SO4, which would re-
duce the complexity of determining the conversion in the HGC. To achieve this, the
following are suggested:
A new feeding system of H2SO4 has to be implemented as well. Feeding the H2SO4
by a syringe pump was considered during the project, but the limitation is that
the evaporation rate of H2SO4 is very low at 150-170◦C. A heated system where N2
is bubbled through a reservoir of H2SO4 (placed on a weight) was also considered
(inspiration from e.g. Zhang et al. [2014] for the backward reaction). These feeding
systems have to be tested together with other, possible candidates. It should be
possible to control/measure the feeding rate of H2SO4. An optimal feeding system
would be a system which could feed a high concentration of H2SO4 at maximum
150◦C where decomposition to SO3 is not occurring.
Thereby cross-sections of
H2SO4 could be obtained, which would make the quantiﬁcation process of H2SO4
and SO3 easier and more accurate.

6.2. FURTHER EXPERIMENTS
The teﬂon line connecting the mixing reactor and the inlet to the HGC has to
be shortened or removed due to H2SO4 deposition inside the teﬂon line.
The interpretation of the measured conversions could be simpliﬁed by having outlet
concentrations instead of averaged concentrations. Therefore, a small cell could be
constructed after the HGC, which could be used to measure the outlet concentra-
tion. This requires a very small cell, where it can be assumed that only a negligible
amount of H2SO4 is decomposed. It also requires a very good spectrometer in order
to get high sensitivity (high signal compared to noise). A complete diﬀerent analy-
sis method could also be used instead of FTIR. Among others, Reiner and Arnold
[1993] use mass spectroscopy to determine the concentration of SO3 and H2SO4.
This would simplify the interpretation of the measured averaged concentrations
signiﬁcantly.

Further Experiments
For future decomposition experiments, it is preferred to perform only one experi-
ment each day, thereby reducing the eﬀect from using a reference spectrum (only N2
in the system) which is obtained at another temperature than the sample spectrum
(N2, SO3, H2SO4, H2O). In this project, the reference spectrum was obtained at
170◦C, whereas the sample spectra at 170-200◦C. This gives baseline problems in
the IR range where H2SO4 absorbs.
To further reduce uncertainties from noise and baseline problems, an automated
standard method to remove these uncertainties could be used. Martens [2015] has
developed some diﬀerent methods (e.g. MSC and EMSC), which is based on Matlab
algorithms, to deal with these problems.
To determine the SO3 concentration from the cross-sections more precisely, it is
preferred to use integrated areas. To do this, the impact from H2O has to be re-
moved by subtracting the H2O spectrum from the SO3 spectrum. It means that
H2O spectra have to be obtained at the respective temperatures.
To reduce the noise in the spectra, the signal-to-noise ratio could be increased. 900
scans (∼7 minutes) were used for most of the measurements, however, test with
4000 scans (∼30 minutes) was also performed increasing the SNR by a factor of
around 2. To get smoother spectra (less noise), even higher numbers of scans have
to be used, especially when the signal is low.
It was found that the reference spectra changed from day to day sometimes. This
has to be investigated further; if it is due to the surroundings or the way of obtain-
ing the spectra.
More investigations have to be used to establish very accurate SO3 cross-sections.
The cross-sections used in this project are the only available cross-sections at higher
temperatures than 25◦C. Ph.D. student Dan Underwood is currently investigating
the cross-sections of SO3 at higher temperatures, by experiments and modelling.
Some unknown phenomena was found in the spectra at higher temperatures in the
HGC. In order to understand the decomposition of H2SO4 at these higher temper-

7. Conclusion
This master thesis concerns decomposition of H2SO4 to form SO3 and H2O at el-
evated temperatures by use of IR spectroscopy. The focus is to estimate reaction
rate constants (k) for the forward and backward reaction.
A literature review was conducted to investigate the available information about
the decomposition and formation of H2SO4, including the mechanisms and reaction
rate constants.
It was found that the reaction H2SO4 (+M) −−⇀
↽−−SO3 + H2O
(+M) is suﬀering from lack of data with respect to the mechanism and k values at
elevated/high temperatures.
An existing, high temperature gas ﬂow cell (HGC) setup, capable of measuring
on reactive gases, was used to investigate the decomposition of H2SO4. Preliminary
decomposition experiments showed that the H2SO4, surprisingly, starts to decom-
pose at relative low temperature (> 150◦C), and is decomposed completely at 500◦C.
A feeding temperature in the mixing reactor equal to 170◦C was found to give
a satisfactory feeding rate of H2SO4 to the HGC with respect to sensitivity. The
repeatability of the feeding rate of H2SO4 was also tested, giving variations less
than 1%.
Problems were encountered in the development of cross-sections of H2SO4. The
weighing procedure proved to be unable of determining the feeding rate of H2SO4
to the HGC, since the time to reach steady state was found to be approximately
1.5 hours. A signiﬁcant part of the H2SO4 was also found to deposit inside the
teﬂon tubes. Another approach was therefore tested by estimating the feeding rate
of H2SO4 by conducting experiments at 500◦C in the HGC. The determined SO3
concentration is then equal to the feeding rate of H2SO4. But, at this temperature,
an unknown phenomenon was observed, making the quantiﬁcation of the feeding
rate of H2SO4, by this approach, impractical. A possible explanation could be that
an unknown adduct is formed or some spectral error.
The traditional method of quantifying H2SO4 in sample spectra by cross-sections
proved to be impractical. Therefore, an alternative method to quantify the concen-
tration of H2SO4 in the HGC was suggested. By applying this method, averaged
concentrations of H2SO4 and SO3 were estimated in the temperature range between
170◦C and 200◦C in the HGC.
Simulations in CHEMKIN, assuming PFR behaviour both in the mixing reactor
115
==PAGE:132==
and HGC, were performed to estimate reaction rate constants at each temperature.
The averaged concentrations of H2SO4 and SO3 lie close to the equilibrium concen-
trations at 190◦C and 200◦C. Therefore it was estimated that no upper limit for k
exists at these temperatures.
A parameter uncertainty and sensitivity assessment were performed, taking into
account uncertainties in the ﬂow rate, temperature in the setup, and the analysis
method of estimating the concentrations of H2SO4 and SO3 (i.e. integrated absorp-
tion integrals of H2SO4 and estimation of SO3 from the cross-section procedure).
Due to limitations in the software, worst-case scenarios were set up to estimate the
uncertainties. The assessment showed that large deviations on k exist at temper-
atures of 190◦C and 200◦C. An explanation could be that the reference spectrum
was obtained at 170◦C, whereas the sample spectra were measured at 170-200◦C.
When the temperature diﬀerence increases, baseline problems occurs, which aﬀects
the interpretation of the absorption spectrum.
The upper and lower limit of the kinetic parameters, pre-exponential factor (A)
and activation energy (Ea), were estimated by use of Arrhenius plots for both the
forward (Af/Eaf) and backward (Ab/Eab) reaction. These values deﬁnes a band for
the kinetic parameters (Af = 4.1·1015-1.7·1034 s−1, Eaf = 34.9-73.1 kcal mol−1, Ab
= 6.3·1013-2.5·1032 cm3 mol−1 s−1, and Eab = 13.4-51.6 kcal mol−1).
It was not possible to calculate a trustworthy Ea by use of the Arrhenius plots, since
a too narrow temperature range was considered. Therefore, Eaf was estimated to
be 43 kcal mol−1 by use of data from literature, which lies within the band of the
kinetic parameters, but closer to the lower limit than the upper limit (Af = 4.0·1019
s−1, Ab = 6.3·1017 cm3 mol−1 s−1, and Eab = 21.5 kcal mol−1). The values of the
upper limit are probably too large to give physical meaning. To reﬁne the band of
the kinetic parameters, more experiments have to be performed in a larger temper-
ature range, making it possible to estimate an Eaf.
Finally, further studies and improvements to the HGC setup have been proposed.
Several limitations exist in the setup. The decomposition of H2SO4 is relatively fast
at lower temperatures and approaches the equilibrium concentration very fast at
higher temperatures due to a too large residence time in the setup. A new cell can
be installed, having a very small diameter, to cope with this issue. A completely
new measuring method are also proposed in order to measure the outlet concentra-
tions of the present compounds instead of the averaged concentrations, which could
make the interpretation process much easier.

When the velocity, u, is decreasing the Bo number is also decreasing, which pro-
motes PFR behaviour. At increasing velocities of the ﬂuid, axial dispersion is a
result of the interaction of velocity variations. When D is increasing, we are on the
left arm of the curve as well. Here axial diﬀusion is dominating, which conﬂicts
with the PFR model. Since the key assumption of the PFR model is that the ﬂuid
is perfectly mixed in the radial direction and not in the axial direction. It means
that mixing in the axial direction has to be small, which requires a larger Bo number.
It is therefore a trade-oﬀbetween having a low velocity and characteristic length
(low Bo) and minimizing the axial diﬀusion (high Bo). Therefore, in order to as-
sume PFR behaviour, Bo numbers in the intermediate region is required (3 < Bo
< 50) [Levenspiel, 1993, chapter 64, p. 17].
