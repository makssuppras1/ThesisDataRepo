Abstract
Knowledge of the mineralogy and groundwater geochemistry is important to understand
cause and eect relationships in aquifers forced by contaminants or in-situ remediation.
Many of the compounds and minerals found in aquifers such as iron, sulphate, sulphide,
manganese, methane, etc., are redox sensitive, and an understanding of the redox processes
is necessary, to predict the fate of contaminants, and challenges concerning remediation of
contaminated sites. Two important parameters to understand how in-situ remediation ef-
fects an aquifer, are redox zonation and redox buering. Redox zonation is used to classify
how reduced the environment is in dierent parts of the aquifer based on the chemical species
undergoing reduction, while redox buering quanties the aquifers ability to resist changes
to the redox state.
A sandy aquifer located at Skovlunde Byvej 96a, served as test site for a geochemical inves-
tigation. A well with six short, depth specic screens was constructed, and intact sediment
samples were taken out from depth coresponding to the screens during drilling. The ground-
water was sampled in each of the six screens, and the groundwater geochemistry was used
to establish the redox zones of the aquifer. The groundwater sampling and quantication
of iron minerals in the sediment were used to determine the redox buering capacity of the
aquifer. Chemical wet extractions with HCl were used to extract iron form the intact sed-
iment samples, and iron(II) and total iron was determined with the ferrozine method and
atomic absorption spectroscopy.
The geochemical investigation revealed incomplete oxidation of iron(II) sulphides within
the rst meter below the water table, due to transport of pore air down into the aquifer.
The aquifer goes from aerobic to iron reducing within the rst meter below the groundwater
table, moves towards sulphate reducing conditions, and back to iron reducing with increasing
depth. The shift toward sulphate reducing conditions is induced by a local peak in contami-
nation with hydrocarbons. Due to dierences in geology, the redox buering capacity of the
aquifer is signicantly higher in the rst meter below the groundwater table, compared to
other depth of the aquifer.
The data from the geochemical investigation was used as input for a model, simulating
the injection of hydrogen peroxide and a lactate, which is respectively used for in-situ chem-
ical oxidation and as electron donor for enhanced reductive dechlorination. The model was
developed in PHREEQC, and was set up as a 1D transport model, including equilibrium
with minerals and cation exchange capacity of the sediment. The model showed that there
is a risk of methane production in the lower parts of the aquifer, and sulphide production
in the upper part of the aquifer during reduction. The time to reach aerobic conditions 3 m
downstream of the injection well during oxidant injection, was estimated to be 2.4 years in
the top of the aquifer, and 0.2 years in the bottom of the aquifer. As signicantly dierent
problems can occur in the upper and lower part of the investigated aquifer, it is suggested
to take this into account when constructing an injection well. This could be by installing to
screens in dierent depth in a new injection well.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Anthropogenic contamination of the soil and aquifers has been ongoing since the mining
of iron minerals started more 1000 years. However, the magnitude of contaminations has
increased with the industrial revolution, and gotten more diverse with the progressions in
the chemical industry. Up until the 1970s the soil was seen as a sink for contaminants and
chemicals were disposed of by simply pouring them onto the ground.
During the 1970s
public awareness grew, and leading to adoption of mitigation policies. Time has proven that
soil is not an innite sink and today, problems related to contmainate sites exist all around
the world (Swartjes, 2011).
Huge amounts of resources are used to investigate and remediate contaminated sites to-
day. It is estimated that there are 342 000 contaminated sites in the 39 countries, which are
members of or associated with the European Environment Agency, and the majority of these
sites cost between 50 000 and 500 000 euro (EUR) each to remediate (EEA (European En-
vironment Agency), 2014). For all countries investigated by EEA (European Environment
Agency) (2014), including Denmark, the fraction of money spent on the remediation was
between 65% and 90% of the total budget for contaminated sites in 2011. Hence, there are
solid economic incentives to optimize existing methods and develop new remediation method.
Knowledge of the geochemistry, mineralogy and groundwater chemistry is valuable when
working on optimization of remediation technologies. Many of the compounds and miner-
als found in aquifers such as iron, sulphate, sulphide, manganese, methane, etc., are redox
sensitive, and an understanding of the redox processes is necessary, to predict the fate of
contaminants, and identify potential challenges during remediation of contaminated sites
(Christensen et al., 2000; Appelo and Postma, 2005). As an example, the eectiveness of
reductive dechlorination used for degradation of chlorinated solvents, is dependent on anaer-
obe conditions. An environment with a large pool of oxidised species can counteract eorts
of reduction, and can cause problems if the composition and reactivity of the oxidised species
are not considered or poorly understood (Appelo and Postma, 2005; Bjerg et al., 2006). An-
other example is the in-situ chemical oxidation (ISCO) of contaminants, where a reduced
environment can counteract eorts of oxidation (Appelo and Postma, 2005; Siegrist et al.,
2011). Furthermore, reduction and oxidation of the aquifer can lead to changes in pH, which
aects other processes such as the carbonate system and transport of trace metals (Chris-
tensen et al., 2000; Appelo and Postma, 2005).
Groundwater geochemistry varies naturally both horizontally and vertically in an aquifer.
This can be caused by heterogeneities in the geology which can have great inuence on the
geochemistry, for example due to dierences in mineralogy, organic matter content and car-
bonate content between sand, clay and chalk. The geochemistry is also dependent on redox
conditions which varies naturally with depth in an aquifer and can be induced locally by
contamination or remediation (Appelo and Postma, 2005; Miotli«ski, 2008; Jakobsen and
Postma, 1999; Postma et al., 1991).
==PAGE:9==
Iron plays an important role in the geochemistry, as it is an abundant metal in many
Danish aquifers. Most iron species in an aquifer is associated with solid minerals in the
sediment, or as part of a clay matrix. To determine which iron minerals that are abundant
in a sample, x-ray diraction (XRD) can be applied. However, XRD is not always eective
in quantication of iron, due to problems regarding identication of small iron fractions.
Instead, series of chemical wet extractions can be applied to dierentiate between fractions,
and thus quantify the iron content of the sediment.
Hypothesis
Iron minerals are the governing species in Danish aquifer sediments, controlling geochemical
processes. Therefore, understanding its role and the connection between groundwater geo-
chemistry and mineralogy is essential to setup a geochemical PHREEQC model and predict
any changes induced by external impacts. By modifying the geochemical model it is possible
to predict the geochemical changes associated with in-situ remediation.
Scope
The aim of the project is to improve the understanding of geochemical processes induced
through heavy reduction or oxidation of an aquifer during in-situ remediation. This will be
achieved by high-resolution investigation of the geochemistry in the sediment and ground-
water of a sandy aquifer, and by developing 1D transport model in order to improve the
understanding of the geochemical changes induced by remediation. The specic objectives
are:
 To investigate geochemical parameters of groundwater and sediment in the aquifer,
both vertically and horizontally, by conducting a eld campaign including groundwater
and intact sediment sampling.
 To investigate the occurrence and distribution of iron species in the sediment using
wet extractions and X-ray diraction.
 To develop a geochemical model which can improve process understanding, and identify
controlling geochemical processes.
 To evaluate predicted geochemical changes initiated by reducing or oxidising the
aquifer environment in connection to in-situ remediation.

Chapter 2
Redox Geochemistry in Aquifers
This chapter focuses on the general redox geochemistry of aquifers, because most important
abundant species in aquifers are redox sensitive. The focus will lie on a presentation of the
basic redox processes in an aquifer, how to classify redox conditions and how to measure
iron in the sediment.
Redox Characterisation of Aquifers
The redox environment of an aquifer is a complex system, with many redox couples and
dierent chemical reactions. A redox couple consists of the same element in two dierent
oxidation states. Any measured redox couple can be used to express "pe", which is dened as
the negative logarithm of the electron activity as seen in equation 2.1 (Appelo and Postma,
2005; Christensen et al., 2000).

A high pe shows that the environment is oxidising, and a low pe shows that the environment
is reducing. The range of pe in groundwater is determined by the stability range of water,
which ranges from approximately pe -7 to 15 at neutral pH. The pe derived from one redox
couple is theoretically valid for the entire system, and can be used to determine the ratios of
every other redox couple, if the system is in equilibrium. However, it has been shown that
eld measurements of Eh (and thereby pe) often compare rather poorly with the calculated
pe based on redox couples. The reasons for this discrepancy are disequilibrium and analyti-
cal diculties, and data collected from eld measurements can mainly be used to classify the
environment as either oxidising or reducing (Appelo and Postma, 2005; Christensen et al.,
2000). Instead, so-called "redox zonation" based on the measured species can be used to
classify the aquifer oxidation state (Christensen et al., 2000).

Kinetics of Redox Reactions in Aquifers
The rate limiting factor, considering the kinetics of key redox processes, is the degradation
of complex organic matter into simple organic matter, which acts as the electron donor. The
key redox processes are relatively fast compared to the degradation of organic matter and
to normal groundwater ow velocities, therefore the system can be assumed to be in partial
equilibrium (also called local equilibrium) (Postma and Jakobsen, 1996; Miotli«ski, 2008).

The partial equilibrium assumption stipulates that when observing a system at a give point
in time, the key redox processes are in equilibrium. This simplies the modelling, as kinetic
calculations of the system are drastically reduced (Miotli«ski, 2008).
Redox Buering in Aquifers
The iron contents of the aquifer are important as iron is a key redox buer (Barcelona and
Holm, 1991; Heron et al., 1994a; Heron and Christensen, 1995). Redox buering can be split

species). The opposite electron transfers are assumed for the RDC except for N and organic

There are disagreements in the literature regarding the role of organic matter as a redox
buer, mainly due to the reactivity of organic carbon. Barcelona and Holm (1991) found
that organic matter had a signicant role in the redox buering, while Heron et al. (1994a)
concluded that organic matter had a very limited eect on the oxidation capacity, but did
not assess the reduction capacity. The reason for this discrepancy can be attributed to the
methods for measuring the OXC, where Barcelona and Holm (1991) used a method which
greatly overestimated the OXC (Heron et al., 1994a). However, the concentration of organic
matter seems to have a larger eect on RDC than OXC (Barcelona and Holm, 1991; Peder-
sen et al., 1991).
Role, Quantication and Identication of Iron Minerals in Aquifer
Sediments
The minerals in the soil also reect the redox state of the environment. However, dierent
ratios between redox couples can be found between the minerals compared to the dissolved
ions, due to dierences in solubility. For example, iron(II) is stable in solution at normal
groundwater conditions, while iron(III) is not (Appelo and Postma, 2005), thus the content
of iron(II) is several orders of magnitude higher than the content of iron(III) due to the very
low solubility of the former. On the other hand both solid iron(II) and iron(III) minerals
(and mixed minerals) can be found in the sediment at all redox conditions, from aerobic to
methanogenic, as shown by (Heron et al., 1994b). Most bacteria have to be in direct contact
with the iron minerals to mediate the dissolution of the minerals, and will favour dissolution
of less stable minerals (Larsen et al., 2006). These observations shows that strongly bound
iron(III), for instance in clay or in stable minerals, is not necessarily reduced even under
methanogenic conditions.

To investigate the stability of iron minerals bound in the sediment of aquifers, two main
methods have been described in the literature (Heron et al., 1994b).
The rst is X-ray
diraction (XRD), which is a relatively fast and easy method, but struggles with identica-
tion of small iron concentrations and quantication in general. The second method is wet
extractions combined with AAS, where dierent extractions can be combined to separate
the iron in the sediment in phases of dierent stability. The wet extractions can detect
small amounts of iron in the sediment, but the method can be quite laborious. Both meth-
ods might be coupled with visual identication of minerals by scanning electron microscopy
(SEM) (Heron et al., 1994b).
The purpose of the wet extractions is to separate the labile irons, from the more stable
irons. Heron et al. (1994b) made an investigation of dierent chemical wet extractions of
aquifer sediment, with CaCl2, natrium acetat (NaAc), HCl, Cr(II)+HCl, HI, Ti(III)-EDTA,
oxalate and dithionite. The use of several of these extraction techniques enables a separation
of the iron pool into subgroups. However many of these extraction techniques only target
one mineral, or are to laborious to be executed within the scope of this project.
Based on the litterature study, it was chosen to use two HCl extraction methods of dierent
strength (0.5 M/24 hours and 5 M/21 days) (Sidhu, 1981; Chao and Zhou, 1983; Heron et al.,
1994b). The 0.5 M HCl extracts akageneite, amorphous ferrihydrite, FeS and siderite, and
the 5 M HCl additionally extracts magnetite, goethite, hematite and maghemite. Neither
of the methods extracts pyrite. By choosing these two methods both the labile pool of iron
minerals and the total pool of iron minerals, minus pyrite, can be identied, however the
specic composition of minerals cannot be identied. The extracts can be analysed with
the ferrozin method to determine iron(II), and by AAS to determine total iron Heron et al.
(1994b). The commonly used dithionite extraction used to estimate the total amount of iron
was discarded, because it had to be repeated to extract acceptable amounts of iron, and even
then it failed extracting all stable iron oxides (hematite and maghemite) Heron et al. (1994b).

X-ray diraction (XRD) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM), are used to analyse sam-

identify the specic iron mineral and visualise them. The XRD identies crystalline struc-

Change in Redox Conditions Induced by Contamination and Remidi-
ation
Pollution of the aquifer can change the redox conditions locally. Contaminants (for exam-
ple hydrocarbons) can work as electron donors and can thereby create reduced conditions.
An example is the complex contamination plumes stemming from landlls which can cre-
ate methanogenic conditions in an otherwise aerobic aquifer (Heron and Christensen, 1995;
Bjerg et al., 1995; Heron et al., 1998; Lyngkilde and Christensen, 1992). One applicable
remediation technique, which targets a variety of contaminants is in-situ chemical oxidation
(ISCO). By injection an oxidant, the degradation rate of the contaminant can be increased
signicantly. However, the oxidant will also be consumed by naturally occurring compounds
in the aquifer for example iron(II) and sulphide. The result of disregarding existing species
can be insucient supply of oxidant (Siegrist et al., 2011).
Another case is contamination with chlorinated solvents.
Chlorinated solvents does not
change the redox state of the environment in itself, but them in-situ remediation of chlori-
nated solvents can impact the redox environment. Chlorinated solvents can both be degraded
by ISCO, or by reductive dechlorination. Reductive dechlorination takes advantage of the
natural degradation of chlorinated solvents in anaerobic environments. Anaerobic conditions
can be achieved by injection of an electron donor, but as for ISCO, naturally occuring com-
pounds in the aquifer can counteract the eorts to reduce the aquifer. Complete degradation
of chlorinated solvents can be achieved, by simultaneous injection of halorespiring bacteria
(Bjerg et al., 2006).

1D Modelling of Injection of
Reducing and Oxidising Agents
Modelling of oxidation or reduction scenarios in an aquifer, can provide extended under-
standing of the impact and challenges of in-situ remediation. The objective with this mod-
elling process is to simulate geochemical processes and how they respond upon injection of
a reductant or oxidant.
The software used for modelling is PHREEQC. PHREEQC is capable of performing a broad
range of geochemical reactions, and can calculate speciation equilibrium and kinetically-
controlled reactions in a batch reactor or 1D transport (Parkhurst and Appelo, 2013).
Conceptual Model and Initial Assumptions
One model have been set up to simulate the ow between two wells similar to well B501.
The results from the six well screens, and from the dierent geological layers have been
extrapolated, between the two wells in six dierent model layers as seen from gure 6.1.
The distance between the wells are set to 3 m. The aquifer is assumed to be homogeneous
for each model layer, both with respect to ow and to geochemistry.
The model have been constructed based on the assumption of fast reaction kinetics in gen-
eral, both for existing species and injected oxidation and reducing agents. Thus, natural
organic matter has been omitted from the model, and the ground water is only assumed to
be in equilibrium with the labile iron minerals, extracted by the weak extraction. However,
organic matter buers geochemical changes, if long term injection is considered. Some of
the stable iron minerals might also be dissolved, but it is dicult to say to which degree,
and how much this will aect the predictions, without further investigations of reactivity.

injection well. The depletion of sulphate is followed by methane production, according to
the redox sequence. The methane concentration is increasing along the ow direction, due
to transport processes.
The injected lactate, is not enough to fully reduce the sulphate
in model layer B501-4 and B501-6, which prevents methane production. In model layer
B501-4 and B501-6 sulphide production is predicted close to the injection well, which is
caused by rapid reduction of sulphate. Sulphide is unwanted in the groundwater and the
concentration in groundwater used fro drinking water should be less than 0.05 mg/L (Dan-
ish Regions Environment and Resources, 2015), which is exceeded approximately 1000 times
where the concentrations are highest. However, sulphide is only observed when dissolved
iron(II) has been depleted, otherwise it would preticipate as iron sulphide. Both the iron(II)
in the injected water, and the akaganeite dissolved by reductive dissolution is available for
precipitation with sulphide. If the measured iron(II) concentrations are extrapolated to the
aquifer further downstream, huge quantities of sulphide have to be produced in order to be
transported a signicant distance before precipitation. In model layer B501-1 the produced
sulphide is not enough to preticipate all the iron(II), but an decrease in iron(II) is observed
along the reduction of sulphate.
As predicted the pH is increasing, however still buered by the carbonate system, which
leads to a decrease in calcium and bicarbonate as calcium carbonate is preticipated.
Overall it is evident that the redox buering capacity of the aquifer have to be taken into
account. The largest RDC and OXC are in the top of the aquifer, and if this is not accounted
for, it might cause problems either by not getting the wanted eect of the oxidation/reduc-
tion, or by excessive quantities of unwanted products such as sulphate, sulphide or methane.
A possible strategy for injection during remediation could be to install two separate injection
wells or well screens, to allow for injection of dierent concentrations or types of oxidant of
reductant in dierent depths.

Discussion, Possible Improvements
and Future Work
Both the methods used in the eld campaign and in the laboratory, and the development of
the model, showed unforeseen challenges and possible improvements. Many of these chal-
lenges have generated new ideas to solution, however, it have not been possible to pursuit
all of the ideas within the time frame of this thesis.
Improvements of the Field Campaign and Additional Analysis and
Sampling
The electrical balance in the water samples from well B501, was outside the quality criteria
of ± 5 %, for ve out of six samples, showing that the sampling or the analysis was awed.
As the measured conductivity, was in agreement with the concentration of anions but not the
cations, the problem was identied to high measurements of calcium. The samples were not
ltered, as advised by the accredited lab, and the high calcium measurements are believed
to stem from calcium carbonate attached to suspended sediment in the sample. For future
eld campaigns this should be considered when samples are analysed by an accredited lab.
Alternatively, calcium can be measured easily by AAS.
The measurements of iron and calcium in the sediment was based on small samples of
0.5-1.5 g each. However, small-scale heterogeneities can lead to uncertainties in the results.
Mumford et al. (2004) have shown that experimental results for the natural oxygen demand
of an aquifer, are consistent for sediment samples over 20 g. This would make it impossible
to take samples in 5 cm intervals, as the samples had to be taken from the middle of the
core as the outside had been in contact with atmospheric air.
The analytically method for quantication of iron(II), failed due to matrix eects in the
5 M HCl extracts. Several attempts were done to overcome this problem, including stan-
dard addition, dilution, pH buering and analysis with a standard test kit. However, all
of these methods failed, due to the nature of the matrix eect. The only viable solution
would be to repeat the analysis for stable iron species by another method, this could be
the dithionite extraction or by extraction with Ti(III) and EDTA (Heron et al., 1994b,a).
However, it was not possible to repeat the analysis as the sediment samples was not stored
anaerobically, and the ratio between iron(II) and iron(III) might have been altered by oxi-
dation.
The total redox buering capacity of the aquifer is split between the oxidation capacity
and the reduction capacity. The reduction capacity is two to four times higher than the ox-
idation capacity, showing that the aquifer is more resistant to oxidation than to reduction.
The redox buering has been calculated based on the concentration of labile iron minerals
and the assumption that the organic matters reaction rate is slow, causing the redox buer-
ing from the organic matter to be negligible. A more conservative estimate would be to
==PAGE:66==
include organic matter and the stable iron minerals in the redox buering calculations. Be-
cause other estimates of redox buering have been calculated including the total iron pool, it
is not meaningful to compare the calculated OXC and RDC to the literature. To determine
the eective redox buering capacity of the sediment laboratory experiments can be carried
out (Barcelona and Holm, 1991; Heron et al., 1994a). This can theoretically support or
falsify the assumption that the stable iron minerals, and existing organic matter does not
inuence the RDC or OXC signicantly. Another approach is to measure the reactivity of
the iron pool. Larsen et al. (2006) proposed a method based on chemical wet extractions,
to determine the reaction rate of the total iron pool. It will, however, be more dicult to
speciate the iron pool. A combination of speciation and reaction rate determination, could
be the basis for developing a more robust model.
The contamination of the aquifer have not been included in the calculations of the re-
dox buering capacity, nor in the model.
Including the contamination will increase the
redox buering capacity of the sediment, and will compete with the existing redox sensitive
species. To get more accurate simulations these processes should be included in the model,
however this requires study of degradation and behaviour of the contaminants.
Possibilities for Developning the Geochemical Model and Rening
the Input
More knowledge can be extracted from the existing models, and it can be build on to include
more processes and compounds. A better understanding of the behaviour of specic com-
pounds can be achieved, by changing parameters such as run-time, or kinetics and isolate
processes by small modications in the model.
The calibration of the model was manually, and the calibration gave acceptable results,
but was physically inaccurate. The calibration could be improved setting up an algorithm
for repeated random sampling (also called the Monte Carlo method), using the concentra-
tion of iron in the sediment, the estimated CEC and solubilities found in the literature
as boundaries. It will be an advantage to couple the calibration with a sensitivity analy-
sis. Another approach is to take advantage of the function "INVERSE_MODELING" in
PHREEQC, which can give a set of minerals and gasses which have to react to go from one
water composition to another (Parkhurst and Appelo, 2013). However, this requires two
datasets, but if an injection will be installed, these datasets would be possible to obtain via
simple injection experiments.
The speciation of iron minerals has a large impact on the modelled redox buering of the
aquifer. The chosen iron(II) mineral was amorphous iron sulde, based on results from the
XRD and by the calibration. This choice increases the reduction capacity with a factor of
nine. This has great impacts on the predicted oxidant consumption, and the assumption
could be qualied through chemical wet extractions of sulphide (Heron et al., 1994b). The
model predicts that it will take 2.4 years to turn the aquifer aerobic between the injection
and the observation well, with the chosen injection concentration. This is a long time to
inject, and it would be expensive to keep remediation actively running for that amount of
time. However, the choice of only one mineral to account for the entire labile pool of iron(II),
is a rough assumption. On the other hand the upper aquifer heavily indicates incomplete
iron sulde reduction, so the assumption that iron sulde is the dominant iron(II) mineral
can be supported. This assumption can be veried by sulde measurements as described by
(Heron et al., 1994b).
The assumed rst order kinetics used for modelling degradation of the injected reductant
60
==PAGE:67==
and oxidant, is a simplied way of looking at these processes. Models already exist for bet-
ter description of the consumption of lactate and hydrogen peroxide, which is respectively
monod kinetics and competitive kinetics (Siegrist et al., 2011; Chambon et al., 2013). How-
ever these models require additional inputs to run, and one can chose to verify the accuracy
of the assumed pseudo rst order kinetics through experiments, or increase the complexity
of the model.
Clogging of the well screen or the pores in the aquifer is possible during remediation of
contaminated aquifers or by injection of aerobic water in an anaerobic aquifer (Siegrist
et al., 2011; Bustos Medina et al., 2013). As the clogging of well can halt entire remediation
projects, or render wells useless, predictions of precipitation of iron minerals would be a
valuable tool. Precipitation of minerals can theoretically be predicted by the model in its
present form. This can be used to predict problems with clogging of the system. However,
this issue needs more attention before any conclusion can be made. An understanding of the
problem might be achieved by running the model for multiple scenarios in a systematic way,
but such investigation are laborious. PHREEQC have some problems with the numerical
solving, when high concentrations are combined with fast kinetics, and this issue should be
solved in further investigation.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The scope of the project was to improve the understanding of geochemical processes in-
duced by heavy reduction or oxidation of an aquifer during in-situ remediation. This was
achieved by high-resolution investigation of the geochemistry in the sediment and groundwa-
ter of a sandy aquifer. By combining the groundwater sampling of six short, depth specic
screens, with intact sediment sampling from the same depths, it was possible to connect
the geochemistry of the sediment, to the geochemistry of the ground water. The vertical
information was extrapolated horizontally, and used as input for a reactive, 1D transport
model, used to evaluate changes of the geochemistry during in-situ remediation, in scenarios
in which a strong oxidant and a strong reductant were injected into the aquifer, respectively.
The groundwater in the primary aquifer at the eld site, Skovlunde Byvej 96a, was found to
be anaerobic, however, it is aected by diusion of pore air in the rst vertical meter below
the water table. The pore air interaction partly oxidises iron sulde in the upper part of the
aquifer, resulting in high iron(II) and sulphate concentrations. The sulphate concentrations
decrease with increasing depth, and iron(II) concentrations decrease readily after the rst
vertical meter below the water table, followed by a steady increase. The low concentrations
of iron are likely caused by precipitation of iron sulphide, due to locally reduced conditions
induced by hydrocarbon contamination. Methane was measured in insignicant amounts.
Based on the presence of iron(II) and sulphate and the lack of methane the aquifer was
classied as iron reducing, and slightly sulphate reducing locally.
The investigation of two intact sediment samples from two dierent depths showed a signi-
cantly dierent geological layer at the top of the aquifer, with a high content of clay and silt,
and a relatively homogeneous aquifer below the top layer. The iron content of the sediment
in the homogeneous part of the aquifer was in the same order as for other Danish aquifers of
comparable geology. High iron content of the sediment was associated with a high content
of nes (grainsize < 0.063 mm). The labile iron pool of the sediment was mainly reduced,
with more than 90 % as iron(II) in the shallow sediment sample, and more than 75 % as
iron (II) in the deep sediment sample. The analytical method failed for the determination
of iron(II) in the total iron pool, and the chosen extraction methods should be reconsidered
for future investigations. However, the lack of knowledge of the more stable iron minerals,
do have limited consequences as their reactivity is assumed to be limited.
Qualication of the iron minerals by XRD proved dicult, due to poor crystalinity of the
iron minerals. After sieving the nes (grains < 0.063 mm) were sampled on the XRD again,
which indicated that the sediment contained amorphous iron sulde, and did not show in-
dications of siderite, but the results were not conclusive.
A geochemical model was developed to improve the understanding of geochemical processes
during injection of a powerful reductant or oxidant, which is relevant for in-situ remedia-
tion. The calibration of the model was used to qualify the determination of the abundant
==PAGE:69==
iron minerals. However, the calibration was perormed manually, which led to a simplied
mineralogy, assuming that only one amorphous iron sulde and akaganeite was in equilib-
rium with the groundwater. The model showed that the geochemistry reacted dierently
in the upper part of the aquifer compared to the lower part. During reduction the model
predicted methane production in the lower part of the aquifer because of a low oxidation
capacity. A limited sulde production in the upper part of the aquifer was predicted by the
model because of a fast sulphate reduction. The chosen amount of injected reductant, was
insucient to reduce the sulphate completely in the upper part of the aquifer.
During oxidation high concentrations of sulphate and iron(II) was predicted in front of
the oxidation front, due to incomplete oxidation of iron suldes in the sediment. The pro-
gression of the oxidation front was governed by the reduction capacity of the sediment, and
the hydraulic variability in the aquifer. The time to achieve fully aerobic conditions in the
model varied between 0.2 years at the bottom to 2.4 years at the top of the aquifer. The very
dierent problems associated with oxidation and reduction of the aquifer at dierent depths,
might be overcome by installing a well with two screens in dierent depths for injection.
There are several possibilities to optimise and rene the developed model.
This can be
by a more advanced calibration of the model, which would be able to provide a more com-
plex answer to which, iron minerals would be responsible for the observed groundwater
composition, and in what quantities they can be found. By rening the chemical analysis of
the stable iron minerals and include these in the calibration, physical accuracy of the model
can be increased. Simple eld scale experiments, can also be used to understand and tune
the model further.
The model can be used to get an qualitative understanding of possible problematic pro-
cesses and the fate of specic species. The simulations shows the dierences in response
vertically through the aquifer. In the lower part of the aquifer the redox forcing have a
high impact due to a low redox buering capacity, which have both positive and negative
consequences. The upper part of the aquifer have a high redox buering capacity, which
make it more dicult to change the redox characteristics of the environment.
