Abstract
As sequencing technologies continue to advance at a rapid pace, Variants of Uncertain Signif-
icance (VUS) continue to be a bottleneck in the expansion of knowledge of disease mechanisms.
Especially in cancer research and treatment, VUS poses a signiﬁcant challenge for clinical man-
agement.
To address this challenge, much focus has gone into utilizing methods of structural
bioinformatics to develop computational frameworks for accurately classifying the pathogenicity of
VUS, thereby advancing the development of personalized medicine. MAVISp is an example of such
a computational framework, a modularized classiﬁcation framework for accurately classifying VUS
based on multi-layered structural analysis. This project pertains to utilizing and further developing
the MAVIsp toolkit to study ﬁve VUS of ARID3A, speciﬁcally on their e↵ects concerning DNA
interaction using structural ensembles generated with all-atom molecular dynamics simulations.
From studying the variants’ e↵ects on interaction stability, functional dynamics, and long-range
communication pathways, variants were found to cause di↵erent damaging e↵ects. Uniquely, S309I
consistently showed damaging e↵ects across all areas of analysis. This project further proposes a
novel analysis implementation based on dynamic correlated motion to complement existing MAVISp
modules within local interaction analysis and long-range e↵ects.

1
Introduction
Advancements in high-throughput sequencing technologies have revolutionized genomics research, en-
abling the comprehensive analysis of genetic mutations across various cancers. These technological
leaps have facilitated the emergence of large-scale genomic projects, such as the Pan-Cancer Analysis
of Whole Genomes (PCAWG) and The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), which aim to map the genetic
landscape of cancer [1, 2].These initiatives have signiﬁcantly expanded our understanding of cancer
biology. They revealed common mutations and pathways driving cancer development and progression,
with a key insight being the identiﬁcation of cancer-driver genes. Among these, ARID3A has emerged
as a critical cancer driver gene, which protein product is involved in chromatin remodeling and gene
expression regulation. However, the vast amount of sequencing data has also uncovered numerous vari-
ants of uncertain signiﬁcance (VUS) whose functional impacts remain unclear. This poses a challenge
for the development of precision medicine, which seeks to harness the genetic information of patients
to develop treatment strategies and drive more e↵ective and personalized therapies [3]. Therefore,
there is great interest in developing computational frameworks capable of handling and rationalizing
the ever-growing VUS data, which might play a central role in expanding our knowledge of cancer pro-
gression and drug target discovery. [4]. This thesis focuses on utilizing MAVISp and novel structural
analysis tools to comprehensively study the e↵ects of ARID3A VUS through ARID3A-DNA structural
ensembles generated from All-atom Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations.
1.1
All-Atom Molecular Dynamics Simulations
Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulation is a computational tool that allows for studying the interactions
of atoms over a period of time by solving Newton’s classical equations of motion [5]. In structural
bioinformatics, MD simulations have proven to be particularly useful in investigating the local confor-
mational dynamics of proteins and their complexes by generating structural ensembles, thus providing
the foundation for intricate insights into their function. While static structures can be useful for some
structural analysis, MD simulations generate an ensemble of conformations representing molecular
systems’ dynamic motion over time. This ensemble approach allows for a more comprehensive un-
derstanding of the molecular ﬂexibility and the range of conformations that the molecule can adopt
under physiological conditions. It enables the study of transient states, conformational changes, and
dynamic interactions that are often crucial for the molecule’s function but are not captured by static

1.2
MAVISp
’Multi-layered Assessment of Variants by Structure for protein’ (MAVISp) is a modular, structure-
based framework for annotating and classifying the impacts of mutations impacting the coding region
of genes and their corresponding protein product. The framework has been developed by the Cancer
Structural Biology group at the Danish Cancer Institute and aims to address the challenge of VUS
by providing a computational framework to streamline variant analysis in a high-throughput manner
within standardized bioinformatics workﬂows. As such, MAVISp aims to characterize the functional
impact of missense mutations through analysis that connects molecular mechanisms to functional an-
notation of selected protein VUS [9].
MAVISp operates in two modes, namely the simple mode and ensemble mode, each designed to handle
the analysis of single protein structures (and/or their complexes) and ensembles of protein structures,
respectively. Within each module, the framework’s modular design allows for individual hand-picking
of analysis modules depending on the case at hand [9]. A visual representation of the MAVISp workﬂow
and modules is included in Figure 1.
Figure 1: Overview of MAVISp modules for the simple and ensemble mode. Modules colored orange
are associated with structure selection and generation, and modules colored blue are related to
structural analysis. This ﬁgure is adapted from Arnaudi et al. [9].
Initially, the framework extracts known cancer variants of the target protein by screening variant

databases including Clinvar [10], COSMIC [11] and cBioPortal [12] and retrieving the variants using
Cancermuts [13] and ClinVarMiner [14]. Then a selection of modules can be utilized from the simple
mode to focus on analyzing single conformations of the target protein. This approach uses static struc-
tures obtained from experimental data, such as X-ray crystallography or NMR, or computationally

signaling, respectively [9].
The ensemble mode extends the analysis to multiple conformations of the target protein generated from
molecular dynamics simulations; representing the ensemble generation module. This mode enhances
the evaluation by considering proteins’ conformational ﬂexibility and motion. The ensemble mode
includes additional modules to study Functional Dynamics using PCA [9]. The modules of the ensemble
mode modules have been particularly central to this project.
1.3
ARID3A
ARID3A belongs to a family of transcription factors and chromatin regulators with essential roles
in the regulation of cell di↵erentiation, proliferation, and chromatin structure [18]. Common to all
proteins in this family is the presence of a very conserved AT-rich interactive domain (ARID) with
DNA-binding properties. This family of proteins is divided into a total of 7 subfamilies based on the
sequence homology of their ARID domain. These families are denoted ARID1-5 and JARID1-2 [19,
20]. Dysfunction of the ARID-containing proteins has been associated with varying human disorders
such as intellectual disabilities, autoimmune disorders, and many types of cancer (e.g. liver, colon, and
ovarian cancer) [18, 19, 20].

1.3.1
Structure and DNA-binding
The ARID3 sub-family consists of three paralogs, namely ARID3A, B, and C - all featuring an ARID
and a multi-functional REKLES domain. This domain further consists of two subdomains: a moder-
ately conserved REKLES↵region in the N-terminal and a highly conserved REKLESβ region in the
C-terminal, which are connected by a variable linker region. It has been reported that this domain has
multiple purposes, including nucleocytoplasmic shuttling and facilitation of multimerization. As such,
ARID3A consists of multiple domains; an acidic N-terminal region followed by an ARID domain, which
is then followed by the REKLES domain. The total length of ARID3A is 593 amino acids (Figure 2a)
[21].
From alignment studies of the present ARID structures available on the Protein Data Bank, a conserved
alignment of six helices (H1 - H6) is forming what is referred to as the ’minimal ARID core domain’
[22]. This region be found ﬂanked by additional helices in the N/C-terminus, which is referred to as
the extended-ARID domain [19, 22]. Two loops, L1 and L2, are essential to the minimal core domain
located between H1 and H2 and H4 and H5, respectively. The DNA-binding capabilities of ARID
are mainly attributed to the Helix-turn-helix (H4-L2-H5) motif in the domain, which is reported to
interact with the major groove of the DNA. However, interactions with DNA are reportedly facilitated
by both L1 and L2, where a β-hairpin in L1 stabilizes the binding by interacting with the backbone
of the minor groove, whereas the L2 is directly inserted into the major groove of the DNA (Figure 2b)
[23].

1.3.2
Biological Function
ARID3A is a transcription factor that interacts with AT-rich sequences through its ARID domain. It
has been reported to interact with many genomic targets, and one of these targets is the nuclear matrix
attachment regions (MARs) in the IgH gene locus, where it is essential for regulating its expresison in
mature B cells. Beyond B cells, ARID3A also inﬂuences hematopoietic processes, where it a↵ects the
development of hematopoietic stem cells [20, 19]. The dysregulation of ARID3a’s functions in B cell
development and di↵erentiation has been reported to contribute to the development of autoimmune
diseases, such as systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), where elevated levels of ARID3A in B cells
correlate with increased antibody production and disease progression [24]. Additionally, ARID3A is
reported to cooperate with the tumor suppressor protein p53 to regulate cell cycle arrest by binding
to the p21WAF1 promoter, causing activation in response to DNA damage. This interaction stabilizes

p53, which highlights ARID3A’s role in maintaining genomic stability and its potential function as
a tumor suppressor [25]. However, in rectal cancer patients, ARID3A has also been reported to be
over-expressed, potentially acting as an oncogene. Therefore, there is evidence supporting the fact
that ARID3A can act both as a cancer promoter and a cancer suppressor, depending on the context.
As such, ARID3A is of high clinical importance yet not entirely understood [26, 27].
1.4
Objectives
The main goal of this thesis is to elaborately characterize the molecular e↵ects of 5 VUS on the
DNA binding functionality of ARID3A using MAVISp-integrated methodologies in the ensemble mode,
among other structural analysis tools. As such, the main objectives for this thesis can be summarized
as:
1. Conduct MD simulations for 5 ARID3A VUS and WT using an ARID3A-DNA complex model.
2. Explore MAVISp as a VUS classiﬁer and investigate novel analysis tools for comparison of struc-
tural ensemble results.
3. Provide characterization of VUS damaging e↵ects on ARID3A DNA binding.
Additionally, throughout the project, we contributed to the ongoing data curation of the MAVISp by
curating the entry for ARID3B and participating in the review process for the ARID3A entry.
2
Materials and Methods
This section outlines the methodological and analytical framework employed in this project, focusing
on the methodologies used to investigate the damaging e↵ects of ﬁve VUS of ARID3A upon interacting
with DNA. While a signiﬁcant portion of the analysis has been facilitated by external bioinformatics
tools, Python 3.10 has been the primary coding language used for analysis. Additionally, R version
4.3.2 has been utilized to create most of the visualizations throughout the project. All scripts developed
during this project are available on the CSB github: Github.
2.1
ARID3A-DNA Complex Model Selection
The ARID3A-DNA complex used as the reference WT model for this project was constructed with
MODELLER [28]. The template structure was built by structural alignment with Pymol of the NMR
Page 7 of 75
==PAGE:16==
Master’s Thesis

structure of drosophila DRI protein in complex with a 15bp DNA molecule (PDB ID: 1KQQ) and
the ﬁrst conformer of the NMR structure of the ARID domain of ARID3A (PDB: 2KK0), which
contains the residues 228 - 351. Using BLASTp [29], the % identity between these ARID3A models
was observed to be almost approx. 80%. All residues in 2KK0, except for the DNA-binding interface
residues, were restrained for modeling. These residues include 261, 263-267, 292-294, 303-304, 306-309,
313, 315, 345-349. A total of 100 models were created and ranked based on the RMSD value of the
target residues. Further, Van der Waals clashes were calculated using Arpeggio [30]. After validating
visual inspection of the structure in PyMol, the model with the lowest RMSD and number of clashes
was selected as the ﬁnal ARID3A-DNA model. This model will further be referred to as the ”WT”
structure.
2.2
MAVISp
All ARID3A variants were collected from ClinVar [10], COSMIC [11], and cBioPortal [12] - using
NP 005215 as the search RefSeq ID. The results from the variant retrieval and MAVISp simple- and
ensemble mode for ARID3A can be accessed from the MAVISp database.
Multiple modules from the MAVISp Ensemble mode [9] were applied to analyze the variants for dam-
aging or neutral e↵ects. The ensemble generation module using GROMACS [31] was applied to obtain
all-atom Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations of the WT ARID3A-DNA complex and ﬁve selected
variants. Further, from the local interaction module, MutateX with the FoldX-5 energy function [32]
was employed to a set of 25 simulation frames from each MD simulation to predict relative binding free
energies (∆∆G) as a metric for ARID3A and DNA interaction stability. Subsequently, the functional
Dynamics module was applied using PCA to investigate the changes in conformational dynamics of
the DNA binding loops, L1 and L2.

2.3.2
System Preparation
A total of six all-atom molecular dynamics simulation systems were prepared using the CHARMM36m
force ﬁeld [35] and the GROMACS version 2024.0 [31] software. The simulation system preparation
was carried out in six steps:
1. Structure preparation
2. Box deﬁnition
3. Solvate box
4. Ion addition
5. Energy minimization
6. System equilibration
Initially, the protein N- and C- termini were capped with NH2 and COOH, respectively, along with the
two DNA chains, which were capped with 3’ and 5’ terminals. A 1.2nm dodecahedron box was selected
as the simulation box geometry, which was solvated using the TIP3P water model as implemented in
CHARMM [36]. The water molecules were neutralized with chlorine- and sodium ions. Finally, peri-
odic boundary conditions were added at a minimum distance of 2.8˚A.
For NVT equilibration, the system ran with a timestep of 0.001 ps for a total duration of 2 ns. The
protein and non-protein components were separately coupled to a v-rescale thermostat at 298 K with
a coupling constant of 0.006 ps. Non-bonded interactions were treated with a cuto↵of 0.9 nm for both
Electrostatic and Van der Waals forces. Hydrogen bonds were constrained using the LINCS algorithm
[37]. Output ﬁles were written every 5000 steps for coordinates and velocities, and energy data were
saved every 1000 steps.
Subsequently, NPT equilibration was conducted to stabilize both temperature and pressure. This phase
ran over a 5 ns period with a timestep of 0.002 ps. As implemented in GROMACS, Temperature and

2.3.3
Productive Simulation Details
The ﬁnal productive simulations were run using GROMACS 2024.0 [31] and the CHARMM36m force
ﬁeld [39] over a 1µs period at a constant temperature of 298K. The default leap-frog integrator was
used with a time step of 0.002 ps, resulting in a total of 500,000,000 steps. As such, each productive
simulation was run for a total duration of 1 µs. The system’s temperature was maintained at 298
K while again using the v-rescale thermostat with a coupling time of 0.2 ps for both protein and
non-protein groups. Electrostatics, Van der Waals, and bond constraints were handled similarly to the
system preparation procedure.
Pairwise distance restraints were incorporated on the heavy atoms (nitrogens and oxygens) in the
terminal bases of the DNA chains to ensure no base-pair opening in the terminal ends. Each pair was
subjected to a harmonic potential using the umbrella sampling method to restrain distances with a
force constant of 10,000 kJ mol−1 nm−2. The initial distances between the atoms were used as the
threshold for initiating the pair-wise pull force.
Finally, output control was conﬁgured to save coordinates and velocities every 200,000 steps, simulta-
neously capturing energy outputs and logs. Compressed trajectory outputs for the protein group were
recorded every 10,000 steps, thus ensuring detailed data collection over the simulation’s runtime.

Free Energy Landscape
Subsequent to PCA, the free energy landscape (FEL) of each loop for each variant was mapped
using the ﬁrst two principal components of each loop as collective variables. This allowed for further
investigation of the stable conformations and transient states assumed by each loop across variant
systems. Using the previously determined ﬁrst two PCs, the FEL was calculated in 2-D PC space as

2.8
Protein Structure Networks
Protein structure networks (PSN) based on atomic contacts were generated using PyInteraph2 [46] on
the MD-generated trajectories. All networks are represented as undirected, weighted graphs. For edge
calculations, residue pairs were retained if their sequence distance exceeded a threshold of 1 and their
physical distance was less than 4.5˚A. Edges were kept if they occurred in more than 50% of the frames
within the ensemble and the interaction strength was greater than 3. Weights were assigned based on
the interaction strength between residues.
The Dijkstra algorithm [47] implemented in [48] was used for a meta-analysis of shortest paths by cal-
culating all shortest paths in the networks. Finally, path analysis as implemented in PyInteraph2[46]
was utilized to determine all shortest paths from any residues in the ARID3A domain and the DNA
binding loops, L1 (residue 256-271) and L2 (residue 301-309).
2.9
Hub analysis
Given the PSNs, the degree and severity of changes caused by the variants were assessed based on the
changes in hubs of the variant networks relative to WT network hubs. Here, a node was considered a
Page 14 of 75
==PAGE:23==
Master’s Thesis

hub if its degree was 4 or higher. Based on this criterion, the following decision scheme was devised:
1. If a node was a hub in the WT, we examined its hub status in each variant network.
2. A loss of hub status was considered damaging.
3. If the node degree was retained in the variant network, we assessed edge conservation using
Jaccard similarity. A Jaccard similarity below 0.5 was considered damaging.
4. Gain of hub in variant network was either considered compensatory or damaging. A new hub
within 4.5˚A of a damaged hub in the WT network was considered compensatory. If the distance
exceeded 4.5˚A, it was considered damaging.

network caused by a VUS:

paths was classiﬁed as damaging. Additionally, any variant a↵ecting distal nodes, as indicated by the
threshold set for ∆BC from the Dynamic Correlation analysis, was classiﬁed as damaging. These com-
prehensive criteria ensure a thorough assessment of each VUS’s impact on the DNA binding function
of ARID3A.

Discussion
ARID3A is a largely understudied transcription factor that is known to play an essential role in gene
expression and has frequently been reported to be a driving factor in the development and progression
of many cancers [56]. Using MAVISp and complementary analysis to study the e↵ects of selected VUS
from a structural point of view may provide valuable insights into the essential molecular dynamics in-
volved in ARID3A’s local interaction with DNA, which is yet to be comprehensively investigated. While
MD simulations and computational frameworks based on structural analysis provide many strengths
when investigating the e↵ects of somatic missense mutations, the validity of our ﬁndings must be
considered in light of the methodological framework used and its possible limitations. The method-
ological framework used in this work can be divided into four main areas: i) mutation e↵ects on global
structural dynamics, ii) binding free energy calculations, ii) graph theory-based analysis to investigate
central communication paths and long-range e↵ects, and iv) functional dynamics of DNA binding loops.
Overall, S309I showed the most drastic damaging e↵ects across all areas of analysis. This variant was
also of initial interest due to its predicted damaging e↵ects on phosphorylation, high pathogenicity
score, and damaging e↵ects on DNA binding from the MAVISp simple mode. Conversely, both R261C
and P263H were classiﬁed as neutral for local interaction with DNA by the MAVISp simple mode, but
several of the methods employed in this project suggest some degree of damaging e↵ects involving per-
turbation of communication pathways and interaction stability. This could indicate the comprehensive
predictive power of the structure-based methods applied in this project. However, benchmarking and
further testing on known damaging variants are needed.
MM/PBSA is generally considered an attractive method for calculating binding free energies due to
its history of reproducing experimental results with relatively high accuracy [40]. Using MM/PBSA,
we classiﬁed four VUS as damaging (R261C, R261H, P263H, and S309I) and A311T as neutral; how-
ever, some of these results were in conﬂict with the predictions made with FoldX, which classiﬁed
all variants as neutral upon using the ensemble structures. Generally, MM/PBSA seemed to predict
much more varied ∆∆G values than FoldX. This might indicate some inaccuracies of the MM/PBSA
method, which is inherently sensitive to sampling inadequacies. Also, multiple reports have previously
been made regarding overestimating the electrostatic contributions [57]. We attempted to overcome
Page 49 of 75
==PAGE:58==
Master’s Thesis
31-07-2024
this challenge by setting the internal dielectric constant to 8 in all simulations to account for the
highly charged DNA binding interface of ARID3A. However, as this value was not iteratively tested,
it might have contributed to a slight overestimation in the ﬁnal calculations. The inherent complexity
of MM/PBSA calculations compared to FoldX and the sampling sensitivity are likely to contribute
greatly to the discrepancy between the predictions of the two methods.
Furthermore, MM/PBSA
provides a detailed representation of binding free energy, including solvation and electrostatics, but at
the cost of higher computational demands and longer run times. Keeping these challenges in mind,
this method might be able to provide nuance to the local interaction module of MAVISp.
During this work, multiple graph-theory-based methods were employed to study the dynamic commu-
nication pathways within the ARID domain of ARID3A upon interaction with DNA. From constructing
PSN networks based on inter-atomic contacts, we identiﬁed hub nodes (degree ≥4) in each system
and, thus, how these central nodes were a↵ected across variants. A total of 6 nodes were identiﬁed as
hubs in the WT network, suggesting that these nodes are central for the functionally relevant struc-
tural signaling when interacting with DNA. The Hub analysis revealed that S309I and R261C were
damaging based on the scoring system. Given the simplicity of this scoring system, development and
optimization can be proposed to enhance its validity. One approach to optimizing the scoring system
involves incorporating machine learning methods for feature extraction and introducing the proper
weighting of the terms. By training supervised learning models such as Random Forest, Gradient
Boosting Machines, or Support Vector Machines on PSN data from variants with known e↵ects. This
approach could ensure that the most relevant features contribute appropriately to the ﬁnal score and
that a proper classiﬁcation threshold can be set. Besides the development of Machine learning models,
iterative testing and validation are crucial for reﬁning the scoring system. This involves validating the
scoring system on independent test datasets and adjusting the weights based on empirical results.
We used PCA to compare the e↵ects of the mutated variants on the essential loop dynamics. By
comparing projections between PC1 with PC2 and PC3 for both loops, we observed clearer clusterings
in the late stages of L1 compared to L2. The calculated RMSIP values show that each loop’s confor-
mations remain very similar across all variants. Given that we only included the loop regions in this
analysis and not larger subregions of the binding interface, only a very limited view of the functional
dynamics can be inferred. For example, given that the adjacent helix regions of L2 are known to have
Page 50 of 75
==PAGE:59==
Master’s Thesis
31-07-2024
central implications for DNA binding stability, including these regions in the PCA could have provided
more clarity on the variant systems’ functional dynamics. Also, multiple limitations can be identiﬁed
by conducting this type of conformation dynamics analysis without enhanced sampling, such as meta-
dynamics. By decreasing the energy barriers such that rare conformation events have a higher chance
of getting sampled and included in the analysis, a much more ﬁne-grained analysis of conformation
dynamics is possible. Metadynamics allows for a more exhaustive exploration of the conformational
space, capturing a wider variety of states and transitions, which ensures that the essential dynamics of
DNA binding loops are accurately represented. This would have been beneﬁcial in further exploring
the FEL of the variants and allowed for a higher resolution of the e↵ects caused by the variants.
Additionally, we developed an analysis pipeline based on dynamic correlation networks to study clus-
ters of correlated motion within the variant systems and allosteric mechanisms. Using dynamic cross-
correlation matrices, we generated network representations and detected communities of strongly inter-
correlated residues. Under the assumption that communities are cluster representations of groups of
residues that are functionally correlated, we used metrics of dynamic community similarity and evalu-
ation to assess functional e↵ects and allosteric mechanisms of the variants. From running the pipeline,
R261H and P263H stood out as causing the most disruption to inter-residue correlated motion, as seen
by the inter-community similarity across variants. However, we observed modularity scores around 0.4,
indicating only a moderate level of community strength within the networks. This moderate modu-
larity suggests that while distinguishable communities exist, their separation is not very strong, which
could inﬂuence the overall results and also why we did not detect a signiﬁcant correlation between
community switching and ∆BC. This outcome can be attributed to the correlation ﬁltering applied,
where only strong positive correlations (above 0.6) were included in the community detection process.
While this ﬁltering was made to enhance the clarity of community structures by focusing on robust
positive connections, it also likely excludes many potentially valuable dynamic negative correlations
and more subtle correlations. As a result, this leads to underestimating the true community com-
plexities and dynamics across the variants. This should be iteratively tested in future versions of the
pipeline. Further, from the ∆BC analysis, P263H and S309I were observed to cause the highest degree
of long-range damaging e↵ects. Overall, these ﬁndings were found to be well aligned with the interac-
tion stability ﬁndings derived from MM/PBSA, thus suggesting that these two methodologies might
capture overlapping complexities of the damaging e↵ects and thus might work well as complementary
Page 51 of 75
==PAGE:60==
Master’s Thesis
31-07-2024
tools. However, applying ∆BC to study long-range damaging e↵ects has inherent limitations. Future
improvements should focus on reﬁning the signiﬁcance threshold so that it can be generalized to other
dynamic correlation networks. Overall, this pipeline can be utilized as a complementary tool to the
MAVISp long-range and local interaction module to identify structural hotspot residues crucial for
interaction stability and function.
For much of the future work on experimental validation and testing of variants, one could also look to
the potential for coarse-grained (CG) simulations as a tool for further investigating the interaction dy-
namics of variant proteins in complex. These simulations can provide a broader view of how mutations
inﬂuence the global dynamics and interaction networks within large, multi-component systems. By
capturing long-timescale processes and large-scale conformational changes, CG simulations enable the
study of how mutations a↵ect the overall stability, ﬂexibility, and interaction patterns of transcription
factors. This holistic approach can uncover additional insights that are diﬃcult to obtain with all-atom
MD simulations alone, which are greatly limited by simulation time and system size. As such, coarse-
grained simulations could o↵er a valuable complementary tool for studying the interaction dynamics
of cancer-related proteins, ultimately contributing to a more nuanced understanding of their role in
disease mechanisms.

Conclusion and Final Remarks
In the present study, microsecond MD simulations were performed to examine the e↵ect of 5 VUS
on the interaction stability, functional dynamics, and long-range e↵ects of ARID3A when interacting
with DNA. We speciﬁcally explored the conformational dynamics of the loops L1 and L2, as they are
functionally essential for facilitating the DNA binding capabilities of ARID3A. To complement the
existing MAVISp modules within ensemble mode, multiple novel features and methods were explored
for their alternative ways of analyzing variant e↵ects and, thus, potential applications within the MAV-
ISp framework. Both R261C and P263H were classiﬁed as neutral for local interaction with DNA in
the MAVISp simple mode but were reclassiﬁed as damaging using binding free energy methods and
multiple graph-based methods based on atomic contacts and dynamic cross-correlation. Further, the
analysis also conﬁrmed and elaborated upon how S309I exerts damaging e↵ects on the local interac-
tion with DNA. This could suggest the predictive power of MAVISp and some of the novel methods
employed. However, further study, testing, and benchmarking should be utilized to set meaningful
Page 52 of 75
==PAGE:61==
Master’s Thesis
31-07-2024
classiﬁcation thresholds and generalize some of the methodologies used in this project.
In conclusion, this project underlines and supports the relevance of applying computational frameworks
and methods within structural bioinformatics to address the challenge of VUS classiﬁcation and further
our understanding of the complex molecular dynamics of the cancer driver protein ARID3A upon
interacting with DNA. By continuing the development and optimization of computational frameworks
for VUS classiﬁcation, many of the challenges regarding VUS can be addressed. This advancement
not only aids in expanding our knowledge of disease mechanisms but also paves the way for the
advancements of targeted therapeutic development and personalized medicine.
