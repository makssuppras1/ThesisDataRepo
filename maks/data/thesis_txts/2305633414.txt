Summary
With an oil price having dipped below 40 USD at the time of writing, the focus on
maximising the value of producing oil reservoirs is stronger than ever. ŌĆØThe valueŌĆØ
can mean various things e.g. net present value, ultimate recovery, or production rate
over the next 30 days. Whichever parameter one seeks to optimise, knowledge of the
subsurface is a necessity in order to accurately model fluid movement.
Information about the subsurface is expensive, whether you choose to obtain it
through drilling, logging existing wells, or by performing surface-based surveys. By
utilising production data, history matching enables reservoir engineers to extract
knowledge about the subsurface without having to resort to these expensive data-
gathering methods.
Automated history matching is based on filtering algorithms that use production
data to refine the estimates of the subsurface. Most attempts at automated history
matching currently utilise the Ensemble Kalman Filter. This thesis investigates an
alternative approach to automated history matching, using the Unscented Kalman
Filter.
We show that the Unscented Kalman Filter fails to outperform the Ensemble Kalman
Filter in an estimation of the entire subsurface, and propose an alternative use for the
Unscented Kalman Filter. By parameterising the subsurface, the filters can be used
to estimate states with dimensionalities in the single or double digits, rather than the
tens to hundreds of thousands of dimensions of real world reservoir models.
The Unscented Kalman Filter is shown to accurately estimate a number of parameters
of up to, and including, the number of wells in the model.
It is shown that the
filter performance varies with the update frequency, and that performing the data
assimilation step as often as practically possible allows for better estimates and overall
faster results.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
We live in a world based on petroleum products. Everything from our transportation
and heating to synthetic clothes and fertilizers are, to greater or lesser extents, de-
pendent on a constant flow of oil. As many people know, this oil is extracted from
underground reservoirs, but to say that it is simply about drilling a hole and watch-
ing the oil flow is an oversimplification. I will assume that the reader was aware of
this and has a fundamental knowledge of petrophysics and petrochemistry e.g. what
porosity is, what the formation volume factor describes, or what a bubblepoint means.
The level of detail in explanations of some of the more basic concepts will reflect this
assumption. For a much more thorough explanation of all of these concepts, and many
more, the reader is referred to the reservoir engineering literature, e.g. [Ahm10].
No effort will be made to describe how the oil reservoirs formed; we simply accept
that they did. Should the reader wish to learn more on this topic, I refer to the
petroleum geology literature e.g. [Cha83].
1.1
Reservoir Engineering
The production of an oil reservoir can be split into three stages.1
The primary
recovery, driven by depletion, is the simplest, and it is the grain of truth in the
misconception about oil production mentioned above. In short, wells are drilled and
oil is forced out by overpressure in the reservoir, expansion of the oil phase or gas
cap / compression of the matrix due to a reduction in pore pressure. In essence it
works like opening a soda - shake it, make a hole and watch the fluids flow to equalise
the pressure. Regrettably, like with the soda, this isnŌĆÖt normally very effective, and
recovery of more than 20 % is rare.
The secondary recovery, driven by improved recovery techniques, requires more ac-
tivity on the surface. Old production wells are converted or new wells are drilled
in order to start injection. Fluids naturally present in the reservoir (gas or water)
are (re)injected to maintain reservoir pressure and sweep the remaining oil towards
the production wells. Imagine taking the almost-full soda from before and holding it
under the faucet. Water pours in, but the bottle doesnŌĆÖt get bigger. Instead, water

In order to determine optimal controls, accurate models of the subsurface are required.
These models are generated by geologists, geophysicists and petrophysicists working
together to interpret log data, seismic data and geological information. They deter-
mine a likely subsurface, and based on this, the optimal control strategy can be found
through reservoir simulation. For additional safeguarding, multiple subsurfaces can
be constructed based on the uncertainties, and the optimal controls are then found
by statistical methods that optimise production across all of them.
Closed-Loop Reservoir Management (CLRM) takes the optimisation one step further.
Consisting of an optimisation step that defines the optimal controls based on the
current model of the system, a simulation step that predicts the behaviour of the
system using these control, and a data assimilation step that updates the model before
repeating the cycle, CLRM is ideally a system that needs no human intervention in
optimising the production of the oil reservoir. This loop, shown in Figure 1.2, is
meant to continue throughout the lifetime of the reservoir to continuously increase
the reservoirŌĆÖs ultimate value[JBD09].
The trend is for CLRM to use the Ensemble Kalman Filter (EnKF) for the data
assimilation step, but to the authors knowledge no work has been published on the
application of the Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF). The UKF potentially offers com-
putational advantages over the EnKF. The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the
UKF for potential use in CLRM.

CHAPTER 2
Optimal Control
Strategies
Optimal controls are, put simply, the production and injection rates/pressures that,
over the lifetime of the reservoir, optimise production with respect to a given set of
objectives e.g. NPV or ultimate recovery. This requires the simulation software to
accurately predict the behaviour of the reservoir. If these predictions are inaccurate,
the optimal controls will be wrong, and the effort might be wasted compared to
implementing a reactive strategy.
For accurate predictions, two things are needed: an accurate model and accurate
input to the model. The main concern of this thesis is determination only of the
permeability field, but in order to show the reader some of the factors that can
limit the accuracy of the model, the following will give a brief introduction to general
reservoir simulation. Should the reader wish to know more about the topics discussed,
[H├Ėr15] performs a thorough derivation and explanation of a multitude of different
topics and approaches.
2.1
Reservoir Simulation
Reservoir simulators are built around principles from fluid mechanics, physical chem-
istry, and numerical mathematics, and they model the flow of fluids and changes in
pressure in the reservoir. The flows and pressure changes are found as solutions to
the set of partial differential equations that describe the state of the system at a given
time. The equations are derived from the mass balance equation and DarcyŌĆÖs law, and
the exact formulation varies depending on the assumptions made about the system
e.g. on compressibility or mass transfer between phases. We are going to assume a
no-flow boundary meaning that only production and injection wells can change the
fluid content in the reservoir. We utilise a black-oil model for the fluid description.
As the flow equations will be solved for discrete systems, we will start by dicretising
the reservoir.

Science attempts to describe the world in which we live in through models and theories.
The origin of these is often found in observations of the world around us. Some times
scientific advances are made by coincidence, e.g. with the discovery of penicilin on
moldy bread. More often than not, however, advances come as a result of hard work
and meticulous study.
The study of any subject often involves measurements: observations made in order
to draw conclusions about properties such as the length or mass of an object.
3.1
Measurements and Observations
In an ideal world the relation between measurement and property would be uniquely
defined e.g. a ruler could be used to find an exact length of a car in ft, the weight in
kg of a square tile with known density by measuring the side length, or the period of
a simple pendulum in s from the length of the pendulum. All of these relations can
be described by exact functions of the measured length as shown in Figure 3.1.
In the real world properties are rarely homogeneous. Car lengths vary, tile sizes differ
depending on clay shrinkage and the gravity field in which the pendulums swing
is inhomogeneous: the properties follow some distribution. Nevertheless, were our
measurements 100 % accurate this would not cause any problems - had we determined
that the length of the car was 3.8 mm longer than specified in the design plans, it
would simply be 3.8 mm longer. The distribution of the property would translate into
a distribution in the measurements, as it is shown in Figure 3.2 for two measurements.
The problem comes because measurements in the real world arenŌĆÖt perfect. Measure-
ments will be prone to noise, and thus, in addition to the distribution of the property,
comes some measurement error, as Figure 3.3 illustrates.
This problem effectively makes measurements meaningless in high-noise applications
and it therefore warranted the foundation of filtering as a mathematical discipline.

4.3.1
Resampling the EnKF
The resampling methods suggested in Sections 4.2 and 4.2.1 were performed in order
to reduce the number of wrong particles and improve the ensemble estimate of the
true solution. Roughening was introduced in order to avoid complete collapse of the
ensemble.
It is shown in [MSO13] that the EnKF based on small ensembles also tends to collapse
over time.
As opposed to the general particle filter, where the collapse happens
because of the resampling, this is caused by a coupling of the ensemble members. The
coupling occurs because the measurement updates are based on common covariances
for the whole ensemble. For high particle numbers the problem diminishes, and it
disappears completely as N ŌåÆŌł×. Rather than increasing the number of particles, a
resampling step can also be introduced to the EnKF.

4.5.2
Uncertainties in Reservoir Models
In the previous and this chapters we have derived several different filters and their
uses in filtering out measurement noise. This is very useful in systems where the
model (the function h) is well defined and representative of the real world. This is
far from the case with oil production.
As has been mentioned, the fluid behaviour is an approximation. DarcyŌĆÖs law is also
valid only for laminar flows, and starts to break down for turbulent flows. Even if we
assumed that a complete description of the phase and fluid behaviours was obtained,
the model of the underground falls short - as was discussed in Section 2.1.1, the
discrete nature of the reservoir discription caused averaging over large volumes of
inhomogeneous subsurface.
Even if computing power developed to the level that we could handle microscopic
descriptions of oil reservoirs, we suffer from the sheer lack of information. Truth about
the subsurface can only really be obtained by drilling wells and logging them, and
wells are expensive. The models are therefore built on qualified guesswork. Geologists
and geophysicists make an enourmous effort to create subsurface models that satisfy
geological history, depositional environments, seismic surveys and much more, but
without measurement, they will realistically always contain mistakes. It is like trying
to build a full model of a city while only being allowed to look up through a handful
of manholes.
Filtering is a clever way to estimate the state of the system, but if the model that
propagates the system is wrong, no amount of filtering is going to correct the errors.
It is with all these limitations in mind that we must interpret the results in the
upcoming sections.

5.3 Homogeneous Permeability

With the dismissal of full-field estimation as a use for the UKF, we turn to a different
approach. Rocks are indeed inhomogeneous on a microscopic level, but rocks formed
under the same conditions are going to show relatively homogeneous properties on
a macroscopic scale. If tectonic movement has caused uplifting of one part of the
underground, you might see two separate zones, but each individual zone will be
relatively homogeneous.
As such the true permeability solution might instead be
contained in a subspace of parameters e.g. the zonal permeability/variance and the
number of zones. By creating a ŌĆØgenerating functionŌĆØ that takes this set of subspace
parameters and generates a full permeability field, the estimation would no longer

and calculate the relative changes in measurements. The procedure is in this aspect
identical to that of Section 5.4, but rather than tracking the sensitivity over time for
a handful of systems, I chose a single (the seventh, as it had the highest sensitivity)

We see that the liquid flow rates start to flatten out for high permeability multipliers.

liquid is going to flow regardless of the injection. With sufficiently high permeability,
the reservoir will very quickly equalise, and any further increase in permeability will
have no effect. The same effect is seen for the pressure sensitivity: with a close-to-zero
permeability, reservoir fluids do not flow at all, and the pressure remains very high.
The same effect also explains why the sensitivities were seen in Figure 5.10 to change
so much within the first few days of production.

6.3.1
Discrete Parameters
Until now, the subspace parameters have been continuous, in that the permeability
estimates could vary continuously. The third parameter introduced above is discrete.
A couple of important points need to be made about this:
ŌĆó The Kalman filters are not designed for discretely defined states.
ŌĆó Forcing the Kalman filters to operate with discrete states is like using a hammer
with a screw rather than a nail - it may work in some cases, but the results are
unpredictable and sub-par for the intended use.
ŌĆó Discrete parameters are likely unavoidable, should one seek a parametrisation
of the subsurface. The number of high-permeable channels is not a continuum
nor is the number of cells with a given permeability, just to give two examples.
A simple example can illustrate the problem. We imagine having two particles. Assign
to them the values 1 and 2 and calculate the state estimate as the mean 1.5. Even
though our particles are discrete, the estimate is not.
Luckily, the covariances P need not be discrete. This means that, at every time step,
the a priori particles can be generated based on whichever method is chosen. Once
the N particles have been generated, the discrete parameters can be rounded off,
passed to the generating function and the filtering is conducted as normal. Once the
a posteriori estimate is obtained, e.g. the 1.5 from the short example above, this too
is rounded off.
Through this procedure, the filters can be implemented in an almost normal fashion.
The rounding will cause errors, but as has been shown thus far, the filtering process
is in the first place not infallible when applied to history matching.
The particle filter is a special case in that the particles experience no incremental
changes. Roughening can simply be disabled for the discrete parameters, and the
discrete parameters will then be resampled as discrete parameters. The only problem
==PAGE:70==

arises when making the a posteriori estimate, if a mean estimator is used. Using the
mode or median estimators, not even rounding of the a posteriori estimate would be
needed. Regrettably, at three dimensions, the particle filter is already reaching the
computational limitations of a standard workstation, so this advantage is not likely
to be utilised.

CHAPTER 7
Concluding Remarks
The purpose of this thesis was to investigate the Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF)
for potential use in the history matching/parameter estimation step of Closed-Loop
Reservoir Management (CLRM). Multiple filters have been introduced, and they have
been used to illustrate the ideas behind, and problems with, automated history match-
ing. We start the concluding remarks to this endeavor with a short summary of the
content and findings of the thesis.
7.1
Thesis Outline
We derived the Kalman Filter for linear problems and showed the concepts behind
the filter. The Extended Kalman Filter was derived to handle non-linear problems by
linearising the equations, and the errors introduced by the linearisation were demon-
strated with a simple 1-dimensional example.
The Particle Filter was introduced as a method to avoid the linearisation problems.
Based on the concept of particles, the Ensemble Kalman Filter (EnKF) was described
as a method of obtaining the statistical parameters necessary for the Kalman Filter
to work without linearising the problem. Following the reasoning behind the EnKF,
the UKF was derived based on ŌĆØintelligentŌĆØ particle selection through the unscented
transform.
We applied the UKF to an inhomogeneous 8 ├Ś 8 ├Ś 1 cell permeability field with each
cell measuring 20 m├Ś20 m├Ś4 m, and with two wells drilled. This gave the reservoir a
lifetime of 180 days. The tests showed that the estimates obtained by the UKF allowed
for production values to be predicted. It was shown that the UKF, while accurately
capturing the production values, failed to estimate the reference permeability field.
Based on poor scaling with the dimensionality, and that real-world reservoirs are
generally very high-dimensional problems, the UKF was dismissed in favour of the
EnKF for full-field estimation.
We suggested that the permeability field might be based on a subspace of parameters.
The UKF could then potentially out-perform the EnKF in problems with up to Ōł╝50
dimensions. Following this reasoning, the idea of a permeability field generating func-
tion was introduced, and the UKF was applied in estimating a single parameter. In
==PAGE:80==
72
7 Concluding Remarks
the investigation of the single-parameter estimation, it was found that the usefulness
of the filter varied over time, and that increasing the update frequency improved the
estimate convergence.
We increased the number of subspace parameters to two and showed that estimating
an extra parameter caused a more erratic estimate behaviour. Following the findings
for the single-parameter estimations, we showed that an update frequency of at least
5 updates per day was required for the filter to consistently converge on the reference
solution. Roughly 25 updates per day in the 180 day reservoir lifetime was suggested
as the optimal choice, as it would result in a stabilised estimate in the shortest
possible time, while still being realistic to obtain. 25 updates per day in the 180 day
lifetime corresponds to an update every two days for a reservoir with a lifetime of 25
years. An estimate for the parameters would thus be obtained in roughly two months,
allowing for defining the optimal well controls with more than 95 % of the reservoir
life remaining.
We suggested that the measurement noise caused the estimates to fluctuate, and it
was shown that overestimating the measurement noise would cause slower, but more
stable, convergence of the filter.
Finally, we defined a three-parameter model in which the third parameter was dis-
crete. The discrete parameter is problematic, because the UKF isnŌĆÖt meant to deal
with discrete parameters. Nevertheless, a parameterisation of the permeability field
is likely going to involve discrete parameters in one way or another, and so modifica-
tions were made to bypass the mathematical limitations. Sub-optimal behaviour for
the filter was thus anticipated. The three-parameter filter was shown to fail at esti-
mating the correct parameters for the two-well model, but was shown to accurately
predict production in a similar way to the inhomogeneous full-field model. Increasing
the number of wells to four, the filter managed to occationally converge on the refer-
ence solution, but the problems with the modifications made to accomodate discrete
parameters showed themselves as the estimates worsening with every update.
Reducing the level of artificial noise showed that the filter was able to accurately
estimate the reference solution, even when this solution included discrete parameters.
7.2
Further Comments
The filters led to convergence on the reference solution in the synthetic, simplified
1/2/3-dimensional systems, while the full-field filtering failed. If the filters were set
up with a set of correlations/constraints for the state, this could effectively serve the
same purpose as the parameterisation of the model and reduce the dimensionality of
the estimated state. While the problem still might be underspecified (depending on
the amount of constraints), various optimisation algorithms could then potentially be
used to help the filters reach correct, or at least better, estimates.
==PAGE:81==
7.3 Suggestions for Future Work
73
Defining both the correlations/constraints and the parameterised models would need
help from other fields of study. Mathematics alone cannot solve the problem, and a
vast multidisciplinary effort is needed if this idea is going to ever work.
That being said; history matching is already being done. Often history matching is
done in broad strokes, and e.g. the permeability of a large section of the reservoir, or
the porosity of a certain layer, might be multiplied by a constant to see if the results fit
production better. These modifications are thought up by reservoir engineers based
on gut feeling and experience. This form of history matching already is based on
a single parameter or two. If filtering algorithms could be made easily accessible in
commercial software, this trial and error approach could be automated. Because of
the low dimensionality of the modifications, the UKF would be an ideal candidate for
the data assimilation.
Fully functional CLRM is the holy grail of reservoir management. Just setting up
an initial model, starting production and letting the computers make you as much
money as possible is a dream. We have, however, shown that, even if the forecast
models and optimisation algorithms were perfect, the parameter estimation of the full
reservoir state prevents this from happening in the foreseeable future. But perhaps
the first step shouldnŌĆÖt be attempting to estimate the entire state, but to use history
matching as a tool to simplify and automate what humans already do.
7.3
Suggestions for Future Work
Some of the findings in this thesis invite for further investigation:
ŌĆó The parameterised model developed in this thesis is crude and overly simplified
for real reservoirs. Work on a more accurate parameterisation is needed, if the
idea is to be applied to real reservoirs.
ŌĆó A parameterised model is likely to involve a certain level of randomness in the
generating function. This randomness may require multiple realisations of the
permeability field from the generating function. Running multiple realisations
may make the UKF more computationally heavy than the EnKF without any
other benefits. A generating function with random elements is thus worth in-
vestigating.
ŌĆó The UKF was here modified to accept discrete parameters. It is not intended
for this, and the behaviour was therefore uncertain. A more complete study of
the problems with discretisation would help solve the discrete filtering problem.
ŌĆó It was shown that the sensitivity of the system depends on several factors,
including update frequency and the time elapsed since first production. Further,
it was shown that the sensitivity dropped to effectively 0 for some periods of
==PAGE:82==
74
7 Concluding Remarks
time. A deeper investigation of the filter applicability at different times would
help determine if/when the filtering process is useful.
