Abstract
In efforts to achieve decarbonization, hydrogen can play a crucial role in reducing emis-
sions in hard-to-abate sectors. The production of green hydrogen, especially when inte-
grated with variable renewable energy sources, is still predominantly within the research
and development stage, with significant efforts focused on simulating the dynamic be-
havior of the process to enhance its understanding and effectiveness. This thesis in-
vestigates the integration and operation of an hydrogen production system within a hy-
brid wind-hydrogen power plant. The research focuses on optimizing the interactions
among wind energy fluctuations, electrolyzer operation, and hydrogen storage. Special
focus is given to the operational constraints and efficiency of PEM (Proton Exchange
Membrane) electrolyzers. Various power allocation strategies are proposed to enhance
system efficiency in a hybrid power context. These strategies are assessed across mul-
tiple operational scenarios, including market participation for both power and hydrogen
sales, as well as through sensitivity analysis concerning hydrogen pricing. The results
emphasize the critical influence of power price, hydrogen price, and hydrogen storage
on decision-making, providing important insights into the system’s overall performance.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The world is undergoing a major transformation in the production, conversion, storage
and use of energy to keep global warming below 1.5°C. There is a growing awareness
of the need to transition to a society where energy does not contribute to climate change
by replacing fossil fuels with renewable energy.
As renewable energy sources become more prevalent in the power sector worldwide, it
is critical to implement solutions that use renewable electricity to decarbonise end-use
sectors through power-to-gas strategies or to convert electricity into high-value chem-
icals or fuels. This need is particularly urgent for sectors where direct electrification is
challenging, known as ’hard-to-abate’ sectors.
Hydrogen plays a fundamental role in this transition as it can be converted into other en-
ergy carriers such as ammonia, methanol, methane and liquid hydrocarbons. Hydrogen
can be used in fuel cells or burned in engines and turbines. Fuel cells can be used in
stationary applications, such as large power plants, microgrids or back-up power gen-
eration, as well as in various transport applications, including fuel cell electric vehicles,
trucks, buses, ferries and ships [1].
Currently, only about 4% of the world’s hydrogen is produced from electrolysis, known as
’green hydrogen’ because it uses renewable energy with zero emissions. The majority
of hydrogen production is the so called ’grey hydrogen’, derived from fossil fuels such
as natural gas (47%), coal (27%) and oil (22%) [2].
While China has the most ambitious strategy for green hydrogen, already accounting for
more than 40% of the electrolysis projects that have reached final investment decision
globally, the European Union aims to reach 40 GW of electrolysis capacity by 2030, with
a target volume of 10 million tonnes of hydrogen per year (MtH2/year), which would
require an additional 40 GW of electrolysis capacity in neighbouring countries [2].
1.2
Problem analysis and Project Objective
While there are strong intentions regarding hydrogen’s role in the energy transition, sig-
nificant challenges remain in its implementation and integration with renewables. These
challenges arise due to the complexity, underdevelopment of the technology, and its
high costs [1]. Consequently, substantial efforts in research and development are fo-
cused on building models to simulate the operation of electrolyzers and scenarios where
this technology can be integrated and operated optimally.
1
==PAGE:13==
Several models for electrolysis plants have been developed to simulate the behavior of
electrolysis systems. However, few models have taken into account parameters such
as temperature, pressure, and projected them in a dynamic operation [3].
The main objective of this project is to build a model for an electrolysis system that
simulates its dynamic behavior and operational limitations. The objective extends to
optimizing the operation of this technology, considering its constraints when integrated
in a hybrid power plant system, enhancing its efficiency, and minimizing the associated
operational costs.
1.3
Research Questions
Given the project objective, this can be divided in three main research questions:
• What are the key processes and constraints in electrolysis, and how can they be
dynamically modeled to accurately represent system behavior?
• How can a large-scale electrolysis system be operated when integrated into a
hybrid power plant to ensure high efficiency and reducing operational costs?
• What strategies can be employed to optimize the operation of the hybrid system
to increase potential revenue?
1.4
Report Structure
This report is structured as follows: Chapter 2 presents a literature review on the elec-
trolysis process, as well as the state of the art of the technologies and active projects,
concluding with key takeaways that justify the chosen technology.
Chapter 3 details the development of the hydrogen system model, including both the
electrolysis, fuel cell and storage systems.
Chapter 4 covers the integration of the model into a hybrid system context, beginning
with system sizing and component selection, follows an introduction of how the model of
a single electrolyzer reflects its dynamic behavior, and it concludes with an explanation
and analysis of the operation strategies.
Chapter 5 discusses the results of the control strategies, highlighting the most influential
parameters. Finally, conclusions and future work are addressed in Chapters 6 and 7.

2.1
Water electrolysis
The electrolysis of water is considered a well-known principle to produce oxygen and
hydrogen gas. The core of an electrolysis unit is an electrochemical cell, which is filled
with pure water and has two electrodes connected with an external power supply. At a
certain voltage between both electrodes, the electrodes start to produce hydrogen gas
at the negatively electrode (cathode) and oxygen gas at the positively electrode (anode).
A combination of cells, connected in series or in parallel, becomes a stack. The amount
of gases produced per unit time is directly related to the current that passes through the
electrochemical cell. The overall reaction is:
H2O →H2 + 1
2O2
(2.1)
There are different methods to achieve this reaction, and the selection of materials and
operating conditions dictate the specific half-reactions within the electrolyzer cell. Nowa-
days, two main technologies are used for large-scale production: Liquid Alkaline and
Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolysis [4]. These methods share a funda-
mental structure. Nevertheless, the materials employed in the cell, leading to different
half-reactions, vary significantly. Section 2.2 will offer an overview of both technologies.
The energy required for the decomposition of water is the enthalpy change of the pro-
cess (∆H). The minimum reversible work necessary for this reaction to take place is
the variation in Gibbs free energy (∆G) that must be supplied to the electrodes in the
form of electrical energy. The remainder is the thermal energy (Q), which is the product
of the process temperature (T) and the entropy change (∆S). These thermodynamic
quantities can be expressed as follows:
∆H = ∆G + Q = ∆G + T · ∆S
(2.2)
The electrolysis process is a chemical reaction that is not spontaneous (∆G > 0) and
endothermic (∆H > 0). The opposite process occurs in fuel cells and is spontaneous
(∆G < 0) and exothermic (∆H < 0). The reversible cell voltage Urev is the lowest neces-
sary voltage for electrolysis to occur. This voltage is a function of ∆G and is determined
by the following equation:
Urev = ∆G
zF
(2.3)
3
==PAGE:15==
where z is the number of charges transferred per hydrogen molecule (z = 2) and F
is the Faraday constant (96.485 C/mol). If the thermal energy T · ∆S is supplied by
electricity, the minimum voltage for water electrolysis is referred to as the thermoneutral
voltage Utn and it is determined by the following equation:
Utn = ∆H
zF
(2.4)
For an electrolysis process to take place at standard ambient conditions (temperature
of 298.15 K and pressure of 1 atm), the required molar electrical work (Gibbs energy) is
237.21 kJ/mol, while the required molar enthalpy is ∆H = 285.84 kJ/mol. Substituting
∆G and ∆H into Eq.2.3 and Eq.2.4, the reversible and thermoneutral voltages of an
electrolytic cell at standard conditions are obtained: Urev = 1.229 V and Utn = 1.481 V
[5].
Figure 2.1 shows the voltage over different operating current density, for electrolysis and
fuel cell operation, together with the reversible and thermoneutral voltage values.
Voltage is crucial in both electrolysis and fuel cell operations, not merely as a facilitator of
the reactions but also in relation to heat generation. In electrolysis, the process absorbs
heat up to the thermoneutral voltage. Beyond this point, it proportionally generates
heat. On the other hand, the fuel cells produce heat across all operating conditions.
The amount of heat generated is directly related to the gap between the reversible cell
voltage and the operating voltage.

2.2
Electrolyzer state of the art
Alkaline Water Electrolysis (AWE) and Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolysis
emerge as the primary methods for green hydrogen production, with Solid Oxide Elec-
trolysis Cell (SOEC) also recognized for its high-temperature operations, although they
are beyond the scope of our current discussion due to the focus on low-temperature
applications.
(a) PEM [6]
(b) AWE [7]
Figure 2.2: Schematics of Proton Exchange Membrane and Alkaline Water electrolyzer
cell.
Alkaline Water Electrolysis is a well-established method for hydrogen production up to
the megawatt scale. In this process, an alkaline solution (KOH/NaOH) at the cathode
produces hydrogen gas (H2) and hydroxyl ions (OH – ), which then migrate to the anode,
yielding oxygen and water. AWE typically operates at 60–80°C using a 20-30% aqueous
alkaline solution [8].
PEM electrolysis, on the other hand, utilizes a solid polymer electrolyte as a proton con-
ductor. PEM water electrolysis is obtained by pumping of water to the anode where it
is spilt into oxygen, protons (H+) and electrons. These protons are traveled via proton
conducting membrane to the cathode side. The electrons exit from the anode through
the external power circuit, which provides the driving force (cell voltage) for the reaction
[9]. PEM is marked by its compact design, higher current density, and rapid response
times, making it suitable for dynamic applications. Despite these advantages, PEM sys-
tems are associated with higher costs due to the use of noble materials like platinum and
iridium oxide as catalysts, and they can be less durable due to the acidic environment
within the cell [10].

2.3
Overview of Existing Projects
A review of green hydrogen production projects reveals significant diversity in their scale,
technologies, and purposes.
Renewables sources and application
The majority of these projects are situated in Europe, North America, and China [11],
employing primarily wind and solar energy, which are characterized by significant fluc-
tuations. Electrolysis is used primarily for small-scale on-site hydrogen production in
industrial applications
Recently, projects have been developed to integrate hydrogen production in the electric-
ity market, a great example is the Energiepark Mainz [12], where a technical-economic
analysis has been performed on a 4 MW PEM based electrolysis plant, operating within
the ancillary services of the German electricity market.
6
==PAGE:18==
Energy storage
Approximately 24% of the operational power-to-hydrogen pilot plants source electricity
from the public grid. Among these grid-connected systems, only 20% use batteries for
energy storage. In contrast, more than half of the projects where the renewable energy
generator is directly connected to the electrolyzer employ a battery bank for storage [11].
Batteries help manage load transients, provide bus stability, and smooth out the renew-
able power output, reducing the need for frequent cycling of the electrolyzer. However,
it has been demonstrated that integrating a wind turbine with an AC-to-DC converter
optimizes system performance and negates the requirement for a battery connection
[13]. In stand-alone energy systems, where among all, mainly the HARI project [14],
the PURE project [15], and Hydrogen Office [16], have been the focus of the literature
review, the support for the electrolyzer operation will come from a fuel cell when there
is no primary energy generation.
Offshore hydrogen production
Offshore hydrogen production is receiving increasing attention due to recent studies
indicating that transporting hydrogen molecules for large-scale projects is more cost-
effective than transporting electrons from offshore wind production through HVDC con-
nections when it is located more than 100-150 km from the coast [17]. Transporting
hydrogen through pipelines is more cost-effective than transporting power through ca-
bles, especially when onshore hydrogen production is used. Some initiatives, such as
Dolphin in the United Kingdom [18], PosHYdon [19] and H2opZee [20] in the Nether-
lands, and Brintø Energy Islands [21], are currently assessing the feasibility of offshore
hydrogen projects in Northern Europe.
2.4
Literature review take-away
In concluding the literature review, the decision to employ a Proton Exchange Mem-
brane (PEM) electrolyzer and a fuel cell in the hybrid system, specifically for stand-
alone hydrogen-only operation coupled with wind energy, was influenced by a variety
of considerations that emerged from an analysis of existing projects and technological
evaluations. The PEM electrolyzer was selected for its dynamic operational capabilities,

electrolyzer varies based on the hydrogen produced inside the stack, and therefore on

Wind-Hydrogen System Integration
In this chapter, the hydrogen model described in the earlier sections of the report is
implemented in a larger system context to illustrate its operational functionality and dy-
namic behaviour. Section 4.1 outlines and defines the dimensions of the hybrid sys-
tem in which this model is implemented. In Section 4.2, the dynamic operation of the
electrolyzer is discussed. Firstly, based on the output and operating conditions of the
electrolyzer model, the operational states of the latter are identified and explained in
subsection 4.2.1, then, all the control dynamic characteristics are analyzed and applied
to a sample simulation in subsection 4.2.2. The chapter then examines various power
allocation strategies, structured as logic control systems, to optimise systems efficiency
and functionality. Section 4.3 deals specifically within the electrolysis subsystem. Sec-
tion 4.4 expands the scope to include power allocation strategies for the entire system,
providing a comprehensive approach to managing energy resources.
4.1
System Overview and Sizing
Although no specific case study has been carried out, the hybrid system has been de-
signed to potentially represent a future scenario, and it is illustrated in Figure 4.1. It
consists of an offshore wind turbine connected to both a land-based hydrogen system
and the electrical grid. As presented in Figure 3.1, the hydrogen system consists in
electrolyzers, gas treatment systems, a compressor, a pressurized hydrogen tank, and
a fuel cell. In this setup, the grid serves only as an export to facilitate certain simulated
scenarios, as will be discussed later in Section 4.4.
The remainder of this Section provides a brief introduction to each component of the
hybrid system, including their role and sizing.
Electrolyzer Setup
The hydrogen production subsystem consists of four equal 1 MW electrolyzers config-
ured in parallel. The electrolyzers specifications are displayed in Table 3.1. The parallel
configuration allows each unit to operate independently through its own power converter
and gas conditioning unit, facilitating independent control of its operation, and ensuring
that power allocation can be optimised in real time to match the variable output of the
wind farm. This operation strategy will be discussed in Section 4.3.

==PAGE:43==
Figure 4.1: Hybrid system configuration
Compressor and Storage System
For hydrogen storage, the system includes a centralised compressor with a capacity
of up to 0.2 MW. The compressor is used to compress hydrogen for storage in a pres-
surised tank operating at a maximum of 300 bar. The capacity of the compressor has
been determined to allow for scenarios where all electrolyzers are operating at full load,
producing hydrogen at the required output pressure of 320 bar, including a safety margin
for operational reliability.
The storage tank has a capacity of 2 tonnes of hydrogen, a size chosen to reflect the
potential demand from an off-taker of green hydrogen. This aspect of the system design,
together with further details of the operational strategy, is discussed in Section 4.4. The
storage tank selected is a Type I pressurised tank, characterised by thick metal walls
and a typically cylindrical or quasi-conformal shape, which ensures durability and safety
in the handling of high-pressure hydrogen storage [46].
Fuel Cell
A 0.5 MW fuel cell has been integrated into the system to generate electricity from stored
hydrogen. Primarily, this electricity is used to support the operation of the electrolyzer,
and it serves as a backup power source, both where no wind is available. Addition-
ally, the fuel cell capacity exceeds these needs, allowing for the simulation of scenarios
where exporting electricity to the grid is beneficial, at very high electricity prices.
Opting for a fuel cell instead of a battery increases the system’s flexibility in various op-
erational scenarios, such as those without off-takers period for the produced hydrogen.
Additionally, fuel cells provide a longer-term storage solution than batteries [58], which
is advantageous in scenarios where wind power is unavailable for extended periods.
Wind Turbine
The system includes a single offshore wind turbine with a capacity of 8 MW located at 40
km from the shore, selected to cover scenarios ranging from all electrolyzers operating
at maximum load to periods of no power availability. The choice of a larger turbine

capacity facilitates the creation of dynamic interactions with the electrical grid, allowing
both the management of surplus energy and the exploration of grid-related strategies.
The wind turbine is not modelled on a physical basis, but it is represented by input

approach focuses on understanding the effects of changing wind conditions on hydrogen

Electrical grid
As previously mentioned in this Section, the grid serves only as an export, and conse-

This approach is chosen first to produce green-hydrogen only, and therefore produced

4.2
Electrolyzer Dynamic operation
Having outlined the system components and their interactions, the focus now shifts to its
dynamic behavior. As explained in Chapter 3, while the fuel cell operation is assumed
unconstrained, the focus of the control analysis has been on the electrolyzer operation.
It is important to note the dynamic nature of the electrolyzer model, which uses only
the power as its input. All outputs are determined by the conditions set in previous
time-steps.
4.2.1
Electrolyzer states
To effectively manage the performance of the electrolyzer, it is necessary to categorize
its operation based on specific states. Each state is defined by three operational pa-
rameters: power input (Pin), hydrogen production (H2,r), and cathode pressure (Pcat).
As noted in the previous Section 3.8, between pressure and temperature, the limiting
factor to reach nominal parameter, is the pressure, as it takes longer time. Therefore,
temperature is not considered as a modeling variable for identifying the state of the
electrolyzer.
In steady conditions, the electrolyzer operates in three principal states [59, 60, 36, 35]:
• On: The system is in normal operating condition. Here, the cathode pressure is
maintained at 30 bar, the power input varies within the load operational range, and
the hydrogen produced is proportional to the power input.
• Off: The system is not operating. In this state, the pressure and temperature of
the system are in ambient condition, no power is consumed, and no hydrogen is
produced.
33
==PAGE:45==
• Hot Stand-by: A percentage of rated power (Psb = 2%) is supplied to maintain
the temperature, pressure, and functionality of auxiliary devices. The cathode
pressure is maintained at 30 bar, as no leakage are assumed, and no hydrogen
is produced.
The dynamic operation of an electrolyzer is more complex due to the need to manage
the transition phases between the principal states. For this reason, three additional
states are introduced:
• Cold Start-Up: The system is in the process of turning on, building up the pres-
sure and the temperature of the stack. As explained in the previous Section, the
cathode pressure remains below 30 bar, the power input varies within the load
operational range, and no hydrogen is released, since it is stored within the stack.
• Switch Off: This state corresponds to the system shutdown process. Pressure
and temperature gradually decrease, no power is consumed, and the hydrogen
stored to maintain internal pressure is released at an approximate rate of 1 mol/s
after treatment for 10 minutes.
• Cold Stand-by: This state is similar to ’Hot Stand-by’ but differs in that the cathode
pressure has not reached the nominal 30 bar, remaining below this threshold. No
hydrogen production occurs, and the same percentage of rated power (Psb =
2%) is provided to keep temperature, pressure, and the functionality of auxiliary
devices.
This particular state is uncommon, as the electrolyzer is typically activated only if there
is a consistent power supply available for the entire cold start-up process. However, in
certain instances, this scenario might occur, and the model is designed to simulate such
condition.

transitions between these states. It is relevant to notice that until the shutdown process
’Switch off’ is complete, and then moving to state ’Off’, the system cannot be turned on.

The physical model of the electrolyzer described in Chapter 3 does not include all the
dynamic constraints necessary for safe operation. These constraints are therefore built
into the control logic.

power ramp rate, the maximum duration of the stand-by mode, and the operational load
range. The maximum power ramp-up rate is set at 10% per second [45, 61], which
is the maximum allowable increase in power input. Conversely, the ramp-down rate,
which is the maximum allowable decrease in power input, is not considered a constraint
in this project and is set to 100%/s. This decision is due to the non-predictive nature
of the model, which could lead to unexpected power reductions, potentially resulting in

given the limited capacity of the fuel cell.

Electrolyzer Power Allocation Strategy
Given the previously explained setup of four 1 MW electrolyzers arranged in parallel,
and their operation just discussed, it is necessary to allocate the input power among the
four units to operate the system efficiently while respecting to the dynamic constraints
of the electrolyzers.
Figure 4.5 depicts the ’Electrolyzer Control Block’, illustrating the environment within
which the ’Electrolyzers power allocation algorithm’ operates, and highlights both its
inputs and outputs. The controller receives only the power input as its external input,
and it outputs the reference power designated for each electrolyzer. As this represents
the input of each electrolysis system model, once processed by the latter, the output
generated includes the amount of hydrogen produced, and consequently the State of
Charge of the storage tank, and the operational state of each electrolyzer. This last
output is then utilized as inputs for subsequent decisions at the next time step.

4.3.1
Control Logic description
The primary objective of the electrolyzer controller is to optimise the use of energy from
the input, with the aim of operating the electrolyzers in their most efficient range while
minimising degradation. Frequent cycling, such repeated cycling on and off, as well as
operating at full load, contribute significantly to degradation [44]. The control logic is
then designed to reduce these damaging conditions.
The strategy consists in constant threshold, namely ’On Threshold’ (OT), ’Low Thresh-
old’ (LT), and ’High Threshold’ (HT). These thresholds guide decisions on whether to
activate, put on stand-by, or turn off one or more electrolyzers at each time-step, based
on their state the previous time-step, which correspond to the input of the controller
together with the total power input (Pin).

All Off

The action of turning on, which means transitioning from the ‘Off‘ state to the ‘Cold
Start-up‘ state, is restricted to one electrolyzer per time-step. This process is further
constrained by the ramp-up rate (Elr-up). Hence, if the power input substantially exceeds
the ’On Threshold’ (OT), all electrolyzers could begin the turning on process within four
seconds. The decision to activate a specific electrolyzer over others is based on the
number of on/off cycles each has recorded in its history, prioritizing the electrolyzer with
the fewest ’Cold Start-up’ cycles.

one electrolyzer is in either the ‘Cold Start-up’ or ‘On’ mode. These are termed ’Active’
electrolyzers, and the ’Mixed State’ condition is indicated by N1 + N2 ̸= 0.

If the setpoint exceeds the High threshold (HT) and there are inactive electrolyzers, one
will be activated, giving priority to electrolyzers on standby over those that are off.

Moving average implementation
A moving average has been implemented to stabilize the control signal and smooth
out fluctuations in the power input, which serves as a decision-making tool for initiating
the ’Cold Start-up’ phase of the electrolyzers. This 10-minute moving average is used
solely to determine the optimal timing for turning the electrolyzers on when in the ’All
Off’ condition, while the normal power continues to feed the electrolyzers for regular
operations.
By focusing on broader trends rather than short-term fluctuations, considering the time-
step of the simulations of 1 second, this approach prevents unnecessary or premature
activations and ensures a smoother and more controlled transition to active states. This
not only streamlines operations, but also avoids the frequent switching that can lead to
increased wear and tear of the electrolyzer. Moreover, it makes the simulation more
representative of real-world conditions.

In this chapter, the performance of the two strategic scenarios is analyzed in Section 5.1
and Section 5.2, each incorporating the electrolyzer power allocation algorithm detailed
in Section 4.3. Follows a direct comparison between them in Section 5.3. Additionally, in

Although the analysis presented in this chapter focuses on a single week (two for the
GSPA scenario), these strategies were applied across 12 different weekly second-based
timeseries. Details on the most varied weeks are available in Appendix B.1.
The primary simulations are conducted using a hydrogen price of 4 €/kgH2 [62] to evalu-

of the electrolyzers power allocation algorithm includes calculating the weekly number

Conclusions
The objective of this project was to create a model that accurately simulates the dynam-
ics of electrolysis, taking into account the essential processes and constraints involved.
Subsequently, the focus shifted to determining the optimal operation of electrolyzers
and their integration into a broader system. This was aimed at optimizing the overall ef-
ficiency and minimizing operating conditions that could lead to significant degradation.
The model addresses the dynamics of the electrolysis system, taking into account vari-
able power inputs and different operational conditions, such temperature and pressure.
Although the model has not been validated experimentally, it aligns with existing litera-
ture and is adaptable to various PEM electrolyzers by adjusting internal parameters.
The operational strategy of distributing power evenly among active electrolyzers, using
defined low and high thresholds to turn on or standby, or off, the electrolyzers, proved
effective. This approach is advantageous as it maintains the electrolyzers mostly in
the partial load region, where system-level efficiency is optimized, achieving average
efficiencies of 64% and 66% including compression. This method also prevents the
electrolyzers from operating at full load most of the time, which helps avoid conditions
that could lead to faster degradation.
The efficiency of the fuel cell, especially when operated at high capacities, limits their in-
tegration into the grid. Its most effective use is as a backup power source, at a kW level,
to support the operation of the electrolyzers. The substantial consumption of hydrogen
impacts the feasibility of meeting gas sales and purchase agreements, while reducing
the round-trip efficiency to approximately 30%.
The price of hydrogen and the involvement of potential off-takers are critical in shaping
the operational strategies of the electrolyzers. Both factors are subject to significant
uncertainty.

Recommendations for future work
The future work section of this thesis suggests a comprehensive techno-economic as-
sessment of the entire system, which should include projections of power and hydrogen
prices to better align with market realities. The results indicate that these values play
a critical role within the current operational strategies, and therefore it is essential to
ensure the reliability of this data.
Currently, the strategies implemented do not incorporate any forecasting for electric-
ity prices or wind production. This omission means that decisions are based solely on
trends indicated by the moving average, without any predictive insights into expected
turbine production throughout the week. The absence of forecast data has been a con-
tributory factor to the failure to meet the obligations of gas sales purchase agreements.
Introducing forecast models, such as model predictive control or stochastic models,
could significantly refine operational strategies by providing foresight into price fluctua-
tions and production trends.
Considering the exploration of additional market opportunities, specifically in ancillary
services such as frequency regulation, could substantially enhance the economic po-
tential of the hydrogen system. Numerous projects and studies have examined the fea-
sibility and advantages of integrating hydrogen systems for frequency regulation [63,
12, 60]. Expanding into this sector is likely to diversify revenue streams and could sig-
nificantly enhance the system’s profitability by leveraging its load flexibility.
Finally, given the low round-trip efficiency of converting electricity to hydrogen and then
back to electricity for grid supply, it is advisable to explore alternative storage solutions.
This is particularly relevant for high-capacity applications such as this, where the storage
system seeks to participate actively in the grid. Investigating different storage options,
such as batteries, could offer more efficient and effective solutions for energy manage-
ment and grid interaction.

A.2.3
Isothermal assumption
Compressing a gas involves work done on the gas, and according to the first law of
thermodynamics (∆U = Q −W), the energy added to the system typically increases
the internal energy of the gas, resulting in a temperature rise. This is characteristic of
an adiabatic process, where no heat is exchanged with the environment(Q = 0).
To maintain an isothermal condition (∆U = 0), where the temperature remains constant
despite pressure changes, specific assumptions are necessary:
1. Effective Heat Transfer: The system must incorporate mechanisms to dissipate
heat from the gas during compression. This may be facilitated by utilizing external
cooling systems to actively remove the heat, thus preventing any rise in tempera-
ture.
74
==PAGE:86==
2. Quasi-static Process: The compression should occur gradually enough to keep
the gas temperature aligned with the ambient conditions. This slow compression
rate allows time for the generated heat to be adequately transferred to the sur-
roundings, thereby maintaining a constant temperature.
