Abstract  
Studies in Danish primary schools have shown that the indoor climate is insufficient and 
does not comply with Danish recommendations and requirements, which is made to 
ensure a sufficient learning environment for the students. Poor indoor climate in 
classrooms has an influence on students learning and health. Few studies have been 
conducted in high schools, where it is expected they have the same problems as in primary 
schools. 
 
The purpose of this thesis was to study indoor climate at high schools in Denmark. The 
indoor climate was studied during the heating season in four high schools with different 
building ages and locations in the northern part of Copenhagen. 
Indoor climate parameters such as CO2 concentration, air temperature, relative humidity, 
sound pressure level and illuminance level were measured in 39 classrooms. In addition, 
a questionnaire survey was conducted on the students perception of the indoor climate, 
in three of the four high schools. 
 
The indoor air quality seems to be the main problem and the ventilation type had a 
significant impact on the CO2 concentration. Classrooms with balanced mechanical 
ventilation generally had lower CO2 concentrations. The worst cases occurred in schools 
and classrooms with exhaust ventilation, where the CO2 concentrations below 1000 ppm 
only occurred 30 to 50% of the occupied time. This was also confirmed by questionnaire 
survey where the students experienced stuffy air. 
 
The temperatures and relative humidity were within the recommended ranges. However, 
at all schools, high temperatures occurred and were generally in the high end of the 
acceptable range. The students felt varying and low temperatures instead of high 
temperatures. The average sound pressure level was around 52-57 dB (A) and complied 
with the recommended values in the schools, but sound levels were close to 85 dB (A) in 
limited time periods. All four schools had illumination levels below the recommended 
limit of 300 lux without the use of artificial lighting. However, this was primarily at 
locations furthest away from the windows. Only when using artificial light, the 
recommended level was achieved in all positions in the classrooms. 
 
Based on the measurements and questionnaires, the conclusion was that only one out of 
four schools complied the recommended level according to sufficient air quality, and 
showed that the indoor climate was insufficient in many high schools in Denmark. The 
biggest challenge for the schools in complying with the indoor climate recommendations 
is air quality and light.  
 
By using a calculation model from InnoByg, it was estimated that the schools can improve 
the students learning performance by 5 to 7% by reducing CO2 concentrations and 
temperature.

 Resumé 
Studier i danske folkeskoler har vist at indeklimaet i de fleste er utilstrækkeligt og ikke 
overholder de danske krav og anbefalinger, der er lavet for at sikre et tilstrækkeligt 
læringsmiljø for eleverne. Det er vist, at dårligt indeklima i klasseværelser har indflydelse 
på elevers indlæring og helbred. Færre studier er dog foretaget i gymnasier, hvor de 
forventeligt har samme problematik med indeklimaet som i folkeskolerne.  
 
Formålet med dette speciale var at undersøge indeklimaforholdene på gymnasier i 
Danmark. Indeklimaet blev undersøgt i opvarmningssæsonen på fire gymnasier med 
forskellige bygnings alder placeret i den nordlige del af Storkøbenhavn.  
Indeklima parametre såsom CO2 koncentration, temperatur, relativ luftfugtighed, 
lydniveau samt lysforhold blev målt i 39 klasseværelser. Derudover blev der i tre af de 
fire gymnasier gennemført en spørgeskemaundersøgelse af elevernes opfattelse af 
indeklimaet. 
 
Luftkvaliteten synes at være hovedproblemet i klasselokalerne og ventilationstypen 
havde en betydelig indvirkning på CO2-koncentrationen. Klasseværelser med balanceret 
mekanisk ventilation havde generelt lavere CO2 koncentrationer. I skoler og klasselokaler 
med udsugningsventilation opstod de værste tilfælde, hvor der kun i 30 til 50% af tiden 
var registreret CO2 koncentrationer under 1000 ppm. Dette blev også bekræftet via 
spørgeskema undersøgelse, hvor de studerende svarede, at de oplevede indelukket luft.    
 
Temperaturerne og den relative luftfugtighed var indenfor de anbefalede værdier. På alle 
skoler opstod der dog høje temperaturer. Temperaturen var generelt i den høje ende af 
det acceptable værdier. De studerende beskrev dog at de følte varierende og lave 
temperaturer i stedet for høje temperaturer. På skolerne var det gennemsnitlige 
lydniveau omkring 52-57 dB(A) og overholdt de anbefalede værdier, men i perioder 
opstod der lydniveauer tæt på 85 dB(A). For alle fire skoler var belysningsniveauet under 
den anbefalede grænseværdi på 300 lux uden brug af elektrisk belysning. Dette var dog 
primært gældende i positioner længst væk fra vinduerne. Ved brug af elektrisk belysning 
overholdte alle fire skoler belysningskravet i klasselokalerne.    
 
På baggrund af de opnåede resultater kan det konkluderes, at kun en ud af fire skoler 
overholdte den anbefalede grænse vedrørende luftkvalitet og derved at indeklimaet er 
utilstrækkelig i mange gymnasier i Danmark. Den største udfordring for skolerne med 
hensyn til indeklima er luftkvalitet og lys. 
 
Ved brug af en beregningsmodel fra InnoByg blev det estimeret at skolerne kan forbedre 
elevernes lærings præstation med 5 til 7%, ved at reducere CO2 koncentrationen og 
temperaturen.

Introduction 
People spend 90% of their life indoor, at home, at school, at the workplace etc. This places 
great demands on the indoor climate in our buildings. The indoor climate quality affects 
people’s health, performance and concentration etc.  
 
In Denmark, people go to school for at least ten years. Many young people choose after ten 
years in primary school to take three more years in high school. This means that the students 
spend many hours indoor at the schools the first many years of their life. The indoor climate 
quality at the schools affects especially young people and children who may be more sensitive 
to the indoor climate exposure (Wargocki et al., 2012; Toftum et al., 2011; Kjeldsen et. al, 
2013).   
 
The first scientific studies of the indoor climate quality in classrooms and the effect on 
students’ performance began in the mid 1950’s (Corgnati, et. al., 2005). Since that time and 
over the past years there have been additional studies of the indoor environment mainly in 
primary schools even though high schools might have similar problems, both in Denmark and 
the rest of the world (Bozkurt et al., 2017; Toftum et al. 2011; Barkmann et al, 2012; and 
others). 
 
The studies in Denmark have shown that the indoor conditions in many schools do not meet 
the Danish requirements and recommendations (Clausen et al., 2014; Larsen et al., 2016; 
Bozkurt et al, 2017; and others). The indoor climate in Danish classrooms at primary schools 
and high schools was investigated nationwide during the Mass Experiments in 2009 and 2014 
(Andersen et al. 2009; Clausen et al. 2014). Spot measurements showed that the indoor 
environment was insufficient in more than 50% of the investigated classrooms. E.g. the 
carbon dioxide, CO2, concentration was too high. In the Mass Experiment 65 high school 
classes participated. Here the average CO2 concentration was around the recommended 
value, but also measurements with very high values was conducted. However, as only 65 
classes participated, it was too small a quantity to determine how the indoor climate was 
generally for high schools in Denmark ([13]; Clausen et al. 2014). 
 
In the rest of the world, study of classrooms has also been conducted. Experiments done in 
Estonia show that high schools had too high CO2 concentrations (Koiv, 2015). The 
experiments were done in six schools, where problems occurred with high CO2 concentration 
and modest air change, even at renovated schools. The same was shown in studies at 
Espergærde gymnasium, Denmark (Pedersen, 2012). This indicate that there may be a 
general problem with the air quality in classrooms at high schools.  
 
A high school student spends approximately 4-6 hours a day in classrooms [8]. Based on the 
number of graduated students during the last years, the average number of students in a class 
seems to increase (Gymnasiekarakter, 2016). Since the classrooms remain the same, the 
indoor climate is challenged with the increased number of students and appropriate 
ventilation is even more important.  
 
==PAGE:14==
Page 2 of 55 
Studies in ventilation in classrooms have shown significant impact on the students' 
concentration skills, etc. The ability to concentrate, perform and the students’ health are 
important factors when discussing the indoor environment and climate at schools. Studies 
have shown that poor indoor environment affect the students badly and that by having the 
right conditions the students perform much better (Wargocki et al. 2012; Sleegers et al. 2012).   
Wargocki et al. showed that by increasing the classroom ventilation rate from 3 to 9.5 L/s per 
person it would improve the performance of schoolwork speed by ˜8% and the amount of 
errors was significantly reduced.   
 
1.1 
Purpose 
The purpose of this thesis was to study indoor climate in the heating season and through that 
investigate the current indoor climate conditions at high schools in Denmark. Four schools in 
the northern part of Copenhagen with different building ages were studied. The schools and 
classrooms were selected in order to give a representative sample of a typical high school in 
Denmark. 
 
The thesis focuses on CO2 concentration, air temperature, relative air humidity, light, sound 
level, user behaviour and the students’ perception of the indoor climate in their classrooms.  
 
Hypothesis: The indoor climate in Danish high schools is above recommended values for the 
above mentioned parameters.   
 
1.2 
Boundary  
In this thesis it was only possible to include four high schools in the data sampling. The 
number of schools was limited by time and resources.   
 
From the beginning of the thesis the intention was to include high schools located in buildings 
of different ages to get the best representation of high schools in Denmark. However, it has 
not been possible to have a “newer” high school from within the last 20 years included in this 
project.   
 
At the schools, only “ordinary” classrooms were selected. Classrooms with science and 
creative subjects/classrooms were not chosen, as these rooms often have different and better 
ventilation installed. These are also often used in a different way than the ordinary 
classrooms.

Theory 
For investigation of the indoor climate at high schools different parameters and theories must 
be considered. The indoor environmental quality has an impact on the students´ well-being, 
productivity, performance and health at high schools (Wargocki et al. 2012; Sleegers et al. 
2012, Toftum et al., 2015). The indoor climate is a mix of several parameters, which altogether 
gives the quality of the indoor environment.  
 
2.1 
Indoor Environmental Quality 
The indoor environmental quality (IEQ) is affected by several parameters. The most 
important are: Air quality, thermal conditions, lighting and acoustics, see Figure 1. The 
parameters affect the students’ comfort level and thus the IEQ.   
 
 
Figure 1 - Parameters that define IEQ 
- 
The Indoor Air Quality (IAQ) depends on the concentration of pollutants. 
- 
The thermal conditions depend on the air temperature, the mean radiant 
temperature, the relative air humidity and the air velocity, which all affect the heat 
loss from the body.  
- 
The light or visual indoor environment is characterised by the daylight and artificial 
light. Amount, distribution and the properties of the light has an effect on the indoor 
quality and the human health. 
- 
The sound or acoustic environment is characterised by the level and properties of 
sound or noise emitted from indoor sources and transmitted from the outdoors.    
 
These are the physical parameters that altogether describe the indoor environment. Several 
other factors also have an impact on the IEQ, such as pollution sources (materials, cleaning, 
maintaining etc.). These parameters have to be accounted for when designing and 
maintaining good indoor environment, but will not be investigated in this project, where it is 
assumed that humans are the main source of pollution.

2.1.1 
Indoor air quality 
The air quality is not always possible to assess by humans, because pollutants not necessarily 
are visible to the human eye and humans quickly get used to the smell.   
The pollutants in the air may come from many different sources. In rooms with high 
occupancy load, as classrooms, it is often the occupants themselves that are the most 
important source to the pollutants. 
  
2.1.1.1 
CO2 
The CO2 concentration is often used as an indicator of the indoor air quality in rooms with 
people as the main sources of pollutants. CO2 can easily be measured and is therefore used as 
an indicator of the pollution level and to see if the ventilation is sufficient.   
The human metabolism consumes oxygen and generates CO2. The rate of CO2 emission 
depends on the activity level, size of body and diet (ASTM, 2002). The older, larger and more 
active the pupils and students are, the more CO2 they will emit. A sedentary adult, activity 
level 1.2 met, produces around 18.7 l CO2/h (DS 3033, 2011).  
 
The outdoor CO2 concentration is around 400 ppm while the indoor concentration typically 
is around 500 to 2500 ppm (Toftum et al. 2011). The CO2 concentration in classrooms is 
increasing during the day due to the high occupancy.  
In a classroom, the CO2 concentration should not exceed 1000 ppm if the air quality should 
be considered satisfactory. The recommendations and the requirements will be described in 
section 4 Requirements and recommendations.  
 
To reduce the high concentration of CO2 in a classroom it is important to ventilate the room.  
Studies has shown that classrooms with mechanical ventilation system, such as balanced and 
exhaust has lower CO2 concentration than classrooms with natural ventilation (Wargocki et 
al. 2012; Bozkurt et al., 2017; Toftum et al. 2015; Clausen et al. 2014; and others). In the Mass 
Experiments 2014 (Clausen et al. 2014) 785 classes from 262 Danish schools participated. 
The study showed that the average of the CO2 measurements for the different types of 
ventilation is; 1099 ppm for balanced ventilation rooms, 1380 ppm for rooms only with 
exhaust and 1813 ppm for natural ventilation rooms.  
 
2.1.1.2 
Ultrafine particles  
Ultrafine particles originate from many natural and man-made sources e.g. dust, fires, diesel 
smoke and tobacco smoke and are normally only generated at very high temperatures. They 
are defined as particles with size >0.1µm (100 nm).  
 
People may feel discomfort when they are exposed to ultrafine particles, as tobacco indoor. 
At a concentration around 50.000 particles/cm3 humans may feel immediate impact [1].   
 
In recent years, studies have shown indications that ultrafine particles are more health 
hazardous than coarse particles [1]. One reason is that ultrafine particles penetrate further in 
the lungs than coarse particles.

2.1.2 
Thermal conditions 
The thermal conditions depend on the air temperature, the mean radiant temperature, the 
relative air humidity and air velocity. These are parameters that affect the human body and 
the perception of the thermal environment ([10]; Danvak, 2006).  
In this thesis only air temperature and relative humidity are considered.  
 
2.1.2.1  
Air temperature 
Temperature has an effect on humans’ comfort and health (Toftum et al. 2011).  
Small differences in temperatures within the thermal comfort zone can have a negative effect 
on our comfort. Especially in classrooms, this is of great importance because the temperature 
can often rise markedly during a lesson. Due to the high occupancy, the heat from computers 
and large windows.  
The temperature can affect the students, so they perform better with the right temperature 
and vice versa at wrong temperature (Wargocki et al. 2012). A study performed by Wargocki 
showed that reducing the temperature from 25 °C to 20 °C increased the students’ speed of 
performing numerical and language tests.   
 
2.1.2.2  
Relative air humidity 
Relative humidity (RH) is the ratio of the amount of water vapor that is in the air at a certain 
temperature and the amount of water vapor that can be at maximum at this temperature 
(Danvak, 2006).  
 
Low relative humidity (<25%) can cause discomfort. It can cause dry mucous membranes, 
throat and eyes. Moreover, too high relative humidity (>80%) increases the risk of mold. If 
RH exceeds 100% the humidity is transformed to water vapor and dew will appear. Normally 
the RH does not cause discomfort in the range of 25-60% [2].  
The vapour pressure will normally be the same in the room, and therefore it will be sufficient 
to measure the humidity at one location (Fanger, 1982) 
 
2.1.3 
Light 
It is important to ensure sufficient lighting conditions in a classroom, when the daylight is not 
sufficient. This applies to both daylight and artificial lighting.  
 
Studies show that artificial lighting can have positive effects on the working speed, accuracy 
and task performance (Barkmann et al. 2011; Sleegers et al. 2012). An experiment done in 
primary schools in the Netherlands, indicated a positive influence of the lighting system on 
student concentration (Sleegers et al. 2012). Sleegers tested the influence on student 
concentration. It was found that lighting at 500 lux had a positive effect on the outcome of the 
reading, writing and math exercises than the recommended lighting at 300 lux. 
  
A study carried out in Germany at two high schools, tested variable light in different 
classrooms (Barkmann et al. 2011). In the study it was found that lighting adjusted to 
“concentration mode” (bright and cold light at 1060 lux, 5800 K) instead of “standard mode” 
based on the DIN 5035 standard (300 lux, 4000 K) reduced the number of errors, increased 
the reading speed and reading comprehension for an average student. The errors were 
reduced with 20.8% and the reading speed increased with 16.8%.

By having the right amount of daylight in the classroom the amount of artificial light can be 
reduced and thereby the amount of energy used for artificial light.  
 
2.1.4 
Sound 
Acoustics and sound are important for students learning. A good acoustic environment in a 
classroom is important, as speech communication is a large part of the learning process in 
schools. 
 
The acoustic indoor climate is characterised by the sound and noise effects experienced in a 
room. Sound pressure level (SPL), reverberation time (RT) and speech transmission 
characterize the acoustic environment.  
 
Sound pressure level (SPL) describes the sound level in a room, from technical installations 
and speech. In a classroom, the SPL can vary throughout the day depending on the activity. 
Reverberation time (RT) describes the time from the sound is emitted until it is has decreased 
by 60 dB. It is measured in seconds.  
A too high RT can make it difficult to understand what is being said due to reflections of sound. 
However, if the RT becomes too low, the sound will be muted too much, so it is difficult to 
hear what is being said. Low reverberation time can be ensured by sufficient sound absorbing 
materials in the classroom. 
 
In this project only sound pressure level will be measured. 
 
2.2 
Ventilation principles 
Studies shows that ventilation system has an impact on the indoor climate (Wargocki et al. 
2012; Clausen et al. 2014; and others).  
Ventilation systems can roughly be divided into four groups (Hviid et al., 2014): Natural 
ventilation, mechanical exhaust ventilation, balanced mechanical ventilation and hybrid 
ventilation.

2.3 
Questionnaire survey 
When making studies of indoor climate, questionnaires completed by the users provide 
important input to the assessment of the IEQ. A subjective questionnaire can be used to 
evaluate the indoor environment. Even though a questionnaire survey is relatively time 
consuming, it gives a good view of the climate. 
 
Different survey, scales and units have been developed to evaluate the indoor climate and the 
pollution strength, since the experienced air quality cannot be measured yet. The only way to 
assess the strength is to use a panel of subjects (human sensory response) judging the air 
quality. The panel judges whether the air quality is acceptable or not (Wargocki, 2004). This 
is often done by using an acceptability scale, see Figure 6 or subjective evaluations by a 
==PAGE:20==
Page 8 of 55 
questionnaire, see Figure 7. The acceptable scale is from 1= clearly acceptable, 0 = just 
acceptable/just not acceptable and -1 = clearly not acceptable. Subject questionnaires and 
scales are given to the occupants with a fixed time: daily, weekly monthly etc. (DS EN 15251). 
 
 
Figure 6 - Acceptable scale (Wargocki, 2004) 
 
Figure 7 - Questionnaires example (DS EN 15251) 
 
A questionnaire survey called the Glostrup scheme “Glostrupskemaet” has been prepared by 
the Danish Center for Indoor Climate and Stress Research for the purpose of making a 
standardized questionnaire survey about indoor air quality. The occupants of 41 randomly 
selected offices, schools and hospitals were asked about their perception of indoor climate 
during the previous month. The questionnaire has been validated relative to adults and used 
at workplaces [12].

Requirements and recommendations 
The rights regarding indoor environment of teachers and students are outlined in Danish 
requirements and recommendations. These were made to ensure a sufficient learning 
environment for the students. 
 
3.1 
Educational Environment Act 
In 2001 The Danish Ministry of Education implemented a law that states the pupils and 
students’ rights of a satisfying educational environment in all types of schools in Denmark. 
The law is called the Educational Environment Act (Undervisningsmiljøloven).  
 
The Education Environment Act does not specify values for indoor environment parameters, 
but provides guidelines to maintain a good education environment in the schools.  
 
Chapter 1, § 1 stated: 
“§ 1. Pupils, students and other participants in public and private education have the right to 
a good education environment so that teaching can take place in a safe and healthy manner. 
The educational environment at schools and educational institutions (educational 
establishments) shall promote participants' opportunities for development and learning and 
therefore also include the educational and psychological and aesthetic environment” [17]. 
 
The authority, board or person responsible for education site shall ensure that the 
requirements are met.  
 
3.2 
The Danish Working Environment Act 
In 2010, guidelines regarding indoor environment were implemented in the Working 
Environment Act (Arbejdsmiljøloven). The guidelines ensure a healthy and satisfying 
working environment for school teachers. The act does not state specific values for the IEQ, 
but provides guidelines by the Danish Working Environment Authority [4].   
 
3.3 
The Danish Working Environment Authority recommendations 
The Danish Working Environment Authority (Arbejdstilsynets vejledninger) gives 
recommendations on how to achieve a good indoor environment.  
 
A recommendation for schools, is a temperature range of 20-22°C and the temperature should 
not exceed 25°C at any time except during heat waves ([2] section 2.1). Furthermore a 
temperature difference between head and ankle ≥4°C should be avoided.  
In a room where people are the biggest pollutant source the CO2 concentration should be 
below 0.1% (1000 ppm). The ventilation is not sufficient if the CO2 concentration exceeds 
2000 ppm for more than a short period during the day ([2] section 3.1).   
For the relative humidity (RH) the recommendation states it should be between 25-60% ([2] 
section 2.6).  
The lighting must – in addition to giving light to work – provide the room in a comfortable 
way ([2] section 2.4). The required lighting strength can be found in DS700 to 200 lux (DS 
700, 2005). Windows should always allow for a view out.

3.4 
Danish Building Regulations  
The Danish Building Regulations (Bygningsreglementet BR15) describes the conditions that 
should be met for new buildings and renovation projects. When old buildings are going 
through a large renovation or change of use, the building has to meet the applicable 
regulation.  
 
No specific requirements to thermal environment in schools are stated in the Building 
Regulations, but healthy and comfortable temperatures corresponding to the activities must 
be maintained in the classrooms ([6] 6.2 §1). 
However the schools and classrooms must be equipped with mechanical ventilation, which 
can provide a ventilation rate of 5 l/s per person and 0.35 l/s/m2. At the same time it must be 
ensured that the CO2 concentration does not exceed 1000 ppm ([6] 6.3.1.3 §2).  
Buildings must be planned, projected, executed and organized to ensure satisfactory sound 
conditions for users ([6] 6.4.3 §1).  
Classrooms must have such access to daylight that the rooms are well lit. Windows must be 
installed, placed and possibly shielded so that sunlight through them does not cause 
overheating in the rooms, and so direct sunlight can be avoided ([6] 6.5.1 §1). 
 
3.5 
Danish and International standards  
Standards are a worldwide and common language. They make it easier to communicate when 
the guidelines are the same [9].     
 
DS 3033 is a standard from 2011 for voluntary classification of the quality of the indoor 
climate in residential houses, schools, children’s day-care centres and offices. The building 
owner and user can voluntarily get the IEQ of their building assessed and classified based on 
some measurements and ratings.  
The standard has five classifications, where A++ is defined as the excellent indoor 
environment, A+ is the good environment, A is the satisfactory environment corresponding 
to the requirements from the Building Regulation. B is the minimum requirement of the 
Building Regulation and C is defined as poor indoor environment (DS 3033, 2011).  
 
DS/EN 15251 from 2007 is a standard with indoor environmental input parameters for 
design and assessment of energy performance of buildings addressing indoor air quality, 
thermal environment, lighting and acoustics.   
The standard operates with four categories. Category I is high level of expectation and IV is 
values that are only accepted for a limited part of the year. Category II is the normal level of 
expectation, used for new buildings and renovation (DS/EN 15251, 2007).

4.1.2 
Building design and typologies 
During the years, the building characteristics have changed. Until the 1930s, the schools were 
mostly built in a strict classical style. During this period, a number of distinctive high schools 
were built. Some of the most outstanding architects at that time were responsible for the 
construction of the schools, but the buildings were still very conservatively designed. Long 
corridors with classrooms along the sides was the normal design [15]. From Hviid (2014) the 
Danish schools can roughly be divided into six different typologies based on the construction 
year. Three of these typologies are matching the four high schools.       
 
The Story school construction period were 1880-1930 and again 1945-
1960 (Hviid, 2014). The story schools were often built of massive 
exterior walls of red bricks. All classrooms were located along a 
corridor and a central entrance with a wide staircase in the middle. The 
typology of School 1 is a story school. 
 
The Aula School were typically constructed in period 1930-1945 
(Hviid, 2014). These schools are characterised by a big aula in the 
centre of the building. The exterior walls are made by concrete or 
yellow brickwork. School 2’s typology is an aula school. 
 
Comb school were often built in 1945-1970 (Hviid, 2014). The special 
structure of the school with branches is similar to a comb, hence the 
name. The exterior walls were often built by concrete or brickwork. 
School 3 and 4 can mostly be characterised as comb schools.

4.2 
Selection of classrooms 
To investigate the indoor climate at the high schools, around 10 classrooms for ordinary 
teaching were selected in each high school. For selection of the classrooms, it was important 
to fulfil the following list of criteria in the best possible way (Larsen et al., 2016) (Bozkurt et 
al., 2016): 
 
- 
Select “ordinary classrooms” (i.e. not science, creative classrooms etc.) 
- 
Select classrooms in each building, if the school consists of more buildings. 
- 
Select classrooms with different orientations, floor heights and levels.   
- 
Select classrooms with different level/grades 
- 
Select classrooms with different ventilation types. 
- 
Select classrooms with normal/high use in the week the measurement is performed. 
By attempting to these criteria, it was ensured that the data would give a broad picture of the 
indoor climate at high schools in Denmark. Moreover, by choosing classrooms with these 
criteria the high schools are easier to compare.

Total value and improvement 
In 2016 InnoByg together with DTU Byg and Teknologisk Institut launched the project "Total 
Value and Indoor Climate" with the purpose of developing concepts and calculation models 
that could clarify and quantify the value of a good indoor climate [16]. 
 
Total value is an overall economic view, which takes into account the overall economic 
conditions of a construction, the social and environmental values of a building [16]. 
 
The Total Value & Indoor Climate Project developed a simple calculation tool based on 
student performance, temperature and air quality. The purpose of the calculation tool was to 
create an easy and useful tool to quantify how much students work performance can be 
increased with improvement of the indoor climate [16]. The tool is based on elementary 
schools, but was used here for high schools.  
 
The model is based on four indoor environment quality categories as well as an "outside 
category". The four categories, I, II, III and IV indicates which conditions are better than 
others, according to the different parameters. The parameters are CO2 concentration and 
temperature. "Out of category" is included as it is possible that there are buildings with 
conditions that do not meet the current standards for new buildings. 
 
In the calculation model, the (estimated) proportion of the occupied time in the different 
categories is entered for each parameter: CO2 concentration and temperature. 
 
The calculation model indicates how much student performance can be increased with an 
improvement in temperature and CO2 concentration. Further, the number of possible lost 
school days per academy year is estimated.

9 
Discussion 
The purpose of this thesis was to study indoor climate in the heating season in high schools 
in Denmark. Measurement data from 4 high schools and 39 classrooms was included in this 
analysis. The results and their significance are considered in this section. 
 
9.1 
Indoor environment parameters 
The ventilation type had a large effect on the classroom air quality. Generally, classrooms with 
balanced ventilation, had CO2 concentration below the recommendation of 1000 ppm. 
Further, only one out of the four schools had CO2 concentrations below 1000 ppm in more 
than 50% of the occupied time. This indicates that the high schools in Denmark have a poor 
indoor air quality. This corresponds with findings from Danish primary schools. 
 
Based on the questionnaire surveys from School 2, School 3 and School 4, it can be seen that 
the students felt stuffy air, headache and concentration difficulties in the classrooms. This 
corresponds well to the measurements, as the three schools did not have sufficient 
ventilation. At the schools, the students had been more bothered by stuffy air than the 
occupants in the Glostrup scheme. This could be because occupant density is higher in 
classrooms than in office environments and the air quality thereby becomes worse if the 
ventilation is insufficient.  
 
Unfortunately, students in School 1 did not fill in the questionnaires. It could have been 
interesting to see how they would have answered, if the percentage of the students that 
experienced stuffy air would decrease as the CO2 concentration was lower in this school. 
 
In the Total Value study it was found that by decreasing the concentration of CO2 to below 
1000 ppm, it was possible to increase the students’ performance up to 5 to 7%. However, it 
requires a lot from the high school in terms of replacement and adjustment of the ventilation 
system, which is an expensive economic cost. 
 
In the results of the total value, the dominating factor was air quality compared to 
temperature. However, there is a relatively high uncertainty about the calculation tool. 
 
To evaluate the thermal environment the recommended range of 20-24°C for the temperature 
in heating season was used. The requirements to temperature were generally complied with 
all schools and were within the range in 61-83% of the occupied time. However, a large part 
of the measurements was in the range of 22-24 °C, which is in the higher end of the 
recommendation for schools. The percentage distribution showed that 10-25% of the time 
the temperature was between 20-22 ˚C and 50-65 % of the time it was between 22-24 ˚C. This 
supports that the temperature was in the high end of the range. It was therefore expected that 
the students would feel warm. This was not the case in the questionnaires, as more students 
felt cold and a varying temperature. The varying temperature was also seen in the 
measurements.  
 
In the literature study and the total value study, it was found that by having the right 
temperature (in the range of 20-22˚C) in the classroom, it is possible to increase the students’ 
performance. The temperature was within the range of 20-22 ˚C in 10-25% of the occupied 
==PAGE:58==
Page 46 of 55 
time. There is potential to improve the students’ performance by decreasing the temperature, 
so a larger part of temperature range is between 20-22˚C. Temperatures between 20-22 ˚C, 
however, are relatively low temperatures and can be discussed if it is comfortable.  
 
The RH in the schools was between 22-68% with a median value of 38-49%. Most people 
prefer the RH between 35-60%, but is recommended below 45% by the Asthma Allergy 
Association (11). The schools were within the recommended range of 25-60% in 95-100% of 
the time. This indicates no problem with the RH in the heating season for the school. A lower 
median RH was observed in the classrooms with balanced ventilation.  
 
In the questionnaire only a small percent of students experienced dry air. This together with 
measured data indicates no significant risk related with the RH. School 3 with balanced 
ventilation had the lowest median RH and also School 3 had the highest percent amount of 
students that was feeling dry air.  
 
Sound pressure levels complied with the recommendations of all four schools regardless of 
ventilation principle or building age. The schools had a median SPL of 52 dB(A) to 57 dB(A) 
which complies with talking strength. School 3 which was located close to a major road had a 
slightly higher median SPL than the other schools. In the questionnaire, some students also 
complained about the noise from the road and reported that it was worse in the summer when 
opening the windows. It is therefore not optimal to place classrooms against major roads that 
disturb teaching or provide higher SPL. 
 
Illuminance levels were below 300 lux in all schools when the artificial lightning was off. 
However, when using artificial lighting, the schools had illuminances above 300 lux. This 
means that only when using artificial light the recommended value can be achieved in all 
positions in the classrooms. By the literature study it was found that by using a brighter and 
stronger illuminance instead of 300 lux as recommended may have a positive effect on the 
errors, the reading and writing. It may therefore be beneficial to improve the lighting in the 
classrooms.    
 
In this thesis, no measurements were made according to reflection and glare. In the 
questionnaire, 15-25% of the students experienced problems with the light, which is higher 
than the Glostrup scheme. However, it is not possible to determine the cause of prevalence on 
the basis of the measured illuminance results.  
 
Based on the measurements, it is not the age of the building and school, but primarily the 
ventilation type installed at the school that is important for the school's indoor climate. The 
biggest challenge for the schools in complying with the indoor climate recommendations is 
air quality and light.  
 
The students and teachers at School 2, School 3 and School 4 were filling out the questionnaire 
during the measuring period. In School 2, the questionnaire was answered 599 times, in 
School 3 1120 times and in School 4 81 times. This is a large difference in the number of 
answers. At School 4, students may only have answered once, as the students in the other two 
schools must have responded several times when they have moved to the different 
==PAGE:59==
Page 47 of 55 
classrooms. The response rate was rather high and gives a good idea how the students 
experience the indoor climate. 
 
The occupants’ extra comments corresponded well with the measurements. The classrooms 
they liked were also the classrooms with low measured CO2 concentration e.g. School 2 
classroom 810 and 822. The same with the classrooms they did not like, here the CO2 
concentrations were high, e.g. School 3 classroom 81. There is a very good correlation 
between objective measurements and subjective assessments  
 
9.2 
Uncertainties and sources of error 
Measurement uncertainties and sources of error can be encountered at experimental and full-
scale measurements which may affect the outcome and the conclusions that can be made.   
 
When performing measurements and questionnaire study they are not always performed 
alike, despite attempting this. People do not react even, and the occupants who are not used 
to filling in the indoor climate questionnaire have difficulty evaluating it. This can be seen in 
this thesis, as some students commented on the layout of the classrooms, placement of 
furniture etc. instead of commenting on the indoor quality. It is therefore difficult to assess 
whether they have understood the questionnaire correctly. 
 
Further, some answers indicated that the students were discussing their responses while 
filling in the questionnaire. This is seen as some comments are alike. This might have had an 
effect on the students’ answers and perceptions as they can affect each other’s opinion.     
 
Despite the potential sources of errors, the students have answered the questionnaire from 
their perception and the responses reflect their attitudes toward the indoor climate at the 
schools.   
 
In addition to the uncertainties, it can be noted that measurement stations measuring CO2, 
temperature and RH had fallen from their location in some rooms, and a few others were 
taken out of the power supply.  
 
The participating schools were selected early in the project and it can be discussed if the 
schools in general represent the Danish high schools. The schools are located in the greater 
part of Copenhagen and have good reputation. This might have an impact on how the schools 
perform. Further, one of the selected schools declined to participate in the questionnaire 
survey, as they were afraid that the students would be dissatisfied and demand changes, 
which the school management would not be able to address.

Further analyses 
Suggestion for further analyses of the data from the four high schools and further studies of 
high schools in Denmark, are listed: 
 
- 
Investigate responses from the questionnaires completed by students and teachers. A 
study of whether students and teachers feel the same and expect the same of the 
indoor climate. 
 
-

there might have better ventilation that provides lower CO2 concentration.  
 
- 
Make more studies of high schools and compare the results with larger populations.  
 
- 
Investigate the impact of user behaviour in the classrooms on the indoor climate.

Conclusion 
The indoor climate at four high schools, 39 classrooms was evaluated in regards to the CO2 
concentration, air temperature, relative humidity, sound pressure level, illuminance and the 
students’ perception of the indoor environment.  
 
Based on the measurements and questionnaires, the results show that the indoor climate was 
as recommended in one out of four high schools.  
 
Of the investigated parameters, the indoor air quality seems to be the main problem. The 
recommended maximum value for CO2 concentration of 1000 ppm was only reached in one 
school in more than 50% of the occupied time. The ventilation type had a significant impact 
on the CO2 concentration. The high school with balanced ventilation in all classrooms had CO2 
concentrations below 1000 ppm in 73% of the time. The three other schools, with exhaust 
ventilation and balanced ventilation in some classrooms, had only 30 to 53% of the occupied 
time below the recommended value. In the questionnaire the students complained about 
stuffy air, which corresponded well with the measurements results.   
 
CO2 concentrations above 2000 ppm occurred in 20% of the occupied time in classrooms with 
exhaust ventilation. In classrooms with balanced ventilation, it occurred 0% of the occupied 
time.    
 
The building ages or typology do not have a clear signification for the indoor climate. Whereas 
the classroom area had an impact in classrooms with exhaust ventilation.  
 
The temperatures in the classrooms generally met the recommendations of 20-24˚C, but 
many measurements were at the high end, and high temperatures occurred in all high schools. 
Temperature requirement of 20-24 °C was observed in 61 to 80% of occupied time for the 
four high schools. In the questionnaire the students felt varying and low temperature more 
than high temperature.   
 
The relative humidity in the classrooms was in the recommended range of 25-60% in 95 to 
100% of the occupied time. No significant risk or problem with thermal comfort or mould 
seemed to occur.  
 
The lighting conditions in the classrooms at the schools complied with recommendations only 
when using artificial lighting. The results showed that locations furthest away from the 
window facade had a greater luminous need than the locations closest to. 
 
The noise level in the classrooms at the schools complied with the Danish Working 
Environment Authority's requirement for noise load. However, values were measured close 
to the limit of 85 dB(A). Classrooms close to a major road had higher median sound pressure 
level and it is recommended not to place school close to major roads or by noise reduce the 
source of noise. There was no correlation between the noise level and the ventilation 
principles or across building typologies due to the small amount of data. 
 
==PAGE:62==
Page 50 of 55 
Using the calculation model from InnoByg about Total Value and Indoor Climate, it was 
estimated that the high schools can increase the learning performance of the students 
between 5 and 7% by reducing the CO2 concentrations and temperature. They can also reduce 
the number of school days lost. 
 
In relation to the answers from the questionnaire 58 to 71% of the students were satisfied 
with the indoor environment quality. However, 27 to 40% of the students felt building related 
fatigue.  
 
Overall, thermal, sound and lighting conditions were satisfactory in the measured classrooms, 
while the air quality was insufficient in many classrooms. Classrooms with balanced 
ventilation had the best indoor climate, while classrooms with exhaust ventilation had the 
worst. The type of ventilation is considered to be the biggest influence on air quality and was 
only sufficient in one of four measured high schools.
