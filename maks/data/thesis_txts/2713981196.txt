Abstract
This thesis describes the process of designing a reinforcement for a VAWT blade. The
core problem is to minimize the weight while designing a component that will endure the
wind loads. First a simple approach was taken to study the blade and progressively the
design becomes more complex, finalizing with a iterative process of Design of Experi­
ments and Topology optimization carried out in Ansys. This study helped concluding how
and where the blade should be reinforced and proposed a final candidate design for a
future assembly of the turbine.

Introduction
1.1
Enegy situation
Every year the world energy situation is becoming more critical due to many different
factors and the global energy demand keeps increasing constantly. Because of global
warming, production and global market of fossil fuels and other (factors), the energy in­
dustry is trying to detach itself from energy sources which will not be feasible to ensure a
bright future and bet in favor of renewable and sustainable alternatives. The use of these
alternative energies also grants the opportunity to provide energy easily to isolated areas
where the transport of energy is an issue or to grant the opportunity to people who want
to become more grid independent.
1.2
Wind Energy vs Other Energies
With the ongoing energy crisis, these alternative renewable energies are one of the key
research topics nowadays. While some of the traditional energies are starting to enter
a decline state, renewable energies show that the generated electricity price is cheaper
than Coal and Nuclear when the capacity is increased. On the other hand, energies like
solar energy have shown a drastic reduction of the price per megawatt hour of electricity.
Apart from wind energy, one of the biggest energy sources which is being adapted to
common households and which is very easy to scale is solar energy. Every day more and
more houses can be seen with small solar panel installations. Depending on the country
this can be a very good option and it is relatively accessible for anyone who wants to
start generating their own electricity. Some locations however are not suitable for this
kind of energy generation systems. It is true that there are some countries which demand
extra payment of taxes when using this alternative methods, but eventually generating
electricity at home will be something available for everyone.
Wind energy is easily scalable comparing to other energies and it is a really good option
for areas with good wind resources. It can be used in small power turbines for personal
use, bigger turbines that provide electricity for small communities or even creating huge
wind farms which contribute to a great portion of a country´s total electricity production.
1.3
HAWT vs VAWT
From a very early stage of the human history there have been many machines which have
been powered by the wind, such as the traditional windmills or even ships. This machines
although nowadays seem rudimental, they were applying concepts which are still applied
on the wind energy industry but now, trying to make the most of the power provided by the
wind. Horizontal axis turbines tend to be the first image someone get on their mind when
the topic of wind energy is brough up. HAWTs have been studies for many years and
nowadays they are installed all over the world under the name of big companies which
help providing energy to many households. Although they are a good alternative to fossil
fuels, they have some (limitations) when it comes to production and installation. These
machines are meant to be used to provide energy to the grid in a big scale and they tend
to be more profitable when they are manufactured in these big sizes. There are some
companies which are trying to give a push to the small sized horizontal wind turbines, but
the VAWTs offer a good opportunity to scale the energy production to lower amounts, in
order to satisfy the needs of the consumption. One of the main advantages, that they

2.2
Loads
The turbine blades will have to endure loads during their lifetime, with varying strengths
and directions. As a way to simplify the design process, two different types of loads are
defined: Centrifugal loading due to the rotation and the loads generated by the wind on
the blade.
2.2.1
Wind Load Cases
Wind turbines suffer different loading conditions during its lifetime. The main damage
will be caused by the rotation during its normal operation but this is not the only time the
blade will undergo stresses and deflections. That is why a set of load cases is defined
by the European Standard ”IEC 61400­2: Design requirements for small wind turbines”.
This standard has been written with HAWTs in mind and there are some specific rules for
VAWTs, which tell to ignore some requirements, for example the yaw of the turbine, as
VAWTs have no yaw system. The load cases are presented in the following table:
For this project, 2 different DLCs will be considered, cases 1 and 5. Each DLCs has to
be studied under certain conditions, depending on the type of loading that is applied. For
DLC 1, power production case, the analysis is done studying fatigue and ultimate loadings.
The other two DLCs, 5 and 6 are only investigated under ultimate loading conditions.

2.3
Materials in Wind Energy
Wind turbines are meant to work without malfunctioning over many years, that is why the
technology advances haven been focusing on improving the materials used in these ma­
chines. One of the main areas of interest is the materials used for the turbine blades. In
big scale wind turbines the blades turn out to be one of the biggest components of the
whole turbine. As they will be spinning for many years, they must be strong enough to
suffer stresses during their operating lifetime. But having these dimensions, if the mate­
rials are not chosen wisely, they can end up weighting to much for the turbine to perform
correctly.
Composite materials have shown to be one of the best options for this kind of task. They
offer high strength while being relatively light weight, they can be piled up in different con­
figurations to give the blades different properties across their length and they are relatively
easy to manufacture and to produce. But even though they have so many advantages,
one of the main downsides, which is becoming recently a visible issue, is their difficulty
to be recycled and reused. Composite materials consist of fibers and a matrix that are
merged by layers and then cured to unify the two items, this ensures a good bonding be­
tween the fibers and the matrix, but it has become a real challenge to undo this process
to be able to reuse the materials. That is why blades are being decommissioned they
become landfill, as glass­fiber does not decompose and emit toxic substances. But this
practice is something that has drawn the attention of a big part of the industry, in order to
ensure that the next generation of turbine blades are manufactured with the recyclability
in consideration.
Turbine blades are not only made from composite materials, but they also use more con­
ventional materials such us steel and aluminum. These two materials have been widely
used in many areas of the industry as they offer good mechanical properties and they can
be recycled more easily than composite materials, especially aluminum. Aluminum has

approximately only 1 ton of aluminum oxide is produced. This process comes with huge,
deforested areas, loss of habitat for wildlife and contamination of water resources. That
is why recycling aluminum is a more beneficial process than extracting the pure metal,
both for the industries and for the planet.
Steel is less malleable than aluminum and it does tend to suffer from corrosion, which
is something in favor of the aluminum, which has good resistance to rust and corrosion.
Steel is also know for it´s great mechanical properties, which is why the use of steel is
interesting for this project. As the turbine spins, the blades will suffer alternating stresses,
which will slowly damage the blade by fatigue. Aluminum is not a desirable material when
it comes to fatigue damage, as it does not have a clear endurance limit which can be used
to dimension a component. On the contrary, steel is a good choice for fatigue applications,
as it has a stress level which determines if the material will be able to perform for a certain
number of cycles, which usually a number that means it will reach ”Infinite life”.
So the aluminum will provide the aerodynamic profile as it is easy to manufacture and
several other mechanical properties, while the steel reinforcement will make up for the
lack of fatigue limit of the aluminum. Now the turbine is a machine which rotates and
generates centrifugal forces, which depend on the weight of the blade and reinforcement.
And here the main problem of the project is presented. Steel is heavier than aluminum, so
it is not possible to have too much reinforcement, otherwise the stresses on the aluminum
will still make it fail by fatigue.

2.4
Topology optimization
2.4.1
FEM
Finite Element Methods are numerical methods which help to carry out calculations and
solving problems which can be related to many areas of engineering. They serve as a
tool to solve problems which are practically impossible to solve by hand, taking into ac­
count high levels of complexity and reducing calculation time. Regarding the area where
FEM area applied, the main concept of FEM is to discretize the problem by dividing the
model into smaller bodies or elements which are connected between them. Then the
specific load situations are applied to the model and the boundary conditions which limit
the problem. Depending on the complexity of the problem and the accuracy needed, it
can be modeled with 1, 2 or 3 dimensional elements. The expansion to 3 dimensions
allows to include more loading conditions and more complex geometries, but this comes
with a significant increase of computational time. In order to analyze the blade properties
a first relatively simple approach is taken, using 1D elements. This approach allows to
analyze the blade taking into account its cross sectional properties. As the blade profile is
not symmetrical and the geometries are too complex to calculate by hand, a methodology
proposed by [1] is followed, in order to characterize the beam’s geometry. The proposed
method aims to be simple but still allowing to design complex geometries with high level of
accuracy. With the 1D beam model, the bending moments, stresses and deflections the
blade suffers can easily be calculated, without ignoring the multiple materials and geome­
tries of each cross section of the blade. But this model might not be the most accurate tool
to design a blade. As mentioned earlier, a 3D FE model allows to implement more com­
plex geometries and alter on it will be used to implement a topology optimization method
for the reinforcement of the blade.

plex way the blade. Having a mainly constant cross section and a beam­like structure,
hexahedron elements are suitable for the model and the posterior optimization.
Once the cross section of the blade is defined, the length and the number of divisions
(ndiv) is specified. The cross section is meshed in the pre­processing software GiD and
using the numbering already defined in the software, the section is repeated an amount
of ndiv in order to create the hexahedron elements.

2.4.2
Topology optimization theory
When it comes to designing a mechanical component, the main requirement is that it
withstands the loading conditions that will suffer during its lifetime. A very straight way
to achieve this requirement is to make solid robust components, which will suffer low
stresses and therefore perform correctly. But this usually means the use of potentially
unnecessary material and ending with a relatively heavy structure. So in order to design
lightweight structures the material distribution in the structure should be optimized. This
distribution takes into account the topology, shape and sizing of the structure in order to
deliver an optimized final design.
If we take a simple 2 support beam as an example, when it comes to a sizing problem, if
the beam modeled with truss structures, the domain of the design model is already spec­
ified at the beginning of the study and the optimization will change the thickness of the
elements while satisfying a design constraint. On the other hand, with a shape optimiza­
tion, the optimum shape of the previously defined domain is found. Topology optimization
determines the shape, dimensions and connectivity of the domain, while minimizing or
maximizing a physical quantity (deflection, stress...) [3].

4.1.1
Profile Shape
The blade is constructed by an extruded aerodynamic profile. In the inside of the profile
some shear webs and ribs have been added in order to increase the resistance to shear
loads and increase the moment of inertia. In this case the study will be done focusing
more on the bending forces which the blade suffers so the shear response will not be
studied in depth. Regarding the ribs, there has been some studies which try to optimize
the weight distribution on aerodynamic profiles, under bending conditions and they show
the similar results which indicate that the material should be located at the top and bottom
of the profile. Due to manufacturing restrictions, the added material is distributed in small
ribs along the profile. This ribs will also help with the assembly as they will help holding
the reinforcement in place.

be focused on the central part of the blade. The areas towards the leading and trailing
edge are omitted for the optimization process. Later on, once the final design is chosen,
it will be simulated on a more accurate shape for the blade.

4.2
Mechanical design
Now that the number of supports and positioning is decided, there are many designs vari­

so that later they can be optimized, the length of the reinforcement and the distribution of
the material throughout the lengths of the blade, the connection between the reinforce­

The following sections will explain each design variable and which will be the target vari­
ables from the simulations which will be used as a metric for optimizing the designs.
4.2.1
Solid vs hollow
The reinforcement concept starts from two main options for a beam. Taking the rectangular­
like shape of the inside of the blade profile, a solid rectangular section beam is proposed
for the reinforcement. A solid beam would present a good option when it comes to in­
crease the section’s moments of inertia, therefore reducing the stress on the blade. This
concept would be compromised by the relatively high amount of material which would
increase the significantly the weight of the blade and the struts would have to bear higher
stresses. When weight is added, the loading also increases as the centrifugal forces be­
come higher. So even though this option is considered, it does present some drawbacks
from the start. That is why another starting geometry is proposed.
From a manufacturing point of view, a good option would be to use pre­made rectangular
shape, as it gives high moments of inertia, while weighting significantly less than a solid
beam. This beam allows to add material far from the center line of the cross section,
increasing the moment of inertia. It is true that this option means to be tied to finding the
28

==PAGE:37==
beam from a manufacturer with an specific thickness, which could be more challenging
than finding a solid rectangular beam.
4.3
Strut assembly
The struts are the components which connect the blades to the rest of the turbine, with
the connection point at the hub. The loads over the turbine will come from the drag of the
wind, but also they will be transmitted from the blades to the hub trough the struts, so the
blade­strut connection must be designed in order to reduce this load transmission. If the
joint between the blade and the strut is a completely solid union, the bending moments
from the blade will be transmitted to the struts, which will compromise the performance
and lifetime of the components in the struts and the hub. Therefore, a bearing­like union
is proposed. This way the blade can deflect and it will rotate around a pinned connection.
As the blade profile will be relatively narrow, if the blade rests on the pin, while it is being
pulled outwards due to the centrifugal and aerodynamic forces, the stress over the blade
section would end up being too high, that is why the focus will be on trying to make the
reinforcement be the responsible to bear the maximum possible loading, in order to make
the most of the material´s mechanical properties.
Figure 4.6: Strut­Wing Union
Figure 4.6 shows the union between the strut and the blade. The strut has two brackets
which enter in the blade through some holes, and through a third hole in the leading edge
of the profile, the pin (black component) is inserted. This connection can be designed

stresses at the pin.

Reinforcement manufacturing
One aspect to consider when designing a key component of an assembly with such a
unique geometry, is the manufacturability of the part. The design is based on an opti­
mization which generates a final design based on algorithms. These algorithms could
end up generating a geometry that satisfies the constraints but in a practical situation,
it could be unmanufactureable. But it is not only the fact that if it can be manufactured
or not. Depending on the complexity of the geometry, the cost of manufacturing can in­
crease significantly. In order to alleviate design problems, the symmetry constraint limits
the options to the algorithm, ensuring a more simple geometry in the end.

Conclusions and Further work
6.1
Optimized geometries
From the results in Section 5.3.5, it can be concluded that applying topology optimization
had beneficial results for mitigating the stresses generated by the centrifugal loads. All
of the optimizations were able to reach convergence and the results show well defined
geometries. Despite the come concepts being different between them, the values for the
stresses and the deflections were never so minimal that one of the designs was clearly
the way to go. This project has ended choosing a hollow beam, with a rectangular cross
section with 10mm of thickness and it was later optimized.
It is worth mentioning that the chosen design is suitable for the load case that it was
designed for, for maintaining the blade under a centrifugal load.
6.2
Code vs Commercial software
At the beginning of the project, the primary idea was to develop a topology optimization
tool which would be used to design the geometry of the reinforcement. The basic funda­
mentals of Topology Optimization have been outlined and with some basic examples the
functionality of the tool has been illustrated.
On a first approach it was though that with a 2D model it was enough to model the rein­
forcement But once the idea of the hollow rectangular profile was proposed, a need for a
3Dcode appeared. This meant that the Matlab code was going to be more complex than
expected and it would require more computational time.
With he help of the meshing software GiD, a simple way to import and read the geometries
was found, and it was linked to Matlab. The Stiffness Matrix, Load Vector and Boundary
Condition application was written so that it was computed as fast as possible, as the input
files that were going to be run they all had the same structure.
Still the Matlab code did not provide very accurate results. The sensibility analysis and
the filter for the ”Checkerboard pattern” are relatively simple compared with a professional
software and the final results were not of enough quality to base a design on them.
Instead of using Matlab, the code could have been written in Fortran, as it is a more
suitable programming language for Finite Element Modeling. It was not possible due to
time constraints but it could have been a better option.
Also, regarding the computation of the stiffness matrices and when solving for the FEM
equation to get the displacements, a Banded matrix storage could have helped reducing
the computational time. This method consist on computing the stiffness matrix just by
saving half of it. As the matrix is symmetrical, the diagonal is stored as the first row in the
so called ”Banded” matrix and the rest of the matrix is rearranged to from a rectangular
shaped matrix. This is helpful when the bandwidth of the matrix is reduced by a correct
numbering of the element connectivity and nodes.
As expected, the results from the commercial software ANSYS were much more detailed
and it gave the opportunity to explore different optimization designs. This shows how
useful a good FEM software can be when dealing with big iterative calculations.
