Abstract
As one of the most important renewable energy technologies, Photovoltaic (PV) energy
has had a rapid growth rate in recent years. The global installed PV capacity grows to
1185 GW by the end of 2022. With the increase in the number of PV plants, the operational
safety of PV plants has gradually been concerned. As one of the most important parts
of PV systems, although the quality of PV modules has been reliable, degradations and
failures still occur frequently. Some of the degradations may be relatively minor and not
result in serious damage. However, some of the degradation may be slight at first but can
worsen rapidly and cause safety issues. Therefore, it is important to monitor the behavior
of the PV modules and find the degradation in an early stage.
Despite visible degradation, there are some types of invisible degradation in PV modules.
Varies inspection methods can be used to detect the invisible degradations of the PV
module, such as current­voltage (I­V) curve measurements, Electroluminescence (EL)
imaging, lock­in thermography (LIT), infrared (IR) imaging, and ultraviolet fluorescence
(UV­F) imaging. The types of degradation that these inspection methods can detect are
different. A combination of these methods would be useful to find all of the degrada­
tions in a PV module. Additionally, some inspection methods have high accuracy but are
time­consuming. With this fact, the combination of different inspection methods would be
helpful.
This report investigates the degradation detection capability of three inspection methods,
which are I­V curve measurement, IR imaging, and EL imaging. Find a way to combine
these methods to shorten the inspection time and higher the results’ accuracy, and is
structured into three parts. The first part is to stress test healthy PV modules to induce
certain degradations to enable comparison of the subsequent measurement results. The
second part includes the measurement procedures and time required for the three in­
spection methods. The last part of this work analyzes the results of the three inspection
methods, compares their accuracy, and proposes optimized methods to shorten the in­
spection time while maximizing inspection accuracy.
Keywords: PV module degradation; stress test; I­V Curve Measurement; IR imaging; EL
imaging; optimized methods

Introduction
1.1
Background and Project Motivation
In recent years, the demand for energy has increased proportionally with population growth.
Historically, fossil fuels have been the primary energy source, but they are non­renewable
and emit greenhouse gases that pollute the environment. As a result, more and more re­
search has been focused on sustainable energy sources, such as solar, wind, tidal, and
biomass energy. Among these, solar power is recognized as the best method for convert­
ing environmental energy into electricity because of its lower cost and greater potential for
energy development compared with other sustainable energy [1, 2, 3]. This is reflected in
the significant rise in installed solar photovoltaic (PV) capacity, which saw an increase of
243 GW in 2022, representing approximately 25 % of the total installed capacity. [4, 5].
Figure 1.1: Renewable Power Total Installed Capacity and Annual Additions
(Reproduced from [4])
As PV capacity increases, ensuring the reliability and longevity of PV plants has become
important. It is necessary for the PV plants to have a minimum 25­year lifespan and to
operate dependably outside [6]. However, due to the distribution of solar energy, most
PV plants need to be installed in places with flat terrain and strong ultraviolet light [3]. In
other words, PV plants need to withstand harsh conditions including high temperatures,
high humidity, hail, storms, and lightning while continuing to function dependably over
an extended duration [6]. PV modules, the core part of PV systems, numerous studies
have been conducted on them to enhance their efficiency, reduce cost, and maximize
power output [7]. Nonetheless, under prolonged outdoor work, the performance of PV
modules still deteriorates over the years, leading to degradation and causing failures in the
end. This raises safety concerns and affects the dependability of their power generation.
According to the statistics in [8], degradation of the module occurs at a high frequency
during the first ten years of its operation, as shown in Figure 1.2.

Degradation in PV modules can be categorized into visible and invisible degradation.
Visible degradation, such as discoloration, delamination, and glass breakage, can be
identified through visual inspection. Invisible degradation, including cell cracks, potential­
induced degradation (PID), disconnected cell and string interconnect ribbons, and de­
fect bypass diodes, requires specialized inspection methods for detection. If undetected
promptly, there is a risk of safety hazards. For example, microcracks in the PV module
may lengthen and deepen, and localized hot spots may develop and eventually risk caus­
ing a fire [9]. The occurrence of fire will not only lead to economic losses but may also
have an impact on the safety of the staff.
To detect invisible degradation, several inspection methods have been developed, such
as current­voltage (I­V) curve measurement, infrared (IR) imaging, electroluminescence
(EL) imaging, and ultraviolet fluorescence (UV­F) imaging. Each of them has their ad­
vantages and disadvantages. For instance, with a lower Voc the I­V curve measurement
can detect various types of degradation, such as broken ribbons between cells, bypass
diode failure, and PID [10]. However, determining the precise type of degradation using
this method alone is challenging. IR imaging can detect failures such as hotspots and
microcracks by the thermal differences among cells [11]. One of the main advantages of
this method is that it can predict failures before they take place [9]. The microcracks within
the module may be seen with clarity using EL imaging, which shows their length and po­
sition [12]. Unfortunately, this method is time­consuming because it needs to be used in
connection with an external power supply to power the PV module [13]. Therefore, each
detection method takes a different amount of time to detect a PV module, and some of
them are time­consuming. At the same time, the types of faults that can be detected by
each detection method are different.
Being able to detect all of the degradations at the early stage of its generation is necessary
for the daily maintenance and operation of PV plants. Thus, whether it is possible to
detect PV modules by combining two or more inspection techniques to achieve a shorter
detection time and at the same time be able to detect all types of degradation within the
module has become the main research direction of this report.

PV Module Degradation
PV module failures have been defined by the International Energy Agency (IEA) Photo­
voltaic Power Systems Programme (PVPS) as any effect that can degrade the PV mod­
ules and that cannot be reversed by normal operation or create safety issues [10, 14].
In this section, the types of PV module degradation will be divided into two primary cat­
egories: the first is visible degradation which can be observed with the naked eye, and
the other is invisible degradation, which needs to be detected with the help of additional
inspection methods.
2.1
Visible Degradation
Visible degradation, degradation that can be observed with the unaided eye alone without
the use of other tools, is very common in PV modules. This type of degradation includes
discoloration, delamination, glass breakage, burn marks, cracked external surfaces, etc
[10, 15, 16]. A few of these degradation, like discoloration, delamination, and glass break­
age, have an impact on the optical characteristics of the module. This leads to unfavorable
optical reflections and a decrease in the penetrating solar flux, which in turn causes the
PV module to produce less current, and eventually shows up as a decrease in output
power [15].
2.1.1
Discolouration
The discolouration of the ethylene vinyl acetate (EVA) or other encapsulation materials
is one of the most obvious ways that PV modules degrade. When the PV encapsulant
(EVA) is exposed to ultraviolet light for a long and continuous time, it will get discolored
and reduce the sunlight reaching the PV modules, which will lower the output power [17].
EVA Discolouration is typically viewed as an aesthetic issue and does not present any
safety concerns unless the discolouration occurs only in a single cell and is so severe
that there is a risk of causing the substring bypass diode to turn on [10].
Studies have shown that discoloration­induced degradation rates are about 0.5­1 %/a [14].
For severely discolored modules, the power loss can be up to 10 % [18]. This indicates
that EVA discolouration cannot cause performance defects in a large number of modules.
In short, EVA discolouration is a degradation that saturates the power loss over time.
2.1.2
Delamination
The adhesion between the glass, encapsulant, active layer, and backsheet of a PV module
can be affected due to factors such as pollution or ambient humidity. Delamination may
occur at the encapsulant­silicon interface or glass­encapsulant interface [19] and can be
relatively easy to observe directly, as shown in Figure 2.1.

While delamination does not always result in safety concerns, it can lead to gaps forming
between various layers of the module, such as the glass­encapsulant and encapsulant­
silicon interfaces, which allow water vapor to enter the module and cause corrosion, which

backsheet may result in an isolation fault and cause some safety problems. The power

encapsulation­glass and encapsulation­cell interfaces need to be examined.
2.1.3
Glass Breakage
There are many reasons for glass breakage, such as mechanical stresses on the exterior
as well as weather causes such as hail, or stresses caused by other influences such
as moisture or internal expansion of the component [14]. Broken glass will crack in its
original position or even shatter, an example shown in Figure 2.2. The consequence of
glass breakage is the penetration of oxygen and water vapor into the PV module, which
in turn leads to performance degradation and power loss ranging from 10 % to 50 % [10,

2.1.4
Burn Marks
Burn marks can result from a variety of factors, including broken ribbons, failed solder
joints, and localized heating caused by applying reverse current flow [10]. Burn marks
usually cause a loss of power. However, if the PV module is able to operate efficiently
despite the failure of a solder bond or any critical connection, then the impact of that solder
bond failure on power output can be negligible [10]. In more severe situations, where all
solder bonds are broken, an electric arc may form as a result of the total obstruction of
the current flow in that string, which could eventually end in a fire [10]. Visual inspection
can be used to detect burn marks first. For illuminated or partially shaded conditions, IR
imaging can be used to check if the module with burn marks needs to be replaced [10].
2.1.5
Snail Trails
Snail trails is a defect caused by the front side metallization’s silver paste becoming dis­
colored, which is visible by the naked eye [8]. Usually, it occurs at the edge of the solar
cells and can be used to reveal cracks. As mentioned above, the performance of the PV
module would not be influenced by discoloration. But the visible snail trails can reduce
the output power of the module, the power loss has a range from 1% to 8% [18].

locally during operation [21, 22]. In addition, there are different patterns of cracks, and
different patterns have different effects on power loss. Once cracks appear, they gradually
propagate to become longer, wider, and deeper, causing inactive areas, which increases
the impact on the function and performance of the PV module [23]. In more severe cases,
if the temperature of a cracked PV module is elevated by 7.6◦or more compared to the

2.2.2
Potential­Induced Degradation (PID)
Potential­induced degradation is one of the main causes of PV module performance
degradation, mainly due to the potential difference between the PV module and the grounded
frame of the module [25]. At the same time, there are many other factors that accelerate
PID. For example, in a high temperature and humid environment, or when there are micro­
cracks in the PV module, the localized heating caused by the cracks can also accelerate
PID [26].

PID is a kind of degradation that is difficult to observe with the unaided eye. Also, in the
early stages, the PID­induced power degradation effect is smaller under high irradiance

2.2.3
Disconnected Cell and String Interconnect Ribbons
The cell interconnect ribbons are used to connect the solar cells in series to get higher volt­
age [10]. Sometimes the ribbons may become weaker and broken ultimately, especially
for the cell­to­cell part [28]. This can happen either during production, transportation, or
long­term operation of the PV module [10]. For instance, mechanical stress during trans­
portation may cause the interconnection between cells to break [10]. In addition, during
long­term operation, if partial shading is applied to a PV module and causes an area to
overheat, such thermo­mechanical stresses can also lead to ribbon breakage [10]. To
detect this kind of invisible defect, I­V curve measurement, EL imaging, and IR imaging
can be used.
2.2.4
Defective Bypass Diode
When PV modules are operated outdoors, the bypass diode, which is a protective ele­
ment, may fail due to weather conditions and pose a risk of burning, for example, if the
PV system is hit by lightning [29]. Bypass diode failures include short­circuit failures and
open­circuit failures, depending on the electrical characteristics. Also, the electrical char­
acteristics determine whether the fault will result in a risk of burnout [29]. PV modules with
shorted bypass diode will have reduced open­circuit voltage value and maximum power
value. Therefore, I­V curve measurement can be used to detect this kind of failure. For
the bypass diode open circuit failure, the shade test could be used. The PV module’s
surface might be covered by sand or shadow, and IR imaging could be used to determine
whether the module is experiencing this issue [10, 30].

Inspection Methods
There are many different inspection methods available to detect invisible degradations
promptly and accurately. Each of these detection methods has different advantages and
disadvantages and can detect different types of degradation. In this chapter, four inspec­

Infrared (IR) Imaging
When a PV module is degraded, its surface temperature distribution may change. This
phenomenon can be detected by IR imaging. IR imaging is a non­destructive method and
can be used to identify various defects within a PV module by capturing and analyzing
the thermal distribution of the PV module [13]. Additionally, this method allows detection
of the PV module while it is in operation, without the need to disconnect the power or do
anything else [33]. Another advantage of IR imaging is safety, as the inspector does not
need to be in direct contact with the heat source to complete the inspection [13]. It is
also a time­saving method as a large number of PV panels can be inspected in a short
period of time [13]. Figure 3.3 illustrates the time required for IR imaging using a handheld
infrared camera. For example, it takes 0 to 10 hours to complete an IR inspection of a
PV plant with a nominal power of 2 MWp with a handheld IR camera [33]. However, a
disadvantage of IR imaging is the relatively low precise images, which are caused by the
reflection from other surfaces when performing the measurements [13].

3.3
Electroluminescence (EL) Imaging
Electroluminescence (EL) imaging, as the name suggests, is a method of converting elec­
trical energy into optical radiation. This is a non­destructive and contactless method with
a higher spatial resolution compared with IR imaging [13, 34, 35]. With the ability to iden­
tify defects in greater detail, EL imaging can show insight into microcracks and also other
defects within the material [33, 34]. However, before inspecting a PV module with EL
imaging, it takes a long lead time to set up the equipment, especially in field environ­
ments [6]. Additionally, the performance of modules with defects is hard to determine
with EL images [33]. Compared with IR imaging, EL imaging requires more expensive
equipment [34], which is also a disadvantage of this inspection method.
When doing the inspection with EL imaging, a power supply that can provide enough
voltage to achieve Isc of the module or a string of modules is needed to compel current
flow and connect the leads with the same polarity of the sample [36]. Additionally, there
are two ways to perform EL imaging, the dark EL, which should be conducted in a dark
environment, and the daylight EL, which could be conducted in a daylight environment.
The cameras that need to be used for these two imaging methods are different. The
quality of the photos that are taken in the dark environment can be improved [34]. The
camera used for EL imaging needs to be sensitive to the emission spectra of the material
and has a particular variety of sensors. The two primary classifications of sensors are
Complementary Metal Oxide Semiconductor (CMOS) and Charge Coupled Device (CCD),
which make up the majority of the sensor variety [33].

3.4
Ultraviolet Fluorescence (UV­F) Imaging
The lamination material of PV modules has a fluorescent effect and is excited by UV light
in the spectral range of 350 nm (glass transmission) to 380 nm (human visibility border)
[38]. This fluorescence can be captured by cameras with highly sensitive optical sensors,
such as CCD and CMOS cameras. By analyzing the fluorescent images captured on the
surface of the PV module, it is possible to analyze whether there are invisible faults in the
module, which is the basic principle of UV­F imaging [38]. This is a non­destructive and
simple method for quick and easy inspection of PV modules. The results of UV­F imaging
have a wide range of contrasts, which allows not only to analyze the yellowing effect of
PV modules but also to estimate the age of the cracks in the module [13, 17]. The extent
of stress­induced changes of the encapsulant can also shown by the UV­F imaging [39].
However, UV­F imaging also has limitations, such as the type of material of the PV module
affects the quality of the imaging. Fluorescence is not obvious in glass­glass modules and
when the EVA of the module has high transmittance [13]. Also, the filters that need to be
used in this method are sometimes expensive.
UV­F imaging in the laboratory should be done in a dark room. While in the field, mea­
surements are best performed at night or at dusk [38]. The equipment used for UV­F
imaging consists of a UV light source and a camera that blocks specific UV radiation [13].

Conclusion
This report investigated the degradation detection capability of three inspection methods
and found optimized methods to shorten the inspection time while maximizing the ac­
curacy. Chapter 2 and Chapter 3 described the overview of the types of degradation
that may occur in PV modules and the inspection methods. The procedures to induce
a specific type of degradation into PV modules are described in Chapter 4. Chapter 5
and Chapter 6 presented the measurement procedure and the results for each inspection
method. The optimized method introduced in Chapter 7, includes the optimized method
for the 26 modules used in this project and extends to 1 MWp PV plants located in the
areas with different weather.
Chapter 2 presented the overview of the two types of degradations, visible degradation,
and invisible degradation. Each degradation was described in terms of its causes, the
effect it has on the PV modules, and the inspection methods that need to be used to
detect it.
Firstly, the types of visible degradation were introduced, including discolouration, delam­
ination, glass breakage, burn marks, and snail trails. Each type of degradation can be
visible to the naked eye and doesn’t need other inspection methods. The types of invis­
ible degradation were presented following. Cell cracks, PIDs, broken ribbons, defective
bypass diodes, and hot spots are the common types of invisible degradation, and they
may grow quickly and affect the performance of the PV modules. Inspection methods
need to be used to detect them in the early stage.
Chapter 3 introduced four inspection methods, I­V curve measurement, IR imaging, EL
imaging, and UV­F imaging. The introduction includes the devices that need to be used,
the advantages and disadvantages, the required conditions when taking the measure­
ments, and the degradation that can be detected for each inspection method.
I­V curve measurement makes it possible for the operators to know the changes for the
main parameters of the PV modules, such as Voc, Isc, and Pmax, the performance of the PV
modules can be known by the decrease rate of these parameters. One disadvantage of
this inspection method is that it cannot identify the types of degradation on its own. Some
types of degradation may cause thermal distribution differences among the PV modules’
surfaces, IR imaging can be used to detect these degradations. This is a time­saving
method, which can finish the inspection for a 2 MWp PV plant in 10 hours. However,
the degradations which don’t cause temperature differences cannot be detected by this
method. EL imaging has higher spatial resolution compared with IR imaging and can show
the details of the degradation such as microcracks. The main disadvantage of this method
is the need to connect to an external power supply when performing measurements, which
increases the detection time. The UV­F imaging was introduced in the end. This method
can be done in a short time but can only be performed in a dark environment.
The procedures of stress tests were presented in Chapter 4. These stress test procedures
induced certain degradation in the healthy PV modules and facilitated the evaluation of
the results of subsequent measurements.
To induce PIDs in the PV module, the modules need to be covered by aluminum foil
and put in a chamber with high voltage for at least three weeks. The mechanical stress
methodology was used to accelerate the appearance of microcracks and ribbon damage

in the PV module. The bypass diode failure simulation was done in two ways. A wire
was soldering on the bypass diode to short­circuit it and cause bypass diode short­circuit
failure. The tool used for the ribbon damage mechanical test was used again to disconnect
the bypass diodes.
Chapter 5 introduced the devices that need to be used and the procedures that need to
be followed for each inspection method in indoor and outdoor conditions. And the time
required for measurements with each method is listed.
A class AAA pulsed solar simulator needs to be used when measuring the I­V data in
the lab. The outdoor measurements used an I­V tracer to measure the I­V data and a
SolSensor to measure the temperature and irradiance instead. IR imaging uses a hand­
held thermal camera both indoors and outdoors. Three types of cameras were used to
take EL images, two for the lab measurements and one for the outdoor measurements.
The procedures that need to be followed are listed in this chapter. Additionally, when
taking outdoor measurements, the spending time was recorded and listed at the end of
each section. Among them, EL imaging is the most time­consuming technique.
Chapter 6 is the analysis part, which presents the results of each measurement. In this
chapter, the methods and principles used in data processing were explained. The results
were shown mainly in two categories, indoor measurements and outdoor measurements.
From the indoor I­V curve measurements, the PIDs, microcracks, ribbon damage, and
shorted bypass diodes decreased the Pmax of the PV module. Additionally, modules have
PIDs, and shorted bypass diodes can also have a decreased Voc. The modules that have
broken ribbons would have a decreased slope at Voc, and the more ribbons that were
damaged, the more the slope decreased. The outdoor I­V data shows that almost every
module can decrease the Pmax, except bypass diode open­circuit failure. Therefore, I­V
curve measurement cannot be used to detect bypass diode open­circuit failure.
Indoor IR imaging worked well to detect PIDs and microcracks which cause inactive areas,
ribbon damage, and shorted bypass diodes. However, the outdoor IR imaging shows less
compared with indoor results. The PIDs cannot be detected, and the microcracks and
broken ribbons can only be detected partially. Importantly, with shading on the surfaces
of the modules, the bypass diode open­circuit failure can be detected with IR imaging.
Both indoor and outdoor EL imaging has a high resolution and can detect all types of
degradation except bypass diode open­circuit failure. With the decrease of the EL inten­
sity, it is possible to evaluate how severely the module was degraded.
Chapter 7 presented the optimized methods. With these optimized methods, the degra­
dation within the PV modules can be found with the shortest inspection time.
The optimized method can be used to detect PIDs, microcracks, ribbon damage and
bypass diode failure within 26 PV modules is combining EL imaging and IR imaging.
Performing EL imaging first and using IR imaging to do the detection for the rest of the
modules. With this combination, the inspection time for the 26 modules can be shortened
to 154 minutes, and finish the data analysis in 7 days.
When the capacity of PV plants is extended to 1 MWp, the optimized method is differ­
ent. The main types of degradation that occur in PV plants installed in different climatic
regions are different. Optimized inspection methods are proposed for 1 MWp PV plants
built in each of the five climate regions based on the frequency and type of degradation.
The main type of degradation that occurs in PV plants installed in the tropics is PIDs.
