Abstract
The Nitrogen-Vacancy(NV)-defect in diamond has a wide range of applications from quantum
computing to magnetic sensing. Magnetic sensing is important in many diﬀerent areas such as
life science and fundamental physics. High sensitivity imaging of living neuron cells with subcel-
luar resolution at room temperature is a challenging task that is not possible with conventional
technology. A NV-based magnetometer oﬀer high special resolution and weak magnetic ﬁeld
detection under ambient conditions, which enables its to solve this challenging task.
In this report we introduce and apply smooth optimal control theory to improve magnetic
sensitivity of the NV-based magnetometers. To achieve this we developed a python code to
simulate smooth optimal control pulses.
In simulation we found a signiﬁcant improvement
compared to a standard square π pulse.
In our experimental setup we found the necessary
system speciﬁc parameter which was used to tailor the smooth pulses for our experimental
setup. The smooth pulse was experimentally realized using an arbitrary waveform generator
and a radio frequency mixer. The generated pulse showed all of the desired frequencies but also
undesired higher order harmonics.
A smooth optimal control pulse was compared to a shorter square pulse in an optical detected
magnetic resonance measurement. The smooth optimal control showed the best signal to noise
ratio for NV ensemble. However, curiously in general the long pulses outperformed the shorter
pulses. Thus more experiments are required to make ﬁnal conclusions on the improved sensitivity
of NV based magnetic sensors using smooth optimal control pulses.

Introduction
In 1913 Niels Bohr presented his atomic model suggesting that the world was quantum. Today,
over a hundred years later, new technologies utilizing the wonders of quantum mechanics are
still in their infancy but have the potential to revolutionize all aspects of life. Consequently,
quantum devices are an immensely popular area of research.
In this report we study the Nitrogen-Vacancy (NV) center, which is a lattice defect in diamond.
The NV center has proven to be a very stable quantum system, in the sence that it conserves
quantum state over a long period of time, even at room temperature. Recently the NV was used
in the pioneering experiment to violate Bell’s inequality [1].
Various device applications for the NV center has been suggested, such as quantum memory[2],
single photon source[3] and magnetometer[4]. The research contained in this report is directed
towards the latter application.
Magnetometers are sensors that detect magnetic ﬁelds and is, for example, used in Magnetic
Resonance Imaging (MRI) scanners in hospitals. Detecting extremely weak magnetic ﬁeld with
high spacial resolution is an important challenge, not only in life science, but also in areas such
as fundamental physics, material science, data storage and nanoelectronics. Highly sensitive
magnetometers on the market today are very expensive and only work at cryogenic temperatures,
which, for instance, preclude the study of inner workings of living cells.
To begin with we introduce how a two-level spin system can be used to sense magnetic ﬁelds.
We can approximate the NV spin system as a two-level spin system.
1.1
Two-Level Spin System as Magnetometer
A two-level spin system can be used as a sensitive magnetometer. The change in relative phase
φ(B) between the two spin eigenstates depends on the local magnetic ﬁeld B:
|ψ(t)⟩= c0(t) |0⟩+ eiφ(B(t))c1(t) |1⟩
(1)
where the states |0⟩,|1⟩are orthogonal spin states and c0, c1 are the real normalized amplitudes.
Global quantum mechanical phases are not observable, thus only relative phases can be measured.
By measuring the relative phase one can extract information about the local magnetic ﬁeld. A
requirement for such a measurement is that the quantum phase should be coherent during the
entire time of measurement. In other words, the spin system must exhibit long coherence times
in order to become a good magnetometer.
As mentioned above the NV center has a very long coherence time. The NV center is a so-called
color center defect in diamond, see Figure 1. It has remarkable properties which qualiﬁes the NV
center as a great magnetic sensor. The coherence time, T2, is on the order of ∼600 µs, which
can be enhanced to incredible ∼0.5 s by dynamic decoupling techniques at low temperatures[5].
The NV center can furthermore operate in a natural environment, i.e. at room temperature and
atmospheric pressure, and has nanoscale size, which allows for ultra high special resolution.
The next section explains how ensembles of NV centers can enhance the magnetic sensitivity.
1.2
Ensemble NV Spin Systems
The spin state of the NV center is measured using an optical signal. A change in spin state will
alter the intensity of the ﬂuorescent light from the NV center. The magnetic sensitivity is limited
by the signal to noise ratio (SNR). Ensembles of NV centers have been proposed to enhance the
sensitivity of an NV-based magnetometer [6], since the SNR of the NV ﬂuorescence scales with
1/
√
N, where N is the number of NV centers in the ensemble [7]. A high NV density diamond
sample is in principle preferable, however, it comes with a cost of high impurity concentration.

Impurities limit the coherence time and therefore also the sensitivity [8]. So the challenge is to

When dealing with ensembles of NV centers coherent manipulation of the spin states is crucial
in order to reach a high sensitivity. For this purpose a microwave magnetic control ﬁeld is used.
However, coherent manipulation of an ensemble of non-identical NV spins with a single control
ﬁeld is not straightforward.
Complications arise from primarily two factors; inhomogeneous
broadening of NV spin transitions and inhomogeneous control ﬁeld.
Inhomogeneous broadening can be seen in an electron spin resonance measurement (ESR) as a
broadening of the resonance peak. Diﬀerent microscopic local environments of NV spins lead
to diﬀerent energy splitting of the spin states, and therefore diﬀerent resonance frequencies, see
illustrations to the right in Figure 2.
An inhomogeneous control ﬁelds is a ﬁeld that has spacial dependence amplitude. The interaction
strength is proportional to the ﬁeld amplitude, which means that two NV centers at diﬀerent
distances to the ﬁeld emitter will have diﬀerent state dynamics. The emitter of the control ﬁeld
is often a simple copper wire placed on top of the diamond, see Figure 2 left.

1.3
Optimal Control of NV Spin Ensemble
Optimal control theory is used in many ﬁelds. The goal is to ﬁnd a control law that minimizes
a certain optimality criteria. Here comes a simple fuel consumption example showing the concept.
How much and when should we use the accelerator in a car to use as little fuel as possible on a
mountain road? In this example the accelerator is called a control and the fuel consumptions is
called the cost functional. The job is now to ﬁnd the control function that minimizes the cost
functional. Furthermore there is a dynamic constrain in the problem, i.e. the car’s end position
should be at the end of the road. The goal is then to ﬁnd the accelerator function that will get
the car to the desired destination and that minimizes the fuel consumption. In this simple case
this can be done analytically, but this it not always the case.
In this project quantum optimal control is used to ﬁnd a pulse c(t) that maximises a ﬁdelity
functional of an NV spin ensemble. An example of such a ﬁdelity functional is:
F[c(t)] = | ⟨φf| ˆU[c(t)] |φi⟩|2
(2)
where |φi⟩, |φf⟩are the initial and ﬁnal states, respectively. ˆU[c(t)] is the time evolution oper-
ator that depends on the MW pulse.
Here the control is an external applied magnetic ﬁeld. We can control of both the magnetic ﬁeld
amplitude and the phase, which means that there are two controls. More precisely the in-phase
and quadrature components of the ﬁeld are used as controls instead of amplitude and phase of
the ﬁeld. The state function of the NV center is the control object that one wants to control in
an optimal way. The goal is to ﬁnd control functions such that a certain ﬁdelity functional is
optimized.
In this project we want to control an ensemble of NV centers in an optimal way. One problem
could be to drive the NV centers from the |0⟩to |1⟩, as shown in Figure 3. Here we seek to ﬁnd

The vector space of all possible control functions c(t) is inﬁnite dimensional and there is no clear

The advantage of this basis is that the Hamiltonian becomes periodic in time. Solutions to the
Schrödinger equation for a time-periodic Hamiltonian can be solved by Floquet theory. Floquet
theory is related to the familiar Bloch theory of spacial periodic Hamiltonians.
By combining perturbation theory and Floquet theory one can simulate smooth optimal pulses
for NV ensembles. Here we assume the NV spins are two level systems and NV spins do not
interact. Furthermore we apply the rotating frame approximation. The smooth optimal pulses
are simulated in a python code and theoretically compared with square pulses.

maximized under dynamic constrains. Formulated with equations, the cost functional is given

2.7
NV Centers
The NV center is a lattice defect in diamond with magnetic properties. Diamond has a tetra-
hedral molecular geometry, which come from the four sp3-hybridization bonds that the carbon
atoms forms with its neighbours. The NV center consists of a substitutional nitrogen atom and
an adjacent vacancy [17], see Figure 1 in the introduction section 1.1. The NV center can have
four possible orientations in the diamond crystal lattice, which means that there are four diﬀer-
ent orientations of the magnetic moment of the NV center. Each of them can be individually
addressed with a MW ﬁeld .
The NV-center exists mainly in two diﬀerent charge states; The neutral NV0 and the negatively
charged NV−1 state. The negatively charged NV center has an additional electron, which comes
from a nearby substitutional nitrogen defect in the lattice. Nitrogen has one more electron than
carbon, which means that the extra unpaired electron is likely to form an NV−1 state instead
of occupying a state in the higher energetic conduction band. In the rest of this thesis only the
negatively charge NV−1 state will be considered.
2.7.1
Electronic structure of NV centers
This section introduces the electronic structure of the NV center based on the detailed analysis
in [18]. Diamond is an insulator, meaning that it has a large energy gab between the valence and
conduction band. The NV center has energy states within the energy gab. The NV center has
six electrons; three from neighbouring carbon atoms, two from the nitrogen atom and one from
a substitutional nitrogen defect. The carbon atoms in diamond form bonds with eight electrons,
so instead of describing the six electrons in the NV center it is easier to describe the two holes.
The NV center is therefore often treated as two spin-1/2 particles. Two spin-1/2 particles form
a triplet and a singlet state.
The NV center has a molecular orbital (MO) ground and excited state with a zero phonon line
(ZPL) of 637 nm, see Figure 9. The ground triplet state has a zero ﬁeld ﬁne structure splitting of
2.87 GHz due to spin-spin interaction. The |±1⟩states can be split by a magnetic ﬁeld parallel
B∥to the NV axis via the Zeeman eﬀect. Similarly the excited triplet states has a zero ﬁeld
splitting of 1.41 GHz. The singlet states has an energy in between the triplet states and are
separated by a ZPL of 1043 nm.
The energy levels are further split in a hyperﬁne structure by the nuclear spin in the nitrogen
atom, this is not shown in Figure 9, but can be seen in resonance measurements in Section 4.3.
The NV center’s electronic structure is very interesting because it allows for optical readout
[19]. The ﬂuorescence from the NV center depends on the spin state. Furthermore the elec-
tronic structure shows how one can tune the energy levels by applying a magnetic ﬁeld. These
are two of the main reasons that the NV center is a great candidate as a sensitive magnetometer.
The optical readout of the NV spin state can be understood by looking at the allowed transitions
and lifetimes of the energy levels. An oﬀ-resonant optical excitation of the NV center can be
done with a green laser, see Figure 10. Typically a green laser with a wavelength of 532 nm is
used. First order optical transitions are spin conserving, which means the NV center conserves
its spin state during the green optical excitation and red radiative decay. However, there are also
non-radiative decay paths via the singlet states that compete with the radiative decays. The
non-radiative decay paths are diﬀerent for the excited |0⟩and exited |±1⟩spin states. The decay
rate from the excited |±1⟩to the eﬀective singlet spin state |s⟩is faster than the decay rate
from the excited |0⟩to |s⟩. This means that an NV center in the excited |0⟩spin state, which
has a weaker non-radiative decay path, is brighter than one in the excited |±1⟩state, which has

2.7.3
NV Spin Relaxations
So far the NV spin states have been treated as a pure quantum states. However, this is not
adequate for describing real experiments where the NV spins interacts with its environment.
Here the density matrix should be used to describe the loss of information to its environment.
The NV center is an open quantum system meaning that the NV spin undergoes relaxation.
From the master equation for open two level systems, one can show that a pure quantum state
turns into a mixed state. This process is also called decoherence, and it means that oﬀ-diagonal
elements of the density matrix exponentially goes to zero with a characteristic time T2 [22]. The
relaxation is limiting the sensitivity in many of the proposed mangnetometry schemes, where
the magnetic ﬁeld sensitivity η for an NV magnetometer is given by [23]:
η ∝
1
C√tmβ
(63)
where C is the contrast of the ﬂuorescence, tm is the measurement time and β is the photons
collected in one measurement. The measuring time is limited by the relaxation process, which
is often described with three characteristic time scales; Spin relaxation T1, decoherence T2 and
pure dephasing T ∗
2 [23].
The ﬁrst type of relaxation is spin relaxation and is mostly due to phonon interaction and is
consequently temperature dependent. This relaxation changes not only the population of the
NV spin state but also causes decoherence. T1 is on the order of milliseconds for an NV center
in bulk diamond at room temperature [24].
The fastest decay process is pure dephasing and this is due to inhomogeneities in the local
environment. For single NV measurements the NV resonance frequency is broadened by slow
temporal spin bath ﬂuctuation (non-Markovian). For ensemble measurements further inhomoge-
neous broadening is introduced. Each NV spin has a diﬀerent local environment in the diamond
lattice, and are therefore subject to diﬀerent local magnetic and electric ﬁelds, local stresses,
local strains and crystal defects. In most samples it is the 1.1 % of natural abundant 13C that

techniques, i.e. it is intrinsic. The major contributions to decoherence are from interaction with
neighbouring NV centers, P1 centers and 13C isotopes.

5
Discussion
This section ﬁrst discusses our simulation of smooth pulses and secondly the experimental results.
5.1
Simulation
Our simulation successfully generates smooth pulses. The code is robust with respect to diﬀerent
initial conditions. We see a signiﬁcant increase in the average ensemble ﬁdelity for a smooth π
pulse compared to a square π pulse. It would be interesting to conduct further research into
whether the ensemble ﬁdelity could be improved by changing the duration of the pulse tf and
number of control amplitudes Na.
A simulation typically takes a couple of hours on a standard PC. However, we have identiﬁed
three ways of improving the program. Firstly the computational speed of the python program
can be improved by writing parts of the code in pre-compiled languages such as C or C++.
Secondly parallelization of the code can improve calculation speed even further by calculat-
ing the time evolution for multiple NV spins simultaneously. Thirdly it is possible to increase
convergence speed by calculating the curvature ∇2
aF and applying it to adjust the step size. [14].
5.2
Experiments
The following discussions of our experiments are structured in accordance with the project goals
as described in Section 1.4; 1) generation of smooth pulses, 2) characterisation of inhomogeneous
broadening, 3) Rabi frequency measurement, and 4) comparison of the smooth and square pulses.
1) The generation of the smooth pulse appeared to contain the correct frequency components
from the AWG through the mixer and after the ampliﬁer. However, as seen in the CW ODMR
experiment, Section 4.3, satellite peaks are present. Three possible explanations for this ob-
servation are imperfect mixing, unmatched impendence or a malfunctioning signal generator.
The mixing process could have generated additional unwanted frequencies. The only detected
unwanted frequencies were higher order harmonics, however, these cannot explain the satellite
peaks as they should be far oﬀany NV spin resonances. If the mixing process is causing the
satellite peaks then there are several potential ﬁxes. High frequency ﬁlters can be used to remove
unwanted frequencies, however, these ﬁlters are impractical in ESR experiments as the NV spin
resonances vary in accordance with bias magnetic ﬁelds. Changing the mixer to an IQ-mixer or
a single-sideband mixer can also be used to remove some of unwanted frequencies. An additional
advantage to changing the mixer is that the down-converted signal does not heat the wire.
The satellite peaks can also be caused by unmatched impendence components. The gold wave-
quides are not impendence matched with the AWG and interferences in the MW chain can
appear.
A third explanation for the satellite peaks could be SG frequency instability. We observed that
the SG has diﬀerent frequency oﬀsets for internal list mode and trigger mode and additionally
this oﬀset is power dependent. Therefore, the SG should be investigated for software or hardware
faults.
In [28] they use a switch after the mixer to increase the on/oﬀratio, which could improve the
playback of the smooth pulse at the diamond, meaning that the experimental pulse is identical
to the theoretically calculated pulse. A good way to test the playback of the pulse would be
with a high frequency oscilloscope inserted after the gold waveguide.
It should be noted that we experienced many problems with external triggering of the AWG.
42
==PAGE:48==

DISCUSSION
Not all trigger signals are registered by the AWG because of trigger deadtime. Trigger signals
within 700 ns of the previous signal are not registered. Therefore the AWG should be used to
trigger other components, and not the other way around.
2) The characterization of an ODMR peak showed an inhomogeneous broadening of 1 MHz.
This is a relatively small inhomogeneous broadening originating from the small probe volume
in the confocal setup. In this confocal setup we expect to see only a small improvement, but as
seen in the characterization of the ODMR spectrum only 10 % of the peak proﬁle is Gaussian,
which means the linewidth is dominated by homogeneous broadening. In a wide-ﬁeld setup the
diﬀerence between a smooth pulse and a square pulse will be more pronounced.
3) The Rabi curve we measured was diﬃcult to ﬁt, as seen in Figure 29. Instead the Rabi
frequency was found to be at maximum 1.1 MHz from the raw data. A possible explanation for
the poor ﬁtting could be misalignment between the bias magnetic ﬁeld and the NV axis, which
decreases the coherence time [29]. Furthermore, we noticed that the Rabi curve did not converge
to a contrast in between the bright |0⟩spin state and darker |−1⟩spin state, as we would have
expected for a completely mixed state. This is more likely the reason for the poor ﬁtting as it
changes the shape of the Rabi curve. In this scenario thermalization is not likely the reason for
the unexpected Rabi shape, as thermalization happens on the ms timescale whereas our Rabi
measurement duration is only 1 µs. A Rabi measurement was conducted without the mixer to
test if the Rabi shape had a connection to the mixing. The results is shown in Figure 34. Here
we see the same shape of the Rabi curve as in Figure 29.
Most probably the NV spins are not doing a full population inversion into the |−1⟩state for any
duration of the MW pulse, but instead the population is only drive into the mixed state. Similar
eﬀect is seen when a two level system is driven oﬀresonance. One explanation for this partial
population inversion could be SG frequency instability.

DISCUSSION
4) Our pulsed ODMR experiment showed that the smooth pulse gave the highest contrast of the
four tested MW pulses. It is also the widest of the four peaks, which means that the smooth
pulse is more robust to inhomogeneous broadening compared to the three other pulses.
The ODMR signal contrast is low (1.5 %) and consequently has a low SNR of 5. This poor SNR
can have many origins, such as uncollimated green light, misalignment between the bias magnetic
ﬁeld and the NV axis, heating, etc. In the pulsed ODMR measurement we saw asymmetric peak
shapes, see Figure 33. This is typically seen when a large bias magnetic ﬁeld of 50 mT is applied
where there is excited-state level anti-crossing (ESLAC) [30]. Here one can polarize the nuclear
spin states. However, we only have 8 mT so this is not a satisfactory explanation. Misalignment
of the magnetic ﬁeld to the NV axis can perturbs the spin states and cause spin mixing. Spin
mixing can change the transition probabilities of the nuclear spin states and this could be another
explanation for the asymmetric peaks.
The pulsed ODMR experiment shows unexpectedly that the long square pulses seem to give
a higher contrast than short square pulse. This is counter-intuitive since for square pulses we
would expect that the largest population transfer to the darker |−1⟩state should happen with a
short π pulse. The Rabi curve in Figure 34 shows that the short square pulse does not transfer
the ensemble to the dark state but to the unpolarized mixed state. Since the obtained π pulse
is not actually a population inversion for the ensembles, the short pulse does the population
transfer to the mixed state equally well or even worse that the longer pulses. This explains why
the long square pulse gives a higher contrast than the short square pulse in the pulsed ODMR
experiment. For the same reason the non-optimal smooth pulse, which is a long 5µs pulse, also
gives a similar contrast than the short square pulse.
In this scenario, with the unexpected Rabi curve, the smooth optimal pulse gives the higher
contrast compared to the square pulse.
Even though the comparison is biased towards the
longer pulses, the smooth optimal pulse is still superior compared to the two other long pulses.
Based on all the experimental evidence we have reason to believe that optimal control theory
can improve the sensitivity NV-based magnetometers.

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
A python program was developed to simulate the smooth optimal pulses for diﬀerent ﬁdelity
functionals. The program successfully produced smooth pulses for diﬀerent NV ensembles. In
the simulation the smooth pulses resulted in a higher average ensemble ﬁdelity compared to the
square pulses.
An arbitrary waveform generator (AWG) was used together with a high frequency mixer to
experimentally produce the smooth pulses. The correct frequency components were present in
the output signal of the mixer. Instability of the signal generator was a challenge along with
external triggering of the AWG.
The inhomogeneous broadening of the probed volume in the diamond sample was found to be 1
MHz from a pseudo-Voigt ﬁtting of an ODMR signal. A maximum Rabi frequency of 1.1 MHz
was determined from a Rabi measurement. Here we saw that the Rabi curve did not converge
to the mixed state but rather to the darker |−1⟩spin state.
In a pulsed ODMR measurement the 5 µs smooth optimal pulse was superior compared to
the 476 ns square pulse. However, the measurements were biased towards the longer pulses.
Therefore, there is reason to believe that smooth optimal pulses can enhance the sensitivity of
NV-based magnetometry.
6.1
Outlook
Optimal control of quantum systems can be used to robustly steer the dynamics of quantum
states in many diﬀerent scenarios. Smooth optimal control oﬀers a relatively simple approach
with a low number of optimization parameters to calculate optimal pulses. Because smooth
optimal control uses Floquet theory, which is a very general approach, it can be used with any
cost functional and hence for many diﬀerent purposes.
In magnetometry smooth optimal control will help boost the sensitivity through robust control
of the NV spins. In composite quantum systems optimal control can be used to create and
maintain entanglement.
