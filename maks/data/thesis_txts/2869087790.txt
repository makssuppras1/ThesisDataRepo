Abstract
For more than 200 years, the worldwide consumption of fossil fuels has been extensive.
Recent
policies designed to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions promote the search for alternatives
to fossil fuels. One of them is bio-oil derived from biomass, however, this has several drawbacks
that must be overcome. Hydrodeoxygenation (HDO) can chemically upgrade bio-oil, thus making
it competitive to fossil fuels. In recent years, continuous slurry reactors have gained interest in the
stabilization of bio-oil. In this study, an innovative continuous slurry reactor design was employed
and tested on model compounds. For a Ni/Al2O3 catalyst tested on a 10 wt% guaiacol in octanol
feed, the guaiacol conversion peaked at 83 ± 1.3% with a low octanol conversion for T = 180◦C
and stabilized products of reaction. However this catalyst deactivated severely in the presence of 50
ppmw sulphur with a more rapid deactivation observed for thiophene compared to 1-dodecanethiol.
Since thiophene resembles sulphur compounds in real bio-oil, the Ni/Al2O3 catalyst is not suitable
for HDO of real bio-oil. Alternatively, a NiMoS/Al2O3 tested at higher temperatures of 180-270◦C
proved to be inferior to the Ni/Al2O3 catalyst in the lower temperature range, but did not suffer
any deactivation in the presence of sulphur. Additionally, the same NiMoS/Al2O3 catalyst showed
promising conversions for guaiacol of 75 ± 2.6% and octanol of 61 ± 0.74% at 270◦C with stabilized
products of reaction. Unique for this catalyst, a high methane yield of 3.3 mol% was detected in the
gas phase at 270◦C and a prominent aqueous phase with no carbon loss appeared at temperatures
above 210◦C. This stems from a high conversion of octanol which was not found for the Ni/Al2O3
catalyst. The NiMoS/Al2O3 catalyst was thus deemed a suitable catalyst for HDO of real bio-oil.
For the Ni/Al2O3, utilizing a 50 wt% furfural, 40 wt% guaiacol and 10 wt% octanoic acid feed led to
a furfural conversion of 46± 3.0% at 150◦C. Doubling the catalyst load from 1 g to 2 g improved the
furfural conversion to 75±2.2 at 150◦C. However, coking was a severe issue for the three-component
mixture evidenced by a mass uptake of 7.6 wt% on the catalyst when utilizing 1 g of catalyst. This
led to plugging of the reactor outlet, highlighting that improvements must be done to the set-up
before catalytic tests can be done on real bio-oil. Otherwise, catalysts retained their initial particle
size of 250-425 µm with X-ray fluorescence (XRF) suggesting that leaching of the Al2O3 support
was more prominent than leaching of the Ni particles. These findings must be verified by additional
analyses. Overall, the continuous slurry reactor design proved to be a feasible reactor design for
HDO on model compounds, however, it must be optimized before HDO of real bio-oil can be carried
out herein.

Preface
When I was about to end my last semester of courses at DTU, I was at a loss for where to write my
thesis. During my studies, I have taken electives in catalysis both at DTU and Politecnico di Milano, so I
figured it would be an interesting field to study a little further. Shortly after sending an e-mail to Anker
asking to know more about his current projects, he had set up a meeting. At this meeting, I received
an interesting, insightful and inspiring presentation followed by an introduction to the set-up by both
Anker and Martin. It was clear to me, that this project would fuel my interest in the field of catalysis
and the green transition. At this meeting and at every meeting since, I have felt included in the project
and I am thus very thankful to both Anker and Martin for dedicating their limited time to this project
and to me.
In my everyday life during this project, Alexander and Rui have supported me in every aspect they
possibly could. From picking up calls at ungodly hours when I was working a bit late in the laboratory
to answering every little question I had, they have always been there to support me to the fullest. Their
enthusiasm and dedication to the work shine through and it has helped me when facing obstacles along
the way. Therefore, I am very thankful for the daily assistance by the both of them, namely tips and
tricks provided by Alexander and valuable guidance from Rui. Receiving silly stickers on WhatsApp has
been an unexpected gift during this project. All of this combined has turned an otherwise tough process
into a delightful and joyful experience.
A special thanks should be given to Albert, Emil, Nikolaj and Ole for the countless lunch breaks and
time we have shared during this project. Spending time in their company has always brought a smile to
my face. Last but not least I am thankful for my now five and a half years of studying at DTU. It has
been a ride to remember with sweet fellow students, educational courses and impactful professors.
Use of generative artificial intelligence (GAI)
In this project, GAI has been used as an assistance tool. Mainly, ChatGPT [1] has been used for fixing
issues in LaTeX and similarly helped out with Matlab scripts used for data-processing. Otherwise, it has
been used as proofreading tool going through written sections and pointing out possible opportunities for
improving. No text has been copied from ChatGPT, neither has it been used as a source of knowledge.

Introduction
Ever since the industrial revolution began around 1750, the consumption of fossil fuels has increased
and dominated the market due to their high energy density, low price and availability [2, 3]. Within
the European Union (EU) alone, the transportation sector accounts for 31% of the energy demand with
fossil fuels delivering 95% of the total energy consumption. Only 4% stem from biofuels and 1% from
electricity [4]. Consequently, the transportation and storage sector was responsible for about 10% of the
total CO2 emissions in the first quarter of 2023 [5]. There is thus a big energy demand, large greenhouse
gas (GHG) emissions and a great potential for pushing forward the green agenda in the transportation
sector. Globally, policies designed to reduce GHG emissions arise and increase the demand for biofuels
as highlighted in Figure 1. For instance, the European Renewable Energy Directive (RED III) demands
a binding share of at least 29% renewables in the final energy consumption in the transportation sector
by 2030. Furthermore, 5.5% must originate from advanced biofuels derived from non-food feedstocks [6]
as observed in Figure 1.
Figure 1: Left: Forecast growth for different biofuels by country. Right: Biofuel share of transport demand
projected for 2021-2027. Adapted from [7]
A promising way of producing biofuels is pyrolysis, yet, the bio-oil obtained from pyrolysis lacks several
important features compared to fossil fuels. Consequently, bio-oil must be upgraded and this can be
done chemically. Till this day, a great focus has been on the choice of catalyst, operating conditions and
deactivation mechanisms in batch and fixed bed reactors. This has led to the discovery of catalysts with
promising conversions at feasible operating conditions, however, coking and plugging of reactors limit
upscaling of the process. Therefore, alternative reactor designs to the commonly utilized fixed bed and
batch reactors must be investigated.

Aim of this project
In this project, hydrodeoxygenation HDO of model compounds simulating real bio-oil was performed
in a continuous slurry reactor.
For this reactor design, fresh bio-oil is instantly diluted with partly
converted bio-oil. This was expected to allow for longer running time in a continuous operation. Herein,
experiments were conducted on a 10 wt% guaiacol in octanol (Gua/Oct) mixture as well as a three-
component mixture of 50 wt% furfural, 40 wt% guaiacol and 10 wt% octanoic acid (Fur/Gua/OcAc).
For the Gua/Oct mixture, the influence of temperature, the presence of two different sulphur poisons and
two types of catalyst were examined and compared to a blank experiment with support only. Regarding
the Fur/Gua/OcAc mixture, the influence of temperature and mass of catalyst were tested. The overall
performance of the continuous slurry reactor was evaluated by thoroughly analysing the upgraded bio-
oil, gas and the spent catalyst. Thus, the aim of the given study is stabilization of model compounds
simulating real bio-oil in a continuous slurry reactor.

2.1.1
Cellulose
Cellulose makes up 40-50% of lignocellulosic biomass and is thus one of the major components as shown
in Figure 2 [9]. It is a polymer consisting of glucose units linked by β-(1→4) glycosidic bonds and a high
crystallinity. In ambient water, it is insoluble and resistant to enzymatic conversions. However, cellulose
has been observed to decompose at 315-400◦C during fast pyrolysis [11, 12, 13].
2.1.2
Hemicellulose
As shown in Figure 2, between 20-30% of lignocellulosic biomass is made up of hemicellulose, a heteropoly-
mer consisting of a wide range of monosaccharides including xylose, mannose, glucose and galactose with
an example shown in Figure 3. Notably, wood hemicellulose is rich in mannan, glucan and galactan.
Compared to cellulose, hemicellulose has a lower degree of crystallinity due to the presence of side-groups
which makes it more soluble and easily hydrolyzed in water at temperatures above 180◦C [13]. In agree-
ment with that, hemicellulose has been found to decompose at temperatures of 220-350◦C during fast
pyrolysis.

2.1.3
Lignin
Together with cellulose and hemicellulose, lignin is the third major component constituting 10-20% in
lignocellulosic biomass as observed in Figure 3. It is classified as an aromatic heteropolymer consisting of
p-hydroxyphenylpropanoid units held together by C-C or C-O-C bonds [13] as shown in Figure 3. Lignin
has been found to be difficult to decompose in pyrolysis as it is occurs in a large range of temperature,
i.e. 160-900◦C which increases the yield of solid residue, too [11].
Converting lignocellulosic biomass into a useful transportation fuel requires a few steps. First of all, solid
materials must be converted to liquids, which is commonly done by hydrothermal liquefaction (HTL),
gasification or pyrolysis.
2.2
Pyrolysis
Pyrolysis is defined as a decomposition brought about by high temperatures [14], more specifically a
direct thermal decomposition of an organic matrix in the absence of oxygen. This produces solid, liquid
and gaseous products. The liquid is particularly interesting as an energy carrier as it has a high energy
density, is easily transported, conveniently stored and flexible in use compared to the solids (chars)
and gaseous products [15]. Within the field of pyrolysis, both slow and fast (or flash) pyrolysis exist.
Conventionally, slow pyrolysis with a residence time of 5-30 min has been employed with the aim of
producing charcoal. Since the vapours do not escape the reactor promptly, components in the vapour
continue to react with each other hereby forming heavier compounds which results in a higher solid
fraction [16]. Thus, slow pyrolysis is undesirable in terms of bio-oil production.
2.2.1
Fast pyrolysis
Opposed to slow pyrolysis, the vapour residence time for fast pyrolysis is commonly < 2 s with a high
heating rate up to 1000◦C/s.
The high heating rate and short residence time minimises undesired
secondary reactions. As shown in Figure 4, for intermediate temperatures of 500-520◦C, the organic
liquid yield of fast pyrolysis of wood is optimum and can be as high as 80 wt% on a dry feed basis [17,
18]. Below 400◦C, condensation reactions lead to a high char yield and above 550◦C, secondary cracking
of volatiles increases the gas yield. Typically, fast pyrolysis is carried out in a fluidized bed reactor
with a conceptual process configuration shown in Figure 5. Upstream the reactor, biomass is dried to a
water content below 10 wt% and grinded to small particles of diameter <2 mm to minimise heat transfer
limitations. Downstream the reactor, solids are separated from vapours and vapours are cooled rapidly
to ensure the desired compounds are obtained. The fluidized bed reactor has the main advantages that
it has high heat transfer rates and a simple reactor configuration [17]. However, other reactor designs
utilized in fast pyrolysis of bio-oil cover ablative reactors in which biomass particles are pressed against a
hot surface. On this hot surface, biomass melts and forms an oil film which vaporises, thus giving access

2.2.2
Pyrolysis products
Pyrolysis produces both solids, liquids and gaseous products. The char is a solid residue rich in carbon,
inorganics and small quantities of oxygen and hydrogen [18]. Additionally, the noncondensable gases
produced during fast pyrolysis differ in composition, but are generally rich in H2, hydro-carbon gasses
(C1-C4), CO, CO2 and traces of H2S [20].
2.2.3
Bio-oil composition
Bio-oil contains 15-35% water along with several organic compounds with molecular weights in a range
of 300-1000 g/mol [21]. For wood bio-oil, the composition ranges are shown in Figure 6 highlighting a
large amount of oxygenated functionalities stemming from a high oxygen content in the wood feedstock
[22].

In addition to water and organic compounds, bio-oil can also contain traces of Na, K, Ca, Mg, Fe,
V, Pb and Ni [23]. These are important to consider in the chemical upgrading of bio-oil as some of
them causes catalyst deactivation [24]. Due to the chemical composition of bio-oil, physical properties

utilization of bio-oil is inefficient and challenging and it must be upgraded in order to become a valuable

When upgrading bio-oil, the aim is to refine the stability and energy density as well as reduce the acidity
by removing both oxygen and water. Ideally, upgrading bio-oil leads to a high-grade bio-oil characterised
by a high hydrogen/carbon (H/C) ratio and a low oxygen/carbon (O/C) ratio with good combustion

properties [27]. Upgrading of bio-oil can be done by utilizing physical techniques such as hot vapour

Opposed to physical techniques, chemical upgrading alters

esterification, catalytic cracking and hydrotreating.
2.3
Hydrotreating of bio-oil
2.3.1
Hydrodeoxygenation (HDO)
In regards to bio-oil, hydrotreating is commonly used to remove certain impurities such as oxygen,
sulphur and nitrogen. This is referred to as hydrodeoxygenation (HDO), hydrodesulphurisation (HDS)
and hydrodenitrogenation (HDN), respectively. HDO is an efficient upgrading process converting oxygen-
containing components into oxygen-free hydrocarbon fuels while liberating oxygen as water [22].
A
simplified reaction for HDO of bio-oil is given in Eq.(1) [19].
CH1.33O0.43 + 0.77 H2 →”CH2” + 0.43 H2O
(1)
Here, ”CH2” represents the fully deoxygenated hydrocarbon product. The heat of reaction is approxi-
mately 2.4 MJ/kg bio-oil making it an exothermic reaction. When a high degree of deoxygenation has
occurred, the liquid product becomes bi-phasic. These typically consists of an aqueous phase and an
oil phase of low polarity. Taking these phases into account, Venderbosch [29] proposed a more realistic
overall reaction for HDO of bio-oil given in Eq.(2). This is based on a specific experiment [19].
CH1.47O0.56 + 0.386 H2 →0.742 CH1.47O0.11 (oil phase) + 0.192 CH3.02O1.09 (aq phase) + 0.285 H2O (2)
In this experiment, the O/C ratio in the oil decreased from 0.56 in the bio-oil feed to 0.11 in the upgraded
oil phase. In accordance with this, a high oxygen content was found in the aqueous phase. Furthermore,
cracking and decarbolynation and decarboxylation (DCO) reactions led to a gas phase rich in CH4,
CO, CO2 and C1-C4. Due to the presence of C1, steam reforming/methanation and water gas shift
reactions might take place during HDO of bio-oil. This depends on the catalyst [29]. For evaluation
and comparison of a given HDO upgrading process, two common parameters include the yield, Yoil and
degree of deoxygenation, DOD. These are defined in Eq.(3) and Eq.(4) [19].

2.3.3
HDO reaction pathways
During HDO, molecules undergo several reaction pathways to form hydrogenated compounds. As the
composition of bio-oil varies and covers a wide range of compounds, only reaction pathways of selected
model compounds will be mentioned.
2.3.3.1
Guaiacol
One of the main group of compounds found in lignin-derived bio-oil is functionalized phenols such as
guaiacol. A general reaction scheme for this compound is given in Figure 9 as presented by Bui et al.[34]
As predicted by thermodynamics, cleavage of the Ar–OH bond to form anisole is tricky and observed
to happen to a minor extent, whereas the demethylation (DME) and demethoxylation (DMO) routes
have been found to dominate on reduced transition metals. Subsequent to DME and DMO, the formed
cresols can undergo either direct deoxygenation (DDO) to form benzene and toluene or a hydrogenation
(HYD) route with oxygen removed during dehydration and hydrogenolysis. Furthermore, benzene might
undergo further condensation to form heavier products such as dimers [34].
Additionally, an acidic
alumina support has shown to promote the formation of methylated products. As shown in Figure 10, a
heterolytically cleaved methylene from guaiacol adsorbed on the catalyst surface induced an electrophilic
substitution on an aromatic ring in the ortho-position.

2.3.3.2
Furfural
Furfural belongs to the group of furans stemming from the dehydration products of cellulose and hemi-
cellulose. Deoxygenation of furfural has been found to mainly occur via two pathways on a Cu-based
catalyst as shown in Figure 11: Decarbolynation of the C––O bond followed by HYD and direct HYD.
The furan formed from DCO can undergo ring-opening to yield n-butanal in equilibrium with n-butanol.
Otherwise, the ring is saturated to form tetrahydrofuran. From direct hydrogenation, furfuryl alcohol
is formed and can subsequently lose its hydroxymethyl group to form furan or it can be hydrogenated
into tetrahydrofurfuryl alcohol or 2-methylfuran. The tetrahydrofurfuryl alcohol can hydrogenate into
tetrahydrofuran or rearrange into δ-valerolactone. Additionally, 2-methylfuran can be converted into
both 1-pentanol, tetrahydro-2-methyl and 2-pentanone in equilibrium with 2-pentanol [35, 36]. Inter-
estingly, hydrogenation of the aldehyde in furfural has been found to dominate on a Cu/SiO2 catalyst
whereas Ni/SiO2 to a larger extent cleaved the C-O bond of the hydrogenated furan ring. This led to
ring opening on the Ni catalyst [37]. In fact, density functional theory (DFT) calculations imply that
the fully filled 3d band of Cu atoms overlaps with the anti-bonding orbital of the aromatic furan ring.
This causes a repulsion of the furan ring [37].

Regarding the HDO of octanoic acid, studies on Pd [38], Ni, Mo [39] and RuSn [40] based catalysts

Sulphides
In HDS, Co- and Ni-MoS2 based catalysts have long been utilised in conventional hydrotreating with Ni
and Co functioning as promoters. Herein, Ni and Co substitute Mo at the edges of MoS2 slabs which

it appears that these vacancies are responsible for the scission of C–O bonds in HDO, hence important
for the activation of the reactant [19]. Cao et al. [43] investigated the synergistic effect between Co and
MoS2 on p-cresol conversion in a continuous fixed-bed reactor operating at 220◦C and 3.0 MPa for 12
hours, leading to the proposed reaction mechanism for p-cresol shown in Figure 14.

2.4.2
Noble metals
Noble metal catalysts including Pt, Pd, Rh and Ru are known to have high catalytic activities in
hydrotreating [36]. Bjeli´c et al. [46] looked into the HDO of eugenol over carbon-supported noble Pd,
Pt, Rh and Ru and non-noble Cu and Ni based catalysts. Catalytic tests were carried out in a batch
reactor operating at varying temperatures of 225-325◦C and pressures of 5-7 MPa for 3.5 h. By setting
up a microkinetic model, they calculated the turnover frequency (TOF) for deoxygenation of 2-methyl-4-
propylphenol resulting in the volcano plot presented in Figure 16. According to Sabatier’s principle, Ru
was superior with an adsorption energy of −310 kJ/mol, while Pt bonded too strongly to the substrate,

2.4.3
Alternative catalysts
In an attempt to find low-priced catalysts, non-noble metals including base metals, metal oxides [48],
phosphides [49, 50], carbides and nitrides have gained attention in recent years [36]. Mortensen et al.
[48] screened multiple non-noble metal catalysts on different supports for the HDO performance. Herein,
phenol served as the model compound and tests were run in a batch reactor operating at 100 bar and
275◦C for 5 hours.
For the oxides and methanol synthesis catalysts, overall conversions were below
10% as they were unable to hydrogenate the aromatic ring. Opposed to that, the reduced non-noble
metal catalysts, Ni, Co and Fe on silicate supports showed promising HDO activities with conversions
and selectivities shown in Figure 17. Notably, the Ni catalyst possessed a high conversion of 80% while

promote both deoxygenation and hydrogenation [51]. However, reduced non-noble catalysts are suspect
to deactivate in the presence of water and sulphur [52].

2.4.4
Role of support
The influence of support has been studied on sulphided catalysts.
Conventionally, Al2O3 is utilised
on sulphided catalysts, however, these are rapidly deactivated by coke deposition due to the acidity of
alumina. Furthermore, γ-Al2O3 is metastable in the temperature range of 140-380◦C under hydrothermal
conditions and may crystallize to form bohemite [53]. Bui et al.[34] tested the guaicol HDO conversion
on a CoMoS2 catalyst supported by either ZrO2, TiO2 or Al2O3. The experiments were carried out
at P = 4 MPa with 100 ppm H2S at T = 300◦C for 12 h. The rate of HDO conversion turned out
to decrease in the order ZrO2 > TiO2 > Al2O3. Thus, decreasing acidity of the support was found
to promote the rate of conversion [34]. Findings by Ranga et al.[54] suggest that decreasing acidity of
the support limits coke formation. This causes the trend observed by Bui et al.[34]. For noble metals,
the role of the support on Cu, Ni, Pd, Pt and Ru catalysts have been tested in a batch reactor run at
225-325◦C at 5 MPa for 3 h [55]. The supports of interest were Al2O3, SiO2, HZSM-5 and TiO2 and the
model compound was eugenol. They found that hydrogenation dominated on the metallic sites while
deoxygenation prevailed on acidic sites. Furthermore, only the very acidic SiO2 –Al2O3 and HZSM-5
supports were able to deoxygenate aromatics while saturated species were deoxygenated on all supports
[55]. Thus, the synergestic effect of an oxide support and metallic crystallite catalyst gives rise to catalytic
systems performing well in both hydrogenation and deoxygenation [55, 48]. Moreover, the acidity of the
support is crucial as a very acidic support might lead to coke formation in some cases [54, 34].

2.5
Deactivation of HDO catalysts
Catalyst deactivation is one of the major issues regarding bio-oil HDO due to the presence of both water,
trace elements and reactive organic compounds [23].

a depletion of the sulphur [24]. Mortensen et al.[24] tested if a sulphur concentration representative

was a mixture of phenol and 1-octanol with an addition of 0.3 vol% of 1-octanethiol to the feed. This
corresponded to 280 ppm of sulphur when a complete decomposition of the feed sulphur compounds was
assumed [24]. As seen in Figure 18a, both the conversion and extent of DOD decreased over time due to
sulphur depletion of the catalyst. However, this could be somewhat avoided by adding 2 vol% dimethyl
disulphide (DMDS) in the feed as shown in Figure 18b.

2.5.2
Poisoning
A poison in catalysis is defined as a substance which adsorbs on the active catalyst sites where it either
blocks the site entirely or competes with reactants [56]. In HDO of bio-oil, common poisons include
alkali metals, halogens [24], sulphur [57] and water [24, 53, 56]. As water is often present in bio-oil
[26] and a common by-product of reaction in HDO of bio-oil [58], catalysts must be resistant to water.
Hence, Mortensen et al.[24] also studied the resistance of water. Ratios of H2O/H2S of 46, 16 and 9.7
were obtained by adjusting the feed flow rate of oil and DMDS. This corresponded to water contents of
20 and 33 vol%, similar to that found in bio-oil [26]. The DOD had a relative decrease of 74% for the
highest H2O/H2S ratio of 46 while it lowered to 45% and 36% for a ratio of 16 and 9.7, respectively.

2.5.3
Coking and fouling
As reactants in bio-oil are prone to polymerize and polycondensate, they coke. This physical deposition
of chemical species lead to fouling.
Mainly phenols and furans are known to be predominant coke
precursors while alkenes, aromatics and heterocyclics are more prone to coke formation due to a strong
catalyst surface interaction [56]. Furthermore, compounds containing two O-atoms have been reported
to promote coke formation on acidic supports to a larger extent than single O-atom compounds [36].
For instance, guaiacol with two oxygen atoms forms doubly anchored phenates when adsorbed on an
alumina support. This means that the phenate from guaiacol bonds to two Al atoms. Opposed to this,
phenol and anisole form only monoanchored phenates on the same support [59]. Gao et al.[60] found
coking to be the major cause of deactivation on Ru/C and Pt/C catalysts during HDO of guaiacol.
The conversion of guaiacol and the results of the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of the catalyst are
shown in Figure 21.
Evidently, the conversion dropped over time.
Regarding the TGA, the weight
loss was low at low temperatures where weakly bonded compounds desorbed from the catalyst.
At
higher temperatures, strongly adsorbed condensed products desorbed leading to a larger weight loss.
Furthermore, condensed-ring products were more prominent on Ru/C leading to a more steep decrease
in weight loss at high temperatures on this catalyst [60]. Acidic supports have been found to induce
coke formation and Brønsted acid sites may supply protons to form carbonium cations acting as coking
precursors [34, 61] Lastly, high H2 pressure, preheating the hydrogen and low temperature inhibits coke
formation [56, 22].

2.5.4
Sintering
Generally, sintering prevails when the temperature is high as this leads to aggregation of metal particles.
Accordingly, the catalyst support may disrupt and the surface area of the catalyst decreases. This causes
irreversible phase transformations of the catalyst and lowers the active surface area [62]. Sintering of
Ni-catalysts is promoted by a smaller particle diameter, a high concentration of single metal atoms while
a high surface area of the support prevents it [63]. However, in a HDO study, sintering been found to
not be a major cause of deactivation [60].
2.5.5
Mechanical alterations
Catalysts can be mechanically altered by both crushing, attrition, abrasion and erosion. It is physical
phenomena causing an irreversible loss of active phase on the catalyst. Commonly, erosion and abrasion
occur in fluidized bed reactors where interparticle collisions and bed-to-wall impacts cause a gradual
wearing of the catalyst [64, 65]. Attrition can vary from pure abrasion where only small dusty particles
chip off the catalyst to total fragmentation where particles break into similarly sized fragments [65].
Pham et al.[66] looked into the attrition resistance of four commercial supports for iron catalysts used
in Fischer-Tropsch synthesis. The supports were suspended in a Durasyn-164 medium in a stirred tank
slurry reactor and tested at a stirring rate of 1200 rpm at 260◦C and 15 bar of N2 for 0, 8, 24 and
168 hours. For an alumina and a silica support, the results are presented in Figure 22. As observed,
no significant fracture appeared for the alumina support. Yet, particles of 1-10 µm appeared after 168
h on stream for the alumina support. This indicates that the support had been subject to abrasion.
Opposed to this, the Davison 952 silica support showed both fracture and abrasion as the particle size
distribution became increasingly bi-modular in nature [66]. Thus, appropriate choice of support may

2.5.6
Leaching
Leaching refers to the loss of active sites due to metal particles leaching into the liquid medium. This
occurs when the surface of the catalyst reacts with gas or liquid to form liquid molecules causing an
irreversible loss of catalyst. In some studies on HDO of bio-oil, leaching has been found to be negligible
[67, 68]. However, in another study, Ni was prone to leach in the presence of carboxylic acids while an
alumina support leached to a minor extent [69]. Thus, the degree of leaching depends on the composition
of the bio-oil with a higher acidity resulting in more leaching [69].
2.5.7
Regeneration of catalyst
Fortunately, some types of deactivation are reversible meaning that the catalyst can be regenerated. For
instance, Mortensen et al.[24] found that removing chlorine from the feed led to a slow regeneration of
the catalyst. Similarly, catalysts deactivated due to coking can be regenerated by burning off the coke
deposits. If this is done to sulphided catalysts, oxidization of the active phase doing burning requires
an additional sulphidation step. This step can also regenerate sulphided catalysts deactivated by water.
For instance, a CoMoS/Al2O3 catalyst deactivated by water was regenerated in this manner [70]. The
sulphidation step was done by impregnating the catalyst with 5.8 wt% DMDS in toluene at 4 MPa at
623 K for 2 hours. After resulphidation, complete regeneration of the catalyst was obtained for the
promoted CoMoS catalyst. In comparison, an unpromoted MoS catalyst was still severely deactivated
after resulphidation [70].
Thus, deactivation due to coking and water poison can in some cases be
overcome by regeneration of the catalyst while other deactivation mechanisms are irreversible [19].

2.6.1
Residence time
For HDO processes, residence time is defined as the contact time of the feedstock with the catalyst.
Lan et al.[49] found that lowering the residence time by increasing WHSV led to a lower conversion of
furfural on phosphide catalysts. The same conclusion has been drawn for noble metal catalysts [71]. In
addition, a long residence time for sulphided catalysts lowers the risk of sulphur present in the upgraded
product [19, 72]. In conclusion, a large residence time has often found to be favoured and is obtained in
continuous operation by lowering WHSV.
2.6.2
Hydrogen pressure
Commonly, hydrogen pressures between 1-200 bar [73, 47] have been employed in the evaluation of HDO
on bio-oil. From a thermodynamic point of view, H2 pressure plays a significant role as it pushes the
equilibrium of HYD towards hydrogenated products which increases the H/C ratio and improves the
HHV of the upgraded bio-oil favourably [32, 19]. In a study on HDO of phenol on a Zn/SiO2-catalyst,
Pourzolfaghar et al.[74] tested the influence of hydrogen flow in a fixed-bed reactor operating at 500◦C
with WHSV= 0.32 h−1. Notably, an increased H2-flow from 0 mL/min to 150 mL/min increased the
conversion of phenol while further enhancement up to 900 mL/min did not increase the conversion,
probably due to catalyst saturation [74]. The increase in conversion from 0 to 150 mL/min in H2-flow
could very well be due to hydrogen deficits and mass transport limitations. From Henry’s law (Eq.(6)),
it is known that the amount of an ideal gas dissolved in the liquid is proportional to the partial pressure
of the gas at equilibrium [75].

2.6.3
Temperature
Typical temperatures in the HDO of model compounds lie within the range of 200-350◦C. Additionally,
when the upgrading is done in a dual-stage configuration, initial stabilization occurs at temperatures
of 100 −250◦C[45]. Ternan et al.[76] found that the coke formation on HDS catalysts increased for
elevated temperatures with a steep increase above 450◦C. Thus, temperatures employed in HDO should
not exceed 450◦C in order to avoid coke formation. Yet, a temperature too low would result in a slow
reaction due to kinetic limitations and an optimum temperature must be determined for a given system.
2.7
Stabilization of bio-oil
2.7.1
Single-stage HDO
When performing HDO in a single-step on real bio-oil, higher temperatures of 300 −400◦C are often
employed. However, operating at this high temperature induces coke formation, especially in the heat-up
zone in fixed beds [56, 33]. To test if the HDO can be performed at lower temperatures, Yin et al.[77]
tested a novel Ni-Cu/SiO2-ZrO2 based catalyst on the HDO of fast pyrolysis liquids in both a batch and
three fixed-beds in series. For both set-ups, van Krevelen plots were generated as shown in Figure 23a.
For the batch set-up, reactive species were converted to alcohols at temperatures below 250◦C. This
is highlighted by an increase in the H/C ratio and a somewhat constant O/C ratio. The same trend
was observed for the continuous set-up, however only up to 180◦C. For reaction temperatures between
200−350◦C, dehydration occurred. This is evidenced by a decrease in the O/C ratio and a slight decrease
in the H/C ratios. Additionally, the carbonyl number (CAN) presented in Figure 23b was observed to
decrease significantly for temperatures below 180◦C. This highlights that reactive species are stabilized
at the low temperatures while complete HDO occurs at higher temperatures [77].

2.7.2
Dual-stage HDO
Overcoming the issues of coke formation due to reactive species in bio-oil might be done by implementing
a dual-stage HDO. The main aim of the first step is to increase the H/C ratio by converting bio-oil into
monomeric phenolic compounds and low molecular weight lignin derivates.
These are less reactive.
This is done at low temperature between 100 −250◦C where carbonyl and carboxyl functional groups
are converted into alcohols. Then, the products of the first step are further converted into aromatic
hydrocarbons and naphthenes at more severe conditions [45]. For the second stage, temperatures between
250 −400◦C facilitate deep oxygen removal and cracking of large oligomeric molecules [22, 78].
In the first step, most common catalysts include Pt, Ru and Pd, while for the following deoxygenation,
common catalysts cover Ru, Ni and sulfided CoMo [22, 79]. Elliot et al.[80] performed HDO of bio-
oil derived from eucalyptus and poplar. In this, two fixed-bed catalyst beds were run in a down-flow
configuration as shown schematically in Figure 24. The catalysts employed were presulfided NiMo/Al2O3
and CoMo/MgAl2O4.

evidenced by a low H2 uptake leading to exothermic pyrolysis condensation reactions and coke formation.

2.8
Reactor designs in HDO
2.8.1
Fixed and ebullated beds
Since the first wood liquefaction technique arose in 1979 [22], heterogeneous catalytic upgrading has been
tested in batch reactors and continuous-flow reactors. Most common continuous flow reactors cover fixed
bed and ebullated bed reactors as shown schematically in Figure 26. In recent years, continuous slurry
reactors have gained attention, too [84, 85, 86]. Regarding the fixed bed HYD process, this is a relatively
mature technology. As shown schematically in Figure 26a, hydrogen gas and bio-oil is sent in at the top
of the reactor in a co-current configuration and distributed well in the gas-liquid distributor. Impurities
and solids are trapped in the fouling basket and temperature is adjusted by sending in cold hydrogen
gas. Evidently, the fixed-bed is simple to operate and has a low investment cost, however, it is prone to
coking. This reduces the operating cycle of fixed bed reactors in HDO to only about 12-15 months [45].

Opposed to the fixed bed configuration, the catalyst is moving in the ebullated bed. Furthermore, gas and
liquid is sent in at the bottom of the reactor. After being well distributed at the gas-liquid distributor,
the upstream flow mixes catalyst, gas and solids in the dense layer. Above the dense layer, the catalyst
is dragged down back to the dense layer and liquid can leave through the overflow outlet. An effective
separation of both gas, liquid and solid is key as it affects both the efficiency of heat and mass transfer,
sizing of equipment and difficulties of operation. Supplementary catalyst must often be added during
operation due to an incomplete separation of catalyst and liquid. This loss of catalyst increases the
operation cost. Additionally, loading and unloading both liquid and catalyst under high pressure and
high temperature conditions demand a series of valves, pumps and containers which increases the cost
of ebullated bed reactors. Thus, ebullated beds have a low utilization and furthermore a poor quality
of the product. Therefore, ebullated bed reactors are not that common in studies of HDO of bio-oil.
Compared to the fixed bed, ebullated beds enable replacement of deactivated catalyst without shutting
down and a more efficient heat transfer which avoids undesired hot spots within the reactor. However,
a higher wear on equipment is connected to ebullated beds due to the collisions of particles [45]. Thus,
research in alternative reactor designs is of great importance.
2.8.2
Batch and continuous slurry reactors
On a lab scale, many studies utilize a batch reactor in the HDO of bio-oil as it allows for a fast and
simple operation. A conceptual design of a continuously stirred reactor able to run both in batch and
continuous mode is shown schematically in Figure 27. During batch operation, bio-oil and catalyst are
added to the reactor prior to operation. During operation, inlets and outlets are closed. A stirrer ensures

a homogeneous mixture and the reactor temperature is controlled by interior thermocouples and adjusted
by exterior heating elements. After running, gases are collected through a gas outlet in the top and the
liquid is separated from catalyst particles. Opposed to a batch, feed is added and products are subtracted
continuously in a continuous flow reactor. The catalyst can be sent in either with liquid the feed as a
catalyst precursor [84] or it can be added to the reactor prior to operation. While the batch is a transient
reactor, the continuous flow reactor operates at steady state after start-up. As noted in Table 3, coke
formation is common in batch reactors. This is due to a high concentration of unconverted reactive
species combined with a long residence time which allows for secondary reactions to occur. Opposed
to that, feed is instantly diluted with partly converted species in continuous operation at steady state.
As the partly converted species are less reactive, secondary reactions should be less prominent in this
configuration compared to batch. Moreover, continuous operation is favoured on an industrial scale.

Comparing a continuous slurry reactor with a fixed bed, several differences are worth mentioning. In
a fixed bed, mass and heat transfer limitations may lead to hot spots within the reactor [35]. This is

in slurry reactors similar to that observed in studies on the Fischer-Tropsch process [66]. In fixed bed
reactors, deactivated catalyst cannot be replaced without shutdown [45]. This can be done in slurry
reactors when the catalyst is introduced as a catalyst precursor [84]. Diffusion of feed and a pressure
drop over the reactor are other common issues in fixed bed reactors which can be overcome in a slurry
reactor.

2.9
Summary
Biomass constituting cellulose, hemi-cellulose and lignin is a feasible and alternative source of carbon
in a world where fossil fuels have for a long time dominated the market [2]. By utilizing fast pyrolysis,
biomass is converted to a liquid product with a wide composition range [19]. It has a high water content,
a low H/C ratio and a high O/C ratio which lowers the HHV of bio-oil undesirably. Furthermore, it is
acidic in nature and unstable due to an increasing viscosity over time [26]. Collectively, these factors
render bio-oil unsuitable for direct use as a transportation fuel. However, bio-oil has the potential to
become a drop in fuel after upgrading. Several physical and chemical upgrading methods exist today
with HDO as a promising method. Herein, bio-oil reacts with hydrogen gas and several catalysts have
been found to promote the reaction in literature. Studies on model compounds simulating real bio-oil
has been carried out on both sulphided [43, 44], noble metal [36, 46, 47, 48] and alternative catalysts
with different supports [48]. Generally, a synergetic effect of an oxide support and a metallic crystallite
catalyst gives rise to catalytic systems performing well in HDO [51]. Yet, each catalytic system has its
own limitations as several mechanisms of deactivation are common in HDO of bio-oil. Namely, coking
due to secondary reactions of reactive compounds in bio-oil and poisons in the feed hinder an efficient and
stable operation of HDO of bio-oil [19]. Thus, choosing the right operating conditions as well as catalyst
is crucial. It has been found that a long residence time [49], hydrogen in surplus [74] and a temperature
below 350◦C [76] all lower the extent of coking while improving the HDO. In recent years, HDO has
been performed in a two-stage configuration with mild conditions in the first step stabilizing reactive
oxygenates and severe conditions in the second step resulting in full deoxygenation [80, 33, 83]. This is
another attempt of overcoming the issue with coking and has found to be a promising route. Present
studies in HDO of bio-oil largely utilize fixed-bed and batch reactors as these allow for an efficient and
simple configuration, however fixed-bed and batch reactors are prone to coke [45, 22]. This feature along
with others limit the up-scaling of these reactor designs. Therefore, recent studies now employ continuous
slurry reactors and these have shown a promising performance in HDO of bio-oil [88, 84, 85, 86, 89].
However, the influence of operating conditions and suppression of coke formation in continuous slurry
reactors are not well understood so further studies must be conducted within this scope.

3.2.1
Experimental procedure
3.2.1.1
Preparation
In preliminary experiments conducted before this project, 5 g of fresh catalyst had been tested and led to
plugging of the dip tube filter and complete conversion of guaiacol. Hence, it was decided to utilize only
1 g of catalyst in most of the experiments to lower the extent of crushing. To investigate the influence
of catalyst load, an experiment was also run with 2 g of catalyst as shown in Table 10. In a further
attempt to avoid plugging of the dip tube filter, the catalyst was grinded and sieved to obtain particles
within a size range of 250-425 µm. This was expected to hinder erosion of the catalyst during run time
due to the lower particle mass. For each experiment, the mass of catalyst was measured on a Mettler
Toledo balance with a precision of 0.01 mg and noted as stated in Table 10. After measuring the desired
amount, the catalyst was transferred to the reactor. No pre-reduction of catalyst was carried out.
Regarding the liquid feed, two mixtures of model compounds were explored, namely a mixture consisting
of 10% guaiacol (Gua) and 90% octanol (Oct) and a mixture consisting of 10% octanoic acid (OcAc),
40% guaiacol and 50% furfural (Fur) as indicated in Table 10. In the following, the two-component
mixture will be denoted Gua/Oct while the three-component mixture will be denoted Fur/Gua/OcAc.
No solvent was added to these mixtures. These were chosen as they have been employed in previous
work conducted at CHEC. Furthermore, the resistance of the catalyst towards sulphur was tested by
adding either 1-dodecanethiol or thiophene as a source of sulphur with concentrations stated in Table 10.
For a desired concentration of sulphur, the appertaining mass of 1-dodecanethiol and thiophene were
calculated as shown later on. Before closing the reactor, liquid feed was pumped through the tubing
until drops appeared at the reactor inlet tube in a glass beaker placed beneath. Furthermore, liquid feed

3.2.1.2
Execution
Closing the reactor was done by tightening the screws with a torque wrench.
After this, an inert
atmosphere within the reactor was achieved by closing all outlets of the reactor while flowing 1000
NmL/min of N2 gas in the reactor. Once the pressure in the reactor had reached 6-10 bar, the outlet
valve was opened, bypassing the BPR until P = 1 bar. This was repeated five times. Subsequently, the
reactor was flushed with hydrogen in the same manner with two repetitions reaching 6-10 bar and a final
flush at a pressure of 15 bar. Then, the desired operation pressure of 100 bar was set on the BPR and
the reactor was left to pressurize with hydrogen.
Once the desired pressure had been reached, the reactor was filled with liquid at a rate of 10 mL/min.
When the first drop of liquid appeared at the outlet of SP1, the flow rate of liquid was decreased to
the desired operation flow rate, Qf = 0.5 mL/min. The stirrer was started at 300 rpm and the heating
jacket was mounted. In order to heat up the reactor and avoid overshooting, a temperature 10◦C lower
than the desired operating temperature was set and introduced with a ramp of 5◦C/min. When that
set-point had been reached in the reactor, the desired operating temperature was entered as the new
set-point with a ramp of 2◦C/min.
When the temperature had stabilised, the reaction was proceeding and left to continue for at least 6
hours before introducing any changes. During this, liquid samples were taken at the outlet of SP1 and
stored in brown sealed glass containers for later analysis. Before introducing any changes to the system,
a gas sample was taken in a 3 L multilayer foil gas sampling bag from Restek with a polypropylene combo
valve and septum. For experiments conducted at different temperatures, an increase in temperature was
introduced as described for the initial heating of the reactor. A decrease in temperature was introduced
as a step change in set-point. For experiments conducted with sulphur containing feed, the feed was
changed while one of the pumps was emptying hereby making sure that no air was pumped into the
system. It was then noted when the pump started pumping the new feed into the system. Every time a
change had been introduced, the system was allowed to reach steady state and liquid samples were taken
frequently to track the ongoing reaction.

3.2.2.1
Choice of hydrogen pressure and flow
As noted in the section ”HDO reaction pathways”, a variety of products were expected for the given
conditions. Hence, it was desired to estimate the minimum hydrogen flow to obtain stable products.
In these calculations, it was assumed that products were formed via the route that demanded the most
hydrogen. This was to ensure a determination of the maximum hydrogen needed, i.e. the most critical
case. Furthermore, experiments were conducted in temperature ranges where hydrogenation is favoured
[69]. The following example was based on guaiacol in the Gua/Oct mixture as this was the most utilized
feed mixture. Similar calculations were carried out for furfural, guaiacol, octanoic acid and octanol,
see Appendix A.4. Regarding guaiacol, this can form catechol which demands 1 mole of hydrogen and
further reaction to form phenol demands an additional mole of hydrogen. Thus, 2 moles of hydrogen is
required to form phenol via the DME two-step route. Phenol can then methylate to form cresol with
no hydrogen consumption. Otherwise, phenol can form benzene or cyclohexanone. The formation of
benzene demands one mole of hydrogen while three moles of hydrogen are needed for cyclohexanone.
This sums up to 3 moles for benzene and 5 moles for cyclohexanone.
The cyclohexanone can then
further hydrogenate to form cyclohexanol, dehydrate into cyclohexene and then hydrogenate to yield
cyclohexane. Cyclohexanol thus demands a total of 6 moles of hydrogen while further hydrogenation
into cyclohexane demands another mole. This sums up to 7 moles of hydrogen needed for cyclohexane.
Lastly, the cresol can dehydrate to form toluene, which also demands one mole of hydrogen. As a result,
2 moles of hydrogen is required for the formation of cresol. Overall, this sums up the stoichiometric H2

3.3
Characterization and analysis
3.3.1
Liquid samples
3.3.1.1
GC/MS-FID
To determine the products of reaction and conversion of reactants, liquid samples were run in a gas chro-
matography/mass spectrometry-flame ionization detection (GC/MS-FID) analysis. It works by injecting
the sample into a liner of 200-350◦C where the sample instantly vaporises. Carried by a He carrier gas,
the gaseous sample is split and a fraction of the sample determined by the split ratio is passed through
the column. For most runs, a split ratio of 20.5 was utilized. Yet, for the last two analyses, the split
ratio was increased to 30 due to column saturation issues. In principle, this corresponds to a dilution of
the sample. This column separates the compounds depending on their boiling point and affinity towards
the column material. After the column, a part of the sample is sent to the FID while the other part goes
to the MS. In the MS, the total ion current is detected for all compounds and plotted over time and
compared to a NIST data base to identify compounds. In the FID, the sample is burned in a hydrogen
flame and the conductivity of the gas is measured. This allows for a quantification of the hydrocarbons
in the sample as burning of these generates ions that cause a change in the conductivity of the gas.
This has the advantage that it detects organic compounds and is resistant to small fluctuations in the
gas flow. Thus, MS allows for an identification of compounds while the FID allows for a quantification.
GC/MS-FID analyses for the liquids were performed on the GC/MS-FID System model 7890B provided
by Agilent Technologies. In this equipment, four columns were present. The first two columns were from
Agilent Technologies both of type HP-5MS 19091S-431 with dimensions 15 m x 0.25 mm x 0.25 µm and
a temperature range of −60 to 350◦C. It should be noted that this column has a low polarity and is not
ideal for the given samples due to their relatively high polarity. The third column was connected to the
MS detector with dimensions 1.86 m x 150 µm x 0 µm and a temperature range of 0 to 450◦C. Lastly,
the fourth column was connected to the FID detector with dimensions 0.34 m x 150 µm x 0 µm and a
temperature range of −60 to 225◦C. Note that these last two columns did not have a stationary phase
meaning no separation of products occurred herein.

For a selected few of the bi-phasic liquid samples, volumetric one-component Karl Fischer titration
was carried out on both the aqueous and oil phase to determine the water content in both phases.

This was carried out on an Eco KF Titrator from Metrohm shown in Figure 36. Since this was one-
component titration, the titrating agent named Hydranal Composite 5 from Honeywell Fluka contained
all the chemicals needed for the Karl Fischer titration. Namely, the reactive alcohol (diethylene glycol
monoethyl ether (DEGEE)), the base (2-(dimethylamino)pyridine), SO2 and I2 [97]. During titration,

inside the cup reached 100 mL, liquid was pumped to the waste beaker (5) and new solvent was pumped
in. The given solvent was Hydranal Medium K bought from Honeywell Fluka. For each sample, at least
five repetitions were carried out on each phase. Once all measurements were done, the cup was emptied
and the instrument was switched off.

3.3.2
Gas samples
3.3.2.1
GC-TCD/FID
Gas samples were analysed in a gas chromatography-thermal conductivity detector/flame ionization
detector (GC-TCD/FID) system provided by Thermo Scientific model Trace 1300. Herein, the gas from
the sample is passed through three channels of which the first is a thermal conductivity detector (TCD)
detecting H2, O2 and CO with N2 as carrier gas. In the second channel, a TCD detecting CO, CO2,
N2 and O2 is present, while He is the carrier gas. This change in carrier gas is important as the TCD
measures differences in thermal conductivity due to the presence of a sample in the carrier gas. Thus,
the carrier gas must not be too similar to the gas sample. In the third channel, a FID with a sensitivity
of 10 ppm detects hydrocarbons. For higher hydrocarbons, the FID is more sensitive and may detect
peaks as low as 1 ppm with a good resolution depending on the given hydrocarbon. For the TCD, the
sensitivity is 200 ppm for traces and 500 ppm for an adequate peak.
Samples were collected during experiments in sample gas bags which were then connected to the GC-
TCD/FID equipment via a plastic tube. A desired gas flow of approximate 10 mL/min was achieved by
adding a load on top of the gas bag and turning a valve to the right position, ensuring that the inlet flow
was connected to the gas bag. Post analysis, each gas bag was flushed five times with in-house nitrogen
and emptied in between each flush by pumping out the nitrogen.

Spent catalyst
Regarding the carbon deposition, any increase in mass among the fresh and spent catalyst was used as

transferring the sample to the sample cup, a polypropylene X-ray film circle from Fluxana was pressed
into the XRF sample cup, making sure it was completely stretched out. Then, the mass of solid sample
was measured and the sample was transferred to the sample cup before closing the lid. It was subsequently
placed on a magnetic disc and transferred to the XRF equipment delivered by Bruker, type S2 Puma.
The measurements for the sampling cup, film circle and sample were entered in the computer connected
to the XRF equipment before running the analysis. As stated in the manual of the equipment, a proper
height of sample is required for a satisfactory analysis. E.g. for a silver anode emitting 22.16 keV, the
appropriate height of a SiO2 sample very similar to Al2O3 is 1.6 cm, corresponding to a volume of sample
of approximately 5 cm3. As the Ni/Al2O3 catalyst has a bulk density of 1.03 g/cm3, the mass required for
a proper analysis is about 5.2 g. However, all of the experiments were conducted with 1-2 g of catalyst.
Hence, XRF was run on the fresh catalyst with mass of 0.5, 1, 2, 3 and 4 g to test whether a low mass
influenced the results. Since the sample cup did not allow a volume bigger than that corresponding to 4
g of catalyst, this was the maximum mass tested.

Results and discussion
4.1
Tuning of continuous slurry set-up
In the first month of experimental work, tuning of the set-up was performed. Issues with an internal gas
leakage, unstable outlet flows and catalyst erosion had arisen in preliminary experiments. Consequently,
those issues must be solved before catalytic testing could be carried out.
4.1.1
Internal gas leakage
In the preliminary experiments, liquid outlet flows were unstable after some time on stream. Firstly, it
was assumed that this occurred due to an internal gas leakage at the fastening of the dip tube as no
external gas leakage was detected. Therefore, temperature measurements were conducted with the aim
of sealing the dip tube fastening with the most appropriate type of sealing. As the reactor was closed
tightly during operation, a thermocouple was mounted on the exterior of the reactor close to the dip
tube fastening as shown schematically in Figure 39. From this thermocouple, the temperature was noted
manually. In this experiment, 50 NmL/min of N2 passed through the reactor while no catalyst and
liquid was introduced. The reactor pressure was 1 bar and no stirring was implemented. An increase in
temperature was not introduced before the reactor temperature had stabilized.
Figure 39: Schematic illustration of the reactor with a thermocouple mounted close to the dip tube fastening
during prelimenary temperature measurements.

and hence, a ramp was introduced instead for later temperature increments. Furthermore, at 220◦C, the
reactor temperature was below the set-point as the heating jacket had reached its maximum temperature.
This switched on the alarm and turned off the heating supply automatically. All of this is observed in
the temperature profiles presented in Figure 40, see Appendix A.6 for the Matlab script used to obtain
the plot shown in Figure 40. Generally, the reactor temperature took a long time to stabilize entirely

4.1.3
Catalyst erosion
The cause of the filter blockage was assumed to be catalyst erosion during stirring.
In preliminary
experiments, catalyst extrudates had been used and after running, a dry powder with a colour very
similar to that of the catalyst was observed. This powder was cleaned with acetone and sieved. This
revealed a broad particle size distribution (PSD) not shown herein. In the following experiments, catalyst
was grinded to ensure a more homogenous particle size distribution and less erosion due to a smaller
mass of each particle. After grinding, catalyst particles were sieved, thus ensuring that only particles
within a range of 250-425 µm were employed.
4.2

Once the set-up had been tuned, proper catalytic tests were carried out. For the Gua/Oct mixture, the
influence of temperature, sulphur poisoning and two different catalysts were tested and compared to a
blank with support only. For the Fur/Gua/OcAc mixture, the influence of temperature and catalyst
loading were tested. During experiments, pressure and temperature data were logged continuously, see
Appendix A.9 for all runs conducted.

Common for all reactants is that increasing hydrogen flows lead to a larger loss of reactant. This is due
to a higher total molar flow and hence a more extensive flushing of vapours. Downstream the reactor,
vapours were condensed in SP1 and collected with the liquid products. Thus, vaporization of reactants
would lead to a higher reactant concentration in the products which lowers the conversion of reactants.
As these calculations were based on ideal gas behaviour and applied to a mixture at a high pressure,
the results should be regarded with caution. At these conditions, gases are known to behave non-ideally
with an enhanced interaction among gas molecules. Furthermore, all feed reactants in this study posses
functional groups able to form hydrogen bonding which would cause an intermolecular attraction. This
would lead to a lower vapour pressure and lower feed loss than predicted for ideal gases. Therefore, a
more detailed model such as Wilson’s equation or Non-Random Two-Liquid (NRTL) model should be
applied to account for this.

In literature, Mortensen et al.[104] tested the influence 0.3 vol% 1-octanethiol in the Gua/Oct mixture
feed in a continuous flow reactor. This corresponds to 500 ppmw of sulphur in the feed. Herein, a

conversion of zero after 12 h on stream [104]. In this study, the relative exposure to sulphur during the
entire experiment was 1.1 mol S/mol Ni, which is lower than the corresponding ratio of 3.3 utilized here.
This ratio was calculated by integrating Eq.(30) from t = 0 to t = 30 h and converting the concentration
to moles in order to divide by the Ni concentration of fresh catalyst determined by XRF, see Appendix
A.13 for determination of this ratio. Thus, the faster deactivation observed by Mortensen et. al.[104]
compared to the one observed here does not stem from a higher sulphur exposure to the accessible
nickel. Instead, it could be related to a higher temperature during operation of 250◦C compared to
150◦C utilized here. For a temperature range of 68 −100◦C, an increased temperature has been found
to increase the rate of sulphur poisoning on Ni catalysts [105]. Oppositely, on a feed mixture consisting

4.2.5.3
Solid products
Additionally, for the sulphuric experiments, the uptake of sulphur was determined by XRF with results
shown in Figure 58. In this, times measured on the x-axis denote the total sulphur exposure time for
each experiment. Furthermore, the sulphur concentration was measured for spent catalyst and divided
by the Ni content determined for the fresh catalyst. Clearly, operating with 1-DDT under identical
conditions but with a sulphur exposure time of 29.2 hours instead of 5.68 hours increased sulphur uptake
significantly. For 5.68 h of sulphur exposure, the S/Ni ratio was 0.052 mol/mol whereas for 29.2 h, it
was 0.18 mol/mol. However, despite the fivefold increase in exposure time, the sulphur uptake only
increased by a factor of about three, indicating that the sulphur uptake rate decreases over time as the
catalyst becomes saturated with sulphur. Comparing Run 4 and 5 with very similar TOS with sulphur

shows that thiophene did not lead to the same sulphur uptake. For the thiophene experiment, the S/Ni
ratio was only 0.060 mol/mol. In spite of this, the catalyst was equally poisoned in both cases as the
conversion was < 1% during and post sulphur exposure. Moreover, in Run 4, a total sulphur exposure
of 0.99 mole was introduced before the catalyst was completely deactivated. This occurred after 22 h on
stream with sulphur in the feed. For Run 5 done with thiophene, the catalyst was completely deactivated
after 20.5 h with sulphur feed. This corresponds to a total sulphur exposure of 0.85 mole. Based on the
XRF concentrations of sulphur measured for spent catalysts, the catalyst in Run 4 had a sulphur uptake
to sulphur exposure ratio of 0.10 mol/mol. For Run 5, the same ratio was 0.04 mol/mol, see Appendix
A.13 for calculations. Furthermore, the Ni and Al2O3 ratios for these two runs were rather identical as
discussed later. Though, it must be mentioned that the XRF results must be regarded with caution.
Especially considering that the XRF analysis revealed surprising results for the spent NiMoS/Al2O3
catalyst. Further studies with different techniques must therefore be carried out on the solid products
of reaction to verify the sulphur concentration stated here.
If the sulphur uptake determined by XRF holds, it suggests that the deactivation observed for thiophene
was not only attributed to the sulphur. It is proposed that the thiophene might bind to the catalyst
both by the S-atom and the aromatic ring. Actually, a magnetic study on the adsorption of thiophene,
carbonyl sulphide and carbon disulphide showed that thiophene could bind up to 9 Ni atoms on a 20
wt% Ni/SiO2 catalyst [108]. This suggests that the more severe deactivation observed for thiophene
could be due to a co-blocking by both sulphur and the aromatic ring. Yet, in the same study, a catalytic
gas phase hydrogenation of benzene at atmospheric pressure and 57◦C showed that thiophene produced
CnHx fragments that desorbed from the catalyst during reaction. Overall, the toxicity of the thiophene
was low at the tested conditions compared to carbonyl disulphide [108]. This suggests that the aromatic
ring in thiophene did not adsorb strongly enough on the catalyst to deactivate it severely for the tested
temperature of 57◦C. Yet, the hydrocarbon group of 1-DDT is known to be even less reactive than the
aromatic ring in thiophene as π electrons of the aromatic ring interact strongly with d-orbitals on surface
Ni atoms. At least, this adsorption of an aromatic ring has been observed for both toluene and phenol
at 140◦C on a Ni/Al2O3 catalyst [109].
To fully conclude the cause of the more severe deactivation observed for thiophene compared to 1-
DDT, additional analyses of the solid products must be carried out. One supplementary analysis to
be carried out is transmission electron microscopy (TEM). In this analysis, an electron source emits
electrons through a vacuum chamber. These electrons interact with an ultra-thin sample, where they
may be transmitted diffracted by its atomic structure. The transmitted electrons are then detected on
a detector and the generated image is magnified. Thus, TEM generates images at the atomic scale,
revealing the structural arrangement of atoms [110]. This would provide insight in the catalyst’s surface
structure and reveal how the sulphur is deposited in relation to the Ni atoms. To quantify both the

4.2.6.1
NiMoS/Al2O3: Liquid products
As the Ni/Al2O3 catalyst showed clear deactivation in the presence of both 1-dodecanethiol and thiophene
with sulphur concentrations simulating that of real bio-oil, it was deemed inadequate for HDO of real
bio-oil. Hence, a sulphided NiMoS/Al2O3 catalyst which demands a continuous feed of sulphur during
operation was tested on the Gua/Oct mixture. This was carried out in Run 6 with 1 g NiMoS/Al2O3
catalyst at higher operating temperatures of 180-270◦C. This temperature range was chosen as previous
work at CHEC found this catalyst to be inactive at lower temperatures. 1-dodecanethiol was the source
of sulphur in the feed with a concentration of 0.03 vol% corresponding to a sulphur concentration of 50
ppmw. For this catalyst, 1-dodecanethiol is assumed to decompose into H2S and dodecane which leaves a
vacancy on the catalyst. As shown in Figure 14, this vacancy is responsible for the binding of reactants.
The experiment was conducted by first heating up to 180◦C and reaching steady state before heating up
to the next operating temperature. Each temperature increase was done in a ramp change. Hence, the
conversion for higher temperatures initiated at the conversion equal to that at steady state for a prior
lower temperature, see Figure 60.
Interestingly, the steady state conversion of octanol was 21 ± 3.5% at 180◦C which is well above the
1.8 ± 2.9% observed on the Ni/Al2O3 at that temperature (Table 12).
Increasing the temperature
further led to improved conversion, see Table 13. The only exception was at 210◦C where the conversion
also fluctuated quite a bit at steady state as highlighted in Figure 59b. Hence, the unexpectedly low
conversion was once again deemed a result of the uncertainties on the GC/MS-FID and the appertaining
preparation of samples. For guaiacol, the steady state conversion at 180◦C for NiMoS/Al2O3 turned out
to be ≈17% which is much lower than 83 ± 1.3% observed on the Ni/Al2O3 catalyst. This indicates
that the Ni-catalyst was more active in hydrogenating guaiacol at lower temperatures compared to the
NiMoS/Al2O3. Among all samples at 210◦C, the conversion of both guaiacol and octanol were somewhat
similar to that at 180◦C indicating that the catalyst was not fully activated at these temperatures.
Opposed to that, a steep increase in the conversion was observed at 240◦C and even more prominent at
270◦C. Actually, increasing the temperature from 240◦C to 270◦C improved the conversion with 77%
for octanol and 51% for guaiacol. Thus, it can be concluded that the conversion improved with higher
temperatures in the tested temperature range for this NiMoS-catalyst, too.

would peak at the decomposition temperature of 280-290◦C, however, the peak at 200◦C was presumably
due to polymerization of the nickel propanoate at higher temperatures. Consequently, further dissolution
of nickel ions was inhibited for T > 200◦C. For T = 180◦C, they proposed that polymerization occurred
after only 5 hours of leach testing [69]. The findings by Pienaar et. al. [69] suggest that leaching of
nickel and polymerization of Ni(RCOO)x could very well be the cause of the black gel observed on the
dip tube. However, additional analyses must be carried out on the gel to verify this. A qualitative and
fast test for Ni could for instance be the dimethylglyoxime method. Herein, dimethylglyoxime reacts
with Ni(II) forming a complex with a distinguishable colour [116]. Due to this filter blocking, no gas or
liquid could exit the reactor which caused the pressure increase observed in Figure 68b. This pressure
increase was more prominent at 180◦C due to gas expansion.

considering that Pienaar et al. [69] found Ni to leach more in the presence of carboxylic acids while
alumina only leached to a minor extent. Generally, XRF is known to be a precise analysis with a high
degree of reproducibility, however, the accuracy is strongly influenced by sample preparation, calibration
of equipment and the composition of the sample [118]. It is thus of interest to validate the findings in
these XRF analyses by employing other techniques such as inductively coupled plasma optical emission
spectroscopy (ICP-OES). The principle behind this method is a bit different from XRF. In ICP-OES,
elements in the sample are ionized and the light emitted when the elements return to their ground
state is detected. The energy required for ionization is provided by an argon plasma operating at high
temperatures of 10,000 K [119]. In XRF, elements are ionized and excited by X-rays. When the electrons
de-excite and return to their ground state, characteristic X-rays are emitted and detected. The energy
of characteristic X-rays is specific for each shell transition and element and furthermore, the intensity is
related to the concentration of an element in the sample. For lighter elements, the X-ray flourescence
is lower than for heavier elements. Thus, XRF has a lower sensitivity for lighter elements with atomic

detectable within XRF. This could explain the unusual results observed for the NiMoS/Al2O3 catalyst.
Thus, verification with the more precise and accurate ICP-OES method is of interest as it has a lower
detection limit than XRF.

interval of 1 hour). Then, the number of moles of each specie was multiplied with either the number of
carbon or oxygen atoms it possessed. The same procedure was carried out for the feed composition with
concentrations determined in the GC/MS-FID. Lastly, the balance was set up according to Eq.(33).

for carbon would be open in this case. Again, the misalignment between the expected result and the
observed molar carbon balance must lie in uncertainties related to the determination of concentrations.

was expected since a prominent aqueous phase of essentially pure water appeared in the bottom of this
sample. Unfortunately, the total volume of the aqueous phase was never measured, hence the total moles
of oxygen present there cannot be included in the molar balance. Ideally, analyses of samples should be
repeated for runs in which the molar balances were outside the range of 90-105 mol% to fully validate
the conclusions drawn in this study.

Reproducibility
In the given study, each experiment was only conducted once. However, for the sulphur experiments,
testing conditions were identical prior to sulphur introduction. Thus, the reproducibility can be discussed
based on these. Namely, the conversion of guaiacol and gas composition for the given experiments show
decent agreement between the three runs as shown in Figure 84. At steady state prior to sulphur intro-
duction, the average conversion of guaiacol for the three runs was 55 ± 3.0%. The similar conversions
are displayed in Figure 84a. As discussed earlier, the yields and selectivities for these three experiments
showed the same pattern prior to sulphur introduction. Thus, the experiments are deemed reproducible
in regards to the liquid products which were the main focus of this study. For the gas composition, these
turned out to deviate a bit, however, as the gas yields were generally very low, even small differences
in concentration would result in large relative deviations. Overall, the experiments discussed here are
deemed reproducible. Since this was a preliminary study with the main aim of evaluating the perfor-
mance of the continuous slurry reactor set-up, achieving precise measurements with accurate values for
comparison was not the main focus. Hence, repeating the given experiments to achieve this was not a
main priority.

5
Conclusion and final remarks
In a world where fossil fuels dominate the market of transportation fuels with a large negative impact on
the environment, alternatives must be found. Bio-oil from lignocellulosic biomass constitutes a sustain-
able replacement. However, it has a high water content, a low H/C ratio and a high O/C ratio. These
factors lower the HHV of bio-oil undesirably. Furthermore, it is acidic in nature and unstable due to an
increasing viscosity over time [26] Hence, it must be upgraded. This can be done chemically by catalytic
hydrodeoxygenation (HDO). In literature, a great focus has been on the development on catalysts leading
to the discovery of promising catalytic systems. However, common studies mainly employ fixed-bed and
batch reactors of which both reactor types are prone to coke [45, 22]. Recently, advances have been made
to overcome these issues by performing HDO in a two-step configuration [80, 33, 83]. Not to mention,
alternative reactor designs have been utilized with an aspiring focus on continuous slurry reactors [88, 84,
85, 86, 89]. However, HDO in continuous slurry reactors is not well understood and additional research
must be conducted within this scope.
In this study, employing an innovative continuous slurry reactor on the HDO of model compounds led
to several interesting findings. Firstly, employing a 10 wt% guaiacol in octanol (Gua/Oct) feed mixture
showed that the conversion of guaiacol increased for increased temperature on a Ni/Al2O3 catalyst. A
maximum conversion of 83 ± 1.3% was found at 180◦C. Additionally, products of reaction revealed some
stabilization of the reactants. For octanol, no significant conversion was observed. Moreover, the Al2O3
support did not possess any notable catalytic activity.
Yet, the Ni/Al2O3 catalyst deactivated severely in the presence of 50 ppmw of sulphur provided by
both 1-dodecanethiol and thiophene. For 1-dodecanethiol, the conversion declined to zero after 22 h
with sulphur in the feed while a faster deactivation was observed for thiophene. Herein, an exposure to
sulphur of only 20.5 h eliminated the conversion entirely. XRF analysis of the spent catalyst revealed
a lower sulphur uptake in the case of thiophene, suggesting that sulphur was not the only cause of
deactivation. However, this must be verified by additional analyses to fully conclude the cause of the
different deactivation observed for 1-dodecanethiol compared to thiophene. As 50 ppmw of sulphur is
below the sulphur in real bio-oil [93], the Ni/Al2O3 was deemed unsuitable for the HDO of real bio-oil.
Luckily, a sulphided NiMoS/Al2O3 catalyst had high conversions at 270◦C of 75 ± 2.6% for guaiacol and
61±0.74% for octanol. Liquid products at 240 and 270◦C were bi-phasic in nature with no carbon loss in
the aqueous phase and a methane gas yield of 3.3 mol% at 270◦C. This was the largest gas yield among
all experiments. A wide variety of products appeared for this catalyst with a profound stabilization
prominent among the major compounds. Moreover, the NiMoS/Al2O3 catalyst had a stable operation
for 90 h on stream with 50 ppmw of sulphur in the feed. Thus, it constitutes a promising candidate for
the HDO of real bio-oil.

Since bio-oil is composed of several different molecules, a three component mixture (Fur/Gua/OcAc)
consisting of 50 wt% furfural, 40 wt% guaiacol and 10 wt% was employed, too. The Ni/Al2O3 catalyst
showed a promising catalytic activity towards furfural with a maximum conversion of 46 ± 3.0% at
150◦C for 1 g of catalyst. At the same temperature, doubling the catalyst load to 2 g increased the
furfural conversion to 75±2.2%. Thus, increasing the catalyst loading improved the conversion desirably.
Similarly, the conversion of octanoic acid at 150◦C increased from 11±4.9% to 18±3.4% when the catalyst
load was 2 g instead of 1 g. However, regardless of catalyst loading, issues with coking and plugging of
the outlet led to issues during operation for this feed mixture. These shortcomings must be overcome
before the HDO of real bio-oil can be evaluated for the continuous slurry set-up.
Post experiments, spent catalysts were measured and analysed by XRF. Mass changes revealed coke
deposition for the Fur/Gua/OcAc feed mixture with a maximum increase in mass of 7.6%.
Corre-
spondingly, sulphur experiments led to an increase in catalyst mass due to a sulphur uptake and coke
deposition of the catalyst. In general, particles retained their initial particle size distribution. Contrary
to expectations, XRF analyses of spent catalysts revealed leaching of alumina to be more prominent than
leaching of nickel. Especially for the Fur/Gua/OcAc mixture. Additional analyses must be carried out
to verify these surprising findings. At last, carbon and oxygen balances for all experiments fluctuated,
thus revealing that uncertainties were related to the determination of concentrations of both reactants
and products. Hence, analyses of samples from some of the experiments should be repeated to validate
the conclusions drawn herein.
In conclusion, utilization of an innovative continuous slurry set-up led to desirable stabilization of model
compounds representative for real bio-oil. However, improvements must be done to the set-up before
HDO of real bio-oil can be tested herein.

Future work
Evidently, upgrading real bio-oil in the continuous slurry set-up is the main aim for future work. However,
a few modifications must be made before this is achievable. Firstly, an internal standard for liquid samples
should be identified in order to minimize the influence of any fluctuations in the GC/MS-FID analysis.
This is not straightforward as an ideal internal standard must have a retention time in the GC different
from any of the products detected herein. For the experiment done with the NiMoS/Al2O3 catalyst, a
broad range of products were detected in the GC. Hence, zones with no peaks in the chromatogram from
this experiment should be determined and a compound with a retention time within this zone should be
employed in the feed for future experiments.
Since bio-oil contains sulphur [16] and stabilization of bio-oil can be done in a temperature range of
100-250◦C [45], the degree of sulphur poisoning should be tested at temperatures below 150◦C. From a
kinetic point of view, this is expected to lower the degree of deactivation for reduced Ni catalysts [105].
In such an experiment, thiophene should provide the sulphur. This would allow for a more representative
simulation of real bio-oil.
In this study, low loadings of 1-2 g of grinded catalyst with dp = 250−425 µm were employed to minimize
mechanical alterations of the catalyst. In a study conducted on the upgrading of real bio-oil with a similar
set-up, an LHSV of 0.5 h−1 was utilized [88]. In the present work, operation with a low load of 1 g of
catalyst and the Gua/Oct mixture corresponds to an LHSV of 26 h−1. Thus, much higher than the one
employed for real bio-oil in literature [88]. As model compounds allow for a more convenient operation
compared to real bio-oil, it is expected that testing real bio-oil demands a higher catalyst load to achieve
the high conversions obtained herein for model compounds. Hence, higher loadings of catalyst would be
interesting to test in order to investigate the resistance to mechanical alterations as well as improved
catalytic activity. Similarly, different catalytic systems should be tested to investigate both the attrition
resistance as well as the catalytic activity in the continuous slurry set-up.
Employing the Fur/Gua/OcAc feed mixture revealed limitations of the current reactor design. Namely,
plugging of the filter at the outlet dip tube. Since this filter ensures a solid-free exit for both liquid
and gas products, simply removing the filter would not be an ideal solution. Instead, refraining the
solid particles within the reactor in a porous retention unit would be ideal. This could have several
different designs, however, as the space within the reactor is limited, so are the available design options.
A proposed design for a catalyst retention unit is presented in Figure 85a. Herein, the idea is to have
the catalyst retained within columns made of porous walls that allow liquid to flow through. This would
hopefully eliminate the need for grinding of catalyst and the filter on the outlet dip tube which is sensitive
to plugging. In this manner, the catalyst becomes more fixed, so it should be taken into account that the
contact surface area between catalyst and liquid is lower than for a conventional slurry. However, this is

A.4
Hydrogen consumption calculations
Initially, the molar flow of a given species, Fi was calculated according to:
Fi = Qf · wi · ρi
Mi
(34)
with Qf denoting the volumetric feed flow rate, wi being the mass fraction of species i in the feed.
ρi and Mi denotes density and molecular mass of species, i, respectively. Subsequently, the hydrogen
consumption, FH2 was calculated by multiplying the stoichiometric coefficient of H2 needed to obtain the
given product with the molar flow of reactant i, Fi. The stoichiometric coefficient was determined based
on the most hydrogen consuming reaction pathway. Applying ideal gas law allowed for a translation of
the hydrogen consumption to a volumetric flow, QH2:
QH2 = FH2 · R · T
P
(35)
in which R is the gas constant equal to 8.3145
J
mol K while T = 273.15 and P = 101325 Pa, i.e. at normal
conditions. This led to the following results for the Gua/Oct mixture and the Fur/Gua/OcAc mixture.

Run 1 and 5 were the last ones analysed on the GC/MS-FID when the column was saturated. Hence, the
split ratio was increased to 30 for these two runs and they were analysed on the GC/MS-FID together.
Consequently, calibration curves for these runs have a smaller slope compared to the other ones.

Run 2 was the first run conducted. In this, pipetting of samples was not done correctly as the pipette
was pushed too hard. This led to calibration curves of lower accuracy than otherwise. However, as time
was limited and the same pipetting error occurred in all samples, the analysis was not repeated.
Figure A.2: Run 2, split ratio: 20.5

A.12
Sulphur concentration
General balance:
IN −OUT + PROD = ACC
(36)
For the given system, no sulphur is produced within the system. Hence, PROD = 0. For a time interval,
∆t, a total mass of sulphur enters the reactor:
IN = Cs,in · Qf · ρf · ∆t
(37)
In which Cs,in is the concentration of sulphur in the feed of 50 ppmw, Qf is the volumetric feed flow
rate and ρf is the density of the feed. Similarly, a total volume of sulphur leaves the reactor:
OUT = Cs(t) · Qf · ρf · ∆t
(38)
in which Cs(t) is a function of time. Initially, sulphur accumulates in the reactor which is described by
the difference in sulphur concentration at time t + ∆t and t:
ACC = V · ρf · (Cs(t + ∆t) −Cs(t))
(39)
As the difference between the sulphur concentration in the reactor and the feed becomes negligible, this
term eliminates. Setting up the balance gives:
Cs,in · Qf · ρf · ∆t −Cs(t) · Qf · ρf · ∆t = V · ρf · (Cs(t + ∆t) −Cs(t))
(40)
