Abstract
Autonomous landing poses a considerable challenge for unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs).
This task can generally be divided into two main components: landing zone identification
and the autonomous control of the drone’s landing process. Essentially, the drone must
autonomously search for a smooth and obstacle-free area and then navigate itself to land
safely within one of the identified candidate landing zones.
In this study, three distinct approaches have been implemented for landing zone identifica-
tion, each with its own set of advantages and disadvantages. All of these approaches have
demonstrated satisfactory accuracy when tested on both indoor and outdoor datasets,
while also exhibiting qualified processing speeds on embedded platforms. Additionally, a
straightforward control algorithm has been developed specifically for the DJI m100 drone.
This algorithm enables the drone to approach and land within the selected landing zone
during the landing process.
The overall system has undergone a number of field tests within an indoor test environ-
ment, confirming its feasibility and robustness. This work represents a significant step
forward in addressing the complex task of autonomous landing for UAVs.

Introduction
The increasing frequency and intensity of wildfires in Europe due to climate change have
prompted the need for the development of a drone solution enhanced with artificial intel-
ligence. This technology aims to provide firefighters with a more effective tool to combat
and manage wildfires, ultimately improving their chances of successfully extinguishing the
flames.
This work focuses on a specific aspect of firefighting drones, namely the development
of an autonomous landing system designed for emergency situations. The significance
of this system for firefighting drones lies in the challenging environments in which these
drones often operate. Firefighting drones are frequently deployed in extreme conditions,
such as above active flames. The potential consequences of a drone crash in such situ-
ations can be problematic. Retrieving a drone that has crashed in a forest, for example,
can be extremely difficult, and the crashed drone itself may pose a risk of starting a forest
fire. Hence, an effective autonomous landing system is crucial to ensure the safe and suc-
cessful operation of firefighting drones in these demanding scenarios. The autonomous
emergency landing system enables the drone to autonomously land without causing dam-
age to human properties on the ground or to the drone itself during emergency situations
when manual control of the drone may not be feasible or available.
In this work, a monocular camera is used for searching the feasible landing sites and the
landing system is able to give real-time commands to land the drone properly using the
information acquired from the monocular camera.In this paper, three different approaches
for landing zone searching and one straightforward control algorithm are elaborated. This
work also includes the implementation and result of a number of simple field tests.
1.1
Objectives
The main objectives of this work are:
1. Develop a landing zone searching system that can autonomously mark possible
landing areas near the drone during flight. The candidate landing area should be
large enough obstacle-free.
2. Developing an autonomous navigation system for DJI m100 quadcopter that can
perform autonomous landing on designated landing area.

3. Integrate the landing zone searching system and autonomous navigation system at
the onboard computer of drones
4. Evaluate the above-mentioned autonomous landing system in terms of speed and

5. Performing field tests for the above-mentioned autonomous landing system and en-

1.2
Limitations
This work presents a few limitations due to technical constraints and the practical condition
of test environment.
1. As an emergency landing system, this system does not consider conditions where
the locomotion of the drone or the condition of the vision module is severely affected
by system malfunction caused by emergency situation.
2. The field tests have only been performed at limited test sites.
3. Only the most light-weight landing zone detection algorithm has been tested in field
tests.
4. The system assumes there exists at least one suitable landing zone in the fields of
view of the onboard camera of the drone.
5. The system doesn’t consider moving obstacles outside the field of view of the down-
facing camera.
6. For larger aerial vehicles, the minimum effective altitude of the control system may
be higher than that in this work. The landing system will fail to give control com-
mands when the area below the drone in the field of view is not significantly larger
than the projection of the drone on the ground.
7. The control algorithm may be too simple to handle rigorous operation environments
or complicated locomotion for some drones.
Despite these limitations, the proposed system still presents a robust and effective method
for improving the flight safety of drones

2.1
State of the art
Currently, there are mainly four types of methods for vision-based drone landing, which
are marker-based methods, deep learning based methods, 3D mapping based methods
and traditional image processing methods.
2.1.1
Landing marker based methods
There exists extensive works about drone landing based on specific markers placed in
the operating environment of the drone(Xia, Mantegh, and Xie 2021; Vidal et al. 2017;
Zamanakos et al. 2020; S. Yang et al. 2014; Qi et al. 2020; Zamanakos et al. 2020). This
kind of method are usually robust and accurate. However, it can’t be guaranteed that the
markers placed in advance can always be reached at emergency situations. Among these
works, some articles(Xia, Mantegh, and Xie 2021; Zamanakos et al. 2020) mentioned
using pre-trained deep neural network to detect possible no landing zone, like person,
car or other drones. This technique may be adapted as a sub-system for this work.
2.1.2
Machine learning based methods
In Mukadam, Sinh, and Karani 2016, a support vector machine(SVM) is based on the color
and texture of the pixels in each frame is trained to determine whether an area is suitable
for mapping. Similiarly, Fraczek et al. Fraczek, Mora, and Kryjak 2018 utilized SVM and
decision tree to find suitable landing zones based on pixel colour, pixel neighbourhood
colour and pixel neighbourhood texture. In Fitzgerald, Walker, and Campbell 2005, a
CNN was trained to detect several type of classes, including water, grass, trees, buildings,
concrete and asphalt, based on pixels’ HSV colour and mean and median of the image
filtered by a Gabor filter set. In Bektash, Naundrup, and Cour-Harbo 2022, a feed forward
neural network is implemented to determine whether an place is suitable for landing as a
whole. In Castellano et al. 2020, CNN is implemented to detect human at the perspective
of drones. In Symeonidis et al. 2021, a CNN based network is developed trained to do

example, tree, people, grass and pavement, etc.
2.1.3
3D mapping based methods
In T. Yang et al. 2018, the drone is supposed to navigate to and land at a large and flat

IMU. The helicopter in this work will try to find a zone with suitable flatness, openness and
slope using the generated stereo map.
2.1.4
Traditional image processing methods
In Garcia-Pardo, Sukhatme, and Montgomery 2002. the normalized average of the nor-
malized edge-image histogram is chosen as a contrast descriptor to identify suitable land-
ing area in each captured frame. In Cesetti et al. 2010 the variation of feature-based
optical flow is used to determine the flatness of the area below the drone. This method is
computationally cheap and the algorithm is simple. However, this approach may fail for
areas lacking obvious features, for example, pavements. In Mejias and Fitzgerald 2013,
a simple algorithm based on Canny edge detector is presented. This approach aims to
find areas with no edge and suitable brightness for drone landing. This work achieved a
satsifying result with an significantly lighter computational burden than methods based on
clustering.
2.2
Decisions
The objectives of the project can be divided in two main parts. The first part is landing
zone searching. The drone should be able to search for an applicable landing zone within
the field of view of its sensors. To achieve this, a raspberry pi 4 is chosen as the onboard
computer and a standard raspberry pi camera with a pan-tilt system is chosen as the
primary sensor. In terms of software, a landing zone searching system with real-time
performance should be developed.
The second part is autonomous landing. An autonomous control system should be devel-
oped for DJI m100 drone to achieve autonomous landing. This system should be able to
generate velocity or position commands based on the output of the landing zone searching
system amd give those commands to the drone.

3.1
Hardware configuration
The aerial platform chosen for this project is the DJI MATRICE 100 (m100) drone, equipped
with the N1 flight controller. To serve as the onboard computer, a Raspberry Pi 4 is inte-
grated onto the drone. Communication between the Raspberry Pi and the MATRICE 100
is facilitated through serial communication protocols. The weight of the drone is around
2431g, the wheelbase of the drone is 650mm, the projection of the drone to the ground is
approximately a square with a side length of around 790mm.
For the task of autonomous landing, the primary sensor employed is a standard Rasp-
berry Pi module v1 camera. This camera is mounted on a pan-tilt platform and directly
connected to the Raspberry Pi 4. The pan-tilt platform is meticulously configured to con-
sistently maintain a downward-pointing orientation for the camera. This setup ensures
that the camera captures the necessary visual data for effective autonomous landing op-
erations. The resolution of the sensor of module v1 camera is 2592x1944 pixels, the
size of each pixel is 1.4µm x1.4 µm. The focal length for module v1 camera is 3.6mm.
Thus, the area within the field of view of the camera when the drone is operating can
be calculated using Area = Lx ∗Ly ,whereLx = 2592 ∗1.4 ∗10−6/(3.6 ∗10−3) ∗h,
Ly = 1944 ∗1.4 ∗10−6/(3.6 ∗10−3) ∗h and h stands for the altitude of the drone. For
example, when the drone is hovering at 10m, it can acquire visual vision from an area
of 10.8m*7.6m on the ground below it. The raspberry pi camera is configured to capture
frames at an resolution of 640x480 in this work.

4.1
Early attempts
4.1.1
Stereo Vision
At the beginning of this work, Zed-2 stereo camera is supposed to be used as the primary
sensor for landing zone detection. Stereo vision is a technique that involves using two
cameras positioned horizontally apart to capture 3D information. The disparity between
the positions of an object as seen by the two cameras is used to calculate the distance
from the camera pair to the object. The pair of cameras used for stereo vision is called
stereo camera. At the altitude of 7-10 meters or more, objects with a relatively low height
is too indistinct in the output of stereo vision(See the mat and small objects in figure 4.1).
As a result, in the rest parts of this work, a standard raspberry pi monocular camera is
used for the primary sensor for landing zone detection.
4.1.2
Optical flow
In Cesetti et al. 2010, the variation of feature based optical flow is used to determine the
smoothness of the candidate landing area. In this work, feature based optical flow is also
tested on a number of indoor samples.At the altitude of 7-10m, it is discovered that the
smooth area which may be candidate may contain little features for feature based optical
flow.(See figure 4.2)
Surprisingly, dense optical flow is proven to have unexpected results. As can be seen in
figure 4.3, it is obvious that the pixels of the same object is likely to have similar optical flow
values and directions. Although dense optical flow cannot provide enough information for
landing zone detection if it’s used alone, this technique may be useful for landing zone
detection if combined with other image processing methods.

Clustering
Clustering is the process of categorizing a set of objects in such a way that objects within
the same group exhibit greater similarity to each other compared to objects in different
groups.
k-means and graph-cut based on RGB values
Firstly, k-means clustering and graph-cut clustering based on RGB value of each pixel in

4.2
Introduction of optical flow
Optical flow refers to the perceived pattern of motion of objects in an image between two
successive frames, resulting from either the movement of objects within the scene or the
motion of the camera itself. It is typically represented as a 2D vector field, where each
vector represents a displacement vector, indicating the movement of points from the first
frame to the second frame.
Optical flow works on 2 assumptions:
• The pixel intensities of an object do not change between consecutive frames.
• Neighbouring pixels have similar motion.

change significantly, signifying that the clustering has stabilized.
At the first step, k centroids(k=2 in figure 4.7) are initialized with random coordinates. At
the second step, all points is divided into the 2 categories based on its distance to the
2 clusters. The points which are more close to one centroid than others are considered
belonging to this centroid. At the third step, each centroid is moved towards the average
of the points belonging to it. Then the second and third steps are repeated until max
iteration is reached or no apparent movement of centroid exists.

Landing Zone detection: The edge
detector based method.
The idea to search landing zone using edge detector is adapted from Mejias and Fitzgerald
2013. The principle of this method is finding smooth areas containing no edges and large
enough.The input images are initially segmented into several independent smooth areas
using an image processing pipeline. Subsequently, the pipeline identifies sufficiently large
areas within this segmented data that are suitable for potential landing sites.
5.1
Canny edge detector
In this work, Canny edge detector Canny 1986 is used for edge detection. Canny edge
detector is a multi-stage edge detection algorithm. In this chapter, the principle of Canny
edge detector will be explained.
Firstly, the input image needs to be blurred because Canny edge detector is sensitive to
noise in the input image. Then the blurred image will be filtered with a Sobel kernel in
both horizontal and vertical direction to get the gradient in horizontal and vertical direction
(Gx and Gy). The aptitude and direction of the gradient of each pixel will be calculated
using G =
√
G2x + G2y and θ = tan−1( Gy
Gx ).
After generated in last step, the gradient image will go through no-maximum suppression
process (figure 5.1). In this process, the redundant pixels which may not be a part of an
edge will be removed. To achieve this, each pixel is compared with it’s neighbours in the
direction of gradient and only the local maximum pixel is kept. For example, in figure 5.1,
pixel A would be compared with C and B. Pixel A’s value will be set to zero if it does not
constitute a local maximum within the group consisting of A, B, and C. The output of this
step will be a binary image with ”thin edges.”
Once the image with thin edges has been generated, an edge-filtering process is applied
using two threshold values, maxVal and minVal. Specifically, edges with a gradient value
lower than minVal are eliminated. Edges with a gradient value higher than maxVal are
retained without further processing. For edges with a gradient value falling between max-
Val and minVal, their connectivity to other portions of edges is evaluated. They will be

The image processing pipeline is presented in figure 5.2. Firstly, the input image is blurred

6.1
Analysis of problem and network selection
The problem of landing zone selection of can classified as semantic segmentation in deep
learning. Semantic segmentation is the problem of associating a label or category for
each pixel in an image. As an emergency landing system working on drones, real-time
performance and amount of parameters are the main consideration of choosing network.
As can be seen in figure 6.1, Fast-SCNN, ESPNet and ContexNet are more in line with
the requirements of this work. ESPNet is chosen for this project because there presents
sufficient reference materials about it on the Internet and it’s easy to implement on a
platform without GPU.
6.2
Introduction of ESPNet
ESPNet is a fast and efficient convolutional neural network used for real-time semantic
segmentation of high resolution images. The network is based on a novel convolutional
module called efficient spatial pyramid (ESP module). ESPNet is fast, small, low power,
and low latency, while it still presents a reasonable segmentation accuracy.

8.1
Strategy for landing site selection
The output of the landing zone searching system is a binary image indicating whether
each pixel is suitable for landing(see Figure 8.1). The goal of the landing zone selection
system is discover a continuous area that fits the size of the drone in the binary image.
Figure 8.1: Raw output of landing zone searching algorithm. (Edge detection method)
Pixel value 255 means suitable for landing, pixel value 0 means not suitable for landing.
The landing zone searching algorithm follows a series of steps to determine the optimal
landing site. Initially, the algorithm outputs undergo an erosion process, with the kernel
size dynamically determined by the drone’s current altitude. This erosion process results
Vision based autonomous emergency landing system for UAVs

==PAGE:42==
in a binary image where pixels with a value of 255 represent centroids of contiguous
square areas slightly larger than the minimum required for drone landing.
In the final step, the centroid closest to the center of the image is selected as the landing
target, and the corresponding square area becomes the designated landing site.
This method is applied to each cluster in the clustering approach, and each applicable
centroid from all four clusters undergoes the final step concurrently to form the ultimate
landing site selection. In the deep learning approach, a binary map is generated, where
areas representing suitable categories are set to 255, while other areas are set to 0. This
binary map serves as the input for the landing site selection algorithm.

Inter-module communication
In this work, inter-module communication refers to the data exchange between the land-
ing zone searching module and the off-board speed control module. Two inter-module
communication is presented in this work.
The first method employs socket communication, with the speed control module serving
as a server terminal and the landing zone searching module as a client terminal. These
two modules exchange data using the TCP protocol via the local address 127.0.0.1. This
approach allows the communication of two modules that may even be programmed in
different programming languages. For example, a combination of one C++ module and
one Python module can function seamlessly using this method.
The second method utilizes multi-thread programming in C++. In this approach, both
modules run in parallel threads within a single main function. Shared parameters, such
as altitude and target position, are communicated through the memory addresses of their
corresponding variables. This method is efficient but necessitates both modules to be
implemented in C++.

bine the edge detector-based landing zone searching module with the off-board speed

8.4
Strategy for landing process control
The overall structure of the autonomous landing process is presented in figure 8.4. Before
the drone takeoff, the onboard OSDK is initialized and the communication between drone
and the onboard computer is established. Besides, the altitude reading of the start point
is recorded. This is necessary in the testing phrase of this work because the drone uses
barometer to acquire altitude information and the atmosphere of the test environment
is a bit higher than normal. This causes the altitude reading at the ground of the test
environment ranges from -33m to -40m. As a result, in the testing phrase, altitude are
calculated using real altitude = altitude reading −startpoint altitude.
Then the drone undergoes a manual or automatic takeoff process, bringing the drone to
an altitude of 8-10m. During this process, the vision module is initialized and starts to give
target location to the main thread.
When the take off phrase is finished, the automatic landing process will start after a start
signal is given. The drone will constantly descend while adjusting its horizontal position
to the top of a clear landing site. The drone will descend at 0.3m/s if it’s directly above a
feasible landing site. The drone will descend at 0.1m/s and move horizontally at 0.1m/s
to 1.7m/s based on the distance from the drone to the found landing site. The horizontal
speed is linear with the horizontal distance from the drone to the landing site (minimum
0.1m/s if horizontal movement is required).
When altitude reaches 2 meters, the two modules for autonomous landing are switched
off and the default autonomous landing process for DJI drone is started, this causes the
drone to land directly to the ground below it.
