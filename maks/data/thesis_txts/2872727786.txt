Abstract
This thesis investigates how ground surface albedo influences irradiance distribution in
vertically mounted photovoltaic (PV) systems. A 3ยญmeterยญhigh southยญfacing setup with
eight mini modules was tested with three surface types: green grass, a white tarp (high
albedo), and a black tarp (low albedo). Results showed that irradiance decreased with
height for grass and the white tarp, with the gradient being stronger for the white tarp due
to its high reflectivity. In contrast, the black tarp produced the lowest irradiance near the
ground and an increase with height. Simulations captured the same heightยญdependent
trends but differed in absolute values. Temperature data from thermocouples was unreยญ
liable and not included in the final analysis. These results show that ground conditions
strongly affect irradiance at different heights and highlight the potential for vertical BIPV
systems in snowy or highยญalbedo environments.

Introduction
1.1
Background
As stated by the United Nations [1], energy is at the heart of both the climate crisis and
its potential solutions. Fossil fuels are by far the largest contributor to global climate
change, accounting for more than 75% of greenhouse gas emissions and nearly 90% of
all carbon dioxide emissions, as shown in Figure 1.1 [1]. However, in the Production Gap
Report published by the UN in 2023, the production and use of fossil fuels have reached
record high levels [2]. To avoid severe impacts of climate change, emissions need to be
significantly reduced and reach net zero by 2050 [1]. This highlights the urgent need to
accelerate the transition to renewable energy sources.
Figure 1.1: Global greenhouse gas emissions by source [1].
Compared to other renewable energy sources, solar energy stands out as one of the
most efficient, ecoยญfriendly, and accessible green energy solutions available today [3].
Unlike wind energy, which often requires complex infrastructure and high maintenance,
solar panels are quieter, easier to maintain, and less disruptive to their surroundings. In
contrast to hydropower, which can significantly alter ecosystems, solar panels produce
clean electricity with minimal environmental impact. This combination of high efficiency,
low emissions, declining costs, and minimal ecological disruption makes solar energy a
leading force in the transition to a sustainable and resilient global energy system [3].
Buildingยญintegrated photovoltaic (BIPV) systems serve a dual function by generating elecยญ
tricity while simultaneously acting as part of the building envelope [4]. The panels can be
integrated into the building envelope as a part of the building structure, and therefore can
replace conventional building materials, rather than being installed afterwards, enabling
good possibilities for architectural integration, as well as saving both time and money. [5].
These systems, which include elements such as skylights, facade panels, and roofing
components, are designed to optimize both energy efficiency and architectural integraยญ
tion [4].
1.2
Motivation
The groundยญreflected component of solar irradiance plays a significant role in the perforยญ
mance of photovoltaic (PV) systems, especially for vertical and nearยญvertical installations

such as buildingยญintegrated photovoltaics (BIPV). In these systems, accurate quantificaยญ
tion of albedo is essential because the reflected irradiance can substantially influence the
total incident radiation on PV modules, impacting their energy yield and thermal characยญ
teristics [6].
Albedo is a unitless quantity that quantifies the fraction of incoming solar radiation reยญ
flected by a surface [7]. It ranges between 0 and 1, where 0 indicates that the surface
absorbs all incoming radiation (a perfect absorber), and 1 means the surface reflects all
incoming radiation (a perfect reflector). While albedo is often related to the perceived
brightness or whiteness of a surface, it fundamentally describes the surfaceโs reflective
properties.
As shown in Figure 1.2, vertically mounted modules (90ยฐ tilt) are more sensitive to albedo
variations and spectral effects compared to tilted systems [8]. This sensitivity highlights
the need for accurate albedo characterization in systems such as BIPV, where groundยญ
reflected irradiance plays a large role in energy yield.
Figure 1.2: Comparison of spectralยญintegrated (ฮฑSI) and spectrally responsive (ฮฑSR)
albedo for a fieldยญmounted (40ยฐ) and vertical (90ยฐ) PV system. The difference between
ฮฑSR (0.95) and ฮฑSI (0.78) leads to a modelling error of 6.3% for the vertical setup, comยญ
pared to 1.4% for the tilted system [8].
Albedo influences not only the temperature of the ground, but also the irradiance and the
temperature of nearby PV modules [9]. Surfaces with higher albedo reflect more sunยญ
light, which, while keeping the surface cooler, increases the total irradiance incident on
the solar cells mounted above [9]. Since only about 20% of the incident light is converted
into electricity and the rest is absorbed as heat [10], higher irradiance typically leads to
higher cell temperatures. This increase in cell temperature reduces voltage and overall
efficiency [11] [12]. Knowledge of PV module temperature is therefore essential for acยญ
curately assessing module performance, as efficiency typically decreases by about 0.4%
per degree Celsius [13].
Most solar panels on the market have efficiency ratings between 15% and 22%, with some
models reaching up to 25% [14]. These values are based on standard test conditions
(STC): 25ยฐC, 1000 W/mยฒ, and an air mass of 1.5 [14]. In contrast to what some might
think, solar panels become less efficient when exposed to higher temperatures. As the
temperature increases, the electrons in the solar cells become more energetic, which
leads to a reduced bandgap of the semiconductor. This leads to several effects, such as
decreased openยญcircuit voltage [15], increased short circuit current, and increased internal

While the influence of albedo on solar energy systems is well established, many studยญ

Vertical Mounting, Albedo, and Thermal Effects
These studies explore how mounting orientation, surface reflectance, and thermal behavยญ
ior influence solar panel performance. Vertical PV configurations, in particular, experience
distinct irradiance and temperature profiles due to reduced selfยญshading, increased expoยญ
sure to groundยญreflected light, and different heat dissipation characteristics.
Andersen et al. (2024) [16] studied the thermal behaviour of vertically mounted miniยญ
modules on a southยญfacing wall under insulated and ventilated conditions. They found
that insulation reduces heat dissipation, causing higher module temperatures, with lighter
and transparent modules showing lower heat absorption. Custom temperature correction
coefficients depending on planeยญofยญarray irradiance improved thermal model accuracy,
especially in ventilated setups. Wind and module positioning also influenced temperature,

Aoki and Mizutani (2014) [17] experimentally measured reflected solar radiation onto verยญ
tical surfaces from terrace materials like concrete tiles, artificial turf, and wood chips. Conยญ
crete showed the highest albedo (2ฬ8%) and reflected heat, while turf and wood chips were
lower. Wind speed had little effect, confirming that surface material properties control

Urbanยญscale modelling by Raybaud et al. (2019) [18] compared specular and diffuse reยญ
flectance on faรงades, showing a single faรงadeโs optical properties can alter irradiance on
opposite surfaces by 25โ68 W/m2. Ray tracing and radiosity methods complement each

Calabrรฒ and Magazรน (2016) [19] linked increased soil albedo from desertification to changes
in local climate through modified heat fluxes and vertical air motion, emphasizing ground
albedoโs role beyond local irradiance effects.
Prado and Ferreira (2004) [20] demonstrated that roofing materials with high albedo and
emissivity maintain lower surface temperatures, reducing heat gain and energy demand
for cooling. Their work advocates for including albedo and emissivity standards in building
materials for energy efficiency and mitigating urban heat islands.
Bensalem et al. (2013) [21] investigated silicon solar cells under thermal gradients, finding
efficiency decreases by approximately 0.063% per Kelvin increase. Maximum efficiency
occurs below room temperature, implying performance losses at higher temperatures.
Together, these studies highlight how mounting configuration, ground and faรงade reยญ
flectance, and thermal conditions interact to shape the energy performance of PV systemsโ
particularly for vertical installations.

Buildingยญintegrated photovoltaics (BIPV) are influenced by both environmental conditions

These findings underscore the need for careful thermal modelling and mounting considยญ

Converting the measured thermoelectric voltage from TCs to temperature requires using
the correct conversion corresponding to the thermocouple type. In this case, the TCs
were incorrectly logged as type J in the datalogger software, while they were in fact type
K. This mismatch led to incorrect temperature readings during the calibration process.
Accurate thermocouple readings require compensation for the temperature at the connecยญ
tion point between the thermocouple wires and the measurement device, known as the
cold junction. Since thermocouples measure temperature differences rather than absoยญ
lute temperatures, this cold junction compensation (CJC) ensures the measured voltage
is correctly referenced. In this experiment, the datalogger applied a fixed compensation
value based on an assumed ambient lab temperature. However, because the datalogger
was also configured for the wrong thermocouple type, the compensation did not align with
the actual sensor characteristics. During recalculation, the cold junction temperature was
manually estimated to be 22 ยฐC (room temperature during calibration) and used to correct
the conversion to type K thermocouples.

Conclusions
This thesis investigated how ground surface albedo influences the vertical irradiance and
temperature distribution on southยญfacing, vertically mounted PV modules. Field measureยญ
ments were carried out for three surface types: green grass (natural lowยญalbedo), a white
tarp (representing highยญalbedo surfaces such as snow), and a black tarp (lowยญalbedo,
lightยญabsorbing surface). Simulations using the bifacial_radiance tool were conducted in
parallel to support interpretation of the measured data.
The results showed a clear relationship between ground albedo and the heightยญdependent
irradiance gradient. For both green grass and the white tarp, the lowest module consisยญ
tently received the highest irradiance, with irradiance decreasing at higher positions. The
effect was significantly more pronounced for the white tarp, due to its higher reflectivity,
which led to stronger groundยญreflected irradiance and amplified the gradient. In contrast,
the black tarp produced the lowest irradiance at the bottom module and an increasing
irradiance trend with height. This was caused by the black surface absorbing incoming
radiation and contributing minimal reflected light to the lower modules.
Simulations reproduced the same general irradiance trends observed in the measureยญ
ments for all three ground surfaces, confirming that the irradiance gradient is primarily
driven by surface reflectance and geometric view factors. However, a direct comparison
between simulated and measured values revealed low agreement in magnitude. This
is most likely due to the limitations of the simulation model, which does not account for
temperature effects or the resulting decrease in cell efficiency at increasing temperatures.
The size of the reflective surface also proved important, especially for modules mounted
higher on the pole. These cells have a broader view of the surrounding area, meaning
they are more affected by portions of the ground not covered by the tarp. This effect was
especially visible in the white tarp case, where insufficient tarp coverage led to reduced
irradiance at higher positions despite the high albedo.
Analysis of module temperature was inconclusive. Several issues, such as incorrect therยญ
mocouple configuration, sensor placement, and the dynamic outdoor environment, introยญ
duced uncertainty in the temperature data. As a result, it was not possible to identify the
relationship between ground albedo and module temperature with confidence.
These findings demonstrate a clear relationship between ground surface albedo and the
vertical distribution of irradiance. The results confirm that ground reflectivity influences
not only the overall irradiance received but also how irradiance varies with height. Highly
reflective surfaces such as the white tarp create stronger heightยญdependent irradiance
gradients, while less reflective surfaces like the black tarp can reverse this trend. This
emphasizes the need to account for both albedo and module position when evaluating or
designing vertically mounted PV systems.

Future Work
Several directions could enhance and expand this study to deepen understanding of
albedo effects and vertical solar installations under varying environmental conditions:
โข Temperature Analysis:
Due to time constraints and issues with thermocouple measurements during this
study, temperature analysis was not included. Future work should focus on obยญ
taining reliable temperature data alongside irradiance measurements to better unยญ
derstand the thermal behavior of solar modules under varying albedo and weather
conditions.
โข YearยญRound Measurements:
Extend data collection to cover a full year to capture seasonal variations. The current
dataset spans only spring and early summer, so including autumn and winter would
provide a more comprehensive picture.
โข Real Snow Conditions:
Although the white tarp used in this study is reflective and simulates high albedo, it
does not fully replicate the optical and physical properties of real snow. Investigating
solar module performance during actual snow cover in colder climates would provide
more accurate insights into the effects of natural snow on irradiance and temperature
profiles.
โข OnยญSite Albedo Measurements:
Perform direct field measurements of ground reflectance under varying surface conยญ
ditions to improve simulation accuracy. Current simulations are based on labยญmeasured
reflectance values, which may not fully represent dynamic field environments.
โข Expanded Sensor Arrays and Heights:
Consider installing additional sensors at more heights and orientations to better
characterize vertical gradients and microclimate effects around solar arrays.
โข Water and Coastal Ground Covers:
Explore the impact of water bodies, such as lakes or sea surfaces, as ground cover
near coastal installations. Reflections from water can differ substantially from land
surfaces and influence irradiance on solar modules.
โข Cloud Cover Characterization:
Incorporate detailed cloud condition monitoring (e.g., cloud type, thickness, dynamยญ
ics) to better understand irradiance fluctuations, especially under overcast condiยญ
tions.
