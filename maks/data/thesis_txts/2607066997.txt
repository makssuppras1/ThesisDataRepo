Abstract 
 
Aquaculture is the fastest growing food production sector in the world. It typically has 
taken place in ponds, lakes, raceways or in the ocean, but over the last decade more 
modern solutions have emerged. Recirculated aquaculture systems are land-based 
facilities with a focus on high degree of water recirculation. As more and of the water is 
recirculated, so does the need for water treatment to uphold good water quality. At the 
same time more legalization and environmental regulations have been passed making 
restrictions for the industry. Therefore, there is a need for green solutions. One of the 
most important commercial species, where this is being done, is salmon (Salmo salar). 
Peracetic acid may be part of the green solution. It is a chemical therapeutant, that quickly 
breaks down to harmless compounds. It could therefore be good alternative to previously 
used disinfection methods.  
In order to investigate the effect PAA has on salmon, a six-week experiment was made 
with weekly dosings of PAA with two different concentrations, Low and High (1,2 and 2,4 
mg PAA/L). During the trial, the fish were investigated for phenotypes like growth, feed 
conversion ratio and oxygen uptake. At the end of the trial, liver tissues were collected for 
the purpose of doing RNA-sequencing and differential expression analysis in order to 
investigate if PAA has an effect on the molecular level.  
It was found that the salmon dosed with PAA had a lower SGR and higher FCR compared 
to the control, but it was not significant. A significant difference was found in oxygen 
uptake between the High treatment compared to the Control and Low. Mortality was 
initially higher in the High treatment, but halfway through the trial all treatments showed 
similar mortality.  
The differential expression analysis showed, that two genes (PER3, NR1D1) related to the 
circadian rhythms were dysregulated in both the Low and High treatment compared to 
the control. Both genes have been linked to adipogenesis. But macronutrient composition 
was not investigated. 
Surprisingly a gene related to growth (IGF-1) was down-regulated in the Low treatment, 
but not in the High.  The Low treatment also showed the lowest SGR and highest FCR. 
These findings could indicate that salmon have an adjustment period to PAA exposure, 
and it may be concentration dependent. Also, PAA exposure may lead to an increased fat 
content in the fish, but this require more research to determine.

Resumé 
 
Akvakultur er den hurtigst voksende fødevareproduktionssektor i verden. Den har typisk 
fundet sted i damme, søer, kunstige kanaler eller i havet, men i løbet af det sidste årti er 
der kommet mere moderne løsninger. Recirkulerede akvakultursystemer er landbaserede 
anlæg med fokus på høj grad af vandrecirkulation. Med øget vandrecirkulering øges 
behovet for vandbehandling, også for at opretholde god vandkvalitet. Samtidig er der 
vedtaget flere love og miljøbestemmelser, der sætter fokus på miljøvenlig produktion. 
Derfor er der behov for miljøvenlige løsninger. En af de vigtigste kommercielle arter er 
laks (Salmo salar).  
Pereddikesyre (PAA) kan være en del af den grønne løsning. Den er et kemisk terapeutisk 
middel, der hurtigt nedbrydes til harmløse stoffer. Det kan derfor være et godt alternativ 
til tidligere anvendte desinfektionsmidler.  
For at undersøge effekten af PAA på laks blev der lavet et seks ugers eksperiment med 
ugentlig dosering af PAA med to forskellige koncentrationer, lav og høj (1,2 og 2,4 mg 
PAA/L). Under forsøget blev fiskene undersøgt for fænotyper som vækst, 
foderomdannelsesforhold og iltoptagelse. I slutningen af forsøget blev der indsamlet 
leverprøver med det formål at udføre RNA-seq og differentiel ekspressionsanalyse for at 
undersøge, om PAA har en effekt på molekylært niveau. 
Det blev fundet, at laksen doseret med PAA havde en lavere SGR og højere FCR 
sammenlignet med kontrollen, men den var ikke signifikant. En signifikant forskel blev 
fundet i iltoptagelsen mellem Høj behandling sammenlignet med Kontrollen og Lav. 
Dødeligheden var oprindeligt højere i den høje behandling, men halvvejs gennem forsøget 
viste alle behandlinger ens dødelighed. 
Den differentielle ekspressionsanalyse viste, at to gener (PER3, NR1D1) relateret til de 
cirkadiske rytmer blev dysreguleret i både Lave og Høje behandling sammenlignet med 
kontrollen. Begge gener er blevet knyttet til adipogenese. Men sammensætningen af 
makronæringsstoffer blev ikke undersøgt. 
Overraskende nok blev et gen relateret til vækst (IGF-1) nedreguleret i den lave 
behandling, men ikke i den høje. Den lave behandling viste også den laveste SGR og 
højeste FCR. 
Disse fund kunne indikere, at laks har en justeringsperiode til PAA-eksponering, og den 
kan være koncentrationsafhængig. PAA-eksponering kan også føre til et øget fedtindhold 
i fiskene, men dette kræver mere forskning for at kunne konkluderes.

1. Introduction 
 
Background 
 
What is aquaculture and why is it relevant? 
 
Aquaculture is the fastest growing food production sector in the world. Aquaculture or 
fish farming is the activity of cultivating aquatic organisms like fish, mollusks, crustaceans 
and seaweed that is intended for human consumption. Aquaculture is taking place on a 
global scale and is the fastest growing food production sector in the world (FAO 2018). 
Aquaculture is practiced everywhere from the poorest regions in the world by simple 
pond production to multinational corporations with highly advanced facilities, that have 
focus on water recirculation and nutrient optimization.  
In 2016, 89% of the worlds wild fish stock was either fully fished or overfished (FAO 2016). 
Approximately 50% of the worlds consumed fish is farmed and with rising demands for 
fish and other aquatic resources it is estimated by the Food and Agriculture Organization 
(FAO) that aquaculture production will increase three to five times by 2050.  In order to 
keep increasing this production and to obtain the United Nations goal for environmental 
sustainability there is an increased focus on minimizing water intake and nutrient 
discharge to the aquatic environment.  
 
What is RAS? 
 
Recirculated aquaculture systems (RAS) is an increasingly more used technology. It is 
recognized as an environmentally friendly option for intensive modern aquaculture. A RAS 
facility typically consists of holding tanks for the fish, in-line water treatment units and 
treatment for effluents. Due to the high degree of water recirculation many different 
steps for water treatment is necessary to have adequate water quality for fish production 
(Badiola et al., 2012). These steps include removal of solid and dissolved organic 
compounds, that comes from feed or metabolic waste from the fish. To achieve this a 
series of mechanical and biological filters are used. Managing biological filters plays a vital 
role for good water-quality and typically is the most challenging water-treatment part to 
manage. Biological filters consist of a filter-medium, that allows nitrifying and denitrifying 
bacteria to grow on the surface and convert ammonia to nitrite, and nitrite to nitrate and 
finally to nitrogen.   
 
The most essential functions RAS provides (Murray F., et al 2014): 
 
• Provide clean environment to insure good growth of fish. 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
• Maintain water temperature.  
• Insure adequate concentration of oxygen in the water. 
• Removal of fish waste and uneaten feed. 
• Protect the fish stock from disease and unwanted pathogens.   
 
 
What are some pros and cons for RAS? 
 
The primary advantages compared to net-pens or flow-throughs are a lower water intake, 
better utilization of feed (nutrients), less impactful discharge to the surrounding 
environment and higher productivity (Badiola et al., 2012). However, they are also 
challenges related to RAS. Fish a cultivated in high density with a longer retention time of 
the water. This can result in an accumulation of particles and organic matter that 
combined with the stable environment of RAS creates good conditions for bacterial 
growth. Bacteria can grow on all possible surfaces from equipment and tanks, but also on 
the fish themselves, most exposed areas include skin and gills (Pedersen et al., 2017).  
 
 
What is RAS - pros and cons. 
 
RAS aadvantages 
 
▪ No added stress or risk from predation on fish stock because of the closed 
environment in RAS. 
▪ Reduced use of antibiotics and other chemical therapeutants help to lower 
production costs and provide opportunities for marketing as “safe” seafood. 
▪ Better feed controls lower operational costs. 
▪ Control of effluents resulting in lower nutrient discharge and potential elimination 
of unwanted pathogens, that can be released to the receiving waterbody. 
▪ Control and possibility of treatment of water-intake increases biosecurity. 
▪ Potential lower production time as a result of optimized growth conditions. 
▪ Lower Feed conversion ratio (FCR) adds to sustainable production and decreases 
the time up to 50%, compared to cage production, to reach market size. 
▪ Less demand for water increases versatility of farm location and alos increases 
possibility for utilizing waterbody otherwise unsuited for pond or flow-though 
systems e.g. groundwater.  
▪ Possibility of harvesting fish within regular intervals, that contributes to a stable 
supply to market.  
▪ 24 hours monitorization with automation improves feed management and lowers 
risk of disease spread, while keeping labor costs down.  
▪ Superior FCRs can help offset the carbon footprint that comes from increased 
energy consumption from pumping and treatment of water.  
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
 
Challenges for RAS technology  
 
▪ Qualified RAS staff and managers are in short supply. Especially is knowledge 
lacking regarding water-quality and the need for constant monitoring.  
▪ High initial investment is required to build RAS facilities.  
▪ Possible lack of awareness for proper effluent management from investors, 
resulting in increased difficulty in securing founding for RAS projects.  
▪ Due to the high complexity of RAS, investors often fail to properly evaluate and 
choose RAS technology packages, that again leads to commercial failures.   
▪ Temperature control of the water can be very energy demanding, and combined 
with high electric prices can decrease the financial viability for certain species of 
fish. Furthermore, these species need to compete with products like imported fish, 
that may be produced with lower cost methods e.g. in cage production in warmer 
waters.   
▪ Financial viability of fish farmed in RAS can depend on selling the products for a 
premium price justified by an environmentally friendly production, but this 
criterion is vulnerable to production form of electricity used and the correlated 
carbon footprint.  
▪ Financial viability is also very dependent on the size of the farm. 
▪ Lack of knowledge and research into utilization of farm waste for byproducts like 
on-site energy production or growth of polychaeta worms as a feed supplement is 
often not correctly estimated, when financial feasibility is to be evaluated.  
▪ Financial viability based on factors like market price, energy cost, interest of loans, 
utilization of waste stream and byproducts are all factors the can and often change 
over time. Furthermore, some of these variables are sometimes based on smaller 
scale projects like pilot or research projects, that do not hold up by large scale 
production.  
 
 
Why is it necessary to use chemical therapeutants in RAS? 
 
One of the main advantages of RAS is the stable environment tailored for optimum fish 
growth. However, the downside is that this environment also provides favorable 
conditions for pathogens/bacterial growth (Delabbio et al., 2004). When a pathogen has 
entered the system and if it is not handled properly and quickly, it can multiply and spread 
to the stock. This can happen relatively fast as a RAS can function as an bacteria incubator. 
This is due to the high degree of water recirculation and low dilution levels. System 
instability caused by variable environmental conditions like temperature and ammonia 
removal rates can also contribute to disease outbreaks.  
 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
Once an unwanted pathogen has established itself in RAS, it can be very difficult to 
eradicate. It is often not possible to use the same water-treatment methods traditional 
used in flow-through systems because of the bacterial colonies in the biofilters, that play 
a vital role in the RAS (Schwartz et al., 2000). 
 
If a disease/pathogen outbreak is detected, either by water sampling or monitoring of fish 
body temperature, it will be necessary to treat the water with chemical disinfectants. At 
the same time the RAS facility needs to keep monitoring the bacterial population in the 
biofilter to avoid buildup from harmful substances like ammonia, nitrite or over 
oxygenation (Murray F., et al 2014).

What sort of qualities we are looking for in the ideal therapeutant? 
 
As chemical therapeutants are added to help provide a clean environment for fish 
cultivation, there are other factors that are important for a good therapeutant. The ideal 
therapeutant would have the following qualities.   
 
• The therapeutant must eradicate or suppress fish pathogens. 
• At the same time, it must also be harmless for the workers, beneficial bacteria (in 
the biofilter) and of course the fish themselves.  
• It should degrade quickly into harmless compounds and should not affect the 
receiving environment of the effluents.  
• Furthermore, it should be easy to use, safe, cheap and widely available.   
 
 
Peracetic acid as a chemical therapeutant. 
 
Peracetic acid (PAA) is a relatively new chemical therapeutant and is considered an 
alternative to formalin (Sortkjær et al., 2008). PAA is a highly reactive peroxygen 
compound, that is proven to have antimicrobial effects on a wide range of pathogens. PAA 
is a mix of acetic acid and hydrogen peroxide and will start to decay immediately after 
being added to freshwater. It will oxidize into harmless acetic acid, water and 
biodegradable residues (Kitis, 2004). The rapid decay into harmless compounds makes 
PAA considered an environmentally friendly therapeutant, but poses a challenge to fish 
farmers and aquaculture technicians.   
 
Why choose peracetic acid? 
 
Peracetic acid is a general disinfectant with stronger oxidizing capacity compared to H2O2. 
It has been proven, that a nominal concentration of 1 mg/L PAA can reduce growth of 
unwanted pathogenic bacteria in aquaculture. Bacteria such as Flavobacterium 
columnare, Aeromonas salmonicida and Yersinia ruckeri can be mentioned (Meinelt et al., 
2015). 
 
Peracetic acid effect on biofilters. 
 
As mentioned earlier one of the most important (and most challenging) part of RAS is to 
manage the biofilters and their microbial communities. In an experiment by (Suurnäkki et 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
al., 2020) PAA effect on the biofilter was tested by dosing in pulses with four different 
frequencies (0, 1, 2, 4 times per week) with a concentration of 1.1 mg/L PAA twice per day 
over a 13-week period and it was discovered that the overall microbial community 
composition was stable.  It was also found that nitrifying bacteria did not suffer from PAA 
use and even increased in abundancy over the trial period.  However, in the trial with the 
highest PAA application (four dosings per week) a lower nitrification rate was observed 
compared to the other three, but TAN values would still be reduced.  
 
A minimum threshold concentration of PAA application was also identified and estimated 
to be 0.3 mg/L for disinfection purposes in RAS, as lower concentration had no effect on 
microbial counts. These values correspond with the findings made by (Davidson et al., 
2019) in a similar experiment.  
 
At the end of the experiment it was found, that dosing twice per week was sufficient to 
decrease biofilm, and higher dosing frequencies would even result in higher biofilm 
production. With regard to the biofilter it was also found, that PAA dosing frequency had 
no significant effect. This was judged to be so because PAA most likely degraded before 
entering the biofilters. It should be noted, that a slightly lower nitrification rate at week 8 
was recorded in the systems with the highest dosing, while at week 13 there was no 
significant difference. This could indicate, that the nitrification bacteria could adapt to 
PAA and became less sensitive. At the end of the experiment no ammonium or nitrite 
accumulation was observed, and it was judged that PAA is safe to use with regard to the 
biofilters.  
  
Peracetic acid effect on fish. 
 
In the same experiment conducted by (Suurnäkki et al., 2020) the affect of PAA on fish 
performance was also investigated. The frequency of PAA (with concentration of 1.1 
mg/L) did not have a significant effect on fish performance in terms of FCR and SGR 
(specific growth rate). The tanks with the highest dosing (four times per week) had 14% 
lower biomass compared to the other three.  Also, in these tanks a higher mortality was 
observed, suggesting that frequent use of PAA (1.1 mg/L twice per day four times per 
week) was too much for the fish. This contrasts with the finding made by (Straus et al., 
2018), where no effect below a concentration of 2.8 mg/L PAA was observed. An increased 
slime formation on the fish was also observed in the tanks with the highest dosing 
frequency. With the first treatments of PAA, fish were observed to swim closer to the 
surface, but after a few weeks this no longer occurred. These finding were also observed 
by (Liu et al., 2017). Both papers agree, that this indicates that fish is capable to adapt to 
PAA a relatively short time after an initial exposure.   
 
Beside growth parameters (Liu et al., 2017) also presented results of improved fish health 
after treatment with PAA 1mg/L twice per week over a three-month period. It was noted, 
that fish that was treated with PAA at the end of the trial had improved gill health as a 
results of lower biofilm concentration. A slightly higher cortisol response in the fish 
treated with PAA was also found, and was present for the whole period. Kidney samples 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
of both the control and treated fish were sampled for leukocytes (white blood cells) and 
no significant difference was reported. It was concluded by (Liu et al., 2017) that periodic 
use of PAA in RAS can have long-term positive impact for health of the fish.  
 
The findings made by (Liu et al., 2017) and (Suurnäkki et al., 2020) both suggests that PAA 
is a viable disinfectant and can be used in RAS with no to little impact on biofilters (after 
an initial start period). Both papers suggest that concentration and frequency of PAA 
applied are important factors and should be kept around 1 mg/L and a be applied no more 
than twice per week. But more research regarding stress response as a result of exposure 
to PAA is needed. Also, there is little knowledge if PAA exposure effects at the molecular 
level on salmon. Here, the change in the genetic expression is a crucial regulator of cell 
functions and intensity and variability of such expression is highly influenced by external 
factors and physiological status. Nowadays, this investigation can be done using high-
throughput techniques that allow to simultaneously profile the expression level of all 
genes in a tissue or organism, thanks to the evolution of next generation sequencing 
technologies. 
 
Next-generation sequencing  
 
The first method for sequencing genetic data was made by Sanger and Coulson in 1975 
and was named Sanger sequencing. This method is still in use today, but due to limitations 
in this technology, popularity is decreasing (Keogh & Chinnery, 2013). To overcome the 
limitations in the Sanger sequencing, scientists have developed new technologies. One of 
these methods is microarray. Microarray is not part of NGS but was the most common 
tool to do differential expression analysis before RNA-seq. A microarray is a chip or slide 
where single strand DNA (ssDNA) patterns are immobilized on the surface. The chip is 
typically made of glass or silicon, where DNA is attached in a grid like fashion. Microarrays 
uses the principle of hybridization between nucleotides. It can do thousands or more 
sequences in a single hybridization. Microarray is most used find expressed genes that are 
correlated with a specific phenotype between samples (Annest et al., 2010).  
 
These newer methods go under the umbrella name Next-generation sequencing (NGS) (de 
Magalhães et al., 2010). Some of the technologies, that goes under the term NGS include, 
Illumina sequencing, Roch454 sequencing and Nanopore sequencing among many others 
(Phillippy, 2017).  The advantage of these methods is, that the sequencing of RNA and 
DNA can be done much quicker and cheaper. This has revolutionized the study of 
molecular biology and genomics (Kumar, Dasgupta, & Ranjan, 2018).  The majority of NGS 
technologies are applied in the study of transcriptional data (de Magalhães et al., 2010). 
They can be used to measure global expression with high accuracy. RNA-seq is one of the 
highest regarded technologies for transcriptome profiling. This technique is known for 
providing very precise measurement of the levels of transcripts (Wang et al., 2009). 
 
 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
RNA sequencing 
 
RNA-seq method cover sequencing of transcribed RNA, mapping and quantification of the 
transcripts. This has proved to be a powerful approach for studying entire transcriptomes 
(Wang et al., 2009; Zhang et al., 2018). Today RNA-seq is used to identify messenger RNA 
(mRNA), non-coding RNAs (ncRNA), measure gene expression and identify gene 
expression patterns (Wang et al., 2016).  
 
RNA-seq can be used to understand many other areas beside the above mentioned, such 
as mechanisms of alternative splicing and discover novel isoforms of mRNA transcripts (D. 
Kim & Salzberg, 2011). Some advantages RNA-seq offer compared to hybridization-based 
methods is it does not require prior knowledge of which gene may or may not be 
expressed. It can therefore detect novel transcripts in organisms, that do not have an 
available assembled genome. Also, it has high reproducibility with low noise levels (Hurd 
& Nelson, 2009). It has also been shown to be an accurate tool for quantifying gene 
expression levels (Zhang et al., 2018). 
 
To summarize: RNA-seq is the first sequencing method that makes is possible to survey 
the entire transcriptome. Furthermore, this is done in a quantitative and high-throughput 
way making it possible to apply bioinformatic methods to better analysis and understand 
the data (Wang et al., 2009; Han et al., 2015). For this reason, it has started replacing 
microarrays as the tool for profiling gene expression (Hurd & Nelson, 2009). 
 
In order to do RNA-sequencing, several steps must be taken. This involves collecting tissue 
samples, extracted the RNA from the samples, purify the RNA, shearing it, convert to 
cDNA and sequencing it on an NGS platform e.g. Illumina (Han et al., 2015). However, NGS 
technologies generate huge amount of data. It has therefore become necessary to 
developed strategies to deal with this data in a scientific and standardized manner giving 
rise to the field of bioinformatics.  
 
Bioinformatics is the field of developing software tools and pipelines to analysis and 
understand large, complex biological data sets.  It is an interdisciplinary field that 
involves not just biology but also, information engineering, computer science, 
mathematics and statistics (Zhang & Liu 2013). 
 
 
Differential gene expression analysis (DE analysis) 
 
Differential gene expression analysis is using statistical analysis on the normalized read 
count data to identify genes that have significantly changed in abundance between 
experimental conditions (Anders et al., 2013). Every gene is then either classified as down- 
or up-regulated. Down-regulated simply means, that gene is more expressed in the 
reference in the analysis. E.g. between two treatments T1 and T2 if T1 is

Much software has been created to identify differential expressed genes among different 
treatments groups (Zhang et al., 2014). One of these software programs is the DESeq2 R 
package. A key factor for DE analysis is the use of experimental replicates (Zhang et al., 
2014). By using replicates, it reduces the risk of getting false positives.  
 
 
Tissue selection 
 
It was chosen to do RNA extraction from liver tissue. As the ability to convert feed to 
biomass and growth rate is very important in commercial aquaculture and the liver is the 
main organ where metabolic functions are regulated. Liver tissue have also been proven 
to be a stable tissue to work with and therefore suitable for people that are inexperienced 
with tissue sampling. 
 
 
Aim of the study 
 
The aim of this study is to compare 3 different treatment groups (Control, Low and High) 
that have been exposed to peracetic acid. And investigate them for:  
 
1) Phenotypical data in form of, oxygen uptake, feed conversion ratio and specific 
growth rate. 
2) Collect tissue samples from the liver that will be used of DE analysis.  
3) Check for the most up- and down-regulated genes as a result of differential gene 
expression analysis.  
4) Identify genes related to growth and stress repose as a result of PAA exposure.

2. Material and methods  
 
Experimental stock 
Fish was obtained from Danish Salmon A/S (Denmark) and all came from the same family. 
The stock had been used for farming over several generations. The experiment was 
conducted at the DTU Aquas facilities at the North Sea Centre in Hirtshals (Denmark). The 
fish were distributed in groups of 35 fish with an average biomass per fish of 202g ± 10g 
in each tank.  
Experimental set-up 
Overall, the system consisted of 12 identical circular polyethylene holding tanks (1m 
diameter 0,85m depth, water volume 620L), each with a swirl separator and individual 
oxygen measurement and supply.  All tanks were connected to a common water supply 
tank with a volume of 13.5 m3 with 10% daily replacement and water kept at 15 °C. The 
water source was tap water from Hirtshals. Also, a biofilter with a volume of 22.5 m3 was 
connected to the system.  
The total volume for each tank + swirl separator and pipes were judged to have a total 
volume of 650 L. That volume was used to calculate the necessary amount of PAA needed 
to achieve the desired concentrations.  
The water would either be recirculated inside the holding tanks or be sent to a biofilter 
before being pumped back to the common water tanks.

Dosing with Peracid acid  
Before dosing started a standard curve for concentration of peracid acid were made. This 
was done by collecting water from the common water tank in the experimental system 
and distribute it in 9 identical 1L containers. From there peracid acid was added to each 
container to make a concentration ranging from 1 to 5 mg/L with an increment of 0,5mg/L. 
From this standard curve (R2-value of 0,997) the concentration could be calculated. 
Also, a turnover rate was made for the Low (1,2 mg/L) and the High (2,4 mg/L) 
concentration. In similar way to the standard curve, water was collected from the 
common holding tank and dosed with PAA corresponding to 1,2 and 2,4 mg/L. Water 
samples were taken every 20 minutes and measured with a spectrophotometer. A 
wavelength of 550 nm was used to measure the amount of peracid acid in the samples.  
Once a week (every Monday) tanks would receive PAA solution as required according to 
their treatment group. A PAA solution was made in the laboratory the same day and 
dosing would start at 08:00. The PAA solution was made by diluting a highly concentrated 
PAA with milli-Q water. Before dosing started, the tanks would be set on recirculation in 
order to avoid contamination of the biofilter and the other treatment groups.  
Every 1,5 hours water samples would be collected in all tanks and immediately measured 
with a spectrophotometer. The reading from the spectrophotometer was used to

Liver extraction  
 
Liver samples were collected at the end of the 6-week trial, 8 hours after the last dosing. 
The tanks were almost completely drained, and the fish was caught by net and transferred 
to a holding tank. The holding tank (volume of 80 L) was a square polyethylene tank with 
a constant oxygen supply.  
Fish was submerged in a bath of water and tranquilizer (Benzoak vet. 200mg/ml). A tank 
of 80 L and 30 ml of Benzoak were mixed creating a solution of 75 mg/L. Approximately 
5-8 fish at a time were submersed in the bath. After waiting around 5 minutes one fish at 
a time was weighted before being killed. A scalpel was used to sever the spine between 
the body and the head. Then the liver was extracted (Figure 4). 
 
Figure 4 Liver extraction 
The outer side of the liver was cut away making it possible to collect the inner parts of the 
liver. Three pieces of approximately 0,5 cm3 in size were cut from the liver and 
immediately put in pre-prepared 2ml Eppendorf tubes with 1 ml of RNA-later. Each tube 
was marked making it possible to track what tank and treatment the fish came from.    
Before and in between the sampling all surfaces and all tools was cleaned with 90% 
ethanol to make sure there was no contamination.  
A total of 117 samples was collected (13 from each tank). The fish, that was used for 
sampling, were randomly selected. After sampling the tubes were placed in a fridge for 
24 hours before being put in a freezer at -20 °C.  
==PAGE:25==

Transport of liver samples 
A Styrofoam box was chosen because of good insulating capabilities.  the Eppendorf tubes 
were stored in cuvette racks before being wrapped in plastic. Then the Styrofoam box was 
filled halfway with ice and the samples were placed in the middle of the box. Then all 
samples were covered in ice before the box was sealed off. The samples were then 
transported in car to DTU’s facilities in Lyngby (Copenhagen). At arrival the box was 
opened and it was observed that there was still plenty of ice in the box. Then the samples 
were stored in a freezer at -20 °C. 
 
 
RNA extraction  
Before RNA extraction toke place a normal distribution based on the weight of the fish 
was made. Only fish that was inside the normal distribution could be chosen as samples. 
Two fish from each tank belonging to the Control and Low treatment and three from two 
different tanks in the High treatment were chosen.  One replica 8tank) from the High 
treatment had issues and was exclude from analysis.  
In order to extract and separate RNA from its cellular environment, the NucleoSpin™ 
(Macherey-Nagel, Germany) method was used. Wash Buffer RA3 and rDNase was pre-
prepared by the laboratory technicians.  
Before extraction took place the work area and all tools were cleaned with 90% ethanol 
and sterilized with UV-lights for 30 minutes. An overview of RNA extraction can be seen 
on (Figure 5) with more detailed explanation in the following section. 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
 
Figure 5 NucleoSpin RNA isolation protocol. Over 9 steps from homogenizing to elute of RNA.  
Liver tissue was removed from the tubes with a tweezer and carefully wiped to remove 
any excess RNA-later. Pieces of the sample were weighted and if needed cut to be 
approximately 30 mg.  
==PAGE:27==

For homogenizing and lysing the samples, the tissue was placed in tubes with two glass 
beads and 350 µL of beta-mercaptoethanol was added. Then they were put in the 
TissueLyser II (Qiagen, Germany) (Figure 6) and shaken at high speed for 10 minutes.  
 
Figure 6 TissueLyser II 
The liquid was extracted using a 1000 µL pipette and transferred to a new tube with a 
column/filter on top. The fluid was pipetted at the center of the filter (without direct 
contact). The tubes were then centrifuged for 1 minute. After centrifuging the filter was 
discarded.  
To bind the RNA, 350 µL of ethanol (70%) was added and the tube was vortexed twice for 
5 seconds. At this point white foam or protein in form of a white substance could appear 
in the liquid. Pipetting only the liquid was done to a new collection tube with a new filter 
and then centrifuging for 30 seconds to bind the nucleic acid (mostly RNA but also part of 
the genomic DNA) to the membrane of the filter.  
The filters were then placed in a new collection tube while the flow-through was 
discarded. In order to remove the salt from the membrane 350 µL of MDB (Membrane 
Desalting Buffer) was pipetted on the center of the filter and centrifuged for 1 minute.   
In order to digest the genomic DNA in the membrane, that needs to be completely 
removed, 10 µL rDNase and 90 µL of Reaction Buffer mixture were made per sample. They 
were combined in a tube and mixed by flickering. 95 µL of the solution was then pipetted 
directly onto the center of the membrane of the column and left to incubate at room 
temperature for 15 minutes.  
After the incubation period, the RDNase was inactivated by adding 200 µL of RAW2 Buffer 
to the membrane and centrifuged for 30 seconds two times.  
==PAGE:28==
28 
 
The filter/column was then placed in a new holding tube. To ensure that all RAW2 Buffer 
is washed away, 600 µL of RA3 Buffer were added and centrifuged for 30 seconds. The 
flow-through was discarded and the column was put back in the holding tube. A third 
wash was then performed with 250 µL of RA3 buffer and centrifuged for 2 minutes to 
ensure the membrane was completely dry.  
The column/filter was then placed in a 1,5 mL nuclease-free Collection Tube. In order to 
extract the RNA from the membrane, 40 µL of RNase-free H2O was pipetted on top of the 
membrane and centrifuged for 1 minute.  
The RNA was evaluated for purity and quantity through spectrophotometry using 
Nanodrop (ThermoFisher Scientific, USA). Approximately 1 µL of the RNA extraction was 
placed on the sensor. The Nanodrop measures absorbance of ultraviolet light at 3 
different wavelengths (230, 260 and 280 nanometers).  
Based on the Beer-Lambert law the amount of light absorbed at 260 nm is proportional 
to the concentration of nucleic acid is the sample. In order to determine what type of 
nucleic acid is present in the liquid, it calculates a ratio of the absorbed lights between the 
260 and 280 nm (A260/A280). The A260/A280 is a ratio of protein contamination while, 
pure RNA has a A260/A280 ratio of around 2,1. 
The Nanodrop also checks for contaminations by calculating a A260/A230 ratio. A 
wavelength of 230 nm is absorbed by chaotropic salts. A A260/A230 ratio above 1,8 are 
considered clean and is suitable for most downstream analysis. For RNA analysis a ratio 
of 2,3-2,4 indicates lack of chemical contaminants. 
It should be noted, that the A260/A230 ratio is more sensitive and has a larger standard 
deviation compared to the A260/A280.  
After all samples were evaluated and were found to have a A260/A280 value around 2,1, 
RNA integrity was assessed via gel electrophoresis (Figure 7). The ribosomal RNA bonds 
were visually inspected. The two ribosomal major subunits should be clearly separated 
and distinguishable. If any of the RNA was judged to be degraded, new RNA extractions 
would be made by the same process as described above.

RNA-seq analysis 
 
The overall analysis process can be described as the following 
• Check the quality of the reads 
• Trim and filter the reads if necessary 
• Align the reads to the reference genome   
• Sort the reads as required to run the read counter 
• Count the number of reads mapped to each annotated gene 
• Compare treatment groups to see differential expressed genes

alignment, HTseq-count identifies the number of reads that map to genes. DESeq2 uses the output of htseq-count to identify 
differentially expressed genes

The RNA data returned by Novogene were in the Fastq format. The first step was to check 
the quality of the generated reads. This was done with the FastQC 0.11.7 package (Sharma 
& Thines 2015). The quality of the reads was judged to be good, but the first bases of each 
read needed to be removed and was done with the Trimmomatic 0.38 package (Bolger et 
al., 2014). The following parameters was used HEADCROP:9 SLIDINGWINDOW:4:15 
MINLEN:36.   
As seen in (Figure 10) the quality of the first 9 bases were not good. They were removed 
with the HEADCROP parameter, that removes a specified number of bases from the 
beginning of the read. In this case it was the first 9 bases.  
The SLIDINGWINDOW parameter cuts the leftmost position in the window once the 
average quality within the window drops below a specified threshold.  
If a read was to short and was below a specified length, it was dropped using the MINLEN 
parameter.  
 
Alignment  
The reference genome and the annotation map used for the alignment can be found here. 
https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/assembly/GCF_000233375.1 
==PAGE:33==

Before alignment could take place, the reference genome needed to be indexed. This was 
done using the Bowtie2 2.2.4 package (Langmead & Salzberg 2012). Bowite2 is both fast 
and memory efficient. It supports gapped, local, and paired-end alignment modes and is 
well suited for comparing genomics (Misale, C. 2014). 
 
Figure 11 Tophat2 pipeline. Describes the alignment process of Tophat2 (Kim et al., 2013) 
Alignment or mapping of reads is the process where the reads are mapped to a reference 
genome. In this study the software Tophat2 was used to conduct the alignment. An 
overview of the alignment process can be seen in (Figure 11). Alignment began at step 2 
Genome alignment. The task at this step was to find the best match for the reads on the 
reference genome (Kim et al., 2013). As the reads are mapped to the reference genome, 
a dataset for aligned and unaligned reads is produced. Without alignment it would not be 
possible to produce a quantitative differential expression dataset.  
TopHat2 2.0.13 package (Kim et al., 2013) was chosen to do a reference-based assembly 
to align the trimmed reads to the reference genome. TopHat2 has shown good results and 
combines the ability to identify novel splice site with direct mapping. This produces 
accurate alignments and is well suited for highly repetitive genomes. Furthermore, 
TopHat2 uses Bowtie2 allowing it to align reads across small indels increasing accuracy 
and making it a good choice for gene transcription expression.  (Kim, D et al., 2013). 
Besides, TopHat2 is an open source software. Tophat2 was run with default parameters 
plus the G option. 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
The G option will make Tophat2 use an annotation (known transcripts) file to algin the 
reads to that virtual transcriptome. Then the reads not fully mapped to the virtual 
transcriptome will then be mapped on the genome. The reads mapped to the virtual 
transcriptome will then be converted to genomic mappings (spliced if needed) and 
merged with the read mapped to the genome.  After alignment an output file is created 
with the mapped reads to the reference genome. The next step is to count the number of 
reads that have been aligned/mapped.  
 
Read, count and sorting  
HTseq-count from the anaconda3 4.4.0 package (Anders et al., 2015) was chosen to count 
the reads for each gene. HTseq-count counts the number of reads, that have been 
sequenced and mapped to genes after alignment (Srinivasan, K et al., 2020).  
The following parameters were used -m intersection-strict --stranded = no. The mode (-m) 
with the strict intersection option was chosen, so reads had to be fully aligned in order to 
be counted. If a read were not fully aligned, it would not be part of the number of reads 
counted. The --stranded=no parameter was used, as RNA-seq data were made without 
strand-specific data. Otherwise a great number of the reads would have been lost.  
HTseq-count require a SAM or BAM file as input and the genes must be in alphabetical 
order.  Therefore, before running the HTseq-count, it was necessary to use samtools sort. 
To sort by name -n parameter was used with the -o parameter to write the file as BAM 
output.   
It also became necessary to modify the annotation table and remove all tRNA as the 
HTseq-count would not run otherwise. This was done with the following command. 
grep -v "gbkey=tRNA" ref_gff | grep -v "gbkey=rRNA" > ref_minus_tRNA.gff.  
 
Differential expression 
Differential expression analysis was made with the R package DEseq2 (Love et al., 2014). 
Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified and clustered by pairwise analysis 
between the three treatment groups (Control, Low, High). The Control was compared to 
the Low treatment and then the High treatment. The low and high concentration was also 
compared.  It was chosen to investigate genes with an adjusted p-value (Padj) under 0,1 
and divided them at another threshold where Padj was smaller than 0,05. This was done 
as relative few genes were found to be differential expressed.  
The adjusted p-value controls the false discoveries in the test, that result in differential 
expressed genes (i.e. significant results). In this analysis an adjusted p-value <0,05 and 0,1 
implies that 5% and 10% respectably of the significant tests will have a false positive. 
Unlike a normal p-value, that will give a false positive in all test (not just the significant). 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
Using an adjusted p-value will result in fewer false positives compared to a normal p-
value.  
The log2FoldChange (log2FC) is the log ratio of a transcript or gene and expresses the 
value of a gene in two different conditions. It is used to identify the differential expression 
between different samples. In this study genes with a log2FC > 0 were considered 
upregulated while a gene with log2FC < 0 was considered downregulated.   
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was made by the DEseq2 software. Samples that 
showed to be outliers compared to the majority of samples were removed.  
Gene with a very high or low log2FC > ± 20 and only expressed in few samples were 
discarded.

4. Discussion  
Peracid acid decay rate 
It has been proved in other studies, that PAA decay in aquatic solutions follows a first-
order exponential rate and depends on the biomass in the system (Pedersen et., al 2009; 
Liu et al., 2014). The PAA decay rates from this experiment all follow a first-order decay 
rate with an R2-value > 0,97 (Figure 12).  
The removal rate constant was found to be positively correlated with the initial PAA 
concentration (Table 3). In the experiment it was found that half-life T½ was around 2,3 
hours for both the Low and High treatment. In the experiment made by (Pedersen et., al 
2009) they found a T½ around 100-minutes with an initial concentration of PAA around 
1,05 mg/L, but with approximately 4 times the biomass in their experiment compared to 
the one conducted here.  
They also found that half-life decreased 30 minutes for every 10 kg in increased stocking 
density per m3. At the end of the trial in this experiment the stocking density was on 
average 14 kg m-3. Not taking the organic load (COD) into account, this should give a half-
life of around 4 hours. (Pedersen et., al 2009) also conducted a trial measuring half-life in 
water (without fish) with low, medium and high organic matter measured as COD. For an 
initial concentration of 1,0 and 2,0 mg/L PAA, the T½ was found to be 1,2 and 2,9 hours 
respectively. 
The COD concentration in the water could explain the difference in half-life between the 
two experiments. It was not measured but was judged to be in the low end. Taking this 
into account the T½ and k1 found in this experiment is very similar to the ones found in 
(Pedersen et., al 2009).   Overall, the 1st-order removal rate constant k1 and the T½ is 
judged to be reasonable.  
 
PAA effect on oxygen, growth and feed uptake 
Oxygen consumption is directly linked to metabolic activity and growth (Rossignol et al., 
2010) and is vital for breaking down macronutrients. Oxygen is an important factor in 
aquaculture and consumption is typically a combination of stocking density, water 
retention, microbial activities and feed input. In this experiment all treatments had the 
same water retention, approximately the same stocking density. Feed input was based on 
a targeted FCR and the same water source was shared. Furthermore, oxygen consumption 
was normalized based on the biomass in each tank. Therefore, the difference in SGR, FCR 
and oxygen consumption should only be a result of the PAA application.   
In this experiment there was no significant difference in SGR between treatment groups, 
but the Control treatment had a higher growth compared to the units, that were dosed 
with PAA (Table 7). This is in line with similar results found for trout (Oncorhynchus 
==PAGE:46==
46 
 
mykiss) by (Suvi et., al 2020), where they found no significant difference in SGR between 
treatments but still a lower growth in the treatments dosed with PAA.  
With regard to oxygen consumption there seems to be a negative correlation between 
PAA concentration and O2 uptake. The Control showed a higher consumption compared 
to the other two treatments, but was only significant different from the High treatment.  
There was an expectation that growth and oxygen consumption would show a correlation, 
but this was not the case. The Low treatment showed a higher oxygen uptake but smaller 
SGR and higher FCR compared to the High treatment (Table 6; Table 7; Table 8). Even 
though there was no significant difference in SGR and FCR between the groups, it was an 
unexpected result.  
No noticeable feed waste was observed at any point during the trial. If any uneaten pellets 
were found, it was very few and never from the same tank. It is judged that feed waste 
cannot explain the difference in SGR/FCR and O2 uptake.  
As there only was one replication in the High treatment, this finding could be a result of 
biological variation (Vihinen 2020).  
Another explanation could be taking the number of down/up regulated genes - compared 
to the control (Table 13; Table 14) - into account for each treatment and theorizing  that 
the fish quicker adapted to exposure of PAA in the High treatment compared to the Low 
(Liu et al., 2017), causing the salmon to have a longer period of physical stress response 
after PAA dosing leading to exhaustion and elevated metabolic activity that would reduce 
growth and increase oxygen consumption (Magnadóttir 2006; Harries & Bird 2000). But 
this is purely speculation and would require further investigation to determine.  
Overall, with so few replicates it was not possible to determine the reason for the lack of 
correlation between O2 uptake and SGR/FCR between the Low and High treatment.  
 
Gene expression as a result of PAA exposure 
All treatments were fed with the same type of feed. The amount of feed was based on the 
biomass in each tank. The tanks were subjected to the same photoperiod (12h light and 
12 dark). The temperature in all systems were the same as the water retention time and 
source was the same for all tanks.  Furthermore, the process of weighting the fish was 
also the same. And as fish all came from the same family it is assumed that the differential 
expressed genes are a result from the different exposure to peracetic acid. 
The number of down-regulated transcripts exceeded those for up-regulated for all 
treatments for both p-values (Table 12). A total of 12 genes regulating for growth and 5 
for stress were found. Out of the 12 growth related genes, 8 were down regulated and 5 
up regulated.   
==PAGE:47==
47 
 
All genes were expressed with a log2FC value between -2,5 and 2,5. This indicates that 
there was poor correlation among samples between treatments (Table 13; Table 14; Table 
15). This is also indicated in the PCA plot (Figure 14) as samples don’t form clusters based 
on their treatment. This is also backed up by the results of the DE analysis as the 
Control/High and Control/Low only have three expressed genes in common, period 
circadian clock 3 (PER3), FYVE, RhoGEF and PH domain-containing protein 3-like (FGD3) 
and nuclear receptor subfamily 1 group D member 1-like (NR1D1). It should be noted that 
the expression of NR1D1 in the Control/High treatment is an isoform (Table 13). 
It is not surprising that more genes involved in growth are differential expressed 
compared to stress. Peracetic acid is a strong oxidant and is shown in other studies to 
cause an antioxidant response (Soleng et., al 2019; Suvi et., al 2020). In an experiment 
conducted by (Soleng et., al 2019) they found, that salmons antioxidant defense was 
mostly responsive in the gills and not in the liver.  
In the following section genes will be discussed in more detail. It was chosen to discuss 
PER3, FGD3 and NR1D1 for the reason that they were differential expressed in both 
Control/High and the Control/Low analysis. It was also chosen to discuss IGF-1 as it has 
been identified as key gene for growth (Solberg et., al 2012; Wood et al., 2005).  
 
PER3 (period circadian clock 3) 
Period circadian clock 3 (PER3) is one of the three genes differential expressed for both 
the Control/High and the Control/Low analysis and was the second and third highest 
regulated respectively and therefor it was chosen as the first gene to discuss.  
The function of PER3 is not studied very much in salmon, but is known to be an important 
factor for the interaction of the circadian system, metabolism and physiology (Haung et 
et al., 2010). The circadian system is impacted by light, temperature, feed and 
environmental stressors.  
This gene has been linked to a stress response to oxidative stressors in humans and 
rodents (Koch et al., 2017; Wilking et al., 2013). Even though the circadian system is well 
studied, the interaction with oxidative stress is not. It was found that exposure to oxidants 
causes dysregulation in the circadian rhythms (Castanon-Cervantes et al., 2010). It was 
not stated by the authors if PER3 was up- or down-regulated.  
In this experiment PER3 was less expressed (down-regulated) in both the Low and High 
compared to the Control. It was found that a deletion of PER3 will promote adipogenesis 
(formation of fat cells) in mice (Aggarwal et al., 2017). If this is also the case in salmon, it 
could cause unwanted effects on the fish. An increase in adipogenesis could lead to excess 
fat deposit, that could compromise both the fish health and quality of the product leading 
to a lower market price (Salmerón, C. 2018). The ratio of macronutrient in the fish were 
==PAGE:48==
48 
 
not investigated and therefore it is not possible to conclude if PAA exposure influenced 
the meat quality.  
All treatments were exposed to the same feed, water temperature and photoperiod, 
meaning these could not have caused a transcription of the PER3 gene.  
The results in this study suggest that PAA may have an impact on the regulation of the 
circadian rhythms as PER3 were down-regulated in both the Control/High and the 
Control/Low differential expression analysis.  Furthermore, a concentration of 1,2 mg/L 
seems to be enough to cause a response in transcription, as PER3 was not differentially 
expressed in the Low/High analysis.   
In a study made by (Wang et al., 2012) they discovered that that prolonged restraint stress 
causes up-regulation of PER3 in brain tissue of mice. This indicates there is still many 
unknowns in regard to PER3s function on stress and expression in different tissue across 
species.   
 
FGD3 (FYVE, RhoGEF and PH domain-containing protein 3-like) 
It was not possible to find any literature on salmon or other fish species, that described 
the function of FGD3. In humans and mice, it is suspected to activate the Cell division 
control protein 42 homolog (CDC42) protein (Pasteris et al., 2000). In an experiment made 
by (Levicán-Asenjo et al., 2019) salmon were exposed to the infectious pancreatic necrosis 
virus (IPNV) and higher levels of CDC42 expression were linked to a partial decrease in 
IPNV infection, suggesting it plays a role in immune response in salmon. An in vitro study 
made by (Mtichell et al., 2013) suggests that CDC42 could be part of redox regulation. This 
fits the finding of DE analysis, as FGD3 is upregulated for both Control/High and the 
Control/Low DE analysis.  
Also, in humans CDC42 regulates signaling pathways, that control a variety of cell 
functions including cell migration (Sakabe et al., 2017). As cell migration is part of wound 
healing and immune response, it could be theorized it is a part of a stress response to the 
strong oxidative potential of PAA. But further investigation of the function of FGD3 and 
CDC42 in salmon is required to determine this. 
 
NR1D1 (Nuclear receptor subfamily 1 group D member 1) 
It has been found in other studies that NR1D1 protects against oxidative and 
environmental stress in mice (Yang et al., 2014). In this experiment it is up-regulated in 
both the High and Low treatment, this could indicate that it is the same case for salmon. 
Unlike the other two genes that were DE in both Low and High treatment NR1D1 is 
expressed with an adjusted p-value < 0,1, increasing the risk for false positives. 
==PAGE:49==
49 
 
Like PER3, NR1D1 is a core circadian gene. As two of the three genes that are differential 
expressed in in High and Low treatment is involved with regulation of the circadian rhythm 
this could indicate that PAA exposure influence genes related to the circadian rhythm.  
It has been shown that NR1D1 represses transcription (Cho et al., 2012). One of the most 
common targeted genes for repression in mice is BMAL1 (Yin et al., 2010). The BMAL1 was 
however not found to be down-regulated in this experiment. In addition, NR1D1 also 
targets a large number of other genes involved with metabolism and cell survival (Bugge 
et al., 2012; Cho et al., 2012).  
 
In humans up-regulation of the NR1D1 gene have been associated with obesity (Garaulet 
et al., 2014). However, the cause of obesity was linked to the repression of BMAL1. As 
BMAL1 was not found to be repressed in this experiment it is unclear if an up-regulation 
of NR1D1 would lead to a higher fat content of the fish.  
 
IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor I) 
In the Control/Low DE analysis IGF-1 is expressed with an adjusted p-value < 0,05 and is 
downregulated. It has been shown in other studies that IGF-1 mRNA levels in the liver in 
salmons are positively correlated with the growth rate (Duan et al., 1993). IGF-1 is part of 
the regulation in most of the physiological processes in fish including growth and 
metabolism (Duan et al., 1993; Reinecke et al., 2005). Furthermore, it has also been 
documented that salmonids reduced their feeding activities as a response to stress 
(Woodward & Stranger 1987).  
The less of expression of IGF-1 in the Low treatment (compared to the Control) could be 
a reason for the low SGR and high FCR compared to the other two treatments (Table 7; 
Table 8) while still having a higher O2 consumption compared to the High treatment. As 
no noticeable feed waste was observed in any of the treatments, it is judged that lack of 
feed intake is not the cause of low transcription of IGF-1.  
It is, however, peculiar that IGF-1 was not differential expressed in either the Control/High 
or Low/High DE analysis. If PAA was the cause, it should be found also in the Control/High 
analysis. However, it has been documented that carps (Cyprinus carpio) show lower levels 
of cortisol in the water as a result of repeated applications of PAA (Liu et al., 2017).  
It can be theorized that the fish in the High treatment adapted quicker to the PAA 
exposure. But there have not been any experiments conducted linking adaption rate to 
concentration of PAA or other disinfection agents.

5. Conclusion 
Weekly exposure to peracetic acid lowers the growth of salmon but not significantly. 
Mortality was initially higher for the High treatment in the first half of the experiment, but 
almost the same in the last half for all treatments. This could indicate that salmon have 
an initial adaption period to PAA. After this initial adaption period, a PAA concentration 
of 2,4 mg/L appear to be equally as safe in terms of mortality to apply as the lower 
concentration of 1,2 mg/L.  
In an industry setting the related cost of a lower growth rate by using PAA is most likely 
offset by the improvement in water quality and lower risk of harmful pathogen and 
disease outbreaks.  
When comparing the results from the differential gene expression analysis and the 
FCR/SGR it seems that the ability of salmon to adapt to PAA could be concentration 
dependent. The DE analysis of the Control/Low treatment was the only one where IGF-1 
(insulin-like growth factor I) was differential expressed and down-regulated.  
The IGF-1 is a key growth gene, and if a weekly dosing of PAA with a concentration of 1,2 
mg/L after six weeks results in down-regulation, while treatment with a higher 
concentration does not, it could indicate (at least on the short term) there is a favorable 
threshold in concentration and it should be above 1,2 mg/L. 
PER3 (period circadian clock 3) was one of the three genes, that was differential expressed 
in both the Control/Low and Control/High analysis, and one of two genes involved with 
the circadian rhythms. PER3 was down-regulated in both analyses, but was most 
differential expressed in Control/Low analysis compared to Control/High. This suggest 
that oxidative stressors have an impact on PER3 regulation in farmed salmon. And 
adaption may be concentration dependent at least on the short term. A dysregulation of 
PER3 has been linked to adipogenesis (buildup of fat cells).  
The second gene involved with circadian rhythm was NR1D1. It had an adjusted p-value 
<0,1 for both the Control/High and the Control/Low differential expression analysis. It was 
up-regulated in both treatments. Not much is known about NR1D1 function in salmon, 
but an up-regulation of NR1D1 in humans and mice has been linked to obesity.  
As two genes linked to the circadian rhythm have been differentially expressed as a result 
of exposure to peracid acid, this could indicate that PAA may have an impact on circadian 
rhythm. Both the PER3 and NR1D1 have been linked to adipogenesis, which may indicate 
that PAA could lead to a fish with higher fat content and thus lower the quality of the 
product resulting in a lower market price. The ratio of macronutrient in the fish was not 
investigated, therefore it is unknown if that is the case. On an industrial scale it is difficult 
to predict the long-term consequences of a dysregulation of the PER3/NR1D1 genes. More 
investigation in this area is needed.  
==PAGE:51==
51 
 
Overall, more down-regulated genes related to growth were differentially expressed in 
the Control/Low analyses compared to the Control/High. The Control/Low also had more 
up-regulated genes involved with stress.  The Low treatment also had a worse FCR and 
SGR compared to the High treatment.  
It is unclear if adaption to PAA in this experiment would be the same if dosing was done 
on a biweekly or monthly base. Or if the adaption would change on an industrial scale as 
exposure time most likely would be lower as a result of a higher organic load.  
It is also unknown how long-term impact on the circadian rhythm will affect the fish health 
and the quality of the meat.   
It was somewhat unexpected that a lower concentration of PAA caused a down-regulation 
of IGF-1 while a higher concentration did not. Before this finding is applied to any fish 
farm, it is recommended that this result should be validated.  
Based on the findings in this experiment it is clear that PAA causes initial stress in the fish. 
Results suggest that there is an adaptation period and it may be concentration dependent. 
PAA may have an impact on the circadian rhythm but the consequence of this is unknown.  
The findings in this experiment could indicate it may be favorable to use a concentration 
around 2 mg/L instead of the current industry standard, that is 1 mg/L.
