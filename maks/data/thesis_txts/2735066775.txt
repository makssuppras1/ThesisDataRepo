Abstract
This thesis explores the implementation of dynamic pricing and allocation strategies
in Autonomous Mobility on Demand (AMoD) systems through a novel integration of the
Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) algorithm with graph neural networks. The approach aims to
optimize fleet management and pricing in real-time, enhancing operational efficiency and
user satisfaction within urban environments. A case study modeled on New York City’s
urban layout employs comprehensive simulations to evaluate the model’s performance
against traditional static pricing and random pricing models.
Results demonstrate that the proposed SAC-GNN model significantly outperforms
baseline strategies, achieving a 40% increase in financial rewards while maintaining service
levels under varying demand conditions. Despite a decrease in demand satisfaction by
23.1% compared to the static pricing model, the SAC-GNN model exhibits a superior
ability to manage and allocate resources efficiently, suggesting a promising avenue for
future urban transportation systems. The findings highlight the model’s potential in
dynamically adjusting pricing to match real-time demand, thus optimizing the balance
between operational profitability and customer service.
This research contributes to the field of intelligent transportation systems by show-
casing the effectiveness of machine learning techniques in enhancing the operational dy-
namics of AMoD systems. It opens new paths for integrating advanced computational
methods in urban mobility solutions, aiming for sustainable, efficient, and user-centric
transportation networks.
Keywords— autonomous mobility-on-demand systems, reinforcement learning, Graph
Convolutional neural network, dynamic pricing, Soft Actor-Critic

Chapter 1
Introduction
According to the latest projections by the United Nations, the global population is
expected to reach 8.5 billion by 2030 and further increase to 9.7 billion by 2050 [1].
This growth is especially significant in urban areas, where 57% of the world’s population
currently resides, a figure anticipated to rise to 60% by 2030 and 68% by 2050 [2]. The
increasing number of people in towns clearly points to rising problems concerning urban
transport, as the infrastructure is not scaling appropriately to meet the growing demand.
According to OECD projections, passenger demand is expected to rise by 79% under
the Ambition scenario1 and by 65% under the High Ambition scenario2 by 2050 [3].
Additionally, the recent changes in consumer habits related to COVID-19 have presented
significant challenges for urban roads. Notably, there has been an increase in private
car usage, as people tend to feel more comfortable in their vehicles rather than sharing
transportation with others.
Currently, private cars are a common choice for commuting within cities due to their
convenient point-to-point transportation. However, they are a major contributor to traffic
congestion and pollution. In the U.S., drivers experience significant delays, with averages
of 155 hours in Chicago and 134 hours in Boston, marking them as the most congested
cities in the country. Similarly, in the UK, London leads in congestion with 156 hours
lost annually per driver, ranking it as the world’s most congested city in 2023. European
cities are not far behind, with Paris and Palermo experiencing 138 and 121 hours of
delays, respectively [4]. Moreover, the majority of these private vehicles use combustion
engines, significantly impacting the environment.
In contrast to this challenging scenario, recent years have witnessed the rise of a new
and promising model of personal urban transportation, known as Mobility on Demand
(MoD). MoD revolutionizes urban travel by blending the convenience of privately-owned
cars with greater flexibility and environmental sustainability. This model distributes elec-
tric vehicles across cities, available at multiple accessible locations. Users can effortlessly
locate the nearest vehicle station, unlock a vehicle using their mobile phone, and drive it
to a station near their destination. This approach not only eases the burden of parking
but also significantly reduces reliance on fossil fuels and decreases pollution, assuming
the electricity for these vehicles is generated through environmentally friendly methods.
MoD, therefore, stands as a beacon of innovation, addressing critical urban transporta-

tion concerns and signaling a shift towards more sustainable and efficient urban mobility
solutions.
Mobility-on-Demand (MoD) systems, while offering innovative approaches to urban
transportation, also face significant challenges.
One major challenge is linked to the
unique patterns of how and when people travel in cities [5]. For instance, the distribution
of trips is often uneven, with more people traveling towards certain areas like downtown
during mornings and the reverse in evenings. This creates an imbalance in the MoD
system, as some areas may end up with too many vehicles while others have too few. As
a result, some areas may experience an accumulation of vehicles, while others might face
a shortage, making the system prone to disturbances and less efficient in meeting the
dynamic needs of urban mobility. To mitigate this, operators can implement strategies
like fleet rebalancing throughout the day, which involves relocating idle vehicles to areas
with anticipated higher demand.
The advent of autonomous vehicles (AVs) in MoD systems marks a significant leap
in fleet management and passenger experience. These vehicles can centrally coordinate
operations without manual intervention, efficiently redistributing themselves to meet
dynamic demands and navigating autonomously to charging stations. This capability not
only enhances operational efficiency but also addresses challenges like the rebalancing of
idle vehicles.
For an Autonomous Mobility on Demand (AMoD) system to be a reliable and perma-
nent option in urban transportation, it must consistently meet user expectations. This
reliability depends on the consistent availability of vehicles, achievable through effec-
tive vehicle rebalancing strategies. Rebalancing can be managed either directly by the
operator or indirectly by incentivizing users. Encouraging users to move vehicles from
low-demand areas via dynamic pricing is a strategic approach. Essentially, users receive
financial incentives to relocate vehicles, which helps achieve an optimal distribution of
vehicles while minimizing the costs associated with idle fleet rebalancing. Integrating an
efficient fleet management policy with dynamic pricing enables revenue maximization and
cost reduction. This synergy not only minimizes rebalancing expenses but also reduces
customer wait times by appropriately adjusting supply to match induced demand.
In this master’s thesis, I explore the use of graph neural networks for central control in
Autonomous Mobility on Demand (AMoD) systems, with a specific focus on designing an
optimal rebalancing strategy through dynamic pricing. I adopt a reinforcement learning
approach to facilitate the adaptive relocation of idle vehicles within an AMoD fleet of
electric vehicles (EVs). My research methodology includes constructing a graph-based
representation of the transportation network, where nodes represent distinct urban areas
and edges denote the connections between them. A critical aspect of my research is the
decision-making process for setting ride prices between every Origin-Destination (O-D)
pair.
I propose that graph neural networks offer several beneficial features for this applica-
tion and introduce an actor-critic model as a comprehensive framework. This model is
designed to develop proactive, scalable, and transferable policies for the rebalancing of
AMoD systems. Such policies are anticipated to improve the efficiency and effectiveness
of urban transportation through the application of dynamic pricing and strategic vehicle
distribution.

1.1
Related work
The current research on controlling AMoD systems is primarily divided into three
distinct categories. The first category utilizes rule-based heuristics [6][7] which are known
for their computational efficiency and ease of implementation, yet they often fall short
in achieving optimal results within complex transportation networks.
The second category focuses on Model Predictive Control (MPC) methods [8][9].
Specifically, the authors in [9] examine a fleet of EVs and propose an MPC approach
that optimizes routing and scheduling, taking into account energy consumption. Mean-
while, [8] introduces a data-driven algorithm. These MPC methods, grounded in network
flow models, require solving an open-loop optimization problem at each time step. This
process generates a sequence of control actions over a receding horizon. However, typ-
ically, only the initial action is executed. Such methods face scalability challenges in
extensive AMoD networks, largely due to their formulation as large-scale linear or inte-
ger programming problems.
The third category, which is of particular relevance to this work, emphasizes the
development of learning-based approaches [10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16]. These approaches
focus on crafting efficient algorithms through reinforcement learning, aiming to maintain
a balance between computational efficiency and optimality in complex AMoD control
scenarios.
In their work, Fluri et al. [10] introduced a cascaded Q-learning method for managing
AMoD systems centrally. This layered approach effectively lessens the quantity of state-
action pairs, facilitating a more efficient learning process.
Nonetheless, this method,
which focuses solely on the existing distribution of vehicles, could be less effective in
situations where travel demand changes over time and traffic conditions are dynamic.
In such scenarios, making decisions based on immediate circumstances could lead to
suboptimal outcomes.
Gueriau et al. [14] investigated the use of decentralized reinforcement learning ap-
proaches, concentrating on the formulation of dynamic policies for rebalancing and as-
signing ride requests. These policies are independently developed and implemented by
each autonomous vehicle employing a Q-learning policy. Although this method is advan-
tageous in terms of computational efficiency, it presents a potential drawback in terms of
reduced overall system performance and optimality, owing to the absence of synchronized
actions among the vehicles.
In my research, the focus is on vehicle rebalancing at the system level, which is crucial
for providers of Autonomous Mobility on Demand services. The challenge of coordinating
the actions of all vehicles simultaneously has led to a growing interest in using centralized
Reinforcement Learning for AMoD management. My approach is similar to the central-
ized deep RL method detailed in Mao et al. [13]. However, there are significant differences
between our methods. While their study also employs centralized deep RL, their model
is distinct from mine. Their investigation is dedicated solely to solving the vehicle rebal-
ancing problem and does not consider the impact of ride pricing on controlling queues.In
general, reinforcement learning-based methods show computational tractability without
appreciably sacrificing optimality.
The study of Turan et. al, 2020 [16] focuses on optimizing routing, battery charging,
and pricing for autonomous electric vehicles in ride-sharing systems. It starts with a static
planning approach and shifts to a dynamic policy using deep reinforcement learning,
3
==PAGE:19==
tailored for variable trip demands and energy costs. Unlike my study, this paper does
not utilize graph neural networks and employs a different form of reinforcement learning,
not the actor-critic method.
The integration of reinforcement learning with graph neural networks presents a
strategic approach for addressing the intricate challenges present in Autonomous Mo-
bility on Demand (AMoD) systems. The foundational work by Gammelli et al,[11] which
investigates graph neural network reinforcement learning in AMoD systems, has signif-
icantly informed the development of the methodology employed in this thesis. Their
approach to deriving passenger flow by solving a matching problem has been adopted as
a key component of the two-step decision-making framework for controlling an AMoD
fleet. This process is critical for the efficient allocation of vehicles to demand spots, serv-
ing as a precursor to the dynamic pricing strategy that defines the second step of the
framework.
Moreover, this thesis extends Gammelli et al’s, exploration by incorporating their
findings on optimization techniques, notably the use of stochastic gradient ascent and the
Adam optimizer for training the Soft-Actor-Critic (SAC) model. By adopting a learning
rate of 0.003 and training the model over 10,000 episodes with a discount factor of 0.97,
this work closely aligns with the parameters and approaches outlined by Gammelli et
al. to optimize both immediate and future outcomes. This strategic adoption ensures
a balanced rate of model updates, facilitating rapid learning while avoiding the pitfalls
of overshooting the optimal solution. Additionally, the focus on Served Demand as a
key metric for evaluating the effectiveness of the AMoD control approach is directly
inspired by their research, highlighting the importance of successfully completed trips as
a measure of system performance.
In summary, the insights provided by [11] have been instrumental in shaping the
operational framework of this thesis. By leveraging their investigations into graph neural
network reinforcement learning and optimization methodologies, this research has been
able to propose a comprehensive model that enhances the operational dynamics and
efficiency of AMoD systems.
This review reveals that while there is a comprehensive exploration of various algo-
rithms in RL within the context of urban transportation, there is a noticeable gap in the
literature concerning the development of neural network architectures that effectively
utilize the graph-like nature of urban transport networks. To date, centralized real-time
management of electric Autonomous Mobility-on-Demand (AMoD) systems, particularly
concerning the redistribution of idle vehicles using dynamic pricing in a Graph Neural
Network within a reinforcement learning framework, remains unexplored. In my study,
I suggest that utilizing the inherent graph structure of urban transportation networks,
particularly through dynamic pricing strategies, offers a promising method to address
critical issues. This approach includes effectively generalizing learned behaviors beyond
the confines of initial training environments and efficiently gaining insights from limited
datasets.

Chapter 2
Reinforcement Learning
This section introduces the fundamental philosophy of Reinforcement Learning (RL).
It includes a discussion on the basic mathematical principles that underpin this area
of machine learning. Additionally, it provides a categorization of various Reinforcement
Learning techniques. The purpose of this section is to familiarize the reader with the core
concepts of RL, thereby enabling them to comprehend the specific RL model employed
in this research more easily and clearly.
2.1
Introduction to Reinforcement Learning
Reinforcement learning represents a significant area within the realm of machine learn-
ing, focusing on developing policies and control strategies to interact with a complex
environment, based on experience [17]. Occupying a unique space where control theory
meets machine learning, RL stands as a key player in the quest for achieving generalized
AI and autonomous systems. The core of both machine learning and control theory is
grounded in optimization principles. Similarly, in order to learn how to interact with
the environment, RL employs a set of optimisation approaches within an experiential
framework.
Reinforcement Learning differs notably from other learning types.
In Supervised
Learning, datasets with examples and labels are used, where each example has a correct
label for classification or output for regression problems. Unsupervised Learning, on the
other hand, deals with unlabeled datasets, identifying hidden structures within the data.
RL, however, focuses on decision-making and action evaluation rather than prediction.
An RL agent will learn from the environment by interacting with it (trial and error) and
obtaining rewards (negative or positive) as feedback for actions performed. This feedback
doesn’t clearly indicate the ’best’ possible action, requiring the agent to learn to choose
actions that maximize long-term rewards. Thus, RL sits between Supervised Learning,
with its strong, labeled data feedback, and Unsupervised Learning, which lacks feedback
and labels, due to its nature of partial and indirect feedback through rewards.
The primary objective in RL is the development of a robust control strategy or ac-
tion sequence, through either positive or negative reinforcement. Reinforcement learning
draws inspiration from biological processes, similar to how animals adapt to their sur-
roundings by learning from both positive and negative outcomes of their actions. His-
torically, the evolution of RL and the broader field of machine learning have been closely
associated with the study of animal behavior and the foundational aspects of decision-
6
==PAGE:22==
making, control, and learning processes [18, 19]. A classical example of how animals
learn to link environmental signals with food rewards is Pavlov’s dog [20]. The concept
of ’reinforcement’ in both human and animal behavior refers to the use of rewards, like
food, to encourage desired actions. In the context of animals, reinforcement is rooted in
cellular and molecular learning mechanisms.
Numerous books and researches cover the expansive subject of deep reinforcement
learning, a field that has seen significant developments over nearly a hundred years.
These advancements are continually reshaping the field. The purpose of this chapter is
not to cover every aspect exhaustively but to familiarize readers with essential principles
and prominent methodologies, particularly in the context of the work presented in this
master thesis.

Policy
A reinforcement learning agent perceives its environment’s state, denoted as s, and
makes decisions or actions, represented as a, guided by a policy π. This policy is con-
tinually refined and optimized through a learning process, with the goal of enhancing
future rewards, indicated as r. The core objective in reinforcement learning is typically
to solve an optimization challenge, where the focus is to develop and fine-tune the policy
π(s, a).
π(s, a) = Pr(a = a | s = s)
(2.1)
This function illustrates the probability of choosing an action a in a given state s, with
the objective of maximizing total future rewards. In its simplest iteration, this policy

impractical because of limitations in resources. As a result, π is often estimated using a

A Markov Decision Process is composed of a set of states, denoted as S, a set of

P(s′, s, a) = Pr(sk+1 = s′ | sk = s, ak = a),

2.3
Categorization of RL Techniques
Almost every problem in machine learning and control theory encompasses complex
optimization challenges. For machine learning, this entails fine-tuning a model’s param-
eters to achieve the best match with training data, as evaluated by a loss function. In
9
==PAGE:25==
control theory, optimization focuses on enhancing a range of control performance met-
rics while adhering to dynamic constraints. Reinforcement learning, which lies at the
convergence of machine learning and control theory, follows this pattern as well.
Numerous methods exist to acquire an optimal policy π, the primary aim in RL. A
key distinction within RL is between model-based and model-free approaches. If the
environment model is known, various techniques can be applied to learn the optimal
policy or value function. These include policy iteration or value iteration, which are
types of dynamic programming based on the Bellman equation. Conversely, when an
environment model is absent, other tactics like Q-learning are necessary.

not exhaustive. The initial significant division is between model-based and model-free RL approaches.
Within model-free RL, a further split exists between methods that use gradients and those that do
not. Lastly, within the gradient-free techniques, there’s a distinction between on-policy and off-policy
methods.
The optimization task in reinforcement learning can be especially complex for sys-
tems that are high-dimensional, feature non-linear and stochastic dynamics, and offer
sparse and delayed rewards. Function approximation methods, like neural networks, can
enhance these techniques by approximating the policy π, the value function V, or the
quality function Q. This is further elaborated in later sections, making these approaches
more applicable to high-dimensional systems. Discussions below will cover model-based,
model-free, and deep learning strategies.
2.3.1
Model-Based RL
This segment offers a summarized perspective of key model-based optimization and
control strategies. Some might argue that these methods don’t fall under the umbrella of
reinforcement learning since they don’t necessarily involve acquiring an optimal strategy
through experimental learning. Nevertheless, there is a close connection. One can learn a
model via trial-and-error and subsequently apply this model using these methods, which
would then be classified as reinforcement learning.
In the simplified scenario where there is a known model in the form of a finite Markov
Decision Process, one can learn the optimal policy or value function. This is achiev-
10
==PAGE:26==
able through methods known as policy iteration or value iteration. Both are types of
dynamic programming that utilize the Bellman equation. Dynamic programming stands
out as an effective method utilized in various domains such as optimal nonlinear con-
trol, reinforcement learning, and more. This approach features algorithms that simplify
the optimization process mathematically, facilitating the introduction of vital concepts
employed broadly in these areas.
Broadly speaking, the optimization in Reinforcement Learning is connected to the
domain of optimal nonlinear control. This field has its foundations in variational theory,
tracing back to the times of Bernoulli and the Brachistochrone problem, which dates
nearly four hundred years ago.
Dynamic programming
Introduced by Richard E. Bellman [21, 22], dynamic programming is a mathemati-
cal approach designed to tackle complex, multi-step optimization challenges, typical in
decision-making and control tasks. Examples of its application in reinforcement learning
include policy iteration and value iteration, which are detailed later. Dynamic program-
ming addresses these multi-step optimization tasks by breaking down the extensive prob-
lem into smaller, recursive sub-problems, allowing for the optimization of local decisions.
This method hinges on Bellman’s principle of optimality, which asserts that an effective
multi-step control policy should also exhibit local optimality at every sub-sequence of
steps.
The Bellman equation 2.5 suggests that the extensive optimization problem encom-
passing the entire trajectory of state-action pairs (sk, ak) can be decomposed into a re-
cursive optimization at each point in the trajectory. Provided that the value function at
the subsequent point s′ is known, solving the optimization at point s becomes achievable.
This is done by optimizing the policy π(s, a) at that specific point.
Naturally, this presupposes knowledge of the value function at all potential subsequent
states sk +1. This knowledge depends on the current state sk, the present action ak, and
the dynamics that control the system. The complexity increases for dynamics that do
not conform to the MDP model, such as the nonlinear control scenarios discussed in the
following subsection.
In cases of even moderately sized problems, the issue of dimensionality becomes a
significant challenge, necessitating the use of approximate methods for solutions. Where
feasible, dynamic programming—which involves dividing a large problem into smaller,
interrelated sub-problems—offers a solution that is globally optimal. There are primarily
two strategies for dynamic programming, known as top-down and bottom-up approaches.
Top down: In this method, a table of subproblems is kept and referenced while
addressing larger problems. When encountering a new problem, this table is con-
sulted to determine if the relevant sub-problem has already been resolved. If it
has, the solution is used; if not, the sub-problem is solved. This method of tabular
storage is known as memoization. However, it can become exceedingly complex for
many problems due to its combinatorial nature.
Bottom up: This technique starts by solving the smallest sub-problems and then
progressively combining these solutions to tackle larger problems. It can be visu-
alized as a reverse engineering process, beginning from every potential goal state.
11
==PAGE:27==
The approach involves identifying the optimal preceding action for each state, then
progressively tracing steps backward - two steps back, then three, and so on.

2.3.2
Model-Free RL and Q-Learning
Policy iteration and value iteration both utilize the quality function Q(s, a), indicating
the combined desirability of a specific state/action pair. These strategies, policy iteration
(2.12) and value iteration (2.13), are part of model-based reinforcement learning, assum-

or model-based estimation of the next state s′ from the current state and action s and a
respectively. Utilizing this model enables forecasting and optimizing across all potential
actions.
In situations where a model isn’t present, various reinforcement learning methods
can be employed to learn efficient decision and control policies for interacting with the
environment.
One direct strategy involves initially learning the environment’s model
through a data-driven active learning approach, followed by applying the standard model-
based methods previously mentioned. Nonetheless, this approach might not be practical
for extremely large or especially unstructured systems.

Monte Carlo Learning
In the most basic form of experiential learning, the value function V or the quality
function Q can be deduced using Monte Carlo methods, which randomly sample the state-
action space through the repeated evaluation of numerous policies. These Monte Carlo
methods necessitate that the RL task is episodic, with a clear beginning and end within
a finite number of actions, culminating in a total cumulative reward at the episode’s
conclusion. An example of episodic RL tasks is games.
In this approach, the cumulative reward at the end of the task estimates the value
function V or the quality function Q by evenly distributing the final reward across all
intermediate states or state-action pairs. This method, the simplest for addressing the
credit assignment problem, shares credit equally among all steps. However, due to this
even distribution, Monte Carlo learning tends to be less efficient in sampling, particularly
in scenarios with sparse rewards.

Temporal Difference Learning
Temporal Difference learning (TD) [23, 24] a sample-based learning strategy, stands
apart from Monte Carlo learning. Unlike Monte Carlo, which is limited to episodic tasks,
TD learning continuously adapts by using current estimates of the value function V or
quality function Q, similar to dynamic programming methods.
TD learning aims to replicate learning in animals, where they often learn from time-
delayed rewards through environmental signals acting as secondary reinforcers before
the actual reward, as in Pavlov’s dog experiment [20].
As a result, TD learning is
generally more efficient in terms of sample use than Monte Carlo learning, leading to
lower variability. However, this efficiency comes with a trade-off of introducing some bias
due to the bootstrapping process.

Monte Carlo and TD learning illustrate the bias-variance tradeoff in machine learning.

Exploration vs exploitation: ϵ-greedy actions
In Q-learning, incorporating random exploration is essential, and various methods
exist for this. The ϵ-greedy algorithm is one such approach for action selection. Here,
the agent usually selects the current optimal action ak = maxa Q(sk, a) , based on the
present Q function, with a probability of 1 - ϵ, where ϵ ∈[0, 1] . Alternatively, with a
probability of ϵ, the agent opts for a random action.
Higher ϵ values lead to more random exploration. Generally, ϵ starts high, often at
1, and gradually decreases during training. As the Q function gets refined, the agent
increasingly chooses the current optimal action. This strategy is akin to simulated an-
nealing in optimization, which simulates the process of a metal achieving a low-energy
state through controlled cooling, mirroring the gradual reduction of randomness in action
selection.

3.1
Introduction to Graph Neural Networks
In recent years, Neural Networks have gained significant attention for their effective-
ness and accessibility in areas like Pattern Recognition and Data Mining. The field of
Deep Learning, applying techniques such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), Re-
current Neural Networks (RNN), and autoencoders, has made advancements in areas like
object detection and speech recognition, encouraging extensive research and development
in Neural Networks.
Deep Learning is particularly proficient at processing Euclidean datasets such as im-
ages, text, and videos. However, it’s also crucial to consider scenarios where data is
graphically represented (non-Euclidean), involving complex relationships between ele-
ments. GNNs emerge as a solution in this context.
What is Graph
As the name suggests, the most important component of Graph Neural Networks is
a graph.
A graph is a collection of two main components: nodes (or vertices) and edges. Each
node is connected to other nodes via edges, forming a network. Generally, a graph is de-
noted as G=(V, E), where V represents the set of nodes, and E signifies the connections
between them. In a directed graph, the edges have arrows indicating directional rela-
tionships. Conversely, in undirected graphs, the edges lack directionality (3.1). Graphs
are commonly depicted using an Adjacency matrix, A. For a graph with N nodes, this
matrix is sized NxN.
Analyzing graphs presents certain challenges. One key issue is that graphs do not
reside in Euclidean spaces, making them incompatible with conventional coordinate sys-

Problems Involving Graph-Structured Data
There are three primary graph prediction task categories:
1. Node Classification
2. Link Prediction
3. Graph Classification
Node-level tasks focus on forecasting the identity or role of individual nodes in a
graph. This problem is often approached through semi-supervised learning, where labels
are only available for a subset of the graph. Common use cases for node classification
encompass citation networks, Reddit threads, YouTube content, and Facebook social
connections.
Link prediction task involve identifying the connections between entities in a graph
and forecasting whether there is a link between two entities.
For instance, in social
networks, this can involve predicting whether two people are likely to become friends
based on their existing connections and interactions. This task would involve analyzing
the network of friendships and interactions to identify potential new connections.
In graph-level tasks, the objective is to predict attributes or properties of the entire
graph. For example, in a network graph representing a computer system, one might aim
to predict the overall robustness of the network or its sensitivity to cyber attacks. This
involves analyzing the entire graph structure to assess the system’s overall behavior or
vulnerability.

3.3
Graph Neural Network Types
The Graph Neural Network, as its name suggests, is a type of neural network specif-
ically designed for application to graph structures. It offers an efficient approach for
prediction tasks at the node, edge, and graph levels.
In literature, there are primarily three categories of graph neural networks:
1. Recurrent Graph Neural Network
2. Spatial Convolutional Network
3. Spectral Convolutional Network
The fundamental idea behind GNNs is that a node’s identity and characteristics are
largely determined by its neighboring nodes and their interconnections. To illustrate
this, consider a node devoid of its surrounding neighbors and connections; it essentially
becomes devoid of meaningful information.
Thus, a node’s essence is shaped by its
adjacent nodes and the links it has with them.
Bearing this in mind, each node is assigned a state x that encapsulates its unique
concept. This node state x is then used to generate an output o, essentially making a
decision related to the node’s concept. The final state of a node, known as the “node
embedding” xn, captures its comprehensive identity. The primary function of GNNs is to
compute this “node embedding” for every node, by utilizing the data from its immediate
neighboring nodes.
We will begin by exploring the earliest version of Graph Neural Network, known as
the Recurrent Graph Neural Network, or RecGNN.

Graph Neural Networks
As previously noted in chapter 3, the remarkable effectiveness of deep neural net-

data, including stationarity and compositionality, via learnable hierarchical structures.
Specifically, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have demonstrated profound success
across a broad spectrum of visual recognition tasks. Fundamentally, CNNs capitalize on
the grid-like structure of data (for instance, image pixels) by employing a set of local,
learnable filters that are applied uniformly across all parts of the input. Of late, there’s
a growing curiosity in adapting these principles to data that resides on non-Euclidean
spaces, such as graphs [37].
This study suggests that graph neural networks, designed to process irregular graphs
without considering the sequence of elements, align well with transportation engineering
and Autonomous Mobility on Demand (AMoD). It observes that areas in a city, similar
to nodes in a graph, lack a predetermined order. Instead, the representation of trans-
portation systems depends on the traits of nodes and edges, such as regional demand
or travel times between areas. An effective control policy should rely solely on these
characteristics, ignoring the order in which areas are evaluated.

and edges, graph neural networks (GNNs) primarily focus on learning a mapping. This

Actor
As introduced in Section 2.3.2, the pricing action is conceptualized as the fare assigned
to trips between two areas, or nodes on the graph.
The input to the Neural Network comprises a 14 × 13 tensor, or matrix, with two
dimensions. The first dimension, with a length of 14, represents the areas on the map
(grid nodes), and the second dimension, with a length of 13, details the state features

Specifically, the feature vector is structured as follows: The initial segment of the
vector indicates the current vehicle availability across each region, normalized by a scaling
factor of 0.01 to adjust the data points to a more appropriate scale for analysis.

prediction, which accounts for both current availability and anticipated changes from
incoming vehicles, is also normalized using the same scaling factor.
Additionally, the vector includes the aggregated potential revenue for each region over
the forthcoming T time steps, derived from demand projections, set prices for each trip,
and normalized by the same scaling factor. With T set to 6, this further contributes 6
features to the vector.

the training phase.

tive learning rate properties, was utilized to facilitate convergence. By setting a learning
rate of 0.003, is carefully balanced the rate of model updates, ensuring rapid learning
without overshooting the optimal solution. The Soft-Actor-Critic (SAC) underwent train-
ing for 10,000 episodes with a discount factor of 0.97, indicating a strong preference for
future rewards, thereby guiding the model towards long-term benefits. This factor, com-
bined with an episode length of T = 60, structured the training process to optimize both
immediate and future outcomes. Additionally, this research also focused on the Served
Demand, which is quantified as the aggregate number of trips successfully completed
under the AMoD control approach. It is necessary to point out that the optimization
model and the hyper-parameters used come from Gammelli et al. [11].

Chapter 6
Discussion
According to the results presented in this thesis, the suggested model (model 1),
achieved the highest reward per episode, indicating a significant improvement in car
allocation efficiency in financial terms.
However, this model also exhibits a notable
decrease in demand served compared to model 2, the baseline model.
This suggests
that while financially beneficial allocation is achieved in certain areas, it results in fewer
customer demands being met. The increase in average price further suggests that the cost
to customers has risen, potentially contributing to the decrease in demand served. This
effect is attributed to the utility function that penalizes higher prices, leading to more
customers rejecting the service. Despite approximately 30% of potential users rejecting
the service due to cost, the model still serves a high percentage of the demand, achieving
substantial rewards. The average cost of approximately $2.1 per minute of ride time is
considered rational.
Furthermore, a comparison between figures 5.3 and 5.4 reveals an interesting obser-
vation. As expected, the number of vehicles allocated in each area decreases, correlating
with the approximately 23% reduction in demand served. Notably, the distribution of
vehicles between areas 6 and 11 also decreases, with area 11 showing a 21% reduction in
vehicle allocation in the first model compared to a 30% reduction in the baseline model.
This indicates that area 11 is likely a more remote area, and the agent prioritizes routes
in this region to maximize rewards. The utility function, which negatively weights price
and positively weights travel time (with a coefficient of 1), influences this outcome. Con-
sequently, for longer journeys, customers are less likely to reject service offers, aligning
with the agent’s strategy to serve these types of trips.
The claims made earlier are further supported by results from model 3, characterized
by a significantly higher average price than the other two models, with increases of 17%
and 52% compared to models 1 and 2, respectively. However, an intriguing observation
emerges from figure 5.5; the difference in vehicle allocation between areas 6 and 11 is
only 3% greater for area 6. This finding is crucial as it supports the hypothesis that
area 6 is more remote. The utility function indicates that customers are likely to reject
nearby trips if the price is high, yet the likelihood of accepting more expensive trips
increases with distance. This suggests a subtle interaction between trip distance, price
sensitivity, and area remoteness in customer decision-making processes. Additionally, it
appears that areas 1, 10, and 12 maintain the same level of demand served as model 2,
despite a 32% reduction in overall served demand. This suggests that these areas might
also be remote. However, this hypothesis requires further investigation.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This thesis presented a comprehensive study on the development and evaluation of
a dynamic pricing and allocation model for Autonomous Mobility on Demand (AMoD)
systems using the Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) algorithm integrated with graph neural net-
works. The primary focus was on optimizing operational efficiency and profitability while
addressing the complexities inherent in urban mobility systems. The findings from the
simulation studies, based on a hypothetical scenario modeled on New York City’s urban
landscape, highlight the model’s ability to learn effective dynamic pricing strategies that
significantly improve financial rewards.
The SAC-based model demonstrated superior performance in maximizing economic
gains, outperforming both the baseline and random pricing strategies in terms of reward
generation. However, this financial optimization comes at the cost of decreased demand
satisfaction, a critical trade-off identified in this research. The model’s inclination towards
higher pricing strategies led to a reduction in demand served, suggesting a potential area
for further refinement to balance profitability with customer service levels.
Moreover, the application of graph neural networks in this context underscored the
model’s capability to efficiently process and leverage the complex, interconnected data
typical of urban transportation networks. This approach facilitated a subtle understand-
ing and prediction of demand patterns, contributing to the model’s overall effectiveness.
7.1
Contributions
The contributions of this thesis extend to several key areas:
• It introduces a novel application of the SAC algorithm in conjunction with graph
neural networks for managing AMoD systems, providing a robust framework for
dynamic pricing and vehicle allocation.
• It offers insights into the trade-offs between maximizing operational revenue and
meeting customer demand, a critical consideration for the sustainable operation of
AMoD systems.
• It demonstrates the potential of machine learning and optimization techniques to
address complex operational challenges in urban mobility, paving the way for more
intelligent, adaptive transportation solutions.

7.2
Implications
The findings of this study have important implications for both theory and practice.
Theoretically, it contributes to the growing body of knowledge on the application of rein-
forcement learning and neural networks in transportation systems. Practically, it provides
a viable framework that can be adapted and expanded for real-world implementation,
offering a strategy to enhance the efficiency and profitability of AMoD services.
7.3
Future work
Despite the successes achieved, this study opens several avenues for future research.
1. Integration of EV Charging Infrastructure: An essential extension of this
research involves incorporating EV charging stations into the AMoD system’s op-
erational model.
Future studies could explore optimal placement strategies for
charging stations, dynamic pricing models for charging services, and their impact
on the overall efficiency and sustainability of the AMoD system.
2. Multi-modal Transportation Integration: Expanding the model to include
multi-modal transportation options can offer a comprehensive solution to urban
mobility, enhancing system efficiency and user reach.
3. Scalability and Real-world Deployment: Testing the model’s scalability across
varied urban settings can provide insights into its adaptability and inform adjust-
ments necessary for real-world deployment.
4. Environmental Impact Assessment: Incorporating an environmental impact
assessment can help in understanding and minimizing the adverse effects of dynamic
pricing strategies on traffic congestion, emissions, and energy use.
5. User Behavior and Social Dynamics: A deeper exploration of user behavior
and social dynamics can enrich the model with insights into human factors like price
sensitivity and environmental consciousness, potentially through interdisciplinary
research.
6. Regulatory and Ethical Considerations: As the deployment of AMoD systems
and dynamic pricing strategies advances, understanding and addressing regulatory
and ethical challenges become paramount. Future research should focus on de-
veloping ethical pricing and data handling frameworks that align with legal and
societal norms.
In conclusion, this thesis lays the groundwork for future advancements in the de-
ployment of intelligent AMoD systems, offering promising strategies for addressing the
complex challenges of urban transportation.
