Abstract
This thesis report presents the results of an investigation of the behaviour of the IN-
NWIND.EU Triple Spar oater. Measurements of movement and response was carried out
with a 1/60 model of the DTU 10MW reference wind turbine in a series of sea states, along
with the performance of an active wind turbine control compared to a xed rotor rpm. The
conducted test results are compared with the calculations and predictions done with a simple
down-scaled numerical model.
The numerous tests carried out in this project took place at the tests basin facilities at DHI
in Hørsholm, Denmark. The report presents selected data to support the overall conclusions
and model understanding of the tests.
The scaling methodology used for the turbine and oater was consistent with Froude scaling,
while the rotor blades were performance scaled due to low Reynolds numbers.
During the test campaign, ongoing calibrations of the equipment was performed, which
turned out to be benecial as 3P excitations was discovered and precautions was made to
ensure a 10% oset between coupled natural frequency and the highest 3P frequency.
To ensure understanding of the results, a comprehensive description of theory and numeri-
cal model is presented. Furthermore, a set of environmental conditions was developed, and
could to a satisfactory extent be replicated at the test site, with a maximum scaled wind
speed of 1.89 m/s, a wave height of 0.2 m with a corresponding peak period of 1.60 s.
The active rotor controller, specically developed for this project and used in the test cam-
paign had larger translational and rotational responses than that of a rotor with xed rpm.
The instability, seen in a land based controller used for oating wind turbines, was success-
fully reduced. Furthermore, the controller showed a more stable power production with a
higher mean output and less uctuations than a rotor with xed rpm under the same con-
ditions.

Introduction
1
Introduction
Since the beginning of this millennium, in Europe alone, the power produced from oshore
wind energy has grown from practically nothing to nearly 2400 MW, with the capacity in-
creased by 200 % in rst half of 2015 compared to the same period previous year [EWEA,
2015]. This speaks of an industry in increasing demand. As the possible sites of bottomxed
oshore wind farms are nite, oating wind turbines, FWT, are a possible source of energy
for the world of tomorrow.
The concept of oating wind turbines has been in development for some years now and sev-
eral large scale projects such as Windoat, Hywind and the Fukushima Floating Oshore
Wind Farm are already operational. The oil- and gas industry have already proven the
protability of oating platforms yet the economical advantages of oating wind turbines
require research and testing to be veried Buttereld et al. [1990]. Towards this goal model
testing is a necessity in the development of numerical models but little material is available
as scaled model test data from large projects such as Hywind and Windoat has not been
published [Larsen et al., 2012]. The model tests can provide reliable data for the validation
of numerical models, because it is cost ecient, requires less time and poses few risk [Mar-
tin et al., 2014]. Essentially the economic aspect of the oating wind turbines will be the
dominating factor for the design. The technical challenges especially arise from the need
to design a concept which can compete with an already competitive and established wind
energy market [Buttereld et al., 1990].
One of the collaborations that seeks to nd these protable solutions is the INNWIND.EU
project, from which the triple spar oater used in this project origins. The INNWIND.EU
project is an international project with 27 European partners and the successor to the Up-
Wind project, coordinated by DTU [INNWIND.EU]. The purpose of the INNWIND.EU
project is to explore new concepts for 10-20 MW wind turbines, placed at 50 metres depth
or more. Both the wind turbine and the support structures are subjected to new design
ideas such as oating structures for the support and lightweight components for the wind
turbine [INNWIND.EU, 2015].
The INNWIND.EU triple spar oater was developed at USTUTT in Germany and shipped
to DTU where it was tested at DHI in Hørholm together with the DTU 10 MW reference
turbine over the course of 27 days. During this period over 600 experiments were conducted
and the main results and observations were pinpointed and are presented in this report.

Project scope
1.1
Project scope
This project is conducted as a master thesis under the INNWIND.EU project in collaboration
with University of Stuttgart and CENER, with a test campaign carried out at DHI. The goal
of the project is to understand the motions of the INNWIND.EU Triple Spar test oater and
to see how well test results can be compared with a numerical model. To achieve this goal,
suitable environmental conditions is established together with a general scaling methodology.
Furthermore, the test period, is thoroughly planned to ensure that as many tests as possible
are conducted with the test equipment including the turbine running with both xed rota-
tions per minute, rpm, and with an active control of blade pitch and rpm. The controller is
developed specically with oshore oating wind turbines in mind and is rened in parallel
with the test execution.
The project includes:
• Planning of tests including denition of environmental conditions, establishment of
scaling methodology and upgrade of wind generator speed.
• Establishment of a simple numerical model for prediction of test results.
• Experiments carried out over a ve week period in May and June 2016 including:
 Mooring calibration,
 calibration of sensors,
 ongoing data processing,
 ongoing comparison to a simple numerical model,
 comparison of fan-generated wind loads and rotor generated wind loads.
• Documentation and calibration of wind eld, wave signals for wave basin and test
equipment.
• Selection of relevant results and nal data analysis and commenting on these.
• Final runs of numerical model and comparison to tests .
As mentioned the overall goal of this project is to understand the oater response and make
it possible to use these observations for the development of numerical models. The ambition
is additionally that the experience and results from this campaign will help future campaigns.
Establishing a common scaling methodology and environmental test conditions, will ease the
comparison of model tests, in this project and to other model tests, within INNWIND.EU and
other collaborations. Upgrading the wind eld at DHI should help provide better results for
oshore constructions as the maximum operating speed should be increased enabling model
tests of more severe environmental conditions. The controller developed in this campaign
will be the rst of its kind at DTU and this controller will hopefully bring the resemblance of
the scaled model closer to that of a full scale prototype and could be used in many campaigns
henceforth.

Semi Submersible platform
The semi-submersible wind turbine oater has been used in ambitious oating wind turbine
projects such the Windoat [Windoat, 2015] and the Fukushima Floating Oshore Wind
Farm project [Oshore, 2015] each presenting their own state of the art solutions. The semi-
sub is a concept where the wind turbine is supported by three cylinders that typically are
interconnected by bracers and it achieves its stabilization through a combination of ballast
and buoyancy. The Windoat concept is described thoroughly in Tande et al. [2014] and
the main concepts will be presented here. The Windoat semi-sub consists of three steel

Tension Leg Platform, TLP
The TLP system is originally developed by the oil industry for deep water oshore rigs, but
Blue H Group started adapting the TLP system to oshore wind turbines. This resulted in
the Blue H FCU in 2009, a TLP with a 2 MW wind turbine [BHE, 2016].
In 2015 Laugesen et al. [2015] made an experimental study of the dynamic response of a 10
MW wind turbine on a Tension Leg Platform. The study included experimental analysis of
a DTU 10 MW wind turbine on a TLP at scale 1/60 in both wind and waves. Here the
foundation was Froude scaled with the wind turbine being performance scaled, resulting in
a rotor diameter of 3 m and a hub height of 2 m. For the TLP system, Laugesen et al. [2015]
has provided an insight into the eects of plane and spread waves with wind dampening,
both aligned and misaligned with the wave direction, as well as providing insight into the
eects of dierent mooring congurations and orientations.
The stability of a TLP system is obtained by excess buoyancy of the platform, with the
bulk often fully submerged and restrained by tensioned mooring lines. This attenuates the
structure in heave, pitch and roll motion while it is exible in surge, sway and yaw motions
[Laugesen et al., 2015].

2
Environmental conditions
Knowledge of environmental conditions form the basis for the design of a oating wind tur-
bine and its feasibility. The importance lies within the correct prediction of maximum loads
from wind and waves, structural lifetime and expected energy production. Simultaneously,
proper working conditions and accessibility to the oating wind turbine is a necessity. The
environmental conditions chosen for the experiments in this project are based on Johannessen
et al. [2001] work on a joint wind-wave distribution in the northern North Sea. According
to the Danish Standard, [En, 2009], statistically sucient site-specic and long-term joint
wind-wave conditions have to be taken into account in the wind turbine design. As oating
wind turbines have to be placed in medium to deep waters, the weather conditions from
the Northern North sea are considered sucient, for representing a general wind-wave envi-
ronment for a oating wind turbine. Furthermore, the structural design lifetime should be
at least 20 years, and extreme conditions should be based on a 50 year recurrence period
[En, 2009]. The following sections, review the general concepts of ocean wave and wind
analysis before, leading into the joint wind wave distribution and the chosen environmental
conditions.

3.3
Control of DTU 10 MW turbine
To achieve maximum power production and minimize damage on the wind turbine, a number
of procedures can be implemented in a control system. The control objects can be divided
into a steady state and a dynamic part [Ele, 2016]. The aim of the steady state control is to
maintain rotational speed within the operational limits while maximizing power generation
until rated conditions. Dynamic control aims might dier but can generally be described
as to minimize loads, minimize variation of torque and keep the turbine within operating
limits. Jonkman et al. [2009] implemented a control system, for the NREL oshore 5-MW
baseline wind turbine, which regulated the generator-torque below rated wind speed and
the blade-pitch above rated wind speed, where the rated wind speed is the minimum speed
where the turbine generates the assigned power output. Hansen et al. [2013] implemented
in their control system simulations, in addition to torque- and pitch-control, a procedure
to control cut-in and cut-out of the DTU 10 MW RWT. The control systems work in real
time, as to adjust the blade pitch with change in wind speeds and turn on/o the power
production.
Control of blade pitch is implemented to maximize the potential of the wind turbine, both
below and especially above rated wind speed. Above the rated wind speed, adjusting blade
pitch makes it possible to stay at or below the rated RPM for the wind turbine and through
this keep a high mechanical power output. Without adjusting the blade pitch, damage to
the wind turbine is inevitable at wind speeds above the rated wind speed. Figure 3.6 shows
the correlation between blade pitch angle and RPM, plotted as two gures at the top. Fur-
thermore, it is seen how the RPM and mechanical power output are directly correlated. As
the blades pitch, the eective area is lowered thus a smaller thrust is achieved, hence they
are inversely proportional.
The cut-in speed denotes the point where the wind turbine starts generating power, with the
cut-out speed being the point where the turbine halts power generation. To avoid damage,
the cut-out speed is usually given such that the RPM of the turbine does not reach a critical
point. The cut-in and cut-out speeds can vary from model to model, but for Siemens tur-

in recorded maximum thrust but as the dierence in the thrust magnitude is insignicant
the thrust force is accepted as scaled correctly.

7
Results and discussion
The test campaign at DHI included more than 600 individual tests. This section investigates
the data deemed most interesting during the weekly meetings with our supervisor, and dur-
ing the course of the test campaign. Consequently, not all the data have been investigated,
thus there is still much information to gather from the test results. The entire collection of
data can be acquired at request. The results are presented as time series, exceedance prob-
ability and power spectrum density, PSD, plots, of which the two latter will be described in
further detail here.

7.5
Startup and shutdown
7.5
Startup and shutdown
The transient responses tied to the startup and shutdown of the turbine are studied in this
section for a wind speed of 1.89 m/s equivalent to sea state 7. As mentioned in section 1
the turbine was operated with both xed rpm and a controller. The startup and shutdown
procedure for each operational method was dierent resulting in dierent responses, though
most apparent in the startup procedure as seen in gure 7.40 and 7.42,.
The star-up procedure implemented by [Boehm and K. Lomholt, 2016] was made to enable
startup of the turbine wind speeds over 1.89 m/s. This startup procedure did however bring
along a negative side eect of large amplitude pitch motions as seen in gure 7.40. As de-
scribed in section 3.3 the controller starts up with two dierent pitch speeds. When the
second pitch speed of 1000 rpm is activated the blade pitches in quickly to 1.5◦. Before the
turbine adjusts the blade pitch to match the wind speed the torque controller is activated
and as these do not operate simultaneously there will be a delay before the blade pitch can
be adjusted to the wind speed as can be seen in gure 7.41. This string of actions in the
controller leads to large pitch motions of up to 8◦whereas the maximum pitch response seen
in previous test is less than 3◦. Similarly the surge motion reaches a value of 0.28 m which
is equal in magnitude to the maximum displacements seen in the surge response plots. This
is o course undesirable and for future tests a more sophisticated startup procedure should
be implemented to avoid these large amplitudes of motions. A successful operation of the
controller was the dampening of the surge motion. Within nine oscillations the pitch motion
is damped to a value of about 2.8◦and is still decreasing. For the emergency shut-down
the controller performs as expected and the maximum pitch and surge response is 3.6◦and
0.06 m respectively, where the system decay is here not dependant on any control mecha-
nisms.
For the xed rpm test the rpm and blade pitch was increased in increments until the desired
values for the associated sea states were reached. For this test this was equivalent to a
rotational speed 71 rpm and a blade pitch of 13.5◦. The surge and pitch motions clearly
underline the startup procedure with alternating pitch and rpm changes as seen in gure
7.42. The heave signal for the shut-down event of the xed controller was included here yet no
real analysis could be pointed out as the data were clearly corrupted. The heave plot suggests
that the heave signal should go from 0.04 m to −0.07 in a matter of 23 milliseconds. This is
clearly not possible and this data corruption was investigated as thoroughly as possible. Due
to the large test database it was obviously not a possibility to compare all results between
the DHI and DTU logging system but from the compared results the problem seemed to
arise from a malfunction in the DAQ unit transferring the data to the DTU logging system
as the same error was not seen anywhere in the DHI logging system.

Conclusion
The project scope is achieved and fullled. The scaling methodology was well established
in accordance with other recognized model experiments, such as [Larsen et al., 2012] and
[Martin et al., 2014], and a statistically based joint wind wave distribution in the north sea
formed the basis of the environmental conditions. These could to a satisfactory extent be
replicated in the test basin though with a maximum scaled wind speed, V , wave height, H
and peak period, Tp of respectively 1.89 m/s, 0.2 m and 1.60 s.
Due to the test campaign being delayed three weeks, the upgrading process of the wind gen-
erators was rushed, and performance test of the individual generators was not conducted.
Thus, an assembly error, leading to a skewed wind eld, was not discovered until the end
of the test campaign. Furthermore, some delays arose during the test preparations; hence
the initial goal of conducting tests for all rotor set-ups in all sea states was not reached.
The skewness of the wind eld was however rectied by individual control of the six wind
generators. The ne tuning of the wind eld successfully created a more even wind eld
resembling natural conditions better at the cost of a lower mean wind speed at hub height.
A comprehensive description of theory and numerical model is presented in the report for
better understanding of test results and model comparison.
It can be concluded that an ongoing check and calibration of such large test equipment and
its numerous components is necessary, both before, during and after the tests.
During the test campaign, daily calibration of sensors was conducted along with calibration
of strain gauges before and after the test campaign. During the course of the test campaign,
the data were processed and analysed from day to day, to detect potential faults in the tests.
These checks exposed 3P frequencies close to the coupled natural frequency, 3.2 Hz, and lead
to further tests to conrm that the largest 3P frequency, 3.55 Hz, was outside the 10% range
of the coupled natural frequency.
Through the test campaign, the controller was developed after going through numerous tests,
before arriving at a third and nal conguration. Compared with the xed rpm tests, the
controller showed larger responses in all translational and rotational degrees of freedom, for
both focused, regular, irregular and irregular misaligned waves. The controller was successful
in creating a more constant power output with a higher mean value and less uctuations.
Moreover the controller was compared with three other controllers, ranging from land based
to oshore, as to see the eects of instability in a land based controller.
The down-scaled numerical model, originally developed by Antonio M. P. Jurado, is used to
get a preliminary idea of the behaviour of the triple spar oater during the test campaign.
Later on, the results from the numerical model is compared to those of the test campaign.
However, the model used is an early version, thus little resemblance occurs in the results of
the model and the tests. As follows, models for complete understanding of the triple spar
oater behaviour needs to be developed and are not included in this project.
The project clearly shows the advantages of developing down-scaled models for testing and
prediction of FWT movements and response in severe environmental conditions. Finally it
can be concluded, that the project has shown the advantages in doing scaled model testing
in a controlled environment, including comparisons with theoretically modelling to reach a

Postscript
To further improve the understanding of the results gained in this test campaign, more
in-depth investigations of the data is needed. A nal version of the numerical model, down-
scaled and tuned with the test results, will facilitate this process tremendously. Furthermore,
a correction of the wind generator assembly is needed in future test campaigns, as to improve
and legitimize the wind eld data collection.
A comparison of the data from the CENER actuator tests with the test data for the DTU
10MW rwt, can improve understandings of how down-scaled test models operate and how
to reduce the cost of future test campaigns using only an actuator to simulate the full wind

DTU Wind Energy is a department of the Technical University of Denmark with a unique integration of research, education, innovation and 
public/private sector consulting in the field of wind energy. Our activities develop new opportunities and technology for the global and Danish 
exploitation of wind energy. Research focuses on key technical-scientific fields, which are central for the development, innovation and use of wind 
energy and provides the basis for advanced education at the education. 
 
We have more than 240 staff members of which approximately 60 are PhD students. Research is conducted within nine research programmes 
organized into three main topics: Wind energy systems, Wind turbine technology and Basics for wind energy.
