Abstract
The focus of this project has been on the multi drug resistant strains of Pseudomonas
aeruginosa know as HiRiCs (High risk clones). These strains are thought to be extremely
good at at acquiring exogenous DNA, and are therefore also know to have a diverse
amount of horizontally acquired genes, including resistance determinants making them
successful in a nosocomial setting. In this project I have discussed and worked with three
gene factors thought to be speciﬁc determinants of HiRiCs and their ability to acquire
DNA horizontally. These factors are the lack of a working CRISPR Cas system, having
speciﬁc O-antigens and having a dprA gene. It was attempted to clone diﬀerent O-antigen
biosynthetic geneclusters on a vector to be able to create isogenic strains with diﬀerent
O-antigen. This was unsuccessful and further work should be put into optimising the
cloning setup.
A dprA knockout was successfully created. This and three other knockouts, an O-antigen
knockout, a CRISPR knockout and a double knockout of both CRISPR and O-antigen
was tested for their ability to acquire DNA by conjugation. This was done by the use
of a ﬂow cytometry based assay developed in this project using the Pseudomonas putida
strain IMG911 as a donor of a GFP tagged plasmid. Results showed that the dprA knock-
out had a slightly lower percentage of transconjugates than that of the wildtype. The
CRISPR knockout showed a very small decrease in percentage of transconjugants com-
pared to the wildtype. The two O-antigen knockouts showed to have a higher percentages
of transconjugant compared to the wildtype. The O-antigen knockout showed to have a
signiﬁcantly higher and the dprA to have a slightly higher amount of donor in the end
sample. Samples started with the same amount of donor, and this result therefore suggest
a growth disadvantage in the community with IMG911 compared to the wildtype.
Results additionally showed that samples needed to mature for around 3 hours from
sampling time to analysis on the ﬂow cytometer for the mCherry marker of IMG911 to
mature. The amount of transconjugants in samples were fairly low and further optimisa-
tion of the assay is therefore needed. It was thought that an isogenic donor would improve
the assay, and work therefore went into creating a PA14 donor which ended up not being
possible.

Introduction
Antibiotic resistance is a large problem worldwide, with more than 700,000 deaths caused
by multi drug resistant bacteria in 2016.[1] With the rise of cases caused by multi drug
resistance, it was estimated by the UK Department of Health that the number of deaths
will rise to 10 million in 2050. This means that infections caused by antimicrobial resistant
(AMR) bacteria will surpass major diseases like cancer and diabetes as illustrated in ﬁgure
1.[1]
Figure 1: Deaths by AMR infections in 2050 compared to other well know deceases. Figure from UK
health department report on AMR.[2]
It is obvious from these numbers that AMR infections will be a major concern in the
future, but due to low proﬁt margins from antibiotics, there is a lack of incentive to invest
in the development of novel antimicrobials, which is illustrated in ﬁgure 2. This is show
by the amount of money spend by the US National Institute for Health on research in
AMR compared to cancer, HIV and diabetes in the years 2010 to 2014.
3
==PAGE:8==

Figure 2: Money spend by the US National institute for health on research of major diseases in the
years 2010 to 2014. [3]
To combat the rise of AMR, diﬀerent strategies have been proposed, which include sus-
tainable use of antibiotics (antibiotic stewardship), good hygiene, and the need for de-
velopment of novel antibiotics. However, to overcome the rise of AMR it might not be
enough to discover new antibiotics since it will be a race with the continued evolution
of AMR bacteria that might become resistant to the new antimicrobials. It is therefore
crucial to unravel the mechanisms causing AMR bacteria to become resistant and thereby
be able to prevent and predict outbreaks of future AMR strains.[4]
The focus of this project has been on the Gram negative AMR bacteria Pseudomonas
aeruginosa .
P. aeruginosa is a very versatile soil bacteria and is an opportunistic
pathogen accounting for many cases of nosocomial infections.
Many of these nosoco-
mial infections are caused by multi drug resistant (MDR) and extensivly drug resistance
(XDR) P. aeruginosa. It has been found that these antibiotic resistant P. aeruginosa are
linked to only a limited number of strains, the so called "High risk strains" (HiRiCs).[5]
These HiRiCs contain a large and diverse amount of horizontally acquired genes including
resistance determinants, but little is know about the mechanisms that make these speciﬁc
clones become HiRiCs and other strains not.[6] Once in a while, new HiRiC strains arise,
and it is therefore of great interest to be able to detect these mechanisms and thereby be
able to detect and act on a new wave before it gets out of hand.
In this project I have worked with three genomic systems thought to be determinants
of HiRiCs’ ability to acquire DNA horizontally. These are the CRISPR Cas system, the
O-antigen gene, and the dprA gene. Correlation between being a HiRiC and these genes
have shown that some of these features to be unique for one or more HiRiC strains and
by looking at the functions of each, they are thought to improve HiRiCs ability to take
up DNA from the surroundings and implementing them into the genome. The ability to
acquire genes give them an advantage, especially in a clinical setting where faster adap-
tation is very advantageous to survive several antibiotic treatments.
These correlations and reasoning’s will be explained further in the background section.

Theory
This section will provide background knowledge for the understanding of the project.
4.1
High Risk Clones - HiRiCs
P. aeruginosa is a very diverse bacteria, and it is essential to be able to separate speciﬁc
strains from each other. Especially in terms of an epidemiological setting, it is necessary
to be able to track outbreaks of infections and to see how new and old strains are aﬀecting
these. HiRiCs diﬀer from other P. aeruginosa strains in having higher and more diverse
amounts of horizontally acquired genes including resistance determinants.[5] Additionally,
HiRiCs account for most MDR/XDR P. aeruginosas and are characterised by being very
widespread, but having a population structure made up of a limited numbers of clones
with ST111, ST175 and ST235 being the most dominant ones.[7] ST235 is thought to have
the widest distribution and is found in many countries and on 5 diﬀerent continents.[5][8]
P. aeriginosa strains are diﬀerentiated from each other by a couple of diﬀerent methods
with the sequences type (ST) being the most widely used.[5][9] The ST is based on the
method multi-locus sequence typing (MLST). MLST is a method of typing bacterial
isolates into know groups by establishment of what alleles they possess. This is done on
basis of known house-keeping genes. Thereby assigning isolates to sequence types on this
basis. The 7 genes used to deﬁne the sequence type in P. aeruginosa are acsA, aroE,
guaA, mutL, nuoD, ppsA, and trpE[10][11]. The serotype is another way of telling strains
apart and it is seen that ST’s may posses diﬀerent serotypes.[10] On the other hand, it has
also been observed in speciﬁc strains like the HiRiC strains that serotypes can be quite
conserved and that speciﬁc serotypes might be connected to certain HiRiC strains.[9] It
is therefore suggested that serotyping could be used as an early screening method for
detecting HiRiCs. [9]
4.2
Horizontal gene transfer
Horizontal gene transfer is the movement of genetic material between cells, whereas ver-
tical gene transfer is the classical transfer of genetic material from parent to oﬀspring.
Horizontal gene transfer is therefore the process of acquiring genes from the surround-
ings. The origin of horizontal gene transfer are usually split into 3 main mechanisms:
Conjugation, tranformation and transduction[12].
4.2.1
Conjugation
Conjugation is the process of transferring DNA via direct cell to cell contact as seen
in ﬁgure 30.B. In the initial part of conjugation, two strands of a plasmid, separate
by rolling-circle replication where one strand moves from one bacteria (the donor) that
establish contact with its sex pili to another bacteria (the recipient). The pili contract
to establish direct contact and merge of the cell membrane of the two cells. The single
5
==PAGE:10==

stranded DNA are then transferred to the recipient and the two strands serve as the
template for the ﬁnal double stranded DNA in each cell resulting in both cells having the
plasmid.[13][14] For the bacteria to be able to perform conjugation, genes coding for the
conjugation machinery were to be present: If this is the Case the cells are said to be F+
meaning they have the machinery for conjugation. Plasmids coding for the conjugation
machinery are said to be self-transmissible since they can mediate their own transfer.
These genes are called the tra genes and are split into two parts: Dtr components used
for DNA transfer and conjugational replication, and the Mpf for mating pair formation.
Besides the tra genes self-transmissible plasmids often contain transposons enabling them
to perform transposition of genes on the plasmid onto the chromosome and from the
chromosome to the plasmids. Thereby sharering some of its own genetical material at
next conjugation event, since self-transmissable plasmids contain all these elements they
are usually fairly large.[13][14]
Most of plasmids are non self-transmissible and rely on a self-transmissible plasmid to
be present. These are called mobilizable elements and they take advantage of the the
conjugtation machinery by having an oriT sequence. At the oriT the site speciﬁc DNA
endonuclease the relaxase, expressed from the self-transmissible plasmid, can bind and
perform a single stranded break and perform the same transfer process.[13][15]
The advantage of plasmid conjugation is that it allow bacterial populations to sample the
horizontal gene pool for adaptive traits that might be advantageous for survival under
local selective pressures like antibiotic resistance. [16]
4.2.2
Transformation
Transformation is the process of uptake, internalization and integration of exogenous DNA
into a recipient cell as seen in ﬁgure 30.A. Compared to the other types of horizontal trans-
fer, transformation is entirely directed by the recipient cell and genes encoding the ability
of transformation is coded in the genome. Not all bacteria are naturally transformable
but natural transformation has been seen to be very conserved across taxa and more
and more bacteria are found to be naturally transformable.[17] This include P. aeruginos
where essential genes for natural transformation have found to be conserved. Recently
experiments have proven the ability of P. aeruginosas to take up exogenous DNA.[18].
The transformation genes are not expressed constitutively but only during speciﬁc condi-
tions. When these bacteria express the genes responsible for transformation they are said
to competent.[17][13] In the uptake by natural transformation DNA binds to a transfor-
mation pilus and the DNA is brought to the cell surface where DNA is cut into smaller
fragments. The one strand of the dsDNA is then degraded and the single stranded DNA
can then be taken up by the cell. Where it will be protected by dprA proteins and be inte-
grated into the chromosome by recA mediated homologous recombination.[17] [13]

4.2.3
Transduction
Trasduction is the process of baceriophages transferring DNA from one cell to another as
seen in ﬁgure 30.C. Transduction is split into to types. Generalised transduction where
random parts of bacterial DNA can be transferred from one bacteria to another. The other
type specialized transduction where only certain genes close to the prophage attachment
site can be transfered.[19] While packing their own DNA the phages sometimes mistakenly
package the DNA of the host cell instead. These wrongly packed phages will still be able
to infect other cells but might not be able to reproduce.
Phages capable of packing
unspeciﬁc DNA are called transducing phages.[19] Since many phages are highly speciﬁc
the newly introduced DNA might have a high degree of homology with the recipient
and may therefore have a high chance of recombining with the new host genome.[13] A
consequence of the phage packaging is also that DNA in phage heads are more stable
than free DNA and may therefore stay longer in the environment.[13] Incoming DNA via
tranduction from one species to another might not recombine due to lack of homology,
but introduction of new functions can be achieved in distantly related species if the DNA
is a broad host range plasmid or if it contains a broad range transposon that can jump
into the receiving cell. [13]

4.3.1
CRISPR Cas
The CRISPR (clustered regulatory interspaced short palondromic repeats) Cas (CRISPR
associated proteins) system is thought of as being the equivalent of an adaptive im-
munesystem. The reason for this comparison is the systems ability of establishing im-
7
==PAGE:12==

munity against phages and plasmids from previous exposures. The CRISPR part of the
system is composed of directly repeated identical sequences around 30bp long separated
by "random" sequences of approximately the same length. Some bacteria only carry loci
with a few repeats whereas other have more than 500. Bacteria often carry more than one
CRISPR loci.[13] The "random" sequences between the identical repeats resembles DNA
from plasmids or phages which have been integrated over time. The matching sequences
of previously exposed DNA on plasmids or phages are called protospacers.
[21] Cas proteins incorporate new spacers next to the the end of the CRISPR locus. The
CRISPR locus is transcribed into one long RNA and is then cut into pieces cutting at the
same spot in the repeating sequences. [21] Each small RNA contains a spacer sequence
and can be used as guide RNA for the endonucelase Cas proteins to identify protospacers
and cut the protospacers. They can thereby cause interference "protecting" the bacteria
against foreign DNA. The 3 steps involved in the CRISPR system is illustrated in ﬁgure 4.
The requirement for an adjacent PAM (protospacer adjacent motif) for the Cas proteins
to cut a protospacer ensures that the spacer sequence in the CRISPR locus is not cut
causing autoimmunity. [13][22]
Figure 4: This ﬁgure shows the three parts of the CRISPR Cas system. Adaptation in the top,
expression in the middle and interference in the bottom.[21]

machinery in bacteria that are capable of natural transformation. In a study on Strep-
tococcus Pneumoniae done by Johnston et al. (2018) showed when dprA was under the
control of a lac promoter. A correlation between the amount of IPTG and the ability of

4.3.3
O-antigen
The lipoplysaccaride (LPS) is a complex glycolipid located on the outer membrane of gram
negative bacteria. The LPS is essential for cell viability and virulence in infections.[27] As
seen in ﬁgure 6 the LPS consist of the conserved membrane bound lipid A linked to the
core oligosaccaride and an O-antigen of varying length attached to the core oligosaccha-
ride. In this project the main focus has been on the O-antigen speciﬁcally in the context
of P. aeruginosa. The terms "smooth" and "rough" are used to describe phenotypes. At-
tachment of O-antigen to the core is called smooth and the lack of O-antigen attachment is
refereed to as a rough LPS. Studies have shown that a smooth phenotype causes virulence
whereas a rough phenotype does so to a less degree. An example of this is a study that
showed in a burned mouse model that the rough phenotype has a 50 percent lethal dose
1000 times higher than that of the smooth. [28] Furthermore, studies have shown that
rough mutants deﬁcient in O-antigen synthesis are sensitive to killing eﬀects of human
serum, while wildtype strains with smooth LPS are serum resistant.[29][30] P. aeruginosa

Background
The purpose of this section is to provide some insight into background of of why it is
interesting to look at the diﬀerent genes.
5.0.1
DprA
As mentioned earlier ST235 is one of the most prevalent of the widespread HiRic clones
and a study by Treepong et al. (2018) looking at the genomes of 79 P. aeruginosa ST235
isolates collected worldwide, identiﬁed dprA as a speciﬁc determinant of the ST235 sub-
linage. They further suggested that since dprA is universal among transformable species
and its role in homlogous recombination provide evidence that its presence in ST235
enhances the ability of acquiring and maintaining foreign resistance elements at a greater
rate than other P. aeriginosa. [35]
To further support this theory it has been seen recently that P. aeruginosa is able of
natural transformation.[18] By analysis of naturally transformable species it has been
shown that dprA is conserved in all of these, and therefore suggest that a functional dprA
is a conserved characteristic of naturally transformable species.[17] It was found that the
regular laboratory strain PA14 also had this gene. It was therefore decided that it would
be interesting to create a dprA mutant although the dprA gene of PA14 did not have 100
percent homology with the dprA gen of ST235. The mutant was to be created by 2 step
allelic replacement.
5.0.2
CRISPR Cas
CRISPR Cas acts as a defence against foreign DNA and it is therefore not surprising
it has been proposed throughout literature that the lack of a functional CRISPR Cas
system should be an advantage in acquiring new genomic material through horizontal
transfer. One of the things supporting this hypothesis is that clinical isolates having a
functional CRISPR systems on average have a smaller genome than those strains with a
non functional system.[36]
It has also been seen that some CRISPR elements have been connected to virulence in P.
aeruginosa and it is therefore thought that an absent or non-functional CRISPR system
may allow P. aeruginosa strains to acquire and maintain these virulence islands.[37] Last
but not least studies have shown a connection between having resistance genes and the
lack of some CRISPR systems.[38] This correlation has been seen in diﬀerent studies to
be true for the major HiRiC strains ST111, ST175 and ST235. [39][36][40]

The O-antigen of P. aeruginose diﬀer in size and composition. The diversity of the OSA
is the basis of separating strains into serotypes where 20 P. aeruginosa serotypes have
been deﬁned. Serotyping is a common way of distinguishing between strains and some
serotypes are correlated to higher virulens.[7] An example of this is an over representation

of O11 strains expressing the TTSS (type 3 secretionsystem) seen in a study by Faure et

In a study by DelBarrio-Toﬁno et al. (2019) where 1445 clinical isolates from around Spain
were analysed for antibiotic resistance and their serotype. MDR and XDR isolates were
compared to the non-MDR isolates and a clear relationship was seen between serotypes
and being either XDR/MDR. It was also shown in the same study that these serotypes
were speciﬁc for some of the most common HiRiC strains.[9]
It has been hypothesised that P. aeruginosa is able of serotype switching, which is sup-
ported by studies done by Wingaard et al. (2015). This study has shown the replacement
of the native O-antigen biosynthetic gene cluster O4 happened by recombiantion with
a "serotype island" and resulted in an O12 serotype. It has been suggested that this
serotype switch has played an important role in relation to the emergence of the HiRiC
strain ST111.[42] It is therefore thought that the change of serotype might be a deﬁning
feature in becoming a HiRiC.
The switch of serotype has been observed in other bacteria, and it has successfully been
proven experimentally that some bacteria can switch their serotype by natural transfor-
mation. An example of this is the serotype switch experimentally shown in a study by
Blokesch et al. (20007) where it was shown that Vibrio cholera was able to switch its
serotype naturally from O1 to O139.[43]
Because of these ﬁndings it was desired to test whether the eﬀect of switching the O-

6.1
RecET- cloning of large fragments
The recET cloning is a cloning method relying on the rac prophage protein recE and its
partner recT that can mediate eﬃcient homologous recombination between to linear pieces
of DNA in a replication independent mechanism.[44] The recET system is used to integrate
large biosynthetic pathways directly o to a vector of choice, as seen in ﬁgure 8, where an
example of vectors are also shown. For the use as a genetic engineering tool, the genes are
incorporated in E. coli under the regulation of an inducible promoter. The recombinering
of the two linear fragments happens within the E. coli after transformation.[45]
The advantages of utilising the recET system in E. coli compared to TAR (transformation
associated recombinering) cloning utilising the native recombinering machinery of yeast.
It take less time, with only 3 days needed for the recET protocol compared to up to 14
days for the TAR protocol. A lot of the time diﬀerence comes from the cloned construct
from TAR cloning needed to be reintroduced into E. coli for further work. Furthermore,
In TAR cloning recombination is continually active causing a stability problem, whereas
the recET system is tightly regulated under an inducable promoter and thereby, enhancing
stable maintenance of DNA especially for constructs with repetitive sequences.[44]

6.2
2 step allelic replacement
The 2 step allelic replacement consists of 2 parts the insertion of a plasmid construct and
the removal of the construct again by a double crossover event to knockout a gene. This
technique has been used to create an dprA knockout.
The allelic replacement construct consists of a colE1 ori for replication only in E. coli
an oriT enabling transfer by conjugation, an antibiotic marker, a multiple cloning site, a
suicide marker (sacB) and homology regions speciﬁc for the insertion site.
The construct works by the use of P. aeruginosas homologous recombination machinery,
which enables recombination between the allelic exchange vector and the chromosome.
After introduction of the plasmid the ﬁrst crossover event will happen where the whole
plasmid is inserted at the position of one of the homology arms. Meridiploids are selected
using the antibiotic marker. The construct contains a sacB gene enabling selection of
a double crossover event. It will happen by itself over time because of the homology of
the insert. Cells positive for the double crossover event can be selected for by plating
on sucrose plates, because cells containing the sacB gene found in the original insert will
not be able to survive on the sucrose plates. In ﬁgure 9 it is seen how the plasmid can
be inserted by either homology arm, resulting in the insertion of the construct either
downstream or upstream of the gene of interest. This results in two sets of homology
regions enabling a second crossover event causing the revertion to wildtype or deletion of
the gene between the homology regions. [26][46]

are the corresponding homology regions on the chromosome. [46]

6.3
Transposon based tagging
Both the mini-ctx2 and the Tn7 construct are transposon based vectors where the wanted
inset is ﬂanked by a speciﬁc inverse repeat enabling site-speciﬁc integration utilising their
respective integrates. These have been used for tagging P. aeruginosa PA14 in an attempt
to create an isogenic donor strain for conjugation experiments.
Mini-ctx2 The mini-ctx2 is a construct containing a resistance gene, an oriT for conju-
gation mediated transfer, pMB1 origen of replication, a modiﬁed CTX phage integrase
gene, a multible cloning site and a CTX attachment site (attP),
The pMB1 make it possible for the construct to replicate in E. coli but not in P. aerugi-
nosa, making sure that selected colonies have integrated the wanted insert on the genome.
The plasmid contains an oriT site enabling transfer by conjugation in the presence of a
helper plasmid strain. The construct contains an CTX integrase gene which is speciﬁc
for the attP site also located on the plasmid, this enables the site specﬁc integration at
an attB site on the P. aeruginosa genome. Furthermore, the plasmid contains a selection
marker and a multiple cloning site ﬂanked by FRT sequences which enables the removal
of the plasmid backbone.[47][48]
Mini-Tn7 The mini-Tn7 contruct has many of the same features as the mini-ctx contruct.
The diﬀerence here is that the integrase TnsABCD site-speciﬁc pathway is contained on
a separate pTNS helper plasmid which is utilising Tn7 sequences on the plasmid and
integrating at attTn7 sites. Tn7 is a more versatile vector and can be used for diﬀerent
Pseudomonas whereas the ctx vector is speciﬁc for P. aeruginosa.[49]
6.4
Flow cytometry
The main function of ﬂow cytometry is to be able to detect speciﬁc cells on single cells
level. This is done by passing cells one by one through a laser and then detecting the
scatter and ﬂuorescence using sensors which can detect voltage pulses in the form of light
intensity. This is shown in ﬁgure 10.1.[50]
Fluorescence can be used to detect cells with speciﬁc functionalities by the binding of ﬂu-
orochromes or as in the case of this project cells producing detectable ﬂuorochromes. The
ﬂow cytometer can detect intensity and can therefore also diﬀerentiate between cells with
little or a lot of expression and could therefore in theory be used to test gene expression
in a population.
Scatter which is a basic measurement in ﬂow cytometry can be used to estimate size,
shape and cell complexity and can be used for quality measures and to deﬁne wanted cell
populations. It is seen in ﬁgure 10.2 how the laser can detect these features. In A the
machine can detect the size of the cone produced after lighting on a cell and thereby pro-
duce an estimate of the size. This is called forward scatter since a sensor is detecting the
light going directly forward. In B it is seen how cells can reﬂect light and cell with more
complex shapes and interior will reﬂect more lights to the sides, a sensor can detect these
16
==PAGE:21==
Master Thesis
reﬂections going to diﬀerent sides and it is therefore called side scatter.[50] Flowcytometry
can additionally be used to detect cell that are "stained" with a ﬂourochrome. This tech-
nique can be used to detect things like cell viability by staining with ﬂourochomes only
being able of bind to either live or dead cells. This can also be used to look at prolifera-
tion where an original population can be stained with a certain amount of ﬂourochrome.
Each time a cell divide it will split the amount of ﬂourochrome in the two cells and it is
therefore possible to detect the amount of cells in diﬀerent generations since a speciﬁc gen-
eration will have a speciﬁc intensity. Furthermore, ﬂowcytometry can be used to detect
surface markers by binding of a ﬂourochrome like an antibody-ﬂourochrome conjugate
and thereby separate cell populations by phenotype. [50][51]

8.1
O-antigen replacement using recET cloning
8.1.1
Overview of experiment
To be able to exchange the O-antigen BGC of PA14 and thereby create isogenic strains
with diﬀerent OSA, as explained in the background section. A setup was created with
the goal of being able to clone each of the 20 diﬀerent O-antigen clusters onto a vector
and thereby be able to introduce these into an O-antigen knockout strain. To do this the
recET system introduced in theory section was used.
For the ﬁrst test of the recET protocol, it was attempted to insert the two O-antigen
clusters for serotype O5 and O11. In this test a positive control consisting of the non-
linearized plasmid showed up as a lawn and the negative control using water had no
colonies.
A few transformants were observed for both BCG’s and a control with the
linearized plasmid. When verifying the inserts by PCR, it was seen that the colonies did
not contain the O-antigen cluster but that the backbone was present. It was therefore
thought that the linearized plasmid might have religated or that the samples had somehow
been contaminated with plasmid template.
To optimize the protocol for better results. Following potential improvements were eval-
uated:
• To exchange the plasmid for one optimized for the protocol or one having a lower
copy number.
• To optimization of gDNA quality after both puriﬁcation and digest.
• To creation of competent cells on the day of transformation
• To use diﬀerent concentrations of gDNA
The recET linear-linear recombineering protocol was attempted a total of 5 times, with
variations between. None of them resulted in constructs with conﬁrmed insertion of the
O-antigen BCG. When doing these experiments a negative control with added water and a
control containing the linearized plasmid were used. In all iterations the negative control
showed no colonies, but the controls transformed with just the linearized plasmid almost
always had approximately the same number of colonies, as on the actual samples with
both gDNA and the linearized plasmid. This suggest that the linearized plasmid somehow
managed to form a viable construct.
Throughout the experiment’s measures were taken to prevent the presence of the origi-
nal template. A gel puriﬁcation of the PCR product from linearization of the plasmid
backbone was done for size elimination, and PCR product was also digested with dnp1
restriction enzyme to cut any methylated DNA.

8.2
CFU based conjugation
A simple CFU based protocol was tested to be able of optimizing conjugation rate. The
CFU was based on the transfer of a conjugational plasmid containing a tetracycline re-
sistance gene from the donor P. putida IMG911 to wildtype P. aeruginosa PA14. The
setup was based on plating conjugation mixtures of the 2 strains on tetracycline plates
and incubate the plates at 42◦to select for P. aeruginoa transconjugates. The growth at
42◦was done since growth of P. putida should be greatly hindered at this temperature,
whereas P. aeruginosa should be able to grow at this temperature. The setup worked in
some way since detectable colonies of PA14 could be detected.
But this protocol was found to be very variable. Reasons for this were that temperature
in the incubator was found not to be distributed properly resulting in P. putida colonies
growing on the plates.
The high temperature also made growth of P.aeruginosa slow and it was therefore diﬃcult
to detect colonies after 24 hours, This resulted in some cases in P. putida colonies also
starting to grow on the plates.
Even though experiments were diﬃcult to interpret, it was found that conjugation mix-
tures incubated at 37◦seemed to result in a higher amount of transconjugants than those
incubated at 30◦.
8.3
Flow cytometry optimization
Flow cytometry is based on a laser detecting ﬂuorescence and parameters need to be
deﬁned when using the ﬂow cytometry. To setup the parameters/setting an initial exper-
iment were run trying diﬀerent conﬁgurations.
The settings that were determined were the forward and side scatter, the GFP channel,
the mCherry channel and a "trigger" meaning when to count something as an event/-
cell. Settings were tried until a deﬁned population of cells and a good separation was
seen. The forward side scatter was ﬁrst deﬁned to determine a population of bacterial
size and complexity. This was a bit diﬃcult since the forward scatter is an arbitrary
unit for size and that bacteria are very small making it diﬃcult to separate "noise" from
bacteria. To do this a mCherry positive putida was used since noise would not show up
as being mCherry positive. The setting was found to be side scatter=505V and forward
scatter=308V. Furthermore, a stain with cyto9 was done as a separate experiment to con-
ﬁrm that the population found was cells. The laser settings for each ﬂuorescence channel
were determined by the setting, where the largest separation between GFP/mCherry pos-
itive cells and ﬂuorochrome negative cells were found. These settings were determined
to be 508v for GFP and 500v for mCherry. The trigger was found on side scatter by
setting a threshold for when cells stopped showing ﬂuorescence, which was at 0.6 scatter
units.

Discussion
In this section each of the main parts will be discussed in terms of possible explanations
and possible optimisation of the setups.
9.1
O-antigen replacement recET protocol
It was not possible to clone the O-antigen BCG in the time duration of this project. Many
factors might have played into this, including the quality of the gDNA. Many measures
were taken to improve the result of the the recET protocol, including limiting the amount
of false positives, which should be further approved upon in future iteration.
In this
project the positive control for transformation was the non-linearized plasmid. However,
an improved positive control should have been used in the form of an actual linear-linear
cloning that was proven to work. A proof of concept of the original protocol with the
use of of the ﬂuorescent marker YFP was previously successfully performed in our lab,
and this could therefore have been used. The advantage of using the YFP is that since
it is ﬂuorescent, mutants can fairly easily be screened under a microscope. The use of a
linear-linear positive control would have allowed to see a potential positive eﬀect of some
of the improvements made along the way.
It was found later that false positive colonies did only show when the E. coli cells were
induced with arabinose, proving that the high number of false positives must result from
some kind of recombining event. It has also been shown that the cloning of the Pseu-
domonas O-antigen into E. coli results in serum resistance[57], and this could therefore
be a way of screening true positive transformants from false positive transformants. It
might be necessary to change strategy and an option could be to use a yeast-based cloning
method, as this has been done before.[58] A disadvantage of this is that it creates addi-
tional time and complexity. Another option might be to integrate a selection marker into
the the O-antigen cluster, which can be used as an extra selection step to eliminate false
positives. The feasibility of this might be great, since it should be possible to create just
one construct that can be used for tagging all BGC’s.
9.2
CFU based conjugation rate
A CFU based setup relying on selection of antibiotic resistance and temperature was
attempted to be used to determine conjugation rate. Even though this was a bit unreliable.
A problem with this setup is that it is very speciﬁc for P. aeruginosa - P. putida growth
since it is using temperature for selection and would therefore not work if both the donor
and the recipient was the same species. Same species conjugation has been suggested
as optimal conjugation setting.[56] If this is the case, it might be possible to tag the
recipients with an antibiotic marker and select for 2 antibiotics. The determination of
conjugation rate by a CFU based method is simple and has the advantage, that it can
be done on basis of resistance markers. It does however cause problems compared to the
ﬂow cytometry method that has also been used in this project.
45
==PAGE:50==
Master Thesis
One of these problems is that the amount of CFUs might not match the actual cell number,
since one colony might not in all cases count for one cell. additionally counting CFUs
does not take non viable or poorly growing cell into consideration.[59] Furthermore, it has
been observed that selective plating yielded signiﬁcantly lower transfer frequencies.[60]
A CFU based protocol is more time consuming and tedious, since more preparation is
needed and there is an extra growth step making it non-suited for high throughput. It
was therefore decided to focus on the use of the ﬂow cytometry based method.
9.3
Optimising conditions for ﬂow cytometry based conjugation
assay
These experiments laid the foundation for the variables used in future experiments. Re-
sults from this part is however not done with more than one sample and have not been
replicated. They therefore therefore be misleading. One of the variables that was contin-
ued was the higher amount of the donors, as a quite high amount was needed to get a
good result. This is supported by the work of Pinedo et al. 2004 where it was suggested
to use OD=1 of their P. putida donor compared to OD=0.2 and 0.3 respectively for their
two P. aeruginosa donors to get the best results.[60] A compromise of these facts were
used when deciding the ratio for the mutant experiment. These tests also showed that
when growing the PA14 recipient at 42◦for 3 hours, more donor would be present in the
end sample. Results from OSA knockouts showed a higher amount of donor in the sam-
ples. One reason for the higher amount of donor in the 42◦could therefore be, that it has
been seen that P. aeriginosa growth at elevated temperature is correlated with decreased
chain length of the O-antigen. [61] A positive eﬀect was seen from a higher concentra-
tion of cells in the inoculum mix, but a negative eﬀect of this on the donor percentage.
This might suggest that the close interaction could beneﬁt the P. aeruginosa or inhibit
the P. putida. This is supported by P. aeruginosas ability to outcompete other bacteria
in mixed cultures.[62] Results additionally showed a higher transconjugate percentage of
the ﬁlter matings, but since puddle mating was used more throughout this project, it was
thought to be the best way of getting consistent results in the last experiments. For future
experiments it might be an advantage to use ﬁlter matings, which is is also supported by
experiments comparing the methods.[63] Filter mating is also seen as the favored method
in many protocols working with conjugation.[55][47][47]
9.4
Comparison of conjugation in mutants
When setting up the ﬂow experiments, it was hypothesized that it was possible to use
equal amount of donor and recipient, as this would result in the theoretically best result.
[55][63] Preliminary experiments and literature however showed that this needed to be
changed.[60] It was hypothesized that a good recipient/donor ratio was found before this
experiment, but as seen from the results, only small amounts of the donor was seen in
the wildtype sample compared to earlier experiments. this adds to the trend of the end

percentage of donor being variable. Additionally, this problem became even more complex
as it turned out that the ratio varied a lot between the mutants tested. Since a higher
amount of donor, might give a higher chance of conjugation events, the donor recipient
ratio might have had a large impact on the results.[55][63]
In the OSA knockout mutants, a signiﬁcantly higher amount of the donor, P. putida, was
seen and it would therefore be interesting to investigate what causes this eﬀect. Possible
scenarios for this could be that it is caused by either self-inhibition/growth impairment,
less repression of P. putida from PA14, or higher repression of PA14 by P. putida. One
possible explanation for the self-inhibition scenario is that the LPS genes have been ob-
served to be required for R2 pyocin resistance.[64] However, no visible growth impairment
has been seen when working with the strain. It could therefore be interesting to test this
hypothesis in a simple growth rate experiment comparing OD600 of the wildtype and the
OSA knockouts.
Another possibility is that the O-antigen has an negative eﬀect on P. aeruginosas bioﬁlm
formation as proposed by Huszczynski et al. (2020). [65] A negative eﬀect on bioﬁlm
formation in O-antigen mutants has also been observed in other bacteria.[66]
It was seen that the O-antigen knockouts had a higher tranconjugant percent. This might
suggest that these have an advantage in taking up the plasmid. Another explanation
which might be more plausible is that this is caused by a higher amount of donor in the
sample which is supported by experiment 2 where it was seen that when the amount of
donor are more similar, the results also approach those of the wildtype. Furthermore,
it has been seen in a study by Hong et al. (2017) that the conjugative type IVb pilus
recognizes lipopolysaccharide of recipient cells within P. aeruginosa strains. This study
has also shown that when the O-antigen is missing, transfer of the PAPI-island dropped 3
fold suggesting that the O-antigen might be a factor in eﬀective conjugation.[67] It would
therefore also been interesting to test this on diﬀerent serotypes as originally planed.
The CRISPR knockout showed both in experiment 1 and 2 a slightly lower transconjugant
percentage than that of the wildtype. This does not correlate with the initial hypothesis
of the lack of CRISPR giving an advantage in acquiring DNA horizontally, but since the
diﬀerence is small, it cannot be concluded that there is a diﬀerence. For the CRISPR
knockout to show positive eﬀect on transconjgant percentage, a CRISPR spacer speciﬁc
for the plasmid used should be present in the wildtype which these results might suggest
is not the case.[21]
The transconjugant percentage of the dprA knockout is a bit lower than that of the wild-
type in both experiments. This correlates nicely with theory since the DprA is suggested
to be used for protection of single stranded DNA although only shown to be important
in transformation. [17] The lower percentage of transconjugants might therefore conﬁrm
that DprA has a positive eﬀect on horizontal transfer by conjugation. Since the diﬀerence
is small this is inconclusive. It was also seen that the drpA knockouts had a positive
eﬀect on the amount of donor in the sample which should have increased the number of
transconjugants, but since no higher number transgonjugant is seen, this supports the
previous statement that the lack of dprA has a negative eﬀect on conjugation. The higher

It was seen that two population of mCherry was seen when when analysed at time 0, it

Letting the samples rest for 3 hours gave a much more deﬁned population, which is con-

9.5
Development of isogenic donor strain
It was seen that PA14 tagged with DsRed and mCherry grew on antibiotic speciﬁc plates
and showed to have a small amount of mCherry expression. Many factors may have made
a diﬀerence in why the brightness of the ﬂuorescence tags are so weak in PA14 but it seems
strange that it should be a P. aeruginosa speciﬁc problem since tagging with mCherry is
not a new concept in P. aeruginosa.[53][69] The dim DsRed can partly be explained by
DsRed’s slow maturation and low photo-stability, whereas this should not be a problem
with the mCherry. [70]
A way to test whether the problem is the construct or the cell is to get the original
construct used to create the IMG911 donor. Since this construct is a mini-TN7 construct
it can also be used to tag PA14.[52] The promoter used in the mCherry construct the
tpr promoter which is shown to have similar strength to the lpp promoter in IMG911.[71]
However, to make sure it is not a promoter problem an option could be to change the
promoter of the mCherry construct to the one already used in IMG911 and thereby make
sure it is under control of the same mechanisms.[53] The creation of an isogenic donor
strain would be a substantial improvement for the ﬂow cytometry conjugation assay, since
chance of negative interactions would be greatly reduced. Furthermore as seen in literature
an isogenic donor would in theory greatly increase the conjugation rate.[56] The general
increase in conjugation events would therefore also give a more deﬁned result since more
events can be detected.

Future work
This section will discuss future work needed to be done to conﬁrm the original hypothe-
sis.
Since the correlations of the tested genes were found in a computational manner, not
much was known about the experimental eﬀect of these genes on the diﬀerent kinds of
horizontal transfer. In this project the focus has been on creating an assay to test the
eﬀect on conjugation rate, but the comparisons should also be performed using a setup
for natural transformation and transduction.
The setup for ﬂow cytometry could in principle be transferred to a setup for natural
transformation, where only one ﬂuorescent marker is used, but in reallity the amount of
tranformants found would be so small it would be diﬃcult to run enough cell on the ﬂow
cytometer for the results to be viable.
As mentioned earlier P .aeruginosa has recently been found to be able of performing
natural transformation, and a fairly simple assay to test for transformation rate has been
proposed by the authors of that article Nolan et al (2020).[18] This protocol is based on
the use of a vector with homology and a resistance marker and was normalised vs total
CFU count. Since the dprA deletion plasmid which has been used in this project has
already been tested and proven to work this could be used for that test. By using this
protocol it would in theory be possible to compare transformation rates.
In relation to the test of the natural transformation it could also be interesting to look
into the pilA gene that is a part of the type IV pilus and has been shown to bind DNA.[72]
It has been seen to has an important function in natural transformation since Nolan et
al (2020) also showed that mutants without pilA had a signiﬁcantly lower transformation
rate.[18] PilA has also been seen to be required for transformation in other Pseudomonas.
[73] The reason this could be interesting is that the pilA gene has been seen to be vari-
able in populations and it and its surrounding elements are thought to be horizontal
transferred.[74][75] It could therefore be hypothesised that some pilA genes are better
than other in relation to natural transformation. As earlier mentioned the type IV pilin is
also thought to bin the LPS and could therefore be interesting in relation to the serotype
comparison.[67]
The eﬀect of transduction rate should also be tested, although a bit more tricky, since
phages are very diverse and have diﬀerent receptor binding sites. One of the issues being
able to do this is that phages are very speciﬁc in receptor bind and it should there-
fore be thought through what phage to use.[13] Phages are shown to bind the LPS of
P.aeruginosa, an example of this is the bacteriophage E79 where it has been seen that
that roughness of the LPS complex is correlated to resistance against the phage. [76] It
would be interesting to use one of these LPS speciﬁc phages to see if a diﬀerence can be
seen in diﬀerent serotypes.

11
Conclusion
Many iteration of the recET protocol was tested but none worked and further work
therefore needs to be done to create isogenic strains with diﬀerent O-antigen. It was
possible to create an assay for determining the amount of transconjugates in a conjugation
mating, which was proven by cell sorting. This setup was also shown to be superior to
the original CFU based setup. A dprA knockout was successfully created and used in
the conjugation experiment. This mutant showed a smaller percentage of transconjugates
suggesting that the lack of dprA has a negative eﬀect on conjugation but the diﬀerence was
to small to conclude on. The CRISPR knockout also showed a slightly lower percentage of
transconjugants but not enough to see a diﬀerence. The two O-antigen knockout showed
a higher percentage of transconjugants compared to the wildtype, suggesting that the lack
of O-antigen has an eﬀect on conjugation. This can not be concluded since these mutants
had a signiﬁcantly higher donor amount compared to the wildtype and that the higher
amount of donor could be the cause of the many transconjugants. The donor recipient
ratio in the OSA knockouts show an eﬀect of OSA on the P. putida - P. aeruginosa
interaction. The amount of transconjugants in the samples were fairly low and further
optimisation of the assay is therefore needed, to get better and more deﬁned results. Work
on a better donor was done but without success.
