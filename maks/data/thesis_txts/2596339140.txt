Abstract
This thesis focuses on the development of non-linear axisymmetric finite element models
in Plaxis to study the behaviour of suction caissons when loaded vertically. In particular,
cyclic dynamic loads are considered, with special focus on frequencies relevant for the
dynamic behaviour of wind turbines.
Two dynamic benchmarks are considered to evaluate if the axisymmetric models devel-
oped in Plaxis are able to correctly capture the behaviour of a suction caisson in dynamic
loading. The results obtained in Plaxis are compared to those obtained by Gazetas 1983
and Latini and Zania 2020.
A parametric study is performed to evaluate the effect of load frequency and intensity
on the behaviour of the suction caisson. Non-linear impedance functions are computed
for the different load levels over a broad frequency range and compared with equivalent
linear-elastic ones.
For the parametric study, the soil considered is a dense sand and two different soil models
available in Plaxis are used to model it: the Hardening Small Strain (HS Small) model
and the UBC3D-PLM model. Their performance is evaluated by comparison with triaxial
monotonic and cyclic experimental tests (Latini and Zania 2016 and Truong et al. 2020).
It was verified that the frequency of the load significantly influences the behaviour of the
suction caisson, regarding transition from transient to steady state, tip and shaft behaviour
and failure surface development. A natural frequency was identified based on the results
from the parametric study. The load intensity also influenced significantly the behaviour
of the caisson, although less than the frequency.
Regarding the impedance functions, it was observed that non-linear behaviour lowers the
real component of the impedance function (i.e. the stiffness) and increases the imaginary
one (i.e. the damping) for lower frequencies. It was also observed that a distinct behaviour
exists for the imaginary component between lower and higher frequencies.

Introduction
Suctions caissons, also referred to as steel-skirted foundations, plated or bucket foun-
dations or even suction cans, consist of an invert steel or concrete bucket embedded in
the foundation soil. The upper lid of the caisson is similar to a normal footing foundation,
while the skirt of the suction caisson penetrates the soil, confining it inside as a sort of plug.
The presence of a skirt usually increases the bearing capacity of the foundation (vertical,
horizontal and overturning moment) and decreases vertical and horizontal displacements
and rotations. In addition, it compensates for irregularities in the soil, improves erosion
resistance under the foundation and enables it to sustain tensile loads, which cannot be
resisted in normal shallow foundations.
Suction caissons have traditionally been used as anchorages for floating offshore struc-
tures of different types (Houlsby et al. 2005). More recently, they have been applied as
foundations for smaller projects, including offshore wind turbines (OWTs). Suction cais-
sons have also been used successfully as foundations for tension-leg-platforms (TLP),
such as Snorre A (in the Norwegian North Sea), shown in Figure 1.1. Typically concrete
is preferred in these cases (Gourvenec and Randolph 2011).
Figure 1.1: Snorre A platform foundation arrangement and suction caisson details (from
Zdravkovic et al. 2001).
The most widely used foundation type for OWTs is a single monopile, typically applied for
water depths between 20 and 40 m. For larger water depths, this foundation becomes too
flexible and other economical alternatives need to be explored (O’Kelly and Arshad 2016).
A possibility is to use suction caissons, either in a tripod arrangement or as a monopod (as
indicated in Figure 1.1). The latter can also be applied for TLP, as mentioned above. This
configuration decreases the overturning moment, which is resisted mainly by compression
and tension in the foundation (Villalobos et al. 2010). In this particular scenario, suction
caissons also have the advantage of being more easily installed and cheaper compared
with piles (Iskander and El-Gharbawy 2011). They also have the benefit of producing less
noise and vibration during installation and overall less environmental impact in the marine
environment.
For OWTs, cyclic loads imposed by wind and waves are the main load component, unlike
the loads typically found for suction caissons applied as anchorages, which are mainly

tural behaviour of the OWTs. The system’s eigenfrequency is also significantly influenced
by the foundation stiffness, which depends on the soil properties, the loads transferred
to the foundation, drainage conditions and other factors, and these change over time.
Therefore, the stiffness of the response may change over the design life of the OWTs
(Bienen et al. 2018a) with changing conditions in the soil.
Practical experience in using suction caissons at full scale for offshore renewable energy
has been rather limited – although some experimental and numerical studies are available
(see Section 2), the study of the behaviour of suction caisson subjected to dynamic loading
is still in need of further development (Bienen et al. 2018b). Trial OWTs structures have
been installed and tested, but only recently are suction caissons being designed for a
commercial scale application (Bienen et al. 2018a).
The majority of the research done in the past was focused on the response of suction
caissons as anchorages, where the dominant load is horizontal combined with a lower
tension load. This is different from the foundation case, where vertical, horizontal and
moment loading are applied. In this case, suction caissons usually have a higher skirt
length-to-diameter (Villalobos et al. 2010).
For OWTs in particular, cyclic loading due to the rotor movement, waves and wind, is of
particular interest. As stated above, if a multi-pod arrangement is used, the overturning
moment produced by these loads will mainly be absorbed at the foundation level as a
combination of compression and tension (Bienen et al. 2018a).
1.1
Installation
Caisson foundations can be installed by pushing (applying a vertical force at the top) or
suction method. In both cases, an initial phase of penetration due to the caisson’s weight
alone is allowed. After this, either method can be applied to complete the installation. The
suction caisson, as its name indicates, is usually installed by applying under-pressure
(‘suction’) through its interior. The difference between the hydrostatic water pressure
outside the caisson and the reduced pressure inside provides a differential that induces
a penetration force in addition to the self-weight. After the installation procedure, the
caisson’s interior is sealed off and vertical loading creates an internal under-pressure,
which mobilizes the end bearing resistance of the soil at the caisson tip (Andersen et al.
2005).
Senders 2008 provides an overview and comparison of the suction installation process
and of existing installation prediction methods. These methods can be based on CPT
data or a beta approach and can include reduction in penetration resistance during suc-
tion installation or not, among other. They typically predict the depth at which the suction
caisson is as the suction progresses. Senders 2008 also developed an alternative in-
stallation method for the case of dense sands, based on centrifuge results done by the
author. Comparing all the prediction methods for suction installation, it is verified that
these give similar results, and that the Houlsby and Byrne method (Houlsby et al. 2005)
is recommended as the most reliable choice by Senders 2008.

Bearing Capacity
After installation, the caisson acts much like a short rigid pile foundation and is capa-
ble of resisting both lateral and axial loads (Andersen et al. 2005). Suction caissons are
somewhat in between a footing and a pile, but traditionally the estimation of their bear-
ing capacity has been based on the classical bearing capacity equations for failure of a

vertically loaded strip foundation according to Gourvenec and Randolph 2011), combined
with factors to account for load orientation, eccentricity, etc.
This approach has serious short-comings when the shallow foundation does not fit the
basic model, as for example if the shape is circular or the shear strength profile is non-
uniform. Its application for offshore foundation design is also questionable, since this
method does not consider tensile capacity, which is mobilized in offshore structures due
to wind or wave loads. The presence of a skirt, as in the case of suction caissons, means
that the interaction between the horizontal and moment degrees of freedom is significant,
becoming more pronounced as the skirt embedment increases (Gourvenec and Randolph
2011).
In addition, the soil plug inside the caisson can be assumed to be rigid or flexible. In the
case of a purely vertical load, this consideration does not affect significantly the bearing
capacity result, but for combined loading considering the plug to be flexible reduces the
capacity of a suction caisson substantially (Foglia and Ibsen 2014). Therefore, it is usually
recommended to account for the soil plug as non-flexible, according to Gourvenec and
Randolph 2011.
When a shallow foundation is subjected to horizontal and vertical loads combined with mo-
ments, the interaction between these different components should be considered when
determining the bearing capacity. One possible way to do this is by representing com-
bined loading through interaction diagrams or failure envelopes (V, H and M). Any load
combination inside the envelope is considered to be safe and the opposite is true if the
load combination is outside the envelope. These envelopes will depend on several fac-
tors, such as undrained or drained conditions, foundations shape, level of embedment,
etc. (Gourvenec and Randolph 2011).

1.3
Motivation
The aim of this thesis is the development of non-linear axisymmetric finite element models
in Plaxis to study the behaviour of suction caissons when loaded vertically. The study will
focus on cyclic loading, particularly for frequencies that are representative of typical loads
for wind turbines: 0.1 to 0.5 Hz for waves, below 0.1 Hz for wind, according to Arany et al.
2016. Very high frequencies are also relevant for the case of an earthquake load, for
example, and are therefore also included.
By performing a parametric study focusing on the effect of the load frequency and inten-
sity on the response, it will contribute to a better understanding of how suction caissons
would behave when changes in these parameters occur. To better evaluate the impact of
frequency and load level on the suction caisson when considering a non-linear behaviour
for the soil, the vertical impedance of the foundation is also estimated and compared to
the case of an linear elastic soil.

Literature Overview
2.1
Caisson Behaviour
In this chapter, a general overview of the literature available for suction caissons is pre-
sented, focusing on vertical and combined (vertical and lateral) loads, both static and
dynamic. Although vertical loads are the main focus of this thesis, a review of the litera-

2.1.1
Vertical Load
Experimental tests
Byrne and Houlsby 2002 conducted experimental investigations into the vertical loading
response of suction caissons. The tests involved cyclic loading around different mean
values (including cycling into tension) and monotonic loading at different rates. The di-
mensions used for the caisson laboratory prototypes were D = 150 mm and L = 50 mm;
D = 100 mm and L = 66 mm and D = 300 mm and L = 100 mm, where D is the diam-
eter of the lid and L is the skirt length. The soil consisted of saturated sand with viscous
silicon oil, to achieve a drainage time representative of offshore conditions.
The cyclic load was introduced in a way as to consider a load train incorporating an ex-
treme event (extreme load inserted in a pseudo-random loading), to simulate what occurs
when large waves pass offshore structures. Besides that, a range of values for the pa-
rameters that define the load train was considered, such as mean load, cyclic load and
cyclic period.
As the amplitude of the cycles increase, the level of the permanent and cyclic displace-
ments also increases gradually. However, there was no definite threshold load after which
there was rapid degradation of the performance (stiffness), even when the caisson was
cycled into tension. There was also no discernible effect of changing the cyclic period.
There was little evidence of cyclic degradation of the response for the displacements and
loads that were applied in cyclic loading and no rate-dependent behaviour was observed.
Monotonic (pull-out) loading tests conducted at different rates were also undertaken to
investigate the tensile response. It was observed that large tensile capacities occurred
for large displacements, but were limited by cavitation of the pore fluid. Surprisingly, the
rate of loading had little effect on this initial transient response. This initial soft response
degraded upon application of successive larger tensile loads.
Kelly et al. 2006 conducted a series of tests on suction caissons subjected to vertical
monotonic an cyclic loading in a pressurized chamber.
A total of 27 tests were per-
formed, varying the pressure (either 0 or 200 kPs, to simulate 20 m of water depth), type of
sand used (Redhill 110 and Oakamoor HPF5), pullout rate and type of cyclic loading (fre-
quency). The model caisson was 280 mm in diameter, had a skirt length of 180 mm and
wall thickness of 3.125 mm. The load is applied as a multiple-amplitude cyclic loading,
meaning that the frequency is maintained but the amplitude is progressively increased in
successive phases.
The test results indicate that increased pressure has virtually no effect on the vertical
stress–deformation response (unless cavitation occurs). Water pressure on the caisson
lid increased with increasing load amplitude and water pressure at caisson tip was a small
fraction of that measured at the lid.

By examining the relative phases of the displacement, velocity, stress and pressure in the

approximately in phase with the velocity for Redhill 110 sand, but closer to the phase of the
displacement in the tests conducted in the finer HPF5 sand. In HPF5 sand, as the cyclic

Regarding cyclic load frequency, the stiffness increased very slowly and at a decreasing
rate throughout the test, which Kelly et al. 2006 state suggests that the stiffness could
be treated simply as constant without significant error. This is supported by the fact that
there was no evidence of degradation of stiffness during cycling – on the contrary, the
stiffness was constant or increasing.
The author also found that the magnitude of the ambient pressure has little or no effect
on the deformations of the caisson during cyclic loading, while in contrast, the magnitude
of the ambient pressure does increase the ultimate tensile load available. It was also
concluded that the magnitude of the pore pressures within the soil is relatively small,
indicating partial drainage conditions. As the rate of cycling increases, the positive pore
pressures beneath the caisson increase, as does the vertical stiffness of the caisson.
Villalobos et al. 2010 studied the response of suction caissons in clay (overconsolidated
Speswhite kaolin clay) when subjected to monotonic and cyclic vertical loads. The cyclic
amplitudes were selected to take the suction caisson into tension. The dimensions for the
caisson used were D = 150 mm, L = 150 mm and t = 1 mm.
The experiments considered three stages: installation, monotonic vertical loading and
cyclic vertical loading. Three sets of tests were carried out, each with a sequence of
increasing load intensity of 10 cycles per load set – the first set with an average load
intensity equal to the bearing capacity of the caisson, the second and third sets with an
average of zero. Between the last two cases, only the method of installation (pushing vs.
suction) was varied. Considering the differences in the soil samples and in the load type,
a total of 8 tests were conducted.
It was shown that the installation method had no substantial impact in the suction cais-
son’s behaviour, although a substantial difference in the amount of uplift was observed. It
was observed that the large magnitude of non-dissipated pore water pressure generated
during the suction caisson installation procedure reduces the amount of uplift.
The tensile loading response softened substantially when the caisson had been previously
loaded into compression failure. In addition, cyclic vertical loading around a compressive
mean value induced settlements in the caisson, while cyclic loading around a mean of
zero produced permanent uplift of the caisson.
Bienen et al. 2018a, 2018b have recently conducted an extensive experimental study
of suction caissons’ behaviour under vertical loading. The research focused on suction
caissons in dense sand conducted through centrifuge experiments. The tests simulated
in-situ conditions as closely as possible – after a full sequence of self-weight penetration
and assisted installation, cyclic load would be applied followed by extraction.
The experiments were designed to simulate tripod supporting structure for an OWT. The
prototype caisson had the following dimensions: D = 80 mm (corresponding to 8 m)
L = 40 mm (4 m) and t = 0.5 mm (50 mm). The load paths were selected to be repre-

Achmus et al. 2013 conducted a parametric study regarding the behaviour of monopod

As a starting point, the FE model was validated with field test results, proving that the be-

to the soil via bedding pressures and skin friction acting in the bucket skirt.

strongly influenced by the geometry and relative density of the sand. Normalized equa-
tions for the load-moment interaction curves are proposed, which describe the caisson’s
capacity as a function of its geometry.

2.1.2
Combined Load
Experimental tests
Houlsby et al. 2005 published a compilation of research work done for design of suction
caissons for offshore wind turbines, including laboratory, field tests and numerical analy-
sis.
Several tests, among them the largest available in the literature (with regard to caisson
size), were conducted on the prototype turbine Vestas V90-3MW in Frederikshavn, Den-
mark. The suction caisson was installed in sand and the tests were conducted for different
horizontal forces at different heights, under constant vertical load. The caisson has the
dimensions D = 2 m and L = 2 m, with a thickness t = 12 mm.
Other additional laboratory studies, conducted mainly at Aalborg and Oxford universities,
were reported in the aforementioned compilation done by the authors, as were laboratory
tests conducted for suction caissons in clay subjected to combined vertical and horizontal
load.
Based on the field and laboratory tests, the authors conclude that monopod foundation
design is dominated by moment loading, while for a tripod/tetrapod foundation, tensile
loading is the most significant. In addition, as the amplitude of moment loading increases,
stiffness reduces and hysteresis increases (the same applies for vertical loads) and once
tension is reached, there is a sudden reduction of stiffness. Lastly, they also observed
that, while high ultimate tensile capacities are possible, this is at the expense of large
movements.

Gourvenec and Barnett 2011 proposed a closed-form expression that enables the esti-

vertical load, H - horizontal load and M - moment) obtained by FEA in the software Abaqus.
This closed-form expression provides a less conservative estimate than classical bearing
capacity theory. The envelopes obtained were validated with previous analytical and ex-
perimental results. Skirted foundations with different values of D/L were considered, the
undrained shear profile was either constant or increasing linearly with depth and diverse

Penzes et al. 2016 analysed the behaviour of suction caissons for offshore wind turbines
subjected to combined loading (V-H) in clay and sand soils. Three dimensional FE mod-
els were developed in Plaxis 3D to study the failure mechanisms of suction caissons in
dense sand and normally consolidated clay. For the sand, two different constitutive soil
models (HS small and Mohr-Coulomb) were examined to analyze the effect on the failure
mechanism, and for clay, one model with an undrained shear strength profile proportional
to depth and one with constant shear strength were considered. The length to diameter
ratio L/D was 1 with D = 6 m and the suction caissons were considered rigid. The force
was applied at different angles from the horizontal axis – 30, 40, 50 and 60 degrees.
For sand, it was observed that the failure surface does not extend to the tip of the skirt
in the horizontal loading case, but as the inclination increases the failure wedge extends
to the tip of the skirt and further laterally as well. As for the effect of the constitutive soil
model, the authors report that smaller displacements are required to reach failure in the
case of HS small model compared to the Mohr-Coulomb case. This difference becomes
more apparent as the inclination increases. The failure envelope was compared with the
experimental results obtained by Byrne 2000 and showed good agreement.
For clay, it was observed that the progress in the failure mechanism was similar regardless
of the profile used for the shear strength. As the inclination increases, the failure surface
expands (on the passive side) and when the load is purely vertical, the failure surface
is concentrated beneath the suction caisson tip. The envelope obtained from the 3D FE
model was compared with the analytical solution proposed by Supachawarote et al. 2004
and again showed very good agreement.
2.2
Soil Models
Several constitutive models are available in Plaxis for soil modelling. For the case of co-
hesionless soils, the recommended models are HS small (Hardening Soil with small strain
stiffness) and UBC3D-PLM, a sand model developed to capture liquefaction phenomena.
These two models are described in this section.
2.2.1
Hardening Small Strain Model
The Hardening Soil model with small strain stiffness (HS small) was formulated by Schanz
et al. 1999 and calibrated for loose sands from available experimental data. The imple-
mentation of the model in Plaxis is further detailed in PLAXIS 2020a.
The HS small model is based in the classical theory of plasticity, with total strains being
calculated using a stress-dependent stiffness, which is different for virgin loading and

UBC3D-PLM Model
The UBC3D-PLM allows modelling of the onset of the liquefaction phenomenon. It is
based on the 2D model UBCSAND developed by Puebla et al. 1997 for the liquefaction of
sandy soils. The model uses isotropic and simplified kinematic hardening rules for primary
and secondary yield surfaces, in order to take into account the effect of soil densification
and produce a smooth transition into the liquefied state during undrained cyclic loading.
By means of a simplified Rowe stress-dilatancy theory, it is capable of modelling cyclic
liquefaction for different stress paths (Petalas and Galavi 2003).
In the original UBCSAND model, the formulation is based on classical plasticity theory
with a hyperbolic strain hardening rule, which is based on the Duncan-Chang approach
with modifications. The hardening rule relates the mobilized friction angle to the plastic
shear strain at a given stress. The model uses a 2D Mohr-Coulomb yield surface and
a corresponding non-associated plastic potential function. This flow rule is based on the
stress-dilatancy theory developed by Rowe 1962. The main improvement of UBC3D-PLM
is the generalization for a 3D-formulation. In addition, a non-associated plastic potential
function based on Drucker-Prager’s criterion is used, to maintain the assumption of stress-
strain coaxiality in the deviatoric plane for a stress path beginning from the isotropic line
(Petalas and Galavi 2003).
The UBC3D model uses the well known Mohr-Coulomb yield functions generalized in the
3D principal stress space. When the stress state is inside the region defined by the yield
surfaces, elastic behaviour is produced. When the stress path touches the yield surface,
plasticity starts to occur. The model includes two yield surfaces in order to distinguish

Finite Element Modelling
Before the axisymmetric finite element models developed in Plaxis are used in the pro-
posed parametric study, it is necessary to evaluate how well the dynamic response is
captured by the model, by comparing this response against published and theoretically
solid examples in the literature. Additionally, the available constitutive models for sand
behaviour need to be validated and calibrated using experimental data, for both static and
cyclic loading.
As such, the first part of the study focuses in reproducing the results obtained by Gaze-
tas 1983 for shallow foundations subjected to dynamic loads induced by machine equip-
ment. In particular, the model of a rigid circular foundation in an elastic soil stratum over
a bedrock is considered. This serves as an initial calibration of the FE models used in
Plaxis for the suction caisson and provides a training phase in the use of the software,
before its application for the main case study being considered.
The second part concentrates in reproducing the results obtained by Latini and Zania
2020 for the vertical impedance of suction caissons. This corresponds to the final phase
of validation for the Plaxis FE models and to a case study which is similar to the model
used for the parametric study detailed in section 4.
Section 3.2 focuses on the calibration of the parameters used in the soil constitutive mod-
els in Plaxis, based on the available experimental data.
Lastly, section 3.3 describes the final parameters considered in this study and includes
an overview of the FE model that is implemented in Plaxis.

Plaxis Model
The model developed in Plaxis is axisymmetric and the surface foundation is modelled
using a rigid plate. A distributed unit load is applied on the plate (see Figure 3.1).
The kinematic boundary conditions are considered as normally fixed in the x-axis for both
limits of the model, and free and fixed for the upper and lower y-axis, respectively the
free surface and the rigid bedrock. A viscous dynamic boundary condition is applied for
the right border (x-max limit), while the remaining borders were considered without any
dynamic boundary condition, since x-min (left border) is the axisymmetric axis, y-min is
the rigid bedrock and y-max is the free surface.
It is recommended (PLAXIS 2020b) that the viscous boundaries are used for problems
where the dynamic source is inside the mesh and free-field boundaries when the dynamic
source is applied as a boundary condition (example: earthquake motion). Since the load
applied is generated at the top of the mesh, viscous boundaries are used.

3.2
Calibration of Constitutive Models
The two soil models discussed in section 2.2 (HS Small and UBC3D-PLM) are considered
in this section as potential constitutive models for the parametric study. The calibration
process is based on triaxial test results from Latini and Zania 2016 and Truong et al. 2020.
Latini and Zania 2016 examined the influence of the relative density on the strength and
deformation characteristics of Fountainebleau sand by performing drained and undrained
compression triaxial tests on saturated sand at different densities and confining stresses.
In these tests, at approximately 50% of the expected peak deviatoric stress, an unloading
and reloading cycle was applied, after which the sample was loaded in displacement
control to full failure (approximately 15% axial strain).
Truong et al. 2020 undertook a systematic study of the response of monopiles to lat-
eral cyclic loading for sands at different densities using beam and drum centrifuge tests.
Monotonic and one-way cyclic triaxial tests were also performed on Fountainebleau sand.
A preliminary analytical estimation of the parameters is presented in section 3.2.1, fol-
lowed by the first calibration based on the results from triaxial tests done by Latini and
Zania 2016 in section 3.2.2.
In section 3.2.3, the parameters determined in section 3.2.2, will be used as a starting
point for the calibration based on the cyclic triaxial test results by Truong et al. 2020, for
a loading duration of 10 cycles and 100 cycles. Based on the results of the calibration
process, a set of parameters will be proposed for the soil models.

Parametric Study
In the parametric study, the model described in section 3.1.2 will be used, adopting the
same geometry but using the soil model HS Small.
As a reference for the level of load to be considered in the analysis, the ultimate load
capacity is first estimated, considering the suction caisson as a shallow foundation em-
bedded on the soil at a depth of 10 meters as a first approximation. These calculations
result in a ultimate load capacity of 50.451 MPa (see Annex A for details).
Section 3.3.1 presents an overview of the parameters considered for this study. Section
3.3.2 presents a discussion of the Plaxis model in more detail, including the dynamic
loading employed.
3.3.1
Parameters
The parametric study considers the variation of two parameters: the load amplitude, de-
fined in relation to the ultimate capacity (Vmax/Vf) and frequency of the load (a0). The

sive load.

fixed: G0 = 175 MPa,Eur = 99.6 MPa, g = 0.175%, m = 0.5 (as described in section

the parametric study.

In this section, the impedance functions based on the results from the parametric study
described in section 3.3, are calculated and commented upon.
A range of frequencies (a0) between 0.2 and 6.1 was considered, which includes the full

Kv is high and frequency ω is low, which results in a high value for Cv. As the hysteresis
loops increase in area with increasing frequency the loss factor 2ξ becomes higher, but
Kv is lower for higher frequencies and ω is high, which implies much lower values for Cv.
From Figure 5.6, it can be said that the higher the load-level, the higher the loss factor,
with a0 = 6.1 being the only exception. In both cases, the non-linear model produces
a much higher loss factor than the elastic model. This is to be expected, as the plastic
behaviour dissipates energy, acting as form of damping.
It can also be seen from Figure 5.5 that Cv shows a very distinct trend between higher and
lower frequencies (the division occurs around a0 = 2). This seems to indicate a transition
between a behaviour in which the damping coefficient is very dependent on frequency to
one where the coefficient is almost entirely independent of frequency (since for a0 > 2,
Cv remains at an almost constant value). This indicates a a transition between mostly
hysteric damping for low frequencies to mostly viscous damping for high frequencies.

6
Conclusions
In this study the non-linear response of suction caissons subjected to a dynamic axial load
was analyzed.
A first benchmark study was done for the case of a circular shallow foundation. The
results were compared with the ones produced by Gazetas 1983 and this comparison
served as an initial calibration of the dynamic FE models in Plaxis, before its application
to the caisson model.
After this, a second benchmark to reproduce the results obtained by Latini and Zania
2020 for the vertical impedance of caissons was done. This allowed for the verification
of the FE model geometry and that estimating the vertical impedance based on vertical
displacements from Plaxis yelds good results.
As a last step to define the FE model, a calibration of the soil model parameters was
done. Two different soil models were considered: HS Small and UBC3D-PLM. A first
analytical estimation of the parameters was done, and these estimations were calibrated
by comparing the results from triaxial tests done by Latini and Zania 2016 with the Soil Test
analyses done in Plaxis. Considering these adjusted parameters, a cyclic triaxial test was
simulated in Plaxis and the results were compared with the results obtained by Truong
et al. 2020. This calibration was done first for 10 cycles of cyclic load application and
then for 100 cycles. The UBC3D-PLM model was verified to be inadequate to accurately
capture the behavior of the dense sand studied. Therefore the only soil model considered
in the parametric study was HS Small.
6.1
Frequency
Based on the displacement values obtained for the different frequencies, it was verified
that there is a natural frequency for the soil layer at approximately a0 = 2.4 (ω = 27
rad/s). The displacement amplification reaches its peak for this frequency and is lower for
the remaining frequencies.
The frequency of the load influences significantly the behaviour at the tip and shaft. For
lower frequencies (in the range 0.2 < a0 < 2.4) both tip and shaft resistance are mobilized
in the first peak of the load train, while for higher frequencies the response of the shaft
and tip takes much longer to develop, being minimal for the highest frequency a0 = 24.2.
The extension of the failure surface is consistent with this observation: for lower frequen-
cies (and particularly those close to the natural frequency), the plastification of the soil is
more extensive, and for the estimated natural frequency a0 = 2.4, the failure surface is
well-defined and matching the theoretically predicted shape.
For high frequencies, in the range of a0 > 6.1, the soil beneath the top of the caissons lid
experiences dilation instead of contraction, which is a phenomena that was not observed
for lower frequencies, in which the soil was always in contraction.
It was observed that the stress path is also significantly influence by the frequency, with
the deviatoric stress being more relevant (and the shear strain higher) in the case of higher
frequencies. For lower frequencies, in which the dynamic load is applied more ”slowly”,
the mean effective stress is more relevant. This would also explain why the soil beneath
the lid of the caisson is experiencing dilation and not contraction for the higher frequencies
(a0 > 6.1).

6.2
Load Level
Although the general behaviour in transient and steady state for different load levels is
similar, slight differences are observed. The point at which the transition to steady state
occurs shifts with an increase in load level. The normalized amplitude of vibration is also
lower for a higher average load, although the maximum displacement reached is nearly
the same for both load levels.
For lower frequencies, the difference between the two load levels are more noticeable,
with the normalized displacement and amplitude of vibration being both slightly lower for
the 40% load level.
For a higher load, the shaft and tip resistance are mobilized more quickly (at the first
peak) and plastification is observed in a larger area surrounding the caisson, as expected.
Despite this, the frequency seems to affect the plastification behaviour more than the load
level, at least for the load levels reported in this study.
The load level also affects the stress path behaviour for the different frequencies. While
the general stress level reached (q and p′) is higher for a higher load, the deviatoric stress
is more relevant for a lower load level while for a higher load level the mean effective stress
is higher and contributes more in determining the stress path for each frequency. This
seems to indicate that, for a combination of both lower average stress and high frequency,
the shearing behaviour has a higher contribution for the stress-strain behaviour of the
caisson. For lower frequencies and higher average load, the stress-strain behaviour of
the caisson is more affected by volumetric strain and pressure effects.
6.3
Impedance
The non-linear impedance was estimated according to the methodology described in sec-
tion 5.1, both the real (Kv) and imaginary (Cv) components. The values obtained for Kv
using the non-linear soil model were significantly lower than the ones obtained for the lin-
ear elastic case, due to hardening of the soil under cyclic loading. The difference between
load levels was not significant, with the curves for 20% and 40% being relatively close.
It was also observed that for higher frequencies (a0 close to 6), Kv becomes negative due
to the out-of-phase behaviour between load and displacement. This was also the case
when analysing the strain-stress history for the higher frequency range.
For Cv it was observed that there is a transition between low and high-frequency behaviour
as well. Up to a0 = 2, the damping coefficient is very dependent on frequency, while for
higher frequencies, it is almost constant. This indicates a a transition between mostly
hysteric damping for low frequencies to mostly viscous damping for high frequencies.

Future Developments
The work presented focused on evaluating the response of suction caissons to different
frequencies of dynamic load and at different load levels by performing a parametric study.
This also included the determination of the non-linear impedance function for the cases
considered.
A more extensive qualitative evaluation of the behaviour of the soil should be the next
step, including analysis of more stresses points, such as at the tip of the caisson, outside
the caisson, etc. This would provide a more detailed analysis of the non-linear behaviour
of suction caissons subjected to dynamic vertical loading, which is outside the scope of
this thesis.
In addition, future work regarding the assessment of vertical non-linear behaviour of suc-
tion caissons can be expanded to evaluate the impact of the load direction, more load
levels and different properties of the soil. More sophisticated soil models could potentially
be tested.
The consideration of different geometries for the suction caisson (diameter and skirt length)
as well as for the soil layer, would also be relevant. A soil layer with different thickness and
non-uniform soil layer will have a significant impact in the non-linear impedance function.
The expansion to include these parameters would allow for the calculation and compar-
ison of more values for the non-linear impedance function, providing a better evaluation
of the non-linear behaviour of suction caissons loaded vertically. The consideration of
non-linearity can also be extended to lateral loads, as well as combined loading.
