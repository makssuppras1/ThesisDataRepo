Abstract
Most labratory experiments utilized to obtain petrophysical and mechanical properties from rocks, of-
ten assumes samples to be isotropic. This holds a potential for biased interpretation of results, as this
assumption will fall if anisotropy arises.
The primary aim of this project is to investigate the possibility to apply additional value-adding in-
formation to experimental results by obtaining information regarding anisotropic stiﬀness by extracting
small samples from regular sized laboratory samples. How well this procedure performs in terms of
obtaining this information is discussed.
The ﬁndings reveals satisfactory results for the porosity and for the p- and s- waves, which concur
well with data from other projects, and shows that it is possible to calculate the elastic parameters, as
well as the anisotropic stiﬀness parameters from the obtained waves. However, certain challenges arise
as the results obtained shows some contradicting behavior. This makes the evaluation of the results a
perplexing matter, and questions the validity to some degree.
The ﬁndings from the data treatment suggests that the experimental procedure conducted in this
study could be utilized in further research, given that some modiﬁcations are applied.

Chapter 1
Introduction
During a geological preliminary survey of an area, materials of the rock in question is collected for lab-
oratory testing in order to obtain information about the petrophysical and rock mechanical properties
of that rock. This is valuable information for a driller or geologist because it is used to characterize
and evaluate the rock strength or stiﬀness of the rock.
Most laboratory experiments utilized to obtain these properties of rocks are conducted assuming that
the rock samples investigated are isotropic, but where anisotropy arises, this assumption fall. Anisotropy
is what isotropy isn’t. Isotropy is the state of uniformity in all orientations, meaning that the vector
measurement of a rock property is the same in all directions. In an anisotropic medium, the vector
measurement varies with direction. Assuming isotropic conditions could mean that a signiﬁcant amount
of information regarding the rock is being let out of the analysis. A consequence of this may be wrong
interpretation of the most basic laboratory data processing, which could lead to a misinterpreted eval-
uation of the rock mechanical properties.
The primary aim of this project is to investigate the possibility of obtaining more information concerning
the mechanical properties from rock samples utilized for laboratory testing through a test procedure.
This is interesting to investigate because it could potentially be an cost-eﬀective and time eﬃcient
method for characterization of anisotropic stiﬀness in a rock. This is done by extracting three small
samples from regular sized samples (with diameter designation BQ). The small samples are extracted
from three angles in respect to an assumed bedding, 0◦, 45◦, and 90◦. A series of experiments are
conducted on the extracted samples. For the report, four diﬀerent rock types have been investigated.
Together, these rocks represents a whole spectrum of properties that often is found in shale. Shale
is the predominant type of sedimentary rock in the world, and is a rock which is extremely variable

in terms of physical, mechanical, and chemical properties. For this reason, the following rocks are
investigated;
• Low porosity sandstones from Bentheimer and Obernkirchen, Germany
• Low porosity Limestone from Copenhagen
• High porosity Chalk from Stevns-Klint, Denmark
This have created the basis for an analysis and evaluation of whether or not it is feasible to carry out
these experiments in order to obtain more information about the samples at hand.

2.1
Anisotropy
The term anisotropy is used to describe materials in which their properties varies depending on the
direction. If the elastic parameters of a material depend on the material’s orientation when load is
applied, it is anisotropic i.e the elastic moduli of the material will vary with the diﬀerent directions in
the material. Materials in which their properties do not depend on the direction are termed Isotropic.
This class of symmetry is most often assumed by geophysicists due to its governing equations being
much more simple and intuitive than for the lower symmetry classes. This could often lead to errors
in the interpretation of seismic data [25, p. 1954] and less accurate geophysical description of a e.g
hydrocarbon-ﬁeld. Anisotropic materials could be divided into ﬁve classes of symmetry [18, p.24-28],
these are as follows:
• Isotropic
• Cubic
• Hexagonal or transversely isotropic
• Orthorhombic
3
==PAGE:15==
• Monoclinic
There are two types of anisotropy, intrinsic and induced.
Intrinsic anisotropy could also be called
lithological due to the orgin of sedimentary rocks. Sedimentary rocks are formed by the transportation
and deposition of particles and minerals. These accumulate subsequently over time, and form sediments.
In the case of a current-dominated sedimentary environment, the grains will tend to orient themselves
in the direction of the water stream, thus the grains inside of the rock will have a preferred orientation
that could be a source for anisotropy. Another source for anisotropy in sedimentary rocks that may
arise during deposition is the subsequent layering of diﬀerent types of sediments, which may produce
beds with varying materials or grain sizes. In general, the origin of intrinsic anisotropy could arise from
the ﬁve diﬀerent properties of the clastic material inside the rock. At least one of them has to show
some degree of alignment in a preferential direction[1, p. 52-53] and [2, p. 36]:
• Composition
• Size
• Shape
• Orientation
• Packing
Induced anisotropy could arise after deposition during diagenesis, which is where the sediments are
transformed into sedimentary rocks by aging and burial of the sediments. Diagenesis involves the phys-
ical, chemical or biological alternation of sediments. Upon burial, the sediments become compressed
by the subsequently layered sediments. This compression may cause rotation of the minerals preferred
direction (which has been obtained during deposition). Laboratory experiments have shown that com-
pressional waves travel fastest in the direction of the applied stress. A reason for this may be that all
stones in a certain degree contain some distribution of micro-cracks [11, p. 38]. The cracks oriented
with the direction of applied stress will close, while the cracks oriented perpendicular will tend to open
[2, p. 40]. Thus the compressional wave will have a ”cleared” path to travel through (see Fig. 2.1).
Leon Thomsen stated regarding induced anisotropy [25, p. 1957] that ”many examples could be listed,
but it is not clear how to pick representative examples”.

Class of symmetry
The most commonly occurring type and relevant anisotropy for geophysicists is transverse isotropy.
This is considered to be a representative symmetry for horizontally layered sedimentary rocks as well
as for stress induced anisotropy [25, p. 1954] and [11, p. 42]. Due to this, transversely isotropic media
is considered in this project.
Earth materials has two preferred styles of alignment:
• Horizontal
• Vertical
Horizontally aligned sedimentary rocks may have a variation in their elastic properties vertically and be
isotropic along the horizontal layers. This means that they will have a vertical axis of symmetry, thus
these mediums are called Vertical Transversely Isotropic, or VTI-mediums. Vertical style of alignment
is referred to mediums in which their weaknesses are found in vertically aligned cracks or fractures.
This implies that they will have a horizontal axis of symmetry meaning that their properties will vary
in the axis perpendicular to the vertical fractures (crossing planes), thus they are called Horizontally
Transversely Isotropic or HTI-mediums [2, p. 36-37]. These two styles of alignment can be seen in
ﬁgure 2.2.

therefore assumed a horizontal style of alignment-Vertical Transversely Isotropic material.
Weak anisotropy
Most subsurface formations are anisotropic , but to what extent is the question.
Leon Thomsen
suggested in his article named ”Weak Elastic Anisotropy” [25] that most sub surfaces are only weakly
anisotropic, and introduced three parameters in order to intuitively describe the degree of anisotropy
in the medium at hand. This approach come with some major advantages:
• Strongly simpliﬁed cases in which anisotropy is needed to be described
• The parameters are non dimensional, so that one may speak of the parameters in terms of %
• The parameters reduce to zero in the case of isotropy, so that small values (<<1) of anisotropy
can be denoted ”weakly anisotropic”.
The corresponding relations can be appreciated in section 2.3.3.

Elastic wave propagation in rocks
The term ”elastic” are given to waves that propagate as a disturbance through a medium without
causing permanent deformation to the solid material. This ”temporary” deformation is created by a
balance between forces of inertia and of elastic deformation [17, p. 9] [8, p. 9]. As an elastic wave
propagates through the solid material it makes the particles inside to vibrate about their mean positions.
This forces the particles into an oscillatory motion. In ﬁgure 2.5 a solid body is placed between a source
which emits vibrational energy, and a receiver. This solid body is divided into a very large number of
layers. These layers represent each particle inside of the body, and are indicated with letters A, B,
C....X, Y, Z. The energy from the vibrations (disturbances) emitted by the source will cause layer A
to push on the next element in front of it, which is layer B. This motion is transmitted until the last
layer, Z is reached. The time it takes for energy to pass from one layer on to the next will be diﬀerent
from layer to layer, thus the time it takes for the waves to reach the receiver will depend on the solid
materials density and elastic modulus[4, p. 8-9][11, p. 175].
The direction of the particle motion is called Polarization of the wave. In an isotropic solid there is
only one wave that exists when polarization occurs parallel to the plane of propagation. This is called
the longitudinal wave. When polarization occurs normal to the plane of propagation, the wave is called

Stevns-Klint Chalk
Stevns-Klint Chalk, or just Stevns as it is more commonly referred to, is quarried from the Sigerslev

though not in the same quantity. The chalk particles in Stevns chalk holds an rounded-subhedral shape
with a loose-ﬁrm interlocking with varying sizes (0.2-3 µm). The coccoliths are typically in the range
of 2-10 µm. This subsection have been based on information from a PhD thesis written by Morten

Saturation

Procedure
For the experiment, two glass desiccators and a beaker is utilized. The beaker is ﬁlled with distilled fresh

are vacuumed for two hours. The pump is thereafter disconnected and the two glass containers are

samples. The samples are standing on top of a porous plate acts as a section between the water ﬁlled
bottom and the samples. The following procedure is then carried through:
1. Water is poured until it reaches the porous plate / Pump is connected and it is vacuumed for 1
hr
2. Water is poured until 1/4 of the samples is covered with water / vacuum 1 hr
3. Water is poured until 2/4 of the samples is covered with water / vacuum 1 hr
4. Water is poured until 3/4 of the samples is covered with water / vacuum 1 hr
5. Water is poured until 4/4 of the samples is covered with water / vacuum 1 hr
6. Samples are turned upside down and vacuumed for 24 hours in order to increase saturation
The weight of the samples are thereafter noted. This is utilized to calculate the porosity of the small
samples. It is assumed full saturation. The ultrasonic measurements are now repeated on the small

Chapter 5
Discussion
This chapter will form a discussion of the results obtained through the experiments. The discussion is
primarily focused on how much information it is possible to obtain from the small samples.
Four types of rock masses have been drilled and extracted, and prepared into cylindrical shaped samples
with adequate dimensions to conduct well established laboratory experiments on. From the ”normal
sized” samples, three small samples were drilled and extracted from three orthogonal angles to the
assumed bedding; 0◦normal to bedding, 45 ◦to bedding , and 90◦parallel to bedding. Experiments
were conducted on these small samples, in dry and saturated state, which created the basis for an
analysis and evaluation of the degree of weak anisotropic stiﬀness the samples may inhibit.
Porosity on the normal sized samples were measured utilizing three methods. From this experiment
the φnitrogenV , φGV , and φP V were obtained. The grain volume porosity, φGV , and the pore volume
porosity, φP V showed comparable values. Porosity on the small samples were measured by obtaining
the pore volume which was found by the help of the dry and saturated weights of each sample. The
determined porosity for both the sandstones showed to be very comparable. The determined porosity
for chalk shows an 7% higher porosity in sample K-ST20 compared to the two subsequent samples
K-ST245 and K-ST290. Although all three sample porosity are as expected in the higher range, this
deviation may impose on the comparison of the three small samples. The higher porosity is due to
sample K-ST20 having absorbed more ﬂuid during the saturating process. The reason for this may be
that there are more accessible pores to be ﬁlled from the direction normal to bedding. Another reason,
which is may be more applicable to the small scale of the samples, is that the raw material in which the
samples have been extracted from shows a varying degree of induration. As it may be observed in the
discussion, permeability is not discussed, because it was only measured on the normal sized samples.
65
==PAGE:77==
The small samples extracted from the Limestone shows that the determined porosity is alike for samples
LS-K45 and LS-K90. Sample LS-K0 has a smaller porosity, and as mentioned for the chalk samples,
the reason for this may be that the samples have been drilled from a section in which the degree of
induration varies between H3 and H4. To summarize the resulting porosity determined on the samples,
they show to be overall very comparable, although there is a small deviation on sample LS-K0 and a
larger deviation in sample K-ST20. These small diﬀerences may indicate that there have been extracted
samples from a rock with shifting degrees of induration. This have to kept in mind as it may have an
eﬀect on the results from the other experiments.
As it was mentioned in the experimental section, the shear waves that were obtained from the ultra-
sonic measurements presented uncertainties in the readings. Reason for this may be that the samples
measured are to small, thus a shear wave will not be able to develop, and instead were observing a
pressure wave. Another reason may be that the equipment utilized is initially made for testing on larger
samples, thus the frequency used to conduct ultrasonic measurements may have a longer wavelength
than the samples, this of course boils down to the same source of reason; size of samples. The strange
shear wave values were observed on the deviation the sandstones samples presented when compared
to measurements conducted on the same rock type by Tobias Orlander. It was therefore decided to
estimate the shear waves by utilizing the IF-model introduced in section 2.3.1. Thus the estimated shear
waves are used on further calculations for all the rocks, and the vs observed in table 4.2 is modeled with
IF. The P-waves presented in table 4.2 are read at the highest applied load. From table 4.2 it is seen
that the vp/vs ratio for the dry velocities lie in between 1.5-2. It is normally expected that the s-wave
would be twice as small. So the estimated s-waves are in some degree underestimated. However, the
saturated ratio sees an increase, and most lie closer to two. This indicates that overall, the estimated
shear waves modelled with the iso-frame model are reasonable. The dry sandstone sample velocities
made a good correlation with velocity data from Tobias Orlander. The velocities determined on the
limestone samples were compared to data from a bachelors thesis preformed by earlier students at DTU
[7]. The velocities obtained in this master thesis for the limestone samples showed relatively good
correlation with their data. The p-wave velocities determined on the chalk sample K-ST2 were plotted
against p-waves measured on wackestone. From this comparison it is seen that the K-ST2 velocities
are much lower than most of the wackestone velocities. This could be that the load utilized for the
K-ST2 samples was to low.
The results from the ultrasonic experiment (for saturated sandstones and limestone samples) were
also plotted against empirical estimates by Han and Castagna [18, p. 363-366] in order to further
validate the accuracy of the obtained experimental data. The sandstone samples showed some lower
s-waves compared to the estimates by Castagna and Han. The limestone samples shows a better ﬁt

saturated samples would not withstand the same load as in dry condition, and could be crushed.
The diﬀerence that is found in between the dry and saturated velocities will have some eﬀect on the rest

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This chapter presents the main ﬁndings of the study and introduces recommendations for future work.
The porosity determined on the small samples showed that they were overall, comparable with each
other, and satisfactory considering the simple approach to obtain the porosity. In regards to the devi-
ation that sample K-ST0 shows, it could be interesting to conduct an induration test which is widely
used in categorizing limestones which have seen to be having a varying degree of induration. The
ultrasonic wave experiment showed reasonable results for the p-wave determination. In contrast, the
determination of the s-waves were a rather tedious task to get a trustworthy reading from. In the
end it was concluded that when measuring on such small samples, the equipment should be adjusted
accordingly. The ultrasonic velocity experiment were conducted on equipment calibrated for samples
with an larger diameter. This could make it diﬃcult to obtain information about the rock at a small
scale. The shear waves were therefore estimated with the ISO-frame model. These results showed
values that were comparable to those obtained by others. Therefore it was decided to use these in
the continuing calculations. The investigated moduli, E and K showed varying values obtained from
the samples. This may indicate that the assumption of isotropy, in which these calculations are based
on, are wrong. Further investigation required to calculate Voigt’s stiﬀness parameters. These were
calculated from the velocity measurements conducted on the three samples belonging to each rock
type. Voigt’s stiﬀness parameters were then used to calculate Thomsen’s parameters. Although more
tests needs to be conducted before any conclusion can be drawn, they did stay within the range which
is considered to be weakly anisotropic (<<1). It is however a clear diﬀerence between the parameters
in dry conditions compared to those in saturated conditions. The observation shows that the ﬂuid has
an signiﬁcant eﬀect on the results.
70
==PAGE:82==
Recommendations
During the ultrasonic velocity experiment it would have been beneﬁcial to conduct the measurements
at more load points. From this it would have been possible to observe the change of the velocities
at diﬀerent stresses and a trend may have been possible to observe for each small sample. This may
contribute to a more thorough evaluation of the elastic moduli for each sample, in terms of how they
vary depending on the angle that the sample have been extracted from. This will make the evaluation
of the weak anisotropic parameters more intuitive, and increase the certainty of the results from the
experiments. In order to assure readable and reliable s-waves, it is recommended to scale the test equip-
ment accordingly to the small samples, in particular the pressure heads. These needs to be adequate for
a small diameter sample. In addition, it would be wise to calibrate the equipment in conjunction with
the small sample prior to testing. The ultrasonic measurements were carried through on equipment
calibrated for larger samples. This may explain the strange s-waves
Voigt’s matrix for transverse isotropic media can be described by the results obtained from an ucs-
test. For this, it is essential to attach an extensometer to the sample being tested. By doing this, it
is possible to compare Thomsen’s parameters calculated from the ultrasonic measurements, with the
parameters calculated from the ucs-test. This will contribute to give a more detailed analysis in regards
to the anisotropic stiﬀness of the rock.
The experimental procedure presented in this report has its limitations in regards to the rock types
that may be used; It is not recommended to conduct these procedures on soft rock. The chalk from
Stevns-Klint presented diﬃculties during the experiment; In order to extract the small samples, many
attempts had to be carried through in order to obtain the satisfactory dimensions and shape. During the
ultrasonic measurements, the applied load had to be very low in order to avoid the risk of crushing the
sample. In saturated conditions they became close to useless. For well cemented rock types however,
this experimental procedure may be very relevant. It could in particular be relevant for shale. One of
the set-backs from the test procedure was the unknown orientation of the minerals in the rock. The
experiments were carried through on an assumed orientation of minerals. Shale is known for having a
well deﬁned preferred orientation of minerals. This would make it easier to deﬁne the axis of symmetry,
and thus increase the certainty of the results. Moreover, it would be recommended to conduct a more
thorough geological characterization where provenance, sediment transport and deposition, diagenisis
and compaction is covered. These factors may give valuable information in regards to the potential
source of anisotropy in the samples at hand.
