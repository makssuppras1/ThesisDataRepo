Abstract
The thesis deals with grid compliance studies for an offshore wind farm with respect to
the grid codes mentioned by the Danish TSO - Energinet in its latest Requirements
for grid connection of generators (RfG) 2019 edition. So, to assess the upcoming off-
shore wind farms’ compliance to the latest grid requirements, a specific Offshore Wind
Farm (OWF) to be developed in West Denmark has been taken as a case of investigation
for the thesis. Namely, Thor OWF, has been modelled and synthesized to analyze and
investigate its compliance to the 2019 RfG laid down by Energinet. The wind farm has
been gauged to undergo different simulation study cases in direct relevance to the grid
code requirements at the Point of Connection (POC), highlighting the investigation to
the most important and critical set of grid code requirements entrusted in the RfG.
For modelling and simulating the grid studies, PowerFactory 2022 was used to model
and size the most critical components based on Thor Offshore Wind Farm’s preliminary
requirements for a 1008 MW wind farm, comprising of 72 units of 14 MW Fully
Rated Converter Wind Turbine Generator (WTG) and every individual compo-
nent of the OWF from Offshore and Onshore Transformers, Array and Export
cables and Shunt Reactors, all being modelled and dimensioned optimally to con-
tribute towards grid stability and compliance with the grid requirements over the course
of the studies. The WTGs were modelled and designed to meet industry standards with
relevant reactive power capabilities, Fault Ride Through (FRT) settings, voltage and
frequency droop control settings defined to meet grid requirements at the POC. The
Wind Turbines were proved to be capable of complying to the voltage setpoints during a
typical FRT analysis while staying connected to the grid, without the need for additional
reactive compensating devices like Static Synchronous Compensator (STATCOM) or
Static VAR Compensator (SVC) on the onshore side of the network. With reactive
power capabilities of the wind farm being optimally fulfilled w.r.t the capacity of the
wind farm and meeting the RfG demanded droop control towards reactive injection or
absorption from the POC to the grid. The active power capabilities were adequate to
meet the requirements for an over-frequency condition that persuade the wind turbines
to regulate the active power production collectively downward at 5% droop control.
Whereas due to the limitation of using a generic fully rated wind turbine model that
consists of only electrical modelling lacking mechanical modelling parameters such as
mechanical inertia on the rotors, which is crucial for under-frequency analysis, making
it infeasible to explore the upward regulation of active power of the investigated OWF.
Finally, the reactive power capabilities of the wind farm at different active power set
points at nominal voltage (P-Q) and different voltage set points at nominal active power
(U-Q) have been proved to be of greater capability than demanded by Energinet’s
2019 RfG. Thus cementing the critical ancillary services of the latest generation of off-
shore wind turbines designed with fully rated converters making them capable of doing
more than necessary towards integration with the grid and are best fit to comply with
Energinet’s latest grid requirements.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
The present world is facing the biggest ever challenge in the name of Climate Change
since the start of the industrial revolution, that led to humanity’s undying exponen-
tial exploration and curiosity to transform and run the planet on fossil fuels.
Over
the decades, the need to fulfill global demands have solely come from energy powered
by fossil fuels. Thus leading to a rise of global average temperatures which have been
creating a long lasting concern among scientists, the science fraternity, and the people
at large who were made aware of the same in the name of ”Global Warming”, which
has been the talking point since the recent few decades. This urge and eagerness to
bring a systematic change in the global energy extraction and production process has
been a primary focus of investigation among countless scientists and engineers across
the globe. The intention has always been one, to harness power from various renewable
and non-conventional sources, which led to the advent of energy from wind, solar, hydro,
geothermal, wave etc. Where wind and solar has been two abundant, proficient and
proven renewable sources of energy that has been the focal point of advanced research
and investigation for large scale development. Where decarbonization using clean and
green energy sources has been the major point of focus since the start of 21st century
and also to meet the Paris Agreement goals for Climate Change, to keep the global hope
of 1.5°C alive by 2050.
Hence Wind energy has been in the forefront of the green transition race to achieve car-
bon neutrality and cut emissions at the lowest possible Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE)
for both Offshore and Onshore wind farms. Thus, making it the most cost optimal and
techno-economically viable in relative to any fossil fuel powered plants or nuclear power
despite its variability with respect to wind conditions, thus making it one of the most
critical and fascinating sources of energy to investigate, on ways to optimize it’s power
production and control.
Due to which more and more Gigawatt sized Wind Power
Plants are on the rise all across the globe, with Offshore wind being the major headlines
maker in the whole of energy sector because of the immense potential in the coastlines
surrounded to almost the whole of earth’s land mass and the better wind extraction far
offshore being the determiners for the same. According to Wind Europe, the global rise
of installed capacity of Offshore wind all across Europe is shown below in Figure 1.1.

1.2 Problem Analysis
3
Due to which grid connection, wind integration, grid stability and grid code compli-
ance are seriously challenging and vital areas to both wind farm developers and the
Transmission System Operator (TSO) of any wind project developing country or state.
Thus over the years the grid codes have been updated and developed in such a manner
that the latest wind turbine generators are capable of complying to them.
1.2
Problem Analysis
In this thesis the focus is on grid compliance studies respecting the latest grid codes
from Danish TSO Energinet updated in 2019, which is designed to comply with the
upcoming wind farms that will soon be integrated in to the Danish power grids. With
the latest wind turbine technologies and it’s active and reactive power capabilities the
latest grid codes are designed to be slightly more stringent than the previous edition of
2016 RfG from Energinet. Where the main focus of investigation will be on reactive
power capabilities of the wind farm at different scenarios at the POC, followed by active
power capabilities as well and finally to analyse the overall reactive power range of the
wind farm at nominal voltage and various active power setpoints (P-Q), and nominal
active power at various voltage setpoints (U-Q), reflected from the POC. The model
used for grid studies in this thesis is based on Thor OWF developed by RWE [2] at a
minimum of 22 kms off the coast of Thorsminde on the west coast of Jutland in Denmark.
The wind farm is likely to have an installed capacity of 1000 MW and is still at it’s very
infancy in the planning and development of the project, making it an interesting wind
farm to investigate grid compliance studies on having recently made an agreement with
Energinet [15] on it’s grid connection as well. Thus to investigate and analyze the ability
of Thor OWF to comply with the grid requirements and to assess the the ancillary
services of wind turbines in meeting the same and the additional reactive compensations
needed for the OWF are the critical findings that are to be made in the thesis.
1.2.1
Motivation
This thesis project is worked on investigating the capabilities of wind farms with the use
of latest wind turbine technologies and how would it comply to the latest changes in the
grid codes and the challenges a wind farm could experience to comply to some extreme
grid events. Thus to analyse and optimize ways to make upcoming wind farms more
grid compliant and reliable source of power, by taking the example of one particular
wind farm in this project.
1.2.2
Investigation Objectives
The objectives of investigation in this thesis is to understand the capabilities of latest
wind farms using latest wind turbine technologies and testing and investigating the
compliance of these wind farms by working through different preliminary and critical
grid code requirements. The areas of investigation within the thesis, is divided into five
main objectives, which are as follows:
==PAGE:16==
1.3 Thesis Structure
4
1. Assessment of wind farm compliance at normal operating conditions of voltage
and frequency within grid code limits.
2. Investigating the FRT or LVRT capabilities of the wind farm to stay connected
and comply to grid requirements.
3. Analyzing the active power capabilities of the wind farm at under and over-
frequency conditions with frequency droop control techniques.
4. Analyzing voltage droop control techniques of the wind farm at under-voltage
and over-voltage conditions at the POC and it’s corresponding reactive power
capabilities towards the same.
5. Assessment of reactive power capabilities at different voltage level (U-Q) at full
active power production of wind farm and reactive power capabilities at various
active power production (P-Q) at nominal voltage in the POC.
1.3
Thesis Structure
Various topics are covered in this thesis, and are structured as follows:
• Chapter 1 introduces the context of the thesis giving an overall understanding of
the background, problem analysis, motivation and investigation objectives of the
thesis.
• Chapter 2 reviews the latest grid code documented by Energinet in its 2019
RfG.
• Chapter 3 demonstrates the overall modelling of individual components of the wind
farm and it’s limitations.
• Chapter 4 investigates the results of normal operation, Fault Ride Through (FRT),
active power and reactive power capabilities of the OWF.

CHAPTER 2
GRID CODES
This is where a complete review of the selected grid code requirements that will be
investigated in this thesis. The grid code consists of a document with a set of technical
specifications which defines the parameters of a specific type of power plant that is or
will be connected to a public electric grid that must ensure safe, secure and economic
functioning of the electric grid[22]. Rules governing the operation, maintenance and
development of the transmission systems that also sets out requirements and procedures
for governing the actions of all transmission system users like any type of power plant[10].
The grid code requirements followed are consigned by the Danish TSO Energinet and
the thesis focuses on the latest grid code requirements updated in 2019 for any power
plant that is to be connected to the grid from thereon. As the thesis is focused on
modelling an upcoming Wind Farm like Thor Offshore Wind Farm which is to be built
in the west coast of Jutland in Denmark, hence an interesting set of investigation is done
in this thesis on the grid compliance of this particular Wind Farm to the latest grid code
requirements from Energinet as mentioned in 1.2.
2.1
Requirements For Normal Operation
According to the latest grid requirements from Energinet, is that a power generating
plant must provide continuous generation without disconnecting from the grid in the
frequency range of 49.0 Hz - 51.0 Hz.[7]
The Normal Operating Voltage (Uc) at the POC is designated by the Distribution
System Operator (DSO), where the power generating plant must be compliant of con-
tinuous generation when the voltage at the POC is within the voltage range of 90% -
110% range of Uc.
The requirements to staying connecting to the grid at different frequency levels and
the time period of staying connected to the grid between the 0.9 p.u - 1.1 p.u of Uc
as shown in Figure 2.1.
A plant must also be designed to withstand transient voltage phase jumps of up to
20 degrees at the POC without experiencing any interruption.

2.3
Requirements For Active Power Control
A power park module must be capable of controlling its active power and must indicate
setpoints in steps of 1% of Pn or better.
Active power control must be performed with a gradient of at least 20% Pn/min for
power park modules. Control must be performed with an accuracy of ±2% of power
generating plant nominal active power. The control accuracy is supposed to be measured
over a period of one minute.[7]
2.3.1
Power Response To Over-frequency
A power park module must be capable of downward regulation of active power at over-
frequency. Two seconds is the time span within which downward regulation of active
power must take place at the POC.[7]
After a point in frequency where the threshold is beyond normal operating range the
Frequency Response for Over-frequency (fRO) must get activated and must follow a
droop as shown in 2.3, regardless of whether the frequency increases or decreases after
fRO.
When a power park module like wind power plant achieves the necessary control and
downward regulation of active power to the minimum level, it must continue to main-
tain that level of active power production until the grid frequency drops again to normal
operating limits or until the plant is disconnected for other reasons.

The WTG Transformers were designed to be of 0.69/66 kV with a rated power of

3.9
Limitations with the models
Despite utmost care was taken for modelling every single component of the Wind Farm
representing Thor OWF and considering the early stages of the Wind Farm develop-
ment and prior confirmation on the array and export cables, WTG manufacturer and
requirement for additional reactive compensation like STATCOM, SVC etc, the com-
plete model was designed to meet optimal and pragmatic grid requirements laid down
in the RfG at its most realistic level possible.
The reactive power capability curve of the WTG for the thesis has been modelled
with reference to present trends of Offshore Wind Turbines with full power converters
and after crude analysis of a generic trend of the reactive power capabilities at specific
setpoints of voltage (U) and active power (P) and was scaled, tailored and modelled
accordingly to imitate similar capabilities of industrial standards for a 14 MW WTG.
Due to the confidentiality and encryption of control settings with an industry ready
PowerFactory model and in order to understand the evolution of converter control

After a complete, careful analysis, optimal design and modelling of Thor OWF, consid-

electrical design of the OWF would look like was a huge challenge on its own. With re-

grid studies without compromising the realistic characteristics and nature of individual
components within an OWF in general. Where the component design and modelling
were built on PowerFactory to be as pragmatic as possible, with its own limitations
as described in section 3.9 and the system complications were simplified slightly with
regards to cable layout of array cables and precise distancing being undecided from the
developer, made it compelling to advance with a simplified layout design as modelled in

CHAPTER 6
CONCLUSION
With the present exponential growth, planning and development of Offshore Wind
Farms across the globe and in Denmark, the need to have the latest configuration of
Offshore Wind Farms, grid compliant to the latest grid codes (RfG-2019) consigned
by Danish TSO- Energinet, so as to have any upcoming Offshore Wind Farms qual-
ified for grid connection. The study case used in the thesis was of Thor OWF for the
same purpose to investigate and analyze how grid compliant would the hitherto largest
Offshore Wind Farm in Denmark[2] would be, when fully operational by 2027[15].
Thor OWF was taken as a representative to how future Offshore Wind Farms look
like and considering the earliest stages of its planning and development of the real sys-
tem. Thus, the most prelusive details of the design of the wind farm from the Developer
and tailoring the model of individual components of the OWF, after the most careful
considerations of standards of potential manufacturers and combination of analytical
estimation of the same led to the modelling of the complete OWF. With the modelling
of WTG sets being the most critical component towards determination of the whole
concept of grid compliance studies for any OWF, which is also the biggest determiner
for future Offshore Wind Farms. Also with the rise in size of generators and ad-
vanced converter controls for Type 4 Fully Rated Converter Wind Turbines, safe
deliberations were made towards having the most relevant reactive power capabilities,
converter control settings and FRT settings that would replicate, prevalent present day
Offshore Wind Turbines with the latest technologies.
Thus from the grid compliance studies made, the OWF has proven to be grid com-
pliant during Normal Operation (4.2) with safe and smooth operation observed over
the course of simulation of the OWF, without experiencing any disturbances when the
grid voltage and frequency were at its nominal level at the POC to the grid. The OWF
when investigated through FRT (4.3) conditions both from a generic LVRT trend con-
signed by the RfG[7] and also an auxiliary study made with 3-phase Short-circuit
fault, established the reliability of the modelled WTGs to feed in needful reactive power
and voltage support to the POC of the grid during the phase of extreme condition en-
countered by the OWF, without disconnecting. Furnishing that the OWF is capable
of enduring extreme fault conditions even in a real scenario and without possible need
of STATCOMs for voltage support to the grid, which is a matter of techno-economical
trade off, that depends on the Developer and on the final optimized model which will be
planned in the immediate future for the real system. But for the studies made here in the
thesis concludes that STATCOMs are not a necessity, which is usually recommended
to support in the reactive compensation needs to the grid, while in the investigations
==PAGE:73==
6 CONCLUSION

made in the thesis exhibits that, WTGs in coalition with Tap-changing Transform-
ers and Shunt Reactors act effectively towards fulfilling the reactive compensation
needs at the POC, further authenticating its compliance to the latest RfG.
Whilst the active power capabilities of the OWF, were partially exercised in terms
of capability of downward regulation of active power towards over-frequency experi-
enced at the POC and capable of meeting grid recommended droop control gains to-
wards required curtailment of active power during over-frequency (4.4). With the Wind
Turbine’s lack of mechanical modelling parameters made it regrettable to exclude the
under-frequency capabilities of Thor OWF. The voltage control capabilities were in-
vestigated to meet recommended voltage droop gains, especially at the circumstances
of under-voltage at 0.95 p.u, with suﬀicient reactive power injection needs met at the
toll of minute over-loading experienced by the OnSS Transformers which is accepted
[6] and was resolved in Chapter 5. Finally the most critical analysis of reactive power
range on P-Q and U-Q capability curves exhibited that Thor OWF, with its early
stage modelling fully complies (4.6) to the grid required capability curves of a power
plant envisaged in the RfG (2.4.1).
Though the thesis covers major grid compliance studies and came under the culmina-
tion that STATCOMs are not a necessity for added reactive compensation because the
modelled system is proven to be well capable of complying all the discussed grid codes
in Chapter 2, making it techno-economically coherent as well. But over the course of
time when the Developer has an optimized system planned with each and every compo-
nents sized and dimensioned with relevant manufacturer’s specifications, then significant
changes will be observed to the overall compliance, with the Wind Turbines playing a
critical role, followed by how export cables are sectionalized in the real system will play a
crucial role, into determining the need to have added reactive compensation like STAT-
COMs or SVCs. Thus, this thesis could be taken as a step forward towards advanced
planning and grid studies for upcoming offshore wind farms, with other grid studies
like Transient and Harmonic studies playing an advanced role towards investigating
the need for Harmonic Filters in OWF or will it be techno-economical to integrate
STATCOMs depending on further findings that would lead up to, towards the final
design and modelling of any upcoming Offshore Wind Farm to remain grid compliant
at all times and is ready for secure grid connection.
