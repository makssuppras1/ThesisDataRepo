Abstract 
 
This project examines the possibility of converting to greener feedstocks of a dimethyl 
ether (DME) power plant that is currently using coal. The examined alternatives were 
bamboo and green electricity, coming from wind energy. Four plants were modelled 
using the software Aspen Plus with coal, bamboo, coal+electricity and 
bamboo+electricity as feedstocks. They were modelled after a coal to olefins plant in 
Inner Mongolia, in the city of Duolun The plants use a Shell gasification technology 
with gas quench and an indirect DME synthesis through dehydration of methanol. 
Torrefaction for bamboo was incorporated in the gasification, and the volatiles were 
also utilized.  The choice of electrolyzer technology was Alkaline electrolysis cells 
(AEC) as they are the most well-established. Heat integration was also performed in 
Aspen Plus, and a simple steam cycle (Rankine cycle) was used to convert the 
additional heat to electricity. The required mass flows of coal and bamboo were 
calculated so that each plant has an annual DME production of 600ktons, assuming it 
is operational in full load for 80% of the year. The mass flows required were 65.77 and 
93.58 kg/s for the coal and bamboo plant, respectively, without electrolysis and 30.51 
and 36.88 kg/s for the same plants including water electrolysis. 
An energy analysis was performed on the four plants, and the resulting efficiencies 
were very comparable, approximately 55% and 53% for the plants with and without 
electrolysis, respectively. Although, the required mass flow of the bamboo plants are 
higher than those of coal for the same DME production, they have a very small effect 
on the plant efficiency as their heating value is considerably lower. Adding electrolysis 
may not significantly increase the efficiency, but it makes it so that much more fuel can 
be produced from the input coal or biomass.  
Finally, an economic analysis was performed, investigating only the investment costs 
of torrefaction and electrolysis, together with the running costs of feedstock purchase 
and transport. The costs or revenues resulting from the net electricity were also 
analysed. The results showed that changing the feedstock significantly increases the 
cost of the plant, so the respective authorities would need to create a different 
economic framework in order for private investors to consider investing in 
environmentally friendly technologies instead of coal.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1 The importance of alternative fuels 
 
Global warming is a phenomenon that is attracting a significant amount of attention in 
the developed world, as the results of climate change are becoming more and more 
apparent. Global warming is caused by the emission of Greenhouse Gases (GHG), 
with the most emitted GHG being carbon dioxide (CO2). The sectors that are 
responsible for the majority of CO2 emissions are the electricity and heat sector, 
followed by the transport sector [1].  
Although the global electricity sector is currently using a significant amount of fossil 
fuels, namely coal, natural gas and oil [2], there is a lot of progress being made to 
switch to more renewable, emission-free sources. Developed countries are making 
active efforts to produce the majority of electricity from wind and solar energy as well 
as hydro power [3], [4]. 
The transport sector is responsible for approximately 24% of the world’s carbon 
emissions [5]. This sector includes the sub-sectors of road, marine and air transport, 
as well as train networks. A significant percentage of trains operate on electricity, with 
the rest of them using diesel fuel [6]. Diesel-like fuels are also used in the majority of 
marine transport, namely Heavy Fuel Oil, Liquid Petroleum Gas (LPG) and Marine 
Diesel Oil, in compression ignition Internal Combustion Engines (ICE) [7]. 
Compression ignition ICEs are also used in heavy-duty transport [8] as well as in 
private vehicles, like cars and motorcycles. Private vehicles can also use spark ignition 
ICEs, which mostly run on gasoline.  The fuel that is used in air transport is called jet 
fuel, which nowadays is produced from oil distillation [9]. It is apparent from all the 
above, that the transport sector is mostly dependent on fossil fuels. Therefore, new 
solutions need to be found in order to convert to new, environmentally friendly energy 
sources.  
Promising solutions that are being examined are Electric Vehicles (EVs) and Fuel Cell 
Vehicles (FCV). EVs do not have any tail pipe emissions [10], so the only pollution 
caused by their usage would be what is used for the production of electricity. However, 
they are not suitable for long-distance travel [11] and the batteries contain toxic 
substances that can be really harmful, if they are not recycled properly [12]. FCVs are 
usually fueled with hydrogen [13], so they also have zero tail-pipe emissions [14]. Since 
hydrogen gas can be hard to store [15], FCVs that can use alternative fuels like 
ammonia or methanol are also being researched [16]. One of the disadvantages of 
both EVs and FCVs is that they are currently unsuitable for heavy-duty and marine 
transport [17], [18]. It is hard to manufacture batteries that are large enough and their 
weight is heavy to transport, using current technology [19]. Regarding FCVs, the power 
density of the Fuel Cells is lower compared to conventional fuels [20]. In other words, 
this would mean a significant loss of storage volume [21]. 
Another highly promising solution for the entire transport sector is the use of biofuels. 
Biofuels are liquid or gaseous fuels that are produced from biomass. They are 
==PAGE:9==

considered carbon neutral, as the plants that are used to produce them absorb CO2 
during their life cycle, which compensates for the CO2 that is emitted during the 
combustion [22]. Figure 1.1 [23] shows a simple schematic explaining this. Based on 
this, it can be argued that biofuels are a sustainable energy source. However, 
arguments could be made that they may not be intrinsically sustainable, depending on 
a number of factors. This part is discussed in more detail in chapter 5.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
Additionally, biofuels have a significantly high energy density, that is comparable to 
fossil fuels [24]. That means that a vehicle can carry a significant amount of fuel, 
without increasing its weight. Most biofuels can also be combusted in the ICEs that are 
already being used in the transport sector, with some minor modifications. For 
example, a spark-ignition ICE can be modified to run on methanol or ethanol instead 
of gasoline [25] and a compression ignition ICE can be modified to run on biodiesel or 
Dimethyl Ether (DME) [26], [27]. The use of DME specifically is explained in more detail 
in the next section. For a complete transition to biofuels, the vehicle engines would 
need to be replaced, as they will not be able to operate properly without modifications. 
Some car manufacturing companies are also producing “flex-fuel cars”, that can 
operate with both gasoline and ethanol [28]. 
Another major advantage of biofuels is that they can help to develop the economy of 
rural areas and provide more jobs for the people living there. That is because the most 
popular biomass feedstocks are plants that need to be grown in farms in the 
countryside [29]. They can also increase the energy safety of countries, as it lessens 
their dependence on fossil fuel imports [30]. Nevertheless, biomass feedstocks are 
only finite, so each country or region needs to examine what kind of feedstocks they 
can utilize and the quantity and type of biofuels that can be produced from them. 
 
1.2 Properties and uses of Dimethyl Ether 
 
Dimethyl Ether (DME) is the smallest ether (containing the fewest carbon atoms). Its 
chemical formula is CH3OCH3, that can be simplified to C2H6O. A 3-D model of the 
DME molecule is depicted in Figure 1.2. Its physical properties are shown in Table 1.1 
[31]. It is volatile in atmospheric conditions, but is usually stored in liquid form at a 
pressure of 5 bar. It can be produced from a variety of feedstocks, such as coal, oil, 
natural gas, biomass and waste products. The production procedure may differ, 
depending on the feedstock. For example, it is produced from natural gas via steam 
reforming [32], which is currently the most popular DME production method. From coal 
and biomass feedstocks, it can be produced via thermochemical gasification, which is 
explained in more detail in chapter 2.

DME’s thermophysical properties are such, that it can be used as a refrigerant [33]. It 
can also be used for the production of olefins, such as propane and ethane [34]. Its 
most prevalent use, however, is as an alternative fuel [35].  
DME’s properties are similar to those of propane and butane, and thus it can be used 
as an excellent substitute to Liquid Petroleum Gas (LPG) [36]. LPG’s most widespread 
application is for household cooking [37], but it is also used as fuel for  a small number 
of passenger cars and medium-duty vehicles [38]. Due to their similarity, the 
technology and infrastructure that already exists for transporting and storing LPG can 
be used for DME [32]. DME is, in fact, consider safer than LPG, as it doesn’t form any 
exploding peroxides [39].  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The use of DME as a fuel for compression ignition ICEs has also been widely 
examined. It has a lower auto-ignition temperature than diesel (under pressurized 
conditions) and its cetane number is estimated to be over 55, which means that the 
speed of combustion is quick enough for a compression ignition engine. Moreover, its 
higher oxygen content, compared to diesel, combined with the lack of a C-C bond in 
its molecule means that it has significantly lower emissions of particulate matter (PM). 
This also means that there will be less smoke coming out in the tail pipe emissions 
[40]. Additionally, DME has a high latent heat (460 kJ/kg) at a low boiling point. As a 
result, an engine running on DME will have fewer nitrous oxide (NOx) emissions than 
a diesel engine [41].

pressurized container, in order to remain in liquid form. As it has been mentioned, DME

DME’s energy density is higher than that of diesel, it has a lower heat of combustion

compression ignition ICEs [49]. Most researchers are looking into PODE as an additive 
to diesel fuel to improve its characteristics [50], [51], but there are also those that have 
shown that it could be used on its own as a diesel substitute, without any modifications 
to the engine [52]. 
 
1.3 Current status of renewable energy in China 
 
China is the world's third largest economy and at the same time it is the world’s largest 
coal producer and consumer [53]. That is due to the fact that China’s soil is rich in coal, 
but poor in oil and natural gas [54]. About 18.5% of the total world population resides 
in China [55]. Currently, the energy consumption per capita in China is lower than in 
most developed countries, but it is increasing rapidly [56]. Hence, the global carbon 
emissions may severely worsen, when their energy consumption per capita starts to 
rival that of Europe and America, if they continue to depend so strongly on coal for 
energy. Furthermore, coal combustion emits a high amount of PM, which is visible in 
the atmosphere and can be directly harmful to human health [57]. It also emits the 
highest amount of sulfur oxides (SOx) of all fossil fuels, which has severe 
repercussions on plant life as well as humans [58]. Finally, coal mining is a water-
intensive process, and thus large quantities of water get polluted [59].  
For all the aforementioned reasons, China is starting to turn to different methods of 
producing energy [60]. The national goals aim to increase the amount of hydro, wind, 
solar, geothermal and bio energy to amount to 15% of the total energy production [61]. 
At the same time, they are focusing on using cleaner technologies that can harvest 
energy from coal, with as few emissions as possible. Such technologies include 
supercritical boilers and fluidized-bed combustion, flue gas desulfurization and NOx 
removal, as well as the technology that is the focus of this project, gasification [54]. 
Gasification allows solid feedstocks, like coal, to be converted into gases that can then 
be used either for combustion, or for the production of liquid fuels. The exact procedure 
is analyzed in chapter 2.2. China has started to use coal gasification to produce 
electricity through Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle Plants.  
==PAGE:12==

The transport sector in China is mostly fueled by fossil fuels and specifically oil by-
products, as is the case for most countries in the world. As it has already been 
mentioned, China is poor in oil, so it currently has to depend a lot on imports. It is for 
this reason that China has opted to use coal gasification to produce liquid fuels. Since 
the 1970’s they have built over 120 coal gasification plants, and construction has 
begun for more than 50 additional plants. The majority of these plants are producing 
methanol or a combination of methanol and ammonia. Those that remain may have 
hydrogen, methane, DME, olefins and synthetic natural gas as possible products, 
among others [62]. Figure 1.4 [63] shows how the production of methanol and DME 
has grown in China until 2014. There are many companies in the market, but there are 
5 companies that, combined, control over 60% of methanol production. Those 
companies are: XinAo Group (ENN Group), Lutianhua, Jiutai Energy Group (China 
Energy), Lanhua Kechuan and Tianmao [64].  
Regarding DME, China is where almost every DME plant for fuel application is based. 
According to ENN Group’s report in 2010 [65], there were 45 plants in China that 
produced DME, with a combined production capacity of about 13  million tons per year. 
However, the actual amount of DME produced was less than 60% of the total capacity.  
That being said, it needs to be stated that different publications indicate different 
annual DME production, so the precise amount of it is uncertain [64].  
 
It is important to note that in China DME is produced through dehydration of methanol 
(see chapter 2.3). Specifically, the aforementioned plants are made to produce 
methanol and at the same time use part of it to produce DME. Except for coal-to-DME 
plants, research is being made into plants that can produce DME from biomass 
feedstocks, which this project is a part of.  
Last but not least, most of the world’s DME is also consumed in China. Approximately 
70% of it is used as an LPG blend for household cooking as well as in vehicles [66]. 
The mixing ratio is usually 80% LPG and 20% DME [67]. The remaining 30% of DME 
produced is used as an additive in refrigerants and diesel-like fuels, with a smaller 
mixing ratio than LPG.

2. Background 
 
2.1 Biomass feedstocks 
 
Biofuels can be categorized based on the type of biomass feedstock that is used to 
produce them, as it can be seen in Figure 2.1 [68]. First generation biofuels use food 
crops that are specifically planted for fuel production. For example, the use of corn or 
sugarcane for the production of bioethanol and the use of jatropha and rapeseed plants 
for the production of biodiesel [69]. Typically, crops that are specifically grown for this 
purpose are called energy crops. Second generation biofuels are produced from waste 
feedstocks. An example can be the production of biogas (a mixture of methane and 
CO2) from municipal/food waste or manure and the production of bio-ethanol or bio-
butanol from agricultural waste [70]. Third generation biofuels are produced from 
microalgae [71] and macroalgae (sea weeds) [72]. There is a large variety of fuels that 
can be produced in this way, although the technology is not yet mature. Wood chips 
and pellets, as well as crop residues that are used for direct combustion without 
conversion to a different fuel, can be called primary biofuels.  
 
Naturally, not all types of biomass are suitable for the production of all biofuels. Some 
characteristics that are interesting to look into when examining biomass feedstocks 
are: the oxygen and moisture content, the amount of cellulose, hemicellulose, lignin oil 
and proteins, the calorific value, and percentage of volatile matter versus fixed carbon. 
These factors’ importance is strongly dependent on the procedure that is used [73]. 
For instance, feedstocks with high cellulose and lignin content are not suitable for 
fermentation procedures, such as the production of biogas and ethanol  [74], whereas 
this is unimportant for the thermochemical procedures that happen in high 
temperatures (gasification). On the other hand, a high percentage of moisture might 
mean that the biomass needs to be dried, if it’s to be used for gasification, which could 
be energy intensive [75]. In the rest of this chapter, biomass that is suitable for the 
production of methanol and DME through gasification is examined.

Research has shown that almost all kinds of biomass can be used for gasification: The 
most well-documented is woody biomass and crop residues (like wood straw), that 
also have real-life applications nowadays. Woody biomass is usually preferrable over 
crop residues, as it requires fewer quantities of pesticides and fertilizers [76]. It also 
has a higher mass yield per unit of land area. Other feedstocks that have been 
researched include municipal and industrial waste, herbaceous biomass and manure. 
Even so, there haven’t been any large industrial applications for the gasification of such 
feedstocks [77]. 
 
2.1.1 Availability 
 
An important factor to consider when looking into the potential of a biomass feedstock 
is its availability. A certain area of land can only grow up to a specific amount of 
biomass in a given time period. The frequency that the plant is grown can play a role 
as well. For example, a tree can take many years to grow, while other plants can be 
harvested multiple times per year. Thus, the biomass availability is seasonal, meaning 
that the availability strongly depends on the time-period, and storage is required to 
ensure some stability in this regard. Also, each plant may require different land 
characteristics to grow.  
As the biomass feedstocks require land usage, growing plants for the production of 
biofuels can directly compete with growing plants for food production. This raises 
ethical issues, as there are still places in the world where food is scarce. Besides that, 
using food crops for biofuel production can cause food prices to skyrocket, as was the 
case with corn in Brazil. For this reason, most governments have laws in place that 
prevent the use of edible crops or, even more harshly, the use of any agricultural land 
for biofuel production [78]. On the other hand, there are certain types of land where it 
is not possible to cultivate food crops, but it may be possible to grow inedible crops for 
the production of biofuels. Lands that have been degraded and lands with harsh 
weather conditions or poor soils (for instance, with high salinity or poor in nutrients) are 
usually included in this category. This kind of land is called marginal land, and it is 
attracting a lot of attention to researchers because it provides available land for 
biomass feedstocks, that does not in any way compete with food production [79].  
As far as China is concerned, they are also trying to estimate the country’s total 
available marginal land, as well as its suitability for various energy crops. As the 
definition for marginal land is not concretely defined, different researchers have 
different estimates that range from 7 million hectares [80] to as high as 130.34 million 
hectares [81]. Figure 2.2  shows a map of the available marginal land, based on the 
land type, as it was studied by Zhuang et al [81]. The majority of marginal land in that 
study is classified as shrub and sparse forest land, followed by grassland 
 
2.1.2 Transport and pre-treatment 
 
Biomass feedstocks generally have a low mass density and a high moisture content at 
the time of their harvest. This makes their transportation highly expensive, as all 
transportation means have a limited volume capacity and a lot of fuel is wasted for the 
transport of moisture weight, which is suboptimal. Thus, the production costs of 
biofuels are higher, and it is harder for them to compete with fossil fuels in the market.  
==PAGE:15==
 
8 
 
Consequently, it is imperative to dry and reduce the size and water content of biomass, 
to decrease the transport costs. Since biomass is normally collected from a larger 
number of farms, it would not be viable to install a lot of equipment on every one of 
them. The most commonly used technique is for every farm to collect the biomass in 
the form of bales. Baling is a process carried out with agricultural vehicles that slightly 
increases the density of the biomass, as shown in Figure 2.3. Depending on the 
weather conditions, the bales can be left in the sun, so that part of the moisture 
evaporates. The bales from all farms are then delivered to a central pre-processing 
unit using trucks, where they are dried extensively using heat and their size is reduced 
further through milling, grinding, compression and/or pelletization. Afterwards, they are 
transported to the biofuel production facilities using trucks or railroad transport. This 
way the drying and size reducing equipment is installed in one place, which is more 
cost-effective. Finally, since some biomass feedstocks are only grown seasonally, the 
central processing facilities may also serve as storage for the periods where there is

Figure 2.3 Baling procedure 
==PAGE:16==

no production of biomass. Nevertheless, storage is only used as a last option, due to 
the fact that biomass can reabsorb water and decay, which means that it deteriorates 
both qualitatively and quantitatively [82].  
Additional pre-treatment is then carried out in the biofuel production facilities, 
depending on the process that is used. In fermentation (bacterial/enzymatic) 
processes for second generation biofuels, such as the production of biogas and 
ethanol, pretreatment is used to break down the heterogeneous structure of 
lignocellulosic biomass, increase their porosity and surface area and reduce 
crystallinity. Examples of such pretreatment can be physical (like additional milling and 
grinding), chemical (as alkaline or acid pretreatment), physio-chemical (namely steam 
explosion and CO2 explosion) or biological, (through the use of fungi or enzymes). 
One of the disadvantages of most pretreatment methods is that they release toxic 
materials into the environment. Finally, pretreatment contributes significantly to the 
production cost of second generation biofuels [83].  
In regards to biomass gasification, breaking down the structure of biomass is not 
required, as it is conducted at a high enough temperature where everything is broken 
down to simple, smaller molecules (H2O, CO, CO2, H2 etc). Therefore, none of the 
aforementioned processes is used, except for milling and grinding, as the feedstock 
needs to contain small-sized particles [84].  
Torrefaction is a thermal treatment process that is commonly used before gasification. 
It is, in fact, a mild pyrolysis process conducted at approximately 200-300 oC, in the 
absence of oxygen. It involves the decomposition of hemicellulose and partial 
degradation (reduction in degree of polymerization) of cellulose and lignin. During 
torrefaction, a significant amount of moisture is removed. Approximately 70-90% of the 
biomass remains as solid, while the remaining is released in gaseous form, consisting 
of water, carbon dioxide, acetic acid and lactic acid. Approximately 10-20% of the 
original biomass energy is lost during torrefaction. However, the torrefied biomass has 
a higher energy density due to its higher carbon content. Torrefied biomass is 
hydrophobic, which prevents it from rotting and makes it more suitable for longer-term 
storage. Torrefaction is a pre-treatment process that only requires heat, without the 
use of hazardous chemicals. Moreover, size reduction of torrefied wood requires 
significantly less energy than raw wood. In a central pre-treatment facility, like the one 
described in the previous section, the volatiles can be combusted to provide heat for 
other processes. Consequently, torrefaction one of the most environmentally-friendly 
forms of pre-treatment [85]. 
Some research papers published by Prins et al. [86] and Clausen et al. [87], [88] have 
studied the possibility of combining torrefaction with gasification in the same plant. 
Since gasification is performed at high temperatures and produces a lot of waste heat, 
it should be possible to use this heat for the torrefaction process. In this case, the 
carbon in the volatiles could also be utilized in the gasification process. Clausen’s 
research showed that integrating torrefaction and gasification can increase the 
biomass to biofuel conversion. On the other hand, it is stated in the same paper that 
integrated torrefaction would incur additional transport costs, as torrefied wood is 
denser than raw wood.

2.2 Gasification Technologies 
 
Gasification is a thermochemical conversion of a solid or liquid fuel in temperatures 
above 700 oC, with the use of a gasifying means, that can be oxygen, steam, CO2, N2 
or a mixture of the above. The solid/liquid fuel is transformed into gas, which is called 
syngas (derived from synthetic gas). Syngas is mainly composed of CO, CO2, H2 and 
can also include methane and small amounts of other hydrocarbons and other 
contaminants like H2S. The precise composition of the syngas depends on the 
feedstock and on the conditions (temperature and pressure) of gasification [89]. 
Syngas can either be used directly for electricity production in gas turbines, in IGCC 
plants or it can be used to produce Fisher-Tropsch fuels, methane, methanol and DME 
through catalytic reactions. The procedure by which methanol and DME are produced 
is described in the next section.  
The most commonly used feedstock for gasification is coal. Coal gasification has been 
well established in the past decades. Naturally, most companies are focused towards 
China, having the largest amount of coal reserves. Some commercial coal gasifiers by 
the major companies in the field are shown in Figure 2.4. These include Shell, GE 
energy (former Chevron Texaco), Lurgi, ECUST, Siemens and SES [90]. Of course, 
each company produces more than one gasifier, so Figure 2.4 only shows some 
examples.  
 
There are 3 general categories of gasification technology. Those are: fixed bed, 
fluidized bed and entrained flow gasification and they are described below. 
 
2.2.1 Fixed bed Gasification 
 
Fixed bed gasification is the simplest form of gasification. It can only handle solid 
feedstocks. Fixed bed gasifiers are able to handle larger particle sizes, which reduces 
the need for excessive grinding. They have a high thermal efficiency, but require a long 
residence time, so all of the carbon is converted. The retention time can be as long as 
60 minutes. 
Figure 2.4 Main Gasifiers used in China [90] 
==PAGE:18==
 
11 
 
Figure 2.5 [91] shows two possible arrangements of a fixed bed gasifier: a) an updraft 
and b) a downdraft gasifier, where the separate phases of gasification are visible. The 
fuel can be coal as well as biomass and the gasifying agent can be oxygen or steam, 
as well as air. In the updraft gasifier, fuel is inserted in the top, while air is blown through 
the bottom. The product gas leaves through the top. As a result, not all tar formed in 
the pyrolysis zone passes through the oxidation zone, so it is not completely broken 
down. This leads to a higher tar content in the produced syngas. The tars have 
economic value and can be marketed, but they need to be separated from the syngas 
first. Tars hinder the catalysts that are used for the production of biofuels, so updraft 
gasifiers are not an ideal option for this case.  However, since the combustion can be 
initiated by the hot gas that is exiting the gasifier, an updraft gasifier can operate with 
fuels with higher moisture content, up to 50%.  
The high tar content can be solved by using a downdraft gasifier. There, fuel is still 
inserted on the top, but air is blown from the sides, close to the center of the reactor, 
and the product gas leaves from the bottom. Since all pyrolyzed fuel goes through the 
oxidation zone, the syngas that is produced is free of tars. On the other hand, 
downdraft gasifiers are harder to scale up, so they are mostly used in small to medium-
scale applications . 
 
 
2.2.2 Fluidized bed Gasification 
 
A fluidized bed gasifier consists of a hot inert solid, like alumina or sand that is fluidized 
by a gasifying agent, (usually oxygen or steam). Fine fuel particles (smaller than 6 mm) 
are introduced at the bottom while the product gas leaves at the top. Good gas-solid 
mixing is required to promote heat and mass transfer. Because of this, there are no 
distinct phases or zones in fluidized bed reactors. They usually operate at 
temperatures around 800-900 oC and atmospheric pressure. They have a much 
shorter retention time than fixed bed gasifiers, that is normally between 10 and 100 
seconds

There are two major types of fluidized bed gasifiers: the bubbling fluidized bed (BFB) 
and the circulating fluidized bed (CFB) gasifier. A schematic of a BFB and CFB gasifier 
is shown in Figure 2.6 [76] and Figure 2.7 [92] respectively. In a BFB gasifier, there is 
no recycling of the bed material. Since there is even distribution, part of the 
unconverted solid fuel is unavoidably removed together with the ash. In a CFB gasifier, 
the particles at the gasifier exit are separated from the solids by means of a cyclone. 
The solids are then recirculated back to the gasifier. Because of this, it can operate at 
higher speeds than the BFB gasifier and promote more uniform mixing. This also 
makes CFB gasifiers more suitable for larger-scale applications, in which case they 
can operate in larger pressures. Fluidized bed gasifiers are suitable for feedstocks with 
high ash and sulfur content, as the latter can easily be retained in the gasifier through 
the addition of a sorbent, such as lime. Due to uniformity, there are no tars in the output 
syngas, so it is a good choice for the production of biofuels. CFB gasifiers are the ones 
that are most commonly used when the feedstock is biomass [93].

2.2.3 Entrained Flow Gasification 
 
Entrained flow gasifiers are co-current gasifiers where the fuel is inserted in the top, 
together with oxygen and steam, CO2 or N2 as a gasifying agent, while the product gas 
leaves close to the bottom of the reactor. An example of an entrained flow gasifier is 
shown in Figure 2.8 [94].  Entrained flow gasifiers are considered versatile, as they can 
also operate on liquid fuel. In the case of solid fuels, they need to be ground and 
pulverized to fine particles that are smaller than 0.1 mm. This size reduction is 
commonly performed on-site, just before the gasifier. Since this process is energy-
intensive, entrained flow gasifiers are best suitable for large-scale, industrial 
applications [95]. 
 
Entrained flow gasifiers operate at a very high temperature, above 1200 oC and at an 
elevated pressure of 20-50 bar. At this temperature, all formed tars are cracked, 
leading to a highly clean product syngas. The retention time is relatively short, lasting 
only a few seconds. Because of this, they can mostly handle feedstocks with low 
Figure 2.8 Entrained Flow Gasifier with Water Quench [86] 
==PAGE:21==
 
14 
 
moisture content, to ensure a sufficient carbon conversion. In case of feeds with higher 
moisture contents, they need to be dried before they are inserted into the gasifier. 
An entrained flow gasifier can be slagging, which means that the ash is removed in the 
form of slag, or non-slagging meaning that the ash is removed in the form of solid. It 
should be noted that, since the operating temperature of the gasifier is almost always 
higher than the ash fusion temperature, a non-slagging gasifier can only operate on 
fuel with considerably low ash percentages (preferably below 5%). 
As it is not possible to treat or condition syngas at such high temperatures using current 
technology, the product syngas needs to be cooled first. The product syngas is usually 
mixed with cold water, gas or another means, for cooling purposes. This is done to 
avoid extremely hot gas entering the gas cooler, to protect it against corrosion. This 
process of cooling is called quench. Having a quench, however, means that entrained 
flow gasifiers have a lower thermal efficiency than the previous two categories. The 
majority of commercial entrained flow gasifiers use a water quench system, as the one 
illustrated in Figure 2.8, but there are also those that use radiant cooling, like GE 
Energy.  
Shell has developed a gasifier with gas quench as it is shown in the schematic in Figure 
2.9 [96]. The feed is usually coal. After the syngas leaves the gasifier, there is a two, 
or three stage cooler, that produces medium and high pressure steam and part of the 
cooled syngas is recycled back to be mixed with the uncooled syngas, reducing its 
temperature to approximately 900 oC. 
 
 
Slag is collected at the bottom of the gasifier. The cooled syngas goes through a 
cyclone, in order to separate the remaining ash particles, that may be in the form of 
fly-ash. The gas quench system of the Shell gasifier leads to a lower CO2 percentage 
in the syngas, compared to other reactors, which can be useful in the production of 
fuels such as methanol and DME, as explained in the next section.

Entrained flow gasification is the most promising gasification technology. Most 
gasifiers that are commercially available are entrained flow and the next generation of 
gasifier technology will probably be based on entrained flow gasifiers. Nonetheless, 
they are not currently used for most biomass feedstocks, as they can be hard to 
pulverize or have a considerably high ash or moisture content. That makes their pre-
treatment very costly and less likely for companies to invest into. Even so, entrained 
flow gasification already has some application on woody biomass, since torrefied wood 
has similar properties to coal, a lower moisture content, and is easier to pulverize in 
small particles.

2.4 Water Electrolysis 
 
The syngas that is derived from biomass has a comparatively low H2/CO ratio. To 
ensure maximum DME production, it needs to be adjusted to a value of 2 or 1, for the 
indirect and direct pathway respectively. The most common method is to use a WGS 
reactor which can produce more hydrogen. Unavoidably, part of the CO will be 
consumed and transformed to CO2, which will need to be removed before the synthesis 
reactors. In other words, part of the carbon will be wasted, as it will be converted to 
CO2. A solution that can solve this issue and greatly increase the amount of DME 
produced is to add hydrogen from an external source to the syngas. This extra 
hydrogen can be produced via water electrolysis. 
In simple terms, electrolysis is the act of splitting water into H2 and O2 gases, by using 
electricity. A positive and negative electrode are inserted in water, and a potential 
difference is applied, which causes the movement of electrons. Hydrogen will gather 
in the cathode, while oxygen will gather in the cathode. Currently, there are 3 types of

electrolyzer cells that are used to produce hydrogen from water and electricity. Those 
are: alkaline electrolysis cells (AEC), proton-exchange membrane electrolysis cells 
(PEMEC) and solid oxide electrolysis cells (SOEC). 
AEC was the first electrolysis technology to be commercialized and is already used in 
large-scale applications. They are the simplest and cheapest technology to produce 
hydrogen. A schematic of an AEC is shown in Figure 2.11 [100]. The cathode and 
anode electrodes are commonly made of nickel. KOH is used as electrolyte, while 
NaOH and NaCl may be used as catalysts. The two electrodes are separated by a 
membrane that allows the transport of hydroxide anions (OH-). AEC can produce H2 
gas of over 99% purity. They operate in the temperature range of 60-80 oC, at a 
maximum pressure of 30 bar. They cannot be operated at higher temperatures as the 
electrolyte will become corrosive. AEC are the most mature hydrogen production 
technology, and they are the cheapest to manufacture, as they do not require any 
noble metals. One of the disadvantages of AEC is that they do not perform well under 
low current density, and thus are not optimal for variable load conditions.  
 
PEMEC, as the name suggests, operate by using a polymer membrane that is proton-
conducting (note: a hydrogen cation is referred to as a proton). The membrane is 
generally thin, between 25 and 250 μm. A schematic is shown in Figure 2.12 [101]. 
The cathode is mostly made of platinum, while the anode normally contains iridium. 
PEMEC operate at around the same temperature as AEC, but can go as high as 200 
bar in pressure. They are more efficient than AEC, able to produce more hydrogen 
with the same amount of electricity. On the contrary, PEMEC is a less mature 
technology, which is not helped by the fact that the platinum required in the cathode is 
expensive. It is a more complex technology, and the water purity required is higher 
than AEC. Furthermore, they have a shorter lifetime than AEC.  For this reason, they 
are mostly used in medium-scale applications.  
SOEC is the latest developed technology for water electrolysis, and for this reason, it 
is not yet widely commercialized. Figure 2.13 shows the image of a SOEC [102]. The 
electrolyte in the membrane can conduct oxygen anions and is usually made of 
Zirconia (specifically Yttria stabilized Zirconia), as is the anode and the cathode. SOEC 
operate at a much higher temperature than PEMEC and AEC, in the range of 800-

2.5 Availability of renewable energy in China 
 
It has already been established that adding external hydrogen to syngas, that is 
produced via gasification, is a good way to control the H2/CO ratio and to increase the 
fuel production from a certain amount of feedstock material. On the other hand, 
electricity is required in order to produce H2 gas. Consequently, the carbon emissions 
that are connected with electricity production become part of the total carbon emissions 
of the fuel’s life cycle. In order to ensure that biofuels remain carbon neutral, or at least 
have fewer emissions than fossil fuels, this electricity needs to come from renewable 
energy sources. 
Nowadays, over 70% of China’s electricity is produced from coal. Naturally, they are 
looking to decrease this percentage, and increase the production from renewable 
energy sources. As far as renewable energy is concerned, China has a large 
hydropower potential and they have already invested in 13 hydropower plants to try to 
harness this power, as shown in Figure 2.14 [104]. It can be noticed that the distribution 
of hydropower is not even, as there are no installations in the west part of the country.  
 
 
Moreover, China has abundant wind resources that are mostly untapped. The windiest 
areas in China are located in the north, as well as the southwest parts of the country 
as it can be seen in Figure 2.15 from the global wind atlas’ website[105]. Unfortunately, 
the areas where there is a high wind potential have lower electricity demand, whereas 
demand in areas with lower wind potential is higher. Since the power load centers are 
located near areas with high electricity demand, investing in wind turbines would 
require several substations and upgrading the electricity transfer grid. On the contrary, 
there is a significant number of coastal areas with shallow water depth, that could be 
used for offshore wind farms. These are much closer to China’s power centers, so 
offshore wind energy seems like a more viable option.  
Figure 2.14 Hydropower stations in China [104] 
==PAGE:27==

Finally, China has a significant power source in solar power, with an average of 2200 
hours of yearly sunshine. Since 2004, the photovoltaic industry is growing rapidly, more 
than 100% per year. This is helped by the fact that the majority of photovoltaic cells 
are manufactured in China and that the wide application of PV cells globally has made 
the technology less expensive and more easily accessible. China accounts for only 
15% of the global solar capacity, so there is still much room for development. It can be 
observed from the map in Figure 2.16 [106] that the areas where the solar potential is 
higher are similar to the ones with high wind potential. Therefore, the same mismatch 
exists between production potential and demand, as in the case of wind, with similar 
associated problems.  
On a final note, it is important to remember that these renewable energy sources do

3. Methods 
 
3.1 Selection of Plant location 
 
The location of the DME production plant was based on an existing plant in the region 
of Inner Mongolia. It is located in the city of Duolun, and was commissioned in 2011 
by Datang International Power Generation Co., Ltd [107]. It uses Shell entrained flow 
gasification technology with gas quench [89], as the one that was described in chapter 
2.2.3. The feedstock that is used is low quality coal, specifically lignite, that is provided 
by the neighboring mine in Shengli, also in Inner Mongolia [108]. Its main product is 
olefins, namely polypropylene, through methanol, with a production capacity of 2.8 
thousand tons per day. Its average yearly production is 460 thousand tons [109].  
The choice of Inner Mongolia is made because it is one of the richest regions in coal. 
Therefore, it has a well-established coal mining and transport network [110]. It also is 
important that the region has a high potential both to grow biomass (see Figure 2.2), 
but for solar and wind power as well (see Figures 2.15 and 2.16). Thus, the potential 
to change the plant feedstock is a realistic option for this area. 
The specific plant was chosen as it already uses a Shell gasifier, which is of particular 
interest compared to other technologies and thus was the one modelled in this project. 
Since the actual plant is producing methanol from syngas and is then converting it to 
olefins, an indirect DME synthesis is the most suitable choice for the project’s model. 
It is interesting to note that, no data was found on Chinese plants that have DME as 
their single product, but only co-producing methanol and DME [89]. Based on this, it is 
deduced that the direct DME pathway is not yet applied in Chinese plants.

of the cooled gas is recycled and mixed with the syngas at the exit of the gasifier. That 
way the syngas is pre-cooled to 900 oC before it enters the cooler. The percentage of 
gas that is recycled is controlled by a design-spec block, to achieve the desired 
temperature. In an actual plant, cyclones are included in order to remove the fly ash 
from the cooled syngas, but this is not included in the model. All solid particles were 
already removed by the slag separator block, since Aspen cannot distinguish between 
fly-ash and slag. However, this does not affect the thermodynamic calculations, as the 
percentage of fly ash is negligible.  
 
 
The cooled syngas is then led to the WGS reactor, which is modelled by an equilibrium 
reactor block, in order to reach the desired H2/CO ratio of 2 (since indirect DME 
synthesis is modelled). The modelled reactor contains only the WGS reaction 
(Equation (3)), with a temperature approach. It operates at 300 oC and 40 bar, since 
there are no assumed pressure losses. These conditions are chosen based on an 
average value from literature review. Some steam needs to be added to the WGS 
reactor, at 40 bar to shift the equilibrium towards the production of H2. Both the amount 
of steam that is inserted is controlled by a design-spec block, in order to achieve a 
H2/CO ratio of 2 in the entrance of the methanol reactor. A different way to control the 
ratio can be to alter the operating temperature of the reactor, which affects the 
equilibrium. However, it was considered better to keep the same temperature in all 
plants, so they are comparable. After the WGS reactor, the syngas is cooled to 30 oC 
and all of the water is removed, before entering the AGR.  
The AGR is assumed to use the rectisol process. It is a multi-step process that involves 
the absorption of H2S, COS and CO2 through the use of methanol [118]. However, the 
detailed modelling of this process was deemed outside the scope of the present 
project. Thus, it was modelled using a separator block. A detailed schematic of the 
Figure 3.3 Gas Quench Simulation 
==PAGE:33==
 
26 
 
rectisol process is included in the Appendix. The gas comes out of the AGR at 0 oC 
[87], which is simulated by a cooler block. Small pressure drops are not taken into 
consideration. All of the H2S is removed from the syngas, as well as most of the CO2. 
The precise amount of CO2 that is removed is calculated through a design-spec block, 
so that the gas entering the methanol reactor contains 3% CO2 (molar). This 
concentration is deemed optimal, to avoid deactivation of the catalysts and is small 
enough that it has a negligible effect on the equilibrium of the reactor [119], [120]. Part 
of the removed CO2 is recycled back to the gasifier, to be used as a gasifying agent.  
The clean gas is compressed to 85 bar and heated to 260 oC, which are the operating 
conditions of the methanol synthesis reactor (according to [87]). The reactor is 
simulated by an equilibrium reactor that includes the methanol formation reaction and 
the WGS reaction (Equations (1) and (3)), with a temperature approach of 15 oC (based 
on [87]). A pressure drop of 5% is assumed. The reactor outlet is cooled to 40 oC before 
it is flashed, in order to separate the unconverted syngas from the product liquid. 5% 
of the unconverted syngas is directed to a gas turbine, while the remaining 95% is 
recycled back to the reactor, after it is compressed and heated back to 85 bar and 260 
oC.  
The gas turbine is modelled as a simple Brayton cycle, as it is seen in Figure 3.4. 
Ambient air is compressed to 19.5 bar and then combusted using the unconverted 
syngas as fuel. The resulting gas goes through a turbine that returns it to ambient 
pressure, to produce mechanical energy. The mass flow of air is controlled by a design-
spec so that the temperature at the exit of the combustion chamber is 1200 oC. If the 
temperature exceeds this level, problems will arise in the turbine blades, as the 
materials cannot withstand such high temperatures [121]. The flue gas from the turbine 
is cooled to 97 oC, in order to utilize the excess heat. Cooling it harder would require 
an extremely large heat exchanger, that is not commonly used. The heat loss in the 
combustion chamber is assumed to 3% of the syngas energy.  
The combined mechanical energy of the gas turbine is the energy output of the turbine, 
minus the energy input of the compressor. The conversion rate from mechanical to 
electrical energy is assumed as 0.95.

==PAGE:34==

After being separated from the unconverted syngas, the liquid products of the methanol 
reactor are expanded to 8 bar and cooled to 30 oC. At these conditions, a distillation 
column separates the absorbed gases from the liquid methanol [122]. The distillation 
column is simulated via the RadFrac block in Aspen. The methanol at the bottom 
product contains a minor amount of water (approximately 1%), but that does not 
warrant the use of a second column to further purify the methanol. The distillation 
column has 10 stages, while the molar distillate to feed ratio and reflux ratio are 
controlled by design-spec blocks so that all the absorbed gases are removed, while 
less than 1% of the total methanol evaporates and is lost in the distillate. If the amount 
of methanol that evaporates is to be reduced further, that would require cooling the 
distillate below ambient temperature, increasing the cost and requiring additional 
energy.  Finally, the feed stage is optimized.  
The purified methanol is pressurized and heated to 56 bar and 230 oC, the conditions 
of the DME synthesis reactor. This reactor is also modeled with a equilibrium reactor 
block, containing the methanol dehydration and WGS reactions (Equations (2) and 
(3)), with a temperature approach of 15 oC. A pressure drop of 5% is assumed here as 
well. Methanol from the distillation columns is also recycled back to the DME synthesis 
reactor.  
A distillation column is used to purify the product DME. This distillation column also 
has 10 stages, is modelled with a RadFrac block and operates at 170 oC and 10 bar 
[123]. The feed stage is optimized, and the molar distillate to feed ratio and reflux ratio 
are controlled with design-spec blocks, so that the DME in the distillate is at least 
99.9% pure and that the bottom product contains less than 1 ppm DME.  
Finally, a distillation column is used to purify the methanol, before it is looped back to 
the DME synthesis reactor. This column operates at 62 oC and 1 bar [123]. Similarly 
to the other two columns it is modelled with a RadFrac block, with 35 stages and 
controlling the molar distillate to feed and reflux ratios so that the methanol in the 
distillate is over 99% pure, while less than 2% of the input methanol is lost in the water 
(bottom product).  
The plant produces enough heat through the gasifier, reactors and coolers, to cover 
the demand for heaters and distillation columns. Heat integration of the plants was 
performed in Aspen. All of the plant’s excess heat is used in a steam cycle to produce 
electricity. The steam cycle is modelled as a simple Rankine cycle, as it is visible in 
Figure 3.5. Pressurized water at 180 bar is heated to produce steam. The mass flow 
of the water/steam is controlled by a design-spec block, so that the steam can reach 
600 oC using the heat provided by the plant. A turbine then uses this steam to produce 
mechanical energy and releases the steam at a pressure of 0.042 bar. The outlet 
steam is then condensed to saturated water and pumped back to 180 bar. The 
temperatures and pressures used in the steam cycle were based upon average values 
from industrial cycles. The mechanical energy production of the steam cycle is the 
energy output of the turbine minus the energy requirement of the pump. The 
conversion from mechanical to electrical energy is the same as in the gas turbine.

The specifications of plant B were almost identical to those of plant C, except for the 
different composition and mass flow of the feed material. Torrefaction was included 
between the blocks of milling and gasification, as in Figure 3.1. However, it was not

part of the cooled syngas bypasses the reactor. The percentage of syngas that 
bypasses the reactor in plant B are controlled by a design-spec block, in order to 
achieve a H2/CO ratio of 2 in the entrance of the methanol reactor.  
 
3.3.3 Incorporation of Hydrogen (plants CE and BE) 
 
Plants CE and BE were modelled to examine the possibility of increasing fuel 
production by adding hydrogen from an external source. AEC were the choice of 
electrolysis technology, as they are the most well-established and cheaper. They were 
modelled as a stoic reactor model, operating at 60 oC and 30 bar (based on [124]). The 
block’s input was pressurized water and the output was pure hydrogen and oxygen 
gas, that were separated by means of a separator block. The hydrogen gas was mixed 
with the cleaned syngas (AGR output). Part of the oxygen was inserted in the gasifier, 
to avoid using an air separation unit. The mass flow of input water was modified with 
a design-spec block, so that the H2/CO ratio of the gas entering the methanol reactor 
was fixed to 2.  
A simple flowsheet of plant CE is shown in Figure 3.6. The flowsheet for plant BE is 
almost identical, with its only difference being torrefaction between milling and 
gasification (as described in the previous section). It can be observed that plants CE 
and BE are similar to plants C and B, except for gas conditioning part. In plants CE 
and BE, the H2/CO ratio is fixed after the AGR. In these plants, after the gasifier the 
cooled syngas (200 oC) is cooled all the way down to 30 oC, and the water is removed

3.5 Economic Analysis 
 
Except for the energy analysis, it was deemed interesting to examine the economic 
impact of switching feedstocks. Several assumptions were made in order to simplify 
the economic analysis, while still being as close to reality as possible.  
It is presumed that plant C is already in place, while the need exists to decommission 
the gasifier. The plant stakeholders are examining the possibility of making the 
conversion to plants B, CE or BE in order to be more environmentally friendly. As it 
stands, most parts of the plant will remain as they are and will not require any capital 
investment. The small changes in the mass flows do not require any change in the 
plants’ equipment. Additionally, since all plants produce the same amount of DME, the 
revenue from selling it will be equal among them. So, the economic analysis only 
included the parts that are different among the plant. 
The components that require additional investment are the gasifier and torrefaction 
reactor in case of bamboo and the AEC in case electrolysis is to be included. Since a 
Shell gasifier will be used both for coal and bamboo, this capital investment is 
considered the same in all plants and excluded from the analysis. Furthermore, it is 
assumed that the operational costs of the plant regarding maintenance and working 
hours are the same in all plants, and are also excluded. The variable costs that were 
included are the costs for purchasing and transporting the feedstock and the electricity 
costs/revenues.  
The cost data that were used in the analysis are displayed in Table 3.3. For most of 
the data there were conflicting sources, so there is high uncertainty in the results. All 
prices were converted to the local currency, renminbi (CNY), using the current 
conversion rates (1 CNY= 0.13 EUR = 0.15 USD). The coal price in China was found 
in the range of 0.4 CNY/kg [131] to 0.6 CNY/kg [132], and the chosen price was 0.6 
CNY/kg. Regarding bamboo price, Gu et al., [133] gives a price of 0.5 CNY/kg for 
bamboo timber and 2.5 CNY/kg for bamboo shoot. Since the plant is assumed to use 
the entire plant, but no data was found on the mass percentages of timber and shoot, 
2.0 CNY/kg was used as the bamboo price. This was deemed reasonable, as the price 
at which bamboo is exported is 3.62 CNY/kg [134]. The transport cost used was 6.54 
CNY per liter of diesel fuel [135].  
The investment for the torrefaction reactor seems to be strongly dependent on the 
scale of the plant, according to the literature [136]–[138]. Since plants B and BE require 
a large torrefaction capacity, the largest scale plant was used a cost model [138]. 
However, the research paper only gives data for the capital investment on the entire 
plant, while in the case of this project only the reactor is needed. The assumption that 
was made was that the reactor would constitute 50% of the referenced cost, namely 
0.76 million CNY per kton/year of installed capacity (amount of biomass that can be 
torrefied).  The investment for AEC was 9.2 million CNY per MW of installed capacity, 
calculated as the electricity consumption [129].

4.2 Energy Analysis 
 
In order to compare the energy inputs and outputs of each plant, they were split into 
the following categories:  
➢ the chemical energy of the feedstock, calculated using HHV 
➢ the net electricity production or consumption of the plant 
➢ the energy required to transport the feedstock to the plant, calculated via the 
LHV of diesel. 
 
Since heat integration was performed in the plants and all the surplus heat is used to 
generate electricity, there is no heat input or output. Finally, the chemical energy of the 
product DME is not included in the analysis, as all plants produce the same amount. 
The results are shown in Figure 4.5. Positive numbers represent an energy input to 
the plant, while negative numbers represent energy outputs. Although the mass flow 
of required bamboo is significantly higher than that of coal, when comparing the energy 
inputs of plant C with B, as well as that of plant CE with plant BE, it is observed that 
the energy requirement is only slightly higher for the bamboo plants. The reason for 
this, is the bamboo’s lower HHV. Since the energy requirement for transport is 
relatively small, the bamboo’s higher mass doesn’t warrant a higher energy 
expenditure for diesel fuel.  
The higher electricity production of plant B compared to plant C can also be attributed 
to the higher mass flow, as the coolers absorb more energy, generating more heat that 
is then converted to electricity in the steam cycle. That is not the case when comparing 
plants CE and BE, as the mass flows are not that different, while plant BE requires 
more electricity for electrolysis, due to the low H2 content in the syngas.

Another interesting calculation, would be the amount of land that would be needed to 
grow the bamboo that are needed for plants B and BE. The plants require 2.36 and

estimate for the bamboo annual yield is 4 tons per hectare, which means that 0.65 and

respectively. The lowest estimate for available marginal land in China that was found 
in literature, was 7 million hectares. Therefore, it is easy to understand that there is 
more than enough land in China that is available to cultivate bamboo for at least 10 
plants like plant B and over 20 like plant BE.

5. Discussion 
 
As far as energy is concerned, the results of this project show that bamboo can stand 
on the same ground as coal, since the energy the gasifier provides can be used for 
pre-treating it on-site by using torrefaction. The overall efficiencies are slightly lower 
than other integrated torrefaction research, by L.R. Clausen [87], [88]. The fact that 
those efficiencies are calculated using LHV rather than HHV, should not affect the 
results a lot, since in this project HHV was used both for the coal/biomass and product 
DME. Therefore, it affects both the numerator and the denominator in Equations (7) 
and (8) Looking at the intermediate efficiencies, it can be noticed that Clausen et al 
have a biomass to syngas efficiency that is close to 80%, while in this project it was 
calculated around 60%. The CO2 content of the product syngas in these studies is 
much lower, around 9%. Clausen also uses CO2 as a gasifying agent, so the difference 
cannot be attributed there. Except for the different gasifying conditions (1300 oC and 
45 bar, as opposed to 1400 oC and 40 bar in this project), there is a difference in the 
way that integrated torrefaction was modelled. In this paper, the volatiles were inserted 
in the top of the gasifier, together with the torrefied biomass, while Clausen inserts 
them later, in the gas quench. Therefore, it may seem that it is more appropriate to 
insert the volatiles at that point, rather than at the top of the gasifier, for a higher 
efficiency and less CO2.  
It is also important to keep in mind that, in this project, torrefaction was not modelled 
as a separate block in Aspen, but the composition of raw bamboo was used as a 
feedstock for the gasification reactions. A more detailed model, could provide some 
insights on the intermediate stream compositions and heat consumption of torrefaction 
and gasification. Other parts of the plant that were not modelled in detail include the 
==PAGE:54==
 
47 
 
air separation unit and the AGR, though the use of well researched energy 
consumptions can be precise enough. After all, the electricity and heat consumptions 
of these two components is but a minor percentage of the plants’ heat and electricity 
streams.  
Another interesting option that could be looked into is the one-step, direct synthesis of 
DME. The carbon flows in Figure 4.7 indicate that almost all of the carbon in methanol 
ends up in the product DME, so there are no significant losses there. On the other 
hand, implementing direct DME synthesis would warrant an H2/CO ratio of 1 instead 
of 2, based on Equation (4)reaction (4).reaction (4).. Since the H2/CO of the product 
syngas is much lower than 1, direct DME synthesis would mean that less of the carbon 
would be converted to CO2 in the WGS reactor (see Equation (3)), or less hydrogen 
would be needed for electrolysis, meaning a smaller electricity consumption. Research 
on the topic seems to agree that the direct synthesis method is more efficient than the 
indirect synthesis that is used in the present project [99], [144], [145]. Nevertheless, 
the indirect synthesis was the choice of this project as it is the one implemented in the 
real plant on which the project was based and due to the lack of pure DME producing 
plants in China [146]. If the DME market in China continues to develop, it will be 
probably better to implement direct synthesis. On the contrary, the indirect synthesis 
would give the plant some flexibility to also produce methanol by lowering the 
production level of DME, depending on the relative market prices of the fuels in any 
given time.  
Regarding carbon emissions, it has been assumed that no carbon emissions are 
associated with energy from biomass or from wind. However, this may not be 100% 
true. The sustainable nature of biomass is widely debated, and it strongly depends on 
the type of biomass and on the scope by which it is examined. For example, some 
recent publications disagree that using forest biomass can be considered carbon 
neutral, as a full-grown tree requires a long time to grow, but is only burned in an instant 
[147]. However, it is widely agreed among scientists that biofuels that are produced 
from fast growing plants are indeed carbon neutral [148]. Bamboo is a plant that is 
harvested every two or three years, so it could be considered fast growing, but not as 
fast as plants that are harvested every year, or more frequently.  
The sustainable nature of biomass feedstocks should also be examined on other 
indicators, like the use of nutrients. Simply phrased, plants require a set amount of 
nutrients to grow mostly nitrogen and phosphorus. These nutrients are incorporated in 
the plant mass, but when gasified are either released in the atmosphere or end up in 
the ash. Since there is only a finite amount of nutrients, infinitely growing biomass may 
result in a global deficit. There has been research on recycling part of these nutrients 
through slag or fly ash to try and circumvent this issue [149], [150].  
The calculation applied for the carbon emissions is a very simplified one, just to get an 
idea on the magnitude of the numbers. The most proper tool to analyse the 
sustainability of the plants would be to perform a life cycle assessment (LCA). An LCA 
can analyse the total emissions of the whole procedure “from cradle to death” 
incorporating emissions connected to coal mining and the harvest of biomass, the 
installation and decommission of the plant components and the wind turbines, as well 
as the fuels and electricity that will be replaced by the plant production [151]. Such a 
method would also provide insight on the nutrient use and other factors that determine 
a plant’s sustainability. LCAs that are performed on biomass gasification are generally 
showing positive results [152], [153]. 
==PAGE:55==
 
48 
 
An alternative possibility to reduce the carbon emissions of the plants is the 
implementation of technologies for carbon capture and storage (CCS). Such 
technologies use underground geological formations, like caverns, to prevent CO2 from 
being released in the atmosphere [154]. Carbon capture and usage (CCU) is another 
technology that is coming up, and involves the conversion of CO2 into valuable 
products instead of simply storing it, though that is more energy intensive [155]. The 
use of the aforementioned technologies in the bamboo plants can even result in net 
negative carbon emissions. Specifically, net negative emissions happen when the CO2 
that is absorbed by the plant, is more than the CO2 released in the atmosphere through 
the production and use of fuels. However, carbon capture and storage/utilization is 
quite costly and thus there is not much incentive to implement it in large scale plants 
[156].  
The economic analysis performed in this project serves as yet another indicator that 
renewable feedstocks are not developed to the point that they can be competitive with 
conventional fuels, even though it wasn’t performed on the whole plant. This is why 
most countries try to incentivise private investments in renewable energy, by giving 
subsidies. The subsidies can have many forms, from a set amount of money, money 
paid for the utilized biomass, or for each unit of produced energy. Some publications 
indicate that China has a subsidy system for the production of electricity from biomass 
[157]–[159], but unfortunately no concrete reference was found on subsidies for biofuel 
production. Another method that governments use to promote the use of renewable 
energy is by adding a carbon tax, or some other trading system like carbon markets 
and green certificates [160]. At the moment, China doesn’t have a carbon tax but they 
are planning to launch a new emission trading system, starting 2021 [141], [161].  
On a final note, some researchers have looked into the possibility of polygeneration 
plants, that can produce more than one product from the same feedstock [162]. The 
products can be fuels, as well as electricity of heat. Such plants can have the flexibility 
to alter their production levels depending on the market prices and results from such 
studies seem to be promising [163]–[166].

6. Conclusion 
 
This project studied the possibility of converting the feedstock of a DME plant located 
in China, in the region of Inner Mongolia, from coal to biomass, electricity, or a 
combination of the two. The goal was to find the amount of feedstock material that 
would be required to produce 600 kton of DME per year, under the assumption that 
the plant is operational for 80% of the year. Using the Shell gasification technology and 
indirect DME synthesis were deemed the most suitable choices. The choice for 
producing hydrogen was Alkaline Electrolysis Cells. The four plant configurations were 
modelled in Aspen Plus, using coal, bamboo, coal plus electricity and biomass plus 
electricity. After performing heat integration, the excess heat was used in a steam cycle 
to produce electricity. 
The energy analysis showed that overall efficiencies of the plants were very close to 
each other, ranging from 52.7% to 55.6%. Comparing the results to others in the 
literature showed that the efficiencies are somewhat lower, which was attributed to the 
larger content of CO2 in the produced syngas. The contributing factor was determined 
to be the insertion of the volatiles, that result from torrefaction, to the top of the gasifier 
instead of the gas quench. Examining the amount of land that would be required for 
the cultivation of bamboo and comparing it with studies for the available land in the 
area showed that there is more than enough land, so the option of harvesting bamboo 
seems promising. The addition of electricity helped to increase the amount of carbon 
that ends up in the desired product, rather than being vented in the atmosphere in the 
form of CO2, although the effect on energy efficiency is not so great. 
Finally, under the assumption that the plant exists, and most of the costs associated 
with it would remain the same, no matter the feedstock, an economic analysis was 
performed. The cost flows that were considered, were those for purchasing and 
transporting the feedstock material, the electricity cost or revenue and the additional 
investment for electrolysis and/or biomass pre-treatment. Although there was quite a 
lot of uncertainty in the costs that were used, the results of the analysis showed that 
the new, “greener” plants are far from being economically competitive with the original 
plant that only utilizes coal. Possible solutions to this problem include the introduction 
of subsidies and some for of economic penalty for carbon emissions.  
Potential ideas for future work would be to perform a sensitivity analysis on the energy 
analysis to find out which parameters are most important for the result. Additionally the 
same plants could be modelled using a direct DME synthesis procedure, in order to 
see how much that would increase the efficiency. Finally, a more detailed economic 
analysis could be performed on the plants, studying all aspects that may contribute to 
the cost and with the use of more reliable data.
