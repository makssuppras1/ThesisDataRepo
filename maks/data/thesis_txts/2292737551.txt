Advanced Total Energy Calculations of Metal Oxides
by Thomas S. Jauho
In the recent years, many promising results using the random phase approximation (RPA) have
seen. However, RPA has some signiﬁcant limitations, speciﬁcally, correlation energies are severely
underestimated with RPA. This has lead to several attempts to improve the RPA framework using
ﬁrst principles.
A new and promising method is the renormalized adiabatic PBE (rAPBE) kernel scheme, which
combines the adiabatic-connection ﬂuctuation-dissipation theorem with time-dependent density func-
tional theory. The rAPBE method has been shown to improve the prediction of the correlation energy,
when compared to RPA and exact results. A review of the RPA and rAPBE formalism is given in
this thesis.
The correlation energy per electron of 35 metals and metal oxides are calculated with both rAPBE
and RPA. Compared to RPA, rAPBE increases the correlation energy per electron by 0.35 eV for
the metals and 0.44 eV for oxides.
Using the rAPBE and RPA correlation energies in combination with the Hartree-Fock energy, the
formation energy of 23 metal oxides, based on group I and II metals as well as two transition metals
(Ti and Ru), are calculated. Compared to RPA, the rAPBE method reduces the mean absolute error
(MAE) per oxygen from 0.38 to 0.21 eV.
The results indicate that rAPBE signiﬁcantly improves the description of the metal oxides, however
at higher computational costs.

1.1
Ab initio...
For almost a century physicists and chemists have sought to ﬁnd exact solutions to the famous
Schr¨odinger equation1
iℏ∂
∂tΨ(r, t)) = ˆHΨ(r, t) .
(1.1)
By solving this equation for a physical system such as an atom, a molecule or a slab of metal, we
could derive all the physical properties of the system. Knowledge of the properties of any system
imaginable would not only help us understand the universe we inhabit, but also be applicable in an
unending range of technical applications. All we need as inputs are the atoms within the system.
Calculations of this kind, starting with only the atoms and the laws of nature, are known as ab initio
methods.
But why have we not found these evasive exact solutions to Eq. (1.1) yet? It has been 89 years
since the formulation of Eq. (1.1) [1]. Why are many great physicists and chemists still struggling
to ﬁnd the solutions to this seemingly simple second-order partial diﬀerential equation? Methods for
solving partial diﬀerential equations are even at the bachelor level at universities.
What makes Schrodinger’s equation such a challenge to handle, lies in the nature of the systems we
wish to describe. Matter is made up of protons, neutrons and electrons, and if we consider anything
but the most simple of atoms and molecules, the many-body interaction between the electrons
becomes so complex that exact solutions are impossible [2]. It is like having the key to the secrets
of the universe but not have the strength to turn it. And knowing that we never will.
Fortunately, this realization has not stopped us from trying.
Knowing that the exact solutions
are beyond reach, developing sophisticated approximative methods has become one of the most
important ﬁelds with both physics and chemistry, the ﬁeld of electronic structure methods. Sixteen

When dealing with electronic structure methods, we face a constant balance of speed versus accuracy,

• Design and engineer novel material properties before synthesis
• Explore properties inside or across broad classes of materials
Especially the ability to design and engineer materials with new and previously unobtainable prop-

1.1.1
Solving global problems using oxides
Perhaps the hottest topic in our globalized economy is global warming and climate change. It is
broadly recognized that in order to tackle this challenge we need to change both behaviour and
technology, and move towards sustainable and clean energy. Common for many of the envisioned
technological solutions is the need for new and better materials with very speciﬁc physical and/or
chemical properties.
Understanding, exploration and design through better electronic structure
calculations will help to improve the performance of existing technology as well as speed up the
general innovation towards new technology.
One of the important classes of materials to use in such technology consists of oxides. These are
frequently used in catalysis, electro-chemistry, batteries and fuel cells. Currently metal oxide nanos-
tructures are considered promising candidates for lithium-ion batteries and supercapacitors [3], as
solid catalysts for synthesis of bio-diesel [4], or as components for improving the charge transport
across solar cells [5]. This makes oxides in general, and metal oxides in particular, a very interesting
and topical class of materials to investigate.
1.2
Previous work
One of the most popular electronic structure methods is the Density Functional Theory (DFT).
This approach combines the challenging terms of the problem into the exchange-correlation (xc)
functional, which depends on the electronic density. DFT can be considered an exact theory, but
the xc-functional is unknown and an approximation is required. The quality of a DFT calculation
then depends of the approximation used for the xc-functional. The fundamental concepts and most
common approximations are reviewed in Section 2.1 of this thesis.

In general two roads have led to better and better results for DFT. The ﬁrst road is to improve
ab initio calculations, which focuses on making better and more accurate approximations to the
xc-functionals.
The second road uses experimental data to improve performance, e.g.
through
hybrid functionals, which combines diﬀerent xc-functionals with adjustable weights, or by statistical
methods and machine learning. In general, these empirical methods require a lot of data to ensure
high quality ﬁtting.
However, as there are inﬁnitely many potential systems of interest, the necessary data might not
always be available, and in many cases ab initio calculations become the best way forward.
In the literature, the two most commonly used approximations to the xc-functional are the local den-
sity approximation (LDA) and the generalized gradient approximation (GGA). The most widespread
GGA functional is the Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) functional from 1996 [6]. As will be discussed
in Section 2.1, both LDA and GGAs rely on an important error-cancellation in the xc-term. The
error cancellation is not always complete and for some systems, such as oxides, this error cancellation
fails signiﬁcantly and large systematic errors occur [7]. It is possible to calculate the exchange part
exactly but this removes the error cancellation and worsens the performance in most cases.
Additionally, the LDA and GGA are local and semi-local approximations, which leads to a self-
interaction error and an inaccurate description of the xc-potential far away from the nucleus [8].
In order to achieve better accuracy it is necessary to go beyond LDA/GGA methods. Quantum
chemical methods such as Møller-Plesset perturbation theory or coupled cluster methods are widely
used for molecular systems, recently both second order Møller-Plesset theory [9] and coupled cluster
[10] have been applied to solids.
In both cases applications are limited to small system as the
computational costs scale badly with N 5, N being the number of particles in the system, i.e. the
number of electrons in most cases.

In another approach, more recent work [11] found that using the Hartree-Fock (HF) energy together
with correlation energy from the random phase approximation (RPA) poses a promising method
for obtaining the xc-term. Speciﬁcally it was found that RPA provides good lattice constants, bulk
moduli, heats of formation as well as adsorption and surface energies [12]. And more importantly it
was found that RPA outperforms PBE for the formation energy of oxides [13]. For comparison the
cost of RPA scales with N 4.
However, RPA is not without fault and while RPA describes long-range interactions better than semi-
local functionals such as PBE, the accuracy of RPA for molecular atomization energies is comparable
to PBE [14]. Additionally, total correlation energies are severely underestimated in RPA [15].
Several improvements on RPA have been attempted, such as RPA+ [16] where a GGA short-range
correction is introduced, or SOSEX [17] where a second order screened exchange term is included in
order to exactly cancel the one-electron self-correlation error. However, neither of these approaches
led to overall improvements as they perform better for some calculations and worse for others.
As will be described in detail in Section 2.3, the exchange-correlation kernel is approximated to zero
in RPA, and a very natural next step towards greater accuracy is to ﬁnd a better approximation for
the xc-kernel. Using the adiabatic LDA (ALDA) kernel instead of RPA, improves the description of
the correlation hole for small q, but at the price of overestimation at q > 2kF [18], as shown in Fig.
1.2.

However, the same study found that this overestimation could be removed by introducing the renor-
malized ALDA (rALDA) through a parameter-free renormalization scheme. The merits of the rALDA
approach was further corroborated by an additional study that found rALDA to perform seven times
lower mean absolute error (MAE) than RPA and four times lower than PBE in calculating molecular
atomization energies [19].
Based on these very promising results for rALDA, the renormalized adiabatic PBE (rAPBE) kernel
was studied [20], and it was found that the renormalized kernels give a signiﬁcantly better description
of the short-range correlations in covalent bonds and a fourfold improvement of the binding energies

the number of k-points and a is the length of the unit-cell along the direction of the corresponding
reciprocal lattice vector [23].

with |G + k|2/2 < Ecut = 600 eV, see Eq. (2.77) and (2.78). The PW cut-oﬀvalue of Ecut = 600

3.3
Convergence of calculations
Convergence is a critical aspect of computational physics. Ideally4, one would always use the ﬁnest
settings possible in order to achieve the greatest possible accuracy allowed within the method used.
However, using very high settings, e.g.
very dense k-point sampling in the Brillouin-zone, will
greatly increase the computational costs, while maybe only changing the results insigniﬁcantly. For
each setting it is therefore desirable to determine if and when ﬁner settings do not change the results
and convergence is achieved.
For the bulk metal and oxide systems, the two most important parameters to converge are the PW
cut-oﬀand the k-point sampling, while for the O2 molecule they are the PW cut-oﬀand the size of
the unit cell. Using PWs as a basis, periodic boundary conditions are imposed, and in order for O2
not to feel this periodicity, the unit cells should be suﬃciently large. The following two sections will
discuss the convergence of O2 and the bulk systems, respectively.
3.3.1
The oxygen molecule
This section examines the convergence of the O2 total energy with respect to unit cell size for PBE,
HF and RPA, and with respect to PW cut-oﬀfor RPA. It is expected that rAPBE converge similarly
to RPA, see e.g. the supplementary material for [20].
For the relatively cheap PBE and HF calculations a PW cut-oﬀof 900 eV was chosen, as this is
considered suﬃciently high to be converged. It is however necessary to study the convergence with
respect to the size of the unit cell.
As seen in Fig. 3.2 convergence is achieved around vac = 3.0. The total energies are tabulated in
Tab 3.3. The unit cell sizes used were deﬁned by the vacuum surrounding the O2 molecule, such
that vac = 3.0 corresponds to a unit cell of roughly 6 ˚A × 6 ˚A × 7.2 ˚A, as the bond length of O2
is 1.2 ˚A.
For the PBE energies the largest deviation after vac = 3.0 is 10 meV, while it is 6 meV for HF,
see Tab. 3.3. A vacuum distance of 3 ˚A can thus be considered to be well converged for these
calculations.
To test the importance of both unit cell size and PW cut-oﬀfor RPA, a large range of set of correlation
energies are calculated for two diﬀerent unit cells, see Fig. 3.3.

Summary
The purpose of this chapter is to introduce the calculations made in this project and the methodology.
In particular this chapter documents the settings used in order to facilitate reproduction and control
of the results presented in the following chapter. The central settings are given in Tab. 3.1 and 3.2.
This chapter also investigates the convergence of the four types of energy calculations made in this
project. Speciﬁcally, the convergence with respect to unit cell size and plane (PW) cut-oﬀis examine
for the oxygen molecule. It is found that PBE and HF calculations for O2 converges with respect to
unit cell around a vacuum distance of 3 ˚A and that a PW cut-oﬀrange of 300 eV to 500 eV is well
converged.
For the bulk systems convergence with respect to Brillouin zone sampling is tested for the PBE and
HF energy calculations. It is found that these energies are easily converged with 10 meV for most
systems. Convergence with respect to PW cut-oﬀis not tested for the bulk systems as the settings
used in this study are higher or equal to converged settings given in the literature.
An important method for achieving converge RPA and rAPBE correlation energies while avoiding
the most expensive calculations is introduced.

4.1
Volume optimization
As the ﬁrst step, a small ensemble of DFT functionals are tested in order to determine which one
does the best job at calculating the volumes and formation energies compared to experimental data1.
The chosen functionals are listed in Section 2.1, Tab. 2.1, with a short description of their essential
characteristics.
Comparing the computed volumes, shown in Tab. 4.1, to experimental values taken from the Mate-
rials Project [21], LDA performs the worst by far. While LDA is consistently predicting too small a
volume, the other functionals show both under- and overestimation. Using the mean average error

The ground state energies came as a free side-beneﬁt from the volume calculations, and the corre-
sponding formation energies are shown in Tab 4.2. These values have been calculated from the total

It is interesting that LDA performs quite well in determining the formation energies at these non-
converged test settings. Besides LDA, the optPBE-vdW functional performs the second best, which
may be because this functional is optimized with respect to O-bonds among others.

4.3
Formation energies
Using the correlation energies of Tab. 4.3 and 4.4 together with the total energies from the PBE and

4.3.2
Comparison to literature
As a further examination into the nature of the errors presented above in Fig.
4.2, I compare
the results to the results given by J. Yan et al. in [13]. Ideally, calculations performed with similar
settings and methods should yield (close to) identical results. As many of the settings3 in the present
work are the same as [13], their results compose an excellent benchmark. The diﬀerences between
the reference values of Yan and the results presented in this work are given in Fig. 4.3.
First of all, it is noted that the mean absolute diﬀerences (MAD) with respect to the reference
are quite small for both the PBE and the HF energies, just 0.07 eV and 0.09 eV for all systems,
respectively. For RPA, on the other hand, the MAD is 0.24 eV over all, with most of the formation
energies higher than the reference, i.e. this study predicts less stable oxides. This is a signiﬁcant
disagreement, and it is interesting to understand the cause.

Without access to the total energies calculated in the reference, it is diﬃcult to determine whether
the large diﬀerence in RPA energies is due to the oxygen, the metal or the oxides. While most of the
formation energies diﬀer with roughly −0.3 eV, there is no clear systematic error for all the oxides,
and the diﬀerence cannot be explained simply by an error in the oxygen molecule total energy.
One possibility might be the diﬀerent PAW atomic setups (version 0.9) used in this work and in the
reference work (version 0.8). However, an error introduced by the setups should be present in all of
the three methods, not only for the RPA energies. Diﬀerences in the k-point sampling could also
lead to a disagreement in energies. Except for ﬁve instances, the present study uses higher k-point
samplings than the reference [13]. In general it is observed that higher k-point samplings lead to
lower total energies. However, as the formation energy is given by the diﬀerence between the total
energies of the oxides and metals, it is unclear whether higher k-point samplings will lead more or
less stable oxides. To determine if this is the cause of the disagreement, the total energies of the
reference are needed. Another possibility for discrepancies might be the PW cut-oﬀranges used and
the extrapolation of the correlation energy to inﬁnite cut-oﬀ. The present study uses the exact same
ranges as given in the reference, but while the reference uses ﬁve points in this range (with unknown
spacings), this study uses six points uniformly spaced in the PW cut-oﬀrange. But as demonstrated
for oxygen in Fig. 3.3, diﬀerent extrapolations only lead to small diﬀerences in correlation energies
of the order of 0.01 eV. Finally, it is possible that the reference did not use Eq. (3.4), but used
a diﬀerent method for converging the correlation energy with respect to k-points. The following
two sections will discuss respectively the quality of the setups and the scheme used for the k-points
convergence of RPA/rAPBE.
4.3.3
Comparison to all-electron calculations
As GPAW makes use of its own set of atomic setups where only some of the electrons are included in
the calculations, it raises the question of how much these setups are aﬀecting the formation energies.
Tab. 4.7 present AIMS PBE calculations4 made by Marcin Dulak5 of the formation energies in
comparison to the DFT-PBE formation energies presented above. The AIMS calculations are an
all-electron electronic structure code.
The mean error (ME) and mean absolute error (MAE) of 0.00 eV and 0.05 eV given in Tab. 4.7
show that it makes a quite small diﬀerence to include all-electrons in the PBE formation energies. It
can therefore be concluded that it is not the setups that cause the large error in the PBE formation
energies. As the PBE results are well-converged, the error must stem from the PBE functional itself.

4.5
Summary
This chapter presents the results obtained in this project using the theory of Chapter 2 and the
parameters of Chapter 3. Through a small test of seven diﬀerent functionals, the PBE functional
is chosen as the most suitable functional of the seven. This is based on PBE’s ability to closely
reproduce both experimental unit cell volumes and formation energies, in addition to being the most
consistent choice of functional to base rAPBE calculations on.
The correlation energies calculated with RPA and rAPBE are presented and compared. RPA exhibits
a clear tendency to estimate the correlation energy per electron 0.4 eV lower than rAPBE. This trend
is consistent with the literature, where it has been shown that RPA in general underestimates the
correlation energies by 0.5 eV. This suggests that rAPBE improves the description of the correlation
energy in both oxygen, metals and metal oxides.
The formation energies found with PBE, HF, RPA and rAPBE are presented and compared to both
experimental values and a reference study. Compared to experiment, it is found that rAPBE are
better than RPA for estimating the formation energies with signiﬁcantly smaller ME and MAE. Com-
pared to the reference study, the PBE and HF formation energies are in general closely reproduced,
however, there is a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the RPA correlation energies.
Furthermore, the PBE results are compared to all-electron calculations, and it is found that it does
not make a signiﬁcant diﬀerence to include all electrons. This eliminates the setups as possible error
source for PBE energies. An examination of the k-point convergence and the computational times
indicates that there is room for improvement.

Conclusion and outlook
This chapter sums up the present thesis in three main points and reviews the work presented in
Chapters 2 to 4. Finally, the outlook will discuss where future improvements can be made.
5.1
Main points of the thesis
The main ﬁndings of this Master’s project can be summed up in the following three main points.
• The rAPBE method improves the correlation energy per electron compared to
RPA. One of the main motivations to go beyond RPA, is that RPA is known to erroneously
underestimate the correlation energy per electron in the homogeneous electron gas by 0.5
eV/electron. Comparing the correlation energies found in this study, rAPBE consistently ﬁnd
correlation energies of roughly 0.4 eV/electron higher than RPA, suggesting that rAPBE, to a
large extent, corrects the systematic error of RPA.
• The rAPBE method improves on the formation energies of oxides. Comparing the
PBE, HF, RPA and rAPBE formation energies calculated in this work with experiment, it is
clear that the rAPBE formation energies are closest to the experimental values, with a MAE of
only 0.21 eV. RPA had almost twice as high MAE at 0.38 eV, and PBE and HF did even worse
with large systematic errors. Especially the superoxides (O−
2 ) are well described by rAPBE,
showing a MAE of only 0.03 eV. Overall, rAPBE also showed the smallest systematic error,
with a ME of only −0.18 eV.
• The rAPBE method is extremely costly as compared to both PBE and RPA. The
improved correlation energies comes at a price. Comparing the computational times of each
method presented in this thesis, I ﬁnd that rAPBE is a factor of ten slower than RPA, which
is in turn a factor of ten slower than PBE. Currently ongoing research is trying reduce the
computational costs of rAPBE by reducing the number of bands required or by optimizing the
evaluation of the kernel.

5.2
Review of the thesis
Due to promising results for the rAPBE method shown in the literature, this project sets out to
investigate how well rAPBE can estimate the correlation energies of group I and II metal and metal
oxides. The oxides were chosen partly because they represent an interesting class of materials that
are widely applicable in new technology, and partly because RPA had been shown to describe the
formation energies of these oxides quite well. Knowing that rAPBE improves on several other RPA
results in the literature, the RPA study of metal oxides provided an excellent benchmark.
Chapter 2 starts out from the very foundation of quantum mechanics and gives a condensed intro-
duction to the main concepts in DFT. More importantly, this chapter gives a detailed derivation of
the correlation energy using linear response theory as well as expressions for both the RPA response
function and the rAPBE exchange kernel.
In Chapter 3, the ﬁrst part presents in detail the parameters that all the calculations are based
on and why these parameters (Tab. 3.1) are chosen. The second part of this chapter focus in the
convergence of the central parameters such as Brillouin zone sampling, plane wave cut-oﬀand unit
cell size. On one hand, the PBE and HF calculations were shown to be easily very well converged.
On the other hand, it is necessary to converge Brillouin zone sampling and plane wave cut-oﬀfor the
RPA and rAPBE calculations separately, as both RPA and rAPBE are very expensive to perform.
Chapter 4 presents the main ﬁndings of this thesis: A comparative analysis of seven diﬀerent DFT
functionals, PBE calculated structures, the RPA and rAPBE correlation energies and the formation
energy of 23 metal oxides using PBE, HF, RPA and rAPBE. PBE is found to be the most suitable
DFT functional based on PBE’s ability to closely reproduce both experimental unit cell volumes
(MAPE of 5.4%) and formation energies (MAE of 0.51 eV), as well as being consistent with the
rAPBE kernel.
The correlation energies found with RPA and rAPBE were compared, and the
rAPBE correlation energies per electron are approximately 0.4 eV higher. The results suggest that
rAPBE improves the description of the correlation energy in both oxygen, metals and metal oxides.
This is corroborated by the increased accuracy of rAPBE in the formation energies when compared
to experiment. As a benchmark the formation energies are compared to a reference study and while
the PBE and HF results are highly similar, the RPA results show a MAE of 0.24 eV, which is
unexplained at this point. Finally, the quality of the atomic setups, the k-point convergence and
the computational times are studied. A comparison of the DFT-PBE results to all-electron PBE
calculations is made and a very small ME of 0.05 eV is found. This indicates that the deviations
found when comparing to experiment or the reference does not lie in the setups.
What the source of the deviations might be instead and how to improve the results in future work
is discussed in the next section.

5.3
Outlook
A computation scientist care about two things. Accuracy and speed. The following will discuss how
we can hope to improve either one of them.
5.3.1
Accuracy
Consider the four phases of the project shown in Fig. 1.4. They are in short 1) DFT test and volume
relaxation, 2) PBE and HF energy, 3) diagonalization and RPA/rAPBE, and 4) extrapolation and
formation energy. I will now discuss in which of these phases there is room for improvement.
• Phase 1, selecting the DFT functional and preparing the structures. This is in essence
a matter of how much the structure aﬀects the rAPBE results. Considering the very similar
results from the initial DFT tests, it is diﬃcult to achieve better structures from ﬁrst principles
only. It might be possible to obtain rAPBE results closer to experiment with experimental
lattice constants. While this will make the results dependent on empirical input, it could be a
subject for future study.
• Phase 2, PBE and HF calculations. Concerning the energies, these calculation were found
to be very well converged, and their MAE seem to be limited to the methods themselves.
However, it can be speculated that orbitals calculated with other functionals (e.g. LDA) than
PBE might improve the rAPBE formation energies.
• Phase 3, diagonalization and RPA/rAPBE calculations.
First, it is a question of
whether the number of bands (i.e. number of plane waves) are converged. As the PW cut-oﬀs
were not tested for convergence for rAPBE, it is possible that the accuracy might be improved
by higher cut-oﬀs, though it does not seem likely considering the linear behaviour seen for all
the systems. In this project, it was assumed that we can converge the PW cut-oﬀs and k-points
separately. As we saw for Mg and MgO this can introduce errors. In particular, it might be
possible that the PW cut-oﬀof 150 eV used for the k-point convergence is too low for some
systems. This is deﬁnitely a point to investigate in future work.
• Phase 4, extrapolation and formation energies. It is a concern that the choice of what
datapoints to use in the cut-oﬀrange will aﬀect the results, however the eﬀect was found to be
quite small for oxygen. The only concern regarding the formation energy is that calculating the
energy per oxygen, might incorrectly give the impression of larger errors for oxides compared
to superoxides, which is the case for PBE and rAPBE.
Summing up, the three most promising areas for improving the accuracy in future are using diﬀerent
structures or investigating convergence of the number of plane waves and the Brillouin zone sampling.

Speed
This section makes the same analysis for improving the computational costs and the speed of the
calculations. However, all phases except for Phase 3 have insigniﬁcant computational costs, so we

• Phase 3, diagonalization and RPA/rAPBE calculations. Considering the computa-

these very large computational times, is therefore a very important topic for future work.
There is currently an ongoing Master’s project investigating a method for reducing the number of
bands required in RPA and Postdoc Christopher Patrick at CAMD recently implemented a new
optimized method for evaluating the rALDA kernel. The results of both projects can be applied
to rAPBE as well. These projects show promise for signiﬁcantly reducing the computational times
while retaining the improved performance of rAPBE that is demonstrated in this thesis, and thus
putting us one step closer to chemically accurate ab initio calculations.
