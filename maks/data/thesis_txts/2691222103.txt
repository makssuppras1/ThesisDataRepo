Abstract—The present study describes a feedforward neu-
ral network-based monaural low-latency voice activity detector
(VAD) system which utilizes log-energy in different frequency
channels to discriminate between spectro-temporal patterns of
speech and noise in reverberant adverse acoustic conditions.
The inﬂuence of the preprocessing STFT analysis window size
on overall VAD performance is systematically investigated by
decreasing window length compared to a set baseline. In addition,
a multi-resolution analysis window approach is included as a
possible solution to improve VAD performance. The effectiveness
of different real-time compatible feature normalization strategies
is also investigated for a range of system conﬁgurations to
investigate the effect on VAD performance. The generalization
performance of the neural network to unseen audio material
and acoustic conditions is evaluated on several different test sets
with matched and mismatched conditions and varying short-term
SNR. A multi-conditional training approach is used to reduce
sensitivity to different room reverberations and noise types. For
the scenarios investigated in this study, shortening the STFT
analysis window length decreased performance independently of
the number of frequency channels, and feature normalization
strategies. The use of temporal context was found to increase per-
formance more for shorter STFT analysis windows than longer,
effectively allowing similar performance in matched conditions.
In all considered acoustic conditions, the proposed VAD system
shows a substantially higher performance in terms of HIT-FA%
compared to a statistical and learning-free VAD algorithm.
Index Terms—Speech detection, Voice activity detection, VAD,
neural networks, ideal ratio mask, IRM, ideal binary mask, IBM,
low-latency, multi-window, analysis window length.
I. INTRODUCTION
Voice activity detection (VAD) is a ubiquitious pre-
processing stage for a wide variety of audio applications
including noise reduction, speech enhancement and automatic
speech recognition (ASR) systems [1]–[3]. In addition, with
the introduction of scene-aware hearing aid signal processing
systems such as scene-aware dynamic range compression
(DRC) and the beneﬁt of these as described in [4]–[6], a low-
latency VAD system is required due to latency constraints of
hearing aid signal processing systems.
In the case of a target talker, low signal-to-noise-ratio (SNR)
caused by room reverberation and noise sources provide a
challenging detection problem, as low SNR and late reﬂections
from the reverberation has been shown to cause a decrease in
performance for automatic speech identiﬁcation [7] and ASR
systems [8]. Since the target speech and background noise
usually overlap in time and frequency, the case of a monaural
signal is especially difﬁcult, while the use of binaural signals
can allow for an easier separation and activity detection
scenario due to the use of binaural features such as the
interaural level difference (ILD), interaural time difference
(ITD),
and
interaural
coherence
(IC).
Previous
studies
have shown the use of these features can improve speech
recognition results by implementing a beamforming operation
as part of a Deep Neural Network (DNN) pre-processing
stage [9], or used directly as part of an ASR system [10].
Previous VAD approaches have included systems based on
statistical models of noise and speech by modelling both as
stochastic processes [11]–[13], and use the models to combine
different SNR estimates using a decision-directed approach
to produce a gain function for monaural and binaural signals
[14]–[16] which can be post-processed to act as binary VAD.
This approach requires a strong statistical model of both noise
and speech in order to be effective, and works best if the noise
is stationary.
Newer approaches increasingly utilize DNNs and supervised
learning [1], [17], [18], where instead of statistical models,
mappings are established between selected features and mea-
sures of speech activity. However, DNNs in general require
great amounts of accurate and diverse training data. For audio
processing, the required training data can be generated from
databases of separate clean speech and noise recordings and
room impulse responses (RIRs) allowing quick generation of
training data. While the training data can be easily generated
due to the use of separate speech and noise databases, this
also acts as a primary disadvantage if not carefully considered
due to the risk of high sensitivity to mismatched acoustic
conditions if the used training data is not sufﬁciently diverse,
or the chosen neural network architecture lacks sufﬁcient regu-
larization such as dropout [19]. The sensitivity to mismatched
conditions can be examined by applying the trained network to
conditions not seen in the used training data such as different
noise and RIRs. To alleviate this issue, the DNN must be
trained on data covering a wide range of conditions, and
which is general enough to allow the model to accurately
predict speech activity in all acoustic conditions. This DNN
generalization is a major challenge due to the high number
of variables that affect the noisy mixture conditions, such as

SNR, spectral properties, and room acoustics. The architecture
of both the DNN as well as the pre- and post-processing of
features is also subject to a large number of parameters such as
the DNN hyperparameters, Time-frequency (T-F) resolution of
inputs, and choice of training target which must all be chosen
prior to training.
A majority of DNN-based systems primarily utilize spectral
features such as mel-frequency cepstral coefﬁcients (MFCC),
multi-resolution cochleagram (MRCG), or log-energy [20].
These features are all typically extracted based on the inputs
short-time Fourier transform (STFT) representation, making
the STFT parameters a vital part of the pre-processing stage.
Two conﬂicting requirements rule the choice of frame-
length for an STFT-based analysis-synthesis solution, as the
STFT window length determines both frequency resolution
and overall latency of the system. A long STFT window is
required to achieve a high frequency resolution for separation
of noise and speech at the cost of increased latency, while a
short STFT window is required to decrease latency and provide
a real-time compatible solution. The latency is caused by the
overlap-add (OLA) block processing system, as latency is a
function of the synthesis window length. Hence, long STFT
synthesis windows are not usable in low-latency applications
like hearing aids or other time-sensitive systems. The analysis
and synthesis window length and frame shift of the T-F pre-
processing stages and their associated latency have been a
large topic over the years, but are often not discussed for
DNN approaches. A general trend for DNN approaches is that
the window lengths are in the range of 20 ms to 32 ms, and
8 ms to 16 ms frame shift [21], [22]. These window lengths
however cause an unacceptable level of latency in latency-
sensitive applications. Latency as low as 10 ms is unacceptable
for closed-ﬁt hearing aids and 5−6 ms for open canal ﬁttings
[23]–[27], and even lower latency has also been found to be
advantageous for sound quality due to a reduction in comb
ﬁltering caused by a mix of processed and non-processed audio
[28].
In addition to the latency introduced by the OLA block-
processing, the use of temporal context is often exploited in
DNN-based systems by stacking of past and future frames
[29]. The use of future frames obviously introduces additional
latency which violates low-latency system constraints. There-
fore, the temporal context used in this study does not include
any future frames.
Several approaches to increase both time and frequency
resolution simultaneously has been introduced, such as the
use of multi-resolution systems [17], [30], variable resolution
windowing [31]–[33] and multi-taper windowing [34].
A variable resolution approach varies a single T-F analysis
resolution using switchable windows in order to maintain
a constant synthesis window length and hence a constant
latency, but varying analysis window length and hence STFT
analysis frequency resolution. As there is only one T-F stage,
a nonstationarity detection system is required to choose the
STFT analysis window length, which adds an additional layer
of complexity. Due to the single T-F analysis stage, the VAD
systems performance will depend on the accuracy of the
nonstationarity detector. However, to avoid this it is possible
to provide a DNN system with an input feature consisting of
several different STFT analysis window lengths, and train the
network to combine these.
A multi-resolution approach allows network access to sev-
eral T-F resolutions simultaneously by conducting several
STFT analysis stages with different window lengths in parallel
and combining these into a single input feature vector. This
requires either a short, constant frame shift for all analysis
stages to have continuously synchronized windows as in the
MRCG feature [20], or an integer division of both window
length and frame shift for each analysis stage and concatenat-
ing multiple shorter windows to synchronise with the longest
window as described in [30].
While the mentioned applications of noise reduction, speech
enhancement, ASR, and DRC are all utilizing several fre-
quency channels, the number of frequency channels varies per
application, with some applications such as DRC typically
using a low number of independent frequency channels and
noise reduction typically operating with a higher number of
independent frequency channels.
The use of feature normalization has been shown to
be a critical processing stage to reduce training time and
increase the performance of neural networks [35]. Mean and
variance normalization (MVN) has additionally been shown
to increase the robustness of ASR features [36]–[38]. A
common way of feature normalization if real-time processing
is not required is off-line normalization by averaging along
entire utterances [39]. As a real-time compatible alternative,
previous studies have proposed different strategies with
varying amounts of latency by using recursive normalization
with auto-regressive moving-average (ARMA) ﬁltering to
smooth the mean and variance estimates, and utilize look-
ahead to increase performance [36], [37]. Similar to the
latency introduced by future frames in temporal context, the
latency introduced by look-ahead and ARMA ﬁltering means
the use of these were deemed unacceptable for this study.
The present study aims at developing a monaural multi fre-
quency channel DNN-based VAD system with a focus on low-
latency processing that can detect speech components in noisy,
reverberant mixtures. A feed-forward neural network (FFNN)
is trained with a set of spectro-temporal features created using
an STFT-based ﬁlterbank pre-processing stage, such that the
system can discriminate between noise and speech patterns.
No ARMA ﬁltering is used during feature normalization and
temporal context is exploited by stacking previous frames
only, to maintain a causal system. Post-processing is used to
produce the VAD system output. To choose an optimal real-
time feature normalisation strategy, several different strategies
are investigated. As the VAD system latency is inﬂuenced by
STFT window length, this length is systematically shortened
to investigate the detection performance of the VAD system
as a function of STFT analysis window length. Based on the
performance increases for ASR found in [17], [30] a multi-
window approach is investigated to utilize both high temporal-
and frequency resolution for increased VAD performance.
Finally, the VAD system is compared against a baseline to
understand if the use of a training based system can increase

II. SYSTEM DESCRIPTION
The VAD application is focused on hearing aids, hence a
binaural reverberant and noisy mixture is used as input. The
system focuses on spectral features and thus the mixture is
converted to a monaural input signal by averaging across the
left and right ear channels, and analyzed by the STFT. A ﬁlter-
bank is used to integrate individual discrete Fourier transform
(DFT) bins into a set of frequency channels which are log-
compressed, producing a log-energy feature for each frequency
channel. Based on this input feature, a DNN consisting of
fully connected layers predicts the ideal ratio mask (IRM),
described in subsection II-F, which can be post-processed by
a thresholding procedure to obtain an ideal binary mask (IBM)
estimate of speech activity in individual frequency channels.
Each step of the VAD system is described in detail in the
following sections.

D. Feature normalization
A range of different MVN strategies are investigated to
normalize the input features. The beneﬁt of each of these are
discussed in subsection IV-A. Pujol et.al, [36], showed the
initial mean and variance estimate was a key factor in ASR
performance for MVN. Based on these results, 3 different on-
line normalization strategies are investigated. These are either
initialized by the mean of the training set statistics or the initial
128 ms of each mixture.
1) Static normalization initialized by mean training set
statistics (’Static training set’)
2) Recursive normalization initialized by mean training set
statistics (’On-line training set’)
3) Recursive normalization initialized by initial 128 ms of
mixture (’On-line UTT2 128 ms’)
The normalization strategies ’static training set’ and ’on-
line training set’ are real-time compatible as no future frames
are used, and initialization is based on training set statistics,
while ’on-line UTT 128 ms’ imposes an initial delay of 128 ms
caused by the initialization using the current mixture statistics.

E. Neural network architecture
A network architecture based on a FFNN was chosen. The
network consists of one input layer, three fully connected hid-
den layers with 1024 neurons and rectiﬁed linear unit (ReLU)
activation functions each, and one output layer with K neurons
and sigmoid activation. The amount of hidden layers and
neurons was determined based on preliminary experiments.
Results for these experiments are shown in Appendix A-A.
The network is implemented and trained using Mathworks
MATLAB and its associated deep learning toolbox [41]. The
sigmoid activation in the output layer is used to bound the
output between 0 and 1 similarly to the IRM training target.
The ADAM optimizer [42] for back-propagation is used with
the parameters suggested in [42] together with a mean squared
error (MSE) criterion to perform a regression task. To reduce
overﬁtting, regularization is applied using a 20% dropout [19]
after each hidden layer activation function during training. The
mini-batch size is set to 2048 frames, and early stopping is
used with a patience of 30 epochs; i.e if the validation error
has not improved over 30 epochs the training is stopped.
Temporal context is exploited by stacking of a number of
previous feature frames based on a 128 ms time constant
post feature normalization. This time constant was determined
based on preliminary experiments. Results of these experi-
ments are shown in Appendix A-B.
With temporal context, the input feature per frame is
changed from a (K, 1) size feature to a (K, N) size feature,
with N being the amount of previous frames. It should be
noted that despite a multi-frame input, the network is only
predicting the IRM for the current frame.

III. EVALUATION
In this section the used speech and noise databases, model
training and evaluation, and the used baseline systems are
presented.
A. Databases
Reverberant and noisy mixtures were created artiﬁcially by
convolving clean speech recordings and real noise recordings
with BRIRs. The BRIRs used in this project were measured by
the University of Surrey [46] using a Cortex (MK.2) Head and
Torso Simulator (HATS) in 4 different rooms (A, B, C, D)
at 37 azimuth angles between −90 deg and 90 deg in steps of
5 deg. The characteristics of each room is described in Table 2
in terms of the direct-to-reverberant ratio (DRR) and the
reverberation time T60, with room D having the longest T60,
and room B the lowest DRR. This is expected to correspond
to being the most challenging rooms.
The used speech material was taken from the TIMIT
Acoustic-Phonetic Continuous Speech Corpus [47], using the
proposed training set and the test set described as the ’Com-
plete test set’. The TIMIT database consists of a total of 6300
sentences spoken by 630 different talkers from 8 major dialect
regions of the United States.
The used noise recordings were from the Tampere Univer-
sity (TAU) Urban Acoustic Scenes 2019 Development dataset
[48]. This dataset consists of 40 hours of noise recordings
divided into 10-second long audio segments recorded in scenes
from 10 different categories: airport, shopping mall, metro
station, pedestrian street, public square, road, tram, bus, un-
derground metro, and park.

B. Model training
The network was trained using a multi-conditional training
(MCT) approach. Speciﬁcally, the training and validation
mixtures were generated using rooms A and C described
in subsection III-A, a single speech ﬁle, and a noise ﬁle to
simulate a talker in a noisy, reverberant environment. Each
mixture was truncated to be 2 s in length, with an initial noise
only segment of 150 ms. The noise ﬁles were randomly chosen
2 s long segments from the categories: ’airport’, ’bus’, ’metro’,
’park’, ’public square’. A 2 s mixture length was chosen to
ensure speech was present through the whole mixture based
on the minimum TIMIT ﬁle length.
The target talker was placed at a random azimuth angle
between −90 deg and 90 deg with steps of 5 deg in the
frontal plane of the receiver. A single noise source was placed
randomly within the same azimuth space, and could overlap
with the target position. Despite the proposed system being
monaural in nature, this ensures any spectral variation caused
by binaural cues are still accounted for.
Each mixtures long-term SNR was randomly selected be-
tween −5 and 10 dB by scaling the reverberant noise to the
target early speech component. Late reﬂections of the BRIR
ﬁltered speech were not scaled, to maintain a natural speech
reverberation decay.
The number of mixtures constituting the training and valida-
tion sets were set to 1000 and 200 respectively. A decimation
factor of 2 was used to maximize diversity while restricting
the size of the training feature space. As wa is varied while
R is ﬁxed, the models see a variable number of data frames
depending on window length, but will always at minimum
see one data frame every two frames at the smallest window
length of wa = 8 ms, ensuring all mixture audio is seen
at least once during training. Higher mixture numbers were
investigated during initial experiments, but provided no change
in experiment results. Due to this, the used number of mixtures
was chosen as the best compromise of training time and model
performance.
The training and validation sets were generated using the
proposed training set splits of the TIMIT sentences and TAU
noise recordings, such that no material overlaps between val-
idation and training datasets. Input features were normalized
as described in subsection IV-A in order to investigate the
inﬂuence of different feature normalization strategies.
C. Model evaluation
Models were tested on 4 different test sets to evaluate both
matched and mismatched conditions. Two sets with matching
noise categories and rooms were considered (”matched, L =
2 s”, ”matched, L = 10 s”). Two sets with previously unseen
noise types and rooms (”mismatched L = 2 s”, ”mismatched,
L = 10 s”) were also evaluated, using BRIRs from rooms B
and D, and the following noise categories: street pedestrian,
street trafﬁc, tram. Long test sets (’L = 10 s’) consisted of
several concatenated speech ﬁles and a single noise ﬁle. A
short-term SNR was randomly chosen between −15 dB and
15 dB at the start of each speech segment and interpolated
to create a continuous gain curve across the whole mixture.
Afterwards the mixture was then randomly set to one of the
long-term SNR levels. The purpose of these test ses were to
simulate varying short-term SNR conditions, and examine the
proposed systems ability to still detect speech.
For all test mixtures, the range of azimuth angles for the
target and noise sources were the same as for the training
mixtures. Each model was tested using all test sets at SNRs

For each of the rooms in a test set (A and C or B and
D) and each noise category, 5 mixtures were created at each
tested SNR resulting in 2·5·5·8 = 400 mixtures per matched

Performance was assessed using the HIT-FA% metric (per-
centage correctly classiﬁed target-dominant (k, l) - percentage
wrongly classiﬁed noise-dominant (k, l)), which has been
shown to correlate with speech intelligibility [49] and can
act as a single metric to evaluate the detection accuracy of
the system. This metric was calculated by thresholding both
the IRM and the VAD system predicted IRM at the set
LC = 0 dB. Hits and false alarms were assessed and the
HIT-FA% score was averaged across each mixture. The HIT-
FA% scores were averaged across testing mixtures for each
set to yield a single metric, or across each tested SNR level to
assess performance as a function of SNR. Hence to provide a
score for each SNR value, the score was averaged across 50
or 30 mixtures respectively for matched and mismatched sets,
and a ﬁnal score taking all tested SNR values into account
was averaged across 400 or 240 mixtures respectively.
D. Baseline systems
Two baseline systems were used during some of the con-
ducted experiments. Experiment A compares the normalization
strategies described in II-D with a baseline off-line solution
(’Off-line UTT’), where each mixture is normalized separately
by averaging mean and variance along the entire mixture
with no updating [39]. This is not a real-time compatible
normalization strategy, but is a common normalization strategy
for non-causal systems and is used to compare the performance
levels of the proposed on-line strategies to an off-line strategy.
Experiment C compares the performance of the training-based
network conﬁgurations with a training-free statistical speech
enhancement algorithm proposed by D¨orbecker and Ernst [16]
to demonstrate the beneﬁt of using a DNN compared to a
conventional training-free system. The implemented baseline
system operates on a noisy binaural mixture and is STFT
based. The algorithm output is an average gain of the two
channels bounded by [0, 1] per (k,l), and can be treated as an
estimated IRM. The ﬁrst 150 ms of each mixture were used
to initialize the noise estimate of the baseline algorithm.
Preliminary experiments showed a relatively low perfor-
mance when using a 0 dB threshold level, with peak HIT-
FA% score when baseline LC was set to ≈−9 dB. The
baseline performance as a function of threshold can be seen
in Appendix B. Thus, two conﬁgurations of the baseline
algorithms were tested at a threshold of 0 dB and −9 dB
equivalent to an IRM of 0.7071 and 0.3344.
IV. RESULTS
In this section, three experiments and their results are pre-
sented and discussed. Experiment A investigates the effect of
feature normalization strategies described in subsection II-D.
Experiment B compares the performance of STFT analysis
window lengths and assesses the performance of a multi-
resolution window system. Experiment C compares the best
DNN-based system based on experiments A and B to a
baseline training-free statistical system. All experiments are
investigated without and with 128 ms temporal context, and
for K = 8 and K = 32 frequency channels.

of frequency channels K when wa is shortened when temporal

test sets can be found for all single-window conﬁgurations of

comparing matched L = 2s and matched L = 10 s set

to predict voice activity in varying SNR conditions. The use

increase performance compared to a single 8 ms window, but
with a worse performance than a single 32 ms window.

can still be observed for shorter wa in Figure 5b. The

the difference in score of 4.47% between ’matched L = 2 s’

decrease for test set ’mismatched L = 2 s’ of 4% when
shortening wa from 16 to 8 ms. For both L = 10 s sets the
introduction of context increases performance of wa = 8 ms
and wa = 16 ms comparatively more than wa = 32 ms.
Again the use of a dual-window system can be seen to increase
performance compared to the lowest performance of each test
set for wa = 8 ms and wa = 32 ms respectively, while only
being comparable in performance to the heighest performing

V. DISCUSSION
These results show a decrease in STFT analysis window
length still impacts performance despite a low number of
ﬁlterbank channels and independently of feature normalization
strategies. As the decrease is consistent and independent of
context, this is expected to stem from a lack of resolution in
the initial STFT analysis window and propagating through the
system. The decreased performance by shortening wa from
32 ms to 8 ms appears to be partly solvable by exploiting
temporal context to gain similar or better performance com-
pared to a wa = 32 ms window for acoustic conditions within
the trained feature-space.
Decreasing the number of frequency channels can also be
observed to decrease test set variability when context is not
included. This is assumed to stem from the reduced spectral
resolution of the low K causing a greater generalization during
training. The dual-window approach for K = 32 performance
was found to be better than the worst performing single-
window conﬁguration, yet worse than the best performing
single-window conﬁguration of the two window lengths used
in the dual-window conﬁguration for all test sets. This is
expected to stem from the two window lengths not being
different enough to capture local and global information when
comparing to previous studies of multi-resolution approaches
such as the MRCG feature [20] and multi-resolution speech
analysis [30]. Toledanos motivation for a dual-window ap-
proach in ASR is to capture different phones in a single
window, with TIMIT phone duration statistics to be between
17.5 ms and 163.62 ms. The length of phone duration is
consistent with the MRCG feature, which proposes 200 ms
based on an experiment, where one window length was ﬁxed
to 20 ms and a second window was expanded to different
lengths and the best performing combination being presented
as MRCG window lengths.
The increased lack of local context when shortening wa
without temporal context can also explain why the use of tem-
poral context increases performance more for shorter window
lengths despite providing a similar total length of 128 ms tem-
poral context. The additional increase for matched conditions
is expected to stem from some degree of overﬁtting during
training. Additional experiments with additional regularization
and a more diverse training set could be conducted to verify
this theory.
Finally, a decreased performance for mismatched sets for
both DNN systems and baseline can be expected, as the BRIRs
used for mismatched conditions were deemed to be the most
challenging based on RT60 and DRR. The mismatched sets
can hence be regarded as a more challenging set in regards to
adverse acoustic conditions, but simultaneously show the loss
in performance when the DNN systems are required to predict
unseen conditions. Test sets with each mismatched condition
separated could be investigated to provide an understanding
of which mismatch decreases VAD performance the most.
A. Future aspects
Based on the ﬁndings of this paper, several additional
aspects could be examined in order to further improve system
performance or better understand the correlation of STFT
analysis window length and performance.
As previous studies have used longer secondary window
lengths in the order of 200 ms [17], [30], the impact of
different secondary window lengths could be examined further.
In addition, preliminary experiments utilizing an asymmetric
window, as described in [31], for the second window were
done, but results were similar or worse than using a long
Hann window for all cases. The use of asymmetric windows
to weight newest data could however prove useful for longer
windows.
Despite the reduction in latency, the minimum window
length used during this paper, wa = 8 ms, still exhibits a
longer latency than desired based on [27], [28]. Hence even
shorter STFT window lengths could be examined to investigate
if the decrease in performance continues with decreasing wa.
The presented VAD system does not include any spatial
processing such as a beamformer in order to utilize binaural
cues for difference in spatial positioning of target and noise
sources. This could be implemented as a delay-and-sum beam-
former [50] or as a separate DNN for binaural to monaural pre-
processing [9]. Additional input features such as spatial cues
could also be introduced directly as ITD, ILD or IC features
to the existing system.
Finally, the use of more advanced architectures such as
long short-term memory (LSTM) [51] has been discovered to
increase generalization in speech separation [52], and has been
used for a single-channel VAD system [53] with good results
using wa = 30 ms. Similar experiments to the ones conducted
in this paper could be done on this DNN architecture to see if
an increased performance can be observed. As recurrent neural
networks and the LSTM cell is designed to exploit temporal
context, and based on the results showing increased perfor-
mance gain from temporal context for short window lengths
in this study suggests this could provide further increases for
multi frequency-channel VAD systems as well.

VI. CONCLUSION
This paper presented a monaural neural network-based
VAD system for mixtures corrupted by both noise and room
reverberation with the aim of low latency VAD in adverse
acoustic conditions. The use of spectral features allow to
differentiate the spectro-temporal patterns of speech and noise
with the frequency- and time resolution determined by the
STFT analysis window. However, system latency is determined
by the STFT synthesis window which is typically linked to
the analysis window, and if a low-latency system is desired
the frequency resolution will be lowered.
To investigate the performance impact of varying STFT
analysis window length, a range of real-time feature nor-
malization strategies was investigated. Normalization based
on a recursive MVN estimation per input feature frame was
found to provide the best performance based on results of a
wa = 8 ms conﬁguration.
It was shown that a reduction in STFT analysis window
length decreased performance independent of number of fre-
quency channels and feature normalization strategy, with the
decrease expected to stem from a lack of initial STFT analysis
window frequency resolution and propagating through the low-
latency system.
Temporal context by stacking of previous frames was found
to increase performance across all real-time conﬁgurations,
with a larger performance increase for shorter STFT analy-
sis window lengths, and additional performance increase for
matched test conditions. This increased performance for short
window lengths in matched conditions allowed similar or bet-
ter performance than longer STFT analysis window lengths in
low frequency channel conﬁgurations when exploiting context.
Combining several analysis windows with different window
lengths provided VAD improvements compared to the low-
est performing single window without exceeding the highest
performing single window for a high number of frequency
channels, but provided general improvements compared to
the highest performing single window for a low number of
frequency channels.
The DNN-based VAD system was compared to a baseline
statistical learning-free algorithm. The VAD system showed
signiﬁcantly higher results in all conditions, with HIT-FA%
scores in negative SNR conditions over 2 times higher than
baseline scores.
Great attention was paid to the generalization of the pre-
sented system through the use of an MCT approach and testing
on several different test sets with matched and mismatched
conditions. This means that the proposed system constitutes a
powerful tool, which can detect voice activity in a wide range
of conditions independently of SNR, noise types, and room
acoustics at low latency.
