Abstract 
 
 
The increasing demand for transportation and sustainability make railways an attractive 
choice. However, inefficient management of common and unexpected disruptions hinder the 
performance of the railways and render them more vulnerable to crises, while railway 
authorities are blamed for not delivering a high level service to their customers. Hence, this 
study focuses on the passenger railway transportation system with the purpose of identifying 
the causes of common railway disruptions and propose a strategic level approach to improve 
their management. On that front the concept of resilience is presented which represents an 
advance way we think, design and review complex systems and in particular railway 
systems. According to that approach there are no standard procedures proposed but instead 
the focus is given to some key attributes that when adopted properly throughout a system, it 
can manage efficiently small disruptions and has a much higher chance of surviving with 
minimal damage from large scale crises. Subsequently, a framework is proposed that 
provides strategic and practical solutions to common disruption management while it helps 
a system adopt resilience qualities. Lastly, a real case study, the Danish railways, is analyzed 
in order to reflect part of the theory.

Chapter 1 
1. Introduction  
Railways have always been a vital part of thriving modern societies. Railway transportation 
is considered to be one of the most important modes of both passenger and freight transport 
providing high-capacity performance especially in cities with high levels of congestion while 
meeting the social and economic demands of a continuously increasing population. The 
growing demand of railway transportation in addition to the highly advanced technology 
development, increase the expectations of a highly satisfactory service which when not met, 
causes public disapproval. 
Despite the well scheduled timetables, during the real time operations of the rails, there is 
sometimes an unexpected service delay or a series of them which are called disruptions. Rail 
service disruptions do not occur only in less developed countries but in highly developed 
countries as well where the service is considered to be upper-level and supplied with 
advanced technologies and infrastructure. Railways are tightly connected and complex 
systems with little slack of resources and time to absorb irregularities. Therefore, disruptions 
can lead to the rapid degradation of the provided service (Kapaptsoglou & Karlaftis, 2009) 
When people choose rails as their mode of transportation they have certain expectations 
regarding the level of comfort and the duration of the trip and destination based on the 
published timetable. The failure of meeting these expectations from the railway’s side causes 
discomfort and frustration to the travelers. In addition, since the passengers’ delay it may 
not be the same with the train delay due to lost connections the impact can be even greater. 
This situation may create aversion of the public towards the public transportation and 
increase the possibilities of them using private vehicles which is not sustainable neither for 
the economy nor for the environment of a country. 
According to the european report on monitoring develepment of the rail market (European 
Commission, 2014) the rail services are ranked quite badly in the Consumer Scoreboard, 
which compares several types of services in the internal european market. Rail services 
==PAGE:16==
2 
 
ranked 27th (out of 31) in 2012 compared to other internal market services in terms of the 
cosumer market performance indicator. On the other side, ailrines, postal services and urban 
transport ranked significantly higher. According to the Eurobarometer survey, the reason 
railways ranked so low was mainly due to dissatisfaction because of problems experienced 
due to service delays (Commission, 2014).  
Therefore, delays in a railway network are one of the biggest problems in the daily operations 
of a railway company (Landex, 2012) and due to the importance for the public on one hand 
and the deregulation of the railway market in many countries on the other, railway authorities 
put more emphasis on their performance than in the past.  
1.1 Terminology: Railway System 
A railway system consists of vehicles steered by a track on a dedicated area which are 
governed by a signaling system. The term railway infrastructure covers all fixed installations 
on routes and stations which are required for the running of trains. Internationally, the degree 
to which railways are distinguished from underground and tramway services, varies. 
However in this study railways will refer only to:  
 Suburban rail (SR): Railway lines running through central cities with many stations, 
with electric traction and short headways 
 Long-Distance Rail (IR): Railway lines operating from key regional cities and 
operate several inbound trains in the morning and outbound in the afternoon 
 Regional Rail (RR): Railway lines operating from more remote towns and they 
consist of few trips per day (also known as local) 
1.2 Disruption Management in the Literature  
A very common problem in railways is that due to the strong interdependencies in the 
railway network and due to cost efficient resource timetables, disruptions are very likely to 
spread over the network in time and space, a phenomenon called knock-on effect. A key to 
a good performance of the railways is therefore to limit the impact of each single disruption. 
Hence, railway timetables should be robust. In addition, the consequences for the passengers 
==PAGE:17==
3 
 
can be reduced even if their arriving train is late by delaying connecting trains. This problem 
is known as delay management, however, is outside the scope of this study.  
So far, the topic of railway timetable robustness in particular and disruption management in 
general has been addressed with Operations Research (OR) techniques. OR models play an 
important role to limit the impact of disruptions and already many railway companies are 
using OR techniques during their planning phase, where the focus is given on the balance of 
the service level offered to the passengers and the efficiency of rolling stock and crew. OR 
techniques have been proven effective already with supporting disruption management 
processes in the airline context  (Jespersen-Groth, et al., 2009) and the recent years a lot of 
research in relation to the railway industry has been undergone as well. The definition of 
disruption management as shown by Jespersen-Groth is: “the joint approach of the involved 
organizations to deal with the impact of disruptions in order to ensure the best possible 
service for the passengers. This is done by modifying the timetable, and the rolling stock and 
crew schedules during and after the disruption. The involved organizations are the 
infrastructure manager and the operators”. 
Organizations 
In many countries including most of the European Union countries like Denmark, The 
Netherlands and UK the organizations directly involved in disruption management are the 
infrastructure manager and the railway operators. These organizations usually have contracts 
with the involved government. 
The infrastructure manager has a contract with the government that obliges it to provide the 
railway operators with a railway network of certain infrastructure capabilities and reliability. 
The infrastructure manager has the responsibility of maintaining the railway network such 
as tracks and signals and ensures that timetables of the different operators are feasible from 
an over-all point of view (Jespersen-Groth, et al., 2009). 
A passenger railway operator gets the authorization to operate passenger trains on the 
network from the government.  The operator has the responsibility of buying and 
maintaining trains, ensuring the availability of crew and setting up plans for departures and 
arrivals, rolling stock and crew. The operator is bound by contract to provide a performance 
that exceeds certain specified thresholds on key performance indicators. For example, there 
==PAGE:18==
4 
 
are thresholds related to the number of train departures per station, the punctuality of trains 
arriving per station, the seating probability. I.e. in Denmark the punctuality is the percentage 
of the trains arriving within 2 or 3 minutes (suburban trains and the rest respectively) of their 
scheduled arrival time at certain stations. The realization of figures on these performance 
figures have to be reported to the government periodically. If an operator does not reach one 
of the thresholds, it has to pay a certain penalty to the government. In case that the 
performance is very poor the government can give the license to operate the trains another 
company. As a consequence, the main objective of the railway operator is to achieve all the 
targets for the key indicator performance set with the government, at the minimum cost. 
In more detail, some important objectives of the operators in the disruption management 
process is to prevent most of the events that lead to a disruption, and when this is not possible 
to minimize the number of the affected passengers and to minimize the inconvenience for 
the affected passengers.  
Therefore the goal of disruption management as find in the literature is to provide the 
appropriate tools and solutions for the railway organizations to modify and adjust timetable 
and re-schedule the rolling stock and crew in the optimal feasible way. In addition a limited 
research is performed about alternative plans during the disrupted railway traffic applied 
usually when the disruption is expected to last over a certain duration. A summary of every 
one of these strategies is provided below: 
Timetable Robustness 
Timetable robustness refers to the ability of the planned timetable to absurd any small 
irregularities and is resolved by adding slack time between departures in order to avoid 
propagation of small mostly small delays. Robustness of the timetable includes also the 
supplement time added in the travel time between specific nodes in the network again so that 
the train can recover itself from small delays. Robustness of railway timetabling has been 
attracting a significant among of research the recent years. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:19==
5 
 
Dispatching Strategies 
Dispatching strategies refers to solutions that train dispatchers choose in order to reduce the 
impact of train delays. Some of the most commonly used dispatching strategies according to 
Clausen (2007) are: 
 Increase the speed of the train: In order to make up for the lost time 
 Change in stop patterns: It can be important in order to reduce the consequences a 
train being late has on the rest of the network but forces passengers on-board to 
change trains and prolongs the waiting time of the passengers waiting on the station. 
 Transferring the passengers of the disrupted train to the next train: Passengers can be 
transferred to the following trains in the timetable. 
 Reducing headways to a minimum: Timetables are constructed given a predefined 
security headway between two departures. The actual headway is the headway plus 
a slack time. In a case of disruption trains can reduce their headway to the minimum. 
 Reducing running time to a minimum: Timetables are designed given predefined 
running time between all sets of stations. The running time is always the minimum 
running time plus the supplement time. During a disruption the running times can be 
reduced to the minimum.  
 Shortening the routes of trains. A train might be turned around before reaching its 
terminals. 
 Swapping the tasks/routes of fast trains catching up with slow trains: In order to 
reduce the knock-on effect it is possible to swap the identity of the two trains so that 
the fast train is changed to a slow one and vice versa. This action will affect the duty 
of the driver/crew and the rotation of the involved trains and will involve re-
scheduling.  
 Overtaking on stations with available tracks: this action will possibly require re-
scheduling as well since the predetermined order of train lines might need to change. 
 Inserting replacement trains for the trains that are delayed: This requires a stand-by 
train unit and a driver, hence re-scheduling of the crew and the rolling stock might 
be needed. 
 Cancelling of entire train lines: In the case of sever disruptions entire lines are taken 
out. This option heavily influences the operation since train units are misplaced and 
==PAGE:20==
6 
 
the drivers/crew knock out of their duties making recovery stage of this action quite 
complicated. 
 Coupling trains: In some case two or more trains can be coupled to one train in order 
to gain some capacity (used commonly in Denmark due to track failures). 
Re-scheduling  
Re-scheduling is about optimally recovering the line-planning, timetable, rolling stock, 
depot and crew schedules in the disrupted environment (Pisinger, 2015). As shown before 
can be triggered also due to a dispatching strategy. Re-scheduling is currently performed 
manually (Pisinger, 2015), however a significant research has been conducted in order to 
perform this operations with the help of OR modes which will be able to minimize the 
cancellations and operational costs and provide a feasible solution.   
Alternative Plans 
Alterative plans refer to other effective ways to accommodate disrupted passengers. 
According to Arisawa et al. (2008) despite the great impacts railway disruptions have, only 
few studies have examined the effectiveness of contingency plans. Alternative plans are 
required usually when the impact of a disruption is great enough that dispatching methods 
are not able to recover the system. The most common approaches for alternative plans 
railway companies have are the (Pender, et al., 2012): 
 Single tracking, i.e. bypassing disrupted areas by using crossovers  
 Re-routing trains onto other lines of the same network 
 Disrupted commuters diverted onto other parallel public transport services 
 Disrupted commuters diverted onto other operating lines 
 Adjusting the frequency of existing bus routes  
 Bus bridging 
 Hiring taxis 
 Chartering planes  
 Postponing service and offering no alternatives to the passengers until service is 
restored  
==PAGE:21==
7 
 
According to Pender et al. in a survey performed about the responses to unplanned service 
disruptions, 86% of the railway companies involved in the study, use bus bridging as a 
solution. The decisions usually performed in the planning of bus bridging as an alternative 
is if the buses should be reserved (purely for rail-replacement purposes), spared ones used 
(not being used by the normal bus operations) or be removed by normal bus operations in 
order to serve the disrupted railway system. The approaches to bus bridging design are 
influenced by the network and disruption characteristics such as the estimated duration of 
the disruption and the time it occurs since during peak hours the number of spare buses might 
be not sufficient.   
Taxis and planes will be used in extraordinary cases and especially in remote towns with 
low frequency and mobility.   Crossovers’ impact in disruption management is a large and 
controversial issue.  Many railway authorities believe that crossovers play an important role 
in disruption management because they allow to minimize the disruption effects. However, 
the objection expressed by the opposition is the significant maintenance that crossovers 
require, which when not performed proactively can become a reason for disruptions 
themselves. (Pender, et al., 2012).  
1.3 Research Objectives 
Problem Statement  
Despite the fact that timetabling, re-scheduling and contingency plans are undoubtedly 
integrals to disruption management this study will perform a research on what else makes 
a passenger focused railway disruption management successful by looking on a more 
broad view and from a strategic level approach.  
The specific objectives researched in this project are: 
 To classify the important factors that contribute in passenger railway traffic 
disruptions 
 To analyze the concept of disruption management before and after the disruption 
 To relate disruption management with risk analysis and risk management and to 
emergency plan and emergency response 
==PAGE:22==
8 
 
 To discuss what a resilient railway system is 
 To summarize the Danish passenger railway planning process, the actors and 
organizations related to disruption management 
 To identify points to be potentially improved in the current operations of disruption 
management, through real data analysis 
 To examine and develop methods and alternative solutions in railway recovery 
response which can be applicable in specific cases 
 To write a technical report in an understandable and well documented form 
 Prepare a clear and understandable oral presentation

Initialization 
Before establishing the learning objectives of this study, it was necessary to have a look into 
what has been done and decide where this research can contribute. Hence, the initialization 
phase was about determining the research gap and the contribution of this study. The 
literature review was chosen as a research method. 
The most fundamental part of this research was the analysis of the existing literature review, 
related to the scope of this study. It is important to look through what the literature provides 
and taking this as a start point to determine possible contribution of this research.  In addition, 
it assists in narrowing down the scope of the project and making it a feasible project within 
the limitations such as lack of pre-knowledge, time, stress etc. 
==PAGE:23==

While reviewing literature review, the scope was narrowed to passenger service railway 
disruptions. The review included all kind of incidents resulting in the disruption of the 
railway traffic service, from small service delays to major disaster events. However, the 
focus was decided to be given to service delay disruptions and emergency situations within 
a certain limit of severity. Hence, major crises such as natural disasters that usually have a 
great impact on a larger scale such as national, where not studied extensively except for 
showing their contribution in planning for having a resilient railway system. 
Taking into account these parameters, a holistic analysis of all possible disruptions as 
defined and shown in this study was performed. 
Starting digging into emergency management literature the most interesting topic discovered 
was that of resilience, a term widely popular recently in achieving a high performance of the 
modern organizations for managing unexpected events. During the study of many different 
cases regarding emergency situations many principles of resilience were underlined despite 
the fact that were not found under that title. Hence, the components that make a railway 
system resilient were investigated in detail and by using examples from real case studies. 
Development of the Disruption Management Framework 
After the initialization phase, the conclusions from the literature review led to the 
development of a holistic disruption management framework which seemed to cover a 
potential literature gap. The framework focuses on addressing the common passenger 
railway disruptions described earlier by using strategies based on some of the resilience’s 
principles. The methods during this phase were discussions with the supervisor, attending 
some conferences and lectures, studying research specified in this field and discovering 
possible gaps in addition to case studies and personal experience by observing current 
processes. 
Appliance and Validation of the Framework 
Finally, the Danish Railway System was described as a case study in order to investigate, 
examine and reflect disruption management as presented in this study. The tools used in this 
stage were statistical data analysis, interviews and documents regarding the Danish railway 
authorities.

2. Railway Service Disruption Analysis  
2.1 Introduction  
The aim of this chapter is to analyze service disruptions of the railway passenger 
transportation. So at first, a short definition of railway service disruptions and delays is 
provided. Then the metrics that are used to quantify the performance of a railway service 
and help approximate the magnitude of the delays, will be discussed. One important detail 
to mention here is that the physical quantity of interest in this study is the train service delay 
time which might be different than the passenger delay time. After that, the major causes of 
railway service disruptions will be identified, classified and analyzed in order to understand 
them better so as to be able to attribute them to the responsible parties. Another issue that 
needs attention in disruption analysis is that train delays tend to cascade which depends on 
other important attributes of disruptions such as duration, location and time, which means 
that they create delays somewhere else in the network. In that case extreme care needs to be 
taken in determining the root disruption. Finally, based on some literature founding, 
monetary value is given to disruptions in order to show the importance of disruptions in 
economic terms.

2.2 Definition of Disruption  
“Disruption is defined as an event or series of events that renders the planned schedules for 
aircraft, crew or other timetable to be infeasible” (Larsen, et al., 2010). In this report is used 
the same definition where aircraft is substituted by rolling stock and all airline operations by 
railway operations. Disruption is hence, by definition, a cause rather than a consequence. 
The disruptions often lead to inconstancy of the scheduled timetable and therefore to service 
delays which create a disrupted situation. Although in terms of terminology is not correct, 
for favor of simplicity it is commonly used in the literature the term disruption instead when 
referring to a disrupted situation (Jespersen-Groth, et al., 2009). 
The national railway networks are often being criticized for not delivering the level of service 
they promise, causing delays to the passengers and cancellations which when not 
communicated plainly and timely they increase the passenger´s discomfort and disapproval. 
It should be noticed that train delays are different than passengers delay since due to lost 
connections the real delay that passengers are experiencing is different than the actual delay 
of the train. Unluckily, rail disruptions are not rare and in most of the cases are very common 
and part of the daily operations of the railways. Debrincat et al. (2007) found that for the 
Paris suburban rail network in 2004, there was a 5% to 16% cases of delayed services during 
its peak hours. The Dutch railway network has approximately 17 disruptions, related to the 
infrastructure, per day with an average duration of 1.8 hours (Jespersen-Groth, et al., 2009). 
In the United Kingdom’s railway network, a total of 800.000 cases of delays were recorded 
in 2006-2007, which was equivalent to 14 million minutes of delay, the cost of which is 
estimated to be at minimum DKK 10 billion (National Audit Office, 2008). 
This chapter describes the most common causes and characteristics of service 
disruptions/service delays. Accidents, passenger’s delays, train breakdowns, track failures 
and weather are some common disruptions which create delays and affect the punctuality 
and reliability among the railways. When disruptions occur, trains become oversaturated, 
bottlenecks are created and travel times are extended causing annoyance among the 
passengers (Alwadood, et al., 2012). Hence, is very important that railway authorities focus 
on the treatment of these delays in order to eliminate them or when not possible reduce their 
duration and effects and the first step towards it is to analyze extensively their source.

2.3 Critical Performance Indicators  
The most important passenger railway performance measures regarding the time perspective 
are punctuality and reliability. Public authorities in cooperation with the railway companies 
set certain performance (i.e. minimum values) targets which when not met lead to fines for 
the railway contractors.  
Punctuality normally refers to whether a train is running on time, with the provision of an 
acceptable deviation and is mostly measured as the percentage of trains departing 
from/arriving at stations within a predefined time threshold for delay (Parbo, et al., 2013). 
Reliability is normally used to describe the rate of cancellations of trains (Preston, et al., 
2009) and is typically measured as the percentage of train arrivals actually performed on the 
day of operation compared to the number of train arrivals planned according to the published 
timetable (Parbo, et al., 2013). According to Nelson and O’Neil (2000), punctuality is a 
critical key performance indicator in the railways for three main reasons: the first reason is 
that the volume of commuters carried per trip and distances covered by railway passenger 
services are relatively very high comparing to other transit modes in addition to the fact that 
railway services cannot recover easily from a delay by running extra or substitute service. 
Hence is very important, the primary service is working well since the alternative plan is 
usually less attractive. The second reason is the fact the passengers’ trains usually operate 
on tracks shared among other rail users i.e. the intercity, suburban and freight rail where in 
some cases is a single track, thus each rail service must obey to the time windows it has. The 
last reason, which in fact applies mostly for reliability, is that the passenger impact in 
eliminating one single trip, especially in low frequent lines, can cause long delays due to lost 
connections. 
In the United Kingdom, the concept of performance targets for delays was introduced in 
1991 requiring the railways to publish information on reliability and punctuality. The 
purpose of this initiative was to motivate Railtrack, who was responsible for the track 
infrastructure and due to monopoly ownership could provide low-quality infrastructure 
which would result in poor punctuality and reliability, to provide a certain level of service. 
If Railtrack or the operating companies were to be blamed for the delays they would have to 
compensate related parties and passengers. Same in Denmark, in 2009 and after a period 
==PAGE:30==
16 
 
with a high number of disruptions and delays a punctuality target was established for the 
infrastructure manager company in order to improve their performance.  
Reliability and punctuality metrics are combined to form a public performance indicator. 
They apply on all the scheduled services and report the percentage of trains running and 
arriving on time compared to the published timetable. Trains are regarded to be on time when 
they arrive within certain time deviation i.e. in Great Britain is 5 minutes and 10 minutes for 
short and long distances respectively, in the Netherlands is 3 min whereas in Denmark 
recently was decided to decrease the thresholds from 2, 5 to 2 minutes for S-bane and from 
5 to 3 minutes for the rest lines. The cancelled or partially cancelled trains are also considered 
late for purpose of calculations. The train drivers in cooperation with traffic controllers are 
identifying and recording these delay causes whenever a train does not appear on the station 
the time is supposed to within its time windows.  
The threshold values for punctuality vary among countries. Apart from the threshold, the 
way it is measured differs as well. In the Netherlands punctuality is measured at the departure 
of the trains in specific stations across the network, in Denmark is measured per arrival in 
every station, whereas in Germany and Norway is measured per arrival in terminal stations 
(Landex, 2012). For this reason only a few international comparisons of train delays are 
conducted.

2.4 Nature of Disruptions  
Among the studies concerning the causes of railway delays many have attempted to 
categorize them into groups in order to understand and deal with them. Higgins et al. (1995) 
grouped them in three categories. The first category is track related delays involving train 
slowing down or stopping due to track problems. The second category is train dependent 
delays caused by train malfunctions. The third category is terminal/stations stop related 
delays, which occur in stations and terminals. This categorization it might be a reasonable 
one, grouping the three main factors of track problems, rolling stock problems and 
operational issues, in causing delays, but needs to be extended and specified. 
Nelson and O’Neil (2000), based on real data analysis of 401.110 scheduled revenue trains 
– mostly in diesel commuter railroads – made a highly holistic categorization of disruption 
==PAGE:34==
20 
 
causes. According to them the three main categories for classifying delays correspond to the 
three disciplines providing railway service to the passengers: 
 Engineering: Issues related to tracks, signals, communications, stations and terminals 
 Mechanical:  Issues related to the rolling stock  
 Transportation: Issues related to the  operation of the trains, the timetable and 
dispatching  
Engineering problems are related to infrastructure matters such as track, crossovers and 
signals problems. According to their findings, about 25% of engineering related delays are 
due to planned maintenance or construction work. This study focus on unexpected delays, 
however planned disruptions sometimes when not implemented well, can have unexpected 
impacts as well, especially when they last longer than estimated. Another quarter of 
engineering related causes is due to temporary right-of-way and track conditions such as 
broken tracks and track failures, speed restrictions, flooding and track-related switch 
problems. The cause with the highest percentage (40%) is signaling problems such as grade 
crossing warning device failures, switch failures, signal failures, black signal bulb, broken 
bond wires and track occupancy lights. In total, engineering/infrastructure problems in 
addition to the mechanical delays had the largest share among the different delay-related 
causes which is partially related to non-regular preventive and corrective maintenance. In 
Great Britain, for the years 2006-07 infrastructure delays hold a share of 42%. 
According to the same study, mechanical delays are due to technical problems of the rolling 
stock that concern the transportation operator. Almost one out of five problems is due to the 
main engine (prime mover) and about 15% are braking system related problems. Other 
common problems are power systems for passengers such as lighting, ventilation, heating 
and air conditioning, coach components such as doors, toilets, windows and wipers in the 
control cabs, the wheels and axles and issues with the control electrical systems.  
Finally, transportation delays which count for the smallest percentages among the rest of the 
factors, are mainly due to problems with the train crews, dispatchers and planning. 
Dispatching problems that stand for more than the half include late train orders, dispatcher 
errors and block operator errors whereas crew problems include crew mistakes, crew swaps 
and crew being late. Problems regarding planning are related with the timetable design, 
==PAGE:35==
21 
 
traffic and overestimation of new rolling stock. Another transportation delay factor can be 
timetable errors and capacity issues.  
In many countries like Denmark, the Netherlands and Great Britain transportation and 
mechanical delays concern the same authority which is the Train Operators. A report on 
research in the United Kingdom during the year 1999-2000 revealed that train delays caused 
by rail operator contributed 50% of all delay minutes (Preston, et al., 2009). Poor rolling 
stocks were the main cause of the train faults which were mainly due to frequent breakdowns 
and low train speed. The other category of delays which comprised 35% of the delay minutes 
is due to the railway infrastructure causes. During the year 2006-07, the percentage of delay 
caused by rail operator has dropped to 38% while the delay caused by rail track has risen to 
42%. The findings indicate that the railway operators have undoubtedly taken significant 
measures in improving their operational performance and bringing the percentage delay 
down (National Audit Office, 2008). The maintenance agencies, on the other hand, should 
be responsive for the increasing number of rail track breakdowns.  
Apart from this categorization the disruptions are also classified for the below mentioned 
factors: 
 Passenger related delays 
 Extraordinary circumstances  
 Cascades 
 Freight and National Railroad Passenger  
Although passengers are rarely the main responsible for delays can be a contributing factor. 
Nelson and O’Neil (2000) found that about one-third of passengers related delays are due to 
passengers with special requirements from the train services or the crew. Such examples are 
injured or sick passengers, handicapped, infirm and elderly, passengers with heavy baggage 
and problems between passengers. Slow and late passengers can attribute in small delays as 
well since the train drivers usually will wait if they see a passenger running. Heavy ridership 
such as schools boarding in the trains increase significantly the passenger load and thus the 
boarding time. Other issues are related to ticketing and revenue collections although are rare.  
The definition of cascade effect – also known as knock on effect – is a controversial one and 
is not coded the same among the railway sectors. In general, as cascade delay is defined the 
==PAGE:36==
22 
 
delay that is caused by other delays in the network. For example a technical problem of a 
train (primary cause) can affect other trains in the network that are using the same line 
(secondary cause).  For this reason cascades are considered to be in the grey zone, since they 
might have their roots in some of the other factors and just be some of their side effects. 
However, instead of being coded due to their root, they might be imprecisely coded as a 
transportation delay. Cascades delays should be analyzed in terms of realizing which are the 
critical points or links in a network where a small disruption can affect many trains and time 
of arrivals.  
Hence, the location is generally very important when something goes wrong. Apart from the 
location, the time - peak hour/off peak-hour – is an important factor on the impact the 
disruptions can have on the network since the railway timetables are designed to have some 
slack on time to absorb small disruptions which during rush hours is very tighten and might 
not be able to absorb as much as during other hours. In addition, the duration of the delay is 
an important factor as well since small disruptions can usually be treated effectively such as 
increasing the speed (or other dispatching methods) whereas other probably longer delays 
will of course have a bigger impact on the delay of the train and the whole network in general. 
Finally, apart from the impact of each one of these factors important is the combination 
among them, i.e. the same track failure on a suburban train network, which operates in busy 
networks with high frequency, will affect much more arrivals than on a regional one which 
are not very frequent.

weather and human accidents due to track intrusion or level crossing. The time of the 
disruption is important since during peak hours resources such as crew and spare rolling 
stock etc are limited in contrast to off-peak hours. Finally, the location is important mostly 
due to restrictions of single tracks and capacity consumption.

2.5 The Role of Weather  
The weather factor due to its complexity is chosen to present the impact it has in the railway 
network in relation to disruptions.  
Traditionally railway transportation is considered to be one of the least weather sensitive modes 
of transportation and expected to run operations even when the rest of modes are being 
disrupted due to extreme weather conditions. However this seems not to be the real case since 
many delays occur due to extreme conditions of temperature, precipitation, wind and humidity. 
Extreme weather conditions can affect railways in different ways both direct and indirect. First 
of all, they can disturb the infrastructure such as thermal expansion of the tracks in the summer 
or ice and snow during the winter may influence the performance of the rolling stock by 
decreased speeds, derailment or even incapability of running. In addition due to bad weather 
conditions, objects such as trees and more can be on the track and if the authorities are lacking 
time to realize them and collect them, they will cause traffic problems. The railway locomotives 
are affected as well since high crosswinds may affect their stability. Another slightly indirect 
way, extreme weather may affect the crew presence due to inability to reach work (Xia, et al., 
2012). 
Xia et al. attempted to show the impact of weather in the Dutch Railway Network which is one 
of the busiest in EU in terms of train kilometers per track length (more than 20.000 km per 1 
track km per year). The data used for the years 2001-2008 recorded 424.768 disruptions which 
were categorized as technical malfunctions for both infrastructure and rolling stock, vandalism 
and weather. It is estimated that 4% are due to bad weather but since is difficult to attribute 
disruptions to weather with certainty due to indirect influences the information is used only for 
comparison. Information of weather condition for the above years was provided by the Royal 
Dutch Meteorological Institute in addition to the data provided by the Dutch train operator 
concerning delays and cancellation (delays are recorded when are equal or higher than 3 
minutes).  
In order to calculate the impact of weather in railway disruptions a standard linear regression 
model was estimated. Input data used are the days of the week, the months, the levels of wind 
==PAGE:42==
28 
 
gust, snow and precipitation, temperature and the difference of maximum and minimum 
temperature within a day. 
The findings of the study show that wind gusts stronger than 19 m/s, precipitation more than 
22.8 mm and snow thinker than 5 cm have significant and positive effects on disruptions. For 
example when snow cover is thicker than 5 cm the daily number of delays will increase by 20 
whereas when the wind gust is higher than 26 m/s the number will increase to 31 more 
disruptions. When the temperature within a day varies significantly the effects in disruptions is 
also significant. For example in November when the difference in temperature between day and 
night is very high there is an increase of 16 disruptions per day. 
Figure 8, shows the effect of extreme temperature on the numbers of disruptions based on a 
relevant temperature (when the temperature is below 12, the relevant temperature is the 
minimum of the day and when the relevant temperature is above 14 is the highest of the day). 
The dotted lines show the confidence level of 95 %. The graph shows that temperatures lower 
than -5 degrees and higher than 25 degrees are related to a significant higher number of railway

been mentioned already before the critical importance of railway disruptions is that they tend 
to cascade (knock-on effect) and affect the whole network and not only in the point where the

In order to achieve this they performed another regression analysis investigating the relation of

Extreme weather conditions count for the highest percentage of external-extraordinary

operator`s percentages. Hence, is very crucial that weather conditions are taken under 
consideration when planning in the railway industry. 
2.6 Economic Impact of Railway Disruptions 
In order to evaluate the impact of passenger railway service disruptions Xia et al. tried to 
estimate an average cost of delays and cancellations of railways for the passengers in the 
Netherlands. They assume that the average delay the passengers are facing is 6 minutes (where 
delays are recorded when the train is late more than 3 minutes after the scheduled time). 
According to them, travelers who face variation in trains’ travel time, value delays around DKK 
187, 5 per hour, which is estimated based on a combination of the valuation of the time related 
to the delay and the valuation of the expected schedule delay cost and the dislike of the travel 
time variation due to delays. Thus, for an average of 6 minutes the cost is about DKK 18, 75. 
Taking into consideration that several journeys include more than one trip and that due to lost 
connections the delay may be longer, an estimation of 30 minutes delay is assumed to cost an 
average of DKK 30. Taking also into consideration other additional costs due to additional 
number of disruptions passengers may have to face, the daily cost is estimated to be 900.000 – 
DKK 1.350.000 which annually is about DKK 330 – 345 million. In addition to the delay 
==PAGE:44==

welfare loses due to delayed train, passengers’ daily average delay due to cancelled trains is 
estimated to be 20 minutes which in money value is estimated to be DKK 60. Like before due 
to the fact that passengers may take more than one connection per trip the cancellation cost per

average cost per minute of delay is DKK 551 which for the year 2006-07 was translated into 
DKK 7.51 billion.

3. Railway Transportation and Resilience  
3.1 Introduction 
The previous chapter analyzed all common railway disruptions and demonstrated how 
important the effective management of such disturbances is for a passenger service focused 
railway system. These service delays, although small, already cost a significant amount of 
money and time to both the passengers/public and the railway authorities, due to their 
frequency. However, far less likely to happen and unique in their occurrence, but posing an 
immensely greater threat, are cases of extraordinary emergency situations and crises such as 
great scale accidents, natural disasters, terrorists’ attacks and general situations with great 
impacts to catastrophic. Due to their nature, there are no recipes that dictate the proper course 
of action to take in the occurrence of such an event, as the corrective plan that is followed is 
created on the spot to match the particular characteristics of the emergency situation. However, 
there exist approaches at a strategic level, that when adopted can make a huge difference in 
saving human lives, money and preventing the escalation of the situation. Thus, to that front, 
this chapter will introduce terms such as emergency response and crisis management and 
provide theoretical clues on how to implement them effectively. Crisis management is about 
preparing to face well known disastrous events but is also about developing mechanisms and 
enforcing attributes that enable an organization to cope with unexpected and unpredictable 
situations. Subsequently, the concept of resilience is introduced. The term of resilience has 
become widely popular when discussing crisis management. A resilient system, is by definition, 
“a system that has the intrinsic ability to adjust its functioning, prior to or following changes 
and disturbances, so that it can sustain operations even after a major mishap or in the presence 
of continuous stress” (Hollnagel, et al., 2011). Hence, in this study, resilient is a system that is 
able to address effectively the common railway disruptions and that is able to mitigate, absorb 
and recover rapidly from emergency and crisis situations. The last part of this chapter describes 
==PAGE:46==
32 
 
the characteristics that make a railway system resilient and examples from real case studies 
have been used that demonstrate how systems can be reformed to embrace resilience. 
3.2 From Railway Disruptions to Crises 
The recent years, the management of disruptions and emergencies has emerged as a very 
important topic in a wide variety of business sectors including that of transportation, and 
subsequently, railway transportation. That is a consequence of the fact that the welfare of the 
society has increased significantly due to advances in sectors such as technology, health, 
communication, etc. But at the same time, that has led to an increased dependency on the 
effective functioning of these sectors (Labaka, 2013). Furthermore, in the era of information, 
these sectors having reached such a high service level, costumers'/passengers' have largely 
increased. Moreover, in general, unforeseen events and threats such as major failures or 
terrorists attacks can take a business down, but in the case of railway transport the stakes where 
in the case of railway transportation can result in great possibility of fatalities and digression of 
service, make the management of all such events from small service delays to significant 
disasters, a dire necessity.  
Another clue that highlights the need for effective crisis management is that current critical 
infrastructures are becoming increasingly interdependent on each other (Labaka, 2013). For 
example, an electrical power blackout can create a disturbance that will spread and affect 
sectors of health and transportation with inconceivable consequences. Events such as the 
Japanese earthquake and tsunami in 2011 resulted in Fukushima nuclear accident, which forced 
the inhabitants of the surroundings areas to leave their houses due to contamination of the 
environment, in addition to the huge disruptions in the supply chain of major companies 
worldwide due to breakdowns in their production plants (Savitz & Martyn, 2011) ; the eruption 
of the Islandic volcano in 2010 (Eyjafjallajökull) resulted in air traffic crises with hundreds of 
routes canceled and with great disturbances in the worldwide supply chain as well (Levinson, 
2010) proving how dependent are the societies, companies and lives on today’s critical 
infrastructure’s reliability and resilience. 
The railway industry seen as a business has to be aware of all possible threats and risks. Supply 
chain professionals understand how critical the supply chain is to global trade and world 
==PAGE:47==
33 
 
welfare, and it is estimated that the consequences of a disruption to the global supply chain, 
whether from terrorist or natural causes, can cost in the range of hundreds of billions of dollars. 
Terrorists have openly declared their goal of disrupting the world trade and globalization, 
attacking oil production and infrastructures both physically and financially. Natural disasters 
such as hurricanes, floods, tsunamis, and bird flu can all disrupt trade and supply chains (Ritter, 
et al., 2007).  Supply chain professionals are aware of these risks and are actively researching 
emergency plans.  In a parallel way railway professionals understand how important to the 
national but also worldwide welfare railways are. Millions of people and goods are dependent 
and transferred by railway services in a daily base.  
The previous chapter identified all possible passenger railway service disruption scenarios from 
infrastructure related failures, to rolling stock problems and passenger’s delays. It did not refer 
however, to large scale events such as major accidents, natural disasters, terrorist attacks and in 
general events with national or international area of effect. Since all stakeholders, passengers, 
railway authorities and government, demand safety over every railway operation, railways that 
are designed to anticipate, prevent, and mitigate unexpected events, have a solid foundation to 
manage crises and improve their handling of common disruptions.  
The common ‘incidents’ causing passengers’ railway delays as seen in the second chapter 
varied from mechanical problems of the rolling stock, to infrastructure problems, external 
factors and accidents. Apparently, not all of these incidents have the same gravity. An incident 
is defined as “a near miss, that is, events that have no serious consequences but have the 
potential to turn into accidents with catastrophic impacts” (Collis, et al., 2014). For example a 
track failure can cause derailment if not addressed on time. As an accident is defined “an 
unwanted or unintended sudden event or a specific chain of such events which have harmful 
consequences such as collisions, derailments, level-crossing accidents, accidents to persons 
caused by rolling stock in motion, fires etc.” (RSSB, 2008).  
In a higher level, disaster is defined as “a serious disruption of the functioning of a community 
or a society that involves extensive environmental, human, material and economic losses and 
impacts, which usually exceeds the ability of the community or society to cope with by utilizing 
only its own resources” (U.N., 2009). Finally, even higher in the scale of impact are the 
catastrophes were most of the everyday community functions are interrupted (Labaka, 2013).

Lebaka distinguishes between emergencies and crises based on the level of severity (Figure 9). 
Hence, she labels incidents and accidents as emergency situations whereas disasters and

consequence of an unexpected and unpredictable event that suddenly strikes the whole system. 
However, sometimes it can be a result of many combined small incidents which when not coped 
with in a timely manner, can evolve and end up as a crisis.  
Although it is difficult to prevent any kind of a crisis, their impacts can be greatly diminished 
and the recovery period can be reduced significantly if they are managed efficiently. This is 
possible by establishing an appropriate and advanced crisis management strategy in place 
before the crisis occurrence (Mitroff & Anagnos, 2000). However, in order for a system to 
maximize its chances of surviving with minimal damage a catastrophic event, it needs to 
embrace resilience, as explained later in this chapter.

Scale of impact refers to the magnitude of consequences of emergencies and crises. Disasters

focused around the location where the triggering event occurs. 
Probability of inevitability refers to the probability of anticipating and preventing a triggering

hence they require solid pre-disaster planning and preparedness to respond. In the case of 
emergency situations the probability of preventing the occurrence of such events is significantly 
higher. 
The last point of interest regarding emergency and crisis management is the post treatment. 
“Planning the post-disaster reconstruction efforts of a damaged transportation network is a

after the disaster, the resources for reconstruction and recovery are very limited especially when 
it comes to public works and transportation since they are large scale projects. In addition, the 
public sector is known for the bureaucracy of decision making since decisions involve many 
different parties and levels of authorities. In small scale emergencies the impacts are local and 
priority is usually given on the recovery of the system in order to restore the service without 
having to worry about prioritizing projects and resource allocation.

the management of incidents is of critical importance in order to avoid cascading of their effects

3.3 Crisis Management  
Crisis management has evolved in a significant way since its origin. Initially, crisis management 
was about developing emergency response plans to cope with emergency scenarios but later on 
prevention measures were also included in order to avoid the crisis from happening in the first 
place (Labaka, 2013). Crises were perceived as events with predicted consequences, where a 
well-established plan and good training would be enough to respond. This might be the case, in 
some situations, but due to the complexity of the modern society's infrastructure it does not 
apply in crises as defined previously in this study. Disasters are unpredictable, the same with 
their consequences, especially due to cascading effects, thus preparing measures only based on 
==PAGE:51==
37 
 
specific scenarios might not be sufficient. According to Lebaka, crisis management should 
focus on preventing and preparing for all kind of unexpected events rather than just adopting a 
triggering event based approach. The training of the involved stakeholders should be on how to 
make appropriate decisions on critical situations and determining which parties need to be 
involved to deal with a specific problem than performing specific predefined decisions or 
procedures.  
Therefore, today’s crisis management is focused on training workers to make flexible and 
creative decisions. This means that workers are trained in such an adequate way that when an 
unexpected event occurs they will have the ability to quickly assess and comprehend the 
situation, to gather sufficient information and data rapidly, and make suitable decisions under 
stress (Turoff & Van de Walle, 2008). Thus when facing unexpected events with large scale 
impacts the most important element is the well trained and prepared workers rather than only a 
well prepared scenario which is primarily important in events where the level of uncertainty of 
the consequences is low. On the other side, in the case of common incidents’ and accidents’ 
management it could be argued that a well-planned scenario is as much important, since the 
impacts tend to be known hence in order to avoid cascading of the effects, being fast and 
efficient is the primary goal which can be achieved by following predefined procedures. 
In addition, crisis management involves the coordination and integration of third parties. 
External actors such as the government, first responders and society play an important role 
while managing crises. Their preparation is almost the same important as of the organizations 
where the crisis occurs since the consequences are shared. Third parties may have different 
preparation plans and training when if not well communicated between the different parties will 
lead to an ineffective management of the crisis. Thus it is very important that the different 
parties share the same crisis management strategy and training in a way of understanding each 
other’s contribution amidst a great level of communication and integration. 
3.4 Resilience  
One of the dominant theories that has recently emerged in the field of crisis management is 
resilience engineering. The proponents of resilience engineering, assume that human systems 
are able to self-examine, self-reflect, anticipate and learn about their own capacity in order to 
==PAGE:52==
38 
 
respond better to future crises (Woods & Branlat, 2011). “The idea of resilience offers the 
promise of an intuitively plausible, attractive and seemingly attainable strategy to prepare for 
and deal with various types of adversity” (Van Eeten & Boin, 2013). A resilient system 
maintains a high level of performance even when environmental pressures and threats arise and 
uncertainties deepen and is able to bounce back quickly and without much effort in the face of 
unexpected events (Van Eeten & Boin, 2013) . Many definitions are given regarding resilience 
and in many cases they provide different perspectives on the matter. For example, some only 
relate resilience with the capacity of a system to absorb disturbances, respond effectively and 
bounce back to the initial state as soon as possible, thus only the response stage. Other 
perspectives, define resilience as a state where prevention and mitigation actions are taken in 
addition to those of making the system able to respond and recover rapidly and with the least 
of negative effects. 
This study sides with the second perspective and considers that a resilient system is a dynamic 
one, thus is able to absorb disturbances and go back to its normal state rapidly but also through 
continuously evaluating existing knowledge and related experiences (learned lessons) and by 
constantly performing risk mitigation, its ability to absorb them is greatly enhanced over the 
time.   
A visualization of the resilience definition is shown in Figure 11 where: 
 Prevention: the capability of a system to foresee and avoid the occurrence of a crisis 
 Absorption: the capability to cope with an ongoing trouble and reduce the extent of its 
impact  
 Recovery: the capability to recover rapidly and efficiently to the normal state 
==PAGE:53==
39 
 
 
Figure 11: A System's Resilience Lifecycle (Labaka, 2013) 
 
Resilience affects all the stages of a system's lifecycle starting from the prevention of the crisis. 
When a triggering event occurs resilient systems are able to absorb the consequences and avoid 
the cascaded damage, due to preparation activities and training performed in the prevention 
stage. Finally, a resilient system will be able to speed up the recovery stage.   
In order to describe the resilience concepts, some authors define the following characteristics 
as its main features (Tierney & Bruneau, 2007): 
 Robustness: the ability of a system and the system´s elements to resist the impact of a 
triggering event without important degradation or loss of performance 
 Redundancy: the capability of the system to substitute components when functionality 
has been lost or reduced 
 Resourcefulness: the ability to efficiently respond to a crisis by identifying and 
prioritizing problems, initializing solutions and mobilizing the right resources 
 Rapidity: the ability to bounce back to the normal state in a timely manner, reduce the 
negative impacts and avoid future disruptions 
Therefore, speed, resources, alternatives and resistance are the four elements that can 
characterize a resilient system.

3.5 Achieving a Resilient Railway System  
Literature provides many definitions regarding the concept of resilience, however the principles 
are mostly theoretical and there are no practical norms and “recipes” to follow in order to 
achieve resilience. “While the idea of resilience is increasingly popular, empirical research on 
resilient organizations is actually quite rare” (Van Eeten & Boin, 2013).  
A first step in order to be able to analyze and understand how a railway system can be resilient, 
is to identify the domains/dimensions of a resilient system: the technical, organizational, social, 
and economic (Tierney & Bruneau, 2007):    
 The technical domain refers to the physical properties of the system, including the 
ability to resist damage and loss of function, hence signalling, emergency braking, fire 
extinguishing are some examples 
 Organizational resilience refers to the organizations and institutions that manage the 
physical components of the systems, such as the infrastructure manager, the operator 
manager (that operates the trains), first responders etc. This domain includes also the 
organizational capacity, planning, training, leadership, experience, and information 
management that improve crisis related railway organizational performance and 
problem solving. The resilience of an emergency management system, therefore 
includes both the physical components of the system, such as emergency operations 
center, communication technology and emergency vehicles—and the properties of the 
emergency management organization itself—such as the quality of the disaster plans, 
the ability to incorporate lessons learned from past disasters, and the training and 
experience of emergency management personnel. 
 Social resilience refers to the ability of the society to either be more vulnerable or more 
adaptable to disasters, and to reduce the impact of a crisis by helping the responders or 
acting as a volunteer  
 Economic resilience refers to the ability of the entity to face the extra costs rising from 
a crisis and it includes both the ability of the entity to make adjustments and adaptations 
prior to the crisis occurrence and the capability for post-disaster improvisation and 
resource substitution  
==PAGE:55==
41 
 
During the research conducted in this study, many case studies have been reviewed in order to 
find the elements that make a system resilient, especially applied in the case of railways. The 
most obvious observations that were made indicated that both the internal and external 
stakeholders, during emergency situations and crises, are dependent on the effectiveness of their 
prevention, response and recovery stages. Therefore, the resilience level of a railway system is 
divided into two different resilience types, the internal and the external. The internal 
corresponds to the resilience level of the railway authorities whereas the external refers to the 
resilience level of the rest involved stakeholders such as the government, the first responders 
and the society. 
Each type is divided into the different domains it consists of, in which case internal includes 
technical, organizational and economic domains and similarly, the external consists of the 
technical, organizational, social and economic domains (Labaka, 2013). 
A framework developed by Lebaka in 2013, summarizes all the internal and external policies 
that can make a Critical Infrastructure system resilient. This study adopts this framework and 
adjusts it, through correlation with the case study analysis, specifically for the railways since 
the railway infrastructure is considered to be critical as well. According to La Porte, (1996) the 
special characteristics that make an infrastructure critical are: 
 Tightly coupled technically, with complex organizational and management 
requirements that have to deal with little slack in terms of resources and no time for 
pausing and re-learning between steps (Collis, et al., 2014) 
 There is no substitute for the services they provide to the public and there are just a few 
competitors (the more effective is the system the more possible is a monopoly) 
 They are driven to achieve maximum performance 
 Cause of public anxiety when the service is interrupted (the more effective the system 
is the more likely is that anxiety will be caused) 
 They are absolutely necessary for preserving the lifestyle of the society the function in 
As it has been described so far, railways are highly coupled and complex systems, difficult to 
be substituted, expected to perform with high standards which when are not met cause 
passengers dissatisfaction and anxiety.

Internal Technical Resilience:  
a) Railway Infrastructure Design and Construction  
Railway infrastructure needs to provide a high level of safety, in order to diminish the 
possibility of a crisis and its consequences. A safe system includes measures for the prevention 
of a crisis but also a high level of preparedness for absorbing the impacts of a crisis.  Having 
redundant systems increases the resilience since redundancy maintains functioning in the case 
==PAGE:57==
43 
 
of a failure of a component or the system. However, apart from redundancy, the simplicity of 
the system is also very important since complex systems are harder to manage, especially during 
the occurrence of a crisis. Loose coupling among different components is important in order to 
avoid cascades effects especially in critical points of the network. The system needs to be 
examined systemically and with increased insight in relation to future events that might 
alternate the current situation. 
The Eurotunnel between France and Great Britain, for example, consists of two single-bore 
tunnels for rail traffic, known as running tunnels. Both running tunnels have bi-directional 
signaling, diversely routed power suppliers and support systems with built-in redundancy (e.g. 
signaling, ventilation, lighting, radio, cooling and drainage). In addition, the service tunnel, 
located between the running tunnels for most of its length, is used for evacuation and 
maintenance (Collis, et al., 2014). 
b) Railway Infrastructure Maintenance  
Railway infrastructure and rolling stock need to be maintained in order to provide the high level 
of service that they are supposed to. Having a good level of maintenance can help the prevention 
of incidents and reduction of their magnitude. Maintenance can be divided into two categories 
the preventive and the corrective ones. The preventive is the one carried out prior to an incident 
by identifying early warnings and dealing with them and by having a tactical scheduled 
maintenance plan. Corrective maintenance refers to activities carried out after the occurrence 
of an incident, in order to repair damages or strengthen the infrastructure. It is critical that even 
little incidents are detected and managed immediately in order to avoid cascaded effects. Once 
the failure has occurred it is very important to analyze its causes and determine the corrective 
actions so it does not happen again. In a railway system every little failure needs to be analyzed 
in depth in order to avoid the accumulation of failures that might lead to major crises. 
Being proactive when it comes to railway infrastructure and rolling stock maintenance is very 
important in avoiding disruptions due to engineering and mechanical failures. Such failures are 
some of the most common disruption factors with a significant impact on the delay time since 
they can block an entire line.  Hence having a tactical scheduled maintenance or using 
technologies that can predict such failures is mission critical. Fink, et al., (2013) developed an 
algorithmic model that uses historical data from occurred events and is able to predict and thus 
==PAGE:58==
44 
 
prevent such failures – with high priority – in the future. The purpose of the model is to reduce 
the number of rolling stock related disruptions events, to increase service reliability, to reduce 
corrective maintenance actions by substituting them with predictive and reduce the overall 
maintenance costs. Extensions of the model are also suggested according to which i.e. 
additional time is included to anticipate/ react or apply to other systems as well, like the railway 
infrastructure. 
On Friday 18 December 2009, there was a heavy snow in Northern France and in the South of 
England. The harsh weather conditions resulted in a series of failures of Eurostar trains that 
blocked the Channel Tunnel and caused inconvenience and stress for thousands of passengers 
(Collis, et al., 2014). Eurostar’s management had failed to realize that their winterization 
procedures were not sufficient, something shown by the frequent winter failures. “The running 
maintenance procedures did not prove sufficient for the extreme winter weather conditions and 
not enough consideration was given to the fact that certain parts of these trains have suffered 
over the years. It is also clear that the design of the power cars does require high levels of 
ventilation whilst at the same time providing adequate protection for sensitive components, 
especially the electronic circuits. This has been proven to be inadequate” (Eurostar, 2010).  
c) Railway Infrastructure Data Acquisition and Transmission System  
This policy is about equipment, such as sensors, that is able to collect data. This requires, 
primarily, that the critical data to be acquired is determined. In addition, the collected data 
should be monitored and transmitted and visualized in such a way that the railway workers are 
able to detect early warning signals and anticipate undesired situations. Proper information 
systems that save the data and allow further analysis for future improvements are very 
important. 
d) Railway Infrastructure Crisis Response Equipment  
Crisis response equipment refers to emergency equipment that railway authorities ought to have 
in order to face emergencies and crises and which provide the safety for the users. The 
equipment such as the rest of the railway’s infrastructure and components need to be maintained 
and updated properly.

Internal Organizational Resilience 
a) Railway Infrastructure Organizational Procedures for Crisis Management  
RI organizational procedures stand for the preparation and the capability of the railway 
organizations to deal with incidents and crises. This policy includes the coordination of different 
actors within the organization, and external agents such as the police, and predefine the 
responsibilities of each agent during the response phase. The procedures should be well 
communicated and understandable by all the parties and they need to be continuously updated 
based on lessons learned from former events. In addition, this policy includes, the establishment 
of incident or crisis management procedures in order for the organization and the workers to 
know what to do. Guidelines should be given for both the infrastructure operations and the 
workers' activities. For example, in a case of a suicide, the personnel must follow specific 
guidelines to know exactly which external agents to inform, where the traffic should be 
disrupted and the workers to know how to react. Such procedures need to be well communicated 
to all the workers and need to be updated continuously.  
Railway systems need to have an incident reporting system and track all the failures and 
incidents that occur and ensure their proper management. When an incident occurs, it should 
be prioritized based on the level of risk, addressed efficiently and analyze the causes of its 
occurrence. Railways should be constantly preoccupied with failures and each kind of incident 
in order to avoid the occurrence of a severe situation. Furthermore, once the incident has been 
managed it is important to document it and evaluate the actions taken in order to identify the 
best practices that will help in the addressing of future incidents (Labaka, 2013).

However, this might sound as the norm but it does not always apply as it should. On the morning

Therefore it is very important to record and analyze carefully all incidents in order to understand 
and help avoid them in the future. Ouyang et al., (2010), suggests a systematic accident model

technical, human and organizational factors in complex socio-technical systems. In the STAMP 
approach, accidents in complex systems such as railways do not simply occur due to 
independent component failures, rather they occur when external disturbances or dysfunctional 
interactions among system components are not adequately handled by the control system. The 
approach, indicated that the sequence of dysfunctional interactions were: misunderstanding 
about the speed limit regulation between the different railway bureau; when communication 
was restored the scheduler in the responsible station had to inform every train that the speed 
limit had changed; when train T195 approached that station the assistant did not confirm the 
actual speed limit with the driver (integrated vehicle control); the driver passed the speed limit 
signs beside the rail show without paying attention to them; the train derailed. The accident of 
T195 happened therefore due to failure of all different source of information. The system can 
be assumed that as quite redundant in terms of alternatives but all of them failed due to bad 
communication and luck of information involving human errors. Figure 12, proves exactly that 
despite the redundant sources of information the speed limit to the driver who was the 
responsible for correcting the speed, the system failed due to miscommunication and 
consequence human error and the train derailed.

further damage. In the example of the Chinese accident, it was argued that the driver would not 
check the speed limit to the fear that if he would delay, he would be punished by having his 
salary cut. This could be avoided if people would firstly be punished if they would avoid any 
safety regulation and then if they would be late. Or maybe instead of punishment, 
encouragement can have better results than fear in many cases.   
 On the other side, under the same thoughts, successful procedures should be also recorded in 
order to be added in the organizational procedures and improve the overall resilience of the 
system.  
Crisis managers need to be well prepared and trained in the occasion of emergency situations. 
They have to be highly skilled and make appropriate decisions. They need to be able to detect 
an incident fast, communicate the corresponding entity and respond properly and effectively. 
Crisis managers need to be able to anticipate events so that to know what to expect, to pay 
attention to signals so that they know what to look for and finally to know what action to take 
(Collis, et al., 2014). In case of unexpected and unpredicted situations where no previous 
experience has been acquired managers must develop sense-making capacity and use their 
knowledge in innovative ways to face the new problems (Labaka, 2013)  
Early warning signals for the managers, in the Eurotunnel incident, it could be said that were, 
apart from the heavy snow, the closure of motorways and the fact that the port was closed due 
to strikes. These factors indicated that Eurotunnel (infrastructure manager) and Eurostar (the 
railway operator) were under excess demand and that managers, personnel and emergency 
authorities would have to be alert for unexpected incidents. However, authorities failed to notice 
these clues. In addition, when the first train breakdown occurred, they failed to perceive the 
evolution of a systemic problem and used the resources exclusively for the first failure without 
taking into consideration the cascading effects that such a failure might have on the tunnel.  
On 23 October 2004, a series of several major earthquakes rocked the Chuetsu region of Niigata 
along the sea-of-Japan coast (Ogura, 2006). The response time of the railway authorities was 
incredible fast – within 4 minutes a response headquarter was established in the Japan Railways 
East Head Office, an indication of how well they were prepared (emergency plan) in a case of 
==PAGE:63==
49 
 
an emergency of that scale. Their first step was to call all executive managers and to confirm 
the state of the railways.  When a train derailment was confirmed and the state of the passengers 
(safety first) was covered, an emergency response meeting was performed were the executive 
director demanded from the rest of the managers’ crew, to be frank and open about everything 
they knew or did not know. According to Ogura, that was the right approach, and set the stage 
for excellent rapport of all the operations during the response and recovery period. The 
president of the company flew from London the moment he heard about the earthquake (did 
not even wait to see if there were any accidents or emergency situations) and arrived in the 
headquarters just few hours later boosting up the motivation of managers and personnel, by 
being there when he was needed in order to guide and empower his people. One of the executive 
managers was directly put in charge of the Field Response Operations to in order to direct and 
supervise the operations of recovering as fast as possible.  
In addition to the crisis managers, operators and other railway workers such as drivers, platform 
personnel, dispatchers, conductors etc. need to be well trained for emergency situations and 
undergo seminars and emergency exercises. However, since in extraordinary and unpredicted 
situations the solutions might be unknown, railway personnel need to have some freedom in 
decision making which implies that they also have to be educated in being flexible and 
adaptable to different situations. In the Eurotunnel incident passengers trapped in the trains 
inside the tunnel prolonged the evacuation process due to the fact that they were allowed to 
evacuate with their luggage and carry them for about 400 m and on a staircase. This indicated 
that the training the personnel had undergone was lacking and not up to the task. In addition, 
passengers later  complained that the Eurostar’s passenger information had been insufficient 
and that the train´s crew approach to passenger’s management was somehow deficient (Collis, 
et al., 2014) . On the Japanese earthquake on the other side, the derailed train carrying 151 
passengers and five other trains with even more passengers, emergently stopped between 
stations were safely evacuated by train crew and employees that rushed from the stations and 
the depots.  The evacuation routes were varied, with some passengers having to climb a shaft 
from a tunnel and others having to go down to rumps. All the employees had undergone a 
rigorous crisis-response training and their professionalism kept passengers calm during all the 
evacuation operations.

All the railway personnel need to be highly alert and aware of the current situation and able to 
notice early warning signs in order to be proactive and predict or anticipate any possible 
incident before it happens. 
In late June 1998, a major storm event in the Adirondack Mountains, New York resulted in a 
great damage of the Canadian Pacific Railway (CPR) Canadian Mainline, with 14 major 
embankment failures and washouts, numerous minor failures, severe foundation scour and a 
major mainline derailment (Palynchuk, et al., 2000). The D&H (the train operator company) 
personnel responded immediately to the derailment site and their primary concern was the 
safety of the passengers and the residents of the surrounding area. They immediately hired a 
contractor to clean up spilled diesel fuel from the derailed locomotive, another contractor to 
remove the derailed cars and locomotive and another one to clear the access road to the 
derailment site from woods. On the other side, CPR’s goal was to restore rail service as soon 
as possible so they contacted an external consultant and expertise in the technical and support 
field, in order to assess the damage and make the best engineering decisions in trying to 
reconstruct the main line. CHA and CPR arrived to the site immediately and remained there for 
the whole duration until the site was completed two months later. CPR assembled a highly 
qualified team of engineers, construction managers, contractors and suppliers. They quickly 
established a War Room, as they called it, at a local motel as the base of operations, setting up 
telephones end emails, and at the same time they provided personnel with cell phones and 
beepers to enhance communication. Their first step was to identify all the failure areas, to 
determine the likely cause and also document conditions after the failures in order to design the 
repairs. All these tasks were executed in parallel for faster completion. In addition, the failure 
areas were documented with extensive photographs and information in order to be analyzed for 
future use. The storm was analyzed in detail in combination with hydraulic analysis of the land 
in order to understand the events leading up to the failures and to learn for the future how to 
avoid the same consequences. 
This case study proves that effective communication and teamwork, the coordination of 
external parties and the high skilled emergency managers were the major factors in getting the 
job done in a timely and cost-effective manner.  “The project was recognized for its innovation 
and ingenuity in assessing the damages quickly and implementing a cost-effective 
reconstruction program over a large area in a remote region in real time. It was a true

demonstration of how to apply time tested engineering principles to help make informed

4. Railway Disruption Management  
4.1 Introduction 
Disruption management as defined in this report refers to incidents and common accidents’ 
management. Some of the incidents are predictable and can be prevented through appropriate 
measures while others occur unexpectedly but they can also be prevented or managed 
effectively if there are routine operations and the railway authorities are properly prepared and 
organized.  
Therefore disruption management has to be part of the business operations and requires the 
inheritance of resilience attributes in order to be adequately prepared to handle the diversity 
of disruptions. In this chapter, some of the disruptions analyzed in chapter 2 will be addressed 
by policies described in chapter 3. The target of disruption management is to provide a 
constantly safe railway service which will be able to: 
 Anticipate and prevent incidents when that is possible 
 To respond fast to  incidents and emergencies  
 To decrease the operational downtime caused by disruptions 
Each one of these targets require that a passenger focused railway system will: 
 Have established an incident management authority which will focus on current 
operations improvements in order to prevent disruptions, when that is not possible 
 Focus will be given in how to improve current processes during the response stage 
 To provide instant availability of relevant real-time information during the disruption 
and emergency situation 
 To improve collaboration and information sharing between all relevant stakeholders  
==PAGE:70==
56 
 
In order to achieve these, it is required that: 
 A constant evaluation and improvement of how current processes 
 A common operational picture is provided, which enables instant insight in important 
information such as the location of the incident, the state of the situation in real time, 
important actions to be taken, people in charge to be alerted and solution available 
In this chapter, based on what has been discussed so far, a holistic disruption management 
framework will be proposed and analyzed. The rational thought process followed is based on 
the risk management field and therefore that will be introduced first in this chapter.  
4.2 Risk Management  
“Risk Management is the arrangements within the daily activities of an organization to discover, 
understand and either accept or modify risk when decisions are being made and to anticipate, 
detect and respond if changes occur later” (Estall, 2015). An effective risk management 
approach is when risks are identified and understood the moment they are created, the risks are 
acceptable to the organization and the risk management strategies are efficient.  
The benefits of an effective risk management can be distinguished into direct and indirect 
(Estall, 2015): 
 Direct benefits  
 it improves the likelihood that organizational success can be achieved through 
more successful pursuit of opportunity, improved efficiency, fulfilment of 
obligations, and better detection and response to change 
 undesirable consequences – such as injury, disruption or loss of organizational 
viability – therefore become less likely 
 Indirect benefits 
 The continual application of the risk management process to decision – making 
across the organization, combined with the organizational insight gained 
through the design and ongoing enhancement of the risk management, improves 
overall organizational performance 
==PAGE:71==
57 
 
In 2009, ISO 31000:2009 published a new and globally accepted standard for risk management, 
Figure 13 (Management, 2010). The purpose of this risk management framework is to provide 
guideline about how to discover, understand and decide whether and how to modify risk.  
 
 
Figure 13: ISO 31000:2009 Risk Management Framework 
 
The first step in risk management (RM), establishing the context, refers to the establishment of 
the RM objectives. Each RM process might have a different objective which can be safety of 
operations, a financial target or something else. In addition to the objectives in this stage all the 
features inside and outside the organization that might be relevant to the decision should be 
considered as well as all stakeholders. Moreover, criteria should be set for measuring and 
accepting risk. 
The first step in risk assessment, risk identification is about discovering all potential risks that 
might affect the objectives. Questions regarding, if, why, how and when should be answered at 
this stage. The second step, risk analysis, is about understanding the scale of the effect that 
every risk has on the objectives. Questions like the consequences, the likelihood of the 
==PAGE:72==
58 
 
consequences and the possible modifiers, aka controls, are addressed at this point. Last step in 
risk assessment is to decide whether the risk will be accepted or it should be modified. 
Finally, risk treatment, refers to the modification of the risk if is not accepted in the previous 
stage.  
The two supporting activities refer to the communication and consultation of all interested 
stakeholders and monitoring and review is about constantly evaluating all decisions made and 
looking for a change. The process is dynamic and responsive to change since new risks may 
appear and because lessons are learned in any stage and in order to facilitate continual 
improvement of the organization.  
All businesses apply risk management, even the ones that do not realize. Railways, traditionally 
have applied risk management processes mostly regarding to the safety of the operations and 
the railways in general. Recently, however, an increasing number of railway organizations are 
applying risk management in all of their activities, like for example the railway infrastructure 
manager of New Zealand’s railways (KiwiRail, 2013).  
Railway on-time performance can be one of the objectives in the risk management process as 
shown before in this case. Possible causes of disruptions will have to be identified, analyzed, 
evaluated and then treated Network Rail have actually started using risk management approach 
in managing delays (NetworkRail, 2013). 
4.3 Disruption Management Framework 
Figure 14, gives an overview of the proposed framework which was developed by the detailed 
application of the strategies introduced in chapter 1 with core principles from chapter 3 on the 
basis of a risk management approach as explained before, with focus and specialization on the 
railway disruption management.  
The objective of the disruption management as shown here, is the high performance of the 
railway services. For this, three important stages are identified: 
 Prevention and preparedness 
 Response, and 
==PAGE:73==
59 
 
 Recovery 
The prevention stage refers to all the measures taken in order to prevent delays and incidents 
from happening. This starts with a disruption-risk analysis, part of which can be based on 
historical data since most of the incidents have been seen before (or very similar cases). After 
the analysis, an evaluation of the disruptions' impacts, such as time of delay, cost of the delay 
and likelihood of the disruption, is performed and the “modifiers” are identified in order to see 
if the cost is beneficial to prevent it. Consultation and collaboration with relevant stakeholders 
can be very useful in order to find the best possible solution. Of course, it is not feasible to 
prevent every disruption and in that case is important to be prepared to respond fast and 
efficiently in order to mitigate the consequences of a disruption and restore service. For this, 
emergency plans have to be established beforehand, staff should be trained and be highly 
knowledgeable and plans should be communicated to all related stakeholders. 
 
Figure 14: A Holistic Passenger Railway Disruption Management Framework 
 
The response stage represents the processes and actions followed to deal with the triggering 
event-disruption. It makes sense that the quicker the reaction time is the higher the probability 
to reduce the duration and impacts of the disruption. Emergency response is interrelated with 
emergency planning and their success is dependent of how well the emergency plan was 
established and how efficiently the response was performed. Dispatching strategies and 
==PAGE:74==
60 
 
alternative plans described in chapter 1 are part of the response stage as well and also dependent 
on an effective planning. Finally what is very important at this stage is that critical business 
functions should resumed, without getting affected by the turbulences caused by the disruption. 
Recovery stage refers to the stage during which the problems are being addressed (by utilizing 
multiple ways depending on the problem), hence it is the continuity of the response stage, until 
problems get solved and the traffic is restored to the original timetable or at least to the revised 
timetable in case of modifications. Furthermore, at this phase processes, controls and resources 
should be available immediately after the triggering event in order to ensure that the services 
are not affected or that can still be provided with slight alterations, and are monitored so as any 
new unexpected delays are treated as well. Re-scheduling is significant during the recovery 
stage in order to provide the best possible timetable for the disrupted service.  
Finally all the stages and actions should be constantly monitored and reviewed and real time 
feedback should be requested from stakeholders involved in order to improve the processes 
while they are performed. At the same time, customers should be kept informed regarding any 
important information that concerns them. 
It should be noted here that risk management is a continuous process performed in every stage 
described above, and while the main objective, punctuality, remains the same, in order to 
achieve it sub-objectives are set and required to be fulfilled (i.e. prevention of infrastructure 
related failures or improvement of reaction time etc.). 
4.3.1 Report and Analysis 
The report of a disruption is highly important for many reasons. Railway companies need to 
document the reason of delays in order to estimate the punctuality and reliability rate and 
present them to the public authorities. This is a main reason why railway companies are keeping 
track of the disruptions that exceed the delay threshold. Indeed this is very important reason, 
however, it is just one among many others.  
Initially, all incidents from smaller to bigger should be documented in order to keep track of 
them and have available the possible source of empirical data. Moreover, documentation of 
incidents is used to evaluate their treatment so that lessons can be learned for the next similar 
==PAGE:75==
61 
 
incident if something went wrong or even to improve current operations by studying successful 
cases of management.  
Secondly, one of the resilience principles suggests that every incident even the smallest must 
be recorded since it might signify the beginning of a much bigger incident and cause problems 
if not addressed in time. As an example, a small incident with a door not closing in a train, even 
if it does not create a delay it should be reported by the driver because this might be a sign that 
the door is about to fail etc.  
In addition, analysis of the data related to disruptions is a valuable source of information in 
order to gain a better understanding of the disruptions. Information regarding location, time, 
season of the year, weather conditions and cause can be an invaluable ally in the prevention and 
management of the incidents. Identified patterns may lead to the solution for many cases. 
Interactions between entities such as location and train can show weaknesses and bottlenecks 
of the system. Measures to prevent future disruptions can be easily conceived like this. 
For example, according to Danish Traffic Center (2015) observations related to suicides, show 
that people usually commit suicide either during Christmas holiday or during the first days of 
spring. This might be an indicator for increasing railway surveillance through cameras and 
trained people during those periods. Another opinion regarding suicides is that they usually 
happen chronologically close or at the same location because if one occurs then the rest tend to 
follow the same pattern. That is also a sign that increased surveillance and attention should be 
given after a suicidal incident in order to have a better chance in preventing more from 
happening.  
Another example is concerning data analysis in correlation with weather conditions. As shown 
in the second chapter, extreme weather conditions and climate change can cause further 
problems in the railroads and trains. The result from such as analysis can provide useful 
information, in order to develop strategies to address the potential climate and weather change 
impact on the railroads sector, including efforts for mitigation, adaptation or railroad system 
operations and infrastructure, and railroad planning under new risks like the one of climate 
change (Rossetti, 2002).  
==PAGE:76==
62 
 
Another reason for analyzing data regarding disruptions is benchmarking both internal and 
external operations in order to measure the performance of the railway organizations under 
certain circumstances and in order to compare the same operations in other companies.  
A continuous reporting system, as a consequence, requires the need of an intelligent information 
system which will be able to prioritize incidents based on the data input and will also highlight 
potential patterns. In addition, it requires constant monitoring from experienced staff that can 
take these information, assess the situation and take action.  
In fact, information systems already exist that are trying to treat such issues especially when it 
comes to the railway infrastructure. “The complexity of today’s railway sector imposes high 
and often conflicting demands for Rail Infrastructure Managers; the management of the railway 
network requires advanced tools and methods to aid humans in managing them effectively. This 
problem has been addressed the recent years by the introduction and application of an Asset 
Management System (AMS) for the railway infrastructure” (Jovanovic, 2005).  
The most widely used definition about Asset Management is the one provided by PAS 55-1 
(International Standard): “Systematic and coordinated activities and practices through which an 
organization optimally manages its assets and their associated performance, risks and 
expenditures over their lifecycle for the purpose of delivering the organization’s business 
objectives”. Therefore, some of the issues that asset management deals with are infrastructure 
inventory, condition monitoring, life cycle management and in order to provide high-quality 
decision-support, for each one these categories, requires a large amount of data to be available 
and carefully selected and propagated to the railway infrastructure asset management system. 
Hence, the quality and reliability of the transferred data represents one of the most crucial issues 
and keys to success of an AMS implementation and utilization in the railway infrastructure. 
Railway infrastructure (RI) life cycle management includes the management of RI objects' 
conditions (degradation modeling), life cycle management principles, developing and applying 
cost-effective maintenance and renewal strategies, deciding between maintenance and renewal, 
life cycle costing and managing risks.  Maintenance strategies include track maintenance. The 
resulting requirements for improvements in the performance of the railway infrastructure mean 
that the requirements made upon the track are becoming more demanding. In order to provide 
a more cost-effective track and meet the demands, it is essential to be able to improve the current

4.3.2 Prevention  
In the prevention stage risk mechanisms “controls” are established in order to prevent incidents 
from happening. It is common place that prevention is better than cure. The methodology used

Infrastructure and Rolling Stock Failures 
Preventive maintenance is a significant preventive action which when not performed adequately 
can lead to failure of infrastructure or rolling stock, leading up to delays or, worse, to greater 
incidents and accidents.  As shown in the second chapter, infrastructure related problems and 
rolling stock mechanical issues have the largest share of delay causes. As it is mentioned in the 
NEA report, (2003) a benchmarking between European countries and Japan showed that Japan 
is considerably more punctual and that is due to rare failures in the service, which is not 
achieved by reliance on modern exotic techniques but by following simple principles of tested 
and time proven robust systems, which include preventative replacement of parts, double or 
triple back-ups systems and better checks and controls when is about rolling stock and by simple 
and robust , with preventive maintenance of vulnerable aspects such as signals and points which 
is finished punctually, when it comes to the infrastructure.

heavily used or certain parts that are more sensitive need to be monitored systematically. In 
every infrastructure aspect the cost of maintenance should be contrasted with its benefits in 
order to achieve the most cost efficient maintenance scheme. In fact there is a sweet spot

services before their economic expansion (UIC, 2015).That is the essence of asset management

strategic exploitation of assets such as yards, siding or tracks in case of disruptions and 
bottlenecks, however this is out of the scope of this study. 
Passengers Delays 
Passenger related delays, even if not significant in terms of duration, create a ripple in the 
system that if not absorbed can trigger a chain of connected delays which are accumulated and 
pose a much bigger threat than the triggering event alone. Passengers’ delays may seem hard to 
prevent, however, taking measures that deal with their causes can lead to a significant reduction 
of their frequency and duration. 
So the first step to preventing passenger delays more effectively is to identify the situations that 
cause delays. The main situations that lead up to delays are the accumulation of many 
passengers in a relative small area and the bottlenecks that are created or time that is lost while 
passengers navigate through the station. Then, the next step is to identify and examine the 
causes of these situations. On that front, examining the passengers' behavior and transportation 
habits can be of a big help. Many railway companies already study behavioral patterns of their 
customers in order to improve their services. In addition, the map of all the routes passengers 
use to navigate through the station should be carefully examined, so as to detect choke points, 
and should be correlated with the arrival timings of the trains in order to discover moments of 
passenger's congestion that lead to bottlenecks and delays. At last, after investigation is 
==PAGE:82==
68 
 
performed, an appropriate solution should be carefully designed and implemented as soon as 
possible. Even if the behavior of the system cannot be alternated in order to induce the desired 
results, other options exist that can alternate the behavior of the passengers. These methods 
include voice records, messages on the screens and posters that can be used to guide people 
through the station. 
As for an example of a common situation that creates delays is when passengers are boarding 
or deboarding massively from a specific door instead of being evenly distributed along the 
platform. The reason why this may happen varies but here are some clues to why this might 
happen and how to address it from examining passengers' behavior and station topography: 
 There is a limited part of the station with roof to protect from rain, cold or heat 
 There are limited seats located on a specific place 
 There is only one way to access the platform and people get stack on that part 
And an example of a real world case where successful evaluation of the situation led to a far 
more efficient system is as follows. In Copenhagen, a measure which dramatically decreased 
the passenger related delays by 40%, was the creation of bike waiting spots in addition to trains 
stopping at a specific location every time. This way passengers with bikes did not have to 
navigate through the station looking for the bike’s wagon. Similar measures can be applied for 
other cases as well such as ramp for the handicapped people etc.  
Suicides 
According to the European Railway Agency (ERA, 2014), accidents costs railways a significant 
amount of money which corresponds to the economic impact of fatalities, serious injuries, costs 
of delays, cost of material damage to rolling stock or infrastructure and environmental costs. In 
suicides, fatality and injury costs may be omitted since are due to human fault however the rest 
remain. Every year close to 3000 suicide fatalities occur in EU railways and is estimated that 
every year there is an increase of 3 % (70% of all railway fatalities). As for example, in 
Denmark, 1n 2012 there were about 75 suicides out of 90 person collisions which in total cost 
to the Danish society of DKK 236 million. In the Netherlands the number of people committing 
suicide every year is about 200 whereas in Switzerland, an average of 1300-1400 suicides are 
recorded annually (one of the principal reasons of mortality among young people aged 15-44 
and the second biggest cause of death for teenagers 10-19) (TheLocal, 2014). Apart from the 
==PAGE:83==
69 
 
physical costs, suicides have other costs that cannot be quantified easily such as the shock of 
train drivers, railway staff and passengers facing the incident. 
Despite the high number of railway suicides no measures had been taken until recently where 
some railway companies decided to work on mechanisms that help prevent suicides. The Swiss 
Federal Railways despite the significantly large numbers of suicides decided in 2014 to install 
posters in station advertising a telephone number to help troubled individuals.  
The high consequences of suicides and measures to prevent them were systematically studied 
for the first time in 2014 from a group funded by European’s Union’s Seventh Framework 
Program for research, technological development and demonstration (RESTRAIL, 2014). The 
study provided a detailed approach of how to look for suggestions and how to implement them.  
Having as a main objective suicide prevention, the first step is to analyze the problem. 
Information regarding location, part of the railway system, time of the day, season of the year, 
and information about characteristics regarding the people committing suicide is important to 
be investigated in order to gain a sufficient understanding of the problem. On the other hand, 
stakeholders related to the problem such as the railway companies, rescue services, police and 
specialized services need to be identified and the costs and  consequences of the incidents need 
to be taken into account in order to find potential partners and resources.  In addition, an analysis 
of the existing measures – if any – and their effectiveness, needs to be performed.  
Once the target situation has been analyzed and understood in depth, the second step is to select 
the potential measures or the combination of them that are applicable by all stakeholders. 
International experience can be used as well. The potential mechanisms need to be evaluated in 
terms of cost and expected impact and questions such as where, when and how need to be 
answered at this stage as well. Potential measures can be: 
 Suicide patrols 
 Fencing 
 Detection and surveillance systems  
 Lighting devices to increase visibility 
 Smart cameras to identify unusual behavior 
 Posters in stations  
 Campaigns to prevent suicides  
==PAGE:84==
70 
 
 Training of staff to investigate suspicious people, etc. 
The next step, is to select the measures to be implemented, to define and communicate 
adequately all the responsibilities to the different stakeholders, to estimate the cost, ask for 
funding, perform all the necessary preparations finally implement them.  
The lists with measures to prevent suicides is quite long. What is important is that railway 
authorities in cooperation with external stakeholders work on the solutions together. Evaluation 
of the effects should be recorded in addition to the costs and unpredicted problems in order to 
improve the processes for the future. Despite the fact that measures preventing suicides have 
only recently started to be implemented hence there are no many figures to see their effects, the 
British Network Rail Company, claim that measures started in 2013 had positive effects in the 
number of suicides and costs related to suicides (about a quarter decrease from 2012) (Meikle, 
2013).  
Trespassing, vandalism, weather and other external issues  
A similar risk management approach as demonstrated in the case of suicides could be followed 
in order to prevent other disruptions such as trespassing accidents (800 trespassing fatalities 
annually in EU, not including injuries and no injuries), cable theft, vandalism etc. In Australia 
an electronic sensor detecting vapor from spray paint and permanent marker pens was 
developed to alert police officers for the ongoing violation and give them a chance to catch the 
vandals. According to the CEO of Sidney Trains, the technology was so successful and effective 
although in early adoption stages, that allowed the transition from a removing graffiti strategy 
which cost taxpayers up to $34 million per year to a preventive one that eliminates the problem 
in its creation (RailwayGazette, 2015).    
Extreme weather conditions can be predicted in collaboration with national meteorological 
centers in order for the prevention mechanisms to be ready (there are many measures and 
technologies developed against lighting strikes, snow and ice, etc.).   
It is therefore obvious, that there are measures to prevent incidents or at least large portion of 
them which provide profitable solutions and performance benefits. Risk management and 
coordination with the external stakeholders is essential and should play a major role in the 
design prevention schemes.

4.3.3 Emergency Plan - Emergency Response  
“In addition to the need to prevent incidents in the first place, it is vital to have clear strategies 
to contain and mitigate emergencies, and then work to restore normal service as quickly as 
possible” (Golightly, et al., 2013). Therefore, emergency planning is about preparing for the 
possibility of a disruptive event in order to reduce its effects when it happens and to develop 
capabilities for effective response and recovery of the system. Emergency response refers to 
the initial response to a disruptive event and is about stabilizing the situation, preventing further 
deterioration and getting the service back to its normal operating condition as soon as possible 
(QueenslandGovernment, 2014). 
During the preparedness stage, plans of action addressing various known possible scenarios 
should be designed and activated, so that can be employed in case of an unplanned disruption. 
As it was mentioned in the previous chapter, emergency plans cannot account for every possible 
case, despite the fact that common disruptions are identified situations that repeat themselves 
in a very similar and predictable way. For that reason, adequate and continuous training of the 
staff and related authorities is required. Training people to follow well thought predefined 
procedures in the occurrence of a possible scenario will allow for an effective management of 
the disruption and minimization of its consequences. However, additional training that will 
enable the staff involved in the management of an incident that are not prepared to face, to 
evaluate the situation and quickly take an appropriate decision is of equal importance.  
According to Golightly et al., (2013) emergency planning is a complex process and when a 
disruption does occur, various aspects of rail socio-technical system come to the fore.  First of 
all, as they were classified in the second chapter, slow moving and minor incidents that can be 
treated without human intervention on the site of the problem, are usually treated by dispatching 
strategies as shown in the first chapter. The success of the response reaction depends mostly on 
the pre-planned strategy chosen, the skills of the dispatcher and the communication between 
the infrastructure manager and the train operator companies. However, during major incidents, 
such as rolling stock or infrastructure failures and fatalities, the complexity increases 
exponentially since other stakeholders are involved and more resources are required while at 
the same time the rest of the operations have to continue their normal course in order to not 
create additional problems.

Incident Detection  
The issues addressed at this step of planning should be regarding standard procedures to be 
followed immediately after an incident occurs. In most of the European countries, including 
Denmark, the traffic control center needs to get informed for every incident that occurs the 
moment it occurs. For example the driver will contact them if there is an obstacle in the track 
or the signaler will if they see indicators of technical failures on their display (Golightly, et al., 
2013).  Then the traffic control center forwards the information to the most suitable authority 
==PAGE:87==
73 
 
to deal with the particular incident. So it is important to establish, in the emergency planning 
phase, which authority should be informed first for every kind of incident. 
Incident Assessment & Diagnosis 
At the development of this step, decisions should be made concerning who will be responsible 
for diagnosing the problem and sent to the location, what skills are required, what training 
should they go through, and how many staff have to be trained. Decisions regarding the 
personnel’s location should also be made in order to effectively cover the whole system and 
minimize the response time. In addition, based on statistical analysis, locations which exhibit a 
particular pattern or high frequency of major incidents should be prioritized. Finally, there 
should be planned and created special routes for particular locations of high importance with 
difficult access so that the staff will be able to reach them. Incident assessment and diagnosis 
should generally include the exact cause of disruption, the estimated duration, the exact location 
and the estimated severity. This very step, regardless of the severity of the incident, should be 
carried out with the utmost care so as to reduce possibilities of mistakes and re-assessment of 
the problem that could have serious consequences and compromise the success of the operation 
as the decision for the course of action is largely dependent on this step. 
Stakeholder’s Information  
This step concerns mostly the way that information regarding an incident is propagated to the 
rest of stakeholders from the infrastructure manager, exchanged among the infrastructure 
manager, the train operators and parties outside railways such as mechanics and first responders 
like the police, paramedics etc. The objective is that all interested parties are informed timely 
and accurately of the incident. Information systems which alert all relevant stakeholders could 
provide significant help. The British railways are already using such a technology (NexusAlpha, 
2015) and Dutch authorities are trying to achieve something similar too (NICE, 2014). Such 
net-centric working technology, should be easy to use and messages should be clear and 
informative on the severity of the situations. The person in charge of this task has to also been 
defined in the emergency plan.  
 
 
==PAGE:88==
74 
 
Choice of Recovery Action & Coordination and Planning of Action  
During these steps the different stakeholders need to cooperate to reach an effective decision 
quickly. This implies that the action plans are determined and that all parties have the same 
understanding of the situation and they know their responsibilities and roles. This requires a 
well communicated plan and adequate training of the different stakeholders. It is no coincidence 
that railway staff in Japan undergo monthly trainings specialized in punctuality assurance 
(NEA, 2003). External authorities that are not familiar with how things work in railways, should 
have followed training and exercises on the field in order to integrate properly with the rest of 
the team. A key element in the success of this step is the accessibility that railway authorities 
have in the suggested actions and solutions which should remain complete at all times. The 
moment the action decision is made all parties should work together as a team, however, 
everyone should be responsible for his own area of expertise and should facilitate the rest. 
Especially when it comes to the national police, due to the strength they have in decision 
making, they need to understand that their understanding and compliance with the railways 
special characteristics is essential to the overall good of the society. As for example, in case of 
a suicide, police can rely in camera records, if no further suspicion exists, instead of extensively 
investigating the location, since considerable time can be saved. During coordination and 
planning, it is important that resources (either materials or human) are available or at least easy 
to reach. This probably means for railways that they are responsible for their own mechanical 
work, that they keep a strategic number of stand-by staff whereas in case of contractors, 
arrangements should be made in order to receive help in every kind of emergency situation. 
Finally, regarding spare parts and materials, railway authorities should also plan their location 
of storage and manage their availability so that they are easy to retrieve when needed and in 
sufficient quantities. 
Action’s Beginning – Action’s End 
During the period that the corrective action is taking place, it is essential that the procedures 
applied are fast and efficient. Having knowledgeable, experienced and trained staff is the one 
requirement. The other is for staff to be highly motivated and highly valuing their task of 
restoring the service to its normal operating condition. The first one can be addressed through 
practicing and training, the second one is more difficult. It is is higher manager’s responsibility 
==PAGE:89==
75 
 
to inspire and provide incentive for the staff so as to boost their motivation. And it is the HR 
department's responsibility to hire highly motivated and dedicated people for key positions. 
System able to operate again 
After the actions and corrective work is performed the railway authorities need to evaluate if 
the conditions are sufficient for the service to start again. If every one of the previous steps has 
been performed successfully, rationally no more problems will exist. However, mistakes can 
happen or successive failures may require assessment, diagnosis and addressing all over again, 
until the system is entirely ready. In this step, there should also be an estimation of how much 
time for the system to be restored and continue with normal operation.   
Follow up and evaluate 
Highly important and often overlooked is the evaluation and documentation of the procedures 
that were performed in order to self-reflect, learn lessons and improve the planning preparing 
for the next time it will come into use. One of the best ways to do that is by letting the 
participating crew fill individual reports regarding key points of the operation, identifying 
deficiencies and suggesting improvements, as discussed above.  
The hypothetical flowchart also raises questions regarding if all the steps follow a linear 
execution or if there is any parallelism involved. 
Finally, it should be mentioned that in terms of organizational structure, an entity/department 
should be assigned to specialize and focus on the operational performance of the railways. That 
would include all the steps that have been described here and that are necessary for reducing 
delays and their consequences, including the preparation of emergency planning. Only if 
disruption management is integrated into business processes, a structural treatment of the 
railway disruption problems will be achieved. 
As a conclusion, emergency plan and response function are strongly interrelated. In order for 
the plan to be successful, the response must strictly adhere to key predefined steps of the plan, 
and in order to respond efficiently plans should have carefully considered all the elements and 
steps that are crucial to the success of the response, Figure 20.

4.3.4 Passenger Information  
A passenger focused railway service needs to think constantly about the passengers. While 
passengers value service punctuality and reliability the most, during disruptions their highest 
requirement is information, in order to be able to determine the best alternative way to reach 
their destination on time.  Especially, nowadays, due to the outspread of technology and social 
media, passengers have increased expectations for precise and timely information. They need 
to know the cause of the delay, the estimated duration and they need to know it the soonest 
possible after the event occurs. When diagnosis is fast, the time required for the emergency 
response is determined and the disruption's duration can be accurately estimated in order to give 
the railway authorities the ability to inform the passengers quickly and precisely about the 
situation.  
Information should be provided by different streams, starting from the deployment of personnel 
in the control traffic centers who during a disruption will be specifically working on information 
circulation and updating the passengers. Websites and platform boards should be constantly 
updated and the use of special app notifications can be employed for direct access to critical 
information through smartphones. Railway staff should also be deployed in the stations in order 
to be available for providing information regarding changes, possible alternative transportation 
==PAGE:91==
77 
 
solutions and reassurance. Finally, static information such as maps and guides to lead the 
passengers to appropriate actions should be situated in key locations of every station.   
Passenger’s Perceived Waiting Time 
At this point, in addition to the train delay and the actual passenger delay, the definition of the 
perceived waiting time should be introduced so as to gain a more complete understanding of 
the issue. Perceived waiting time refers to the passenger’s perception of the time spent while 
waiting for a particular service (Cheng & Tsai, 2014). Improving the passenger’s perceived 
waiting time is important since it is related to the overall satisfaction of railway passengers in 
relation to delays. Passengers have a different perception of time while waiting for a delayed 
train which is related to other environmental factors as well such as security, comfort, safety 
and information related to the arrival of the vehicle. Moreover, passengers have a different 
perception of time based on their individual characteristics such as gender, age and the purpose 
of their trip, which can be for work, study, leisure or something else. Traditionally railway 
traffic authorities focus only on how to reduce the actual waiting time underestimating the 
human factor, which is hard to be modeled, and the fact that both the actual and perceived 
waiting time should be reduced in order to provide the best service and have satisfied 
passengers. 
Exploring the waiting experience can help authorities understand the factors that improve it or 
make it worse. “An effective management strategy that improves uncomfortable waiting 
experiences for customers can minimize the negative effect on waiting on customer 
satisfaction” (Cheng & Tsai, 2014). It is very important that psychological factors are taken 
under consideration in railway traffic disruption management when designing a resilient and 
high performance operation system. 
Cheng & Tsai, studied railway passengers' tolerance of waiting by using a psychometric model. 
They based their research on other studies that have proven the relation between the perceived 
waiting duration and satisfaction, studies that have shown waiting time as an anger and 
uncertainty factor and other founding proving that the perceived time is more bearable when 
spent well and under certain environmental conditions. For example keeping passengers busy 
and filling their time with activities can improve their perception of time or playing the music 
when costumers are waiting in the line can increase their satisfaction. Indeed, personal 
==PAGE:92==
78 
 
differences, the type of trip and travel distance can result in different psychological states of the 
passengers’ perception of time but in most of the cases certain strategies improve the overall 
waiting experience.  
Strategies such as providing wait-related information and appropriate messages of apology can 
soften the negative emotions when passengers are on standby, with the apology messages being 
of greater appeal to low tolerance passengers. The British National Passenger Survey for 
autumn 2007, showed that 29% of the passengers were unhappy with the way the delays were 
handled and 75% of them claimed that was due to insufficient information (National Audit 
Office, 2008). Cheng & Tsai performed a survey in Taiwan which indicated other factors that 
improve costumers' waiting experience and seem to apply for all passengers’ regardless gender, 
average monthly riding frequency, age, educational level and monthly income. These factors 
are shown in a form of suggested strategies in Table 3.

5.2 A brief Organizational Overview 
The major organizations involved in the Danish passenger railway are: 
 The Danish Transport Authority 
The Danish Transport Authority (Trafikstyrelsen) is the responsible agency under the ministry 
of Transport for the railway sector. Is in charge of regulation, planning, safety and transport co-
ordination nationally and internationally. The authority is also responsible for advising the 
Ministry of Transport regarding strategic development of railway transport (i.e. construction) 
Finally, it has the responsibility for licensing and railway-related training of personnel and 
collecting data in order to evaluate safety and punctuality levels within railways 
(Trafikstyrelsen, 2015).   
 Infrastructure Manager 
Rail Net Denmark (Banedanmark), the Danish infrastructure manager, is a state owned railway 
infrastructure agency that oversees the biggest part - 90% (Schittenhelm, 2013) – of the Danish 
railway network and it has a contract with the Danish Transport Authority (Trafikstyrelsen). 
The agency has the overall responsibility for planning, overseeing maintenance and 
modernizing the Danish railway and signal system infrastructure as well as monitoring the 
railway traffic. Moreover, it manages the allocation of the railway infrastructure capacity to 
railway operators (MinistryofTransport, 2011). Banedanmark is in charge of 2095 km (Table 
4) of railway tracks and is estimated that more than 3000 train departures are initiated every 
day and about196 million passengers are transferred every year (Banedanmark, 2015). The rest 
of the 10% of the Danish railway network belongs to private companies, which are both 
infrastructure managers and train operators; however, in this study those companies have not 
been included. 
Banedanmark is accountable for the overall performance of the Danish railways and has the 
primary responsibility for managing all kind of disruptions.  
 Train Operating Companies (TOCs) 
DSB is the biggest independent public company owned by the Danish state and is the main 
operator of passenger trains in Denmark estimated to provide services to more than 195 million

passengers per year (DSB, 2015). DSB operates both the main lines of the Danish railway 
system (regional and long distance trains - fjernbane) and the metropolitan s-bane service in the

maintaining trains, ensuring the availability of qualified crew and setting up the planning for 
arrivals and departures, the rolling stock and the crew.

or any contingency plan. The control center will adjust the signalling based on the request of

5.4 Punctuality and Reliability in Danish 
Railways  
In 2010 after a long period with falling punctuality levels, mainly on the coastal line operated 
by DSB Øresund, the Danish Rail Punctuality Task Force was created and is a collaboration 
==PAGE:101==
87 
 
between Banedanmark, the Danish Transport Authority and the TOCs (Schittenhelm, 2013), in 
order to monitor and improve the on-time performance of the trains. 
The punctuality performance measure used mostly in Banedanmark takes into account both 
punctuality and reliability and is expressed as a percentage of trains arriving at all stations, on 
time.  
In 2014, in order to improve the punctuality level Banedanmark in cooperation with the rest of 
the authorities decided to decrease the delay thresholds. The trains from 2014 and onwards are 
punctual if they arrive within the following thresholds (Banedenamark, 2015): 
 S-trains: 2 minutes  
 Regional and Intercity trains (fjernbane): 3 minutes  
In this report, however, due to data and information retrieved for the range of years 2009-2014, 
the old thresholds will be used. Therefore, the punctuality level until 2014 was calculated as 
(Banedanamrk, 2015):  
 S-bane: (all arrivals – cancelled arrivals – arrivals delayed >=2,5 min)/all arrivals 
 Regional and Intercity (aka fjernbane): (all arrivals – cancelled arrivals – arrivals

 Trains that are cancelled up to 72 hours before their scheduled departure

established after the Punctuality Task Force’s creation and is universal for both Banedanmark 
and train operators (before that DSB had already its own reliability and punctuality targets). 
Banedanmark records and calculates punctuality daily and publishes the performance in a 
monthly base. Figures 22 and 23, show the punctuality rate during 2014 for fjernbane and s-

5.5 Data Analysis for 2009-2014 
5.5.1 Data acquisition and methodology 
Banedanmark keeps track and records of every train delay that exceeds the limits of accepted 
delays (as shown in the previous section). Analyzing the data related to the disruptions, apart 
from calculating the service punctuality, is highly important in order: 
 To understand the nature of the disruptions 
 To look for patterns  
 To investigate their causes and impacts in order to be prepared better  
 To examine if improvement measures taken were effective and had the expected output  
 To determine the range of  the expected on – time performance  
 To be used for benchmarking and, 
 To highlight incidents that need further and more detailed investigation   
The data analyzed in this section are based on source data provided by Banedanmark for the 
years 2009 to 2014: The data contains: 
==PAGE:104==
90 
 
 The hour the disruption occurs  
 The day the disruption occurs 
 Responsible authority 
 Main cause of delay 
 Secondary delays 
 Duration of delay 
In Denmark, the causes of delays are assigned to the responsible authority. Banedanmark is 
responsible for the infrastructure related disruptions, signalling and traffic related problems. 
Train operators take responsibility on passenger related delays, crew and operators’ errors and 
rolling stock related delays. For the regional and intercity trains delays are attributed to freight 
operators when freight trains are the cause of delays. Finally, all the rest of causes such as 
weather, vandalism, collisions etc. are assigned to external factors. 
When a train is delayed, the traffic control either connects the delay to an existing failure or 
creates a new report with the cause of delay. Based on this information, Banedanmark is able 
to calculate the impact of a given failure on the rest of the network which is represented by the 
number of affected trains and arrivals per a single incident (secondary delays). Therefore, the 
secondary delays (aka cascade delays) are recorded in the form of: 
 The number of trains affected by one single root delay 
 The number of arrivals affected by one single root delay 
The duration of the delay is recorded only when is necessary to have staff at the scene to make 
sure the error/problem/accident is corrected (major incidents). In this case, the duration is 
calculated from the moment the staff is summoned to the moment that the traffic is normalized. 
However, data observation showed that in many cases this had been omitted. Therefore, the 
duration of an incident has not been used since the results would not have been valid. 
The analysis has been performed separately for s-bane and fjernbane since s-bane owns its own 
network and does not intervene with other lines. On some of the sections the s-bane tracks run 
alongside the outer, regional/intercity mainlines. There are a number of track connections with 
the rest of the lines however, s-bane has its own signaling and power standards hence it has an 
entirely independent track network. S-bane is generally equipped with reserving facilities and 
==PAGE:105==
91 
 
sidings and diversionary routes in order to provide a decent degree of operational flexibility 
(PARSONS, 2011). 
In addition, the delays recorded for s-bane are for trains that are more than 2, 5 minutes late 
whereas for the rest of the trains is 5 minutes, therefore any direct comparison is impossible.  
The finding of the analysis are presented as follows: 
 Number of (root) incidents per year, month, weekday and hour 
 Causes of disruption and impact in secondary delays 
The results will be further discussed in the discussion section of this chapter.

5.6 Emergency Plan and Response 
Regarding emergency planning and response in Denmark the only source of information 
available were the interviews conducted within Banedanmark and the general emergency plan 
manual available for the public.  
Danish railway authorities recognize the importance of well-established and prepared 
emergency plans and the significance of an efficient and effective response during emergency 
situations, in order to restore traffic as soon as possible and with high level of safety. This is 
highlighted in all the formal documents where emergency planning actions are referred.  
The general emergency plan, Figure 35, defines the general principles of Banedanmark. 
 
 
Figure 35: Principles of Banedanmark's Emergency Plan (Banedanmark, 2014)

 General preparedness of Banedanmark’s in unexpected situations which consists of  
 Emergency/Security plan and refers to the preventive measures to avoid the 
occurrence of incidents and accidents 
 Action cards are specific actions (emergency plans) that must be followed during

==PAGE:119==

 Emergency/Response which refers to the measures implemented during the 
occurrence of an incident  
As an emergency are considered all kind of incidents, accidents and crises that might disturb 
the railway traffic. 
In order to prevent accidents and crises and provide high level of railway safeness Banedanmark 
focuses on high level of safety rules especially after 2004 when a serious derailment resulted in 
a different safety culture/awareness and caused a more structured and systematic approach to 
track inspection and evaluation (Carter, et al., 2008). The best measure of safety level according 
to the railway safety performance in the European Union report is the accident risk, which has 
been aligned with the trend in casualty numbers. Considering all railway fatalities (excluding 
suicides), the fatality risk per million train-kilometers (system risk) in the period 2010-2012, in 
EU was 0.3 killed per million train-km (EuropeanRailwayAgency, 2014).  Denmark scores 7th 
in the list with a rate of less than 0.2 railway fatalities and weighted serious injuries per million 
train-km for the years 2007-2012 which is lower than the accepted risk for those years (Figure 
61 Appendix). 
In addition, Banedanmark has analytic emergency plans for different kind of emergencies. The 
measures to ensure the effectiveness of these plans are (Banedanmark, 2014): 
 A management team specialized in emergency management 
 Risk analysis  
 Training of the staff, emergency exercises etc. 
 Guidelines with prefix solutions  
 Plans and guidelines regarding the communication among different parties  
 Information handling during and after the emergencies  
 Proper number of necessary resources  
 Post-emergency evaluation 
 Review of the plans tactically every 2 years  
All the above information in combination with the low rate of accidents in the Danish railways 
indicate that safety rules are performed sufficiently in order to prevent accidents and a crisis 
management plan has been established. However, since the focus of this study is mostly about 
managing the disruptions in order to get the service back as fast as possible, there has been a 
==PAGE:120==

lack of information regarding how efficient are the emergency operations in restoring back the 
service. The crisis management manual of Banedanmark focuses mostly on safety and 
preventing escalation of the problem. Therefore, due to lack of information, the analysis of this 
stage will be limited on some findings from the interviews and which are potentially points that 
need to be reconsidered, during emergency planning.    
Training of staff and evacuation exercises 
In the documents provided by Banedanmark the training of staff during emergency situations 
and the performance of evacuation exercises is highlighted many times. However, no 
information was given regarding how these exercises are performed and which steps they 
include. In the interview performed, in the answer regarding how well are staff prepared to 
evacuate during emergencies, the response was that the personnel is not trained to provide 
guidelines and help to the passengers. Instead, only recorded tapes that urge people to evacuate 
the stations are used. According to the interviewee, the need for such exercises is mandatory 
since there are stations i.e. Roskilde Station where is hard to navigate and it takes time and 
passengers might be trapped if not guided. Apart from that, during emergency situations people 
are usually stressed and many times are not able to think clearly. The staff is necessary to be 
well trained in order to keep the passengers calm and provide security.  In general, emergency 
training of stuff is important in order to develop skills and be ready for unforeseen events. 
Communication with external parties 
Communication within and outside Banedanmark is proved to be the biggest problem for the 
company. According to Banedanmark’s reports when something goes wrong is most of the 
times due to bad communication between different parties.  
The communication between Banedanmark staff and the operating companies’ staff has 
improved significantly since they moved in the same building. The integrated traffic operation 
center is helping to improve performance through quicker communication and more effective 
decision making, between the two parties. However, problems in communication remain 
between Banedanmark and train managers in local stations. While, it is of a highest priority that 
the traffic operation center is informed for every decision, it has happened many times that 
information has been neglected or delayed causing this way problems and potential delays in 
the network.  
==PAGE:121==
107 
 
For this reason standardization of certain procedures is a necessity. Apart from that, a common 
information interface which would alert all sides would be also useful. 
The biggest problem remain between Banedanmark and emergency services. Most of the first 
responders do not deal in a frequent basis with the railway industry and do not have sufficient 
information to enable them to make the right contact when is required. This way 
miscommunication often leads to prolonged delays. Banedanmark has tried to train people from 
emergency services to always call them but this measure has not been very effective maybe 
another solution like the common interface in GB where information is shared among all the 
relevant parties, would be more effective.  
Another problem is when emergency authorities such as the Danish National Police, which by 
law is the highest authority in Denmark and is the lead organization when investigations 
undergo in railways, requires actions that are not necessary and cause many disturbances and 
further delays in the network. For example when the police wants to perform investigation on 
the rail tracks they frequently request that all the electricity power shuts down. However, this 
is not necessary and in contrast it has many drawbacks since after shutting down the electricity 
the traffic will take some time to open again and i.e. if a person is under the train will be trapped. 
In other cases, the police requires from the traffic center to halt the traffic in a specific location 
in order to perform investigation. However, due to lack of ability and experience in defining 
the exact location in the network, misunderstanding has led to near accidents. Such signs 
indicate that there is lack of knowledge and training of external authorities. However, based on 
the data analyzed earlier that police, only, intervenes with the Danish railway about 1000 times 
a year it makes the addresses of this problem an imperative need.  
Locating people on the track has been a challenge in other cases as well. It happens very often, 
that mechanics access the track for a scheduled work and due to their ignorance, they end up 
on a place where they should not be or they move outside the safety zone that they are allowed 
to be. Training is the key also for this problem in combination with GPS technologies for all 
the people that have access in the tracks.  
Another problem identified it is the communication between the traffic center and the control 
center (signalling room) when construction work is scheduled. Mechanics will access the tracks 
and get permission from the signalling room at the time that is scheduled, however, the 
==PAGE:122==

procedures are performed without asking permission from the controllers who might have a 
delayed train coming over and that needs to pass. Standardization of the procedures could also 
be the answer to this problem. 
Knowledge Management 
An additional problem is information and knowledge sharing across the organization. It seems 
that sometimes knowledge about technical and mechanical issues are restricted to some people 
only and not shared in a common base unless there is a serious incident, which highlights the 
specific lack of knowledge. The positive thing is that Banedanmark pays attention usually on 
such problems and records them, so that they do not happen again. However, knowledge should 
be shared and should be easy to access. Considering the fact that most of the current employees 
have been working for decades it is essential that they share their knowldeg with the future 
generations. In addition, as it has been mentioned before, training of the stuff for emergencies 
is important in order to know what to do and which decisions to make. By having access on an 
information system that will identify who is the most relevant and experienced employee on a 
specific task the rest of the employees can approach him when they are unable to solve the 
problem by themselves.  
5.7 Passenger Information  
Banedanmark in cooperation with DSB have been working closely together in order to improve 
the quality of traffic information especially in the event of disruptions. An important step 
towards it was the fact that in 2009 both companies were able to obtain information from the 
same data source trying to ensure consistent information on screens on platforms and in 
departure halls (DSB, 2009). 
In the annual report of DSB in 2009, it is mentioned, that the staff of both DSB and 
Banedanmark involved with traffic information were provided with guidelines on how to 
provide information to the costumers based on their needs. They developed a joint 
communication concept, known as Klar Besked (Clear Information), the purpose of which was 
to motivate employees to put themselves in the place of the costumer in order to understand 
better their needs. In addition, the two agencies developed common guidelines about the traffic 
information equipment in the railway stations. Moreover, they made new decisions such as new, 
==PAGE:123==
109 
 
larger and better screens and signs, posters and English translations. DSB has been focusing on 
training the staff in order to improve their service when it comes to customer information such 
as the train crew standing by the doors to the train to greet the customers and to suggest 
alternative transport options in the event of irregularities in the traffic. 
Rejseplanen.dk is a digital solution that integrates all different transportation systems in 
Denmark. It contains all schedules and current departures and arrivals for all the transport 
modes and users can find information regarding their departure and destination the travel etc. 
However, the system still faces challenges in giving real time information in case of delays. For 
this reason, improvements are necessary for informing passengers in a reliable and timely 
manner and provide alternative ways of reaching their destination.

the number of disruptions has decreased about 33%. Two of the three main responsible parties’

no significant changes, whereas the operator’s related incidents have been dropping steadily 
every year and especially in the long and regional trains where the decrease has been almost 
50%.

with a small difference the last years, their impact in secondary delays is less significant than 
Banedanmark’s and external causes. Especially from 2011 and onwards, their impact has

Cable theft in Denmark/Vandalism 
Cable theft is one major problem with impacts in terms of delays but also in terms of high costs 
According to the Danish Transport Authority, the costs associated to the cable thefts are 
calculated based on three main categories: 
 Banedanmark’s costs for materials and replacement  
 DSB’s costs for bus bridging  
==PAGE:129==
115 
 
 The economic costs of passengers’ delays  
A single incident of cable theft in January 2015 affected more than 175000 passengers and 
caused more than 78000 hours delays. The costs for replacement were estimated about DKK 
0.4 million and the bus bridging about DKK 1.6 million. The cost for the loss of time of 
passengers is estimated to be around DKK 17 million. In total only a single cable theft event 
caused cots of about DKK 19 million (Transportministeriet, 2015).  
Cable theft is quite often and only in 2014, there were about 173 incidents recorded.  For this 
reason, in 2015 Banedanmark started collaborating with the National Police in order to prevent 
cable thefts. According to the National Police, the people involved in these events follow 
specific plans and motives, which can be considered as patterns in order to stop them. 
Banedanmark, states that the increase in surveillance cameras functions as a successful 
preventive measure since those areas are deselected from the thieves and also during tests when 
suspicious moves were recorded guards were sent and potential thieves were gone. Other goals 
are regarding GPS devises on the cables and an advanced labelling of the cables in order for 
potential buyers to avoid buying Banedanmark’s property. In addition, legislation and penalties 
have been set and communication with the rest of the countries where cable theft is performed. 
Another solution considered is to replace the stolen cables with aluminum. Finally is 
highlighted the increase of social awareness so that citizens inform the authorities when 
something is suspicious.  
The results of the measures will be more obvious in 2015 analysis since most of them are about 
to be implemented or have started after the period this analysis has been performed. However, 
the camera surveillance especially in the regional and long distance trains averted potential 
thieves from stealing in 2013 which is proven also by the reduced number.  
In general the number of vandalisms remains high and especially in s-bane has been increasing 
annually. Considering the length of s-bane it would be assumed that less vandalism events 
would occur since monitoring the suburban train is more easy that monitoring the whole Danish 
network.

Suicides and trespassing in Denmark (Person Collision)    
Regarding suicides and trespassing accidents Banedanmark measure is to fence most of the 
tracks in order to prevent non-authorized trespassing (Banedanmark, 2015). However, fencing 
all the tracks apart from being difficult due to the large scale requires that the fences are 
monitored as well since are very sensitive in vandalism or animals. While fencing the entire 
network is hard fencing the s-bane network should be easier. Despite that, there are sections in 
s-bane where there is only little or no fencing to prevent from trespassing (PARSONS, 2011). 
Regarding suicides no measures have been taken despite the extreme high costs (90 incidents 
of person-collision in 2012 costed about DKK 236 million without including the costs of close 
collision). However, what seems to have improved during these years especially after 2012 is 
that the impact on delays on the rest of the network has decreased. This is due to a decision to 
that in case of a person collision investigation and other procedures should not take more than 
2 hours. This is actually a big step and decision made by Banedanmark in collaboration with 
the national police and the rest of the stakeholders. Since the impact of person in terms of 
cascade delays in the highest additional measures, especially in s-bane further improvements 
should be considered. 
Police and railways 
Police investigation and activities involved in the railways create further delays as it has been 
mentioned already.  Due to the prolonged delays and problems in communication the 
importance to address the problems and have an understudying between the different parties 
has come to realization recently for Banedanmark. This is proven by the numbers of affected 
delays that especially in 2014 have dropped. Just like in the cases of person collision (which 
require always investigation) the importance of getting service back to normal the soonest the 
possible has eventually been underlined between the two parties. However, there is no 
information on what usually causes the police involve in the first place. It is rational that police 
involvement is sometimes unavoidable but maybe some issues can be addressed better if more 
security guards are involved in the daily operation of the railways.  
Leaves in Denmark 
During the autumn season (middle October-November), tree leaves falling cause problems on 
the tracks and make traffic difficult and sometimes impossible. Leaf falling impacts seem to

have dropped the last years in S-bane however the problem has deteriorated for the rest of the 
network. According to Banedanmark in 2013 due to the increased number of such problems in 
order to address this problem four special machines were dedicated to work constantly during 
that period. However, the increasing number of incidents does not prove any improvements in 
preventing relevant disruptions. This is another sign that Banedanmark is acting reactively and 
not proactively at least towards external factors. It might have reacted more drastically towards 
such incidents in s-bane (due to the high sensitivity of the network in delays) but no mitigation 
measures have been taken and under stress and pressure, the rest of the network becomes a 
second priority. Whereas is impossible to prevent leaves from falling, efficient anticipation and 
preparation is essential. Banedanmark should be collaborating with the meteorological agency 
and get updated constantly in regards to weather conditions and changes. In addition historic 
data can prove useful in order to decide strategically the number and location of the machines 
and the point in time where preparations should begin. A structured approach as defined earlier 
in this study can prove to show the weak points and reinforce the strong ones.  
Weather impact in Denmark 
Winter and generally extreme weather conditions have a great effect on the Danish railway 
network. Weather conditions can have a great impact on the railway traffic for many reasons as 
it was shown in Chapter 2, therefore is important to be extremely well prepared and have 
extinguished weak points that could deteriorate the situation. However, the change of the 
seasons seem to always attract many disruptions and there is a suspicion that this is due to non-
sufficient preparedness. In addition, during extreme weather conditions it seems that railways 
are nearly paralyzed.  
Considering the fact that most of the external factors require communication and cooperation 
among different parties within and outside Banedanmark in both prevention and preparedness 
which according to Banedanmark is its weakest point a strong focus should be given in 
establishing well coordination cooperation between external parties.  
In general, some decisions mostly the recent years seem to have put Banedanmark in a more 
defending position towards external disruptions, although the measures are relatively new and 
no evaluation can be performed regarding their effectiveness and time resilience.

5.9 Limitations  
The data presented in this study were acquired after many efforts in order to get them in the 
final form and meet some of the writer’s expectations. However, many compromises were made 
since this report was conducted within academic context only.  In addition, most of the data and 
documents were provided in Danish and this was an additional difficulty while studying and 
analyzing them.  
Furthermore, data analysis would have been more precise and complete if the location variable 
would have been included. However, this was not possible due to lack of information and the 
fact that this study had to come to realization within certain time limits.   
Overall, the disruption related data could have been analyzed in more depth. However, that was 
not sustainable and affordable within the time limits. Nonetheless, the scope of this project was 
not this. The target was to emphasize the importance of using the data and in order to relate 
them with the theoretical framework developed in the study. 
Regarding emergency plan and response, a more complete and precise opinion would have been 
formed if the number of interviews was not limited and it included more interviewees from 
different parties. Listening to more than one side is the best approach, however since that was 
not possible, it is recommended that in order to investigate in an accurate way the processes 
followed during emergencies, a valid number of interviews is performed. 
5.10 Conclusion, and Future Recommendations  
This chapter performed an analysis and investigated how Danish railway authorities are 
managing disruptions and how well are performing in regards to the disruption management 
approach as was described in this study. The output of the analysis demonstrates the following 
conclusions: 
 During the years 2009-2015 and after the creation of the Danish Rail Punctuality Task 
the incidents disrupting the railway service in Demark have decreased about 30 %  
==PAGE:133==
119 
 
 The main reason for this has been the largest Danish train operator, DSB, and the 
establishment of improvement strategies in order to increase punctuality 
 They achieved this by adopting preventing measures for their common disruptions 
such as rolling stock failures, staff errors and passengers related delays  
 When prevention measures were not possible they trained personnel in reducing the 
impact of the disruption and responding in an efficient way, they even established a 
special punctuality team in order to improve the communication between staff and 
departments  
The success of DSB is relevant also with the fact that during the past years they have been 
following a passenger focused service strategy aligning their expectations with their 
costumers’. One weak point regarding DSB seems to be passenger real time information during 
disruptions.  
Nonetheless, railway systems are complex and their success does not depend only on one 
organization but instead on all the relevant stakeholders and their coordination. Therefore, is 
important that same targets and improvements are followed by the rest of stakeholders.    
The infrastructure manager of the Danish railway transportation, Banedanmark seems to have 
a stable share of disruptions during the years with no improvements but no deterioration either. 
Banedanmark’s focus is on new projects that will facilitate the passengers in the future and 
decrease delays. More specifically a new signalling system is expected to reduce delays related 
to signalling about 80% for the regional and long distance trains and 50 % for s-bane. However, 
the current service should not be sacrificed either and special attention should be paid on factors 
that could potentially increase the number of incidents and delays. It is recommended that 
Banedanmark follows also a customer focused strategy and a disruption management approach 
to reduce disruptions and their impacts.  
In regards to external factor, despite the fact that they amount only for 10-13% their impacts 
seem to be the largest. Since most of them are outside the railway companies control the impacts 
and costs they cause are significant. No significant improvements has occurred during the years 
however, there has been a progress the last two years because of an imperative need.  Especially 
due to the fact that such events are outside the railway control, it is very important that relevant 
stakeholders such as first responders, municipality and all the relevant authorities have a clear 
==PAGE:134==
120 
 
and well - established communication that will facilitate decision making and improve the 
performance. Training of the railway stuff in order to develop skills so that can respond 
efficiently to these events is the same important also because some of them they might involve 
larger risks than delays. 
It is therefore recommended that the Danish railway should focus more on the prevention, 
mitigation and management of the external factors by increasing its resilience abilities. In order 
to achieve this, a strategic disruptions management approach should be followed but at the same 
time the ability of all levels of the organizations must be enhanced in a constant way in order 
to create processes that are robust yet flexible and to use their resources proactively and 
efficiently during unforeseen events or under long stressful situations.   
 Not many similar analysis have been performed in order to benchmark the Danish Railway 
with other railway companies especially regarding external factors. The only two studies 
relevant were in the U.S (Nelson & O´Neil, 2000) were time horizon was not determined. 
However, external factors counted for about 13% of the total number of delays. In addition to 
that, National Rail that for the years 2006-07 the external related delays counted for about 20% 
of the total delays. However, no systematic approach has been found to show the impact of the 
external factors in railway traffic delays and to suggest methods for improvements. 
It is recommended therefore, that an extensive analysis of the external factors that disturb the 
Danish railways is performed in addition to their impacts in cost and delays. Furthermore, 
prevention, mitigation and measures to manage these events should be identified and reviewed 
based on their effectiveness. Finally, a benchmarking analysis with other countries is important 
in order to avoid mistakes, learn lessons and adopt successful strategies.  
Regarding emergency plan and response, there should be a review of incidents that caused long 
delays in the network in order to investigate how emergency response and recovery processes 
are being handled. The Danish Transport Authority investigates incidents in detail when there 
is a severe disruption of service and particularly when there is an accident 
(Havarikommissionen, 2015), however, questions are raised regarding whether these 
investigations include major common disruptions and information about how they were 
managed. It is suggested therefore, that an exclusive qualitative analysis of an adequate sample 
is performed, where investigations will focus on:

 Diagnosis speed and quality 
 Availability of equipment (materials, machinery and tools) 
 Staff availability and level of expertise

Conclusion 
 
This study performed a research on passenger railway disruption management. The literature 
gap identified in the beginning of this process was the motivation for a research in identifying 
the essential elements of a successful disruption management system that also happen to 
contribute in the addressing of large scale crises. From the prism of the resilience policies as 
demonstrated in this study, an important pursuit should be to strengthen relationship among the 
emergency response partners and improve communication with all relevant stakeholders, to 
protect corporate assets and reputation, to improve understanding of functions and opportunities 
for improvement, to strengthen the organizations capacity and skills and to stabilize the effects 
of a disruptive event and restore the operations to their normal condition as quickly as possible. 
It has been said that “when no advance preparations are made, uncoordinated Government 
responses can combine with tremendous public confusion and uncertainty to leave the urban 
transportation system in a state of near paralysis” (Meyer & Belobaba, 1982) therefore 
resilience is a systemic attitude of continuous preparation and improvement of the railway’s 
assets and organizations by adopting the core qualities of resilience. The disruption 
management framework utilized some of the resilience policies to propose a strategic yet 
practical approach to dealing with common and frequent railway disruptions. After all, 
resilience engineering makes a strong point on how significant is to address small incidents 
with the utmost devotion in order to avoid escalation of the problematic situation in something 
far more severe. The disruption management framework having as a primary focus the 
passenger, highlights the key steps that will lead to reduced delays and increase the performance 
of the railways in order to provide the best possible service for the costumers. Disruption 
prevention and mitigation through a well-established analysis and plan are essential for 
increasing the on-time performance of the railways. These steps are continuous and involve 
cooperation of all stakeholders.  
At last, it was a pleasant surprise to see that one of the main stakeholders in the Danish railway 
system since the adoption of the passenger oriented approach and the application of a systemic 
disruption management framework experienced a significant boost of its performance.
