Abstract
Nitrogen-vacancy (NV) centers have emerged as a promising spin-based platform in the
field of quantum sensing and spin-mechanics. Their nanometer size and high sensitivity
in detecting magnetic fields make them suitable for various applications, ranging from
fundamental scientific research to practical applications in material science, biology, and
beyond. Spin-mechanical systems, in particular, offer intriguing possibilities for using
the interaction between spin and mechanical motion to enable entanglement between
quantum computers, which is a crucial step towards building a quantum network.
This work focuses on the development of methods for sensing the motion of a mechanical
oscillator with a frequency around 80 kHz. Initially, a comprehensive model is developed
to understand the coherence of NV centers when the Rabi frequency is close to the fre-
quency of the mechanical oscillators. The model is used to measure the coherence of
two NV centers over a wide range of Rabi frequencies. Subsequently, various protocols
for sensing AC signals, including pulsed sequences, dressed state sensing, and the Qdyne
protocol, are considered. These protocols are implemented, and their performance and
limitations are assessed. Experimental data show that the sensitivity of measuring a
signal with a frequency of 80 kHz is 0.6 µT/
√
Hz. Furthermore, a numerical simulation
framework is developed to investigate the magnetic properties of a thin magnetic disk.
The measured magnetic field is compared to the simulation results to validate the relia-
bility of the simulations. This framework improves our understanding of the magnetic
behavior of the disk and verifies the consistency between experimental measurements
and simulation outcomes.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The study of quantum mechanics has brought exciting advancements in scientific ex-
ploration and in potential new technologies. These advancements have revolutionized
sensing, computing, and communication. Quantum sensing, in particular, has emerged
as a remarkable tool for accurately measuring physical quantities. Quantum sensors,
built upon the principles of quantum mechanics, provide unmatched levels of sensitivity
and precision.
Among the various platforms investigated for quantum sensing, spin-
based systems have captured considerable attention due to their distinct properties and
capabilities.
In recent years, NV (nitrogen-vacancy) centers in diamonds have emerged as a promising
platform for high-performance magnetometry, enabling the detection and characteriza-
tion of magnetic fields at the nanoscale [1].
This has significant implications across
diverse fields, ranging from fundamental science to practical applications in materials
science [2], biology[3], and beyond. NV centers in diamonds are atomic-scale defects
formed when a nitrogen atom replaces a carbon atom in the diamond lattice, adjacent
to a vacant lattice site. These defects possess exceptional characteristics that render
them highly suitable for magnetometry applications.
The electronic spin of the NV
center can be optically manipulated and read out, enabling the precise measurement
of magnetic fields with exceptional sensitivity. Moreover, NV centers exhibit long spin
coherence times, allowing for stable operation even at room temperature.
The field of quantum computing has generated considerable interest due to its poten-
tial for solving certain problems more eﬀiciently than classical computers, including
breaking classical encryption methods. However, the practical applications of quantum
computers are still in the early stages of development, and significant challenges need to
be overcome. Superconducting Quantum Interferometers (SQUIDs) have emerged as a
promising platform for quantum computers, with rapid advancements in scaling up the
number of qubits from a few to thousands. To further enhance the power of quantum
computers, it becomes necessary to entangle multiple quantum computers together into
a cluster.
This ”clustering” could be achieved by connecting multiple quantum processors based
on Superconducting Qubits (SCQs) via a quantum network consisting of a spin-photon
interface at each node. Distant entanglement of three spin-qubits has been demonstrated
and there are protocols to potentially scale this number up from 3-nodes to more, without
exponential cost in time. The challenge of clustering SCQs lies in coupling an SCQ to the
spin-photon interface. The direct SCQ-spin electromagnetic coupling via proximity is
too weak, and coupling to photons is hindered by the energy disparity between microwave
and optical photons. One proposal is to use mechanical motion or phonons as a quantum
==PAGE:10==

bus between spins and SCQs. This is possible because a mechanical oscillator can be
functionalised to interact with a variety of fields and forces. In fact, micro-mechanical
oscillators have already been coupled to SCQs to create non-classical states of motion
and used as memories.
The coupling of optically active spins with phonons remains in the classical regime.
Nonetheless, there has been exciting progress using NVs.
For example, in reference
[4], the thermal motion of a cantilever with a magnetized tip was measured using NV
centres at room temperature. It was also shown that this scheme has the potential to
detect the zero-point fluctuations of mechanical oscillators, allowing for the resolution of
single phonons. In reference [5], an ensemble of NV centres was used to cool the motion
of a rotating microdiamond, offering the possibility of controlling the temperature of
mechanical oscillators and cooling them to their ground state. Once the ground state
is reached, the spin-mechanical interaction can be used to create non-classical states in
the mechanical oscillators, which, due to the high-quality factor of recent mechanical
oscillators, can persist for hours and be used, for example, to generate entanglement.
However, the coupling strengths between spins and mechanical systems remain relatively
low, and achieving coupling to single phonons is still a challenge. To address this, we
aim to leverage the high-quality silicon nitride membranes fabricated at DTU with their
high mechanical quality factors. The current idea is to fabricate a micro magnet on
the trampoline resonator and position it in close proximity to an NV centre, aiming to
achieve single spin-phonon coupling of 100 kHz by reaching distances of 10-100 nm. This
approach holds the potential to enable coherent coupling between spins and mechanical
systems, facilitating the exploration of novel quantum phenomena and the creation of
non-classical states.

1.2
Introduction to NV centers
The nitrogen atom in the NV center has five valence electrons, with three of them
covalently bonded to the surrounding carbon atoms. The remaining two electrons form
a lone pair. The vacancy, on the other hand, has three unpaired electrons, with two of
them forming a quasi-covalent bond. This configuration, known as NV0, is the neutral
state of the defect. However, NV0 is not typically used for most applications. It is
possible to introduce an additional electron into the defect, resulting in a net charge of
-1. This charged configuration, known as NV−, is commonly referred to as an NV center.
In the NV−configuration, the defect exhibits a total spin of S = 1 and is the state of
interest for various quantum applications.
1.2.1
Fabrication process
The fabrication process of the NV center begins with a high-quality diamond substrate,
which is carefully grown in the laboratory. The diamond substrate used for NV center
production is typically isotopically purified near the surface to minimize the presence of
naturally occurring 13C isotopes where the NV will be implanted. In our case, we use
a diamond substrate isotopic purity of 99.99%. To create the NV center, nitrogen ions
are implanted into the diamond substrate. This is achieved by directing high-energy
==PAGE:12==
4
1 Introduction
nitrogen ions, typically with an energy of around 20 keV, towards the diamond surface.
During this process, some of the nitrogen ions displace carbon atoms from the diamond
lattice, effectively taking their positions. In our case, we use a diamond with implanted
only 15N nitrogen isotopes.
Following the implantation step, the diamond sample is subjected to a high-temperature
annealing process, typically performed at temperatures above 700 ◦C. This annealing
step serves multiple purposes. Firstly, it helps the vacancies (created by the displace-
ment of carbon atoms) to migrate closer to the implanted nitrogen ions, promoting the
formation of NV centers.
Due to the crystal structure of diamond, NV centers can have four different orientations
depending on the relative placement of the vacancy with respect to the nitrogen atom.
The specific orientation of the NV centers depends on the fabrication conditions and can
impact their optical and spin properties.
Figure 1.1: Orientation of the four NV axis in the crystal lattice. Figure from [6].
1.2.2
Energy structure
The NV center exhibits two optical energy levels: the ground state 3A2 and the excited
state 3E. Both states possess a triplet state with a total spin value of S = 1 and a
singlet state with S = 0. The splitting between the triplet and singlet states arises due
the different symmetry of the spatial electronic wavefunctions. Consequently, the singlet
and triplet states feature distinct electron distances, resulting in different magnetic and
Coulomb interactions between them. Furthermore, within the triplet state, specifically
for ms = ±1, the electrons share the same spin state. As per the Pauli exclusion principle,
they are compelled to occupy different orbitals. Conversely, in the ms = 0 state, the
electrons possess different spin states, allowing them to occupy the same orbital. Thus,
the energy levels associated with ms = ±1 and ms = 0 experience a zero-field splitting

through the non-radiative pathway. The non-radiative decay of |0⟩is suppressed, hence
only the states |±1⟩will decay to either |0⟩or |±1⟩in the ground state. Consequently,
if the laser pump is suﬀiciently strong, after a certain time, the entire population is in
|ms = 0⟩of the ground state.
Once the NV center is initialized in the ground state, a static external magnetic field
B0 can be introduced to split the |±1⟩levels via Zeeman interaction. Now, it becomes
possible to select one of the two levels and induce transitions using an external magnetic
field that is resonant with the energy gap (in the microwave regime). Essentially, a qubit
has been effectively created that can be addressed at room temperature.
Several other factors can influence the energy structure of the NV center, including
temperature, electric field, pressure, strain and interaction with surrounding nuclear
spins.

Introduction to the cantilever
The mechanical structure exhibits various longitudinal, transverse, and torsional modes,
each characterized by distinct mode shapes, frequencies, and quality factors. For exam-
ple, Figure 1.4 shows the first four lateral normal vibration mode shapes of a cantilever
beam. However, our specific focus lies on the mode where the fixed end remains sta-
tionary while the free end oscillates up and down, commonly known as the first normal
mode.
Figure 1.4: First four lateral normal vibration mode shapes of a cantilever beam of
uniform cross-section. Figure from [9].
The cantilever we used in our experiment has a first normal mode frequency of 75 kHz
and a quality factor (Q) of approximately 220. The quality factor is the ratio between
the frequency of the mode (ωm) and the dissipation rate (γm). It indicates the number of
oscillation periods the oscillator can undergo before its energy is dissipated or damped
out. A higher quality factor implies a lower dissipation rate and a longer decay time,
allowing the oscillations to persist for a greater number of cycles.
In classical mechanics, the amplitude of oscillation for a cantilever is a continuous real
number. By applying an external force at the same frequency as the mode, we can drive

Experimental setup
The experimental setup is composed of four main components. Firstly, a green laser
is employed to initialize and readout the NV center. Secondly, there is an electronic
system responsible for generating the electromagnetic signal that is transmitted to the
MW antenna. This signal is used to manipulate the state of the NV center. Finally, a
confocal microscope is used to focus the laser light onto a specific NV center and collect
the fluorescence emitted by it. The collected fluorescence is detected by a photodetec-
tor. Additionally, a cantilever is attached to a Smart stage, enabling control over the
coupling between the cantilever tip and the NV center. Figure 2.1 shows a schematic
representation of the experimental setup with the key components.

the pulsed mode, the switch is closed on the output of the arbitrary waveform generator

The diamond sample contains shallow NV centers around

to change the polarization of the light. The polarizing beam splitter (PBS) selects one
polarization component, while the orthogonal component is discarded. By rotating the
half-wave plate, the amount of light at the output of the PBS can be controlled. An
acousto-optic modulator (AOM) is used as a fast switch with a specific transition rate.
The AOM is crucial for creating pulsed sequences and is controlled by the AWG.
To collect the light emitted by the NV center, a confocal microscope is used. The con-
focal microscope consists of a dichroic mirror, a movable objective, a focusing lens, and
a pinhole (the fiber coupler). The dichroic mirror selectively reflects the green light,
allowing the emitted light from the NV center to pass through. The movable objective
focuses the green light onto the NV center and collects the emitted light. The lens then
directs the red light onto the fiber coupler, which acts as a pinhole. Consequently, only
the light from the focal point of the objective enters the fiber. The photons within the
fiber are subsequently detected by an avalanche photodiode (APD). The time for each
photon count is registered by a time-tagger connected to the AWG. This synchroniza-
tion allows for the correlation between the timing of the pulsed sequence and the photon
counts.
Moreover, to couple the spin of the NV center to the mechanical part, a beam cantilever
with a tip coated with a magnetic material is used and positioned in close proximity to
the NV center. The cantilever is attached to a silicon chip mounted on a Smart Stage,
which enables control over the movement and rotations of the cantilever in all three
dimensions.

CHAPTER 3
Dissipation mechanisms in
NV Centers
In this chapter, our focus is on characterizing the NV center, specifically by measuring
the dissipation rates of the Rabi oscillations.
The first part of the chapter delves into the theoretical framework developed to describe
the interaction between the magnetic field and the NV center. We begin by presenting
a simple model and then gradually introduce more complexity to accurately describe
the dynamics of the NV center, considering various interactions with its environment.
In particular, we derive a comprehensive model based on Bloch and Torrey’s solution
equation, which accounts for the impact of dissipation processes on the Rabi oscillations.
Through this theoretical development, we aim to establish a foundation for understand-
ing and characterizing the decoherence time of the NV center.
In the second part of the chapter, we focus on the energy structure of the NV center
and proceed to measure the hyperfine splitting. Additionally, we perform measurements
of T1 and Rabi oscillations in two NV centers operating at different regimes. These
measurements are performed using the models developed in the first part of the chapter.
We show that when the Rabi frequency is around 80 kHz, the Rabi oscillation behavior
can only be accurately described using Torrey’s solution.
3.1
Rabi model
The Rabi model, named after its creator Isidor Isaac Rabi, is a fundamental theoretical
framework in quantum mechanics that describes the interaction between a two-level
quantum system and an external electromagnetic field in the simplest way. This model
has been widely studied and applied in various fields, including atomic physics, quantum
optics, and solid-state physics. The Rabi model provides insights into the dynamics of
the system and allows for the manipulation and control of quantum states.
In our specific case, the external electromagnetic field is generated by the signal generator
or AWG and is transmitted through the MW antenna located near the NV center. It is
important to note that the Rabi model is a semi-classical model, as it does not explicitly
account for the quantized nature of the electromagnetic field B(t) = B0 cos (ωt). The
interaction between the two-level system, such as the NV center, and the external field
occurs through a dipole interaction.

3.1.1
Rabi model including experimental constraints
While the Rabi model provides a useful framework, it assumes an ideal scenario with
a perfect two-level system and no dissipation mechanisms. In practice, the situation is
more complex. By applying a suﬀiciently strong magnetic field, we can approximate the
NV center as a two-level system and initialize it in the ground state. However, during
the readout process, the photodetector counts the number of photons emitted by the NV
within a given time interval. Photons are emitted when the NV is in the ground state
(bright state) at the start of the readout. Additionally, due to competing transition
probabilities and dark counts, even if the NV is in the excited state (dark state), a
nonzero signal is still measured. The contrast C represents the difference between the
signals in the bright and dark states and is typically around 30%. Consequently, the
measured signal Sg(t) can be described by the following expression:
Sg(t) = R(1 −CPe(t))
(3.3)
where R is the rate of photons counted by the APD.

the crystal lattice, which leads to thermalization, as well as the interaction with neigh-
boring spins, causing dephasing and the hyperfine interaction. Dephasing arises when
the NV center interacts with neighboring spins that undergo state fluctuations. Each
spin flip imparts a random kick to the NV center’s spin, altering its phase in the Bloch
sphere. Since these kicks occur randomly, the phase information of the NV center be-
comes increasingly uncertain over time, resulting in a decay of the amplitude of the

3.2

To derive a solution for the Rabi model while incorporating longitudinal and transverse
relaxation times, we consider the time evolution of the state using the Pauli matrices,
denoted as ⃗σ = (σx, σy, σz)T. Specifically, we are interested in the time evolution of σz
since the excited state probability can be expressed as Pe(t) = 1
2(1 + ⟨σz(t)⟩).
If the MW drive is along σx, it induces a rotation in the Bloch sphere around the x-
axis. In the interaction picture and under the Rotating Wave Approximation (RWA),
this rotation leads to a mixing of the σy and σz components proportionally to the Rabi
frequency Ω.
When the drive is slightly detuned from resonance with the two-level
system, the effect of detuning is to introduce a mixing between the σx and σy components.
This can be visualized as a tilt in the rotation axis, which is no longer perfectly aligned
along the x-axis.

Sensing DC signals
Static magnetic fields along the NV axis will shift the energy levels of the NV through
Zeeman interaction, so they can be measured by probing the energy structure of the NV.
However, it is important to note that the NV center is also sensitive to perpendicular
magnetic fields, even if with a much lower sensitivity compared to fields along the NV
axis.
3.3.1
Optically detected magnetic resonance
The simplest way to investigate the energy structure of the NV is to use the continuous
wave optically detected magnetic resonance (CW-ODMR). It is the same protocol that
was described to initialize the NV in its ground state |0⟩. It consists in applying the green
light to NV and readout the fluorescence at the same time while changing the frequency
of the magnetic field applied through the MW antenna.

to implement as it does not require pulsed sequences or phase control. However, the
sensitivity is not great as the contrast CCW that is usually achieved is not great. This
is because the readout and initialization happen at the same time, so the NV is never

Sensing AC signals
Sensing oscillating fields can be a challenging task, particularly when the signal has a
frequency different from the resonance frequency of the sensor. In the previous chapter,
we discussed methods for sensing signals that do not change over time so they have
non-zero average value in time. However, when dealing with signals of frequency fs and
zero mean value, more sophisticated sensing methods need to be devised.
If the sensor, such as the NV center, has a resonance frequency, it becomes relatively easy
to sense signals with the same frequency, as they would induce changes in the sensor’s
state. The real challenge arises when trying to measure signals with frequencies far from
the resonance of the sensor. While it is theoretically possible to tune the resonance
frequency of NV centers by adjusting the external bias field, this approach is not always
practical.
To address this issue, more advanced methodologies have been developed to make NV
centers sensitive to arbitrary frequencies. Figure 4.1 illustrates some common protocols
that have been developed to sense signals across different frequency ranges.

techniques exist to mitigate the impact of imperfect pulses, with one common approach
being the use of XY pulse sequences. In XY sequences, π-pulses along the x-axis are

CHAPTER 5
Magnetic properties of a
thin disk
This chapter focuses on establishing a reliable model for simulating the magnetic prop-
erties of magnetic structures. The objective is to use the fabrication capabilities of our
laboratories to create high-quality trampoline oscillators [19] with the aim of achieving
strong coupling between NV centers and the mechanical oscillator.
To achieve this,
it is necessary to fabricate magnetic structures on these mechanical systems, enabling
the coupling of motion with the spin of the NV center. The fabrication of small mag-
netic structures is a novel endeavor within the group, and this chapter aims to lay the
foundation for developing this expertise.
During the course of this project, successful deposition of thin permalloy disks was
achieved by Dr. Dennis Høj. In this chapter, we present a numerical simulation-based
model that aligns with our expectations and validates the success of the deposition
process.
Specifically, we will use MagTense [20], which is a powerful software tool used for sim-
ulating micromagnets, to examine the physical effects that play a critical role in the
dynamics of domains in micromagnets interacting with an external field. This will al-
low us to estimate the magnetic field required to magnetize the magnetic structure so
that the field generated by the structure can be detected by the NV. Then, we will
use Magpylib [21], a Python library, to simulate the magnetic field generated by the
structure with a specified magnetization. Finally, we will compare the simulation with
the measured field using an NV positioned near the disk. Based on our findings, we will
outline some potential possibilities and limitations of the proposed approach.
5.1
Conditions for a suitable magnetic
structure in spin-mechanics
In this chapter, we aim to explore the conditions required for a suitable magnetic struc-
ture in spin-mechanics. As described in Section 1.4, the coupling strength between the
mechanical oscillator and NV centers is proportional to the magnetic gradient Gm in the
direction of the oscillation x′ generated by the magnetic tip. While a higher gradient is
desirable, it is not the only factor to consider. If the magnetic field components perpen-
dicular to the NV axis are too high, the fluorescence of the NV decreases substantially.
==PAGE:68==
60
5 Magnetic properties of a thin disk
This effect is called quenching and it happens because the excited and ground optical
states of the NV have a different zero-field splitting. Therefore, the perpendicular com-
ponents of the magnetic field can mix the states |0⟩and |±1⟩differently in the excited
and ground optical states. If the mixing is too different, the optical transitions, that
preserve the spin, used to initialize the NV will not be effective. To avoid quenching, we
must ensure that the perpendicular magnetic field components Bx and By are less than
10 mT [22].
Another crucial consideration is that the gradient should be uniform near the NV. Both
the NV and the mechanical oscillator could vibrate or move slightly around a given
position during the experiment. If the gradient changes during this time, the measure-
ment will be noisy as the coupling strength would randomly change. To overcome this
challenge, we need to ensure that the gradient is uniform in a volume close to the NV.
Specifically, ∂Gm
∂x , ∂Gm
∂y , and ∂Gm

should be as small as possible.
In summary, we require a high magnetic gradient to increase the coupling strength,
small fields in the perpendicular directions to avoid quenching of the NV, and a uniform
gradient in a volume close to the NV. Achieving these conditions is not trivial.

Permalloy is classified as a soft magnet due to its tendency to align magnetic domains
to an external field. One way to conceptualize magnetic material is to imagine it as
a collection of very small domains, or microdomains, each with a magnetization M(x)
represented by an arrow with the same magnitude Ms but different direction m(x).
Therefore, the magnetization of the object can be represented as M(x) = Msm(x), where
Ms is the saturation magnetization and is a characteristic of the material, and m(x) is

a unit vector representing the direction of magnetization of each microdomain. The
overall magnetization of the structure in one direction is the average of the component
of the magnetization vectors along that direction. When all the domains are aligned,
the material will have a magnetization equal to the saturation magnetization.
However, the dynamics of the microdomains are influenced by several competing factors,
making it non-trivial to identify the conditions necessary for the disks to achieve a
high overall magnetization. These factors include magnetostatic interactions between
the domains and exchange interactions between the atoms in the material. In the next
section, we will develop a model based on simulations to study the behavior of the
magnetic domains in thin permalloy disks.

magnetic structure of our sample. The idea is to divide the object into microdomains

final configuration of the object.

Results from the simulations
In this section, we will investigate the dynamics of the domains of the thin disk to
understand the role of saturation magnetization, exchange stiffness, and shape of the
magnet. This will provide a more intuitive understanding of the types of interactions
present in the system.
We will begin by studying the dynamics of an initial state in which each domain has a
random orientation of the magnetization, and then let the system evolve with an external
magnetic field. Unfortunately, MagTense does not currently support circular grids, so
we will use a square grid with a length equal to the diameter of the disks. Our primary
focus will be on the object with a side length of 16 µm and thickness of 50 nm.
The first challenge is to determine the input parameters for the simulation, such as the
anisotropy constant, exchange stiffness, and saturation magnetization. The decay pa-
rameter α and gyroscopic ratio γ can be set through trial and error to ensure proper
convergence of the simulation. Regarding the other parameters, it is diﬀicult to deter-
mine the correct value, especially when dealing with thin magnetic films. The deposition
method for permalloy involves depositing grains onto silicon, resulting in varying direc-
tions of the easy axis for each grain. As a result, assuming that the direction of the easy
axis for each grain is random, we set Keff = 0, meaning that no crystal axis is preferred.
Furthermore, since permalloy is a soft magnet, the anisotropy constant is small anyway
and does not significantly affect the dynamics.

5.6
Discussion and outlook
Using the MagTense simulations, we anticipate that the permalloy disks will exhibit a
significant challenge in achieving out-of-plane magnetization. Instead, we expect the
magnetization to occur predominantly in-plane when applying a field of approximately
5 mT within the disk plane. With this information, we can position the disk in close
proximity to the NV center and use Magpylib simulations to predict the field that the
NV would measure.
Figure 5.13 demonstrates the consistency between the simulation results and the mea-
surements, assuming a saturation magnetization equivalent to bulk permalloy (Ms =
860 kA/m). It should be noted that the error associated with the measurements is likely
overestimated, as the estimation involves fitting the lobes using Gaussian functions,
which may not precisely be the true functional form. Despite this approximation, Gaus-
sians still provide a reasonable approximation given the computational complexity of
obtaining the analytical solution. Moreover, it was not feasible to acquire measurements
closer to the disk due to the excessive fluorescence signal emanating from the silicon
support, impeding the acquisition software’s ability to track the NV’s position during
the scan. The excessive brightness of the silicon support presents a challenge that needs
to be addressed in order to perform measurements closer to the disk.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In conclusion, this project has successfully investigated and implemented state-of-the-
art methods for sensing the motion of a mechanical oscillator with a frequency around
80 kHz.
Chapter 3 focused on characterizing the NV center by measuring the dissipation rates
of the Rabi oscillations. Through a comprehensive theoretical framework, we developed
models that accurately described the dynamics of the NV center and its interaction with
the magnetic field. The derived model, based on Bloch and Torrey’s solution equation,
accounted for dissipation processes and provided insights into the decoherence time of
the NV center. Experimental results supported the validity of the model, especially in
scenarios where the Rabi frequency approached 80 kHz. It was observed that factors
such as detuning, decoherence, and thermalization played significant roles in the sys-
tem’s dynamics and needed to be considered in a comprehensive model.
In Chapter 4, the focus shifted to the theory of pulsed sequences, with particular em-
phasis on the XY8 sequence, and a comparison was made with the dressed state sensing
approach for a mechanical oscillator. The advantages and limitations of each protocol
were discussed, with the XY8 sequence showing a better performance by a factor of 1.62.
Despite the XY8 sequence having higher harmonic issues, the dressed state sensing ap-
proach was susceptible to technical noise that could not be corrected. Hence, the XY8
sequence was chosen for the measurements. The implementation of the XY8 protocol
in the lab was successfully achieved using the new arbitrary waveform generator. Noise
characterization was performed by examining the decay time with an increasing number
of pulses in the sequence. It was observed that up to 24 π-pulses, the expected enhance-
ment of the decoherence time was observed, but for more pulses, the decoherence time
of the NV center did not increase further, consistently with previous findings. Addition-
ally, measurements were conducted to detect a signal from the signal generator with
a frequency of 500 kHz and determine the coupling strength of approximately 30 kHz.
However, due to low contrast, resolving a signal at a frequency of 80 kHz would require
an impractical measurement time. To enhance the sensing capabilities, the Qdyne pro-
tocol was implemented, leading to the successful sensing of an artificial signal at 80 kHz
with a sensitivity of 0.28 mHz and a signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of 20. The sensitivity
followed the expected T −1 dependence, while the SNR followed the expected T 0.5 de-
pendence. The resolution achieved was better than that reported in the Qdyne paper
[13], even though the SNR was lower. This discrepancy in the SNR is attributed to the
lower contrast of the target frequency in this project. Moreover, by measuring the am-

In Chapter 5, two simulation frameworks were successfully employed to reliably sim-

for verification purposes, confirming the reliability of the theoretical framework and the
success of the deposition method. Additionally, it was observed that magnetizing a thin
film out-of-plane requires significantly higher fields than what could be delivered in the
lab, whereas achieving in-plane magnetization is relatively easier. These findings laid
the foundation for further investigation into more complex structures that could be de-
posited on high-Q mechanical oscillators to achieve a high and uniform gradient along
one direction, thereby enhancing the spin-mechanical coupling without compromising
fluorescence.
In summary, this project has provided valuable insights into the characterization of NV
centers and their interaction with magnetic fields, successfully implemented state-of-the-
art quantum sensing protocols and built the base for further research and development
of novel magnetic structures in spin-mechanical systems.
