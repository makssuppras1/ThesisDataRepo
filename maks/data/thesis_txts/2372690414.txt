Abstract 
 
In this thesis a time domain poly reference technique is used to identify the modal properties of a 
system when non-linearities in the form of friction are introduced.  
First the approach is tested on a linear response with no friction using Fourier transforms on the 
signal to calculate the response. Next a discrete time state space formulation is used on the same 
linear signal in order to verify its accuracy. Friction is then added in the two translational modes 
and identification is performed again.  
The results show that despite OMA assuming linear systems modal properties can still be estimated 
with high accuracy when friction is introduced. Additionally it seems that the friction goes into the 
damping ratios of the modes that are affected by friction.

Introduction 
Most of the oil and gas platforms in the North Sea are approaching their design life time. Therefore 
there is an urgent need to improve monitoring of the existing platforms in order to estimate their 
current state to evaluate a possible life extension. 
Operational Modal Analysis is the subject of identifying the modal parameters of a system based on 
vibrational data obtained through sensors mounted around the structure in question. Once the modal 
parameters are identified the response can be extrapolated to the rest of the system and thereby 
calculating an estimate of the stress history at any given point on the structure. 
The theory of OMA is well established and for linear damping proven, through numerous 
experiments, to produce reliable results - see e.g. Skafte, A. et. al. [6] and Graugaard-Jensen, J. [7]. 
This project aims to investigate the feasibility of OMA in the non-linear domain. More specifically 
the non-linearities are introduced in the form of friction.  
 
The hypothesis is that despite OMA assuming linear systems it should still be possible to identify 
the modal parameters since what is measured on the structure is the displacement, whether there is 
friction or not.  
 
Experiments have shown that it is possible to identify modal parameters with frequency domain 
identification techniques such as FDD (Frequency Domain Decomposition) and pLSCF (poly-
reference least squares complex frequency domain). This thesis investigates identification in the 
time domain using TDPR (Time Domain Poly Reference). 
 
This thesis uses the Matlab toolbox OMA_toolbox developed by Rune Brincker as well as a 
Matlab routine developed by Tobias Friis for calculating a response using a Discrete Time State 
Space formulation.

Simulation 
 
The identification is demonstrated on a model of a platform shown in Figure 1. Channel 1-3 are the 
locations where the structure is excited by the random input signals and the corresponding response 
calculated. Fc(1) and Fc(2) in the middle of the top plate denotes where the friction force is placed in

to prevent rank deficiencies in the Mass and Stiffness matrices due to numerical constraints the legs 
are modeled with a slight variance in side lengths. This kills the axi-symmetry of the platform and 
forces the translational modes into direct x- and z-direction.

of the platform. SG1 and SG2 denotes the “Strain Gauge” placement 50 mm bellow the top plate. 
For the top plate the FE model is made with 4-node shell elements of side length 12.5mm while the 
four legs are modeled with 4-node beam elements of L = 25mm in length.

Results

Figure 8 MAC matrix for the identified modes compared to the FE modes in the linear response. 
It is clear that the identified modes correlate very well with the FE modes - all modes correlate with 
a precision over 95 %. It seems like mode 2 and 3 also has some correlation. However that is due to 
a lag of sensors. On the model you have 2 sensors in the z-direction and one in the x-direction. In 
the rotational mode the two sensors in the z-direction will pick up the rotation as they will move in 
opposite directions whereas in the x-direction the sensor will only pick up movement in one 
direction. Hence the rotation will leak into mode 2.

Conclusion 
 
In this thesis it is investigated whether it is still possible to identify modal parameters with a Time 
Domain Poly Reference technique (id_TDPR) when friction is introduced to the system. When the 
modal parameters are determined they can be extrapolated using the SEREP technique to estimate 
the strain response in any given point across the system.  
First the identification is done on a linear response calculated with FFTsim to establish a 
simulation model that is proven in the linear domain. 
Then a Discrete Time State Space (DTSS) formulation is introduced in order to calculate the non-
linear response. To verify its accuracy the method is tested on the same linear response as for 
FFTsim. Due to different assumptions in initial conditions the two methods start out very 
differently but quickly converges to the same response. 
Friction is applied such that only the two translational modes are affected and id_TDPR is 
performed on the now non-linear response.  
Results show that it is still possible, at least in a simulation, to identify the modal parameters of the 
system and calculate an accurate estimate of the strain response at an arbitrary point on the 
structure.  
Additionally it seems like the friction goes into the damping ratios of the affected modes. 
 
Future works 
It could be interesting to investigate the influence of the friction force applied - how does different 
magnitudes of the force affect the identification?  
This thesis only focuses on simulating a non-linear scenario. The obvious next step is to try the 
identification on a real model where the response comes from accelerometer-data.  
Furthermore it could be interesting to see if it is still possible to identify the modal parameters when 
the signal is disturbed by another system - e.g. if two platforms are connected through a bridge.
