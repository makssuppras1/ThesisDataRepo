Abstract
Speech intelligibility in complex acoustic environments remains a chal-
lenge for hearing impaired listeners, regardless if they are hearing-aid users
or not. One of the reasons for the deteriorated intelligibility is a poor ability
to resolve different spectral components, caused by broadening of the audi-
tory ﬁlters. The leakage of energy from neighbouring auditory ﬁlters, also
referred to as spectral masking, limits the ability to perceptually separate dif-
ferent sound sources. To reduce the impact of spectral masking, the spectral
content can be split into frequency bands that are presented alternately to
the two ears. This approach is known as binaural split. The objective of this
study was to investigate the binaural split effects on speech intelligibility and
spatial perception.
The split was implemented by dividing the input signal into 26 frequency
bands with equivalent rectangular bandwidth spacing. The odd channels
were presented to the left ear and the even channels to the right ear. Dan-
tale II and Danok speech intelligibility tests were conducted to evaluate sen-
tence and consonant recognition, respectively. Both experiments were con-
ducted in normal-hearing and hearing-impaired listeners in four conditions:
(i) unprocessed (reference) condition, (ii) split applied to both target speech
and noise, (iii) split applied only to the target speech and not to the noise, (iv)
split applied only to the noise and not to the target speech. Binaural listen-
ing abilities and the bandwidth of the auditory ﬁlter centered at 2kHz were
assessed with state-of-the-art psychoacoustic measurements and related to
speech intelligibility. Additionally, the spatial perception with and without
the binaural split was evaluated.
The main ﬁnding of this project is that the binaural split improves speech
intelligibility in hearing-impaired listeners in the sentence recognition task,
supporting the hypothesis that the split provides a release from spectral mask-
ing. In contrast, no impact of the full-split was found in normal-hearing
listeners. This may conﬁrm that spectral masking is not a limiting factor
in people with intact hearing. In both groups, speech intelligibility degra-
dation was observed when the split was applied only to the target speech
and not to the noise. A potential explanation of this effect can be found in
the central masking phenomenon, where the target signal can be masked
by noise presented contralaterally. Finally, substantial speech understanding
improvements in both listener groups were observed with the split applied
to only the noise while the speech was presented diotically. This ﬁndings are
well explained by the better-ear listening phenomenon and supported by a
computational model simulations. Similar results in all split conditions were
found in the consonant recognition test.
It was hypothesised that the beneﬁt in speech intelligibility induced by
the binaural split is correlated with the auditory ﬁlter bandwidth. Neverthe-
less, no correlation between the two measures was found. A second hypoth-
esis was that the binaural split is more beneﬁcial for speech intelligibility

when the binaural summation abilities are intact. However, no correlation
was found between the binaural summation ability measures and the binau-
ral split beneﬁt observed in speech recognition tasks.
Detrimental effects of the binaural split on sound localisation abilities
were found: the sound source was perceived farther from the original one
and image splits were introduced, i.e., the perception of additional illusion-
ary sound sources. Nevertheless, relatively good sound localisation abilities
were observed, even though the interaural level differences responsible for
sound localization are markedly disrupted when splitting the signal. This
suggests that cues other than the interaural level differences are utilised in
the sound localization process.
The overall outcome of this thesis motivates further investigations of the
binaural split as a potential implementation of hearing-aid signal processing
strategy for improving speech intelligibility.

Chapter 1
Introduction
My grandmother is 85 years old. She has a moderate age-related sensorineu-
ral hearing loss treated with two high quality hearing aids. She does not
go around without them anymore, however, I can still see her struggling
at the family events with many interfering talkers and a high-level back-
ground noise. Such environments are referred to as ’cocktail-party-situation’
(Cherry, 1953) and are particularly challenging for hearing-impaired (HI)
people, regardless if they are hearing-aid users or not. Despite the sophisti-
cated technology enclosed in a very small device, special features reducing
the noise, enhancing the speech, focusing on the target source direction, the
cocktail-party situation still remains a challenge. Good speech comprehen-
sion in all acoustic situations is fundamental for social interactions, safety,
and overall well being. This is a great motivation for improving existing
signal processing strategies or developing new ones.
When providing solutions for the HI, it is essential to understand how
normal-hearing (NH) people extract the target speech from the accompany-
ing noise. There are several known mechanisms in a healthy auditory system
allowing good speech understanding in adverse acoustic situations. These
phenomena often utilize binaural cues, i.e., differences between left and right
ear. Nevertheless, there are some mechanisms not involving interaural dif-
ferences, yet helping to resolve the cocktail-party problem.
A ﬁrst phenomenon providing better speech intelligibility in noise is re-
ferred to as a ’better-ear-listening’. Amazingly, the human brain can select
the ear with higher signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) (Edmonds and Culling, 2006)
at time-frequency tails (Jelfs et al., 2011). Secondly, the differences between
the information delivered to the left and the right channel help to localise
the sound source and subsequently understand speech in challenging situa-
tions. Time and level differences at both ears often allow spatial separation
of the target speech and the masker, helping to distinguish one from another
(Dirks and Wilson, 1969). Thirdly, the human brain can select and combine
the information from the time and frequency domain, relevant for speech un-
derstanding. Warren et al. (1995) showed that the information from different
frequency regions can be combined for better speech intelligibility. Further-
more, the plasticity of the human brain allows ﬁlling-in the gaps of missing
speech portions. Miller and Licklider (1950) showed that halving the content
of the individual words by introducing a phoneme-size silence intervals still
allows 80% of the word recognition.

These properties of the human brain are crucial for solving the cocktail
party problem in both NH and HI listeners. Certainly, the mechanisms of the
healthy auditory system that allow good speech comprehension in adverse
acoustic scenarios are complex and sophisticated. Nonetheless, investigating
them may be advantageous for developing strategies aimed at improving
speech understanding in HI listeners.
The redundancy of the speech signal utilized by NH people for solving
the cocktail-party problem does not seem to be useful in HI individuals. In
the results of speech audiometry in quiet, a roll-over at high presentation
levels can be observed in HI listeners. This reduced speech recognition score
at the high levels suggests that the speech signal may mask itself. French
and Steinberg (1947) described the phenomenon of the ’inter-band masking
of speech’ where the signal in one peripheral frequency region is masked by
the speech from other frequency regions. According to this theory, the sound
from the low-frequency bands ’leaks’ into the higher-frequency areas. In this
thesis, it is hypothesised that the modiﬁcation of the spectral content of the
signal may reduce the inter-band masking. This is realised by applying a
binaural split. The binaural split refers to dividing the signal into a number
of frequency bands and presenting the odd channels to one ear and even
channels to the other ear. By applying the split, the spectral content of the
signal of each ear is reduced by half: what would normally be presented to
both ears, with the binaural split is presented to either one. On each side, the
leakage from the neighbouring channels is reduced by removing the spectral
energy from those channels. What may seem paradoxical, using only half
of the spectral content of the sound as an input to the human brain may
improve speech intelligibility in noisy environments.
Previous studies have shown that dividing the spectral content of the
sound between the two ears improves speech intelligibility. Lunner et al.
(1993) divided the original signal into 8 frequency bands of approximately
700 Hz bandwidth with a good separation between stop/pass bands (Figure
1.1 top right) and presented alternately to both ears. The results of Hagerman
speech-in-noise test on three HI listeners showed an improvement of 2 dB in
speech reception threshold (SRT), deﬁned as the SNR at which 50% of the
words were correctly recognised. Chaudhari and Pandey (1999) used a pair
of critical-bandwidth-wide comb ﬁlters with complementary magnitude re-
sponses (Figure 1.1, middle left) and increased band separation. This design
led to an improvement between 5 and 9.5% in consonant recognition scores
for NH listeners with a simulated hearing loss. In a similar test, Cheeran and
Pandey (2004) increased the spectral separation of the processing bands (Fig-
ure 1.1, middle right) and observed an advantage between 7 and 20%. The
further work of Kulkarni et al. (2012) investigated the difference between a
split based on ﬁxed-bandwidth and auditory critical bandwidth (CB) (Fig-
ure 1.1, bottom) in NH listeners with simulated hearing loss as well as in
listeners with moderate bilateral hearing loss. In both ﬁlter designs, the di-
chotic split provided up to 31% improvement in the consonant recognition

These outcomes suggest that changes of the signal processing parameters
aimed to increase the stop/pass band separation may lead to even larger
improvement of speech intelligibility.
However, it is not yet understood
why the binaural split leads to improved results. A possible explanation
suggested in earlier studies is that the split releases spectral masking. Nev-
ertheless, no further measures were taken to verify this. Additionally, it was
expected that the binaural split disturbs the spatial perception of the sound
source, as the natural binaural differences utilised in the sound localization
process are disrupted in the split condition. The accuracy of the source direc-
tion was investigated by Kulkarni et al. (2012), but without considering the
image split phenomenon, that is the perception of additional illusory sound
sources. The selective separation of the spectral content between the two
ears provides contradicting spatial cues across frequency channels, therefore
potentially leading to the perception of multiple sound sources. Further-
more, the impact of the split was mostly veriﬁed with consonant recognition
experiments. A more ecological assessment of speech intelligibility might
be obtained with stimuli that are closer to real-life. Finally, the impact of the
binaural split on the general sound quality was not investigated in the pre-
vious studies. It would be relevant for the potential application of the split
to verify whether the sound quality of the split signal is perceived negatively.
Based on the results and the limitations of the previous studies, the fol-
lowing hypotheses have been made:
1. The binaural split reduces spectral masking in HI listeners.
2. A reduction in spectral masking improves consonant recognition and
word recognition scores in HI listeners.
3. The beneﬁt of spectral split is related to the individual auditory proﬁle
measurements, namely the auditory ﬁlters bandwidth and the ability
to merge the right and the left ear signals.
4. The binaural split disrupts spatial perception and introduces sound im-
age splits.
5. The binaural split degrades general sound quality.
Several psycho-acoustic tasks were employed to verify the hypothe-
ses stated above.
Speech comprehension was assessed with a consonant
recognition test to conﬁrm the ﬁndings of previous studies. Additionally,
the assessment of speech intelligibility test was extended to more realistic
speech stimuli by including a sentence recognition experiment. An addi-
tional monaural speech intelligibility test was conducted to examine the
presence of inter-band speech masking. Auditory ﬁlter bandwidths were
estimated to assess the possible effects of spectral masking. Moreover, to
utilize the binaural split feature, listeners need to be able to merge the input
from left and right channel into one coherent sound. This ability was veriﬁed
with two binaural experiments. Eventually, a spatial perception experiment

Chapter 2
Theory
The human hearing apparatus is a complex system where several physi-
ological mechanisms operate together in the processing of input sounds.
The physics of such processes and their organization as a whole structure
is only partially demonstrated and understood, with evidence often sup-
ported by experiments on mammals or by psycho-acoustic experimental
investigation. Some of these mechanisms can be well explained in terms
of theoretical models and signal processing methods. In this chapter, the
relevant background knowledge on the signal-processing mechanisms of the
human auditory system and the related psycho-acoustic theories necessary
for a better understanding of the analysed topic are presented.
2.1
Hearing process in short
The acoustic wave emitted by a sound source reaches the outer ear where
it is ﬁltered by the pinna. It is farther funneled along the ear canal to the
eardrum and transformed into mechanical vibrations ampliﬁed by the set of
the ossicular bones in the middle ear. The mechanical vibrations are trans-
ferred to the basilar membrane (BM) located in the cochlea. The non-uniform
stiffness of the BM makes it responsive to different frequencies in different re-
gions. When a BM portion vibrates in response to an input sound, hair cells
activated in that region convert a mechanical vibration into a neural signal.
The neural representation of the input stimulus is then transferred along a
complex neural network, where it is further processed, ﬁnally reaching the
higher cognitive regions of the auditory pathway.
2.2
Frequency selectivity and spectral masking
The ability of the mammalian auditory system to differentiate various spec-
tral components of a complex sound is referred to as frequency selectivity
(Moore, 2012). As a result of the tonotopic characteristic of the basilar mem-
brane, that is frequency-to-place mapping of each spectral component, dif-
ferent regions of the cochlea dynamically respond to different frequencies.
This process can be interpreted as a mechanical-to-electrical transformation
of the incoming signal, changing it from the time domain to the frequency

domain. The locally-tuned motion of the BM is transformed into a neural
signal which transfers frequency selectivity along the higher levels of the au-
ditory pathway. Frequency selectivity is crucial for accomplishing several
auditory tasks: it supports separating out the sounds originating from dif-
ferent sources, for example different instruments playing simultaneously in
a big band or a talk with a noisy background (Plack, 2018). Intuitively, if fre-
quency selectivity is impaired, it may lead to a poor sound separation and,
what is most often experienced, difﬁculties in understanding speech in noisy
environments.
Frequency selectivity is often researched with tone-in-noise detection ex-
periments, which measure the amount of masking, i.e., increase in detection
threshold of a pure tone signal in the presence of noise. Helmholtz (1863);
Von Békésy and Wever (1960); Fletcher (1940) suggested that the frequency
selectivity phenomenon of the human peripheral auditory signal processing
can be described by means of a set of ﬁlters tuned to speciﬁc frequencies with
overlapping pass-bands. This approach, later investigated and conﬁrmed by
other researchers (Patterson, 1976; Moore and Glasberg, 1983; Glasberg and
Moore, 1990), laid the foundation to the concept of auditory ﬁlter and audi-
tory ﬁlter-bank.
There are different methods of estimating the shape and the bandwidth of
the auditory ﬁlters. The band-widening experiment introduced by Fletcher
(1940) is a simple experiment belonging to the class of tone-in-noise detec-
tion paradigms. It assumes a symmetrical, rectangular shape of the auditory
ﬁlters. The detection threshold of a pure tone is measured as a function of
the bandwidth of a noise band centered around the tone frequency. Figure
2.1 shows the results of a tone in noise detection task for 2 kHz stimuli. The
masking effect on the tone increases for noise bandwidths up to 400 Hz and
saturates above.
This phenomenon is explained by introducing the "power spectrum
model" (Patterson, 1976). The model assumes that only the noise passing
through the auditory ﬁlter centered at the tone frequency contributes to
masking. More speciﬁcally, it states that the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) at
the output of the auditory ﬁlter centered a the tone frequency is constant.
Therefore, the tone-detection threshold increases as a function of noise band-
width until a value referred to as critical band (CB). If the noise bandwidth
exceeds the CB, it has no effect on the tone detection threshold as the noise
energy falling into the auditory ﬁlter does not increase. Fletcher’s theory uses
a strong assumption on the rectangular shape of the auditory ﬁlters which is
conﬁrmed not to be valid. The auditory ﬁlter shape can be estimated for ex-
ample by using the notched-noise method where the tone is presented in the
spectral gap of the noise (details in section 3.6). The estimated shape of the
auditory ﬁlter is often approximated by a rounded exponential (roex) ﬁlter -
with rounded top and relatively steep slopes. When the ﬁlter shape is not of
interest, but only its bandwidth is, a measure called equivalent rectangular
bandwidth (ERB) can be used. It speciﬁes the bandwidth of the rectangular
ﬁlter with the same minimum attenuation and the same area as the analysed
ﬁlter with non-vertical slopes. Based on notched-noise experimental data on

2.3
Physiology of the binaural auditory system
Understanding the physiology of the auditory system helps to further in-
terpret binaural hearing phenomena and potentially explain some of the ob-
served results. The neural connections between the periphery and the higher,
cognitive stages of the auditory system form a rather complex, and yet not
fully understood, processing system. De facto, the left and right channels
of the hearing apparatus do not work as separate units but as a uniﬁed sys-
tem. Receiving and processing acoustic stimuli with two ears is referred to
as ’binaural hearing’, in contrast to the ’monaural hearing’ where the signal
is received by only one ear. Binaural hearing can be further categorised to
’diotic’, when the signal reaching left and right ear is exactly the same (e.g.,
mono recordings played back over both channels of a pair of headphones),
and ’dichotic’ when the signals reaching the two ears are different.
The mechanical processes that take place in the periphery transform the in-
put sounds into a neural signal that is provided to the central auditory sys-
tem. Figure 2.2 shows the left half of the central auditory pathway schematics
leading to the right auditory cortex. In reality, all the structures are symmet-
rical and the same schematics can be drawn for the right ear (Middlebrooks,
2015).
The auditory nerve ﬁbers create three terminal regions with the Cochlear
Nuclei (CN). Within each region, the frequency-speciﬁc organisation of the
input sound is preserved. On each side of the brain, the CN represents three
maps of frequencies. This complex spectral representation is believed to sup-
port up-down and front-back sound source localisation through the spectral
differences between sounds coming from different directions, emerging from
different ﬁltering by the pinna. The output of the CN is further directed to the
contralateral side of the brain forming three major structures: the ’trapezoid
body’, the ’intermediate acoustic stria’ and the ’dorsal acoustic stria’ which
terminates either in the contralateral inferior colliculus (IC) or the superior
olivary complex. The latter is known to be the ﬁrst stage of the binaural
convergence. The superior olivary complex is composed of several perioli-
vary nuclei as well as three main nuclei: the medial nucleus of the trapezoid
body (MNTB), the medial superior olive (MSO) and the lateral superior olive
(LSO). The last two components play a crucial role in the sound localisation
in the horizontal plane. Here the time and the level differences between the

2.4
Binaural listening and spatial perception
The ability for distinguishing the sound source direction is highly dependent
on binaural hearing. Only with two ears it is possible to utilise so called
interaural time differences (ITD) and interaural level differences (ILD)
==PAGE:24==
12
Chapter 2. Theory
(Moore, 2012).
The ITD is a result of different distance between the lateral sound source
and both ears. The ear closer to the sound source receives the signal prior to
the farther ear. The time differences are more salient in the low frequencies
and extent up to ±700µs. Amazingly, the sensitivity to the time difference is
as small as ±10µs which is 33 000 times shorter than an average eye blink.
The ITD is equivalent to the interaural phase difference (IPD) and in low
frequencies provides unambiguous information about the sound source
location. However, ambiguity starts when the period of the sound wave
is two times longer than the maximum ITD, i.e.
1400µs corresponding to
715 Hz.
The ILD is a result of the acoustic shadow effect on the opposite side
from the sound source.
In other words, the head is seen as an acoustic
obstacle attenuating the acoustic wave. This binaural cue is mostly exploited
in frequencies above 3kHz when the average head size is larger than the
wavelength and when only little diffraction of the sound is observed,
subsequently causing larger attenuation of the sound. The magnitude of the
ILD can be as big as 20dB in high frequencies.
Employing both ITD and ILD mechanisms for the sound localisation is
referred to as a ’duplex theory’. Recently, Ahrens et al. (2019) showed that
while the time differences are indeed utilised only in low frequencies, the
level differences provide the localisation cues across frequencies. Moreover,
it was shown that various frequency bands have different weighting for lo-
calising and lateralizing the sound source. It was shown that the ERB-wide
band with a center frequency of 442 Hz and 5544 Hz have signiﬁcantly high-
est weight in terms of ITDs and ILDs respectively. This is in line with the
duplex theory. However, various weights of the different frequency bands
may determine the spatial perception of the spectrally dispersed sound. This
is valid in case of the applied spectral split. Deciding on which ear receives
the lowest and the highest frequency band may determine how the spatial
picture is skewed.
It should be noted that the phenomena described above relate mostly to
the sound localisation in a horizontal plane and do not consider the source
elevation, its size or externalization, i.e., whether the sound is perceived
inside or outside the listener’s head.
Additionally, there exists a pinna
effect providing localisation cues based on the spectral ﬁltering done by the
physical properties of the ear. Humans can also beneﬁt from the source and
head movements for more accurate sound localisation.
Moreover, binaural hearing does not only help with the sound localisa-
tion. It has been shown that having two ears enables humans to hear better
in challenging, cocktail-party situations. The basic example of the binaural
contribution to the sound recognition in noise is the phenomenon of the bin-
aural masking level difference (BMLD) (Moore, 2012). When the tone and

spatial separation of various sources improves detection and recognition of

The spatial perception is also inﬂuenced by the access to the visual cues
(Moore, 2012). It seems that the brain combines inputs from various senses.
For example, in a reverberant room, the level and the time differences be-
tween direct and reﬂected sounds are combined with a visual information
on the distance between the head and the wall and the sound source. This
together helps to resolve the sound source location. Gil-Carvajal et al. (2016)
showed that visual cues in psychoacoustic experiments lead to more accurate
spatial localisation of the audio stimuli.
Another phenomenon originated from the binaural hearing is a ’better-
ear listening’ effect (Edmonds and Culling, 2006). It was shown that humans
can automatically utilize the ear with the better SNR. Moreover, the better-
band theory, being an extension of the previous one, states that this can be
done in independent frequency channels (Jelfs et al., 2011). With this ability
intact, target speech can be easier understood. This approach is used in many
models for speech intelligibility prediction with a great success. It has been
shown that in HI individuals, the better-ear listening effect is not reduced
(Best et al., 2015).
2.5
Speech signal and its intelligibility
According to the Oxford Research Encyclopedia (Leben, 2018) there are about
7000 languages spoken around the world. Undeniably, speech is the main
communication mode between humans. The signal produced by the vocal
==PAGE:26==
14
Chapter 2. Theory
tract of one person and received and interpreted by another person is the
most important stimuli for our auditory system. Aristotle said that ’Man
is by nature a social animal’ and excellent speech understanding is crucial
in social interactions. Despite of the language, the speech signal is one of
the most complicated sounds to be comprehended by people. This is due to
its complexity in terms of spectral content, periodicity, prosody, variability
in terms of levels or timbres, from person to person, from one time to an-
other (Moore, 2012). Many external factors contribute to the ability of decent
speech understanding and even small changes of the internal (e.g. accent,
prosody, coarticulation) or the external (e.g. interfering noise, distortions)
parameters may cause detrimental effects. It is valid to ask the question how
it is possible to comprehend the speech even if part of it is missing or ob-
scured. Speech is known to be a highly redundant signal - it contains more
information than is actually needed for good intelligibility (Moore, 2012). De-
spite changes to the original signal, like spectral smearing imitating loss of
frequency selectivity or substantial ﬁltering of the signal emulating sensitiv-
ity loss, the speech can still be well understood. There is a certain level of
temporal redundancy described by Drullman et al. (1994) showing that re-
moving temporal modulations above 4Hz has little effect on speech intelligi-
bility. Warren et al. (1995) found that human brain can combine information
from different frequency areas. In their experiment the speech signal was ﬁl-
tered with two narrow band pass ﬁlters with a center frequency of 370 Hz
and 6000 Hz. While presented separately, the speech was barely intelligible,
when presented together the word recognition score increased by factor ex-
ceeding 2. This suggests that the brain can selectively combine important in-
formation for better speech comprehension. Additionally, according to stud-
ies by Miller and Licklider (1950), even when the speech content is halved
by introducing phoneme-size intervals, the word recognition can reach 80%.
This amazing ability of the brain is utilised by humans everyday in adverse
acoustical situation and can be successfully employed to improve speech in-
telligibility.
What is more, speech understanding extends way beyond the physical
properties of the signal (Moore, 2012). Context, for instance, extends beyond
the auditory domain, playing substantial role in the speech comprehension.
Knowing the topic of the conversation or associating certain themes with spe-
ciﬁc words may improve general understanding as some of the speech that
was missed out can be deducted. Another important aid during a conversa-
tion is a visual cue. This may be an observation of the environment and/or
lip reading ability.
It can be seen that dealing with a speech signal is extremely complex.
With many factors to consider and plenty of unexplained phenomena hap-
pening in human brain make the research very comprehensive. On the other
hand, for the same reasons, many conclusions can be drawn based on the
speech intelligibility results.

Participants
All experiments were conducted on two listener groups: NH and HI. Ten

otoscopic examination followed by a pure tone audiometry.

using balanced latin-square design to counterbalance the order of presenta-
tions for the experimental blocks and subsequently avoid the order effects.
Additionally, a subjective sound quality was assessed via online question-
naire.

3.7
Measures of the binaural summation abilities
The objective of the binaural summation tests is to assess how well the lis-
tener can integrate the inputs from the left and the right ear. Since the bin-
aural split involves dichotic presentation of the signals and merging them
into one coherent sound, it is relevant to assess whether the test subjects can
do it successfully. There were two experiments employed, namely interaural
phase difference (IPD) and binaural pitch (BP). Both tests are part of Better
Hearing Rehabilitation (BEAR) test battery developed to cluster hearing im-
paired people and ﬁt them more accurately with hearing aids. The original
BEAR script was used to run IPD and BP tests.
==PAGE:34==
22
Chapter 3. Methods
3.7.1
Interaural phase difference
The IPD test allows obtaining the highest frequency at which the phase dif-
ference of π between two ears is still perceivable. The IPD discrimination is
a measure of binaural temporal accuracy (King et al., 2014) and it was em-
ployed to assess the listeners’ ability for processing the input from both ears.
King et al. (2014) showed that the IPD discrimination is declined by the sen-
sorineural hearing loss and the age, not only by the binaural abilities. Con-
sidering the average age of the NH and HI groups (24.6 and 41.1 respectively)
and the average audiometric thresholds, it is expected to see the difference
between the two groups.
To accurately estimate the highest frequency at which the IPD is perceiv-
able, 2-AFC procedure was employed. Listeners were presented with two
sound intervals - one with binaural diotic presentation of the tonal stimuli
and the other one with alternating dichotic and diotic, phase-reversed pre-
sentation. The phase reversal in one channel causes the impression of the
sound alternating between the ears. The listeners were asked to select the
interval with the alternating presentation. The tone frequency was adapted
during the procedure with a one-up-two-down tracking rule to estimate the
70.7% point of the psychometric function (Levitt,1971). The experiment was
conducted at the level of 35 dB SL, since it was shown (Hopkins and Moore,
2010) that the IPD discrimination is independent on the level above 30 dB SL.
The IPD sensitivity was measured on both left and right ear.
3.7.2
Binaural pitch
Alongside the IPD test, the BP test is another way to evaluate the ability of
merging two sounds from the left and the right ear necessary to utilize the
binaural split. It was shown that the reduced frequency selectivity negatively
impacts the perception of the BP due to the distorted internal representation
of the interaural-correlation spectrum produced by a binaural signal (San-
turette and Dau, 2007). The deterioration of the BP perception is not de-
pendent on the hearing sensitivity. However, spectro-temporal deﬁcits con-
tribute to decreased performance. It was shown (Santurette and Dau, 2007)
that some hearing impaired listeners may perform as normal hearing, thus
this test may not be found unequivocal.
The experiment was conducted with 10 diotic and 10 dichotic, randomly
allocated, 3-tone melodies (C5-E5-G5) in noise. The diotic melodies are pos-
sible to be detected monaurally.
The dichotic melodies are generated by
adding a phase-difference patterns to the constant noise presented to both
ears throughout the experiment. This is known to create a perception of pitch
(Cramer and Huggins, 1958). The melodies presented dichotichally can only
be detected if the binaural abilities are unharmed. The TAs were asked to in-
dicate with a mouse-click when the 3-tone melody was heard. The Receiver
Operating Characteristic (ROC) points were established based on the per-
centage of the correct responses and the false alarms. The ROC describes the
performance of each listener based on their internal criterion.

3.8
Speech intelligibility
The ultimate goal of the signal processing strategies is to obtain good speech
understanding for people with auditory deﬁcits. Therefore, two speech in-
telligibility tests in four conditions were included in the assessment of the
binaural split of the spectral content of the sound.
3.8.1
Word recognition score measured with Dantale II
It is important to assess the speech intelligibility with a test material close to
real-life situation. For this reason, Dantale II test was employed. Dantale II
(Wagener et al., 2003) is known as a Danish version of Swedish Hagerman
or Oldenburg sentence tests (OLSA). This speech in noise test is designed to
determine the speech reception threshold (SRT), i.e. the signal-to-noise ratio
providing 50% intelligibility. All Dantale II sentences have the same syntac-
tical structure consisting a name, a verb, a numeral, and adjective and an
object. Figure 3.5 shows the matrix used for the self assessment. The sen-
tences in the test are semantically unpredictable (nonsense) which prevents
the listeners from memorising them.
FIGURE 3.5: Word response matrix used for self-assessment in
Dantale II experiment. Participants were asked to select one
word from each column to reproduce the sentence presented
over the headphones.

One round of Dantale II test was run prior the experiment to familiarise
the listeners with the response matrix. This also reduces the training effect
for the further rounds by 1 dB (Hernvig and Olsen, 2005). Two out of 16 exist-
ing lists with 10 sentences each were presented for each condition described
in section 3.5.1. The initial level of the speech was set to 65 dB SPL (sound
pressure level deﬁned as 20 ∗log10( p
p0) where p is a root mean square sound
pressure and p0 is a reference sound pressure of 20 µ Pa ) and remained
constant throughout the entire experiment for NH listeners. NAL-R ratio-
nale was implemented to apply the frequency dependent ampliﬁcation for
HI listeners as described in section 3.4. SNR was changed by adjusting the
level of the noise with an adaptive procedure based on Brand and Kollmeier
(2002). Most of the standard adaptive procedures, like forced-choice or up-
down transformed response, allow only the binary response, for example
’true’ and ’false’ or ’correct’ and ’incorrect’. However, Dantale II test allows
scoring each word in the sentence independently. Adjusting the step size
based on the percentage of the correctly recognised words in each sentence
helps to optimise the time of the experiment. The step size (∆L[dB]) is calcu-
lated from equation 3.5.
∆L = f (i) · (prev −tar)
slope
(3.5)
Where ’prev’ is a discrimination value obtained in the previous sentence,
’tar’ is a target convergence value set to 0.5 corresponding to 50% intelli-

Dantale II is set to 0.126 (Wagener et al., 2003). f(i) deﬁnes the rate of con-
vergence and it is deﬁned as fi = 1.5 · 1.41−iterationi, calculated for each
iteration. For the ﬁrst iteration calculated value of f(1) is above one provid-
ing the big step size and converging towards smaller values with proceeding
experiment. This ensures that the presentation levels stabilise near the target.
Speech shaped speech modulated noise (SSSMN) was used as a masker.
First, a white noise with randomised phase was generated as a carrier noise.
The carrier was convoluted with an envelope of each sentence creating indi-
vidual noise for each sentence.
3.8.2
Consonant recognition score measured with Danok
Very often speech intelligibility tests look at the macroscopic view of words
or sentence recognition with a certain acoustic disturbance. It is a good ap-
proach to estimate closer-to-real-life comprehension. However, these mea-
sures do not look into smaller speech tokens. Moreover, with a stimuli close
to real speech, the missing acoustic information can be restored with a con-
text, semantic predictability or lexical information. It may be interesting to
assess the speech comprehension in a more microscopic view. By remov-
ing higher-level semantic and lexical cues, it is possible to focus more on
the acoustical transmission channels. This is possible when using a non-
sense stimuli, like a combination of consonants and vowels. By assessing

the consonant recognition, with their emphasised high-frequency-energy na-
ture, it is possible to look at the acoustic transmission of the important high-
frequency speech components. Since the binaural split, investigated in this
project, disturbs the frequency distribution in a very unnatural way, it is in-
teresting to look at the microscopic impact on the speech portions. For these
reasons the Danish nonsense test (Danok) was employed.
Danok test contains a consonant-vowel-consonant-vowel (CVCV) struc-
tures where the vowels are given and the listeners are asked to select the
consonants they hear. Figure 3.6 shows the user interface used in this exper-
iment. The TAs are asked to choose the ﬁrst consonants from the ﬁrst group
and the second consonant from the second one and guess if they could not

when the consonants were not heard at all.

Test round is conducted at the beginning of the experiment to make the

one SNR of -3dB for NH listeners and +3dB for HI. The SNR values were

recognition score (Wiinberg et al., 2018), where the small difference in the
stimuli could yield higher beneﬁt due to the steepness of the psychometric
curve. Additionally, it helps to avoid ﬂoor and ceiling effects in the four con-
ditions.

3.11
Models used for speech intelligibility predic-
tion
There are several models created to predict speech intelligibility in various
conditions. Two models were employed in this project for hypothesis state-
ment, selection of the test parameters and better results explanation.
3.11.1
Jelfs, Culling, Lavandier model as a better-ear listen-
ing predictor
The model by Jelfs, Culling, Lavandier provides an energetic analysis of the
input signal compared between the two ears. The model investigates the
properties of the input signal without taking into account the signal process-
ing at the higher stages of the auditory pathway. It is relevant for this project
to look into the physical properties of the stimuli and its differences between
the two ears. The model was set up to use a similar method for dividing the
==PAGE:40==
28
Chapter 3. Methods
signal into frequency channels, to the method used in the binaural split im-
plementation. This allows studying the impact of the split on the energetic
properties of the stimuli across frequencies.
Jelfs’, Culling’s, Lavandier’s model was designed to predict a binaural
speech intelligibility in reverberant rooms (Jelfs et al., 2011). Even though
the reverberant environment is not the topic of this project, the model uses
simple concepts of binaural unmasking and the better-ear listening. The bin-
aural unmasking in the model is calculated based on the Equalisation Can-
cellation theory which utilizes the interaural phase difference. However, the
binaural split does not introduce a time, and subsequently a phase difference
between the ears, hence there is no binaural unmasking beneﬁt computed by
the model. The better-ear listening is calculated as a weighted sum of the
SNRs in 26-ERB wide channels, corresponding to the frequencies 0-10kHz.
The weights are taken from the Speech Intelligibility Index (SII, ANSI-S3.5,
1997) where the SNR values are weighted based on the importance of the
frequency band for speech intelligibility. The better SNR between two ears
is selected across all frequencies and taken for the weighted sum calculation.
The model assumes that the binaural unmasking and the better-ear listening
mechanisms function independently, thus the second part of the model can
be successfully utilised separately.
20 concatenated Dantale II sentences with their corresponding SSSMN
were used as an input to the model. The sound ﬁles were calibrated to rep-
resent -10 dB SNR, based on the experimental results of Dantale II test with-
out the split in NH listeners. Number of channels was set to 26 ERB-spaced
bands with center frequencies matching the binaural split implementation.
The SRT predictions can be found in section 4.5.1.
3.11.2
Hearing-Aid Speech Perception Index (HASPI) model
The HASPI model predicts speech intelligibility using the auditory periphery
model and subsequently taking into account the impact of the hearing loss
(Kates and Arehart, 2014). It also considers various distortions, like back-
ground noise, frequency compression, noise reduction algorithm and other
features impacting the original signal. The input to the HASPI model is only
one-channel sound ﬁle, thus the predicted speech intelligibility with the bin-
aural split is given for the left and right channel separately. 20 concatenated
Dantale II sentences and the corresponding SSSMNs in fours signal process-
ing conditions, audiogram for NH and the audiogram averaged across all HI
listeners tested in this experiment was used as an input to the model. The
predicted binaural split beneﬁt is shown in section 4.5.2.

Chapter 5
Analysis and discussion
This chapter contains in-depth discussion and possible explanations of the
results obtained in this thesis. The impact of the binaural split on speech in-
telligibility, spatial perception, sound quality and interactions between these
measures are analysed.
5.1
Frequency selectivity is degraded in hearing-
impaired listeners
Wider auditory ﬁlters are measured in HI than in NH listeners (Figure 4.1).
The difference is consistent with the existing literature (Plack, 2018; Hopkins
and Moore, 2010). In fact, it is expected that the auditory ﬁlters broaden with
increased audiometric threshold. This difference, though not statistically sig-
niﬁcant, indicates that the frequency selectivity is decreased in the tested HI
listeners. The NH listeners exhibit similar auditory ﬁlter bandwidths, vary-
ing within a small range. On the contrary, the results for HI listeners display
a larger standard deviation. It is reasonable to ascribe this wider variability
as a consequence of the heterogeneity of the HI group, which exhibit vari-
ous degrees of the hearing loss. In the HI group, four outlying results can
be seen: one listener with a very broad auditory ﬁlter (HI8) and three lis-
teners with close-to-normal auditory ﬁlter bandwidth (HI6, HI9 and HI10).
Such severe broadening of the auditory ﬁlter in HI8 with a mild-to-moderate
hearing loss (PTA = 33.75 dB HL) is not expected according to the literature.
It may suggest that the notched-noise experimental data did not provide a
good estimation of the auditory ﬁlter bandwidth. It may be caused by lack of
attention during the experiment and it should be repeated for higher reliabil-
ity. The results of the notched-noise experiment in HI10 with a mild hearing
loss (PTA = 35 dB HL) are not surprising. However, it was expected to ob-
serve broader auditory ﬁlters in HI6 (PTA = 64.38 dB HL) and HI9 (PTA =
44.38 dB HL) due to the severity of the hearing loss. It should be noted that
the age of these two listeners was 21 and 23 years old, respectively. Patterson
et al. (1982) showed that the auditory ﬁlters broaden across frequencies as
a function of age, thus sharper frequency tuning is usually found in young
NH listeners. Nevertheless, accompanying hearing loss was expected to neg-
atively impact the frequency selectivity.

5.2
Binaural summation abilities
Earlier in this thesis, it was hypothesised that better binaural summation abil-
ities correspond to higher beneﬁt from the binaural split. The ability to in-
tegrate the input from left and right ear was determined in this project by
means of interaural phase difference (IPD) and binaural pitch (BP) assess-
ment, whose results are discussed as follows.
5.2.1
Interaural phase difference experiment reveals de-
graded temporal ﬁne structure sensitivity in hearing
impaired listeners
The results of the IPD experiment in NH listeners differ between left and
right ear. This is most likely due to a training effect, since the right ear was
always tested ﬁrst. Therefore the maximum frequency where the IPD is per-
ceived in the right ear is lower compared to the left ear. However, this differ-
ence is not observed in the HI listeners, suggesting that they are less prone
to improve the IPD discrimination with practice. Nevertheless, this result is
not of interest in this study, thus, the average values between the ears in both
groups were used for further analysis.
The IPD discrimination is, on average, poorer for HI listeners compared to
NH. This is consistent with the existing literature (King et al., 2014) showing
that the temporal ﬁne structure sensitivity deteriorates with the sensorineu-
ral hearing loss. Moreover, previous literature (King et al., 2014) manifests the
negative impact of the age on the IPD discrimination. The average age for HI
listeners in this experiment is higher than the NH group (41.1 vs 24.6 years
old), hence this is another factor supporting the difference between the two
listener groups. This conﬁrms that binaural temporal integration abilities in
HI listeners are diminished (King et al., 2014). As for other experiments, the
large standard deviation in HI listeners originates from the wide range of age
and hearing loss severity within this group. The IPD experiment is generally
considered a good measure of binaural listening abilities, even though it is
an experimental paradigm that operates on time-domain characteristics of
the stimulus and not on frequency-domain ones.
5.2.2
Binaural pitch results are not conclusive
The binaural pitch experiment did not reveal statistically signiﬁcant differ-
ences between NH and HI listeners, nor between the diotic and dichotic pre-
sentation conditions. Only two HI listeners did not perceive 100% of the
dichotic pitch presentations. However, it is reported in literature that the
perception of BP is impeded with hearing loss (Santurette and Dau, 2007).
The present results are not aligned with the literature and can not be consid-
ered conclusive. More HI listeners, with more diverse hearing loss proﬁles,
and subsequently more variable results in BP detection should be tested to
obtain more solid results that can conﬁrm or reject the relationship between
hearing loss and binaural pitch detection.

5.3
Speech intelligibility experiments
To assess the consonant and word recognition abilities, Dantale II and Danok
speech intelligibility tests were conducted with the unprocessed speech ma-
terial and in three split conditions. The results of the experiments are dis-
cussed below. It was hypothesised that release of spectral masking, better-
ear listening and central masking phenomena can successfully explain the
ﬁndings.
5.3.1
Dantale II
A difference between NH and HI listener results was observed (Figure
4.4). Despite the sensitivity compensation applied for HI listeners, a poorer
performance was observed, indicating that the deterioration of the speech
intelligibility in noise does not originate exclusively in the sensitivity loss,
but may also be caused by the ﬂawed spectro-temporal resolution and/or
cognitive abilities, for which no compensation was provided in this experi-
ment.
Normal hearing listeners do not beneﬁt from the binaural split
In the Dantale II results obtained in NH listeners, no difference was found
between the SRT measured in the unprocessed and the full-split conditions.
The fact that the binaural split did not affect speech recognition in NH lis-
teners could indicate that spectral masking was not a limiting factor for this
group of listeners, at least at moderate presentation levels.
A degradation of the speech intelligibility in the split-speech condition
was observed in NH listeners. A plausible explanation can be found in the
phenomenon of central masking. The central masking theory is based on the
additivity of the right and left neural input at the superior olive stage and
higher levels of the auditory pathway (see section 2.3). A main assumption of
this theory is that a binaural mixing process takes place at this stage as a lin-
ear summation of the neural inputs from left and right ears (Zwislocki, 1972).
A consequence of this cross-interaction process is that the signal presented to
one ear can mask the signal presented contralaterally and can support the
following explanation of the observed elevated SRT in the split-speech con-
dition. The speech spectrum was selectively divided between left and right
ear in alternating ERB-wide channels, interspersed by channels with noise
only. For every channel containing speech and noise in one ear, the corre-
sponding contralateral channel contained noise only. In the neural represen-
tation at the output of the hypothesised binaural mixing stage, the noise from
the contralateral channel may contribute to masking the speech signal on the
ipsilateral channel containing speech information. The overall result of this
process was an elevated SRT in speech-split condition.
It was hypothesised that in the split-noise condition the better-ear listen-
ing was utilised by the listeners. While the noise was presented to every
==PAGE:58==
46
Chapter 5. Analysis and discussion
second channel on one side and the speech was presented in all, every sec-
ond channel on each side contained clear speech and therefore high SNR.
Thus, human brain could select the favorable SNR between both ears and
utilise the clean speech signal. Subsequently, the overall SRT was lower, i.e.,
the speech intelligibility was improved.
An additional analysis of the better-ear listening effect in support of this
hypothesis is proposed in section 4.5.1 based on computational model simu-
lations.
Hearing impaired listeners beneﬁted from the binaural split and from the
better-ear listening
The difference between the SRT obtained with the unprocessed sound and
the full-split was not signiﬁcant.
However, the trend seen in ﬁgure 4.4
showed speech intelligibility improvements in the second condition. It was
hypothesised that the improvement seen in the individual results was caused
by reduced spectral masking and the consequent reduction of inter-band
masking. The signal from an ’empty’ channel did not have any masking
effect on the neighbouring frequency regions, hence the signal without any
interference was processed by the corresponding auditory ﬁlter. On the other
hand, in one listener (HI4), speech intelligibility decreased by almost 3dB. It
is unclear why the degradation of the speech intelligibility was observed.
Overall, the results imply that the binaural split is on average beneﬁcial for
HI listeners, but cannot be considered as a universal processing strategy as it
may have detrimental effect in some individuals.
There was almost no difference found between the test with no split and
split-speech condition in HI listeners. This indicates that, even when half of
the target signal is removed and presented to one ear only, the speech intelli-
gibility is not compromised. This result might be explained by the interaction
of two possible mechanisms that compensate each other. On one hand, the
inter-band speech masking is potentially reduced by removing the speech
signal from every second channel. This would result in lower measured SRTs,
since the listener can tolerate more detrimental SNRs. On the other hand, the
central masking phenomenon (Zwislocki, 1972) might produce an elevation
of the SRT. These effects seem to counterbalance each other. In HI1, HI6 and
HI7 a speech intelligibility improvement was observed, suggesting that the
spectral masking release had more pronounced effect in those individuals.
Larger improvements on speech intelligibility can be seen in the split-
noise condition, with respect to the unprocessed condition.
This again
strongly supports the better-ear listening theory for the following reasons.
First, it has been shown that the better-ear listening phenomenon is not com-
promised by sensorineural hearing loss (Best et al., 2015). Furthermore, since
the beneﬁt from the split-noise condition in HI wass higher that in NH listen-
ers (-3.58 dB and -2.32 dB respectively), it is possible that the beneﬁt seen in
HI listeners may have been further enhanced by the spectral masking release.
The results of the monaural speech intelligibility test (Figure 4.5) with
the unprocessed signal and in the full-split condition suggested that there

may be two counterbalancing effects: speech intelligibility improvement due
to the spectral masking release and its degradation due to missing several
frequency components. On one hand, some relevant information has been
completely removed from the signal and this may be the reason why the im-
provement observed in the binaural test was not present with the monaural
one. In the same time, the spectral masking was reduced by removing the
input in every second channel which may explain the absence of detrimental

The deviation of the results for HI6 from the other two listeners require

5.3.2
Speech intelligibility data predictions with an energy-
based model
The model developed by Jelfs, Culling, Lavandier (Jelfs et al., 2011) repro-
duced the experimental data with unprocessed sound and in full-split con-
dition in NH relatively well (Table 4.1). Similar trends in split-speech and
split-noise conditions were seen in the model simulations and in the experi-
mental data. Differences between the two approaches are described below.
For the unprocessed condition, no better ear could be observed. Triv-
ially, this can be explained by the fact that the signal was presented diotically,
yielding in both ears the same SNR in each frequency band. The left ear was
selected arbitrarily by the model for the SRT calculation. The computed SRT
(Table 4.1) is aligned with the experimental result (Figure 4.4).
As observed for the experimental results, the predicted difference (4.1)
in speech intelligibility between full binaural split and unprocessed condi-
tion was negligible. This is plausible since the spectral content of the unpro-
cessed and split signal was the same, however in the split condition it was
only available on one side in each frequency band. Obviously, the model
takes into consideration the SNR of the side containing the speech-and-noise
signal, while the channels without any content are disregarded.
The model can not very well capture the behavioral data from the exper-
iment in the two remaining experimental conditions, split-speech and split-
noise.
For the split-speech condition, a 3 dB discrepancy was observed between
the experimental data and the model predictions.
The deviation is most
likely caused by the way the signal is processed in the model, by ﬁlters that
do not exactly match the ﬁlters used for splitting the signal in the experiment.
The binaural split processing divided the spectral information between left
and right ears by means of a band-notching FIR comb ﬁlters (Section 3.5.1).
However, the model applies a ﬁlterbank of 26 ERB-wide, gammatone ﬁlters
according to Glasberg and Moore (1990). The exemplary differences at 1 kHz

Danok
Spectral masking did not impact NH performance but better-ear listening
theory was supported
The results from Danok experiment on NH listeners are aligned with the
ﬁndings from Dantale II test. No improvement was seen in the full-split
condition. This may be a conﬁrmation of the negligible impact of spectral
masking in NH listeners. It is unclear why almost no speech intelligibility
degradation was seen in the split-speech condition. The central masking the-
ory can not be applied here and the results should be further investigated.
The results in the split-noise condition again supports the better-ear lis-
tening theory, where the listeners can select the ear with the higher SNR
across frequencies, ipso facto improving consonant recognition.
No beneﬁt from the binaural split was observed in HI listeners, however,
better-ear listening was supported
Unlike Dantale II results, Danok results in HI listeners did not show any
improvement with binaural split in terms of the consonant recognition score.
It is unclear why two speech intelligibility tests yielded different results.
Previous studies showed the consonant recognition improvement of var-
ious magnitude in the full-split condition that was not seen in the current
study (Chaudhari and Pandey, 1999; Cheeran and Pandey, 2004; Kulkarni
et al., 2012). The discrepancy is most likely to originate in a different imple-
mentation of the binaural split. The sharpness of frequency band separation
seems to have a positive effect on the consonant recognition score. In the cur-
rent study, the overlap between the right and left ﬁlter magnitude response
was relatively high compared to the existing literature. Therefore, a better
frequency band separation should be considered in future work to conﬁrm
this hypothesis. Furthermore, another aspect that may have contributed to
the variability seen in the outcome of the mentioned studies, was the employ-
ment of different experiments to evaluate the consonant recognition abilities.
It is unclear why the results obtained in Dantale II and Danok differ. Nev-
ertheless, two considerations can be done to explain such difference. Firstly,
a test using full sentences like Dantale II, provides several additional cues,
beyond the spectral ones that are made more prominent in a CVCV test like
Danok. For example, the length of the words (e.g. ’Per’ vs ’Henning’) or the
presence of linguistic factors may have facilitated the responses. Secondly, it
is hypothesised that the binaural split requires some acclimatization time. It
has been shown that both experienced and novice hearing aid users beneﬁt
==PAGE:62==
50

from the acclimatization time to new signal-processing features. Such bene-
ﬁt is revealed by higher scores in various speech intelligibility tests (Ellis and
Munro, 2015; Habicht et al., 2018). It is possible that adaptation time in HI
listeners would bring further beneﬁt in consonant recognition score.
Similarly to Dantale II results, the split-speech condition in Danok test
degraded the consonant recognition score by about 10%. This is aligned with
the previous ﬁndings and supports the central masking theory.
On the contrary, the split-noise condition improved the consonant recog-
nition score in HI listeners. As in the Dantale II results, the improvement is
justiﬁed by the better-ear listening. However, the beneﬁt compared to the
NH listeners is moderate (15% in NH and 5% in HI). This may be due to the
different SNR used for testing the two groups. The psychometric function
around 50% is the steepest, thus even small changes in the signal processing
can determine large changes in the measured SRT. In HI listeners, the used
SNR targeted points along the psychometric function, in a region where the
curve is shallower. Hence, the observed beneﬁt may not be as pronounced as
in NH.

5.4
Spatial perception
5.4.1
Evidence of the binaural split inﬂuence on spatial per-
ception
It is quite challenging to summarise the raw, comprehensive data from the
spatial perception experiment (Figure 4.9). However, few conclusions can be
drawn at a ﬁrst glance.
• NH people seemed to be able to indicate the direction of the sound
source more accurately than HI listeners.
• the binaural split introduced image splits (grey circles in ﬁgure 4.9 de-
pict the secondary sound source). However, it does not seem to have
any effect on the dominant sound source localization ability
• binaural split seemed to produce a perceived internalization of the
sound.
• all angles except the extreme left and right (−90o and 90o) seemed to
have the perceived image skewed towards the right side. This may
originate from the artiﬁcial HRTFs. It is possible that the HRTFs were
recorded with HaTS tilted towards the right side.
It should be noted that the natural ILDs are non-existent in the split con-
dition. Artiﬁcial ILDs were introduced by removing the signal in every sec-
ond channel alternately from each ear. Moreover, the ILDs across frequencies
were inconsistent, i.e., they indicate opposite directions of the sound source
in each channel. The important question is: how can both NH and HI lis-
teners still recognise the sound source direction? A possible explanation is
twofold. 1) According to the duplex theory, the ILDs are mostly utilised in
the high frequencies where the diffraction on the head is weaker, while the
ITDs are most prominent at low frequencies. The binaural split did not in-
troduce any artifacts to the the ITDs. Hence, it may be possible that the ITDs
were used to determine the sound source direction. 2) The ILDs have differ-
ent weights across frequencies. Ahrens et al. (2019) showed that the lowest
frequency ERB-wide band (center frequency = 442Hz) has the highest weight
in terms of the ITD contribution to sound localization. At the opposite side
of the spectrum, the highest ERB-wide band (center frequency = 5544Hz) has
the highest importance in terms of ILDs. In the binaural split, the lowest and
the highest frequency channels were presented to the opposite ears and ac-
cording to the theory of the highest spectral weights of these bands, these
cues seem to counterbalance each other.

5.4.2
Binaural split made the perceived sound source smaller
but farther from the original location in both NH and
HI listeners
In general, the perceived size of the sound source in HI listeners was bigger
than in NH (Figure 4.10). In both listeners groups, the binaural split de-
creased the perceived source size. This may be due to the calculation method
of bubble size representing the responses when multiple sound sources were
perceived. In this case, the average of all response bubbles was computed,
leading to an underestimated average size. Looking at the raw data (Fig-
ure 4.9, it can be seen that the secondary source in most of the cases was
perceived as much smaller as the main source or the single source. Never-
theless, it is relevant that in both listener groups the trend was the same. It
should be noted that in NH listeners the size of the response bubbles did not
change across angles. On the contrary, it seems that in HI listeners the re-
sponse circles are smaller for −90o, 0o and for 90o compared to the interim
angles. This may be caused by the highest ITDs at the extreme angles (−90o
and 90o) being easier to interpret and counterbalanced binaural cues at 0o.
At a negative and positive 30o and 60o, where the binaural cues were not as
prominent, the angle recognition accuracy declined.
The distance of the response bubble from the original sound source in-
creased with the binaural split in both NH and HI people, indicating less
accuracy in the angle recognition. This may be caused by the internalization
due to the binaural split seen in the raw data (Figure 4.9). Especially at the
angles different than 0o, the difference was higher in NH than in HI listen-
ers. This may indicate that the impact of the binaural split was more pro-
nounced in the ﬁrst group. The distance increase in both groups was most
likely caused by the internalisation of the sound and not necessary by poorer
angle-discrimination.
In general, it should be noted that the inaccuracy in the spatial perception
experiment may originate from the experimental setup. Firstly, the artiﬁcial
HRTFs are known to provide poorer results in spatial perception experiments
compared to individual HRTFs (Wenzel et al., 1993). Secondly, a lack of visual
cues is known to deteriorate the accuracy of the sound source localisation
(Gil-Carvajal et al., 2016). For this reasons, more realistic testing conditions
should be considered in the future experiments.

Sound quality
The binaural split is an unnatural signal processing mechanism that is absent
in the human brain. For potential future applications, an acceptable sound
quality must be ensured. Therefore, the subjective sound quality of the real-
life sounds was evaluated in NH (Figure 4.11 and HI (Figure 4.12) listeners.
5.5.1
NH listeners perceived the difference between the un-
processed sound and the split conditions
In all acoustic situations the unprocessed sound is always preferred over any
split condition by NH listeners. Moreover, there was no difference in terms of
subjective sound quality and preferences between all split conditions, since
they were equally disliked.
5.5.2
HI listeners did not perceive any difference between
the unprocessed and split sound in cocktail-party sit-

Similarly to NH listeners, HI ones highly preferred the unprocessed sound
with musical stimuli over any of the split ones. For the forest ambient sound,
there seem to be a slight difference between the original sound and all split
conditions, with a preference for the ﬁrst one. Interestingly, in cocktail-party
situations, i.e., cafeteria noise, male speech in canteen noise (’In the cantina
1’) and female speech in canteen noise (’In the cantina 2’), no difference be-

the utmost interest since the binaural split is intended to be implemented to
improve speech intelligibility in noise. This indicates that the binaural split
could be potentially implemented in hearing aids as a strategy for improving
speech intelligibility, without compromising the subjective sound quality in
cocktail-party situations.

6.0.1
Conclusions
This thesis investigated how the binaural split of the stimulus’s spectral con-
tent impacts speech intelligibility, spatial perception and subjective sound
quality perception. The binaural split separates the stimulus into 26 ERB-
wide frequency channels and distributes odd channels to the left ear and
even channels to the right ear. State-of-the art experimental methods were
employed to evaluate the effects of such processing on speech intelligibility
and the results were cross-checked with a computational modeling approach.
Moreover, various psycho-acoustic tasks were employed to assess auditory
proﬁle measurements, namely IPD, binaural pitch discrimination, auditory
ﬁlter bandwidth and audiometric threshold. Their outcomes were related to
the speech intelligibility beneﬁt in different split conditions.
The results of the speech intelligibility experiments were discussed in
terms of three main potential underlying auditory mechanisms: spectral
masking, central masking and better-ear listening. As for NH listeners, ev-
idence was found that the binaural split did not have any effect on speech
intelligibility, in neither sentence nor consonant recognition tests, supporting
the assumption that spectral masking is not observed in NH people. On the
contrary, central masking and better-ear listening phenomena were observed
in the split-speech and the split-noise conditions, respectively.
A word recognition improvement was instead observed in the HI listen-
ers when applying the binaural split the unprocessed sentence stimuli. This
was consistent with the hypothesis of the spectral masking when the unpro-
cessed signal is presented to the listener, and the spectral masking release
in the split condition. The degradation in speech intelligibility in the split-
speech condition supports the theory of central masking where the signal
from the channels containing noise only masks the speech signal presented
contralaterally. The proposed explanation of the speech intelligibility im-
provement in the split-noise condition is twofold: (i) inter-band speech mask-
ing was reduced by removing the speech signal in every second channel (ii)
the improvement was further enhanced by the better-ear listening where the
listeners can utilise the clear speech signal presented alternately to the two
ears. As a results, the measured SRT decreased.
Consonants, in comparison to full sentences, seem to be less vulnerable
to the spectral masking as no improvement was observed in the consonant
==PAGE:68==
56
Chapter 6. Summary
recognition with the binaural split in HI listeners. This difference may be
caused by the different nature of the tests, as discussed in section 5.3.3.
No correlations were found between the split beneﬁt in speech intelligi-
bility experiments in HI listeners and their IPD discrimination abilities, audi-
tory ﬁlter bandwidth and the absolute hearing threshold. Since no signiﬁcant
evidence was found that any of the auditory proﬁle measures could predict
the speech intelligibility improvement in any of the split conditions, more
sophisticated test battery should be developed for more accurate proﬁling of
the individual hearing loss.
Spatial perception was found to be inﬂuenced by the binaural split, for
both NH and HI listeners. The perceived location of the sound source was
less accurate in the split condition, compared to the one with the unprocessed
signal. Moreover, it was shown that the binaural split introduces the percep-
tion of more than one sound source, i.e., the so-called image splits. This
effect was present regardless of the actual angle of the stimulus presentation.
However, a relatively good spatial perception of the primary sound source
was observed in both listener groups, suggesting that cues other than ILDs
(i.e., the spatial cues modiﬁed by the binaural split) are utilized in the sound
localization process.
Finally, it was shown that, in NH listeners, the binaural split had a detri-
mental effect on the perceived sound quality evaluated in various acoustical
situations. The difference was not perceived by HI listeners in the simulated
scenarios ’classical music’, ’contemporary music’ and the ’forest ambient’.
However, in HI listeners, the difference between the unprocessed sound and
the split sound was not found in the scenarios representing the most crucial
situations, i.e., ’canteen noise’, ’female speech in noise’ and ’male speech in
noise’.
In summary, it was concluded that the binaural split can improve speech
intelligibility in the HI listeners by reducing the spectral masking, without
disrupting the spatial perception of the sound source and general sound
quality in the most relevant acoustic situations. However, no predictor of the
split beneﬁt was found amongst the psycho-acoustic experiments conducted
in this study.
6.0.2
Future work
The extent to which the binaural split contributes to better speech intelli-
gibility in HI listeners has still to be conﬁrmed. A more robust approach
for assessing the potential beneﬁt of this processing method would target
more fundamental mechanisms of the auditory system. These mechanisms
are usually investigated with simple acoustic stimuli, whereas a speech-in-
noise stimulus, like the one employed in this study, is a complex signal with
a high spectro-temporal variability that cannot be fully controlled. To use a
simpler stimuli, a tone-in-noise detection paradigm could be employed. For
example, detection of binaural complex tones masked by broadband noise is
==PAGE:69==
Chapter 6. Summary
57
an experimental paradigm that can potentially be used as a baseline exper-
iment. The binaural split can be implemented in a way that even and odd
harmonic components of the complex tone and the coincided noise are sep-
arately assigned to the two ears. Based on the ﬁndings of this thesis, lower
detection thresholds for split complex tones can be expected, compared to
the threshold measured with the unprocessed stimuli.
Additionally, it would be possible to preserve natural ITDs and ILDs by
applying the split only in the speech frequency region.
This would pre-
serve the most prominent binaural cues having the highest importance for
the accurate sound localization. By maintaining the lowest and the highest
frequencies unprocessed, it could be possible to utilize natural binaural dif-
ferences in the sound localization process, simultaneously releasing spectral
masking in the the most crucial frequency range for good speech comprehen-
sion.
Finally, various parameters could be modiﬁed in the binaural split imple-
mentation to ﬁnd the optimal settings for the largest improvement of speech
intelligibility. The simplest alteration would be to change the number and
the bandwidths of the frequency channels used. A coarser or ﬁner resolu-
tion in the frequency domain could potentially provide different results and
may lead to better explanation of the spectral masking phenomenon. The
limit of the spectral binaural summation should be investigated to suggest
restrictions for processing the signals. Furthermore, applying asymmetrical
split based on spectral features of the stimulus could be considered. As an
example, instead of presenting odd and even frequency channels alternately
between the two ears, it would be possible to present the channels containing
spectral peaks of the stimuli to one side and the rest of the spectral content to
the opposite side. This could potentially enhance the most prominent spec-
tral components by reducing spectral masking.
