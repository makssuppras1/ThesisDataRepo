0.1
Abstract
Coastal reefs in Denmark are a vital habitat environment for a variety of marine organ-
isms. This thesis investigates two types of coastal reef structures, biogenic, and geogenic,
and their role in coastal restoration efforts. Biogenic reefs are investigated through the
finalization process of a manuscript for journal submission, using data from a mussel reef
based in Helnæs Bay. Conversely, geogenic reefs are examined by working through the
fieldwork and set up process of an experiment to map mobile fauna surrounding a reef
in Tybrind Vig. With both reef projects taking place geographically close to one another,
both reef restoration efforts contribute to the objectives of Natura 2000 and the EU Habi-
tats Directive. Using insights from both reef types can lend to future restoration efforts
by developing an understanding of how they could potentially work with and against each
other. This thesis contributed to a deeper understanding of the current state of coastal
reefs, and their use in future restoration endeavors.

Introduction
The EU Habitats Directive of May 1992, is an EU council directive pertaining to the con-
servation of natural habitats and wild fauna and flora (European Union 2013a). Within
the directive, habitats which are to be protected are designated as special areas of con-
servation. Of these areas in coastal and halophytic habitats, reefs (Habitat Nr. 1170) are
named (European Union 2013b). The Interpretation Manual of EU Habitats specifies that
reefs can be either biogenic concretions or of geogenic origin, so long as they are com-
prised of hard compact substrata upon solid or soft bottoms which arise from the seafloor
in sublittoral or littoral zones. Biogenic concretions are to be defined as ”concretions, en-
crustations, corallogenic concretions, and bivalve mussel beds” which can originate from
either dead or living organisms, an example can be seen in Figure 1.1a. Geogenic origin
is defined as reefs which have been formed by non-biogenic substrate, including rock,
boulders, and cobbles, and an example can be seen in Figure 1.1b (European Union
2013a).

Reef conservation in all aspects is becoming more vital to the prolonged sustainability of
our oceans today. With coastal habitats degrading (OSPAR 2023) it is more important than
ever to turn our efforts towards coastal habitat restoration, and a good first step towards
that is the restoration of reef habitats in those coastal areas. This thesis project delves into
two different types of reefs; biogenic and geogenic. It focuses primarily on mobile fauna
surrounding both reef types and how each reef type can be beneficial to the improvement
of the areas which they reside in. This thesis is separated into two major components.
The first being the finalization of a manuscript covering the impact of a biogenic mussel
reef in restoring taxonomic biodiversity and Good Ecological Status (GES) to a coastal
area. The second aspect focuses on the fieldwork and methods involved in a study which
analyzes the movement of cod present surrounding a man-made geogenic reef.

2.1
Biogenic Mussel Reef Manuscript
The manuscript, titled ”Mussel reefs promote taxonomic biodiversity and host a unique
assemblage of mobile marine fauna in a eutrophicated coastal bay of poor ecological
status” involves a study in which data collection took place in 2019 to observe the area of a
biogenic bivalve reef and its impact on surrounding taxonomic biodiversity, and ecological
status of the coastal area. This section will explore the manuscript, describing its sections,
in order to gain a general overview of its content.
2.1.1
Background
Mussel reefs are a vital coastal habitat, providing important ecosystem functions and pro-
tecting key species (Jordan, O’Higgins, and Dittmar 2012). For marine fauna, they pro-
vide vital points of shelter, feeding, spawning, and nursery areas (Heckwolf et al. 2021).
Mussel reefs can also host unique species assemblages compared to other areas (Sea,
2022). Despite various pieces of EU legislation like the Water Framework Directive, Habi-
tats Directive, and Marine Strategy Directive making attempts at protecting these areas,
the threats they are facing remain (Carstensen 2016).
The area selected for the study in the manuscript is a coastal body in the southwest of
the island of Fyn in Denmark called Helnæs Bay. In previous years, the bay was domi-
nated by mussel beds, however, in recent years, various anthropogenic factors such as
mussel fishing and other commercial activities have caused a significant decline (Dahl
and Petersen 2018). These factors combined with eutrophication are actively threatening
many coastal areas and causing a decline in reefs and their hosted species (Andersen
et al., 2017). Restoration efforts carried out in 2010-2011 saw the establishment of new
mussel reefs, which initially led to an increase in fish abundance and diversity (Kristensen
et al. 2015). Additionally, a 2018 survey led to a ban on bottom-towing fishing gear, which
should lead to less benthic damage (Landbrug og Fiskeri Ministeriet for Fødevarer 2019).
The goal of the study was to investigate the impact of mussel reefs on mobile fauna by
comparing to control sandy bottom sites, while overall assessing the environmental status
of Helnæs Bay.
2.1.2
Methods & Materials
Helnæs Bay is located on the southwestern coast of Fyn, and is enclosed by the Helnæs
peninsula, which is a Natura 2000 protected area (European Union 2013a). It is classified
as a type M1 estuary, which is a mesohaline, stratified estuary, with long residence times
and low freshwater impact (Schernewski and Wielgat 2004).
The habitats selected included shallow mussel reefs and control sites, at a depth of 0.5-1.5
m, and deep mussel reefs and control sites, with depths of 7-9 m. In order for a mussel reef
to be classified as a biogenic reef, it needs to have a minimum of 30% mussel coverage
(Dahl and Petersen 2018). In the control sites, the mussel coverage was considered ”low

==PAGE:14==
coverage” and ranged from 0-29%, while the reef sites had ”high coverage” of 30-100%
coverage.
Mobile fauna surrounding the reefs were monitored using stereo-Baited Remote Under-
water Video Systems (BRUVS). Cameras were mounted onto a metal frame attached to
a rope and surface buoy, with a 1 m long metal bait arm attached to the frame between
the cameras. A mesh bag of bait containing 500 g of chopped Atlantic Herring was at-
tached to the arm. Each deployment was soaked for 60 minutes, plus an additional 5 to
allow for sediment settling. Videos were analyzed using SeaGIS EventMeasure software,
and organisms were identified to the lowest taxonomic level possible, and counted. Mo-
bile fauna were classified as fish and large invertebrates, and were counted according to
relative abundance, which is the maximum number of individuals of one taxon per time
frame across the whole period. Each recording collected the species present, relative
abundance, mussel coverage, and functional visibility.
The environmental assessment was carried out using two indicators for biological quality
elements, Eelgrass Depth Limit (EDL) and summer Chlorophyll a (Chla) concentration.
In order to gauge the ecological status of an area, the Water Framework Directive (WFD)
uses these indicators to ascertain its status between 5 different levels, bad, poor, mod-
erate, good, and high, with each having thresholds the indicators need to achieve. The
approach selected in this study was the ”face-value” approach, which states the mean
value of the parameter of interest should be in the range for the status it achieves, dis-
regarding the confidence intervals. Furthermore, the WFD applies a ”one out, all out”
principle, stating that the ecological status of the water body in question corresponds to
the lowest status seen in any of the indicators used. EDL is defined as the maximum
depth where a minimum of 10% eelgrass coverage is found, and as growth is directly
correlated to water transparency, it is negatively impacted by eutrophication, making it
a good indicator of ecological status (Henriksen et al., 2014). EDL data was collected
from 2011 to 2018 by divers and analyzed for annual averages and 6-year averages, with
reference values based on historical data sets. Measuring Chla concentration is used as
a proxy for assessing phytoplankton biomass. The WFD uses phytoplankton biomass as
an indicator, as increases in biomass due to eutrophication result in decreases in water
transparency. The study used a publicly available data set containing Chla concentra-
tion measurements from 1983 onwards, measured bi-weekly or monthly. Annual summer
values and a 6-year average were calculated and compared to reference values.
The statistical software R was used to conduct the statistical analysis for this study.
Three taxonomic diversity indices, including species richness, Shannon-Wiener index,
and Pielou’s evenness, were applied in order to evaluate the effect of habitat type and
sampling depth. These indices were determined for every deployment using the vegan
package (Oksanen 2010) which then grouped them by site depth and mussel coverage.
The glmmTMB package (Brooks et al. 2017) was employed to fit generalized linear mixed
models (GLMMs) with SiteID taken as the random effect while depth, mussel coverage
and their interaction were used as fixed factors. To adjust for functional visibility as a
covariate, it is included in the model along with other variables. AIC values were as-
sessed for model selection purposes. The species richness model was modeled using
Conway-Maxwell distribution since there was underdispersion, while the Shannon-Wiener
index and Pielou’s evenness index were modeled using Gaussian and Beta distributions
respectively. Values far away from normality range of evenness measurements were al-
tered to fit into beta distribution limits. In order to identify differences in diversity indices
across habitat groups, pairwise comparisons of estimated marginal means for each level
of habitat group x variable interaction were done using the emmeans package (R. Lenth

The vegan package in R was also used to perform multivariate analysis for the com-
parison of community composition among sites. However, one deployment without any
species occurrence was excluded from the study. First, an unconstrained ordination by
non-metric multidimensional scaling (nMDS) using Bray Curtis dissimilarity index (Faith,
Minchin, and Belbin 1987) as a distance matrix was done. Its stress value, which indicates
how well the dimensionality reduction method worked, initially exceeded the acceptable
threshold value of 0.2, prompting the increase in dimensionality to three dimensions and
resulting in a reduced stress value of 0.14. Following this, canonical analysis of princi-
pal coordinates (CAP), where depth and mussel coverage, were used as environmental
predictors to constrain ordination in order to test whether there are distinct species com-
munities associated with mussel reefs. The CAP analysis combined with its respective
ANOVA including all environmental factors being tested determined their overall signifi-
cance in relation to each other’s impact on the system under observation, while further
examination of these effects utilized indicspecies package (Cáceres and Legendre 2009).
2.1.3
Results
The BRUVS system had 115 successful deployments overall, with 25 unique mobile
species observed, 19 of which were fish species, and 6 invertebrates. Of the 25 species,
18 taxa were seen at shallow mussel sites, and 13 at deep mussel sites. 10 taxa were
seen at shallow sandy bottom sites, with 11 seen at deep sandy bottom sites. Pairwise
comparison shows that the number of unique species per camera site were significantly
higher at mussel sites than sandy sites, with pshallow = 0.004 and pdeep = 0.046. There was
no impact of depth on species richness at sand sites where psand = 0.943 or at mussel
sites where pmussel = 0.360.
The Shannon-Wiener diversity index, which measures species diversity, varies from 0 to
2, indicating a range of diversity levels across the sites. There was a significant positive
effect of mussel coverage on Shannon diversity, with p = 0.02, with mussel sites showing
higher diversity, though this was only significant at shallow sites where pshallow = 0.22.
Depth did not significantly affect Shannon diversity at either sand (psand = 0.852) or mussel
(pmussel = 0.248) sites.
The average species evenness, which measures the distribution of individuals among
species, was 0.78 ± 0.24, with no significant differences between habitats. Mussel cov-
erage did not significantly affect species evenness at shallow (pshallow = 0.175) or deep
(pdeep = 0.329) sites, and there were no significant differences in evenness with depth at
either mussel reefs (pmussel = 0.348) or sand sites (psand = 0.519). However, functional
visibility was found to negatively affect evenness where pvisibility = 0.018, indicating that
greater visibility might lead to less even distribution of species across the sites.
The multivariate analysis revealed significant differences in community composition across
the four habitats: shallow-sandy, shallow-mussel, deep-sandy, and deep-mussel. Un-
constrained ordination illustrated a clear separation of these groups, with no overlap of
centroids. PERMANOVA results indicated significant effects of depth, mussel coverage,
and their interaction on species communities with a p < 0.001. Correspondingly, the CAP
analysis demonstrated clear separation among all habitat types, with depth and mussel
reefs significantly affecting community composition where p = 0.001. 10 out of 25 species
were identified as significant drivers of these community composition differences. Indica-
tor species analysis highlighted specific species associated with distinct habitats: rockpool
shrimp (C. crangon) was predominant in shallow sand sites; three-spine stickleback (G.
aculeatus), nine-spine stickleback (P. pungitius), and garfish (B. belone) were character-

istic of shallow mussel sites; starfish (A. rubens) was typical of deep mussel sites; and
two-spotted goby (P. flavescens) and black goby (G. niger) were found in areas with high
mussel coverage. Conversely, sand goby (P. minutus) was associated with low mussel
coverage sites, lion’s mane jellyfish (C. capillata) with shallow habitats, and flounder (P.

The results of the EDL analyses showed Helnæs Bay to have a ”moderate” ecological
status. Annual mean values fell consistently within ”moderate” limits of 3.9-5.7 m, often-
times including the full confidence interval (CI). Overall, using the face-value approach,
the status remains moderate, despite 3 annual values with CI’s overlapping the ”poor”
status. The 6-year average EDL from 2016 to 2021 was 5.34 ± 0.13 m, which is entirely
within the moderate range. There was no general trend detected over time.
For Chla concentration, annual means were larger than the CI of the 6-year average.
This indicates strong seasonal fluctuations, which was an expected pattern due to sea-
sonal variations in phytoplankton productivity. This pattern wasn’t discernible in the EDL
measurements, as they were taken less frequently. Due to the high variation, Chla mea-
surements spanned between four status classes, however annual mean values showed
a ”moderate” to ”poor” status, with most values falling in the ”poor” category. The 6-year
average value of 3.63 ± 0.23 µg Chla l−1, showing its ”poor” status, being in between 3.2
and 6.3 µg Chla l−1. Again, there was no general trend seen over time.
Overall, the ecological assessment, applying the ”one out, all out” principle by the WFD,
shows the ecological status to be the lowest quality status of any indicator. Helnæs Bay
achieves ”poor” status overall based on the Chla concentration.
2.1.4
Discussion
This study was an attempt to begin to rectify the fact that mussels, despite being key
habitat providers for many types of marine fauna, are critically understudied, especially
in the Baltic Sea (Westerbom et al. 2018). The environmental assessment in Helnæs
Bay showed that the eutrophicated bay remains in poor ecological condition. Despite
this ecological state, the BRUVS monitoring showed that the mussel reefs in the area
are capable of supporting higher species richness and diverse mobile fauna. This was
emphasized by the fact that locations with high mussel coverage had significantly higher
species richness when compared to low mussel coverage sites.
2.1.4.1
Mussels and Environmental Interactions
Helnæs Bay reflects the poor ecological conditions which are seen in many other Danish
coastal waters, where only 5 out of 109 reach Good Ecological Status (Danish Environ-
mental Protection Agency 2015). The eutrophication present in the area, as evidenced
by the high Chla concentration, poses a generally harmful presence to most marine or-
ganisms, but in some cases can benefit mussels by increasing food availability (Riemann
et al. 2016). Mussels can enhance water quality through filtration, which can help pro-
mote habitat diversity (Capelle et al. 2017). However, the mitigation potential of mussel
reefs in terms of eutrophication is limited when compared to mussel farms due to nutrient
recycling within the system (Stadmark and Conley 2011).
As seen in the results, the mussel reefs in Helnæs Bay significantly increased the diversity
of mobile marine fauna compared to soft-bottom areas. Different species were preferential
to different habitat areas, such as starfish preferring deep mussel reefs, while garfish
and sticklebacks preferred shallower reefs. These species-habitat associations can help
indicate the ecological significance of both deep and shallow mussel reefs.

2.1.4.2
Comparing Deep and Shallow Mussel Reefs
Deep and shallow mussel reefs experience different abiotic conditions and impacts from
human activities due to the areas they are exposed to (Andrade et al. 2021). Both habitat
types were capable of supporting higher species richness, and distinct communities of
mobile fauna. The current state of EU protection statuses favor deeper mussel reefs,
neglecting the importance that shallower, intertidal reefs can present. This is evident in
that small bodied mesopredators as found in shallow reefs need more protection in order
to maintain ecosystem health (Wilms et al. 2021).
2.1.4.3
Study Limitations
The study conducted presents with a few limitations and things to work towards. BRUVS
methodology is limited, for example, by its bias towards predatory species due to the
bait-bag, and its reliance on visibility conditions (Goetze et al. 2015). Further research
is needed in order to assess the mitigation potential of mussels against the impacts of
eutrophication. Overall, this study provided an insight into the ecological status of Helnæs
Bay, and emphasized the importance of protecting remnant mussel reefs.
2.2
Atlantic Cod mapping to document the effects of a rocky
reef
Rocky reefs were previously plentiful in Denmark, however rock extraction during the
1900s and earlier has left many of these reefs lacking or missing completely. Given their
ability to provide shelter, feeding, nursing, and spawning areas for many marine species,
this project aims to document the ability of a new man-made reef to provide a suitable
habitat for Atlantic Cod in the area.
2.2.1
Atlantic Cod Decline
Atlantic cod stock in Denmark has been steadily declining, both in the Baltic and North Sea
areas (ICES 2023a, ICES 2023b). The decline can be credited to various environmental
and anthropogenic factors, such as the hydrological characteristics of their habitats, and
a history of extreme fishing pressure (Birgersson 2022). These various factors combined
have caused a reduction in large cod individuals, body condition, and productivity (Birg-
ersson 2022). The damage to cod stocks in the Baltic Sea/Kattegat areas is so severe
that the International Council for the Exploration of the Sea (ICES) advice regarding sus-
tainable catch limits for 2024 is not to catch any fish at all (ICES 2023a), while advice for
the North Sea areas suggests a fishing pressure of 0.29 to attempt to rebuild stock (ICES
2023b).
2.2.1.1
Environmental Pressure
Over the past several decades, Atlantic Cod (referred to hereafter as cod) has been facing
an array of environmental pressures, putting strain on productivity and overall stock health
(Birgersson 2022).
Eutrophication in areas with cod has caused significant damage, impacting the habitat
of the cod, as well as their physical condition (Köppen and Reckermann 2020). Their
growth is impacted directly, affecting them physiologically (Brander 2020) and indirectly,
by limiting their benthic prey availability (Neuenfeldt et al. 2020).
Hypoxic conditions caused by eutrophication are negatively impacting suitable available
habitats for cod. With habitat-forming species such as eelgrass and brown algae decreas-
ing, juveniles have less suitable nursery areas and are more susceptible to predation
(Boström et al. 2014).
Climate change is also playing a large factor in cod decline. Temperature increases are
causing the cod, which are a cold water species, to have faster growth at younger life

leads to a loss in reproductive potential overall (Lindmark 2020). Temperature increases
are also harming the zooplankton food sources desired by larval cod (Möllmann 2019),
leading further to their decreased growth. Climate change-induced salinity decreases are
currently being looked into for their negative impact on cod stocks. Increased runoff of
freshwater caused by climate change is decreasing the salinity levels, especially in Baltic
Sea areas, leading to possible limits on food availability and further impairing oxygen
conditions necessary for reproduction and recruitment (BACC II 2015; Orio 2019).
2.2.1.2
Fishing Pressure
Pre-World War 2, the Baltic cod stock was relatively high, more than twice as high as
it currently is, with an estimated fishing mortality of 0.2 (Eero, Köster, and MacKenzie
2008). Throughout World War 2, fishing pressure and exploitation of the stock increased
significantly, with fishing mortality rising to 0.3 in the early 1940s and 0.6 during the war
(Eero, Köster, and MacKenzie 2008). Alternatively, North Sea cod were relatively stable
but experienced what is known as the ”gadoid outburst” from the late 1960s to 1980s. In
this period, cod and other Gadidae produced exceptionally large year classes compared
to previously. This led to an increase in landings, which in turn led to an increase in fishing
pressure. Despite the outburst dying down and recruitment levels falling around the late
1980s and 1990s, fishing pressure remained high, decimating stock size, which has not
recovered since (Engelhard, Righton, and Pinnegar 2014; Eero, Brander, et al. 2023).
As of 2023 ICES advice, both stocks are still not doing well. Spawning Stock Biomass
(SSB) and recruitment are still in some of their lowest ranges for Baltic cod Kattegat sub-
stock, while North Sea Viking sub-stock is similarly low but somewhat better than the
Baltic.
As shown in Figure 2.1, the Viking sub-stock of North Sea cod is slowly recovering from
a recent dip, with an SSB above where stock is considered to have reduced reproductive

2.2.2
Mapping & Area
The area being used for mapping is based in Tybrind Vig off the west of Fyn in Denmark.
The local municipality voluntarily created a Marine Protected Area (MPA), gaining accep-
tance from local fisher groups to establish it. Naturpark Lillebælt subsequently created
the MPA in Tybrind Vig and established a rocky reef in that location.
The study areas are comprised of the rocky reef area and the control site area. The
rocky reef, as stated, is a man-made reef made of granite rocks covering an area of
approximately 60 m × 160 m. The depth of the reef area ranges from approximately 7-9
m. The granite rocks lie on the seafloor and are immovable by the current and waves.
Additionally, Naturpark Lillebælt has enacted a voluntary conservation act in which local
fishermen and those in the area have agreed not to fish in an area of 8 km2 around the
reef. This conservation act will last from 2023 until 2028. The control site chosen for this
study is a flat (no protrusions) area which needed to be at least a minimum of 150 m away
from the reef site to ensure an adequate buffer zone. The buffer zone is necessary, so
the cod from each reef don’t interact with one another, or switch habitats. The control
site was also selected due to its average depth of approximately 7-9 m. Figure 2.3 below
visually depicts the two sites.

2.2.3
Acoustic Telemetry
Acoustic telemetry was first developed for fish use by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife service in
order to observe salmon movements and behavior, in relation to the Columbia River dams
(Trefethen 1965). It was based upon principles of sonar technology (sound navigation and
ranging) which was developed to detect submarines in World War I. Acoustic telemetry
is favorable to use when working in deep waters with high conductivity and relatively low
turbulence (Winter 1996). Trefethen’s original study in 1956 used external tags fastened
right by the pectoral fin and tracked movement using a boat and fixed receivers, recording
data onto paper and magnetic tape (Trefethen 1965). Since then, acoustic telemetry has
come an extremely long way to the internal tags and receiver arrays used today, thanks
to advancements in battery life maximization and tag miniaturization (Hockersmith and
Beeman 2012).
Aquatic tracking has been used in a wide array of studies in order to deepen our un-
derstanding of fundamental biological processes such as life history variation in migration
timing, reproductive investment and spawning behavior variation, and various survival fac-
tors (Crossin et al. 2017). One of the most important uses, however, is within the applied
realm where population-level responses to environmental change can be observed, re-
vealing mechanisms which shape and disrupt fish populations and communities (Crossin
et al. 2017).
The major components of acoustic telemetry are the hydrophones and tags used. Hy-
drophones are essentially an underwater microphone.

2.2.3.1
Hydrophones
Hydrophones are high-sensitivity underwater listening devices crucial for acoustic teleme-
try. These devices capture and transmit underwater sound, enabling researchers to track
and monitor aquatic life and environmental conditions. At the core of a hydrophone’s func-
tionality is its role as an electroacoustic transducer. This means it converts sound waves
into electrical signals, which can then be analyzed and recorded.
The hydrophones used in this study are Thelma Biotel’s TBR 800 Release models. The
TBR 800 Release is based off of the standard TBR 800 model just with an added release
feature, however it works essentially the same way as the standard. The TBR 800 oper-
ates within the 63-77 kHz range, and is able to monitor and adapt to background noise
levels, allowing for maximum data reception. Data is received and stored in a 32 MB
internal memory and includes temperature data, noise conditions, inclination, power con-
sumption, and battery status. The receiver can also communicate with the DeckBox Field
Kit when deployed, allowing for distance ranging, status updates on number of detections,
battery status, and other features.
The TBR 800 release includes the same features as listed above, with the addition of
a specialized release mechanism. The release mechanism is a motor driven, remote
operated release function which can be sent from the aforementioned DeckBox. A motor
physically releases the locking lug at the bottom of the receiver, and allows the buoys
attached to float the receiver to the surface for recovery. This setup is known as the
Mooring Recovery System (MRS). It includes a spherical buoy which fastens over the
receiver, with a rope drum that the receiver can be screwed into. The rope drum can be
weighed down with anchors as necessary. Figure 2.4 shows a receiver screwed into a
rope box.

Tags
The tags used in this study are Thelma Biotel’s 7 mm transmitters. Thelma Biotel allows
for almost complete customization of their transmitters, allowing the user to choose from
an extensive list of features that can be tailored to match their needs. Due to their small
size, 7 mm tags were selected. The tags are 33.1 mm in length and have a weight of 3.8
g in air and 2.4 g in water. Each tag has a transmit interval of 150 seconds, meaning that
every 150 sec. it will send out a ping of the features it is tracking. As the study will last
for approximately 6 months from tag insertion to completion, a battery life of 7 months
was selected, and a power output of 141 dB. The full specification sheet is included in
appendix A (Thelma Biotel AS 2023).

2.2.3.3
YAPS
While the tags have many functions and extra features they can record, the most important
feature is their use in recording animal positions over time. In order to accurately make use
of the data provided by the tags and hydrophones, some type of positioning system needs
to be utilized. Though not utilized in the current scope of this project, a brief description
of the positioning solver; Yet Another Positioning Solver will be given as it is intended to
be used in the future when the data is collected.
Yet Another Positioning Solver (YAPS) is a positioning model designed to improve esti-
mation of positions of aquatic animals using acoustic telemetry data. It is an open-source,
hardware-agnostic tool which is beneficial for studies which involve multiple institutions,
for example, if they each use different hydrophone models (OceanTrackingNetwork 2021).
YAPS uses Time-of-Arrival (TOA) data, which comes directly from hydrophones, as op-
posed to using Time Difference of Arrival (TDOA) methods as was previously typical.
TOA is the measurement of the exact time a signal from a transmitter arrives at each hy-
drophone. Each receiver records the absolute arrival time of the signal. Knowing these
precise arrival times, as well as the speed of sound in water, the distance between each
receiver and the transmitter can be calculated, allowing the position of the transmitter to
be triangulated from those distances. The TOA method requires all receiver’s clocks to
be accurately synchronized, as the method relies heavily on the absolute timing of the
signal arrivals. TDOA on the other hand, measures the difference in arrival times of a sig-
nal at a pair of receivers. Each receiver pair calculates the difference in the arrival times
of the signal. Using the differences in arrival times, hyperbolic equations can be created
that will describe possible locations of the transmitter, which is found in the intersection
of the hyperbolas. This method does not require receivers to have synchronized clocks,
however the differences in timing do need to be accurately measured. TDOA also usually

Biogenic Reef Manuscript Project
Publishing Process
The main goal of this section of the thesis project was to finalize an unfinished manuscript
for publication in an established scientific journal. In order to complete this, several steps
were required throughout the five-month duration of the thesis project.
3.1
Initial Edits
The beginning stages of this portion of the project included editing and cleaning up the
manuscript. The manuscript was initially written as a thesis project by another author in
2022, and had gone through several iterations, with multiple other authors. The draft used
for editing had multiple comments and fixes to be made. These fixes primarily included
grammar, sentence structure, word choice, and background knowledge. Given the differ-
ent authors this paper has had, it was also necessary to ensure all sections of the paper
were collaborative and none were contradicting one another unintentionally.
One of the most time-consuming corrections was to update the sources used in the paper.
As the paper was from two years ago, it was vital to update the paper with as many
recent sources as possible in order to make it more relevant, and more cohesive with
new research found in the two years since its initial drafting. A final stage of this step
was to create the source library of sources used in the paper. In the draft provided, the
”Works Cited” section listed only five sources, which was an inaccurate depiction of the
actual sources used in the paper. This was quite challenging as the in-text citations within
the manuscript were only done using the format (Last Name, Year), and some were very
difficult to find. Luckily, one of the previous co-authors was contacted, and they were able
to provide around half of the 135 total sources used, the rest were found manually using
search engines such as SCOPUS.
3.2
Co-Authors and Journal Selection
Once all edits had been made and the manuscript was cleaned, the next step was to send
the finished version to all the co-authors involved. Each author got a copy of the draft,
along with any notes or questions directed their way. The authors were given a rough
time frame of a month to return their feedback.
Once drafts were sent out, the next step was to begin the selection of which journal to
publish with. This is an important part of the process, as the journal eventually selected
will determine who the primary audience of the paper is, and how far its reach can be.
Journals with higher impact factors will have a higher chance of getting an article to a wider
following, however the drawback is that usually they are more difficult to get a successful
publication in, with longer response times and reviewing processes.
The process of selecting a journal was guided by an in-depth analysis of the journals cited
within the manuscript, as well as its content and message. The journals I most frequently
referenced were the Journal of Sea Research, the Journal of Experimental Marine Biol-
ogy and Ecology, and the Marine Ecology Progress Series. Consideration was given to
the distinct aims and scopes of each journal, which significantly influenced the decision
on where to publish. Ultimately, the Journal of Sea Research was selected due to its
alignment with the manuscript’s aims and scope. Although the Marine Ecology Progress

numerous reviewers, which would potentially slow the review process considerably.
The final step at this stage was to begin the selection of reviewers for submission to

subject, those considered experts in the relevant field. Reviewers cannot be from the

3.3
Co-Author Revision
This stage of the project presented the largest challenge, which was getting everyone’s
responses in a timely manner. Many of the co-authors involved in this paper naturally
have other responsibilities and are not necessarily prioritizing a paper they contributed
to two years ago, however part of the responsibility of taking on the editing and submit-
ting process was to push and encourage speedy response. Setting solid deadlines, and
maintaining follow up with the co-authors, was vital to this part of the process.
Once the co-authors have completed their revisions, the next step is to incorporate the
new feedback into the manuscript. In this case, it proved somewhat difficult given the
different timings that co-authors gave feedback, requiring multiple editing sessions of the
same content where each author wanted something slightly different, or moved some-
where. One of the main challenges of this section was taking very different feedback and
navigating the best way forward using combinations of everyone’s suggestions. For ex-
ample, the section about the BRUVS was under contention about its placement through-
out the manuscript, with a pretty even split amongst co-authors regarding it staying in the
introduction versus moving it to the methods section. My decision to eventually place it
in the methods section came from the content of it being very focused on its use in the
experiment, along with changes I made to make it fit more seamlessly into that section.
3.4
Submission and Next Steps
At this stage, the manuscript is almost ready for submission. Given the time frame, a
second round of co-author revision was advised against, however there will be a chance
for another co-author revision after the feedback from reviewers is received. At this point,
the journal, and reviewers have been selected, and the remaining steps are to complete
the necessary supplementary material for submission. This includes a cover letter to
the editor-in-chief of the journal, conflict of interest statements, a full acknowledgements
section, and a graphical abstract, all of which can be seen in appendix A. Once these
final aspects were completed, the manuscript was submitted using the selected journals’
online submission portal.
At this point in the thesis, the manuscript has been accepted for the review process and
its status states ”Under Review”. The manuscript can remain in this state anywhere from
1 to 3 months, depending on the reviewers. Given the uncertain nature of the revision
process, we are looking at an expected published article in around December 2024 at the
earliest.

4.5.1
Tagging
The tagging process took place on the boat, as caught fish were kept aboard in a well
within the boat’s hull. Around 25 fish were caught at this first tagging stage, however
around 10 were put back due to injuries such as bulged eyes (exophthalmia), or severe
lacerations. Transmitters were turned on individually, and checked using a backup hy-
drophone to ensure they were transmitting signals. Two tubs were set up, both with sea-
water siphoned onto the boat. One tub was kept as is and all fish being used for tagging
were placed within. The other tub was prepared with benzocaine in order to sedate the
fish. The dosage of benzocaine should be 7.5 ml per 5 l of water. The tub held approxi-
mately 70 l of water, so approximately 105 ml of benzocaine was used. Throughout the
sedation process, as fish take up the benzocaine, it needed to be added again in small
doses in order to continue working effectively.
Tubs were initially aerated using a pump and airstone, however after generator failure,
tubs were aerated manually by scooping out water and replacing to introduce oxygen, or
in the benzocaine tub scooped up and recycled back in so as not to dilute the sedative.

Release
In order to release the fish after tagging, a device referred to as the ”fish elevator” was
created. Its purpose is to be able to get the tagged fish down to the sea floor and then
release them so that they don’t miss the intended site while swimming down 7-9 m from
the surface. A diagram of the mechanism is shown in Figure 4.10. Ropes at least 8 m
long were attached to the rod protruding from the top (rope 1), and to the handles on

loaded in at a time. Once cod were loaded, the device was gently lifted over the side of
the boat and lowered into the water by rope 1. Once the device reached the seafloor,
the person holding rope 2 pulled up harshly, dislodging the top from the mesh bottom

were released at the reef site at this time. Transmitters of deceased fish were recovered

Discussion
5.1
Connection
The EU Habitats Directive Interpretation Manual of EU Union Habitats describes reefs as
either biogenic concretions or of geogenic origins. In this thesis project, both types of reef
are represented, mussel reefs formed of Mytilus edulis, and a man-made rocky reef. The
mussel reef acts as a hotspot for biodiversity, offering food, shelter, and breeding grounds
for many marine organisms. Similarly, the rocky reef introduces similar features for marine
wildlife, also aiding with increasing biodiversity. Both reef structures are capable of hosting
a variety of species, as evidenced by the wide array of species seen present at the mussel
reef in this study, one of which includes cod.
The primary goal of this thesis was to develop an understanding of the two reef types
and how they functioned individually, in order to attempt to understand how both reef
types can be used to improve reef restoration efforts. Connecting the two types of reef
research can prove beneficial as by integrating research on mussel reef biodiversity and
cod reestablishment on man-made reefs, conservationists can develop comprehensive
strategies that enhance marine biodiversity and ecosystem health. These efforts not only
contribute to the objectives of Natura 2000 and the EU Habitats Directive but also support
sustainable fisheries and resilient marine environments.
Using insights from mussel reef studies such as habitat structures and other conditions
that support high biodiversity, management plans for man-made geogenic reefs can be
more precisely refined to what is successful. If certain formations made by biogenic reefs
prove the most beneficial as, for example, nursery or spawning areas, geogenic reefs can
be intentionally shaped and formed to adhere to those conditions. While the two reef types
have similarities in terms of what they can provide, they also have some key differences.
Rocky reefs tend to be more stable overall, being less susceptible to wave action and
tides than mussel reefs. Conversely, mussel reefs, while lacking in stability, are able to
expand over time and also provide the filtering capability of the mussels that make them.
Mussels can also grow on boulder reefs, allowing for some speculation on future projects
where the two can potentially be combined effectively. Using a hybrid approach with both
types combined can lead to more resilient and productive ecosystems by providing a
wider range of habitats and ecological functions. Integrating both habitat types can offer
a suite of ecosystem services, such as improved water quality from mussel filtration and
increased coastal protection from the physical structure of rocky reefs.
The two projects in this thesis were also connected by location. Both taking place some-
what near each other on the southwestern coast of Fyn. While the rocky reef is located
within a Natura 2000 protected area (European Environment Agency 1998), Helnæs Bay
unfortunately is not. Natura 2000s protected areas are designed to ensure the long-term
survival of Europe’s most valuable and threatened species and habitats.
Research and conservation efforts on both biogenic and geogenic reefs contribute to this
goal by protecting and enhancing marine biodiversity, and can pave a way forward to
future coastal restoration.

6
Conclusion
This thesis has provided a comprehensive exploration of two distinct but interconnected
projects: the ecological significance of mussel reefs and the fieldwork mapping cod move-
ments near a man-made rocky reef. Both studies aimed to enhance our understanding of
these reef types and their roles in coastal habitat restoration, aligning with the overarching
goal of developing effective conservation and management strategies.
The study on mussel reefs underscored their crucial role in supporting taxonomic diver-
sity, even in degraded environments like Helnæs Bay. Despite the bay’s poor ecological
status and ongoing eutrophication, mussel reefs were found to support significantly higher
diversity of mobile fauna compared to soft-bottom sites. Both intertidal and subtidal reefs
hosted unique assemblages of species, emphasizing the need for habitat-specific con-
servation measures. The findings highlighted the importance of mussel reefs in coastal
conservation efforts and suggested that large-scale restoration of bivalve reefs could be
a critical strategy for achieving GES in European water bodies.
In contrast, the fieldwork mapping cod movements near the man-made rocky reef has not
yet yielded definitive findings but provided valuable hands-on experience with the practical
aspects of marine research. This included the setup and deployment of equipment es-
sential for tracking marine species. Although the primary focus was on gaining fieldwork
experience rather than generating data, the project reinforced the importance of rocky
reefs as unique habitats under protection by Natura 2000 and the EU Habitats Directive.
Participating in these projects has significantly enhanced my understanding of reef ecosys-
tems. The insights gained from studying mussel reefs, in particular, were new and en-
lightening. The hands-on experience with fieldwork also provided a practical perspective
on the complexities of marine research. Overall, my research experiences have aligned
well with my initial goals, although I would have liked to delve deeper into the species
diversity associated with the rocky reef.
In conclusion, this thesis has contributed to a deeper understanding of the ecological roles
of mussel and rocky reefs and highlighted their potential for enhancing coastal habitat
restoration efforts. By integrating ecological studies with practical fieldwork, future con-
servation strategies can be better informed and more effective in preserving these vital
marine ecosystems.

ACTIVITY
The total acceleration in the activity sensor transmitter is measured in three axes (X, Y and 
Z) and consists of two components, static and dynamic acceleration. The tag runs the 
raw acceleration data through a low-pass filter  where the static component, with gravity 
and sensor offset, is subtracted from the acceleration reading. The remaining part of the 
reading is the dynamic part with information about the animals movement.
The measurement output transmitted can be calculated using the equation for ODBA 
(overall dynamic body acceleration) or RMS (root mean square). We propose to chose 
RMS or ODBA based upon the studies your work refernce to, aligning your results for 
comparison.
“A number of studies have demonstrated a clear individual-specific relationship between 
oxygen consumption (VO2) and ODBA, showing that ODBA can be used as a proxy for 
energy expenditure during the tested activities on the tested species” (Wilson et al., 
2020). “An important caveat is that acceleration-based proxies only quantify mechanical 
energy use and cannot account directly for metabolic energy consumed by physiological 
processes such as thermoregulation” (Gleiss, Wilson, et al., 2011).

STORAGE
If the transmitters are not going to be used 
immediately upon arrival, we propose to inspect 
that all transmitters are de-activated. Place a 
hydrophone/receiver close to the transmitters 
and check that no signals are received within the 
maximum transmission delay period.
Storage at low temperatures (i.e. in refrigerator) may 
have a positive effect on the battery life. It is however 
not recommended to store batteries at temperatures 
below 0°C. The transmitter will consume some 
power while in storage. This will have some effect on 
battery lifetime, depending on transmitter type and 
specification.
TRANSMISSION INTERVAL &  
TRANSMIT COLLISIONS
Coded transmitters generally transmit an ID and 
optional sensor value at a pseudo-randomly selected 
time between a pre-set minimum and maximum 
time delay. The delay times can be chosen freely to 
optimize for battery life or data rate. It may also be 
set to a fixed interval, and this interval can be used 
as a secondary identifier.
Random transmission times is implemented to avoid 
repeated code collisions. Collisions occur when two 
transmitters transmit at the same time. The rate of 
collisions will increase with the transmitter density 
and increased rate of transmissions. The transmitting 
interval will also affect the battery lifetime of the 
transmitters.
Our TBR receivers differentiate between several 
power levels and store the strongest transmits 
even though there are overlapping, weaker signals 
present. They also support multiple frequency 
channels which can space tags and reduce collisions 
overall.
Contact Thelma Biotel for further details and advice 
regarding your proposed study.

Abstract:
Coastal ecosystems worldwide face increasing anthropogenic pressures, with
implications for the local ecological status and biodiversity. Using a temperate coastal
ecosystem, this study firstly documented ecological status measures across decades,
and secondly revealed the importance of blue mussel (Mytilus edulis) reefs in
supporting mobile marine fauna (e.g., fish), as well as the potential role of such reefs in
improving the ecological status of the area. The Water Framework Directive (WFD) of
the European Union was applied to assess the ecological status across time, based on
the indicators eelgrass (Zostera marina) depth limits and summer chlorophyll a
concentrations. Using baited remote underwater video systems (BRUVS), we
compared mobile marine fauna associated with A) mussel reefs in shallow and deeper
parts of the area and B) corresponding  soft-bottom sites. The present study shows
that the ecological status of the examined coastal ecosystem remains poor, according
to the WFD indicators, largely caused by fertilizers (especially nitrogen) from land and
associated coastal eutrophication. Our BRUVS findings demonstrate that blue mussel
reefs harbor a higher taxonomic diversity of mobile fauna compared to the
corresponding soft-bottom sites. Furthermore, the species community associated with
mussel reefs exhibits distinct composition from the soft-bottom sites. Despite efforts to
mitigate eutrophication, thepoor ecological status of the coastal ecosystem highlights
ongoing challenges in coastal management. Our results have important implications for
management of coastal resources and conservation of marine biodiversity. Despite its
ecological challenges, the examined ecosystem serves as a representative case for
many temperate waterbodies emphasizing the need for improved management
outcomes. Additionally, the presence of mussel reefs offers potential mitigation
opportunities, because mussel reefs may counter eutrophication and support a diverse
assemblage of mobile fauna. Incorporating mussel reef conservation and restoration
into temperate management actions could enhance ecosystem resilience and local
biodiversity in many coastal ecosystems subjected to anthropogenic pressures.

cators, we assessed the ecological status of the area across decades, revealing a 
discrepancy between the observed poor conditions and the indicator values for 
"good" ecological status, according to the European Union 
 
Our research highlights the importance of mussel reefs as key habitats in support-
ing biodiversity, particularly of mobile marine fauna. Utilizing baited remote under-
water video systems (BRUVS), we conducted surveys to assess the taxonomic di-
versity and composition of mobile fauna associated with mussel reefs and nearby 
soft-bottom habitats. This was accomplished in shallow and deeper areas and 
compared directly. Our findings demonstrate that mussel reefs often harbor a sig-
nificantly higher taxonomic diversity of mobile fauna compared to adjacent soft-bot-
tom habitats, suggesting their critical role in maintaining biodiversity, even in eu-
trophicated coastal bays. 
 
We believe that our study contributes valuable insights into coastal ecosystem 
management and conservation, particularly in the context of addressing eutrophi-
cation and promoting biodiversity. Given the relevance of our findings to the 
broader scientific community engaged in coastal ecology and management, we be-
lieve that publication in the Journal of Sea Research would provide an excellent 
platform to disseminate our research.

Abstract 
23 
Coastal ecosystems worldwide face increasing anthropogenic pressures, with implications for the local ecological status and 
24 
biodiversity. Using a temperate coastal ecosystem, this study firstly documented ecological status measures across decades, 
25 
and secondly revealed the importance of blue mussel (Mytilus edulis) reefs in supporting mobile marine fauna (e.g., fish), as 
26 
well as the potential role of such reefs in improving the ecological status of the area. The Water Framework Directive (WFD) 
27 
of the European Union was applied to assess the ecological status across time, based on the indicators eelgrass (Zostera 
28 
marina) depth limits and summer chlorophyll a concentrations. Using baited remote underwater video systems (BRUVS), we 
29 
compared mobile marine fauna associated with A) mussel reefs in shallow and deeper parts of the area and B) corresponding  
30 
soft-bottom sites. The present study shows that the ecological status of the examined coastal ecosystem remains poor, 
31 
according to the WFD indicators, largely caused by fertilizers (especially nitrogen) from land and associated coastal 
32 
eutrophication. Our BRUVS findings demonstrate that blue mussel reefs harbor a higher taxonomic diversity of mobile fauna 
33 
compared to the corresponding soft-bottom sites. Furthermore, the species community associated with mussel reefs exhibits 
34 
distinct composition from the soft-bottom sites. Despite efforts to mitigate eutrophication, thepoor ecological status of the 
35 
coastal ecosystem highlights ongoing challenges in coastal management. Our results have important implications for 
36 
management of coastal resources and conservation of marine biodiversity. Despite its ecological challenges, the examined 
37 
ecosystem serves as a representative case for many temperate waterbodies emphasizing the need for improved management 
38 
outcomes. Additionally, the presence of mussel reefs offers potential mitigation opportunities, because mussel reefs may 
39 
counter eutrophication and support a diverse assemblage of mobile fauna. Incorporating mussel reef conservation and 
40 
restoration into temperate management actions could enhance ecosystem resilience and local biodiversity in many coastal 
41 
ecosystems subjected to anthropogenic pressures.

1. Introduction 
64 
Coastal habitats such as eelgrass meadows and biogenic reefs, are of key importance to marine mobile fauna, including 
65 
commercially important and protected species. These habitats provide a variety of ecosystem functions, such as 
66 
provisioning of spawning and feeding sites as well as nursery grounds and shelter (Gutiérrez et al., 2011; Heckwolf et al., 
67 
2021; Seitz et al., 2014). Likewise, coastal habitats offer important services to humans, including the protection of 
68 
coastlines from wave energy, control of water quality and food production (Airoldi & Beck, 2007, Jordan, O'Higgins, & 
69 
Dittmar, 2012). Among coastal habitats, bivalve reefs are often considered as some of the most diverse systems, providing 
70 
hotspots of biodiversity within coastal areas (Suchanek, 1992; Benjamin, 2022; Sea, 2022). Bivalves are classified as 
71 
ecosystem engineers, modulating both biological and physical components of their surrounding environment, e.g. by 
72 
creating spatially complex three-dimensional habitats, altering flow dynamics and sediment retention, and by affecting 
73 
benthopelagic coupling (Gutiérrez et al., 2011; Kent, 2017; Walles, 2015). The reef-building capacity of bivalves results 
74 
in increases in habitat complexity that have been linked to higher abundances and diversity of associated marine 
75 
communities (Airoldi & Beck, 2007; Norling & Kautsky, 2007, 2008, van der Ouderaa 21). Previous studies have shown 
76 
that mussel reefs support unique species assemblages compared to surrounding areas (Buschbaum et al., 2009; Sea, 2022), 
77 
making biogenic reefs highly important habitats in coastal areas. Due to the roles that mussel reefs play, working towards 
78 
environmental management strategies to best conserve mussel reefs and halt deteriorating environmental conditions 
79 
(Stounberg et al., 2024). This study highlights that mussel reefs are key to improving degraded coastal systems through 
80 
enhancing/strengthening the food webs within these areas and increasing local biodiversity. Existing research has largely 
81 
focused on infaunal communities associated with mussel reefs and rarely compared intertidal and subtidal mussel reefs, a 
82 
gap that this study, in part, also aims to address. 
83 
 
84 
The blue mussel Mytilus edulis (hereafter referred to as mussel) is the dominant reef-building mussel species and 
85 
benthic filter feeder in the Baltic Sea region (Dahl & Petersen, 2018). However, mainly due to high levels of 
86 
eutrophication in Baltic Sea coastal waters over the past century (Andersen et al., 2017; Elmgren et al., 2015; Altieri & 
87 
Witman, 2006), a variety of coastal habitats, including mussel reefs, are at risk and have been declining (HELCOM, 
88 
2013). At the turn of the century, an estimated 85% of European coastlines were degraded (EEA, 1999), highlighting the 
89 
urgent need for effective management actions. In 1992, the EU Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC) was implemented to 
90 
conserve biodiversity by maintaining and restoring natural habitats and protecting wild species of European importance. 
91 
Biogenic reefs were considered important habitats, requiring all EU member states to ensure their protection. In addition, 
92 
the EU adopted the Water Framework Directive in 2000 (WFD; 2000/60/EC), further calling for the conservation of all 
93 
European surface waters to reduce damage to natural habitats and the associated species. The WFD requires classification 
94 
==PAGE:80==
 
5
of all water bodies according to their ecological status into five status classes (bad, poor, moderate, good and high) using a 
95 
set of indicators. Phytoplankton biomass, using chlorophyll (Chl) a concentration, was selected as one indicator and is 
96 
intercalibrated for the Baltic Sea to ensure consistency (Henriksen et al., 2014; Carstensen, 2016). Additionally, Denmark 
97 
adopted eelgrass depth limit (EDL) as an indicator, which responds to water transparency phytoplankton biomass 
98 
(Carstensen et al., 2008). Increases in phytoplankton concentrations due to eutrophication result in decreased water 
99 
transparency, which limits the light available for eelgrass and the depth to which it can grow. The aim of the WFD is to 
100 
achieve Good Ecological Status (GES) for all surface waters, with “good” being the second highest ecological status class. 
101 
In 2008, the Marine Strategy Framework Directive (MSFD; Directive 2008/56/EC) was put into effect, reinforcing the 
102 
goal of achieving GES and calling for further minimization of anthropogenic eutrophication and the sustainable use of 
103 
marine resources. The MSFD recognizes biogenic reefs as special habitats (Annex III) and limits EU member states to 
104 
exploit populations within safe biological limits. However, even with these measures to protect marine habitats, recent 
105 
assessments of the ecological status of Danish coastal waters revealed that 98% of the monitored water bodies are 
106 
degraded and do not reach GES (EEA 2021). This ongoing degradation of coastal environments remains one of the main 
107 
threats to mussel reefs in the region, as eutrophication induced hypoxia  (Beck et al., 2009; Mcleod et al., 2019; Maus, 
108 
2024; Banke et al., 2024). 
109 
 
110 
 
In addition to environmental degradation, biogenic reefs and other coastal habitat are facing increasing pressure 
111 
from destructive dredging, bottom trawling, and overexploitation (Airoldi & Beck, 2007). Despite the protection of 
112 
biogenic reefs through EU legislation, the reefs may be exploited if they hold economic or cultural value (EU Habitat 
113 
Directive, 1992). In Denmark, the government allows bivalve fisheries to operate in selected marine protected areas if the 
114 
fisheries are considered to be of great importance to the industry. While sustainable methods that limit the impact on 
115 
vulnerable habitats and species have been proposed (Nielsen et al., 2021), disruptive fishing methods such as dredging are 
116 
often used (Fitzsimons, 2019; Mcleod et al., 2019). Dredging has severe effects on coastal habitats and benthic 
117 
communities, such as reducing community biomass as well as the complexity and quality of benthic habitats. This often 
118 
induces resuspension of sediments by removing structural elements, and can cause shifts in species composition (Cook et 
119 
al., 2013; Frandsen et al., 2015; McLaverty et al., 2020). Recovery periods for bivalve beds often take multiple years even 
120 
after a single dredging event (Cook et al., 2013) and in areas with repeated impact, recovery can take decades, or not 
121 
occur at all (Cranfield et al., 2004). Bivalve reefs are among the most endangered coastal habitats globally (Airoldi & 
122 
Beck, 2007; Beck et al., 2009) and are drastically declining in areas subjected to fisheries using bottom-towed gear (Strain 
123 
et al., 2012). 
124 
 
125 
 
The study site of the presented research, Helnæs Bay, used to be dominated by mussel reefs (Rask et al., 2000) 
126 
==PAGE:81==
 
6
but has faced many anthropogenic pressures in the past, including mussel fisheries and extraction of sand and gravel 
127 
(Kristensen, 2016). Impacts from commercial activities in combination with eutrophication caused a decline in ecological 
128 
status, ultimately resulting in the disappearance of many marine habitats in the area, including mussel reefs (Altieri & 
129 
Witman, 2006). To combat this decline, researchers collaborated with local stakeholders in 2010 and 2011 to establish 
130 
approximately 121.000 m2 of new mussel reefs from > 40 tonnes of blue mussels in Helnæs Bay. The aim was to enhance 
131 
structural integrity and environmental conditions, thereby ultimately increasing fish abundance and diversity in the area 
132 
(Kristensen et al., 2015). In the short term, within the first year of the restoration, an increase in biodiversity and 
133 
abundance of fish around the reefs was observed. However these mussel reefs have since disappeared from the areas 
134 
where they were established. Still, the mussels may have functioned as source sites for other mussel reefs within the bay 
135 
that were able to settle and persist in other areas (Elsäßer et al., 2013; Van der Heide et al., 2014). In recent years, interest 
136 
in the area for mussel fishing has increased and a dredging company (Wittrup Seafood A/S, Horsens, Denmark) carried 
137 
out an explorative survey in Helnæs Bay in 2018 to map stocks for potential exploitation. After local stakeholders and 
138 
fishermen expressed concerns, a ban on the use of all bottom towing fishing gear in the area, including mussel dredging 
139 
(§4, Bekendtgørelse om trawl- og vodfiskeri, BEK nr. 366, 2019), was enforced. The ban provided a unique opportunity 
140 
to investigate the remaining mussel reefs in the bay and examine the importance of their continued protection.  
141 
 
142 
 
The aim of this study was to assess the ecological status of Helnæs Bay as well as to investigate the effect of 
143 
mussel reefs on mobile fauna by comparing natural reefs to unconsolidated soft-bottom habitats within the bay. 
144 
Specifically, we tested the following hypotheses: 
145 
 
146 
(1) The current environmental conditions in Helnæs Bay do not meet the requirement of GES by the WFD. 
147 
 
148 
(2) Mussel reefs host a significantly higher taxonomic diversity of mobile marine fauna in comparison with nearby 
149 
soft-bottom sites 
150 
 
151 
(3) The species community of mobile marina fauna found at subtidal mussel reefs is compositionally distinct from the 
152 
community found at intertidal mussel reefs.

2. Materials & Methods

2.1. Study area 
157 
The study site Helnæs Bay is located in southern Denmark on the southwestern coast of the island Funen (Fig. 1). The 
158 
west side of the bay is closed off by Helnæs peninsula, a Natura 2000 protected area, yet the bay itself is not a protected 
159 
area (92/43/EEC). In the south, the bay is open and only separated by three small islands stretching from East to West, 
160 
==PAGE:82==
 
7
entering into the Little Belt Strait. The strait is located between Funen and the Jutland Peninsula and connects the Baltic 
161 
Sea to the Kattegat. Water levels within the bay are influenced by the levels in the Baltic Sea, with wind conditions 
162 
determining the in- and outflow of water. Water depths can reach 15 m and the seafloor is largely covered with soft, sandy 
163 
sediment with patchy eelgrass meadows (Zostera marina) and mussel reefs, the latter being a historically commercially 
164 
exploited resource. The bay has minor streams draining into it and is classified as a type M1 estuary, a mesohaline, 
165 
stratified estuary with a low freshwater impact and long residence time, based on the Danish coastal water typology 
166 
(Christiansen et al., 2004). The WFD requires a classification of water bodies on a national level to ensure better type-
167 
specific status monitoring, evaluation of reference condition and implementation of conservation measures (European 
168 
Commission, 2003a).

2.2. Selecting mussel reef habitats and control sites 
177 
Four different habitat types located in Helnæs bay were selected (Fig 1): intertidal mussel reefs, intertidal sandy bottom 
178 
control sites, subtidal mussel reefs, subtidal sandy bottom control sites. Shallow, intertidal sites ranged between 0.5-1.5 
179 
meters; deep, subtidal sites had a depth of 7-9 meters. Control sites had a low mussel coverage ranging between 0-29%, 
180 
==PAGE:83==
 
8
while high coverage was defined as a mussel coverage between 30-100%. For Denmark, a minimum coverage ratio of 
181 
30% is specified for a mussel reef to be classified as biogenic reef (Dahl & Petersen, 2018). Similarly, the OSPAR 
182 
Convention (2015) defines mussel reef density as high when the coverage is at least 30%. Before the initial deployment at 
183 
each chosen site,  Mussel coverage and identification of sandy bottom control sites was visually confirmed using a 
184 
Trident OpenROV (SoFar Ocean Technologies, USA) underwater drone. For each habitat type, four different sampling 
185

186 
video analysis, one sampling site was identified to contain both habitat types (mussel and soft-bottom), which lead to the 
187 
site being split, resulting in a total of 17 unique sampling sites (Fig. 1). To minimize potential overlap of sighted 
188 
individuals, all sampling sites deployments were separated by at least 300m.

2.3. Environmental assessment 
191 
The ecological status of the ecosystem was classified using the WFD classification system and environmental data from 
192 
Helnæs Bay was monitored as part of the Danish nationwide monitoring program NOVANA. It is recommended to 
193 
include several indicators in the status assessment, to reduce the risk of classification errors (Krause-Jensen et al., 2005). 
194 
Here, we selected two indicators for the biological quality elements: depth limit of eelgrass and summer chlorophyll a 
195 
concentration. There are five status classes separated by four threshold values (Table 1). The WFD describes three 
196 
possible approaches for status assessment in case of overlap between confidence intervals and the reference thresholds 
197 
(European Commission, 2003c). When using the ‘benefit-of-the-doubt approach’, a water body may pass for a particular 
198 
class if part of the upper confidence limit lies within the associated range, even if the mean value of the parameter of 
199 
interest (P) falls below this range. In contrast, when using the ‘fail-safe approach’, a system with P within a higher class 
200 
will still fail it if the lower confidence limit lies in a lower class. Finally, the ‘face-value approach’ disregards the confidence 
201 
interval entirely and classifies a system solely based on P, which is the approach selected in this study. The WFD further 
202 
specifies the application of the “one out, all out” principle (European Commission, 2003b), meaning that the ecological 
203 
status of the analyzed water body corresponds with the lowest status indicated by any of the quality indicators used. 
204 
Therefore, if one of the applied indicators does not meet the quality standard, the ecosystem as a whole fails to meet the 
205 
requirements for the quality class.

2.3.1. Eelgrass Depth Limit 
213 
The marine angiosperm Zostera marina is widely distributed throughout the entire northern hemisphere and is the dominant 
214 
sea grass species in the temperate North Atlantic (Short et al., 2007). In Denmark eelgrass can be found in most coastal 
215 
waters and many previous studies have focused on the use of EDL as a bioindicator (Krause-Jensen et al., 2005; Staehr et al., 
216 
2019), resulting in a large amount of site-specific reference values available for Danish coastal waters, including Helnæs Bay 
217 
(Timmermann et al., 2020). The depth limit of eelgrass is defined as the maximum water depth at which at least 10% 
218 
coverage of eelgrass is found (Henriksen et al., 2014). Growth depth directly responds to water transparency, which is 
219 
negatively influenced by eutrophication. In clearer waters the light penetrates deeper and therefore eelgrass and other marine 
220 
vegetation can grow at greater depth (Nielsen et al. , 2002). The indicator is limited by the water depth of the study area and 
221 
generally not useful in very shallow waters where the maximum water depth might be less than the potential growth depth of 
222 
eelgrass, which is not the case in Helnæs Bay. 
223 
 
The EDL data used in this study was collected as part of the Danish national monitoring program operated by the 
224 
Danish Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) in the years 2011 to 2018. Annual surveys during the main growth period 
225 
of eelgrass (Bruhn et al., 2013) in the summer months (June – September) were performed by divers who carried out 
226 
transects in 7 locations within the bay (Fig. 1), measuring the depth extensions of the eelgrass vegetation. The data was 
227 
normally distributed and mean values with 95% confidence intervals were calculated, first per transect site and subsequently 
228 
per year. Additionally, one average value for the six most recent years was calculated (Danish Marine Strategy II, 2019). The 
229 
confidence intervals (CIs) for the annual values were calculated from all measurements of the corresponding year and 
230 
represent the within-year variation, while the CI for the 6-year average was derived from the mean annual values and 
231 
represents the year-to-year variation. The annual depth limit value for the bay was then compared to reference values (Table 
232 
1), obtained from the Danish National Centre for Environment and Energy (Timmermann et al., 2020). These reference 
233 
==PAGE:85==
 
10
values are based on extensive  historical datasets from 1880 to 1930 where conditions are assumed to be nearly unaltered 
234 
by anthropogenic impacts.

2.3.2. Phytoplankton Biomass 
237 
A commonly used metric for assessing phytoplankton concentration is the concentration of the photosynthetic pigment 
238 
chlorophyll a, a proxy for phytoplankton biomass. Phytoplankton biomass was selected by the WFD as a key parameter 
239 
for the assessment of ecological status and eutrophication of marine systems. Average summer chlorophyll a 
240 
concentration for the months May-September has been intercalibrated for the Baltic Sea region (Carstensen et al., 2016), 
241 
and is widely applied as an environmental indicator (HELCOM). In Denmark, chlorophyll a is one of the most commonly 
242 
implemented parameters for environmental assessment and a large set of waterbody type-specific reference values 
243 
obtained from models is available, including limit values calculated for groups of comparable water bodies (Timmerman 
244 
et al., 2021). For Helnæs Bay, a data set of biweekly or monthly chlorophyll a concentration measurements, starting in 
245 
1983, is publicly available, monitored by the station FYN0018232 (Fig. 1) as part of the National monitoring program. 
246 
The WFD recommends a minimum of monthly data points in order to avoid biased classification. Similar to EDL’s, 
247 
annual summer average values and a 6-year average were calculated and subsequently compared to the reference values 
248 
for M1 waterbodies (Table 1; Danish Environmental Protection Agency 2014). 
249 
 
250 
2.4. Mobile fauna monitoring 
251 
Mobile fauna in Helnæs Bay were monitored using stereo-baited remote underwater video systems (BRUVS). BRUVS are 
252 
a commonly used tool for the investigation of species composition in a variety of aquatic habitats, especially ones that 
253 
might be difficult to access for SCUBA divers or where the presence of divers could introduce unwanted bias (Langlois et 
254 
al., 2010). Another advantage is that the method is non-extractive and non-destructive compared to fishing-based 
255 
techniques, e.g. catch-effort methods where organisms are removed from the ecosystem (Mallet & Pelletier, 2014; Schmid 
256 
et al., 2017). Other benefits include their cost efficiency and adaptability to the study design and location (Haggitt et al., 
257 
2014). Potential limitations include inhibition by low water visibility and selectivity of the sampled species with 
258 
difficulties detecting cryptic species (Haggitt et al., 2014; Watson et al., 2005). The principle of BRUVS is odour 
259 
propagation by the bait, which attracts organisms towards the cameras. This could introduce bias by drawing mobile fauna 
260 
from a larger radius or favouring certain species. However, recent studies have shown that using bait does not obscure 
261 
species-habitat relationships and produces the most consistent assemblage representation (Jones et al., 2020; Rhodes et al., 
262 
2020). BRUVS are thus considered a powerful non-invasive tool to monitor marine habitats and organisms, and were 
263 
==PAGE:86==
 
11
therefore used in this study to assess the mobile fauna communities within Helnæs Bay. The BRUVS setup consisted of a 
264 
metal frame (100cm x 65cm x 50cm; Fig. 2), attached to a rope and surface buoy. Two SONY DFR-X3000 cameras with 
265 
an additional external battery (ANKER PowerCore 10000 mAh) in SeaGIS camera housing (SeaGIS Pty Ltd, Australia) 
266 
were horizontally mounted onto the frame, 30 cm above the seabed and 60 cm apart at an angle of 8° convergence. A 100 
267 
cm long metal bait arm was horizontally attached to the frame between the cameras. A mesh bait bag containing 500 g of 
268 
chopped Atlantic herring (Clupea harengus) was attached to the end of the bait arm. A soak time of 60 minutes was 
269 
chosen for all deployments, which is the recommended soak time for stereo-BRUVS (Langlois, 2020), with an additional 
270 
5 minutes designated immediately after the camera deployment to allow for settlement of disturbed sediments.  
271 
 
BRUVS deployments were carried out by boat in April and May 2019 during daylight hours. Camera units were 
272 
placed on the seafloor at the sampling sites that were either mainly dominated by mussel reefs or sandy sediment and at 
273 
two different depths (Fig. 1). To minimize the potential overlap of sighted individuals during temporally overlapping 
274 
deployments, all deployments were separated by at least 300 m. 
275

2.5. Video analysis 
281 
Video recordings were analyzed using the SeaGIS EventMeasure software (https://seagis.au.com/event.html). Recorded 
282 
organisms were identified, ideally to species level and otherwise to the lowest taxonomic level possible, and were 
283 
subsequently quantified. Mobile fauna taxa included fish and large invertebrates (e.g. crabs, cnidarians and mollusks). Sea 
284 
snails from the family Caenogastropoda were omitted from the study since video analysis failed to produce reliable count 
285 
==PAGE:87==
 
12
estimates. Organisms were quantified in relative abundance, referred to as MaxN, which describes the maximum number of 
286 
individuals of one taxon per frame (Priede et al., 1994, Cappo et al., 2004) across the full soak period. By using this measure, 
287 
the bias from repeated counts of individuals can be minimized and a more conservative measure of species abundance is 
288 
obtained (Cappo et al., 2004; Watson et al., 2005). For each recording, data was collected on: 1) species present, 2) MaxN, 3) 
289 
mussel coverage and 4) functional visibility (m). Mussel coverage was estimated as the percentage of visible seabed covered 
290 
by mussels. Functional visibility, i.e. the greatest distance at which an organism was still identifiable (Rhodes et al., 2020), 
291 
was estimated for all recordings using the bait arm as a reference. Deployments were considered unsuccessful and 
292 
subsequently excluded from analyses when 1) cameras had failed to record for the full 60 minutes, 2) the frames were tilted 
293 
on the side and the cameras were not recording horizontally or 3) the camera setup got entangled in seagrass or macroalgae 
294 
and the field of view was fully obstructed. 
295

2.6. Statistical Analysis 
297 
2.6.1. Effect of habitat on species diversity 
298 
Statistical analyses were carried out using R statistical software (version 3.6.1). Effects of habitat type and sampling depth 
299 
were assessed using three taxonomic diversity indices: species richness, the Shannon-Wiener index and Pielou’s 
300 
evenness. The diversity indices were calculated for each deployment using the vegan package (Oksanen et al., 2019) and 
301 
subsequently deployments were grouped based on site depth (m) and mussel coverage (%) (Fig. S1, Supporting 
302 
Information). Generalized linear mixed models (GLMMs), incorporating fixed and random effects via maximum 
303 
likelihood, were fitted to each of the diversity indices using the glmmTMB package (Brooks et al., 2017). All models 
304 
included depth, mussel coverage and their interaction as fixed factors, while SiteID was defined as a random effect. 
305 
Functional visibility was included as an additional covariate in all models to account for variations in the field of view. 
306 
Akaike information criterion (AIC) values were compared to identify the distribution and model structure resulting in the 
307 
best model fit for each response variable (Table S1, Supporting Information). Model diagnostics revealed underdispersion 
308 
for the species richness model, both when fitting a regular Poisson and negative binomial distribution, which was 
309 
remedied by fitting the Conway-Maxwell distribution with a log-link function instead (Lynch et al., 2014). The 
310 
Shannon-Wiener index model was fitted using a Gaussian distribution with an identity link, which achieved a better fit than 
311 
regular Gamma and zero-inflated Gamma distributions and is commonly used by other studies assessing species diversity 
312 
with GLMMs (Greenwood & Booker, 2015; Lezama-Ochoa et al., 2018). Pielou´s evenness index is bound between 0 and 
313 
1 and fits a Beta distribution (Douma & Weedon, 2019), which is used for response variables between, but not including, 0 
314 
and 1. The model uses a logit-link function. A small number of deployments had extreme evenness values of 0 or 1, and 
315 
the data was transformed following (Smithson and Verkuilen, 2006): 
316

2.6.2. Community composition and indicator species 
323 
Multivariate analysis was used to test for differences in the community composition at the different sites and was carried out 
324 
with the vegan package (Oksanen et al., 2019). One deployment without any species occurrence was excluded from the 
325 
analysis. In the first step, we performed an unconstrained ordination using non-metric multidimensional scaling (nMDS) 
326 
and a Bray Curtis dissimilarity index (Faith et al., 1987), to calculate the distance matrix. The stress value reflects the 
327 
accuracy with which the reduced dimensions represent the multidimensional data and should ideally not exceed 0.2, with 
328 
lower values allowing for more reliable inferences from the ordination plot. Two-dimensional ordination revealed a stress 
329 
value of 0.21, therefore the dimensionality was increased to three yielding a decrease in stress value to 0.14. Next, we used 
330 
a canonical analysis of principal coordinates (CAP; Anderson & Willis, 2003) where the ordination was constrained by 
331 
depth and mussel coverage as environmental predictors, to test our hypothesis that mussel reefs host distinct species 
332 
communities. The significance of the overall CAP analysis, as well as for each of the environmental predictors included, 
333 
was tested with an analysis of variance (ANOVA). To further investigate which species were statistically driving the 
334 
differences, as well as their association with one or a combination of the explanatory variables, indicator species analysis 
335 
was carried out using the indicspecies package (De Caceres and Legendre, 2009).

3.1.2. Chlorophyll a indicator 
349 
For the chlorophyll a concentration, the CIs of the annual means were notably larger than the CI of the 6-year average (Fig. 
350

351

352

357

the years 2018 to 2023, the ecosystem clearly reflects a poor ecological status.  No general trend over time was observed.

4. Discussion 
464 
To date, one of the key functions of mussels as habitat providers for a diverse range of marine fauna remains critically 
465 
understudied in many areas, including the Baltic Sea (Westerbom & Koivisto 2022), despite the ongoing degradation of 
466 
mussel reefs across the globe. Not only does this limit our understanding on the importance of mussel reefs for marine 
467 
biodiversity, but it also hampers effective management efforts to conserve the integrity and functioning of these coastal 
468 
habitats. In this study, we carried out an environmental assessment of Helnæs Bay, a eutrophicated coastal area of the SW 
469 
Baltic Sea where mussel reefs were historically abundant but have been disappearing in more recent years, as calcification 
470 
and growth rates have been severely reduced (Sanders et al., 2021). Subsequently, we conducted BRUVS monitoring in the 
471 
bay to investigate the effects of remnant mussel reefs on mobile marine fauna. Our environmental assessment indicates that 
472 
Helnæs Bay does not meet the requirement of a ‘Good Ecological Status’ (GES) set by the WFD and is in an overall poor 
473 
ecological state, based on the analysis of a multi-year data set covering EDL and summer chlorophyll a concentration. No 
474 
temporal trends were observed for these indicators, implying no improvement of the ecological status over the past decades 
475 
and since the introduction of the WFD. Despite the degraded status of the bay, our study demonstrates that locations with 
476 
high mussel coverage support significantly higher species richness and diversity of mobile fauna relative to sites with low 
477 
mussel coverage. In addition, mussel reefs at both intertidal and subtidal depths hosted unique communities of mobile fauna 
478 
that were distinct from those observed at soft-bottom habitats of similar depth. Our study shows that current management 
479 
approaches aiming to reduce eutrophication in the study area are insufficient, while concurrently highlighting the importance 
480 
of mussel reef habitats in promoting biodiversity, even in environmentally degraded systems. Mussel reefs need to be 
481 
recognized as key habitats within marine conservation strategies, both for their potential to mitigate coastal eutrophication 
482 
and to support diverse marine ecosystems.   
483 
 
484 
4.1. Interactions between mussels and their surrounding environment 
485 
The poor ecological status recorded in Helnæs Bay reflects the conditions of the majority of Danish coastal waters. 
486 
Currently, only 5/109 assessed water bodies reached a GES, while 23 were classified as “moderate”, 55 as “poor” and 26 as 
487 
“bad” (EPA 2021). Similar conditions were recorded throughout the entire Baltic Sea (EEA 2021). While the effects of 
488 
eutrophication, as indicated by the high chlorophyll a concentration, are usually reported as negative for many marine 
489 
organisms (Burkholder et al., 2007; Cloern, 2001; Gray et al., 2002; Riemann et al., 2016), mussels on the contrary may 
490 
==PAGE:100==
 
25
benefit from elevated nutrient levels as long as it does not result in hypoxia. Wołowicz et al. (2006) demonstrated how 
491 
enhanced primary productivity, resulting in increased food availability for filter feeders such as mussels, could positively 
492 
impact these organisms by improving their physiology and extending their depth range. Enhanced grazing potential by 
493 
increased abundance of mussels in deeper waters could have a strong influence on the water quality, as enhanced filtration 
494 
and removal of phytoplankton and other suspended particles can reduce local turbidity (Capelle et al., 2017; Nielsen & Maar, 
495 
2007; Wołowicz et al., 2006). Greater water transparency could also positively impact aquatic vegetation, e.g. by extending 
496 
the depth range at which the vegetation can grow (Wall et al., 2008; Zhu, 2006) and thus promote habitat diversity. In 
497 
addition, mussels can play a key role in organic matter transfer into sediments through filtration and biodeposition (Kautsky 
498 
& Evans, 1987), thereby influencing benthopelagic coupling (Peterson & Heck Jr, 1999). Many studies (e.g. Green et al., 
499 
2009, Kotta et al., 2020 & Petersen et al. 2014) have described the potential of mussels to mitigate eutrophication effects by 
500 
removing nutrients from the water column. However, these studies all focused on mussel farms, where eventual harvesting of 
501 
the mussels ensures permanent removal of the nutrients from the system. This mitigation potential might not be as strong for 
502 
natural mussel reefs, where the nutrients are instead stored for a short time period and partially recycled back to the water 
503 
column as waste products, thereby remaining in the system (Nielsen & Maar, 2007). Additionally, the accumulation of feces 
504 
and pseudofeces in proximity to mussels could lead to an accumulation of nutrients such as ammonium or phosphate 
505 
(Nielsen & Maar, 2007) and result in hypoxia within sediments (Stadmark & Conley, 2011). The relationship between wild 
506 
mussel reefs and their environment, as examined in this study, is complex and poorly understood. Further research is 
507 
therefore needed to understand how filter feeders interact with eutrophic surroundings to assess their potential mitigation 
508 
ability. 
509 
 
Another critical relationship that should be considered is the one between mussels and eelgrass. Apart from being an 
510 
important biogenic habitat type for marine communities and a widely used environmental indicator, eelgrass also plays a role 
511 
in regulating the surrounding environmental conditions (Heckwolf et al., 2021). Eelgrass meadows have high capacity as 
512 
carbon sinks (Röhr et al., 2016) and can facilitate nutrient uptake (Asmala et al., 2019), which may help to improve the 
513 
ecological status of coastal waters. A review by (Gagnon et al., 2020) revealed that the majority of bivalve-eelgrass 
514 
interactions described in the literature are positive, with both species mutually facilitating each other. Eelgrass presence can 
515 
facilitate bivalve settlement, recruitment and survival by reducing hydrodynamic disturbances and may locally enhance food 
516 
supply for bivalves, depending on the density and size of eelgrass patches and local hydrodynamic conditions (Gagnon et al., 
517 
2020; Aucoin & Himmelman, 2011; Reusch, 1998; Reusch & Chapman, 1995). Bivalve survival and abundance are typically 
518 
elevated in vegetated habitats, as eelgrass offers shelter from predators (Caroll et al., 2015; Peterson & Heck, 2001). 
519 
Likewise, eelgrass may benefit from bivalve presence through increased concentrations of inorganic nitrogen and phosphorus 
520 
in the sediment and thereby a higher nutrient availability for eelgrass (Peterson & Heck, 2001; Vinther et al., 2008). 
521 
==PAGE:101==
 
26
Sediment stabilization by smaller bivalves such as mussels can also promote plant survival (Gagnon et al., 2020). However, 
522 
in eutrophic areas, such as Helnæs Bay, the mussels could have negative rather than positive effects on the eelgrass, adding 
523 
to already negative effects of eutrophication on eelgrass (Burkholder et al., 2007). The addition of nutrients by mussels to 
524 
already nutrient rich sediments might result in sulfide toxicity to the detriment of eelgrass development (Vinther et al., 2008; 
525 
Vinther & Holmer, 2008). In addition, enhanced nutrients could facilitate epiphyte growth on eelgrass leaves further 
526 
inhibiting eelgrass development (Vinther et al., 2008; Vinther & Holmer, 2008). The diverse effects of mussel reefs on 
527 
eelgrass show the importance of improving the ecological status in coastal waters. When a “good” status is not maintained, 
528 
some synergistic interactions between mussels and eelgrass could become antagonistic, potentially resulting in the loss of 
529 
eelgrass meadows. 
530 
 
The largely facilitative interactions between mussel reefs and eelgrass meadows highlight the benefits of an 
531 
ecosystem-based management approach relative to a species-specific one. Conservation measures need to consider species 
532 
interactions and the structural functioning of the ecosystem as a whole, to achieve an improvement of the ecological status. If 
533 
these complex habitats and the associated ecosystem functions are lost due to a poor ecological status, this could lead to 
534 
further degradation of Danish coastal waters. Therefore, mussel reefs and other coastal habitats must be protected and 
535 
restored to maintain their ecosystem functions and mitigation potential, ultimately to prevent further degradation of Helnæs 
536 
Bay and other coastal waters in Denmark.   
537 
 
538 
4.2. The importance of mussel reefs for mobile marine fauna 
539 
4.2.1. Species diversity 
540 
Despite the poor status of Helnæs Bay, the presence of mussels significantly increased the taxonomic diversity of mobile 
541 
fauna at the mussel reefs, relative to soft-bottom areas. This study highlights the value of complex habitats in hosting more 
542 
diverse faunal communities than unstructured habitats (van der Ouderaa et al., 2021; Viswambharan et al., 2018; Orfanidis et 
543 
al., 2021). To better understand the functional processes linked to the mussel reefs it is important to examine the species 
544 
associated with the compared habitats and how they might utilize them. The present study indicates that the species 
545 
communities of mobile fauna in Helnæs Bay are shaped by both depth and mussel coverage. Ten species were found to be 
546 
associated with either one or multiple habitats, highlighting the importance of each habitat individually. The loss of a specific 
547 
habitat (e.g. intertidal mussel reefs) could therefore have major implications for the associated species. 
548 
 
549 
4.2.2. Species-habitat associations 
550 
==PAGE:102==
 
27
Starfish (Asterias rubens) are common predators of mussels and showed strong association with deep mussel sites in this 
551 
study. This habitat preference for subtidal mussel reefs is commonly observed (Capelle et al., 2017; Norling et al., 2015) and 
552 
is likely due to the effect of tidal exposure at shallow mussel reefs. Intertidal habitats experience extreme conditions and 
553 
fluctuations (Saier, 2002; Smaal et al., 2018) and potential risks include predation by birds and desiccation (Gallagher et al., 
554 
2008), which may explain lower starfish presence at intertidal reefs. In addition, subtidal mussels often have smaller 
555 
adductor muscles than intertidal mussels (Hancock, 1965), making it easier for predators such as starfish to open subtidal 
556 
mussels (Reimer & Tedengren, 1996). Intense predation by starfish, especially on subtidal mussel reefs, can affect the 
557 
survival of mussel populations (Capelle et al., 2017) and high densities of starfish have been documented to invade and 
558 
deplete large patches of mussel reefs (Saier, 2001). 
559 
 
Three species displayed significant association with shallow mussel reefs: garfish (Belone belone) and two members 
560 
of the Gasterosteidea family, three-spined stickleback (Gasterosteus aculeatus) and nine-spined stickleback (Pungitius 
561 
pungitius). Garfish are commonly associated with mussel reefs and are frequently recorded near mussel farms (Callier et al., 
562 
2018; Sheehan et al., 2020). While garfish are usually pelagic fish, they migrate into coastal waters in spring for spawning in 
563 
seagrass meadows (Polte & Asmus, 2006), a habitat also present in Helnæs Bay and predominantly showing facilitative 
564 
interactions with mussel reefs (Gagnon et al., 2020). Gasterosteids are mesopredators and common components of garfish 
565 
diet (Dorman, 1991), which could potentially explain the increased garfish presence at the mussel reefs. Sticklebacks are 
566 
vulnerable to predation from larger piscivorous fish, such as garfish or cod, and prefer habitats with high structural 
567 
complexity because those habitats provide shelter and have been associated with higher survival of small-bodied fish 
568 
(Gagnon et al., 2019; Nelson & Bonsdorff, 1990). Further, sticklebacks feed on a variety of pelagic and benthic organisms, 
569 
including small crustaceans, molluscs and annelids (Jakubaviciute et al., 2017; FishBase), which are often present in high 
570 
quantities at mussel reefs (O’Connor & Crowe, 2007). 
571 
 
In contrast, the presence of sand goby (Pomatoschistus minutus), another mesopredatory fish, was linked to soft-
572 
bottom sites without mussel coverage. A previous tank experiment by Magnhagen (1988) found a weaker association of sand 
573 
gobies with vegetated areas than displayed by other Gobiidae species and a regular presence in open habitat, consistent with 
574 
the present study. This was attributed to their cryptic coloration and ability to bury themselves in sediments, a potential 
575 
explanation for the reduced need for shelter (Magnhagen, 1988). Although sand gobies are often the dominant species of 
576 
mobile fauna found in soft-bottom habitats (Verdiell-Cubedo, 2013; Perry, 2018), they are still frequently observed in more 
577 
complex habitats (Zander, 1990; Green, 20 12; Franca, 2009). They were also found at all sites in this study, hinting at a 
578 
potential reliance of sand gobies on one or more ecosystem functions provided by complex habitats, such as mussel reefs.  
579 
==PAGE:103==
 
28
  
Finally, two species showed significant association with depth rather than mussel coverage. The Lion’s mane 
580 
jellyfish (Cyanea capillata) predominantly occurred at shallow sites. As this jellyfish species does not directly interact with 
581 
benthic habitats and remains mostly near the water surface, a higher number of observations in shallow areas, where the 
582 
water surface was often visible within the video frame, was expected. In contrast, European flounder (Platichtys flesus) was 
583 
only found at deep sites. The flounder is a migratory species, known to inhabit inshore areas of widely ranging salinity 
584 
levels, including brackish and freshwater environments, and mostly migrate to deeper, more saline waters in winter to spawn 
585 
(Dando, 2011; Sims, 2004; Baden, 2022). However, the depth ranges in the present study (0.5-9 m water depth) are all within 
586 
the shallow coastal zone and we hypothesize that the complete absence of flounder at intertidal sites (0.5-1.5 m depth) may 
587 
instead be due to local environmental conditions (e.g. temperature). While the abundance of flounder was not explained by 
588 
variations in mussel coverage as suggested by our indicator species analysis, flounders are highly reliant on shelled molluscs 
589 
as a food source. In the Baltic Sea, the diet of flounder is dominated by blue mussels (Westerbom et al., 2018) and the 
590 
removal of their food source could have dramatic effects on the species. Flounders are less flexible in terms of habitat and 
591 
food selection relative to competitor species (Karlson et al., 2007; Westerbom et al., 2018) and destruction or changes in 
592 
their habitats could lead to their displacement. 
593 
 
594 
4.3. Comparing intertidal and subtidal mussel reefs 
595 
Mussel reefs in the intertidal zone experience different abiotic conditions in terms of exposure, temperature and physical 
596 
disturbance, relative to mussel reefs located at subtidal depths (Smaal et al., 2018). In addition, intertidal and subtidal 
597 
habitats are affected differently by anthropogenic impacts. Intertidal habitats could experience greater impacts from shifts in 
598 
hydrological dynamics, e.g. due to coastal alterations (Mainwaring et al., 2014), and from increasing submerged time due to 
599 
sea level rise (Andrade et al., 2021). Subtidal reefs in turn may experience increased vulnerability to human-induced hypoxia 
600 
events (Meng et al., 2018). These differences highlight the importance of allocating research efforts to both intertidal and 
601 
subtidal mussel reefs for conservation management. However, few studies have directly compared mobile fauna 
602 
communities inhabiting intertidal and subtidal mussel reefs (De Mesel, 2015; Saier 2002), which may be due to differences 
603 
in accessibility and methodological requirements when sampling across the different tidal zones.  The use of stereo-BRUVS 
604 
in this study made it possible to follow identical sampling protocols for both depths and achieve comparable results. Both 
605 
intertidal and subtidal reefs were associated with significantly higher species richness of mobile fauna, while intertidal reefs 
606 
also hosted a higher Shannon diversity relative to soft-bottom sites of the same water depth. In addition, the community 
607 
composition of mobile fauna significantly differed between the two depths, a result similar to the study by Saier 2002, which 
608 
found that non-attached epifaunal communities of mussel reefs were strongly shaped by tidal zone. Therefore, while both 
609 
intertidal and subtidal mussel reefs can promote the taxonomic diversity of mobile fauna relative to soft-bottom areas, even 
610 
==PAGE:104==
 
29
in degraded coastal areas, the two habitat types are also ecologically distinct, which should be reflected in their respective 
611 
conservation measures. 
612 
 
The importance of mussel reefs occurring in different tidal zones is currently not reflected by their protection status 
613 
in the EU. At present, the Habitat Directive (1992) only protects subtidal reefs and intertidal reefs connected to subtidal ones, 
614 
while independent intertidal reefs are not protected. In Helnæs Bay, the current legislation (§4, Bekendtgørelse om trawl- og 
615 
vodfiskeri, BEK nr. 366, 2019) only bans mussel dredging, which usually targets subtidal mussel reefs due to depth 
616 
limitations. Intertidal mussel reefs can still be harvested using other techniques such as handpicking (Bhendekar 2019), 
617 
creating a gap in protection for these intertidal habitats and associated organisms that can potentially compromise ecosystem 
618 
functioning and health. Especially small-bodied mesopredators, an important food source for larger, commercially important 
619 
fish, were found in higher abundances at the intertidal reefs in this study and are often found to be associated with 
620 
structurally complex habitats, likely utilizing these habitats for foraging, shelter or reproduction (Gagnon, 2019; Amundsen 
621 
& Forsgren, 2001; Wilms, 2021). Therefore, conservation measures should encompass mussel reefs across both tidal zones 
622 
to achieve healthy and diverse ecosystems. Likewise, these measure must be designed specifically for mussel reefs at each 
623 
tidal zone, as these different habitats may not have the same requirements. Intertidal and subtidal mussel reefs differ not only 
624 
in their morphological (e.g. muscle and shell size; (Saier, 2001; Bustnes, 1998) and physiological attributes (e.g. growth and 
625 
metabolic rates; (Tagliarolo, 2012; Arranz, 2016)), but as this study has demonstrated, the two habitats also host distinct 
626 
communities of mobile marine fauna, and therefore require conservation measures which are designed specifically for the 
627 
depths they inhabit. 
628 
 
629 
4.4. Study limitations 
630 
The methodology of BRUVS is a valuable tool in the detection of differences in abundance and diversity as well as 
631 
community structure of mobile fauna between different sites, as shown by the results presented in this study. However, there 
632 
are some general limitations related to the use of BRUVS, including potential bias in favor of predatory species due to the 
633 
use of bait (Watson, 2010; Goetze, 2015), although sampling of herbivores is generally not precluded (Harvey, 2007) and 
634 
only a few organisms were observed to actively feed on the bait in this study. The methods reliance on good visibility is high 
635 
and a potential implication of variations in functional visibility becomes apparent from the negative effect of visibility on 
636 
Pielou’s evenness detected in this study. High visibility led to a larger field of view and increased the area size within which 
637 
organisms can be observed, possibly leading to an increase in sightings of large-bodied species relative to smaller species at 
638 
greater distances from the camera, implying a less even species count. Fluctuations in visibility may thus affect recorded 
639 
diversity indices; accounting for visibility variations in model structures may help reduce bias by disentangling potential 
640 
visibility effects from the main population-level effects of interest, as was done in this study. 
641 
==PAGE:105==
 
30
 
It was beyond the scope of this study to investigate the mitigation potential of the mussel reefs to counteract 
642 
eutrophication. However, future studies could examine the phytoplankton and nutrient concentrations at mussel sites 
643 
compared to the control sites. This could be achieved by applying methods similar to Nielsen & Maar (2007), who took CTD 
644 
casts including fluorescence and measured chlorophyll a and nutrient concentrations with a high vertical resolution from 
645 
water samples at sand and mussel reefs. From such measurements, conclusions could be drawn on whether the mussels 
646 
significantly alter the plankton and nutrient concentrations above the mussel reefs and whether such alterations have 
647 
measurable effects on eutrophication levels of the surrounding habitat. A potential challenge for our study could be the 
648 
constant in- and outflow of water in Helnæs Bay, caused by tidal dynamics. This could make small-scale differences in the 
649 
measured parameters hard to detect, implying further research into feasible methodological approaches would be required. 
650 
Moreover, future studies should increase the temporal coverage of the study and collect data both throughout multiple years, 
651 
to avoid potential bias from inter-annual variation, and during all four seasons, as habitat use by mobile fauna can vary 
652 
seasonally. Our study only provides a snapshot of the use of mussel reefs by mobile fauna during spring and inferences for 
653 
other seasons have to be made with caution. In addition, there is no certainty on annual variability. Finally, this study could 
654 
not investigate potential diurnal patterns as data were collected exclusively during daylight hours. Additional deployments 
655 
during nighttime could be used to investigate diel patterns. Still, this study provided an insight into the ecological status of 
656 
Helnæs Bay as well as the effect of the mussel reefs on mobile coastal communities, demonstrating the key importance of 
657 
continued and extended protection of remnant mussel reefs as well as an improvement of the ecological status at the study 
658 
site and more general in Danish coastal waters. 
659 
 
660 
5. Conclusions 
661 
Our study has provided fresh insights into the importance of mussel reefs for mobile marine communities, even in 
662 
environmentally degraded areas. Helnæs Bay is representative of many coastal waterbodies in Denmark and, more generally, 
663 
of the Baltic Sea as a whole. Over the monitored period, the ecological status of the bay was poor and no improvements in 
664 
the status were observed, despite efforts to reduce eutrophication. This indicates potential deficiencies in current 
665 
management approaches and highlights the need for increased efforts and improved strategies. However, despite this poor 
666 
status, the mussel reefs supported a significantly higher diversity of mobile fauna relative to soft-bottom sites, with intertidal 
667 
and subtidal reefs hosting a unique assemblage of mobile fauna. Our results are highly relevant for developing strategies 
668 
towards multiple habitat consideration conservation measures. In that, they can be adapted to be more ecosystem-specific 
669 
once the habitat in question is better understood. Not only is the improvement of the ecological status of Helnæs Bay of key 
670 
importance to improve the ecosystem on a local scale, but it also represents a crucial step to reach the goal of Good 
671 
==PAGE:106==
 
31
Ecological Status in all European water bodies set by the WFD. The mitigation potential of mussels should be considered 
672 
within management actions, in addition to other measures to counteract eutrophication such as reducing land-based nutrient 
673 
runoff. Mussel reefs must be recognized as habitats of major importance for coastal conservation to achieve healthy and 
674 
diverse marine ecosystems. Current protection measures should be reconsidered and extended towards both intertidal and 
675 
subtidal reefs, as our study has highlighted that these habitats support distinct communities of marine fauna. In addition to an 
676 
improved protection status for currently existing mussel reefs, future efforts should be made towards large-scale restoration 
677 
of bivalve reefs to improve coastal habitats and achieve GES.
