Introduction
Recent strides of abrupt changes in the climate and growing concern around the world re-
garding the future of the planet have contributed signiﬁcantly in the growth of the renewable
energy sector. The recently concluded Paris Climate Summit, for the ﬁrst time in many years
brought together all countries as one for safe guarding the future of our planet by focusing on
developing clean mechanisms of energy production. This will further lead to huge amounts of
renewable energy penetrating the power grid. Voltage-Source Converters (VSC) are an attrac-
tive technology for integrating oﬀshore Wind Energy, interconnection of weak networks and
long transmission. In addition, VSC technology in HVDC systems allows a decoupled control
of active and reactive power. Increase in number of controlled power electronic converters calls
for a relatively easy way to assess the stability of the system. Integration of converters with the
grid requires compliance with the grid codes and detailed analysis of instability problems. The
detailed analysis of the interaction grid connected VSC with the grid as well as other subsys-
tems in close proximity needs to be carried out. Impedance modelling oﬀers a relatively simpler
way to assess the stability in which detailed control models of converter are not required. If the
admittance at the point of common coupling with the grid oﬀers a non-negative conductance,
then the converter cannot generate instabilities. This simpliﬁcation oﬀers a less complex way
to analyse the stability of the system. In this thesis, the impedance modelling based approach
is used to assess the behaviour of the output admittance of grid connected-VSC.
1.1
Objective of the Thesis
As described in the introduction, the admittance of the VSC is calculated at PCC through
which the stability is analyzed. However, the controller for the VSC is never fully decoupled.
For a full admittance matrix in synchronous or a stationary frame, the stability analysis does
not remain that straightforward neither the control of active and reactive power is decoupled.
Recently, PR controllers are being used instead of PI controllers due to less computational
burden and better performance. However, the system is strongly coupled in stationary frame.
Therefore, a methodology is required which can decouple the system which allows a simpler
analysis of the stability as well as a decoupled control of power. Sequence Decomposition is
an attractive tool for analysing unbalanced system. It is known that most of the times when a
matrix in stationary three phase system is transformer to sequence network matrix, it decouples
positive, negative and zero sequence networks from each other. Hence, the same motivation is
used to carry out sequence decomposition for the output admittance matrix of grid-connected
VSC.

Impedance Based Modelling of Grid-connected Voltage-
Source Converter
The transition to renewable energy sources and increase in inter-connectivity of power systems
across countries has led to increase of High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) technology. HVDC
transmission is preferred for long transmission overhead lines, underground and undersea ca-
bles because of the control of power and eﬃciency it provides.Voltage-source converters(VSCs)
are used for the connection for HVDC technology. VSCs are being used HVDC transmission,
Flexible AC Transmission (FACTS), back-to-back converters, connection of oﬀshore wind farms
and other distributed generation. The basic circuit diagram of an HVDC link connected to a
grid through VSCs is shown in Figure 1. The grid codes for connecting VSCs to the power grid
varies across countries. It also depends on the kind of generation source is being connected.
Hence, the use of ﬁlter on the grid side and a current controller is necessary to ensure power
quality and stability for the grid connected converters. In current scenario, while connecting
a new converter to the grid only the characteristics at the Point of Common Coupling(PCC)
are considered while designing the control for VSC. The integration of VSC in a power grid
can cause instabilities because of interaction with other VSCs or other dynamic subsystems in
close proximity. These local instabilities can cause small-signal oscillations which have been
discussed in [1]. There are many reasons for instabilities. Among them are: constant power
control [2] and dynamics of control loop with pulsewidth modulator(PWM) in VSC [3].
Figure 1: Circuit Digram of an HVDC link connected to Grid via VSCs
The integration of VSCs with the grid requires detailed studies for the instability problems.
Classical control stability methods involving root locus and other time and frequency domain
techniques requires detailed models of the converter control.
These control models should
account for grid impedance as well as coupling with other dynamic subsystems such as other
grid-connected converters.
Weak grid which has high impedance value can destabilize the
current control loop of the converter [4] and the eﬀect of proximity of other grid-connected VSCs
has been discussed in [5] in which it was found that a parallel converter can cause instability
3 of 100
==PAGE:12==
problems. However, the objective is to avoid such high complexity which requires such detailed
control models of converter and to analyze the grid stability. Therefore, for grid stability studies
the external behaviour of the converter is of more importance than the internal model of the
VSC. Harnefors et al. [1] assessed the closed loop stability of grid-connected converter system
using impedance-based approach. In the reference, the value of real part of input admittance
(conductance) at PCC can become negative in certain frequency range which degrades the
overall damping of the system and can further cause sustained oscillations. Impedance-based
modelling oﬀers a relatively simpler way to assess the stability of interaction between converters
and grid because it avoids the need of remodelling converter when the grid impedance changes.
In Impedance modelling, the impedance or admittance of the converter is calculated at the
PCC and its behaviour is assessed with the grid impedance value by either calculating the
poles of the closed loop system or using Nyquist criteria on the open loop transfer function.
Impedance based stability criteria is discussed in detail in the next chapter.
The connection of a grid-connected VSC is a strongly coupled non-linear system which is studied
in the frequency domain as a Multi-Input Multi-Output (MIMO) system. Since, grid-connected
converters are controlled in the current injection mode [6], the VSC is modelled as a current
source with an admittance [1] connected at the PCC. Depending upon the direction of the
current, it can be called input or output admittance of the VSC. The admittance of the VSC
is calculated in this chapter which will be later used in the impedance-based stability analysis
of the grid-connected converter system in the next chapter.
VSC control consists of fast inner current control loop and slower outer control loop shown
Figure 2: Block Diagram of a typical control setup for a VSC.
in Figure 2. The grid impedance is assumed to be zero. Through the inner control loop CC,
the injected current to the PCC is obtained as function of external set points. AC voltage
controller (AVC) provides the Q axis reference point to current controller whereas Direct-
Voltage Controller (DVC) provides the D axis reference point.
Active power is controlled
through Id whereas reactive power is controller through Iq. Along with the measured current
and voltage at the point of PCC, CC has three inputs. Phase-Locked Loop(PLL) provides the
angle for transformation from stationary frame to synchronous frame. The output of CC gives
the reference voltage for PWM. The main focus of the thesis is to study the dynamics of the
AC side of VSC. The dynamics of the outer loops are much slower than the inner current loop.

The inner current control loop is used to track the current reference set point.
The basic
connection of a VSC to a grid at the point of common coupling (PCC) is shown in the Figure 3.
L is the phase reactor of the VSC. Transformer model will be considered later on. R represents

converts the voltage and current phasors from stationary reference frame to rotating reference
frame which results in DC quantities. Therefore, PI (Proportional Integral) controllers can be
used to eliminate the steady state error for the reference tracking control. The DC integrators
in PI controllers forces the steady state error to be zero by introducing an inﬁnite DC gain. For
implementing decoupled control, the non-linear three phase system equation can be transformed

the controller for two quantities is simpler and more cost eﬀective compared to three quantities.
Therefore, Clarke’s transformation (see Appendix B) is used to transform three quantities to
two quantities in αβ frame in which the zero sequence component is ignored while designing
the controller. Hence, in stationary frame all the AC side quantities will be expressed in αβ
stationary frame. The inner control loop provides the voltage reference for the switching valves
of the VSC.

controller.

2.3
Modelling of Current Controller in Stationary Reference Frame
For assessing the stability of the VSC due to the interaction with other subsystems which
can be grid or other converters connected to the grid, it is more convenient to carry out the
analysis in abc or αbeta frame. As it is easier to represent these sub-systems in their original
reference frame which is a three phase system in stationary frame. Representation in αβ is
carried out as it is quite convenient to analyse two quantities and conversion to αβ reference
frame doesn’t aﬀect the reference frame of the original three phase quantities and whole system
can be represented in αβ frame without much mathematical calculations. In this section, the
controller which is originally designed in dq synchronous frame will be transformed to αβ frame
and its will be modelled in αβ frame. The representation in stationary reference frame is shown
in Figure 19. The plant model will no longer have the coupling. In addition, the equivalent
of PI controller and the decoupling matrix of the controller needs to be found in stationary
reference frame which is done in the subsequent sections. It is found that the decoupling matrix
is equal in both rotating and stationary reference frame whereas for the PI controller, the oﬀ
diagonal terms are introduced which makes the controller coupled in αβ frame.
Figure 19: Current controller in stationary reference frame.
2.3.1
PI controller in Stationary Reference Frame
The control structure developed until now is in rotating synchronous frame and PI controllers
are used for the synchronous frame. However, implementation in dq requires a complex com-

3.1
Suﬃcient Criterion for Stability
There are two ways to analyse the stability, ﬁrst by calculating the closed loop poles and the
second one is by applying Nyquist criteria. For higher order systems and if the time delay is also
present, then it is more convenient to use Nyquist Criteria [14]. Nyquist Stability Criteria is a
stability test for time-invariant linear systems. The stability of the system can be assessed by
using Cauchy’s principle of argument for an open loop transfer function which can be obtained
from the characteristic equation of a SISO system. The summation of number of unstable open
loop poles, P and the number of encirclements of the open loop transfer function around the
critical point (−1 + j · 0), N are equal to the unstable poles of closed loop transfer function,
Z. For a stable system, Z must be zero which implies N = −P. If P is equal to zero then
the system is stable if the contour of open loop transfer function does not encircle the critical
point.
This deﬁnition of Nyquist stability criteria can be extended and a suﬃcient condition can
be derived to ensure the stability of the closed loop transfer function given in (51). This is
condition is not necessary for stability but if it is met than the system stability can be ensured.
If converter output admittance,Yc(s) and grid impedance,Zg(s) are both stable; consequently,
the stability of open loop transfer function Yc(s) · Zg(s) can be ensured if it does not encircle
the critical point (−1 + j · 0). A similar condition has been derived in [15]. The intersection

In the previous chapter, the stability from the admittance matrix was assessed. The Nyquist
criterion was used to analyze the stability of the controller in dq frame for the ideal case in which
measurement delays and dead time were not considered. This was due to the fact that the oﬀ

make the current controller design even more complex. In addition, analysing the controller
in αβ domain is more convenient as the whole system data is available stationary frame. But
it was seen that a strong coupling was introduced in form of oﬀ-diagonal terms when the PI
controller is analysed in stationary frame. Due to this, the nyquist criterion used in previous
chapter cannot be used directly for a MIMO system. Hence, it is desired to decouple the sys-
tem, so that the proper eﬀect of oﬀ-diagonal elements as well as the stability of the system can
be analysed.
For unsymmetric systems, sequence decomposition is a well known technique to analyse the
system properly by sequence decomposition. In a three phase unbalanced system, the system
is divided into three diﬀerent systems namely positive sequence, negative sequence and zero
sequence. In most cases, positive,negative and zero sequence networks are decoupled and can
be analysed separately. To carry out steady state studies, it becomes essential to carry out
sequence decomposition. The motivation to carry out sequence decomposition in this chapter,
is to see the eﬀect of decomposition on the coupling in stationary frame and synchronous frame.
If the similar methodology is developed for αβ frame and dq frame, maybe the circuit in sta-
tionary and rotating frame will be decoupled and the system can be treated as a SISO system.
Henceforth, the Nyquist criterion can be applied in a simpler way to assess the stability. In this
chapter, an analytical way has been developed to decouple the admittance matrix into positive
and negative sequence matrix in α and β frame or d and q frame.

Fast Fourier Transform(FFT)
To verify the the results from Simulink with that of Matlab, the output signal from Simulink
is saved in Matlab workspace as a discrete signal with a constant time interval. To verify the
frequency response of the closed-loop transfer functions, it is essential to convert this output
signal into frequency domain. It is known that Fourier Transform is a very convenient tool
to convert a time domain signal to a frequency domain signal. When a signal is discrete in
one domain, it will be continuous in another and vice versa. For the analysis, the output from
Simulink is saved as a discrete signal with ﬁxed time interval. The step size is chosen as 20e−6
[s]. The complex Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) can be used to convert the complex valued
time domain signal to a complex values frequency domain signal. If there are N samples for
time domain signal x(n), then DFT will produce an array X(K) of length N where X(K) is
given as follow

[20]. FFT is a quicker way of calculating DFT if the signal length is a power of 2 by using

It was found in Chapter 4, that if the controller is designed in such a way that the positive and
negative sequence response are added, it will produce a diagonal matrix where the oﬀ-diagonal
elements are zero or close to zero. So, if Method 1 is considered, the diagonal elements of
positive and negative sequence matrix needs to be analysed to comment on the stability as the
addition of both results in a SISO system. The stability criteria developed in Chapter 3 can
be extended to positive and negative sequence in this case. If the real part of both positive
and negative sequence diagonal elements is positive for all frequencies then the stability of the
VSC can be ascertained. In the region around 100 Hz, the addition of both responses leads
to a value of around -0.0002 p.u. whereas for the region around 50 Hz, the value becomes
highly negative because of the resonance. If there are resonances in this region and are not
damped properly, it can generate an instability. For higher range of frequencies, the value of
admittance is still negative but approaches zero (−4.7e−7 p.u. at 1800 Hz). Since, most of the
times, the resonances are properly damped and VSC won’t cause instability. In addition, the
output admittance can be shaped with the controller parameters.

6
Conclusion
In this thesis, impedance based modelling of Grid-connected VSC was carried. Only the AC
side dynamics of VSC were considered while modeling the inner current control loop. Then
step by step, the measurement delay, Voltage feed-forward and Dead-time delay were modelled.
The equivalent control structure of the controller in dq frame was translated to αβ frame as it
is relatively easier to analyse the controller in stationary frame. Since, PLL is a very essential
part for current controller as it provides the angle for the transformation from synchronous to
stationary frame and vice-versa, only this outer loop was modelled. It was found that distur-
bance characteristic transfer function is nothing but the output admittance at the point of PCC.
After ﬁnding the admittance value, it was essential to develop the stability criteria to anal-
yse the passive behaviour of the admittance. Since the method developed for analysing the
behaviour is an extension of nyquist criteria, it could only be applied for SISO systems.
Sequence Decomposition is an attractive tool to analyse an unbalanced system. Also, most of
the times it decouples the positive, negative and zero sequence networks. Hence, the method-
ology of sequence decomposition was analytically extended to assess its eﬀect on the coupling
terms both in stationary and synchronous frame. If the sequence modelling removes the cou-
pling, it becomes very easy to assess the admittance behaviour along with controlling the active
power through d part of the controller and reactive power through q part of the controller. Two
methodologies were developed in which one used a Psuedoinverse and in the other it was delib-
erately avoided to make use of it. In the last chapter, the analytically derived results of sequence
decomposition were veriﬁed in a time-domain simulation environment using Simulink. A sweep
signal varying with frequency in time was injected as the disturbance to the controller. The
discrete ﬁxed step output of Simulink was converted to frequency domain using FFT. Further
the voltage and current vectors were decomposed; then the admittance calculated from these
was compared with the output of the disturbance characteristic transfer function.
The important results found after analyzing the simulation output are:
• It was found that in synchronous frame, the positive sequence outcome from Method
1 (with Psuedoinverse) matched perfectly with the output of Simulink. However, there
was a small diﬀerence while calculating negative sequence admittance matrix with the
same method. For method 2, it provided correct result for diagonal elements for the ideal
case and other times, it only provided correct results for the positive sequence behaviour.
Hence, it can be concluded that Method 1 is the correct one.
• The important outcome is that sequence decomposition does not remove the coupling
between α and β frame nor d and q frame. In the ideal case of dq frame , in which there
was no coupling, when the sequence decomposition was carried out; it in fact introduced
the coupling which made the ideal dq controller a MIMO system.
This is the exact
opposite of the objective which was to assess if the systems could be decoupled through
sequence modelling.
• However, with the method 1, the addition of positive and negative matrices leads to a

system can be treated as a SISO system. Hence, if the controller is designed in such a

6.1
Future Work
Due to time constraint, the following areas and cases could not be analysed. These can be
further studied to fully analyse the methodlogy developed for sequence decomposition.
• The analytical derivation of PLL modelling can be veriﬁed using time-domain simulation
environment.
• It has been found that the mode in which the converter is operating in also aﬀects the
admittance [?]. This can be further included in sequence decomposition.
• The outer control loops namely AVC and DVC also aﬀect the output admittance. These
can be modelled and further considered for accurately assessing grid-connected VSC sta-
bility.
• The eﬀect of weak grid which can lead to parallel resonance with the VSC should also be
analysed.
• While modelling the VSC, there was no ﬁlter considered. LCL ﬁlter can also lead to
parallel-resonance. Hence, the ﬁlter should be modelled to calculate the correct admit-
tance at the point of PCC.
• The controller parameters can be varied to shape the admittance. Further research should
be carried out to see the eﬀect of the Controller gains on the sequence admittance matrices.
Furthermore, a parametric Sensitivity study for the admittance should be carried out.

function [TM]=TransformAB2DQ(G,f)
TF=G;
w=2*pi*f;
[num,den]=tfdata(TF);
syms x;
G_sym=poly2sym(cell2mat(num),x)/poly2sym(cell2mat(den),x);
G1=simplify(subs(G_sym,x,x+1j*w)) ;
G2=simplify(subs(G_sym,x,x-1j*w));
 
[num,den]=numden(G1);
num1=sym2poly(num);
den1=sym2poly(den);
G1_tf=tf(num1,den1);
 
[num,den]=numden(G2);
num2=sym2poly(num);
den2=sym2poly(den);
G2_tf=tf(num2,den2);
 
H1=minreal((G1_tf+G2_tf)/2);
H2=minreal((G1_tf-G2_tf)/2);
 
T=[H1 1j*H2; -1j*H2 H1];
TM=ss(T);
 
end

function [TM]=Sequence(G,a)
 
%valid for extracting positive and negative sequence from alhpa beta and dq
%NOTE:output will be in the same refrence frame as the input's reference frame
%a=1, if positive sequence is required
%a=2, if negative sequence is required.
 
 
syms x w;
 
%Replacing s by j.w
for i=1:2
    for j=1:2
        [num,den]=tfdata(G(i,j));
        y_sym=poly2sym(cell2mat(num),x)/poly2sym(cell2mat(den),x);
        y_w=simplify(subs(y_sym,x,1j*w));
        Y(i,j)=y_w;
    end
end
 
%Sequence Decomposition
J=[1 1j; -1j 1];
J_T= transpose(J);
 
if a==1 %for positive sequence
    y=simplify(Y(1,1)+Y(2,2)+1j*(Y(2,1)-Y(1,2)));
    T_w= simplify((1/4)*y*J);   
end
if a==2 %for negative sequence
    y=simplify(Y(1,1)+Y(2,2)+1j*(Y(1,2)-Y(2,1)));
    T_w=simplify((1/4)*y*J_T);
end
 
%to get the sequence admittance matrix as a function of frequency, replacing w=-j.s
%This is just a way to pass back the sequence matrix to main script and should only be 
%this also makes it easier to get the frequency response because control toolbox can be
 
T_x=subs(T_w,w,-1j*x);
 
for i=1:2
    for j=1:2
        [num,den]=numden(T_x(i,j));
        num1=sym2poly(num);
        den1=sym2poly(den);
        Y_seq(i,j)=tf(num1,den1);
    end
end
TM=ss(Y_seq);
end
