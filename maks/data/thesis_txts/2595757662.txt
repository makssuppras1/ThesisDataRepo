Abstract
Fire in structures that need to be evacuated is a frequent event that can have minor
up to severe consequences. With the relevant fire codes, which contribute to both
fire prevention and evacuation in case of fire, the consequences can be minimized.
Many details need to be accounted for when designing a building, and despite fol-
lowing the codes through, the chances of fire are never fully eliminated.
In an evacuation, various aspects can affect the time it takes to evacuate a building.
One of those aspects is the occupants themselves and their ability to comprehend
and respond rapidly to warnings indicating the initiation of evacuation. Occupants
are a diverse group of people with different characteristics, which makes them react,
think, and move heterogeneously. To simplify simulations and calculations, pedes-
trian flows are often represented homogeneously. It can lead to misinterpretation
of the occupants by disregarding features that contribute directly to the outcome
of an evacuation. This thesis is designated to contribute knowledge concerning het-
erogeneous pedestrian flows in an evacuation.
The purpose of the project was to investigate how heterogeneous pedestrian flows
can be taken into account in different numerical models and compare them as well as
demonstrate the importance of including the heterogeneous characteristics in simu-
lations. Four cases of occupants containing different combinations of heterogeneous
characteristics were simulated in this project, which was carried out in two different
evacuation models, Legion and Simulex. The heterogeneous characteristics tested
were walking speed, individual size, and reaction and decision time by obtaining the
evacuation time and queuing time in three different scenarios.
The results demonstrated how different combinations of characteristics give both
different evacuation times and queuing times for the same scenarios. Furthermore,
there was no correlation between the queuing time and evacuation time in the same
scenarios.
The results also revealed that the characteristics can have an impact
on each other, where one characteristic can be dominant and reduce the effects of
another characteristic. It was concluded that heterogeneous characteristics do have
an impact on pedestrian flows, which affects the evacuation itself. The interaction
between the characteristics demonstrates the importance of both being aware of the
miscellaneous characteristics and including them when simulating an evacuation to
the best of the current ability, as they affect each other.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Fire is something that happens every day, all over the world, and is an ancient
scourge, where the consequences can differ from minor or none up to multiple fatal-
ities and severe property damages [1–3].
Examples of fires with severe consequences are the fire in Grenfell Tower in London
in 2017, where a 24-story building was caught on fire, and 72 people lost their lives.
The Notre Dame fire in 2019, where the famous cathedral got severely damaged,
the fire at the football stadium in Bradford in 1985, killing 56 people, fire in the
Sejong Hospital in South Korea, killing 37 people and the fire in the Kiss nightclub
in Brazil, killing 236 people [4–8].
These examples demonstrate how fires can happen in different types of structures
and indicate the importance of fire prevention. The first fire prevention codes were
based on bitter experiences and were primarily based on various severe fires from the
past. As time has passed, the fire codes have been refined and developed as a deeper
understanding, and findings are continued to be made in the effort of minimizing
losses [3,9]. Despite the fire codes, complete prevention of fire has been unattainable,
and the risk of fire will always be present, which makes it important to ensure that
occupants in a structure are able to evacuate a building to prevent fatal incidents [3].
Occupants of a structure can be a very diverse group of people in different shapes
and sizes. People’s ability to move is no exception, which needs to be taken into
account in case of evacuation. Over the past few years, more focus has been on
people with disabilities. The understanding of the limitations due to disabilities
has shifted from physical or medical perspective to a person’s physical, social and
political context, and considerable progress has been made in the accessibility for
all, though it can always be improved further [10].
On the contrary, the ability to egress has not received as much attention as access.
The ability to egress does not need to be the same, however, the level of safety
should be the same for everyone [11]. In the built environment, the conditions have
disproportionate effects on individuals with disabilities, particularly in the case of
an evacuation resulting in the need for improved alternatives of evacuation routes.
To be classified as an accessible facility, advanced evacuation routes and procedures
need to be provided, where as many accessibility elements as possible need to be
addressed when evaluating the evacuation in a model [12].
Fire and evacuation models are developing with time as computer capability in-
creases. The models play an important part in the transition from prescriptive fire

safety codes to performance-based fire safety codes, where it needs to be proven that
users of a structure can evacuate before getting exposed to critical conditions. To
do so, many factors need to be taken into account in the simulation, for example,
occupant’s behavior and movement, possible congestions, exit routes, etc. [13,14].
One of the more problematic and complex factors is understanding and representing
an individual’s behavior. The task becomes more complicated due to the partial
understanding of the matter. It is even further compromised as a consequence of
the tendency to oversimplify the problem at hand. The focus on the physical aspects
of evacuation is to blame, instead of focusing on the psychological and sociological
aspects [15]. Research conducted between the 70s and the 80s still represents the
main core of the literature that the foundation of evacuation modeling is based on,
which partly is the cause of the limited representation of human factors in evacua-
tion [16].
On top of that, planning should not only assume that individuals with evident phys-
ical disabilities will be the only ones having difficulties evacuating. Individuals with
hidden disabilities, such as asthma and heart conditions, can also have trouble with
evacuation [12].
Pedestrian flow is often simulated homogeneously for simplification purposes, or
when using existing modeling software, there is no other option than to expect all
pedestrians to have the same speed and neglect a portion of the individuals despite
their importance. It is known to be unrealistic, and people moving heterogeneously,
such as people with disabilities and the elderly, are then disregarded in fire scenar-
ios, which can have severe consequences in the case of fire. Most people will, at
some point in their life, have difficulty moving, for instance, due to a broken or in-
jured leg, being pregnant, getting old, recuperating from an injury, etc. Therefore,
people with some type of permanent and short-term disability are not taken into
consideration and not adequately addressed in the simulated populations. Accord-
ingly, the diverse pedestrian flow should always be taken into account, which can
get tricky as the proportion of people moving heterogeneously needs to be deter-
mined. When proving structure’s fire safety, it can be impossible and unrealistic to
assume that everyone is moving heterogeneously, except in places such as hospitals
or nursing homes. Nevertheless, there is always a portion that can not move homo-
geneously, which needs to be taken into account to provide an equal level of safety
for all. [12–14].
All of that leads to the important question to be answered in this thesis:
Do different heterogeneous characteristics have an impact on evacuation?

1.1 Objective
1.1
Objective
The purpose of this project is to investigate how heterogeneous pedestrian flows can
be taken into account in different numerical models when evacuating structures and
comparing them using the same geometry and scenarios. Different models have dif-
ferent features to simulate the occupants of the structure in question, for example,
with occupants’ speed, size, behavior, etc., which can affect the results of a simula-
tion.
The aim of this project is to demonstrate the importance of including various het-
erogeneous pedestrian characteristics in simulations. Specifically, by using various
combinations of heterogeneous walking speed, individual size and, reaction and de-
cision time for predetermined categories of occupants in a building simulated in
the evacuation models Legion and Simulex. The findings are hoped to display the
significance of having reliable data concerning their characteristics in an evacuation
and the importance of having calculation tools available to simulate heterogeneous
populations. Increased understanding and data of occupants in a stressful situation
such as evacuation will result in a more realistic and safe evacuation strategy for all.

Structure of the Report

cepts for fire safety engineering and evacuation are introduced along with other
fundamental topics related to heterogeneous pedestrians and computer evacuation

floor plans, drawings, and other details regarding the building. Additionally, raw
data from both software, which is used to obtain the results from the simulations,
and other information needed regarding the evacuation models, are presented along
with a detailed comparison of the staircases from both evacuation models.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
In the following sections, the theoretical topics related to the objective of this thesis
are introduced and are the outcome of an extensive literature review. First, the con-
cept of fire safety engineering is described along with the building fire codes. Next,
the fundamentals and general concepts of the current evacuation theory is explained
in detail. The evacuation theory is based on homogeneous movements where vari-
ous simplifications are made and is followed by a section concerning heterogeneous
pedestrians. Disabilities are discussed along with the challenges of high-rise struc-
tures and the usage of elevators during a fire as a potential option in an evacuation.
Finally, the computer evacuation models are examined with regards to methods,
applications, and other features as well as simulation modes. By acknowledging the
multiple factors that influence an evacuation, a better understanding of the complex
task of predicting the outcome of an evacuation is obtained, which leads to more
realistic simulations and more reliable results.
2.1
Fire Safety Engineering
The application of engineering and scientific principles in an attempt to protect
people and its environment from the destructive effects of fire is called fire safety
engineering (FSE). The main goal of FSE is the safety of life and property, which can
be a complicated task to achieve as the level of fire safety is a composite of various
fire-related factors, such as fire initiation, fire growth, fire spread, the response of
building components, the occupants, etc. The objectives of fire safety engineering
can be accomplished by [3]:
• Minimizing the fire occurrence.
• Controlling the fire growth and spread.
• Providing safe and accessible fire escape routes.
Fire safety engineering can be divided into two main aspects, fire safety and life
safety. The fire safety aspect contains fire prevention, where materials and main-
tenance are considered, and fire protection, which can be passive or active. The
design of egress routes and pressurization of protected spaces are subjects that fall
under the life safety aspect [3].

2.1 Fire Safety Engineering
2.1.1
Prescriptive and Performance-Based Codes
To ensure the fire safety of a building, fire codes are used and have been used since
at least the 18th century. The codes have developed with time both due to experi-
ences with fire, good and bad, and experiments [9]. Nowadays, two types of codes
are used, the prescriptive codes and the performance-based codes. The prescriptive
codes are based on a traditional design where an acceptable level of safety can be
achieved using rather simple guidance, which has proven to work empirically. The
code contains prescriptive pre-approved rules concerning various parameters, such
as travel distance, floor space factors, exit requirements, etc., which can affect more
than the specified parameters [17]. The disadvantages of the prescriptive codes are
that it is made for a traditional design where it is specified how a structure should be
built, disregarding its intended occupation and offering a very limited opportunity
for more rational designs [18].
As building design has developed through the years, complex structures have in-
creased, which has made it hard to comply with the traditional prescriptive codes.
Performance-based fire engineering designs are used to meet the required fire safety
standard of non-traditional designs to provide efficient and effective fire safety in
structures. The performance-based codes specify objectives and minimum perfor-
mance requirements to ensure fire safety and permit the goals to be achieved in
various ways, given that safety can be demonstrated [18]. The occupants need to be
secured sufficient time to evacuate before fire conditions become critical [19]. This
methodology determines the degree of life safety provided by quantifying both the
available safe egress time (ASET) and the required safe egress time (RSET), which
is explained thoroughly in the next section, section 2.2 [15].
The prescriptive codes were first introduced in Denmark in the mid-1960s as the
fire legislation became general law, which was based on prescriptive codes. As the
development continued, more and more exemptions were made to the relevant fire
legislation. It resulted in technical exchanges being accepted in cases of intricate
building designs where fire safety was adapted to the building, which is, in fact, a
performance-based approach [9]. In the 1990s, various countries, such as Japan, New
Zealand, Australia, Sweden, and England, shifted to performance-based codes [20].
Denmark started working on defining the performance-based codes in the late 1990s,
which led to the introduction of the codes in 2004 [9]. Currently, both prescriptive
and performance-based codes can be used depending on the design of the structure.
If the design is traditional, prescriptive codes can be used. If not, the performance-
based codes are used [21].

2.2
Evacuation
To ensure a structure’s safety, it is necessary to prove that its occupants can evacuate
in case of fire before critical conditions occur. It can be a complex task depending
on the structure, the building’s usage, the geometry, and more. Besides, critical
scenarios have to be predicted and accounted for when guaranteeing fire safety.
In evacuation theory, two main time frames are generally used. The first one is
identified as available safe egress time (ASET), which is the time from the ignition
of the fire until critical conditions occur. While the second one is known as the
required safe egress time (RSET), which is the time from ignition until occupants
have reached safety, also known as the evacuation time [9, 22, 23]. Fire safety of
occupants is guaranteed when the following applies:
ASET > RSET
(2.1)
That is, occupants have evacuated before critical conditions occur. To determine the
critical condition time frame, ASET, which is often denoted as tcrit, various factors
need to be taken into account. These factors are visibility, radiation, temperature,
the height of the smoke layer, oxygen, toxicity, the fire spread to other rooms, and
fire spread to other buildings. Each factor has its criterion, often referred to as
acceptance criteria, and by using hand calculations or computer simulations (zone
models or computational fluid dynamics (CFD) models), the value for each factor
can be calculated and compared to the relative criterion [9,24].
The required time frame for a safe evacuation, RSET, is composed of various time
components and is often defined by the following equation:
RSET = tdet + tal + trec + tres + ttrav
(2.2)
Where:
tdet
is the time interval from ignition to detection, called detection time [min].
tal
is the time interval from detection to alarm or warning, called alarm time [min].
trec
is the time interval from a warning to recognition, called recognition time [min].
tres
is the time interval from recognition to response, called response time [min].
ttrav
is the time interval from response until the evacuation is complete, called travel/movement time [min].
The time components are shown in figure 2.1, where the evacuation time, tevac,
and pre-movement time, tpre, are also shown and are assembled by different time
segments.
The margin of safety, tmarg, also displayed in the figure, is identified
as the time between occupants reaching safety and conditions becoming untenable,
which is the difference between ASET and RSET. Ideally, tmarg should be as long
as possible to ensure that an acceptable margin of safety is provided. It should be
noted that the abbreviations and terms used to describe the time components can
vary between sources [22].

The RSET is often divided into phases. In this report, the three phases used are
warning time, reaction and decision time, and travel time [9,24]. In some literature,
the warning time is divided into detection and notification time [25].
2.2.1
Warning Time
Warning time, twa, is the time from fire ignition until occupants of a building are
aware of the fire, and is composed of the detection time and the alarm time (tdet +
tal) [9, 24]. The detection and alarm can both be automatic or manual, which can
be the use of technical solutions or simply human intervention [22,25].
Detection of fire varies between a few seconds up to a few hours, depending on the
fire type and the detection devices [26]. The response time in an automatic fire alarm
(AFA) system is often considered as the warning time since the system is often first
to detect a fire, and an automatic warning system is usually connected directly to
an AFA system. The AFA system is also capable of calling the emergency services,
advising trained personnel, and activating secondary functions. It is often based on
smoke, ion, optical, or heat detectors, depending on the room’s usage. In Denmark,
the system must be designed and installed according to DBI Guideline 232, Au-
tomatic fire alarm systems, and comply with DBI Regulation 001-005, Automatic
fire protection system. Other systems that can be activated by an AFA system are,
for example, fire door closing system, smoke ventilation system, compartment extin-
guishing system, sprinkler system, and as previously mentioned, warning system [9].
However, there could be a delay, for example, if there is a malfunction with the
AFA system or other detection devices, manual activation at a pull station is needed
to activate the fire alarm. Therefore, is it necessary to calculate an elapsed time

determine the warning time, and a qualified estimate is used. Alternatively, people
inside or outside the structure will discover the fire and alert other occupants [9]. At

2.2.2
Reaction and Decision Time
The reaction and decision time, trd, also known as pre-evacuation time, pre-movement
time, or delay time, is composed of recognition time and response time (trec + tres)
and is the time from awareness of a warning until initiation of movement towards
an exit starts [9,24,25,27,28].
In the past, this phase was neglected, which simplified the calculations extensively
as this time phase can be hard to determine since it depends on predictions concern-
ing human behavior. Therefore, the results gave inaccurate evacuation times [26].
Nowadays, human behavior can be arranged as a sequence of actions in four stages.
The initial stage is receiving the information of a potential fire through fire alarms,
the sight of smoke, or warning by others. The following stage is an interpretation of
the information, which can take some time as occupants either ignore the warning
or investigate further and seek more information. In the third stage, occupants pre-
pare to act on the information received. The preparation can be to instruct others,
explore cues, or see how other occupants react. Finally, in the last stage, occupants
act on the obtained information and evacuate the building, try to fight the fire, warn
others, or wait [26, 27]. Figure 2.2 shows the sequence of human behavior in fire,
which is a dynamic process that does not necessarily follow a particular pattern and
can be repeated during an evacuation [27].

2.2.3
Travel Time
Travel time, ttrav, also known as movement time, is the time from the beginning of
evacuation until all occupants are in a safe place, which can be in or outside of a
building [9, 24]. To guarantee an effective evacuation, the travel time needs to be
as short as possible to prevent situations where occupants doubt their route choice
and consequently start acting inappropriately [24]. Active fire protection, such as
panic and emergency lighting, can have a significant impact on assuring occupants
of their path towards safety.
The travel time is the collective time of walking towards an exit, queuing at the
passing doors in the escape route, and any other potential bottlenecks and possi-
ble subsequent congestions [9]. Therefore, the time is often divided into two time
components, walking time, twalk, and flow time, tflow. Where twalk is the time spent
moving towards a safe place, and tflow is the time spent in queues and congestions,
controlled by flow characteristics [25].
The flow of people during emergencies has been researched in recent years, which
has provided methods to predict their movements. Various researchers have con-
tributed to these methods and their development, as the majority of the research is
compatible and supportive of each other. The relation between the speed of move-
ment and population density of occupants’ evacuating stream is the foundation of
the methods. The following assumptions are used when deriving equations from the
researches to predict the flow:
• Occupants start to evacuate at the same time.
• The individuals will not interrupt the occupant flow with decision making.

• Occupants disabilities that affect the ability to keep up with the movement of
a group are neglected.
This approach is known as the hydraulic model of emergency egress [25,31].
Even though the model is widely used, it has its disadvantages. It tends to estimate
an optimistic evacuation time as it assumes continuous usage of the exit paths at
maximum capacity from the moment of alarm until the total evacuation is com-
pleted. Additionally, discussions concerning the validity of several data-sets, which
the approach is based on, have taken place. However, as the data-set contribution to
the approach is broadly comparable, has not been proven invalid, and other equiv-
alent comprehensive data-sets are unavailable, the model is still used. Therefore,
it is important to consider it as a baseline calculation and extend it to account for
delays caused by human decisions, behavior, notifications, and other factors [25,31].
The travel time predicted by the model is the utilization of expressions that re-
late data from tests and observations to the hydraulic model of human flow. The
primary considerations are population density, travel speed, effective width, flow
characteristics, time for passage through a component (flow time), and transitions
between components. The egress movement can finally be quantified by using these
considerations in the hydraulic model [25].

2.3
Heterogeneous Pedestrians
In real life, pedestrian flow is heterogeneous as people’s mental and physical charac-
teristics vary. Many other factors can also impact the flow, which are not movement-
related, such as environmental and behavioral factors [32]. Heterogeneous pedestri-
ans can have a disability, which somehow prevents the ability to move at the speed
of a non-disabled adult. This part of heterogeneous pedestrians will be further in-
vestigated as it takes them the longest to evacuate, and they are often neglected, for
example, as shown previously in section 2.2.3 in the assumptions to derive equations
for travel time in the hydraulic model.
2.3.1
Disabilities
More than one billion people or around 15% of the world’s population has some sort
of disability. The population with disabilities was higher than previously estimated
in the World Report on Disabilities and is expected to be growing as the population
is ageing, and chronic health conditions are increasing, among other causes [33–35].
The number is presumed to have reached two billion people by 2050 [10]. The aware-
ness of disability-inclusive development is increasing globally. The United Nations
Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) contributes signif-
icantly to the awareness as it promotes full integration of people with disabilities
internationally with laws addressing their rights. The 2030 Agenda for Sustainabil-
ity Development also supports disabled people with seven targets in the Sustainable
Development Goals (SDGs), which specifically refer to persons with disabilities. A

Disabilities can appear in various forms, where symptoms can either fluctuate or

an emergency. Since disabilities come in multiple forms, they are categorized into

due to broken bones, surgery, illness, or other causes. Thus, mobility or physical
stamina is reduced for a period of time while healing. The majority of individu-

lifetime, which will decrease mobility and the ability to use the built environment
effortlessly. Individuals with multiple disabilities are also common, which requires
more options to ensure their safety during emergency evacuation [37].
Furthermore, an individual’s overall ability often reduces with age, which leads to
slower movements or even difficulty with movements. Other individuals that can
also have trouble with movements are people of extreme size and weight [37]. Cur-
rently, there is a debate regarding the calculations of people flows as the number of
people with some sort of physical disability, temporary or permanent, are increas-
ing [18]. A considerable amount of the original work on people’s movement was
accomplished several decades ago. The fact raises concerns about the validity of the
data used as the population profile has changed and will change even more in the
next few decades since people are ageing and the fitness level is decreasing due to an
increasingly sedentary lifestyle [39]. Research is needed to determine the legitimacy
of previously used data and suggest modification if needed [18].
Recent modeling of an exemplar high-rise building, using the EvacuatioNZ Monte
Carlo network evacuation model, concluded that evacuation times might increase
due to changes in the population profile. When comparing 2006 and future 2031 New
Zealand scenarios, the evacuation time is expected to grow by about 5-8%. While
comparing 1971 Canadian data to the future 2031 New Zealand scenario, the time
will increase even further, up to 20%. Evacuation strategies for high-rise buildings
need to take into account current and future occupant population profiles. More
people will have reduced stair descent and ascent ability, which impairs mobility
leading to the need for assistance during evacuation or other alternatives such as
elevators [32].
It becomes evident that alternatives are needed for disabled people to guarantee a
safe evacuation from all buildings. One of the main challenges is people’s inability or
difficulty to move and finding a suitable and efficient solution to ensure safe egress in

2.3.2
High-rise Structures
The disabled community in America has pointed out that access to most build-
ings has been facilitated for disabled people contrary to emergency egress [40].
There are only a few studies that explicitly consider a heterogeneous population
consisting of people with and without disabilities [41]. One study found compares
evacuation strategies for disabled people in a high-rise building evacuation, which
is often considered problematic, especially for those in wheelchairs. For a phased
evacuation strategy, it was found that all occupants of a high-rise building except
wheelchair users (both motorized and non-motorized) evacuate faster than in a si-
multaneous evacuation strategy. The occupants in the study were non- disabled,
motorized, and non-motorized wheelchair users, visually impaired, hearing impaired,
and stamina impaired users. By using elevators in an evacuation, the evacuation
time of wheelchair users is reduced dramatically compared to situations where only
stairs can be used, which does not come as a surprise. However, an interesting result
of using elevators for wheelchair users is that the aggregated evacuation time signifi-
cantly reduces for building users. It suggests that for high-rise buildings, appropriate
fire escape elevators should be seriously considered to ensure the occupants’ safety.
For the study, a maximum comfortable structural capacity of a high-rise building
was used to determine the number of people with and without disabilities who can
evacuate without heavy congestion and delay during the evacuation. It is done by
considering the flow rate per minute and the move frequency ratio. The authors
of the study believe it can help evacuation administers and planners regulate the
admission of residents and visitors to maintain a safe environment and provide op-
timized service quality [41].
A few other studies have also concluded that using stairs as the only exit route can
have serious limitations, and by using both elevators and stairs, the overall building
evacuation time can significantly improve [42–44].
Although elevators can reduce evacuation times significantly, there are concerns re-
garding the usage of elevators during emergency evacuations, such as the negative
effects that smoke, water, and heat can have on lifts as well as the complexity of
elevator operations [43].
In addition to elevators, suitable safe refuge areas are another approach that can
be used. Refuge area is defined as an „area that is both separated from the fire by
fire-resisting construction and provided with a safe route to a floor exit, thus con-
stituting a temporarily safe space for mobility-impaired persons to await assistance
for their evacuation” [44]. It can be used by occupants who are unable to use stairs

when/if elevators are unavailable and should be designed to prevent critical condi-
tions in case of malfunctions of elevators or other unforeseen events [42, 44]. The
disadvantage of refuge areas is that some, if not all, occupants are not comfortable
waiting for a long time during an evacuation and fear that they will not be rescued
in extreme situations [44]. It might also be unethical to make people wait in an
emergency.
Although there are some skeptical regarding elevators in fire evacuations, several
international high-rise buildings take into account the usage of elevators during fire
emergency evacuation in their fire strategies. Examples of high-rise buildings that
do so are Burj Khalifa in the United Arab Emirates, which is currently the tallest
building in the world, Petronas Twin Towers in Malaysia, Shanghai World Financial
Center in China and Taipei 101 in Taiwan [44].
2.3.3
The Usage of Elevators
For evacuation strategies that take into account the usage of elevators to work as
planned, occupants will have to be willing to use them during emergencies [45].
Statistically, elevators are the safest mode of transportation as the odds of dying
in an elevator accident are 1 in 122,824 lifetimes compared to 1 in 85 for cars, and
1 in 5862 for airplanes [40]. However, fire safety elevators are rare, and therefore
the statistics give a potentially inaccurate representation of elevators in a fire emer-
gency. Furthermore, more than statics might be needed to convince people that
elevators are safe to use in a fire.
A study made on people‘s attitudes towards elevator usage in case of fire was con-
ducted in two cities in China. It revealed that people would skip using elevators
in low-rise buildings as they assumed that taking the stairs would be faster and
safer [45]. Nevertheless, the majority of the participants in the study did consider
using an elevator in a high-rise building and were of the opinion that an evacua-
tion plan including elevators would be essential and important to guarantee a safe
evacuation. The main concerns regarding elevators were power failure, and machine
fault as most of the participants were skeptical that elevators would work safely
in fires. It might be a consequence of people being trained for over two decades
that elevators are unsafe in fires and staircases should be used [40, 45]. Two main
factors influencing the choice between stairs and elevators were fireman’s instruc-
tions and the location of the occupants. If guided by firefighters, people would feel
more confident and assured using elevators. The other main factor unveiled that if
a participant were located on the 20th floor or above, they would be more willing
to use elevators. Consequently, training and drills are necessary in structures where
evacuation elevators are taken into account in fire escape strategies to help people
come to terms with the usage of elevators in case of fire and demonstrate that it is
safe and allowed [45].
As the usage of elevators is approved for non-fire emergency egress, an opportunity
is provided to evaluate responses of the public to a different alternative egress route.

2.4 Computer Evacuation Models
2.4
Computer Evacuation Models
Research regarding quantification and modeling human movement and behavior has
been ongoing for over 50 years and has developed into two main paths. The first
regards the regular movement of people in non-emergency situations. The second
one concerns the ability to predict individual movement in an emergency, such as a
fire emergency, which is emphasized on in this thesis [46].
Evacuation research, on the other hand, is more recent. One of the first available
papers on modeling emergency evacuation due to fire was published in 1982. Just a
few years later, researchers already started comparing different computer models for
evacuation analysis. The development of the methods for evacuation analysis and
the computers’ capability were parallel to each other, which is somewhat the case
today. New software or new updates to enhance current software are frequent, and
further research regarding emergency egress enlightens analysts on factors regarding
the evacuation method used, such as human behavior [46,47].
In all fields of engineering, the usage of computer models has increased, especially
for evacuation models, as computer capability has developed quickly in the last
decades, while its cost has decreased. The technological development is not the only
reason for the expanded computer usage. The expansion of intricate designs de-
mands flexible techniques to handle the design, which can make calculations highly
complicated, really fast. Therefore, the computational egress models are frequently
used to assess structures as a part of the performance-based analysis as occupants’
safety is not managed with prescriptive building codes [13,48].
2.4.1
Modeling Method
Evacuation simulations can be divided into two fundamental model categories. The
first model category contains models that only consider human movement and is
known as a ball-bearing model or simply as a physical science model. The focus is
exclusively on the structure’s carrying capacity and its numerous components. The
model considers individuals as objects that are unable to think and respond auto-
matically to external stimuli. When notification of emergency has been received,
and awareness of the threat has been comprehended, it is assumed that occupants
stop their activity immediately and evacuate. Other assumptions are that physical
considerations solely determine the direction and speed of egress. In an extreme
case where this model is used, the population’s individuality is ignored, and their
egress is treated as one. The second model category takes into account both physical
characteristics of the enclosure and individual’s response to the potential threat and
is known as the social science model or psychological model of human interactions.
Individuals are treated as active agents where hazards and individual behavior like
personal reaction times and exit preferences are accounted for [18,46].
23
==PAGE:34==
2.4 Computer Evacuation Models
These two categories are generally used in three different ways when modeling. The
methods are known as movement models, behavioral models, and partial behav-
ior models. The movement models are categorized within the first model category
described and are mainly used to show potential congestion areas, queuing, or bot-
tlenecks inside a structure and have no behavioral data. The behavior models fall
within the second model category. Occupants’ decision-making, actions, and move-
ment towards a safe place due to conditions in the structure are integrated into the
model. The evacuation performance of an individual or the whole population can
detract due to the simulated behavior. The partial behavior models are a mix of
both modeling categories, where the movement of occupants is the main focus. Their
behaviors are also implicitly represented in some way, for example, by distributions
of pre-movement time among the occupants, overtaking behavior, or occupant’s
unique characteristics. These models base the occupant’s movement on observed
human behavior data [13,49].
2.4.2
Model Application
Different approaches can be used to treat evacuation situations. Evacuation analysis
in computers is carried out by using one of the three following approaches [31,46]:
• Optimization
• Simulation
• Risk Assessment
Each of the approaches is fundamentally different, whereas the model capabilities
differ from the chosen method due to different underlying principles. The optimiza-
tion models assume that individuals can evacuate in as efficient way as possible,
neglecting any other activity. The chosen evacuation routes and the flow charac-
teristics of people and exits are presumed as the most optimal option. This type of
model is often used for large groups of people or where individuals are considered as
a homogeneous entity. Therefore, individual behavior is not accounted for, which
makes this type of model a plain movement model. To attempt a representation
of individuals’ behavior and movement in emergencies, a different kind of model
is used, a so-called simulation model. They can give a realistic view of the paths
taken and the decisions made during an evacuation. However, as various models
exist, the behavior is interpreted in different ways into the models, which affects the
results and their accuracy. Hazard identification due to emergency evacuation and
quantification of resultant risks are attempted in risk assessment models. Repeated
runs are implemented to assess statistically significant variations corresponding to
changes in the compartment designs or fire protection measures. Additionally, fac-
tors within each concept can also be represented differently. These factors are the
enclosure, the population, and the occupant’s behavior [31,46].

2.4 Computer Evacuation Models
2.4.2.1
Enclosure
The enclosure where evacuation takes place must be represented in some way in all
models. The representation is usually done in two ways, either by a fine network
or a coarse network. Both methods are space divided into subregions, where each
region is connected to its neighbor. The division of the subregions distinguishes the
two methods. The fine network approach divides the entire floor of the enclosure
into multiple small nodes. Both the sizes and the connectivity differs between the
nodes, thus the number of neighboring nodes. The method enables an accurate
representation of the geometry, internal obstacles, and location of each individual
throughout the evacuation. The coarse network approach defines the geometry in
terms of the structure’s partitions. That is, a room or a corridor is represented as
one node regardless of its physical size. The connectivity between nodes is repre-
sented by arcs within the structure, which shows its real linkage. In this approach,
the occupants move between rooms or corridors without an exact location defined
within the designated room, contrary to the fine network approach where occupants
move from one area of a room to another. When including local movement and
navigation such as overtaking, avoiding obstacles, and solving local conflicts, the
coarse network has its disadvantages due to the vague position of individuals within
a room. The limitation of the coarse approach needs to be kept in mind when be-
havioral components are examined as a detailed calculation of individual movement,
and interaction between individuals is improbable and uncertain. Even though the
fine network approach represents the enclosure more precisely, the coarse network
approach has its advantages. The representation is straightforward and effortless,
and the speed of computation is faster [18,31,46,49].
2.4.2.2
Population
Similar to the enclosure, the population can be represented in two perspectives, an
individual or a global perspective, both in how the model views the occupants and
how the occupants view the building [18,31,46,49].
For the model vision on the occupants, the individual perspective enables per-
sonal attributes to be assigned either randomly or by the user, which are used
in the decision-making process and movement of an individual. An individual path
throughout an evacuation can be followed due to the independence of the process
as other occupants in the simulation do not affect it. Each occupant can, therefore,
be given certain characteristics, which results in a diverse population. It should
be noted that group behavior can be implemented in the individual perspective as
independent decision-making can result in group behavior. The global perspective
treats the evacuation population homogeneously, neglecting the characteristics of
individuals.
This approach focuses on the number of occupants who manage to
escape. Their condition and the effects of the event, such as the effects of smoke
and toxic gases, are disregarded. An average effect or the proportion affected is
only established over the entire population overlooking all indications of individual
endurance. Thereby, groups such as the elderly or the disabled are ignored in this
25
==PAGE:36==
2.4 Computer Evacuation Models
perspective [31,46,49].
When occupants view the building, the individual perspective allows the routes to
be decided by user-defined criteria, personal experience, and other information re-
ceived by the occupants from the floor or other occupants. The occupants might
all know the main entrance but are not necessarily all familiar with the structure’s
available exit paths. This approach is more computationally intensive but can be
more beneficial depending on what the user is trying to establish. The occupants’
global perspective assumes that occupants know the building where they choose the
best alternative, thus the fastest and the shortest exit route. The occupants are
therefore considered to know the structure and its exits. The global approach suits
situations where all the occupants are particularly familiar with the structure and
all its different egress paths. When looking for the most optimal solution, the global
approach is also a better fit [49].
2.4.2.3
Behavior
The behavioral perspective is influenced by the population and geometry approaches
taken, making it one of the most complex features to represent. The decision-making
process of occupants in an evacuation needs to be represented in some way, despite
its complexity, with an appropriate method to establish behavior in the model. The
behavioral systems are categorized in the five following categories [31,46,49]:
• No behavioral rules
• Functional analogy behavior, also known as conditional behavior model
• Implicit behavior
• A rule-based behavioral system, also known as probabilistic models
• An artificial intelligence-based behavioral system
Models with no behavioral rules only simulate the movement of occupants. The
physical influence of the population and the geometry control the outcome of the
occupant evacuation completely [46].
An equation or set of equations are applied in the functional analogy behavioral
models. It determines the behavior of all the occupants of the structure in question.
Individual behavior can be defined in these types of models. However, the prerequi-
site undermines individual behavior as it governs the population in the model. The
function applied to the model can be taken from a different field of study, such as
physics, which is presumed analogous to human behavior. Therefore, the function
controlling the human behavior and the occupant movement in the model might be
adjusted to human movement or a random function unmodified to human behav-
ior [31,46,49].
26
==PAGE:37==
2.4 Computer Evacuation Models
Implicit behavior models presume the behavior to be represented through physical
methods. Secondary data is used to incorporate psychological or sociological in-
fluences in an attempt to simulate human intelligence throughout an evacuation.
Therefore, the quality of the secondary data, namely the accuracy and the validity,
has an enormous effect on the models and their precision [31,46,49].
Rule-based behavioral systems have the same fundamentals as in functional analogy
behavior models. Occupants are able to make decisions based on the pre-defined
set of rules, making the model able to recognize behavioral traits in various circum-
stances. Unlike the functional analogy behavior models, these models are stochastic,
not deterministic, which can result in variations of the outcome through the repeti-
tion of certain simulations [31,46,49].
In the artificial intelligence-based behavioral system, occupants are attempted to
represent human intelligence according to the surroundings. This feature applies
throughout the evacuation simulation, which gives an accurate representation of
the decision-making of an individual but reduces the user control of the occupants
in the simulation [31,46,49].
The surroundings of occupants have a significant impact on their behavior, especially
in an evacuation. Interactions can affect the decision making of an individual and
therefore alter the outcome. The interactions can encounter in the following ways
[46]:
• People - people: interactions between occupants.
• People - structure: interactions between occupants and the enclosing structure.
• People - environment: interactions between occupants and the fire affected
atmosphere.
Moreover, the complexity of human behavior can get even more complicated as the
interaction between occupants and their surroundings can appear in three ways;
psychologically, sociologically, and physiologically [31,46].

2.4.5
Validation of Models
The variety of different modeling methods results in various evacuation models with
a different original intended purpose, but all have the same aim of increasing safety
in emergency evacuations [46].
Even though the models are made in the attempt to simplify calculations and assist
in evaluating the capability of safe emergency evacuation from specific structures,
there are few things that users need to beware of. Many oversimplifications exist in
current models, such as in regards to the representation of actual evacuee response.
Evacuee performance in a fire is a challenging and complex task to comprehend
and display. The comprehensive, robust, and certified theory concerning occupants’
behavior during a fire emergency evacuation needs to be generated to enable a reli-
able representation of evacuee performance [52,53]. The main obstacle of predicting
building movement is, therefore, not the movement. It is the ability to predict the
decision making of occupants and their behavior. The user needs to be aware of
the gap in the simulation of human decision making [54]. Numerous current models
make rough assumptions that prevent various expected evacuation behaviors from
being taken into account. In contrast, other models allow the user to exploit perfor-
mance elements such as pre-evacuation time and route choices [52]. As some models
do not predict evacuees’ choices due to pre-defined choices made by the user, the
results can lead to either too optimistic or too conservative evacuation time. It re-
sults in the production of either an insufficient and unsafe design or an unnecessary
and expensive design [53]. Despite the importance of human behavior and decision
making, the goal of the design is not to predict human responses. The aim is to
design surroundings that enable occupants in chaotic and confusing situations to
make the best and most practical decisions concerning the exit route chosen [54].
Everything above underlines the importance of understanding the background of
the model, its purpose, and what features are available [13]. All models have their
limits of application, which need to be accessible to prevent using a model beyond
its capabilities [18]. The validation of a model should also be available to the user
to make sure it is legitimate. A variety of techniques are used to validate models
today, such as validation against code requirements, validation against fire drills, or
people movement experiments and validation against other models [13].
It becomes evident that choosing and utilizing a computer evacuation model can be
a complicated task for the user. The models tend to be input-intensive and require
a considerable amount of information regarding the structure that occupants must
travel. It demands expertise from the user to guarantee accurate results [47,48].

Legion
Legion is an agent-based simulation tool mainly used to assist with design, opera-

to model human behavior in various market sectors, such as sports and stadium
events, public realm and commercial buildings, traffic, and more [55]. The modeling
method is behavioral, where artificial intelligence is used, and social, physical, and
behavioral characteristics are assigned by probabilistic. The occupants are viewed as
individuals by the model, and the building is viewed from an individual perspective
by the occupants as well. The occupant movement is determined by inter-person
distance as well as certain conditions [49, 56]. The software is available through

3.1.1
Setup
Legion uses the layout of the structure, which is analyzed as a base for the model.
The layout is imported as drawing in a .DGN, .DWG or .DWF file made in a
computer aided design (CAD) software such as AutoCAD. When importing the file,
a layer section appears where three option tick boxes are available for each layer
from the drawing. The options are import, simulation, and auto-navigation. The
import option is to decide if the layer should be imported or not, some layers can
be irrelevant for the simulation and should be disregarded. If the simulation option
is ticked, the layer will be included in the simulation. These layers should contain
genuine obstacles to the occupant’s movement. The auto-navigation option is used
for layers that affect the occupants’ route choice [57].

the entity size, luggage size, and speed profile. A supply type is a composition of
entity types, created in the data template by indicating the percentage of each entity
type [57]. In the data template, other features such as the origin-destination matrix,
known as the OD matrix, is also filled out, and other data profiles can be created
as well. Various other options are available that are not used in this project and
will, therefore, not be discussed further. When adding occupants in a zone, other
settings available are the population amount, final destinations, assignment method
for final destinations, alarm time, pre-movement time, link options, entity filters,
and more [57].
Individuals are modeled as two-dimensional with a circular body that moves in 2D
continuous space in a 0.6-second time step. [58]. When making an entity size profile,
the percentage of people that should have a certain diameter range is entered. Each
range has a variation of 0.05 meters, starting with a range of 0.25 m up to 0.30 m,
with the next range <0.35 m and so on. The speed profile is created similar to the
entity profile where the percentage of people traveling on a certain speed is entered.
Each range has a variation of 0.1 meters per second, starting with the range of 0.0
m/s up to 0.1 m/s, with the next range <0.2 m/s and so on. In figure 3.2, an
example of a speed profile is shown. The entity size profile is created in the same
manner.

3.1.5
Simulation Principles
The individuals’ movements are calculated and assigned by auto-navigation, where
no manual intervention is required, given that individuals have been assigned targets
or final destinations. The software uses the concept of minimum cost when calcu-
lating the movements, where individuals try to minimize their dissatisfaction when
choosing their route. Physical and psychological factors cause dissatisfaction, which
degrades the quality of the journey. The factors contributing to individuals’ dissatis-
faction in Legion are frustration, inconvenience, and discomfort. Individuals become
34
==PAGE:45==
3.1 Legion
frustrated when they need to slow down in congested spaces. An inconvenience is a
degree to which an individual needs to divert from its preferred shortest distance.
Individuals’ discomfort is when there is a lack of adequate personal space [57].
The decisions made by the navigation are at three levels, strategic, tactical, and lo-
cal. The strategic decision is the choice of target, which is the process of translating
an individual’s objectives, such as buying a ticket for a train or leave a stadium.
In numerous cases, the objectives can be achieved in multiple ways by the usage of
links. These links can add multiple intermediary targets to the final target, or the
final destination can be changed due to some new information perceived on the way.
An example of an intermediary target can be buying a ticket before entering a train.
The tactical decision of the navigation is the route selection and macro-navigation.
That is the process of finding the shortest route from the current position to the next
target. The structure’s geometry and the distance of several routes are considered
and compared when deciding on the shortest route. Finally, the local navigation de-
cision is micro-navigation, which the individual’s route is based on. Each individual
has a perception zone in Legion that is dynamically adjusted and is established by
instantaneous information and accumulated memories [57].
Individuals make most of their movement decisions based on the auto-navigation
system. However, the user’s inputs can override the system, making it essential to
understand the principles governing the navigation and the various ways of inter-
acting with them [57].
3.1.6
Limitations
A few of the limitations reported are how groups of people within a crowd can not
be modeled, such as families, and how the psychological state of an individual can
not be accounted for, like stress or emotions. Other aspects mentioned are how
time-consuming the simulation process can be in a complex environment and the
need for much computing power to support the software [55].
3.1.7
Validation
The software is validated frequently by observing and analyzing crowd movements
at multiple locations in different situations. Papers in preparation for a quantitative
validation have been published, emergent crowd behavior and movement have been
qualitatively reproduced, and a third-party comparison against another software has
been carried out. Examples of validation studies that have been carried out are at
the Monaco Grand Prix in 2000, 2001 and 2002, in the London Underground and
Grand Central Station in New York City [55,56].

3.2
Simulex
Simulex is an agent-based evacuation model, which is capable of modeling large and
complex multi-story buildings. Peter Thompson originally developed it as a part of
his Ph.D. thesis. Since then, it has evolved and improved, with various updates and
versions [59, 60]. The modeling method is partial behavior with implicit behavior,
where inter-person distances affect the walking speed of individuals. The ability
for individuals to overtake, rotate their body, step sideways and back-step in small
degrees is also present [49,56]. The program is available under a license from IES,
Integrated Environmental Solutions. In this project an academic license of Simulex
was used, version 2019.1.0.0
3.2.1
Setup
The basis of Simulex is the layout of the building in question and is imported from
a CAD software in a .DXF format into Simulex, one floor at a time. The .DXF file
should preferably only contain information regarding the building shape, which are
the walls, as Simulex uses the drawing to define the space available for evacuation.
Other building contents can be on the drawing but are then regarded as unmovable
objects and are considered as an obstruction in an evacuation, even though in reality,
they could be moved. Therefore, it is recommended only to use the building shape
in the drawings. Other functions, such as exits, stairs, and occupants, are defined
after the drawings have been uploaded [60,61].
3.2.2
Exits
The exits are imported as holes in the drawings where exit markers are added within
the software. The exit choice of occupants can be the closest exit to the occupant’s
initial location, or it can be specified to a predetermined exit.
For Simulex to
model occupant’s movements after reaching through the last door opening, it is
recommended to place the exit outside the building to ensure that the final doorway
effects are included in the simulation. When occupants reach the exit marker, they
are removed from the model and have successfully exited the building [60,61].

==PAGE:47==
3.2 Simulex
3.2.3
Staircases
Staircases are defined within Simulex and are always visually represented as one
continuous staircase, despite the possibility of containing a landing or being a spiral
staircase. When defining a staircase, the stairs’ width and length are entered, where
the length is the true length of the stair measured as the slope length. The total
length of staircases with landings is represented as the sum of the sloping parts and
the landing. The staircases are used to link the floors together, enabling occupants
to move between floors. Each staircase has two links, one on each end of the stairs
to connect to the corresponding level. The walking speed of occupants is reduced in
stairwells compared to walking speed on horizontal surfaces. The speed of occupants
descending a staircase is reduced by 50%, while the speed is reduced by 65% for
occupants ascending a staircase according to the literature found on the subject
[60, 61].
However, later it was discovered when working with Simulex that the
walking speed is only reduced by 40% when descending a staircase and 50% when
ascending a staircase. It is further discussed in appendix C.1.
3.2.4
Occupants
The occupants can be added one at a time or in groups inside the model. First, the
characteristics desired are chosen. The characteristics are divided into a physical
and psychological part. In the physical part, the occupant type can be determined,
where the body dimensions and walking speed are taken into account in the model.
In Simulex person’s body is represented by three circles, as shown in figure 3.3,
where the middle circle represents the torso, and the two smaller circles represent the
shoulders. The dotted circle represents the total radius of individual’s body [60,61].
Figure 3.3: Occupants body representation in Simulex [61].
The walking speed is put in the model as a steady speed, which can be affected
by the surroundings. The relationship between walking speed (velocity) and inter-
person distance is shown in figure 3.4. Inter-person distance is defined in Simulex as
37
==PAGE:48==
3.2 Simulex
the distance between the circle centers of the torso. Individuals reduce the speed as
they get closer to one another, creating a more realistic simulation. As mentioned
previously in section 3.2.3, the walking speed reduces in staircases as well [60,61].
Figure 3.4: Walking speed representation in Simulex [61].
The body dimensions and the walking speed are used to make body types with
different sizes and speeds. The occupant types are then made as a proportion of
various body types. For example, the occupant type’ Office Staff’ is made of two
body types, the type ’Adult Male’, which is 60% of the occupant type ’Office Staff’
and ’Adult Female’, which is 40% of the ’Office Staff’. The body types ’Adult Male’
and ’Adult Female’ have different sizes and speeds [60,61]. Body types and occupant
types can be custom made in a .INI file named populations, which is a part of the
files created when installing the software and is read by Simulex. It was pointed
out by an IES employee, and no further information concerning this possibility was
found in any literature nor the user manual.
In the psychological part, the response time is entered as a mean value and the
desired deviation of the mean. The actual response time assigned to each occupant
is chosen by one of the three following distributions; uniform (random), triangular,
or normal distribution [60,61].
Another feature in the psychological part is the distance (route) that individuals
determine to take. Occupants are directed to the closest available exit by using
distance maps. The distance map is a mesh composed of 0.2 m by 0.2 m spatial
blocks, representing the total building space.
Each block has a numerical value
assigned to it corresponding to the total traveling distance to an exit from that
block. Final destinations can be chosen by using multiple distance maps if the closest
exit strategy is undesired. The preferred exits are then selected in a new distance
map, and when specifying the occupants, the desired distance map is chosen. This
strategy is used in certain situations, for example, when people are unfamiliar with
exits, when certain exits are blocked or when people exit the building by the same
route they entered.
The default distance map directs occupants to the nearest
exit [60,61].

3.2 Simulex
3.2.5
Simulation Principles
The main principles and assumptions stated in the Simulex manual are the following
[61]:
• Each occupant is assigned normal unimpeded walking speed.
• As occupants get closer together, the walking speeds are reduced.
• Each occupant heads towards and exit by taking a direction from the right
angles to the contours shown on the distance map.
• Overtaking, body rotation, sideways stepping, and small degrees of back-
stepping are all accommodated.
3.2.6
Limitations
One of the software’s limitations reported is how the walking speed and direction of
individuals are specified. It depends on inter-person distances, which provides equal
space between individuals. In reality, individuals are not equally spaced, leading to
inaccuracy to some extent [59].
Other limitations reported are how the computer capacity can limit the software
and how occupants can get ’stuck’ in the model’s links during certain simulations.
The user manual provides suggestions to common problems such as the occupants
getting stuck, error messages, and more [56].
3.2.7
Validation
Several validation studies have been made for Simulex. One study was made for
a supermarket where the flow rates through exits were examined and compared to
hand calculations as the developers did not have the actual data. The software
produced results that corresponded to the calculations [56,62].
Another study was made in three university buildings where evacuation times and
occupant movement were examined. The buildings were modeled in Simulex and
compared to actual data from an evacuation where the total evacuation time, pre-
movement time and evacuation behavior were noted. The study results showed that
Simulex provided similar evacuation times to the actual evacuation [56].
3.2.8
Outputs
The output of Simulex contains an overview of the building and is available as a
.SLX file or .TXT file. The files contain information such as the number of created
staircases, number of exits in the building, number of occupants evacuating from
the building, overall evacuation time, total number of occupants through each exit,
and more similar data [56].

Five entity types were made in the Excel data template to define the occupants, one
for each category. Six profiles were created to customize the entity types according
to the characteristics found for each type. Four speed profiles were made, one for
visually impaired where 100% of the occupants had the speed range 0.9 - 1.0 m/s.
One for hearing impaired with 50% of the occupants with the speed range of 1.1 -
1.2 m/s and the other 50% with the speed range of 1.2 - 1.3 m/s. One for wheelchair
users with all the users containing the speed range 0.6 - 0.7 m/s. And finally, the
speed range for mobility-impaired where everyone has a speed range of 0.7 - 0.8
m/s. Two occupant size profiles where made, one for wheelchair users and one for
mobility impaired. In both profiles, all the occupants have a diameter range of 0.80
- 0.85 m.
The five entity types had all equal gender ratio, no luggage, and inferred walking
speed on the staircases as the default settings. The effects of a staircase have been
taken into account with the weighted distance discussed above. The non-disabled
category had the default walking speed of a UK commuter provided by Legion, and
the non-disabled, visually impaired and hearing impaired all had the default oc-
cupant size. A supply type was made with the five entity types made, where the
categories had the population composition according to table 4.5, which was previ-
ously displayed in section 4.2. When inserting the occupants, an evacuation zone
was made in each room containing individuals according to tables 4.2 - 4.4 and the
floor plans, which are shown in appendix A. Legion randomly distributes individ-
uals from all the occupant’s categories according to the composition each time the
simulation is played.
Delay profiles were made in each room to account for the reaction and decision time,
delaying occupants depending on their characteristics category by the time given in
table 4.8. It was done as the pre-movement time could not be linked to the occu-
pant’s category. The reaction and decision time used for the non-disabled category
is 28 seconds, according to a British study in a business occupancy [22]. The char-
acteristics were turned on and off according to the case being simulated at each time.
When choosing the final destination, the assignment method used was the shortest
distance. The occupants then aim for the exit closest to their position at the be-
ginning of the evacuation. Another setting in the assignment method used is the
% of people considering congestion ahead. This feature enables occupants to avoid
excessive congestion if it arises and periodically re-evaluate their target exit. There-
fore, the occupants can switch their target exit to another one that is not necessarily
closest to their initial position or the current position [57]. This setting was used on
all the occupants of the building.

5.4
The Software
The evacuation models used in the project have very different interfaces in all the
phases of usage. That is the setup, running, and outputs. Simulex has considerably
fewer features available compared to Legion. That was expected as Simulex is a pure
evacuation model, while Legion is used for multiple purposes, such as to optimize
building space with regards to design, operations, safety, and more. The availability
of more features has both its advantages as well as disadvantages. Additional fea-
tures increase the risk of misinterpretation of the occupants and misunderstanding
of how the software functions. Meanwhile, fewer features can result in neglecting
essential aspects of the occupants that affect the outcome. Therefore, it is vital to
know the concepts behind the software’s features to guarantee accurate results.
In both models, the option of simulating a small group traveling together, such as
family, friends, or coworkers, is not available. Meaning that all occupants only think
of themselves when evacuating. It is not ideal as it would have been preferred to
assign an assistant to occupants in the disabled category to assist in the evacua-
tion. For example, assistants would guide visually impaired people, alert hearing
impaired people, assist wheelchair users in the staircases, etc. Additionally, it is
quite unrealistic that everyone travels alone, not caring for others.
Furthermore, many behavioral factors are insufficiently interpreted as it can both
be hard to interpret the decision process, and there are many aspects of individual
behavior. Although, inadequate data is available to implement it into a software.
Examples of behavioral aspects neglected are occupants looking for someone or
something during the evacuation, resulting in counter flow in the staircases and
other places, and panic behavior. In a panic, people can, for example, freeze or
push other people out of the way. However, it is possible to simulate counter flow
and occupants carrying luggage in Legion, enabling the ability to interpret these
behavioral factors to some extent.
In Legion, the occupants’ reactions and movements were more realistic when simu-
lating as people moved out of the way for others when containing different reaction
and decision time. Meanwhile, in Simulex, people could get stuck behind others,
resulting in occupants from different categories containing the same reaction and de-
cision time. Nevertheless, the representation of the occupants themselves in Simulex
is more realistic, both visually and geometrically. In Legion, occupants are repre-
sented as one circle containing the same width and depth. In contrast, in Simulex,
the occupants are represented with three circles, where different width and thickness
can be assigned, providing a more realistic interpretation of an individual.
In Simulex, it was problematic assigning all the characteristics at once using oc-
cupant types according to a predetermined composition like was done in Legion.

5.4 The Software
Therefore, the user determined where the occupant types were located by using Ex-
cel to distribute the occupants in Simulex to some extent randomly. The categories
containing a low percentage of the population were not located in rooms containing
few occupants, such as the reception and the kitchens. The categories containing
disabled people are assumed to have more effects in bigger crowds, as more people
can be affected by them, and queues or congestions can form. Despite this assump-
tion, there is no indication of any effects of potentially more disabled individuals in
the bigger rooms in Simulex. Not many rooms contain few people and, therefore, it
might not matter. The rooms’ location can also reduce the effects. For example, the
reception uses an exit on the ground floor, which few other occupants use, and the
people in the kitchen blend in with the open office staff very quickly after initiating
evacuation. Therefore, the distribution of people is considered rather even com-
pared to what an automatic random distribution could do, which is used in Legion.
If many disabled people are located in the same room, congestion can form, and the
staircase queues can take a longer time compared to an even distribution. However,
the occupants in Legion can reroute if a long line or congestion is ahead, which
should reduce the queuing time and the evacuation time due to a better distribution
into the staircases. Nonetheless, the most troubling area on the meeting room floor
enables occupants to reach one staircase due to the atrium in the middle of the
floor, allowing occupants to only reroute to another entrance to the same staircase.
During the peak of the evacuation, the occupants will always have to wait a similar
time despite the option of rerouting.
Overall, Legion provides higher evacuation times and, in the majority of the cases,
a higher queuing time. When searching for probable causes for the difference, the
input options in both models were analyzed when entering the characteristics and
when defining the staircases.
The reaction and decision time is inserted with one value for each category in both
evacuation models, while for the walking speed and individual size, the input method
differs. In Simulex, the reaction and decision time is entered as a value for each occu-
pant, but in Legion, delay profiles are used. The delay profile captures the occupants
inside each room as soon as the evacuation time starts for the time corresponding
to the occupant’s category. As the evacuation time in all the cases in scenario 1,
where only the ground floor is evacuated, is almost the same for both models, the
effects of reaction and decision time are not considered to contribute to the time
difference. The walking speed and individual size are both put in as a range of values
in Legion for each category, while Simulex uses one value. The walking speed for
the disabled categories in Legion is made of ranges containing lower walking speed
than in Simulex. For example, the occupants with mobility impairment contain a
walking speed from 0.7 m/s to 0.8 m/s in Legion. In comparison, it is 0.8 m/s for
all occupants with mobility impairment, in Simulex. On the contrary, the walking

the project at hand.
5.5
Other aspects
The simulations containing the heterogeneous reaction and decision time seem un-
realistic when monitoring them, especially in the closed rooms containing few occu-
pants. It seems more logical, for example in the kitchen, that the five people there
would travel together or in two smaller groups, not leaving one behind. Communi-
cation between occupants can have much impact on the reaction and decision time,
and people would decide together, or the natural leaders would decide for the group
when or if they should start evacuating. In large and open areas, however, chaos can
form, where people think more about their own benefits when evacuating, rather
than the interests of the group. With a bigger crowd, the reaction and decision time
is believed to stretch over a more extended period due to people deciding what to
take with them, etc. However, the recognition of a threat is presumed to be at a
similar time.
Another thought regarding the simulation is the choice of exit. As mentioned pre-
viously in section 2.2.3, 70-80% of occupants choose the same exit as they entered
the building. In the simulations in both evacuation models, the occupants choose
the shortest route as they are assumed to be familiar with the building and had
some sort of evacuation training. The shortest route for the vast majority does not

contain the main entrances of the building. Only a few occupants on the ground
floor choose the main entrance based on the shortest path selection, and all the
occupants on the other two floors choose other exits. In a panic, with a large crowd
and confusion, it is probable that a proportion of the occupants aim for the same
exit as they entered, despite being familiar with the building’s exits. That would
add few seconds to each evacuation time as the distance is longer, but not result in

that exit and then needing to reroute. Active and passive fire protection is used in

shown in appendix A.2.
For people with a movement disability to evacuate through stairs seems a very op-
timistic option without any equipment, especially in a multi-story building. The
people using wheelchairs are believed to be misinterpreted in the staircases, despite
the slower walking speed and the software’ method of slowing the pace even further
in the stairwells. Equipment such as stair sliders or some sort of sitting elevators in
the staircases is presumed to be the minimum equipment needed to guarantee a safe
evacuation for all if occupants are supposed to be able to evacuate by themselves or
with minimum assistance. Another option is using fire safety elevators, which could
be a good and efficient option. This option is used in high-rise buildings in Asia, as
previously mentioned. However, those types of elevators are not well known, and
little data exists on the usage during real evacuation. Another aspect is convincing
people to use fire elevators in an evacuation, as standard elevators cannot be used
in evacuations. Therefore, all elevators are often interpreted as unsafe in a fire.
The data used in this project regarding the composition of people and the charac-
teristics are all found through research. A part of the data in the same categories is
from different years and sources, leading to some error potential. The data for the
reaction and decision time is all obtained from the same source but is for a hospital
evacuation as little or no other data regarding heterogeneous reaction and decision
time was found, which might overestimate the time. Another possible source of
error is the models, as they contain various settings and features that affect the
outcome, making the user’s input and understanding vital, as indicated in section
2.4. Despite potential errors, the project is considered to give accurate results with
the data and resources currently available.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In this chapter, the conclusions of the project are stated. The key findings of the
simulations are summarized along with the comparison of Legion and Simulex. Then
suggestions are made for future work.
6.1
Conclusions
Four cases containing different combinations of heterogeneous characteristics were
simulated in three different scenarios of the same building using two evacuation
models. The aim was to investigate the impact of different characteristics and com-
pare the two models used. The results of the simulations were used to make the
findings.
The effects of heterogeneous reaction and decision times and individual sizes were
investigated in the four cases, all of which contained heterogeneous walking speed.
The main findings regarding the characteristics was the phased evacuation on each
floor caused by the heterogeneous reaction and decision time, which contributed to
a steadier flow throughout the building. When the heterogeneous reaction and deci-
sion time was combined with heterogeneous individual sizes, the effects were as little
as none when evacuating only the ground floor. However, as levels were added, the
effects increased. That indicates that the phased evacuation effects caused by the
heterogeneous reaction and decision time have less impact as more floors are added
due to more people using the staircases, which causes congestions. Consequently,
the staircases have more impact when more levels are included in the simulations.
Overall, the simulations show different results for different cases indicating that the
varying characteristics combinations used in each case had different impacts on both
the flow, which was demonstrated with the queuing time and the evacuation itself.
The evacuation models used in the thesis, Legion, and Simulex, showed varying
results in the simulations except in scenario 1, where only the ground floor was
evacuated. That is due to no staircases in that scenario. All the other simulations
gave a considerable time difference, where Legion had higher queuing and evacua-
tion times in the majority of the simulations. The models are noticeably different
in usage, they vary in the features available, and the availability of outputs differs.
After analyzing the effects of the characteristics and the staircases in both models,
the time difference between the evacuation models is concluded to be due to dif-
ferent modeling methods, which use different ways of interpreting the behavior of
occupants in the simulations. Legion is more advanced software, where it takes time
to learn how it works, more features are available, and outputs for the data, which

The goal of this project was to investigate heterogeneous pedestrian flows in differ-

Do different heterogeneous characteristics have an impact on evacuation?
The answer is yes, as demonstrated in this project, the heterogeneous characteristics
affect the evacuation results, both the queuing time and the evacuation time, despite
little or no relation between those two assessments in this project.
One of the

each other, and how one can minimize the effects of the other. It demonstrates the
importance of including different characteristics when simulating an evacuation to
guarantee reliable and accurate results.
6.2
Future Work
The thesis provides information and knowledge regarding heterogeneous character-
istics and the evacuation models Simulex and Legion. However, more questions and
thoughts have come up during the process of the project regarding heterogeneous
characteristics and other aspects that can be used for future work.
One of the main aspects is the data used for the characteristics. The population
composition used in this thesis is gathered from various data from Denmark and Nor-
way and are not from the same years. Countries could have the number of people
or the composition of people with different disabilities available, and even provide
more detailed categories than are used in this project. It is believed that the data
exists for many countries as it is used in various purposes within each country and
is used, for example, when working with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).
The same applies to the characteristics themselves as they are composed of various
studies. It can be sorted out with more experiments, where people that belong to
different categories go through the same layout and are timed with regards to travel
time, walking speed, and reaction and decision time. As the composition of people is
continuously changing and with the population aging as mentioned in section 2.3.1,
these experiments get outdated and need to be regularly updated. Experiments can
update the current values used and assist in further research regarding less known
features such as behavior. Though behavior is something that will never be fully

C.1
Occupants Definition in Simulex
In this section, a more detailed description is given on the occupants’ definition in
Simulex and what properties were given to each new body type and population.
The six new body types made were for the occupant categories with walking speed
according to table 4.6 in section 4.2.1, and for wheelchair users and mobility im-
paired with walking speed according to table 4.6 and the individual sizes according
to table 4.7 in section 4.2.2. The parameters available when making a body type
are the total radius of the body circle, the radius of the main torso circle, the radius
of shoulder circles, unimpeded walking velocity on flat terrain, distributed variation
of unimpeded walking velocity on flat terrain and multiplication factors for walking
speeds going up and down staircases. The body types made for different walking
speeds use the median values from the existing median type (body type no. 0) in
the .INI file for all parameters except for the walking speed (unimpeded walking
velocity on flat terrain). For the body types made for walking speeds and individual
sizes, the same procedure was used except changing the total radius of the body
circle and the radius of the main torso circle as well as the walking speed. It was
determined to keep the shoulder circles as the median values.
It should be noted that the multiplication factor in the .INI file does not correspond
to the reduction of the walking speed in staircases found in literature ( [60, 61]),
which was presented in section 3.2.3. The walking speed for descending staircases
was reduced by 40%, not 50%, as stated in the literature, and the walking speed
for ascending staircases was 50%, not 65%, as stated in the literature. It was de-
termined to use the numbers present in the .INI file (40% reduction for descending
a staircase and 50% reduction for ascending a staircase) as the software is assumed
to have been updated, while the literature has not.
The seven new populations were made using the new body types. When defining
a population, a number of different body types can be used in various proportions.
Each new body type got its own population, and in addition, a new office staff
population was made containing only the existing median body type.

C.2 Assignation of Characteristics
C.2
Assignation of Characteristics
In this section, the method used to assign characteristics randomly to occupants
in Excel is explained. Five simulations with different assignations of characteristics
are used in all the scenarios for all the cases. An average of the five simulations
is used when comparing the results to other scenarios or cases. A diagram of the
simulations for one case is shown in figure C.1.
Figure C.1: Simulations for one case.
First, it was decided to calculate the proportion of each category for all the rooms
in the building. In table C.2, the proportion for each category is shown depending
on the number of people in the room.
Table C.2: Proportion of people in each category.
98
==PAGE:109==
C.2 Assignation of Characteristics
As the values in table C.2 above contain decimals, a margin of error needed to be
determined. The chosen margin of error was 15%. Table C.3 shows the margin
of error for each category, depending on the people in the room. The names of
the occupant categories are abbreviated with the first letter of the category in the
following tables. N stands for non-disabled, V for visually impaired, H for hearing
impaired, M for mobility impaired, and W for wheelchair users.
Table C.3: Margin of error for each category.
Next, the upper and lower limit was calculated for all the categories in all the rooms.
The limits were multiplied with the correct number of rooms to account for all the
people in the building. For example, there are 10 meeting rooms with 15 people in
the building, and therefore, the limits for 15 people were multiplied with 10. The
rooms, the number of each type of room, the people per room, and the total number
of people in each type of room are shown in table C.4. It was determined to treat
single offices as one room to get a wider range of characteristics of the people in
those offices.
Table C.4: Information regarding each type of room.
In tables C.5 and C.6, the limits are shown for each type of room. The values were
rounded to an integer to represent the occupants of the building. The lowest and
the highest percentages for each characteristic category are all so shown. The non-
disabled people were calculated with regard to the other categories. For each room
type, the number of people with disabilities is subtracted from the occupant load in
the room, giving the number of non-disabled people in the room.
99
==PAGE:110==
C.2 Assignation of Characteristics
Table C.5: Low limit of occupants.
Table C.6: High limit of occupants.
Excel was then used to pick a random number between the lower and higher limits
for the disabled characteristics categories for all 10 room types. For the room types
containing 2 or more identical rooms, and for the single offices, Excel was used to
distribute the disabled people between rooms randomly. These room types are the
2 kitchens on the canteen level, the 10 small and medium meeting rooms containing
15 people on the ground floor and the meeting room floor, the open offices on the
canteen floor and the big meeting rooms on the ground floor and the meeting room
floor. The rooms were then filled with non-disabled people.
As shown, the calculations are done with the whole building in mind when distribut-
ing the people, and therefore can the percentages of the characteristic categories have
a larger margin of error in scenario 1 and scenario 2 when only the ground floor and
the two lower floors are being simulated. It is considered acceptable as the percent-
ages of people in each category are not sacred and are averages over a great number
of people, which can appear higher or lower in different areas, buildings, regions, etc.

APPENDIX D
Staircase Comparison
In this appendix, the interpretation of the staircases and the travel time is com-
pared between Legion and Simulex. The travel time is considered as the time for an
undisturbed occupant to walk between floors.
In Legion, the staircase length is calculated as a weighted length to account for
the extra effort used when descending a staircase. The length of the staircase is
calculated as discussed in section 3.1.3 and shown in figure 3.1. Where the distance
factor is 1.49 for downwards movement, and the same assumed route is used as in
figure 4.5 in section 4.4.2.2. The staircase length is calculated as:
3.08 m · 1.49 · 2 + 0.75 m · 2 + 1.6 m = 12.28 m
The occupants walking speed is then used unchanged in the staircases for the
weighted length. The walking speed in Legion is put in as a range, and in table
D.1, the walking speed is given for the low and high value of the ranges used.
