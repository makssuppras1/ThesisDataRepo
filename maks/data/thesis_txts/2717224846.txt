Abstract
Drug assisted weight loss is a developing area of research that helps reduce obesity,
which is becoming a serious problem in society. To streamline the process of drug cre-
ation for weight loss, the objective of this project was to create a dashboard that could sim-
ulate body weight changes in rodents given it’s initial body weight, food intake and drug
parameters. An energy expenditure model is used to fit control groups from experimental
data. A PK/PD model is used to account for pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics
of drugs used in experiments. The energy expenditure model is modified with Michaelis-
Menten equation in order to incorporate drug effect into the overall model. Optimal values
for parameters of upgraded model are found and used to simulate the body weight data.
Results show that the model I created can simulate the body weight well. The outcome
of this work is (i) a dashboard that can be adapted to simulate body weight changes for
drugs with known PK profiles, (ii) a working modelling approach to drug assisted energy
expenditure that can be further improved by introducing additional complexity.

Introduction
1.1
Obesity
Worldwide obesity has nearly tripled since 1975 [1]. It is a medical condition in which
excess fat accumulated in an organism may have negative effect on health and therefore
increase the risk of other diseases such as cardiovascular diseases, musculoskeletal dis-
orders or some types of tumors.
Obesity is caused by taking in more energy than the organism uses over a sustained
period of time, therefore it is caused by overeating. As our body is built of many differ-
ent organs and tissues, the body weight can be considered heterogeneous. It can be
presented as a two compartment model where the body weight consist of fat mass and
fat-free mass, often called lean mass, consisting of bones, water and body cell mass [2].
When losing weight the purpose is to reduce the fat mass while maintaining lean mass.
Weight loss can be challenging due to metabolic adaptation. It is a process that strives to
adjust organism’s energy expenditure to it’s energy intake via adapting its resting energy
expenditure.
1.2
Drug assisted weight loss
To battle obesity, one can use drugs that reduce appetite, reduce energy intake or enhance
energy expenditure. Drugs can help weight loss in many different ways, be it influencing
organism’s brain and changing eating priorities and wants or influencing the way the body
processes, produces and stores fat. Weight loss can be a side-effect of certain drugs such
as stimulants, opioids, alcohol. Weight loss is for example associated with cocaine use
[3] due to fundamental perturbations in fat regulation. For those reasons it is important to
conduct research on how drugs influence weight loss and to create safe, non-addictive
drugs that can help with weight reduction.
This project uses data collected during experiment on mice. Different groups of rodents
where monitored over multiple days while consuming food consisting of different diets and
being dosed different amount of drug.
1.3
Simulation of drug affected energy expenditure
To speed up drug discovery and creation, it is possible to simulate the effects of a drug on
rodents to check whether the drug achieves expected results. This helps in drug creation
process by being able to quickly simulate and change the drug properties before conduct-
ing physical experiments that take months to prepare and conduct and are also subject
to regulatory restrictions. This work focuses on simulation of drugs enhancing energy
expenditure. It describes how to modify energy expenditure model to accommodate the
drug effect.
1.4
Project overview and objectives
The objective of this project is to model energy expenditure with drug intake in rodents
and create a simulation workflow that allows for conducting computational experiments on
drug assisted energy expenditure via dashboard. The dashboard will provide simulation
playground and present the data on charts and via downloadable output files. The work
also analyzes how well the created solution can characterize a drug through a set of opti-
mized parameters. At the beginning, in chapter 2 Theoretical Background, all theoretical

2.1
Body composition, energy intake and energy expenditure
Due to the law of thermodynamics, weight remains stable when energy expenditure (EE)
is equal to the energy intake (EI). Prolonged energy surplus or energy deficit leads to
weight gain or weight loss respectively. Calculating energy intake is simple as it is a
product of amount of food (macronutrient intake) and it’s caloric density (energy density)
that an organism consumes. Modelling energy expenditure is a lot harder problem from
mathematical standpoint as there are a lot of variables that influence how much energy
an organism expands.
Equation 2.1 represents mass conservation principle. It is straightforward, body weight is
composed of two compartments, lean mass (LM) and fat mass (FM) and must equal to
total body weight.

(2.1)

conservation law.

2.2
Pharmacokinetics
Pharmacokinetics (PK) is a branch of pharmacology that researches the fate of sub-
stances administered to a living organism. It analyzes chemical metabolism and what
happens to the drug from the moment it is consumed up to the point at which there is no
trace of it in the organism. Pharmacokinetics helps adjust and monitor dosing of drugs
accurately. It is a study of how an organism affects the drug.
2.3
Pharmacodynamics
Pharmacodynamics (PD) is a branch of pharmacology that studies the biochemical and
physiologic effects of drugs. A drug’s pharmacodynamics can be influenced by many
different aspects such as a disorder or disease, aging process or other drugs. It is a study
of how the drug affects the organism.
2.4
Multi-compartment model
There are many different pieces that require modelling in pharmacokinetics and pharma-
codynamics. A mathematical model suitable for conveying such characteristics is a multi-
compartment model, which describes how things like energies or material are transmitted
among different pieces (compartments) of a system. These models are very convenient
as they help to reduce complexity of equations by discretizing the problem and reducing
the number of parameters. These models are quite flexible and can treat variables as
continuous or discrete, as well as stochastic or deterministic.

2.5
PK/PD model
PK/PD modelling is a field that combines both pharmacologic disciplines of pharmacoki-
netics and pharmacodynamics. It combines aspect of both areas into one mathematical
model that allows to describe over the course of time the effect intensity in response to
administration of a drug dose.
Main application of PK/PD models is modelling the relationship of exposure-response or
concentration-effect relationship [12]. In order to express these relationships, a variety of
approaches exists, from simple linear model, Emax model or sigmoid Emax models [13] to
more complex models which capture multiple additional factors such as delay between the
drug administration and drug effect or temporal dissociation of the drug [14, 15]. PK/PD
modelling plays a central role in drug development [16, 17] and is an industry standard
procedure that must meet certain guidelines on how exposure-response studies should
be performed before the drug is certified by government administration bodies such as
The Food and Drug Administration in USA[18].
2.5.1
PK profile
PK profile is a set of parameters that describe the absorption, distribution, metabolism
and excretion of drugs in the body. PK profile needs to be provided by the researcher for
simulation purposes as it describes how the drug behaves in the administered body and
therefore it influences how the drug affects energy expenditure over time.
The half-life or t 1
2 is a concept of PK that concerns repeated administration of a drug.
When the quantity of drug eliminated during time t equals the quantity of the drug which
reaches the systemic circulation in time t, a so called steady state is reached. The half-life
represents the time required to reduce the plasma concentration of the drug reached in
steady-state by 50%.

Methods
In this chapter, all modifications required to perform simulations are described.
4.1
Energy expenditure model
To make sure a model can simulate data relevantly, it requires data to fit it’s parameters
to. In case of energy expenditure model:
EE = K + β∆EI + λBW + γLMLM + γFMFM + ηFM
dFM
dt
+ ηLM
dLM
dt
(4.1)
As mentioned in Chapter 2 Theoretical Background, the parameter to fit is λ. Other pa-
rameters are constant or have a fixed value based on experiment conditions.
Since a good estimate for a range of lambda values per diet for a mouse can be made
based on other studies (for high-fat diet we can estimate lambda value around λ = 0.2±0.1
[6, 21, 22]), we perform a simple exhaustive search to find the most optimal value. The
error function is simply the difference between simulated values and real data values
which we try to minimize. The software currently uses a range from 0 to 1 for lambda,
but that can be changed to be provided as input by a dashboard user in the future when
other animal types are incorporated into the software. The fit is done on non-drug groups
as this will yield real, natural lambda value without drug effect.
4.2
PK/PD Modelling
To calculate substrate concentration over time, the PK/PD model is needed. In this project,
a two-compartment model is created:

distinction in distribution of drug to different tissues and elimination of the drug. The central

in a manner where they produce the smallest error across all drug dose-level groups
combined. Parameter values obtained in such way should produce the most realistic
simulations when simulating doses that there is no data for.

4.4
Simulation
Simulations are done twice for every drug dose-level. First simulation is done using dose-
level specifically optimized Michaelis-Menten parameters and second simulation is per-
formed using universally optimized Michaelis-Menten parameters across all dose-levels.
For all simulations, an optimized lambda value found by fitting non-drug group is used.
Modified energy expenditure alongside PK model is used to perform simulations. The
output is simulated lean mass and fat mass.

Dashboard
An integral part of this project was building a dashboard that would allow researchers to
perform simulations without having to code. Experience in this area shows that the input
data often varies in size, type, labelling and correctness. This makes it significantly harder
for pharmacologic researchers to perform simulations on drug induced weight loss as it
requires a significant amount of time to pre-process files, adjust appropriate model and
receive results each time. Dashboard built during this project streamlines this process
and reduces go-to simulation time significantly. Since it is a no-coding-required solution,
anyone with appropriate data files can get and present results. Below, a ”welcome screen”
of the dashboard is presented in fig 5.1:
Figure 5.1: Welcome screen of the dashboard after launching the app.
As mentioned previously, the complexity of modelling drug induced weight loss can be-
come extreme by introducing a lot of detail and the dashboard was built with the perspec-
tive of being easy to use. For this reason, besides body weight and food intake data,
only the necessary PK parameters are asked from researchers as input parameters. The
pre-processing code has many edge cases programmed which ensures that if the input
data has in general a recommended format, even with some minor problems, the soft-
ware takes care of extracting important information that is needed for modelling such as
dosing, diet information or drug name. As in every expertise area, domain knowledge is
extremely important. The dashboard has an option to set the value range of parameters
Vmax and Km, which are responsible for drug effect. This serves two purposes. Firstly,
while exploring optimization techniques that best suit this problem was a smaller part of
the project, due to constant changes in modelling approach, choice of input parameters
and the parameters to be optimized, as well as time constraints of this project and the
goal of ensuring that the dashboard works for many different kinds of data, the final opti-
mization choice in the dashboard is a naive exhaustive search technique. For this reason,

Discussion
As presented in section 6.1, energy expenditure model can fit to the data from groups with
no drug very well. The model correctly simulates that the body weight drop happens due
to lose of fat mass, while preserving lean mass.
Created PK/PD model can calculate the substance concentration correctly as shown in
section 6.2. Due to the parameters in the PK profile, there never occurs an increased
volume of drug accumulation in the central compartment. This stems from the fact that
half-time for the used PK parameters equals 8 hours. Given that we dose the drug once
every day, the majority of drug is eliminated from the plasma before the next dose is
administered. Absorption in central compartment happens linearly as it should with given
PK parameters. It can also be noticed that the peak drug concentration changes from day
to day as the dosage is adaptive given changes in body weight.
Section 6.3 presents how well the complete model can simulate the body weight changes.
The simulations were done for every drug group twice - once with modified energy ex-
penditure model where parameters were fitted specifically to the drug group and second
time for universal parameter set that was the best fit across all drug groups. While the
simulations using specifically optimized parameters are more accurate, the computations
that use universal parameter set also reflect the characteristics of the body weight change
pretty accurately. One could argue that to simulate a new drug dosage, it would be prefer-
able to use the parameters of group where dosage amount was the closest to the drug
dose we wish to simulate. However drugs can have both known and unknown side effects
which influence the behavior of the animals that are not resembled in the data. These side
effects might be not present in different drug dosages. The model has the capabilities to
still make a good fit, but it has no parameters that could show or explain how much the en-
ergy expenditure is affected by effects other than the main objective of the drug. Because
of that, the universal across groups optimized parameter set could be the most balanced
and yield the best generalization when simulating different doses.
The drug used in the experiment, Lira, has a known side effect. It reduces the desire to eat
at the start of drug administration cycle. Because of how the experiment was conducted,
the food intake data did not reflect this behavior. That is because the mice are free to take
as much food into the cage as they wish, however if they later do not consume all the food
they had taken, it is not measurable. Therefore, the data indicates that the food intake
in the first days is higher than it in fact was. This explains why the body weight of the
animals has dropped quickly in the first days of the experiment and why the simulations
do not reflect this.
Figures fig 6.14 and fig 6.15 show how the Michaelis-Menten parameters affect the curve
of the simulation. When dose-level specific parameter fits were used, the simulation had
characteristic ”bumps” in value every full day. The model tried to perfectly fit to data
points by choosing Km value that would allow for that. These spikes correlate with new
doses being applied and therefore drug concentration raising rapidly. We can see that
the value of Km for the specific dose-level fits increases with dose amount. However, it is
known that this constant is not affected by the concentration of drug [23]. That suggests
that while the specific dose-level parameter fit makes numerical sense given used error
function of minimizing difference between real and simulated data points, it is a wrong
approach given domain knowledge. The universal fit across all dose-levels selects the

value of Km = 0.21 and smaller maximum reaction rate Vmax = 5. That removes the
numerical manipulation around real data points/new doses and produces a smooth curve
that still provides a very good fit compared to dose-specific fit. This shows that the idea
described in section 4.3 of making a fit across all dose-level groups all at once as it might
yield the best generalization and most realistic results was a good assumption.

Conclusions
Modelling energy expenditure vs energy intake proved to be a challenging task. An enor-
mous part of the project was data analysis and data wrangling. Every experiment that
is conducted in this space measures the metrics in slightly different way and produces
files that have different layout, different amount of data and values have different units.
Writing a dashboard that can take all of this into account took a lot of brainstorming and
re-adjustments. Because there is no standard way to perform a study like this, there were
a lot of nuances that required small tweaks, additional checks and very often backtracking
on some progress in order to accommodate for these new aspects that came into the light
as the model was getting more complex. It sometimes happened during the project that a
need for additional data arose in order to simulate the whole process. At the end I recon
that the created model achieved the objective and can simulate the body weight change
very well given the real data. Unfortunately, by the time the model and dashboard become
fully functional, there was no time to allow researchers to use it and update it according to
their feedback. Guo and Hall [6] performed similar optimization procedure for the unmod-
ified energy expenditure model using Markov Chain Monte Carlo method. Implementing
this technique would certainly be an improvement that would allow better scaling to other
animal types as well as improve computational time for large parameter ranges. Unfortu-
nately due to frequent decision changes during the project regarding choice of parameters
to be optimized and model in general, a naive optimization was implemented to progress
with the project with goal of enhancing it later, but the time constraints of thesis did not
allow for this. Nevertheless, my optimization yielded results that were satisfactory with
values of similar magnitude as Guo and Hall. Unfortunately due to confidentiality, using
drug specific PK parameters from Novo Nordisk was not possible, therefore a PK profile
describing linear absorption from a different study was used [24] and there was no suit-
able PK profile for non-linear absorption to conduct an experiment on alongside the data
I had.
I think that the project managed to create a good modelling approach and produced good
results. The dashboard serves as a great tool to build upon as it lays a firm foundation for
future research. Given the project would last longer, I see areas for improvement such
as increasing PK/PD model complexity by implementing various dosing techniques such
as intravenous and subcutaneous injections, different dosing intervals or enhancing pa-
rameter optimization techniques to allow for bigger parameter complexity. Additional time
would allow the researchers to use the dashboard and come back with feedback that could
be used to upgrade the application. Keeping time constraints of this project in mind, I think
that the time split between different problems of this project (data pre-processing, mod-
elling, optimization, dashboard building, simulations) was well balanced which resulted in
a complete working product with good foundations for future improvements.
