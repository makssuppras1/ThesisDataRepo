Abstract 
Time preferences and subjective stress are crucial for understanding behaviour of entrepreneurs 
given the challenging and stressful environment faced by them. Despite that, the relationship 
between time and stress within the context of entrepreneurship has received little attention among 
researchers. This study develops a questionnaire for a self-assessment of temporal disposition and 
perceived stress from entrepreneurs based on existing and validated scales that measure time 
urgency, time awareness and perceived stress at the individual level. The paper starts by arguing 
that time urgency is positively related to perceived stress and that time awareness is negatively 
related to perceived stress. Furthermore, it is expected that gender moderates both relationships. 
The questionnaire was responded by 54 founders of new ventures established in Denmark and its 
findings confirm the prediction regarding time urgency by illustrating a positive correlation 
between time urgency and perceived stress. Furthermore, gender is found to moderate such 
relationship and a positive relationship is found between time urgency and time awareness. 
 
Key words: Entrepreneurship, Founders, Time Urgency, Time Awareness, Perceived Stress, New 
Ventures. 
 
Tidspræferencer og subjektiv stress er afgørende for at forstå iværksætternes adfærd i betragtning 
af det udfordrende og fulde af stressmiljøer, som de står overfor. På trods af dette har forholdet 
mellem tid og stress inden for kontekst af iværksætteri fået lidt opmærksomhed blandt forskere. 
Denne undersøgelse udviklede et spørgeskema til en selvvurdering af tidsmæssig disposition og 
opfattet stress fra iværksættere baseret på eksisterende og validerede skalaer, der måler tids 
haster, tidsbevidsthed og opfattet stress på individuelt niveau. Det forventedes, at 
tidsnødvendighed positivt ville relateres til den opfattede stress, og at tidsbevidstheden ville være 
negativt relateret til den opfattede stress. Derudover forventedes det, at køn ville moderere begge 
forhold. Spørgeskemaet blev besvaret af 54 stiftere af nye ventures etableret i Danmark, og dets 
konklusioner bekræfter det forventede resultat med hensyn til tids haster ved at illustrere en positiv 
sammenhæng mellem tids haster og opfattet stress. Derudover blev det konstateret, at køn 
modererede et sådant forhold, og der blev fundet et positivt forhold mellem tids haster og 
tidsbevidsthed.

Introduction 
In the field of management, special attention has been given towards time and its importance:  
“We can use more knowledge or more brawn. But there is no substitute for time. Everything 
requires time. It is the one truly universal condition. All work takes place in time and uses up 
time.” 
(Drucker, 2002, p. 26) 
Time is something unmodifiable which can be managed and understood in different ways by 
different people. Some people are more patient than others while some individuals acknowledge 
the passage of time in higher levels than others. With that in mind, it is important to assess the way 
we perceive it (i.e., how aware we are of time) and how we rush into it (i.e., our sense of urgency 
towards time). Such awareness and sense of urgency towards time are incorporated by the 
individuals’ temporal disposition. Temporal disposition comprises different time constructs (e.g., 
time urgency and time awareness) and there are several temporal constructs that constitute one’s 
temporal disposition, however the definition of which of these constructs form one’s temporal 
disposition remains unclear with research still emerging aiming to explore and define new 
temporal constructs (Tang et al., 2020). Temporal disposition exerts influence over numerous 
matters and scholars have researched this relationship while investigating the effects generated by 
time preferences over specific aspects such as strategic activities (Chen & Nadkarni, 2017; 
Nadkarni & Chen, 2014), team performance (Mohammed & Nadkarni, 2011) and competitive 
aggressiveness (Nadkarni et al., 2016). Time urgency is a crucial temporal construct to be 
investigated since it may affect people’s health, decision-making, and performance from teams 
and firms (Mohammed & Harrison, 2013) and time awareness - although crucial for understanding 
one’s understanding of time - remains emerging in literature and has received little attention.  
On top of its universal importance, time is especially significant for entrepreneurship and given 
that entrepreneurship is of great economic significance, the investigation of time within 
entrepreneurship represents significant importance for better understanding two important 
concepts: time and entrepreneurship. Furthermore, despite the awareness towards the economic 
benefits of entrepreneurial activities, the outcomes for entrepreneurs at the individual level remain 
unclear (Stephan, 2018). Entrepreneurs experience great sense of autonomy and fewer restrictions 
==PAGE:9==
 
 
 
 
 
9 
of time with the absence of a supervisor (Prottas & Thompson, 2006) which may give them a false 
sense of control over time. For instance, they have the possibility to prioritize meetings and 
activities that they regard as more meaningful for their business. Nonetheless, being in control of 
one’s own schedules does not eliminate the fact that time remains a limited resource and the way 
time is perceived may affect entrepreneurs’ wellness (i.e., perceived stress). The way time is 
perceived by entrepreneurs influences the way they use it and therefore affect important outcomes 
of entrepreneurship such as stress. Stress represents an important outcome for entrepreneurs, given 
that their work environment is perceived as stressful, uncertain and full of challenges. Despite high 
levels of autonomy and acquired sense of realization and achievement, being an entrepreneur has 
been regarded as one of the most stressful careers (Stephan, 2018). Some research has focused on 
investigating stress outcomes from entrepreneurs, but with a different approach than the one here 
developed. For instance, Hessels and colleagues (2017) investigate the difference between stress 
levels from self-employed in comparison to wage workers. Despite the fact that studies regard the 
environment surrounding entrepreneurs as stressful and challenging, it is important to note that the 
entrepreneurs’ behavior and personality are important characteristics that may influence their 
wellness as well as the external factors do. Individuals are not only affected by circumstances of 
the environment but also by their own attitudes and personality and here special attention is given 
to time personality. 
Gap in the literature 
Some authors suggest that literature about time remains little and slowly emerging (Lévesque & 
Stephan, 2020) and others have claimed that studies towards time comprise an extensive literature 
(Ancona et al., 2001). The existing research towards time indicates that studies focused on 
individual perspective of time remain emerging and mostly to the extent of temporal individual 
differences among group members with relation to its effect on team performance (e.g., 
Mohammed & Harrison, 2013; Mohammed & Nadkarni, 2011) instead of its effects for the 
individual. The focus on time research and its effects on stress at the individual level is especially 
important for entrepreneurship given that founder entrepreneurs face many challenges during their 
path while establishing and developing new firms in a context of high failure rate and stress (Baù 
et al., 2017). Research on time preferences from entrepreneurs remains little and is still emerging 
with some focus given towards the relationship between temporal disposition and other concepts 
such as: strategic change (West & Meyer, 2011), alertness-effectuation relationship (Sirén et al., 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
 
 
 
10 
2019) and stress (Bluedorn & Martin, 2008). The aforementioned research on time preferences 
related to stress from entrepreneurs comprises different temporal constructs (e.g., pace of work, 
polychronicity, temporal depth and temporal flexibility) than the ones explored on this present 
study (i.e., time urgency and time awareness). All things considered, the literature gap lies within 
researching time preferences of entrepreneurs at the individual level especially in relation to their 
level of hurriedness, awareness of time and perceived stress. Furthermore, the importance and need 
for investigating time and time variables for entrepreneurship has been recently highlighted among 
researchers: 
“[O]ur results illustrate the importance of time and temporal variables for understanding the 
behaviors of entrepreneurs and the contexts in which entrepreneurs work.”  
(Bluedorn & Martin, 2008, p. 19) 
“Entrepreneurship is a process, a road, a path, a career …, and regardless of how one views 
entrepreneurship, it always involves the notion of time. It is therefore time we talk about time in 
entrepreneurship.” 
 (Lévesque & Stephan, 2020, p. 178)  
Important to note that, as highlighted by Schjoedt (2009), not all self-employed individuals are 
necessarily entrepreneurs, since they might be working with inherited or acquired businesses. 
Schjoedt argues that the founders of new ventures, on the other hand, are all entrepreneurs, since 
they are the individuals who created the business. Such observation has been noted even earlier by 
Schumpeter (1949), where he recognizes that entrepreneurs are characterized by their creative 
attitude: 
“Finally, there is the joy of creating, of getting things done, or simply of exercising one’s energy 
and ingenuity.” 
(Schumpeter, 1949, p. 93) 
With that in mind and considering that the present paper aims to investigate certain characteristics 
and preferences from entrepreneurs, this present study concerns the entrepreneurs who are the 
founders of new ventures.

Research question 
With the aforementioned background and importance given in the literature towards time and 
entrepreneurship and considering that the appraisal towards time may be an important personality 
characteristic that influences the wellness outcomes for entrepreneurs, the present study aims to 
investigate the relationship between time constructs and perceived stress of entrepreneurs. That 
being said, this paper attempts to answer the following research question: 
 
How is the perceived stress from founder entrepreneurs affected by 
their time urgency and time awareness? 
 
With the purpose of answering above question this paper will first measure the level of time 
urgency, time awareness and perceived stress from the entrepreneurs through the application of a 
self-assessment questionnaire, followed by a linear regression analysis to find the correlation 
between each time construct (i.e., time urgency and time awareness) and perceived stress.

Literature review 
There is little research investigating the relationship between temporal disposition and wellness 
from entrepreneurs. Bluedorn and Martin (2008) appear, to the best of my knowledge, as the only 
study dedicated to such association, however the temporal constructs used by them differ from the 
ones applied here; they found that future temporal depth and perceived temporal flexibility are 
negatively associated to general life stress. Since there is lack of research regarding time urgency 
and time awareness’ association with perceived stress from entrepreneurs, this chapter aims to 
clarify these concepts including their importance for entrepreneurship. The literature research for 
such topics was conducted with focus on lead journals1 within the fields of entrepreneurship, 
management and psychology given that these research fields encompass the areas of investigation 
from this paper.  
Individual temporal disposition and entrepreneurship 
Tang and colleagues (2020) produced a multilevel research investigating extensive literature on 
temporal research and introduced a framework that allocates temporal constructs according to their 
concepts’ nature. According to this framework, the four generic classes of subjective time are: 
individual temporal disposition, collective temporal disposition, individual temporal state and 
collective temporal state. Given that this paper focuses on time preferences on the individual level, 
attention is here given to its respective individuals classes of time (i.e., individual temporal 
disposition). According to Tang and colleagues (2020), individual temporal state represents 
constructs that are transitory and adaptable according to situational consequences representing how 
time is perceived through different experiences (e.g., “perceived time pressure”). In contrast to 
temporal state and also referred to as “temporal personality”, individual temporal disposition 
comprises various temporal constructs that are more stable and enduring (e.g., pacing style and 
time urgency) which are unique to each individual representing how they feel, use, perceive and 
value time (Chen & Nadkarni, 2017; Tang et al., 2020). According to Tang and colleagues (2020) 
there are at least eight temporal constructs within the scope of individual temporal disposition. 
This study focuses on two important temporal constructs that assess both the entrepreneurs’ 
perception and behavior towards time: time urgency and time awareness. On the one hand, time

urgency (i.e., level of concern towards time that generates the feeling of chronical hurriedness 
from individuals) represents one’s behavior towards time. On the other hand, time awareness (i.e., 
the understanding of time as a finite resource) comprise people’s perception of time. The 
importance of time urgency has been recently noted by Chen and Nadkarni (2017). On their 
research, they select time urgency as one crucial time construct from CEO’s temporal disposition 
in order to examine their influence on corporate entrepreneurship. Time urgency has also been 
explored by other scholars (e.g., Mohammed & Nadkarni, 2011) where it was found to be related 
to other temporal constructs as well as to team performance. Time awareness has received little 
attention to its importance for entrepreneurs and, to the best of my knowledge, no research 
regarding it has been conducted within the entrepreneurship field. 
Time urgency  
Also referred to as “individual hurriedness” (Jansen & Kristof-Brown, 2005; Shipp et al., 2009), 
time urgency represents the degree to which individuals are concerned about passage of time and 
to what extent they feel chronically hurried (Landy et al., 1991). It comprises a behavioral 
propensity that is related to the pace of actions in the present time (Shipp et al., 2009). Individuals 
with high time urgency, also known as “time urgent individuals” (Briker et al., 2020; Waller et al., 
1999), are mentally caught up in the passage of time and feel chronically rushed. Higher levels of 
time urgency were seen to be associated with health outcomes (Conte et al., 1998; Landy et al., 
1991). On the other hand, time-patient individuals are inclined to be more relaxed towards time, 
often underrating the passage of time and executing their tasks with a slower and unhurried 
approach (Briker et al., 2020). Individual differences on time urgency characteristics from 
entrepreneurs tend to result in different prioritization for resource allocation (Zahra & Wright, 
2011), however, research about individual preferences of time urgency from entrepreneurs remains 
little. The speed of work is crucial for many possible advantages for a business, such as market 
opportunities, staying ahead of competition and strategy setting (Chen & Nadkarni, 2017) which 
is essential for entrepreneurs since they may face fast-changing environments (McKelvie et al., 
2018) and many unknown variables regarding their firms given the fact that they start it from 
scratch.

Time awareness 
Likewise referred to as “temporal awareness”, time awareness represents the understanding and 
interpretation of time as being a finite resource that has to be accounted for (Aeon & Aguinis, 
2017). Time awareness refers to the fact that the number of hours in a day is limited and therefore 
activities are characterized by a temporal cost. With that in mind, likely outcome behaviors from 
time awareness are time tracking and temporal accounting (Aeon & Aguinis, 2017). Individuals 
with high temporal awareness perceive their time as a nonrenewable asset and consequently they 
are more realistic towards the amount of time necessary to complete their tasks. For instance, 
people with high time awareness would likely ask themselves: “How much time would it cost me 
to read this manuscript?”. On the other hand, individuals with low time awareness have their 
capability of effective time management weakened due to the fact that they tend to allocate more 
or less time than necessary for the completion of tasks. Given the aforementioned importance of 
performance from entrepreneurs to guarantee the success of their business it is of most importance 
to acquire high temporal awareness in order to plan activities with a realistic duration for 
completion (Aeon & Aguinis, 2017). 
Perceived stress and entrepreneurship 
Another segment of investigation explored on the present study is the degree to which 
entrepreneurs perceive stress. Entrepreneurs have many responsibilities (e.g., developing and 
following a business plan), challenges (e.g., gathering capital investment and recognizing 
opportunities) and risks (e.g., failure from a product launch). Such challenging work environment 
makes it crucial to be able to cope with stress given that such demand comprised by 
responsibilities, risks and challenges generates a more stressful environment. In other words, stress 
levels indicate the aspect of how entrepreneurs are coping with their tasks throughout their 
entrepreneurial journey. Investigating perceived stress from entrepreneurs helps understanding one 
crucial aspect of the challenges they face and how they perceive the entrepreneurial journey while 
having to cope with such challenges. 
Perceived stress  
Dr. Sheldon Cohen has extensively researched stress (e.g., Cohen et al., 2014; Cohen & Hoberman, 
1983; Cohen & Wills, 1985) while developing a 10-item perceived stress scale (Cohen & 
Williamson, 1988) that is applied worldwide in several institutions such as health clinics and state 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
 
 
15 
departments (e.g., Arbejdsmedicin, 2020; Department, 2020). Cohen and colleagues (1997) 
suggest that stress is split into three different facets: environmental, psychological and biological. 
They argue that environmental stress comprises events that exceed the capability of an individual 
to cope with given situations, where the psychological part of stress deals with the subjective 
appraisal from the individual in regard to their capability to perform under the given circumstances 
from these events and, finally, the biological stress represents the physiological symptoms 
presented given both psychological and physical demanding circumstances. More specifically, 
certain environmental demands are perceived in particular ways that generate biological stress 
responses. According to their research, the focus and understanding on the perception of stressful 
events improves the capacity for confronting and adapting to such challenging demands which 
then can generate beneficial outcomes for the individual. Such comprehension represents a 
significant advantage for the entrepreneurial field, given that entrepreneurs tend to face stressful 
situations such as the ones pointed out by Baron and colleagues (2016): their work environment is 
susceptible to rapid change, high levels of risk, they have responsibility for their firms and frequent 
experience financial constraints. Furthermore, Baron and colleagues (2016) also draw attention to 
other important stressful situations within entrepreneurship: entrepreneurs are the ones who 
develop the business plan which is sometimes followed by pitch competitions where they face 
high pressure environments in order to perform well and win the prizes displayed; entrepreneurs 
face the possibility of business failure or poor performance and that could terminate their current 
work position without further notice.

Hypotheses 
Entrepreneurs’ time urgency and perceived stress 
Time urgency represents the level of hurriedness perceived by an individual and higher levels of 
time urgency indicate that people are acutely concerned with the passage of time (Landy et al., 
1991). On the one hand, time urgency has been perceived as an advantage for CEOs and top 
management teams while being found positively related to CEO’s temporal leadership (i.e., the 
way they manage the temporal aspects from activities within the top management team) (Chen & 
Nadkarni, 2017). The referred authors argue that time-urgent leaders motivate their team members 
for the completion of tasks by positioning time as a focal point throughout the activities in the 
team. Based on this point of view, it can be argued therefore that time urgent individuals may feel 
more motivated, given that such motivation passed to the team may also represent a motivation for 
the entrepreneurs themselves. Such motivation may interfere on their levels of stress by lowering 
it. On the other hand, time urgency has been regarded as one basic element from the so called 
“problematic lifestyle” of Type A behavior pattern, which characterizes a relatively2 more 
competitive and stressful approach to life that has been linked to coronary heart disease (Landy et 
al., 1991). With the two contrasting point of views aforementioned, two scenarios related to time 
urgency and perceived stress of entrepreneurs emerge: (1) time urgency may decrease perceived 
stress of entrepreneurs; (2) time urgency may increase the perceived stress of entrepreneurs. 
Taking into account that type A behavior pattern is characterized by hurriedness and it has been 
related to stress, I expect that the second scenario is more relatable to the relationship between 
entrepreneurs’ individual time urgency and their levels of perceived stress. Therefore, the 
following is expected: 
Hypothesis 1: Entrepreneurs’ levels of time urgency will be positively correlated with their levels 
of perceived stress. 
Entrepreneurs’ time awareness and perceived stress 
Some individuals are more conscious about time than others, in such a way that they are more 
aware of its passage which leads them to consider time as a more valuable resource while planning

17 
or executing activities (Aeon & Aguinis, 2017). On the one hand, individuals that are more 
concerned about time may perceive this time awareness as a tension during periods of higher 
demands and therefore become more inclined to experience physical and psychological symptoms 
associated with the pressure perceived by them (Landy et al., 1991). On the other hand, it is 
suggested that these individuals with greater awareness of time may be capable of planning their 
tasks and projects more carefully while perceiving time as something to be accounted for, and 
therefore budgeted with a more realistic approach (Aeon & Aguinis, 2017). The aforementioned 
opposing points of view lead to the consideration of two different contexts: (1) tension generated 
by high levels of time awareness; (2) capability of more accurate planning of tasks derived from 
high levels of time awareness. Based on this second point of view, I predict that such cautious 
planning of tasks would decrease the level of stress from entrepreneurs, which goes in line 
with Bluedorn and Martin (2008) who recognized that planning may be a decisive factor towards 
reducing life stress from entrepreneurs. It can also be argued that stressful periods perceived by 
entrepreneurs (i.e., periods of higher demands) can be planned in advance with the consideration 
of time as a constraint, where the pressure perceived by the individuals would decrease during 
course of these “peak” periods. Entrepreneurs as the founders of firms do not report to a supervisor, 
consequently they have control over their own time allocation and schedule which suggests that a 
considerable part of these peak periods where there are high demands of tasks are managed by the 
entrepreneurs themselves. With consideration of the arguments here proposed, the following 
assumption is therefore developed: 
Hypothesis 2: Entrepreneur’ levels of time awareness will be negatively correlated to 
their levels of perceived stress. 
The moderating role of gender 
It has been noted that although female and male might experience similar levels of stress, the 
effects of their experience may differ between men and women (Karatepe et al., 2006). For 
instance, it has been argued that men perceive stressful situations with more confidence than 
women (Karatepe et al., 2006) which may indicate a better management of stress from men even 
with increased levels of time urgency. Therefore, the following hypotheses are expected: 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
 
 
18 
Hypothesis 3: The positive relationship between time urgency and perceived stress is 
stronger among female than male entrepreneurs. 
Hypothesis 4: The negative relationship between time awareness and perceived 
stress is stronger among female than male entrepreneurs.

Methodology 
Data collection  
The data for this study was collected in 2020 through an online questionnaire3 filled by the 
founders of Danish new ventures. The data collection started on the 12th of May and ended on the 
9th of June, covering a duration of 4 weeks. The first e-mail invitation to the questionnaire included 
a message presenting the purpose of the study and stressing the need for the respondent to be a 
founder in order to complete the questionnaire. As done in previous research (Bluedorn & Martin, 
2008; Srivastava et al., 2001) a follow-up invitation was forwarded via e-mail 2 weeks after the 
first invitation to the respondents who had not yet filled the survey. Initially, prior to the email 
invitations, phone calls were conducted4 aiming to improve the response rate while assessing the 
interest of the potential participants on the research conducted. According to the phone 
conversations, the response rate may have been affected by the pandemic and crisis caused by the 
COVID-19. One of the founders contacted by phone informed that he was closing his firm due to 
COVID-19, where three other respondents reported that they were busy handling issues faced by 
their ventures that were caused by the crisis.  
The research design prior to the data collection consisted of identifying feasible firms with contact 
information available and they were selected from the Market Profile database provided by 
Bisnode. Formerly known as Greens Online, Bisnode is a European provider of smart data and 
data analytics and the Market Profile database provides information regarding Danish companies. 
Such information includes, among others, company status, year of foundation, number of 
employees, industry and contact information. Bisnode was selected as data source for this research 
given 2 reasons: (1) the search tool from Bisnode is licensed by DTU – Technical University of 
Denmark – which granted access for data gathering (DTU, 2020); (2) Bisnode presents credibility 
of its information given the fact that it is licensed by other institutions (e.g., CBS, 2020; Library, 
2020). 
To ensure a representative sample, the following filters were simultaneously applied: (a) active 
companies - in order to secure that the firms were operating by the time the survey was running; 
(b) firms established throughout the past 7 years - as previously adopted (Dai et al., 2019) in

(n = 35; 65%), have an average existence of 5.4 years (SD = 1.2; Range: 2013 to 2019), which 
represents a representative and random sample given that the 663 ventures contacted have an 
average existence of 5.9 years out of which 61% were from industry M and 39% were from 
industry M.  
The survey was forwarded to the e-mail addresses available on Bisnode’s Market Profile. The 
survey was sent to only one person from each firm, given 2 reasons: (1) the research aims to 
investigate individual measures; (2) there was one contact available per firm on the data source 
platform.  
Questionnaire  
The questionnaire is a self-assessment instrument where the responses were anonymously 
collected, and it was validated with two respondents prior to being sent to the participants. Both 
respondents validated the questionnaire and did not find any difficulties with answering the 
questions which indicates that the questionnaire was understandable, and no adjustments were 
judged necessary. The questionnaire was implemented and forwarded through Survey Monkey 
which is an online survey platform with more than 17 million active users as of 2020 (Monkey, 
2020). The choice for the platform was based on 2 reasons: (1) possibility to automate reminders 
eliminating the need for manual follow-up of the response rate; (2) credibility based on its high 
number of users. 
Measures 
The questionnaire follows a quantitative approach, given several advantages it presents for the 
present research: (1) it allows precision for the specification of dependent and independent 
variables to be investigated; (2) it allows objective conclusions for the hypothesis testing by 
determining causality relationships between variables; (3) it minimizes the subjectivity of 
judgement; (4) it facilitates the replicability of the study. 
 
6 Other roles informed by the respondents are CFO, CTO and director.  
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
 
 
22 
A total of 6 variables was measured throughout the questionnaire and implemented in the analysis. 
There is a total of 3 different types of measures: (1) multiple choice where the respondent is faced 
with multiple possibilities of an answer and can only choose one option as the answer - e.g. the 
construct “gender” comprised 3 options including ”male”, ”female” or ”other”; (2) single text box, 
where the respondent inputs an answer – e.g. typing age in numbers for the variable “age”; (3) 
Standardized 5-point Likert scales were applied for all the variables measured by scales in line 
with previous studies (e.g., Baron et al., 2016; Wach et al., 2020). 
The validity of the scales applied in quantitative research within the field of entrepreneurship is 
critical in order to facilitate the replication for research findings (Maula & Stam, 2019). Taken that 
into consideration, all the scales applied on the survey for this study were taken from previous 
studies where each scale was validated. Appendix B illustrates the questionnaire including all the 
scales applied. Furthermore, the Cronbach Alpha was calculated as a reliability coefficient for each 
variable containing scales, in line with previous studies as it has been extensively applied in the 
entrepreneurship field of research  (e.g., Baron et al., 2016; Hmieleski & Corbett, 2008; Schjoedt, 
2009). The value of 0.60 and higher has been previously accepted as good reliability of scales for 
studies regarding temporal preferences and decision making from entrepreneurs (e.g., Sirén et al., 
2019). 
Variables 
The variables used in the analysis are described below. All the reverse scored questions are 
indicated on each respective question presented in Appendix B. 
Dependent variable: perceived stress 
Perceived stress was assessed through a 10-item scale that measures the level to which the 
entrepreneur perceives life situations as stressful. The 10-item scale “Perceived Stress Scale” was 
developed by Cohen & Williamson (1988) and recent applied in research about entrepreneurs’ 
stress (Baron et al., 2016). The scale is widely accepted and it is applied in many different sectors 
such as health clinics and state departments in Denmark and worldwide (Arbejdsmedicin, 2020; 
Department, 2020). According to previous research (Baron et al., 2016) the responses were 
averaged individually to create an overall score for the scale. Cronbach Alpha for perceived stress 
is 0.85 and the scale applied in the questionnaire for the assessment of the variable perceived stress 
is displayed on Appendix B, question Q9.  
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
 
 
23 
Explanatory variables: time constructs 
There are two explanatory variables from the hypotheses which are applied in two different 
models: time urgency for hypothesis 1 and time awareness for hypothesis 2. 
Time urgency is assessed through the six-item scale utilized by (Mohammed & Nadkarni, 2011) 
that captures the level of hurriedness from the respondent while performing activities inside and 
outside the work environment. The scale is adapted from Landy and colleagues (1991) and other 
variations from it were applied in prior research (e.g., Chen & Nadkarni, 2017; Jansen & Kristof-
Brown, 2005). The Cronbach Alpha for this measure is 0.59 and the scale for the variable time 
urgency is displayed on Appendix B, question Q7. 
Time awareness measures the awareness of time from the entrepreneur through adapting the 
“Awareness of Time Use” scale from Schriber & Gutek (1987) by changing the referred from each 
sentence to the entrepreneur instead of the organization – therefore words “people” were replaced 
by “I”. Cronbach Alpha for time awareness is 0.58 and the question containing the scale used for 
the variable time awareness can be found of Appendix B, question Q8. 
Control variables: gender, age and children 
Gender was included in line with previous studies that related entrepreneurs’ gender with some of 
the variables present on this research, such as temporal depth and stress (e.g., Bluedorn & Martin, 
2008). Furthermore, in relation to other variables here assessed, it has been reported that women 
generally experience lower levels of job satisfaction than men (Jansen & Kristof-Brown, 2005). 
The options for the respondent’s choice of gender are illustrated in the Appendix B, question Q3. 
Age is present in previous studies investigating entrepreneurs’ preferences of time in relation to 
their wellness (e.g., Bluedorn & Martin, 2008; Bradley & Roberts, 2004). For instance, lower 
levels of stress were reported by older entrepreneurs (Bluedorn & Martin, 2008). The variable is 
indicated in years and represents the age of the respondent. For the question regarding gender 
please refer to Appendix B, question Q4. 
Children measures the presence of children younger than 18 years old living in the same household 
as the entrepreneur, and it has been previously included in the literature in order to investigate the 
relationship between the number of children and entrepreneurs’ stress (Hessels et al., 2017). The 
variable is binary coded ”1” indicating presence of children and ”0” where there are no children 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
 
 
 
24 
in the same household as the respondent. The question regarding the variable children can be found 
in Appendix B, question Q5. 
Analytic approach 
Once all the data was gathered through the questionnaire by its ending date, the relationships 
between the explored variables were investigated through statistical models with the addition of 
logical reasoning for the choice of the control variables. The present analysis aims to find the 
relationship between temporal constructs and wellness from the respondents which was 
investigated with the application of multiple linear regression models through SAS software. 
Regression analysis was chosen given two main reasons; (1) the possibility of providing the 
relationship between dependent and independent variables which are the object of this study; (2) 
the validity of the tool, given that linear regression models have recently been applied in the 
literature to explain possible causes, for instance, to the stress of entrepreneurs (e.g., Baron et al., 
2016).  
Boundary conditions 
The amount of 54 observations on this study may represent a limitation for linear regressions, 
given that the more data available, the more may be explained through a – possible – closer to 
normality distribution of the data. Therefore, not all the variables were included in the model and 
with that in mind, logical reasoning was applied during the methodology development in order to 
select the most suitable variables. Nevertheless, the effectiveness of liner regression for a relatively 
small number of observations has been demonstrated through previous studies which have applied 
linear regression models with the similar condition of around 50 observations. Furthermore, the 
context of the COVID-19 may have lowered the response rate as it has been informed by the initial 
phone calls conducted that COVID-19 generated more activities in order to cope with the 
challenges brought by it as well as it was the cause of some business being shut down due to its 
economic consequences.

Before running the models, important to note that the variables were coded accordingly, with the 
consideration of reverse scores. The regression models were then constructed in order to test the 
hypotheses, therefore perceived stress was placed as dependent variable and the time constructs

Discussion 
Time personality and life matters such as stress are regarded important constructs for entrepreneurs 
and so is the relationship among them (Bluedorn & Martin, 2008). The preference for doing things 
fast may interfere in important decisions such as choosing the best time for new product launches 
and investments. These decisions generate consequences for the ventures and therefore may affect 
the levels of stress perceived by the entrepreneurs. On top of that, the level of hurriedness has also 
been regarded to be related to health outcomes such as coronary heart disease (Landy et al., 1991) 
and it may generate impulsive decision making and then increase error and inefficient use of 
resources (Mohammed & Harrison, 2013). The effect on decision making represents strong 
consequences for the entrepreneur given that their decisions directly affect the course of their 
ventures and in case wrong decisions are in place they face the risk of failure. With that in mind, 
job pressure and stress have been recognized as strong challenges for owners of ventures which 
occurs  due to their perceived pressure for “working hard” in light of high failure rates within small 
ventures (Prottas & Thompson, 2006). Nevertheless, the literature concerning stress of 
entrepreneurs is scarce and presents mixed results (Hessels et al., 2017). On the one hand, studies 
have indicated that entrepreneurs experience high levels of stress in part due to the importance of 
their decisions as well as the high levels of responsibility faced by them (Adebowale, 1994; Cardon 
& Patel, 2015). On the other hand, researchers have recently reported that founder entrepreneurs 
experience low levels of stress in association to high psychological capital (i.e., self-efficacy, 
optimism, hope and resilience) ( Baron et al., 2016). 
The present study has found similar results as the ones reported by Baron and colleagues (2016) 
indicating that the founder entrepreneurs perceive low levels of stress. That being said, this present 
study investigated relationships between temporal constructs and perceived stress from founders 
of new ventures to argue whether specific time constructs (i.e., time urgency and time awareness) 
play an important role in relation to the increase on the stress levels experienced by entrepreneurs. 
The first hypothesis here investigated which suggested that higher levels of time urgency result in 
higher levels of perceived stress has been supported by the positive correlation found between time 
urgency and perceived stress from entrepreneurs. This indicates that the notion of time as a finite 
resource leads to higher levels of hurriedness. Del Pino and colleagues (2005) found that it is 
possible to control time urgency and therefore reduce it with psychological treatment, for instance. 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
 
 
 
32 
The positive correlation here found between time urgency and perceived stress from entrepreneurs 
indicates that it might be advantageous for founder entrepreneurs to be aware of their time urgency 
and therefore try to reduce it in order to better cope with stress.  
Although hypothesis 1 was supported, hypothesis 2 was rejected. However, the positive correlation 
found between time urgency and time awareness may explain part of the reason why there was no 
support found for hypothesis 2. Hypothesis 2 suggests that high time awareness would be 
associated with lower levels of perceived stress partially based on the notion that time awareness 
generates more careful planning of time and tasks due to the understanding of time as a finite 
resource. However, the relationship found between time awareness and time urgency indicates that 
the notion of time as limited resource leads to increased levels of hurriedness which suggests that 
high levels of time awareness may result in entrepreneurs worrying about the restraint regarding 
the amount of time available and then experiencing more stress from such perception. 
Curiously, the moderation of gender on the relationship between time urgency and perceived stress 
occurred in the opposite way from what was expected; the predicted effect appears stronger for 
male entrepreneurs instead of for female entrepreneurs (i.e., it was found that women perceive 
lower levels of stress for higher levels of time urgency and men perceive higher levels of stress 
with higher levels of time urgency). It has been reported that women are more likely to associate 
possible causes for stress with economic issues where men are more inclined to relate work as a 
source of stress (APA, 2012). Given that the time urgency scale applied on the questionnaire for 
this study comprises some focus on tasks performed at work through the sentence “I tend to be 
quick and energetic at work”, it may be the case that the time urgency perceived by men is more 
related to tasks from work and the time urgency from women may be originated by other 
circumstances that are not work related. That being said, perceived stress among women and men 
may be affected in different ways by their time urgency due to the fact that there are different 
aspects originating such time urgency among the two groups. Furthermore, it has been reported 
that men are more likely to work over time than women (Xu, Xiao et al., 2015) which may explain 
such interaction effect between gender and time urgency on perceived stress. For instance, if men 
tend to work more, it may lead to more tasks to be completed, which then would increase their 
time urgency and perceived stress at the same time. At the same time, if women tend to work less 
it may mean that they are performing a relatively lower number of tasks which would lead to lower 
levels of perceived stress even with higher sense of time urgency. Finally, the positive and almost 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
 
 
 
33 
marginal relationship found between children and time urgency may also indicate a possible 
explanation for the moderation of gender in the relationship between urgency and perceived stress 
for men and women. It may be the case that women spend more time with their children which 
then leads to a higher sense of hurriedness for the completion of tasks at work in order to get home 
the earliest possible but with a decrease on their perceived stress because of possible positive 
feelings caused by the presence of children. A triple way interaction with children, gender and 
urgency was applied in order to investigate such possible explanation but no interaction was found. 
Nonetheless, there is the possibility that such triple interaction was not found due to a relatively 
low number of responses to the questionnaire.

Limitations and future research 
 
The present study comprises several limitations, among them: location and the context of COVID-
19. Given that most of the participants have Danish nationality and that the ventures were 
established in Denmark, there is the possibility that cultural norms influence the time preferences 
of the entrepreneurs that participated on the study. Finally, Denmark has been recognized as the 
“happiest country” worldwide which may also exert influence on the low levels of stress perceived 
by the participants. On the other hand, the context of COVID-19 may have affected the levels of 
perceived stress from the participants given that some people were forced to adapt their way of 
working, for instance, by transforming their homes into offices or by having online meetings 
instead of meeting their stakeholders in person. Nonetheless, such circumstances create a 
possibility for the replication of this study in two wways: (1) on a second occasion where the 
challenges faced by COVID-19 are better managed providing then less impact and challenges to 
the businesses which may lead to higher number of respondents and possible different outcomes; 
(2) on a different location than Denmark where cultural norms may differ. 
Regarding future research, there is also the possibility to apply different analytic models and 
approaches to the data here explored and investigate other outcomes, for instance which scenarios 
(i.e., combinations of factors such as temporal preferences and job characteristics) would most 
influence stress and work outcomes. Considering the fact that this study focus on the individual 
entrepreneur, future research could extend this study by applying the questionnaire within groups 
of founder entrepreneurs and therefore investigate how individual temporal disposition from 
founders may be influenced by other team members’ behavior towards time. Furthermore, since 
this study measured self-reported outcomes, I acknowledge that it may be insightful to conduct the 
research where the respondants fill the questionnaire in relation to other team members’ behavior. 
Nonetheless, there are possible extensions for the present study also concerning the individual 
level. For instance, it may be interesting to investigate polychronicity (i.e., preference for 
performing several tasks simultaneously) as another temporal construct in addition to the nature 
of the tasks performed by the entrepreneur and how it may affect work satisfaction. I also 
recommend to investigate two other chraracteristics that may influence time urgency: (1) 
procrastination; (2) presence of full time job “on the side” and the amount of time spent on either 
the job and/or the venture.

Theoretical contributions and practical implications 
This study has, to the best of my knowledge, pioneered the finding of the positive correlation 
between time urgency and perceived stress from founder entrepreneurs. This supports 
entrepreneurship research by filling the gap in the literature regarding the need for further 
investigation of temporal constructs of entrepreneurs. Additionally, it generates the encouragement 
to investigate the relationship of temporal disposition with wellness from entrepreneurs. 
In addition, and also to the best of my knowledge, the questionnaire developed in this study is 
unique and has not been applied in previous research, although it was built based on existing scales. 
The questionnaire can be applied in different countries in order to explore possible new findings 
related to different contexts and habits from different regions and cultures.

Conclusion 
The results provided by this study demonstrate the importance of temporal constructs in 
understanding part of the behaviour of entrepreneurs and its reflections on their physical and 
mental wellness (i.e., perceived stress). In response to the research question which concerns the 
relationship between time urgency and perceived stress and between time awareness and perceived 
stress from entrepreneurs, the analysis here developed demonstrates that individual hurriedness 
affects levels of perceived stress for founder entrepreneurs which indicates that lower levels of 
time urgency are beneficial for them. Given the limitation faced by this paper by choosing and 
exploring specific time constructs and considering that there are other numerous characteristics of 
time personality that can also be taken into account when building one’s temporal disposition, 
there are possible extensions to the present study by finding new relationships among variables 
that were not here considered. Furthermore, given the limitation imposed by the choice of the 
location of the study, it is also possible to find different relationships within distinct circumstances 
such as different countries where the context may differ given possible culture influences on 
behaviour towards time and perceived stress. 
Regarding the learning objectives proposed at the beginning of this paper, the problem identified 
in the entrepreneurship field of research lies with regards to the importance of temporal disposition 
from entrepreneurs and its reflections on their mental and physical health which faces a gap in the 
literature in relation to such matters. With that in mind, perceived stress has been regarded as an 
important outcome from entrepreneurship given the importance it presents for entrepreneurship 
especially considering the attention given to it on the existing emerging literature regarding 
entrepreneurs. That being said, a literature review was built illustrating the existing literature on 
temporal disposition, perceived stress and entrepreneurship. The relevant data collection method 
capable of providing input regarding temporal disposition for analysis of the question comprised 
a quantitative approach through the development and application of a self-assessment 
questionnaire and linear regression was applied as a relevant tool for the analysis of the research 
question. The results were then arranged in a comprehensive manner which makes it possible for 
the experiment to be replicated. Finally, the outcome identified (i.e., perceived stress) has been 
found related to one temporal disposition construct (i.e., time urgency). That being said, all the 
learning objectives presented at the beginning of this study have been satisfied.
