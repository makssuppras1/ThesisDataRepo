Summary
This project concerns the design of a protection structure, designed to protect subsea
pipelines against dropped objects. The maximum impact energy it was required to with-
stand was 300 kJ, and the dropped object was deﬁned as a oﬀshore shipping container. It
also needed to be designed to withstand all other loads during its lifetime, like transport
loads, installation loads, in-place loads and recovery loads. Further the structure was
required to be created in metal, and have a maximum mass of 40 ton.
First task was to identify a method for dissipating the kinetic energy from the impact.
After investigating current solution used in the oﬀshore industry and also other indus-
try’s solutions for dissipating impact energy, seven energy dissipation system concepts was
created. The seven concepts had diﬀerent ways of dissipating the energy, like through de-
formation or utilizing water to absorb it. After comparing and assessing the concepts,
two was chosen for further analysis. The two solutions both had the same basic principle,
where a deformation element was placed on top of support structure. If the structure
was hit by a dropped object the idea is that all the kinetic energy would dissipate by
deforming only the element on top and not the support structure below. This would sim-
plify the support structure and also making the structure less complex. The two solutions
was analyzed using ANSYS Explicit Dynamics, against three diﬀerent impact cases. The
solution called Corrugated Multi-Panel was considered to be the best suited solution for
further work, due to the possibility of adding elements on top of each other to increase
the impact energy it could handle, and also because it preformed in a more predictable
way during the ANSYS analysis.
Further a model of the structure, except the deformation zone, was built in STAAD.Pro.
All loads and load combination during the structure lifetime was identiﬁed and calculated
using NORSOK and DNV standards. The load combinations was then inserted onto a
STAAD.Pro model, and a structural analysis was done. The model passed the structural
analysis in STAAD.Pro, and then the geometry was rebuilt in Autodesk Inventor. The
deformation zone was placed on top of the support structure, and other details was in-
serted in the the CAD model. The total mass of the protection structure ended up on
36428.7 kg, under the limit of 40 ton deﬁned in the project description.
The ﬁnished designed protection structure was then compared to two similar solutions
Subsea 7 uses today. With the conclusion of although the Energy Dissipation System for
this project is in it self a more complex part than just a pipe, the total complexity of the
structure is less than for the Valhall and Martin Linge structures due to their deformation
elements are placed in the columns.

1.1
Project Description
The scope of this report is design of a protection structure for subsea pipelines and
umbilicals, to protect against dropped objects, visualized in ﬁg. 1.1. The design criteria
and project requirements have been deﬁned by Subsea 7. The main focus of the project
will be to design a structure that can withstand a signiﬁcantly higher accidental load then
current solutions. It will also need to be designed to cope with diﬀerent load cases during
its lifetime, like transport loads, installation loads, in-place loads and recovery loads. The
structure has a weight limit, due to restrictions of the lifting capacity of the installation
crane.
Figure 1.1:
Illustration of the pipe with protection structure and falling object
[Molven, 2019a]
The structure have to protect the pipeline against any potential destructive accidental
loads. Accidental load was in this case deﬁned as a dropped object load, and will not
consider trawling loads or anchor drop loads.

Chapter 1. Introduction
The weight limit restricts the possibility to base the design on pure strength and stiﬀness,
and more a dynamic design will be used. Elements can be inserted into the structure to
dissipate the kinetic energy from the impact object. Due to the importance of this single
element to the overall success of the protection structure it have been given a higher fo-
cus. Diﬀerent energy dissipation method will be designed, and some will be chosen to be
analyzed in ANSYS. The results for ANSYS analysis will be used to compare the diﬀerent
solutions against each other. The best suited solution will be chosen to be part of the ﬁnal
structure. The rest of the structure will be designed using a structural analysis software
called STAAD.Pro and the loads deﬁned and calculated by use of NORSOK and DNV
standards. Then a CAD model of the entire structure will be created in Autodesk Inven-

by Subsea 7, in use today.
1.2
Background
Most oﬀshore petroleum production systems are built up in a similar way. They consist of
a production platform, several wells stationed on the seabed and umbilicals and pipelines
to transport the oil and gas from the wells to the platform for processing, illustration
can be seen in ﬁgure 1.2. A subsea well with its associated equipment is called a subsea
production system, or SPS, and it is where the well head comes up through the ground
from the petroleum reservoir below. Due to the large consequences of losing control of
a subsea well, a set of valves are placed on the well head to be able to shut is down if
necessary. To be able to control these valves from the platform a signal and hydraulic
cable, called an umbilical, needs to run from the platform down to the SPS. The ﬂow of
oil and gas from the well gets transported to the platform in pipes laid on the seabed. It
is crucial to protect the pipeline and umbilical all the way form the SPS to the platform.
If the umbilical is damaged the signal and hydraulic pressure to the SPS can be lost and
the system can no longer be controlled. If the pipe gets damage, it might rupture and oil
and gas may ﬂow out into the ocean.
The possible danger the pipe and umbilical needs protection from is impact from trawl-
ing nets, anchor drop and other dropped objects from the surface. The most common
method of protecting pipes and umbilicals is with rock-dumping or with a combination
of rock-dumping and GRP covers. Rock-dumping is just covering the pipes with a layer
of rocks and the GRP cover is a structure made in GRP placed over the pipe to protect it.
Close to platform however, the risk of a dropped object is higher, due to all the ac-
tivities on the platform. The platform constantly needs supplies lifted on-board from
supply vessels. Any of these lifts may fail and the cargo is dropped into the ocean, where
there is a chance of it hitting a pipes. The impact of a object dropped from the platform
may be to high to use GRP covers and close to the platform there is usually not allowed to
use rock-dumping, see ﬁgure 1.2. Under these circumstances, where conventional pipeline
protection can not be used, specialized solutions have to be designed. Around the plat-
form there is a 500 meter restriction zone against trawling, so trawling will not be an
issue close to the platform.
The platform type referred to in this project is a jacket platform. A jacket is a steel truss
structure used as foundation for a platform, illustrated in ﬁgure 1.2.

Weight Limitation
All platforms have a safety zone around it, where there is restrictions to all vessel move-
ments. This is due to the risk of a vessel losing control and colliding into the platform.
The safety zone makes it impossible to position the installation vessel directly above the
installation zone when installing the structure, due to it being inside the safety zone. In-

crane at a distance for the installation. The problem when using the crane at a distance
is at the total lifting capacity of the crane gets lower the longer away from its center the
load is. A crane may have a maximum lifting capacity of 120 ton at 10 meters distance
from its center, but then only have 40 ton at 25 meters.

• Lift of the structure through the splash zone
• Lift of the structure from fully submerged to the seabed
In-Place Phase
• Aﬀect of currents on the structure
• Aﬀect of earthquake on the structure
• Accidental dropped object hitting the structure and remaining on
Recovery Phase
• Recovery lift, with added marine growth

Chapter 4
Energy Dissipation System
One of the main criteria for the design of the structure is weight, due to the limit of the
crane described in section 2.3 and the overall mobility of the structure during installation.
To be able to design a lighter structure that still can withstand the required impact energy,
a method of channelling the kinetic energy away from the pipe is needed to be deﬁned.
This method needs to dissipate all or most of the kinetic energy from the accidental
impact in an eﬀective and reliable way. Four diﬀerent categories have been deﬁned based
on methods of energy dissipation. In the category Statically Based, all the solutions
are designed to deﬂect the dropped object away from the pipeline. The Fluid Based
design uses the energy to move the surrounding seawater. Friction Based is designed
to be pushed into the soil and the energy dissipates to overcome the friction between the
soil and the structure. The fourth category is Deformation Based, where the energy is
used to deform a speciﬁc part of the structure. The four categories are displayed in the
solution chart shown below in ﬁgure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: Solution Chart for Energy Dissipation Systems
A total of seven concepts was designed based on solutions used in other industries, such
as the railway industry and the space industry.
Each concept will be described and
evaluated, and the concepts which are deemed to most likely too succeed will be analyzed
in the next chapter.

Chapter 4. Energy Dissipation System
4.1
Statically Based
Statically Based solutions are solutions which simply directs the energy away from the
pipeline, instead of absorbing it. If an object hits the structure the main idea is that
most of the energy gets channeled away from the structure, due to the object hitting the
structure at a steep angel. The object then bounces of the structure and lands on the
seabed next to the structure. The steep angel of the roof beams only absorbs parts of the
impact force. See ﬁgure 4.2. The solutions are built up of a strong frame, to be able to
withstand the impact force and some of the kinetic energy.
Figure 4.2: Illustration of the principle for Statically Based concepts [Molven, 2019a]
4.1.1
Rounded Shape
The concept have semi-circular shaped frame for structural strength. Due to the shape
the structure can be designed very compact, and use less space around the pipes. It also
uses a steep impact angle to redirect the dropped object. It will however have challenges
if the object hits the structure straight at the top, where the structure is less angle.
Figure 4.3: Illustration of the Rounded Frame [Molven, 2019a]

4.1.2
A-Frame
The A-Frame has a simple design based on two beams supporting each other at an angle
and a horizontal beam between them for more support. The advantage of this design is
that the angle of the roof can be designed as steep as desired, with the only disadvantage
of the structure height and weight. A steeper angled roof gives a steeper impact angle
of the dropped object which leads to a smaller amount of the energy transferred into the
structure.
Figure 4.4: Illustration of the Shock Absorber in use [Molven, 2019a]
4.2
Fluid Based
The ﬂuid based solution will be using the surrounding seawater to dampen the accidental
impact load.
4.2.1
Shock Absorber with Water
The Shock Absorber uses the kinetic energy from the accidental load to force seawater
out of a cylinder. The component consists of a Casing, a Piston and an Accidental Failure
Component. The upper part of the column is connected to the Piston and the lower part
to the Casing, and the Accidental Failure Component is a part that connects the two.
The idea is the under normal conditions and during installation the Accidental Failure
Component keeps the Piston and Casing clamped together, but when the structure is
hit by and accidental load the Accidental Failure Component will fail and the Shock
Absorber gets activated. The Casing is perforated to let the surrounding sea water into
the cylinder, and since water is essentially a non-compressible ﬂuid, the water will cushion
the movement of the Piston when the Shock Absorber is activated. The Kinetic energy
from the impact is then used to drive the Piston to push the seawater out cylinder of
trough the holes in the Casing.

4.3
Deformation Based
Deformation based solutions absorbs the impact energy by use of deformation compo-
nents. The kinetic energy from the impact is used to deform a speciﬁc component of
the structure, to get rid of the energy. The idea is inspired by the automotive industry.
Where the use of deformation zones are crucial to making a car safer for the passengers
if an collision occurs. A modern car is design so that the front and rear can deform and
absorb most of the impact energy, and the passenger compartment is designed stiﬀto
protect the passengers.
The same principle will be used to protect the subsea pipelines.
One or more defor-
mation zones will be able to absorb most of the impact energy, either mounted on top of
the structure or as a part of the vertical support beams. When the components deform as
designed the rest of the structure is still intact to protect the pipes, illustrated in ﬁgure

4.3.1
Deformation pipe
The Deformation Pipe solution is an vertical beam mounted solution. The concept is
inspired by an impact energy absorber used to reduce the damage on trains under impact.
On each end of the train the there is mounted energy absorbers, so if the train goes rogue
and hits the end of the train track, they deform and absorb some of the impact energy
[Voith, 2019].

[Molven, 2019a]
The deformation Pipe consists of four main parts, there is a top plate, a bottom plate, a
stabilization piston and the deformation element. The idea is that when the structure is hit
by the dropped object, the energy travels from the impact point on the roof down through
the vertical beams. The beams then get compressed which activates the Deformation

Pipe. The Deformation Element has a slightly larger pipe diameter then the Bottom
Plate, and at the connection area between the two the Deformation Element diameter
is extended even more to overlap the Bottom Plate.
When the component then gets
compressed the Deformation Element gets drawn over the Bottom Plate, which causes
the Deformation Element to plastically deform and absorb the energy. The Stabilization
Piston has mainly two tasks, to only allow the Deformation Element to move vertically
even when it is deforming. The other task is to connect the Bottom Plate and Top Plate
when the structure is lifted, since the Deformation Element is not constrained to the
Bottom Plate.
Figure 4.8: Illustration of the Deformation Pipe in use, [Molven, 2019a]
4.3.2
Honeycomb Cell Structure
The Honeycomb Cell Structure is a roof mounted solution, which uses plates mounted
normal to the roof in a hexagonal honeycomb pattern. The plates are designed to de-
form on impact to absorb the kinetic energy from the dropped object. The hexagonal
honeycomb pattern was chosen due to its strength which makes it possible to use less
material on the deformation zone of the structure thus making the structure lighter. The
concept is inspired by similar honeycomb patterns used in Formula 1 race cars and as
energy absorbing elements by NASA for the space industry.[McFarlandJr., 1964]
The idea with the concept is that when the honeycomb plates deform they absorb most
or all of the falling object’s kinetic energy, which allows the support structure to only
experience static loads.

4.3.3
Corrugated Multi-Panel
The Corrugated Multi-Panel concept is built up of several corrugated plated with ﬂat
space plates in between. It is roof mounted like the Honeycomb Cell Structure, and is
also designed to absorb all or most of the kinetic energy from the dropped object to only
make the support structure statically loaded. The idea is that when the structure is hit
by the object the energy is used to straighten the corrugated plates, or bend the spacer
plate over the elevation in the corrugated plate. The advantage of the concept is that
with the multi-panel design the only change needed to redesign the structure to cope with
a larger impact energy is to add more panels.

Friction Based
The Friction Based solution uses the friction between the soil and the structure foundation

Ground Penetrating Columns
The idea with the concept is that the energy from the impact is used to push the whole

Comparison and Evaluation of Concepts
When evaluating the diﬀerent concepts some aspects have to be focused on:
• The degree of diﬃculty of analyzing the concept
• Simplicity, must work 100% of the time
• Assessment from Subsea 7
The Rounded Shape solution is a simple design, and will be easy to analyze. But the
risk of the structure failing if a object hits it straight at the center, makes it not suitable
for further work. Also Subsea 7 has expressed that a solution where the object is directed
away may not be optimal, due to the risk of the object then hitting other important

Chapter 6
Dynamic Analysis
The main task of the protection structure is to shield the pipe from dropped objects. This
object may be dropped from a nearby platform or vessel due to activities either internally
on the platform or vessel, or operations where objects are lifted between the platform and
vessel. If the accident happens the protection structure needs to be ready. Due to the
very small probability of an object actually hitting the pipes when dropped into the sea
100 meter above, it has been determined that the structure can be designed on a fail on
impact principle. This means that the structure only needs to withstand one impact, and
then lifted up either for repair or to be replaced. The only criteria to the structure is to
absorb/dissipate the kinetic energy from the impact and then be standing with the object
still on the roof until both can be lifted away. The structural loads can be divided into
two main phases, the ﬁrst phase is a dynamic situation, where an object collides with the
structure and the kinetic energy needs to be absorbed or dissipated. The second phase is
an mostly static situation, where the structure needs to be standing before and after the
collision. The ﬁrst phase will be described below.
6.1
Approach
In the dynamic phase the structural, as described in chapter 4, both the solutions chosen
for further analysis was designed on around the same principle, the EDS was placed on the
roof of the structure to act as an impact zone. The idea simpliﬁes the design of the rest
of the structure, by determining that all the kinetic energy gets absorbed by the impact
zone and the support structure only experience the static load of the impact. The impact
zone elements therefore needs to be designed to absorb all of the impact energy, approx
300 kJ. The method for determining if the solutions can absorb all the kinetic energy have
been chosen to be by analysis in ANSYS Explicit Dynamics. ANSYS Explicit Dynamics
is designed to analyze impacts, collisions and explosions. Due to the complexity of the
dynamics of an object colliding into an element designed to deform to absorb energy, the
ANSYS analysis will be the only tool used in the analysis of the dynamic phase of the
project.

6.2
Assumptions
To be able to analyze the functionality of the structure when hit by the dropped object
some assumptions had to be made about the surroundings. The ﬁrst assumption made
was to not include water in the ANSYS analysis. When the structure is in-place it will be
surrounded by seawater which deﬁnitely will have a impact on behavior of the structure
when it is hit, but due to complexity of simulating water in a case like this.
It has
therefore been determined to simulate the impact like it was happening in air. It can also
be argued that by running the analysis without water the results gets more conservative,
due to water requires more energy to be moved then air, and thus more of the total energy
would be dissipated by moving the water. The second assumption was to assume that
the dropped object was totally rigid, and that it would not deform on impact with the
structure. This assumption was made due to the diﬀerence in dropped objects that may
hit the structure which can have diﬀerent strength and stiﬀness, as a conservative measure
the impact object was then assumed to be rigid.

in the deformation zone, the support structure was not included in the ANSYS model.
The deformation zone was also simpliﬁed by only using a section of approximately 10

6.2.3
Analyzing Time
ANSYS Explicit Dynamics is designed and optimized for analysis of impacts and explo-
sions which usually happens over a very short time frame, to analyze a scenario with
a longer time frame therefore extenders the time the analysis takes run. Also a more
detailed and larger model extends the analyzing time even more. When running early
models of the solutions in ANSYS Explicit Dynamics with the a simpliﬁed structure but
full weight impact object at the speed stated in section 5.1.4, the analysis had a estimated
end time of 100 hours. The analysis was therefore aborted. Some modiﬁcations had to be
made to the model be able to run the analysis, and the two main elements to reduce the
analysis time is model size combined with the mesh and the time frame of the impact.
The model was simpliﬁed, as was described in section 6.2.1, but the analyzing time is still
to long. The only action to reduce the total analysis time was to shorten the impact time
frame, to do this the impact speed had to be increased. But when the impact speed in-
creases the total kinetic energy of the impact also increases. The only way to increase the
speed without rising the energy is to reduce the mass of the impact object, see equation
6.1 and equation 6.2. The impact objects that was used in the ANSYS analysis had to
have the same geometry and size at the impact point, mentioned in section 6.2.2, but the
overall volume could be reduced. The density of the impact was also reduced, and since
the impact object was analyzed as rigid the impact object seemed to reacted similarly
to a higher mass impact. This is of course a important assumption, that reducing the
impact objects mass does not change the behavior of the dynamic part of the impact.

ANSYS Explicit Dynamics set-up
Running an analysis in ANSYS Explicit Dynamics diﬀerent steps had to be followed,
which can be see in Appendix B section ??. The important parts was ﬁrst to chose mate-

to structural steel was chosen, with yield strength of 355 MPa and then deﬁning "Material
Failure" at strain at 15%. Then the deformation zone parts was assigned with the "Ex-
plicit Material" and deﬁned as "Flexible" and the impact object was deﬁned as "Rigid"
and assigned a low density material. A mesh was deﬁned, with a ﬁner mesh at the impact
zone and less ﬁne in the rest of the model. Then a initial velocity was assigned to the
impact object based on 6.2 and the mass of the impact object. The Deformation zone
was constrained with "Fixed Supports" underneath, in a pattern similar to if supported
by the support structure. The analysis was then started.
The result data that will be collected from ANSYS is "Energy Summary", which describes
the energy in the model vs the simulation cycle. The kinetic energy of the impact object
start at approx. 300 kJ and decreases when the object hits the structure. The "Energy
Summary" also displays a failure energy, "Hourglass Energy" and "Contact Energy",
which gets larger if there is problems with the model, like the size of the mesh. In some
cases the analysis had to be stopped and redesign due to large failure energy, but some
failure energy is accepted.

Selecting Solution
Both the Honeycomb Cell Structure and the Corrugated Multi-Panel perform similar i
regards to energy dissipation in the ANSYS analysis. They both varies more depending
om which impact object is used than to each other. So both solutions could be used for
further work, but since the Corrugated Multi-Panel deformed in a way closer to what was

preform as well on impact with the plate, but the stress was not as high in the bottom
as in the top. On the other two impact tests the Corrugated element both deformed and
transferred stress in a way similar to the expected way, and therefor is chosen for further
work.

STAAD.Pro Model
When starting STAAD.Pro the geometry of the structure have to be deﬁned ﬁrst. All
the beams making up the frame have to be deﬁned with a start and end coordinate. The
main idea with the frame as it is shown in ﬁgure7.1 is that the pipe elevated towards
the platform and therefore the structure need to be tall at the base of the platform. But
the pipe exits the structure on the seabed, so the structure does not have to be as tall
at the back as the front. To have a angled roof saves some weight at the back. It was
chosen to use a truss principle as the columns to be able to keep the structure stiﬀwithout
making the structure to heavy. The roof is built of a grid of beams with 2 meters distance
between them, except the beam at both ends which have a distance of 1.5 meter to the
next parallel beam. The roof have a span of 6 meter between the two sets of columns on
each side of the pipes, therefore it was decided to have beams going from the base up to
the roof at an angle for more support of the roof. Since the structure will be placed strait
on the seabed, and not on a pre-installed foundation, it needs to have mudmats on the
end of the columns to keep it from sinking into the soil. The mudmats only task is to
divide the pressure out from columns to a larger area, to keep the pressure down.

other parts of the structure to be scaled up as well. Which leads to a heaver and more

not most likely require a rebuilding of the entire support structure.
The decision to mount the Energy Dissipation System on top to the structure instead of
as part of the columns does not only simplify the design of the support structure, but also
makes the structure less complex and therefore less likely to fail. The Martin Linge struc-
ture has its deformation pipes mounted in the columns, which requires the roof to columns
joint to be totally rigid to transfer the energy strait into the deformation pipe. The pipe
also have to be designed to withstand the installation phase, as it is load bearing. The
Valhall structure have the deformation pipe place above the stiﬀeners for the columns,
which puts a lesser load and requirement to the part when the structure is in-place. The
top mounted deformation zone on the designed structure is not load bearing or make the
installation phase more diﬃcult.
Although the Energy Dissipation System for this project is in it self a more complex part
than just a pipe, the total complexity of the structure is less than for the Valhall and
Martin Linge structures due to their deformation elements are placed in the columns.

Chapter 11
Conclusion
The purpose of this project was to design a high impact protection structure, for protect-
ing subsea pipelines against dropped objects. The main goal was to design a structure
able to withstand a single impact with a total kinetic energy of up to 300 kJ. In addition
to the main goal the structure also needed to be designed against other loads that can
occur while the structure is under transportation, being installed and under normal in-
place conditions.
The maximum impact object was deﬁned as oﬀshore shipping container with with a mass
of 16500 kg and a terminal velocity in water of 6 m/s, resulting in a kinetic energy of
approximately 300 kJ. Due to uncertainty concerning both the movement in water and the
structural buildup of the container, three impact cases was deﬁned with diﬀerent impact
angles and all with a impact object assumed rigid. The diﬀerent impact cases aﬀected
the structure in a diﬀerent matter, where the object that hits the structure with a single
corner ﬁrst resulted in the highest single stress, which caused material failure in the top
part of the plates. The plate hit on the other hand caused lower stress, under material
failure, and the structure had to deform to dissipate the kinetic energy.
The result of the ANSYS impact analysis was that both the Corrugated Multi-Panel and
Honeycomb Cell Structure was able to dissipate the kinetic energy. But due to the Corru-
gated Multi-Panel solution behaved more similar in the analysis to what was predicted in
advance, and the possibility of just adding more levels of corrugated plates to the solution
to be able to possibly withstand a higher impact energy, it was chosen to be used as the
energy dissipation system on the structure.
Further the relevant loads for each sub-activity of the life phases was identiﬁed, and with
data from Subsea 7 and use of NORSOK and DNV standards, the worst case loads com-
binations was added to the STAAD.Pro model. The load cases resulted in a maximum
stress at any point during the structure’s lift, besides the impact, to be 54.7 MPa. The
maximum stress is during load combination 506, which is the load due to the weight of the
dropped container laying statically on the roof at a rear center position. The max stresses
occur in both rear angled support beams, which is going from the mudmat up to the rear
horizontal roof beam. Nearly all the maximum stresses occurs in beams close to either the
front or the rear of the structure, which is due to the 1.5 meters of overhanging sections
in the front and the back, less supported. The structure has an overall low utilization,
causing the structure to be over engineered. But due to the uncertainty of the dynamic
analysis, the low utilization was accepted to make the hole structure more conservatively
designed. The total mass of the ﬁnalized structure was 36428.7 kg, lower than the 40 ton

Subsea Dropped Objects
Because objects falling overboard from our installations and 
vessels are unlikely to cause injury, their consequences may 
often be underestimated, and many such incidents will go 
unreported.
Statistics show that during vessel, lifting and overside operations we 
are exposed to a greater risk of potential subsea dropped objects, 
often involving large equipment and items of significant mass.
The risk with subsea dropped objects is principally that of damage 
to subsea pipelines and facilities. 
Where such pipelines and facilities bear hydrocarbons, any 
damage or breach of containment could have potentially 
catastrophic consequences.
The impact energy of an object falling overboard as it hits the 
seafloor is influenced by several factors, including the mass (weight) 
and shape of the object, the water depth and prevailing currents.
