Abstract
In this thesis an Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) has been implemented for
the Patient Admission Scheduling (PAS) and the Dynamic PAS problem (DPAS). In the
PAS problem a set of patients has to be assigned to a hospital bed, during their stay of
predetermined length, subject to a number of constraints ensuring the correct treatment of
the patients. The ALNS implemented consists of four destroy methods, random removal,
worst removal, gender removal and capacity removal, and three repair methods, greedy
insert, best insert and optimal insert.
The ALNS implementation performed poorly
compared to other metaheuristics used to solve the PAS problem, where especially the
inability to solve one constraint was costly. The ALNS implementation for DPAS was
better able to solve that constraint, but suffered under very long run times, resulting in
low amount of iterations.

1. Introduction
In 2020 the largest hospital in Denmark, Rigshospitalet, had 357,123 unique patients
where 77,946 of those patients where hospitalized for an average of 4.74 days. All those
patients have to be assigned to one of the 1182 beds at Rigshospitalet during their stay. To
treat the patients the organisation consists of around 14,000 employees across a wide range
of departments [1][2]. Managing this amount of patients and employees is a complex task
and is likely increasing in complexity as patient demands increases, treatments become
more specialized and expenses grow. Due to this utilizing hospital resources efficiently
poses a core problem in healthcare today, and using operations research is an opportunity
to optimize the different processes in the hospital. Solving optimization problems in the
hospitals is likely to result in cost savings, as it would ensure a more efficient use of
hospital resources, which are stretched thin already [3].
Optimization in the healthcare sector is not a new phenomenon as is evidenced by the
amount of literature dedicated to solving different problems. The problems include, but
are not limited to, the nurse rostering problem [4], CT-scan scheduling problem [5], master
surgical scheduling problem [6] and the patient admission scheduling (PAS) problem [7].
Common for these problems are the computational complexity required to solve them to
optimality, especially as the size of the instances grow. As a result different solutions
using metaheuristics has been attempted in order find satisfying solutions. This thesis
will focus on solving the PAS problem.
1.1
Introduction to the problem
The PAS problem is one of the more recent healthcare problems that have been studied.
In the PAS problem you have a list of patients that need to be admitted to the hospital
during a planning horizon. Each patient has a predetermined admission and discharge
date and a specialism they need to be treated for. They also have a list of room properties
they need, a list of room properties they desire and a wish for the size of the room. With
this information the task is to distribute the patients into the available rooms in a way so
as many of the patients’ needs are satisfied. This is illustrated with a very small example
in Figure 1.1, where five patients are scheduled over five days.
Figure 1.1: Five patients inserted into three beds over a planning horizon of five days
It is easy to see that as the number of patients, rooms and the planning horizon increases,
the complexity of the problem also increases drastically.
The complexity of the problem along with the general implication of optimization in
3
==PAGE:5==
healthcare makes this a very interesting problem to work on, and will therefore be the
focus of this thesis. To solve the problem this thesis will implement an Adaptive Large
Neighborhood Search (ALNS) [8]. The ALNS algorithm is a metaheuristic used for other
optimization problems such as the vehicle routing problem, pickup and delivery problems
and educational time tabling problems among others. It has also seen use for scheduling
problems in [9][10]. This thesis will also aim to explore extensions to the problem provided
in [7], e.g making the problem dynamic by being able to change the admission dates of
the patients.
The problem was originally formulated by Demeester et al. in 2010 in [7] and they came
up with a solution for the problem using a hybrid tabu search algorithm with a token-ring
search included. Since then many different papers have been written dealing with the
problem and variants of it.
In 2011 Ceschia and Schaerf solved it using local search
methodology providing two different local search methods, one where a patient is limited
to no transfers during their stay and one where patients are limited to one transfer[11].
The same two authors has also extended the original problem with several additions which
resemble real-world features such as emergency patients, uncertainty in the length of stay
and possibility of delayed admissions [12]. Here they also compare the solutions of a local
search metaheuristic to that of an exact integer programming model. In 2014 Range et
al. solved the problem using an column generation approach [13]. Lusby et al. solved
the dynamic patient admission scheduling problem (DPAS) using ALNS combined with
a Simulated Annealing framework [14]. Bolaji et al. provided a new solution approach
to the problem by using a late acceptance hill climbing algorithm [15]. Another new
approach was proposed in 2020 by Abu Doush et al. using a harmony search algorithm
combined with a range of different methods [16]. The problem being the focus of a large
number of papers further emphasizes the appeal of trying to solve it. A more thorough
literature review follows in the next section.
1.2
Literature review
The problem is originally formulated in [7] so most of the terminology is introduced here,
which will be described in the terminology section of the report. The background for the
problem described in this paper is that a hospital wishes to maintain a sufficiently high
occupancy rate, and optimizing the assignments of different patients to beds can solve
this goal. As with the terminology this paper describes the set of constraints related
to the problem. These consists of eight hard constraints (HC) and five soft constraints
(SC), but some of the hard constraints are implemented as soft constraints in that the
violation of them is penalized in the objective function rather that outright prohibited by
a constraint in the mathematical model. In order to solve the problem they implemented
a tabu search (TS) algorithm with a token ring search where neighborhoods are switched
after a certain amount of non-improving moves. They also use a diversification strategy
which is based on increasing the tabu list length in order to escape from local minima.
The two neighborhoods they use in the paper is a Swap-Beds neighborhood and a
Move-Patient-To-Another-Department neighborhood.
One extension is described in the paper [11]. This extension is done to make the problem
more realistic. In the original problem it is assumed that the admission and discharge
dates are known in advance. Since that is not necessarily the case, they look at a dynamic
version of the problem, Dynamic Patient Scheduling Problem (DPAS). Based on the
4
==PAGE:6==
problem definition they propose two steps of preprocessing the data, first changing from
bed assignment to room assignment since every bed in a room have the same properties.
Second since many of the constraints are related to the room a patient is placed in, they
can be compiled into a single room-patient penalty matrix. The solution technique they
use is based on local search. After an the initial preprocessing step they look at the
solution space of room-patients rather than bed-patients. Another change they make is
to only look at subsets of the entire solution space. The two subsets is S0 and S1, where
transfers are either prohibited or allowed one time per patient. They argue that it is
unlikely that a patient is transferred more than once, and therefore S1 should suffice.
Four different neighborhood relations are explored in their solution: Change room, swap
patients, partial change room and partial swap patients. While experimenting with both
Simulated Annealing (SA) and TS they end up using SA due to better results. They
test their implementation on a range of data sets varying between small size and short
planning horizon, to large size with a long planning horizon.
The conclusion is that
their implementation of SA is very competitive compared with compared with other
solvers and with an integer linear programming (ILP) solver. The implementation even
solved many of the larger instances where the ILP solver was not able to solve them in a
reasonable timeframe.
Another solution method is explored in the paper [13] by Range et al. where they use
a method based on column generation to solve the problem. The column generation
approach also incorporates a dynamic constraint aggregation procedure in order to
improve the performance of the algorithm.
The pricing problems in the column
generation are solved by either selecting patients greedily or by a dynamic programming
algorithm. In order to force integrality of the solution, they use a heuristic based on the
branch-and-bound method. The solution method described produces new best solutions
for five out of the twelve standard data sets. However they also mention that the five
new best solutions are for five of the six small data sets, and therefore the method may
need improvements for the larger data sets.
In a different paper by Bolaji et al.[15] they propose a solution using a Late Acceptance
Hill Climbing Algorithm (LAHC). The LAHC is a one-point solution, meaning it
improves upon a generated solution until a local optima is reached. It works similar to
a classical Hill-Climbing algorithm, except it does not compare the new solution with
the one just generated, but rather a solution a pre-determined number of steps prior.
Within the LAHC algorithm, they make use of three different neighborhood structures.
A move neighborhood where a patient is simply moved to another bed in the solution,
while maintaining feasibility. A swap neighborhood where two patients are swapped in
the solution, and the last is a move-swap neighborhood where one patient is moved to a
empty spot, while a another patient is moved to the spot previously occupied by the first
patient. The results obtained in this paper are close to the best solutions found to the
project, and also obtained in less time for the datasets, and the authors thus conclude
that the LAHC implementation is a competitive way of solving the PAS problem. It
should be noted that they too only solve the first six datasets.
In another paper, Abu Doush et al.[16] use an algorithm called Harmony Search (HS),
which is a population-based search algorithm, to solve the PAS problem. The algorithm
uses three different operators to generate a new solution, a solution from the memory, a
random generated solution and adjusting the solution using a predefined pitch. They
use five different pitch strategies, which are similar to the neighborhood functions in
previous papers. One where a patient is transferred into another randomly selected bed.
5
==PAGE:7==
The second where two patients have their rooms swapped for their entire stay. The next
one is similar to the first by transferring a patient to a randomly selected bed, however
the new bed must satisfy at least one of the soft constraints. The fourth is similar to
the move-swap neighborhood from the previous paper in that a patient is swapped into
another patients bed, which in turn is assigned a random bed that satisfies at least one
soft constraint. The fifth and final pitch, if a patient move requires a relocation, a new
room, a relocation day, and the entry and release date is selected. Compared with nine
other state-of-the-art methods to solve this problem, this solution ranks number five on
average, and number six on standard deviation. The comparison is only done on data
sets 1-6 as with the previous papers.
Guido et al.[17] solves the PAS problem using an offline optimization model in the paper
Offline Patient Admission Scheduling Problems. Here they solve the dynamic problem
using a mathematical model instead of a heuristic.
This allows them to experiment
with the soft constraints presented in the original paper. Primarily they examine how
the solution changes if the transfer penalty is lowered, and is below e.g the penalty for
having both genders in a room, or patients not having the preferred equipment in the
room. As previous papers have stated, solving large instances using a MIP model is
not possible within a reasonable timeframe. In this paper they solve 50 instances with
a planning horizon of 14 days with 50 patients, which is a much smaller instance than
what the other papers tests.
In the next paper Borchani et al.[18] uses the Hungarian method, which is a process used
to deal with assignment problems. It works by iteratively transforming a cost matrix
into a sequence of equivalent matrices until it finds the most favorable solution. In the
first heuristic they propose, they decide to relax the transfer penalty, which is the most
severe penalty, and set the cost to zero for violating that. By having no violation for the
transfer of patients, each day can be scheduled independently. The second heuristic does
contain the penalty for transfers. In terms of results they compare the first heuristic
with [11] where they also relaxed the transfer constraint. They find that the Hungarian
method returns the best solution in 11 of the 12 instances.
In the non-relaxed PAS
problem they perform fifth best in the small instances on average, compared to the 14
other methods listed, however they always achieve results on the larger instances, which
other papers have not been able to do.
An extension to the problem is described in the paper [12].
Here they propose a
metaheuristic to solve both the static and the dynamic problem.
The proposed
metaheuristic is SA and a complex neighborhood structure. As the same two authors
discussed in [11] the original static problem lacks in realism, since the actual problem
the hospitals face is the dynamic or day-to-day problem.
This can be described as
predictive-reactive scheduling with a rolling time horizon policy. The possibility to delay
the admission date for a patient is argued since in real life, hospitals can normally
delay admission of a non-urgent patient, and it should be allowed. They also explore
uncertainty in the duration of a patient, again to mirror real-life where the duration
might be dependent on multiple things. In the model they include an component in the
objective functions which accounts for the possibility that a room is overcrowded on a
specific day. The addition of the possibility of delay in the problem, means that the
admission days of patient becomes decision variables, and thus increases the complexity.
This new problem is defined as Patient Admission Scheduling with Uncertainty (PASU).
They use an (ILP) and a local search solution method. The ILP model is solved by
CPLEX and the local search is solsved using SA. The ILP model only finds solutions to
6
==PAGE:8==
42% of the instances while the SA local search finds solutions to 97% of the instances.
In another paper, the problem defined in [12] is examined by Lusby et al. [14], and
they use an ALNS utilizing a SA algorithm to solve the problem. Within the ALNS
algorithm they utilize three destroy methods and two repair methods. The three removal
methods are random, worst and related removal. The two repair methods implemented
is a greedy insertion and a regret insertion. The implementation is tested on 50 different
instances divided into 9 sub-categories by size (small, medium or large) and time horizon
(short, mid or long). Their findings is that ALNS finds better than average solutions on
all instances other than the small ones, and in general it appears to perform better the
larger the instance gets. However while Ceschia and Schaerf [12] use a time limit on 60
seconds per day, this solution does not use that, which especially on larger instances is
violated.

2. Problem description
The PAS problem is an optimization problem that aims to find the optimal assignment of
patients to beds in a hospital over a specific planning horizon. As mentioned in section 1.2,
the problem was originally formulated by P. Demeester, W. Souffriau, P. De Causmaecker
and G. Vanden Berghe in [7], and this thesis will use the terminology established there.
2.1
Terminology
The terminology used in this thesis is introduced below. It is not every term used in the
original paper that is relevant in this thesis.
• Patients are one of the central terms in this thesis. A patient has an admission
date and a discharge date and needs to be assigned a bed in a room during the
stay. Original paper distinguishes between admitted patients and planned patients,
however that distinction is not made here.
• Transfer: The act of moving a patient from one room to another room during their
stay
• Room: A room has a number of beds, which a patient can be admitted to defining
the capacity of the room.
Along with that different rooms can have different
properties such as availability of oxygen or telemetry. Some rooms will also have
age requirements or specific gender policies.
• Specialism: Different departments specialize in different areas of treatment, which
is represented by specialisms. These specialisms are also noted for the rooms in the
specific departments.
• Night: The smallest unit of time, which measures how long a patient has to stay in
the hospital.
• Planning horizon: The number of nights each data set contains.
The terminology relating to the mathematical model, such as decision variables will be
introduced in a later section.
2.2
Description of the problem
As mentioned earlier, the core of the problem is to find the optimal assignment of patients
to beds in a hospital during a pre-determined planning horizon. What makes this problem
very difficult to solve is the number of constraints the model requires in order to account
for real-life needs and wishes of the patients. In the paper by Demeester et al.[7] they
define eight hard constraints along with five soft constraints. Some of the constraints has
to be kept while some of them will instead incur a penalty if broken. The constraints are
now presented:
8
==PAGE:10==
Hard constraints
• HC1: During the planning period a room has to be available. A blocked room,
due to e.g maintenance cannot be used.
• HC2: The admission and discharge date of a planned patient cannot be changed.
• HC3: For each admission of a patient, the length of stay is contiguous.
• HC4: Two patients cannot be assigned to the same bed at the same time.
• HC5:
Male and female patient should be assigned to appropriate rooms if
applicable.
• HC6: Patients should be assigned to a department that is suitable for their age.
• HC7: The medical treatment of a patient may require that the patient is assigned
to a room with special requirements, which is mandatory.
• HC8: Some patients have to be assigned to a single room due to medical reasons
(quarantine).
Soft constraints
• SC1: The patients room choice (single, twin, ward) has to be respected if possible.
• SC2: A patient is preferably assigned to a department with the right equipment
and staff to treat the patient’s disease.
• SC3: A patient is preferably assigned to a room that corresponds to the specialism
that is needed to treat the patients clinical picture.
• SC4: The medical treatment of a patient may require that the patient is assigned
to a room with special equipment. This is a soft constraint of the hard constraint
HC7.
• SC5: The number of unplanned transfers should be minimized.
These are the constraints that the problem is subject to. Naturally some of them are more
important than others, with the hard constraints intuitively being more important than
the soft constraints, but some of the soft constraints are also more important than others.
This is common in optimization problems, and it also makes sense in this problem. It
might be more preferable to place a patient in a larger room than requested, and violating
SC1, so they can stay there during the entire stay, rather than have them transferred
during their stay so they can be put into a room of their choice for a sub-part of their
stay. Demeester et al.[7] also present a weighing of the constraints in their paper, and for
consistency the same weights will also be used here.

Even though they are called hard constraints, it is possible to violate HC5 through
HC8, but incurring a penalty for doing so. This is most likely because HC1 to HC4
are unbreakable hard constraints due to setting the outer framework for the solution e.g
not allowing more than one patient per bed. HC5 to HC8 on the other hand could
be broken without making the solution infeasible, if the hospital faced a severe planning
crisis, but should preferably not be broken. While there are eight constraints where it
is possible to incur a penalty, two of them are not included. The first of them is HC8
which is not included since it does not appear in the data set when a patient has medical
reasons to be quarantined, other than the patient who prefers a single room. However
there is no way of telling them apart in the data sets so it is disregarded. The second one
that is dropped is SC2, again since the equipment of the departments does not appear in
the data. If the constraint refers to the specialisms this is covered by SC3 as the rooms
in a department will have a combination of the specialisms covered by the department.
How the weighing of the constraints are done, and how they are implemented specifically
will be covered in an implementation section later.

Before looking into the data sets, a couple of things has to be noted, which are denoted

• Data set 6 and 9 have days where the number of patients that needs a bed in

Examining the data sets, starting with the patients it is seen that the first nine data sets
contain a similar amount of patients, ranging from 631 to 895. Data sets 9 and 10 then
contain around twice as many patients, while the two last remaining data sets contain
around triple the number of the first nine data sets. Moving on to the rooms, the data

properties the data sets are split equally with seven data sets having two room properties
and six data sets having four room properties. The last quality of the data sets shown is
the planning horizon. The first nine data sets all have a planning horizon of four weeks
or less, with two weeks being the most common. The last three data sets expand on the
planning horizon, with data set 10 looking at an eight week window, and the last two
looking at approximately a three month planning horizon.

parameters, which should allow for proper testing of the implementation in a later section.

The following chapter covers the theory behind ALNS and the subsequent implementation

3.1
Introduction
The metaheuristic used in this project is Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS)[8]
which is an extension to the Large Neighborhood Search (LNS). LNS explores large
neighborhoods by repeatedly destroying a feasible solution using a destroy method, and
then repairing it again using a repair method. This type of algorithm was originally
proposed by Schrimpf et al.
in [19] as the ruin and recreate principle.
This was
formulated in order to solve complex problems with many constraints, as problems of
this size could often contain uneven neighborhoods, as in a small change could either
result in an infeasible solution or drastically worsen the current solution. By ruining
a large part of the solution and recreating it under the constraints, a feasible solution
is guaranteed, and hopefully the repaired solution is better than it was before it was
repaired.
ALNS extends LNS by using multiple destroy and repair methods. This makes it a good
choice to deal with complex problem such as the PAS problem. The ALNS has also
been used to solve a variety of other problems. In a paper by Dang et al. the ALNS
algorithm is used to the scheduling of mobile robots[9]. In this problem a set of jobs
each consisting of a set of operations has to be performed by a set of machines. The
processing operations at some of the machines along with the transportation between
the machines are done by mobile robots. A solution to this problem is a sequence of
operations, robot assignments to transportations and robot assignments to the processing
of operations which need a robot. This implementation splits the heuristic into two sets,
an exploration set and a exploitation set. The exploration set of the heuristic destroys
and repairs a large part of the solution each time while the exploitation set only destroys
one or a couple of elements from the solution. Each of the sets consists of nine heuristics.
They conclude that the ALNS implementation for this problem is very competitive when
comparing it with different solutions to the problem.
In another paper Wen et al. uses an ALNS implementation to solve the electric vehicle
scheduling problem [10]. In this problem a set of timetabled bus trips each starting from
and ending at a specific location and at specific times, has to be carried out by a set of
electric buses or vehicles based at a number of depots and with limited driving ranges.
To solve this problem they have implemented an ALNS with three destroy methods and
a class of regret insertion methods to repair the solution, each with a different way of
choosing the next insertion. The repair methods are all implemented in a stochastic and
deterministic version. The heuristic proposed in this paper creates good solutions on
large instances and optimal or close to optimal solutions to small instances.
While the structure of the PAS problem does differ from the problems described in the
18
==PAGE:20==
two papers above, they do show that ALNS can perform at a high level for problems that
have different mathematical structures and that ALNS could also be a suitable choice
for the PAS problem. The arguments presented above, along with prior knowledge of
implementing ALNS is the reason ALNS is chosen as the metaheuristic in this thesis.
In ALNS a neighborhood is typically defined as the combination of a destroy and a repair
method.
Since the destroy methods normally contain some element of stochasticity
it will allow the meta heuristic to search the entire solution space. Since it has more
combinations of destroy and repair methods, than LNS, it will naturally also explore
more neighborhoods to search for the best solution.
It also allows the metaheuristic
to take advantage of the strong points of different methods. For instance a completely
random destroy method e.g remove k random elements from the solution, creates a
large degree of stochasticity in the solution which can be useful for jumping to different
parts of the solution space. However a potential downside may be that the elements
that makes the solution worse might not be removed from the solution consistently and
will therefore remain in the reconstructed solution. This could for instance be solved by
using a destroy method that removes k of the worst elements, which will then ensure
that the worst elements will be reinserted into the solution, possibly in a better spot. It
does however come with a cost of less stochasticity in the destroy method. The same
logic also applies to the repair methods, where one of the main trade-offs is that of
complexity versus quality of the solution. An example of this could be a greedy insertion
method that simply takes the removed elements one by one and insert them in the
solution at the best spot for that element. This will not require much time to do however
as with many greedy methods the solution might not be very good. As opposed to this,
one could use an insertion method that inserts the elements back into the solution in
the optimal way, for instance by using a mathematical model. While this will get the
optimal solution to the subproblem, it will most likely require significantly more time to
perform, which will then allow for fewer iterations of the metaheuristic.

Finding the best λ value for the specific implementation will require testing of different
values, and even with testing, it is not guaranteed to find the optimal λ value, since it
might also depend on other things, like the total amount of iterations that is done.
It is not only the λ value that determines how the overall ALNS changes during
iterations. As seen in 3.1 the other factor involved in updating the weights, is the score
given to the destroy and repair methods used in the current iteration. The idea is to
reward methods that bring the search forward by finding a better solution, while possibly
punishing methods that do not do that. How much the methods are rewarded for finding
a new solution will naturally affect how much the probabilities change between each
iteration. The ideal set of weights chosen would result in an increase in the weights so
that there is a sufficiently higher probability of selecting the best performing methods,
while still allowing other methods to be chosen. One other thing worth considering when
scoring the different methods, is the time spent by the methods, where the difference
in time spent in the repair methods often are most significant for the time spent by an
iteration, as described earlier. A way to account for this when scoring is also to consider
the time spent by the repair method. If a exact repair method spends a lot of time
improving the current best solution by a little bit, it may have moved the search forward,
but the time may have been spent sub-optimally, and by that hindered the solution in
the long run. If that happens it would not make sense to reward the method with the
highest score but instead subtract some amount from the score, based on the time spent.
Before considering the specific implementation for the PAS problem, it is useful to
touch upon the selection of destroy and repair methods again. As mentioned earlier in
this section, the choice of destroy and repair methods are important because different
methods have different advantages and disadvantages. In order to describe this in a more
precise way, two terms are introduced, diversification of the search and intensification
of the search. These terms relate specifically to the destroy methods where a method
that diversifies will for instance explore random parts of the solution. A method that
intensifies the solution will instead remove a number of ”critical” variables, which could
for example be variables with a high cost in the objective function, or variables that in
other way affect the current solution negatively. Destroy methods may not always fall
into either category, but it still makes sense to consider them when designing the ALNS,
to make sure that enough different neighborhoods are explored, while also exploring
them in enough depth to be able to possibly reach a local optima.
3.2
Implementation of ALNS for PAS
This version of ALNS is implemented in the programming language Julia using the Atom
IDE. Julia is chosen due to prior knowledge of Julia as well as the calculation speed.
Before diving into the specific destroy and repair methods, the specific problem along
with the goals the methods should try to achieve needs to be discussed. As explained in
the problem description, the overall goal of the problem is to produce a feasible schedule
that assigns every patient to a room during their stay, while also considering a number
of practical restrictions, formulated as constraints in the model. In the original paper
[7], they distinguished between soft and hard constraints. Hard constraints e.g room
capacity are constraints that cannot be violated by a solution, whereas soft constraints
21
==PAGE:23==
such as gender policy can be violated, but penalized in the objective function if they are.
This implementation will also use that distinction which makes it straight forward to
create an initial solution for the ALNS. The initial solution should primarily accomplish
two things, first it should generate a feasible solution to the problem, and second it
should be time efficient. Whether or not the initial solution is a good solution is deemed
less important than the time spent creating it, as less time spent will allow the ALNS to
run more iterations which can give a better solution in the end. This, as with the repair
methods, has a trade-off between time spent and the quality of the solution, and here
time is prioritized.

and discharge date, since they can be swapped directly between rooms. Other ways of
calculating how related patients are is also a possibility. This method together with the
chosen implementation are all fairly simple methods, but more complex destroy methods
could also be implemented, however they might require extra running time.

The main part of the ALNS is composed of the four destroy methods and three repair
methods described in this section. One of the disadvantages of heuristics is the potential
of ending in a local optima, and the heuristic being unable to escape that point. In order
to combat this, a conditional destroy method has been integrated in the ALNS framework.
If the ALNS algorithm runs 10 iterations without an improvement of the best solution it
will run a big random destroy method, which is simply the random destroy method
explained earlier, with a large number of patients removed. The reasoning is that should
the method end in a local optima, one of the ways to escape it, is to scrap a large part
of the solution and re-solve that part. The implementation here uses 300 patients as
its large number since for many of the smaller sets it is just below half of the patients.
For the larger data sets it would make sense to increase it, however that could result in
problems with the running time of the repair methods. The 10 iterations threshold for
calling this method is chosen somewhat arbitrarily. It allows for the algorithm to work
itself out of local optima if possible, while not being stuck in the same optima for too
long, which could happen if the number of iterations required were increased to much.

4.1
Selection of run time
With the ALNS implementation described it is necessary to take a closer look and analyze
the different parts in order to optimize the performance of the algorithm. The most
important factor in creating the best possible solution is the overall running time of the
algorithm. Most heuristics will, given more time also be able to find a better solution
than with less time. As an extension to the overall running time is the performance of
the different repair methods, as their running time will determine how many iterations
is possible in the allocated time. Beyond those, possible optimizations are the sensitivity
of ALNS, which can be modified by either the λ parameter or the weights given to the
score and the amount of patients removed by the individual destroy methods.
Several factors are important to consider when deciding on the overall running time of
the algorithm. These include the application of the algorithm, comparability between
different implementations and the general performance of the algorithm.
Going through them one at a time starting with the application of the algorithm. It is
important to consider what the algorithm is used for and how often it needs to be used.
If the algorithm has to be used daily, for instance at the beginning of the day, then a total
time of more than an hour would be undesirable since it would take up too much of the
day. If it instead is used to plan one or more months in advance, a total time of several
hours or even a day should be allowed, if that creates a better solution. This specific
implementation has elements of both examples, in that the planning horizon might be
several weeks or months, which should allow for a long running time. However it is also
to be expected that there will be daily changes as patients might cancel and new patients
could be inserted. One could also imagine that the planning horizon roll forward as the
days goes by so a new set of patients will have to be inserted.
The second consideration is comparability between implementations, which is more of
an academic requirement. In order to properly measure different implementations and
algorithms against each other, setting a standard amount of time will ensure they compete
on the same terms and thereby making it easier to determine the best algorithm. The
authors of the original paper suggests two standard times, 300 seconds for the short run
time, and a longer run time of 3000 seconds.
The final consideration explained here is the performance of the algorithm. Letting the
algorithm run for a while, will make it possible to see how the objective function improves
over a long time. If the objective function improves very little after e.g two hours it will
not make sense to extend it much beyond that point as the potential gains will be very
small, and probably not worth the extra time spent. This has been done on data set 0
where 10 iterations ran for two hours each, which can be seen in Figure 4.1. The reason
for choosing two hours as the maximum run time here, is based on the prior argument
about the application of the algorithm. This time of algorithm should be used relatively
often, and should therefore not take many hours or days to run through. The second
31
==PAGE:33==
argument is that the suggested times are 5 and 50 minutes, so allocating much more than
possibly two hours would just not make sense.

seconds, that is one hour. While the solutions do get better after the one hour mark the
it is not by a large amount, and it is not justified to let it run two hours. This graph
would suggest that one hour is a good amount of time for the long running time. It is
also possible to see on the graph that the solutions improve a lot the first 1200 seconds,
or 20 minutes. This could also be a good selection of time for the short solution, which

running time, where the difference should be larger the more patients they are required

spent on ALNS should not be lower than that, and even with a total time of a couple of
hours it might not reach a good amount of iterations.
Now that both the performance of the overall algorithm and the performance of the
individual insertion methods has been considered, a final decision has to be made on the
run time of the algorithm. A long run time of 3600 seconds was discussed when looking at
the overall performance of the algorithm, and it is clear that a run time of 3600 seconds
would also work with the considerations the individual run times presented. A long run
time of 3600 seconds or one hour is decided.
As for the short run time, that proves a little bit more challenging. The suggested run
time of 1200 seconds should work with the smaller data sets however the large data sets
would not allow for too many iterations during the short run. A solution to this problem
would be to only test the smaller data sets on the short run time and disregard the big
data sets here. It is of course not ideal, but without changing the insertion methods it
does not make sense to simply do one or two iterations of an algorithm. Therefore a short
run time of 1200 seconds or 20 minutes is decided.
Another option would be to define the run time with a number of iterations. The main
reason this is not done is due to the differences in the run times of the repair methods.
The potential variance in the overall run time could be very large, and thus difficult to
control. The differences in the sizes of the data sets also vary, and the time it takes to do
a certain amount of iterations in the small data sets might be much less than the same
amount of iterations in the larger data sets. Uncertainty in how much time is required
to run the algorithm makes tuning of the parameters a more difficult process. The final
argument against using number of iterations as a stop parameter here is the practicality
of the PAS problem. If it has to generate placements of patients in real life, reliability
regarding the run time has to be one of the important parameters. That the responsible
person should know that a solution is always generated within the same time frame.
4.2
Tuning of the λ parameter
With the total amount of time decided, it is time to look at other possible optimizations
of ALNS. As mentioned earlier one of the things to explore is the sensitivity of the model,
34
==PAGE:36==
that is, how long it takes before it starts to prefer different methods, and how much they
are preferred. There are two factors that are determining this, which are the adaptive
decay parameter λ and the score given based on performance of the algorithm. While
it might be possible to optimize both of them, it would require a lot of testing as they
influence each other. Lower scores will make the model less sensitive, which can also be
achieved by increasing the λ value. So doing both might make the model too rigid, while
doing the opposite could make it too volatile. Based on this, only the λ parameter will
be tested in order to find the best possible λ value.
Since the ALNS algorithm relies on stochasticity to explore a larger part of the solution
space, there will naturally be some runs where it will hit some lucky removals and
insertions and some where the opposite is true. To minimize the impact of that, each
λ value is tested using 10 different seeds, and then taking an average of the objective
values to determine the performance of that particular λ value. As an addition to that,
there might also be a difference between how well values perform across datasets, so the
testing also uses four different datasets, as a solution to that. As for the chosen values
of λ, a λ value close to one would result in a more rigid algorithm that retains most of
the weight from prior iteration, whereas a low λ value means the prior iterations have
less importance as it is the most recent weights that influences it. Because the algorithm
should have some memory of prior performance, it has been chosen to test values above
0.5 until 0.9.

4.4
Further tuning options
The parameters tuned in this section are not the only parameters it is possible to tune
in the ALNS algorithm.
The individual weights given based on the performance of
the destroy and repair methods are also an opportunity to improve the algorithm as
mentioned earlier. As it was argued earlier, changes to the weights could be achieved by
changes to the λ value and vice versa. One thing that the λ value does not influence and
the weights does, is how the scores are compared to each other. In this implementation
the score ω1 for finding a new global best is ω1 = 10, while the score ω2 for finding a
solution that is better than the current one is ω2 = 5. This indicates that finding a new
global best is twice as good as finding a solution that is better than the current one. This
relationship is not necessarily the optimal one, and could be further tested.
The parameters relating to the big random destroy method has not been tested either.
The number of patients removed and the number of iterations before calling the method
are two parameters that could likely see some change with tuning. There is a good chance
that they are in the right order of magnitude, but small calibrations could improve the
final result somewhat.
Other than the tuning of different parameters, the parameters that was tuned, could also
have a broader test set. For instance, the λ parameter is purposely only tested from 0.5
to 1, but a thorough testing might use the entire interval from 0 to 1 in order to get
the most precise value. Likewise the intervals used for the size testing leaves some gaps,
which could be tested as well if one were to do a more thorough testing.

5.1
One hour results
This chapter will cover the results produced by the ALNS algorithm covered in earlier

When looking at the results, there is one number that is the most important to look at,
the objective value. The objective value is the measure for how good the final solution is,
and it is also the value the algorithm tries to minimize. Other than the objective value
it could also be useful to look at the constraints of the problem. As described earlier,

most accurate way of calculating it is by using the first method, however in order to show
how the progress over the number of iterations the second method is better.

Patient

6.1
Introduction
The extension to the problem discussed here is making the problem dynamic rather
than static, and thereby introducing the Dynamic Patient Admission Scheduling (DPAS)
problem. In a real life setting it is likely that the hospital has the possibility to change the
admission date of the patient. It could be a situation where a patient has a consultation
with the hospital and afterwards is booked for a operation and is then given a preliminary
date for admission. Afterwards the hospital could then consult their patient schedule in
order to precisely schedule the patient, which could then include moving the patient a
few days in either direction.
In the static problem the patient could only be moved along the room dimension, where
the dynamic problem creates the possibility of moving patients along the time dimension.
Doing this creates more potential places to place the patient, and a possibility of finding a
better place to place the patients. Let d be a variable indicating how much the admission
date of a patient can be changed in either direction. If d = 0, the admission date can not
be changed, and the dynamic problem becomes static, and if d = PH, the patient can
be placed during the entire planning horizon.
Expanding the problem also changes the insertion algorithms since they have to search
a greater area.
For greedy insert and best insert these changes means that the
algorithm searches a set of admission and discharge date combinations.
This means
the algorithm initially changes the new admission date ADn to ADn = ADo −d up
to ADn = ADo + d, where ADo is the old admission date. Of course some practical
restrictions are implemented e.g the admission date of each patient must lie within
the planning horizon.
The changes to the mathematical model that is implemented
in optimal insert is comprehensive, and the updated mathematical model is presented
below.

While the mathematical model used to solve the static problem was rather small and
compact, this one is quite the opposite. First the objective function has been expanded
to now include the penalty for violation of HC5 denoted gp. The three equations 6.2,
6.3 and 6.4 together ensure that each patient is assigned to the appropriate amount of
days and have an admission date that lies within the correct time frame. The constraints
6.5 are the same as in the original model, and they make sure the capacity of each room
is not exceeded.
The set of constraints in 6.6 sets the new admission date for every
patient. The transfer constraints presented in 6.7 are changed a bit with which values
of k is used, but otherwise they are the same as the PAS implementation. The three
sets of constraints in 6.8, 6.9, 6.10 are related to the genders of the room, where the two
first identifies if there is a male or a female in the room, and the third one sets a binary
variable to one if both genders are present. The last two sets of constraints 6.11 and 6.12
fixes the rest of the solution so it does not get changed. 6.13 are the variable definitions.
The model introduces two new parameters, losi, that is the length-of-stay for each patient
and maxPH which is defined as PH + max(los). The new decision variables introduced
is admi,k which is 1 if the patient i is admitted on day k and 0 otherwise. The variables
fej,k and maj,k are 1 if a female or male respectively is present in room j on day k and
0 otherwise. The last new decision variable is boj,k which is 1 if both genders are present
in room j on day k and 0 otherwise.

It is clear here that the solutions created by the dynamic version achieve a better
objective value than the static one. Since the initial solution just assign the patients
to the first available room, there should not be a pattern for which of the solutions is
the best. There might be one explanation that changes the perspective, which is that
the objective value only takes into account the days in the planning horizon. Imagine a
patient that is admitted on day 11 and is discharged on day 14, in data set with a 14 day
planning horizon. With d = 2, the patients admission date could be changed to 13 and
the discharge date would then be 16. But since days 15 and 16 are not in the planning
horizon, the patient only spends two days in the hospital as opposed to four before the
change. As a consequence of that, patients might spend less time in the hospital in the
dynamic version than in the static version, which naturally will lower the impact of
that patient in the objective function. There does exist patients with discharge dates
outside the planning horizon, which could be moved earlier into the data set, and thus
have more days in the hospital that is included in the objective value. However, moving
patients further into the planning horizon is deemed unlikely as that would increase the
objective value unnecessarily.

the parameters for the algorithm would be tuned again for the DPAS implementation.
However this is not done because even though the problem have changed slightly, the
overall algorithm is the same, so likely the optimal parameters would lie within the same
area as the prior tuning. The new variable d would also be interesting to tune on, as it
would come directly at the expense on the running time.

explanation is that because of the increased amount of places to put each patient, the
algorithm have more flexibility to find a room where the gender penalty is not violated.
In the static implementation, if the rooms available to the patient already had patients
from the opposite gender, there was no way to insert the patient without violating the
penalty. In the dynamic implementation the patient can be moved in the time dimension.
That opens a possibility of a bed that does not violate the gender penalty. Either due
to another room opening up or because a patient is discharged from the rooms originally
available. The second possible explanation is the absence of optimal insert, which is

7. Conclusion and future work
7.1
Conclusion
This thesis has dealt with the PAS problem, which is a computationally hard problem
to solve to optimality. Due to that the problem is often solved using different heuristics.
In this thesis the heuristic of choice has been the metaheuristic ALNS, which has been
implemented with four different destroy methods and three different repair methods.
The destroy methods chosen has been random destroy, worst destroy, capacity
destroy and gender destroy.
These four destroy methods has been chosen to get
good combination of intensifying and diversifying the search. The three different repair
methods are greedy insert, best insert and optimal insert, the two first being greedy
heuristics and the third a simplified mathematical model built on Gurobi optimizer. Each
bring a different combination of the time necessary to construct a solution and the quality
of the solution.
In order to optimize the performance of the algorithm two different
parameters were selected for tuning, the decay parameter λ and the number of patients
removed each iteration. These tests showed that a λ value of 0.65 was the optimal value
of those tested, and a small removal size of 50-75 patients was selected.
Two different run times were selected for creating the results, a long run time of one
hour and a short run time of 20 minutes. The one hour run time was selected because
the improvement of the solution slowed drastically down there, and because the long
run time used in the literature was on 50 minutes, so they are comparable. Ideally then
needed time should be as low as possible, so the short run time was chosen as most of the
improvement had already happened by 20 minutes which meant it would be preferable if
the generated solutions was of high enough quality there.
The results generated by ALNS using the long time improved the initial solutions by a
significant margin, and especially on the smaller data sets from 0 to 8 this was noticeable.
On the largest 4 data sets the improvement was not too noticeable, which was due to
the time it took per iteration. It was especially two constraints that this implementation
of ALNS had difficulties in solving, the gender constraint HC5 and the room preference
constraint SC1. The room preference constraint was the least important constraint only
carrying a weight of 0.8 so whether the problem lies in solving it, or simply the low
priority given to it is unclear. The results by the short run time were naturally not as
good as the results using the long run time, and they were not of sufficient quality to
justify using the shorter run time. Compared with the results from other implementations
of metaheuristics the results in this thesis were significantly worse which is primarily due
to the inability of solving HC5.
The PAS problem was extended to the DPAS problem in order to create more realism
in the problem, and the new problem was solved with the same ALNS implementation.
The results of DPAS were of the same quality as the PAS solutions when comparing
the long run time, despite the fact that the implementation of DPAS had significantly
less iterations. Using the short run times the DPAS results could not compete with the
PAS results, indicating that the DPAS implementation requires more time to create good
solutions than the PAS implementation does. The DPAS implementation did appear to
66
==PAGE:68==
solve the gender constraint problem that the PAS implementation was not able to do,
which can likely attributed to the increased number of options for placement of patients.
Tests on an extra long run time of two hours confirmed that the DPAS implementation
was able to solve the gender constraints HC5.
7.2
Future work
As with many projects there is some things that could have been implemented if there
had been more time, or if the focus was changed.
Analyzing the results from the PAS implementation it was clear that the gender
constraint, HC5, was not reduced by the neighborhoods produced by the ALNS. As
all other constraints with the exception of the least important SC1 showed reductions, a
destroy method specifically targeting the gender constraint would be a good choice. This
implementation did have destroy method aimed at targeting this constraint in gender
destroy, but from the results it is clear it did not work as intended. One reason why
it might not have worked as intended is that when removing specific genders from the
solution there exists two possibilities. The first possibility is that the removed patient was
in a room with patients of the same gender, which would mean no penalty incurred, and
second one is that the patient was in a room with mixed genders, and thereby incurring
a penalty. Since all patients removed is from the same gender, then reinserting into the
rooms they were removed from will always result in the same penalty as before. The only
exception is if there is available space in different rooms, which is not guaranteed to be
empty or of the correct gender. Increasing the amount of removed patients by gender
destroy will not solve this problem, so a different approach is needed. The approach
suggested for future work could be a gender destroy targeted at specific rooms, where
it clears a number of rooms that have mixed genders of patients, and thus allowing them
to be reinserted into the empty rooms without mixing the genders.
The other major problem this ALNS implementation had was the amount of iterations
that was very low.
The low amount of iterations means that a very small part of
the solution space is explored, so naturally the quality of the found solutions will be
bad. One way to improve this is by making optimizations of the insertion algorithms so
they run much quicker. The way they were implemented in this ALNS was simple and
straightforward, which is also reflected in the run time. Designing some insertion methods
that take advantage of the data structure of the problem, in order to implement different
shortcuts to shorten the run time. It was also discussed that the Gurobi based repair
method optimal insert was not suitable for DPAS variation, so implementing another
repair method instead could be a good idea. Lusby et al. had great success using a regret
insert in their ALNS implementation in [14]. A regret insert is also a greedy heuristic like
two of those implemented here, which would result in three greedy heuristics, but that
should not be a problem.
Going forward it would also be good idea to reevaluate all the neighborhoods created in
this implementation. Every destroy method except capacity destroy has been created
with a direct focus on the patients. Testing different methods of random removal, e.g
removing patients from a random number of rooms, or removing patients from a random
department. It was also shortly mentioned that a possible related destroy method could
be included, where a random number of patients is selected for removal, and are removing
together with some of the most similar patients.
67
==PAGE:69==
Making the problem more realistic by extending it further should also be an important
consideration for possible work going forward. This could be done in several ways, like
making the discharge date uncertain as [12] has done, or dynamic in the sense of not
knowing all the patients beforehand as is done in [14]. However based on the results the
implementation achieved, the focus should be on optimizing this algorithm before making
it even more complex.
