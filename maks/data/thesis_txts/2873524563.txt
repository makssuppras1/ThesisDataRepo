Abstract
Selective non-catalytic reaction (SNCR) is a common NOx control method implemented
in waste-to-energy (WtE) plants. However, maintaining a high and stable NOx reduction
remains a challenge due to fluctuations in operating temperature and flue gas composi-
tion. This thesis addresses the effect of a fluctuating temperature and CO concentration
on the NOx reduction by SNCR.
The NOx reduction by the reducing agents, is investigated by sampling measured data
from Affald+ WtE plant at Næstved, Denmark and modelling the reaction in Cantera based
on the sampled conditions. This is investigated independently by sampling fluctuating
temperature for a constant CO concentration, fluctuating CO concentrations for a constant
temperature, and sampling both distributions simultaneously. The expected conversion
of the samples is modelled using relevant kinetic mechanisms and thermochemistry in
Cantera. The model results are validated against Chemkin model results and experi-
mental results from the literature. Multiple sampling methods are evaluated based on
error convergence, and the variations of Latin hypercube sampling (LHS) are used for the
sampling. The inverse cumulative distribution function with LHS is used for sampling tem-
perature and CO concentration independently, and LHS with orthogonal arrays is used
for bivariate sampling.
The reducing agents investigated are NH3, urea and cyanuric acid. Sampling of temper-
ature and CO concentration independently highlights that improved expected NOx con-
version is achieved at higher temperatures, from 1284 K to 1329 K and in the absence
of CO, depending on the reducing agent used. However, the presence of CO results
in a decreased NOx reduction, and can potentially lead to additional NOx formed at the
same temperature. The sampling suggests that a similar expected NOx conversion can
be achieved in the presence of CO, if the operating temperature is reduced by 200 K-300
K. However, if the temperature is decreased in the absence of CO, no NOx reduction is
achieved, and the expected conversion is approximately 0. The bivariate sampling reflects
similar results, but also suggests that the NOx reduction achieved at lower temperatures
is due to NO oxidising to NO2. The estimated expected NOx conversion for fluctuating
temperature and CO concentration for the reducing agents NH3, urea and cyanuric acid
are 0.32, 0.50 and 0.64, respectively.
Different mixtures of reducing agents (ammonia, urea and cyanuric acid), additives (hy-
drogen) and residence times (100/T(K)-800/T(K)) were explored to investigate the po-
tential improvements. Whilst higher expected NOx conversion is achieved by increasing
all the before-mentioned parameters, the sampling suggests that the improvements also
yield an increase in emissions of other pollutants, ammonia, isocyanic acid, and N2O.
Overall, the impact of fluctuating temperature and CO concentration has a negative impact
on the stability of the SNCR process. Cyanuric acid resulted in the highest expected NOx
conversion, of 0.64, and remains the most stable with fluctuating CO and temperature.
However, operating with cyanuric acid might result in 148 ppm of HNCO and 91 ppm of
N2O emitted at the optimum operating temperature of 1171 K.

Background
Combustion of solid waste emits nitrogen oxides (NOx), which continue to be a major
environmental concern. Currently, NOx emissions from waste to energy (WtE) plants are
controlled mainly by selective non-catalytic reduction (SNCR) methods where ammonia
or urea are injected to react with NO in a narrow temperature range around 1000oC. Due
to fluctuations in temperature and CO concentration in the freeboard of WtE units, caused
by the inhomogeneous waste fuel, it is challenging to achieve reduction levels with SNCR
that accommodate the increasingly stringent NOx emission regulations. Due to this, novel
SNCR strategies for NOx reduction in grate-firing of waste must be developed.
Objective
The objective of the project is to evaluate the potential of advanced SNCR strategies for
use in a WtE plant environment with fluctuating temperature and CO level. This aim will
be met through screening of current commercial additives and additive mixtures, using
modeling to assess their behavior in fluctuation conditions.
Content
A literature study, including an assessment of current in-situ deNOx methods and SNCR
agents/mixtures, and their applicability to WtE plants. Validation of available chemical
kinetic models for the selected deNOx methods. Use of kinetic modeling to identify the
SNCR additive combinations with the largest NOx reduction potential under fluctuating
temperature and CO conditions.

1
Introduction
Transitioning waste management methods from landfills to Waste-to-Energy (WtE) plants
results in a more efficient and sustainable use of the waste. As a result, the importance of
grate-fired WtE plants increases as land becomes more scarce due to population growth,
urbanisation and increasing energy demand. A common incineration method is grate
firing technology, which is commonly implemented for combusting a wide range of solid
waste and biomass. The objective of grate fired WtE plant is complete waste burnout and
harnessing energy from the heat released during combustion [1].
Figure 1.1: Simplified process diagram of Affald+ WtE plant at Næstved [2]
One example of a WtE plant is Affald+ in Næstved, Denmark, which uses grate firing
technology to burn municipal waste. The general process is shown in Figure 1.1. The
feedstock, or municipal waste, is transported from storage to the combustion chamber,
where it gets incinerated. The heat released during combustion is used to generate elec-
tricity, utilising a steam turbine. The solid waste and particulates get removed, and the
remaining flue gas gets treated further downstream through a wet absorption section [3].
In 2024, Affald+ at Næstved generated 382 000 MWh through combustion of municipal
waste [4].
However, emissions of pollutants remain a significant concern for the operation of WtE
plants.
Presence of nitrogen (N) components in the fuel and incomplete combustion
results in additional emissions of nitrogen oxides (NOx), nitrous oxide (N2O), ammonia
(NH3), and isocyanic acid (HNCO). NOx emission considers both nitrogen oxide (NO) and
nitrogen oxides (NO2). The pollutants emitted have a severe environmental impact, con-
tributing to photochemical smoke, acid rain, climate change. In addition, overexposure to
the released pollutants can also affect human health, resulting in severe long-term con-
sequences [5, 6]. Therefore, stricter regulations were imposed by the European Commis-
sion in 2019, resulting in greater challenges to stay within the mandated emission limit [7].

The presence of carbon monoxide (CO) in the flue-gas increases the difficulty of meeting
the more stringent regulations. This is due to the operating temperature decreasing and
becoming narrower. Furthermore, inconsistent feedstocks and therefore inhomogeneous
flue gas composition result in fluctuating operating temperature, leading to a greater dif-
ficulty in maintaining a sufficient NOx reduction [1, 4, 8]. As a result, the objective of the
present work is to evaluate the potential strategies for use in a WtE plant environment to
achieve stable NOx reduction with fluctuating temperature and CO concentration.

1.2
Grate firing
One of the main purposes for WtE plants is to harness energy from the combustion pro-
cess to generate electricity using a steam turbine, as shown in Figure 1.1. Incineration of
the waste takes place in the combustion chamber on a grate. Various technologies are
implemented to ensure full burnout of the waste and harness the energy released, whilst
minimising the impact of emissions on the environment and human health.
1.2.1
Technology
Solid fuel and biomass are loaded onto a grate in the combustion chamber, where the
waste is combusted. The grate is typically split into 4 sections, where different amounts
of oxygen (O2) is supplied depending on the primary air ratio required for the respective
stage in the combustion process [1]. The primary air (PA) is supplied from below the grate,
perpendicular to the direction of the fuel, as shown in Figure 1.2.
In-situ NOx Control in Waste-to-Energy Plants
3
==PAGE:20==
Figure 1.2: Schematic of grate firing combustion with different air supply zones [9].
As shown in Figure 1.2, PA supplies the required O2 for combustion, and ensures even
mixing of it with the fuel in the different sections. The first section consists of drying
the fuel, the second and third sections are where pyrolysis, ignition and gasification take
place. The combination of the first and second stages is the fuel-rich zone, where most
of the combustion takes place. Furthermore, a fuel-rich zone promotes the reduction of
NOx to atmospheric N2, which reduces stack emissions. However, the limited amount
O2 yields higher CO formation due to incomplete combustion. The final section is where
ash formation and complete burnout take place, which is promoted by creating an O2 rich
environment [1].
The secondary air (SA) is supplied to the front and back of the combustion chamber and
through over-fire air nozzles, denoted as OFA in Figure 1.2. OFA can be supplied above
the grate to create a fuel-lean region with excess O2. As a result, any CO formed in the
fuel-rich zone can fully oxidise to carbon dioxide (CO2) in addition to ensuring complete
burn-out of the waste. OFA is also supplied to create a horizontal recirculation zone to
further reduce the potential emissions by improving mixing [1].
1.2.2
Emission
From the solid waste, NOx, composed of NO and NO2, are emitted along with N2O, CO,
CO2, NH3 and isocyanic acid (HNCO). Other pollutants are also present in the stack,
including heavy metals, SO2 and dust (Table 1.2), but are removed by the downstream
processing (Figure 1.1), thus not the focus of this study.
NOx
Environmental impact NOx are mainly attributed to NO2, whilst NO is a precursor to NO2.
NO2 contributes to both ozone formation and acidic rain through atmospheric chemistry
[1, 5]. The human health impact of NOx is limited in small amounts. In general, the
risk associated with short-term exposure is limited to inflammation of the airways and
a decrease in respiratory function. However, extended exposure to NOx can result in
chronic pulmonary disease and cardiovascular problems [1, 10].

N2O
N2O is a significant greenhouse gas, with a global warming potential approximately 300
times greater than that of CO2. In addition, release of N2O into the atmosphere also
contribute to ozone layer depletion [11]. Overexposure to N2O can result in severe human
health consequences. N2O can interact with folate metabolism, which results in genotoxic
and neurotoxic effects [12]. It is also important to highlight that there is no regulation for
N2O emissions in the EU [7].
NH3
NH3 has various impacts on the environment, including eutrophication and acidification.
This is due to the high solubility of NH3 in water, which can then interact with the environ-
ment. In addition, NH3 causes various effects in human health. Low levels of exposure is
limited to irritation of exposed areas, but extended exposure results in more severe health
impacts associated with the lungs [13, 14].
HNCO
HNCO can potentially be a precursor for other pollutants such as NOx due to the atmo-
spheric chemistry [8]. When considering human health effects, studies show that HNCO
is linked to protein carbamylation reactions, which contribute to cardiovascular impair-
ments, cataracts, and rheumatoid arthritis. The upper limit for inhaled concentration of
HNCO is as low as 1 ppb due to its high water solubility [6, 15, 16]. Despite there being
no emission limit implemented by the EU for HNCO, emission of it has to be considered
due to the severe impacts it has in low concentrations [7]. Studies suggest that HNCO is
water soluble, and the emission could be minimised by a wet scrubber [17].
CO & CO2
The environmental impact of CO2 is well understood. The main environmental impact
associated with CO2 emission is global warming [5]. Similarly, CO effect on human health
is also well understood. CO has a higher affinity for haemoglobin compared to oxygen,
which causes CO poisoning, which can have severe effects. [18].
1.2.3
Emission Control
The most significant contributors to emission control in the grate firing design includes
the grate and the addition of air in the combustion chamber. The function of the grate is
to transport the waste particles of different sizes and types. An equally important role of
the grate is to distribute the primary air for the combustion of the fuel. By ensuring that
the air is well distributed, there will be less incomplete combustion, which reduces CO
formation. In addition, the secondary air and OFA contribute to reducing both NOx and
CO emissions. As mentioned earlier, the primary air reduces NOx by creating a fuel-rich
region, which promotes the reduction of NOx to N2. OFA reduces the CO emission by
supplying excess O2 for oxidation to CO2, and reduces emission of particulate matter by
introducing horizontal turbulence to improve mixing in the freeboard [1].
1.2.4
Challenges
Real WtE plants operate with fluctuating CO concentration and temperature. Measure-
ments were taken by Jepsen [1] from Affald+ WtE plant at Næstved. The measured
temperatures and CO concentrations is shown in Figure 1.3.

From Figure 1.3, it can be seen that there are fluctuations in both temperature and CO
concentration across time. The temperatures shown are measured at different points,
where the lower temperature is measured closer to the flame and upper temperature is
measured further away from the flame. Consequently, both temperatures show significant
fluctuation, by over 100 K at the most. Fluctuations are also observed for CO concentra-

a significant impact on the NOx reduction achieved, but also affect at what temperature
window conversion occurs [8, 19].
There are multiple possible causes for the fluctuations of temperature, which vary with
time and position within the reactor. One possible reason is the injection point of air or
any additives, such as ammonia [1]. In addition, uneven mixing can result in local regions
of high and low CO, O2 and fuel composition, which can yield various amounts of NOx
reduction. If the temperature increases too much, NOx may be formed rather than reduced
[8]. Studies show that on an industrial and on a lab scale, the introduction of CO lowers
the optimum operating temperature and makes the operating window narrower [19, 20].
1.2.5
Objective of the project
The objective of the study will be to evaluate the potential NOx reduction strategies for use
in a WtE plant environment with fluctuating temperature and CO levels. This aim will be
met through screening of current commercial reducing agents and additive mixtures, and
modelling is used to assess their behaviour in fluctuating conditions. The stochastic na-
ture of temperature and CO is modelled using the Monte Carlo method and sampling. The
fluctuations are sampled first independently, and then simultaneously to assess their indi-
vidual and combined effects on potential selective non-catalytic reduction (SNCR) strate-
gies.
1.3
NOx formation
During the operation of grate firing WtE plants, formation of NOx mainly occurs through
oxidation of N atoms from the solid fuel. However, if the fuel has low N content (e.g.
woody biomass and plastic waste), combustion of atmospheric N2 is the main contributor.
As a result, the variation of fuel and operating condition results in different mechanisms
for NOx formation, thermal NOx, prompt NOx and fuel NOx [8]. The major steps in the
formation of the three mechanisms are displayed in Figure 1.4.

1.3.1
Thermal NOx
Thermal NOx is the result of oxidation of N2 in the atmosphere, which starts at relatively
high temperatures. The widely accepted reaction mechanism is known as the Zeldovich
two-step mechanism, which occurs as following [1].
N2 + O
NO + N
(R1)
N + O2
NO + O
(R2)
At high temperatures, O2 forms free O atoms, which react with N2 to form NO through
chain reactions. However, the proposed mechanism was shown to underpredict the real
formation of NO. Therefore, an additional fast reaction was added to the mechanism,
which is referred to as the extended Zeldovich mechanism [1]. As a result, the reaction
mechanism, R3 was added to make up the discrepancy [1].
N + OH
NO + H
(R3)
The resulting reaction mechanism is temperature sensitive, and a temperature decrease
to less than 1600 K -1800 K results in less NO formed by thermal NOx [21]. The rate
limiting step for thermal NOx is R1. This is the initial step where the oxygen radical attacks

limited amount of O2 available to oxidise N2 [1, 21].

oxidise to form NOx or react with H to form N2, which then oxidises to NOx (R6-R10). NH3
can also oxidise to form NO and the reaction path is shown in Figure 1.6 [8].

1.4
NOx Control
As NOx emission poses a risk towards both the environment and human health, various
control methods are implemented to reduce the amount formed during the operation of
grate firing WtE plants. As discussed earlier, formation of NOx can be sensitive to the pro-
cess operating condition, including temperature and flue gas composition. Thus, specific
operating conditions can be taken advantage of to reduce overall NOx in stack emissions.
Figure 1.7 shows common NOx reduction methods applied in industry.
Primary control methods are shown in Figure 1.7 on the left, and aim to reduce NOx
formation by limiting available O2 and reducing temperature to inhibit thermal NOx and
Fuel NOx formation. Secondary control methods, shown on the right in Figure 1.7, focus
on treating the flue gas directly rather than affecting the NOx formation. However, real
WtE plants tend to operate with a combination of primary and secondary control methods
to achieve the required NOx reduction [23].

1.4.1
Air staging
The purpose of air staging is to create a region that limits the available O2 for fuel NOx to be
formed. This is accomplished by splitting the combustion air into two injection regions with
10
In-situ NOx Control in Waste-to-Energy Plants
==PAGE:27==
different O2 concentrations. A section of the air is injected below the grate to de-volatilize
the fuel, known as the PA. A secondary injection, SA, is supplied into the combustion
chamber to ensure oxidation of CO and burnout of fuel. In industrial grate fire burners,
the ratio tends to be four parts to six for primary and secondary air, respectively. The
primary air zone is a fuel-rich zone which favours the reduction of NO to N2 [23]. The
downside of the limited O2 concentration is an increase in CO formation due to incomplete
combustion. As discussed in Section 1.2.3, the purpose of the secondary air injection is
to increase the available O2 above the grate. This allows CO to fully oxidise to CO2 and
ensure burnout of the fuel [1]. In general, air staging is able to reduce the NOx emissions
by 30-60% compared to single stage injection [23]. However, a study by Wang et al. [24]
suggests that air staging could result in increased CO emmisions. Despite this drawback,
air staging is commonly implemented in WtE and can be seen in 1.2. However, with more
stringent emission limits, air staging alone is not able to meet the required emission target
of NOx.

FGR aims to recycle a portion of the flue gas back into the combustion chamber to partly
replace the secondary air injection.
This reduces temperature and O2 concentration,
and thereby reducing the NOx formation. When implementing FGR, it is essential to

1.4.3
Secondary NOx reduction measures
Secondary NOx control methods treat the flue gas directly, which is often used in com-
bination with primary control measures [23]. This is done to meet the newer and stricter
emission tolerances posed by governing bodies. A common industrial application is to in-
troduce reducing agents such as NH3, or urea. NH3 and urea react with NO as following
[1]:
3 NH3 + 3 NO + O2
4 N2 + 6 H20
(R19)
CO(NH2)2 + 2 NO + 0.5 O2
2 N2 + CO2 + 2 H2O

These reactions can be achieved by either selective catalytic reduction (SCR) or selective
non-catalytic reduction (SNCR). Cyanuric acid (CA) is also explored as a reducing agent,
as it decomposes to HNCO, which can reduce NO [8].
Selective catalytic reaction (SCR)
SCR uses metal-based catalysts, often oxides including vanadium pentoxide (V2O5) and
silicon oxide (SiO2) [27]. As a result of the catalyst, reduction of NOx by reducing agents
can be achieved at lower temperatures compared to when no catalyst is used. In addition,
the catalyst improves process stability due to the widened operating window and reduces
heating requirement, making it suitable for large-scale implementation [23]. NOx removal
by SCR is able to achieve a reduction of 90% between the temperatures of 493 K and 612
K [27]. However, potential downside of implementing SCR in municipal waste incineration
can be catalyst deactivation caused by the release of inorganic species from the waste.
These include metals, sulphur and chlorides, which all form during grate firing as shown
in Table 1.2, and worsens NOx reduction [23]. As a result, operational costs will increase
due to catalyst deactivation and the need to replenish it.
1.4.4
Selective non-catalytic reaction (SNCR)
An alternative secondary measure for NOx reduction is selective non-catalytic reduction
(SNCR). SNCR achieves the NOx reduction by injecting amine based components such
as NH3, urea CO(NH2)2 and cyanuric acid (CNOH)3 (CA), referred to as Thermal DeNOx,
NOxOut and RapReNOx , respectively [1]. These reducing agents react with NOx to form
atmospheric N2. Compared to SCR, SNCR achieves the reduction without a catalyst. As
a result, the temperature window in which the reaction takes place shifts to a higher tem-
perature, and the range becomes narrower around 1300 K [8]. Operating temperatures of
grate fired WtE plants are normally high, and potential heating requirements for the pro-
cess can be lowered. Furthermore, the absence of a catalyst makes the implementation
of SNCR easier and can be fitted more readily for existing plants compared to SCR.
In general, SNCR technologies are able to reduce NOx emissions by 30-90%, but vary
depending on applications [23]. The cause of the high variation in performance can be
related to the sensitivity of the process to its operating conditions, including temperature
and CO concentrations in the flue gas.

Modelling Method
A modelling approach is used to better understand the impact of temperature and CO
variations on the operation of the SNCR process. The modelling tool used is open-sourced
Cantera [32], which can be operated through Python. In addition, the model is validated by
comparing it with experimental data from literature and industrially used software Chemkin
by Aspen. The main objective is to identify an optimal set of operating conditions. This is
defined as a high conversion of NOx which is stable and does not fluctuate significantly
with changes in the operating conditions, most notably CO and temperature.

3.2
Sampling technique
The method of gathering samples is essential to the study. Sampling has a direct im-
pact on the portrayal of the population, and ineffective sampling can result in a misrep-
resentation of it. However, oversampling of the population can result in redundancies
and excess computational load. Generally, sampling can be separated into random and
strategic sampling.
3.2.1
Random Sampling
Random sampling uses computer algorithms to generate non repeating and non replicable
sequences of numbers. The main strength of the sampling method is the low computa-
tional power and the full random nature of the sample. However, the drawback is that the
sample might not fully cover the entire sample space, resulting in poor representation of
the true population. As a result, strategic sampling will be used to ensure that the whole
sample space gets covered [38].
3.2.2
Strategic Sampling
As discussed, one of the main drawbacks of random sampling is that the samples might
not cover the sample space. Consequently, random sampling can be adapted to allow
for sampling methods which guarantee an even coverage of the sample space, known
as strategic sampling. Various strategic sampling approaches can be considered, often
In-situ NOx Control in Waste-to-Energy Plants
31
==PAGE:48==
taking advantage of sequences to generate the samples which cover the space evenly
[38, 39].
3.2.3
Orthogonal Array
Orthogonal array is a strategic sampling method. The sample space is covered by sepa-
rating the sample space into smaller subspaces and randomly sampling each subspace.
The sample space is separated into j subspaces, where each subspace is randomly sam-
pled once, therefore j = N. Furthermore, the number of subspaces is constrained to
j = zd where z is an integer and d is the number of dimensions or sampled parameters.
As a result, the sample of d parameters happens simultaneously. The sampling can be
defined as an orthogonal array (OA), OA(N, d, z, t), where t is a strength factor defined
by λ = N
st and λ is the number of times each combination is sampled. The main ben-
efit of implementing the sampling method is that it allows for each variable combination
to be sampled at least once. Due to the constraint of the intervals, increasing the sam-
pling number will result in a significant increase in computational time. Therefore, other
sampling methods should be considered [39].
3.2.4
Latin Hypercube Sampling
Another strategic sampling method is Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) which aims to
cover the whole sample space strategically. This is accomplished by splitting the sample
space into n intervals and ensuring that each interval is randomly sampled at least once
[38]. As a result, the number of samples is defined as N = nd, where n is the number
of intervals and d is the number of dimensions. Different from OA, LHS samples each
dimension or parameter individually, then combines the individual samples randomly into
tuple of d samples. This ensures that the whole sample space for each parameter is
accounted for and guarantees samples from the tail ends of the probability distribution
[37, 38].

LHS is mainly effective with normal distributions and uniform distributions and is found to
overestimate the mean for the skewed distribution of CO (appendix D). As a result, the
sampling method is adjusted to sample a uniform space from [0,1), where one sample
is taken from n intervals. The resulting sample is a value between 0 to 1, which can be
interpreted as a percentage. This percentage can be used to determine the respective
sample value from its cumulative frequency density distribution. This is referred to as
inverse cumulative distribution frequency with LHS (ICDF LHS) [40]

3.3
Defining probability
Finally, the probability for each distribution needs to be defined. In general, probability can
be defined as a discrete or continuous probability. The benefit of discrete probability is
the reduced computational and numerical requirements compared to those of continuous
probabilities. Furthermore, handling a discrete data set enables easier data handling as
the mathematics of discrete integers are limited. On the other hand, continuous probability
allows for a more mathematical flexibility and varied analysis approaches. This includes
estimating inverse cumulative probability functions, estimating conditional probability and
others. However, due to the discrete nature of the data set, estimating a continuous
probability function required too many samples to achieve convergence and therefore is
not considered (see Appendix C) [36].

Discussion & Evaluation
The results of the bivariate sampling highlights the effect of a temperature variation and
CO variation on NOx reduction achieved by SNCR. The effect of the non constant oper-
ating condition is investigated for the reducing agents NH3, urea and cyanuric acid. The
presence of CO affects the optimum temperature (Topt,i), resulting in significant varia-
tion of expected NOx conversion (ET,CO(XNOx)) as temperature and CO concentration
(yCO) fluctuate. Based on the measured data from Affald+, the maximum NOx conversion
(ET,CO(XNOx)max) and corresponding Topt,i and the results are shown in Figure 5.1. The
different reducing agents are supplied at the ratio 2:1 with respect to N atoms as discussed
in Section 4.1. Whilst expected conversions for different reducing agents are estimated
based on kinetic modelling and sampling, it is beneficial to understand the effect of how
yCO and temperature variation are affecting the process. It is also important to evaluate
the sampling process, in addition to highlighting uncertainties and potential improvements
which can be made to the methodology.

5.1
Uncertainties
As the results are simulated based on sampling of stochastic variables, temperature and
yCO, uncertainties associated with it have to be addressed. Consequently, uncertainties
are present to different degrees as they affect the sampling method, population distribution
and accuracy of the kinetic model.
Population distribution
The measured data used in the study are a good representation of the range and distribu-
tion of temperature fluctuation of the Affald+ WtE plant. As a result, it gives an accurate
insight into the reducing agent requirements and the respective emissions when using
them for SNCR. However, the temperature distribution used is specific to Affald+ WtE
In-situ NOx Control in Waste-to-Energy Plants
69
==PAGE:86==
plant at Næstved, and the probabilities considered may not be representative for other
WtE plants. Furthermore, the CO distribution is even less indicative, as the fuel type and
composition change at a continuous rate. Therefore, the uncertainty of the temperature
distribution is relatively low and the general behaviour of NOx reduction as a response to
temperature fluctuations can be considered accurate. However, as the CO distribution
changes significantly depending on the fuel of the day, which could potentially lead to
significant fluctuations in yCO formed. Therefore, the resulting distribution is considered
a weaker representative. As a result, the uncertainty of the CO distribution is consid-
ered relatively high. This is further emphasised due to the measured values not being
applicable for other WtE plants, due to the fuel composition being plant specific.
Sampling Temperature & CO concentration
The method for sampling of fluctuating temperature with constant yCO, and fluctuating
yCO with constant temperature is done with LHS through inverse cumulative distribution
function (ICDF LHS). This method is chosen due to the increased convergence of the
sampled mean of the CO distribution. The bivariate sampling is done with orthogonal
LHS (OA LHS). Both sampling methods yield accurate estimations of the means of the
distribution and ET,CO(XNOx). In general, there is a degree of uncertainty associated with
any sampling as it is a stochastic analysis method. However, the uncertainty can be re-
duced by evaluating the number of samples required as the error is inversely proportional
to the sample size, ε ∝
√σ
N .
The method for sampling temperature is effective and yields accurate approximations
of the population mean and standard deviation. This is expected as LHS is effective
at sampling a normal and uniform distribution. In addition, few samples were required,
361, relative to the population size, 1073, to estimate the population distribution. LHS
sampling was also effective from a computational perspective, resulting in low calculation
times, further emphasising its effectiveness at sampling the temperature distribution.
Whilst ICDF LHS gives a reasonable sample estimate for the CO distribution, the esti-
mated error for the sampled mean and standard deviation is approximately 1e-2. This
is above the error tolerance of 1e-4, which is chosen and achieved when sampling the
temperature distribution as discussed in Section 3.4. This uncertainty is further empha-
sised by the required number of samples being relatively high compared to the number of
data points. For CO, 361 data points were sampled out of 402 sample points. Whilst an
improved error is achieved by adjusting LHS to ICDF LHS, it is also important to acknowl-
edge that the sampling method can be improved. This can be in the form of sample cover-
age, computational time and convergence of estimated values. Therefore, more sampling
methods should have been considered, as a different one could potentially improve sam-
pling results. Other sampling methods which could be considered are alternative strategic
sampling approaches, aiming to improve computational times, sample space coverage or
convergence. Sobol sequences could potentially improve coverage of the sample space
and convergence, but require a higher computational load. Overall, ICDF LHS does yield
better results than standard LHS initially considered, when sampling yCO.
Finally, OA LHS is implemented for the simultaneous sampling of CO concentration and
temperature. OA was introduced to LHS to strengthen the sampling, which results in a
more uniform coverage of the sample space. This reduces the uncertainty of the estimated
expected conversion, resulting in improved convergence.
The resulting improved estimation of sampling comes at the cost of increased compu-
tational load. Furthermore, as the number of samples is constrained by N = sd, s is a
prime number and d is the number of dimensions or parameters sampled, and the possi-

Accuracy of modelling
In general, the modelling results are accurate as shown in Section 2.2. This is due to a
well developed kinetic model, which is able to accurately replicate experimental results.
However, validation with experimental results for SNCR reduction with H2 additive should
be considered as well to ensure that the sampling results are valid. Furthermore, when
comparing Cantera model results to Chemkin model results, the difference between the
NOx reduction achieved is minimal. Therefore, little uncertainty is associated with the
modelling software.
One limitation of the results, is the resolution for the bivariate results. In the code, the ex-
pected conversion was calculated for every 50 K over the investigated range, to reduce
the computational memory and time required. This results in all maximum expected con-
version of NOx to be at the same temperature. In reality, it is expected that the maximum
NOx is approximately at 1121 K, but there should be a difference between the tempera-
tures for the respective reducing agents. However, the results still serve as an indicator to
show the effect of CO fluctuation and temperature fluctuation on the expected conversion
which is achieved.

ever-increasing stringency on emission limits. The flue gas from the combustion gets
processed during the first pass by SNCR and then scrubbed further downstream. The
process of Affald+ at Næstved, Denmark is shown in figure 5.2

In general, the temperature fluctuations alone result in a relatively small change in ET (X)
for all three reducing agents in the absence of CO. This is reflected by the small difference
between the ET (X) and Xmax in the analysis performed in Section 4.3. However, this is
an unrealistic scenario highlighted by the data collected at the WtE plant, Affald+, by
Jepsen et al. [1].
When accounting for fluctuations in CO and temperature, the stability of the NOx conver-
sion decreases. For lower yCO, a higher ET,CO(X) is achieved at higher temperatures,
whilst higher yCO results in higher ET,CO(XNOx) at lower temperatures. Thus, the large
range of potential yCO of 0 ppm to approximately 7000 ppm makes it difficult to operate in
a specific temperature region to consistently achieve a high ET,CO(XNOx). However, at
that temperature, the absence of CO results in a low NOx conversion. In addition, when
comparing ET,CO(XNO) with ET,CO(XNOx), the analysis suggests that most of the NO
conversion achieved at low temperatures is due to NO oxidising to NO2. This highlights
the importance of evaluating both NO and NO2 when discussing NOx removal by SNCR.
Therefore, measures to reduce the CO concentration or fluctuations can result in an im-
provement in stability of the NOx conversion across a greater temperature range. For
grate firing WtE plants, the grate is the main contributor of CO control, as even mix-
ing promotes complete combustion. A common additional measure taken is air staging,
which supplies a secondary air stream to promote oxidation of CO to CO2. In addition,
secondary air can be injected to create horizontal turbulence in the freeboard which im-
proves mixing and further reduces CO concentration. Consequently, implementation and
optimisation of secondary air can potentially reduce or stabilise the CO formation in the
combustion chamber.
If CO can be maintained at a specific concentration region, the operating temperature can
be lowered appropriately to accommodate the decrease in temperature window. Whilst
improved NOx reduction is achieved, NH3 and HNCO slip should be kept in mind as there
is a potential increase of side emissions when the operating temperature is lowered. Fur-

result, it is more stable than NH3, but still suffers if the temperature distribution shifts away
from the optimum.
Considering the EU limits of NOx, the 0.50 conversion results in a potential ET,CO(X)
emission of 100 ppm, which is below the emission limits for existing and new WtE plants.
Significant temperature fluctuations can potentially increase the NOx emission up to 118
ppm which is closer to the NOx limit for new plants. Consequently, the sampling suggests
that urea is able to meet the EU NOx emission limits despite a change in temperature.
However, a temperature decrease of 50 K could potentially cause the NH3 slip to exceed
the limit of 10 ppm, which is the upper limit of the emission limit. Therefore, significant fluc-
tuations in temperature could result in the WtE operation to pollute more than the allowed
amount. Furthermore, there is no EU limits on HNCO emissions despite its potential im-
pact on human health. The HNCO slip at the optimum operating temperature of 1171 K
is approximately 78 ppm. This is a significant amount, considering that the NOx which is
removed is 100 ppm, whilst producing 60 ppm of N2O. Consequently, more consideration
should be taken to limit the N2O and HNCO emissions as they pose a significant impact
on the environment.
Cyanuric acid
Finally, cyanuric acid yields the highest NOx conversion compared to NH3 and urea. The
highest ET,CO(X) = 0.64 is achieved at Topt,CA= 1171 K, which also yields an HNCO and
N2O slip of 148 ppm and 97 ppm, respectively. Whilst the NOx conversion is significantly
improved, even more care has to be taken for the additional pollutant. Cyanuric acid can
potentially reduce 126 ppm of NOx, but produces nearly the same amount in N2O. Similar
to urea, cyanuric acid does not respond as much to changes in mean temperatures as
NH3. A change of ± 50 K results in a decrease in ET,CO(X) by 1%-9% where the larger
change occurs when the temperature decreases. Comparing the relative change to urea,
the response to changes in Tx for cyanuric acid is smaller. Therefore, the results suggest
that cyanuric acid is the most stable for the reducing agents for fluctuating temperature and
CO concentration about Ti,opt. However, the stability comes at the cost of the increased
N2O and HNCO emissions.
Currently, there are no emission limits for HNCO and N2O in the EU. Therefore, if imple-
mentation of cyanuric acid as a reducing agent for SNCR becomes more popular, emis-
sion limits for HNCO and N2O should be considered. Some of the NOx reduction could
be attributed to NO2 reacting to form N2O (R17, R18) and have to be taken into account
as well. This is potentially the case as N2O concentration decreases after all the NO2 is
consumed. This could potentially result in superficial NOx reduction as it is emitted as
N2O instead. However, it is difficult to say based on present work and additional analysis

Residence time
Residence time for the process is unique for specific WtE plants, and therefore the rep-
resentative is relatively weak. However, the analysis highlights how increasing residence
time can potentially increase ET,CO(X) without increasing N2O formation significantly.
The major challenge with increasing residence time is ensuring the isothermal operating
condition as heat loss becomes more prevalent. Consequently, maintaining the flue gas
at a constant high temperature comes with a significant additional operating cost. The
short residence time of 100/T is initially considered as the temperature is assumed to
be constant. Therefore, if residence time increases, there is a chance that the operating
temperature decreases. Whilst some degree of decrease can be assumed to be within
the temperature fluctuation, as Tx decreases, it will have more of an impact on the NOx
conversion.
Ratios, mixtures and additives
The effect of the ratio of reducing agent supplied, mixtures of reducing agents supplied,
and H2 as a combustible is explored as well. Adjusting the ratio of reducing agent directly
affects the NOx reduction, as it increases available reducing agent and in turn increases
ET,CO(X). However, continuously increasing the amount of reducing agent supplied can
increase NOx formation due to oxidation of NH3. Additional incentive to not increase
the ratio of reducing agent to NO is due to the increase of HNCO and NH3 emission.
Therefore, operating around the initial 2:1 ratio, of reducing agent to N atoms, results
in a reasonable compromise of NOx conversion and side emissions. Considering the
results, operation of SNCR equipped WtE plants with excessive supply of reducing agent
is inadvisable and optimisation of the required amount should be considered to reduce
the NH3 and HNCO slips whilst maintaining a high NOx conversion.
In addition, introducing H2 to the flue gas resulted in minimal impact on the ET,CO(X)
for NOx. All reducing agents experienced negligible change in the ET,CO(X) when small
amounts of H2 are injected. Excessive supply of H2 resulted in a lower NOx conversion for
all of the reducing agents. In addition, increasing the amount of H2 increases the amount
of N2O formed. Whilst the increase in N2O formed is small, it is important to consider
during the operation of SNCR. Overall, H2 addition can be considered, but excessive
addition of it hampers the operation and therefore have to be added carefully through
optimisation.
Downstream processing
In real incinerators, flue gas cleaning is often done with SNCR and additional downstream
processing as there are additional pollutants formed during combustion, including HCl,
SO2 and other volatiles. Consequently, SNCR is ineffective in reducing the remaining
pollutants which are not NOx. As shown in Figure 5.2, Affald+ at Næstved has a wet
scrubber implemented, which results in additional emission control. The wet scrubber is
effective at removing SO2, HCl, NH3 and NO2 through absorption. The solubility of HNCO
is less understood, but studies suggest that it is relatively soluble in water and thus could
be removed with absorption.
Therefore, the implementation of a wet scrubber can have a significant impact on the
stack emission of the WtE plant. If a wet scrubber is present, the relevance of NH3 and
potentially HNCO slip decreases. Therefore, the downside of increasing the ratio of NH3
as a reducing agent becomes less of a challenge, as the additional NH3 emission can be
negated further downstream. In addition, the effect of NO2 formation is also diminished
as it can also be captured by the scrubber. Consequently, the overall NOx reduction in
the stack is also improved by the downstream processing.

However, N2O and NO emissions remain a challenge. NO is mainly removed by SCR
and SNCR, and the possibility of SNCR is explored in the present work. SCR is difficult
to implement due to potential catalyst poisoning due to the presence of metals in the
flue gas. Similarly, N2O is commonly removed by catalytic conversion. This is similar
to the thermal dissociation described in R17 and R18, but assisted by a catalyst. The
general concern with the implementation of N2O removal by catalyst is similar to SCR, as
metal particles and inorganic material result in catalyst deactivation in addition to potential
heating requirements and process modifications.
To summarise, the results show that CO variation in SNCR has a negative effect on the
NOx conversion. However, operating at a lower temperature to improve NOx conversion,
but results in increases in emissions for NH3, HNCO and N2O, which have a negative
impact on the environment or human health. This highlights the importance of complete
combustion taking place to reduce the amount of CO present in the flue gas and stabilize
yCO which could improve the operating temperature window. Evaluation of process impli-
cation allows for the impact of additional pollutants to be diminished by the implementation
of a wet scrubber.
5.3
Future Considerations
Whilst the study serves as an initial indicator of the effect of temperature fluctuation and
CO concentration fluctuation on NOx conversion, there are still aspects which lack un-
derstanding. First of all, the data is based on measured values from Affald+ in Næstved,
where the CO concentration and temperature are measured at different time intervals.
Therefore, it is difficult to form any relationship between the operating temperature. A
possible correlation between temperature and CO concentration could lead to a better
understanding of why the parameters fluctuate and possibly negate the effects. As a re-
sult, more measurements taken at the same time interval should be considered for future
studies, as more comparisons can be made between the fluctuating parameters. It is also
important to highlight that the measured distribution, both temperature and CO concentra-
tion, is representative of only Affald+ at Næstved. Therefore, additional measurements for
different WtE plants are recommended to investigate the trends and if they differ across
different plants.
Another aspect which can be considered is the effect of downstream processing on the
required NOx reduction. As legislation is only concerned with the stack emission, the pres-
ence of a wet scrubber could change the overall emission requirements and, by extension,
the operating conditions. As discussed, the wet scrubber could allow for an increase in
operating temperature and increase the amount of NH3 supplied, as NH3 slip is less of a
concern. Final process condition to investigate the effect of secondary air and the hori-
zontal turbulence it introduces. Present work considers the CO distribution to be purely
stochastic, with no changes in distribution, but in reality, the CO distribution is dynamic
and changes constantly. As a result, it is of interest to investigate the direct impact of the
horizontal turbulence and the extent of improvements which can be achieved.

Conclusion
A Monte Carlo sampling approach is taken to develop a better understanding of the impact
of temperature and CO fluctuations on NOx conversion. This is investigated independently
by varying temperature for different concentrations of CO, varying CO concentrations for
a constant temperature, and sampling both distributions simultaneously. The modelling
results were obtained using the Cantera program suite. To ensure that the results are
replicable, a validation of the modelling software and the kinetic model was conducted
by comparing the NO conversion to experimental results from literature, and the results
from an industrially used software, Chemkin. An error analysis of the sampling method is
conducted to ensure accuracy and convergence of the results.
The sampling of a constant temperature and constant CO highlights the independent ef-
fect of the respective fluctuations. In the absence of CO, a higher operating temperature
is preferred, whilst the presence of CO lowers the optimum operating temperature. When
the CO distribution is sampled for a constant temperature, the result show that the NO
reduction is unstable at higher temperatures, and quickly results in NO formation. Lower
temperatures give more stable NO conversion for a broader range of CO concentrations,
but NO conversion is zero when the concentration of CO is 0 ppm. This is due to CO re-
plenishing the radical pool, causing the temperature window to decrease. Therefore, CO
fluctuations results in the temperature window for the reaction to shift and to become more
narrow. Thus, the lowered operating temperature results in unstable operating conditions
especially when considering the probability that CO concentration is zero.
Bivariate sampling further highlights the effect of CO concentration on expected NOx con-
version. The presence and fluctuations of CO results in the the operating window where
NOx reduction can be achieved to shift to a lower temperature and becomes narrower.
Partially of the NOx removal at lower temperature is due to NO oxidising to NO2. Using
the same NO/reducing agent ratio, it is observed that a higher NOx removal is achieved
with cyanuric acid compared to NH3 and urea, 0.64, 0.32 and 0.50 respectively.
The residence time, ratio of reducing agent and addition of other combustibles is evaluated
with bivariate sampling. In general, expected NOx conversion increases as more time for
the reaction to take place and reactants are present increases. However, this comes with
the cost of increasing the potential additional pollutants including chN2O, NH3 and HNCO.
In general, increasing the ratios results in increase in emission of additional pollutants
whilst H2 had negligible change in small quantities. Furthermore, mixing the reducing
agent did not significantly improve the NOx conversion, and increasing residence time
increases NOx conversion. Therefore, the temperature fluctuations and CO fluctuations
result in the NOx conversion to occur at the lower temperature and become less stable.
The impact of fluctuating temperature and CO concentration has a negative impact on
the stability of the SNCR process. The different reducing agents considered were NH3,
urea and cyanuric acid, where cyanuric acid resulted in the highest conversion. Differ-
ent expected conversion shows similar stability, but in general cyanuric acid is the most
stable. However, operating with cyanuric acid results in a significant amount of HNCO
and N2O emission. Whilst neither pollutants are limited by EU law, careful consideration
should be taken when implementing cyanuric acid and urea as they have significant con-
sequences on the environment. The sampling approach is successful at taking account
the distribution of the temperatures and CO concentration to estimates its impact on the
