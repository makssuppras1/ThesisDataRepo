Abstract
This thesis introduces and explores a novel deep reinforcement learning approach for
molecular structure search and discovery. We utilize the Atomic Simulation Environ-
ment (ASE), the XTB energy calculator, and the Polarizable Atom Interaction Neural
Network (PaiNN), an equivariant graph neural network implementation, combined
with the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) reinforcement learning algorithm. Our
approach aims to minimize the energy of molecular systems by dynamically reposi-
tioning atoms and molecules in 3D space. The reinforcement learning algorithm is
trained to maximize a reward based on the system’s energy.
Recent advancements in equivariant graph neural networks have made it feasible
to train models that can significantly accelerate the molecular structure prediction
process. Accurate structure prediction is crucial for understanding chemical inter-
actions and reactions, which typically require computationally intensive simulations.
We demonstrates that our machine learning algorithm can generalize from training
structures to predict low-energy configurations for previously unseen molecules.
We conducted experiments in environments with distinct atoms and implemented a
variant of the architecture capable of rotating molecules. Our approach was applied
to solvation-like structures using water as a solute. We evaluated and compared the
results against a classical low-energy configuration search method, known as minima-
hopping, demonstrating the eﬀicacy and potential of our proposed method.

Introduction
In recent years, the application of machine learning to discover new molecular struc-
tures has become both feasible and promising, thanks to significant advances in com-
putational power and the reduced costs associated with computation. The advent
of GPU parallel computation has enabled the eﬀicient use of neural networks for a
variety of tasks.
Machine learning offers a substantial advantage over traditional
methods: while it requires a significant initial investment in training. The trained
models can be reused multiple times, providing accurate predictions for previously
unseen structures at a fraction of the time and cost. A key modification to the original
PaiNN network was implemented to enable the prediction of molecular orientations.
Instead of representing individual atoms, we now use an abstract representation that
allows the network to predict orientations for entire molecules. This modification
facilitates the replacement of atoms with molecules, such as water molecules. We
will use this modified network to predict solvation-like structures, such as solvation
structures, where a central molecule is surrounded by water molecules.
The development of message passing graph neural networks (GNNs), specifically E(3)
invariant and equivariant representation networks, has enabled the application of ma-
chine learning in atomic and molecular environments. These methods are frequently
used for fast and precise molecular dynamics simulations and energy potential pre-
dictions. In this thesis, we will explore the use of a specific variant of an equivariant
neural network, Polarizable Atom Interaction Neural Network (PaiNN), in a novel
approach for predicting solvation-like structures. This approach involves the trans-
lation and rotation of atoms and molecules within an environment and training the
model using reinforcement learning.
First, we will briefly review the literature on conventional molecular dynamics and
energy calculation methods, followed by an examination of the machine learning tech-
niques used in molecular discovery and property prediction. We will then discuss
the current state-of-the-art in machine learning and reinforcement learning, focusing
on the available algorithms and their differences. Additionally, we will explore the
importance of invariance and equivariance properties in neural networks, explaining
their significance and relevance to our work.

CHAPTER 1
Literature review
Molecular structure plays a crucial role in predicting various properties of atoms, mak-
ing it essential to develop methods for predicting molecular shapes and their energy
in different environments. These structural predictions can significantly accelerate
and reduce the costs of chemical production, as well as aid in designing novel drugs
with desired properties. Energy is particularly important because systems tend to
favor lower energy configurations, which are more stable.
1.0.1
Conventional Approaches
One conventional approach to predicting molecular structure is molecular dynam-
ics and relaxation using quasi-Newtonian methods such as the Broyden–Fletcher–
Goldfarb–Shanno (BFGS) [1] algorithm . This algorithm solves unconstrained non-
linear optimization problems. Another conventional method predicts energy and po-
tential using Density Functional Theory (DFT). While DFT provides relatively high
accuracy, its computational cost can be prohibitive, making the relaxation process
slow and expensive.
An alternative conventional approach is the use of genetic algorithms [2] to find low-
energy structures . Unlike methods that calculate the energy surface, genetic algo-
rithms sample the low-energy segment of the molecular conformation space, offering
a different strategy for achieving low-energy configurations.
1.0.2
Machine Learning Approaches
Recent advancements in machine learning, particularly with the development of graph
neural networks and E(3) invariant neural networks, have introduced new methods for
predicting molecular structures and properties. These networks, unlike a traditional
multi-layer perceptron (MLP), can handle 3D data with various symmetries, allowing
for effective redundancy free representation of atoms in a 3D space as graph nodes.
==PAGE:10==
4
1 Literature review
Several neural network approaches do not require equivariant properties, for example
Simplified molecular-input line-entry system SMILES and text-based learning tech-
niques like application of transformers on SMILES strings [3]. While these methods
can potentially lead to novel drug designs, they struggle to capture the 3D properties
of molecules and may have limitations in representing complex molecular structures
like solvation structures.
Surrogate models, such as DeepDFT [4], aim to predict electronic charge density
around atoms by training on DFT data, offering faster and more feasible predictions
after training. Additionally, there are multiple graph neural network implementations
with necessary invariant and equivariant properties, such as PaiNN [5] , NequIP [6] ,
and TensorNet [7] , which utilize O(3) equivariant message-passing.
Inspired by the success of transformers and attention mechanisms [8] in natural lan-
guage processing and image generation, networks like Equiformer [9] are designed for
3D data. Generative models also offer novel molecule discovery, they can be trained
on a set of molecules and generate new, valid molecular graphs with desired properties.
MolGAN [10], using Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) where two networks
contest with each other (Generator and Critic) and in result learning and generalizing
the training dataset’s distribution.
Diffusion models like MiDi [11] are also popular.
These models can be very suc-
cessful as in the field of image, audio and video generation these models were used
with great results. The idea behind diffusion models, basically reverse a gaussian nois-
ing process and transform noise back to the original structures similar to the training
dataset. These methods are also using the invariant and equivariant type networks.
These methods are primarily used to optimize and determine molecular structures
of organic molecules.
They leverage the inherent behaviors and bonding rules of
these molecules. Additionally, since organic molecules constitute living systems, they
are instrumental in drug design, the discovery of various protein properties, and the
study of viruses.
However, other molecular structures, such as solvation structures, which have many
seemingly unorganized low-energy states, also play significant role in the industry. For
instance, accurately predicting solvation structures is crucial in battery electrolytes.
This can lead to enhanced performance and reduced costs in lithium-ion battery pro-
duction [12].
Our machine learning solution addresses solvation structure prediction by utilizing
reinforcement learning, thereby advancing research in this relatively less explored
area.

1.1
Reinforcement learning
A less covered area of machine learning technique in molecular design is reinforce-
ment learning, a notable example of this approach is MolGym [13] which tries to use
the equivariant network to select atoms and place them on a virtual canvas in order
to find novel low energy molecular structures. Our approach is also using reinforce-
ment learning to achieve the low energy structures, but it is using a different type of
environment.
1.1.1
Reinforcement Learning Algorithms
Reinforcement learning (RL) can discover new properties of systems and new molec-
ular structures by maximizing rewards.
Unlike supervised learning, RL does not
require large datasets; if the environment is available, and we have a well designed
reward function, the agent can collect samples to find optimal solutions by maximiz-
ing rewards without external expert knowledge. Our approach can be seen as a game
where the goal is to minimize the system’s energy by moving atoms around. Recent
successes in RL include controlling robots and achieving superhuman performance in
video games [14] [15](e.g., Dota 2, League of Legends) .
1.1.1.1
Categories of RL Algorithms
RL algorithms are broadly categorized into Model-Free and Model-Based RL. Model-
Based RL has the advantage of using classical planning to explore future steps if a
world model is available. However, it introduces complexity with additional hyper-
parameters and is challenging with continuous actions. A successful Model-Based
RL implementation is AlphaZero [16], which excelled in playing board games like Go,
which have exceptionally large state spaces. These system are using Monte Carlo tree
search (MCTS) combined with deep learning to achieve superhuman performance.
For simplicity and due to available implementations like stable baselines and molgym,
Model-Free policy optimization methods were chosen. RL is mathematically formu-
lated as a Markov Decision Process (MDP). Q-learning is an technique for solving RL
problems. Q-learning approximates a Q-function that is a mapping of state-action
pairs (S × A) to real numbers (R), indicating the benefit of taking specific actions
from specific states.
Q : S × A →R
(1.1)
==PAGE:12==
6
1 Literature review
Originally, Q-learning stored action-state pairs in a Q-table [17], updated iteratively
to achieve higher rewards. This method can theoretically reach an optimal policy
but has many limitations and struggles with large state-action spaces due to memory
constraints and decreasing likelihood of visiting specific states as the state space
growing.
1.1.1.2
Advances in Deep RL
DeepMind’s success with Deep Q-learning (DQN) [18] with playing Atari games
demonstrated that deep neural networks combined with Q-learning could approxi-
mate Q-values in unseen states, solving some of the Q-table issues. However, DQN is
not suitable for continuous action spaces, which led to the development of the Deep
Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG) [19] algorithm, which effectively handling con-
tinuous action spaces.
Model-Free RL approaches include Q-learning and direct policy optimization. Policy
optimization methods are divided into off-policy and on-policy methods. Off-policy
methods learn from actions generated by any policy, including expert data, while
on-policy methods learn only from actions generated by the current policy. DDPG
is an off-policy algorithm modified to work with continuous action spaces, hence it
could handle 3D vectors, which makes them suitable to be used to treat atoms.
Twin Delayed DDPG (TD3) [20] improves upon DDPG by learning two Q-functions
and performing fewer policy network updates to prevent Q-value overestimation and
ensure policy stability. Since it is an upgrade to DDPG it can also handle continuous
action spaces.
Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) [21] is another off-policy algorithm balancing Q-learning and
policy optimization, using entropy regularization. Because this algorithm also can
handle continuous actions space, it makes it available as an option to handle atom
coordinates.
The last method which we present here is Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) [22],
which is an on-policy algorithm, was ultimately chosen for its stability and simplicity.
PPO optimizes policies without a Q-network, learning from samples generated by
the current policy, and has proven effective in various complex environments. This
method is relatively simpler then the others, requires fewer networks and less param-
eters to tune.

Method
This section details the methods used to develop our final model. We begin by describ-
ing reinforcement learning, focusing specifically on the Proximal Policy Optimization
(PPO) algorithm and Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) more specifically Equivariant
GNN’s. We discuss their functionality, advantages, and the theoretical underpinnings.
Next, we outline the modifications made to achieve rotation of molecules, followed
by an overview of the agent’s design. We then describe the environment setup and
reward design. Finally, we examine the energy calculator and parameters used in our
training setup.
3.0.1
Reinforcement learning
Reinforcement learning (RL) is mathematically formulated as a Markov decision pro-
cess (MDP). An MDP can be defined by the tuple (S, A, Pa, Ra) where:
• S is the set of states.
• A is the set of actions.
• Pa is the set of transition probabilities regarding states.
• Ra is the set of reward regarding actions.
This framework allows us to model problems where the outcomes are partly random
and partly controlled by an agent. The model satisfies the Markov property hence
the transition probabilities could be expressed as:
Pa(s, s′) = Pr(St+1 = s′ | St = s, At = a)
(3.1)
Here, the set of states St includes all possible states of the system at time t, and
St+1 are the states to which the system can transition at time t + 1 based on actions
chosen from the set At. The reward function Ra(s, s′) assigns an immediate reward
for transitioning from state s to state s′ via action a.
==PAGE:20==
14
3 Method
Figure 3.1. Agent environment interaction
In reinforcement learning, the primary concept involves an agent and an environment,
as depicted in image 3.1. The agent interacts with the environment by selecting ac-
tions at in state s with the objective of maximizing the cumulative reward over a
series of actions. In our specific scenario, the environment is a 3D space that contains
atoms and molecules. The state is the configuration and type of the objects, and
the possible actions include translations and rotations of these objects. The reward is
determined by the reduction in energy at each step and, by the total energy reduction
at the end of the episode.
A reinforcement learning agent interacts with its environment in discrete time steps.
At each time step t, the agent observes the state st and receives a reward rt. Based
on the observed state and the set of available actions, the agent chooses an action at.
This action causes the environment to transition to a new state st+1 where the agent
receives a reward rt+1 associated with the transition (st, at, st+1).
Our goal is to optimize a policy π : S × A →[0, 1], π(s, a) = Pr(At = a | St = s).
This policy should select actions based on the observed state in a way that maximizes
the expected cumulative reward. In deep reinforcement learning, the agent or policy
is represented by a deep neural network, which is trained to approximate the optimal
control policy and improve the resulting cumulative reward.
3.0.2
Proximal Policy Optimization
One well-studied solution in deep reinforcement learning is Proximal Policy Opti-
mization (PPO) [22]. The primary motivation behind PPO is to achieve the largest
possible improvement in policy performance while avoiding sudden and catastrophic
drops. Actor-critic methods often exhibit sensitivity to small changes in their param-
eters, which can result in significant changes in policy behavior. PPO tries to solve
that issue.

maximize the expected return over a trajectory τ generated by the policy:

3.0.2.1
Graph Neural Networks
Graph neural networks (GNNs) excel in eﬀiciently processing data that can be rep-
resented as graphs. This capability makes them particularly suitable for handling
geometric data and applications involving 3D objects. One of the features of GNNs is
their permutation equivariance, meaning that if the input node values are permuted,
the output node values will be permuted in the same manner.
Intuitively, these graph nodes are passing message to each other via their edges, and
the nodes update their representations by exchanging messages with their neighbors.
This process allows GNNs to learn higher-level abstractions of the data, making them
usable for a wide range of tasks such as natural language processing, combinatorial
optimization, molecular biology, and atomic structure optimization.

To eﬀiciently train a model for 3D atomic data manipulation, traditional Multi-Layer
Perceptrons (MLPs) are insuﬀicient. One of the advantage of GNNs over MLPs that
they can have variable input and output dimensions, allowing training on n graph
nodes and evaluation on m nodes, where m is usually greater than n. In GNNs, to
provide unique features to different graph nodes, we use embedding vectors. These
vectors learn high-dimensional representations of the node types. The meaning of
node types can vary depending on the context. For instance, in natural language pro-
cessing, they might represent letters or words, whereas in atomic simulations, they
could represent different element or molecule types.
3.0.2.2
Equivariant neural networks
The reason we want to use equivariant GNNs is that effective training must account
for the inherent redundancies in the molecular setup. Specifically, molecular struc-
tures exhibit rotation and translation invariance: the model’s output should remain
consistent regardless of how the atomic coordinates are rotated, translated, or re-
flected. In our scenario, we require rotationally equivariant outputs, which means
the outputs should transform equivariantly with rotations of the input.
A practical implementation which has these properties is the PaiNN (Polarizable
atomic interaction Neural Network) architecture.
PaiNN utilizes a partially con-
nected graph structure, eﬀiciently handling varying numbers of neighbors, making it
suitable for large molecular systems. Typically, networks like PaiNN are employed
to learn atomic forces and potentials in a supervised manner.
PaiNN’s can have two outputs: one is rotational equivariant, ensuring that a rotated
input results in a correspondingly rotated output vector:
Rf(⃗x) = f(R⃗x)
(3.12)
where R is a rotation in 3D space and ⃗x is a 3D vector. The other is rotation and
translation invariant, meaning the output remains unchanged under global transla-
tion and rotation of the input. This invariance helps reduce redundancy, makes the
network eﬀicient.
PaiNN’s feature-rich message representation propagates both equivariant and invari-
ant messages through the network, enabling it to achieve good performance. This
ability to handle complex transformations and relationships within molecular data
makes PaiNN a powerful tool for modeling and predicting atomic interactions accu-
rately.

3.1

During experimentation, we used Stable Baselines 3 as the foundation for implement-
ing various reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms [23]. This framework facilitated
the experimentation with different RL algorithms, including Proximal Policy Op-
timization (PPO), Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG), Soft Actor-Critic
(SAC), Twin Delayed DDPG (TD3), and Advantage Actor-Critic (A2C). Among
these, DDPG, SAC, and TD3 require one or more Q-networks. Consequently, we mod-
ified the policy architecture to incorporate these additional Q-networks. Q-networks
require inputs for both states and actions, prompting us to switch the original MLP
implementation to the Equivariant Network to handle these inputs. Ultimately, we
successfully implemented these modifications, enabling us to conduct experiments
with these three algorithms as well as with A2C, which does not require Q-networks.
We selected PPO as the primary method due to its ease of use, robustness, effective-
ness, and simplicity. PPO is an actor-critic method where a policy (actor) network
and a value (critic) network are trained. These networks can either be shared or
separate. Stable Baselines 3 supports different output MLPs for policy and value net-
works and allows the use of either shared or separate actor-critic networks. Although
Stable Baselines 3 policies can handle convolutional networks, our use case with
PaiNN, a graph convolutional network, required custom adjustments. The default
policy was unable to handle variable inputs and outputs, beacuase it was primarily
designed for MLPs. Thus, we developed a new policy type, maintaining compatibility
with existing policy implementations. PPO’s advantage lies in not requiring multi-
ple Q-networks for policy learning, simplifying training, performance monitoring and
reducing hyperparameter count.

PaiNN Embedding vectors
The PaiNN network assigns a type to each node, represented as an integer, which
passes through an embedding layer to produce an embedding vector for the node
type. In our setup, node types can be interpreted in multiple ways. For instance,
when manipulating atoms in the environment, the node type might represent the
element number in the periodic table.
In more complex scenarios with coarse-grained molecules and directions, these node
types take on a more abstract meaning, representing elements or molecules. The en-
vironment design accommodates mapping molecules as coarse-grained point objects
in space. These embedding vectors are eventually learning a representation of the
elements and moleclues behaviour in each other’s proximity.

Actor PaiNN network

The network’s hidden state size h can be set heuristically, influencing training time

• Summing all elements in the three vectors to obtain a 3D vector.
• Using a learnable linear combination of the vectors.
• Selecting one 3D vector from the h

Once an option is selected, we can decide whether to normalize the 3D vector to unit

the maximum length while maintaining a continuously differentiable length value.
The network also has a rotation-invariant part, usable for the critic or in conjunc-

stability issues and not to limit the vector output’s importance, as the the invariant
and equivariant messages are depend on each other within the network, during mes-

revealed stability issues in some cases, while other approaches limited learning speed.
Normalizing with a sigmoid on the length showed good performance and stable train-
ing. In later experiments, we removed the sigmoid from the output as changes in the
action sampling function provided more stable results. In our final experiments, we
used the learnable linear combination version to reduce the equivariant output to 3D
vectors.

3.1.1
Modifications to achieve rotation and direction
To effectively handle molecules, such as water molecules, within our framework, it’s
essential to ensure that certain atoms remain fixed relative to each other in clusters.
We explored two approaches to achieve this constraint.
The first approach involved placing each atom in the 3D space while maintaining
their relative position fixed, when they are in the same cluster. We can treat the
atom clusters as rigid bodies, where equivariant vectors act like forces on these bod-
ies. This method didn’t require introducing additional node types; instead, we can
use the atomic element numbers to represent atom types in the network.
The second approach is regarding molecules as coarse-grained objects. Here, each
object has a translation vector, next to two direction vectors, which we can use to
create a 3D rotation. In this scenario, we create new embedding types to accommo-
date these new objects. Both approaches were tested, demonstrating that the second
version yielded better results.
3.1.2
Rotation based on the centroid and the cross product.
Using the first method, we do not have to make modifications to the policy directly;
we only have to change the environment. The policy will treat each atom distinct as
before, and the environment will handle the clusters, making the atoms stay fixed to
each other. This solution has the advantage that the nodes could receive messages
from the atoms in the other clusters, can generalize on the building blocks of the
clusters, and it does not require modification to the policy.

3.1.6
Environment design.
The training environment is a crucial component of the machine learning training
scheme. The Stable Baselines library provides a framework to set up an environment
in a standardized way that integrates with all other parts of the training cycle. The
environment handles two main external events:
• Reset: Initializes the node types, positions, and rotations, and determines which
nodes are fixed during training.
• Step: Handles node position changes, sets node rotations, translates abstract
molecule types to atoms, performs energy calculations, and calculates rewards
from energy at each step, along with logging.
Our environment implementation can run in modes using either vector directions or
rigid body movement and rotation prediction.
3.1.6.1
Reward design:
Designing the reward function in reinforcement learning can be challenging. In many
applications, such as board games and video games, there are predefined reward func-
tions based on game rules that assign points for different actions. In our case, we
construct a reward function based on the property of the system we want to optimize.
A well-designed reward function can significantly enhance learning speed, while a
poorly designed one can hinder the agent’s ability to learn.
For our system, we set the reward function to reflect the total energy reduction
from the initial step to the final step. If we assign rewards only at the final step, the
agent receives weak signals about its earlier actions. Therefore, we decided to provide
rewards at every step based on the energy reduction achieved from the current step.
Additionally, in some model variations, we give a significant reward at the end of
the episode to indicate the quality of the entire trajectory. We experimented with
both approaches, rewarding only at the last step and providing step-by-step rewards.
Another enhancement could involve applying molecular dynamics relaxation to the
final step configuration and using the resulting energy reduction as a reward. This
approach could improve training by leveraging conventional methods.
For reward calculation, we faced limitations due to the iterative nature of energy
calculators, which converge to a value within a predefined error margin. Unlike regu-
lar molecular dynamics simulations, our method does not consider the energy gradient
of the system, leading to unrealistic atom translations and configurations. One com-
mon unrealistic configuration is when two or more atoms are overlapping, which is
rare in molecular dynamics due to the high repulsive forces.

When the energy calculator fails to converge, we used two solutions:
• Terminate the episode: Assign 0 as the last reward when the energy calculator
fails to provide a value.
• Continue the episode: Allow the agent to continue moving atoms until a valid
energy value is obtained, and using the last known converged energy for reward
calculation.
This approach allows the agent to explore different paths to energy minimization
compared to regular molecular dynamics methods.
3.1.6.2
Fixing atoms for the model.
We added an option to fix the positions of certain atoms to facilitate feature-rich
training of setups with central molecules. This is achieved by using a mask for each
node, which allows them to be fixed and excludes them from the gradient updates of
the actor and critic. If the central molecule were treated as a single node, it would
hinder generalization, as the network would lack information about the constituent
elements. Allowing the system to move atoms within the central molecule, when we
are not using rigid body rotation, would disrupt its structure.
3.1.6.3
Action sampling modification, to be predict orientation.
Stable Baselines offers different methods for sampling new actions for the policy. The
standard approach uses a Gaussian distribution with a certain mean and standard
deviation to perturb the action vector. Initially, our model used this default struc-
ture. However, to improve training performance, we modified the action sampling
distribution to eliminate non-equivariant biases caused by clipping the action values.
The default framework clips values outside a defined box boundary, introducing a
bias. We adjusted this by normalizing the direction vectors during action sampling,
ensuring they reduce their error and move toward their normalized versions. The
translation vectors for the atoms, after we add Gaussian noise to them to sample new
actions, were normalized to unit length. If the resulting vectors were smaller than
unit length, then their length were left unchanged. This way we could avoid the bi-
ased clipping. For directional vectors we used similar procedure, that we normalized
them after sampling.

The results were compered to the best minima hopping results using normalized

Our idea was to run a quick relaxation on the output of the model and compare the
resulting RDF plots. The possible solution to dismiss the big discrepancy between the
model and the minima hopping is making a quick relaxation step on the best model
output. We used the BFGS algorithm, which is available in ASE, to make the quick
optimization. We used this algorithm with the parameter fmax = 0.05. If we run
BFGS optimization on the initial perturbed fibonacci latice configuration, we get 1-2%
normalized return, but if we run that on the structures predicted by the model, we can
get 92 - 98% normalized return. In some cases we got over 100% normalized return,
which means it resulted in a lower energy structure then the minima-hopping result.
Although it worth to note that these exceptional cases were just a 0.5% percent better
then the minima hopping best result. In the set of 70 result per central molecule we
only had one such result for 2-one-3-ynebutanol, 2-hydroxy-3-ynebutanal and Allyl
alcohol each.

CHAPTER 5
Conslusion
This study demonstrates the success of our setup for optimizing molecular configu-
rations. Our model showed significant generalization on the central molecule case.
This can make it useful for real world application. With further experimentation,
the model can potentially be adapted for various scenarios involving different types
and numbers of molecules. While the model’s current accuracy is not yet suﬀicient
for standalone real-world applications, it shows promise when combined with conven-
tional methods to accelerate molecular structure searches. When the model reaches
a significantly accurate level we could try training and evaluating the model using a
high accuracy energy calculation method like DFT. In the current state of the model
it would not benefit us to change the low accuracy and fast energy calculator to a
more accurate and slower one. In future development it could be an interesting case
to try. We could train the model until it gets close to the accuracy of the fast cal-
culator, and continue training using a more accurate energy calculator. The model
shows instability after long training and could collapse. In future work the collapse
of the training is a key problem which have to be solved.
5.1
Potential Extensions with Other Equivariant
Networks
Although the PaiNN network yielded good results, our setup is flexible enough to
accommodate different neural networks without altering the entire training framework.
Various other neural networks with E(3) equivariant properties could be explored.
Equiformer [9], inspired by the success of the Transformer architecture and attention
mechanisms [8], is a notable option. Additionally, TensorNet [7], an O(3) equivariant
network, has shown promise in accelerating molecular dynamics.
==PAGE:50==
44
5 Conslusion
5.2
Testing with Different Numbers and Types of
Molecules
It would be valuable to test the model with a larger number of molecules and with
different types of molecules to test its robustness and adaptability. The model’s node
embedding could easily handle hundreds or thousands of different type of molecules,
which makes the model capable of training on amino acids or with different organic
molecules. Because of the nature of reinforcement learning we could give more de-
grees of freedom to complex molecules by combining the environment and the reward
function with conventional methods.
5.3
Integrating BFGS Optimization at Each Step
A potential modification worth exploring is to incorporate local optimization using
the BFGS or other optimization algorithm after each step. This approach could train
the model jointly with conventional optimization methods, leveraging the flexibility
of reinforcement learning to formulate diverse reward functions. Alternatively, relax-
ation could be applied only at the final step. Some experiments were made using this
method but the model did not get to a level where it could give meaningful results,
although the evaluations during training were promising.
5.4
Optimizing for Other Properties
Given the versatility of the reinforcement learning setup, it is worthwhile to explore
reward models based on properties other than energy. This could broaden the appli-
cation scope and enhance the model’s utility in various optimization tasks. We could
add angle and shape requirements of the molecules to the model.
