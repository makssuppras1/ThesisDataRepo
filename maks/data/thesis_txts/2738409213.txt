Abstract
This thesis investigates the feasibility of establishing a sustainable district heating network
in Armentiéres to enhance energy efficiency and reduce greenhouse gas emissions through
the integration of renewable energy sources. The study assesses the heating demands of
various buildings, identifies potential renewable energy sources, and designs multiple net­
work layouts. Three distinct scenarios were analyzed: Scenario 1 with a network length of
11.9 kilometers, Scenario 2 with a network length of 8 kilometers, and Scenario 3 with a net­
work length of 6.4 kilometers. Each scenario has two configurations: Variant 1 involves using
biomass as the primary energy source with gas serving as a backup, while Variant 2 includes
biomass and the Waste Water Treatment Plant (WWTP) heat recovery system as prioritized
energy sources, with gas serving as a backup.
Using a combination of technical, economic, and environmental assessments, the results in­
dicate that Scenario 3 Variant 2 offers the optimal solution with the highest renewable energy
rate, lowest capital and operating expenses, best financial outcomes in terms of the target
heat price, and the most environmentally friendly profile due to the least CO2, dust, NOX, and
SO2 emissions. However, the adoption of Scenario 2 Variant 2 is highly recommended for
Armentiéres as it achieves a balanced approach between cost, technical feasibility, and envi­
ronmental impact. This configuration demonstrates a favorable financial profile with competi­
tive target heat prices and substantial subsidies, ensuring economic viability. It also achieves
low emissions of CO2 and other pollutants, while renewable energy covers 75.1% of the de­
mand, exceeding the objective.
The study concludes that implementing a district heating network in Armentiéres is feasible,
with Scenario 2 Variant 2 being the best choice for its results. The proposed methodology
can be adapted to other district heating network schemes in any scale or any infrastructure,
and it could be modified to integrate more aspects and constraints.
Future research should focus on improving data collection methods, refining simulation mod­
els to include more realistic assumptions, and addressing infrastructure and retrofitting chal­
lenges to ensure long­term sustainability and effectiveness.
Keywords : Sustainable energy, district heating network, renewable energy sources, biomass
energy, Waste Water Treatment Plant (WWTP), heat recovery, CO2 emissions reduction,
energy efficiency, economic viability, urban development, sustainable urban development,
energy transition.

Introduction
The growing demand for heating across a variety of industries, along with the restrictions
around greenhouse gases, led nations to explore alternate methods, such as district heating
networks (DHN), for the purpose of heating buildings in the most efficient way possible. De­
spite the fact that district heating concepts have been around for centuries, it was not until
the past twenty years that they were widely implemented as a method for energy­efficient
building heating[1].
Due to the fact that the global population is anticipated to approach 9.7 billion by the year
2050, which would result in a seventy percent increase in the number of homes, it is antici­
pated that the residential and commercial sectors will be responsible for a substantial amount
of energy consumption and CO2 emissions in Europe. Despite a predicted fifty percent in­
crease in energy demand, these numbers show the urgent need to cut CO2 emissions by half
by the year 2050, with the goal of achieving net­zero energy buildings within the commercial
real estate sector [2]. The EU has updated its energy targets, now aiming for at least a 11.7%
reduction in energy consumption and a 42.5% share of renewable energy sources by 2030,
underscoring the commitment to these goals [3, 4].
To achieve those objectives, various strategies, including integrating renewable technologies,
have been proposed to conserve energy in the building sector. DHN presents a viable so­
lution, but its global market share remains low, primarily due to the lack of tools for effective
design, analysis, and optimization.
However, the City of Armentiéres offers a unique case for DHN implementation, given its ge­
ographic and infrastructural landscape. The Lys canal, running through the city, represents a
potential source of energy. Furthermore, the presence of key social facilities, including a Hos­
pital Center and a Public Mental Health Institution across 33 hectares, highlights the demand
for an effective and dependable energy solution.The planned Vega 1965 leisure complex in
the city’s southwest introduces additional considerations, promising opportunities for sustain­
able energy integration while significantly raising the city’s energy requirements.
Recognizing these opportunities, the European Metropolis of Lille has initiated a project, en­
listing a collaboration with Manergy a company with expertise in energy transition, to perform
a thorough feasibility study for a new urban heating network in the City of Armentiéres. As
i am part of the company team, my role involves leading this investigation through all the
different phases.
This project aims to overcome some of the common barriers to DHN implementation, such as
infrastructure constraints and the need for effective assessment. The findings from this study
are expected to significantly contribute to the city’s sustainable development objectives. Thus,
this thesis stands as a contribution to the field of sustainable urban development, promising
not only to address immediate energy efficiency and sustainability challenges but also to lay
the groundwork for future initiatives in Armentiéres and beyond.

1.1
Project context
Under the guidance of Dr. Michele Tunzi and the mentorship of Mr. Alex Charpignon, the
project leader from the company ”Manergy,” we initiated a thorough feasibility study of de­
veloping a sustainable district heating network in Armentiéres, leveraging renewable energy
sources to enhance energy efficiency and reduce greenhouse gas emissions, aligning with
regional and national objectives for environmental conservation. This project is conducted
in collaboration with the European Metropolis of Lille (MEL) and the City of Armentiéres in
partnership with the company Manergy where we carried out all the study.
The company’s presentation, mission, expertise, and similar projects previously conducted
are detailed in the Appendix A.
1.2
Purpose of the Study
The objectives of this thesis project are designed to address specific research tasks and
contribute to the broader field of sustainable urban development. They are as follows:
• To evaluate the potential for sustainable district heating in Armentiéres assess the city’s
ability to support a district heating network by examining geographical, infrastructural,
and environmental factors.
• To analyze the current and future expected heating demand within the city by identifying
the consumption patterns and potential for energy savings.
• To identify and analyze Renewable Energy sources by examining Armentiéres available
energy resources and determining their suitability and capacity for integration into the
district heating system.
• To design different distribution network layout for the heating network in the city network
that connect energy sources with the end­users, incorporating strategies.
• To conduct a technical feasibility study by analyzing the technical aspects of establishing
a district heating network which encompasses network design, source identification,
and the adaptability of existing infrastructure to heating solutions.

2.1
The chronological evolution of district heating networks
2.1.1
An overview of district heating networks throughout history
District heating, the centralized production and distribution of heat to multiple buildings, has
ancient roots and has significantly evolved over time. A number of creative approaches that
were created by the Romans and other early implementations in medieval Europe can be
traced back to the origins of the concept.
1.Ancient beginnings
It is commonly believed that the Romans were the first people to design and implement a
system of district heating. Their hypocaust systems were an innovative strategy for heating
public baths as well as the residences of wealthy individuals. The system consisted of a
furnace that heated air, which was then pumped beneath raised floors and throughout the
walls through flues, thereby providing a steady level of warmth. This would result in the
heat from the furnace, which was often powered by wood or charcoal, being drawn through
these pathways, which would effectively heat extensive structures. For its time, this method
was quite effective, and it was important in laying the groundwork for later developments in
centralised heating systems [5].
Chaudes­Aigues, a small town located in the Massif Central area of France, was the site of
the first occurrence of district heating as it existed in France during the 14th century. For the
purpose of heating buildings, hot spring water was utilized. The natural geothermal resources
made it possible to establish a basic district heating system. This system consisted of hot
water distributed through wooden pipes in order to heat residences and public buildings. This
early example illustrates the use of natural resources obtained from the surrounding area for
the purpose of district heating, a practice that remains relevant even in the present day [6].
2.Advances in the 19th century
During the period of the Industrial revolution, notable technological and infrastructure break­
throughs were made, which ultimately led to the development of contemporary district heating
systems. An inventor and engineer named Birdsill Holly was responsible for the establishment
of the first modern district heating system in the year 1877 in the city of Lockport, New York.
Holly’s system was able to heat many buildings using steam that was produced in a central
boiler and distributed over a network of pipes. These findings constituted a turning point in
the history of district heating, marking the shift from methods that centered in a specific area
to an approach that was more expansive and scalable. Holly’s method demonstrated that it
is possible to heat entire neighborhoods or districts from a central source, which led to more
acceptance and advancements [7]. As a result of Holly’s revolutionary work, district heating
systems started to become more known in both the United States and Europe. Large­scale
district heating networks were created in places such as New York, Boston, and Chicago
throughout the last half of the 19th century. At the same time, towns in Europe such as Paris
and Berlin implemented systems that were quite similar to these, frequently making use of
the coal supplies that were available in the area to feed the central boilers. During this time
period, the necessity of viable heating solutions in rapidly developing urban areas was the
driving force behind the spread of district heating [8].
3.Advances in the 20th century
A substantial amount of progress was made in the field of district heating technology and
infrastructure during the 20th century, particularly in Europe, where the aftermath of World
War II drove significant innovations. Following the end of World War II, several European

2.1.2
The historical evolution of district heating networks in France
1.Early development and expansion
Paris, Strasbourg, Grenoble, and Chambéry were some of the first places in France to have
district heating networks. They were built in the 1930s. Large industry companies set up
these networks to meet the high demand for power in cities. During this time, district heat­
ing systems mostly used coal­fired stoves to make heat, which was then sent to surrounding
areas through hot water or steam pipes. For instance, one of the longest district heating net­
works, Paris, used coal­fired boilers placed near factories and homes to successfully provide
heat. [12].
2.Post­War expansion and modernization
From 1950 to 1970, France’s district heating networks grew quickly, at the same time that new
neighborhoods were being built. Boilers that burned oil or coal often drove these networks,
which brought hot or superheated water to homes and businesses. Modernized distribution
systems now have lines that are better insulated and heat transfer points that work better.
Steel pipes were used a lot, and they were often hidden to keep heat in and hide the pipes
from view. But near the end of this time period, growth slowed down because cities had too
many products on the market and the old methods didn’t have the latest technology. [13].
3.Diversification and technological advances
In response to the oil shocks of the 1970s, the government changed the country’s energy mix.
This led to an increased interest in district heating networks and a change in the types of heat
sources used in the 1980s. Deep geothermal energy use became popular, especially in the

2.4
Principles and components of district heating networks
2.4.1
Principle and functioning
District heating network (also known as heating networks or district heating) is a system for
distributing the heat generated in a central location via a system of insulated pipes for residen­
tial and commercial heating requirements, such as space heating and hot water generation,
it generally includes :
• A central heating plant converts one or more primary energy sources into heat energy.
It produces thermal energy, which will be distributed throughout the network.[44]
• A distribution network for the heat transfer fluid (usually pressurized steam or hot water).
The underground pipes in this system are highly insulated to reduce heat loss during
the transportation process, ensuring that the end users receive the maximum heat.[8]
• District heating Substations (Network Substations) are responsible for transferring heat
from the distribution network to the internal heating systems of the buildings. Their
main role is the supply of heat to different types of final consumers, such as industries,
municipal buildings, hospitals, commercial establishments, and residential houses.[45]

2.4.2
Composition of a district heating network
Every district heating network includes the following main elements, as depicted in Figure 2.7
• The Heat production unit: can be, for instance, a waste incineration plant (WIW), an
oil, gas, wood, etc.­fueled boiler, or a deep geothermal power plant. Typically, a network
includes a primary unit that operates continuously, and a backup unit used to reinforce
during peak hours or as a replacement when necessary [47, 48].
• primary distribution network consists of pipelines through which a heat transfer fluid
(steam or hot water) transports heat. A supply circuit carries the hot fluid from the pro­
duction unit. A return circuit brings back the fluid, which has released its heat (negative
enthalpy change) at the exchange substations. The central heating plant then sends
the fluid back into the circuit after reheating it (creating a positive enthalpy change). The
network is designed to ensure a high thermal density (number of connected buildings
per kilometer of pipeline) to maintain the network’s economic viability (investment cost
is highly related to pipeline length; revenue is linked to the number of users) [49].
• Exchange Substations: located at the base of buildings, facilitate the transfer of heat
via an exchanger between the primary distribution network and the secondary distri­
bution network, which supplies the emitters within a building. Legally, the secondary
network is not part of the district heating network, as it is managed not by the district
heating network operator but by the building manager [48].

The heat production unit
The way heat production units are built and how they work are very important for making sure
that urban heating networks have a stable and efficient energy source. These units are built to
be resilient and dependable, which are important qualities to make sure that the energy source
stays on. Many energy sources, such as conventional fossil fuels, renewable energy sources,
and recovery energy, can provide heat, each of these sources adds something special to the
total energy mix.
Conventional energy sources
Traditional energy sources, such as natural gas and fuel
oil, generate heat by undergoing combustion, which is a chemical reaction that releases a
substantial quantity of greenhouse gases. Although they have a negative influence on the
environment, these energy sources are highly efficient in meeting high heat demands dur­
ing peak times because of their high energy density and well­developed infrastructure. One
example is the extensive usage of natural gas due to its widespread availability and compar­
atively lower carbon dioxide emissions in comparison to coal and oil [51]. Modern systems
generally optimize the utilization of these fuels to improve efficiency and reduce pollution.
Renewable energy sources
Renewable energy sources are becoming more popular in
heat production units as part of worldwide efforts to minimize greenhouse gas emissions.
Biomass and deep geothermal energy are the two most common renewable sources used in
these systems.
• Biomass energy: The combustion process that takes place in boilers made specifi­
cally for this purpose produces heat from biomass, which includes wood, agricultural
waste, and energy crops. The carbon dioxide that is released during the burning pro­
cess is compensated for by the carbon dioxide that is absorbed during the growth of the
14
A feasibility study on developing a sustainable district heating network in Armentiéres
==PAGE:27==
biomass, which is why biomass is regarded as carbon neutral [52]. When compared
to fossil fuels, biomass is a more environmentally friendly alternative because modern
boilers for biomass are fitted with sophisticated emission control systems that reduce
the amount of pollutants produced. In rural and forested areas where biomass is easily
available, this technology will prove to be extremely useful.
• Deep geothermal energy: Deep geothermal energy refers to the process of obtaining
heat from deep aquifers, usually located at depths of at least 1500 meters. The move­
ment of heat is facilitated using heat exchangers in order to establish a reliable and
environmentally­friendly heat supply. Geothermal energy systems possess a minimal
ecological footprint and have the capability to offer consistent heating, known as base­
load heating, which is crucial for maintaining the stability and dependability of urban
heat networks [53].
Recovery energy
Recovery energy is designed to make use of waste heat generated by a
variety of processes, such as the burning of garbage and operations carried out in industrial
sectors.Waste­to­energy (WtE) plants, for example, are responsible for the incineration of
municipal solid waste, which results in the production of heat that can be added to district
heating systems. As an additional point of interest, industrial facilities like refineries and gas
compression plants generate quantities of waste heat, which can be reclaimed and utilized for
heating purposes [54]. By using heat that would have been wasted, these recovery systems
not only save energy but also help cut down on waste and protect the environment.
2.4.3
The primary distribution network
The primary distribution network forms a loop that carries the heat transfer fluid from the heat
production unit to the exchange substations. Three types of fluids are used.
Types of heat transfer fluids
• Hot water network: The hot water network has a temperature range between 60°C
and 110°C. It is mainly used for residential or office buildings, as well as hospitals and
industrial establishments that do not consume steam. This type of network is suitable
for moderate heating needs and is often chosen for its efficiency and safety in residential
and tertiary environments [55].
• Super heated water network: The super heated water network has a temperature
range between 110°C and 180°C, under a pressure exceeding 4 bars. It is mainly used
in large­scale networks that supply buildings requiring high temperatures, such as laun­
dries, slaughterhouses, textile industries, etc. This network meets the more demanding
industrial needs in terms of heat and pressure, ensuring efficient heat transfer over long
distances [56].
• Steam network: The steam network has a temperature range of 200°C to 300°C. Its
use is increasingly limited. It is mainly present for industrial heat supply, but Paris uses
it for its district heating network (CPCU network). This type of network is suitable for
applications requiring very high temperatures, although its use is declining in favor of
more modern and efficient systems [57].

Mechanisms of heat transfer
The heat transfer between the primary and secondary networks can be achieved through a
heat exchanger.
2.Heat exchanger
A heat exchanger transfers heat without mixing the fluids. It typically
consists of plates or tubes where hot water from the primary network flows on one side, and
the cooler return water from the secondary network flows on the other side. The heat from
the primary network is transferred to the secondary network through the walls of the plates
or tubes.
Operation of a heat exchanger
• Primary network flow: Hot water from the district heating network flows into the heat
exchanger.
• Secondary network return flow: Cooler return water from the building’s heating sys­
tem flows into the heat exchanger on the opposite side.
• Heat transfer: Heat is transferred from the hot water to the cooler water through the
walls of the heat exchanger without mixing the two fluids.
• Distribution to secondary network: The heated water from the secondary side of the
heat exchanger is circulated back into the building’s heating system.

arrows show the flow directions.

Common types of heat exchangers
• 1.Plate and frame heat exchangers: Consisting of many thin plates that work together

Components of a heat exchange substation

• Control valves: Regulate the flow of water through the heat exchanger, ensuring that

• Control of energy costs: More control and predictability over energy expenditures are

Energy efficiency

Safety, space, and maintenance
Buildings that are part of a district heating network have extremely high levels of heat supply

2.6
Drawbacks and limitations of district heating networks
Despite the numerous advantages of district heating networks, they face several significant
drawbacks and limitations that impact their implementation and efficiency:
• High initial costs: A significant amount of financial expenditure is needed for infras­
tructure when establishing district heating networks. This includes creating substations,
constructing centralized production facilities, and laying insulated pipes. The upfront
costs may be expensive, particularly in areas with little financing and subsidies. For
instance, the EU projects that billions of euros in investments will be required to signifi­
cantly decrease natural gas usage through district heating.[74, 75].

• Infrastructure and maintenance: The extensive network of pipelines and industrial fa­
cilities requires complicated and expensive maintenance. Leak prevention and effective
functioning depend on routine maintenance and inspections. To satisfy contemporary
efficiency and environmental regulations, older systems would need to undergo major
improvements.[76, 77].
• Heat Losses: District heating systems can lose heat, even with the best protection,
especially when the heat is sent over long distances. This is a bigger problem in places
with fewer people because buildings are farther apart, which makes the system less
efficient generally.[74, 74].
• Regulatory and institutional challenges: The rules for district heating are compli­
cated and vary a lot from region to region. The growth and progress of district heating
networks may not always be supported by those rules. Issues such as price regulation,
third­party access, and the natural monopoly status of district heating companies can
create barriers to entry and operational inefficiencies[76, 77].
• Consumer protection and pricing: There is often little protection or control for con­
sumers living in properties connected to district heating networks. Many consumers
lack the ability to switch providers, and pricing can be less competitive compared to al­
ternative heating options. Recent data shows that some consumers have experienced
heat network price rises of up to 700%, highlighting the need for better regulatory frame­
works and consumer protections[75, 77].
• Space Requirements: District heating systems require space for combined heat and
power (CHP) plants and large­diameter insulated pipes. The need for an ”energy cen­
ter” and the infrastructure for the hot water network can be significant, especially in
urban areas[74].
District heating is still an essential part of sustainable urban energy systems, despite those
limitations. The promise of district heating networks may be fully realized in the future by
addressing these disadvantages through technological innovation, regulatory assistance, and
customer participation.

To normalize data based on climatic variations, the concept of Unified Degree Day (UDD) has
been used. UDD is a measurement used to quantify the demand for heating energy relative
to the outside temperature. It essentially represents the difference between a base tempera­
ture and the daily average temperature, summed over a specific period. For instance, if the
base temperature is set at 18°C, and the average temperature for a day is 10°C, the UDD
for that day would be 8, it helps in understanding how much heating energy is required to
maintain a comfortable indoor temperature.
The reference UDD is a standard value established to enable comparisons of energy needs
across different years. By using a reference UDD, one can determine whether a particular
year’s energy consumption is higher or lower than average due to temperature variations.
Lastly, the annual UDD is the actual UDD calculated for a specific year. It reflects the real
heating energy demand based on that year’s temperature data.

4.1.3
Criteria for selecting prospects
The choice of a potential building is based on two main criteria. First, the prospect must use
collective gas for its heating and domestic hot water (DHW) needs. Additionally, the tempera­
ture regime at which the building’s emitters (such as radiators or underfloor heating systems)
operate must align with the district heating network’s temperature regime which typically op­
erates between thirty and one hundred degrees Celsius (30 and 100°C) on the network side.
However, it is important to note that the emitters within the buildings usually operate at tem­
peratures above 75°C.
The anticipated heating and DHW needs must be supplied by collective gas meaning that
these buildings already have a centralized system for distributing heat, making them easier
to convert to or integrate with a DHN. As a fact, connecting a prospect using individual gas
would be more costly and complex, as it would require the installation of new pipelines and
a new exchanger room to distribute heat within the building. This does mean that prospects
using individual gas are excluded, as their connection presents additional challenges.
Additionally, the outlook for buildings that use gas air heaters for heating is not as good.
These systems are usually built to work on their own, using direct gas combustion to produce
heat. Connecting them to a district heating network would mean major changes to the build­
ing’s current heating system, as well as the costs of making the building’s gas air heaters
compatible with the district heating system.
On the other hand, industrial prospects whose process requires steam­type heat, with a tem­
perature regime higher than that of the potential network (more than one hundred ten degrees
Celsius), cannot be connected to the network. Steam is no longer used for district heating
networks due to its high temperature, making these prospects incompatible with the standard
district heating network.

4.1.4
Use of renewable sources and heat recovery
The purpose of this step is to find heat sources in the area that can be used in a district heating
network. Building a network that works well depends on both having the right infrastructure
and finding and making the best use of energy sources that are already there.
It’s important to think about both sustainable energies (RE) like geothermal, solar thermal, and
biomass, as well as heat recovery sources (HR) like waste heat from industrial processes.
Waste heat is the thermal energy that is lost in manufacturing processes but can be collected
and used to heat cities again.
Before picking an energy source, it’s important to rank the choices based on cost, efficiency,
long­term viability, and impact on the environment. These objectives might change based on
how things are in Armentiéres.
We followed the ADEME’s (French environment and energy management agency) prioritiza­
tion method for energy sources,also referred to as the ”energy hierarchy” or ”energy pyra­
mid.”This method is meant to help use energy in a more safe and smart way. This method is
set up in a way that encourages using the greenest and most efficient energy sources first.
This lowers the total carbon footprint and encourages sustainability. Here is a more in­depth
description of the pyramid’s structure and levels:
• Connection to existing networks: The first priority is to check the possibility of con­
necting the new district heating network to an existing network. This improves the equip­
ment that is already there and lowers the cost of installation
• Non­relocatable and existing energies: If connecting to an existing network isn’t
available, the next step is to use existing energies that can’t be moved, like waste heat.
These sources are cost­ and energy­effective because they use energy that is already
available in the area without building a lot of new infrastructure.
• Creation of non­relocatable energies: Next, the creation of non­relocatable energies,
such as geothermal energy, is considered.Even though they require big investments at
first, these energy sources are sustainable and stable in the long run.
• Relocatable energies to create: Lastly, it is preferred to use fuels that can be moved
around, like solar thermal and biomass. Even though they might not be as efficient as
sources that can’t be moved, they are still good choices in places where other sources
aren’t available or aren’t enough.
Following this hierarchy is essential to getting subsidies and support from ADEME, as it
demonstrates a commitment to reducing environmental impact and promoting sustainable
energy practices.
Choice of heat sources
The heat sources chosen for the project are biomass and wastewater treatment plants (WWTP).
These sources were picked based on a number of practical and theoretical considerations.
Heat recovery from WWTPs as they produce significant waste heat that can be efficiently
captured and reused, reducing greenhouse gas emissions and reliance on fossil fuels. This
method is cost­effective, as it utilizes existing heat production processes, and aligns with
sustainability and regulatory goals. Additionally, WWTPs are located near urban areas, min­
imizing heat transmission losses and enhancing the overall efficiency of the DHN.
Biomass has been selected for the project due to its environmental benefits and abundant
availability, it is an important part of the region’s plan for sustainability. Our energy needs

heating network, but the infrastructure might need to be more extensive to cover all demands.

of a centralized urban heating network. These zones are often rural or semi­rural with widely
dispersed buildings, making centralized heat distribution inefficient. Other solutions, such as
individual heating systems or micro heat networks, would be more appropriate here.

3.Consumer modeling tab
Consumer modeling involves recording connection dates, renovation periods, and energy
savings. Heating needs are defined by consumption distribution throughout the year for each
structure, considering non­heating temperatures (18°C). In this part, we focused on defining
and understanding the primary and secondary heating systems and their respective temper­
ature regimes associated with the conditions at the substation level.
The primary heating system is the main source of heat for the network of buildings. It is
responsible for generating and distributing hot water throughout the system. This system
typically includes a heat source such as a boiler, and the water it produces is circulated
through a primary loop. So here the primary system for DHW typically refers to the part of
the system that heats the water and in our case we chose a high­temperature regime for this
system as the primary supply and return temperatures for DHW are set at 65°C for supply,
55°C for return outside drawing periods, and 40°C during maximum drawing.These choices
are guided by several key considerations.
For the supply and return temperatures the reasons of our choice are the following:
Firstly, to ensure safety and hygiene, the DHW needs to be maintained at a minimum tem­
perature of around 60°C. This is essential to prevent the growth of Legionella bacteria, which
can pose a health risk if allowed to proliferate in the water system and following the french
regulation that states that the DHW in the circulation loop needs to be kept at a minimum tem­
perature of 50°C and preferably 55°C to prevent the growth of bacteria such as Legionella.[79]
[80]. Secondly, we’ve chosen high temperatures to enhance the efficiency of heat distribu­
tion. This is particularly important in our district heating networks that cover large area and
serve a variety of building types and a higher temperature regime would allow for more effec­
tive heat transfer, ensuring that all parts of the network receive sufficient heat.
Lastly, our choice of temperature regime is also influenced by the characteristics of our heat
sources.Biomass boilers and heat recovery systems, operate efficiently at high temperatures,
typically between 80°C and 95°C. By aligning the primary system’s temperature with these
sources, we can maximize their efficiency and output, leading to a more effective and sus­
tainable heating system.
The choice of a 40°C temperature during maximum drawing in the primary system for Do­
mestic Hot Water (DHW) is influenced by several considerations.
Firstly, a temperature of 40°C is generally deemed comfortable for most domestic hot water
uses, such as showering or washing hands, contributing to user comfort.
Secondly, operating at this lower temperature during periods of maximum drawing can en­
hance the system’s energy efficiency. This is because heating the water to 40°C requires less
energy compared to higher temperatures, leading to energy savings. Lastly, this temperature
helps maintain a balance in the system’s performance during peak usage times. It ensures
that the system can meet the high demand for hot water without overheating or overloading,
contributing to the overall stability and reliability of the system.[81]
The secondary heating system associated with the end user level within each building. is
responsible for distributing the heat generated by the primary system to various end­use ap­
plications within the building, such as radiators, underfloor heating, or air handling units. This
system operates independently from the primary loop, allowing for different temperature set­
tings and flow rates to suit specific heating requirements.The secondary system in this model
is chosen to operate with a high­temperature regime as well with a supply and return temper­
atures (80°C and 60°C at full load, 20°C at zero load) and a 2°C pinch point at full load for
both supply and return.
The need for higher temperatures in these systems arises from several factors:

Firstly, traditional radiators were designed to function with high­temperature water. This is
due to their lower efficiency at heat transfer compared to modern designs, and thus, higher

initial data that we had that the majority of the city buildings operate with traditional emitters.
Secondly, older buildings, which these systems often serve, typically have less insulation.

Lastly, the design of older heating systems often incorporates a significant temperature differ­

Supply Temperature: The temperature at which the heated water is delivered to the build­
ing’s heating system.
The supply temperature (Tsupply) at any given time t is dictated by the highest tempera­
ture required by all consumers connected to the network. This approach ensures that even
the consumer with the highest heating requirement receives sufficient heat. Typically, the
required supply temperature varies with the external temperature, increasing as the out­
side temperature drops to maintain adequate heating. These temperature calculations are
for managing heating and DHW systems efficiently. By dynamically adjusting temperatures
based on demand and external conditions.
Return Temperature: The temperature of the water returning from the building’s heating
system back to the heat exchanger or the district heating network.
1.Domestic hot Water (DHW) return temperature: The return temperature of DHW varies
according to the level of usage. Specifically, two key scenarios are considered:
Minimum drawing periods (Low usage): : During periods of low or no DHW usage, the
return temperature tends to be higher because the water in the circulation loop is mostly
maintained at a set temperature with minimal introduction of cold water.
Maximum drawing periods (High usage):During peak DHW usage periods, the return tem­
perature tends to be lower. This is because a significant amount of cold water is introduced
into the system, which mixes with the hot water and lowers the return temperature.
The return temperature at any given time t is determined through linear interpolation between
the minimum and maximum drawing periods. This interpolation is based on the current level
of DHW usage, providing a dynamic response to changing demand.
During periods of low demand, there is less cold water entering the system. This means that
the water circulating in the system is not being cooled down as much by the cold water. As a
result, the return temperature approaches the higher end of the spectrum, reflecting minimal
cold water influx and primarily maintaining circulation14.
On the other hand, during periods of high demand, more cold water is typically introduced into
the system12. This cold water absorbs heat, reducing the temperature of the water returning

water that needs to be heated.
This dynamic adjustment of return temperatures allows the district heating network to respond
efficiently to changing demand, ensuring optimal operation and energy efficiency.
2.Heating return temperature:The return temperature for heating systems also fluctuates
based on the load. The key conditions are:
• Full load:The return temperature is relatively high when the heating system operates
at full capacity.This is because the system is working hard to maintain the desired in­

significant amount of heat from the building.
• Zero load: When there is no heating demand (i.e., when the external temperature is
equal to the non­heating temperature), the return temperature is significantly lower.This
is because the water circulating in the system is not absorbing much heat from the
building, so it returns to the boiler or heat source at a lower temperature.

Prioritizing energy sources,the configuration of producers, such as biomass boilers, heat

We have three scenarios, each with two settings. In each scenario, setting 1 involves using
biomass as the primary energy source with gas serving as a backup. This means that the
energy demand is primarily met through biomass, and gas is used only when biomass cannot
meet the demand.
In setting 2 of each scenario, the primary energy sources are biomass and the heat recovery
system integrated into the wastewater treatment process. Gas is again used as a backup

impacts of integrating these energy sources.
The software defines a fixed priority order for the different energy productions. It starts with
the productions that depend on temperatures, then moves on to temperature­independent
productions like biomass or gas.
For temperature­independent productions, the software is based on simple rules:
• If the power demanded is less than the minimum power, it moves on to the next pro­
duction.
• If the power demanded is between the minimum and maximum, it produces between
these two values.
• If the power demanded is greater, it produces at maximum.
For temperature­dependent productions, the software adds additional parameters such as a
minimum and maximum operating time, to avoid too frequent starts/stops.
In your case, the heat recovery system integrated into the wastewater treatment process is
prioritized first. This means that the system will first try to meet the energy demand using the

Heat production Calculation
The software automates the calculation of heat production from multiple sources through
several key steps. It begins by collecting data on power production from various sources,
outside temperatures, and other relevant parameters, organizing this data chronologically
and segmenting it into specific time intervals, typically on a hourly basis. For each interval,
the software calculates the total power production by summing the power produced by each
source using the formula:
Total Power Production = Power from Source 1+Power from Source 2+Power from Source 3
(4.10)

the formula:
P = m · CP · ∆T
(4.11)
where m is the mass flow rate, CP is the specific heat capacity, and ∆T is the temperature
difference. This analysis helps understand how temperature variations impact power demand
and supply. The software identifies trends and patterns by comparing hourly power produc­
tion values with corresponding hourly average outside temperatures. Finally, the software
compiles and reports the hourly power production from each source and the total production,
providing insights into the system’s performance and efficiency over time,using the prioritiza­
tion method that was defined in the input for the calculations of the productions.

To evaluate the economic performance based on the heat sale price in our project, we com­
pare different scenarios and variants putting the Internal Rate of Return (IRR) at 6.5% for all
cases,to just examining the heat prices.
The Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is a financial metric used to evaluate the profitability and
efficiency of an investment or project. It represents the discount rate at which the net present
value (NPV) of all cash flows (both incoming and outgoing) from an investment equals zero.
In simpler terms, IRR is the rate of return at which an investment breaks even in terms of net
present value.
For each solution studied, a projected profit and loss account has been established to provide
a detailed financial outlook. The business plan included in the Appendix D supports these
analyses and ensures comprehensive planning [82, 83].
4.5
Environmental impact assessment
The environmental impact assessment of the district heating network project in Armentiéres
involved a systematic approach, focusing on determining particularly the CO2 emissions us­
ing the classical method and the life cycle method and then other by­product pollutant emis­
sions of the network. Each step was designed to quantify the project’s environmental impact.
4.5.1
CO2 Emissions Assessment
1.The classical method
These are the direct emissions produced during the operation of the heating network. This
includes the CO2 released from burning fuels or other energy sources used to generate heat.
CO2 emissions = Energy consumed (MWh) × Boiler efficiency × CO2 Emission factor
(4.39)
Where:
• Energy Consumed (MWh useful): The amount of useful energy consumed.
• Boiler Efficiency: The efficiency of the boiler used in the heating network.
• CO2 Emission Factor (t/MWh PCI): The CO2 emissions per unit of energy (in tons per
MWh PCI) for each energy source.
Hypotheses for energy sources:

• Boiler efficiency: 0.85
• CO2 Emission factor (t/MWh PCI): 0
For fossil gas (Natural gas):
• Boiler efficiency: 0.9
• CO2 Emission factor (t/MWh PCI): 0.205
For the heat pump (Electric input):

• Boiler efficiency: Not applicable (as it directly uses electricity) but for reference, a coef­
ficient of performance (COP) is considered.
• CO2 Emission Factor (t/MWh PCI): 0.079
2.Life cycle emissions (LCA CO2)
These emissions have all stages of the heating network’s life, from production and installation
to operation and decommissioning. This approach ensures that all environmental impacts,
including those from manufacturing materials and energy used during the network’s lifespan,
are considered, but in our case we only considered the CO2 emissions.
LCA CO2 emissions =
∑
(Energy consumed (MWh) × Life cycle CO2 emission factor)
(4.40)
Where:
• Energy consumed (MWh): The total energy consumed over the life cycle.
• Life cycle CO2 Emission factor (tCO2/MWh): The CO2 emissions per unit of energy (in
tons per MWh) for each energy source, considering the entire life cycle.
Life cycle emission factors for different energy sources are given. For instance:
• Solid Biomass (Wood): 0.013 tCO2/MWh
• Fossil­based Gas (Natural Gas): 0.227 tCO2/MWh
• Heat pump (Electric input): 0.079 tCO2/MWh
4.5.2
CO2 Emissions avoided through Renewable Energy Integration
After that the goal was to quantify the CO2 emissions avoided by the use of renewable energy
sources in the DHN by comparing all the scenarios to a scenario where 100% of the energy
demand is met using natural gas in the year 2035. This comparison highlights the environ­
mental benefits of implementing renewable energy solutions, and to see which scenario is
more friendly to the environment. To determine the CO2 emissions avoided we subtract the
CO2 emissions of the renewable scenario from the baseline CO2 emissions (which uses only
natural gas).
CO2 Avoided = CO2 EmissionsBaseline −CO2 EmissionsScenario
(4.41)
• CO2 EmissionsBaseline: The total CO2 emissions if 100% of the energy demand is met
using natural gas.
• CO2 EmissionsScenario: The total CO2 emissions for the district heating network using
a mix of renewable energy sources and natural gas.
By calculating the difference between these two values, we can quantify the amount of CO2
emissions avoided.

6.1
Prospection
The prospection phase of the study involved identifying suitable organizations and collecting
detailed energy consumption data to assess the city’s energy needs. While the systematic
classification of buildings ensured targeted data collection. Yet,excluding non­responsive
organizations could result in missing suitable candidates in this project.
Data processing involved separating heating and domestic hot water (DHW) consumption by

6.3
The technical feasibility analysis
The technical feasibility study used Simulix, a simulation program created by Manergy, to do
operational estimates for district heating networks hour by hour. This software was picked
because it can predict how well a heating network will work in different situations by com­
bining complex data like climate data, network parameters, and consumer profiles to predict
heating network performance under various scenarios.
The study assumed the use of modern pre­insulated networks with an average thermal loss
coefficient of 0.2 W/m2/°C across all scenarios. This assumption aimed to reflect realistic and
efficient insulation standards. The use of a weighted average method to determine thermal
loss coefficients based on pipe lengths and their specific properties ensured accuracy in ther­
mal loss assessments. However, this assumption might not account for potential variances
in actual installation conditions,in practice, pipes might have different diameters, materials,
and insulation levels, which can affect their thermal loss coefficients.
Climatic data configuration was based on selecting a weather file corresponding to the geo­
graphic area, adjusted to a base temperature of ­9°C and 2500 Heating Degree Days (HDD).
This approach provides a conservative estimate.While it ensures that the system is capable
of handling the worst­case scenario, it could lead to an overestimation of heating demand,
especially in milder winters.
The methodology used simplified load profiles and does not account for night, weekend, and
school holiday reductions. While this approach simplify the simulation, it may overlook sig­
nificant variations in energy demand that could affect system performance and efficiency.
The primary heating system was set with supply and return temperatures for domestic hot
water (DHW) at 65°C and 55°C, respectively. The secondary heating system operated at
higher temperatures (80°C and 60°C) in convenience with the traditional radiators and less
insulated buildings of the city of Armentiéres.
This configuration classifies the network as a third­generation (3G) district heating system
which was chosen to avoid the need for expensive upgrades or replacements, making the
transition to district heating more feasible for many consumers as we know from the data
analysis that most building are very old and did not have recent renovations. However, it is
essential to recognize the challenges that accompany this system, thermal losses, energy
efficiency concerns, and the need for future­proofing must be addressed to ensure viability.
While 3G DH systems are well­suited for the current infrastructure, there is a growing trend
towards fourth­generation (4G) and fifth­generation (5G) district heating systems, which op­
erate at lower temperatures (typically below 50°C). These future systems promise higher
efficiency and greater integration with renewable energy sources as mentioned in the liter­
ature review. Transitioning from 3G to 4G or 5G systems in the future of Armentiéres may
require additional investments and should be studied.
The simulations for different scenarios provided insights into the performance of the district
heating network over time, focusing in the results on projected energy consumption for 2035.

6.8
Limitations
In this thesis, some limitations were discovered during simulations and after analysing the
data:
Data collection and accuracy
The data collection phase took a significant amount of time and proved to be less effective
than anticipated. Not all identified consumers responded to our calls or emails, and we were
unable to obtain comprehensive data from organizations that could potentially be connected
to the network and have substantial needs. If we had been able to include these consumers
in our data set, the study would have been more accurate.
Accuracy of data
Our feasibility study depends a lot on getting correct information about factors like heating
needs, geography, and infrastructure. Even though we used a number of tools to improve
this data, any mistakes or differences could have a big impact on the results of the study. For
98
A feasibility study on developing a sustainable district heating network in Armentiéres
==PAGE:111==
example, changes in the way buildings use energy or changes to infrastructure that aren’t
recorded could cause mistakes in network design and predictions of how efficient it will be.
We are aware of this limitation and agree that even though we do our best to make sure the
data is correct, mistakes could happen and change our results.
Simulation constraints
Software limitations
The economic and technical models were run with certain software tools and assumptions that
might not fully reflect how complicated things are in the real world. Simulix software was used
to model different situations, but the accuracy of these models relies on the assumptions and
parameters that were set in the software, which might not include all the possible variables
and unknowns.
Network design assumptions
Idealized conditions
The network design assumptions were based on ideal conditions, such as building distribution
and optimal pipeline routing. It’s possible that the simulation gave an overly optimistic view of
network designs because it didn’t fully take into account real­world limits like physical barriers,
property lines, and existing underground utilities.
Economic assumptions
Static economic conditions
The economic analysis suggests that prices for energy, inflation rates, and subsidy levels will
stay the same. These things can change because of how the market works, when policies
change, and when the economy goes up and down. Because the economic assumptions are
fixed, the long­term financial viability of the suggested scenarios may not be fully shown.
Lack of sensitivity analysis
The economic analysis did not include a comprehensive sensitivity analysis to account for
uncertainties in key financial parameters such as energy prices, interest rates, and subsidy
levels .
Retrofitting Challenges
The feasibility study may have overlooked or not thoroughly considered the financial and
practical difficulties involved in updating existing buildings so they can be linked to the district
heating network. Specifically, the study might assume that it is easier and cheaper to integrate
these buildings than it actually is. This could lead to unrealistic expectations about the cost
and effort required for retrofitting, potentially impacting the overall accuracy.
Environmental impact simplifications
Simplified CO2 calculations
The environmental impact assessment, particularly the CO2 emissions calculations, relies
on certain assumptions and simplifications. For instance, the lifecycle analysis of emissions
may not fully capture indirect emissions from all stages of the energy supply chain, such as
transportation and manufacturing of biomass fuel.
Assumptions on renewable sources
The environmental impact study also assumed that biomass and other renewable energy
sources would work equally well over time. Because things like problems in the biomass
supply chain, changes in fuel quality, and new rules about the environment weren’t taken into
account, real emissions could be different from what was calculated.

Conclusion and Future Work
In this study, a combination of technical, economic, and environmental assessments was
used to evaluate the feasibility of various district heating network designs for the city of Ar­
mentières. The primary objective was to determine the most suitable network configuration
that could meet the city’s heating demands while minimizing environmental impact. The study
spanned from identifying heating needs to designing network layouts and assessing feasibil­
ity from multiple perspectives.
It revealed that Scenario 3, particularly Variant 2, emerged as the most favorable option in
terms of cost­effectiveness and sustainability. This scenario offered the lowest CO2 emis­
sions, highest renewable energy integration, and optimal operational efficiency. However,
despite its advantages, Scenario 3 is limited in terms of coverage area and could presented
challenges for broader adoption.
Scenario 1 provided extensive coverage and the highest energy production but was burdened
by significant investment and operational costs. This scenario’s higher CO2 emissions than
the other scenarios and complex infrastructure requirements made it less desirable for long­
term sustainability.
Scenario 2, especially Variant 2, presented a balanced approach, offering moderate invest­
ment and operational costs while incorporating a Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) recov­
ery system to enhance resilience and flexibility. This scenario maintained a high renewable
energy rate and efficient heat distribution, making it a viable and practical choice for the city’s
specific needs.
While the study did not reject the main hypotheses regarding the superiority of Scenario 3
variant 2, it highlighted the practical benefits of adopting Scenario 2, Variant 2, for Armen­
tières. This variant ensures an adaptable district heating network capable of meeting current
demands. It provides a necessary balance between environmental performance, economic
feasibility, and coverage efficiency, making it the most practical choice for our project.
In summary, this feasibility study provides a solid foundation for implementing a sustainable
district heating network in Armentières. It offers valuable insights into optimizing network
designs and integrating renewable energy sources, contributing significantly to the city’s sus­
tainable development goals.
Future research and development should focus on refining these findings,using updated as­
sumptions, addressing the identified limitations, and exploring further innovations to enhance
the district heating network’s performance and sustainability. Specifically, it could explore,
the incorporation of additional renewable energy sources and strategies to lower the network
temperatures to improve efficiency.To assess how this 3G network could upgrate for 4G and
if it is feasible. These steps will not only enhance the performance of the district heating net­
work but also ensure its long­term sustainability and adaptability to evolving energy demands
and technological advancements.
