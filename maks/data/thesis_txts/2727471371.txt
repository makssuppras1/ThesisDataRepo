Fatigue Life Estimation of Steel Joint
Abstract
The aim of this master’s thesis is to employ fracture mechanics to study the fatigue life of steel
joints subjected to a time-varying force.
This investigation specifically seeks to provide insight
into the methodology of applying fracture mechanics for fatigue lifetime predictions. The primary
purpose of the current study is to demonstrate this method, by a combined use of well-founded
analytical work and detailed finite element simulation. To obtain this, a study of the critical joints
in two frame structures is conducted. The study focuses on tubular joints in offshore structures.
The global response of the structures is obtained by the open-source software OpenSees, which
facilitates transferring of the sectional forces to a detailed finite element model of the examined joint
in the commercial software Ansys. The crack propagation is calculated using Ansys SMART Crack
Growth (Ansys SMART). Initially, a validation of Ansys SMART is executed using a through-the-
thickness crack in a three-point bending specimen (3PB) and a semi-elliptic surface crack in a plate
subjected to tension (TP). The numerical findings obtained via Ansys SMART closely correspond to
the analytical calculations, indicating a reliable agreement between the two approaches. Regarding
the crack propagation, the maximum difference is below 1 % and between 4 % - 7.5 % for the
3PB and TP, respectively. Next, OpenSees is utilised to model a simple frame to determine the
global response when subjected to a simulated wave load. The global response is transferred to a
detailed finite element model in Ansys of the structures’ K-joint. This facilitates a crack propagation
simulation based on fracture mechanics of an assumed initial crack with the critical forces applied.
A similar methodology is used to evaluate a TK-joint in a more realistic jacket structure.
The
computation time of the numerical analyses was found to be comprehensive, therefore, no fracture
criteria have been reached for the K-joint and TK-joint models due to the time limit of this study.
By the investigation of the K-joint and TK-joint, it becomes evident that the propagation rates
increase with the crack size. It is observed, that the number of cycles is relatively steady towards
the end results where the crack grows rapidly. This number of cycles and the initial crack sizes of
ai = 4.02 mm and 2ci = 9.12 mm yield a fatigue lifetime of approximately 42 years for the K-joint.
For the TK-joint with initial crack sizes of ai = 0.74 mm and 2ci = 1.64 mm, the estimated fatigue
lifetime will be approximately 8 years. These crack sizes are the minimum values inducing stress
intensity factors above the threshold value. The associated sensitivity analyses for both joints found
that the lifetime is highly dependent on the initial crack.

Introduction
The application of fracture mechanics principles in fatigue life estimations has led to significant
improvements in the safety and reliability of structures and materials and continues to be an active
area of research and development in engineering.
Fracture mechanics is essential in the design of safe and reliable structures, as it enables engi-
neers to predict the behaviour of materials and structures under real-world loading conditions. By
understanding the mechanisms of crack growth and fractures, engineers can design structures with
appropriate safety margins and take measures to prevent crack initiation and propagation.
Offshore wind turbines (OWT) have experienced increasing interest and demand in recent years
[1] [2].
The development has been rapid with growing turbine size and wind farms seizing ar-
eas with increasingly deeper seabeds [3]. The requirements of the structures are likewise growing,
with the support structures of the OWT of particular interest given their essential contribution to
cost-effective installations [4]. Usually, monopile foundations are used for near-shore wind farms.
Nevertheless, for larger wind turbines and in deeper water, the monopiles are substituted by space
frames, such as jacket structures, see Figure 1.1. These jacket structures are lightweight and stiffer
than monopiles, which can become immense and impractical in deeper water. Furthermore, the
jacket structures are possibly the cheaper choice in deeper water [2].
Figure 1.1: Offshore wind turbine with jacket support structure [5].
The engineering practice regarding the design and installation of OWT support structures is rather
new. Hence, it relies on existing knowledge and experience from the offshore oil and gas industry [6].
The dynamic response of OWT jackets is, however, of greater importance than traditional platforms
known from the offshore oil and gas industry. This is due to the wind loads [4]. This additional and
significant wind load must be investigated to enable the design of safe and reliable structures. The
increased dynamic load induces an augmented load level of fatigue loads combined with a higher
number of cycles. Studies have found that the contribution to fatigue damage caused by wind loads
can be more than 60 % when analysing welded multi-planar tubular joints [7]. A representative
welded K-joint and weld toe crack of are shown in Figure 1.2. Nevertheless, this project is focused

The objective of this master project is to investigate the fatigue life of steel joints subjected to

this methodology, which includes the combination of advanced simulation (FE) tools, well-founded

intensity factor and crack propagation, among other parameters, are determined using both ana-

which are necessary for the complex subsequent analyses, are verified by the analytical benchmark
models. The analytical method is usually applied to simple geometries and loading conditions, while
the numerical methods are used to simulate more complex conditions. The analytical method may
not be sufficient to handle complex load cases, while the numerical methods can describe the stress
states more realistically. Following the initial benchmark models, a simple frame is modelled by an

2
Literature Review
This section presents and discusses relevant literature on the topic of fatigue and fracture mechanics.
The literature review will establish the significance of the presented study and will provide an
overview of the current state of knowledge, identify gaps in the literature, and highlight areas for
future research. The literature considered mainly involves recent research and current standards.
Given their significant impact on the study of fatigue, S-N curves and probabilistic techniques are
briefly covered in this thesis, however, this thesis exclusively uses fracture mechanics.
2.1
S-N Curve Approach and Fracture Mechanics Approach
The concern and research of fatigue performance are rather new. In the early 1970’s the interest
in the fatigue criteria of fixed offshore platforms started evolving [7]. Hereby, the methods and
approaches for fatigue assessment are quite recently established. With developing industries and
requirements the techniques are accordingly evolving [17].
The main methods are the S-N curve approach and the fracture mechanics approach. These methods
will be further described in Section 3, where the theoretical framework is outlined. Several studies
compare the two methods, where the S-N curve is the older method with experimental data as the
base, whereas the fracture mechanics approach is the newer numerical technique. Consequently, the
S-N curve approach is more costly and time-consuming given its experimental nature of modelling
approach. The incorporation of fracture mechanics as a simulation method is hence resource-saving.
Fracture mechanic models offer detailed descriptions of crack growth by considering stress cycles,
structural and crack geometry, and material parameters. These models specifically focus on the
second phase of fatigue (crack propagation), assuming the presence of an initial crack in the struc-
ture. In contrast, the S-N curve approach represents all three fatigue phases, from crack initiation
to fracture, with a single curve. This makes fracture mechanic models more advanced compared
to S-N curve approaches due to their ability to provide comprehensive crack growth predictions.
Additionally, the fracture mechanics approach gives the opportunity to easily implement new devel-
opments and discoveries such as new welding processes, new materials, or new construction methods.
The S-N curve for new discoveries will by contrast need to be made by costly and time-consuming
laboratory experiments in order to obtain the needed data for fatigue life estimations. Nowadays,
the use of fracture mechanic models for designing support structures in offshore wind turbines is
limited due to various practical challenges, such as the complexity of the models and the uncertainty
surrounding material parameters. As stated by DNV/GL-RP-0005, the fatigue life estimated using
fracture mechanics is expected to be shorter than the one obtained through S-N curves [9]. This is
because S-N curves account for the time required for crack initiation, whereas fracture mechanics
modelling assumes that crack initiation has already occurred.
Noteworthy studies in this context include the work of Shittu et al. [2]. The stated aim of the
study was to identify the fatigue reliability using the S-N curve approach compared with the FM
approach for a conventional welded OWT jacket support structure. As a result, the structural reli-
ability prediction made using the S-N curve technique delivered conservative values when compared
to those made using the FM approach at the beginning of the structure’s service life, but the results
may be too optimistic near the conclusion of the design life. The appertaining graph demonstrating
this is found in Figure 2.1. From the aforementioned, it was concluded that the S-N approach should
be utilised throughout the design phase, and the FM approach should be employed as the structure
nears failure.

A similar survey by Maheswaran and Siriwardane [18] performed a comparative study of tubular
joints’ fatigue life between the S-N curve and fracture mechanics approach.
The fatigue life of
the tubular joints in an offshore jacket was determined according to the stress concentration fac-
tors (SCF) in DNVGL-RP-0005 and Abaqus/CAE [9]. When predicted SCFs and fatigue lives are
compared, it is generally concluded that even though the fatigue life, which is computed based
on code-given SCFs, provides a more realistic prediction to simple uniplanar joints, there is some
uncertainty for complex joints and geometry, where joint classification is not available. The study
also emphasised the significance of carefully examining SCFs while taking into account the precise

geometry of complex tubular joints in order to provide an appropriate assessment of fatigue life.
The difference between the two approaches is mostly due to an increase in SCFs from the FEA tech-
nique (Abaqus/CAE). The primary cause of this deviation is that the non-dimensional parameters
of the given parametric equations in DNV/GL-RP-0005 do not accurately capture the flexibility of
complex joints, whereas the FE approach captures accurate geometry, which is more beneficial for
fatigue assessment of offshore tubular joints. However, the findings of SCFs in Abaqus/CAE are
more closely related to both the experimental test results in the HSE report and the results provided
by Efthymiou equations, according to the verification of the FE model and the analysis method of
a specific uniplanar joint. These findings suggest that while fatigue life, which is computed using
code-given SCFs, gives a more accurate forecast for basic uniplanar joints, there is some uncertainty
for complicated joints and geometries in cases when joint categorisation is not supplied.
The aforementioned studies indicate the limitations of the fracture mechanics models, used to pre-
dict crack propagation, can lead to errors in the prediction of the crack growth rate and the critical
crack size. The prediction of crack propagation therefore requires careful consideration of the testing
conditions and the development of models and methods that can account for the variability of the
loading conditions and predict the damage and fatigue life of a material. The risk of gross errors
is greater for the fracture mechanics technique compared to applying S-N curve, because of the
complexity of the fracture mechanics. Nevertheless, fracture mechanics are becoming more reliable
than the S-N approach as the time after construction increases and are more precise for complex
geometries. These factors demonstrate why there is a great interest in the development and re-
finement of fracture mechanics. Additionally, the fracture mechanics approach can provide insight
into the crack growth rate and direction, which can inform maintenance and repair decisions. It is
however important to note, that it is at the expense of the fracture mechanics approach requiring
more detailed information about the component’s geometry and material properties than the S-N
approach, and that these input values can affect the results significantly. Another benefit of the
fracture mechanics technique is the opportunity to update the input parameters throughout the
lifetime of the structure. Inspections are often difficult and costly for offshore jackets, but if made,
the fracture mechanics approach allows more accurate and updated results. This will be discussed
later on the basis of pertinent articles.
Another relevant investigation was performed by Ziegler and Muskulus [3]. The purpose of this
developed framework was to compare a fracture mechanics model to the S-N curve approach for
jacket-supported offshore wind turbines. The study found it possible to calibrate the fracture me-
chanic parameters semi-empirically in order to match the model with the predicted lifetime from
the S-N curve. Only the set of parameters that were selected for this calibration are valid. Fig-
ure 2.3 shows the obtained curves for the S-N curve approach and a demonstration of the fracture
mechanics approach depicting the influence of the Paris Law parameter C. The figure shows, that
since variations in crack growth caused by parameter uncertainty of C are only apparent after 15
years, the exponential crack growth relationship makes inspections more pertinent near the end of
the service life. With regard to minor crack diameters, there is a low likelihood that a fatigue crack
will be found. Additionally, the crack development rate increases quickly for bigger and more visible
fatigue cracks. As a result, there is only a brief window of opportunity for crack detection and
repair before failure. Because of this, updating lifetime estimations based on inspection results is
challenging throughout the majority of the fatigue lifespan; yet, fracture mechanics is an appealing
method for deciding whether to extend the lifetime of offshore structures. The article highlights an
insufficiency of the fracture mechanics approach. S-N curves include the crack initiation (it includes
all three stages of crack development as mentioned previously), whereas this has to be specified in the
fracture mechanics approach. Current crack initiation prediction approaches have some limitations.
One of the limitations is that the prediction of crack initiation is difficult due to the complex nature
of the phenomenon, which is influenced by many interacting factors. Another limitation is that the

current approaches are based on empirical models that may not be applicable to all scenarios. The
accuracy of the prediction depends on the quality of the input data, such as the material proper-
ties, loading conditions, and environmental factors. Furthermore, the current approaches may, as
aforementioned, not be able to predict the initiation of small cracks, which can lead to catastrophic
failures. Thus, there is a need for more accurate and reliable crack initiation prediction approaches
that can account for the complex nature of the phenomenon and the variability of the input data.
Even though fracture mechanics allows for inspection planning and updates from these, the methods
for crack detection are still to be improved in order to get sound input values. It is therefore, once
again, dependent on the quality of the input values.
Moreover, it is deduced in the article by Ziegler and Muskulus [3], that the final crack depth at
the end of the lifetime is identical for both analysed weather simulations: random weather occur-
rence and persistent weather simulation with annual seasonality. Hence, this addresses the issue
of loading sequence. The influence of loading sequence on fatigue crack growth is a result of the
non-linear nature of Paris’ Law. This mathematical effect arises because the crack growth per load
cycle at time step i is dependent on the crack depth at the previous time step, i −1. As a result,
the specific order in which stress ranges occur in a given time series can possibly cause a significant
impact. If high stress ranges are experienced early on, followed by lower stress ranges later in time,
the overall crack growth tends to be larger compared to the reverse scenario. Nevertheless, given
the identical results for both analysed weather simulations in the given case, it is indicated to be a
problem of minor importance for the given wind loads.

3
Theoretical Framework
Fatigue is a phenomenon in engineering that refers to the degradation of material strength due
to repeated loading and unloading over time.
It is a critical factor in the design and analysis
of structural components, as it can result in catastrophic failures and accidents if not properly
accounted for. Fatigue can occur in any type of material and is influenced by several factors. The
majority of fatigue failures occur because of cyclic loads that are significantly below the loads that
would cause material yielding. The study of fatigue involves understanding of the mechanisms of
crack initiation and propagation, developing models to predict fatigue life, and designing components
to withstand the expected fatigue loading conditions. This section will provide an overview of the
fundamental concepts and principles of fatigue, including the influencing factors, different stages of
a crack, and two methods for predicting fatigue life.
3.1
Influential Factors
The behaviour of materials under fatigue loading is influenced by various factors and parameters
that can affect the rate of material degradation and eventual failure. Some of the key factors and
parameters that influence fatigue behaviour include:
• Material Properties
The properties of the material, such as its elastic modulus, yield strength, and fracture tough-
ness, play a critical role in determining its fatigue behaviour. Materials with high fracture
toughness and fatigue limits are more resistant to fatigue failure.
• Geometry
The geometry of the material, including the shape and size of the specimen and the crack, can
affect the behaviour of the material under stress. For example, materials with sharp cracks
are more susceptible to failure than those with blunt cracks. If the surface area is increased,
the probability of surface defects is enhanced too. Likewise, if the volume is increased, the
probability of internal defects, such as voids or internal cracks, is enchanted.
• Loading Conditions
The loading conditions, such as the magnitude and distribution of the applied load, can have
a significant impact on the behaviour of the material. Different loading conditions may result
in different modes of crack propagation, such as tension or shear. Load factors such as the
mean stress, stress range and frequency highly affect the fatigue life.
• Surface Condition
The surface condition of the material, including the presence of surface defects or roughness,
can affect its fatigue behaviour. Materials with rough surfaces or surface defects are more
susceptible to fatigue failure. Different types of surface treatments may also impact the fa-
tigue. Microstructural changes, i.e. welding, can cause surface defects thereby augmenting the
possibility of fatigue failure.
• Environmental Conditions
Environmental conditions, such as temperature, humidity, and chemical exposure, can also
affect the behaviour of the material.
For example, it can affect fatigue life negatively, if
materials are exposed to a tough environment, i.e. salt water. Furthermore, high temperatures
may affect the elasticity of the material and thereby influence the fatigue. Corrosion can also
affect the fatigue behaviour of a material. Corrosion can accelerate material degradation and
lead to premature failure.
This is a consequence of corrosion thinning the component, as
described in [18]. Corrosion might be dependent on other influencing factors as the mentioned
environmental conditions and surface treatments.

Understanding these factors and parameters is critical for designing safe and reliable structures that
can withstand cyclic loading, analyzing the behaviour of materials under stress, and developing new
materials with improved fracture resistance. By analyzing these factors and parameters, engineers
and researchers can better predict and prevent failures in materials and structures [22].
3.2
Stages of Crack Development
Crack initiation and propagation are key processes that can lead to material failure under various
loading conditions. Understanding these processes is critical for designing reliable and safe structures
and for developing materials with improved fracture resistance. The process of a crack can be divided
into several stages:
• Crack Initiation
The first stage of crack formation is the initiation of a small crack or flaw in the material. This
can occur due to a variety of factors, such as the above-mentioned influential parameters. Once
a crack has formed, it can act as a stress concentrator, leading to further crack propagation.
• Crack Propagation
Once a crack has initiated, it begins to propagate through the material. The rate of crack
propagation is, like the initiation, determined by a combination of factors, including the above-
mentioned involved parameters and the later described stress intensity factor. As the crack
propagates, it can interact with other defects or features in the material, leading to changes
in the stress field and further crack growth.
• Fracture
The final stage of crack propagation is fracture, which occurs when the crack reaches a critical
length or stress intensity factor. At this point, the material fails and can no longer support
the applied load.
Fatigue crack initiation is a complex phenomenon influenced by many interacting parameters. The
process is impossible to predict with absolute certainty because it is influenced by numerous factors,
which have been described previously. The earliest stages of fatigue crack nucleation and growth are
dominated by the microstructure, and few models are able to predict the fatigue behaviour during
these stages because of a lack of microstructural physics in the models.
The process of crack propagation can be described mathematically using fracture mechanics prin-
ciples. In this project, only the crack propagation is investigated because of the choice of fracture
mechanics. The stress intensity factor, K, is a key parameter used to describe the magnitude of
the stress at the crack tip. The rate of crack propagation can be described using Paris’ Law, which
relates the rate of crack growth to the stress intensity factor and the material properties. Both
phenomena are described in the subsequent sections along with other relevant parameters.

3.3
Fracture Mechanics
Determining whether a crack will propagate is often crucial, particularly for sharp cracks. However,
simulating this process can be challenging. When loading is applied to a perfectly sharp crack, it
creates a singularity with infinitely large stress at a single point, which is not obtainable in numerical
simulations. Consequently, the results depend heavily on the mesh used in the simulation, which is
undesirable. Real-world cracks are not perfectly sharp and always have a finite thickness, but this
thickness is very small, making it difficult to measure and consider numerically. Since the stress
depends strongly on the mesh and thickness of the crack, it is not a reliable measure for determining
whether a crack will propagate. Instead, the stress intensity factor and J-integral are commonly
used measures [24]. These fracture parameters will be described in the following subsections. Linear
elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM) approaches have been reliably used for modelling the behaviour
of fatigue cracks. The basis for establishing fracture criteria is shaped by the loads and fracture
characteristics. They enable the quantitative evaluation of the crack formation.
3.3.1
Stress Intensity Factors
One of the most important parameters in fracture mechanics is the stress intensity factor (SIF). The
stress intensity factor, K, is a measure of the stress at the tip of a crack in a specimen induced by a
remote load or residual stresses. The stress intensity factor is a critical parameter in determining the
stress level at which a crack will propagate and cause failure. The parameter depends on specimen
geometry, location and size of the crack or notch, and the magnitude and distribution of loads on
the material. Usually, the stress intensity factor is given a subscript indicating the mode of loading,
i.e., KI, KII, or KIII. The modes are visualised in Figure 3.2. If several (or all) modes are present
simultaneously it is a mixed-mode problem.

3.3.2
Energy Release Rate
The energy release rate is another important parameter in fracture mechanics.
It describes the
energy required to propagate a crack in a material. The concept of energy release rate is closely
related to the stress intensity factor. The energy release rate is defined as the amount of energy per
unit area required to create new surfaces at the crack tip as the crack propagates. Materials with
high energy release rates require more energy to propagate a crack and are, therefore, more resistant
to fracture. In contrast, materials with low energy release rates are more prone to fracture and failure.
The energy release rate can be calculated along a curve using a line integral. This line integral
is specifically known as the J-integral. The J-integral is utilised to calculate the strain energy re-
lease rate per unit fracture surface area. The integral has shown useful not only in linear-elastic
fracture mechanics but also when analysing inelastic material behaviour [25]. In this report stress
intensity factors are used, however, the J-integral is another widely used parameter.

3.3.4
Algorithms within Fracture Mechanics
Within the field of fracture mechanics, several software algorithms have been developed. A few to
be mentioned here, are the SMART algorithm by Ansys and XFEM by Abaqus Simulia. These
algorithms facilitate studying propagating fatigue cracks under LEFM conditions.
The eXtended Finite Element Method (XFEM) is implemented in both Ansys and Abaqus. XFEM
is an efficient method for modelling multiple crack growth in complex geometries, including arbitrar-
ily shaped cracks subjected to mixed-mode loading. Compared to Ansys SMART, XFEM is better
in terms of simulating internal cracks [27] [28].
Ansys has developed the Separating, Morphing, Adaptive and Re-meshing Technology (SMART)
algorithm for crack growth prediction. The SMART algorithm is based on the unstructured mesh
method (UMM). As the name indicates, the algorithm automatically uses a combination of morph-
ing, adaptive, and re-meshing methods to update the mesh changes while the crack grows [27] [29].
The results of the SMART algorithm have been found to be consistent with other numerical crack
propagation studies and experimental data [30] [31].
A critical step that influences the performance of finite element analysis is mesh generation. The
mesh must be consistent and generated with minimum computational effort, by a robust and reliable
generator. Moreover, errors caused by shear locking and hour glassing should be avoided.
SMART updates the mesh automatically at each solution step based on the changes in crack ge-
ometry due to crack growth. This means that re-meshing is limited to a localized re-mesh function
at each iteration.
In contrast, XFEM uses enrichment areas (splitting) to model the crack tip,
which requires a more complex implementation and can lead to convergence issues. Unlike XFEM,
SMART can be scaled up for larger projects because re-meshing is limited to a small area around the
crack tip at each iteration. The local re-meshing focuses the computing power on the vital area of
the analysis. Consequently, this increases the computational efficiency and simulation speed which
facilitates up-scaling [2] [31].
A few main assumptions and limitations of the SMART crack growth are:
• Material behaviour is assumed to be linear elastic isotropic.
• Plasticity effects, nonlinear geometry effects, load-compression effects, and crack-tip-closure
effects are not considered.
• Only includes Mode I crack propagation.
• SMART supports 3-D crack growth only.
The algorithms are still evolving and are regularly improved. Furthermore, numerous other algo-
rithms exist, such as Zencrack by Zentech, which automatically generates focused cracked meshes
and the virtual fracture closure method (VCCT) [32] [29].

Conclusion and Future work
8.1
Conclusion
This study aims to investigate welded joints in offshore structures using fracture mechanics with
realistic stress distribution in the joint. This report successfully validates the idea through the com-
bined use of OpenSees and Ansys. The following conclusions can be drawn based on the study:
A validation of Ansys SMART is executed using a through-the-thickness crack in a three-point bend-
ing specimen (3PB) and a semi-elliptic surface crack in a plate subjected to tension (TP). Based on
the benchmark models it was discovered by comparison of the mode I SIF derived analytically and
numerically via Ansys SMART, that the numerical values are higher and more conservative. Simi-
larly, a comparison of the crack extension as a function of the number of cycles found the numerical
results more conservative, with greater crack extensions than the analytical values. The numerical
findings obtained via Ansys SMART closely correspond to the analytical calculations, indicating a
reliable agreement between the two approaches. Regarding the crack propagation, the maximum
difference is below 1 % and between 4 % - 7.5 % for the 3PB and TP, respectively.
The global response of a simple frame structure is obtained by the open-source software OpenSees.
This facilitates transferring the sectional forces to the commercial software Ansys, where crack prop-
agation is computed using Ansys SMART Crack Growth (Ansys SMART). By the investigation of
the K-joint, it becomes evident that the crack depth is increasing by smaller crack extensions than
the crack tips. Mode I is the primary load case for the K-joint crack. The mode II and mode
III SIFs are significantly lower in comparison, constituting merely 0.5 % and 3 % of the mode I
SIF, respectively. The shear in the crack can therefore be seen as almost inconsequential. For the
K-joint the maximum crack extension is found to be approximately 40 mm in length and 14 mm in
depth. Similarly, the maximum SIF for mode I is roughly 1/4 and 1/5 of the SIF threshold value
for respectively the tips and the crack depth. The K-joint is, thus, not in a critical situation yet.
Nevertheless, with the presented data, it is concluded that the crack growth rate increases quickly
for larger cracks. The number of cycles is relatively steady towards the end results where the crack
grows rapidly. A sensitivity analysis with this steady number of cycles as the point of fracture found
that the lifetime is highly dependent on the initial crack. The minimum crack sizes inducing stress
intensity factors above the threshold value are ai = 4.02 mm and 2ci = 9.12 mm. The estimated
fatigue lifetime of these initial values is approximately 42 years.
The TK-joint of a more realistic jacket frame is analysed by a methodology similar to the K-joint
in the simple frame. Based on the analysis of the TK-joint, it is concluded that the crack depth
is increasing by smaller crack extensions than the crack tips. The mode II and mode III SIFs con-
stitute 0.6 % and 4 % of the mode I SIF, respectively. Due to this minor contribution, they can
be neglected and it is seen mode I is the primary load case. The overall results of the K-joint and
TK-joint are relatively identical, hence, the similarity of these setups is established. The TK-joint
is not in a critical situation given the maximum crack extension is found to be approximately 10
mm in length and 4 mm in depth. The SIF for mode I is roughly 1/2 and 1/4 of the SIF threshold
value for respectively the crack tips and depth. The number of cycles is relatively steady towards
the end results where the crack grows rapidly. This number of cycles and the initial crack sizes of ai
= 0.74 mm and 2ci = 1.64 mm yield a fatigue lifetime of approximately 8 years. These values are
the minimum crack sizes inducing stress intensity factors above the threshold value. The sensitivity
analysis found that the lifetime is highly dependent on the initial crack.
The results could be improved by allowing more time to perform longer calculations, and by elimi-
nating some of the simplifying assumptions. This is further described in Section 8.2 beneath.

8.2
Future work
The main work to perform, in order to improve the current study, is to extend the numerical results
by Ansys SMART to reach a through-the-thickness crack. This would allow a more precise estima-
tion of the fatigue lifetime. An extension of the calculations would also allow the examination of
the possible stress redundancy when a crack grows to a large size.
The results could be verified by comparison to other modelling techniques or experimental data.
However, the approach regarding experimental data may be problematic and expensive. The bench-
mark models found a difference between the results by analytical functions obtained by experiments
and the simulated results by Ansys, thus, the experimental approach could be a valuable verification
method. Alternatively, the reliability of other software with fracture mechanics implemented could
be explored.
The convergence of the results could also be verified. A convergence study of the member length
in the detailed modelling could be produced in order to secure the reliability of the results. The
member length affects the stiffness, which consequently affects the stress distribution. Thus, the
convergence study could ensure the distribution of the stress is indeed trustworthy.
The main focus of this report is to investigate the methodology of applying fracture mechanics
for fatigue lifetime predictions. This methodology can be extended to other structures, for which
the input parameters should be updated accordingly. Several simplifications and assumptions are
made in this report, hence, the study could be improved by eliminating these to obtain more specific
and precise results. These focus areas could also be applied on other structures. To improve the
results found in this thesis several parameters can be scrutinised. The possible improvements can
be divided into two categories: Parameters in the environment of the analysed joint and parameters
involved in the analysis by Ansys SMART. The potential improvements are listed below:
