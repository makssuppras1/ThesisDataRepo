Abstract
This thesis investigates the viability of space situational awareness using a DTU star cam-
era from a ground station. A data set of the non-stellar objects captured by the camera
is filtered and satellite tracks identified from it. The identification of the satellites from the
tracks is two-fold. First using TLE’s from the time of data collection, starlinks are prop-
agated and plotted using SGP4 and compared with the observed tracks and saving any
matches with a standard deviation under 3.2 degrees. Secondly, orbital parameters are
calculated from the tracks directly by using the Gauss method of preliminary orbit deter-
mination and by using a circular method which assumes the satellite orbits are circular.
This is then compared to a list of known satellites in the sky at the time of data collec-
tion. This results in 17 identified starlink satellites as well as 3 other satellites from all
three methods. Furthermore the light intensity of the satellite’s reflection is investigated
for any cyclic motions using a FFT. No frequency components were found in the plotted
light intensities for any of the tracks.

Introduction
Ever since the first man made object made it into space in 1949, the number of artificial
satellites in the orbital environment around Earth has steadily increased. And their effect
on humanity has been overwhelming, enabling communication and entertainment, reveal-
ing unseen truths about our world, changing the face of warfare, and helping us navigate
the ground we tread on[1].
But while the space around Earth is vast it is not limitless. Some orbits are more popular
than others which increases the amount of satellites found here. And old debris and parts
scatter the orbital lanes, either due to limited foresight or gross negligence.[2].
Thus satellites are at a constant risk of collision with both debris and other satellites[3].
In a worst case scenario this could result in the infamous Kessler Syndrome, an uncon-
trolled collisional cascade, a run-a-way effect which would destroy the orbital infrastructure
around Earth.
In an optimal world humans would take this to heart, create a sustainable and fair alloca-
tion scheme, and refrain from putting each other’s satellites at risk.
Sadly this is not a perfect world[4].
Therefore the tracking and identification of satellites and orbital debris is an important task,
commonly known as space situational awareness (SSA). The North American Aerospace
Defense Command (NORAD) is perhaps the biggest organisation doing this and main-
tains a list of orbital objects around the Earth. Other organisations, such as ESA [5], is
also developing their own capabilities.
SSA also has a potential for use in national security, as it can be used to identify black
satellites owned by international competitors, which in the current geopolitical climate is
becoming increasingly relevant.
However the infrastructure required for a full SSA system is expansive and expensive.
But this cost could be reduced if it was possible to hijack already established orbital in-
frastructure.
One example could be star trackers which are optical devices onboard almost all satel-
lites. If their data could be used to identify other satellites, a decentralised SSA system
could be realised.
This report will take the first step for such a system. It will use a ground based star tracker
to identify flying satellites above it.

Coordinate Systems
When discussing stationary or moving targets it can be rather useful to know their position
[6]. Be it in relation to the observer, Earth, the galactic disc, or any other point of interest.
But to ensure any degree of uniformity several coordinate systems exist to describe such
positions.
The most relevant systems will be introduced here. They can broadly be divided into the
spatial reference system (SRS) and the astronomical coordinate system (ACS) [7] [8].
2.1
Spatial Reference Systems
SRS is used to describe locations inside, on, or within the Earth, depending on the exact
system of which there exist several.
2.1.1
Geographic
The Geographic Coordinate system is one of the oldest. It consists of the two coordinates
Latitude and Longitude which defines a position on the surface of the Earth.
Latitude denotes the angle between the equatorial plane and the point of interest, with a
range between ±90°, and as such denotes the North/South position.
Longitude denotes the angle between the reference meridian and the point of interest,
with the reference meridian crossing through Greenwich, London from the North Pole to
the South Pole. It has a range between 0°and 360°, with a direction east of the reference
meridian. Sometimes latitude is denoted N/S to avoid negatives, and longitude denoted
W/E to avoid large numbers.
Figure 2.1: Illustration showcasing latitude and longitude on the sphere. N.P denotes the North
Pole and S.P Denotes South Pole. Redrawn from [9].
As such the origin of this coordinate system is found just west of the coast of Africa on a
point known as Null Island [10].

2.1.2
Geocentric Cartesian
The Geocentric Cartesian coordinate system, also known as the Earth-centered, Earth-
fixed coordinate system, has it’s origin at the center of mass of Earth. A point is defined
by three coordinates (X,Y,Z), with the X-Y plane equal to the equatorial plane, with the
X-axis pointing through the reference meridian of the Geographic coordinate system, and
the Y-axis placed as to make the whole system right-handed.
At last the Z-axis is placed through the North Pole. Which is illustrated in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Illustration showcasing the ECEF coordinate system with the XYZ-axis shown through
the spheroid. Redrawn from [8].
The Geocentric system offers a high degree of precision and can used when describing
the position of satellites and other objects in orbit around Earth relative to the surface.
2.2
Astronomical Coordinate Systems
ACS is used to describe the direction or 3D-location of celestial objects or satellites. The
concept of the Celestial Sphere is important for these systems.
It is analogous to the Earth-sphere but inverted, with coordinates projected on to it. They
are described in either angular coordinates, which gives a pointing towards an object, or
in spherical/rectangular coordinates, which also provides a distance to or position of an
object.
2.2.1
Horizontal
The Horizontal coordinate system places it’s origin on the observer, and uses the hori-
zon as it’s fundamental plane, dividing the celestial sphere into two half-spheres, with the
point tangential to the horizontal plane and the observer called the zenith, and the point
opposite called the nadir. [11].
A point is defined by the angles altitude and azimuth. Altitude is the angle between the
horizontal plane and the zenith ranging from 0°- 90°.
Azimuth is the angle along the horizontal plane, most often defined by the direction to-
wards the North Pole and increasing eastward ranging from 0°to 360°.

Equatorial
The Equatorial coordinate system is defined with the fundamental plane being set by

The angular coordinates is defined as the Declination δ and the Right Ascension α, with
the declination measuring the angle between a point and the equator ranging from ±
90°δ, and the RA measuring an angle between 0°to 360°α with 0 defined from the Vernal
Equinox.

The equinoxes is defined from the points where the equatorial plane intersects with the
orbital plane of Earth around the Sun, called the ecliptic plane. They are called the Vernal
Equinox and the Autumnal Equinox and happens when the Sun’s declination is 0°, with
the Vernal Equinox changes from negative to positive values, and the Autumnal Equinox
being 180°RA apart.
If using rectangular (X,Y,Z)-coordinates, the x-axis is set through the vernal equinox, the
y-axis placed as to make the system right-handed, and the z-axis going through the North
Pole.
This system can be geocentric, with the origin set in the center of the Earth, or topocentric
with the origin being at the observer’s position.
2.2.3
Ecliptic

The Ecliptic coordinate system is defined with the fundamental plane being set by the or-
bital plane of the Earth around the Sun, and is projected outwards on the celestial sphere
creating the Ecliptic, which is tilted 23.4°, when compared to the equator of Earth.[13].
The Ecliptic system also uses the vernal equinox to define it’s coordinates.
The angular coordinates are called the Celestial Latitude and Longitude. The celestial
latitude is defined as the angle between the ecliptic plane and the point and ranges be-
tween ±90°. The celestial longitude measures an angle between 0°- 360°with 0 defined
from the Vernal Equinox.
If using rectangular (X,Y,Z)-coordinates, the x-axis is set through the vernal equinox, the
y-axis placed as to make the system right-handed, and the z-axis being orthogonal to the
ecliptic plane.

3.2
Satellite State Representation
A total of six orbital parameters is needed to describe an orbit in three dimensions. These
are:
• e, eccentricity
• h, specific angular momentum
• θ, true anomaly
• Ω, right ascension of the ascending node
• i, inclination
• ω, argument of perigee
The first two parameters, eccentricity and angular momentum, is enough to describe the
orbit in the plane.
The eccentricity e defines the shape of the orbit, with a value of e = 0 being circular, a
value of 0 < e < 1 being elliptical, a value of e = 1 being parabolic, and a value of e > 1
being hyperbolic.
Linked to the eccentricity is also the eccentricity vector e, which points from the apoapsis
to periapsis, with a magnitude equal to e.
The specific angular momentum h is the angular momentum of the body divided by it’s
mass. It is at times replaced with the semimajor axis a of the orbit.
To specify a point one need the true anomaly θ, which is the angle between the direction
of periapsis and the current position of the body, as seen from the main focus of the ellipse.
The last three parameters are the Euler angles and is needed to describe the orientation

3.3
Deviations from the Ideal Model
The ideal model of Earth and a satellite would assume that they are the only two bodies
in the system, that Earth is a perfect sphere, and with a perfect rotation around the polar
axis. However this is not the case. As it will be shown there are many different sources
of disturbances. These will be described below.
3.3.1
Shape of Earth
The Earth is not a perfect sphere but rather an oblate spheroid, which means that the
equatorial radius of Earth Re = 6378km, is around 21 km larger than the polar radius
Rp = 6357km. The oblateness, or flattening, is then defined as,

4
Projecting Orbits
Projecting an orbit can either mean to predict the future position of a known satellite from
it’s TLE, or it can mean identifying a satellite’s orbital parameters using only observations.
This chapter will look at both situations. How to model an orbit and how to determine it.
4.1
Orbit Modelling
Orbit modelling concerns itself with predicting the future or past state of a satellite using
a current or previously known state.
While a classic two-body equation achieves this to a certain degree, there are too many
disturbances in the earth-orbital environment for it to be precise, as described in Sec-
tion 3.3.
As such several models have been developed that takes these disturbances into account
this. The most used being the Simplified General Perturbations 4 (SGP4) model.
SGP4 is an umbrella term for a set of models, named after the most commonly used
model, which is used by NORAD and National Aeronautics and Space Administration
(NASA) to predict the future position and motion of satellites in orbit around Earth, given
an initial set of orbital parameters at a specific time [23].
Currently five different models exist,
• SGP
Developed by Hilton and Kuhlman in 1966. The gravitational model is based on a
simplified version of Kozai’s model which was developed in 1959. Furthermore the
drag effect on mean motion is treated as linear in time.
It however failed when trying to propagate motion for longer than a week [24]. Used
for near-Earth satellites.
• SGP4
Developed by Ken Cranford in 1970 for use on near-Earth satellites. For the grav-
itational model it uses a simplified version of the the analytical theory presented
by Lane and Cranford (1969), which in turn is based on Brouwer’s (1959) solution.
Furthermore it uses a power density function for it’s atmospheric model.
• SDP4
An extension of SGP4 but is used for deep space satellites. In this case deep space
is considered an orbital period of 225 minutes or more. The equations for these were
developed by Husjak in 1979 and accounts for the gravitational influence of both the
moon and sun. It also takes into account tesseral and sectoral Earth harmonics.
• SGP8 & SDP8
Developed to account for special cases of orbital decay and reentry. They use the
same gravitational model as SGP4 but integrates the differential equations much
differently. [25]

4.2
Preliminary Orbit Determination
Preliminary orbit determination, also referred to as orbit determination in this report, is an
extensive subject with many different procedures and methods. The idea is to determine
the state-vectors or orbital parameters of an object in orbit. This can then be a basis for
further orbit modelling, or used to identify the object in question.
The methods can be divided into two categories; observations of range and angles-only
observations.
Observations of range has the location of the ground-station, angular measurements and
a range measurement, which is often obtained from a radar. Angels-only observations
only have the location of the ground station and the angular measurements of the satellite
as seen from said ground station.
In the following sections the Gibbs Method will be introduced, which is a observations of
range-method, and the Gauss method, which is an angles-only method. Furthermore a
special case for assumed circular orbits will be discussed as well.

Star Trackers
Keeping a spacecraft oriented in space can be a difficult task due to the lack of reference
points as known from Earth; ground-level, terrain, etc.
This can be solved by using a star tracker. It photographs the night sky and compares
the pictures with stored stellar maps, thereby identifying the orientation of the craft.
This is possible due to the parallax of the stars being negligible due to the distance to
them from Earth.
One of the best star trackers, if not the best, is the DTU Star Camera which has been de-
veloped by the brilliant researchers on DTU Space. So far it has flown onboard more than
50 international missions in space [28]. It typically contains between 10-20 thousand of
the brightest stars in its stellar map, from which it can determine the direction its pointing
with an error as small as 1.5 arc-seconds per tracking, with a FoV of 20°, a 4Hz refresh
rate, and visual magnitudes down to 7-9 mV.[27].
Figure 5.1: A picture of the DTU camera used in the project for data collection.
During normal operations the star camera ignores any observations which does not match
with the stellar map. The origin of these so called false stars can be several:
• Stars not found in the catalogue
• Lens flares from bright objects
• Lens distortion effects
• Image sensor noise
• Damaged pixels
• Non-stellar objects, such as planets, satellites, and space debris.
However it can be modified to instead output these false stars which has potential for SSA
purposes.

Design Goals
The design goal of the project is to create a pipeline that takes a set of optical observations
of the night sky and identifies any satellites flying through the picture.
These will be collected with a DTU Star Camera which only outputs angular observations.
The camera is set to only output the false stars registered, disregarding any true stars in
its catalogue, as explained in Chapter 5.
This output data should then be filtered, leaving only the observations belonging to satel-
lites. Furthermore these observations should be sorted into their respective tracks, as to
make identification possible. See Chapter 7.
The identification process is two-fold. It can either be done by propagating a list of known
satellites, and then compare the propagated tracks with the observed tracks. See Sec-
tion 8.1.
The other method is to calculate the orbital parameters from the observed tracks. These
parameters can then be compared with the parameters of known satellites to identify the
observed tracks. See Section 8.2.
Thus a list of candidates can be made for the observed tracks thereby identifying which
satellites flew overhead.
In the following chapters different methods will be outlined which can help achieve these
goals, which will be summarised in Chapter 10, before moving on to the results and dis-
cussion.

Track Detection
The first step in orbit determination is to pick out actual satellite tracks and remove any
noise from data sets consisting of angular observations.
7.1
Minor Movement Method
When a satellite moves across the night sky its motion can be seen as more or less
constant when directly above. Furthermore, while the track of a satellite might be a curve,
the movement between the individual points is close to linear. This is exploited by the
Minor Movement (MM) method which uses the expected movement of satellites to single
them out from the noise.
7.1.1
The Basis
An observation (δ1, α1) at t1 is compared to an observation (δ2, α2) at t2, with their distance
calculated as,
d =
p
(δ2 −δ1)2 + (α2 −α1)2
(7.1)
Figure 7.1: An illustration of three situations with three observations at different times t1, t2, and
t3. The inner ring symbolises the lower bound for detection, while the outer ring symbolises the
upper bound. In the first situation the observations are below the margin, and the observations
are thus not a satellite. The second situation they are within the margin and is thus a candidate
for further investigation. In the third situation they are outside the margin and is thus not deemed
from the same satellite.
If the distance between them is above some margin, the observations are not considered
to be from the same object, as the distance they cover is too great for a satellite to feasibly
cover.
If they are too close to each other they are also not considered from the same object, as
the distance they cover, or lack thereof, is considered to be too minor to be a satellite.
But if the distance is between both the upper and lower margin, the observation is eligible
for further investigation.
This is illustrated in Figure 7.2 with the three situations illustrated with three different times-
tamps for each. The circles indicate the margins centred around their respective obser-
vations.

Furthermore only when at least three consecutive observations is found to be within their
respective margins they are to be further investigated.
The next step is to check the linearity and equidistance of the three points.
Since the time between the three timestamps is the same, the distance they cover are
also expected to be the equal for the two pairs. Furthermore the points is expected to
be close to a straight line. This can be calculated as the parameter EQD, which checks
how linear and equidistant the points are, with a value of EQD = 0 representing a perfect
alignment and equal distance.
EQD = abs(α1 + α3 −2α2) + abs(δ1 + δ3 −2δ2)
(7.2)
Which again is compared to some chosen margin.
This should produce a range of tracks if any is present. However, to ensure that tracks
with gaps in them are still connected correctly, a collinearity check should be performed
between the endpoints of one track and the start points of a second track. That is at least
2 points from each track.
Furthermore the range of the collinearity should be considered, that is how wide a po-
tential gap in the tracks can be. Make the range too small and it might not connect all
disjointed tracks, make it too large and it might connect unrelated tracks.

θ = arccos(||r1|| · ||r2||)
(7.4)

Orbit Determination
The methods of preliminary orbit determination are described in Chapter 4. This sections
will explain the implementation of these procedures.
Since the data is angular the Gibbs method will not be used, and only the Gauss Method
and the constrained circular will be implemented
While the Gauss and Constrained Circular method is more or less set, one of the more

8.2.1
Smoothing of the Data
While the identified tracks appear smooth from a distance they may contain significant
amounts of deviations from the expected line.
Figure 8.3: An example of how noise could influence identified satellite tracks.
This deviation can stem from the failure to remove all noise in the filtration and identifica-
tion of the satellite tracks, or be inherent in the camera itself.
One example is when two observations meet. It could be a satellite flying over a star, or
two satellites crossing each other. This makes it hard for the camera to make a distinction
between the two objects and instead label them as one.
Another example could be an observation on the border of two pixels, where the outputted
coordinate would then swing between the pixels, thus creating an oscillating effect.
Either way this noise has the potential to degrade the orbital determination of the tracks
and must therefore be removed. The simplest way to do this is to fit a polynomial to the

track thereby significantly lowering the influence of a few noisy observations.
In this project an automatic fit of a 2-degree polynomial is found to be adequate to achieve
an almost total removal of the noise.

as a function of time. It can be seen how this smooths the data.

function of time.

8.2.3
Subsequent Identification
When the orbital parameters has been found for an object belonging to a track the next
step is to identify the satellite proper.
The most obvious solution to this is to compare the parameters with a list of known satel-
lites and the associated TLE’s.
In an ideal world all parameters would thus have a perfect match with a unique TLE.
But the preliminary orbit determination might have significant measures of uncertainty in
some or all of the calculated parameters. So it can be advisable to use a margin of error
and a set of filter-parameters for the matching to use, with the knowledge that false posi-
tives might occur due to these uncertainties.
Secondary parameters can also be chosen for specific satellite candidates.
This margin should be larger for TLE’s acquired some time after the initial data collection,
as perturbations can make the orbital parameters drift, as explained in Section 3.3.
This matching can be improved further if the list of known satellites has a time span and
a compass heading associated with it, since it is then known when and where they ap-
peared over the horizon, and when and where they disappeared below it again. These
values can be compared with the tracks. Do they match with the given time frame? And
do they travel in the same direction?

Object Characterisation
When a satellite track has been identified and its orbital parameters calculated, the ob-
vious solution for identification would be to search databases with known satellites, and
try to match the found satellite’s parameters with those of the known satellites. But there
might be another way.
As the satellites travel in their orbits some of them will spin around their own axis. This
can be by design or by accident. For example, some types of SAR-satellites rotate or
swing back and forth when scanning [31]. And rocket parts or debris might start spinning
when ejected from the main craft [32].
Since the satellites might have surfaces with varying albedo, or just different cross-section
area, their tumble might be seen in the varying magnitude of their reflected light.
Figure 9.1: A sketch of how a tumbling satellite with a different albedo on its two sides. L1 and
L2 represents the magnitude of the reflected light, ω the angular velocity of the tumble, and t1 and
t2 the different times.
This pattern might be unique for each satellite and might therefore be usable for an iden-
tification. This of course requires multiple observations over time to see if the predictions
are true.
However the tumbling might be rather chaotic, which makes it hard to find any harmonic
characteristics in the curves. Thus a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) can be performed to
try and identify any frequency amplitude present within the noise.

13.2.4
Discussion on the Orbital Determination
The preliminary orbit determination produced mixed results.
The Gauss method produced parameters with significant variation between the tracks.
Especially the height of the satellite and the eccentricity seemed unreliable for many of
the tracks. However the i and Ωseemed stable and was thus subsequently chosen as
the filter-parameters for the subsequent identification.
The different data division schemes seemed to have little influence on the parameters.
There are some notable differences for some of the tracks, but no consistency in this can
be observed. The number of observations in a track also seemed to have little influence
on the subsequent parameters.
The explanation for this mixed performance might be found in the D0 parameter from
Equation 4.19.
D0 is used to calculate the values A from Equation 4.21, B from Equation 4.22, and the
slant ranges ρ1 from Equation 4.26 and ρ3 from Equation 4.28.
For all four values it is used in a denominator. Since D0 is often a very small number,
D1 << 1, if any small errors occurs when calculating it, it can cause the four terms to
change dramatically.
The Circular method proved to be quite robust when calculating i, ω, and H.
This is especially clear for the Starlink satellites, which was identified by the Skytrack
method, as the Circular method correctly determined the parameters, H, i, and Ωwithin
a 10% error of the true values, where Starlinks usually have a height of 550 km, and an
inclination of 53.1°.[34] Furthermore the Starlinks used in the Skytrack procedure all had
an Ωaround 300 °.
Thus it is a good approximation that most of the observed satellites have a circular orbit.
The filter-parameters for the subsequent identification were chosen for several rea-
sons. The Ωwas chosen from the comparison between an old TLE of a Starlink and a
new version. It could be seen that the regression of the Ωwas around 10°. Since the
other non-Starlink TLE’s were saved in June, it was assumed this was a fair margin to
use.
Furthermore it could be seen that the orbital parameters from both Gauss method and
the Circular method seemed to deviate within 10 °of the Starlink values for i and Ωfor
the tracks identified as Starlinks by the Skytrack procedure. Thus the 10 °was deemed
appropriate.
The reason for the secondary filter-parameter for the Starlinks was due to the precision
and accuracy of the satellite height as calculated by the Circular method. Therefore the
extra check was thought to add an extra layer of credibility to the candidates.

The subsequent identification could use some streamlining in the future, since some of

Overall the feasibility of the preliminary orbit determination seems viable. The Gauss
method needs more work or should be dropped in favour of another method. The Circu-

In the future other methods of preliminary orbit determination should be investigated
to evaluate their performance compared with both the Gauss method and the Circular
method. Many exist, but especially one specific field is of interest, namely small-arc orbit
determination.
Small-arc orbit determination can be crucial for when an asteroid is first discovered [35],
but it can also be used on satellites and might even have applications on space-based
star cameras as seen in [36].
Here they use a three-step approach on in Geosynchronous Earth Orbit (GEO) to deter-
mine the orbital parameters, with one of the steps also assuming circular orbits.
However further investigation is needed in this area.

Discussion on the Characterisation
The FFT failed to discover any spectral components in the light curves, which means that
the noise on the curves might just be that; noise.
However this does not mean that the satellites does not tumble, just that the data collec-
tion failed to identify it.
One problem is the length of the observations. Track 24 is the longest track identified with
a duration of 25 seconds.
However the angular velocity of the rotations might just be so slow, that to identify any
oscillating behaviours the sampling time must be increased to several minutes or tens-of-
minutes.
It could also be that the satellites spin too fast compared to the sampling frequency of 4
Hz. This is more likely for debris instead of satellites, as spinning can be a detriment for
their operational life-span.
A solution to both of these would be to switch from an optical platform to a radar based
platform. Radar is already used widely in orbital tracking and determination [12][15]. It
can potentially provide better tracking capabilities, thereby increasing the duration of the
sample, an increase in sampling rate, thereby increasing the angular resolution, and might
also increase overall resolution, thereby allowing identification of finer details on the craft
itself. But more investigation into this is needed.

Conclusion
The implementation of a SSA system built upon a DTU Star Camera was a success.
The data collection from a ground based station was realised without any major problems
and provided an hour of usable observations.
The track identification from this data also found 32 satellite tracks for use in further anal-
ysis. Some of the tracks were from the same satellite but this had little influence on the
end result.
The orbital modelling was a success. The Groundtrack procedure correctly propagated
a satellite back in time using it’s TLE and for which the found poistion corresponded with
online trackers.
The Skytrack procedure successfully identified 17 Starlink satellites in the data by using
a list of known Starlinks above the ground station at the time and is deemed a reliable tool.
The orbital determination was also successful. The Gauss Methods identified a total of 5
satellite candidates with three of them correlating with the Starlink candidates found with
the Skytrack procedure.
However the Gauss method did produce inconsistent parameters, which most likely was
due to the parameter D0, which is used in the Gauss calculations.
The Circular method identified a total of 18 satellite candidates with 17 of them correlating
with the Starlink satellites found by the Skytrack procedure. It thus performed well on the
circular orbits.
An investigation into the a characterisation of the satellites from their varying light inten-
sities as they flew across the sky yielded no usable results.
No frequency components were found within the noise of the light intensity. It was theo-
rised that longer time spans or better resolution was needed to achieve usable results.

found in Table 15.1.

Satellite Tracking and Identification
lvii
==PAGE:139==
This thesis investigates the viability of space situational awareness using a DTU star camera from a
ground station. A data set of the non-stellar objects captured by the camera is filtered and satellite
tracks identified from it. The identification of the satellites from the tracks is two-fold. First using TLE’s
from the time of data collection, starlinks are propagated and plotted using SGP4 and compared with
the observed tracks and saving any matches with a standard deviation under 3.2 degrees. Secondly,
orbital parameters are calculated from the tracks directly by using the Gauss method of preliminary
orbit determination and by using a circular method which assumes the satellite orbits are circular.
This is then compared to a list of known satellites in the sky at the time of data collection. This results
in 17 identified starlink satellites as well as 3 other satellites from all three methods. Furthermore
the light intensity of the satellite’s reflection is investigated for any cyclic motions using a FFT. No
frequency components were found in the plotted light intensities for any of the tracks.
