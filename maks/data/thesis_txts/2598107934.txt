ABSTRACT
The soil classiﬁcation in sands and clays can be accurately obtained as several researches
have been conducted in these types of soil. By contrast, current engineering practices lack
standardized guidance regarding the test procedure and data interpretation in intermediate
soils. However, it is of crucial importance to obtain accurate soil properties and drainage
conditions for the geotechnical design of the structures, such as retaining walls and pile
foundations. Hence, silty sand was selected to be tested in order to broaden the database and
the understanding of the behaviour of intermediate soils.
Nowadays, oﬀshore wind farms have been shown as one of the most promising renewable
energy sources, where the majority of them have monopile foundations. Pile foundations are
subjected to huge lateral loads such as earthquakes or waves and they are designed by using
the Winkler approach, where the monopile is simulated as a beam and the soil as a set of
uncoupled springs (p-y curves). Most of the monopile foundations can be treated as short
rigid piles and since the recommended p-y curves were developed by experimental work in
slender piles embedded in sandy and clayey soils, issues are introduced when they are used to
design monopiles in intermediate soils.
Soil characterization can be done by using ﬁeld tests, where cone penetration tests are often
used. In the present case study, cone penetration tests with pore pressure measurements
were carried out in silty sand samples with varying ﬁne content, in the centrifuge facilities at
Technical University of Denmark. In addition to cone penetration tests, pile testing under
lateral loading were performed so as to investigate the lateral response of a pile embedded in
silty sand soil with non plastic ﬁnes.
Initially, silty sand samples were prepared by using undercompaction method owing to the
high degree of uniformity and homogeneity that samples showed during previous case studies.
Besides silty sand samples, two samples were also prepared, one consisted entirely of sand and
one of silt, in order to assess the sample preparation method and the behaviour of the soil
sample. Thereafter, samples were taken, by using cylinder sampler, to be tested in triaxial
apparatus. Supplementary tests, such as particle size distribution and bulk density tests, were
also performed and compared with the results obtained by the other tests (CPTU, pile tests).
The outcomes of this case study can be divided into three focus areas, the sample preparation
method, the results from CPTU interpretation and lateral response of piles embedded in
silty sand soil. In general, undercompaction method found to be an appropriate method for
the preparation of silty sand samples of varying FC, as sample characteristics are of similar
magnitude and no grain segregation is observed.
The results obtained by CPTU tests are controversial. The majority of the CPTU tests are in
agreement with soil classiﬁcation scheme proposed by Robertson (2009) [36]. Fine content
eﬀect can be easily identiﬁed by the SBTn chart, as curve shifts from ﬁfth to fourth zone
when ﬁne content increases, however the inﬂuence of ﬁne content is not clear when it comes
to CPTU soundings. Speciﬁcally, pore pressure measurements showed the lowest dependency

on the ﬁne content comparing to the rest of the CPTU soundings. As far as the tip resistance
and sleeve friction are concerned, an increasing tendency is captured in respect of the decrease
of ﬁne content, however, the existence of some outliers should be highlighted. It can be
seen that the ﬁne content plays signiﬁcant role when it is lower than 30%, otherwise CPTU
measurements do not deviate largely.
Lateral response of piles embedded in silty sand soil is evaluated, initially, in terms of the load
deﬂection curves. The load deﬂection curves indicated higher ultimate capacity in respect of
the decrease of ﬁne content. On the other hand, soil resistance is not related with ﬁne content
in the same way as the ultimate capacity. What is more, ultimate soil resistance found to be
linearly related to the tip resistance for the samples of 20%, 40% and 50% FC. Silty sand
sample of 30% FC showed peculiar behaviour concerning the soil resistance, therefore further
investigation is required. Additionally, the lateral response of the soil samples is investigated
in conjunction with the resulted pile installation forces. It should be pointed out that the
results from the installation of the pile are in agreement with the ones from the lateral pile
test.
Finally, the strength of the soil samples were evaluated based on diﬀerent approaches, related
with CPTU and pile test results. In general, Robertson & Campanella correlation predicts
higher values of eﬀective friction angle comparing to the rest of the CPT based methods. On
the other hand, the correlation suggested by Kulhawy showed the lowest values of friction
angle. The distribution and the magnitude of the friction angles calculated based on the
results from pile testing are similar, with the friction angle estimated by the installation force
to be the higher one.

INTRODUCTION
Soil is a natural resource that can be categorized into diﬀerent soil types, each with distinct
characteristics that provide growing beneﬁts and limitations. Most of the researches have
been focused on sandy and clayey soils where the knowledge is extensive, however, there
is a limited available database concerning the behaviour of intermediate soils. When using
ﬁeld tests to characterise a soil, cone penetrations tests are often used. Particularly, the soil
classiﬁcation scheme proposed by Robertson (2009) is the most widely used method to predict
the soil behaviour type. In general, CPT-based methods are in a good agreement with USCS
based classiﬁcation, nevertheless, there are some cases such as in intermediate soils where
diﬀerences may arise. Except the identiﬁcation of the soil type, CPTU classiﬁcation provides
reliable estimation of strength (φ, cu) and stiﬀness parameters (E, G) or the ﬁne content
which is an essential parameter regarding the assessment of liquefaction.
The right soil properties and site conditions can be easily established by full-scale tests,
however, this method can be quiet expensive and the ultimate limit state cannot be reached.
An alternative method for reaching the right soil conditions is the centrifuge modelling, where
a small scale test is performed through an increased gravity ﬁeld. Recently, consistent results
regarding the CPTU based prediction of the behaviour of intermediate soils by centrifuge
modelling were achieved during a master thesis [9], performed at Technical University of
Denmark.
In relation to the soil behaviour of intermediate soils, several issues have been arisen for the
geotechnical design of structures such as retaining walls or pile foundations. Pile foundations
have been widely used over the last decades owing to the dominance of wind energy, where
pile foundations are often used. More and more countries have already taken advantage
of wind energy by constructing oﬀshore wind farms where the eﬃciency of the wind farms
increased signiﬁcantly due to the stronger winds that blow oﬀshore. However, the design of
the foundation of an oﬀshore wind turbine is challenging as the structure is subjected to huge
laterally loads such as waves and wind. Meanwhile, pile behaviour under lateral loading has
been investigated mainly for clayey and sandy soils, where the p-y method is the most broadly
used nowadays for the design of the laterally loaded piles.
The main principles of this method is the representation of the soil resistance by using
non-linear springs and the capture of the relationship between the applied load and lateral
deformation of the pile. This method was ﬁrst developed in the 1940’s and 1950s where energy
companies constructed oﬀshore piles that they had to withstand big lateral loads. The earliest
recommendations on p-y behaviour date back to 60s and 70s where Broms and Matlock carried
out experimental studies, while the ﬁrst recommendations were proposed by the American
Petroleum Institute (API). As the recommended, by API, curves have been established based
on ﬁeld tests in slender piles, uncertainties arise when they used for the design of monopiles
(rigid piles).

1. Introduction
It should be highlighted that centrifuge modelling have been already applied successfully to
pile related problem. Nevertheless, most of the studies have focused on sandy or clayey soils.
For instance, Klinkvort and Hededal [23] performed experiments for the evaluation of the
load eccentricity and embedment length in the ultimate capacity of the pile with satisfactory
results. What is more, Klinkvort tried to narrow the gap concerning the eﬀect of the pile
diameter in the soil-pile stiﬀness.
1.1
Objective of project
The main objective of this thesis is to broaden the knowledge concerning the soil behaviour
of intermediate soils. To do so, several experiments have been performed in order to reach
an accurate outcome. The behaviour of the intermediate soils is strongly related to the
particle size distribution and the permeability of the sample. As intermediate soils may consist
of a wide range of particle sizes, the drainage conditions may vary from fully drained to
fully undrained depending on the prevalent soil type. In the present case study, silty sand
samples have been prepared and tested and the response can be considered fully-drained to
partially-drained since insigniﬁcant excess pore pressure was developed.
Additionally, this thesis dealt also with the experimental demonstration of p-y curves for the
design of laterally loaded piles in intermediate soils. Initially, a cone penetration test was
carried out, if it was possible, so as to determine the soil properties and verify the homogeneity
of the sample. Thereafter, pile made of aluminium was examined under lateral load at a
speciﬁc acceleration level.
Prior to the pile testing in intermediate soils, supplementary cone penetration tests with pore
pressure measurements (CPTU) were carried out so as to evaluate the sample preparation
method in this type of soil and expand the available database of CPTU tests in silty sand
samples with non plastic ﬁnes. In addition to centrifuge testing, samples were taken so as
to be tested in a triaxial apparatus. The main aim of this is the determination of strength
parameters and portraying the soil behaviour. Supplementary laboratory tests, such as sieve
analysis, were also carried out for the validation of the obtained results.
A literature review has ﬁrst been carried out to investigate practices that have been already
applied for the interpretation of CPTU measurements in intermediate soils and existing
researches in laterally loaded pile testing. Due to the fact that intermediate soils is a soil
category that most of the soils can be part of, it is really diﬃcult to point out systematized
methods that can be used for the analysis of geotechnical issues in silty sand. What is more
silty sand does not have speciﬁc characteristics, attributed mainly to two reasons. The ﬁrst
one is the grain size distribution that varies in respect of the dominant soil type and the
second is the type of the ﬁnes, since the type of ﬁnes aﬀects the drainage behaviour of the soil.
Subsequently, all the methodologies followed during this case study are discussed in chapter 3.
To sum up, the results from all the tests carried out in the case study and a comparison of
them with analytical or experimental models are discussed in detail in chapter 4 and chapter 5
respectively.

With the increase in oil and gas structures over the last decades, several issues, concerning the
accurate derivation of the soil characterization and therefore the design of structures such as
pile foundations, have been arisen. However, the methods developed during the seventies are
still in use for the design of laterally loaded piles. Initially, the principles and the uncertainties

Scaling eﬀects
If all the linear dimensions in the model should be scaled with the same scale factor, that
includes grain size as well. A change in the grain size can cause a diﬀerent soil behaviour
as cohesion may be introduced in the model case while coarse grained material was the
investigated one. Therefore, scaling in the grain size is not applied. The grain size eﬀect
may be applied also in case of a structure, such as pile foundations due to the fact that the
ratio between a characteristic dimension, in this case, diameter of the pile D, over the average
grain size d50 would be diﬀerent. In reality, this ratio is very large for full-scale monopiles
and sand is considered to act like a continuum Klinkvort et al [23] while this is not valid
after a threshold value. A wide range of results have been published until now regarding
the magnitude of the threshold value with the proposed values varying from 40 to 88 [23].
Subsequently, the grain size eﬀect for cone penetration tests conducted in centrifuge needs to
be considered. As far as the CPTU tests, Balachowski (2007) [4] reported that there is no
need for corrections when the ratio between the cone width and average grain size is higher
than 20.
A scale eﬀect regarding the lateral component of the acceleration may be introduced as the
acceleration ﬁeld is generated around a ﬁxed point. The acceleration ﬁeld is inﬂuenced on the
eﬀective radius and the width of the model and therefore an evaluation of this eﬀect need
to be checked. What is more, the area in the vicinity of the wall of the container is wider
inﬂuenced, and consequently, the testing should take place in the central region so as to ensure
that the tests are not aﬀected by this.

2.2
Sample preparation methods
A literature review regarding the available sample preparation methods is crucial to be
carried out. Particularly, there are several sample preparation methods that can be used for
reconstituted soil samples. The most widely used are (a) dry or moist tamping, (b) dry, wet
or mist pluviation and (c) slurry deposition method. The selection of the most appropriate
one depends on the requested outcome. By means, the deposition method aﬀects the way
that the grains are structured, therefore the stress-strain behaviour will be also diﬀerent as it
is dependent on the grain structure.
Kuerbis & Vaid (1988) [24] were the ﬁrst one mentioned the points that a sample preparation
method should fulﬁl and those points are presented below:
i The sample should be uniform and this can be evaluated by the void ratio throughout
the sample.
ii Specimens with varying densities, from loose to dense, can be produced by a method.
iii The sample should be homogeneous, in terms of particle distribution which is quite
crucial in intermediate soils, and no particle segregation should be observed.
iv The method should simulate a natural deposition method.
Thereafter, the characteristics of the methods used greatly for the preparation of a silty sand
sample would be stressed out.
Dry and moist tamping
The principle of this method (for both dry and moist samples) is the preparation of a sample
in several layers. Each layer has been tamped with a speciﬁc force and tamping frequency.
The tamping frequency can also be determined by the desired height of each layer. Kim et al
(2014) [19] prepared silty sand samples by using dry tamping method and setting the desired
height of each layer.
Ladd (1978) [25] proposed a modiﬁed method based on dry or wet tamping, the so-called
under-compaction method. The advantage of this method is the lower possibility of particle
segregation to occur. The principle of the under compaction method is the increased tamping
that applied at each new layer that has been constructed. By means that the ﬁrst layer is
slightly compacted while the last one is the most compacted. The classical tamping method
can lead to a bottom layer with a higher degree of densiﬁcation comparing to the others. The
undercompaction method was also applied by Zania et al. (2019) [47] for preparing silty
sand samples of varying ﬁne content. The samples prepared during that case study were
homogeneous and uniform, especially for the higher ﬁne contents.
Dry and wet pluviation
The material is poured into a container and it is deposited either dry or moist. In case of dry
deposition, the height of the fall aﬀects the density of the sample, by means that the higher
the falling height of the soil, the denser the sample would be. Also, the characteristics of the
funnels (diameter, tube) may aﬀect the preparation of the sample, since contradictory results,
regarding the eﬀect of the funnel in the sample preparation, have been reported by diﬀerent
researchers. The main diﬀerence between dry and wet pluviation is that the deposition occurs
in water and not in the air, simulating an alluvial deposition.

Slurry deposition method As it was already mentioned, slurry deposition method was
evaluated by Kuerbis & Vaid (1988) [24] and the outcome was quite satisfactory as uniform
samples were prepared and no particle segregation was observed. It should be stressed out
that this method was used for the preparation of specimens for triaxial testing, which are way
smaller comparing to the ones being tested in centrifuge facilities.
2.3
General CPTU Interpretation
Cone penetration test [CPT] is internationally one of the most widely used and accepted
methods for the determination of the geotechnical properties of the soil. The basic reasons why
the CPT is so widely used are the low cost, the high productivity, the operator independency
of the results, the suitability of the implementation even if the soil is soft and ﬁnally the
availability of a large theoretical basis [37]. To perform a CPT test with pore pressure
measurements, commonly named CPTU, a piezocone is needed, as it is able to take continuous
measurements of cone resistance qc, sleeve friction fs and pore pressure u2. Particularly, cone
resistance qc is the total force that acts to the cone divided with the projected area of the
cone and sleeve friction fs is the ratio of the total force acting into the sleeve friction and the
area of the sleeve [37].
The cone tip resistance needs to be corrected when pore pressure measurements are taken
due to the eﬀect of the pore pressure developed during the penetration. The correction of the
cone resistance is shown in Equation 2.5. A similar correction should be applied also for the
sleeve friction, however, ASTM standard [3] requires that the cones have an equal end area
friction sleeve, which eliminates largely the need for such a correction.
qt = qc + (1 −α)u2
(2.5)
The penetration rate is of crucial importance since the drainage conditions are mainly
determined by this . Speciﬁcally, a typical penetration rate of 20 [mm/s] causes undrained
conditions for pure clay soil and drained conditions for sandy soil. However, the drainage
conditions cannot be easily established for the case of intermediate soils. Lunne (1997) [26]
suggested that the soil response of the intermediate soils are most often partially drained
for this magnitude of penetration rate. The soil response depends also on the permeability
and compressibility properties of the soil. Eventually, the characterization of the soil and
the estimation of the soil properties would be aﬀected, as undrained conditions indicate a
classiﬁcation similar to clay, while the opposite applies in case of drained conditions.

2.5
Laterally loaded piles
A proper understanding of the load transfer mechanisms in piles is mandatory for their design
and analysis. Piles can transfer both axial and lateral loads through diﬀerent mechanisms.
Particularly, a pile may be looked as an axially loaded column in case of axial loads while the
behaviour changed to a transversely loaded beam in case of lateral loads. In the latter case,
piles transfer loads to the surrounding soil by using the lateral resistance of the soil. The
primary mechanism of load transfer for lateral loads in piles is governed by the bending or
rotation of the pile due to the shift of the pile horizontally.
Piles can be divided into two categories regarding their behaviour, rigid or ﬂexible. The
behaviour of the pile is essential as it deﬁnes also the failure mechanism and the way that
the ultimate capacity pu should be estimated. In particular, ultimate capacity pu of a rigid
pile is estimated based on the ultimate capacity of the soil (a soil failure is assumed) while
a pile failure is assumed for ﬂexible piles. There are many available expressions deﬁning
whether a pile responds as ﬂexible or rigid pile, the so-called ﬂexibility criteria. The most
widely used are the ones proposed by Broms (1964) [6] and Poulos & Davis (1980) [32]. The
aforementioned criteria are shown in Equation 2.17 and Equation 2.18 respectively. If the
inequalities are fulﬁlled, the pile has ﬂexible behaviour.

Like all methods, p-y approach has also some limitations that have to be taken into
consideration when a pile is designed. API recommendations are based on a limited amount
of laboratory tests where most of the tested piles diﬀer from piles used for oﬀshore turbine
foundations in size and slenderness. Therefore, oﬀshore engineers argue whether results based

pile displacement to a given force, which for some structures can be devastating.

2.6
Small scale pile testing
Pile testing conducted in both full and small scale can provide accurate information regarding
the pile response under lateral loads. It is clear that the cost of carrying out a full scale pile
test is high enough comparing to a small scale test in centrifuge. In addition to that, higher
amount of tests can be carried out in small scale based on the simplicity and the equipment
needed during these tests. Therefore, laterally loaded pile testing conducted in the centrifuge
facilities have been largely introduced, however, the disadvantages and the limitations inserted
by this would not be neglected. The limitations of centrifuge modelling have already been
presented in section 2.1.
To fully understand the behaviour of laterally loaded piles, there are many parameters that
need to be considered such as stress level, type of loading, load eccentricity and pile ﬂexibility.
Several researches have been carried out in order to investigate the eﬀect of such parameters
in the behaviour of laterally loaded piles. Some of them also have been conducted as a
combination of centrifuge modelling and a FEM software.
Hededal & Klinkvort (2010) [21] performed a test series of modelling for both monotonic and
cyclic loading and they tried to investigate the eﬀect of the scaling factor. It can be seen that
scaling factor has an eﬀect but further investigation was needed. Also, they reported that the
small piles tested at high g-levels showed higher capacity. The same authors, four years later,
they investigated the eﬀect of the stress level and the load eccentricity [22]. The outcome of
this case study was that the initial stiﬀness seems to be unaﬀected by the increase in the load
eccentricity while the ultimate capacity tends to increase by increasing the load eccentricity.
Klinkvort (2012) [20] performed several tests in dry sand with varying embedment length
during his PhD diploma, in which the pile capacity and the initial soil-pile stiﬀness found to
be underestimated and overestimated respectively comparing to the ones proposed standard
p-y curves. Also, a dependency between initial stiﬀness of p-y curves and the embedment
depth was reported, by means that the stiﬀness found to vary with the square root of the
depth.
Contradictory results have been published by diﬀerent authors concerning the eﬀect of the
pile diameter on the initial stiﬀness. Terzaghi (1955) [44] was the ﬁrst one suggested that
there is no eﬀect of pile diameter on the initial stiﬀness of the pile-soil reaction curves. The
same conclusions reached also by other authors such as Pender et al.(2007) [31], Fan & Long
(2005) [10] and Ashford (2003) [2]. On the other hand, Sørensen et al (2009) [42] suggested
that there is an eﬀect of the diameter on the initial stiﬀness of p-y curves. In the latter case, it
is of crucial importance to be mentioned that the geometrical similarity was not kept constant
which might inﬂuence the failure mechanisms.
The response of a monopile supporting an oﬀshore wind turbine is still not well described as
there are many factors that inﬂuence largely the response of a monopile under lateral loads.
The eﬀect of each parameter should be investigated separately.

RESULTS
In this chapter, the results of this case study are presented and discussed with a reference to
the two main aims of the study, the evaluation of silty sand characterization based on CPTU
tests performed in centrifuge facilities and the experimental demonstration of p-y curves for
laterally loaded piles embedded in silty sand samples with non-plastic ﬁnes.
As far as the CPTU tests are concerned, ﬁve samples have been prepared and tested in the
centrifuge facilities. All the samples were prepared by using the under-compaction method
with the same water content of 12% but varying ﬁne content. On the other hand, seven

Hereafter, the results from the laterally loaded pile tests are presented in this section. Seven

change in the test conﬁguration, two tests were performed. It should be mentioned that the
construction of p-y curves from the results of these tests is not possible since the pile was not
able to rotate freely, and deformation predictions are not of high accuracy.
An accurate estimation of both soil and pile properties is of vital importance for a soil-pile
interaction problem especially when it comes to intermediate soils. One CPTU test was
planning to be carried out for the evaluation of the homogeneity of the sample and the
determination of the soil properties. However, that was not possible due to a technical issue
in strain gauges of the piezocone. Hence, the homogeneity of the sample would be evaluated
by the installation force measurements. Strength parameters, such as peak friction angle
are related to the existing CPTU database, if possible, and back-calculated according to the
results from the various tests. The results from the laterally loaded pile tests are presented

DISCUSSION
The results obtained by all the tests carried out during the present case study are compared
and discussed in this chapter. Initially, the sample preparation method should be evaluated
since it plays an essential role for the assessment of the rest of the results (CPTU, Pile test).
The second approach regards the results from CPTU tests, and particularly, the eﬀect of g
level among samples of similar characteristics and the accuracy of the obtained results. It has
to be highlighted that only the tests that were in agreement with soil classiﬁcation chart were
used in this chapter, while the results from all the tests that carried out during the present
case study are illustrated in detail in chapter A. Subsequently, the inﬂuence of the ﬁne content
in the matter of tip resistance, sleeve friction and pore pressure would be discussed as well.
Finally, the results from the pile tests are assessed and combined with the outcomes from the
rest of the tests.

5.2
Discussion of CPTU results
5.2.1
Eﬀect of g level
Sandy samples: CPTU1,CPTU3
Initially, the aim was the preparation of two samples of diﬀerent relative density, however,
the second sample showed dilatancy behaviour during the saturation, mainly attributed to
the increased applied compaction. The more compacted the granular material is the more
prone to dilate, as grains are interlocking during compaction and therefore cannot move freely.
According to this, undercompaction method does not seem appropriate for the preparation of
dense samples of sand as they are prone to dilate if overcompaction being applied. Finally,
the two samples have a relative density of similar magnitude, where the ﬁrst one has a relative
density of Dr=35 % and the second Dr=40 %. The comparison of the CPTU measurements
of the two samples is shown in Figure 5.3.
The eﬀect of the increased g-level is the expected one as all CPTU measurements increased
with g-level. Tip resistance seems to be unaﬀected by the g level during the penetration of the
ﬁrst 70 [mm] while the deviation is clear beyond that point. Furthermore, the tip resistance
qt is grouped in respect of the g-level, which veriﬁes the repeatability of the results among
samples of similar characteristics. However, sleeve friction and pore pressure measurements
do not show a clear tendency of variation, but in general, both measurements seemed to be
increased with respect to the g-level.
Figure 5.3: Comparison of CPTU soundings in respect of depth for the samples of sand of
diﬀerent relative density.

the pressure distribution changes. Hence, a comparison of the location of the point of rotation
in respect of the pile tip is done and the resulting plot for all the silty sand samples of varying
ﬁne content and free cap conditions is shown in Figure 5.26.

varying FC (PT-4:PT-8).
The point of rotation could be identiﬁed from both displacement and soil resistance
distributions along the pile, and insigniﬁcant diﬀerences could be noticed. It should be
pointed out that the point of rotations for PT-5 & PT-7 are changing during the test which
may be attributed to the limited available displacement measurements. On the other hand,
the rest of the tests showed a consistent behaviour, where the point of rotation varies from
(0.64-0.72)·D in respect of the pile tip. In addition to that, the point of rotation is estimated
also according to Brinch Hansen theory, where the resulting location of rotation point found to
vary from (0.25-0.30)·D. Nevertheless, the point of rotation according to D-pile simulation
is located at a distance of (0.92)·D from the pile tip.
What is more, the point of rotation can be determined also based on the soil resistance
distribution. It can be seen that the point of rotation based on soil resistance is not located
in the same location as the on deﬁned based on displacement. Even if the one established
by the soil resistance is more reliable, owing to the independence of it’s estimation, the
obtained locations found to vary greatly during the test (comparing to the one deﬁned by the
displacement distribution).

CONCLUSION
The main objectives of the present case study have been the investigation of the lateral
response of piles embedded in intermediate soils and in conjunction with that, the suitable
characterization of the soil material and the evaluation of geotechnical properties such as
strength and stiﬀness. To do so, thirteen silty sand samples have been prepared, where various
tests have been carried out by using centrifuge modelling technique.
The sample preparation method used is the undercompaction method and it’s assessment
in terms of sample homogeneity is vital. Samples were taken, from both top and bottom
of the sample container, so as particle distribution curves to be determined. Additionally,
homogeneity is assessed in terms of measurements given during cone and pile penetration.
Since no particle segregation was observed in none of the samples and sample characteristics
are of similar magnitude if the same procedure is followed (repeatability), undercompaction
method is a reliable method for the preparation of silty sand samples of varying ﬁne content.
A reliable estimation of the soil properties is of crucial importance for the better understanding
of the lateral response of the pile, especially in intermediate soils where the drainage response
is unknown. Owing to the technical issue regarding the response of the strain gauges of the
cone, six CPTU tests were performed. The results have been compared with CPTUs carried
out in samples of similar characteristics. It can be noted that the higher the ﬁne content
the more repeatable the results are. Particularly, tests performed in samples of low FC (less
than 20%) show a wider range of results which can be attributed to their vulnerability to
compaction.
The soil classiﬁcation scheme proposed by Robertson (2009) was used for the classiﬁcation of
the soil based on CPTU measurements. Although the dimensions of the cone used and the
penetration rate are not the standards one, the SBTn predicts accurately the soil classiﬁcation
in most of the cases. It should be mentioned that there were some tests that do not fulﬁll the
SBTn proposed by Robertson (2009), however this is attributed to the issue with the strain
gauges of the cone. The drainage response of the soil samples tested in centrifuge facilities
are assessed based on the development of excess pore pressure. Speciﬁcally, trivial excess pore
pressure have been developed in all samples, therefore the drainage response varies from fully
drained to partially drained. Furthermore, there is not a particular way of the magnitude of
excess pore pressure build up in respect of ﬁne content.
Three diﬀerent correlations have been used for the prediction of the ﬁne content. Particularly,
correlations proposed by Robertson & Wride (1998) and Idriss & Boulanger (2008) tend to
underestimate the ﬁne content compared to the one suggested by F.Yi which shows to be in
agreement with the measured ﬁne content.
As far as the lateral response of the piles embedded in silty sand soils is concerned, the
results showed to be well established since the results from pile installation and pile test
98
==PAGE:116==
6. Conclusion
under lateral loading are consistent. Nevertheless, forces measured during the pile installation
and tip resistance are not in agreement for the samples of 30% and 84% FC. Speciﬁcally,
installation forces of the aforementioned samples are higher comparing to samples (20%, 40%
FC) that have higher tip resistance. For the sample of 30%, the higher installation force
can be attributed to the fact that the lowest excess pore pressure was developed during the
installation.
The behaviour of the pile was rigid for all the silty sand samples that prepared and tested
during this case study. The pile found to rotate in a distance of (0.60-0.80)· D from pile tip
while the point of rotation according to Brinch Hansen (1961) theory found to be in a distance
of (0.25-0.30)· D from the pile tip. The ultimate pile capacity has been reached at all tests,
showing a decreasing tendency with respect to the increase of ﬁne content. Nevertheless, soil
resistance do not vary with the same way as the ultimate pile capacity. The ultimate soil
resistance found to be linearly related with the tip resistance for the silty sand samples of
20%, 40% and 50% FC.
Finally, all the tests carried out were combined for the evaluation of the peak drained friction
angle. The approaches used for the prediction of friction angle were based on :
i . CPTU measurements, Robertson & Campanella
ii . CPTU measurements, Kulhawy & Mayne
iii . CPTU measurements, NTH method
iv . analytical method for the prediction of installation force, Meyerhof.
v . ultimate soil resistance suggested by API.
vi . ultimate pile capacity, Brom’s (1964) theory
It should be mentioned that all the methods above have been established for clean sands.
Among the CPT based methods, Robertson & Campanella is the one that predicts the highest
values of friction angle while the Kulhawy & Mayne predicts the lowest values of friction angle.
The distribution of friction angle that is back-calculated by the ultimate soil resistance based
on API is similar for all the silty sand samples except the one of 30% FC which veriﬁes that
soil resistance is higher at shallow depths. In general, peak drained friction angle decreases
with respect to the increase of FC.
6.1
Further studies
Since there is a lack of standardized guidance for the evaluation of the behavior and the
properties of intermediate soils by using CPTU measurements, several topics related to this
can be examined in the future. As far as the sample preparation method is concerned, the
undercompaction method is an appropriate method, however the two bottom layers of the
some samples were overcompacted. This indicates that a further investigation regarding the
applied tamping frequency at each layer is needed. What is more, saturation of the samples
needs to be improved as air bubbles arose in the soil surface even if the sample seemed to be
fully saturated. To do so, CO2 may introduced in the sample to ﬁll the air gaps, since CO2 is
lighter and water would ﬁll the gaps easier.
Currently, the interpretation followed for intermediate soils is the one for sand or clay,
depending on drainage response. Greater database is needed for the better evaluation whether
99
==PAGE:117==
6. Conclusion
the theory for sand or clay can be applied in intermediate soils. This requires that CPTU tests
should be carried out in silty sand samples of varying ﬁne content and various penetration
rates.
Similar to the above, larger database concerning pile tests under lateral loading is needed. In
conjunction with this, supplementary tests such as direct shear, triaxial and oedometer tests
should perform so as a reliable outcome to be achieved, concerning the estimation of strength
and stiﬀness parameters. In accordance to that, a CPT based formulation can be derived for
the prediction of p-y curves for piles embedded in silty sand soils with non plastic ﬁnes.
Finally, the axial response of the pile embedded in intermediate soils, such as silty sand, is
also an unfathomed area, since methods determined for sand are currently used.

installation. Initially, the strain gauges need to be calibrated, however the response of the pile
during the axial calibration was not the expected one. Particularly, the applied load varies
bi-linearly with respect to the output signal. As a consequence, calibration factors could not
be precisely established. By juxtaposing the output signal from the results of pile installation
and the one during the calibration, it can be noted that the output signal from tests is located
in the linear part of the calibration curves (see Figure B.4).
