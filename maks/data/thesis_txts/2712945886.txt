Abstract
Technical University of Denmark
1
Abstract
Microbial communities and their interactions have a large impact on our everyday life and the Earth
we live on. The interactions span from intra-species to inter-domain, and several prominent examples
of the latter are known from the Bacteria and Eukarya. While the Archaea are known to engage in
positive interactions with organisms from the other domains, their potential for competition across
domain-boundaries remains uncovered.
This project aimed at investigating the potential role and distribution of three putative archaeal
competition mediators: acyl-homoserine lactone (AHL) lactonases, lytic transglycosylases (LTs) and
ribosomally synthesized and post-translationally modified peptides (RiPPs). The former two were
searched for in GenBank as well as the ’Genomes from Earth’s Microbiomes’ catalog using profile
hidden Markov models. The prevalence of RiPPs was explored by subjecting all archaeal GenBank
genomes to an antiSMASH analysis. It was found that RiPPs made up a significant portion of the
archaeal biosynthetic gene cluster content and that novel RiPPs potentially could act as mediators
of competition. A total of 72 putative AHL lactonases were found in the 5602 archaeal GenBank
assemblies. The lactonases were most commonly found in the TACK superphylum (68%), but due to
the hyperthemophilic nature of these organisms, the interference with bacterial quorum sensing by
these lactonases was questioned. Lastly, 56 LTs (strict curation) were found in GenBank and these
were most prevalent in DPANN (63%) and Euryarchaeota (30%). While a role in competition seemed
likely for Euryarchaeota, it was concluded that DPANN were more likely to utilize the putative LTs
for cell-attachment to symbionts or prey.
In conclusion, it is likely that the Archaea participate in competition with the Bacteria and that they
use RiPPs, and possibly enzymes such as AHL lactonases and LTs, to mediate this competition.

Resumé
Mikrobielle samfund og deres interaktioner har stor indflydelse på vores dagligdag og kloden vi bor
på. Typerne af interaktioner spænder bredt, og finder sted mellem både organismer af samme art,
samt forskellige domæner. For sidstnævnte findes der mange vigtige eksempler mellem Bacteria og
Eukarya. På trods af at Archaea er kendt for at lave positive interaktioner med de øvrige domæner, er
deres potentiale for at konkurrere med samme endnu ikke afdækket.
Formålet med dette projekt var at undersøge udbredelsen og mulige roller af tre potentielle konkur-
rencemekanismer i Archaea mod de øvrige domæner. Disse mekanismer var N-acylhomoserinlakton
(AHL) laktonaser, lytiske transglykosylaser (LT’er) og ribosomalt syntetiserede og post-translationelt
modificerede peptider (RiPPs). Profile hidden Markov models blev anvendt til at søge efter de to
førstnævnte mekanismer i GenBank samt ’Genomes from Earth’s Microbiomes catalog’. Udbredelsen
af RiPPs blev undersøgt ved at lave en antiSMASH analyse på alle GenBank genomer fra arkæer.
Undersøgelsen viste, at RiPPs udgjorde en betydelig del af alle biosyntetiske genklynger fra arkæer,
samt at hidtil ukendte RiPPs potentielt kunne fungere som konkurrencemekanismer. Der blev fundet
72 mulige AHL laktonaser i de 5602 genomer i GenBank, der stammede fra arkæer. Laktonaserne var
mest udbredt i gruppen, TACK (68%), men grundet disse organismers hypertermofile natur, blev der
sat spørgsmålstegn ved at disse laktonaser skulle interferere ved bakteriers quorum sensing. 56 lytiske
transglykosylaser (streng udvælgelse) blev fundet i GenBank og disse var mest udbredt i DPANN
(63%) og Euryarchaeota (30%). Det virkede sandsynligt, at LT’erne havde en rolle i konkurrence for
Euryarchaeota, men at DPANN brugte LT’erne til celletilknytning i forbindelse med symbiose eller
prædation.
Det kunne konkluderes, at Archaea sandsynligvis indgår i konkurrence med bakterier og at de bruger
RiPPs, og muligvis enzymerne AHL laktonaser og LT’er, som konkurrencemekanismer.

Background
Microorganisms live in communities that can be both complex and intimate [1][2]. The communities
may consist of highly similar species, but can also form across domain boundaries [3]. Cross-domain
communities is a well-known phenomenon, especially between bacteria and fungi. Nonetheless, the
interaction of these two with Archaea is much less explored, and many aspects remain to be discovered.
Microbial communities are found in every major niche on Earth, from the deep oceans and to the soil
in our backyard [4]. Even though these communities may seem insignificant to us as humans, they
have a large impact on our everyday life and the Earth we live on. This impact is both positive and
negative, and the same is true for the microbial interactions that causes it. A prominent example of a
positive cross-domain interaction, which is important to the very existence of humans, can be found in
the distant past, more specifically about 2 billion years ago [5]. At this time, eukaryogenesis took place
[5], meaning the evolutionary formation of the Eukarya [6]. Even though the puzzle of this process is
not fully solved, most recent evidence support the scenario in which a bacterial endosymbiont merged
with an archaeal host [7]. This highlights the important role of microbial interactions in general, but
also that the less explored archaea play an important role.
Other examples of positive interactions with positive impact constitute more than a single event and
takes place continuously. This includes biogeochemical cycles, which are pathways that circulate es-
sential elements of living matter [8], e.g. hydrogen, carbon, nitrogen and oxygen. The biogeochemical
cycles are largely driven by microorganisms [9][10], and even though we do not see them in our daily
life, the cycles are critical to the very existence of life, because they transform matter and energy into
bioavailable forms [11]. More specifically, most biogeochemical transformations are mediated by
multispecies communities performing synergistic cooperation [10]. An example where this happens
across domain boundaries is in methane consumption [2]. Here, sulfate reducing bacteria consume
hydrogen and thereby create an environment favorable for anaerobic methane oxidation by archaea
[10][2].
Humanity does not only benefit from positive microbial interactions, but we have also managed
to exploit microbial warfare to our benefit. This is the case when the compounds used to mediate
competition is used as a source of natural product discovery. One famous example of this is penicillin,
which was discovered in 1928 when a fungus inhibited bacterial growth on a culture dish [12]. Since
then, penicillin has saved millions of lives [12] and also sparked the discovery of new bioactive natural

products. This clearly illustrates how important microbial communities have been throughout history
and still are today.
Despite these examples, there are also many instances of microbial communities having a significant
negative effect on us. This is for example the case when bacteria cooperate to form a biofilm [13],
which in turn can cause biofouling [14]. This is a large economic and technological problem in settings
such as medical devices, wastewater treatment, bioreactors and aquaculture [14].
Even though much have been uncovered about microbial communities and their interactions, much
still remain to be discovered. This is especially true for the Archaea. As illustrated above, several
examples exist where archaea participate in positive interactions. Furthermore, they are known to
produce archaeocins which are toxins effective against archaea [15][16]. Despite this intra-domain
competition, there is no evidence of direct, antagonistic interactions (interference competition) with
bacteria or eukaryotes. This is despite the fact that archaea are not only found in extreme environments,
but are widely distributed on Earth [17][18], and they are likely to compete with bacteria for nutrients
and space [19]. Furthermore, interference competition is widely observed between bacteria and fungi,
which makes it seem likely that archaea would participate in similar interactions.
Uncovering the archaeal potential for cross-domain interference competition is of interest for several
reasons. First, this could help answering fundamental questions of microbial interactions and broaden
the knowledge gained from bacteria and fungi. This, in turn, could deepen our understanding
of processes driven by archaeal cross-domain interactions, such as biogeochemical processes and
eukaryogenesis. Second, the cross-domain interference competition could potentially be a source
of novel bioactive compounds. These could for example be quorum quenchers that could be used
as anti-fouling agents [14], or perhaps novel antimicrobial compounds. Additionally, since archaeal
interactions are less explored than the interaction between bacteria and fungi, there may be a higher
potential for the discovery of completely novel compounds.
This project will focus on investigating three potential mediators of archaeal interference competition,
namely acyl-homoserine lactone (AHL) lactonases, lytic transglycosylases (LTs) and ribosomally
synthesized and post-translationally modified peptides (RiPPs). The AHL lactonase could potentially
be a mediator of competition by degrading AHLs used in bacterial quorum sensing and thereby
disturbing communication. The lactonase have been proposed as mediator because four proteins of
the same lactonase family have been discovered within the Archaea [20][21][22][23], but the AHL

Technical University of Denmark
5
Introduction
Microorganisms are rarely, if ever, found to live in isolation [13]. Rather, they are part of complex and
diverse microbial communities [1], where the microorganisms are not only in close physical proximity,
but they also interact through a variety of mechanisms. These interactions can impact the participating
organisms in different ways, namely positively (win), neutrally or negatively (loss) [13]. Different
combinations of impacts on the involved organisms allows for classification of the interactions into
mutualism, commensalism, parasitism, predation, amenalism or competition (Fig. 1) [13].
Figure 1: Summary of the categorization of interactions, based on the effect (positive, negative or
neutral) on species 1 and 2 (red and green). Source: Faust et al. [13].
Mutualism is a win-win situation in which both organisms benefit from the interaction (Fig. 1), by
for example exchanging metabolic products (cross-feeding) [13]. In commensalism and amenalism,
one organism is unaffected, while the other is positively (commensalism) or negatively (amenalism)
affected (Fig. 1) [13]. This is for example the case when metabolic products are released, which
can be utilized by other community members (commensalism), or which alter the environment and
negatively affect other organisms (amenalism) [13]. In both predation and parasitism, one organism
benefits at the expense of another (Fig. 1): both predators and parasites consume nutrients from the
prey/host, but they differ in the aspect that the predator kills the prey, while in parasitism the host stays
alive [13]. Lastly, in competition both organisms are negatively affected (Fig. 1) [13].

5.1
Microbial competition
5.1.1
Competition: drivers and types
What drives an interaction, which is deleterious to both participants? The answer lies in a scarcity
of resources [28]. The reason is that scarcity forces organisms to fight for the same finite resources
to ensure their survival. The most important resources for microbial survival are space and nutrients,
including carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen and phosphorous [28]. Consequently, a prerequisite for competi-
tion is that the competitors overlap in resource need [28]. This exact requirement also pinpoints how
predation and parasitism differs from competition [28].
The amount of competition varies between environments and depends on several factors [28]. Condi-
tions that tend to favor competition are: (i) similar resource requirements of coexisting strains, (ii)
spatial mixing of different strains and (iii) high cell density compared to available resources [28].
The prevalence of competition not only differs between environments, but also between different
taxonomic groups. For example, several families of the Actinomycetales order dedicate more than 5%
of their coding potential to the production of secondary metabolites [29]. These metabolites include
antibiotics and other damaging agents, but in some cases the function is also unknown. In contrast to
this, some groups of organisms, such as the Candidate Phyla Radiation (CPR), have small genomes
and likely a parasitic lifestyle and in these cases, the participation in competition remains unclear [30].
The competition itself also takes different forms and generally it may be divided into two types:
exploitative competition and interference competition [28]. Exploitative competition is an indirect
and passive form of competition, where the competitor consumes some of the limited resources,
thereby reducing its availability to its competitors [28]. Examples hereof include secretion of digestive
enzymes, increased nutrient uptake through fast growth and rapid colonization of uninhabited spaces
[28]. In contrast, interference competition is a direct and active form of competition, where the
microorganisms damage or kill each other [28]. While the classic example of this is the production
of antimicrobials, interference competition also involves disruption of the competitive phenotype
of co-inhabitants through e.g. protease production, or quorum quenching [28]. While interference
competition is common among microbial eukaryotes and bacteria, thus spanning the domain boundary,
evidence of cross-domain interference competition involving archaea remains to be uncovered.

5.1.2
Arsenal of interference competition
The microbial arsenal used during interference competition is very diverse. For example, the weapons
can either be mechanical, biological or chemical [30]. The mechanical weapons physically damage
cells by e.g. puncturing the cell membrane in the case of tailocins [30]. Prophages take the role
of biological weapons when they excise themselves from their host and goes on to infect and kill
competitors, but not clone mates, which are immune to superinfection [30].
Nonetheless, the most commonly employed weapons are chemical weapons [30]. These can be
delivered in an contact-independent manner through diffusion or vesicles, or alternatively in a contact-
dependent manner via syringe-like protrusions [30][28]. Just as the delivery method differs, the
chemical weapons span a wide chemical and biosynthetic spectrum. Classification of the compounds
can be done in a number of ways, but one approach is to put emphasis on the biosynthetic pathway as
done by Caulier et al. [31]. These authors divide antimicrobial compounds into four main groups:
ribosomal peptides, non-ribosomal peptides, polyketides and volatiles [31].
Antagonistic ribosomal peptides are synthesized by the ribosomal machinery in the cell and the group
is generally comprised of enzymes and ’ribosomally synthesized and post-translationally modified
peptides’ (RiPPs). In both cases, the proteins are encoded by genes, but in the case of RiPPs, a
precursor peptide is post-translationally modified to produce the mature RiPP [31].
These posttranslational modifications expand the chemical space for RiPPs and additionally, they are
used to categorize RiPPs into different classes [32]. At least 40 different classes are currently known
[32][33] and examples include lasso peptides and lanthipeptides [33]. A well-known RiPP family are
the bacteriocins [34].
Enzymes can be mediators of competition by for example being lytic or involved in quorum quenching
[31]. The lytic enzymes are cell wall degrading enzymes and comprise cellulases, proteases, chitinases
and glucanases [31]. In contrast to this, quorum quenching enzymes are considerably more subtle
in their mode of action. Quorum quenching (QQ) is the disturbance of quorum sensing (QS) - a
form of cell-cell signaling [30]. This signaling can be used to express phenotypes such as motility,
biofilm formation or virulence on the basis of population-density. The signaling can be mediated by
N-acylhomoserine lactones (AHLs), which are molecules consisting of a central lactone with an acyl
chain of variable length and substituents. It is exactly these molecules that are degraded or modified
by QQ enzymes, which can be either of four catalytic classes: lactonases, amidases, reductases and

cytochrome oxidases [14]. Since the QS is used to express competitive phenotypes, QQ enzymes are
mediators of interference competition.
In contrast to RiPPs and enzymes, nonribosomal peptides (NRPs) are not synthesized by ribosomes
[16]. Instead, large, modular synthetases assemble the NRPs in an assembly-line fashion, where
nonproteogenic amino acids also can be used as building blocks [35]. Similar to NRPs, polyketides
(PKs) are usually produced by large, modular (mega)enzymes in an assembly-line fashion [35].
However, other types of polyketide synthases (PKSs) exist, where the polyketide is produced iteratively
[36]. Lastly, volatile compounds include for example terpenoids, as well as fatty acids and their
derivatives [31].
It is clear that the chemical weapons for interference competition spans a large chemical spectrum. In
addition to this, they also cover a large spectrum of taxonomic reach, meaning how taxonomically
different the target organism is compared to the producer, ranging from intra-species to inter-domain.
For example, bacteriocins have a very narrow spectrum and are known to target closely related species
[30]. In contrast, other compounds both have a broader spectrum of activity and can cross domain
boundaries. These inter-domain antagonistic compounds include e.g. lytic enzymes, antibiotics and
volatile metabolites [37]. In cross-domain interference competition between bacteria and fungi, it
is especially PKs and NRPs that are the most well-known mediators. Among these mediators some
of the most famous and used antibacterials and antifungals can be found, including e.g. penicillins,
tetracyclines, cephalosporins and nystatin.
In contrast to the bacterial-fungal antagonism that has been widely studied and laid the basis for the
field of natural product discovery, the bacterial and fungal competition with Archaea is much less
studied.

5.2
Archaea - the unknown player in inter-domain interference competition
5.2.1
The hurdle of unculturability
Archaea is a domain that have experienced great development and expansion since its classification as
a separate domain less than 50 years ago. It was Carl Woese and George Fox who proposed, in 1977,
that the Archaea formed a domain separate from Bacteria and Eukarya [38]. For a long time, archaeal
diversity was based on culturable representatives, and between 1990 and 2002, only two archaeal
phyla were recognized: Euryarchaeota and Crenarchaeota [39]. Culturable species from these two
phyla were mostly extremophiles (thermophilic, acidophilic and halophilic [40]) and methanogens,
which led to the perception that Archaea mainly comprised of extremophiles [41].
However, this view changed with the advancements in DNA sequencing methodologies, which enabled
the study of archaea directly from environmental samples and thus allowed for the description of
unculturable archaea [42]. Notably, this led to the discovery that archaea are much more widely
distributed than first assumed. They were found to be present in many non-extreme environments,
from soil and aquatic systems to the human body [43][44][45]. In addition, this also meant that
archaeal diversity was greatly expanded, from the initial 2 phyla to 27 proposed phyla in 2020 [40].
However, only 6 of these phyla have cultured representatives (Fig. 2) [40], which clearly illustrates
the challenge of cultivation recalcitrance in the microbial world. The 27 phyla are grouped into three
recognized superphyla: Asgard, DPANN and TACK (Fig. 2), where the last two names are acronyms
of the first phyla present in the groups [40]. In addition to these, the phylum Euryarchaeota, which
contains a large part of archaeal diversity, does not fall within a superphylum (Fig. 2) [40][46]. In
general, archaeal taxonomy is very uneven at higher taxonomic ranks [46], and alternative standardized
taxonomies have been proposed to the NCBI taxonomy [46].

The hurdle of unculturability complicates the study of archaea in several ways. One aspect, which
is difficult to study, is their potential for cross-domain interference competition. The reason for this
difficulty is that the standard procedure to study microbial interactions are co-culture experiments [13].
Since these experiments are based on the cultivation of microorganisms together, their use is restricted
to culturable microorganism. Hence, in the study of archaeal interactions, scientists mainly have to
rely on culture-independent methods or alternatively, enhancing archaeal culturability.

5.2.2
Genome mining: looking for the hidden potential
One example of a culture-independent method is genome mining, which is the prediction of natu-
ral products based on genomic information [47]. The mining is performed using sequence-based
comparison software, which search the genome for conserved biosynthetic machinery involved in
natural product synthesis [47]. As a result, PKs, NRPs, RiPPs and many other natural products can
be identified. While genomes are usually mined with drug discovery in mind [47], the bioactive
compounds also have an ecological role, which could be as a weapon in interference competition.
Thus, genome mining can give an indication of potential mechanisms involved in interactions and
competition in specific organisms.
Genome mining of archaea has been performed previously on several occasions [48][49][27]. Wang et
al. searched specifically for PKSs, NRPSs and hybrids thereof, and while the authors found that these
enzymes were widely distributed in bacteria and fungi, they were only found rarely in archaea [48].
The same conclusion was reached by Nayfach et al. in their reporting of biosynthetic gene clusters
(BGCs) in two archaeal phyla: Halobacteriota and Thermoplasmatota [27]. Here, it was found that
Halobacteriota contained no PKSs and few NRPSs, but in contrast, a relatively large amount of RiPPs
and terpenes was observed [27]. Thermoplasmatota contained some PKSs, but more than 50% of their
BGC content consisted of terpenes [27]. Terpenes were also found to make up a significant portion of
the archaeal BGC content reported by Paoli et al. [49].
As described in the Background (section 4), the archaea are likely involved in cross-domain interference
competition. This is based on their geographic distribution, and the fact that competition is commonly
observed for bacteria and fungi. Nonetheless, the enzymatic machineries for some of the most well-
known mediators of interference competition, NRPs and PKs, are only sporadically found in the
Archaea [48]. This could suggest that archaeal PKSs and NRPSs are too different from the bacterial
and fungal counterparts to be recognized by sequence comparison software, or that archaea use other
mediators of competition. These could be RiPPs, which are found in some archaeal genomes, or the
widely distributed terpenes [27][49]. A widely recognized role of RiPPs is the role as mediators of
competition, but there is limited knowledge of the chemical ecology of environmental RiPP producers
[50]. Terpenoids have also been suggested as mediators of competition, however evidence for their
competitive benefits remain scarce [51]. Furthermore, terpenoids make up a very large and chemically
diverse class of natural products, some of which play important roles in intra- and inter-specific

Alternatively, it is possible that the mediator is different than the commonly observed mediators in

5.3
AHL lactonases
As previously mentioned, AHL lactonases are a class of quorum quenching enzymes, which function
as mediators of interference competition by interrupting QS and thereby the competitive phenotypes
it might lead to. The AHL lactonases currently known in archaea are more specifically known as
phosphotriesterase-like lactonases (PLLs). This is because the lactonases are homologs of phosphotri-
esterases (PTEs) [20], which are enzymes that cleave toxic compounds called organophosphates [52].
It was indeed also this activity that Merone et al. were searching for when they discovered the first
PLL, SsoPox, from the thermophilic archaeon, Saccharolobus solfataricus [52]. However, while the
researchers only measured a low phosphotriesterase activity [52], it was later discovered that SsoPox
was a proficient lactonase and the concept of PLLs was born [20].
SsoPox was initially identified because of its sequence identity with PTE, albeit low (∼30%) [52].
Despite said low diversity, the enzymes are similar in structure and both contain a (β/α)8-barrel
(TIM-barrel) with a bimetallic center making up the active site at the C-terminus of the barrel (Fig. 3)
[23].

distribution throughout the archaeal domain remains unknown.

role. If the lactonases function in interference competition via quorum quenching, does this competition
happen within or across the archaeal domain boundary? Alternatively, QQ enzymes have also been

5.4
Lytic transglycosylases
Lytic transglycosylases (LTs) are another type of enzyme, which might be involved in archaeal inter-
domain competition. This enzyme was first speculated to be an archaeal mediator of competition
by Rinke et al., who saw evidence of LTs in the genomes of two Nanoarchaeota [26]. LTs cleave
peptidoglycan (also known as murein) and are ubiquitous in the Bacteria [56]. However, the Archaea
does not possess peptidoglycan in their cell envelope [57] and while a similar polymer, called
pseudomurein, exists in the Archaea [58], the structure is different [58]. Furthermore, protein surface
layers (S-layer) are much more widely distributed in archaea [57]. This begs the question why LTs
would be found in archaeal genomes? As a possible answer, Rinke et al. speculated that the LTs are
secreted from the cell and are used either as a defense mechanism against bacteria or as a facilitator of
cell-to-cell interaction [26].
Lytic transglycosylases (also called lytic murein transglycosylases) belong to the superfamily of
glycoside hydrolases [59]. While this implies a hydrolytic reaction involving water, the reaction
performed by LTs is in fact non-hydrolytic [59]. Here, the peptidoglycan is cleaved by concomitant
formation of a new bond (Fig. 5), hence the name transglycosylase. LTs cleave the β-1,4 glycosidic
bond between the sugar residues of peptidoglycan, N-acetylglucosamine (NAG) and N-acetylmuramic
acid (NAM) (Fig. 5) [56].
Figure 5: Shows the activity of LTs on the peptidoglycan strands (NAG-NAM)n. Source: [59].
LTs have been described as autolysins, because they can cause cell lysis if not controlled properly
[56]. However, when controlled, the LT activity is utilized to accommodate changes in the cell wall,
19
==PAGE:21==
5
Introduction
Technical University of Denmark
for example for excavating the cell wall to insert flagella, pili and secretion systems [59]. It has been
suggested that a matching LT exist for each of these tasks, but functional redundancy also exists
between them [59]. In addition, the primary task performed by each LT is unknown in many cases,
but it is known that knockout of all LTs in an organism results in cell death [59], highlighting their
essentiality.
LTs can be characterized in multiple ways. First, LTs can have a preference for either exolytic or
endolytic strand cleavage [59]. Second, LTs can be either soluble (located in periplasm) or membrane-
bound, in which they are associated with the periplasmic side of the outer membrane (inner leaflet)
[56]. In addition to this, the LTs are also divided into families. LTs are found in both Gram-negative
and Gram-positive bacteria, and the latter has two families [59]. The Gram-negative LTs are currently
divided into 6 families, in which each family has a unique catalytic LT fold [59] . The largest Gram-
negative family of LTs is family 1, which consists of LTs, which are diverse in both function and
sequence [59]. Four consensus motifs are used to assign LTs to family 1 and varieties and these motifs
are used to divide the LTs into subfamilies [59].
After the discovery of LTs in archaeal genomes in 2013 by Rinke et al., a similar observation was
made by Castelle et al. in 2015 in a Woesearchaeota archaeon [24]. In comparison, the putative
LTs found by Rinke et al. were ∼200 amino acids, while the one found by Castelle et al. was 2166
amino acids [26][24]. Nonetheless, they were both predicted to have an LT domain and to be secreted
[26][24]. These findings were further established when Castelle et al. found that a protein family,
containing a murein-like lytic transglycosylase domain, was widely distributed within Woesearchaeota
and Pacearchaeota while mostly absent from other lineages [25]. In addition to the potential role in
defense against bacteria, the authors suggested that LTs could be used for host attachment in symbiotic
relationships [25]. Contrary to this, the authors of another study, Vigneron et al., suggested that the LTs
they found in metagenomic-derived Woesearchaeota, were used for predation rather than symbiosis
[60].
Overall, the ecological role of the putative archaeal LTs remains to be discovered, despite the multiple
suggested roles [26][25][60]. Furthermore, the presence of LTs in the Archaea currently relies solely
on genomic information and it remains unknown whether the LTs are expressed or not. In addition,
the putative LTs have so far only been discovered in members of the DPANN superphylum. However,
their distribution throughout the entire DPANN as well as other phyla, remains to be found.

6.1
antiSMASH analysis: RiPPs as potential competition mediators
To investigate the potential role of RiPPs as mediators of archaeal cross-domain interference competi-
tion, all archaeal genomes were subjected to analysis using the antibiotics and secondary metabolite
analysis shell (antiSMASH) [61]. To do this, all archaeal genomes available in GenBank were down-
loaded in genbank format (.gbff) (downloaded on 20/04/22), using the Python package, ncbi-genome-
download (0.3.0) [62]. The genomes were then analyzed using the Python package antiSMASH (6.1.0)
[61].
The analysis was initially performed in minimal mode, where Prodigal was used as ’genefinding-tool’
in the antiSMASH function. The SeqIO function from the Biopython package (1.79) [63], was
then used to extract and analyze the results from the output folders. Based on the results, genomes
containing at least one BGC were re-analyzed in a full-featured run. Here, the following parameters
were specified in the antiSMASH function: –genefinding-tool prodigal –cb-general –cb-knownclusters
–cb-subclusters –asf –pfam2go –smcog-trees. Results were once again analyzed with Bio.SeqIO in
Python and BGCs were grouped into 6 broad categories based on BIG-SCAPE classes, but with a few
modifications [64]. The BGC categories were: NRPS, PKS, RiPP, Terpene, Other and Hybrid, where
’Hybrid’ includes cross-category hybrids, but not intra-category hybrids which are simply placed in
the given category.

6.2.1
Construction of profile hidden Markov models
AHL lactonase
The profile hidden Markov model (pHMM) for the AHL lactonase was generated using known
archaeal and bacterial PLLs-A. Protein sequences were retrieved from GenBank [65] using accession
numbers from Bzdrenga et al. 2014 (supplementary material) [23], as well as Hiblot et al. 2012
and 2013 [21][66]. For those accession numbers where the record had been removed, the suggested
100% identical protein was chosen instead, if available. A multiple sequence alignment (MSA) was
generated from the protein sequences with MEGA11 [67], using the MUSCLE alignment method with
default parameters. This alignment was exported in FASTA format and used as input in the hmmbuild
function in the HMMER software (3.3.2) [68] to generate the pHMM.
Lytic transglycosylase
The same approach was used to generate the LT pHMM, but the sequences were acquired in a different
manner (Fig. 6). The sequence of the putative archaeal LT from the study by Rinke et al. [26], was
found in the IMG/M database (gene ID: 2264952250) [69]. This sequence was queried against the
non-redundant protein database for archaea (taxid: 2157) using BLASTp [70]. Hits with an e-value
below 0.01 and a query coverage above 50% were selected for further work. To avoid highly similar
sequences in the final pHMM, a pairwise BLASTp was made between all hits. For hits with a pairwise
percent identity above 90%, only the hit with the lowest e-value was included. Protein sequences
for the selected BLAST hits were downloaded with NCBI Batch Entrez using the Identical Protein
Groups database [71].
To increase sequence diversity in the pHMM, known bacterial LTs were also included. For this purpose,
protein sequences from different bacterial LT families were obtained from NCBI Protein database [65]
by using locus names from the review by Dik et al. [59]. For gene records that were discontinued,
the encoded protein was still included if the protein was a non-redundant protein sequence, since this
meant it was found in other genomes. For genes where the protein record itself was discontinued,
the suggested 100% identical sequence was included instead, if available. Protein sequences for the
Gram-positive LTs were retrieved from UniProt [72] by matching information from Dik et al. [59]
with the database, including name and length of the protein, as well as the organism of origin.
To construct the most specific pHMM, not all bacterial LT families were included in the pHMM, but
only the LT family most similar to the archaeal LT found by Rinke et al.. The most similar family

Selection of proteins for expression

Hydration
Twist Bioscience DNA products were hydrated by spinning down the tubes and subsequently adding

Cloning
For cloning, competent Escherichia coli dh5α-cells from -80 °C were thawed on ice. For each cloning

Archiving
Plates were checked for successful transformation, meaning colony growth on Twist gene plates and
positive controls, and furthermore no growth on negative controls. Given a successful transformation,
single colonies of E. coli transformed with Twist genes were restreaked onto an LB+Amp plate for
archiving. The archive plate was incubated O/N at 37 °C and subsequently stored at 5 °C.
Liquid cultures and purification of plasmids
Colonies (two biological replicates for each protein) were transferred from the archive plate to 5 mL
liquid LB+Amp medium. Cultures were incubated O/N at 37 °C, 150 rpm.
Plasmids were purified using the GenElute™Plasmid Miniprep Kit. Kit protocol was followed, with
the specification and changes specified below. 4 mL O/N cultures were pelleted (consecutive pelleting
of 2 x 2mL in same tube) by centrifugation (12,000 g, 1 min). Debris was pelleted at 16,000 g for
10 min. 750 μL cleared lysate was transferred to the prepped column. The optional wash was not
performed. After spinning to dry the column, the columns were transferred to new collection tubes
and placed at 60 °C for 10 min with lids open to completely dry columns. Milli-Q was used instead of
elution solution and it was added in two steps. First, 50 μL Milli-Q was added to the column, followed
by 10 min wait. The column was centrifuged (12,000 g, 1 min), followed by another addition of 50 μL
Milli-Q and centrifugation. Purified plasmids were analyzed using NanoDrop with Mili-Q as blank.
Purified plasmids were stored at -20 °C.

Restriction enzyme digestion and purification of gene inserts
1000 ng purified plasmid (concentration determined using NanoDrop) was digested with restriction
enzymes. This was done by adding Milli-Q (to have a total reaction volume of 20 μL), 2 μL CutSmart
buffer and 1 μL SacI and PacI. Tubes were mixed, spun down and incubated at 37 °C for a maximum
of 16 h. The reactions were stopped by incubation at 65 °C for 25 min. The digested DNA was run
on a 1% agarose gel and subsequently, the gel band which matched the expected size of the plasmid
insert, was cut out of the gel.
To purify the DNA inserts, the GFX PCR DNA and GelBand Purification Kit from Cytiva was used.
Kit protocol was followed, with the specification and changes specified below. 300 μL capture buffer
type 3 was added to the gel slices and the mixture was heated to 60 °C for 15 min (mixed halfway
through). After loading the column with sample, the flow through was added to the column again
and the same centrifugation was used. The wash step was performed one extra time, in the same
manner. Following washing, the column was centrifuged at 16,000 g for 30 sec to dry the column
and subsequently it was heated to 60 °C for 10 min with lids open. Elution was performed with
Milli-Q, where 20 μL was initially added to the column, followed by incubation for 15 min and
subsequent centrifugation. Following this, another 20 μL was added and the sample was centrifuged
again. Concentration and purity of DNA inserts were measured using NanoDrop (Milli-Q as blank).

8
Discussion
Interference competition is a direct form of competition, which arises when microorganisms compete
for the same resources, for example space and nutrients [28]. It can happen between closely related
species, but it is also a well-known phenomenon between bacteria and fungi, and this interaction
has been exploited as a source of bioactive natural products. Even though the Archaea has gained
more attention with the advancements of sequencing methodologies, their potential to participate in
cross-domain interference competition is much less investigated and currently lacks evidence. This is
despite the fact that the archaea are widely distributed on Earth and therefore likely inhabit the same
environments as bacteria and fungi and compete for nutrients.
This project investigated potential mediators of archaeal cross-domain interference competition,
including the exploration of RiPPs as well as two proposed enzymatic mediators, namely AHL
lactonases and lytic transglycosylases.
8.1
The distribution and diversity of putative novel, archaeal RiPPs
Based on the genome mining performed in this study, it was found that biosynthetic potential differed
between taxonomic groups, both in terms of numbers and identities of BGCs (Fig. 13). This
observation, that taxonomy is a driver of biosynthetic potential, was also made by Nayfach et al. [27].
These authors found that Halobacteriota (NCBI: Euryarchaeota phylum) encoded almost no NRPSs and
PKSs, but many RiPPs and terpenes [27]. However, in another taxonomic group, Thermoplasmatota,
the BGC content consisted of close to 20% PKS, in addition to 68% terpene gene clusters [27],
thereby illustrating the difference between taxonomic groups. Ultimately, the results found here and
by Nayfach et al. provides increased resolution to a previous study by Wang et al., which concluded
that PKSs and NRPSs were rarely found in archaea [48][27].
In the domain-wide analysis performed here, it was found that PKSs and NRPSs generally made up
a small portion of the total BGC content, but they were more prominent in groups such as Asgard,
Thermoplasmatota and Euryarchaeota (Fig. 13). However, it should also be noted that differences
existed between genomes within the same taxonomic group, as illustrated by the large standard
deviations (Fig. 13). Another point for consideration is that genome mining only aims at predicting
which BGCs might be present in the genome, and this might not reflect reality. One parameter,
which is important for prediction accuracy, is high genome quality. This entails a genome that is
51
==PAGE:53==
8
Discussion
Technical University of Denmark
not too fragmented, because fragmentation would result in scattering of BGC genes across multiple
contigs [83]. This could potentially pose a significant caveat in the genome mining performed in
this study, since many archaeal genomes available in GenBank are fragmented. This can be seen
from the assembly level, where more than half of the assemblies are assembled to contig level (Fig.
11). In addition, it is noted by Blin et al. that special care should be taken for metagenomic data,
where reliable identification of multi-gene BGCs is only possible for well-assembled genomes [83].
More than 80% of the genomes analyzed here are metagenome-derived and as discussed above, not
assembled to very high levels. Therefore, it is important to note that the prediction might be less
accurate for large BGCs such as PKSs and NRPSs and more accurate for the smaller BGCs such
as RiPPs and terpenes. This notion seems to be supported by the results because more NRPSs and
PKSs are located on contig edges, compared to RiPPs and terpenes, and are therefore likely to be
scattered across multiple contigs [84]. More specifically, 90% of NRPSs and 87% of PKSs are located
on contig edges, while the same number for terpenes and RiPPs is closer to 50% (Fig. 14). Ultimately,
a consequence might be an overestimation of the number of NRPSs and PKSs. For example, one
genome in the data set contained 27 NRPSs, which were all located on contig edges and 13 of the
NRPSs consisted of a single module.
In contrast to PKSs and NRPSs, the results for RiPPs and terpenes are likely a better reflection of
reality. Here, it was seen that these two BGC categories constituted a large part of the total biosynthetic
potential (Fig. 13). Specifically, RiPPs made up a large part of the BGC content in TACK, while
Thermoplasmatota encoded many terpenes (Fig. 13).
While terpenes are not commonly known as mediators of interference competition but rather of
communication, the present view is that RiPPs act in competition [50]. Further investigation of the
RiPPs found in archaea, showed that the most abundant RiPP type across taxonomic groups when
normalized to number of genomes, was RRE-containing BGCs (Fig. 15). RiPP recognition elements
(RREs) are domains found within RiPP tailoring enzymes, which can be used to bind RiPP precursor
peptides [61]. Clusters identified as ’RRE-containing’ contain an RRE and moreover are not identified
by other detection rules [61]. Consequently, these clusters are potentially novel RiPP clusters. The
same might be true for the RiPP-like clusters, which was the second most observed RiPP type (Fig.
15). This RiPP type has profiles commonly seen in RiPPs, but the profiles are insufficient to classify
them as RiPPs [61]. The function of these two groups of potential RiPPs can only be speculated at this

point, but it is possible they are novel, archaeal RiPPs used for inter-domain interference competition.
The use of RiPPs, rather than PKs and NRPs, for competition would also make sense because archaeal
genomes are smaller than bacterial genomes [85], and therefore a mediator encoded by a smaller BGC
seems more likely.
Nevertheless, it is also possible that the identified putative novel RiPPs are genomic artefacts or that
they serve other functions than mediating competition. Furthermore, it should be kept in mind that
several, less frequent RiPP types were found in the archaeal assemblies, but these have not been
discussed here. Regardless, additional potential mediators are explored below, which might act in
concert with, or instead of, the RiPPs.
8.2
AHL lactonases in high-temperature environments
Quorum quenching enzymes are well-known from the bacterial and eukaryotic domain [14]. Moreover,
AHL lactonase activity have been observed in archaeal enzymes [21][23][22] and a role in interference
of bacterial quorum sensing has been proposed. Nonetheless, their true ecological role remains to be
uncovered and the same is true for their taxonomic distribution.
In this study the putative AHL lactonases were mainly found in the TACK superphylum (∼70%),
both according to GenBank and the GEM catalog. The remaining AHL lactonases were distributed
approximately evenly between Euryarchaeota and Asgard. Within TACK, the putative producers
mostly originated from Crenarchaeota and herein 3 genera were especially prominent: Saccharolobus,
Sulfolobus and Vulcanisaeta. The known archaeal PLL-A producers also originate from these three
genera and hence, the archaeal PLLs-A included in the pHMM are produced by members of the
identified genera. Within the three genera, the same species as the known producers were recovered,
but in addition, species not known to be producers were also found.
Several putative AHL lactonases encoded by organisms isolated from thermal springs (GEM catalog),
grouped within the clade of known archaeal PLLs-A (Fig. 21). They were all identified to be produced
by the same S. islandicus strain and they were most closely related to known PLL-A, SisLac, which is
also produced by S. islandicus. Hence, these proteins likely do not represent novel PLLs-A. Another
group of putative AHL lactonases were all produced by a Vulcanisaeta species and most closely related
to VmoLac (Fig. 21). These proteins might be novel PLLs-A. Lastly, a large group of putative AHL
lactonases (GEM catalog) were not encoded by known PLL-A producers, and they were more closely

sequence and not just the active site, they might still have lactonase activity rather than PTE activity.
In addition to the GEM data discussed above, the putative AHL lactonases in GenBank, were also
found both within and outside the group of known PLL-A producers.
To determine if the identified AHL lactonases are in fact used as mediators of inter-domain interference
competition, several factors can be taken into account. First, it is worth considering that Crenarchaeota
has been known for harboring many extremophiles [40][39]. This is also true for the three prominent
Crenarchaeota genera found here, which are all hyperthermophiles. Moreover, it is in accordance
with the results from the GEM catalog, where these Crenarchaeota were isolated from thermal springs
and ’non-marine saline and alkaline’, where the latter is made up by the habitat ’Ferrous microbial
mat and aquatic’. The high-temperature ecology could potentially indicate that the AHL lactonases
are not used for competition across domain boundaries, since most extremophiles are found within
the Archaea and not the Bacteria [86]. However, it is still possible that the archaea interfere with the
smaller portion of bacterial thermophiles in the habitat. Nevertheless, another possibility is that the
AHL lactonases have a different ecological role.
Two alternative ecological roles are clearing and recycling of AHLs [14]. However, since these
phenomena are typically found in microorganisms that produce QS-molecules themselves [14], the
roles seem unlikely for the AHL lactonase producers found here. This is based on the fact that no AHL
synthases were found in the assemblies of the lactonase producers. A reason for this could be that
the pHMM is unrepresentative of archaeal AHL synthases because it is based on bacterial sequences.
Nonetheless, the result is in accordance with literature: Hiblot et al. found no other AHL components
with VmoLac [22] and moreover, there is only little evidence of quorum sensing even existing in the
Archaea [87].
Another potential ecological role of AHL lactonases is detoxification, where the lactonase is used to
degrade AHLs because the organism is sensitive to the molecules [14]. However, as just established,
quorum sensing is not well-described in archaea, which leads us back the initial question of whether
the AHL lactonases are used to degrade bacterial AHLs, regardless of the purpose being competition
or detoxification. Furthermore, the very presence of AHL lactonases in high-temperature environments
can also be questioned, based on the suggestion that AHL-mediated quorum sensing is unlikely to
take place in environments with high temperature, because the lactone ring is heat-labile [87][88].

possess AHL lactonase activity in previous studies [23][21][22]. Hence, the question remains of what
AHL lactonases are used for in an environment where AHLs potentially cannot exist? While this will
not be discussed in depth here, it could be suggested that the AHL lactonase activity is not the primary
catalytic function or perhaps the proteins are genomic artefacts, which are not expressed. Besides, it
should also be noted that about 30% of the putative AHL lactonases were found in Euryarchaeota and
Asgard, where another ecological function might be possible.
In addition to the taxonomic distribution and potential ecological role discussed here, it is necessary
to consider the credibility of the results. This is first of all based on the credibility of the pHMM
to make a good representation a PLL-a, so it can be used to search for other PLLs-A. The pHMM
used to search for putative AHL lactonases was constructed solely from known PLLs-A, which have
been experimentally proven to be catalytically active. While the archaeal PLLs-A all originated from
the same class of organisms (Thermoprotei), bacterial PLLs-A were also included in the pHMM.
This does not guarantee that all PLLs-A within the Archaea are discovered, but the addition of the
bacterial counterparts likely ensures broader diversity of results than if only proteins from one class
of organisms were used. While this ensured a larger diversity in the results, curation was used to
filter the results to get those most promising to have AHL lactonase activity. Based on curation, it
has been ensured the proteins have the conserved amino acids necessary to coordinate the metals in
the active site [21], which makes it more likely that they have the enzyme activity. Furthermore, the
chance that the putative AHL lactonases have lactonase activities rather than PTE activity is increased
by accounting for the length of loop 7, which is shorter in PLLs [23]. Regardless, the results are
still based on predictions from the genome, which means that it is unknown whether the predicted
AHL lactonases are expressed and whether they have the suggested ecological function. Ultimately,
laboratory experiments are needed to shed light on this.
8.3
Lytic transglycosylases: a tool for competition, symbiosis or predation?
It has previously been speculated that the putative LTs observed in archaeal genomes, could be used as
defense mechanisms against bacteria [26][25]. To explore this hypothesis, it is interesting to know the
taxonomic distribution of LTs throughout the Archaea. This is because, the distribution can be used to
evaluate the importance of LTs as a competition mechanism throughout the domain and furthermore
55
==PAGE:57==
8
Discussion
Technical University of Denmark
get a hint towards their ecological function based on the LT-containing organisms.
In this project it was found that approximately 1.5% and 1% of all archaeal assemblies from GenBank
available as protein FASTA files, harbored an LT when using relaxed and strict curation, respectively.
With both curation strictnesses, most LT-containing organism belonged to the DPANN superphylum,
while Euryarchaeota contained the second highest number (Fig. 23). The same two major LT-
containing groups were found in the GEM catalog, but here Euryarchaeota (Halobacteriota phylum
(GTDB taxonomy)) was found to harbor most, while DPANN (Nanoarchaeota and Micrarchaeota
phyla) was second. DPANN as one of the major LT-containing groups corroborates the results found
by Castelle et al. and Rinke et al., who found LTs in the phyla; Woesearchaeota, Pacearchaeota
and Nanoarchaeota, which all belong to DPANN [25][26]. Nevertheless, to my knowledge, LTs
have not previously been reported in Euryarchaeota. Interestingly, Euryarchaeota made up 10% of
LT-containing organisms in the non-curated data, while making up 20% and 30% in the relaxed and
strictly curated data, respectively. This means that a larger portion of Euryarchaeota passes through
curation compared to DPANN, which could indicate that Euryarchaeotal LTs are more similar to the
bacterial family 1 of LTs.
Based on the pHMM search performed in this project, it seems like LTs are present in the Archaea.
However, this does not answer the question of their ecological function: are they indeed used as medi-
ators of inter-domain interference competition or are they used for cell-attachment in e.g. symbiosis or
predation, or something else entirely?
To assess this question, we can start by evaluating the likeliness that the LTs are used for the same
purpose within DPANN and Euryarchaeota. A phylogenetic tree was constructed in an attempt to shed
light on this (Fig. 35), but the bootstrap values were too low for anything to be concluded. Another
indication of their potential difference is the size of the LTs within the two groups. Here, it was found
that, while the medians were similar (412 and 441 amino acids), there were a lot more outliers with
large sizes within DPANN (1000-3500 amino acids) (Fig. 24). This might be a true representation
of all LTs found within DPANN and Euryarchaeota, but it also possible that it is caused by a larger
sample size of DPANN LTs, which increases the probability of more outliers. Interestingly, the large
LTs found in DPANN are in fact comparable in size with the LT domain-containing proteins found by
Castelle et al., which were up to 3000 amino acids in size [25][24]. Another indication of whether the
LTs have different functions in the two groups, is the genome size of the LT-containing organisms.

Here, it was found that DPANN typically had small genomes (500-100 kb), while Euryarchaeotal
genomes were generally larger, and the most frequently observed size was 3000-3500 kb (Fig. 25).
This is interesting when taking into account the LT protein size, because DPANN, the group with the
smallest genomes, also has the largest LTs. This observation was also made by Castelle et al. for the
two DPANN phyla where they observed LTs; Woesearchaeota and Pacearchaeota [25].
Small genome size is a well-known feature of DPANN and for many phyla within DPANN, their
genome size range overlap that of obligate symbionts [89]. In fact, some DPANN species are known
to be episymbionts [25], meaning that they live as symbionts attached to surface of the host cell [89].
Moreover, symbiosis, or at least dependence on other organisms to fulfil metabolic requirements, has
been proposed as a general feature of DPANN species [89]. This is both based on their small genome
sizes, but also their limited metabolic capacity, where many DPANN species e.g. lack complete
pathways for amino acid production and they cannot perform de novo nucleotide synthesis [89]. If
the episymbiotic lifestyle was indeed true for the LT-containing DPANN species found in this project,
the LTs could be used for attachment to the host as also suggested by Castelle et al. [25]. Based on
this, one can speculate that it makes sense for LTs to be retained in the DPANN genomes, despite the
LTs’ large size: they are essential for the symbiotic interaction, which is needed to fulfil the species’
metabolic requirements.
An alternative hypothesis for the role of LTs in DPANN has been proposed by Vigneron et al., and it
follows along the same lines of reduced genomes and lacking metabolic capabilities. However, instead
of being used for symbiosis, the LTs are used for predation by degradation of the prey peptidoglycan
[60]. The authors support this with the fact that the observed LT-containing species in their study also
encode putative pore-forming toxins [60]. Both of these features have been observed in a predatory
bacterium, Vampirococcus lugosii, which belongs to a bacterial group (CPR) similar to DPANN [60].
From the data obtained in this project it is not possible to determine whether the putative LTs observed
in DPANN are used for competition, symbiosis, or predation. However, based on their small genome
and lack of metabolic capacities, one could argue that it seems more likely that the DPANN species
obtain nutrients via a host or prey, rather than competing with bacteria for nutrients in the environment.
The hypothesis of LTs being involved in symbiosis or predation can likely not be applied to Eu-
ryarchaeota. This is mainly based on the fact that their genomes are much larger, both for the
LT-containing Euryarchaeota observed here (Fig. 25), but also Euryarchaeotal species in general [89].

This motivates the question of whether LTs could be used as mediators of interference competition
by Euryarchaeota. Clues to the ecological role of LTs can potentially be gained from the specific
Euryarchaeotal group of origin and their environmental niche.
In the GEM catalog it was found that the LT-containing Euryarchaeota originated from bioreactors and
wastewater, more specifically the habitats ’Switchgrass degrading’ and ’Anaerobic digestor sludge’.
This sheds only limited light on the ecological significance and therefore a deeper dive is taking into the
taxonomy. Here, all the identified LT-containing Euryarchaeota in the GEM catalog, as well as some in
GenBank, belonged to the Methanosarcinales order (Halobacteriota phylum (GTDB) or Euryarchaeota
phylum (NCBI)). Pseudomurein is found as a cell-wall polymer in Euryarchaeotal methanogens
[58] and it can therefore be tempting to suggest that the archaeal LTs might act analogously to
bacterial LTs, but on pseudomurein. However, the pseudomurein-producing archaea are found
within the Methanobacteriales and Methanopyrales orders [58] and not Methanosarcinales order as
the LT-producing organisms seen here. This leads to the question of whether the LTs are used in
competition. For this to be possible, the Methanosarcinales must have overlapping resource needs
with bacteria, since this is a prerequisite for competition [28]. Members of the Methanosarcinales
order are acetoclastic methanogens [90], which means they consume acetate [91]. In some cases, the
acetoclastic methanogens must compete with syntrophic acetate-oxidizing bacteria (SAOB) for the
acetate [91] and they may also compete with sulfate-reducing bacteria for the same resource [92].
Consequently, the requirement for competition is fulfilled and hence, LTs as mediators of interference
competition remains a possibility.
Overall, the results suggest that the putative LTs are mainly found within DPANN and Euryarchaeota,
where they might have different ecological roles. However, these results are also associated with a
level of uncertainty that needs to be taken into account to establish the results’ validity. First, the
pHMM was constructed from putative archaeal LTs, but also known, functional bacterial LTs, where
the latter strengthens the model’s credibility. Furthermore, the evaluation of model prediction showed
that the pHMM found the same LTs as Vigneron et al. [60], but also an additional LT in a genome
not predicted to harbor LTs. Nonetheless, only one of the LTs discovered in the genomes predicted
to harbor LTs, also passed through strict curation. This could indicate that the pHMM in this study
together with curation is more conservative, while also discovering different LTs. Exactly the curation
ensures that known LT motifs are present in the putative LTs and with the correct distancing between

8.4
Expression of promising AHL lactonases and LTs
In the sections above, RiPPs, AHL lactonases and LTs have been explored as mediators of inter-domain
competition in archaea. However, their distribution throughout the domain relies on predictions from
genomes and hence, the result accuracy is limited by quality and accuracy of e.g. genome assemblies,
protein prediction, models and databases. Therefore, as with many bioinformatic analyses, laboratory
experiments are ultimately needed to confirm the results. In this project, 2 putative AHL lactonases
and 2 putative LTs were chosen for laboratory characterization. However, due to slow shipments of the
synthesized genes, only 3 out of the 4 proteins were included in the experiments and moreover, only
the initial experiments of heterologous protein expression were completed. The result of the concluded
experiments was purified inserts, the ends of which had been digested with restriction enzymes. For
all samples, except one, the purity was somewhat below the A260/A280 ratio of 1.8 (Table 1) that is
regarded as pure for DNA [93]. However, a low ratio can be caused by very low concentrations (>10
ng/μL), which was the case here (Table 1) and hence, the true purities might be higher.
To accomplish expression of the AHL lactonases and LTs in yeast, several experiments should have
been performed, if more time had been available. First, the yeast expression vector backbone, pSP-
GM1, had to be cut with restriction enzymes matching the insert (PacI and SacI) and subsequently
purified and ligated with the purified inserts. The plasmid should then be transformed into E. coli DH5α
and colony PCR should be performed to verify the presence of the insert in the plasmid backbone.
Transformed E. coli should then be inoculated into liquid medium, from which the plasmid including
the insert should be purified. Lastly, the plasmid had to be transformed into S. cerevisiae CEN.PK
113-5D, which is auxotrophic for uracil. Transformed yeast colonies should be selected on media
without uracil, where the URA3 marker on the plasmid restores uracil production and allows for
growth. Finally, the yeast had to be cultivated and the proteins would be expressed.
During construct design, a yeast secretion signal (α-factor) was fused to the expressed protein. The
expectation is therefore that the yeast would secrete the protein to the surroundings, which enables an

was taken by Hiblot et al., who expressed archaeal PLLs-A in E. coli [21]. While these authors had
success with their approach in E. coli, S. cerevisiae was chosen as expression host in this project. The
primary reason for this was to avoid any toxic effects that overexpression of LTs might inflict on a
bacterial host.
If the experiments had been completed and the protein expression was successful, there are several
experiments which could have been made to characterize and investigate the enzyme function of
the putative AHL lactonases and LTs. In this project, the experiments would first of all be focused
on providing clues as to whether the AHL lactonases and LTs are used as mediators of interference
competition. For the AHL lactonases, the quorum quenching potential could be investigated by
observing the degradation of AHLs using e.g. a biosensor or a pH-based assay. The effect of LTs
could potentially be investigated by adding the putative LTs to a bacterial culture and observe if there
are any changes in survival rate.
Even though time did not allow the above-mentioned experiments to be carried out, it can still be
speculated whether the chosen AHL lactonases and LTs had the predicted enzyme function. Regarding
the AHL lactonase, it was found that the putative AHL lactonase produced by S. shibatae clustered
together with the known archaeal PLLs-A and was closely related to SisLac. This, in addition to the
curation, provides a strong indication that the protein does indeed have lactonase activity. Contrary to
this, the putative AHL lactonase from the Poseidoniales archaeon clustered with PTE rather than the
lactonases. This might indicate that it has PTE activity instead of lactonase activity, but it could also
be an indication of sequence divergence in the non-conserved part of the protein. This could be related
to the fact that Poseidoniales is distantly related to the Thermoprotei class to which the other archaeal
producers belong.
Both putative LTs clusters together with the known bacterial LTs from family 1. Additionally, neither
is predicted to be membrane-bound, which increases the likeliness that the proteins will be secreted.

Conclusion
The aim of this project was to investigate the likeliness of three types of proteins to act as mediators of
archaeal inter-domain interference competition, as well as their prevalence throughout the archaeal
domain.
First, it was found that RiPPs made up a significant portion of the archaeal BGC content and the most
commonly observed RiPP types were RRE-containing and RiPP-like BGCs. These BGCs encode
potentially novel archaeal RiPPs, and it seems likely that they could mediate competition with bacteria
and fungi, despite other roles being possible. Another potential mediator was phosphotriesterase-
like AHL lactonases, which were found to be most prevalent in the TACK superphylum, which
also contains the known archaeal PLL-A producers. Since the known and putative producers were
hyperthermophiles, the role of lactonases in inter-domain competition was questioned. This was based
on the fact that high-temperature environments harbor mostly archaea and moreover, AHLs might be
too heat-labile to even exist in these environments. Ultimately, the AHL lactonases’ ecological role
remains unclear in the TACK superphylum, but the lactonases might still be important for competition
in Euryarchaeota and Asgard, where they were also discovered. Lastly, lytic transglycosylases were
found mostly in DPANN and Euryarchaeota, where the enzyme seemed likely to fulfill two different
roles. Because of small genomes and reduced metabolic capabilities in DPANN, it seems more probable
that LTs within this group are used for cell-attachment during symbiosis or predation. In contrast, the
LT-containing organisms from Euryarchaeota had larger genomes and have previously been found
to utilize the same resource as bacteria, thereby making inter-domain interference competition a
possibility.
The results are associated with a level of uncertainty because they are based on genome predictions,
and it is therefore not certain that the proteins are expressed or have the predicted catalytic activity or
ecological role. To investigate this, heterologous expression of AHL lactonases and LTs was started,
but not completed.
Ultimately, the significance of this project was to deepen the understanding of microbial interactions
already known from bacteria and fungi, and moreover to explore a potential new source of bioactive
compounds. While a definite mediator of inter-domain interference competition was not found, it was
determined that all three potential mediators were plausible. Based on this study, further research can
focus on promising AHL lactonase and LT candidates as well as certain archaeal groups.
