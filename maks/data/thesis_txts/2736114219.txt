Abstract 
 
 
– i – 
Abstract 
The primary aim of this thesis is to enhance the control mechanisms of ventilation systems 
through the integration of a digital twin (DT) and continuous commissioning (CC). By lever-
aging DT technology, this research unveils significant opportunities for energy savings and 
offers deeper insights into the overall performance of the ventilation system.  
A pivotal achievement was the development of a DT that was able to showcase energy savings. 
Through manual optimizations and enhanced trim and respond control strategies, reductions in 
system pressure by approximately 15% and 30%, respectively, were accomplished. Moreover, 
the study identified inefficiencies in the operational schedule, achieving an additional 45.3% 
reduction in the air handling unit’s (AHU) total operating hours. That’s the equivalent of 64,062 
kWh/year, through simple adjustments. Implementing the most efficient changes in just one of 
the 17 ventilation systems could potentially yield an annual reduction exceeding 67,071.5 
kWh/year.  
The insights garnered from this research are not only replicable across similar system configu-
rations but also serve as the foundation for a robust framework for future use and scalability.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
The built environment is responsible for 42% of annual CO2 emissions, which equals 15.3 GT 
of CO2e. 27% comes from building operations, including heating, and cooling of buildings, 
whereas the remaining 15% stems from the embodied carbon embedded in the infrastructure 
and material use of just four materials: aluminum, iron, cement and steel (IEA, 2020). 
Climate change knows no borders and is most likely the greatest threat in modern times. En-
hancing energy efficiency and leveraging technological innovation and knowledge can poten-
tially serve as pivotal tools in fostering a resilient and sustainable future.  
With the built environment as one of the foremost contributors to carbon emissions and energy 
consumption, Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning (HVAC) systems emerge as the pri-
mary culprits within this category. This underscores the paramount importance of addressing 
the topic of HVAC systems (Chen et al., 2023).  
Originating in the Roman Empire (McKoy, Raymond, & Yaa, 2023), HVAC systems have 
evolved into a vital element of industrial progress, and it is a central heating and cooling source 
for most buildings, particularly large commercial buildings. Today, they present one of the most 
significant opportunities to address the world's most pressing environmental challenge: Climate 
Change.

Commitment to Net Zero requires corporations to reach the state of Net Zero by 2050 or sooner. 
The term refers to a state in which the greenhouse gases going into the atmosphere are balanced 
by the removal of greenhouse gases from the atmosphere (University of Oxford, 2024). The 
scientific consensus is that the environment is at a tipping point in terms of failing to make the 
green transition in time and therefore also avoiding the worst possible ramifications brought 
about by climate change. In order to comply with the requirements of avoiding that scenario, 
stakeholders must align with the 1.5°C Paris Agreement pathway, ensuring that the average 
global temperature will rise by a maximum of 1.5°C by 2100 (IEA, 2023). That transition is 
measured through carbon emission reductions, often concerning energy consumption, with 
HVACs being one of the focal points. 
Heating and cooling systems were developed to improve comfort and health. In the 19th century, 
the Industrial Revolution led to a growing demand for heating and cooling systems in factories 
and large buildings, subsequently leading to the development of early versions of the HVAC 
systems in their current format (McKoy, Raymond, & Yaa, 2023).  
The invention of the first modern air conditioner by Willis Carrier in 1902 revolutionized the 
HVAC industry, making it possible to cool large buildings. Carrier’s invention, while originally 
developed with the purpose of removing moisture from the air, turned out to have the ability to 
cool the air as well. In the 20th century, the refinement of the technology and the use of elec-
tricity led to significant advancements in HVAC systems. Research findings indicate that 
HVAC systems, while valuable for maintaining optimal thermal comfort and a favorable indoor 
environment, also account for a substantial share of the energy consumption within the broader 
context of a commercial building's energy consumption profile (Gonzalez-Torres, Perez, & 
Coronel, 2022). Given their significance in overall energy consumption, it is worthwhile paying 
attention to their impact as sustainability becomes an increasing priority for businesses and 
consumers alike.

1.2 Problem Statement 
Traditional control strategies of HVACs often result in suboptimal energy consumption and 
operational inefficiencies. These approaches, based on static setpoints and manual adjustments, 
fail to adapt to dynamic conditions influenced by building occupancy and external factors. Con-
sequently, there is a critical need to enhance HVAC operations through advanced methodolo-
gies that leverage advanced methods such as DTs and CC. By integrating CC and DT tech-
niques, the aim is to achieve energy savings, analyse system performance, and ensure optimal 
occupant comfort. This study seeks to explore these innovative approaches to HVAC controls 
and their potential impact on energy efficiency and operational costs. 
1.2.1 Objectives  
In order to operationalize the problem statement, five objectives for the research have been 
developed. These objectives will be explored systematically throughout the report. The objec-
tives are the following: 
1. Initial system analysis: Conduct an initial system analysis of a ventilation system 
in order to identify hotspots and low-hanging fruits. 
2. Development of a digital twin: Create a DT of the ventilation system of a com-
mercial office building. 
3. Validation of the model: Use real-world operational data from a Building Man-
agement System (BMS) to validate the DT, ensuring that it is reliable and repli-
cates the real-world performance. 
4. Identification of optimization opportunities: Analyse the validated model to 
identify opportunities for energy savings through adjustments in operational set-
points, pressure settings, and controls. 
5. Articulation of practical solutions: Propose practical and implementable solu-
tions for energy optimization tailored to the ventilation system of a large commer-
cial office building.

2 Literature Review 
In the quest for sustainable building practices, HVAC systems represent both a challenge and 
an opportunity for energy conservation and efficiency. The escalating demand for indoor envi-
ronmental quality in commercial buildings coupled with the urgent need to curtail energy con-
sumption underscores the importance of innovative approaches to HVAC design and operation. 
This literature review synthesizes the current body of knowledge surrounding HVAC energy 
optimization, with a particular focus on the utilization of DT models within large office spaces. 
By examining existing research, this review aims to highlight key insights and identify gaps 
that the current project will address. 
2.1 HVAC Systems in Commercial Buildings 
Unlike water-based DH systems, air-based HVAC systems are less mature in terms of innova-
tive and efficient optimization solutions and tools. Air-based HVACs, or ventilation systems, 
are a complex asset with several components that serve their functionality. In general, ventila-
tion systems are designed to provide conditioned air to inhabited zones to maintain a predeter-
mined level of thermal comfort and air quality (Sugarman, 2020). The building industry tends 
to dimension and develop ventilation systems wrong due to either over- or under-sizing the 
system, which can impact the efficiency, air quality, and comfort in the conditioned zones. As 
a system, ventilation systems have a variety of key components to consider when optimizing 
the system. For mechanical ventilation systems, the following components are essential to con-
sider as they can be the key indicators of a system’s performance (Sugarman, 2020). Each serves 
a specific purpose and contributes to the overall pressure loss. 
• Fans or ventilators are the main components of an Air Handling Unit (AHU)to supply 
air to the conditioned zone. They facilitate the circulation in the system from the AHU 
through distribution systems to the desired zone. 
• Filters remove particulates like dust, pollen, and allergens from the air, protect HVAC 
equipment from damage, prevent duct contamination, and support indoor air quality for 
occupant health and comfort. Dirty filters decrease the overall performance of an AHU. 
• Heat exchangers transfer heat between fluids. They ensure that a certain temperature is 
transferred to the supply air from the exchanger.  
• Dampers regulate airflow in ductwork, distribute air efficiently, and aid in zoning. They 
also help in balancing pressure and can be used for safety purposes, like in fire and 
smoke control.  
• Ducts ensure distribution of air throughout a building. They are critical for efficient 
airflow management and maintaining the desired distribution of air. In addition to ducts, 
fittings and bends play a critical role in the distribution of this air to the desired zones 
as well. 
• Silencers, also known as mufflers, reduce noise from an HVAC system.  
To control HVAC systems, four different key components are important to understand. These 
control systems work together to improve the system’s performance. 
• Building Management Systems (BMS) are the central nerve systems of modern build-
ings, integrating and controlling various building services, including HVAC, lighting, 
==PAGE:15==

and security. Depending on the system, BMS monitors several factors, including energy 
usage, occupancy, pressure, and outside weather, and ensures the efficient operation of 
building systems. BMS collects data, executes control actions, and provides a user in-
terface for building operators to track and adjust settings (Joseph, 2018). 
• Transducers play a critical role in HVAC systems by converting physical conditions 
such as temperature and pressure into electrical signals. These devices are crucial to 
providing accurate, real-time data that is essential for monitoring environmental condi-
tions and system performance. They act as sensory inputs that feed important infor-
mation to the control systems for decision-making and adjustments. 
• Proportional (P), Proportional-Integral (PI), and Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) 
controllers are the cornerstone of HVAC control systems. They interpret signals from 
transducers and make calculated adjustments to the HVAC operations, balancing the 
performance with a link to occupant comfort. These controllers determine the action 
taken in response to deviations from setpoints, ensuring system stability and efficiency 
(Kasahara & Tadahiko, 1999). 
• Motorized damper controls are integral to managing airflow within HVAC systems. 
They adjust the position of dampers in the ductwork based on commands from the con-
trol system. These adjustments regulate the distribution and volume of airflow, directly 
impacting air quality and temperature consistency throughout the building (Klima 
Oprema, 2022). 
The synergy between BMS, transducers, controllers, and motorized dampers creates a cohesive 
and efficient HVAC system. BMS acts as the command center, processing data from transduc-
ers to understand the current state of the building environment. Controllers like PID utilize this 
data to make informed decisions, executing precise control over HVAC components such as 
motorized dampers. This integrated approach ensures optimal performance, energy efficiency, 
and occupant comfort and demonstrates the interconnected nature of intelligent building man-
agement systems. 
2.2 Continuous Commissioning  
The concept of the ‘building performance gap’ is central to understanding the challenges in 
achieving energy efficiency in commercial buildings. This gap, as Jradi and Liu note, is the 
discrepancy between predicted and actual energy consumption (Jradi & Liu, 2022). It is a wide-
spread issue that affects both new constructions and retrofitted buildings, often resulting in sig-
nificantly higher energy use than initially anticipated. The reasons behind this gap are multi-
faceted, ranging from design and simulation inaccuracies to construction variances and opera-
tional inefficiencies (Xu, Patrick, Sanjayan, & Wang, 2018). Understanding and addressing 
these causes are critical for energy optimization and sustainability in building operations. 
Continuous commissioning emerges as a potent solution to the performance gap problem. CC 
is a quality-oriented process for achieving, verifying, and documenting whether the perfor-
mance of a building’s systems and assemblies meets defined objectives and criteria (Jradi & 
Liu, 2022). Unlike traditional commissioning, which is usually a one-time process conducted 
during the building handover, CC extends throughout the operational life of the building. This 
ongoing process ensures that the building’s performance continually meets the designed intent, 
adapting to changes in usage, occupancy, and other dynamic factors. Ideally, CC functions as

system malfunctions. This approach is particularly beneficial for large buildings where opera-

2.3 Fault Detection & Diagnosis 
The progression of fault detection and diagnostics (FDD) in HVAC systems, supported by data-
driven methodologies, forms a crucial precursor to the integration of more sophisticated tech-
nologies like DTs. This review encapsulates the key aspects of data-driven FDD, highlighting 
its significance as a foundational element for these advanced modelling approaches 
FDD involves continuously monitoring the system's performance using sensors and data col-
lection tools. Ultimately, the system looks for any deviations from normal operating parameters 
that might indicate a problem. Once a fault is detected, FDD tries to pinpoint the root cause of 
the issue. This may involve analyzing the collected data, using diagnostic algorithms, or fol-
lowing established procedures based on the type of system (LBNL, 2020). By identifying the 
faults and anomalies in the system, it enables quicker repairs, improved system performance 
and prevention of future malfunctions if the FDD tool’s intelligence is based on predictive al-
gorithms (Chen et al., 2023). Transitioning to DTs, it is possible to utilize the advancement of 
data-driven FDD, particularly in ventilation systems. DTs, representing virtual replicas of phys-
ical systems, rely heavily on accurate, real-time data for simulation and analysis. Data-driven 
FDD, with its emphasis on precise data analysis and fault diagnosis, provides a robust frame-
work for feeding accurate, actionable data into DT models (Chen et al., 2023). 
Data-driven FDD not only plays a vital role in enhancing the efficiency and reliability of HVAC 
systems but also lays the groundwork for the successful integration of advanced modelling 
techniques like DTs. FDD tools can be used similarly to commissioning tools (LBNL, 2020),

especially, when talking about ventilation and pressurization within ventilation systems. Too 
high pressurization within a system causes malfunctions, and FDD is a concept to neutralize 
these risks. 
2.4 Digital Twins  
DTs, representing the virtual counterparts of physical systems, might have integral shares in 
modern building management, particularly in ventilation systems. 
The concept of DTs, representing a synergy of multiple digital technologies, has revolutionized 
the process of achieving thermal comfort and in the meantime maximizing energy efficiency in 
buildings (Arowoiya, Moehler, & Fang, 2023). This advanced digital replication not only en-
hances the understanding of building dynamics but also fosters significant energy optimiza-
tions, especially for large commercial buildings where AHU is considered an energy drainer. 
The technology essentially virtualizes replicas of physical entities and facilitates bi-directional 
information flow between the physical and digital realms (Arowoiya, Moehler, & Fang, 2023). 
This technology can incorporate BIM and IoT for example, allowing for real-time monitoring 
of existing buildings.  
It is known by now that energy efficiency in buildings, particularly in ventilation systems, is a 
critical aspect of modern building management. DTs, through their implementation of data 
analysis and real-world data monitoring capabilities, enable precise control over energy use and 
provide the opportunity to learn, train and experimentalize with the respective system. These 
attributes play a central share in large commercial buildings where a ventilation system needs 
to be adaptable and flexible for changing environments. The DT provides the opportunity from 
a theoretical perspective to train, visualize, and conceptualize an idea before exporting the con-
cept to a real setting. Compared to a sandbox environment, it can be equipped with the tools to 
explore every possible idea freely. Here, experimentation comes at no cost, and through training 
and learning, one can derive the most crucial insights from a theoretical perception. Replicating 
the theoretical implications from the sandbox, it offers significant energy reductions for build-
ings, as well as FDD and enhanced occupant comfort – a key aspect in the productivity and 
well-being of occupants (Arowoiya et al., 2023). 
As DT evolve, they have become a stepping stone to more sophisticated advanced models. The 
integration of DT technology in commercial buildings marks a significant step toward intelli-
gent energy/facility management and can assist managers as a CC-tool.

2.5 Trim and Respond Logic 
Trim and Respond Logic (TRL) is an advanced control strategy employed to dynamically adjust 
setpoints such as pressure or temperature, based on inputs from multiple sources. Guideline 36 
(ASHRAE, 2021) advocates for the use of TRL in resetting duct static pressure and supply-air 
temperature setpoints in multi-zone VAV systems. 
In essence, a well-calibrated TRL control loop prioritizes a balance between energy efficiency 
and occupant comfort. Initially, the control system "trims" or adjusts the setpoint to conserve 
energy while maintaining a certain level of comfort. However, if the downstream equipment 
detects a deviation in comfort levels, it generates a "request" prompting the system to "respond" 
by readjusting the setpoint. This iterative process continues to ensure optimal comfort and en-
ergy efficiency (ASHRAE, 2021). 
Figure 5 illustrates a multi-zone VAV system the damper within the VAV box adjusts airflow 
to individual zones based on heating or cooling requirements. Consequently, this system affects 
the pressure within the supply ductwork. To optimize fan energy consumption, the duct static 
pressure setpoint is dynamically recalibrated in response to the VAV damper's position. A

2.6 Output of Research 
In the industry, there's a prevailing notion that HVAC systems lack robust energy efficiency 
tools compared to water-based DH systems. Denmark's leading role in DH technology, with 
companies like Grundfoss and Danfoss setting international standards, provides a potential rea-
son why HVAC systems have historically received less attention. Additionally, air-based sys-
tems are often perceived as more complicated. Unlike DH systems, which operate purely on 
consumption, air-based systems are influenced by occupancy patterns and indoor air quality. 
Furthermore, the regulatory framework for DH systems is generally considered more stringent 
than that of air-based systems. This stricter regulation necessitates greater investment in ad-
vanced tools and expertise. In contrast, air-based systems, requiring less specialized expertise, 
provide HVAC companies with easier market access compared to their water-based counter-
parts.  
In reality, HVACs constitute the most critical asset in building operations, especially in large 
buildings (Gonzalez-Torres, Perez, & Coronel, 2022). Hence, the need for efficient and great 
industry tools for air-based ventilation systems is high.  
Considering this, the literature review in this thesis has systematically explored various aspects 
of HVAC systems in commercial buildings, with a focus on CC, FDD, TRL and DT. Each of 
these elements has the potential to play a vital role in enhancing the energy efficiency and op-
erational effectiveness of HVAC systems. At the time of writing, there is a lack of efficient 
tools for the optimization of HVAC. However, this thesis aims to contribute to this matter by 
focusing on the key concepts explored in the literature review with the thesis objectives and 
hypotheses in mind, as explained in section 1.2 and elaborated upon in section 3.

5.1 Upstream Volumetric Flows and Pressure 
The building's intricate system initially posed a considerable challenge for examination, leading 
to several misconceptions regarding its operation. However, through closer analysis of the sys-
tem, its data, and its components, important insights into its functionality emerged. 
Initially, it was believed that the dampers in the shaft and various zones on floor 5 were regis-
tering damper volume flows. However, further inspection revealed that the zone dampers were 
damper positions designed and programmed to adjust the damper position based on inputs from 
a measuring cross. 
Figure 11 illustrates volumetric flows and pressurization at shaft dampers and AHU. A visible 
correlation exists between the system's operational schedule and the recorded flow rates. Nota-
bly, shaft dampers across various floors exhibit an elevated volumetric flow demand, peaking 
around 3:00 p.m. daily. Data from a sensor in the AHU's technical room depicts a consistent 
trendline, oscillating between 275-285, barring anomalies observed on January 31st - indicative 
of potential sensor malfunctions or misconfigured logging mechanisms within the BMS. All 
datapoints are logged at approximately 12-minute intervals. 
Placing a pressure sensor directly after the AHU can compromise data accuracy due to several 
factors. Initially, the airflow post-AHU may remain turbulent and irregular, yielding imprecise 
pressure readings. Airflow stabilization requires a certain duct length to achieve uniformity. 
Moreover, the duct system experiences dynamic alterations and variations in velocity, pressure, 
and temperature as air traverses it. Close proximity to the AHU may hinder the sensor's ability 
to capture these fluctuations accurately. 
Positioning the sensor downstream 2/3 into the ductwork enables it to gauge the average pres-
sure conditions throughout the system, offering a holistic view of its performance. In contrast, 
a sensor near the AHU might only reflect localized conditions, vulnerable to operational fluc-
tuations like start-up surges or transient effects, potentially sloping pressure readings.

6.1 Software 
Dymola, powered by the Modelica language, stands as a leading platform for multi-domain 
system modelling and simulation. Modelica, an object-oriented, equation-based language, pro-
vides a standardized framework for describing complex physical systems across various do-
mains, including the mechanical, electrical, thermal, and hydraulic domains. Considering this, 
Dymola offers a great opportunity for the creation of DTs and for conducting CC on the existing 
systems.  
Dymola provides high-fidelity simulation, making it ideal for developing DT that accurately 
replicates the behavior of physical systems. By leveraging Modelica's equation-based approach, 
Dymola can simulate intricate system dynamics with precision, enabling virtual replicas that 
closely resemble their real-world counterparts. 
Moreover, Dymola enables integration with real-world data that facilitates the integration of 
real-world data streams into DT models, facilitating continuous monitoring and analysis of sys-
tem performance. This integration allows DTs to dynamically adjust their behavior based on 
incoming data, enhancing their effectiveness in real-world applications. 
Another significant attribute of the software is its support for multi-domain modeling, which 
enables the creation of DTs that capture the interactions between different system components. 
This interactive approach is essential for modelling complex systems like HVAC systems, 
where interactions between mechanical, thermal, and control components play a crucial role in 
overall performance.

6.2.3.2 Tuning PID Controllers 
Tuning Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controllers are pivotal to obtaining an efficient 
and well-functioning system. A methodical approach was adopted to tune the PID parameters, 
considering the specific dynamics and requirements of the system. The ensuing steps outline 
their tuning process with the closed-loop logic. 
The closed-loop test involves subjecting the system to a step change in the setpoint or a disturb-
ance while monitoring the controller's output. The objective is to observe the system's response 
and adjust the PID parameters to minimize error and achieve desired performance metrics, such 
as settling time, overshoot, and steady-state error. A simple, yet systematic approach to the 
tuning process was conducted with every PID controller and can be explained in five steps: 
1. Initial Setup: Configure the system with the PID controller and establish baseline oper-
ating conditions. 
2. Step Change: Introduce a step change in the setpoint or a disturbance to the system. 
3. Data Collection: Record the system's response, including process variable (PV) and 
controller output (CO), over a specified time period. 
4. Analysis: Analyze the collected data to evaluate the controller's performance. Key met-
rics to consider include rise time, settling time, overshoot, and steady-state error. 
5. Parameter Adjustment: Adjust the PID parameters based on the analysis to improve the 
controller's performance. This may involve iterative adjustments until desired perfor-
mance metrics are achieved. 
While PID controllers offer enhanced flexibility and control capabilities, a PI controller is often 
sufficient for many applications due to its simplicity, stability, and reduced risk of overcom-
pensation. For this model, PI controllers were used as they suggest more stable outputs to the 
controller. Nevertheless, as the real-world system is controlled by a PID controller, it is almost 
impossible to manage and predict the outputs without knowing the PID controllers’ nature and 
configurations. The most serious consequence of a misconfigured PID controller is arguably 
the risk of unstable behaviour. Unstable controllers can produce inconsistent and unpredictable 
outputs, making it challenging to interpret or trust the controller's actions – something one might 
suspect applies to the AHU, VE02 at Nordea’s office. 
6.2.4 Model Calibration & Validation Process 
Objective 3 (model validation) is critical in DTs owing to several reasons. Firstly, it ensures 
accuracy by verifying that the models accurately represent real-world systems, thus preventing 
errors in adjustments. Secondly, validation lends reliability to the model, bolstering confidence 
in the model's predictive capabilities and in the changes made, which is essential in decision-
making processes. Thirdly, it helps in error detection and correction as validation identifies 
inaccuracies, thereby allowing for improvements. Lastly, validation assesses the generalization 
of models, ensuring accurate representation of system behaviour across different conditions, 
which allows the user to begin experimenting with the model.

7.1 Energy Savings from the Low-Hanging Fruits 
By conducting a simple commissioning of Nordea's ventilation system, significant energy sav-
ings and emission reductions can be achieved. Adjusting the operation schedule to turn off the 
VE02 during weekends, similar to nightly setbacks, can lead to yearly savings of 64,062 kWh, 
a reduction of 14,734 kg CO2e in Scope 1 & 2 emissions, and savings of DKK 179,373 on

2023Q1 to 2024Q1 (Statistics Denmark, 2024), average fan power of 21.24 kW for supply and 
exhaust twin fans and considering 52 weekends per year. 
Typically, the AHU transitions into night setback mode at 7 pm. However, on weekends, it 
operates continuously from Friday 7 pm to Monday 5 am, totalling 58 hours without significant 
occupancy. This operation mode results in the system running pointlessly for 45.3% of its total 
operating time, which spans 128 hours per week.

7.2 Dymola System Optimizations 
System optimizations can be achieved through various means, including maximizing the effi-
ciency of AHUs by optimizing design, adjusting setpoints and control settings, and implement-
ing energy-saving measures. The second half of this section will focus on exploring energy-
saving opportunities using DT supported by Dymola models. In the subsequent sections, a sen-
sitivity analysis by varying two parameters in Dymola is conducted and, based on the findings, 
recommendations for system improvements are proposed. 
7.2.1 Sensitivity Analysis 
Using a sensitivity analysis, the resulting outcomes may enable a better understanding of input 
parameters and their impact on system behaviour, allowing for the identification of key influ-
ences and quantification of their effects. Furthermore, the analysis would concretize the thresh-
old on which the system can’t be optimized further given the specific set of parameters.  
The sensitivity analyses were conducted against pressure setpoints as they were the key drivers 
of this system. The first analysis aimed to get more clarity on the pressure upstream to the shaft 
damper, whereas the other analysis was based on the downstream pressurization of the shaft 
damper using sweeping parameters. 
7.2.1.1 Upstream Pressurization 
For the upstream pressurization test, the AHU pressure setpoint is adjusted to sweep from 
101,650 Pa to 101,560 Pa with 6 intervals in between. The AHU is initially controlled by a 280-
pressure setpoint which is the equivalent of 101,605.   
Figure 43 highlights the great potential of the upstream pressure setpoint for the office areas. 
Generally, it is the two zones linked to the “west flank” that fluctuate and react more sensitively 
to pressure changes. Without losing too much mass flow, the zones seem to have a breaking 
point somewhere in between 101,596 to 101,560 Pa. The critical zone of the four office areas 
is zone 5H13a. Here mass flow is lost over the full trendline when the setpoint reaches 101,560 
Pa, a 45 Pa reduction from the initial setpoint.

Discussion 
 
 
– 64 – 
8 Discussion 
In essence, the main objectives and hypotheses were to demonstrate energy saving and control 
optimizations by the use of DT. The results insinuate that the potential is there, as it has been 
possible to showcase significant energy savings and pinpoint control optimizations by: 
• Changing the operational schedule with standby mode during weekends. 
• Manually reducing the pressure setpoint of 280 Pa to 240 Pa upstream. 
• Implementing a dynamic TRL control setting to reduce downstream pressure setpoint 
by approximately 30% and ensure thermal comfort during the cooling season. 
These actions may potentially deliver a reduction of 67,071.5 kWh/yr on just VE02. 
While these findings are promising, they come with certain limitations, primarily stemming 
from the assumptions made in the model. To enhance the accuracy and applicability of the DT, 
optimization of the TRL, expansion of the DT model and further investigation into specific 
floor zone dampers and variables are required. By incorporating this additional data, energy 
managers could utilize the DT as a commissioning tool to predict and prevent system failures 
with more accuracy and increase overall performance. 
8.1 Granularity on Floor Five 
Currently, the DT faces limitations in user case transparency. While significant insight into 
floor five was obtained, crucial information remains unavailable due to contractual disputes. To 
bolster the model's reliability, a deeper examination of individual components is essential. For 
instance, the control mechanisms of zone dampers in relation to temperature and CO2 sensors 
are of significant importance. Additionally, incorporating supplementary validation points in 
the form of temperature and CO2 measurements would enable more precise modelling of the 
dampers and facilitate more accurate optimization recommendations for their controls. 
8.2 Expansion of the DT 
Expanding the scope of the project to include floors two, three, four, and six would represent a 
significant advancement and validate the proof of concept established in this study. Incorporat-
ing these additional floors into the model would lead to a more comprehensive representation 
of the building's ventilation system, approaching a true 1-to-1 reflection of reality. By doing so, 
several key benefits and insights could be realized: 
Improved realism: As mentioned, incorporating more floors enhances the model's accuracy, 
reflecting the building's ventilation dynamics more faithfully. This heightened realism improves 
system behavior simulation and analysis by considering interactions between zones and com-
ponents. 
Enhanced detail: A broader model scope captures diverse operational conditions, enabling de-
tailed examinations of system performance. This improved level of granularity reveals potential 
inefficiencies or optimization opportunities that may be overlooked in a narrower view. 
Identification of unknowns: Including additional floors uncovers unknown or assumed ventila-
tion system details. Comparing simulation outcomes with real-world data identifies discrepan-
cies and enhances understanding of system behavior. Gaining deeper insights into other floors 
==PAGE:75==
 
Discussion 
 
 
– 65 – 
would enable the model to consider upstream pressurization more comprehensively. While the 
project currently emphasizes the significance of the fifth floor due to its size, incorporating 
other floors would offer a more accurate assessment of potential reductions in upstream pres-
surization. Ultimately, the most substantial energy savings are often achieved by reducing up-
stream pressures at the AHU, rather than focusing solely on downstream adjustments. 
Validation of Assumptions: More floors may validate the initial modeling assumptions or reveal 
new data, improving model accuracy. Comparing simulated and observed data refines assump-
tions, boosting the model's reliability and predictability. 
Expanding the model not only enhances the overall capability of the CC-tool but also facilitates 
the broader implementation of the dynamic TRL control mechanism. Ideally, each floor should 
be modelled with components and settings tailored to a TRL mechanism. These individual 
floor-level TRL systems would then communicate with a centralized TRL mechanism linked 
to the AHU, thereby achieving tangible energy savings across the building. 
8.3 Trim and Respond Logic 
Besides the expansion of the DT by modelling all floors, the significance of fine-tuning the 
TRL mechanism is important too. Finding the right pressure setpoints is important to ensure the 
right performance and reduce energy consumption. The DT and TRL implementation have doc-
umented potential energy savings for Nordea during heating seasons. However, the model can’t 
demonstrate the potential impact during the cooling season before the BMS starts to register 
data during those months. Finding seasonal pressure setpoints would be a simple and efficient 
next step. Once the model is improved to ingest data aligned with the cooling season require-
ments, the model can act as a CC-tool to support the process. It is evident that the building 
struggles to meet thermal comfort during cooling season and it is partially attributable to the 
high exposure of sunlight. 
Refining the TRL mechanism to mitigate the oscillations observed in the results section would 
not only decrease mechanical wear but also enhance overall system performance. Further re-
finement could entail developing a more sophisticated automated trimming algorithm or reas-
sessing the controls and design of specific open-ended office area zones that appear to contrib-
ute to the issue.

Conclusion

9 Conclusion 
In the face of escalating climate concerns and the urgency to mitigate carbon emissions, the 
research contributes valuable insights to the realm of sustainable building operations. By 
demonstrating the efficiency of simple system analysis and more advanced DTs as a CC-tool 
in driving measurable energy savings, this study highlights the importance of these tools for the 
HVAC industry. In light of the objectives outlined at the onset of this research, it becomes 
evident that plenty can be done. 
From conducting an initial analysis, developing a DT, and validating its accuracy, to exploring 
optimization opportunities through adjustments in setpoints, pressure, and control optimiza-
tions, Objectives 1-4 established the foundation for the broader hypotheses.  
Through DT simulations and CC, pressure reduction optimization strategies quantified potential 
energy savings and assessed their impact on system performance. As a result, a significant en-
ergy saving of one AHU was identified. Nordea can reduce their energy bills by 67,071.5 
kWh/year, the equivalent of 15,427 kgCO2e, and the potential is increased significantly if the 
framework is replicated in all of the ventilation systems on the site. With this in mind, Hypoth-
eses 1 and 2 can be addressed.  
Hypothesis 1 is supported by the DT combined with real-world ventilation system data, show-
ing potential pressure-setting reductions in the AHU from 280 Pa to 240 Pa without compro-
mising thermal comfort. However, this figure is limited by the fact that the model fails to take 
the entire system into account. 
Hypothesis 2, also validated by energy savings and enhanced thermal comfort, reveals even 
greater potential. Utilizing TRL with the DT model, a 29.5% reduction in the downstream pres-
sure setpoint over an arbitrary working day was observed. This reduction could be extended to 
other floors and the AHU, assuming the fifth floor is the critical branch. Yet, this outcome is 
constrained by the same underlying assumption as highlighted in the previous paragraph. 
The study offers practical solutions (Objective 5) for HVAC systems in large commercial build-
ings, grounded in robust analyses and simulations, thereby supporting global sustainability 
goals like Net Zero. As stated, HVACs account for more than 12% of the global energy con-
sumption and the market for CC-tools is under-explored. DT serves as a promising framework 
for FDD- and CC-tools - tools that companies urgently need, and which support the journey 
towards reducing energy consumption. 
As the world navigates the intricate challenges posed by climate change, the pressing question 
persists: How can we collaboratively leverage innovation and technology to transform HVAC 
operations and pave the way for a greener, more sustainable future? The industry is in dire need 
of actionable solutions and insightful guidance. It is our collective responsibility to drive 
change, embracing new methodologies and advancements to safeguard our planet for future 
generations. It is the hope of the author of this thesis that this paper serves as a stepping stone 
in the right direction, inspiring other work and catalysing transformative actions toward a more 
sustainable future.

Future Work 
 
 
– 67 – 
10 Future Work 
In future work, several avenues can be explored to enhance the model's accuracy and broaden 
its applicability. These potential directions aim to address current limitations and further opti-
mize HVAC system performance: 
• Expansion of Model: Further expansion of the model would leverage more understand-
ing and better estimates of energy savings and system improvements. Developments 
could be made on the Dymola DT by incorporating: 
1. Modelling of floor two, three, four and six.  
2. Expansion of the model with temperature and CO2 measurements based on new 
insight into the zone dampers. 
3. TRL extended to more zone dampers, shaft dampers and AHU. 
• Algorithm Refinement: In addition to the third expansion suggestion, further research 
and development efforts should focus on refining the algorithms used for TRL to ensure 
optimal performance under varying conditions. An example of how to adapt the algo-
rithm could be by incorporating gradually adjusting requests depending on the setpoints 
and building the setup as follows: 
1. Determine number of requests sent by: 
1. If damper is < 90% open: Send 0 requests. 
2. If damper is > 90% open: 
1. Send 1 request until the damper position is < 80% open. 
2. If open for over 2 minutes and airflow is < 70% of setpoint: Send 
2 requests. 
3. If open for over 2 minutes and airflow is < 50% of setpoint: Send 
3 requests.  
2. Determine the optimal number of requests possible and the amount ignored. No 
standardized guidance currently exists on how many that should be.  
3. Incorporate importance-multiplier to serve more critical zones. 
• Field Testing and Validation: Once developed, the DT outputs should undergo rigor-
ous field testing and validation to assess their effectiveness in real-world environments. 
Collaborations with building owners, facility/energy managers, and industry partners 
can facilitate pilot implementations and provide valuable feedback for further refine-
ment.
